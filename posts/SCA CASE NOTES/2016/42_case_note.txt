Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# Case Note: GrainCo (Pty) Ltd v Van der Merwe (20693/2014) [2016] ZASCA 42

### F - Facts

The case involves the appellant, GrainCo (Pty) Ltd (GrainCo), and the respondents, Mr Jacobus Alewyn van der Merwe, Mr Johannnes Jacobus Kitshoff, and Perdigon (Pty) Ltd, set against the backdrop of a business sale involving goodwill. GrainCo, originally known as "old GrainCo," was established in 2000 by Van der Merwe and Kitshoff, thriving in the grain commodities market ([para 4]).

In 2008, BKB Limited negotiated an amalgamation agreement to purchase the business of old GrainCo, including its goodwill, leading to the liquidation of old GrainCo ([para 8-17]). As part of the agreement, a significant restraint of trade was included, preventing Van der Merwe and Kitshoff from competing with BKB or soliciting its customers for five years ([para 13]).

However, following a downturn in business, Van der Merwe resigned as GrainCo’s managing director in March 2013. Shortly after, both he and Kitshoff established a competing business, Perdigon, which began operations nearby, using former GrainCo employees ([para 20-22]). GrainCo sought to interdict Van der Merwe and Kitshoff from soliciting its clients based on an implied prohibition against such actions when goodwill is sold, but the Western Cape High Court dismissed the application, prompting the appeal ([para 1-2]).

### I - Issues

The core issue in this case centers around whether an implied prohibition against canvassing customers, when a business is sold with its goodwill, binds parties other than the seller. Specifically, the question is whether Van der Merwe and Kitshoff, who were not direct sellers of the business, are nonetheless bound by this prohibition ([para 2]).

### R - Rules

The principles applicable to this case largely derive from established case law concerning the sale of goodwill and implied terms within contracts. The leading case, *Trego & another v Hunt*, elucidates that a seller of goodwill cannot solicit former customers post-sale, solidifying what is known as the Trego prohibition ([para 23]). Moreover, *A Becker & Co (Pty) Ltd v Becker & others* amplifies the application of this prohibition in South African law, affirming that it applies as an implied term imbued by law, particularly in cases concerning the sale of businesses with goodwill ([para 25-26]).

### A - Application

In applying these principles, the court noted that while the ruling in *Becker* supports the notion of an implied prohibition against solicitation of former customers, it is limited to those who are actual sellers of the business. Here, the amalgamation agreement clearly outlined that only GrainCo was the seller of the business to BKB. As such, Van der Merwe and Kitshoff, who were not parties to this agreement, could not be bound by its terms or the implied prohibition arising from it ([para 27]).

The court meticulously dissected the implications of the sale agreement’s clauses, emphasizing that while Van der Merwe and Kitshoff were involved in the business, they were not directly engaged in the sale transaction’s legalities. Thus, the judicial application ultimately rested on the understanding that the prohibition against soliciting wanted to prevent unfair competition from those who directly sold goodwill, a determination not applicable to the respondents in this case ([para 28]).

### C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the lower court’s ruling, dismissing the appeal with costs, including those of two counsel. The judgment reinforced that an implied prohibition against canvassing customers applies solely to sellers of goodwill, deriving from the contractual legalities surrounding the transaction in question. This delineation not only clarified the role of implied terms in business transactions but also addressed the scope of enforceability concerning individuals not party to a sale ([para 30]). 

This case not only reflects the nuanced interpretation of contract law in South Africa but also establishes clear boundaries regarding the extent to which non-parties can be held liable under provisions designed to protect the goodwill of a sold business.
