Pepkor Retail (Pty) Ltd v Truworths Ltd (900/2015) [2016] ZASCA 146

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 146

FIRAC Method Case Note

F - Facts

In this trademark dispute, Pepkor Retail (Pty) Ltd (hereafter referred to as "Ackermans") appealed against an order of the Western Cape Division of the High Court, where Truworths Ltd (hereafter referred to as "Truworths") had successfully obtained an interdict against Ackermans from using the trademark "THE LOOK" and its derivative phrase "THE LOOK FOR LESS" in its retail operation ([1]). Both parties operate in the fashion retail market, competing primarily in clothing and accessories. Ackermans brands itself as a value retailer, while Truworths is recognized as a more upscale competitor ([2]).

Truworths holds a registered trademark for "THE LOOK" since 1 April 1998 under the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 ([3]). However, it only began utilizing the mark significantly in 2009 (with evidence of store openings featuring "THE LOOK" as a secondary branding) ([4]). Conversely, Ackermans initiated rebranding in 2011, adopting the phrase "THE LOOK FOR LESS," which it began using prominently in early 2012 ([5]).

Disagreements culminated in Truworths objecting to Ackermans' use of the phrase and subsequently obtaining a court order against it. Ackermans responded with a counter-application to remove Truworths's trademark from the register based on claims of its non-distinctiveness and generic nature ([6], [8]). The initial judgment favored Truworths, leading to this appeal ([9]).

I - Issues

The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal centered on:

  1. Whether Truworths's trademark "THE LOOK" possesses inherent or acquired distinctiveness necessary for it to remain on the trademark register ([12]);
  2. The interpretation of the statutory provisions under the Trade Marks Act, specifically concerning s 10 relating to unregistrable trademarks ([10]).

R - Rules

The key legal provisions considered in this case were primarily contained in the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993. Notably:

  1. Section 9 requires that to be registrable, a trademark must have the capacity to distinguish the goods of one trader from those of another ([10]).
  2. Section 10 specifies certain categories of non-registrable trademarks, including those that lack distinctiveness, are descriptive of goods, or have become customary in trade ([12]).

Precedents referenced included the principles articulated in Beecham Group plc & another v Triomed (Pty) Ltd [2002] ZASCA 109, emphasizing that distinctiveness is fundamental for trademark validity ([10]).

A - Application

In its analysis, the Court evaluated the distinctiveness of the mark "THE LOOK." It noted that inherently descriptive phrases in general use (e.g., "the look" in fashion contexts) do not qualify for trademark protection, as demonstrated by Ackermans' evidence of how the phrase conveys generic meanings in the industry ([17], [18]).

The Court highlighted that Truworths failed to provide compelling evidence that the mark had acquired distinctiveness through its use since the mark was frequently displayed in association with the well-known "TRUWORTHS" brand ([21]). The lack of market survey evidence or substantial independent sentiment showing public association with Truworths undermined their claims ([20]).

Therefore, the court concluded that "THE LOOK" did not serve as a distinctive badge of origin as required by trade mark law, leading to the determination that Ackermans's counter-application for removal of the mark was valid ([22]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that Truworths's trademark "THE LOOK" did not meet the necessary criteria of distinctiveness and was liable to be removed from the trade mark register, effectively overturning the High Court's ruling in favor of Truworths ([23]). The court’s decision underscored the importance of distinctiveness in trademark law and established precedents for assessing trademark validity in the fashion retail industry. This ruling has significant implications, reinforcing the principle that descriptive marks cannot claim exclusivity within common language usage and giving insight into judicial expectations surrounding trademark distinctiveness in competitive markets.

The appeal was upheld, the court ordered costs to be paid by Truworths, and the counter-application for the cancellation of the trademark was granted ([23]). This case reiterates the balance that must be struck between protecting trademarks and ensuring that the language used in commerce remains accessible to all traders within an industry.
