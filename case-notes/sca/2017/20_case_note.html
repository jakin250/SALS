Supreme Court of Appeal

ZNK Investments CC v Luckytso Transport and Construction CC (328/2016) [2017] ZASCA 20

The case arose from a dispute concerning the validity of a sale in execution of a property belonging to Luckytso Transport and Construction CC (hereinafter referred to as "the first respondent"), which had been deregistered at the time of the sale.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 20

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts:

The case arose from a dispute concerning the validity of a sale in execution of a property belonging to Luckytso Transport and Construction CC (hereinafter referred to as "the first respondent"), which had been deregistered at the time of the sale. On 19 October 2011, the first respondent acquired an immovable property, secured by a mortgage bond in favor of ABSA Bank Limited (the "third respondent") ([para 2]).

After the first respondent failed to submit its annual returns, it was deregistered on 1 March 2013 in accordance with section 82(3)(a) of the Companies Act 71 of 2008 ([para 3]). Following this deregistration, the Sheriff of Pretoria East (the "fourth respondent") attached the property for sale in execution, which originally was set for 30 October 2013 but was postponed due to the second respondent's (Mr. Lucky Kgomotso Mokwena, the sole member of the first respondent) intervention. Ultimately, the property was sold to ZNK Investments CC ("the appellant") on 29 January 2014 for R2,520,000, without any of the parties being aware of the deregistration ([para 4]).

Subsequently, the first and second respondents sought relief from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, requesting the reinstatement of the first respondent and the nullification of the sale in execution ([para 5]). The court a quo granted the application, reinstated the first respondent, and declared the sale invalid, reasoning that the deregistration rendered the first respondent nonexistent, thereby making AWBA’s claim unenforceable ([para 6]). The appellant opposed this order but initially failed to overturn it in the court a quo.

I – Issues:

The primary legal question before the Supreme Court of Appeal concerns the effect of the retrospective reinstatement of the first respondent's registration on the validity of the sale in execution that took place while it stood deregistered. Specifically, the court needed to determine whether the reinstatement in accordance with section 82(4) of the Companies Act validated the sale executed during the deregistration period ([paras 7–9]).

R – Rules:

The applicable legal provisions included:

  1. Companies Act 71 of 2008: Specifically, section 82(4), which facilitates the retrospective reinstatement of a company or close corporation and validates its activities during the period of deregistration.
  2. Close Corporations Act 69 of 1984: Stipulates that provisions relating to deregistration must align with certain sections of the Companies Act.
  3. Case law precedents impacting the matter included:
  • ABSA Bank Ltd v Companies and Intellectual Property Commission & others 2013 (4) SA 194 (WCC), which held that property becomes bona vacantia upon deregistration, thus passing to the State.
  • Newlands Surgical Clinic (Pty) Ltd v Peninsula Eye Clinic (Pty) Ltd 2015 (4) SA 34 (SCA), which established that reinstatement under section 82(4) automatically and retrospectively reverts property ownership to the entity previously deregistered ([paras 8–9]).

A – Application:

In analyzing the facts, the Supreme Court of Appeal articulated that before the property could be considered validly sold, the legal ramifications of the reinstatement under section 82(4) must be examined. The court confirmed that this provision retroactively reinstates the property rights and validates corporate activity that occurred during the deregistration ([para 9]).

The court distinguished between the scenario presented, wherein the respondents failed to demonstrate any substantive claim for amelioration under section 83(4) that would challenge the retrospective effect of the reinstatement. It pointed out that the respondents had not made a case that they were unaware of the deregistration or had any defence against ABSA's claim ([paras 11–12]).

This reasoning effectively invalidated the ruling of the court a quo, establishing that despite the sale occurring while the first respondent was deregistered, the consequent reinstatement retroactively validated the sale, thus reinstating the property rights in the first respondent and nullifying the grounds on which the initial judgment rested ([para 10]).

C – Conclusion:

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, ruling that the retrospective effect of the reinstatement rendered the sale in execution valid ([para 13]). The prior ruling by the court a quo was set aside, and the respondents were directed to bear the costs of the application, including those incurred for two counsel ([para 13]). The implications of this judgment reinforce the principle that a deregistered company can regain its previous rights and obligations upon reinstatement, thus ensuring legal certainty in property transactions affected by deregistration. This case clarifies the operational impact of the Companies Act's reinstatement provisions, specifically regarding the validity of actions taken by companies during periods of deregistration.
