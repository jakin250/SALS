Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2002

# FIRAC Case Note: Telkom SA Limited v The Member of the Executive Council for Agricultural and Environmental Affairs: KwaZulu-Natal

### Citation
Telkom SA Limited v The Member of the Executive Council for Agricultural and Environmental Affairs: KwaZulu-Natal and Others [2002] ZASCA 26; Case No 516/2000, delivered on 5 September 2002.

### F – Facts
The appellant, Telkom SA Limited (Telkom), sought to lay a telecommunications cable on the seabed within the territorial waters off the coast of KwaZulu-Natal. The appellant argued that it was entitled to do so without a lease, as required by section 3(1)(m) of the Sea-shore Act 21 of 1935. The first respondent, the Member of the Executive Council for Agriculture and Environmental Affairs in KwaZulu-Natal, and the fifth respondent, The Prawn Fisheries and Development Association, opposed this claim. The objection centered on the assertion that the seabed had not been let to Telkom in accordance with the Sea-shore Act and that the proposed cable's route would significantly disrupt local prawn fishing activities ([1–3]).

The High Court had ruled in favour of the respondents, declaring the planned laying of the cable unlawful in the absence of the necessary leasing agreement ([4]). This ruling prompted Telkom to appeal the decision. By the time the appeal was heard, the cable in question had already been laid, though there was no indication that Telkom had acted in contempt of the court's earlier order ([5]).

### I – Issues
The principal issue before the Supreme Court was whether Telkom had the lawful right to lay the telecommunications cable on the seabed without obtaining a lease as stipulated by the Sea-shore Act. This brought forth legal interpretations of the following:
1. Does the Sea-shore Act require a lease for the laying of undersea cables?
2. Is Telkom exempt from such a requirement under section 70 of the Telecommunications Act 103 of 1996, which it argued supersedes the Sea-shore Act in this context? ([1–10]).

### R – Rules
The relevant laws and provisions discussed included:

1. **Sea-shore Act 21 of 1935**:
   - Section 2: Declares ownership of the sea and the seabed by the President of South Africa.
   - Section 3(1)(m): Empowers the competent authority to let portions of the seabed for various purposes, including laying cables.
   - Section 12A: Criminalizes the use of the sea without a lease.
   - Section 13(e): Exempts certain Acts, specifically the Post Office Act, from the Sea-shore Act.

2. **Telecommunications Act 103 of 1996**:
   - Section 70: Permits fixed-line operators to access land for the establishment of telecommunications facilities.

3. **Interpretation Act 33 of 1957**: Relevant for determining whether provisions from repealed legislation could apply to current statutes ([6–10], [40]).

### A – Application
The court analyzed the relationship between the Sea-shore Act and the Telecommunications Act, emphasizing that the statutory framework surrounding telecommunications had evolved over time, leading to the current interpretation of obligations and rights for telecommunications service providers. The key arguments included:

1. **Statutory Analysis**: The court concluded that the language of section 70 of the Telecommunications Act did not extend Telkom's rights to the seabed without the necessary lease established under the Sea-shore Act. It interpreted 'land' in the context of the Act as not including the seabed, distinguishing between terrestrial and aquatic contexts ([32–39]).

2. **Legislative Intent**: The historical context illustrated that the provisions regarding the laying of cables, both under prior legislation and current frameworks, were deliberately designed to ensure state control over marine resources. The court identified that the intention was to require a lease for any subsea cable installation to protect various stakeholders, including local fishing communities ([24–40]).

3. **Impact on Existing Rights**: The interpretation advocated by Telkom would undermine state control over marine cable management and did not align with the obligations set forth in the Sea-shore Act. The potential for multiple operators to lay cables without a lease was viewed as detrimental to order and regulation within the territorial waters ([39–40]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the High Court's ruling that Telkom required a lease under the Sea-shore Act before proceeding with the laying of undersea cables. The judgement reinforced the necessity for compliance with the Sea-shore Act's provisions, emphasizing the importance of regulatory controls and stakeholder consultation in marine and undersea activities. Consequently, this ruling established significant precedent regarding the interaction between telecommunications law and environmental regulatory frameworks within South Africa ([40–42]).

The costs of the appeal were also awarded against Telkom, as the respondents successfully obtained relief in the lower court ([41]).
