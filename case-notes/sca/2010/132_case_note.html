La Lucia Sands Share Block Ltd v Barkhan (37/10) [2010] ZASCA 132

Supreme Court of Appeal 2010 [2010] ZASCA 132

F – Facts

The case La Lucia Sands Share Block Limited v Barkhan ([1]) involves an appeal against a judgment from the KwaZulu-Natal High Court that mandated La Lucia Sands (the first appellant) to provide access to its register of members to the respondents, Howard Barkhan and Errol Glasser ([1]). The dispute, initiated in 2006, arose when Barkhan and Glasser sought to acquire membership in La Lucia Sands by reaching out to its shareholders. However, La Lucia Sands denied their requests to access its members' register, citing privacy concerns after prior tensions between the parties, including accusations of a "hostile takeover" attempt by renowned entities ([3]–[4]).

Barkhan and Glasser's initial request for access was made in May 2006, but it was rebuffed by George Wolfe, an attorney and director of La Lucia Sands, leading to a resolution by the company members that restricted member information disclosure ([4]). Following unsuccessful negotiations and communication, Barkhan and Glasser proceeded with an application in the Durban High Court, resulting in the order compelling the company to provide copies of its register or alternatively to allow its inspection ([1]). The Minister of Trade and Industry was also made a party to the case due to the statutory context provided by the Companies Act 61 of 1973 ([5]).

The High Court ruled that section 113 of the Companies Act justified the respondents' request for information, seeing it as pivotal for transparency and public interest ([6]). La Lucia Sands appealed this judgment, raising several arguments, including concerns about privacy enshrined in section 14 of the South African Constitution ([5]).

I – Issues

The appeal raised several pertinent legal questions:

  1. Interpretation of Section 113: Did the KwaZulu-Natal High Court correctly interpret and apply the provisions of section 113 of the Companies Act to justify the order requiring access to the register of members? ([2])
  2. Right to Privacy vs. Right to Access Information: How does the constitutional right to privacy of company members reconcile with the right to access information under section 32 of the Constitution? ([5], [13])
  3. Costs Order: Should the costs order in the High Court be reversed based on claims that an important constitutional issue was raised? ([5], [20])

R – Rules

The legal framework primarily revolves around the Companies Act 61 of 1973 and relevant constitutional provisions.

  • Section 113 of the Companies Act: This section empowers any member to inspect the register of members or seek copies thereof and outlines the obligations of the company to comply unless a legal exception applies ([8]).
  • Section 105 of the Companies Act: Discusses the requirement for companies to maintain a register of members that is accessible for transparency ([7]).
  • Section 32 of the Constitution: Provides everyone with the right of access to information held by another person, essential for protecting mine rights ([13]).

Precedent within case law, notably Bernstein v Bester and Pathescope, often emphasizes the public nature of company operations where shareholder information is concerned ([9], [21]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal assessed the High Court's interpretation of section 113. In the High Court, it was reasoned that the intention behind this legislative provision was to ensure access to crucial information relating to company governance and shareholder accountability, a public interest matter that outweighs individual privacy concerns ([6], [9]).

The court articulated that the request for information by Barkhan and Glasser to engage with shareholders regarding their interests in La Lucia Sands was legitimate, regardless of the appellants' apprehensions about possible hostile intentions ([12]). The preceding court also highlighted that accessibility bolstered transparency, a tenet of corporate governance. Thus, the rationale behind the section was deemed sound and applicable in the current context.

On the constitutional argument raised by La Lucia Sands, the court reflected on previous jurisprudence, notably stating that the disclosure of information associated with public interests does not constitute an infringement of privacy rights as prescribed by the Constitution ([21]). The court further noted that any request for the register of members does not necessitate the requester to provide a rationale, thus upholding the efficiency of section 113 and indicating that the privacy aspect was speciously invoked in this commercial context ([10], [12]).

Regarding the costs order, the court was unconvinced by La Lucia Sands' claim that the constitutional matter warranted a reversal of the costs against it. The demeanor and inflexible approach adopted by La Lucia Sands throughout the litigation were critiqued, emphasizing that such conduct merited a costs order against them ([20], [22]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed La Lucia Sands' appeal, affirming the High Court's directive to allow access to the register of members ([24]). The court reaffirmed the pivotal role of section 113 in fostering corporate transparency and the limitations to privacy claims in corporate operations, drawing from established case law. The decision has significant implications, stressing that the public interest in corporate governance must prevail in cases involving access to shareholders' information. The appeal dismissed and costs awarded, including those associated with two counsel, reflects the court's emphasis on accountability and conduct in corporate disputes, shedding light on the evolving landscape of company law and disclosures in South Africa.

Overall, this judgment underscores the imperative for companies to maintain transparency, especially concerning shareholder engagement in matters that may affect company control and operations, while also reiterating the crucial balance between privacy and public information access.
