Muldersdrift Sustainable Development Forum v Mogale City (20424/14) [2015] ZASCA 118

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 [2015] ZASCA 118

F – Facts

The appellant, Muldersdrift Sustainable Development Forum (hereafter "the Forum"), is an unincorporated voluntary association seeking to challenge the appointment of the third respondent, Mr. Dan Metlana Mashitisho, as the municipal manager of the first respondent, Mogale City Local Municipality (hereafter "the municipality"). The executive mayor, the second respondent, had initially appointed Mr. Mashitisho for a five-year term starting from October 2, 2006, which expired on September 30, 2011 ([1–2]).

On June 9, 2011, the municipality's council delegated certain powers to the executive mayor but did not specifically include the appointment of the municipal manager. In a contentious move, the executive mayor unilaterally extended Mr. Mashitisho’s contract on November 11, 2011, leading to his reappointment until September 30, 2016 ([3]). This extension was merely noted by the council in a meeting on February 28, 2012, without formal approval or proper procedures being followed as mandated by the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 (LGMS) ([4]).

The Forum filed a review application on October 22, 2012, concerning the municipal manager's decision to establish an emergency site. After learning of the reappointment, the Forum filed a second application (the current appeal) over a year later, seeking to declare the recent appointment invalid due to alleged breaches of statutory provisions, namely that the appointment should have been made by the municipal council, not the executive mayor ([5–6]).

I – Issues

The key legal issues before the court included ([6–7]):

  1. Whether the Forum had the locus standi to challenge the legality of the municipal manager's appointment.
  2. Whether the appointment of the municipal manager was valid under the LGMS, specifically concerning the required procedures for such an appointment.
  3. The extent to which the procedural defects in the reappointment affected the legitimacy of the appointment and the Forum's right to seek relief.

R – Rules

The applicable legal principles and legislative provisions included ([9–12]):

  1. Section 54A(1)(a) of the LGMS, which stipulates that the municipal council must appoint the municipal manager.
  2. Section 54A(3)(b), which renders any contract concluded in contravention of the LGMS null and void.
  3. Section 59 of the LGMS, specifying that a delegation of powers must not conflict with the Act itself, underscoring that the municipal council cannot delegate its power to appoint a municipal manager.
  4. Precedent from Oudekraal Estates (Pty) Ltd v City of Cape Town & others ([10]) which established that the right remedy must be sought by the right entity in appropriate proceedings.

A – Application

The court considered the procedural irregularities in the reappointment of the municipal manager, recognizing that the council had failed to follow the legislative requirements when delegating its powers to the executive mayor ([11–14]). While the extension of Mr. Mashitisho’s contract was procedurally defective, the court emphasized that a mere procedural irregularity does not automatically confer standing to the appellant.

Regarding locus standi, the court reiterated that to seek a declaration, the Forum needed to demonstrate a legally recognisable interest in the subject matter. The court referenced previous cases ([16–18]) which established that merely being a stakeholder (like a ratepayer or resident) is insufficient to qualify as a necessary interest unless further justified. The court noted that while residents have an interest in proper governance, this alone does not grant eligibility for court intervention without additional compelling reasons ([19]).

The court highlighted that procedural defects could not be remedied purely through an application for declaratory relief without establishing a legal interest, which the Forum failed to do ([24]). The Forum’s application appeared to be an auxiliary attempt to bolster a related but distinct legal challenge concerning the municipal manager’s decisions regarding the emergency site ([24]).

Moreover, had the Forum pursued its claims under section 38 of the Constitution or the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA), it would still encounter difficulties regarding timeliness and procedural propriety, as the appointment’s critiques were significantly delayed ([22–23]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the judgment of the court a quo, dismissing the appeal with costs. The court concluded that the Forum lacked the necessary legal standing to challenge the reappointment of the municipal manager. The dismissal reinforced the principles that not only must an applicant demonstrate sufficient interest to pursue declaratory relief, but the appointment’s procedural irregularities, while present, did not necessitate the invalidation of the municipal manager's position at that juncture ([25]).

In summary, the court's ruling validated the legality of the municipal council's decisions despite procedural missteps, promoting the principle of finality in administrative law and maintaining stability in local governance ([25]). The implications of this case underscore the importance of clarity in standing requirements for parties seeking judicial intervention in administrative decisions and reaffirm the necessity of following proper legislative processes in local government appointments.
