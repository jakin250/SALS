Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note

### Case Citation
**The Competition Commission v Computicket (853/13) [2014] ZASCA 185 (26 November 2014)**

---

### F - Facts
The matter at hand arises from a series of complaints against Computicket (Pty) Ltd, lodged with the Competition Commission (the Commission) pursuant to section 49B of the Competition Act 89 of 1998 (the Act) ([1]). The complaints alleged anti-competitive practices, specifically concerning exclusivity clauses in contracts between Computicket and various stakeholders in the entertainment sector.

After conducting an investigation, the Commission concluded that Computicket's activities constituted prohibited practices under the Act, leading to the referral of these complaints to the Competition Tribunal ([2]). Prior to the commencement of substantive hearings at the Tribunal, Computicket sought to review the Commission's referral decision, alleging that it lacked the requisite reasonableness and objectivity ([3]).

Following this request, Computicket filed an interlocutory application for the production of documents that informed the Commission's decision ([4]). The Tribunal's initial refusal to grant this request was later overturned by the Competition Appeal Court (CAC), which directed the Commission to produce certain evidentiary materials ([3]).

The Commission subsequently applied to the CAC for leave to appeal that decision, which was denied on September 20, 2013. The Commission then pursued an application for leave to appeal in the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ([4]).

---

### I - Issues
The principal issues revolve around:
1. The appellate jurisdiction of the SCA under the recently amended section 168(3) of the Constitution, particularly its implications on the jurisdiction to hear appeals concerning competition matters ([4]).
2. Whether the CAC's order concerning production of documents constitutes a "constitutional matter" as envisaged in section 62(2) of the Act, thereby qualifying the SCA to hear the appeal based on the criteria established in sections 62 and 63 of the Act ([6]).

---

### R - Rules
The legal framework relevant to this case includes:
1. **Competition Act 89 of 1998** – Specifically, sections 62 (concerning appellate jurisdiction) and 63 (pertaining to appeals and constitutional matters).
2. **Section 168(3) of the Constitution** – As amended, it delineates the SCA's jurisdiction and explicitly limits its appellate capacity in competition matters to those designated by statute.
3. **Case Law**:
   - **American Natural Soda Ash Corporation & another v Competition Commission** ([6]) and **National Union of Metal Workers of SA v Fry's Metals (Pty) Ltd** – Establishing that the appellate jurisdiction of the SCA could previously extend beyond confines set by specific statutes under the earlier interpretation of section 168(3).

---

### A - Application
The court conducted a thorough examination of the implications of the amended section 168(3) regarding appellate jurisdiction. It was determined that the amendment limits the SCA's ability to hear appeals on competition matters unless explicitly permitted by the provisions of the Act ([9]).

The SCA found that the CAC's judgment fell squarely within the ambit of section 62(1)(b), which rooted jurisdiction in the procedural functions of the CAC ([12]). However, the court ruled that the interlocutory nature of Computicket's request, while essential to the review application, does not equate to a question concerning the jurisdiction of the Tribunal as expressed in section 62(2)(a) ([13]).

Furthermore, the Commission's argument for the appeal being a "constitutional matter" was scrutinized through the lens of constitutional jurisprudence. The court acknowledged that while the principle of legality and the right to a fair hearing invoke constitutional considerations, these did not solely create a constitutional matter under section 62(2)(b) ([14][15]). 

The court concluded that the matter did not rise to the level of a constitutional issue warranting SCA jurisdiction. Furthermore, if it were to fall under constitutional matters, section 63(2) explicitly reserved such matters for the Constitutional Court exclusively, resulting in a denial of jurisdiction for the SCA ([17]).

---

### C - Conclusion
The SCA ultimately dismissed the application for leave to appeal, citing both a lack of jurisdiction to hear the appeal and separate grounds concerning the merits of Computicket's requests for the documentation ([23]). The ruling reinforces the constraints imposed by the amendments to the Constitution regarding appellate jurisdiction in competition law, illustrating a distinct separation of powers between the SCA and the Constitutional Court.

This judgment reflects a significant interpretation of the 17th Amendment and its practical implications for competition matters. The case underscores the importance of clear legislative delineation of appellate pathways within the South African legal system, especially in specialized fields such as competition law.
