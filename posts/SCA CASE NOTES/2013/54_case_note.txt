Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: De Klerk v Steven-Lee Properties (297/12) [2013] ZASCA 54

## F – Facts

At the center of this dispute lies the interpretation of clause 15.1 of two identical sale agreements. These agreements were entered into by the appellants, Eugene Bernhard De Klerk and Tanya De Klerk, and the first respondent, Steven-Lee Properties (Pty) Ltd, concerning two properties within a development located in Vanderbijlpark, part of the Emfuleni Local Municipality ([1]–[2]). The agreements were signed in March 2007, and the properties were subsequently registered in the names of the appellants in May and June 2007 for purchase prices of R261,000 and R271,000 respectively ([3]).

Clause 15.1 states: "The DEVELOPER shall make the arrangements to the satisfaction of the appropriate local authority for the provision of essential services to the street border of the property" ([4]). Before the sale, the first respondent sought approval for the proposed township development from both the municipality and Rand Water Board. Rand Water approved the proposed sanitation system, contingent on upgrades to existing municipal pump stations. The requisite consent from the municipality was granted in February 2006 ([5]).

However, in late 2009, disputes arose regarding the municipal pump stations, leading to a refusal by Rand Water to approve further building until the municipality addressed the inadequacies in the effluent waste management ([6]). The appellants contended that clause 15.1 created a suspensive condition which had not been fulfilled. They further asserted that the term "appropriate local authority" referred specifically to Rand Water, not the municipality ([7]).

The appellants sought a court ruling declaring the agreements unenforceable or asserting their lawful cancellation due to non-performance by the first respondent ([2]).

## I – Issues

The case raises several significant legal questions:

1. **Interpretation of Clause 15.1**: Did clause 15.1 create a suspensive condition regarding the provision of essential services?
2. **Definition of "Appropriate Local Authority"**: Who constitutes the "appropriate local authority" referenced in the clause?
3. **Compliance with Conditions**: Did the first respondent fulfill its obligations as outlined in the sale agreements?
4. **Existence of a Tacit Term**: Was there a tacit term within the agreements entitling the appellants to construct dwellings within a stipulated time?

These issues necessitated a thorough examination of the contractual obligations and the interpretation of related municipal and statutory provisions ([8]).

## R – Rules

The ruling relied on established principles of contract law, especially regarding suspensive conditions. A suspensive condition, as defined in *Design and Planning Service v Kruger*, refers to obligations that are dependent on uncertain future events. The court distinguished between conditions that suspend the operation of contractual obligations and terms that impose mandatory duties ([9]).

Furthermore, the court referenced the South African Constitution, particularly Chapter 7, Section 151, which governs local government, and outlined the distinction between a municipal authority and statutory bodies like Rand Water ([16]). 

Relevant precedents from ***Ehlers v Rand Water Board*** and ***Rand Water Board v Milner*** clarified the statutory functions of Rand Water and emphasized that it does not qualify as a local authority ([16]).

## A – Application

In applying these rules, the court concluded that clause 15.1 did **not** constitute a suspensive condition. The circumstances at the time of the sale's conclusion indicated that the necessary municipal approvals had already been met prior to the agreements being executed ([10]). The clause was interpreted as a requirement for the developer to make reasonable arrangements with the local authority for service delivery, rather than a conditional obligation that was yet to be fulfilled at the time of registration ([10], [13]).

The court found the appellants' position unsubstantiated regarding their assertion that "appropriate local authority" referred to Rand Water. It reasoned that the appellant's reliance on documents they were not party to (the Services Agreement and Guide Plan Annexure C) was misplaced and did not support their argument ([15], [18]). 

Additionally, the court noted that the issue of a tacit term was inadequately formulated by the appellants, lacking precise terms necessary for its inference. Therefore, there existed no tacit term giving rise to an obligation on the part of the first respondent to secure approvals from Rand Water after the properties were registered ([18]).

The court concluded that any failures in the municipal infrastructure could not be attributed to the first respondent, who had acted in accordance with the agreed terms, and hence there was no basis for cancellation of the agreements ([20]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the ruling of the South Gauteng High Court, thus holding that the agreements were enforceable despite the municipality's infrastructural shortcomings. The court's ruling emphasized the contractual interpretation of clause 15.1 as a term rather than a suspensive condition, recognizing the municipality, not Rand Water, as the appropriate local authority in reference to Clause 15.1 ([22]). 

This case underscores crucial principles of contract interpretation, highlighting the importance of clarity in contractual terms, especially concerning obligations dependent on third-party compliance. The ruling not only clarifies the responsibilities of property developers in South Africa in relation to local government regulations but also delineates the limitations of contractual liability concerning indirect dependencies on municipal infrastructure services.
