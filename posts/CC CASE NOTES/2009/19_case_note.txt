# FIRAC Case Note: **Hassam v Jacobs NO and Others** 

## F – Facts

The case revolves around an application for the confirmation of a declaration of constitutional invalidity regarding **section 1(4)(f)** of the **Intestate Succession Act 81 of 1987** (the “Act”). This provision was declared inconsistent with the Constitution by the Western Cape High Court, which found that it discriminated against widows of polygynous marriages celebrated according to Muslim rites by limiting the definition of “spouse” to a single spouse only ([1]).

The applicant, **Fatima Gabie Hassam**, was married to **Mr. Ebrahim Hassam** in accordance with Muslim rites. Upon the death of Mr. Hassam in 2001, it was revealed that he had married a second wife, **Mariam Hassam**, without Fatima's knowledge or consent, and died intestate. The first respondent, **Johan Hermanus Jacobs**, as the executor of the deceased’s estate, refused to recognize Fatima as a spouse for the purpose of the Act despite her marriage ([3]–[4]).

The High Court's decision addressed the failure of the Act to recognize multiple spouses in polygynous Muslim marriages, constituting discrimination against the widows of such marriages ([2], [8]). Notably, the applicant's legal challenge also sought to amend her status affecting the Maintenance of Surviving Spouses Act, although this was not directly addressed in the constitutional declaration ([6]).

## I – Issues

The core issues for the Court's determination included:

1. **Does the exclusion of spouses in polygynous Muslim marriages from the intestate succession regime violate section 9(3) of the Constitution?**
   - i. Does the exclusion constitute discrimination?
   - ii. If so, does it constitute unfair discrimination?
   - iii. Is any unfair discrimination justifiable under section 36 of the Constitution?
  
2. **If this exclusion violates section 9(3), can “spouse” in the Act be interpreted to include spouses in polygynous Muslim marriages?**

3. **If such an interpretation is not feasible, what is the appropriate relief?** ([20]–[21]).

## R – Rules

The judgment relied extensively on the principles of equality enshrined in **section 9** of the Constitution, which prohibits unfair discrimination based on various grounds including marital status, gender, and religion ([22]). The Court cited its own previously established reasoning in **Harksen v Lane NO** and **Daniels v Campbell NO** to elaborate on the appropriate considerations for establishing whether discrimination had occurred and whether it was unfair ([23]).

The key regulatory framework included:
- **Intestate Succession Act 81 of 1987** – specifically how it defined a “spouse.”
- **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996** – particularly sections 9 (equality), 15 (freedom of religion), 30, and 31 (cultural rights) ([30]).

The interpretive approach mandated by **section 39(2)** of the Constitution demands that any law or provision be interpreted in accordance with the spirit and values of the Bill of Rights ([24]) and reinforced the notion that protections must be extended to all social realities, including those surrounding polygynous Muslim marriages ([28]).

## A – Application

The Court reaffirmed that the Act’s exclusion of spouses in polygynous Muslim marriages clearly discriminated against the applicant based on her marital status, gender, and religion. It outlined that such exclusion had serious practical consequences for women confined to polygamous marriages, noting that they were often left without legal recognition and protection ([30]–[34]). 

The application of the **Harksen** test revealed that the differentiation made by the Act did not achieve any legitimate governmental purpose and resulted in unfair discrimination ([28], [38]). 

The Court also acknowledged the need to respect and accommodate diversity in the South African context, stating that the current legislative framework must reflect an ethos of tolerance, pluralism, and respect for human dignity ([24]). Thus, it determined that the Act's existing wording that defines "spouse" without considering polygamous arrangements failed to uphold these constitutional principles ([47]).

Moreover, the failure to include multiple spouses in the definition detracted from the intended protections of the Act, thereby violating the principles of substantive equality ([34]).

## C – Conclusion

The Court concluded that the exclusion of widows in polygynous Muslim marriages from the protection of the Act is inconsistent with constitutional provisions, and therefore, invalid. It ordered that the term "spouse" be interpreted to include "spouses" wherever it appeared, thus providing equal protection under the law for widows in polygynous marriages ([57]). 

The operative declaration of invalidity was made retroactive, providing relief to those in similar situations who had been previously excluded from the Act's protections since **27 April 1994**. The Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development was ordered to pay the applicant’s costs, recognizing that state inaction regarding the legislative reform contributed to the case ([56]). 

This ruling emphasizes the transformative role of the Constitution in recognizing the rights of marginalized groups and the necessity of legislative frameworks that reflect the diverse realities of South African society. The Court's decision led to a significant shift in the legal understanding of matrimonial status, cultivating a more inclusive approach to marriage laws in South Africa.
