Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2005

## FIRAC Case Note: The Road Accident Fund v Petrus Jacobus Delport NO (Case No. 183/2004)

**F - Facts**: 
The respondent in this case is the curator ad litem of Helen van Rooyen, who suffered catastrophic and permanent injuries due to a motor vehicle collision on 16 January 1997. Following the accident, the curator instituted a legal action against the Road Accident Fund (the appellant) to recover damages for the patient’s injuries. At trial, the Pretoria High Court, presided over by Hartzenberg J, awarded a total of R3,616,697.57 in damages, comprising past medical expenses (R525,774.58), loss of earning capacity (R1,840,923.00), and general damages for pain and suffering (R1,250,000.00) ([paras 1-2]). The appellant only appealed the components concerning the loss of earning capacity and general damages, seeking a reduction of these amounts ([para 2]). 

The patient, rendered totally disabled by the accident, had a documented work history including roles as a personnel clerk and later a personnel officer, where she earned significant income ([paras 3-5]). The trial court found that the patient had a life expectancy of 22 years in 2003 and that she had been highly motivated and industrious, likely returning to work shortly after her injury ([paras 4, 16]). Her injuries included severe physical and cognitive impairments, profoundly affecting her quality of life and ability to work ([paras 9-13]).

**I - Issues**: 
1. Did the trial court err in its assessment of the loss of earning capacity?
2. Was the amount awarded for general damages for pain and suffering appropriate given the severity of the injuries and loss of amenities of life? ([paras 2, 21]).

**R - Rules**: 
The applicable rule for assessing damages in personal injury cases establishes that courts have discretion in awarding damages for non-patrimonial loss, like pain and suffering ([para 22]), and they primarily rely on the evidence and findings of fact presented at trial. The appellate court generally does not interfere with the lower court's award unless there is a clear misdirection or substantial disparity between awards of like cases ([para 22]).

The reasoning in **Marine & Trade Insurance Co Ltd v Katz NO** [1979] 4 SA 961 (A) provided a precedent for evaluating general damages ([para 23]), while **De Jongh v Du Pisanie NO** [2004] 2 All SA 565 (SCA) discussed the importance of consistency and proportionality in awards ([para 24]).

**A - Application**: 
The court analyzed the appellant's arguments regarding the loss of earning capacity. It was determined that the patient would have likely returned to the workforce around August 1997 instead of March 1998, which the appellant disputed ([para 16]). The trial court's use of an income of R46,560 annually for calculations was also challenged. However, the appellate court supported the lower court's judgment, stating that the assumptions made favored the appellant and were substantiated by the uncontested evidence provided by the patient's husband about her earnings ([para 17]).

Furthermore, regarding the claim that the patient’s institutionalization reduced her living expenses, the court emphasized the lack of evidence supporting this assertion. The trial court had correctly dismissed such arguments, asserting that the appellant could not claim operational cost savings derived from the patient’s condition resulting from the tortious act ([para 19-20]).

Concerning general damages, the court found no misdirection in the trial judge's assessment, confirming that the award of R1,250,000 aligned well with the patient's extensive and severe suffering, loss of marital relationship, and ability to engage in life's normal activities. Comparisons with previous case awards revealed that the trial court's decision fell within acceptable parameters given the uniqueness of each case ([para 22-25]).

**C - Conclusion**: 
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appeal had no merit and dismissed it with costs, emphasizing the appellant's conduct post-trial as indicative of a lack of regard for the statutory obligations owed to the patient. The court underscored the importance of timely compensatory action by entities, such as the Road Accident Fund, particularly in light of the devastating consequences faced by victims of road accidents ([paras 26-30]). The court's ruling affirms the autonomy of the trial court in awarding damages while reinforcing the standard practice of deference to its judgments unless compelling justification exists for repeal. 

This case reinforces both the importance of accurate and comprehensive medical and personal testimony in informing damage assessments and the moral and legal obligations of parties in civil liability contexts to mitigate suffering through prompt compensation.
