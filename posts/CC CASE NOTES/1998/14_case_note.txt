Source Folder: 1998

### FIRAC Case Note: Moosa Osman and Mohamed Shiraz Osman v The Attorney-General for the Transvaal

**F – Facts**  
The case, Moosa Osman and Mohamed Shiraz Osman v The Attorney-General for the Transvaal, came before the Constitutional Court of South Africa on appeal from the Transvaal High Court ([1]). The appellants were charged under section 36 of the General Law Amendment Act 62 of 1955 for possession of stolen property, specifically tyres, for which they allegedly could not provide a satisfactory explanation ([2]). At trial, the appellants contended that section 36 was unconstitutional, arguing that it conflicted with the rights afforded by sections 25(2)(c) and 25(3)(c) of the interim Constitution ([3]). 

Given the procedural history, the appellants initially sought to have the proceedings stayed but ultimately agreed to the jurisdiction of the High Court under section 101(6) of the interim Constitution ([4]). The High Court dismissed their application, finding no conflict between the statute and constitutional rights ([5]). Subsequently, leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court was granted ([6]).

**I – Issues**  
The principal legal question before the court was whether section 36 of the Act infringed upon the rights to a fair trial as articulated in sections 25(2)(c) and 25(3)(c) of the interim Constitution ([6]). Specifically, the court had to determine whether the requirement for an accused to provide an account of possession constituted a violation of the rights against self-incrimination and the right to silence ([9]).

**R – Rules**  
The court examined the applicable provisions of the interim Constitution, focusing particularly on sections 25(2)(c) and 25(3)(c), which enshrine the right not to be compelled to make admissions that could incriminate oneself and the right to a fair trial ([3], [10]). The court also referenced preceding cases such as *S v Mhlungu* and *Du Plessis v De Klerk* to establish the legal framework surrounding these rights and the presumption of innocence ([6], [10]). 

Significantly, section 36 itself was scrutinised; it establishes a framework whereby possession of goods with a reasonable suspicion of having been stolen constitutes an offence if the possessor cannot satisfactorily account for such possession ([8], [16]). The court noted the critical jurisprudential principle that the burden of proof lies with the state to demonstrate guilt beyond a reasonable doubt, and the presumption of innocence is paramount in criminal proceedings ([12],[16]).

**A – Application**  
The court methodically applied these legal principles to the facts at hand. First, it clarified that section 36 does not compel an individual to provide a statement; rather, the right to choose whether to furnish an explanation for possession exists ([11]). The court asserted that while the inability to provide a satisfactory explanation may contribute to an inference of guilt, such a condition arises due to the factual circumstances of each case, rather than any direct imposition by the statute itself ([19], [23]).

The court rejected the argument that the statute exerted coercive pressure on the appellants, noting that the element of ‘inability to provide an explanation’ does not infringe upon the right to silence as long as the burden of proof remains with the prosecution ([12], [16]). The appellants’ right to a fair trial, as articulated in section 25(3)(c), was deemed unimpacted by section 36 ([24]). 

Moreover, the judgment recognized the historical context of these rights, reflecting on past abuses under apartheid and the necessity to uphold the integrity of the judicial process during trials ([10], [11]). Citing precedents, the court concluded that it is acceptable in adversarial legal systems to draw adverse inferences if the state substantiates a prima facie case while the accused opts to remain silent ([21], [24]).

**C – Conclusion**  
The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the appeal, concluding that section 36 of the General Law Amendment Act does not violate the rights enshrined in sections 25(2)(c) and 25(3)(c) of the interim Constitution ([27]). The court's ruling confirmed the notion that the legal responsibility to provide evidence lies with the prosecution and that an accused’s failure to testify does not equate to a violation of their constitutional rights; instead, it highlights the inherent risks in the adversarial system of justice ([23], [24]). The judgment affirms the delicate balance between state interests in prosecuting crime and the protection of individual rights, particularly in a transformative constitutional context.
