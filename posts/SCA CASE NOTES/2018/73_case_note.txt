Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: ST v CT (1224/16) [2018] ZASCA 73 (30 May 2018) 

## FIRAC Analysis

### F - Facts 

The case `ST v CT` presents a contentious divorce proceeding. The parties, Mr. ST (the Appellant) and Mrs. CT (the Respondent), were married in Germany on July 17, 1992. They entered into an antenuptial agreement that established their marriage as out of community of property, incorporating the accrual system ([1-3]). Key issues in dispute included spousal maintenance, accrual of assets, and the validity of a maintenance waiver clause contained in their antenuptial contract.

Upon marriage, the couple's respective estates were declared as nil, and both parties had substantial professional backgrounds—Mr. ST as an experienced advocate and Mrs. CT as a lawyer, albeit not qualified in South Africa ([4-6]). 

Their relationship deteriorated around early 2010, leading Mrs. CT to initiate divorce proceedings in November 2010, claiming maintenance, a declaration of Mr. ST's assets, and half of accrued growth in his estate. Mr. ST counterclaimed for property-related expenses, seeking restitution for joint property expenditures ([7]). 

The dispute revolved significantly around the enforceability of Clause 9 of their antenuptial agreement, wherein Mrs. CT waived her right to maintenance, with the High Court holding this clause to be invalid ([8-10]). The court awarded partial maintenance, held Mr. ST's estate accrual at R8,892,482, and scrutinized numerous undisclosed assets and financial discrepancies presented by Mr. ST throughout the legal process ([18-24]).

### I - Issues 

Key legal issues arising from this case included:

1. Whether the antenuptial agreement's waiver of maintenance (Clause 9) is enforceable or violates principles of public policy ([9-10]).
2. How the accrual of both parties’ estates should be calculated, including the necessity for full disclosure of assets in terms of Section 7 of the Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984 ([11-12]).
3. What principles govern the burden of proof concerning excluded assets from accrual calculations and whether the living annuity is included within Mr. ST's estate for accrual calculations ([41-42]).
4. Determining appropriate maintenance amounts for the Respondent given both parties' financial circumstances and earning capacities ([137-156]).

### R - Rules 

The judgment referenced primary statutory principles and case law, including:

1. **Section 7 of the Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984**: This section mandates full disclosure of estate particulars and governs accrual calculations in divorce proceedings ([33-34]).
2. **Public Policy Considerations**: The court examined cases referencing public policy relating to maintenance agreements, particularly under the Divorce Act provisions that illustrate the inherent rights for spousal maintenance in divorce scenarios ([171-174]).
3. **Burden of Proof**: The court applied a standard requiring that the Appellant prove exclusion of particular assets from accrual calculation, per previously established judicial precedents ([37-38]).
4. **Legal Precedents**: Papers from past cases (e.g., `Dhlumayo`, `Brookstein`, `M v M`) provided context for the court's reasoning on matrimonial agreements and maintenance rights ([12, 19, 40]).

### A - Application 

In applying the law to the facts:

1. The court affirmed the High Court's determination that the maintenance waiver was unenforceable, aligning with broader public policy conceptualizations that aim to protect spouses from enduring financial hardship post-divorce, irrespective of prior agreements ([9-10, 176]).
2. It concluded that Mr. ST had notably failed to demonstrate transparency with his asset disclosures as required under the **Matrimonial Property Act**, which severely affected his credibility and the calculations surrounding his estate's accrual value ([44-47, 57-59]).
3. The living annuity was ultimately excluded from Mr. ST's estate for accrual calculations based on findings from analogous cases and the legal commentary suggesting that such annuities do not represent liquid assets within a marital estate ([102-109]).
4. Maintenance was structured where the Respondent was awarded R18,500 for the first year, followed by R13,500, then R8,500 for subsequent years, adjusted for inflation, reflecting a nuanced consideration of marital contributions and future financial needs ([137-156]).

### C - Conclusion 

The Supreme Court of Appeal granted the appeal in part, affirming the High Court's judgment regarding the unfeasibility of the maintenance waiver while adjusting the maintenance and accrual awards. The final ruling held Mr. ST liable for half of the designated accrual following the challenging assessment of asset ownership, while requiring complete clarity in disclosures relevant to future earnings and obligations. The case underscores the critical intersection of contractual agreements and statutory requirements within South African matrimonial law, particularly highlighting the courts' ongoing commitment to fair outcomes that reflect marital contributions ([168-169]).

This judgment may set a significant precedent in matrimonial jurisprudence, particularly addressing the enforceability of prenuptial agreements and limitations on maintenance rights under divorce laws, echoing the evolving landscape of marital responsibilities and financial rights in South Africa.
