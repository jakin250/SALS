# Case Note: Loureiro and Others v iMvula Quality Protection (Pty) Ltd [2014] ZACC 4

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

The applicants, **Licinio Loureiro**, **Venessa Loureiro**, **Luca-Filipe Loureiro**, and **Jean-Enrique Loureiro**, initiated a claim against the respondent, **iMvula Quality Protection (Pty) Ltd**, following a violent robbery at their home in Melrose, Johannesburg, on **22 January 2009**. The Loureiro family had contracted iMvula to provide 24-hour armed guarding services due to previous criminal incidents, including a robbery at their prior residence. The service agreement required iMvula to take "all reasonable steps" to prevent unauthorized access to the property ([paras 5–9]).

On the fateful night, the security guard on duty, Mr. Mahlangu, inadvertently permitted robbers—disguised as police officers—entry into the house by opening the pedestrian gate without confirming their identity, thus failing to adhere to the explicit instructions of Mr. Loureiro ([paras 15–16]). In total, the intruders made off with valuables worth over R11 million ([para 10]).

The Loureiros successfully argued in the High Court that iMvula was liable in both contract and delict for the damages sustained. However, this decision was overturned by the Supreme Court of Appeal, leading the Loureiros to seek leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court ([paras 18–20]).

### I – Issues

1. **Leave to Appeal**: Should the Constitutional Court grant leave to appeal based on the constitutional issues raised about the balance between private contract law and the constitutional rights to security and protection?
  
2. **Breach of Contract**: Is iMvula liable for breaching its contractual obligations under the agreement with the Loureiros?

3. **Delictual Liability**: Is iMvula liable in delict for the negligent actions of its employee, Mr. Mahlangu, which resulted in the loss sustained by the Loureiros? ([paras 6–8]).

### R – Rules

The Court relied on various legal principles, including:

1. **Contract Law**: The interpretation of contractual obligations is determined by the intention of the parties involved. Notably, the prohibition against allowing entry without prior authorization was deemed to impose strict liability ([para 41]).

2. **Delict Law**: The elements of delict require a finding of wrongfulness and negligence. The standard employed for determining negligence is based on the conduct of a reasonable person in similar circumstances ([paras 49–56]).

3. **Common Law and Constitutional Rights**: The Court acknowledged that the law of contract and delict must align with the constitutional rights to security and protection, understanding that civil liability provides an avenue for enforceable rights under the Bill of Rights ([paras 33–34]).

### A – Application

The Constitutional Court outlined that for Mr. Mahlangu’s actions to constitute a breach of contract, the key issue was the interpretation of the contractual clause that prohibited unauthorized access. The Court concluded that this clause indeed imposed strict liability, as it was clear that the parties intended to prevent unauthorized access without requiring a fault standard. Thus, Mr. Mahlangu’s opening the gate breached the agreement, making iMvula liable ([paras 38–46]).

Regarding delictual liability, the inquiry turned to whether Mr. Mahlangu acted negligently. The Court assessed whether a reasonable security guard would have foreseen the risk of an unauthorized individual claiming to be a police officer. The circumstances, such as the nature of the vehicle and the manner of the man's entry, indicated that Mr. Mahlangu failed to exercise the requisite standard of care expected from a trained security professional ([para 59–66]). This failure resulted in both a breach and a wrongful act, cementing iMvula's vicarious liability for the loss suffered by the Loureiros.

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court upheld the appeal, reversing the decision of the Supreme Court of Appeal. The Court declared iMvula liable in both contract and delict, emphasizing that the guard's failure to verify the identity of claimed police officers breached the contract and constituted negligence under delict law. The Court's finding affirms the increasing importance of enforcing private security companies' accountability in safeguarding citizens' rights and maintaining constitutional values of safety and security ([para 67–69]). 

This case underscores the significance of clarity and accountability in security contracts, reinforcing the dual obligation of performance and duty of care standard expected from private service providers alongside constitutional imperatives ([paras 1–2]).
