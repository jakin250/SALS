Case Title: The Teddy Bear Clinic for Abused Children & RAPCAN v. Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development & National Director of Public Prosecutions
Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa
Case Number: CCT 12/13
Judgment Date: 3 October 2013
Citation: [2013] ZACC 35

Facts

This case arose from an application for confirmation of a ruling by the North Gauteng High Court that declared certain provisions of the Criminal Law (Sexual Offences and Related Matters) Amendment Act 32 of 2007 (Sexual Offences Act) constitutionally invalid. The applicants, The Teddy Bear Clinic for Abused Children and RAPCAN, argued that sections 15 and 16 of the Act, which criminalised consensual sexual conduct between children aged 12 to 16 years, infringed their constitutional rights ([2], [25]).

The Sexual Offences Act aimed to protect children from exploitation and abuse, but the applicants contended that the breadth and harshness of its provisions harmed the very children it intended to protect, exposing them to the criminal justice system in a detrimental manner ([10], [19], [28]). Both applicants had significant experience working with child victims of sexual abuse, emphasizing the harms of criminalisation and the need for support rather than punitive measures ([5], [6]).

The High Court found that the provisions indeed violated the rights to dignity, privacy, and the best interests of the child under sections 10, 14, and 28(2) of the Constitution, ruling that these violations were not justifiable under section 36 ([27]).

Issues

  1. Rights Limitation: Whether the sections of the Sexual Offences Act imposed limitations on fundamental rights as guaranteed by the Constitution ([37]).
  2. Justification for Limitations: If so, whether these limitations are reasonable and justifiable under section 36 of the Constitution ([37]).
  3. Appropriate Remedy: What the appropriate remedy is for any constitutional invalidity found ([37]).

Rules

The key legal principles involved in this case include:

  • Protection of Children: The Constitution mandates that the best interests of the child are paramount in all matters affecting them ([28(2)]).
  • Rights to Dignity and Privacy: Sections 10 and 14 of the Constitution guarantee the rights to dignity and privacy for all individuals, including children ([59]).
  • Constitutional Validity and Section 36 Analysis: The criteria for justifying limitations of rights under section 36 require that limitations must be reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society ([36]).

The court relied on previous case law to affirm that children enjoy fundamental rights and that limitations, especially those imposed by the state, must be scrutinised rigorously ([38], [39]).

Application

The court established that sections 15 and 16 of the Sexual Offences Act limited adolescents' rights to dignity, privacy, and the principle of their best interests:

  1. Dignity: The court acknowledged the degrading nature of the criminalisation of consensual sexual conduct among adolescents, which contributed to stigma and shame ([52], [55]). The robust definition of dignity in South African jurisprudence underscores the importance of respecting adolescents as individual rights bearers, not merely extensions of their parents ([53]).
  2. Privacy: Sections 15 and 16 intrude upon the core personal realm of adolescents’ intimate relationships, thereby violating their right to privacy. The court held that the compulsive reporting requirements exacerbate this invasion and deter open communication about sexual issues between adolescents and responsible adults ([60], [62]).
  3. Best Interests: The court found the impugned provisions could not be justified as serving the best interests of children because they may lead to unjust exposure to the criminal justice system, ultimately harming adolescents by cutting off avenues for guidance and support ([72], [73]).
  4. Justification Failure: The state failed to provide persuasive evidence that the provisions effectively deterred risky sexual behaviour among adolescents. Expert testimony indicated that criminalisation often drives these behaviours underground, contrary to intended protective outcomes ([87], [90]).

Conclusion

The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court's ruling, declaring sections 15 and 16 of the Sexual Offences Act unconstitutional regarding the criminalisation of consensual sexual conduct among adolescents aged 12 to 16. The court ordered a suspension of the declaration of invalidity for 18 months, allowing Parliament to amend the law in a manner consistent with constitutional principles ([117(2)]).

Moreover, a moratorium was imposed on all related criminal investigations and prosecutions concerning adolescents under these sections until legislative reform occurred. The court emphasized that while the provisions served to protect children from sexual exploitation, they were ultimately irrational and counterproductive when applied to consensual conduct among peers ([112]). Consequently, the respondents were ordered to pay the applicants' legal costs ([116]).
