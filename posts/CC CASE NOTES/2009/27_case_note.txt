# Case Notes: Ptrue Bothma v Petrus Arnoldus Els and Others

**Citation:**  
_Lang-List_ [2009] ZACC 27; Case CCT 21/09

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

Mrs. Ptrue Bothma, the applicant, initiated a private prosecution in 2007 against Mr. Petrus Arnoldus Els for rapes allegedly committed between 1968 and 1970, when she was a minor ([1]). The applicant contended that these acts persisted for over two years. Mr. Els, an older family friend, denied the allegations and sought a permanent stay of the prosecution from the Northern Cape High Court, asserting that the long delay would impair his right to a fair trial ([1]–[8]).

The High Court granted the stay, attributing full culpability for the delay to Mrs. Bothma and deeming her explanations—primarily related to psychological trauma—as unpersuasive. The court concluded that the resulting prejudice to Mr. Els was such that he would not receive a fair trial, thus effectively closing the judicial doors on Mrs. Bothma’s prosecution ([18]–[21]).

Mrs. Bothma appealed to the Constitutional Court, arguing that the High Court’s decision ignored the nature of the offense and the factors surrounding her delay, including psychological impacts that contributed to her inability to report previously ([18]–[24]). 

### I – Issues

The core issues addressed by the Constitutional Court included:

1. Whether the High Court erred in granting a permanent stay of prosecution due to unreasonable delay.
2. Whether full culpability for the delay could justly be assigned to Mrs. Bothma, particularly considering her psychological trauma and its effects on her ability to report the crime.
3. The interplay between the right to a fair trial for Mr. Els and Mrs. Bothma’s right to have her complaints heard in a court ([27]–[39]).

### R – Rules

The Court referenced the South African Constitution, specifically Section 35(3)(d), which provides that every accused person has the right to a fair trial without unreasonable delay ([28]). Relevant case law included _Sanderson v Attorney-General, Eastern Cape_, which elaborated on the balancing test for adjudicating claims of unreasonable delay. The Court emphasized the significance of the nature of the crime, particularly child rape, and the public interest in prosecuting such offenses ([19]) ([30]–[34]).

### A – Application

The Constitutional Court, led by Justice Sachs, articulated that while the delay of 37 years was indeed significant, this factor should not preclude Mrs. Bothma’s complaint from being adjudicated. The Court underscored the special circumstances surrounding child rape cases, where victims, often burdened by feelings of shame and fear, may take years to come forward ([62]–[66]). The judgment criticized the High Court's narrow focus, which failed to adequately incorporate how the nature of the offense contributed to Mrs. Bothma’s delay and her credibility as a witness ([84]–[88]).

The Court rebutted the High Court’s assertion of irreparable prejudice to Mr. Els, clarifying that the loss of evidence due to time lapse, while impactful, does not inherently equate to irreparable trial prejudice. The fairness of the trial must be ensured through the trial process itself, which includes evaluating all evidence presented ([76]–[85]).

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded that the High Court had erred in its judgment by not giving sufficient regard to the nature of the offense and the complexities surrounding the delay in reporting the crime. Therefore, the Court set aside the permanent stay of prosecution, allowing the matter to proceed to trial ([7], [100]). The ruling highlights the importance of balancing the rights of the accused with the rights of victims, particularly in cases involving sexual violence. The decision affirms the necessity for judicial systems to adapt in acknowledging the unique dynamics involved in sexual crimes, hence protecting the rights of marginalized victims while also maintaining the integrity of the accused’s right to a fair trial ([90]–[96]).

**Outcome:** Mrs. Bothma was permitted to pursue her private prosecution against Mr. Els, with costs awarded in her favor for both the High Court and Constitutional Court proceedings ([100]). The judgment reiterates the importance of careful consideration of the rights of both parties involved, advocating for a just process that allows for the truth to be determined through a fair trial.
