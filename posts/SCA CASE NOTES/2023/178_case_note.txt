# Case Note: Nedbank Limited and Another v Survé and Others (Case no 160/2023) [2023] ZASCA 178

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The case arose from a complaint lodged by Dr. Mohammed Iqbal Survé and various related entities, part of the Sekunjalo Group, against the appellants, Nedbank Limited and Nedgroup Private Wealth Stockbrokers (Pty) Ltd. The respondents claimed that the closure of their bank accounts constituted unfair discrimination on the basis of race, seeking an interim interdict under the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000 (the Equality Act) ([1–2]).

The events triggering the closure stemmed from the Mpati Commission of Inquiry, investigating allegations against the Public Investment Corporation (PIC), which revealed considerable financial misconduct involving entities within the Sekunjalo Group, particularly Ayo Technology Solutions (Pty) Ltd ([5–6]). After significant negative media coverage and reputational concerns, Nedbank reviewed its banking relationship with the respondents, ultimately deciding to terminate their accounts after issuing termination notices ([8–9]). The respondents filed their applications in the Equality Court seeking an interim order to prevent account closures and to reinstate already-closed accounts ([3]).

The Equality Court initially granted the interdict but denied leave to appeal ([4]), prompting the current appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([4]).

### I – Issues
Two primary legal issues were presented for resolution:

1. **Appealability of the Interim Interdict**: Whether the order granted by the Equality Court was final in effect and thus appealable, despite being characterized as an interim order ([12]).
   
2. **Establishment of a Prima Facie Case**: Whether the respondents successfully established a prima facie case demonstrating unfair discrimination based on race as required under the Equality Act ([12]).

### R – Rules
The relevant legal rules and standards for the case derived from:

- **Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000**: This act mandates the prohibition of unfair discrimination and outlines the burden of proof in cases involving alleged discrimination ([28]).
- **Common Law Principles on Interim Interdicts**: The established criteria for granting an interim interdict necessitate the presentation of facts where an applicant must show a prima facie case that will likely succeed in the main proceedings ([19]).
- **Judicial Precedents**: The considerations regarding appealability were guided by several precedents, including *Zweni v Minister of Law and Order of the Republic of South Africa* and *United Democratic Movement and Another v Lebashe Investment Group (Pty) Ltd and Others*, which clarify the conditions under which an interim order may be appealable when the interests of justice necessitate it ([15–18]).

### A – Application
The Court engaged in a comprehensive analysis of the appealability of the lower court's order. The judges underscored that while interim orders are typically not appealable, exceptions arise when a decision's implication significantly affects a party's rights. This situation was particularly pertinent due to the serious nature of the accusations of racial discrimination noted in the Equality Court's order ([30]).

The court also probed the merits of whether a prima facie case of unfair discrimination had been established by the respondents. The allegations relied largely on the assumption that there was a racially motivated selective termination of accounts when compared against reflecting companies like Steinhoff Group and others ([21]). However, the Court found this reasoning insufficient; substantive evidence demonstrating discriminatory impact based on race was absent from the respondents' submissions. The court noted that the comparison to other entities lacked empirical underpinnings regarding their racial classifications or their treatment by Nedbank, thus failing to provide a basis for the alleged discrimination ([20–25]).

Moreover, the Court pointed out that Nedbank's defense indicated material reasons—reputational risk linked to the findings of the Mpati Commission for differentiating in treatment between the Sekunjalo Group and other firms, which had taken measures to rectify past issues ([26–27]).

### C – Conclusion
The result of the appeal was a unanimous ruling that the respondents had not established a prima facie case of unfair discrimination and that they had failed to adequately justify the interim interdict that had been granted by the Equality Court. The Supreme Court of Appeal set aside the previous order, dismissing the application with costs ([31]). 

This judgment signifies a critical reaffirmation of the standards required to claim unfair discrimination and marks an important development in the interpretation of the Equality Act, illustrating the necessity of solid factual foundations over conjecture or perceived grievances in the judiciary's approach to discrimination claims.
