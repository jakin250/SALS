Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 109

F – Facts ([paras 1-6])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dealt with the matter of T[....] v T[....], focusing on an application for a protection order under the Domestic Violence Act 116 of 1998 (the Act) initiated by the appellant, T[....]. The appellant and the respondent were married but living separately at the time of the application on 3 June 2019; the appellant resided in Pretoria while the respondent lived in Bloemfontein. They had one minor child, who was aged two years at the time of the application, and later divorced in October 2020.

The appellant sought extensive relief against the respondent, including restraining her from any act of domestic violence. The Bloemfontein Magistrate’s Court dismissed the application, concluding that the appellant failed to demonstrate that the respondent had committed acts of domestic violence. After the dismissal, the appellant appealed to the Free State Division of the High Court, which upheld the magistrate's decision. The Supreme Court of Appeal received the matter with special leave.

In support of his application, the appellant alleged that the respondent sent him several text messages between December 2017 and April 2019 that were verbally abusive, alleging emotional and psychological harm. He also claimed that the respondent verbally abused him during phone calls regarding their child, and denied him reasonable contact rights.

I – Issues ([paras 6-7])

The principal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the contents of the SMSes sent by the respondent amounted to repeated verbal, emotional, or psychological abuse as defined under sections 1(vii)(c) and (f) of the Domestic Violence Act. Related to this was the determination of whether the appellant established a prima facie case of domestic violence that warranted the issuance of a protection order.

R – Rules ([paras 1, 10, 20])

The Supreme Court of Appeal relied on the definitions within the Domestic Violence Act. Emotional, verbal, and psychological abuse is characterized as a pattern of degrading conduct including repeated insults, ridicule, and the like ([para 10]). The definition of harassment includes conduct that induces a fear of harm to the complainant ([para 10]). The Act stipulates that for a protection order to be granted, the applicant must establish, on a balance of probabilities, that an act of domestic violence has occurred and that undue hardship would ensue if an order were not issued ([para 20]).

The judgments of the lower courts drew on precedents that clarified the necessity for a pattern of conduct demonstrating persistent abuse rather than isolated incidents or non-threatening communication.

A – Application ([paras 11-19, 21-22])

Upon rigorous scrutiny of the SMSes presented by the appellant, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that the contents of these messages—while possibly hostile—did not meet the threshold for verbal, emotional, or psychological abuse as defined by the Act. For example, the initial SMSes from December 2017 expressed anger concerning the breakdown of the marriage but did not constitute insults that rose to the level of emotional abuse. Likewise, subsequent messages primarily focused on the state of the marriage and sought the finalization of divorce without exhibiting a pattern of degrading or humiliating conduct ([paras 12-19]).

The Court noted that there were significant gaps between the communications—indicating these were not persistent or repetitive—but rather part of an emotionally charged divorce involving mutual allegations ([para 17]). The appellant’s reliance on the SMSes that were months old when the protection order was sought further weakened his position. His argument was perceived as contrived and lacking bona fides, as his actions suggested an abuse of legal process rather than a legitimate claim for protection ([para 22]).

C – Conclusion ([paras 17, 21-24])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appellant failed to demonstrate, on the balance of probabilities, that the respondent had committed acts constitutive of domestic violence, as defined by the Act. It upheld the findings of the lower courts, which had determined that the claims were not substantiated by sufficient evidence of a continuous pattern of abuse.

The Court dismissed the appeal with costs, asserting that the appellant's application amounted to an attempt to misuse the protections intended for true victims of domestic violence and reflected poorly on his conduct during ongoing legal proceedings. The decision emphasized the necessity for genuine claims under the Domestic Violence Act, reinforcing its purpose to protect actual victims rather than serve as a tool for harassment.

This case ultimately serves as a reminder of the legislative intent behind the Domestic Violence Act and the imperative for courts to distinguish legitimate claims from those intended for harassment or manipulation, thereby protecting the system's integrity and its intended beneficiaries.
