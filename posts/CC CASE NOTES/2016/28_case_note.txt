Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Transport and Allied Workers Union of South Africa obo MW Ngedle and 93 Others v Unitrans Fuel and Chemical (Pty) Limited [2016] ZACC 28 

### F - Facts 
This case arises from the dismissals of 94 employees by Unitrans Fuel and Chemical (Pty) Ltd on **2 November 2010**, which followed a series of industrial actions initiated by the **Transport and Allied Workers Union of South Africa (TAWUSA)**. The backdrop to this dispute involves Unitrans' termination of a lucrative contract with **Shell Petroleum Company** in February 2009, which significantly affected the wages of the retained employees, notably the "Shell seven" who refused to sign new contracts with reduced remuneration ([para 1–3]).

By August 2010, a dispute had developed over four demands regarding wage discrepancies, unilateral wage cuts, extra allowances, and procedural changes to a provident fund, leading TAWUSA to issue a strike notice ([paras 4–6]). Following a Labour Court interdict, TAWUSA proceeded with a strike notice on **28 October 2010** after a certificate of non-resolution was issued by the bargaining council, asserting the legality of two of its demands concerning wage cuts and discrepancies ([paras 10–13]).

Despite initial protected strike action, on **1 November 2010**, Unitrans issued a final ultimatum stating that continuing with the wage cut demand constituted an unlawful strike ([para 115]), prompting subsequent dismissals when the employees did not return to work ([paras 15-16]). The Labour Court found the dismissals were fair based on TAWUSA's strike being unprotected, a decision upheld by the Labour Appeal Court ([paras 16-18]).

### I - Issues 
The essential issues before the Constitutional Court included:
1. Was the strike action taken by TAWUSA and its members protected under the provisions of the Labour Relations Act (LRA) and previous judgments?
2. Did the addition of impermissible demands convert an initially protected strike into an unprotected one?
3. Were the dismissals of the employees substantively or procedurally unfair, and if so, what remedies were appropriate ([paras 23–24])?

### R - Rules 
The court's analysis relied heavily on:
- The **Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995** which governs the right to strike, specifically sections 64 and 68 that outline the conditions for a protected strike and the implications of unprotected strike participation.
- Precedents established in **Afrox Ltd v SA Chemical Workers Union** and **Moloto**, which detail the worker solidarity principle allowing workers to strike in support of others who are directly affected by the disputes ([paras 17, 19, 20]).
- **Judgments from the Labour Court and Labour Appeal Court** that provided context on permissible demands under the relevant collective agreements ([paras 10–14, 110–116]).

### A - Application 
The court emphasized that the strike remained protected for **the initial four days**, during which there was a robust legal framework supporting TAWUSA's demands. The legality of the strike hinged on the specific demands made on behalf of the employees, specifically regarding wage equalization and restoration of wages for the Shell seven. The introduction of new demands during negotiations was problematic; however, the core demands that had previously been authorized by the first Labour Appeal Court judgment remained permissible ([paras 36, 156–170]).

The Constitutional Court also ruled that Unitrans’ final ultimatum did not transform the strike into an unprotected one due to the specific nature of the demands remaining at play. The court asserted that since Unitrans did not fully comply with the wage cut demand (unlike the inclusion in the ultimatum), the striking workers maintained their rights to continue withholding their services; thus, the strike was still protected ([paras 118–125]).

On the issue of fairness, the court found that the dismissals were automatically unfair as they directly related to the employees exercising their constitutional right to strike. It was clear that any unprotected conduct was insufficient against the backdrop of protected action over far greater periods ([paras 141–176]).

### C - Conclusion 
The Constitutional Court ultimately **granted the appeal** and ruled that the dismissals inflicted by Unitrans were automatically unfair due to the employees being dismissed for participation in a protected strike. As a remedy, it ordered the reinstatement of the discharged workers with full retrospective effect, emphasizing that their rights under the constitution outweighed the employer's operational concerns.

The court made clear that Unitrans had not only failed to demonstrate that the strike had transitioned to unprotected status but also reinforced the necessity for fair treatment of unionized workers under labor law, with implications that such dismissals could undermine the critical framework of collective bargaining ([paras 198]).

The implications of this decision are significant for the labor landscape in South Africa, affirming the robustness of workers' rights as enshrined in the Constitution and the LRA, while also setting clear expectations for employers regarding compliance with labor laws in managing employee relations.
