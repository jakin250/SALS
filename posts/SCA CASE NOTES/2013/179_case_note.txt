Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# Case Note: Tonkin v The State (938/12) [2013] ZASCA 179

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts ([1–10])

The appellant, Oliver Jon Tonkin, was convicted of malicious injury to property by the Harrismith Magistrate’s Court. His conviction was primarily based on a single incident where he intentionally broke a window-pane, resulting in damages quantified at R120. The prosecution initially charged him with three counts: (a) malicious injury to property, (b) assault with intent to cause grievous bodily harm, and (c) crimen injuria. However, he was acquitted of the latter two counts due to the court’s assessment of the prosecution's witnesses as unreliable. On his appeal against the malicious injury conviction, the high court denied him leave to appeal under section 309B of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977. Following this, Tonkin petitioned the Judge President of the Free State High Court for leave to appeal, which was also rejected. Subsequently, he applied for leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal, which was initially granted, resulting in an inconsistent determination regarding his prospects of success on the appeal ([1]).

### I – Issues ([1, 10–12])

The primary legal question in this case is whether the Supreme Court of Appeal could apply its jurisdiction to examine the merits of the appeal directly rather than merely the refusal of leave to appeal by the high court. Specifically, could the court look into whether the high court had erred in denying the appellant’s leave to appeal? Of particular concern was the interpretation of "dolus eventualis" as it relates to the appellant's intention at the time of the incident and whether the magistrate properly applied the necessary legal tests regarding subjective foresight of consequences and reconciliation with those consequences.

### R – Rules ([2–3, 10–12])

The court’s analysis was predicated on several legal principles. Section 20(4)(b) of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959 delineates the limits of the Supreme Court's jurisdiction, impacting appeals against decisions made by the high court. It affirms that appeals can only be made to the Supreme Court with the leave of the high court or, if that leave is denied, with its own leave. Furthermore, the court referred to established jurisprudential standards regarding "dolus eventualis," which requires two components: (1) that the appellant subjectively foresaw the possibility of harm resulting from their actions, and (2) that they reconciled themselves with that possibility ([10–12]). The decisions S v Khoasasa [2003] and S v Matshona [2008] provided foundational interpretations in this area, asserting that the refusal of leave to appeal by the high court is an appealable order, thus framing the appellate court's review into the high court's exercise of discretion regarding leave ([2]).

### A – Application ([6–13])

The Supreme Court of Appeal determined it could not directly review the merits of the appeal due to limitations on its jurisdiction as defined by the statutes. The court highlighted that the high court's refusal of leave did not constitute a decision on the merits; therefore, hearing an appeal against conviction directly would contravene established legal principles. While the court acknowledged the appellant's frustration with the lengthy appeals process and contemplated the possibility of exercising inherent jurisdiction, it ultimately concluded that it lacked the authority to entertain the merits of the appeal without prior leave having been granted. 

The court turned its focus back to examining whether the magistrate properly considered the legal standards surrounding dolus eventualis in the appellant’s conviction. Specifically, it scrutinized whether the magistrate appropriately assessed the element of reconciliation. The magistrate seemed to have established subjective foresight but failed to adequately explore whether the appellant, despite that foresight, believed the consequence (breaking the glass) would not materialize due to the nature of the door's mechanism ([11–12]). The Supreme Court pointed out that if the appellant genuinely held such a belief, his intention would not meet the standard for dolus eventualis, thereby questioning the legitimacy of the conviction.

### C – Conclusion ([14])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal against the high court’s order denying Tonkin leave to appeal his conviction, setting aside the previous ruling and granting him leave to appeal to the Free State High Court. The case illustrates the complexities of appeals in criminal proceedings and emphasizes the necessity for lower courts to adhere strictly to legal standards concerning intention and foresight in determining culpability. This ruling not only benefits Tonkin by allowing the matter to be re-examined but also sets a critical precedent regarding the appellate process in the context of criminal convictions and the interpretation of dolus eventualis, potentially prompting scrutiny of legislative frameworks around appeal procedures in South Africa ([14]).
