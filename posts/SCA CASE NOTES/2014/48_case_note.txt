Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

### FIRAC Case Note: Spenmac (Pty) Ltd v Tatrim CC (216/2013)

**F – Facts:**
The appeal concerns a sale agreement for a sectional title property between Spenmac (Pty) Ltd (formerly Bobcart (Pty) Ltd), the appellant, and Tatrim CC, the respondent. The sale was concluded on 8 October 2010, for a price of R10.5 million, and included a ‘voetstoots’ clause alongside an acknowledgment by the purchaser that no representation had induced the contract ([para 1]).

Prior to the conclusion of the contract, Mr. Joseph Thompson, representing the appellant, was allegedly misled by the respondent’s representative, Mr. Spendley. The critical misrepresentation involved the status of a neighboring unit (unit 2), which had purportedly been permitted to subdivide without the required consent from the owner of unit 1, thereby affecting the value and desirability of the property Thompson intended to purchase. It was represented that unit 2 could not be subdivided without unit 1’s owner's consent ([paras 2–6]).

Upon learning after the sale that unit 2 had, in fact, been subdivided into 110 units, Spenmac sought to cancel the contract, claiming that this fundamental misunderstanding precluded consensus ([paras 10–12]). In contrast, Tatrim CC denied any misrepresentation and counterclaimed for the retention of the deposit due to Spenmac’s failure to fulfill payment obligations ([para 5]).

**I – Issues:**
The key legal issues revolve around:
1. Whether the alleged misrepresentation by the seller’s representative resulted in a fundamental mistake about the terms of the contract, thereby precluding valid consensus ([para 2]).
2. The applicability and effect of the exemption clause in the sale agreement, particularly in light of the nature of the misrepresentation (innocent vs. fraudulent) ([paras 21–23]).

**R – Rules:**
The applicable legal principles can be drawn from both common law and prior judicial decisions:
1. A misrepresentation can vitiate consent and render a contract void if it leads to a fundamental mistake (see Trollip v Jordaan 1961 (1) SA 238 (A) and Allen v Sixteen Sterling Investments (Pty) Ltd 1974 (4) SA 164 (D)) ([paras 23–25]).
2. Exemption clauses may be rendered unenforceable if the mistake induced by misrepresentation is material (see Morgan Air Cargo v Sim Road Investments & Another [2009] 4 All SA 249 (GNP)) ([para 27]).
3. An agreement is void if the parties were mutually mistaken regarding its subject matter such that no valid consensus was reached ([para 29]).

**A – Application:**
The court's analysis began with assessing whether there had been a misrepresentation regarding the number of units in the sectional title scheme and the implications for Thompson’s right to prevent subdivision ([paras 18–20]). The High Court judged that both parties labored under the belief that the property was comprised solely of two units with specific rights of veto, thus ruling the absence of genuine consensus ([paras 29–31]).

Although Spendley’s misrepresentation was found to be innocent—resulting from his forgetfulness regarding prior approvals—the court maintained that this did not negate the existence of a material mistake concerning the nature of the contract ([paras 31]).

Additionally, the court supported the view that the exemption clause could not shield Tatrim CC from liability due to the fundamental nature of Spenmac’s mistake. The premise was that a significant misrepresentation, whether made intentionally or not, still invalidates the formation of a contract ([paras 26–30]).

**C – Conclusion:**
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming that the parties were mutually mistaken about the nature of the property sold, which meant that no consensus had been achieved. The contract was held to be void ab initio on the grounds of the induced mistake, irrespective of the nature of the misrepresentation ([paras 30–32]).

Consequently, this case reinforces essential principles relating to contract law in South Africa, highlighting that both parties must fully understand and agree upon the material elements of a contract for it to be enforceable, and that misrepresentations—regardless of intent—can have significant repercussions on contractual validity. The decision serves as a critical precedent in examining how misrepresentation interacts with exemption clauses and the overarching doctrine of consensus in contractual agreements.
