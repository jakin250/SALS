South African Diamond Producers Organisation v Minister of Minerals and Energy N.O. and Others [2017] ZACC 26

Facts In this case, South African Diamond Producers Organisation (SADPO), a voluntary association representing diamond producers and dealers, challenged the constitutionality of section 20A of the Diamonds Act 56 of 1986, as amended (the Act).

Constitutional Court 2017 [2017] ZACC 26

FIRAC Method

Facts

In this case, South African Diamond Producers Organisation (SADPO), a voluntary association representing diamond producers and dealers, challenged the constitutionality of section 20A of the Diamonds Act 56 of 1986, as amended (the Act). The provision, which was inserted by the First and Second Diamonds Amendment Acts, prohibits unlicensed persons from assisting licensed dealers during the sale, purchase, or viewing of unpolished diamonds outside of designated Diamond Exchange and Export Centres (DEECs) ([paras 1–3]). SADPO argued that this prohibition resulted in a substantial deprivation of their property rights under section 25 of the Constitution and infringed upon their right to freely choose their trade under section 22 ([paras 14, 28]).

Prior to the amendments, SADPO members operated tender houses where unpolished diamonds could be anonymously tendered to licensed dealers, a practice now deemed unlawful under the new regulations ([paras 7–10, 12–13]). The respondents were various governmental entities, including the Minister of Minerals and Energy and the Department of Minerals and Energy, who defended the constitutionality of the provision ([paras 4–6]). Initially, the High Court declared section 20A unconstitutional, asserting that it infringed on both section 22 and section 25 of the Constitution; however, it was appealed to the Constitutional Court for confirmation of this order ([paras 18]).

Issues

The key legal questions before the Court were:

  1. Whether section 20A of the Diamonds Act arbitrarily deprives SADPO’s members of property rights, as envisaged in section 25 of the Constitution.
  2. Whether section 20A of the Diamonds Act violates the right of SADPO’s members to choose their trade, occupation, or profession freely under section 22 of the Constitution. ([paras 28–29]).

Rules

The analysis was governed by the relevant sections of the Constitution of South Africa:

  • Section 25(1) states that no one may be deprived of property except in terms of law of general application, and no law may permit arbitrary deprivation of property.
  • Section 22 provides that every citizen has the right to choose their trade, occupation, or profession freely, although this right may be regulated by law ([paras 34–35, 64]).

The Court employed a structured approach based on prior jurisprudence, particularly First National Bank of SA Ltd v Commissioner, South African Revenue Service (FNB), which set forth a multi-step analysis for deprivation of property ([para 34]). The case also referenced the rationality test applied in Affordable Medicines Trust v Minister of Health for limitations on the right to choose a profession ([paras 74–76]).

Application

1. Deprivation Analysis under Section 25: The Court scrutinized whether the impact of section 20A constituted a "deprivation" of property. It concluded that ownership of diamonds and diamond dealer licenses is legally protected property. However, the Court emphasized that for a deprivation to be actionable under section 25, it must be substantial. It found that SADPO failed to demonstrate a significant impact on their ability to trade effectively or realize fair market prices due to the prohibition on unlicensed assistance. Changes in business strategies, while challenging, did not equate to a threshold of deprivation necessary for constitutional engagement ([paras 34–56]).

2. Regulation of Trade under Section 22: The Court determined that section 20A did not impair the fundamental choice of trade for SADPO’s members. The regulatory intent behind section 20A was aimed at promoting local diamond beneficiation and ensuring compliance with international standards, which the Court deemed rationally related to legitimate governmental objectives ([paras 77–80]). Effects on trading practices did not amount to a limitation on the freedom to choose that trade. The prohibition on unlicensed assistance was thus found to be a regulation of practice, not a restriction on choice, and was upheld as constitutionally valid ([para 72]).

Conclusion

The Constitutional Court, led by Khampepe J with unanimous agreement, dismissed the appeal against the High Court’s declaration of invalidity, confirming that section 20A does not violate sections 22 or 25 of the Constitution. The Court articulated that while the regulation may impose certain operational constraints on producers and dealers, it does so within the confines of a legitimate regulatory framework aimed at benefiting the broader industry ([paras 80–83]). As such, SADPO was left without legal recourse to challenge the prohibition, establishing a critical precedent that affirms the state's regulatory authority over trade practices in the interests of public policy and economic governance ([para 80]).

The order dictated the late filing for appeal was condoned, with the cross-appeal on costs dismissed and no order as to costs from the High Court affirmed ([paras 81–83]). The ruling reinforces the delicate balance between regulatory intervention and individual rights, particularly in economically significant sectors such as diamond trading.
