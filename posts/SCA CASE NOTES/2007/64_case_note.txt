Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2007

# FIRAC Case Note: Van der Merwe v Road Accident Fund [2007] SCA 64 (RSA)

## F – Facts

The appellant in this case, Dr. C.J. van der Merwe, is an anaesthetist who rendered medical treatment to Mr. Grundlingh after he suffered injuries from a motor vehicle accident on 2 October 1998. The treatment was administered on 20 February 2002, which was more than three years post-accident, for an amount of R 1,319.82. Van der Merwe sought to recover this amount directly from the Road Accident Fund (the Fund) by submitting a claim under section 24(3) of the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 on 27 June 2002 (see [1]). 

Following the lack of response from the Fund, Van der Merwe issued a magistrates’ court summons on 11 February 2003. The Fund responded with a special plea of prescription, arguing that Van der Merwe’s claim had become prescribed since it had been submitted more than three years after the cause of action arose ([1]). The magistrate ruled in favor of the Fund, dismissing Van der Merwe's claim, an outcome that was upheld by the Pretoria High Court, although the court granted leave for appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([1]–[2]).

Further facts presented during the appeal revealed that Grundlingh had submitted a claim for his bodily injuries to the Fund on 1 September 2000, which did not include the appellant’s claim. This submission occurred before Van der Merwe provided treatment. The Fund had not yet finalized Grundlingh's claim by the time Van der Merwe submitted his claim ([2]). 

## I – Issues

The fundamental legal question before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether Van der Merwe's claim for recovery of costs could become prescribed despite the fact that Grundlingh's claim had not prescribed at the time Van der Merwe’s claim was submitted ([6]). This inquiry necessitated interpretation of relevant statutory provisions under the Road Accident Fund Act, particularly concerning the nature of accessory claims and their relationship to the primary claim of the injured party ([5]–[6]).

## R – Rules

The key statutory provisions pertinent to this case are encapsulated in section 17(5) and section 23 of the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996. 

1. **Prescription under Section 23**:
   - Section 23(1) specifies that a claim from the Fund becomes prescribed three years post-cause of action ([3]).
   - Section 23(3) introduces an exception for claims lodged in terms of section 24, which states these claims shall only prescribe five years post-cause of action ([3]).

2. **Accessory Claims under Section 17(5)**:
   - This section allows a supplier (like Van der Merwe) to claim costs directly from the Fund, provided the third party (Grundlingh) has a valid claim. The supplier's claim is subordinate to the third party's claim, hence deemed an accessory claim ([6]–[7]).

## A – Application

In determining the outcome of the appeal, the Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed how the claim of an accessory party, like Van der Merwe, depended on the timing and status of the principal party's claim, Grundlingh's. It found that since Grundlingh had lodged his claim within the requisite three-year period, that claim had not prescribed. Thus, Van der Merwe's claim, which was an accessory to Grundlingh’s, could not be considered prescribed either ([4]–[9]).

The Court rejected the Fund's argument that both claims were subject to identical statutory timeframes, affirming instead that the specific provisions of section 17(5) clearly allow suppliers to claim when the third party retains an enforceable claim against the Fund. The Court acknowledged that the Fund's approach would undermine the statutory basis allowing suppliers direct recourse ([5]–[10]). 

The decision underscored the principle that statutory provisions should not operate in a manner that negates the intended benefits to suppliers, thereby confirming the essential right of healthcare providers to recover treatment costs when the injured party's claim is valid and ongoing ([8]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Van der Merwe’s appeal, ruling that his claim could not have become prescribed while Grundlingh’s claim was still active and had not prescribed. The Court's decision clarified the relationship between accessory claims and primary claims under the Road Accident Fund Act; accessory claims linked to valid primary claims remain actionable even if they fall outside the ordinary prescription period applicable to direct claims from the Fund ([10]).

This judgment has important implications for medical service providers and suppliers under the Road Accident Fund framework, affirming the viability of recovery actions when their patients have valid claims, thus reinforcing the legislative intent of protecting these suppliers’ rights and financial interests. The Court ordered that the appeal be upheld with costs, including the costs of two counsel ([10]).
