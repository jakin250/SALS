Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# Case Note: Sand Hawks (Pty) Ltd and Another v Labonte 5 (Pty) Ltd and Others (190/2023) [2024] ZASCA 122 (16 August 2024)

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

The case of **Sand Hawks (Pty) Ltd and Another v Labonte 5 (Pty) Ltd and Others** involves a dispute over mining rights for certain properties located in Limpopo Province ([1]). The first and second appellants, Sand Hawks (Pty) Ltd and Seacrest Investments 129 (Pty) Ltd, engaged in mineral resource exploitation ([2]). In July 2010, *Labonte 5 (Pty) Ltd*, the first respondent, submitted an application for mining rights under the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA) ([2]). 

On **20 September 2010**, the Regional Manager partially accepted Labonte’s application but omitted two specific properties based on a mistaken belief that a third party held rights over them ([2]). Following Labonte’s partial acceptance, Sand Hawks lodged applications for mining rights over the same properties in **August 2011**, which were subsequently accepted in **November 2011** ([3]). 

In **2013**, the Regional Manager then accepted Sand Hawks’ mining rights application that included the previously excluded properties ([4]). Labonte sought to appeal this decision in **April 2013**, as well as the Regional Manager’s previous partial acceptance, which involved several administrative decisions issued over subsequent years ([5]–[8]).

By **December 2019**, the Director-General upheld Sand Hawks' appeal and confirmed the Regional Manager’s initial partial acceptance, stating that the latter was functus officio, meaning he could not correct his previous decision ([7]). Labonte then sought a judicial review of this decision based on various grounds, arguing that the Director-General had erred and failed in his duties, particularly regarding the nature of the Regional Manager’s decisions ([8]) and the issue of condonation for the late filing of the appeal initiated by Sand Hawks ([9]).

The High Court found merit in Labonte’s arguments, ruling that the Regional Manager’s initial decision was not final and allowed for correction ([12]). This led to an order remitting the appeals back for proper consideration ([10]-[11]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal questions for determination by the Supreme Court of Appeal were:

1. Whether the Director-General’s decision on **19 December 2019** regarding Sand Hawks’ appeal should be declared unlawful and set aside ([13]).
2. Whether the Regional Manager was functus officio after his partial acceptance of Labonte’s application, or whether he retained the ability to correct that decision ([13]).
3. Whether the Director-General reasonably condoned the late filing of the Sand Hawks appeal and properly considered the grounds for such a decision ([13]).

Additional concerns pertained to the administrative nature of the Regional Manager’s decisions and the application of the principle of legality under administrative law ([40]).

### R – Rules

The court’s analysis was anchored in the **MPRDA**, particularly sections 9, 22, and 96, which outline the procedures for application and appeal concerning mining rights ([14]–[19]). 

The standard for assessing whether the Regional Manager was functus officio draws from the established principles of administrative law and the doctrine of functus officio as restated in cases such as *Mncwabe v President of the Republic of South Africa* and *Retail Motor Industry Organisation* ([27]). The appeal also referenced *Aurecon South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Cape Town City*, which set out the parameters for evaluating condonation applications and the scope of administrative discretion ([21]). 

Key legal principles involved:

- **Functus Officio**: A decision-maker’s inability to revisit a final decision (relevant to the Regional Manager’s actions).
- **Administrative Action under PAJA**: The definition and scope of administrative action in contexts where rights are materially affected ([40]).

### A – Application

The Court held that the Director-General's decision on **19 December 2019** was unlawful due to a failure to properly consider the circumstances surrounding the appeal ([45]). 

Regarding the functus officio doctrine, the Court ruled that the Regional Manager’s initial decision to partially accept Labonte's application was not final. It emphasised the preliminary nature of that decision, providing a framework for subsequent corrections ([11]). The Court further elaborated on the inherent responsibilities of the Regional Manager, indicating that his role involved verification without the exercise of discretion ([31]).

The Court also scrutinised the Director-General's decision to condone the late filing of Sand Hawks’ appeal. It noted that the DG did not adequately consider pertinent factors, including the length of the delay and its justification ([24–26]). The lack of inquiry into whether the appeal maintained its integrity further warranted a finding of irrationality in the DG's decision, thus violating the principles established under PAJA.

The interlinked nature of both Labonte’s and Sand Hawks’ appeals necessitated their consideration together, allowing the Administrator to assess both internal appeals concurrently ([10]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal filed by Sand Hawks, noting the procedural irregularities present in the actions of the Director-General and the previous decisions of the Regional Manager ([46]). 

The Court affirmed the High Court’s ruling that the functus officio principle did not apply given the administrative context wherein the Regional Manager acted. This decision affirms the view that preliminary decisions can be revisited under appropriate circumstances, thus retaining opportunities for correctability within administrative processes. 

Ultimately, the judgment underscores the importance of sound administrative practices, particularly concerning public powers exercised under statutory frameworks, and highlights the need for decision-makers, such as the Director-General, to rigorously apply mindfulness to their decisions. The Court’s findings suggest broader implications for managing appeals within administrative law, emphasizing strict adherence to procedural justice and the principles enshrined in the Constitution. 

The implications of this case extend to the regulatory framework of mining rights, where erroneous decisions can significantly affect parties involved in competitive applications for mineral exploitation. The decision elucidates the nuances of administrative duties surrounding the MPRDA and underlines the imperative for clarity, fairness, and accountability in administrative processes that command public confidence and legal compliance.
