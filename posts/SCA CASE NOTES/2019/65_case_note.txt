**Case Note: Eskom Holdings Soc Ltd v Sidoyi and Others**  
*Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa (SCA), Neutral Citation: (1207/2018) [2019] ZASCA 65 (28 May 2019)*

---

**FIRAC Method Case Note**

**F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–12])**

On 17 July 2017, Eskom Holdings SOC Ltd (Eskom) conducted a disconnection operation in the Zwelitsha area. This included the removal of electrical apparatus that had been illegally connected to the national grid, posing public safety risks. The disconnections affected Mr. Siphelele Sidoyi, the first respondent, whose property had previously been supplied electricity using a meter reportedly installed by a contractor acting under Eskom’s authorization ([paras 1–2]).

Following the disconnection, Mr. Sidoyi and the other respondents initiated proceedings in the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, seeking an order that determined the disconnection to be unlawful and sought restoration of their electricity supply ([para 2]). Initially, they requested reasons for the disconnection, which Eskom provided, affirming that the supply was terminated due to its unlawful nature. Mr. Sidoyi’s application evolved into a test case for the other respondents’ claims ([para 3]).

The High Court upheld Mr. Sidoyi’s claims, declaring the disconnection unlawful and mandating the reinstallation of electricity within 30 days. However, Eskom sought to appeal, prompting the current proceedings ([paras 3–4]).

**I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 13–17])**

The main legal question arose from the characterization of Eskom’s disconnection actions. Specifically, the court grappled with whether the removal of the disputed connections constituted "administrative action" as per the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA). If Eskom's actions met this criterion, the procedural safeguards outlined in PAJA would apply, requiring due process ([para 14]).

A secondary issue concerned the legitimacy of Mr. Sidoyi's claim that he had a lawful right to the electricity supply, based on his previous usage and procurement of electricity, despite the disconnection taking place under allegations of unlawfulness ([paras 15–17]).

**R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 12, 14])**

The court examined several legal principles: 

1. **PAJA**: Defines "administrative action" as any action that adversely affects a person’s rights ([para 14]).
2. **Joseph v City of Johannesburg**: Established precedents regarding rights to electricity supply and the conditions under which disconnections can occur ([para 14]).

The court also considered the implications of Regulation 7(7) of the Electrical Installations Regulations under the Occupational Health and Safety Act 85 of 1993, which dictates that unauthorized or unsafe connections can be removed to protect public safety ([para 12]).

**A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 18–23])**

The court concluded that significant disputes of fact existed regarding the legality of the installation on Mr. Sidoyi’s property. It evaluated the claims about the supposed authorization of the installation and the implications of voluntary electricity purchases against the backdrop of PAJA’s requirements ([paras 18–21]).

Eskom argued that the disconnection did not constitute administrative action because it was predicated on the unlawful nature of the supply, asserting no rights or legitimate expectations could arise from such an unlawful association. The court recognized the complexity of Mr. Sidoyi’s defense that prior normalcy in purchasing electricity suggested legitimacy, ultimately deciding that sufficient evidence was lacking to definitively classify the installation's legality from the papers submitted. Accordingly, it highlighted the need for oral evidence to be presented ([para 22]).

Furthermore, the court critiqued the initial High Court’s orders, noting that they failed to consider the ramifications of reinstating an unlawfully installed connection, thereby necessitating a distinct legal analysis and resolution based on factual findings regarding the installation's legitimacy ([paras 23]).

**C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 24])**

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of Eskom, setting aside the High Court’s order and determining that the substantial factual disputes warranted a referral for the hearing of oral evidence to conclusively address the lawfulness of the installations ([para 24]). 

The implications of this decision extend beyond the immediate parties involved. It underscores the critical importance of proper procedure and the need for definitive legal standards when utilities enact disconnection policies, particularly where issues of public safety and compliance with legal norms intersect.

Eskom's obligation to ensure safe and lawful electricity provision is balanced against the rights of consumers claiming entitlements based on their transactional histories. This ruling thus reflects a pivotal moment in the interpretation of administrative justice as it pertains to utility regulation and consumer rights in South Africa, potentially establishing a procedural roadmap for similar future disputes.
