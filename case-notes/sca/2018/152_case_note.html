Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 152 Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of Sout

Case Title: Gwababa v The State (1290/2016) [2018] ZASCA 152

Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Date: 08 November 2018
Coram: Cachalia, Saldulker, Mbha JJA and Matojane and Rogers AJJA

F – Facts

The case involves an application for leave to appeal a murder conviction against Mr. Lungisa Gwababa, a member of the South African Police Service, who was one of nine accused in a trial before the Gauteng Division of the High Court ([para 2]). Gwababa and his co-accused were convicted of the murder of Mr. Silvesta Jossefa Marcia, who died following a violent arrest on 26 February 2013. During the arrest for a traffic violation, Mr. Marcia was handcuffed and subsequently dragged behind a police vehicle driven by Gwababa, ultimately leading to his death from severe injuries sustained during the arrest and subsequent handling by police officers ([paras 5–6]).

The High Court dismissed the applicants' applications for leave to appeal on 11 December 2015 ([para 2]). However, subsequent to the refusal of leave to appeal by two judges of the Supreme Court of Appeal on 25 August 2016, the then Acting President, Maya P, referred the decision for reconsideration under section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 ([para 1]). Key doubts arose about the trial court's application of the common purpose doctrine and the validity of the trial court's rejection of Gwababa's defense that he was unaware of the deceased being dragged.

I – Issues

The primary legal issues identified in the case include:

  1. Whether the trial court erred in applying the doctrine of common purpose as established in S v Mgedezi ([para 7]).
  2. Whether there was a reasonable prospect that another court might reach a different conclusion regarding Gwababa's conviction ([paras 8–11]).
  3. The broader question of whether any injustice would result from denying leave to appeal, considering parallel cases where co-accused received favorable permission to appeal ([para 10]).

R – Rules

The legal framework guiding this case includes:

  • Common Purpose Doctrine: The principles surrounding this doctrine stem from S v Mgedezi 1989 (1) SA 687 (A), which underlies the liability of individuals who act in concert and are complicit in a crime ([para 7]).
  • Section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013: This section provides the Supreme Court of Appeal the jurisdiction to reconsider a decision by two judges refusing leave to appeal if it appears that it is in the interests of justice to do so ([para 1]).
  • Mens Rea and Dolus Eventualis: The court examined the required mental state for liability, questioning whether the trial court's finding of dolus eventualis was justified given the circumstances ([para 8]).

A – Application

In evaluating the merits of the leave to appeal, the court assessed the evidence presented by the trial court, particularly focusing on the credibility of witness testimonies and the interpretations of intent ([para 11]). The Supreme Court highlighted the lack of direct evidence connecting Gwababa to the assault on Mr. Marcia in the holding cells, as it rested on a single witness who failed to identify the specific actions of each accused in explicitly assaulting the deceased ([paras 7–8]).

Furthermore, the Supreme Court expressed uncertainties about the reasoning that led to the conclusion that Gwababa had the requisite mens rea for murder, particularly questioning the trial court's dismissal of his claim of ignorance regarding the deceased being dragged. This assessment is central as it could illuminate whether a reasonable person in similar circumstances would have foreseen the outcome of their actions ([para 8]).

Additionally, the Supreme Court noted that parallel decisions granting leave to appeal to co-accused justified re-evaluating Gwababa's case ([para 10]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately concluded that the decision refusing Gwababa leave to appeal could lead to a grave injustice. The court emphasized that there were substantial grounds for reassessment of the conviction, with a reasonable prospect of a different outcome under appellate review ([para 11]). Thus, the order was made to set aside the previous decision and grant Gwababa leave to appeal his conviction in the full court of the Gauteng Division of the High Court ([para 12]).

The implications of this ruling extend beyond the individual case of Gwababa, potentially affecting the application of the common purpose doctrine in South African criminal law and ensuring a fair review process for convictions made under such legal scrutiny. The court's recognition of the need for careful consideration in instances of conflicting evidence reinforces the foundational principle of justice and the necessity of thorough judicial review in criminal proceedings.
