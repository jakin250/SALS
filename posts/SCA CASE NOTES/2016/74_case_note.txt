Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of **Cochrane Steel Products (Pty) Ltd v M-Systems Group (Pty) Ltd** [2016] ZASCA 74

## F - Facts

The appellant, **Cochrane Steel Products (Pty) Ltd** ("Cochrane"), an operator in the security fencing industry, filed a suit against **M-Systems Group (Pty) Ltd** ("M-Systems") for unlawfully using the trademark **CLEARVU**, which Cochrane claims to have developed in 2008 for its high-density security fencing product. Cochrane alleges that M-Systems' use of the term CLEARVU in **Google AdWords** advertising constituted unlawful competition and passing off ([1]–[5]). At a lower court level, the Gauteng Local Division dismissed Cochrane's application for a restraining order against M-Systems' use, leading to Cochrane's appeal ([2]).

Cochrane, while having pending applications for trademark registration since 2010, had not yet acquired official trademark protection under the **Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993** ([3]). The core of Cochrane's claim rested not on statutory infringement but on the common law principles of passing off and unlawful competition ([5], [6]). 

The court extensively examined the mechanics of Google AdWords, noting how advertisements appear in response to specific search queries and how various advertisers bid on keywords, including trademarks ([4]).

## I - Issues

The case raised significant legal questions regarding intellectual property rights, focusing on two primary legal issues:

1. **Passing Off:** Whether M-Systems' use of the term CLEARVU as a keyword in Google AdWords constituted passing off, thus leading to customer confusion regarding the source of goods ([5], [15]).
   
2. **Unlawful Competition:** Whether M-Systems' actions amounted to unlawful competition, infringing upon the boni mores and appropriating Cochrane's commercial goodwill without statutory protection ([16], [24]).

## R - Rules

The court relied upon several legal principles and precedents in its analysis:

1. **Common Law of Passing Off**: To succeed in a passing off claim, a plaintiff must demonstrate that their mark has acquired distinctiveness and reputation, and that the defendant's conduct is likely to cause confusion among consumers ([5], [6]).

2. **Unlawful Competition Principles**: The common law recognizes the right to compete freely but prohibits wrongful interference with another's trade. Relevant precedents cited include **Schultz v Butt** [1986] (3) SA 667 (A) and **Dun and Bradstreet (Pty) Ltd v SA Merchants Combined Credit Bureau** [1968] (1) SA 209 (C), which outline that competition must remain within lawful bounds ([16], [18], [19]).

3. **Foreign Jurisprudence**: The court referenced decisions from *Cosmetic Warriors Ltd v Amazon.co.uk Ltd* [2014] and other comparable jurisdictions, indicating a trend towards accepting the use of a trademark as a keyword, provided that it is clearly labeled as an advertisement ([7], [10], [21]).

## A - Application

In its analysis, the court evaluated the factual context surrounding M-Systems' keyword bidding. It considered whether M-Systems' bidding practices could be reasonably perceived to cause consumer confusion regarding the source of its goods. The court concluded that while Cochrane had established some level of reputation associated with CLEARVU, it failed to sufficiently demonstrate that the use of the term by M-Systems created a likelihood of confusion among consumers. The differentiation between sponsored ads and organic search results was deemed sufficient for consumers to recognize competing advertisements ([14], [18]).

Moreover, the absence of actual confusion—a crucial aspect in passing off claims—was noted as a significant gap in Cochrane's argument ([19], [15]). The court further emphasized that a mere act of imitation does not equate to unlawful competition unless it transcends mere competition into deceit or misrepresentation ([24], [25]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa ultimately dismissed Cochrane's appeal, holding that M-Systems' use of the term CLEARVU as a keyword in Google AdWords did not amount to unlawful competition or passing off. The ruling significantly reiterates the principle that competition, even when involving similar trade marks, is generally permissible unless it unlawfully hinders another trader’s business interests. This case serves as a cornerstone for future interpretations surrounding keyword bidding in digital advertising, emphasizing market dynamics while safeguarding competitive integrity ([2], [26]).

In conclusion, **Cochrane Steel Products (Pty) Ltd v M-Systems Group (Pty) Ltd** underscores the evolving landscape of intellectual property rights in the digital marketplace and the stringent requirements plaintiffs must meet to claim passing off or unlawful competition, especially in contexts where the goods and services offered by competing entities are distinct and recognizable by the consuming public.
