Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2008

# Case Note: Mantsha v The State (432/2007) [2008] ZASCA 121

### FIRAC Method

#### F – Facts
In June 1998, Kholisile Mantsha (the appellant) was convicted of multiple offenses in a regional court and subsequently received a sentence of 15 years' imprisonment on 7 August 1998 ([1]). Immediately after sentencing, he expressed a desire to appeal and was advised to seek assistance from the Legal Aid Board. An attorney, Mr. Lloyd Fortuin, was appointed on 29 August 1998, but failed to take action on the appeal ([1]). It was not until 10 September 2002—over four years later—that the appellant, representing himself, submitted a late notice of appeal along with an application for condonation ([2]).

The appeal process faced further complications as the record of the trial, essential for the appeal, was reported lost, along with the presiding magistrate's notes. As a result, reconstruction of the record was impossible, which is critical for the appeal ([2]).

The appellant's application for condonation was heard by the Cape High Court in 2005 but was dismissed by Thring J and Irish AJ. They found the explanation for his delay in filing the appeal to be inadequate and struck the matter off the roll ([3]). In this current appeal, Mantsha challenged this decision as he did not seek leave to appeal from the court below ([3]).

Despite being represented by an attorney in the lower court, the notice outlining the reasons for the delay was prepared by Mantsha himself, with no additional information provided by his legal counsel ([4]). The applicable court rule (Rule 67 (1)) mandated that an appeal notice be lodged within 14 days of sentencing ([5]).

The application for condonation had two critical elements that needed to be established: a satisfactory explanation for the delay and reasonable prospects of success on the merits of the appeal ([5]). The explanation provided by the appellant was considered inadequate, resulting in the court's dismissal ([6]-[8]).

#### I – Issues
The primary legal issues presented in this case include:
1. Whether the appellant provided a satisfactory explanation for the delay in filing his notice of appeal, which exceeded four years ([6]).
2. Whether the appellant demonstrated reasonable prospects of success on appeal, especially in light of the lost trial record ([15]).

#### R – Rules
The key rules guiding the court's decision include:
1. **Rule 67 (1)** of the Magistrate’s Court Rules, which stipulates that a notice of appeal must be lodged within 14 days following sentencing ([5]).
2. The requirement to demonstrate "good cause" for the granting of condonation, which entails two components: a satisfactory explanation for the delay and showing that the appellant has reasonable prospects of success ([5]).
3. The principle from *Mabaso v Law Society, Northern Provinces* 2005 (2) SA 117 (CC), which asserts that condonation applications are exercises of judicial discretion based on fairness to both parties ([9]).
4. The consideration by courts of various factors, including the importance of the case, respondent’s interest in finality, and overall administration of justice ([11]).

#### A – Application
In addressing the appeal, the court highlighted that the appellant's application fell short in terms of detailing the reasons for the significant delay. His narrative regarding consultations with Mr. Fortuin was found lacking in clarity and did not sufficiently cover the entire delay period, which opened the application to criticism ([12]). Despite the attorney's concession that the explanation was inadequate, he argued that the court erred in stating that "nothing happened" during the intervening years ([12]). This argument, however, did not ameliorate the lack of a comprehensive account for the delay.

The court cited previous cases, such as *Uitenhage Transitional Local Council v SA Revenue Service* 2004 (1) SA 292 (SCA), to underscore the importance of thoroughness in explaining delays ([12]). Mantsha's failure to provide an affidavit from Fortuin, corroborating his claims, further weakened his position ([13]).

In discussing prospects of success, the appellant argued that the lost trial record should favor his case, referencing *S v Chabedi* 2005 (1) SACR 415 (SCA) ([14]). However, the court clarified that the absence of a trial record merely complicates the appeal and does not inherently suggest success, as the appellant needed to substantiate claims regarding his conviction—and he failed to do so ([15]).

The court concluded that the lower court had not exercised its discretion improperly and that the elements for granting condonation were not met, leading to the dismissal of the appeal ([16]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal against the Cape High Court’s refusal to condone the late filing of the notice of appeal. The court reinforced the stringent requirements for condonation applications, stressing the necessity for a satisfactory explanation and demonstration of prospects for success ([16]). This case underscores the critical importance of diligent legal representation in appeals and the severe consequences of procedural failures, particularly in the context of a lost trial record. Additionally, the ruling has significant implications for future appellants who may encounter similar challenges regarding delays in appeal filings.
