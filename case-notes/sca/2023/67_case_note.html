Supreme Court of Appeal

Top Lay Egg Co-op Ltd & Another v Minister of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries & Others (400/2022) [2023] ZASCA 67

This case arose from a protracted legal dispute regarding the proper designation of an assignee under the Agricultural Product Standards Act 119 of 1990 (the Act).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 67

F - Facts ([paras 1–7])

This case arose from a protracted legal dispute regarding the proper designation of an assignee under the Agricultural Product Standards Act 119 of 1990 (the Act). The appellants, Top Lay Egg Co-operative Limited and George Schwartzel Boerdery (Pty) Ltd, along with other poultry product producers, contested the designation of Agency for Food Safety (the third respondent) as an assignee by the Minister of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries (the first respondent). The Minister's designation was made through a letter dated December 9, 2016, which referred to the third respondent using its trade name, raising confusion regarding its legal identity.

Following the Minister's designation, the third respondent mandated its subsidiary, Agency for Food Safety and Quality (the fourth respondent), to conduct relevant inspections. The appellants initiated review proceedings in the Gauteng Division of the High Court seeking to clarify the identity and powers of the designated assignee, challenge the legality of fees imposed for inspections, and contest the alleged irrationality of the fee-setting process.

The High Court dismissed the appellants' application, affirming that both the third and fourth respondents were properly designated and had the requisite powers under the Act to carry out inspections and charge fees. Subsequently, the appellants appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal, seeking a reversal of the High Court's decision and a declaration that the fees charged were arbitrary and unlawful.

I - Issues ([paras 8–10, 16–20, 22–24])

The appeal presented several legal issues for determination:

  1. Designation of Assignee: Whether the Minister's designation of the third respondent as the assignee was valid, particularly in light of the appellants' claim that no such entity existed.
  2. Scope of Power: Did the Minister's letter limit the powers of the assignee to the specific sections of the Act referenced, consequently excluding the powers to conduct inspections, determine fees, and execute other related powers under sections 3(1A), 7, and 8?
  3. Fee Determination Process: Were the fees determined and charged by the assignee arbitrary, capricious, or irrational under the provisions of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 2 of 2000 (PAJA)?
  4. Procedural Fairness: Was procedural unfairness present in the fee determination and inspection processes undertaken by the respondents?

R - Rules ([paras 2–3, 16–20, 26–27])

The applicable legal frameworks in this case include:

  • Agricultural Product Standards Act 119 of 1990: The Act empowers the Minister to designate officials and assignees for enforcing compliance regarding agricultural products. Notably, sections 2(3), 3(1A), and 4A are relevant for defining the powers conferred to assignees.
  • Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 2 of 2000 (PAJA): This Act provides grounds for reviewing administrative actions based on legality, rationality, and procedural fairness. Sections 6(2)(e)(ii) and (f) specifically offer grounds for contesting decisions perceived as arbitrary or irrational.

The jurisprudence surrounding the interpretation of legislative frameworks was underscored in cases such as Bertie van Zyl (Pty) Ltd t/a ZZ2 and Others v Minister of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries and Others ([2021] ZASCA 101), which elucidated the Minister's and assignee's powers under the Act.

A - Application ([paras 10–29])

The Supreme Court of Appeal conducted a meticulous examination of the appellants' claims against the backdrop of the established legal framework:

  1. Confusion Regarding Entity Identity: The Court found sufficient evidence to establish that the third respondent, Agency for Food Safety, is indeed the entity designated by the Minister. The Court deemed the appellants' arguments about identity confusion as unmeritorious, given the available documentation that clarified the relationship between the third and fourth respondents.
  2. Extent of Powers Conferred: The appellants' interpretation of the Minister's designation was rebutted at length. The Court affirmed that the powers referenced in the designation letter should be read in the context of the entire Act. It highlighted that the assignee’s powers derived from both the letter and the statute itself, including the critical powers necessary for effective inspection and fee determination.
  3. Fee Assessment Legality: The Court scrutinized the procedure followed in determining and charging fees, referencing participatory consultations that involved industry role-players, including the appellants. The Court concluded that the fee-setting process was not arbitrary or capricious but rather robust and grounded in consultations that led to a reduction of fees due to industry feedback.
  4. Procedural Fairness: The appellants' late submission of arguments related to procedural unfairness was deemed not permissible as it was not part of the original grounds of application in the High Court. The Court emphasized that any procedural deficiencies were not sufficiently established or attributable to the respondents.

C - Conclusion ([paras 29–30])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, holding that the designation of the assignee was valid and that the assignee had appropriately exercised its powers under the Agricultural Product Standards Act. The determination and imposition of fees were concluded to be lawful, rational, and procedurally fair. The appellants were consequently ordered to pay the costs of the appeal, reflecting the Court’s position that their arguments lacked substance.

The implications of this judgment reinforce the importance of clarity in administrative designations and the necessity for compliance with procedural standards in administrative law. The case serves as a pivotal reference for future disputes regarding the powers of designated assignees and the lawful frameworks guiding their actions.
