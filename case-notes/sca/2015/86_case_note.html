Supreme Court of Appeal

The Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service v Candice-Jean van der Merwe (20152/2014)

Facts The case concerns an appeal from the Western Cape Division of the High Court regarding the appointment of a curator bonis under the Tax Administration Act 28 of 2011 ([paras 1–4]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015

Facts
The case concerns an appeal from the Western Cape Division of the High Court regarding the appointment of a curator bonis under the Tax Administration Act 28 of 2011 ([paras 1–4]). The appellant, the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (SARS), asserted that the respondent's father, Mr. Gary van der Merwe, had accrued a substantial tax debt exceeding R66 million and had failed to declare assets, potentially obstructing tax collection ([para 2]). The controversy escalated with SARS applying for a preservation order to secure assets, including a purportedly large sum of money transferred to the respondent, Candice-Jean van der Merwe, which they alleged were effectively controlled by her father ([paras 3–5]).

Initially, a provisional preservation order was granted on August 30, 2013, which restricted the respondent from dealing with various assets ([para 6]). However, the full order was only confirmed in February 2014 without appointing the curator requested by SARS ([para 8]). Subsequently, the respondent failed to pursue her appeal timely, which led to its lapse ([para 9]).

In May 2014, SARS continued with their appeal after the respondent failed to file a notice of appeal within the prescribed timelines, necessitating a condonation application ([paras 9, 10]). The respondent’s father provided an affidavit that suggested the delay resulted from a misunderstanding about the relevance of the appeal ([para 13]). However, this explanation was later criticized by the court for being inadequate ([paras 16–18]).

Issues
The court's primary consideration was whether the respondent's failure to prosecute her appeal should be condoned and whether the appeal should be revived ([para 1]). The issues also encompassed the procedural validity concerning the appointment of a curator bonis and whether the High Court should have included such provision in its order ([para 20]).

Rules
The applicable statutes included the Tax Administration Act 28 of 2011, specifically Section 163, which permits the court to make ancillary orders including the appointment of a curator bonis under circumstances of asset preservation ([para 20]). Furthermore, the standard for granting condonation in a judicial context requires a detailed explanation of the causes of delay, an evaluation of the case's importance, and a consideration of the respondent's interest in finality ([para 11]).

Application
The court found that the respondent's application for condonation illustrated a flagrant breach of procedural rules, lacking a satisfactory explanation for the significant delays ([para 19]). The inexplicability of her father's defense and the inadequate articulation of prospects of success cultivated skepticism regarding the legitimacy of their claims ([paras 15–18]). The court indicated that even if prospects of success were favorable, the prior breaches of procedural norms justified the denial of condonation ([para 19]).

On the issue of the curator, the court emphasized the importance of such an appointment under Section 163 of the TAA following a valid preservation order ([para 20]). It ruled that the appointment was necessary to manage and possibly secure the respondent’s assets, especially given SARS' claims that her father’s actions were obstructing tax recovery efforts ([paras 26–27]).

Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled to dismiss the respondent’s condonation application and upheld SARS’s appeal ([para 28]). Notably, the order of the lower court was set aside, and a curator bonis was appointed to oversee the respondent's assets ([para 28]). The judgment underscored the necessity of maintaining procedural integrity in tax recovery actions, illustrating the consequences of non-compliance with judicial procedures. The implications extend not only to the specific individual involved but also serve as a warning to practitioners regarding adherence to procedural rigor within the appeals process.

References
The case illustrates the significance of sections 163 and 172 of the Tax Administration Act, as well as broader legal principles surrounding condonation ([including Uitenhage Transitional Local Council v South African Revenue Service [2003] ZASCA 76; 2004 (1) SA 292 (SCA)]). The decision reiterates the principle that procedural failures may outweigh substantive legal merits in appeals ([Rennie v Kamby Farms (Pty) Ltd [1989] ZASCA 171]).
