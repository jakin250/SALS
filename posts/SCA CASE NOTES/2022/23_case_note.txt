Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality v Brooklyn Edge (Pty) Ltd and Another [2022] ZASCA 23

### F – Facts

On 31 July 2003, the City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality ("the City") and Brooklyn Edge (Pty) Ltd ("Brooklyn Edge"), formerly known as Nieuw Pivot Investments (Pty) Ltd, entered into a deed of sale concerning specific immovable properties located within the City’s jurisdiction ([1]). The properties in question were a public open space which had been resolved for permanent closure and subsequent sale by a council decision dated 20 June 2003.

The deed provided for a purchase price of R9.5 million, with a 10% deposit due immediately, which the City conceded was duly paid ([2]). The balance was R8,550,000, conditional upon completion of closure and rezoning processes, which the City had committed to executing ([3–4]). Brooklyn Edge's second claim was based on the notion that a nominee, Pivot Property Development (Pty) Ltd, was permitted to enforce the deed. The court a quo upheld Brooklyn Edge's claim, ordering the City to facilitate the statutory processes leading to property transfer. The City subsequently appealed against the order, which represented a significant dispute over the enforceability of the deed of sale and the ensuing obligations between the parties ([8]).

### I – Issues

The principal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal revolved around the enforceability of the deed of sale and included:
1. Whether the deed of sale was subject to a tacit suspensive or resolutive condition regarding the completion of closure and rezoning ([9]).
2. Whether the purchase price was sufficiently determined ([9]).
3. Whether the City failed to comply with section 79(18) of the Local Government Ordinance 17 of 1939, and if this affected the validity of the transaction ([9]).
4. Whether section 14(2) of the Local Government: Municipal Finance Management Act 56 of 2003 impacted the enforceability of the sale ([9]).
5. Whether Brooklyn Edge's claim was premature or had prescribed ([28–30]).

### R – Rules

Several legal rules and statutory provisions underpinned the court's analysis:

1. **Tacit Conditions**: The court emphasized that a tacit term is one inferred from the express terms and the context of a contract. A tacit term must be determined necessary for business efficacy based on the bystander test ([16]).

2. **Determined Purchase Price**: The court referenced the principle that a valid contract necessitates a clear purchase price, asserting that vagueness in this regard may render an agreement unenforceable ([19]).

3. **Local Government Ordinance**: Specifically, section 79(18) outlines the procedures for asset disposal by municipalities, including requirements for public advertisement and property valuation ([23]).

4. **Municipal Finance Management Act**: Section 14(2) mandates conditions governing the transfer of capital assets and was also analyzed for its retrospective application regarding agreements made prior to its enactment ([26]).

5. **Prescription**: Relevant case law suggested that statutory claims regarding property transfers do not necessarily equate to debts as understood under the Prescription Act 69 of 1969, thus impacting claims for enforcement of the closure and rezoning processes ([30]).

6. **In Duplum Rule**: This common law principle stipulates that interest on a debt ceases to accrue once it equals the original capital amount, protecting debtors from being exploited through excessive interest ([31]).

### A – Application

**Tacit Condition**: The court analyzed arguments regarding whether the absence of closure and rezoning constituted a tacit condition. It concluded that the deed of sale did not imply a requirement for a timely closure, establishing that the agreement's intent was to remain valid notwithstanding potential delays in these processes ([10–18]).

**Determined Purchase Price**: The court affirmed that the purchase price was explicitly stated and thus determinable, rejecting claims that reliance on potential set-offs (for facility relocations) rendered it vague or indeterminate. Clauses concerning set-offs did not negate the overall clarity of the deed ([19–22]).

**Compliance with Local Government Ordinance**: The arguments concerning the advertisement and valuation process were dismissed due to lack of evidence showing non-compliance. Rather, evidence sufficiently supported that requisite valuation and procedures had been followed ([23–25]).

**Municipal Finance Management Act**: The court construed that section 14(2) could not apply retrospectively to transactions concluded prior to its enactment as this would infringe existing contractual rights. Legislative intent against retroactive effect was emphasized ([26–27]).

**Premature Claims and Prescription**: The claim was held not to be premature since the court maintained that procedural obligations remained for the City to effectuate closure and assist in rezoning ([28–29]). Additionally, the rights to the closure and rezoning processes were not considered debts under the Prescription Act ([30]).

**In Duplum Rule**: The court reasoned that the specifics of the interest accrual did not invoke the in duplum rule since the interest had not been classified as arrear, and the structured arrangement for interest in the deed was sufficiently detailed to avoid the unintended consequences of the rule ([31–33]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Brooklyn Edge's claims to some extent, affirming the deed of sale’s enforceability without tacit conditions that necessitated timely closure and rezoning. The court determined the purchase price was sufficient and the City had complied with statutory obligations relevant to the transaction at issue. Sections of the Municipal Finance Management Act and local ordinances were interpreted not to retroactively nullify the agreement. While the City had some success regarding interest calculations, the overall approval of the sale agreement had significant implications for municipal sales of immovable property and their statutory frameworks ([34–35]).

This judgment reiterates the principles of enforceability in municipal contracts and conditions under which definite rights and obligations arise, particularly in the context of property transactions involving public entities. It underscores the courts' roles in ensuring that both procedural compliance and substantive contractual terms are rigorously adhered to, thereby fostering the integrity of municipal financial management and operational accountability.
