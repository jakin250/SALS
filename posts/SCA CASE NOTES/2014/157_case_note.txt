Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: Shuttleworth v South African Reserve Bank (864/2013) [2014] ZASCA 157

## F – Facts
The case revolves around Mark Richard Shuttleworth's legal challenge against the South African Reserve Bank (SARB) regarding the imposition of a ten percent exit levy on capital sought to be exported as part of his emigration from South Africa. (See [1]–[2]) Shuttleworth, an entrepreneur known for selling Thawte Consulting for $575 million, emigrated to the Isle of Man in 2001 to liberate funds for international investment, claiming that South Africa’s exchange control system hampered such investments ([3]).

Upon emigration, his capital became subject to "blocked status" under the Exchange Control Regulations, which were enacted following Section 9 of the Currency and Exchanges Act 9 of 1933. He was allowed to remit interest on his blocked loan accounts but needed SARB's permission to transfer capital out of South Africa ([5]–[6]). In March 2008, Shuttleworth applied to transfer R1.5 billion of his blocked assets. His request was granted, subject to a ten percent exit levy, which he paid under the assumption that it was lawful ([7]–[8]).

In June 2009, Shuttleworth sought to transfer his remaining assets but was informed the exit levy’s lawfulness was contested, resulting in his subsequent application to transfer the assets while reserving the right to challenge the levy ([9]–[10]). The SARB maintained that the levy was legally justified under Exchange Control Circulars, asserting it to be a policy tool rather than a tax ([12],[26]).

Shuttleworth’s challenge culminated in the North Gauteng High Court, where he sought to declare the levy and various provisions of the exchange control system unconstitutional ([14],[19]). The court held the exit levy as lawful, leading Shuttleworth to appeal ([20]).

## I – Issues
The key legal issues presented in this case include:

1. Whether the ten percent exit levy imposed by the SARB was lawful, given Shuttleworth's contention that it constituted a form of taxation that required parliamentary approval under Sections 75 and 77 of the Constitution ([1]).
2. Whether various provisions of the Currency and Exchanges Act and the Exchange Control Regulations were unconstitutional and inconsistent with the rights protected under the Constitution ([14],[19]).
3. Whether Shuttleworth is entitled to a repayment of the exit levy on the grounds that it was improperly levied ([33]).

## R – Rules
The court examined a multitude of legal principles and statutory provisions:

1. **Statutory Framework**: The Currency and Exchanges Act 9 of 1933, particularly Section 9, authorized regulations concerning currency and capital export in South Africa ([25]). 
2. **Constitutional Principles**: The notions of "no taxation without representation" are embedded in the Constitution (Sections 75 and 77), which stipulate that taxation requires specific legislative authorization and procedure ([30]).
3. **Judicial Precedents**: The court drew upon prior constitutional interpretations concerning revenue-raising mechanisms, such as _Union Government (Minister of Finance) v Gowar_ ([33]), which allowed recovery of funds paid under protest as long as a claim is properly reserved.
   
## A – Application
The court evaluated the SARB's actions against constitutional standards. Initially, it examined whether the ten percent levy was a tax or a lawful regulatory fee under the exchange control framework. It determined that the levy constituted a revenue-generating mechanism imposed without appropriate legislative backing, as it had not been approved by Parliament under Section 9(4) of the Act. This was crucial in concluding that the SARB exceeded its powers by levying the tax without fulfilling constitutional requirements ([31]).

Subsequently, the court embraced a broader interpretation, understanding that even though the SARB characterized the exit levy as non-taxation, its general application revealed its nature as a revenue-generating charge. The imposed exit levy was thus inconsistent with the constitutional provisions requiring proper legislative processes for the introduction of taxes ([32]).

The arguments brought forth by Shuttleworth regarding the opaque nature of SARB's 'closed-door policy' and the inability for affected individuals to challenge decisions were significant but ultimately set aside due to the more immediate constitutional violations identified ([37]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Shuttleworth’s appeal, declaring that the imposition of the ten percent exit levy was unconstitutional and invalid. The court ordered the SARB to repay R250,474,893.50, with interest from the date of payment under protest. The court emphasized the adherence to constitutional principles in the exercise of power, underscoring the absence of legislative approval for what effectively constituted a tax ([38]).

The findings not only vindicated Shuttleworth's position but also highlighted critical gaps within the South African exchange control framework that necessitated legislative scrutiny for the future safeguarding of constitutional rights related to property and taxation. The implications of this ruling extend beyond Shuttleworth's personal financial interests, stirring necessary debate about the integrity and transparency of South Africa's exchange control measures. 

In essence, the judgment reflects a staunch judiciary commitment to uphold constitutional principles against administrative encroachment, reaffirming the principle that all public revenue mechanisms require clear Parliamentary endorsement to be considered lawful ([39]).
