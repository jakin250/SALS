Constitutional Court 2005

F – Facts

In the case of Sibiya and Others v Director of Public Prosecutions ([1]), the four applicants—Willy Aaron Sibiya, Purpose Khumalo, Jacobus Petrus Geldenhuys, and David Nkuna—were convicted in the early 1990s of serious crimes that were punishable by the death penalty under the law at that time. They received death sentences before the Constitutional Court's landmark decision in S v Makwanyane (1995), which declared the death penalty unconstitutional under the interim Constitution. Following Makwanyane, Parliament enacted the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 to facilitate the substitution of the death penalty with appropriate alternatives for those who had been sentenced to death before the decision took effect ([1], paras [1]–[2]).

The applicants challenged subsections (1) to (5) of section 1 of the Act as unconstitutional, claiming these provisions violated their constitutional right to a fair trial as set out in section 35 of the Constitution ([2]). After the High Court ruled the provisions unconstitutional, affirming the need for compliance with fair trial rights, the applicants sought confirmation from the Constitutional Court ([2], paras [2]–[24]).

I – Issues

The case raised several key legal issues:

  1. Constitutionality of the Impugned Provisions: Whether the provisions of the Criminal Law Amendment Act, specifically the subsections concerning the substitution of death sentences, were constitutional ([24]).
  2. Fair Trial Rights: Did the procedure prescribed in the impugned provisions infringe upon the applicants' right to a fair trial as guaranteed by section 35 of the Constitution? ([29]–[34]).
  3. Effect of the High Court's Rulings: Should the High Court have addressed the constitutionality of the impugned provisions, particularly given that only one applicant was directly affected by them? ([27]).

R – Rules

The primary legal authorities and statutes relevant to this case included:

  1. The Constitution of the Republic of South Africa: Particularly section 35(3), which enshrines the right to a fair trial, and section 12(1)(a), which protects against arbitrary deprivation of freedom ([33]).
  2. Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997: This Act provided the framework for substituting death sentences with alternative punishments. The provisions under scrutiny were those which detailed the procedures for such substitutions ([18]).
  3. Judicial Precedents:
  • S v Makwanyane [1995] 3 SA 391 (CC): This case declared the death penalty unconstitutional and mandated that existing death sentences be substituted with lawful sentences in a manner consistent with fair trial rights ([2]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court, led by Yacoob J, carefully analyzed the provisions of the Act in light of the preceding Makwanyane decision and the applicants' claims. The Court concluded the following:

  1. Fair Trial Rights: The Court identified that while section 35(3) of the Constitution conferred specific rights to a fair trial, those rights did not retroactively apply to individuals whose sentences were imposed under the laws before the new constitutional order came into effect. Thus, the applicants had not been deprived of rights under section 35 since their trials predated the current Constitution ([31]).
  2. Procedural Fairness: The mechanisms of the Act for substituting death sentences were assessed. The substitution process allowed for judicial consideration, albeit limited, which Yacoob J determined was adequate in light of the extraordinary circumstances, given that the death penalty was rendered unconstitutional ([33]).
  3. Administrative Function and Separation of Powers: The Court accepted that the involvement of the President in substituting sentences—after judicial advice—did not violate the separation of powers doctrine. The President was mandated to impose the judicial recommendations without exercising personal discretion ([39]).
  4. High Court's Ruling on President's Conduct: The Court ruled that the High Court's order regarding the President's decision to substitute a sentence could not be upheld as there was no application to confirm this aspect, and the procedural issues raised regarding the necessity of the trial judge’s involvement were found to be unfounded ([43]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court did not confirm the High Court's order declaring the impugned provisions unconstitutional. The Court held that the processes established by the Act were fair under the unique circumstances of dealing with past death sentences. It ruled that these provisions did not violate the rights of the applicants as they pertained to fair trial rights per the Constitution ([64]).

Moreover, the Court emphasized the pressing need to expedite the processing of outstanding death sentence substitutions in light of the significant delays already experienced ([60]). The judgment underscored both the importance of upholding constitutional rights while also recognizing the legislative body’s role in facilitating justice in extraordinary situations.
