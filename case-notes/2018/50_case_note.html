FIRAC Method for Mkhize N.O. v Premier of the Province of KwaZulu-Natal and Others [2018] ZACC 50

The case concerns an application for leave to appeal following a series of disputes regarding the recognition of traditional leadership in the Mbuyazi Traditional Community in KwaZulu-Natal.

Constitutional Court 2018 [2018] ZACC 50

F – Facts

The case concerns an application for leave to appeal following a series of disputes regarding the recognition of traditional leadership in the Mbuyazi Traditional Community in KwaZulu-Natal. The main actors are Sithembile Valencia Mkhize (the applicant, acting as the executrix of her late husband, Inkosi Zwelibhekile Sibusiso Mbuyazi), and multiple respondents including the Premier of KwaZulu-Natal and members from the royal family.

The late Inkosi was recognized as such after an internal selection by umndeni wenkosi (the royal family) under the KwaZulu-Natal Traditional Leadership and Governance Act 5 of 2005 ([paras 2–6]). This recognition came into dispute upon his removal, based on the findings of a Zulu law expert favoring his brother as the rightful Inkosi, leading to complex litigation over who could represent the deceased's interests after his death on 7 July 2012 ([paras 6–11]).

Initial proceedings began with the deceased’s urgent application to the High Court to review the Premier’s withdrawal of his recognition and to seek reinstatement ([paras 7–8]). After the deceased's passing, the standing to pursue this review became contentious, with arguments over whether the rights and claims were transmissible to his heirs ([paras 12–15]). The High Court opted to dismiss Mkhize's application to substitute her late husband in ongoing lawsuits, including review and funding applications as her claim was said to be personal and non-transmissible ([paras 16–17]).

After multiple judgments from the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal that framed the discussion around issues such as res judicata, the applicant sought leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court, raising questions about proper legal standing, statutory compliance, and the interpretive approach to customary law ([paras 18–20, 23–24]).

I – Issues

The core issues before the Constitutional Court involved:

  1. Whether Sishi J's relief to dismiss further proceedings was competent given previous high court and appellate decisions.
  2. The correct interpretation of the Supreme Court of Appeal's judgment concerning the rights of the deceased and their transmissibility.
  3. Whether the interim relief granted to the deceased should be dispensed with or maintained, given the context of ongoing litigation and community governance ([paras 26–30]).

R – Rules

The court was guided by several critical legal principles:

  1. Res Judicata: This doctrine prevents the same issues from being litigated again once final adjudication has been made in prior proceedings, raising questions over whether the issues surrounding Mkhize's standing had been substantively determined by earlier judgments ([paras 36–46]).
  2. Standing Under PAJA: The Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA) allows for a broad interpretation of who may challenge public decision-making. This would suggest that parties with a substantial interest, such as the executrix or the deceased's heir, could have standing in substantive claims for review ([paras 70–71]).
  3. Interpretation of Customary Law: The applicability of customary law rights, especially regarding traditional leadership, emphasizes a unique lens through which rights should be considered rather than through rigid common law principles ([paras 65–68]).

A – Application

The court primarily focused on the interpretation of previous judgments regarding the deceased’s rights to seek review. Dlodlo AJ indicated that while the review application itself became moot upon the deceased's death, the principles derived from both customary law and the constitutional stance on administrative review suggested Mkhize still held a substantial claim based on her executorship ([paras 50–62]).

The court noted that the interpretation by Sishi J potentially violated the principle of res judicata by failing to adequately reflect previous rulings on jurisdiction and standing, leading to an overreach concerning the finality of matters already litigated ([paras 46–64]).

Furthermore, the ruling reinstated the notion that traditional claims made under customary law could indeed be pursued even posthumously, drawing from precedents that recognize the necessity of having one’s interests defended through lawful means ([paras 65–74]). The consolidation of claims—monetary and succession—was emphasized as essential for judicial efficiency and justice for all parties involved, whilst preserving the interim relief arrangements ([paras 73–75]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately upheld Mkhize’s appeal, recognizing her rights to pursue a review of the deceased's removal as Inkosi despite his death. The court concluded that standing is afforded not just on the rigid basis of testamentary succession but also encompassing broader interpretations of interest as demanded in public law contexts ([paras 1–5]).

This judgment reinforces the necessity of recognizing customary law within the framework of constitutional rights, underscoring the importance of finality in legal proceedings, and establishing the pathway forward for both claims regarding traditional leadership and the financial interests of the deceased’s estate. The outcome also promotes community governance stability, which had been hampered by the absence of an established Inkosi for nearly a decade ([paras 74–75]).
