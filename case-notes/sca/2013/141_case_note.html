Supreme Court of Appeal

Chake v State [2013] ZASCA 141

After his conviction, Chake appealed to the High Court, seeking to challenge the validity of the life sentence, but did so without the necessary leave of the trial court as required by law.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 141

F – Facts

The appellant, Matsheng Jacob Chake, was convicted by a regional magistrate of raping two young girls, each aged 12, for which he received a life sentence ([1]–[3]). The incidents occurred on October 9, 2009, at Chake's home, where he allegedly lured the girls with money, locked them in, and subsequently raped them while threatening to kill them if they reported him ([2]). Chake pleaded not guilty and denied any wrongdoing. The life imprisonment sentence was based on the minimum sentencing provisions of section 51(1) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, which mandates a life sentence for such offences unless substantial and compelling circumstances are found ([3]).

After his conviction, Chake appealed to the High Court, seeking to challenge the validity of the life sentence, but did so without the necessary leave of the trial court as required by law. The High Court initially heard the appeal but dismissed it, asserting that the automatic right to appeal existed based on a preceding decision in S v Alam ([4]–[5]). The High Court’s ruling on the existence of this right was crucial given the legal changes brought about by the Child Justice Act 75 of 2008, which modified the appeal rights for children and affected the provisions for individuals sentenced to life imprisonment by regional magistrates ([12]–[13]).

I – Issues

The core legal issue concerned whether the High Court properly concluded that Chake had an automatic right to appeal his life sentence without prior leave from the trial court. This revolves around the interpretation and validity of amendments made by the Child Justice Act to section 309(1)(a) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([16]). Specifically, it required the court to analyze whether the Child Justice Act intended to repeal the automatic right to appeal sentences for adults and the implications of this for Chake's case and others similarly situated ([12]).

R – Rules

The relevant legislative provisions include:

  1. Section 51(1) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, which established life imprisonment as a minimum sentence for certain sexual offenses against minors.
  2. Section 309(1)(a) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which prior to amendments allowed offenders sentenced to life imprisonment by regional courts an automatic right to appeal. The amendment by the Child Justice Act removed this automatic appeal right, mandating an application for leave instead ([11]).
  3. The decision in S v Alam [2011] held that since April 1, 2010, offenders like Chake require leave to appeal their sentences following the Child Justice Act's amendments ([5]).

These sections interact to demonstrate a shift in the legislative landscape regarding sentencing minors versus adults, raising questions of fairness and legal protection for those sentenced under severe penalties such as life imprisonment.

A – Application

The court in this case critically examined the previous conclusions of the High Court and the applicability of the Child Justice Act amendments. It noted that the legal provisions enacted under section 99(1) of the Child Justice Act clearly indicated a repeal of the automatic right of appeal ([11]–[13]). The appellate court highlighted the importance of adherence to the legislative intent as conveyed through proper statutory interpretation, rejecting any notion of creating an alternate legal framework based on assumptions about legislative wisdom.

The judicial analysis stressed the separation between legislative changes and judicial interpretation, reinforcing that the removal of the automatic appeal right must stand unless deemed unconstitutional, which was not contested by either party ([15]). Moreover, the court discussed that the appellant had alternate legal avenues available through formal applications for leave to appeal under sections 309B and 309C, thus underlining the necessity to follow procedural norms ([16]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that Chake’s appeal before the High Court was not properly lodged due to his failure to secure leave to appeal in accordance with the amended legal provisions. The court decided to set aside the High Court’s judgment and ruled that Chake’s appeal was to be struck off the roll ([18]).

The ratio decidendi confirms that the appellate court's duty is to interpret the law as legislated, and the removal of the automatic appeal right was valid, limiting the prospects for offenders like Chake. The ruling has significant implications for future cases involving similar sentencing issues, requiring alignment with procedural requirements established by subsequent legislation, thus underscoring the importance of statutory compliance in maintaining judicial integrity.
