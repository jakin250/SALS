Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note

## CCT Case 11/13 [2013] ZACC 30

### 1. Facts

The case brought before the Constitutional Court of South Africa involves a dispute between several development companies (the "Applicants") and the Saldanha Bay Municipality ("the Municipality") regarding the legality of capital contributions imposed by the Municipality on the development applications submitted by the Applicants ([paras 1–2]). The Applicants, during the period from April 2003 to August 2008, submitted six applications for the rezoning and subdivision of properties, which were conditionally approved by the Municipality. The conditions included specific tariffs for capital contributions related to the necessary public services ([para 1]).

Initially, the Municipal Council utilized a tariff of R55 until July 1, 2007, after which a new tariff of R35 was implemented. The last three of the Applicants' six applications were subject to this new tariff ([paras 7–8]). The Applicants challenged the legality of the tariff system in the Western Cape High Court, contending that it was unlawful and sought an order for repayment of overpayments alleged to have been made ([para 2]).

While they obtained some relief at the High Court, the Supreme Court of Appeal reversed the decision, asserting that the conditions imposed by the Municipality were lawful under the relevant provisions of the Land Use Planning Ordinance, specifically section 42 ([paras 9–10]). After that, the Applicants sought leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court, but limited their appeal to a constitutional issue concerning the Municipality’s duty to account for alleged overpayments made by them ([para 2]).

### 2. Issues

The central issue for determination by the Constitutional Court was whether the Applicants had a constitutional right to compel the Municipality to account for the alleged overpayments made concerning validly imposed capital contribution conditions, especially in light of the Supreme Court of Appeal's findings concerning the lawful imposition of these conditions ([paras 12–14]).

### 3. Rules

The applicable legal framework includes:

1. **Section 195 of the Constitution**: This section mandates that public administration should uphold democratic values, including accountability and transparency ([para 15]).
2. **The Land Use Planning Ordinance (LUPO)**: Specifically, section 42 outlines the conditions for granting development authorizations, which validate the Municipality’s powers to impose these capital contributions ([para 10]).
3. **Case Law Precedents**: The Court referenced decisions such as *Chirwa v Transnet Limited*, where it was established that broader values of the Constitution do not create enforceable rights independent of those indicated in the Bill of Rights ([para 16]). 

### 4. Application

The Constitutional Court, through Justice Froneman, analyzed the Applicants' argument that the alleged overpayment creates an independent constitutional right, invoking section 195 to establish a "duty to account" from the Municipality. The Court noted that such obligations derived from the principles of democratic accountability do not translate into actionable rights under the law ([paras 14–17]). 

The Court explained that constitutional mandates regarding accountability and transparency do not provide grounds for individual claims and reaffirmed that remedies for the alleged excess payments lay within the existing legal frameworks and statutory provisions, including the option to claim repayment through pending civil proceedings ([paras 19–21]). Notably, the Court emphasized that the remedies available within the statutory framework, such as the right of access to information under section 32 of the Constitution, could be pursued by the Applicants without resorting to constitutional litigation ([para 20]).

Moreover, the Court concluded that the existence of statutory obligations requiring municipalities to account for financial transactions adequately addresses the concerns expressed by the Applicants ([para 19]). Consequently, as the Applicants already had avenues to seek redress regarding their payment claims, pursuing a constitutional angle was unnecessary and unwarranted ([para 14]).

### 5. Conclusion

Ultimately, the Court refused to grant leave to appeal, determining that the matter was fundamentally a commercial issue rather than a constitutional one, and that the Applicants had not demonstrated a reasonable prospect of success regarding any cognizable constitutional issue ([paras 22–24]). The Court ordered that the Applicants bear the costs of the application, underscoring the commercial nature of the dispute ([para 23]).

This judgment serves to clarify the limitations of constitutional claims in contexts where statutory remedies are available, reiterating that not all grievances relating to local government financial obligations warrant constitutional adjudication.
