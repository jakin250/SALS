Case Title: Director of Public Prosecutions, Limpopo v Mokgotho (068/2017) [2017] ZASCA 159 (24 November 2017)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 159 (24 November 2017) ## F – Facts: The case ar

Case Title:

Director of Public Prosecutions, Limpopo v Mokgotho (068/2017) [2017] ZASCA 159 (24 November 2017)

F – Facts:

The case arises from a criminal trial in the Limpopo Division of the High Court, Polokwane, where the respondent, Kagiso Kodishi Mokgotho, was indicted on four counts: murder (count one), contraventions of the Firearms Control Act (counts two and three), and robbery with aggravating circumstances (count four) ([para 2]). Mokgotho pleaded not guilty and contended, through a statement in terms of section 115 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA), that he was present in the deceased’s minibus taxi when one of his companions shot the driver, the deceased ([para 3]).

The State's evidence consisted mainly of testimonies from two eyewitnesses, Oscar Malepe and Thabang Morena, who identified Mokgotho as being present at the shooting. However, both witnesses were unable to definitively assert that Mokgotho fired the fatal shot ([paras 5–9]). Following the trial, the court acquitted Mokgotho on all counts, stating the State had failed to prove its case beyond a reasonable doubt, citing issues with the reliability of the witnesses ([para 15]).

The Director of Public Prosecutions, Limpopo (appellant), applied for leave to appeal, claiming that the High Court erred in its legal evaluation regarding the acquittal, particularly the test applied in evaluating the evidence ([para 1]).

I – Issues:

  1. Whether the trial court erred in its application of the legal standard when evaluating evidence, leading to Mokgotho's acquittal.
  2. Whether the State’s appeal raises a question of law as defined by section 319 of the CPA.

R – Rules:

  1. Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA) – Section 319: This section allows for a reservation of questions of law arising during a trial, where a court may refer such questions to a higher court for consideration ([para 10]).
  2. Standard of Proof in Criminal Trials: The State must prove the accused’s guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. If there exists a reasonable possibility that the accused is innocent, he should be acquitted ([para 18]).
  3. Case Law Precedents:
  • S v Shackell ([2001] 2 SACR 185 (SCA)): Outlines the principles of evaluating the evidence and the onus resting on the State ([para 11]).
  • Director of Public Prosecutions, Gauteng v Pistorius ([2015] ZASCA 204): Sets the standards and expectations for appeals concerning the evaluation of evidence, particularly when an accused does not testify ([para 26]).

A – Application:

The Supreme Court of Appeal found the trial court’s approach flawed in understanding and applying the legal standards for assessment after the evidence was submitted. It criticized the lower court for misunderstanding that the absence of testimony from Mokgotho should have shifted the focus onto whether the State provided a prima facie case rather than assessing the likelihood of Mokgotho's innocence ([para 37]).

The judgment emphasized that the trial court mistakenly applied an incorrect 'test of veracity' that generally requires an accused to establish a version that is reasonably possibly true. This standard was inappropriate since it assumed that Mokgotho had presented a defense when he had not provided testimony ([paras 32-36]).

In light of these considerations, the appellate bench concluded that the fundamental misapplication of the law constituted an error warranting the setting aside of Mokgotho's acquittal. The State's assertion regarding the test to be applied was deemed to be of sufficient legal gravity to warrant intervention under section 319 of the CPA, which corresponds to a legal issue rather than merely a factual one ([paras 27, 39]).

C – Conclusion:

The Supreme Court of Appeal granted the State's application for leave to appeal, upheld the appeal, and set aside Mokgotho's acquittal on the murder charge ([para 40]). The court concluded that the trial court's reasoning reflected a misapplication of the law, specifically the evaluation of the evidence where the accused had not testified, thus, the issue raised was indeed a question of law as defined in section 319 of the CPA ([para 39]). Consequently, the matter was returned for potential further proceedings, leaving the discretion of whether to prosecute anew to the Director of Public Prosecutions ([para 39]).

The outcome underscores the necessity for trial courts to apply the appropriate legal standards in evaluating both the evidentiary weight and the presence or absence of testimony when adjudicating matters of serious criminal allegations.
