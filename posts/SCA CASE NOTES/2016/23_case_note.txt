Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Rumdel Construction (Cape) (Pty) Ltd v South African National Roads Agency SOC Ltd (234/2015) [2016] ZASCA 23

## F – Facts

The case arises from a construction contract dated December 2010 between the appellant, a joint venture between Rumdel Construction (Cape) (Pty) Ltd, EXR Construction Holdings (Pty) Ltd, and Mazcon Joint Venture, and the respondent, the South African National Roads Agency SOC Ltd, for the improvement of the Umgeni Road Interchange in Durban. The contract stipulated a substantial price exceeding R352 million ([1]).

The appellant applied to the KwaZulu-Natal High Court seeking an order that the respondent pay R926,000 monthly from 4 June 2014 for additional costs incurred due to violence and unrest in the area impacting site operations, as well as declaratory relief regarding force majeure under the contract ([1], [10]). The application was dismissed, leading to an appeal ([1]).

The contract expressly outlined the responsibilities of the contractor in ensuring site safety and compliance with applicable regulations ([3]). It included provisions for civil commotion and defining force majeure, emphasizing that events beyond control, such as riots or violence by outsiders, fell under the employer’s risk ([4], [19.1]).

Violent incidents escalated beginning June 2011, pressuring the appellant to employ local residents for the project, which led to work stoppages and conflicts with local communities ([5], [6]). The appellant described severe safety issues resulting from violent attacks on its employees on 20 and 27 May 2014, prompting its request for urgent court relief due to an untenable working environment ([7], [8], [10]).

Ultimately, the court a quo found that the appellant failed to demonstrate that it could not perform its contractual obligations due to force majeure ([11]). 

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues in this case include:

1. Whether the violent incidents occurring on 20 and 27 May 2014 constituted force majeure under the terms of the construction contract ([11], [15]).
2. Whether the appellant had standing to seek declaratory relief in light of the fact that the initial monetary claims had been abandoned or rendered moot ([13], [15], [17]).
3. Whether the court should exercise discretion to grant a declaratory order when the matter has become purely academic ([15], [18]).

## R – Rules

The legal framework governing the case primarily consists of contract law principles as established in the construction agreement, which included provisions relevant to force majeure (clause 19.1) and employer’s risk (clause 17(3)(c)) ([4], [11]). The court also referenced the standards surrounding declaratory relief, asserting that a court's ability to issue a declaratory order is contingent on the presence of an active dispute that is not academic in nature ([15], [16], [18]).

Key rules include:

- **Force Majeure Definition**: Events beyond a party's control that could not have been avoided or overcome ([4], [19.1]).
- **Declaratory Relief Criteria**: The applicant must demonstrate an existing, future, or contingent right requiring determination to warrant declaratory relief, and the court must ascertain the matter is not abstract or hypothetical ([15], [16], [18]).

## A – Application

The court applied the principles surrounding both force majeure and declaratory relief in evaluating the appellant’s claims. Initially, while accepting the possibility that the events could be classified as force majeure, the court considered whether the appellant had sufficiently proven its inability to carry out the contract due to the incidents of violence ([11], [12]).

Furthermore, developments post-application, including the completion of the contract and the abandonment of the monetary claims, shifted the court's perspective significantly. The court noted that the appellant no longer had standing to seek a declaratory order since the primary claim was no longer under contestation, demonstrating that the matter had transformed into an abstract issue devoid of practical implications ([13], [16], [18]).

The reference to potential future disputes regarding force majeure was also dismissed as it failed to establish a current live issue necessary for a declaratory order ([17], [18]).

## C – Conclusion

In light of the lack of a live dispute and the abandonment of the monetary claims tied to the request for declaratory relief, the Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately struck the appeal from the roll. The judgment posits that courts generally refrain from adjudicating matters that have become purely hypothetical ([18], [19]).

Furthermore, the decision of the court a quo to deny the initial application was maintained, not due to endorsement, but because of the prevailing circumstances leading to the academic status of the appeal ([18]). The implications of this decision reflect the importance of ensuring that legal actions maintain relevance and practical consequence, thus upholding judicial efficiency and integrity.
