August v. The Electoral Commission (CCT 8/99)

Constitutional Court 1999 CCT 8/99

F – Facts

The case deals primarily with the voting rights of prisoners in the context of the South African electoral system. The applicants, Arnold Keith August and Veronica Pearl Sibongile Mabutho, brought forth an appeal after the Transvaal High Court, presided over by Els J, ruled against their claim that the Electoral Commission had an obligation to ensure that prisoners could register and vote in the upcoming elections scheduled for June 2, 1999 ([1]).

The interim Constitution permitted universal adult suffrage without expressly disqualifying prisoners, although the 1993 Electoral Act disqualified individuals imprisoned for serious offenses from voting, which included August—a convicted prisoner—and Mabutho, an awaiting trial prisoner ([2]). The relevant legislation post-interim Constitution, the 1998 Electoral Act, did not enumerate prisoners among disqualified voters either ([4]).

Despite the legal framework allowing prisoners to vote, the Electoral Commission had made no specific arrangements to facilitate the voting process for prisoners. The applicants, after numerous attempts to consult the Commission regarding their rights, filed an application to ensure their right to register and vote, emphasizing that logistical challenges posed by their incarceration should not preclude them from exercising this fundamental right ([6]–[10]).

I – Issues

The critical legal issue at hand was whether the Electoral Commission had a constitutional duty to facilitate voting for prisoners, particularly in the absence of any disqualifying legislation. The specific questions arose from the rights enshrined in the Constitution:

  1. Do prisoners have the right to vote under the 1996 Constitution despite their incarceration?
  2. Is the Electoral Commission obliged to take positive steps to ensure that prisoners can engage in the electoral process without being disenfranchised due to their imprisonment? ([11]).

R – Rules

The legal principles at play stem from several legal frameworks:

  • The 1996 Constitution of South Africa enshrines the right to vote for every adult citizen, articulating that the right may not be limited without legislative backing; hence, if there is no express legislative disqualification of prisoners, they cannot be denied voting rights ([3], [16]).
  • The Electoral Act 73 of 1998 requires the Commission to take reasonable steps to compile and maintain voters’ rolls and facilitate voter registration ([17]).
  • The common law tradition recognizes that prisoners do not lose their personal rights entirely upon incarceration, maintaining that they retain all rights not expressly removed by law, or those that are not inherently inconsistent with the conditions of their imprisonment ([18][19]).

Additionally, previous judicial interpretations underscore the importance of enfranchisement and the necessity for the state to facilitate voting rather than impede it ([20]–[22]).

A – Application

The court assessed the implications of Els J’s ruling, which emphasized prisoners as "authors of their own misfortune," suggesting that their failure to register was due to their actions, thus absolving the Electoral Commission of responsibility ([8], [10]). However, it also noted that the right to vote imposes an obligation on the Commission to enable that participation for all eligible citizens, including those incarcerated.

The Constitutional Court critically evaluated whether the definition of "ordinarily resident" could be interpreted to include prisons as residences for the purpose of voting registration ([24]–[28]). The Court found that prisoners, given their physical and logistical constraints, should be considered as ordinarily resident in the prisons where they are incarcerated, enabling them to engage in the electoral process without administrative hindrances. The court firmly rejected the idea that a lack of disqualifying legislation could be construed as permission for electoral exclusion ([30]).

Moreover, the Court acknowledged the practical aspects of implementing arrangements for voting in prisons, noting that logistical solutions could be achievable through measures like special voting provisions or designated polling stations within correctional facilities ([28], [33]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded that the applicants were effectively being denied their constitutional rights to register and vote, primarily due to the Electoral Commission's failure to make necessary arrangements enabling them to do so. Hence, the Court granted the applicants’ relief:

  1. It declared all prisoners, during designated registration periods, are entitled to register and vote ([42]).
  2. It mandated the Electoral Commission to formulate and present a compliance plan to facilitate prisoner voting, including how they would carry out these requirements for the upcoming elections ([39]–[41]).

This ruling establishes a precedent emphasizing the obligation of the Electoral Commission to ensure voting rights for all citizens, reinforcing the importance of universal suffrage in a democratic society while mandating a proactive approach to enfranchisement, emphasizing that logistical concerns cannot serve as a barrier to constitutional rights.
