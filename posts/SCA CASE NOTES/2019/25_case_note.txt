Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: Van Rensburg v Minister of Police

## F – Facts
The appellant, Mr. Casparus Janse van Rensburg, initiated a lawsuit against the respondent, the Minister of Police, in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, claiming damages for wrongful arrest and detention ([para 1]). The central event leading to the arrest occurred on 28 June 2013, when the appellant's trailer struck and killed an 18-year-old cyclist while he was towing it with a Ford Ranger bakkie. Van Rensburg contended that he had attempted to safely navigate around the cyclist, who veered unexpectedly, leading to the collision with the trailer ([para 2]).

After the incident, the appellant promptly called the police and emergency services. Upon the arrival of Constable Shibambu, the appellant presented his expired driver’s license and was subsequently informed that he was being arrested for culpable homicide and for operating an unroadworthy vehicle due to the expired license discs on both the vehicle and the trailer ([paras 3–4]). The arrest occurred despite the appellant's cooperation, during which he was detained for approximately 70 hours before appearing in court, where the charges were later provisionally withdrawn ([paras 5–6]).

The respondent asserted the legality of the arrest based on the provisions of sections 40(1)(a) and (b) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA), arguing the arrest was justified in light of the appellant committing offenses in the officer's presence and the reasonable suspicion of culpable homicide ([paras 7–8]).

The High Court ruled in favor of the respondent, concluding that the arrest was lawful, which prompted the appellant to appeal the decision ([para 1]).

## I – Issues
The salient legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal were twofold:
1. Whether the respondent successfully proved that the warrantless arrest of the appellant was permissible under section 40(1)(b) of the CPA through a reasonable suspicion of culpable homicide ([para 9]).
2. If the above was established, whether the appellant met the burden of demonstrating that the arresting officer exercised irrational discretion ([para 9]).

## R – Rules
Key legal rules and statutory provisions applicable to this case included:
- Section 40(1)(a) and (b) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which stipulates circumstances under which an arrest without a warrant is permissible ([para 7]).
- The principles outlined in *Zealand v Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development* [2008] ZACC 3 and *Minister of Safety and Security v Sekhoto* [2010] ZASCA 141 regarding the necessity for the respondent to justify the legality of an arrest ([para 8]).
- The necessity for the arresting officer to possess an objectively reasonable basis for suspicion, as established in *Duncan v Minister of Law and Order* [1986] 2 SA 805 (A) and *W v Minister of Police* [2015] 1 SACR 409 (SCA) ([para 10]).

## A – Application
The court meticulously analyzed whether Constable Shibambu had a reasonable suspicion that the appellant had committed culpable homicide. For the suspicion to be lawful, it must not only be universally accepted that Shibambu suspected culpable homicide, but it must also be demonstrated that this suspicion rested on reasonable grounds ([para 10]).

Key findings of the court included:
- Shibambu had only the appellant's narrative regarding the incident, and no eyewitness statements were collected from bystanders to corroborate the suspicion of negligence ([paras 12–14]).
- The physical evidence was consistent with the appellant’s account, undermining the narrative of the officer that negligence led to the fatality ([paras 13–14]). As such, the court noted that mere fact of the trailer striking the cyclist could not automatically lead to an inference of negligence, particularly without supporting investigation or facts indicating reckless behavior ([paras 14–18]).
- It was explicitly acknowledged that Shibambu had failed to consider the requisite element of negligence as part of culpable homicide and instead based his suspicion on irrelevant factors, such as the expired vehicle registrations ([paras 15–17]).

Given these factors, the court concluded that the respondent failed to establish that the arresting officer had both the subjective suspicion of culpable homicide and that this suspicion was based on reasonable grounds ([para 18]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled that the arrest of the appellant lacked lawful justification, leading to the successful appeal against the High Court's decision. The initial ruling by the court a quo was set aside, mandating that the respondent compensate the appellant with R150,000 in damages as agreed upon by both parties ([paras 19–24]). 

Moreover, the court's judgment also included a critical reflection on the inappropriate conduct displayed during the trial, emphasizing that acrimony among counsel and excessive judicial intervention could compromise the efficacy of legal proceedings ([para 23]). This decision serves as a pertinent reminder of the legal standards required for lawful arrest and the protection of individual rights against unwarranted detention by law enforcement agencies. 

### Order
1. The appeal succeeds, with costs awarded to the appellant.
2. The order of the court a quo is revised, with the respondent ordered to pay the appellant R150,000 plus interest and costs at the magistrate’s court scale ([para 24]).
