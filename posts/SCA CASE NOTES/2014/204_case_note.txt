Source Folder: 2014

# Irac Case Note: Mkhize v The Premier of the Province of KwaZulu-Natal and Others

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
In the case **Mkhize: In re Mbuyazi v The Premier of the Province of KwaZulu-Natal and Mbuyazi v Mbonambi Community Development Trust** (822/2013) [2014] ZASCA 204, the appellant, Sithembile Valencia Mkhize, is the executrix of the estate of her late husband, Zwelibhekile Sibusiso Mbuyazi. The deceased had been recognized as the Inkosi (traditional leader) of the Mbuyazi Community but was removed from that position by the Premier of KwaZulu-Natal in January 2010. Following the deceased's death in July 2012, the appellant sought to be substituted as applicant in certain legal proceedings initiated by the deceased concerning his removal and claims for unpaid salaries ([paras 1-4]).

During his lifetime, the deceased contested his removal as Inkosi by filing urgent applications, including a review application and a funding application related to his legal costs. Upon his death, these matters remained unresolved, prompting the appellant to petition for substitution both as the deceased's executrix and as the legal guardian of their minor son, Phathokuhle ([paras 7-11]).

The KwaZulu-Natal High Court dismissed the appellant's substitution applications, stating that the deceased’s right to be reinstated as Inkosi was a personal right that died with him and could not be transferred ([para 11]). The court did allow substitution for the damages claim related to arrears of salary owed to the deceased, but this aspect was also contested ([paras 11-12]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal questions involved in this appeal were: 
1. Whether the appellant could be substituted for the deceased in the review application challenging the Premier's decision to remove him as Inkosi, given that the right to reinstatement was deemed personal and non-transmissible upon death ([para 12]).
2. Whether the appellant could be substituted in the claims for loss of salary, which could potentially be viewed as damages flowing from the wrongful removal of the deceased, thus transmissible to his heirs ([paras 12-16]).

### R – Rules
The applicable legal framework primarily derives from the **KwaZulu-Natal Traditional Leadership and Governance Act 5 of 2005**, particularly Section 19, which outlines the recognition and appointment of an Inkosi and mandates adherence to certain procedures ([para 15]). The court also referenced established case law regarding the nature of personal rights, notably the precedent set in **Government of the Republic of South Africa v Ngubane** 1972 (2) SA 601 (A), which clarified that certain rights cannot be transferred upon death ([paras 11-12]).

Additionally, the court considered the principles surrounding damages claims, confirming that such claims are generally transmissible to heirs, provided they accrue during the deceased's lifetime ([para 16]).

### A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld part of the appellant's appeal concerning her substitution as applicant in the deceased’s claims for unpaid salary. The court distinguished the nature of the reinstatement claim, which was deemed personal to the deceased and, thus, could not be substituted or pursued after his death. It noted that the claims for damages resulting from wrongful removal, however, were separate and related to a financial entitlement that should be represented by the deceased’s estate ([paras 16-18]).

The court found it convenient for the appellant to pursue the damages claim directly as it related to losses incurred while her husband was alive, affirming that the estate had a right to claim these amounts as damages ([paras 16-18]). The assertion put forth by the Premier and other respondents regarding the extinguished nature of the deceased's rights was dismissed in the context of the salary claim because it represented a financial remedy rather than a reinstatement of personal status ([para 11]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal partially upheld the appeal. It determined that the appellant could be substituted as the applicant representing the deceased's claim for damages related to his salary but not for the reinstatement application, which was deemed inherently personal and thus extinguished upon the death of the deceased. The ruling emphasized the distinction between personal rights and transmitable financial claims, suggesting that while certain rights die with an individual, others, particularly financial entitlements, can be pursued by their estate ([para 19]). The implication for traditional leadership cases is significant, as it clarifies the boundaries of heirs’ rights under customary law and statutory provisions.

This case underscores the complexity of interplaying traditional leadership rights within the broader legal system while highlighting the importance of procedural adherence in claims involving traditional leaders in South Africa.
