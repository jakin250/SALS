# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of *Mahlangu and Another v Minister of Labour and Others* [2020] ZACC 24

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–18])

The case concerns the tragic incident involving Ms. Sylvia Bongi Mahlangu, who worked as a domestic worker for 22 years before drowning in her employer's pool on March 31, 2012. At the time of her death, she was financially supporting her daughter, the first applicant. Upon her death, the daughter sought compensation under the Compensation for Occupational Injuries and Diseases Act 130 of 1993 (COIDA). However, she was informed by the Department of Labour that neither COIDA nor any unemployment insurance benefits were available to her due to her mother's exclusion from the definition of "employee" under section 1(xix)(v) of COIDA, which specifically excludes domestic workers.

Accompanied by the South African Domestic Service and Allied Workers Union (SADSAWU), the applicants initiated proceedings in the Gauteng High Court seeking a declaration of constitutional invalidity regarding the aforementioned exclusion. The High Court declared the provision unconstitutional but provided no substantive reasoning for this conclusion. Specifically, it did not make findings concerning whether the exclusion operated retrospectively, which was subsequently addressed in October 2019 by a second order that applied the decision retroactively from April 27, 1994. The matter proceeded to the Constitutional Court, which needed to confirm the invalidity order.

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–18])

The primary legal issues in this case include:

1. **Constitutionality of Section 1(xix)(v) of COIDA**: Does this provision, which excludes domestic workers from the definition of "employee," violate the rights to equality, human dignity, and access to social security under sections 9, 10, and 27 of the Constitution?
  
2. **Justification for Exclusion**: Is there a legitimate governmental purpose justifying this exclusion, and does the treatment of domestic workers violate the equality provisions of the Constitution?

3. **Remedy**: What is the appropriate remedy, specifically concerning the retrospective application of the order of constitutional invalidity?

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 19–47])

The Constitutional Court relied on several sections of the Constitution, notably:

- **Section 9 (Equality)**: Affirms that everyone is equal before the law and prohibits unfair discrimination.
- **Section 10 (Human Dignity)**: Protects the inherent dignity of every person and affirms their right to have that dignity respected and protected.
- **Section 27 (Access to Social Security)**: Guarantees everyone the right to have access to social security and associated measures necessary for those unable to support themselves.

The Court has used a rationality test—established in *Prinsloo* and *Harksen*—to assess whether the distinctions made by legislation are reasonably connected to a legitimate purpose, and whether they result in unfair discrimination.

Furthermore, the Court referenced key international instruments, such as the International Labour Organization’s (ILO) standards, which frame the rights of domestic workers and emphasize the need for equitable treatment.

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 48–118])

In addressing the constitutional validity of section 1(xix)(v) of COIDA, the Court found that the exclusion of domestic workers from COIDA creates a clear distinction that lacks any rational connection to a legitimate government purpose. The governing principles of equality and non-discrimination were underscored, revealing that domestic workers, largely composed of Black women, experience compounded forms of discrimination based not merely on duty but through intersecting identities of gender, race, and class.

The majority judgment reflects on the significant socio-economic role played by domestic workers, asserting that their exclusion perpetuates cycles of poverty and marginalization. The application of an intersectional framework demonstrated how this legal exclusion exacerbates the vulnerabilities faced by domestic workers, who bear unique social and economic burdens. 

The Court emphasized that by denying domestic workers equal access to social security benefits, the provision undermined the aspirational purposes of the Constitution, particularly regarding the goals of human dignity and remedial justice for the most disadvantaged sections of society. This led to the broader conclusion that protecting the rights of domestic workers is essential for achieving substantive equality.

Concerning the issue of retrospectivity, the Court ruled that the declaration of invalidity should operate from the inception of the Constitution, thereby rectifying historical injustices.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 119–131])

The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court's declaration of invalidity regarding section 1(xix)(v) of COIDA, establishing that the exclusion of domestic workers is unconstitutional. The ruling underscored the importance of ensuring that all employees, irrespective of their occupational sector, receive equivalent protections under social security legislation.

The Court ordered the ruling to be applied retrospectively from April 27, 1994, thereby recognizing past injustices endured by domestic workers. Importantly, the Court highlighted that the valid recognition of domestic work within the legal framework is pivotal for the broader goal of social equity and justice in South Africa.

This judgment serves as a landmark decision in affirming the rights of domestic workers and sets a precedent for advancing equality in labor relations. It emphasizes the need for ongoing legislative reform to align with constitutional mandates, promoting the protection of vulnerable workers and enhancing the recognition of their contributions to society. The ruling reaffirms that a truly egalitarian society necessitates the inclusion of all workers under protective legislative frameworks.
