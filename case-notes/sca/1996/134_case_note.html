Supreme Court of Appeal

Cape Dairy Co-operative Ltd v Ferreira [1996] ZASCA 138

In late 1992, Ferreira decided to terminate his membership due to his intention to market his dairy products in competition with the appellant.

Supreme Court of Appeal 1996 [1996] ZASCA 138

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case before the Supreme Court of South Africa revolves around the appellant, Cape Dairy Co-operative Limited, and the respondent, Frank Wallace Ferreira. The fundamental issue was whether Ferreira remained a member of the co-operative as of 1 November 1993. The appellant is a dairy co-operative established under the Co-operative Act 91 of 1981.

In late 1992, Ferreira decided to terminate his membership due to his intention to market his dairy products in competition with the appellant. He communicated this intention to Thompson, a director of the appellant, and later met with Marais, an employee, in April 1993, where he completed and signed a form requesting termination of his membership.

On 16 June 1993, Ferreira received a letter from the appellant's management informing him that his application for termination had been approved and enclosed a check for R700, representing his share capital. However, in September 1993, the members of the appellant adopted special resolutions that conferred certain benefits starting from 1 November 1993. Ferreira contended that he was entitled to these benefits, which he argued were due since, according to clause 33(1) of the appellant's statute, his membership termination was effective only at the end of the book year on 28 February 1994.

The appellant opposed Ferreira's claims, asserting that his membership had effectively terminated upon the board's June 1993 approval of his application. The Cape Provincial Division, under Van Zyl J, ruled in favor of Ferreira, granting him the declaratory orders sought. The appellant was then granted leave to appeal ([paras 1–5]).

I – Issues

The core legal questions raised by this appeal were:

  1. Was there an effective termination of Ferreira's membership prior to 1 November 1993?
  2. Did the board of directors of the appellant have the authority to consent to a summary termination of Ferreira's membership, despite the provisions outlined in the appellant's statute, particularly clauses 29 and 33?
  3. Was the intent behind Ferreira's resignation communicated sufficiently, allowing for a summary termination to occur ([paras 6–7])?

R – Rules

The court considered several relevant statutory provisions and principles of contract law:

  1. Co-operative Act 91 of 1981: Section 81 allows for the termination of membership, while section 107(1) authorizes the board of directors to exercise the powers of the co-operative subject to its statute.
  2. Appellant’s Statute:
  • Clause 33(1): Outlines that a member's resignation is effective only at the end of the book year, provided a notice of such resignation has been given at least three months prior.
  • Clause 29(1): Provides the framework for the withdrawal of shares held by a member who has terminated their farming operations through written notice and board approval ([paras 9–10]).

The court also relied on precedent in Savage v Cape Dairy Co-operative Limited (unreported), which offered insight into the interpretation of similar statutes in relation to resignations and terminations.

A – Application

The court critically examined whether Ferreira's termination could have been deemed effective by mutual consent of the member and the board, diverging from the strict provisions of clause 33 of the statute.

Van Heerden JA analyzed the existing miscommunication surrounding the standard form filled out by Ferreira and the board's approval. The court noted that Ferreira's intent was clear: he sought to terminate his membership to begin competing in the market. The board’s approval of his request on 16 June 1993, along with the accompanying check payment, indicated consent to Ferreira’s immediate termination of membership rather than simply acknowledging a resignation.

The court delineated that an agreement between Ferreira and the board was permissible under the statutes, particularly given the absence of an explicit clause forbidding such termination by mutual consent. It considered the circumstances of Ferreira’s application, including the nature of the request and the board’s affirmative response, concluding these indicated an unequivocal agreement to terminate his membership ([paras 11–15]).

The court rejected the arguments suggesting that a clear waiver of statutory rights was necessary for concurrent termination by mutual consent, positing instead that the nature of the membership relationship inherently allows for such agreement ([paras 15–16]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of South Africa allowed the appeal, concluding that Ferreira’s membership was terminated prior to 1 November 1993, thereby making him ineligible to claim the benefits arising after that date. The court held that there was sufficient mutual consent between the parties to validate the summary termination, contrary to the lower court's interpretation of the statutory requirements. As a result, Ferreira was not entitled to the benefits associated with the membership post-termination.

The appeal was granted with costs, including the costs of two counsel, emphasizing the court's position on the validity of the termination agreement within the bounds of the cooperative's statutes ([paras 16–16]).

This case underscores the importance of clear communication and mutual agreement in matters of contractual and statutory interpretation, particularly within cooperative entities.
