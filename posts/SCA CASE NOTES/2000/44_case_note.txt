# Case Note: Rapporteerbaar

**Court:** DIE HOOGSTE HOF VAN APPèL VAN SUID-AFRIKA  
**Case Number:** 657/98  
**Appellant:** GF WESSELS NO  
**1st Respondent:** CJ DE JAGER NO  
**2nd Respondent:** JF PEYPER NO  
**Judges:** Van Heerden WndHR, Smalberger, Olivier, Zulman ARR, Farlam WndAR  
**Hearing Date:** 15 August 2000  
**Delivery Date:** 22 September 2000  

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

[1] The central issue in this appeal revolves around the legal consequences following the sequestration of an individual's estate. Specifically, it concerns a situation where an insolvent individual’s wife, married in community of property, takes out a life insurance policy naming the insolvent as a beneficiary, and subsequently dies intestate. The insolvent refuses to accept the benefits from both the insurance policy and the inheritance. The key question is whether these benefits became vested in the curator of the insolvent estate by virtue of their refusal to accept said benefits.

[2] According to Section 20(2)(b) of the Insolvency Act 24 of 1936, property that would have accrued to an insolvent during their insolvency vests in the curator of the insolvent estate. Furthermore, "goods" as defined in Section 2 includes movable property and conditional rights.

[3] The appellant contends that the rights conferred upon the insolvent in the given scenario constitute movable property, a claim noted by the court as evidently unfounded.

[4] The appellant acknowledges that should their argument be grounded in solid principles, the right to accept gifts during insolvency would indeed vest in the curator. The legal dictionary defines “right” as a network of contractual obligations requiring a mutual relationship between parties involved. Acceptance of such an offer grants the insolvent a mere prerogative rather than an enforceable right, as the offer can be rescinded at any time by the proposer.

### I – Issues

[5] The primary legal question is whether the insurance benefit and intestate inheritance vested in the curator of the insolvent estate once the insolvent refused to accept these benefits. This inquiry addresses the nature of the rights of the insolvent husband, his agency over the benefits, and the implications of his refusal to accept.

[6] Secondary considerations include whether the rights lost by the insolvent upon refusal of benefits can still be claimed or formed part of the curator's estate, and whether these rights could ever be considered contingent or conditional.

### R – Rules

[7] Key legal principles highlighted in the case reference Section 20(2)(b) of the Insolvency Act, which stipulates that goods acquired during insolvency form part of the insolvent estate. This is integral to understanding what constitutes a vested right.

[8] The court also looks into precedents that examine rights and duties in contract law, specifically referencing the concepts of offers and conditional acceptance rights. The case law of Kellerman NO v Van Vuuren 1994 (4) SA 336 (T), Klerck and Schärges NNO v Lee 1995 (3) SA 340 (SE), and Boland Bank v Du Plessis 1995 (4) SA 113 (T) play into the rationale of conditionality and the timing of rights in contract law.

[9] Additionally, the writings of De Groot and Voet are cited to reinforce the argument regarding the implications of death on the validity of offers and thereby connecting offer acceptance to the rights conferred.

### A – Application

[10] The court analyzes the fundamental basis of the argument presented by the appellant and ultimately finds that the rights attributed to the insolvent were merely prerogatives awaiting acceptance. The court reaffirmed that failure to accept these rights means they could not automatically transfer to the curator's estate.

[11] The ruling dictates that the rights associated with the insurance benefit and inheritance don't become enforceable until explicitly accepted by the recipient. Thus, the curator's position was not fortified by the insolvent's refusal. The reasoning echoes the principle that contingent rights, lacking acceptance, fail to survive independently of the proposer’s intentions and actions.

### C – Conclusion

[12] The appeal is dismissed with costs in favor of the respondents, and a further order regarding the unnecessary inclusion of discussion on procedural matters is noted. The decision reinforces that rights of inheritance and benefits from insurance remain contingent upon acceptance and do not irrevocably vest in the curator merely due to the insolvency status.

[13] The legal implications draw attention to the intrinsic nature of rights in insolvency, shining a light on the responsibilities and privileges of not only the insolvent but also the curator. This case thereby underscores the necessary precision in navigating the intersection between insolvency proceedings and contract law, particularly in the context of conditional benefits. The dismissal serves as a cautionary note for those in similar circumstances regarding the significance of timely acceptance and acknowledgement of their rights.
