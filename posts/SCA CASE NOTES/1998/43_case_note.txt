# Case Note: Valentino Globe B.V. v. Anthony Howard Phillips and Another (Case No. 6/96)

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

The appellant, Valentino Globe B.V. (hereinafter "Valentino"), a Dutch corporation originally known as Globelegance B.V., held a registered trademark for "Valentino" in class 25, which relates to clothing. The trademark was registered on 28 November 1973 under number B73/6127 ([1-13]). Valentino licensed several continental businesses to manufacture clothing under this mark and had local retailers selling these items in South Africa since 1976. Notably, Valentino itself did not engage in any business under this trademark within South Africa.

In contrast, the first respondent, Anthony Howard Phillips, operated in men’s clothing retail in Johannesburg and applied for the registration of the "Valentino" trademark on 5 November 1979 ([7-11]). Phillips sought to remove Valentino’s trademark from the register on grounds of non-use, filing his application at the start of 1980. However, the case remained dormant until it was eventually heard in late 1995. At that hearing, the parties acknowledged two preliminary issues: locus standi (standing to sue) and proof of non-use, agreeing that if Valentino failed on these two points, the application would be granted as decided in *Adcock-Ingram Laboratories Ltd v SA Druggists Ltd* ([3-5]).

The trial court, following *Adcock-Ingram*, found for Phillips and ordered the removal of Valentino’s trademark ([9-11]). Valentino appealed on two grounds: whether Phillips was legally a "person aggrieved" as defined by s 36(1)(b) of the Trade Marks Act 62 of 1963, and the correctness of the *Adcock-Ingram* ruling as it pertained to third-party use of a trademark.

### I – Issues

The primary legal issues presented in this case were:
1. Whether Phillips held the necessary locus standi to apply for the removal of Valentino’s trademark, given the interpretation of "person aggrieved" under s 36(1)(b) of the Trade Marks Act 62 of 1963 ([11-13]).
2. The correctness of the precedent set in *Adcock-Ingram Laboratories Ltd v SA Druggists Ltd* regarding "use" of a trademark by unregistered users or licensees ([11-15]).

### R – Rules

The applicable legal rules originate from the Trade Marks Act 62 of 1963 and relevant case law. 

- According to s 36(1)(b) of the Act, a trademark may be removed if it has not been used for a continuous period of five years ([1-5]). 
- In *Adcock-Ingram*, it was established that the "use" of a trademark by someone other than its registered proprietor does not suffice to prevent removal due to non-use ([9-11]).
- Case law interpreting "person aggrieved" indicates that a party does not qualify unless they have a substantive interest in the trademark or would be materially impacted by its presence on the register ([15-17]).

### A – Application

The court found that Phillips’ locus standi was problematic. Although he was the individual who applied for trademark registration in conjunction with trading as a clothing retailer, the evidence demonstrated he was not operating under the "Valentino" trademark in a meaningful way. His claims regarding the proprietary rights in "Valentino" stemmed from his business interests in Fran & Antonio (Pty) Ltd, which he managed, but which did not hold a legal claim to the trademark that could establish him as a "person aggrieved" ([17-19]).

The court critiqued Phillips for failing to clearly distinguish between his personal interests and those of the corporate entities with which he was associated. As established in prior cases, a party must substantiate their claims to be aggrieved, particularly rooted in direct trading intentions or rights over the mark. The court summarized that Phillips had never genuinely traded under the "Valentino" mark, undermining his position as an "aggrieved party" ([19-21]). 

The court also explored the implications of corporate law concerning ownership and control. The interpretation of Phillips’ claims through the lens of various entities illustrated a neglect of the corporate veil doctrine, failing to substantiate that he could bring forth a legitimate application for trademark removal due to a perceived grievance ([21-23]). The consideration of "use" as determined in *Adcock-Ingram* suggested that even if the court were to relax the corporate distinctions as Phillips argued, he still could not meet the criteria for trademark ownership or intended use required to satisfy registration criteria ([21-23]).

### C – Conclusion

The court ultimately concluded that Phillips did not possess locus standi as a "person aggrieved" under s 36(1)(b) of the Trade Marks Act 62 of 1963. Valentino's trademark was held valid on the basis that there was no substantive evidence indicating Phillips' engagement in legitimate trading under the "Valentino" trademark, nor any registration or submission documents that established a proper claim ([11-15]). As a result, Valentino's appeal was upheld, the order of the lower court was set aside, and the application for removal was dismissed, illustrating the importance of substantiated ownership and intended commercial use in trademark law ([11-15]).

In essence, this judgement reaffirmed the principles of trademark ownership and use while clarifying the concept of locus standi in trademark disputes. The implications extend to those engaging in trademark licensing and the corporate structure’s complexities when such structures are not adequately delineated in legal claims. The case stresses the strict adherence to statutory requirements around use and ownership in trademark law.
