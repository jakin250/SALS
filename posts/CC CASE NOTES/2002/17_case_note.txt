Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2002

# FIRAC Case Note: Adriaan Secundus Van der Spuy v The General Council of the Bar of South Africa (CCT 48/01)

## F – Facts
[1] The applicant, Adriaan Secundus Van der Spuy, is an advocate of the High Court of South Africa and a member of the Independent Advocates Association of South Africa (IAASA). On 2 April 1998, the Pretoria High Court found him guilty of unprofessional conduct, resulting in a six-month suspension from practice. This judgment, cited as *General Council of the Bar v Van der Spuy* (1999), arose from a complaint by the General Council of the Bar of South Africa (GCB) alleging that Van der Spuy had violated the "referral rule," which mandates that advocates must receive instructions and fees for their work through attorneys ([1]).

[2] Following his suspension, Van der Spuy sought leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which was denied. His petition for special leave to appeal was also dismissed on 19 September 2001, leading to no further action on his part, including an application to this Court.

[3] Van der Spuy applied for direct access to the Constitutional Court under Rule 17 of the Constitutional Court Rules, arguing that the referral rule contravenes the Constitution's provisions in sections 22, 34, and 35(2)(b). The application received considerable interest, with various parties invited to intervene, including the GCB, the Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development, and the Law Society of South Africa ([4]).

[4] The GCB opposed the application, asserting that Van der Spuy had failed to demonstrate why direct access was justified, as well as presenting an implausible case on the merits. The Minister contended that the referral rule hinders young black and female entrants to the legal profession.

## I – Issues
[5] The primary legal issue revolves around the constitutionality of the GCB's referral rule and whether the applicant can substantiate the claim that this rule violates his constitutional rights, thus meriting direct access to the Constitutional Court ([3]).

[6] Additional considerations concern the procedural legality of the path taken by Van der Spuy after his initial judgment, specifically his decision not to appeal to the Constitutional Court after the SCA's dismissal of his appeal to evaluate the merits of the referral rule ([8]).

## R – Rules
[7] The applicable rules of law include:
- **Section 22 of the Constitution**, which affirms the right to freely choose a trade, occupation, or profession, subject to legal regulation.
- **Section 34 of the Constitution**, which guarantees the right to have disputes resolved in a fair public hearing before an independent and impartial tribunal ([3]).
- **Section 35(2)(b)**, ensuring the right to consult with a legal practitioner.

Additionally, Rule 17 of the Constitutional Court's Rules requires that for direct access to be granted, the applicant must demonstrate the importance of the constitutional issues and why it is in the interests of justice ([6]). 

## A – Application
[8] The Court examined whether the circumstances warranted granting direct access, as such applications are typically confined to exceptional cases. It emphasized multiple factors, including the significance of constitutional issues and how bypassing lower courts could hinder justice ([7]).

[9] Van der Spuy's failure to pursue the typical appellate route following the SCA's dismissal led the Court to question the necessity and urgency of adjudicating this matter directly. It noted that significant constitutional questions associated with the referral rule had been previously addressed in cases like *De Freitas v Society of Advocates of Natal* ([9]), suggesting that Van der Spuy's current application might resemble a disguised appeal against prior decisions without following appropriate legal procedures.

[10] The Court highlighted that adjudication of issues involving factual disputes is best conducted in a high court, which has the authority to consider evidence. The lack of a full record from previous hearings was also a significant limitation, as new factual issues raised by the Minister and intervenors were complicating the matter ([12]).

## C – Conclusion
[11] Ultimately, the Constitutional Court denied the application for direct access, concluding that it would not serve the interests of justice to grant such access given the unresolved factual disputes and Van der Spuy's bypassing of normal procedural channels ([20]).

[12] The Court stated that direct access should not be used to circumvent the disciplined management of legal proceedings, recommending that constitutional matters should be appropriately initiated in high courts, followed by appeals to the SCA, and only later to the Constitutional Court if necessary ([14]).

[13] The order dismissed the application for direct access without an order for costs, reflecting a consensus among the justices ([21]). This decision reinforces the importance of adhering to procedural norms and illustrates the Court's commitment to ensuring that constitutional matters are adjudicated within established legal frameworks.
