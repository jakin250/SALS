Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

# FIRAC Case Note: Nduna v The State ([2010] ZASCA 120)

## F – Facts
The case of **Nduna v The State** ([1]–[3]) centers on Michael Nduna, the appellant, who was convicted of two counts of armed robbery. The events leading to this case began on June 11, 1999, when David Alexander, the first complainant, was robbed in the Bo-Kaap area of Cape Town just after withdrawing R4,000 from a bank. He described the assailant as a dark-complexioned man who threatened him with a firearm, leading to the loss of the cash. The assailants escaped in a white Venture van, the registration of which Alexander noted ([4]–[6]).

On January 26, 2007, Joshua Abrahams, the second complainant, was similarly robbed after withdrawing R54,300 from a bank while driving a company Isuzu bakkie. He described a coordinated armed attack involving multiple suspects bearing firearms, from which he sustained physical harm. Notably, there were no gloves worn by the assailants, and Abrahams was unable to provide detailed descriptions due to shock ([7]–[8]).

Both robbery scenes became significant for fingerprint evidence. Inspector Greg Hail found Nduna’s left thumbprint on the Venture van involved in Alexander’s robbery ([10]). Similarly, Inspector Hendrik Schreuder identified Nduna’s right palm print on the Isuzu bakkie associated with Abrahams’ robbery ([11]). Despite the absence of direct witness identification, the prosecution hinged its case on the fingerprint evidence. Nduna denied any involvement, claiming to have been working at the time of the first robbery and asserting an innocent explanation for the fingerprints ([13]).

Subsequently, Nduna’s appeal to the Western Cape High Court regarding his convictions was dismissed, setting the stage for his appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa ([2]–[3]).

## I – Issues
The legal questions presented in this appeal involve:
1. Whether the fingerprint evidence alone provides sufficient grounds for a conviction for armed robbery beyond a reasonable doubt, particularly in light of Nduna's defenses ([3], [14]);
2. The applicability and admissibility of similar fact evidence in establishing his guilt across multiple robbery charges ([17]–[19]);
3. The inference of guilt arising from circumstantial evidence, specifically the connection between Nduna's fingerprints and the modus operandi of the robberies ([21]).

## R – Rules
In resolving these issues, the court relied on several important legal principles:
- **Probative Value of Fingerprint Evidence**: Established through precedent that suggests such evidence significantly contributes to establishing identity ([12]), as in *S v Legote* 2001 (2) SACR 179 (SCA).
- **Inference of Guilt**: Based on *R v Blom* 1939 AD 202, where it was established that for an inference of guilt to be drawn, it must be consistent with all proved facts and exclude any reasonable doubt ([14]).
- **Similar Fact Evidence**: The law holds that while similar fact evidence can be admitted to establish identity and modus operandi, it cannot be used as confirmatory material for other counts without independent corroboration ([17]). The principles articulated in *S v Gokool* 1965 (3) 465 NPD and *R v Matthews* 1960 (1) SA 752 (A) guide the court on how such evidence relates to multiple counts ([19]).

## A – Application
The Supreme Court examined the fingerprint evidence through careful scrutiny of its origin and context. The court identified that the prints were the sole evidence linking Nduna to the robbery charges. The appellant's defense contended that the time elapsed since the crimes (eight years for the first robbery) and the absence of evidence regarding the age of the fingerprints diminished their probative value ([16]).

However, the court maintained that the existence of Nduna’s left thumbprint and right palm print on vehicles related to the robberies established a direct linkage to the crimes ([12], [22]). The court analyzed Nduna's explanation that his prints could have been transferred innocently, but found the argument unconvincing due to the nature of the robberies being nearly identical in methodology. Thus, the inference that he was involved in both robberies was not only consistent with the evidence but also the most reasonable conclusion based on the circumstantial evidence presented ([21]).

The court also emphasized that similar fact evidence was appropriately utilized to demonstrate a distinct pattern of criminal behavior, thereby countering the assertion that they should be adjudicated merely as isolated incidents ([18]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Nduna’s appeal, affirming both convictions for armed robbery and the reasoning of the lower courts ([23]). The court concluded that the fingerprint evidence was sufficient to establish guilt beyond reasonable doubt, despite Nduna’s defenses. The implications of the judgment reaffirmed the value of fingerprint evidence in criminal proceedings and clarified the standards for drawing inferences from circumstantial evidence. The upholding of the convictions signals the judiciary's commitment to leverage forensic evidence in establishing accountability for crimes such as armed robbery in South Africa ([23]).
