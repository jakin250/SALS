Z v Z (556/2021) [2022] ZASCA 113

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 113

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts ([paras 1–6])

The case before the Supreme Court of Appeal involves a divorce proceeding between the appellant, Mrs. Z (the mother), and the respondent, Mr. Z (the father). The couple married on January 10, 1995, and has two financially dependent children: a son, R, born on May 21, 1997, and a daughter, B, born on March 13, 1999. Following the deterioration of their marriage, the father vacated the family home in April 2018, prompting the mother to initiate divorce proceedings on April 9, 2019. This included claims for maintenance for herself and for the children.

The father filed a counterclaim, also seeking a decree of divorce. Central to the father's defense was a special plea asserting that, since the children had reached the age of majority, they possessed the necessary legal standing (locus standi) to pursue their own claims for maintenance, rendering the mother without standing to claim maintenance on their behalf. Disputing this, the mother contended that Section 6 of the Divorce Act 70 of 1970 allows a parent to claim maintenance for an adult dependent child in a divorce context.

I – Issues ([paras 6–10])

The primary issue before the court was whether a parent has locus standi in judicio to claim maintenance from the other parent on behalf of an adult dependent child following a divorce. Specifically, the court needed to determine the applicability and interpretation of Section 6 of the Divorce Act in light of conflicting decisions in lower courts regarding this legal capacity.

R – Rules ([paras 7–10])

The legal framework guiding this appeal is primarily found in the Divorce Act 70 of 1970, specifically:

  • Section 6(1): Mandates that no decree of divorce shall be granted until the court is satisfied regarding the welfare of any minor or dependent children.
  • Section 6(3): Empowers the court to make any order regarding the maintenance of a dependent child, with no distinction made between minor and adult dependent children.

Additionally, the court relied upon the established test of statutory interpretation, which dictates that statutory provisions must be given their ordinary grammatical meaning but must also be interpreted purposively and contextually, ensuring consistency with constitutional provisions.

A – Application ([paras 11–22])

The court began by acknowledging the legislative purpose of Section 6, which aims to safeguard the welfare of children—both minor and adult dependent—during divorce proceedings. Various judicial precedents were reviewed, illustrating a historical disparity in interpretations of Section 6 regarding a parent’s ability to claim maintenance for adult dependent children.

The court found that interpreting Section 6 in a manner that excludes a parent’s ability to claim on behalf of an adult dependent child would undermine the constitutional rights to dignity, emotional well-being, and equality. It emphasized that many young adults might not attain financial independence as soon as they reach the age of majority, and thus should not be deprived of parental support simply because they have “aged out” of minor status.

The court also noted practical implications: if a parent lacked standing to claim on behalf of an adult dependent child, this could lead to unnecessary conflict, prompting adult children to choose sides in their parents' disputes. By contrast, the court's decision enables both parents to collectively support their children’s financial needs without exacerbating familial discord.

Moreover, drawing on socio-economic considerations and gender inequalities highlighted in cases like Bannatyne v Bannatyne, the court concluded that maintaining the status quo would perpetuate disparities between custodial parents—usually mothers—and non-custodial parents—typically fathers, leading to adverse outcomes for children.

C – Conclusion ([paras 22–23])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, ruling that the mother possesses the necessary locus standi to claim maintenance for the dependent children despite their status as adults. The court's interpretation of Section 6 of the Divorce Act affirms the right of parents to jointly support their adult dependent children post-divorce, thus enhancing financial stability and promoting gender equality.

The previous order of the High Court was set aside, and the father's special plea dismissing the mother’s claims for maintenance was rejected. The judgment not only clarified the legal standing issue but also emphasized the importance of protecting dependent children from the complications that divorce proceedings entail. The court ordered costs to be awarded to the mother, reflecting the successful litigation effort she undertook.

This ruling has significant implications for future divorce proceedings in South Africa, particularly concerning how courts navigate maintenance claims for dependent adult children, thereby reinforcing their welfare at the forefront of such considerations.
