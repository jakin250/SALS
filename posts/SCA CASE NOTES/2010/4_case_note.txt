# Case Note: Standard Bank of South Africa Ltd v The Master of the High Court (103/09) [2010] ZASCA 4

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts [1–42]
The case concerns the winding-up of Intramed (Pty) Ltd ("Intramed") and the conduct of its joint liquidators, Basil Brian Nel and Michael Leo De Villiers. Prior to its liquidation, Intramed was part of the Macmed group, which faced significant financial challenges, leading to its eventual collapse and the appointment of Nel and De Villiers as liquidators in 1999. Standard Bank, a registered commercial bank, was a proved creditor of Intramed, with claims amounting to R107,728,463.64.

The primary issues arose from allegations by Standard Bank that Nel and De Villiers had failed in their fiduciary duties by unduly favouring Macmed’s claims over those of other creditors like Standard Bank. Concerns were raised regarding the admission of a R325 million claim by Macmed against the estate of Intramed, which Standard Bank contested on grounds of irregularity due to conflicting records and improper use of Intramed funds for personal litigation costs incurred by the liquidators.

On appeal from the Eastern Cape High Court, where Standard Bank sought the removal of the liquidators and a reduction of their fees, the Supreme Court of Appeal had to decide whether the liquidators demonstrated proper conduct in their duties and whether they should be removed or sanctioned.

### I – Issues [2–3, 10]
1. Whether the liquidators, Nel and De Villiers, discharged their duties satisfactorily in the winding-up of Intramed, particularly with regard to acting independently and in the interests of all creditors.
2. Whether the R325 million claim from Macmed, against which Standard Bank lodged a complaint, should have been admitted by the liquidators.
3. Whether the liquidators misappropriated Intramed’s funds and whether their fees should be disallowed or reduced.

### R – Rules [1, 11–12, 124–136]
The legal framework governing this case includes:
- The Companies Act 61 of 1973, specifically sections 384(2) (regarding remuneration of liquidators) and 394(7) (penalties for improper use of funds).
- Section 41 of the Insolvency Act, which necessitates the calling of meetings by trustees to address creditors' concerns.
- Established case law asserting the duties of liquidators to act with independence, impartiality, and in the best interests of the estate and all creditors, as outlined in the precedent cases, including Hudson NNO v Wilkins NO, Ma-Afrika Groepbelange, and Estate Friedman v Katzeff.

### A – Application [1–121]
The court critically analyzed the conduct of the liquidators, with special emphasis on their handling of Macmed’s claim of R325 million. It examined the complexities of the Peregrine financing structure, which underpinned the claim and the liquidators' reliance on this and other legal advice. Key findings included:
- The court expressed concern that Nel and De Villiers appeared to act in concert with the interests of Macmed, failing to interrogate the validity of the claim against Intramed sufficiently. 
- The liquidators had not sought an independent legal opinion regarding the validity of the claim, nor had they explored the financial records of Intramed adequately before admitting the claim.
- Regarding the alleged misappropriation of funds, it was found that Intramed’s monies were used to pay for legal costs related to a review application brought by the liquidators in their personal capacities without proper authority or approval from the creditors.

In addressing the liquidators’ use of Intramed's funds, the court highlighted their failure to promptly repay the amount following criticism of their conduct and the legal orders from prior proceedings.

The court concluded that the cumulative effect of Nel and De Villiers' actions evinced an apparent conflict of interest, lack of objectivity, and failure to uphold their fiduciary duties, thereby warranting their removal from the liquidator role.

### C – Conclusion [138–138]
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Standard Bank’s appeal, ordering the removal of Nel and De Villiers as joint liquidators of Intramed and reducing their remuneration by five percent. The court ruled that their conduct did not meet the requisite standard of independence and proper administration expected of liquidators, emphasizing the importance of impartiality in the liquidation process for safeguarding the interests of all creditors.

The implications of this decision underscore the obligations of liquidators to act in the best interests of the estate and maintain transparency in their dealings with creditor claims. This judgment serves as a significant reminder of the fiduciary responsibilities imposed on liquidators and the courts' readiness to enforce these standards to ensure fair outcomes in insolvency proceedings.
