Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: The South African Broadcasting Corporation SOC Limited v Democratic Alliance and Others

## F – Facts
This case stemmed from a report by the Public Protector of South Africa, released on February 17, 2014, concerning complaints about the conduct of the South African Broadcasting Corporation (SABC) and its Chief Operating Officer (COO), Hlaudi Motsoeneng. The Public Protector's investigation highlighted significant governance deficiencies and irregularities in Motsoeneng's appointment, including accusations of fraud regarding his qualifications ([paras 4–6]). Specifically, the report noted failures in corporate governance that permitted Motsoeneng, who misrepresented his qualifications, to ascend to and justify increased remuneration in his appointed role ([paras 6–8]).

Following the Public Protector's findings, the SABC Board unilaterally resolved to appoint Motsoeneng as COO without adhering to the necessary legal procedures. This decision was made despite ongoing investigations into the allegations against him ([paras 9–10]). The Democratic Alliance (DA), the official opposition party, challenged this decision, seeking urgent relief to suspend Motsoeneng pending a disciplinary inquiry and set aside his permanent appointment.

The High Court first ruled that the Public Protector's findings were not binding; it determined that state organs could disregard her findings under certain conditions, leading to a conclusion that the SABC's actions were rational despite the problematic background provided by the Public Protector ([paras 19–20], [22]).

The DA's appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) contested the validity of the High Court's judgment regarding the authority of the Public Protector and the nature of her remedial action. The SCA had to examine the constitutional framework establishing the Public Protector's role, considering how state institutions interact with her findings and whether those findings hold legal effect ([paras 22, 52]).

## I – Issues
The principal issue was whether the findings and remedial actions of the Public Protector are binding on state organs and therefore enforceable in the face of explicit contravention by the SABC and the Minister of Communications. The case raised broader questions about the role of Chapter Nine institutions in South Africa's constitutional democracy, particularly concerning the separation of powers and the accountability of state actors ([para 23]).

1. Are the findings and remedial actions of the Public Protector categorically binding on state institutions?
2. Does the conduct of the SABC and the Minister of Communications in appointing Motsoeneng violate the principle of co-operative governance under the Constitution?
3. How do the binding or non-binding nature of the Public Protector’s directives affect the accountability of state actors in governance?

## R – Rules
The constitutional grounding for the Public Protector’s powers is found primarily in Section 182 of the Constitution of South Africa, which explicitly authorizes the Public Protector to investigate misconduct in government affairs, report upon that conduct, and take appropriate remedial action ([para 28]). The interactions between the Public Protector's findings and the administrative duties of state institutions are further governed by the Public Protector Act 23 of 1994, which outlines her investigative and remedial authority ([paras 32–38]).

The principle of legality, embedded in the Constitution, obligates state institutions to exercise their power within the confines of the law ([para 59]). It holds that public officials are accountable and that the exercise of power must conform to constitutional principles, particularly regarding transparency, ethical conduct, and responsiveness in governance. 

In previous cases, such as *Public Protector v Mail and Guardian Ltd* [2011] ZASCA 108, this court established that the nature of the Public Protector’s decisions should be respected and upheld, symptomatic of a broader commitment to good governance ([para 52]).

## A – Application
The SCA examined the statutory and constitutional foundations of the Public Protector's office, determining that the act of disregarding the Public Protector’s findings fundamentally undermines her role as a guardian against state misconduct. The court underscored that a refusal to comply with her findings effectively negates the ethos of accountability, which is central to constitutional democracy in South Africa. The argument advanced by the defenders of Motsoeneng's appointment—that the findings were not binding—was rebutted, highlighting the necessity of upholding the rule of law and the principles of co-operative governance ([paras 52–53]).

The SCA's decision emphasized that the failure of the SABC and the Minister to observe the Public Protector’s findings represents an egregious disregard for the cornerstone of South African constitutionalism: that public officials are accountable for their actions. Thus, the court determined that the appointment of Motsoeneng was unconstitutional and irrational, given the overwhelming evidence of misconduct presented by the Public Protector ([paras 59–62]).

Moreover, the High Court's notion that the findings of the Public Protector are merely recommendations was criticized for being fundamentally at odds with the constitutional mandate of the Public Protector to effectuate meaningful oversight against maladministration and corruption ([paras 45–49]).

## C – Conclusion
The SCA dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming the High Court's order for disciplinary proceedings against Motsoeneng and reiterating that the findings of the Public Protector must not be disregarded by state institutions. The court underscored the need to uphold the office of the Public Protector as a crucial component in enforcing accountability within state structures, thus ensuring the integrity of governance.[para 70].

The ruling represents a significant reaffirmation of the accountability framework established by South Africa’s Constitution and emphasizes the binding nature of administrative findings made by Chapter Nine institutions. This case enhances the scrutiny of public administration, advocating for a governance paradigm that steadfastly respects the investigative authority of the Public Protector within the broader context of constitutional checks and balances ([para 66]).

This ruling holds implications for future interrelations among state institutions and the Public Protector, emphasizing a co-operative approach that respects the authority and findings of this critical oversight body ([para 53]).

---

Thus, this case notably shapes the landscape of public accountability and the role of the Public Protector in South Africa's constitutional framework.
