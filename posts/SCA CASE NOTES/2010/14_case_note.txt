# Case Note on Lachman v The State (432/09) [2010] ZASCA 14

**FIRAC Method**

---

### F - Facts

The case involves Akash Lachman (the appellant), who was employed as an auditor by the South African Revenue Service (SARS) in East London. Lachman was convicted in a regional court of corruption under section 1(1)(b) of the Corruption Act 94 of 1992. The conviction stemmed from accusations that he solicited a bribe from Mr. Kwame Mokoena, a business owner with tax problems ([1]-[2]).

Upon conviction, Lachman was sentenced to five years' imprisonment, with two years conditionally suspended. His appeal to the Eastern Cape High Court was dismissed, prompting the current appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([2]). The core of the evidence against Lachman arose from a series of text messages (SMSs) sent to Mokoena from an anonymous cellphone that purportedly originated from within SARS, suggesting that the sender could assist with Mokoena’s tax issues for a fee ([5]-[10]).

On October 16, 2003, Mokoena facilitated a payment of R20,000, at the request of the anonymous SMS sender, through a controlled delivery coordinated with law enforcement. Mokoena deposited R5,000 in an envelope, which was later collected by Lachman at the SARS office, leading to his arrest when police conducted a search of his workplace ([11]-[18]). During the searches, several cellphones were located, including one (the brown cellphone) alleged to have been used in sending the incriminating messages ([15]-[19]).

Lachman denied any involvement or knowledge about the bribery scheme and suggested that he was being framed, arguing that the evidence was either obtained unlawfully or constituted a police trap ([20]-[21]). The trial court found the prosecution witnesses credible and Lachman's defenses unconvincing ([22]-[24]).

---

### I - Issues

The primary legal questions presented in this appeal include:

1. Did the actions of the police constitute a "trap," and was the evidence obtained as a result thereof inadmissible? ([25])
2. Was the search conducted by the police lawful, and did Lachman consent to the search? ([34])
3. Was the circumstantial evidence sufficient to establish the appellant's guilt beyond a reasonable doubt? ([40])

---

### R - Rules

The legal foundation for assessing the case revolves around the following statutes and precedents:

- **Corruption Act 94 of 1992**: Defines the offense of corruption that Lachman was charged with.
- **Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977**: Specifically Section 252A, which addresses the admissibility of evidence obtained through traps; Section 22 outlines the conditions under which police may conduct searches without a warrant.
- **Case Law**: The ruling relied upon principles established in **S v Malinga** [1963] and others, which delineate the definition of a "trap" in the context of criminal procedure. The precedent established categories of conduct that differentiate lawful police actions from entrapment ([28]-[30]).

---

### A - Application

The court meticulously analyzed whether Lachman’s defense regarding entrapment held merit. It was determined that Mokoena, not the police, initiated the solicitation of bribe. The police actions were framed as a controlled operation, whereby Mokoena acted to expose corruption rather than to create an opportunity for crime ([31]-[32]). Based on the evidentiary submissions, the court concluded that the police operation did not amount to a trap, as Mokoena was already under pressure and the scheme was not police-induced. Furthermore, even if it were deemed a trap, the police's handling of the situation would still fall within permissible boundaries per section 252A, as it provided Lachman the opportunity to commit corruption without influencing him to do so ([33]).

Regarding the search conducted at Lachman’s workplace, the court ruled that he had effectively consented to the search, countering the argument that the police needed to advise him of his rights prior to the search. The appellant’s admission that he allowed the police to search him further validated this position ([35]-[36]).

The circumstantial evidence included Lachman's specific knowledge of Mokoena's case and the immediate link between the messages and the appellant's actions. The court concluded that the combination of the circumstantial evidence and the direct evidence provided by colleagues observing Lachman with the implicated cellphone led to an overwhelming case against him ([41]-[47]).

---

### C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the conviction, concluding that Lachman was guilty of corruption based on the cumulative weight of circumstantial evidence and the conclusions drawn regarding the police conduct not constituting unlawful entrapment or search violations. The judgment not only reinforces the standards governing police conduct in corruption cases but also reaffirms the importance of circumstantial evidence in establishing guilt beyond a reasonable doubt ([47]). 

This ruling carries significant implications for future cases involving allegations of unlawful searches and entrapment, clarifying the extent of permissible police actions in controlled operations spearheaded by private individuals ([22]-[47]).

---

The appeal was dismissed, affirming the judgments of the lower courts ([2], [22], [47]).
