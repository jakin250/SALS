# Case Note: Steenkamp and Others v Edcon Limited ([2016] ZACC 1)

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The cases in question, CCT 46/15 and CCT 47/15, arose from a dispute involving Edcon Limited (the Respondent), which employed approximately 40,000 employees across Southern Africa. In response to operational requirements, Edcon issued notices under section 189(3) of the Labour Relations Act (LRA) on 18 April 2014, inviting consultation about potential dismissals due to restructuring ([2]–[3]).

Approximately 3,000 employees were subsequently retrenched. Notably, Edcon issued termination notices before the mandatory 30-day consultation period had elapsed, specifically under section 189A(8)(a) and (b) of the LRA, which stipulates that no dismissal notices may occur until the designated periods expire. This breach of statutory procedure was the core of the dispute ([7]–[8]).

The applicants, comprising former employees and the National Union of Metalworkers of South Africa (NUMSA), argued that the dismissals were invalid due to Edcon's failure to comply with the notice provisions regarding the consultation process. The Labour Appeal Court had produced conflicting decisions on this matter, prompting the applicants to seek a determination from the Constitutional Court on whether such dismissals were indeed invalid ([1], [2], [6], [9]).

### I – Issues
The primary issues addressed in this case included:
1. Whether leave to appeal should be granted ([24]).
2. Whether dismissals executed in violation of the statutory time periods stipulated in section 189A of the LRA were invalid ([24]).
3. Whether the procedural and substantive rights provided to employees under the LRA were adequate remedies for breaches of the statute ([24]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework governing this case includes:
- The Labour Relations Act, particularly sections 189 and 189A, which outline the processes and requirements for dismissals based on operational requirements, including minimum notice periods ([2], [4]).
- Judicial precedents from the Labour Appeal Court, including *De Beers Group Services (Pty) Ltd v National Union of Mineworkers* and *Revan Civil Engineering Contractors and Others v National Union of Mineworkers and Others*, which maintained divergent views on the validity of dismissals made on short notice ([3], [9]).
- The definition of "dismissal" according to the LRA, indicating that an employer's termination of employment—whether lawful or unlawful—constitutes a dismissal ([12]).

### A – Application
The Constitutional Court, in a majority decision, assessed the Labour Appeal Court’s prior rulings and arguments presented by the respondents. The Court analyzed the purpose of statutory compliance within the LRA. It concluded that non-compliance with section 189A(8) did not render the dismissal null and void per se but rather constituted a wrongful or unfair dismissal subject to the ordinary remedies granted under the LRA, including reinstatement and compensation for unfair dismissal ([19]–[20], [22]). 

The Court emphasized the legislative intent behind section 189A—creating a "dismissal-free zone" during specified consultation periods to facilitate negotiation and prevent arbitrary terminations ([6], [44]). However, it concluded that dismissals made during the prescribed "cooling-off" period were still categorized as dismissals under the LRA even if deemed procedurally unfair, thus enabling affected employees to seek remedies under the statutory provisions rather than invoking common law declarations of invalidity ([25], [85]).

### C – Conclusion
In conclusion, the Court did not uphold the position taken by the Labour Appeal Court in *De Beers* and *Revan*. It determined that the statutory framework did not support the idea that dismissals executed in breach of the notice provisions were automatically invalid. Consequently, the appeal was dismissed, reaffirming that although Edcon breached procedural requirements, the dismissals were valid within the LRA's context as unfair dismissals rather than void ones. As a result, employees had access to adequate remedies under the LRA, including reinstatement under specific conditions, rather than relief based on invalidity claims ([85]–[86]). 

The implications of this decision clarify the interpretation of procedural compliance requirements in large-scale retrenchments under the LRA and reinforce the mechanism designed to balance employer rights against employee protections in labor relations.
