Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: **Afriforum NPC v Nelson Mandela Foundation Trust and Others** [2023] ZASCA 58

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–11])

The case centers around the gratuitous public display of the old South African flag, a symbol associated with apartheid and white supremacy. The appellant, **Afriforum NPC** (Afriforum), organized and participated in the **Black Monday protests** on October 30, 2017, against the murder of farmers, during which the old flag was reportedly displayed. In response, the **Nelson Mandela Foundation Trust** (NMF), as the first respondent, lodged a complaint with the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg, alleging that such displays contravened the **Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000** (the Equality Act).

The second and third respondents, the **Minister of Justice** and the **Department of Justice and Correctional Services**, were added to the proceedings along with the **South African Human Rights Commission** (SAHRC), which sought a declaratory order that section 10(1) of the Equality Act was unconstitutional for limiting its scope to the ‘words’ only.

The High Court, with Judge Mojapelo presiding, found against Afriforum, interpreting section 10(1) of the Equality Act as prohibiting not only words but also actions that express hate speech. The court ruled that the display of the old flag constituted hate speech, unfair discrimination, and harassment under sections 10(1), 7, and 11 of the Equality Act. Afriforum appealed the decision.

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 12–18])

The core issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the prohibition of gratuitous public display of the old South African flag infringed on Afriforum's right to freedom of expression under section 16(1) of the **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa**. Specific questions included:

1. Could the display of the old flag be categorized as hate speech, unfair discrimination, and harassment under the Equality Act?
2. Does the prohibition infringe on rights to dignity and freedom of assembly for those publicly displaying the old flag?
3. Did the Equality Court have the jurisdiction to grant the relief sought, particularly regarding prospective conduct not yet manifested?

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 29–64])

The Supreme Court interpreted and applied the following legal standards:

- **Section 16(1)** of the Constitution guarantees freedom of expression but excludes hate speech as defined in **section 10(1)** of the Equality Act, which broadly prohibits expressions that promote hatred or incite harm based on race, among other grounds.
- The **Equality Act** delineates frameworks for addressing hate speech (section 10), unfair discrimination (section 7), and harassment (section 11).
- The court referenced significant constitutional principles and prior decisions, such as **Qwelane v SAHRC** ([2021] ZACC 22), which affirmed the need for broad interpretation of hate speech regulations, and **Islamic Unity Convention v IBA** ([2002] ZACC 3), which emphasizes the state’s interest in regulating hate speech to maintain social harmony and the core values of dignity and equality.

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 29–64])

### Constitutionality and Interpretation of Section 10(1)

The Court built upon the findings of the High Court by reinforcing that the gratuitous display of the old flag is emblematic of a past marked by oppression and racial discrimination. The court noted:

1. **Symbolism**: The old flag served as a potent reminder of apartheid's hateful ideology, rationalizing that its display cannot be disentangled from its historical context, which inherently incites harm and propagates hate ([paras 33–50]).
  
2. **Intention and Impact**: The court adopted an objective test, contending that a reasonable observer would interpret the act of displaying the old flag as intentionally harmful. The emotional and psychological fallout from such displays particularly impacts black communities, perpetuating a sense of inferiority and endorsing systemic racism ([paras 42–50]).

3. **Incitement and Hate Speech**: The court validated the High Court's determination that the displays constituted hate speech by supporting its interpretation of section 10(1) of the Equality Act as inclusive of conduct—not merely words. This perspective aligns with constitutional imperatives to eradicate systemic discrimination ([paras 33–57]).

### Jurisdiction and Prohibition of Conduct

The appeal court firmly rejected Afriforum's assertions regarding the court's lack of jurisdiction or the inappropriateness of prospective declarations:

- Clarifying the role of the **Equality Court**, the court emphasized its function to provide swift justice to victims of discrimination and harassment, thus validating the high court's jurisdictional basis ([paras 21–25]).

- Addressing arguments concerning freedom of assembly and dignity, the court noted Afriforum's failure to demonstrate how the displays implicated the dignity of those displaying the flag, emphasizing that rights to dignity must be weighed against the dignity of marginalized groups adversely affected by such expressions ([paras 12–20]).

### Adjustments to High Court’s Order

Although the court reaffirmed the findings regarding public displays, it also amended the High Court order concerning private displays. The court ruled that while the NMF's application was focused on public displays, the court could not extend its order without adequate grounds or argument regarding private displays of the flag. Future cases regarding private displays would need to be adjudicated separately, reflecting careful judicial restraint ([paras 70–74]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 75])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the High Court’s ruling with specific amendments. The specific holding declared that any gratuitous public display of the old South African flag constitutes hate speech, unfair discrimination, and harassment under sections 10(1), 7, and 11 of the Equality Act, subject to the proviso contained in section 12.

### Implications

The decision carries significant implications for South Africa's ongoing struggle against the remnants of its apartheid past. It illustrates the judiciary's commitment to curbing hate speech and promoting equality within a constitutional democracy, while also asserting the need for careful delineation between public and private expressions. By clarifying the parameters of acceptable and unacceptable expressions under the Equality Act, this ruling aims to foster a more inclusive society where human dignity is upheld and systemic inequalities are actively challenged.
