# Case Note: Tswelopele Non-Profit Organisation v City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality [2007] SCA 70 (RSA)

## Factual Background

### Facts
In the early hours of March 31, 2006, approximately one hundred individuals were unlawfully evicted from their makeshift homes situated on vacant land in Garsfontein, Pretoria, by officials from three government agencies: the Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality (first respondent), the Department of Home Affairs (second respondent), and the South African Police Services (third respondent) ([1]).

The eviction, executed without a court order, involved not only the removal of these individuals but also the destruction of their crude shelters and personal belongings. Sixteen individuals, lacking South African documentation, were arrested and subsequently deported ([1], [2]). Despite the constitutional provision protecting individuals from eviction without a court order, the officials maintained that their actions were lawful, citing a supposed operation against 'alien vegetation' ([5]).

In response to this unlawful eviction, the Tswelopele Non-Profit Organisation (first appellant), dedicated to supporting marginalized communities, filed an urgent application in the Pretoria High Court ten days later. The application was joined by twenty-three evicted residents, who sought the restoration of their possessions and temporary shelter ([3]).

### Procedural History
The Pretoria High Court, presided over by Jordaan J, dismissed the application based on the ruling from *Rikhotso v Northcliff Ceramics*, which stated that the mandament van spolie (a common law remedy for dispossession) cannot apply if the property has been destroyed ([6]). However, the court declined to impose costs against the applicants, highlighting that the governmental agencies had acted unlawfully ([7]).

Leave to appeal was granted, leading to a significant change in the respondents' stance during the appeal, where they acknowledged their role in the unlawful eviction and apologised for their actions ([8]). 

## Legal Issues

### Issues
1. Whether the appeal had become perempted due to the applicants' conduct subsequent to the High Court ruling ([10]).
2. What form of relief, if any, was appropriate given the respondents' unlawful eviction of the appellants ([8], [19]).
3. Whether the mandament van spolie could be adapted to allow for the restoration of possession when the property in question has been destroyed ([20]).

## Legal Rules

### Rules
1. The Constitution of South Africa, particularly sections concerning the right to property (s 25(1)), the prohibition against arbitrary eviction (s 26(3)), and dignity (s 10) ([5], [15]).
2. The *Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE)*, which prohibits eviction without a court order ([2], [4]).
3. The common law doctrine of mandament van spolie, which protects possession irrespective of the legality of the possessor’s right ([22]).

### Relevant Case Law
- *Rikhotso v Northcliff Ceramics* 1997 (1) SA 526 (W) ([6]).
- *Fose v Minister of Safety and Security* 1997 (3) SA 786 (CC), which emphasizes the need for effective remedies for constitutional breaches ([17]).

## Application

### Analysis
1. **Peremption**: The court held that the appellants did not abandon their right to appeal despite subsequent court proceedings and a settlement in which some occupants were provided shelter. The identity of the applicants differed, and the nature of the relief sought was not identical ([12], [13]). Hence, the appeal was not perempted.

2. **Unlawful Eviction and Relief**: The court established that the respondents' actions violated not only the right to property but also the personal security and dignity of the affected individuals ([15]). The respondents’ acknowledgment of their unlawful conduct, along with the destructive consequences of their actions, reinforced the need for an effective remedy that addressed the urgency of the occupiers' plight ([8], [17]).

3. **Mandament van Spolie**: While the appellants sought a constitutional adaptation of the mandament van spolie to mandate reconstruction of destroyed homes, the court noted that while the general principle of restoring possession remains vital, extending this remedy to include reconstruction would alter its fundamental character ([22]-[24]). The court emphasized the necessity of developing a separate constitutional remedy that would both respect the rule of law and provide immediate relief to the affected individuals, that not only restores their homes but also acknowledges the broader societal implications of such constitutional violations ([20], [27]).

4. **Conclusion on Remedy**: Ultimately, the court decided that the appellants should receive temporary habitable dwellings, capable of being dismantled, to ensure respect for their rights under PIE and the Constitution. This decision sought to balance immediate relief with recognition of their status as unlawful occupiers ([28]).

## Conclusion

### Holding
The appeal succeeded, with the Supreme Court of Appeal ordering the respondents to construct temporary habitable shelters for the individual applicants, recognizing both the immediate needs of the occupiers and the imperative to uphold constitutional rights ([28]).

### Implications
This judgment marks a significant development in constitutional law in South Africa, particularly in relation to housing rights and the obligation of the state to respect individual dignity and security against unlawful state actions. The ruling reinforces the critical role of effective remedies in constitutional adjudication and affirms the judiciary’s responsibility to protect vulnerable populations against state overreach.

In summary, *Tswelopele Non-Profit Organisation v City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality* serves as a landmark case that articulates the balance between legal remedies available under constitutional provisions and common law, addressing the historical injustices faced by marginalized communities in South Africa while prioritizing immediate and practical relief to those affected. The court's commitment to upholding the rule of law while recognizing the urgent needs of marginalized persons exemplifies a progressive interpretation of constitutional rights in action.
