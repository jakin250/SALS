# FIRAC Case Note: Leon Joseph and Others v City of Johannesburg and Others; CCT 43/09 [2009] ZACC 30

## F – Facts 
The case revolves around an application for leave to appeal against a decision made by the South Gauteng High Court regarding the disconnection of electricity supply to Ennerdale Mansions, a block of flats rented by the applicants ([1]–[2]). The applicants, who are tenants, averred that the electricity supply was disconnected without prior notice on 8 July 2008 due to their landlord's substantial arrears with City Power, the municipal electricity supplier ([6]–[7]). Despite tenants making regular payments to the landlord, they were left without electricity for over a year, leading to poor living conditions ([9]).

The applicants pursued judicial relief from the High Court, arguing that they were entitled to procedural fairness, specifically a notice and an opportunity to make representations prior to the disconnection of service as mandated by the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([10]–[11]). The High Court, however, dismissed their application, asserting that the Electricity and Credit Control By-laws did not apply to the tenants as they were not recognized as “customers” under these statutory provisions ([10]–[11]).

Following this, the applicants appealed to the Constitutional Court, asserting violations of their rights under sections 10 and 26 of the South African Constitution, which guarantee human dignity and access to adequate housing ([12]–[13]).

## I – Issues
The Constitutional Court identified several core issues for determination: 

1. **Were the applicants entitled to procedural fairness under section 3 of PAJA before City Power terminated the electricity supply?**
2. **If so, what is the scope of procedural fairness required in the circumstances?**
3. **Were the Electricity By-laws and Credit Control By-laws consistent with PAJA and the Constitution?**
4. **Was City Power's non-compliance with the procedural fairness requirements justifiable under section 36 of the Constitution?** ([17]–[20]).

## R – Rules 
The court relied on the following legal principles and statutes:

1. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: Section 3 mandates that administrative actions adversely affecting rights must be procedurally fair ([11]). The court emphasized the broad interpretation of “rights” under this Act ([22]–[30]).
  
2. **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996**:
   - Section 10 (right to dignity).
   - Section 26 (right to adequate housing).
   - Section 33 (entitlement to lawful, reasonable and procedurally fair administrative action) ([12]–[13]).

3. **Municipal By-laws**:
   - The Electricity By-laws and Credit Control By-laws were scrutinized for their procedural provisions regarding the termination of electricity supply ([11]–[75]).

## A – Application 
The Court began by contextualizing the rights of the applicants within the framework of public law, signifying obligations of the state as enshrined in the Constitution and various statutes. It emphasized the underlying principle that municipalities have an obligation to provide basic services like electricity ([34]–[41]).

The court critically analyzed the definitions of “customer” in the municipal by-laws and ultimately found that the applicants, while not formal customers, had an entitlement to procedural fairness due to their reliance on the municipal service they were effectively receiving ([34]–[48]).

On the question of procedural fairness, the Court determined that City Power's handling of the disconnection lacked necessary consideration for the applicants' rights. It mandated that notice must have been provided before any disconnection, including adequate time for tenants to respond, thus respecting their rights under PAJA ([56]–[62]).

Lastly, the Court ruled that portions of the municipal by-laws that allowed for disconnections “without notice” were unconstitutional and invalid, thereby protecting the procedural rights of all affected individuals, irrespective of their contractual status with City Power ([66]–[76]).

## C – Conclusion 
The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal and upheld the appeal, concluding that the termination of electricity supply was unlawful. City Power was ordered to reconnect the electricity supply immediately ([78]). The court found that the inconsistent provisions in the municipal by-laws regarding notice and disconnection were unconstitutional ([77]–[78]). This ruling has significant implications for administrative justice and the treatment of tenants reliant on municipal services, reinforcing the procedural rights of individuals even in contractual disputes between landlords and service providers.

In summary, the ruling underscored the Constitutional Court’s commitment to ensuring that public service provision aligns with principles of fairness and accountability as critical to protecting human rights in local governance.
