Supreme Court of Appeal

FIRAC - MEC for the Department of Health v D[…] N[…] (924/2013)

In 2012, the doctor instituted action in the Free State High Court against the MEC, claiming damages for the injuries she sustained, arguing that they occurred in the course of her employment.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2014

F - Facts

This case concerns a claim for damages by Dr. E[...] J[...] D[...] N[...] (hereinafter referred to as "the doctor"), a female medical doctor employed by the Free State Department of Health. On October 30, 2010, at approximately 02:00, while on duty as a paediatric registrar at the Pelonomi Hospital, the doctor was raped by an intruder who accessed the hospital premises ([1]). The doctor sought to hold the MEC for the Department of Health liable for damages resulting from this heinous act.

In 2012, the doctor instituted action in the Free State High Court against the MEC, claiming damages for the injuries she sustained, arguing that they occurred in the course of her employment. The MEC filed a special plea asserting that the claim was precluded by section 35(1) of the Compensation for Occupational Injuries and Diseases Act 130 of 1993 (COIDA), which mandates a no-fault compensation system for employees injured during the course of their employment ([2]).

The parties agreed on several facts pertinent to the claim, including the circumstances leading up to the assault and the doctor’s responsibilities during her shift. Notably, the assailant was not affiliated with the hospital and gained illicit entry while under construction ([3]).

The High Court ultimately ruled that the incident did not arise out of and in the course of the doctor's employment as mandated by COIDA ([5]), and dismissed the MEC's special plea ([5]). The MEC appealed the decision ([5]).

I - Issues

The primary issue in this appeal is whether the doctor’s claim for damages arising from the rape is excluded by the provisions of section 35(1) of COIDA, specifically whether the incident constituted an "accident" that arose out of and in the course of her employment ([4], [10]). This issue requires consideration of the intent behind COIDA and the nature of the assault suffered by the doctor, inviting an inquiry into the broader implications of how such cases should be interpreted under existing legislation ([6], [32]).

R - Rules

The relevant legislative framework is embodied in COIDA, particularly section 35(1), which stipulates that employees cannot sue their employers for damages related to occupational injuries unless those injuries are compensated under the Act. Section 35(1) specifies:

> “No action shall lie by an employee... for the recovery of damages in respect of any occupational injury... against such employee’s employer” ([9]).

An "occupational injury" is defined as a personal injury sustained as a result of an "accident," which itself must arise out of and in the course of the employee's employment ([9]). The court referenced various precedents that delineate the criteria under which injuries may be deemed to arise from employment, including case law such as Khoza, in which the nature of the assault and its connection to employment was critically evaluated ([16]).

A - Application

The court scrutinized whether the rape constituted an "accident" within the meaning of COIDA. The High Court had concluded that the rapist’s attack bore no relation to the doctor’s employment, despite occurring on the hospital premises ([5]). The Supreme Court of Appeal reiterated that for a claim to be precluded under COIDA, the injury must be a risk inherently connected to the employment ([17], [19]).

The court emphasized the serious and egregious nature of rape, stating that it could not be deemed incidental to the doctor's role as a paediatric registrar ([32]). The ruling also juxtaposed other forms of assault with the unique circumstances surrounding rape, noting that unless there were specific conditions that made such a risk inherent in the working environment, the claim should not be restricted to COIDA's provisions. This aligns with wider judicial frameworks that have recognized the exceptional nature of sexual crimes ([27], [29]).

Ultimately, the court found that the causal connection between the employment and the injury was severed since the act of rape did not represent an inherent risk associated with the doctor's employment, thereby permitting the claim to proceed outside the confines of COIDA ([32]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the decision of the Free State High Court, dismissing the MEC's appeal with costs ([34]). The ruling reaffirms that rape and sexual assault are not merely workplace hazards and that the strictures of COIDA should not inhibit a victim's pursuit of justice and compensation in such circumstances.

The court's ratio decidendi highlighted the need for a nuanced interpretation of occupational injuries, advocating for the protection of vulnerable groups in the workplace while urging courts to interpret legislation in ways that uphold constitutional values and social justice ([33], [34]). This case serves as a crucial precedent in extending protections for employees against violent acts that fall outside traditional interpretations of workplace risks, particularly in contexts where the safety of women is at stake.
