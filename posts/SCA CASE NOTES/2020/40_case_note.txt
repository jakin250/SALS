Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# Case Note: Minister of Environmental Affairs and Another v ArcelorMittal South Africa Limited ([2020] ZASCA 40)

## F - Facts

The case arises from a dispute between the Minister of Environmental Affairs (First Appellant) and the Deputy Director-General: Legal, Authorisations, Compliance and Enforcement (Second Appellant), and ArcelorMittal South Africa Limited (AMSA) concerning environmental regulations and the management of Basic Oxygen Furnace slag (BOF slag) ([paras 1-2]). AMSA, a major steel manufacturing company, acknowledged generation of BOF slag as a by-product of its operations since the 1970s, coming from two primary sources: "current arisings" and "reclaimed slag" ([para 10]). 

The Department of Environmental Affairs conducted inspections and subsequently issued a compliance notice and directive to AMSA on December 7, 2015, asserting that AMSA was disposing of BOF slag without a Waste Management Licence (WML) as required by the National Environmental Management: Waste Act 59 of 2008 (NEM:WA) ([paras 13–14]). 

Following AMSA’s internal appeal against these decisions being dismissed by the Minister on July 5, 2016 ([para 19]), AMSA sought judicial review in the High Court, which ruled in favor of AMSA. The High Court found the Minister's decisions to be materially flawed and ordered the review and setting aside of the directive and compliance notice issued against AMSA ([para 20]).

## I - Issues

The core legal issues in this case include:

1. Did the Deputy Director-General (DDG) act within his statutory powers when issuing the compliance notice and directive?
2. Was the Minister correct to dismiss AMSA’s appeal against the directive and objection to the compliance notice?
3. Was AMSA subject to the provisions of the Environmental Conservation Act 73 of 1989 (ECA), NEMA, and the NEM:WA based on its historical operations prior to their enactment?
4. Was AMSA required to obtain WMLs under the NEM:WA for activities concerning its old BOF slag disposal site operational since the 1970s? ([paras 27–28]).

## R - Rules

The relevant legal framework includes several statutes designed to protect the environment:

- **Constitution of South Africa, 1996**: Section 24 guarantees the right to an environment not harmful to health and well-being ([para 4]).
- **National Environmental Management Act 107 of 1998 (NEMA)**: Establishes principles for environmental governance ([para 26]) and provides for permitting under section 22(1), preventing pollution ([para 24]).
- **National Environmental Management: Waste Act 59 of 2008 (NEM:WA)**: Defines "waste" and specifies that activities related to waste management require a WML ([para 31]). 

The repeal of sections of the ECA by the NEM:WA, particularly section 80(4), permits the continued operation of waste disposal facilities that existed prior to the Environmental Conservation Act without necessitating a WML unless specifically called upon by the Minister ([para 80(4)]). 

## A - Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal conducted a thorough examination of the statutory framework governing waste management, focusing on statutory definitions and the historical context surrounding AMSA's operations. The Court underscored that "waste" is defined in the NEM:WA, which includes materials that are unwanted, rejected, or discarded ([para 31]). AMSA contended that by virtue of its operational history and the nature of BOF slag, both "current arisings" and "reclaimed slag" did not meet this definition as they hold commercial value and are actively sold ([paras 39-40]).

The Court discerned the provisions of the law in conjunction with AMSA's intentions and the historic context to determine that the DDG's directives lacked legal grounding on the assertion that BOF slag constitutes waste requiring a WML. Further, it noted that AMSA's operations have been consistent and ongoing since before the enactment of the laws in question, and thus, AMSA retained its pre-existing rights under the statutory provisions ([paras 42-47]).

Compelled by the instability imposed by the DDG’s notices, AMSA's need to seek declaratory relief was justified; the High Court found merit in their claim, acknowledging that AMSA's operations were not subject to the strictures applied by the Respondents due to their historical status ([para 53]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal brought by the Appellants, confirming the High Court's decision to set aside the compliance notice and directive as flawed. It reiterated that AMSA is not subject to WML requirements for its historical slag operations due to statutory provisions protecting pre-existing rights and the lack of any retroactivity in the application of the newer waste management statutes. AMSA was deemed to operate lawfully within the parameters established by prior legislation ([para 53]).

This judgment carries significant implications not only for AMSA but also for the broader environmental regulatory landscape in South Africa, clarifying the bounds of liability under environmental statutes for companies with longstanding operations prior to the enactment of newer, more stringent regulations.
