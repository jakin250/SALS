Machaba & Another v The State (20401/2014) [2015] ZASCA 60

The appeal involves two appellants, Bushi Mike Machaba (first appellant) and Elvis Boy Mbuyane (second appellant), who were convicted in the North Gauteng High Court of murder and robbery with aggravating circumstances, with substantial sentences imposed on December 15, 2006...

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 [2015] ZASCA 60

F – Facts

The appeal involves two appellants, Bushi Mike Machaba (first appellant) and Elvis Boy Mbuyane (second appellant), who were convicted in the North Gauteng High Court of murder and robbery with aggravating circumstances, with substantial sentences imposed on December 15, 2006 ([1–2]). The record of the trial was notably incomplete as key portions, including witness testimonies and critical parts of the judgment, were untranscribed and lost, preventing proper appeal adjudication for the conviction aspect ([2]).

Both appellants were implicated in a crime that occurred on May 3, 2002, when a man, Cyprian Mthembu, was fatally shot during a robbery involving three assailants ([13]). The key elements presented against the appellants included testimonies identifying them as participants and the recovery of the deceased's stolen cell phone, along with a firearm linked to the shooting ([14–15]).

During the bail hearing, both appellants offered their versions, albeit these were not formally admissible at trial ([17]). Notably, crucial testimonies involved admissions presented in a warning statement by the first appellant, which the court later noted to be admissible against the second appellant due to hearsay, despite redundancy in their evidentiary weight ([22]–[24]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues arising from this case include:

  1. Admissibility of Evidence: Whether the extra-curial statements made by the first appellant could be used as evidence against the second appellant.
  2. Completeness of Trial Record: Whether the incomplete trial record undermined the appellants' right to a fair trial and justified setting aside their convictions.
  3. Proper Administration of Oath: Whether the proper administration of the oath under section 162 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA) was adhered to during the trial ([3–8]).

R – Rules

Key legal principles relevant to this case include:

  1. Hearsay Rule: As established in S v Litako & Others, extra-curial confessions or admissions of one co-accused are inadmissible against other co-accused unless they are corroborated by other evidentiary sources ([24]).
  2. Trial Record Standards: An adequate trial record is cardinal for appeals, underscoring that an incomplete record may impede the pursuit of justice, as indicated in S v Chabedi ([4]). Claims of procedural completeness, however, do not guarantee absolution of convictions especially when there is no prejudice shown toward the appellants.
  3. Section 162 of CPA: This section mandates that all witnesses in criminal proceedings must be examined under oath by the presiding officer, which is seen as essential for the testimony's admissibility ([7–9]).

A – Application

The Court applied these rules through thorough scrutiny of the presented testimonies and procedural compliance.

  1. Hearsay Evidence: The court noted that the hearsay evidence of the first appellant, claimed to have implicated the second appellant, was inadmissibly used against him. This finding directly influenced the court's evaluation of the state’s evidence ([22–24]).
  2. Incomplete Record: Despite the incomplete record, the Court determined that sufficient evidence existed to support the convictions of both appellants. It argued that the fragments of evidence, particularly testimonies unchallenged by the defense and the relationship between the accused during the robbery, sufficed for conviction ([5], [34]).
  3. Administration of Oath: The claim that witnesses were not duly sworn under section 162 was decisively rebutted by the Court, which clarified that the abbreviations used in the transcripts did not definitively prove improper administration of the oath. The Court affirmed the legal presumption that the oath requirements were rigorously followed unless explicitly demonstrated otherwise ([10–11]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeals against convictions while upholding the appeals regarding the sentences imposed. It found that while key procedural violations existed concerning the admissibility of certain statements and the incomplete record, these did not sufficiently hinder a fair trial outcome ([46]). The overall sentences were subsequently revised, reflecting a more just consequence for the appellants’ actions, with the new sentencing orders being 20 years for murder and 10 years for robbery, to run concurrently with retroactive effect ([46]).

This case underscores the tensions between procedural integrity in trial records and the substantive realities of ensuring that justice is achieved, emphasizing the necessity for vigilant adherence to rules of evidence and procedure in criminal law.
