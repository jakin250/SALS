# FIRAC Case Note: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Case Citation:** Thebus v The State [2002] ZASCA 61; Case No: 338/2001

**Judges:** Olivier, Navsa JJA and Lewis AJA

**Heard:** 9 May 2002  
**Delivered:** 30 August 2002

---

## F - Facts

The events underlying this appeal stem from a violent incident involving a vigilante group in the suburb of Ocean View, Cape Town, on 14 November 1998. The appellants, Abduraghman Thebus and Moegamat Adams, along with two co-accused, were implicated in a shooting that resulted in the death of a seven-year-old girl, Crystal Abrahams, and injuries to two others, Riaan van Rooyen and Lester September ([para 1-5]).

The State's case relied primarily on the testimony of Gregory Edward Kiel, who attested to witnessing the violent actions of the vigilant group. Kiel identified Thebus as a participant holding a pick-handle and Adams as another member who collected spent cartridges during the incident. The identification of Thebus relied heavily on Kiel’s assertion that he addressed Thebus by his nickname, "Maantjie" ([paras 1-4]).

In the ensuing trial, the appellants were convicted of murder and attempted murder, while their co-accused were either acquitted or discharged at various stages of the proceedings. The trial court imposed sentences of eight years' imprisonment, suspended under specific conditions ([para 1]).

Both the State and the appellants appealed: the State on the grounds that the sentences were unduly lenient, and the appellants contesting their convictions ([para 10]).

## I - Issues

The primary issues presented in this case relate to:

1. **The reliability of the identification evidence** provided against Thebus, particularly given Kiel's identification of the appellants contrasted with his inconsistent assertions regarding other accused ([para 14]).
   
2. **Whether the alibi evidence presented by Thebus** was credible and sufficiently established to create reasonable doubt about his presence at the scene of the crime ([paras 10-13]).

3. **The appropriateness of the sentences** imposed in light of the serious nature of the charges, particularly concerning the application of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, which prescribes life imprisonment for murder committed in furtherance of a common purpose ([para 16]).

## R - Rules

The legal principles pertinent to this case involve:

1. **Evidence of a single witness:** The principle that a conviction based on the evidence of a single witness can only be made if the evidence satisfies the court in all material respects. Reference is made to cases such as *S v Mgedezi* 1989 (1) SA 687 (A) and *R v T* 1958 (2) SA 676 (A) concerning the need for caution in reliance on such testimony ([para 20]).

2. **Common purpose doctrine:** The legal doctrine allowing for individuals in a group to be held jointly liable for actions taken in furtherance of a collective intention ([para 16]).

3. **Sentencing principles under the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997:** This Act mandates life sentences for murder committed in an organized group setting unless "substantial and compelling circumstances" warrant a lesser sentence ([para 16]).

## A - Application

### Identification Evidence
The court examined Kiel’s identification of Thebus, determining that it was based on a longstanding acquaintance. Despite Kiel’s claims, the trial court found inconsistencies in his identification of other co-accused, leading to doubts about the reliability of his witness testimony regarding Thebus ([paras 3-14]). The majority opinion viewed Kiel’s evidence as compelling based on factors such as proximity and the clarity of the incident, despite criticisms from Navsa JA regarding the totality of identification reliability ([para 14]).

### Alibi
The alibi presented by Thebus was scrutinized. His assertions that he was elsewhere during the incident were deemed suspicious because it arose only at trial and not earlier during police interrogations. The court acknowledged the consistent testimonies of Ms Van Rooy and Ms Jacobs but raised doubts about their credibility given the timing and nature of their accounts ([paras 10-13]). Therefore, the court concluded that the alibi was not sufficiently established to create reasonable doubt about Thebus’s presence at the scene ([para 13]).

### Sentencing
In evaluating the sentences, the court held that the actions of the appellants fell squarely within the purview of the Criminal Law Amendment Act. However, Lewis AJA expressed unease at imposing life sentences, citing various social factors and personal backgrounds of the appellants, which should be weighed carefully ([paras 16-24]). While acknowledging the gravity of the offences and the societal need for deterrence, the dissenting judge argued for a lesser sentence of 15 years for each appellant, emphasizing that the actual shooting was not premeditated and that they were not the direct shooters ([paras 22-24]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Thebus's conviction while dismissing Adams's convictions. The court concluded that Kiel's identification was reliable despite inconsistencies regarding other co-accused and that Thebus's alibi was weak and uncorroborated. However, the court also recognized a lack of premeditated intent in the actions that led to the shooting and ultimately imposed a 15-year sentence for each appellant instead of life imprisonment.

The outcome reflects the court's balancing act between maintaining rigorous legal standards concerning evidence and recognizing the nuances of individual circumstances in sentencing ([paras 25-34]). The considerations in this case highlight the ongoing challenges of vigilante justice within communities and signal a broader evaluative framework for similar cases in the future.
