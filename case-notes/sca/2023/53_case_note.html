Case Title: Mashinini v The Member of the Executive Council for Health and Social Development, Gauteng Provincial Government (335/2021) [2023] ZASCA 53 (18 April 2023)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 53 (18 April 2023) ### F – Facts: The appellant,

FIRAC Case Note

Case Title: Mashinini v The Member of the Executive Council for Health and Social Development, Gauteng Provincial Government (335/2021) [2023] ZASCA 53 (18 April 2023)

F – Facts:

The appellant, Mrs. Nommqabelo Nellie Mashinini, a professional nurse, experienced a surgical mishap on 16 May 2014 at Tambo Memorial Hospital, resulting in serious injuries that necessitated multiple surgeries thereafter ([paras 1–2]). On 18 January 2017, she initiated a medical negligence claim in the Gauteng Division of the High Court against the respondent, the MEC for Health and Social Development ([para 2]). The basis for the claim included a range of damages—past and future medical expenses, loss of earnings, and general damages. The MEC conceded liability for the initial injuries, thus the case primarily revolved around the quantum of damages, particularly concerning future medical expenses ([paras 3–4]).

The MEC raised a 'public healthcare defence,' asserting that future medical treatment could be provided through state facilities rather than monetary compensation. This was met with dissent from Mashinini, who argued that the quality and accessibility of care in public hospitals could not equate to private healthcare standards and that proper evidence was lacking to support the MEC's claims ([paras 3–4, 21–22]).

I – Issues:

The critical legal question was whether the high court was correct in denying Mashinini's monetary claim of R879,314 for future medical and surgical treatment, which she argued was necessary given her complex medical condition and reliance on specialized care ([paras 6–7, 8]). Specifically, the court needed to evaluate whether the MEC provided adequate evidence to substantiate her contention that Carl Hani Baragwanath Hospital could render services of comparable quality at a lower cost or at no cost to the appellant ([paras 12, 27]).

R – Rules:

The court analyzed established principles of Aquilian liability, which necessitates that a defendant make good the difference between the value of the plaintiff’s estate after the delict and what it would have been without it ([paras 9, 24]). The principle articulated in Ngubane v South African Transport Services emphasized that the evidentiary burden is on the defendant to demonstrate that adequate public healthcare could be provided at no cost or less than the private healthcare sought by the plaintiff ([paras 10, 11, 12]). Additionally, the Constitutional Court in MEC for Health v DZ clarified that defendants can only rebut claims for future medical expenses if compelling evidence exists indicating that the required services can be provided at public facilities ([paras 13, 27]).

A – Application:

The court determined that Mashinini successfully proved her entitlement to a monetary award for future medical expenses, substantiated by expert testimony regarding her complex condition and necessary treatments ([paras 14, 24, 26]). In contrast, the MEC provided no substantial evidence to refute Mashinini's claims regarding the insufficiencies of public healthcare for complex cases or the expert’s assessments regarding the need for specialized treatment ([paras 26, 27]). The trial court's reliance on the public healthcare defence essentially shifted the burden onto Mashinini to demonstrate that she could not receive comparable care, a misapplication of the burden of proof established in Ngubane ([paras 9, 10, 24]). Furthermore, the MEC’s reference to MSM obo KBM as a precedent did not adequately support the need for developing common law in the context presented, thus misguiding the high court’s decision ([paras 20–26]).

C – Conclusion:

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Mashinini's appeal, ruling that the high court misapplied the principles surrounding the public healthcare defence. The court directed that Mashinini receive the full amount claimed, including R3,213,564.40 for various damages, interest on that amount, and the costs of future medical treatment ([paras 28]). This judgment reinforces the burden on defendants to provide concrete evidence when proposing that public healthcare can equitably substitute private healthcare in claims for future medical expenses. The implications extend to the interpretation of rights under Section 27 of the Constitution, highlighting the necessity for public healthcare systems to provide quality care for individuals with complex medical needs.
