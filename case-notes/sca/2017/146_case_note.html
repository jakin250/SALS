Supreme Court of Appeal

Zuma v DA; ANDPP v DA 2017

F – Facts

On October 13, 2017, the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa delivered a judgment concerning former President Jacob Gedleyihlekisa Zuma and the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA) following a prolonged legal battle over the decision to discontinue his prosecution for serious criminal charges, including corruption and racketeering ([paras 1–2]). Initially, in 2005, the NPA brought charges against Zuma, leading to numerous court cases and significant public interest. After a series of legal maneuvers, the acting National Director of Public Prosecutions (ANDPP), Mokotedi Mpshe, decided to discontinue the prosecution on April 1, 2009, citing concerns about political interference and alleged abuse of process linked to the timing of the prosecution ([paras 6–8]).

The Democratic Alliance (DA), South Africa's official opposition party, sought to have this decision overturned, arguing it was irrational ([paras 1–3]). The case ultimately focused on various legal and constitutional issues, including the NPA's decision-making process and the implications of political influences on prosecutorial discretion ([para 37]).

I – Issues

The central legal issues in this case revolved around:

  1. Whether the ANDPP’s decision to discontinue Zuma's prosecution was rational and valid.
  2. Whether the ANDPP had improperly invoked constitutional provisions in making this decision ([paras 1, 8]).
  3. The implications of potential political interference in prosecutorial decisions ([para 37]).

R – Rules

The significant legal frameworks underpinning this case included:

  • Section 179 of the South African Constitution: It outlines the powers of the National Director of Public Prosecutions, including the ability to review decisions regarding prosecutions.
  • Case precedents: Notably, the Supreme Court's previous decisions, such as National Director of Public Prosecutions v Zuma ([2009] ZASCA 1), which clarified the standards for prosecutorial independence and the relevance of motives in prosecutorial actions ([para 29]).
  • The principle of rationality in administrative actions: As established in various South African legal cases, administrative decisions must have a rational connection between means and ends ([para 82]).

A – Application

The court applied the aforementioned legal principles to evaluate Mpshe's decision. It noted the irrationality of his conclusion that discontinuing the prosecution was necessary to preserve the integrity of the NPA based on unsubstantiated claims of political interference ([para 84]). The judgment critically examined the prosecution's internal deliberations on whether the timing of the indictment was manipulated for an improper purpose. However, the court found that these assertions were unsupported by the evidence and acknowledged that the NPA's own records indicated there was a strong case against Zuma, rendering the decision to discontinue the prosecution problematic ([paras 88–92]).

Moreover, the court found that Mpshe's reliance on certain legal precedents was misplaced, asserting that decisions regarding abuse of process should be left to trial courts rather than being determined through the NPA's administrative processes ([paras 86-89]). The court ultimately recognized that the decision's rationale did not withstand scrutiny, thereby reinforcing the principles of judicial review and accountability within prosecutorial conduct ([para 92]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that both Zuma and the NPA conceded that the decision to discontinue the prosecution was irrational and thus would be set aside. It held that the grounds cited for discontinuance—especially political manipulation and the alleged abuse of process—lacked evidentiary support, and it reaffirmed the independence and thoroughness expected of prosecutorial decision-making ([paras 94-96]).

The implications of this judgment underscore the essential role of the judiciary in reviewing prosecutorial decisions to maintain accountability and ensure that decisions are based on sound legal principles rather than political expediency. The court's ruling serves as a pivotal reminder of the delicate balance between political considerations and lawful prosecutorial discretion in ensuring justice in South Africa ([para 94]).
