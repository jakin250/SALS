Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2002

**Case Note: FIRAC Approach for THE COMMISSIONER OF THE COMPETITION COMMISSION v THE GENERAL COUNCIL OF THE BAR SOUTH AFRICA**

**F – Facts**: 
The case revolves around the appellant (the Commissioner of the Competition Commission) and the multiple respondents representing regional associations of advocates in South Africa. The General Council of the Bar South Africa (GCB), which serves to regulate the conduct of these associations, suspected that some of their rules might violate section 4 of the Competition Act 89 of 1998. They applied for an exemption from the Act towards the end of 1999, which the Competition Commission took nearly a year to respond to ([paras 1-2]).

Upon receiving the Commission's decision on 8 November 2000, the respondents discovered that while an exemption had been issued under section 10(2)(b) of the Act, it was coupled with conditions that they disputed. Ultimately, the respondents sought a review of the decision in the Transvaal High Court ([para 4-6]). The court found that the Commission's decision should be set aside due to the breach of the audi alteram partem principle (the right to a fair hearing), but there remained contention over whether it should be remitted to the Commission for reconsideration or the court should grant exemptions directly ([para 2]).

**I – Issues**: 
The legal questions at the heart of the dispute were:
1. Whether the Competition Commission's decision regarding the respondents' application for exemption was procedurally flawed and thereby deserved to be set aside.
2. If the matters were to be remitted to the Commission, whether it had the requisite capacity to reconsider its previous decision fairly and competently.
3. The extent to which the court itself could and should rule on the request for exemption in light of the failed processes by the Commission ([para 2-4]).

**R – Rules**: 
The governing legislation is the Competition Act 89 of 1998, specifically section 4, which inhibits restrictive trade practices, and Schedule 1, which allows professional associations to apply for exemptions from these restrictions. The principles of administrative law also underpin the ruling, particularly the audi alteram partem principle, which mandates that parties are granted the opportunity to be heard before an adverse decision is made against them. Legal precedents cited include Johannesburg City Council v Administrator, Transvaal,[1969 (2) SA 72 (T)] and Livestock and Meat Industries Control Board v Garda [1961 (1) SA 342 (A)] addressing the courts' discretion in reviewing administrative decisions ([paras 4-14]).

**A – Application**: 
The court examined the procedural failings of the Commission, particularly the timeline of events leading to the respondents' awareness of the Minister's unfavourable comments and the absence of adequate reasoning provided for the decisions made by the Commission ([paras 8-12]). The court noted that the Commission’s failure to properly adhere to its obligations as outlined in Schedule 1 indicated a lack of competence in handling the exemption request.

The majority judges expressed concern about the Commission's grasp of its functions under item 1(b) of Schedule 1, which required a nuanced assessment that balanced professional standards against competition interests. That the Commission became mired in broader reform debates suggested a failure to respect the statutory confines of its decision-making authority ([paras 10-12]). Moreover, the court assessed the salient facts regarding the referral rule and other contested rules against the legal backdrop that allows courts to enforce common law without administrative distortion, finding that Roos J correctly exempted the referral rule but made errors regarding two other rules that he exempted ([paras 19-20]).

**C – Conclusion**: 
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal in part, recognizing the necessity to cancel the Commission's decision while invalidating the remittance to it for reconsideration due to procedural unfairness. The court declared that the referral rule was justifiably exempted from the Act; however, it refrained from accepting the other exemptions without sufficient economic rationale. The ruling highlights that while courts must exercise caution in intervening in administrative decisions, fairness must guide such decisions, allowing courts to act decisively when necessary ([paras 21-22]). Each party was directed to bear its own costs, thus serving the broader principle of equitable administrative justice while reinforcing the appropriate boundaries of professional regulatory authority ([para 22-24]). 

This establishes significant precedent regarding the interplay between administrative review proceedings and professional governance in South Africa.
