# FIRAC Case Note: Rodney Jackson v The State (Case No: 35/97)

**Citation:** 
Rodney Jackson v The State [1998] ZASCA 20, 1998 (2) SACR 1 (A)

---

### F – Facts: 

[2–6] The appellant, Rodney Jackson, was convicted of attempted rape of a 17-year-old schoolgirl after an incident that transpired during a night outing. On 26 December 1993, the complainant, accompanied by her sister and friends, went to a park in Sydneyvale, Bishop Lavis, to enjoy some leisure time with a case of beer. The appellant, a married 24-year-old policeman previously known to the complainant, offered to take some girls for a drive while also providing driving lessons. During the drive, the appellant engaged the complainant in conversation and later requested to stop the car, allegedly to finish his beer.

[3–4] At the stop, the appellant attempted to kiss the complainant, who resisted, leading to increasingly aggressive behavior. He restrained her, forcibly lowered her pants, and attempted intercourse while overpowering her physically. The complainant fought back and screamed, at which point the appellant desisted. Following the altercation, she fled the vehicle and sought immediate help but found no assistance until she returned to her group of friends in a state of hysteria where she accused the appellant of rape.

[7] In contrast, the appellant claimed that the encounter was consensual, asserting that the complainant reciprocated his advances. He denied all allegations of exposure or attempted rape. Notably, he suffered scratch marks on his forehead and ear, which he attributed to an altercation with his wife—his explanation was dismissed both by the trial court and the Court of Appeal.

[8–10] The regional court magistrate found the appellant's testimony unconvincing and accepted the complainant's account. However, the subsequent appeal to the Cape Provincial Division resulted in an irregularity determination regarding the non-disclosure of a police statement by the prosecutor, thus casting doubt on the credibility of the trial court’s findings.

---

### I – Issues:

[8–12] The primary legal question addressed by the court was whether the trial court had committed errors in evaluating the evidence, particularly regarding the credibility of the complainant's testimony in light of the so-called "cautionary rule" applicable to sexual assault cases.

1. Did the trial court err in applying the cautionary rule in a manner that affected the outcome of the conviction?
2. Was there a misdirection in the assessment of whether the complainant's statement to the police aligned with her in-court testimony regarding penetration?

---

### R – Rules:

[11–14] The cautionary rule concerns the evidence submitted by complainants in sexual offences, suggesting the need for corroboration due to perceived reliability issues. This rule was articulated in **S v Snyman 1968 (2) SA 582 (A)**, which emphasized the potential for false accusations in sexual crimes, thereby requiring judicial caution. 

The court also considered whether the cautionary rule should remain in place given arguments suggesting that it is outdated, discriminatory against women, and undermines the State’s burden of proof in rape cases. Notable is the case law from comparative jurisdictions, which have moved away from such obligatory rules, indicating a gradual legal and social shift in perspective.

---

### A – Application:

[10–12] The court assessed the credibility of both the complainant and the appellant. It found that the complainant's actions—fleeing the vehicle, seeking help, and subsequent testimony—were consistent with being a victim of a sexual offense. 

The appellant's defense was undermined by a lack of empirical support for his claims, and his explanation of injuries was found implausible. Additionally, the judge emphasized that even if the cautionary rule was to be considered, the particular facts of this case did not necessitate such a stringent application of the rule since the evidence supported the complainant’s description of events.

Critically, the appeal court maintained that the credibility determinations made by the lower court must be upheld in the absence of a clear misdirection. The evidence examined—including the district-surgeon's report with findings of vaginal abrasions—did not convincingly support the appellant's narrative as consensual.

---

### C – Conclusion:

[23–24] The Supreme Court of Appeal confirmed the conviction and sentence of the appellant, thereby dismissing the appeal. The court concluded that the appeal against the conviction lacked merit, emphasizing that the actions of the complainant were consistent with a trauma response and negating the appellant’s version of events.

The ruling underscored the importance of reevaluating the cautionary rule in sexual offenses, recognizing that it could perpetuate bias and discrimination. Furthermore, the implications of the ruling suggest a need for legal reform to better align with contemporary understandings of consent and sexual victimization.

In sum, the appeal was dismissed, reinforcing the conviction of the appellant for attempted rape and ensuring the protection of victims’ rights in sexual assault cases.
