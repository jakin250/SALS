# Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
[1] The respondent, BOE Bank Limited, initiated legal action against the appellants, Michael de Villiers and Brian Basil Nel, in their capacity as joint liquidators of Intramed (Pty) Ltd, for the payment of R101,458,341.07 plus interest. The claim was predicated on three similar written loan agreements concluded on 17 June 1999, wherein Intramed had borrowed amounts of R40 million, R20 million, and R40 million, all secured by a combination of notarial and mortgage bonds, a cession of book debts, and deeds of suretyship. The loans became payable upon the borrower’s insolvency. 

[2] The lower court, presided over by Judge Ludorf, ruled in favor of the bank, ordering the appellants to pay various amounts with interest and costs. The appellants received leave to appeal, contending that the loan agreements were not duly authorized, arguing that Alan Hiscock lacked authority to bind Intramed. They also took the position that Intramed had not received the R100 million as it had been paid to Macmed Health Care Limited, and contended that the agreements had lapsed due to the non-fulfilment of a suspensive condition within the agreements.

[3] The background involved Macmed, a listed company, aiming to acquire parts of a business, with Intramed set up as a vehicle for the acquisition financed in part through the loans from the bank. This acquisition process included negotiations that predated the conclusion of the loan agreements.

### I – Issues
[4] The appeal raised three primary legal questions:
1. Whether the loan agreements and the cession of book debts were duly authorized by Intramed.
2. Whether Intramed received the R100 million.
3. Whether the non-fulfilment of a suspensive condition within the loan agreements caused them to lapse.

### R – Rules
[5] The court examined principles of corporate governance, particularly regarding the authority vested in directors and the necessity of formal resolutions, citing precedent cases such as *Alpha Bank Bpk en Andere v Registrateur van Banke en Andere* 1996 (1) SA 330 (A) and *Randcoal Services Ltd and Others v Randgold and Exploration Co Ltd* 1998 (4) SA 825 (SCA). The court noted that although formal procedures for adopting resolutions are preferred, companies can bind themselves through unanimous assent or actions reflecting collective approval. 

[6] The loan agreements contained suspensive conditions that had to be satisfied for the agreements to remain valid. Specifically, clauses 2.1 – 2.2 outlined that the conditions were to the bank's benefit, and any waiver or extension of these conditions had to be communicated in writing.

### A – Application
[7] The court's analysis centered on whether Hiscock, who signed the loan agreements, had the required authority to act on behalf of Intramed. It found that despite Hiscock not being a director, he functioned as a key figure within the corporate structure and had possessive control over its subsidiaries. Importantly, the court noted that the directors and shareholders were aware of the loan agreements and that the actions taken aligned with the overarching objectives of the holding company, Macmed.

[8] In addressing the second issue, the court held that the manner in which the funds were allocated to Macmed's rights offer account, while ostensibly bypassing Intramed's direct access, did not absolve Intramed of liability as it was still recognized as the borrower under the agreements.

[9] Finally, regarding suspended conditions, the court concluded that the bank had tacitly waived compliance by engaging in ongoing authorization and negotiation processes that suggested a satisfaction of the conditions, despite no formal agreement having been reached. The court emphasized that the interpretation of the agreements must respect the parties' intentions and ongoing conduct rather than adhere strictly to written requirements if compliance was not an issue raised until later.

### C – Conclusion
[10] The court partially upheld the appeal by concluding that the loan agreements had lapsed due to the non-fulfillment of a suspensive condition. However, it ultimately ruled that Intramed was still liable for restitution, calculating the amount owed to the bank at R113,177,568.51, reflecting adjusted liabilities following partial acknowledgement of the agreements' lapsing. The decision reinforced the need for companies to adhere to both internal governance procedures and external contractual obligations, underscoring that corporate structures can lead to complexities surrounding authority and consent. 

[11] The implications of this case extend to corporate governance practices, signifying the need for clarity in decision-making processes and formal resolutions within corporate transactions to mitigate risks surrounding authorisation disputes. The ruling thus serves as a critical reference point for future cases involving the obligations of directors and the nuances of corporate finance within group structures.
