Source Folder: 2002

# FIRAC Case Note: The Islamic Unity Convention v The Independent Broadcasting Authority [CCT 36/01]

### F - Facts

The applicant, the **Islamic Unity Convention**, operates Radio 786, a community radio station licensed by the **Independent Broadcasting Authority (IBA)**. On **8 May 1998**, Radio 786 broadcast a program titled “Zionism and Israel: An in-depth analysis,” featuring Dr. Yaqub Zaki, who expressed controversial views regarding the legitimacy of Israel as a state, as well as statements about the Holocaust ([1]). Following the broadcast, the **South African Jewish Board of Deputies** (the fourth respondent) lodged a formal complaint alleging contravention of clause 2(a) of the **Code of Conduct for Broadcasting Services**, claiming the broadcast was likely to prejudice relations between different population groups (Jews and others) ([2]).

Initial correspondence between the IBA's **Monitoring and Complaints Unit** and the applicant resulted in confusion over whether the complaint had lapsed, ultimately leading the complaints unit to determine the complaint was valid. The matter was then referred to the **Broadcasting Monitoring and Complaints Committee (BMCC)** without notifying the applicant ([3]). As a result of miscommunication, the applicant filed a High Court application challenging the referral and seeking a declaration that clause 2(a) was unconstitutional, arguing it infringed the right to freedom of expression protected under section 16 of the Constitution ([4]–[5]).

The High Court ruled in favor of the applicant concerning the procedural flaws in handling the complaint, but it refrained from deciding the constitutional issue related to clause 2(a) ([6]–[8]). The applicant sought direct appeal to the Constitutional Court, which was unopposed by the respondents ([9]).

### I - Issues

The case presented two central legal issues:

1. **Whether the High Court erred by not addressing the constitutionality of clause 2(a) of the Code of Conduct.** The court held that it was unnecessary to consider the constitutionality given the procedural flaws found in the referral process, deeming the constitutional issue moot ([8]).
   
2. **Is clause 2(a) of the Code of Conduct unconstitutional because it limits the right to freedom of expression as stated in section 16 of the Constitution?** ([5]–[6]).

### R - Rules

The primary legal principles at play are:
- **Section 16 of the Constitution**: Guarantees the right to freedom of expression, which encompasses freedom of the press and other media, the right to receive and impart information and ideas, artistic creativity, and academic freedom ([25]).
- **Section 36(1) of the Constitution**: Establishes the criteria for justifiable limitations on rights, requiring any limitation to be reasonable and justifiable in an open democratic society ([38]).
- **Precedents such as *S v Mhlungu* and *JT Publishing* reinforce the courts' obligations to declare laws inconsistent with the Constitution invalid** ([10]).

The **Code of Conduct**, particularly clause 2(a), prohibits broadcasting material deemed indecent, obscene, or potentially prejudicial to public order, including relations between segments of the population ([22]).

### A - Application

The Constitutional Court found that:
- The provisions of clause 2(a) imposed a **limit on the right to freedom of expression**, as it aimed to prevent broadcasting that could harm relations between groups without adequately defining what constitutes "prejudice" ([35]).
- An analysis of whether clause 2(a) could be interpreted in a manner consistent with the Constitution found such an interpretation unlikely due to its vague language. This lack of precision meant it violated the constitutional requirement that laws regulating expression must be clear and distinct ([41]).
- The Court discussed the need to balance freedom of expression with the rights to dignity and equality, recognizing the historical context within which the law must operate. It ruled that the clause in its current form did not align with these principles and was unduly broad ([30]), potentially stifles expression and constrains robust discourse necessary for democracy ([37]).
- The absence of substantive justification for the provision by the respondents was pivotal, as they failed to demonstrate any compelling state interest justifying the infringement on the right ([47]–[51]).

### C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded that:
- The High Court should have considered the constitutionality of clause 2(a) of the Code, given the ongoing nature of the issues it presented ([14]) and its implications for future regulatory actions.
- Clause 2(a) was found to be inconsistent with section 16 of the Constitution and thus invalid specifically concerning the prohibition against broadcasting material "likely to prejudice relations between sections of the population" ([60]).
- The Court ordered notional severance, which would allow for continued regulation of public broadcasting without unduly infringing on freedom of expression ([57]), ensuring that protections remained in place for unprotected speech as outlined in section 16(2) of the Constitution ([60]).

The decision emphasizes the balance between regulatory frameworks and constitutional rights, directing future legislative consideration to ensure compliance with democratic principles ([59]).
