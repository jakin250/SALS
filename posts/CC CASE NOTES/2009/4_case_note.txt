Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2009

# FIRAC Case Note: **THE APARTY AND ANOTHER v MINISTER FOR HOME AFFAIRS AND OTHERS (CCT 06/09) & KWAME ONKGOPOTSE MOLOKO AND OTHERS v MINISTER FOR HOME AFFAIRS AND OTHERS (CCT 10/09)**

## F – Facts
The case involved two urgent applications for direct access to the Constitutional Court of South Africa concerning the voting rights of South African citizens residing abroad. The first application (CCT 06/09) was initiated by *The AParty* and Mr. Andrew Pepperell, who challenged various sections of the Electoral Act 73 of 1998. The applicants sought declarations that these sections invalidly excluded South African citizens living abroad from registering to vote and from the right to apply for a special vote on election day ([14–15]).

The second application (CCT 10/09) included twelve applicants, all South African citizens living abroad, who sought similar relief concerning the same sections of the Electoral Act. Their argument was grounded on the unconstitutionality of provisions that limited their ability to register and vote from outside South Africa ([18–19]).

Both cases were heard together due to the urgency presented by the imminent elections, only a month away ([1], [2]). The applicants did not seek urgent relief that would require immediate registration for the elections; instead, they sought declarations of invalidity that would allow for future voting provisions ([15], [16]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal questions in these applications centered on:
1. The constitutionality of specific provisions in the Electoral Act that prevent South African citizens living abroad from registering to vote and from applying for a special vote.
2. Whether the applicants could establish sufficient grounds for direct access to the Constitutional Court without first exhausting remedies in lower courts ([26]).
3. The implications of granting direct access, especially concerning the urgency and public importance surrounding the electoral process ([60]).

## R – Rules
The key constitutional and legislative provisions relevant to the case included:
1. **Section 19 of the Constitution**, which guarantees every adult citizen the right to vote in elections ([5]).
2. **Sections 7 and 8 of the Electoral Act**, outlining voter registration and the requirements for being ordinarily resident at a voting district ([38]).
3. **Section 33(1)(e)**, which restricts the ability of South African citizens abroad to vote under specific circumstances ([32], [73]).
4. Precedents and legal principles derived from prior case law, particularly on direct access, such as established requirements from *Bruce v. Fleecytex* regarding the necessity for compelling reasons to bypass lower courts ([29]).

## A – Application
The Court's analysis focused on the constitutional challenges to the provisions of the Electoral Act. The applicants argued that the legislative restrictions were inconsistent with their rights under sections 1, 3, 9, and 19 of the Constitution, particularly the right to vote and the right to equal treatment ([6], [15]).

The Court acknowledged the urgency of the matter, stressing the need for voters to have their rights recognized in a timely manner, but it also expressed concerns about the implications of granting direct access. Direct access bypassed the High Court, where these issues might be more thoroughly examined, thus risking a hasty resolution that could undermine the electoral process ([28], [70]).

The Court ultimately granted direct access for the challenges to section 33(1)(e) of the Electoral Act, supporting the applicants' right to vote but rejected direct access concerning sections 7, 8, 9, and 60 on the grounds that these provisions had not been sufficiently explored in earlier proceedings and required a more in-depth legal analysis ([34], [80]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court declared certain words within section 33(1)(e) and the corresponding election regulations invalid, thereby recognizing the right of South African citizens abroad to apply for a special vote under reasonable circumstances. However, it ruled against granting direct access for challenges related to voter registration, emphasizing that such constitutional issues were best resolved through the High Court first.

The Court concluded that while the urgency of the matter was acknowledged, the necessity to preserve the integrity of the electoral timetable and ensure thorough judicial examination of complex electoral arrangements warranted restraint in granting direct access ([82], [86]). Consequently, the applicants were entitled to costs for their successful challenge on section 33(1)(e) but would not recover costs for challenges where they were unsuccessful ([83]).

This decision emphasized both the Court's commitment to upholding constitutional rights and the necessity for careful judicial processes in electoral matters, balancing urgency with the importance of detailed judicial scrutiny ([79], [84]).
