Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note: Tshopo v State (29/12) [2012] ZASCA 193

### F – Facts

In Tshopo v State, the appellants – S J Tshopo, N M Nqulo, and N D Makathini – were convicted of fraud in relation to a tender process of the Free State Department of Education. The case arose from a tender issued for the delivery of school books, with specific provisions aimed at preventing nepotism and ensuring transparency ([1]). 

The first appellant, Tshopo, was employed by the Free State provincial administration and had familial connections to high-ranking officials, including his marriage to an MEC ([4]). The second appellant, Nqulo, acted as a front for the tender application, signing the necessary documents and declaring no connections to provincial employees, despite her brother's significant involvement in the business's operations ([8]). 

The State's allegations necessitated that the tender application disclose any relationships with persons employed by the Province ([3]). The prosecution argued that the failure to declare Tshopo's connections amounted to fraudulent misrepresentation, influencing the Department's decision to award the tender to their entity, Abelusi Enterprises ([4][22]). Upon failing in their initial appeal at the High Court, the appellants subsequently sought relief from the Supreme Court of Appeal.

### I – Issues

The primary legal questions addressed in the appeal included:

1. **Was there fraudulent misrepresentation sufficient to establish the conviction for fraud?** The State needed to prove that the appellants' representations regarding their non-relationship with provincial employees were false and that they were done intentionally to deceive ([22]).
2. **Did the sentencing court correctly assess the applicable factors in relation to sentencing for fraud in the context of public procurement?** Particularly, the Court considered whether a custodial sentence was appropriate given the nature of the offense and the conduct of the appellants ([27]).

### R – Rules

The court's evaluation hinged on several critical legal principles:

1. **Fraud and Misrepresentation**: The essential elements of fraud require that false representations be made with the intent to deceive another, who relies on the misrepresentation to their detriment ([3][22]) and must be established beyond a reasonable doubt.
2. **Public Tender Regulations**: Specific statutory frameworks aimed at ensuring transparency and fairness in the public procurement process were heavily referenced, particularly the requirement to disclose relationships that might lead to potential bias ([27]).
3. **Sentencing Guidelines**: South African case law establishes that a custodial sentence is appropriate where the offense undermines public confidence in the procurement processes and public institutions ([39]). 

### A – Application

In applying these rules, the court conducted a rigorous examination of the evidence provided:

- The false declarations made in the tender application were found to be deliberate, as both Tshopo and Nqulo were aware of Tshopo's employment and position that could potentially influence the tender evaluation ([22]).
- The magistrate’s comments indicated a clear understanding that the misrepresentations intended to deceive the tender board directly by hiding connections that could undermine the integrity of the tendering process. Evidence presented by the State indicated that Tshopo was controlling the finances of Abelusi and withdrawing funds directly from its account, which reflected fraudulent conduct ([26][17]).
- Regarding sentencing, the Court acknowledged the nature of the fraud, noting that it was particularly detrimental due to the inherent potential for nepotism and corruption within public procurement ([36]). The magistrate had imposed a sentence of four years. However, the Supreme Court found that there were substantial misdirections regarding the consideration of prejudice and the nature of the fraud in assessing moral culpability ([30][33]).

### C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeals against conviction but varied the sentence of the first appellant from four years to three years ([40]). The court's decision underscored the necessity for strict adherence to transparency in public procurement processes and the courts' role in maintaining public confidence through appropriate sentencing practices in cases of fraud ([39]). The case also highlights the critical importance of accountability for individuals involved in public tenders to combat corruption and ensure equitable access to government contracts.

In summary, the court's findings not only affirmed the convictions but also refined the approach to sentencing in cases of fraud related to public tenders, stressing the need for custodial sentences to reinforce public trust in governmental processes.
