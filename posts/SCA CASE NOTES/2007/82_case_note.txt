Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2007

### FIRAC Case Note: Singh v National Director of Public Prosecutions [2007] SCA 82 (RSA)

#### F – Facts
The case revolves around a preservation order for immovable property located at 21 Bell Grove, Berea, Durban, sought by the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) under section 38(2) of the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (POCA). The NDPP argued that the property was instrumental in drug dealing violations under section 5(b) of the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 140 of 1992, as it allegedly facilitated numerous drug-related offences ([1]-[2]).

The subject property, owned by the appellant, Sheila Devi Singh, was administrated by her son and was let out to a tenant named Ms. Joan Cele, who operated an unlicensed boarding establishment on the premises. The NDPP highlighted that between 1996 and 2003, the police made several arrests (36 in total) at the property for drug offences, with nine convictions stemming from these incidents. Some of the arrested individuals named the property as their residence ([3]-[4]).

Evidence presented included the discovery of various illegal substances, indicating that the property was being used as a site to deal drugs. The NDPP claimed that despite being warned by police, neither Singh nor her son took appropriate measures to prevent such activities, thereby allowing the property to become a haven for drug dealers ([5]-[6]).

Singh’s challenge to the preservation order initially focused on the ex parte nature of the proceedings, which she later abandoned. Instead, she contended that immovable property could not be seen as an instrumentality of a drug-related offence. The High Court dismissed Singh's application, asserting the overwhelming evidence of drug dealings led to an irrefutable conclusion that the property was instrumental in these activities ([7]-[8]).

Singh sought leave to appeal, which was granted following the realization that preservation orders are, in fact, appealable, contrary to prior assertions ([9]-[10]).

#### I – Issues
The central legal question before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the NDPP had established reasonable grounds to believe that Singh's property was an "instrumentality of an offence" as articulated in POCA and whether the property played a substantial role in the commission of drug-related offences ([11]).

#### R – Rules
The definition of "instrumentality of an offence" as found in POCA identifies it as any property involved in the commission or suspected commission of an offence. The interpretation of this stipulation must avoid “unbounded literalism” to prevent arbitrary deprivation of property, as articulated in relevant cases like *National Director of Public Prosecutions v R O Cook Properties (Pty) Ltd* and *First National Bank of South Africa Ltd t/a Wesbank v Commissioner of SARS* ([11]-[12]).

The court held that for property to qualify as an instrumentality, there must be a reasonably direct link between the property and its criminal use, and its use must not only be incidental but significantly functional to the commission of the crime ([12]).

#### A – Application
The court analyzed the NDPP's claims regarding the role of the property in facilitating drug dealings, including the frequency of arrests and the nature of the activities conducted there. Notably, the appellant's premises operated as an unlicensed establishment, fostering an environment conducive to illicit behaviour ([13]-[14]).

However, the court found that while the property was utilized for drug dealing, the lack of a direct and sustained connection weakened the NDPP's argument. The incidents of drug dealing were characterized as sporadic and varying by different individuals rather than a consistent operation, which negated the premise that the property itself was crucial to the commission of drug offences ([15]-[16]).

The court determined that the established link between the property and the drug-related offences was too general and largely incidental. The activities at the property could not be directly traced to Singh or her son's actions or any modifications made to the property to facilitate drug crimes. Thus, the NDPP did not successfully demonstrate that the property served an essential role in the illegal activities ([17]).

#### C – Conclusion
Consequently, the appeal was upheld, setting aside the High Court's preservation order, and directed the NDPP to bear the costs associated with the application. The court clarified that the mere presence of illegal activity on a property does not suffice to classify that property as an instrumentality of an offence. The ruling emphasized the necessity for a clear link between the property and the criminal acts alleged ([18]). 

This case underscores the judiciary's capacity to assess the evidential basis for claims of property being an instrumentality in the context of criminal law, particularly regarding the constitutional protection against arbitrary deprivation of property.
