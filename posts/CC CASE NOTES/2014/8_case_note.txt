Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: South African Informal Traders Forum and Others v City of Johannesburg and Others; South African National Traders Retail Association v City of Johannesburg and Others [2014] ZACC 8

## Facts
The Constitutional Court of South Africa heard two urgent applications on December 5, 2013, from the South African Informal Traders Forum (CCT 173/13) and the South African National Traders Retail Association (CCT 174/13) ([1]). These applications sought leave to appeal an order by the South Gauteng High Court, which had struck their application for interim relief from the roll due to a lack of urgency ([13]). The applicants were informal traders who had been unlawfully evicted during “Operation Clean Sweep” initiated by the City of Johannesburg. This operation involved the forced eviction of the applicants, who had previously been trading lawfully with permission from the City ([6]–[10]). The City asserted that the evictions aimed to eliminate unsightly and disorderly trading but also admitted to not distinguishing between lawful and unlawful traders during these evictions ([7]). Negotiations took place between the parties following the evictions, culminating in an agreement for a verification and re-registration process for traders ([9]).

The applicants argued that the City’s actions constituted a violation of their rights to trade and dignity as guaranteed under the Constitution. They contended that they faced irreparable harm from their evictions, which left them without a means of livelihood ([11]). The High Court had ruled the matter was not urgent, effectively dismissing the application ([13]) without written reasons, prompting this appeal ([1]).

## Issues
The key legal issues presented to the Constitutional Court were: 
1. **Appealability of the High Court's interlocutory order**: Whether the applicants could appeal the High Court's ruling that dismissed their application for interim relief.
2. **Urgency of the matter**: Whether the extraordinary circumstances warranted urgent consideration of the interim relief sought by the applicants.
3. **Merits of the interim relief**: Whether the applicants had established a prima facie entitlement to the relief sought, which included the return to their trading stalls while the matter was pending in the High Court ([15]).

## Rules
The primary constitutional provisions governing the case include:
- **Section 167(6) of the Constitution**: This section allows for appeals to the Constitutional Court when it is in the interests of justice ([16]).
- The court cited relevant jurisprudence on the appealability of interim orders, emphasizing that it must decide whether hearing an appeal serves the interests of justice ([17]) and referenced cases such as *Magidiwana v President of the Republic of South Africa* ([18]).
- Legal criteria for granting an interim interdict are also important, which include the presence of a prima facie right, the risk of irreparable harm, consideration of the balance of convenience, and the lack of an alternative remedy ([24]).

## Application
Upon hearing the urgent circumstances, the Court found it appropriate to grant immediate relief. The Court assessed the appealability of the High Court’s decision, suggesting that allowing the appeal was justified by the severe irreparable harm the traders would face if the order was not granted. It ruled that the application for interim relief was indeed urgent given the immediate and acute hardships resulting from the evictions, which rendered the applicants destitute ([29]).

The Court confirmed that the traders had a prima facie right under the By-Laws to trade at their previous locations and that the City had acted unlawfully by failing to adhere to the mandated processes under the Businesses Act in relocating traders ([26]). It noted that the City had acknowledged its failure to comply with these provisions, fundamentally undermining the legality of the eviction ([26]).

In weighing the balance of convenience, the Court found that the hardship faced by the applicants far outweighed any inconvenience that might be faced by the City, which had not provided any compelling justification for its actions ([30]–[34]).

## Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately held that the appeals were justified and granted the interim relief sought by the applicants, permitting them to resume trading at their original locations pending the outcome of the High Court proceedings ([1], [3]). The Court emphasized the importance of the right to trade and the dignity of the affected individuals, alongside the rule of law. The ruling highlighted significant implications for the treatment of informal traders and set a precedent for how local government actions must align with constitutional rights ([31]) and statutory requirements. The City was directed to pay the applicants' costs in both courts, reinforcing the accountability of public authorities in upholding the rights of vulnerable communities ([39]).
