Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: Law Society of the Northern Provinces v Mabando (736/10) [2011] ZASCA 122

**F – Facts:**  
This appeal arises from a judgment of the Mafikeng High Court, where the Law Society of the Northern Provinces (appellant) sought the removal of Kashan Ramokoka Mabando's (first respondent) name from the roll of attorneys. The application was dismissed, and a reprimand was issued against Mabando for unprofessional conduct ([1]-[2]). The first respondent, an attorney admitted to practice in June 1991, faced multiple complaints regarding his failure to pay his correspondents, respond to communications, and account for trust funds ([5]-[25]). The appellant contended that Mabando's conduct warranted removal from the roll, citing his lack of cooperation during disciplinary proceedings and ongoing disputes over fees owed to his colleagues ([2]-[4]). Mabando disputed the appellant’s jurisdiction over him, arguing that he was a member of the Law Society of Bophuthatswana and somehow claimed that the applicable statutory provision (s 84A of the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979) was unconstitutional, though he failed to pursue this line in a proper manner before the lower court ([3]-[4]).  

**I – Issues:**  
1. Did the High Court err in dismissing the application to strike Mabando's name from the roll of attorneys?
2. Was the first respondent to be considered a fit and proper person to continue practicing as an attorney, given his multiple acts of unprofessional conduct?
3. Did the appellant possess jurisdiction over the disciplinary matters involving Mabando within the context of s 84A of the Attorneys Act?  
4. Was the unconstitutionality claim against s 84A properly before the court, particularly in light of procedural requirements that were not met? ([1]-[3], [36], [40]).

**R – Rules:**  
The relevant statutory provisions include s 84A and s 71 of the Attorneys Act, which afford the Law Society of the Northern Provinces concurrent disciplinary powers over attorneys in the former Bophuthatswana. The Act also lays out a three-stage inquiry for removing an attorney from the roll ([11], [51]). According to existing case law, particularly **Mabaso v Law Society, Northern Provinces** and **Law Society, Northern Provinces v Mogami**, the concurrent jurisdiction is recognized and upheld, emphasizing the duty of the court to protect the integrity of the legal profession ([41], [42]). The failure to join requisite parties for constitutional challenges was illuminated in **Van der Merwe v Road Accident Fund**, indicating that the constitutional validity of legislation cannot be adjudicated without the presence of relevant executive authorities ([49]).

**A – Application:**  
The court applied the three-stage inquiry to assess whether the first respondent's conduct constituted unprofessional, dishonorable, or unworthy conduct. It found that factual conduct was established on a preponderance of probabilities, acknowledging the reprimands the first respondent received from the disciplinary committee and the gravity of his persistent failure to pay debts owed to his colleagues ( [54]-[58]). While Mabando admitted some conduct, his defenses centered on jurisdictional issues and alleged procedural failures on the part of the appellant, rather than adequate engagement with the substantive complaints ([26]-[30]). The court rejected these arguments, asserting that even if the concurrent jurisdiction was contested, the integrity of the legal profession necessitated the examination of his professional conduct ([59]-[61]). 

The court further chastised the second respondent (Law Society of Bophuthatswana) for undermining the disciplinary process and shielding an attorney who failed to meet ethical obligations ([62]). Thus, despite the appellant's assertion of jurisdiction being validated in prior cases, it was Mabando's conduct that led to the conclusion that he was unfit to practice as an attorney. The court emphasized that mere belief in jurisdiction or constitutional merits could not excuse professional misconduct.

**C – Conclusion:**  
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that the High Court had erred in its leniency towards Mabando’s actions. It held that Mabando was not a fit and proper person to continue practicing as an attorney, ending his ability to practice under the roll. The court upheld the appellant's appeal, criticizing Mabando’s failure to address legitimate grievances seriously and calling for a review of the regulatory responsibilities of the Law Society of Bophuthatswana ([62],[63]). The court ordered costs against both respondents on an attorney-and-client scale, reflecting the seriousness with which it viewed the disruptive turf war between the two regulatory bodies. The ruling reinforces the primacy of ethical standards and professional accountability in the practice of law in South Africa.
