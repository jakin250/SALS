Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Minister of Human Settlements, Western Cape v Penhill Residents Small Farmers Co-operative (429/2015)

**Court Citation**: Minister of Human Settlements, Western Cape v Penhill Residents Small Farmers Co-operative (429/2015) [2016] ZASCA 99 (3 June 2016)

**Coram**: Lewis, Cachalia, Saldulker, Mathopo JJA, and Tsoka AJA

---

### F – Facts

[1] The case centered around a significant tract of land known as Penhill Farms, located near Stellenbosch and owned by the Western Cape Provincial Government. Originally unoccupied until 1994, the area became home to indigent small-scale farmers who initiated livestock farming on the land. Over the years, the number of occupants increased, leading to the formation of the Penhill Residents Small Farmers Co-operative Ltd (the first respondent) in 2000, reflecting their efforts to organize as a farming group ([1–2]).

[2] By 2011, the Provincial Government sought to repurpose unoccupied portions of Penhill Farms for another group of farmers, known as the Ithemba farmers. Despite being made aware of this initiative, the Penhill Farmers continued occupying unoccupied land and erecting structures without consent from the landowner. Consequently, the Department of Human Settlements issued a notice in February 2011, requesting the demolition of the illegally erected structures, which went unheeded ([3]).

[3] An urgent application was filed by the Minister of Human Settlements in May 2011, seeking an interdict against the Penhill Farmers to prevent further unlawful occupations and the erection of new structures. At the hearing, the only remaining respondent was the Penhill Farmers, as other individual respondents had died or left their positions ([4–5]).

[4] The High Court initially dismissed the application, acknowledging what it termed a 'legitimate expectation' of the farmers to use the land, which led to procedural shortcomings in the interdict process. The full court upheld this finding, leading to a subsequent appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([5–6]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal questions in this case included:

1. **Did the Penhill Farmers have a legitimate expectation of occupying the entire property?** 
   
2. **Was there consent for the Penhill Farmers to occupy the total expanse of land on Penhill Farms?** 

3. **Did the actions of the Provincial Government to reallocate portions of the land constitute administrative action impacting the rights of the farmers?** 

These issues were vital in determining the validity of the interdict sought by the Minister of Human Settlements ([11–12], [29]).

### R – Rules

The court applied several legal principles and precedents, notably regarding consent and legitimate expectation:

1. **Consent**: Following principles established in *Standard Bank of South Africa Ltd & another v Ocean Commodities Inc & others* 1983 (1) SA 276 (A), where for a tacit contract to be established, unequivocal conduct of the parties must indicate a shared intention ([25]). 

2. **Legitimate Expectation**: Referring to *Residents of Joe Slovo Community, Western Cape v Thubelisha Homes & others* [2009] ZACC 16, where the court highlighted that consent signifies more than mere acquiescence but a voluntary agreement between parties ([27]). 

3. **Administrative Action**: The court considered whether the decision by the Provincial Government to allocate land for the Ithemba farmers constituted an administrative act impacting the Penhill Farmers' occupation rights ([29]).

### A – Application

In its analysis, the court meticulously assessed the evidence surrounding the consent given to the Penhill Farmers to occupy the land:

1. **Lack of Actual Consent**: The court found no demonstrable evidence of actual consent from the Provincial Government for the occupation of the entire property. The negotiations, while extensive, primarily focused on regularizing existing use rather than permitting new unauthorized occupations ([24]).

2. **Mutual Exclusivity of Claims**: The findings from the High Court, which concluded that there was both actual consent and a legitimate expectation of prior notice before eviction, were deemed mutually exclusive. The court determined that if actual consent were proven, then questions of legitimate expectation would be unnecessary, indicating confusion and flawed reasoning in the previous rulings ([12]).

3. **Absence of Administrative Action**: The court held that the Provincial Government's decision to allocate land for housing or new farmers did not amount to administrative action. The Penhill Farmers did not hold rights over the land they claimed, and thus their expectation to be consulted on its usage was unwarranted ([29–30]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appeal by the Minister of Human Settlements was valid. It overturned the previous decisions by the High Court and the full court and granted the interdict sought, effectively restraining the Penhill Farmers from unlawful occupation. The findings elucidated the importance of clarifying the essence of consent and legitimate expectations in property law.

Additionally, the court raised concerns about the implications of the lower courts’ decisions, noting that allowing unlawful occupiers to have rights over land under governmental negotiation would hinder future land management and housing developments ([32–34]).

This judgment underscores the necessity for clear legal frameworks governing land use and occupancy, crucial for balancing the rights of current occupants against the sovereign authority’s interests in managing land for public good. The court also ordered that the Penhill Farmers cover the costs incurred during the proceedings. 

--- 

*This case illustrates the nuanced tensions between unlawful occupation, consent, and the rights of government to manage land effectively.*
