Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 97 (21 June 2017) Date: 21 June 2017 Be

The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Neutral Citation: The NDPP v Media 24 Limited & others and HC Van Breda v Media 24 Limited & others (425/2017) [2017] ZASCA 97 (21 June 2017)
Date: 21 June 2017
Bench: Ponnan, Leach, Mbha, Zondi, and Van Der Merwe JJA
Heard: 18 May 2017

F – Facts:
The case arose within the context of a high-profile criminal trial involving Henri Christo Van Breda ([paras 3-4]). The appellant, charged with the murder of three family members, attracted widespread media attention. Media 24 Limited sought permission to broadcast the trial proceedings via video cameras, which was opposed by both the appellant and the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) ([paras 3-5]).

The high court initially granted Media 24 limited broadcasting rights, including still photography and restricted live coverage ([para 5]). Notably, paragraph 1.3 of the high court's order permitted the installation of two video cameras to record and broadcast proceedings under specific conditions. Van Breda and the NDPP appealed against this order ([para 7]).

I – Issues:
The primary legal question addressed by the court was whether the media should be permitted to broadcast court proceedings and to what extent, balancing the rights to freedom of expression and fair trial ([paras 8-9]). A secondary issue was whether a blanket prohibition on broadcasting could be justified based on concerns for a fair trial, particularly surrounding witness intimidation and the sanctity of court proceedings ([paras 9-12]).

R – Rules:
The court relied on multiple constitutionally and legally recognized principles. Central was §16 of the Constitution of South Africa, which guarantees the right to freedom of expression, including that of the press, alongside considerations of an open justice principle rooted in common law ([paras 8, 11]).

The guidelines from previous decisions about media presence in court were instructive, particularly the need for restrictions to demonstrate a clear and present danger to the fair trial rights of defendants ([para 36]). This was established in prior rulings such as Midi Television Pty Ltd v Director of Public Prosecutions (Western Cape) ([para 12]). Discretionary powers under §173 of the Constitution, permitting courts to regulate court processes, were also pivotal to the court's reasoning ([para 59]).

A – Application:
The court undertook a detailed analysis of the need for a careful balance between competing constitutional rights. It acknowledged the critical role of open justice and media coverage in promoting transparency and public confidence in the judicial system, while also weighing the potential risks to fair trial rights associated with media coverage ([paras 11-12, 20]).

The court noted, specifically, that speculative concerns regarding prejudice could not suffice to justify limiting press freedom. Instead, demonstrable risks needed articulation. Instead of endorsing a blanket prohibition against broadcasting, it emphasised a case-by-case approach to determine broadcasting on its merits ([paras 73-74]). The detailed scrutiny of the NDPP's argument against broadcasting showcased a judicial commitment to adapt existing frameworks to the realities of modern media technology ([paras 60-62]).

C – Conclusion:
The court concluded that the high court's blanket restriction on broadcasting part of the trial proceedings was insufficiently justified and therefore invalid ([para 76]). Thus, it set aside paragraph 1.3 of the high court order, emphasizing that the matter should be returned for reconsideration based on the principles laid out in its judgment ([paras 77-78]).

The decision set critical precedents for balancing media freedom with fair trial rights in South Africa, recognizing that the courtroom should remain an open space for public scrutiny while ensuring the dignity of proceedings is maintained. The order also held Media 24 responsible for the appellant's costs ([para 78]).
