# FIRAC Case Note: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa – The Minister of Safety and Security v Pedro Souza De Lima

### F – Facts
In this case, the respondent, Pedro Souza De Lima, was shot by Jose Andrade Dos Santos with a revolver on 6 March 1996 in Goodwood, resulting in De Lima becoming a paraplegic ([1]). Dos Santos had obtained a firearm licence from the Parow Police Station on 27 June 1995, following an application submitted on 14 November 1994. At trial, Judge Veldhuizen in the Cape Town High Court found the police negligent in issuing the licence due to failure in properly assessing Dos Santos's suitability to possess a firearm ([1]). The respondent consequently sought damages from the Minister of Safety and Security, who appealed the trial judge's decision ([1]).

### I – Issues
The primary issue on appeal was whether the police acted negligently in granting the firearm licence to Dos Santos, as determined by the trial court ([2]). Specifically, the court examined the adequacy of the police's investigation into the conditions surrounding the assault charge against Dos Santos, which had been withdrawn, and whether this constituted negligence under the legal standards applicable to firearm licensing at that time ([2]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework governing the issuance of firearm licences is primarily derived from the Arms and Ammunition Act 75 of 1969, which grants the Commissioner the discretion to issue licenses to suitable persons ([3]). Regulation 2(1) stipulated the required procedures for applying for a firearms licence, particularly emphasizing the assessment of the applicant's suitability ([3]). Additionally, the Special Force Order relevant to the issuance of firearms licences mandated that the police officer must confirm the applicant's suitability beyond just their criminal record, taking into account their character and temperament ([3]–[4]). Precedents, particularly the decision in *Minister of Safety and Security v Hamilton* 2004 (2) SA 216 (SCA), provided further legal context regarding the police’s duty to verify information provided by applicants ([2]).

### A – Application
The court found that Sergeant Basson, who processed Dos Santos’s application, failed to adequately assess the circumstances of the assault charge and its withdrawal ([10]). Basson focused solely on whether Dos Santos had any convictions rather than whether he was a fit and proper person to possess a firearm ([4]–[5]). Notably, Basson’s understanding of the situation was limited; he neglected to delve deeper into the withdrawal of the assault charge, which could signify potential issues of aggression or temperament ([10])—critical factors for assessing a person’s suitability for firearm ownership.

Moreover, the court determined that the police exceeded their discretion by failing to conduct an essential inquiry into the nature of the complaint and the context surrounding the incident ([5]–[6]). This gap in investigation was not merely a trivial oversight; it contributed directly to the tragic outcome of De Lima's injury. In determining negligence, the court underscored that the police had a duty to take reasonable steps in investigating an applicant's background and to not disregard red flags indicated during the application process ([6]–[9]).

Had Basson made a thorough inquiry and reviewed the representations made to the prosecutor, it is likely that he would have deemed Dos Santos unsuitable for licence issuance, culminating in a different outcome not only for De Lima but also potentially averting the subsequent injury ([9]–[10]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the trial judge's finding of negligence on the part of the police ([12]). The court reiterated the importance of adherence to due diligence in the licensing process for firearms, emphasizing that the police must ensure that applicants are thoroughly vetted to maintain public safety. The outcome established a direct line of accountability for police negligence in the context of firearms licensing, setting a robust precedent about the expectations of law enforcement in the licensing process. The implication of this decision underscores the judicial affirmation of the principle that actions of police officers can have profound real-world consequences and that negligence in their duties can lead to civil liabilities for the state ([12]). 

The case thus serves as a pivotal reference for issues related to negligence in firearm licensing, reaffirming the necessity of thorough investigation and the responsibilities of law enforcement in protecting public safety.
