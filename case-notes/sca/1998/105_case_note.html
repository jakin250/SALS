Supreme Court of Appeal

Phillips v Botha [1998] 3 SA 948 (W)

The charges stemmed from an incident on 21 July 1993, when Botha gambled at the appellant's casino.

Supreme Court of Appeal 1998 [1998] 3 SA 948 (W)

F – Facts

This civil appeal originates from private prosecution proceedings led by Andrew Lionel Phillips (the appellant), who is the owner of a casino, against Pierre Botha (the respondent), an attorney. Following the issuance of a certificate of nolle prosequi by the Attorney-General for the Witwatersrand, Phillips instituted private prosecution against Botha for fraud under section 7(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([para 2]).

The charges stemmed from an incident on 21 July 1993, when Botha gambled at the appellant's casino. Over the night, he issued a total of 22 cheques as payment for gambling counters, which amounted to R105 000. On the morning of 22 July, Botha instructed the Trust Bank to stop payment on these cheques, which were subsequently returned as "payment stopped." The criminal summons accused Botha of fraud, claiming he intended to defraud Phillips by issuing cheques that he knowingly would not be honored ([paras 3-5]).

The Regional Magistrate acquitted Botha’s case, concluding that Phillips could not claim injury due to the unenforceability under gambling law of debts incurred in this context ([paras 10-13]). Phillips appealed unsuccessfully to the Witwatersrand Local Division before obtaining leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 2]).

I – Issues

The following legal questions arose from this case:

  1. Did the appellant demonstrate a "substantial and peculiar interest" sufficient to qualify him as a private prosecutor under section 7(1) of the Act?
  2. Did the appellant suffer an "injury" as required by section 7(1) that arose from the respondent's actions pertaining to fraud?
  3. Could the appellant maintain locus standi, considering the underlying illegal nature of the transactions involved ([paras 13-17])?

R – Rules

The critical legal statutes and precedents that guided the court’s decision included:

  • Section 7(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977: which allows a private individual to prosecute if they prove substantial and peculiar interest due to injury suffered from the commission of the alleged offense ([para 12]).
  • The principle of ex turpi causa non oritur actio (from a dishonorable cause an action does not arise), which prevents a party from claiming an action where they are implicated in illegality ([para 16]).
  • Precedents such as Mullins v Pearlman ([1917] TPD 639) and Ellis v Visser ([1954] 2 SA 431 (T)) were cited, emphasizing that if there is no civil remedy available for the injuries alleged, the right to pursue a criminal remedy through private prosecution may also be negated ([paras 15-16]).

A – Application

The court scrutinized Phillips' claims regarding his “substantial and peculiar interest” in the alleged trial issue. It was found that Phillips could not establish such interest since the cheques he relied upon were intrinsically linked to an illegal gambling contract, rendering any recovery impossible in both civil and criminal contexts. Phillips’s claims of injury were based on a potential loss that stemmed not from Botha’s fraudulent representation but rather from the legal boundaries surrounding gambling debts, which are deemed unenforceable ([paras 9-29]).

The appeal court underscored that private prosecution requires proof of a legitimate injury from an actionable offense, which Phillips failed to establish due to the illegitimate nature of the underlying transactions. The majority opinion indicated that allowing a private prosecution to proceed in this scenario would effectively serve to neglect the legal consequences of illegal activities, contravening public policy and the established legal framework ([paras 22-31]).

C – Conclusion

The court ultimately dismissed Phillips' appeal, upholding the Regional Magistrate’s discharge of Botha. The judgment emphasized that the appellant did not possess locus standi to institute a private prosecution as he did not suffer an "injury" cognizable under the terms of section 7 of the Act, given that the claims were based on an illegal contract. The court recognized that allowing such proceedings could lead to the exploitation of the criminal justice system for private gain in relation to inherently illegal conduct. The implications of this decision underscore the principle that individuals cannot seek redress through lawful processes arising from their own illegal activities ([paras 30-31]).

The judgement reflects a clear stance against the potential abuse of various forms of legal process and the courts' reluctance to provide remedies for violations arising from transactions deemed illegal by the law. Phillips was also ordered to bear the costs of the appeal, including those incurred by Botha’s representation ([paras 31]).
