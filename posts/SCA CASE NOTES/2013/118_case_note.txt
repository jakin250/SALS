Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

### Case Note: The Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration v Law Society of the Northern Provinces (CCMA v Law Society)

**FIRAC Case Note**  
**Neutral citation**: CCMA v Law Society, Northern Provinces (005/13) [2013] ZASCA 118 (20 September 2013)  

#### F – Facts
[1] This case concerns the constitutionality of rule 25(1)(c) of the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA) rules. The rule limits legal representation in arbitration proceedings assessing the fairness of dismissals for misconduct or incapacity, allowing such representation only with the consent of both the commissioner and all parties. Tuchten J of the North Gauteng High Court declared this provision unconstitutional, permitting a suspension of this declaration for 36 months to allow for legislative amendment. The appeal followed this decision, with the CCMA and various governmental ministers as appellants and the Law Society of the Northern Provinces as the respondent [2].

The Law Society argued that rule 25(1)(c) unfairly discriminates against legal practitioners, infringing upon their rights under s 9(3) of the Constitution, which prohibits unfair discrimination, as well as undermining the rights encompassed in ss 22 and 34 of the Constitution, which protect the right to choose an occupation and guaranteed access to just administrative action, respectively. The Law Society further contended that the nature of CCMA proceedings is judicial, rather than merely administrative, thus warranting the necessity of legal representation [2].

#### I – Issues
[3] The principal legal questions arising from this case are:
1. Does rule 25(1)(c) unconstitutionally infringe on the right to legal representation and discriminate against legal practitioners under s 9(3) of the Constitution?
2. To what extent does the discretionary power granted to commissioners under this rule conform to the rights protected by ss 22 and 34 of the Constitution?

#### R – Rules
[4] The court primarily examined the applicability of various constitutional provisions including:
- **Section 9(3)** of the Constitution: Prohibiting unfair discrimination.
- **Section 22**: Protecting the right to freely choose trade and occupation.
- **Section 34**: Ensuring the right to fair legal hearing and access to justice.

Additionally, the statutory framework included:
- The **Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995** (LRA), specifically the rules established under s 115(2A) that empower the CCMA to regulate representation in disputes [4].
- The **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000** (PAJA), particularly s 3(3)(a), which asserts that in matters of serious or complex nature, legal representation should be conferred at the administrator’s discretion [8].

#### A – Application
[5] The court analyzed how Tuchten J's ruling suggested a misinterpretation of the requirement for rationality and the application of the law. The judgment previously rendered by Tuchten J rested on the purported irrationality of the rule, contending that it unduly discriminated against legal practitioners while failing to account for the context in which the CCMA operates. The judge emphasized that the proceedings pertaining to dismissals are serious matters, contrary to CCMA’s assertion that these disputes could be processed without counsel [7].

The court rebutted this conclusion by clarifying that the CCMA is designed to resolve disputes with a view towards being non-formal and efficient, which justifies restricting legal representation in specific contexts. The court highlighted that while the limit might seem to present an imbalance, it serves the larger purpose of expediting dispute resolution in predominately simpler dismissal cases, falling within the public policy ambit outlined during the legislative drafting of the LRA [17].

The court concluded that Tuchten J had failed to consider that the delegated authority to commissioners allows for a degree of discretion, ensuring that legal representation can be granted if circumstances require it—often addressing concerns of serious cases or complexities that might arise in individual arbitrations [21].

#### C – Conclusion
[6] Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, reversing Tuchten J's decision and validating the constitutionality of rule 25(1)(c). It found that the rule does not unconstitutionally discriminate against legal practitioners and allows for sufficient flexibility to ensure fair representation is available when necessary through the discretion afforded to commissioners [27].

The ruling underscored that legal representation is not an unqualified right in administrative tribunal settings like the CCMA, reaffirming that the principles of rationality and public interest guide administrative procedures. This decision illustrates the balance between facilitating expedient labour dispute resolution and upholding procedural fairness within South Africa's constitutional framework. 

**Order**:
1. The appeal is upheld with costs.
2. The court below’s order is set aside and replaced with the dismissal of the application with costs.
