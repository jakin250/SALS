Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2008

# Case Note for *Du Toit v The Minister of Safety and Security* (467/07) [2008] ZASCA 125

## FIRAC Structure

### F – Facts ([paras 1–8])

The appellant, W.A.L. Du Toit, formerly served as the National Commanding Officer of Technical Support Services within the South African Police Service (SAPS). He was convicted on 14 June 1996 of four counts of murder and subsequently sentenced to 15 years imprisonment on 27 June 1996. In accordance with section 36(1) of the South African Police Service Act 68 of 1995 (hereafter, "the SAPS Act"), this conviction deemed him discharged from SAPS.

Du Toit appealed his conviction, which was postponed while he sought amnesty under the Promotion of National Unity and Reconciliation Act 34 of 1995 (hereafter, "the Amnesty Act"). He communicated with the National Commissioner of the SAPS on multiple occasions regarding his potential reinstatement, particularly in light of his successful application for amnesty. This amnesty, granted on 23 December 2005, stipulated that his conviction be deemed expunged. However, the National Commissioner and the Chief of Staff responded negatively, asserting that reinstatement was not permissible under the statutes governing the SAPS.

Subsequently, Du Toit sought an order from the Pretoria High Court for his reinstatement. The High Court dismissed his application, leading to the current appeal.

### I – Issues ([paras 8–19])

The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:

1. Whether section 20(10) of the Amnesty Act operates retrospectively, thereby invalidating the consequences of Du Toit’s conviction and discharge from SAPS.
2. Whether section 36(2) of the SAPS Act allows for reinstatement of individuals granted amnesty in light of a previous conviction.
3. Whether a letter from the National Commissioner could constitute a binding agreement for reinstatement.

### R – Rules ([paras 9, 13–19, 22])

The pertinent legal provisions in question included:

- **Section 36(1) of the SAPS Act**: This section automatically deems a police member discharged upon conviction and sentencing to imprisonment without an option of a fine.
- **Section 20(10) of the Amnesty Act**: This section provides that a conviction for which amnesty has been granted shall be considered expunged from records and treated as if it never occurred. 
- Judicial principles regarding the presumption against retrospective application of statutes were emphasized, particularly as articulated in *Bellairs v Hodnett* (1978) and *Sunshine Porcelain Potteries Pty Ltd v Nash* (1961).

### A – Application ([paras 10–19, 21])

In its analysis, the Supreme Court of Appeal addressed the appellant’s claim regarding the retrospective effect of section 20(10). The Court upheld the presumption that statutes operate prospectively unless explicitly stated otherwise. It concluded that the legislative intent appeared to promote reconciliation without undoing the legal consequences of the past. 

The appellant’s arguments for retroactive application were found unpersuasive, as the language of section 20(10) did not clearly indicate the legislative intent for retrospective effect. Moreover, the Court opined that to interpret this section otherwise could lead to unintended and widespread implications, affecting numerous existing contractual and statutory relationships adversely.

Regarding section 36(2) of the SAPS Act, the Court distinguished between appeal processes and amnesty proceedings. The criteria for reinstatement under section 36(2) were not met, as Du Toit’s conviction was not set aside through judicial review but was effectively deemed not to have occurred under amnesty provisions.

Finally, the Court dismissed any notion of a legally binding agreement from the National Commissioner’s letter, asserting that it merely articulated the Commissioner’s legal interpretation rather than constituting a contractual commitment to reinstate. 

### C – Conclusion ([paras 23])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Du Toit's appeal with costs, including the costs of two counsel. The ruling underscored the importance of legislative clarity and adherence to procedural statutory frameworks. The Court’s interpretation not only reaffirmed the finality of statutory consequences arising from criminal convictions but also highlighted a legislature's intentional distinction between different mechanisms of legal redress, namely amnesty and criminal appeals. Consequently, Du Toit remained discharged from the SAPS without entitling him to reinstatement post-amnesty. 

This decision reflects the balance between individual justice and the broader aims of national reconciliation and legal finality in the context of South Africa's complex past.
