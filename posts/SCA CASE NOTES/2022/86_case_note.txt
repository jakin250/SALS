Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: Thembinkosi Mekuto v The State (1120/2020) [2022] ZASCA 86

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([1]–[6])

In the case of **Thembinkosi Mekuto**, the applicant, along with four co-accused, faced serious charges stemming from a violent robbery that took place on December 18, 2007. The incident involved armed robbers attacking two security officers transporting cash, resulting in the death of one officer, Mr. Norawuzana, and serious danger to the other, Mr. Mabhikwana. The robbers, after committing the crime, fled in a getaway vehicle that subsequently crashed, leading to their arrest. Mekuto and his co-accused were charged with robbery with aggravating circumstances (count one), murder (count two), attempted murder (count three), possession of unlicensed firearms (count four), and illegal possession of ammunition (count five) ([1]).

At trial, Mekuto and his co-accused were found guilty on all counts, and harsh sentences were imposed: 15 years for robbery, life imprisonment for murder, and 15 years for attempted murder, among other sentences for firearms crimes ([3]). Mekuto, aggrieved by the trial court's decision, initially sought leave to appeal against all convictions and sentences. The appeal was partially granted for his co-accused but not for him. After realizing that a co-accused received special leave to appeal, Mekuto applied for reconsideration of his application for special leave ([4]–[5]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was tasked with determining whether the initial refusal to grant him leave to appeal was justified, particularly in relation to the attempted murder charge, where his sentence was disproportionately severe compared to a sentence subsequently imposed on Mr. Mpuqe for the same crime ([6]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([7]–[8])

The primary issues before the SCA were:

1. **Condonation for Late Filing**: Whether the court should condone the late filing of the application for reconsideration of the previous refusal for special leave to appeal ([7]).

2. **Reconsideration of Sentence**: Whether the two judges of the SCA correctly regarded the interests of justice in refusing to grant Mekuto leave to appeal against his sentence for attempted murder and whether there existed special circumstances warranting reconsideration of that decision ([7]–[8]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([9]–[18])

The court's analysis was governed by the following legal principles and provisions:

1. **Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997**: This Act establishes minimum sentencing frameworks under sections 51(1) and 51(2), and provides that a court may impose life sentences for murder and significant incarceration for other violent crimes like robbery ([3], [11]–[12]).

2. **Common Law Principles**: The court referred to the long-standing common law principle that sentencing is a prerogative of the trial court. Interference on appeal requires clear evidence of misdirection that would justify altering a sentence ([33]).

3. **Case Law Precedents**: Several precedents were cited, including *S v Zinn*, which outlines the triad of considerations for sentencing: the nature of the offence, the offender, and the interests of society. Additional cases, including *S v Legoa* and *Mpuqe v S*, helped establish how courts should interpret the law and the guidelines on minimum sentences ([13]–[25]).

4. **Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013**: Section 17(2)(f) allows for an application to reconsider decisions related to appeals, specifically for a reconsideration of whether special leave should have been granted in previous proceedings ([5]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([9]–[37])

1. **Condonation for Late Filing**: The SCA found that the State did not oppose the applicant’s request for condonation. The court deemed the circumstances of the late submission significant enough to merit granting condonation in the interests of justice ([7]).

2. **Reconsideration of Sentence**: Regarding the attempted murder conviction, the SCA drew a parallel between Mekuto’s circumstances and those of Mr. Mpuqe, whose convicted sentence was deemed excessively harsh compared to the new 10-year sentence for the same charge ([33]). The justification for varying the sentence centered on the concept of proportionality, arguing that both applicants were similarly situated and should therefore receive comparable punishment.

   The court emphasized the need to consider the nature of each specific case and the personal characteristics of the offenders. Given that Mr. Mabhikwana, the attempted murder victim, sustained no injuries thanks to protective gear, the actual danger posed was less severe than initially appraised in Mekuto's case and justified a reconsideration of his sentence ([36]).

3. **Final Assessment of Other Counts**: The SCA ultimately upheld all sentences related to counts one, two, four, and five, confirming that these did not merit further appeal due to the absence of special circumstances justifying a reconsideration. Particularly, they found no reasonable grounds for questioning the minimum sentences prescribed for robbery and murder due to the clear objective nature of these crimes and the serious consequences they entail ([32], [32]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([38])

In conclusion, the SCA held that the application for condonation of late filing was granted. In respect to the attempted murder charge, the court varied the prior refusal of leave and granted Mekuto a new appeal on this count, ultimately reducing the sentence from 15 years to 10 years’ imprisonment. This judgment underlines the SCA’s commitment to ensuring uniformity in sentencing among similarly situated defendants ([38]).

The decision reinforced that disparities in sentencing must be addressed to adhere to principles of justice and equality in the application of law, particularly in instances where the same facts and legal frameworks are applicable ([33]–[37]). Therefore, this ruling is significant not only in adjusting Mekuto’s sentence but also in providing guidance on the court's approach to the reconsideration of sentencing in criminal matters.

The outcome illustrates the balancing act courts face in maintaining the integrity of sentencing guidelines while ensuring justice is served in a manner that reflects both the crime's gravity and the offender’s particular circumstances.
