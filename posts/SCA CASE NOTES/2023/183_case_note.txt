Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: Secona Freight Logistics CC v Samie and Others (1074/2022) [2023] ZASCA 183

## F - Facts
The case centers around Secona Freight Logistics CC (the appellant), a logistics entity that leases Erf 329 Cato Manor, which was historically a cemetery. The first respondent, Koobendran Samie, a resident of Yellowwood Park and an environmental advocate, filed an application seeking to restrain the appellant and other respondents from engaging in activities on the property, asserting that such activities contravened various environmental protections, including the National Heritage Resources Act 25 of 1999, the National Water Act 36 of 1998, and the National Environmental Management Act 107 of 1998 ([paras 3–4]). Samie's claims derive from his personal connection to the cemetery, which he considers an important cultural and heritage site ([paras 6–7]). 

The appellant challenged Samie's standing (locus standi) to bring the application, raising this as a preliminary issue (point in limine) during the hearing at the High Court of KwaZulu-Natal. The court initially heard the locus standi issue as it intended this ruling could be dispositive of the entire application ([paras 9–12]). The High Court ruled that Samie had standing under sections 38 and 32 of the South African Constitution and the National Environmental Management Act, which allows individuals to sue in the public interest ([paras 10–12]). Consequently, the appellant appealed the ruling on the basis of locus standi and sought condonation for the late filing of its notice of appeal ([paras 1, 15]).

## I - Issues
The key legal questions pertained to:
1. Whether the High Court's order related to locus standi was of a final nature and, therefore, appealable to the Supreme Court of Appeal.
2. Whether the appellant's application for condonation for the late filing of the notice of appeal should be granted and the appeal reinstated ([paras 2, 14]).

## R - Rules
The legal principles guiding the court's consideration included:
1. **Locus Standi**: Defined by sections 38 and 32 of the Constitution and the National Environmental Management Act, which permit anyone acting in the public interest to approach the court for relief ([paras 10–12]).
2. **Finality and Appealability**: An order is appealable if it is final in effect, definitive of the rights of the parties, and disposes of a substantial portion of the relief claimed ([paras 19–20]). The case law cited included *Zweni v Minister of Law and Order* 1993 (1) SA 523 (A) regarding the characteristics of appealable orders ([para 19]).
3. **Interests of Justice**: Courts now consider the interests of justice as a factor in determining appealability alongside formal characteristics of finality ([para 21]). 

## A - Application
The court first evaluated the procedural aspect of the late filing. It noted the inordinate delay of one year and six months but was persuaded by an environmental disaster's impact on the operations of the courts and found it reasonable to grant condonation ([para 16]). 

Addressing the core issue of the appealability of the High Court's order on locus standi, the Supreme Court determined that orders made in the course of litigation which do not resolve the core claim are ordinarily deemed interlocutory and not appealable ([para 22]). Given that the High Court's decision did not bar the applicant from proceeding with their case and merely postponed further considerations, it lacked the finality necessary to be appealable ([para 23]). 

The court also recognized concerns about potential misuse of procedural rules to perpetuate litigation, specifically with challenges to procedural rulings that lead to piecemeal appeals. It emphasized the importance of minimizing litigation delays so that legitimate disputes can be resolved more expediently ([para 29]).

## C - Conclusion
In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal granted the application for condonation for the late filing of the notice of appeal and reinstated the appeal; however, it struck the appeal off the roll, establishing it was not appealable ([paras 31–32]). The judgment emphasized the necessity for litigants, particularly well-resourced ones, to avoid tactical delays in legal processes and also highlighted the need for courts to remain vigilant against such strategies ([paras 29, 30]). The case illustrates the court’s commitment to upholding procedural integrity while reinforcing the doctrine that access to the courts shall not be unduly hindered by strict interpretations of standing, especially in environmental matters.
