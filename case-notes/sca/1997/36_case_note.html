In the matter between Petrus Mahlangu and Sipho Charles Nkosi v The State

Case Title: In the matter between Petrus Mahlangu and Sipho Charles Nkosi v The State Case No: 642/93 & 448/94 Court: Supreme Court of Appeal Judges: E M Grosskopf, Nienaber, and Schutz JJA Heard: 5 May 1997 Delivered: 9 May 1997 Judgment: Schutz JA

Supreme Court of Appeal 1997 Delivered 9 May 1997

FIRAC Case Note

Case Title: In the matter between Petrus Mahlangu and Sipho Charles Nkosi v The State
Case No: 642/93 & 448/94
Court: Supreme Court of Appeal
Judges: E M Grosskopf, Nienaber, and Schutz JJA
Heard: 5 May 1997
Delivered: 9 May 1997
Judgment: Schutz JA

F – Facts

The appellants, Petrus Mahlangu (first appellant) and Sipho Charles Nkosi (second appellant), were found guilty of murder and robbery with aggravating circumstances. They were sentenced to death for the murder count and to 12 years' imprisonment for the robbery count. Only the second appellant appealed against his conviction. The trial court found that both appellants participated in the brutal murder of Jacob Blignaut on the night of July 31, 1992, at the deceased's home. Both assailants were also involved in the robbery of various items from the residence.

On August 1, 1992, the deceased’s stolen vehicle was found crashed near Siyathuthuka, containing items stolen from his home. Evidence used to convict the second appellant included his presence with the first appellant while transporting stolen goods, witness testimonies placing him near the crime scene, their conversation in custody, and his statements to the police potentiate his involvement in the murder ([paras 2–6]).

I – Issues

The primary issues revolved around the second appellant's conviction for murder:

  1. Whether the evidence presented was sufficient to confirm the second appellant's participation as a co-perpetrator in the murder of Jacob Blignaut ([paras 2–4]).
  2. The validity of the evidence obtained during a police interrogation and the subsequent pointing out by the second appellant and whether it could be accepted to corroborate his involvement ([paras 9–12]).

R – Rules

The court relied upon principles of criminal law, particularly regarding the standards of proof for conviction, the admissibility of evidence, and the significance of implied confessions during police custody. Notably, the ruling referenced the precedential case S v Makwanyane ([1995] 3 SA 391 (CC)), which invalidated the death penalty as unconstitutional. This ruling necessitated a re-evaluation of the death sentences imposed on the appellants, rendering them void ([para 13]).

A – Application

The court carefully assessed the evidence against the legal standards required for the conviction of murder. The evidence implicating the second appellant could be categorized in four parts:

  1. Association with stolen goods: The appellants were seen transporting stolen items shortly after the murder, which was a critical factor in their identification as accomplices.
  2. Witness Sibanyoni's testimony: Sibanyoni testified to seeing the appellants near the deceased's residence. The second appellant's denial of being present contradicted his later admissions, weakening his defense ([paras 6–8]).
  3. Cell conversation: The trial court considered the implications of the testimony where the first appellant recounted details of the murder to a witness, while the second appellant remained silent—a silence the court took as an uncontradicted acknowledgment implicating him ([para 9]).
  4. Pointing out evidence: The admissibility of the second appellant's pointings out and his statements made during police questioning were scrutinized. The court accepted the police’s account over the appellant’s claims of coercion due to the absence of substantive evidence supporting his claim of duress. Thus, this evidence was crucial in confirming his involvement in the murder ([paras 10–12]).

The cumulative weight of these evidences led the court to find an overwhelming case against the second appellant, affirming that a reasonable conclusion could be drawn regarding his guilt.

C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the court dismissed the appeal against the second appellant's conviction for murder, affirming the trial court’s findings based on the strong evidence presented. However, the court overturned the death sentences against both appellants due to the unconstitutionality established in S v Makwanyane. The matter was remitted to the trial court for the imposition of new, competent sentences on the murder count, as there was no subsequent appeal regarding the robbery conviction ([paras 14–15]).

This case underscores the balance between the evidentiary standards in criminal proceedings and the evolving landscape of constitutional law concerning capital punishment in South Africa.
