Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# FIRAC Case Note: Zuma v Secretary of the Judicial Commission of Inquiry into Allegations of State Capture, Corruption and Fraud in the Public Sector Including Organs of State and Others [2021] ZACC 28

## F – Facts
The case arises from an application for rescission submitted by Jacob Gedleyihlekisa Zuma, former President of South Africa, concerning an earlier order issued by the Constitutional Court on June 29, 2021. This order found Zuma in contempt of court for failing to comply with directives from the Judicial Commission of Inquiry into Allegations of State Capture. The Commission had sought an order compelling Zuma to appear before it and testify. The Court ordered him to serve a 15-month prison sentence due to his non-compliance, which Zuma did not oppose during the initial contempt proceedings. Subsequently, he sought rescission of the contempt order on grounds he believed constituted serious errors and breaches of constitutional rights ([1]-[6], [8]-[11]).

In his affidavit, Zuma argued that his application was urgent and laid out various claims: his health conditions, the claimed harshness of the sentence, the alleged unconstitutionality of the proceedings, and the assertion that he had not been afforded a fair trial ([13]-[18], [24]-[28]). He contended that the order failed to recognize his right to free expression and to be afforded an opportunity for a fair hearing ([19]-[20], [138]).

## I – Issues
The central legal questions in this case concern:
1. Whether the Constitutional Court may rescind its own orders and, if so, under what circumstances.
2. Whether Zuma's claim to be treated as a party entitled to due process rights under the Constitution was valid given his non-participation in the contempt proceedings ([46]-[47],[134]-[135]).
3. Whether the procedures followed in convicting and subsequently sentencing him violated sections 12(1)(b), 34, and 35 of the Constitution, particularly in relation to the notion of "detention without trial" ([251]-[252]).

## R – Rules
Key legal principles involved include:
- **Rescission of Judgment**: The grounds for rescinding judgments are set out in Rule 42 of the Uniform Rules of Court, which states that a court may rescind an order if granted erroneously in the absence of any party or due to ambiguity or error. This rule, while applicable, is narrowed by its interpretation to promote legal certainty and finality ([52]-[54]).
- **Constitutional Rights**: Sections 12(1)(b) (right not to be detained without trial), section 34 (right to just administrative action), and section 35(3) (rights of accused persons in criminal proceedings) provide the frameworks within which the issues of law are assessed ([12]-[14], [223]).
- **Finality and Certainty**: Judicial decisions, especially from apex courts, are to be treated as final to uphold the integrity of the legal system, preventing an avalanche of rescission applications ([137]-[138]).

## A – Application
In the majority judgment authored by Justice Khampepe, the court found that Zuma had not established appropriate grounds for rescinding the order. Notably, the court emphasized that the absence of an appeal does not translate into an automatic right to rescind; an applicant must demonstrate that the order was granted in their absence and must also prove an error that would have precluded the granting of the order ([56]-[63]).

The court ruled that since Zuma actively chose not to participate in the originals proceedings despite being able to do so, he could not later claim his absence constituted grounds for rescission ([67]-[74]). It further asserted that the procedures followed in the contempt case complied with constitutional requirements and that it was within the court's authority to impose a custodial sentence for contempt. Therefore, Zuma's arguments that the motion proceedings were inconsistent with constitutional protections via sections 12 and 35(3) were found unpersuasive ([124]-[137]).

### Dissenting Opinion
In contrast, Jafta J noted that the majority's decision effectively created a significant procedural defect due to the lack of adequate safeguards in the contempt proceedings aligned with sections 12 and 35 of the Constitution, suggesting that such procedural omissions rendered the imprisoning order invalid ([249]-[252]). The dissent argued for the need to correct what could be perceived as a failure of justice, using Molaudzi as a precedent to assert that the court retains discretion to intervene when grave injustices are presented ([248]-[252]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court dismissed Zuma's application for rescission, thereby upholding his sentence for contempt of court. Justice Khampepe’s majority decision underscored the importance of finality in judicial decisions while emphasizing that Zuma's failure to engage with the contempt proceedings barred him from successfully invoking grounds for rescission ([128]-[132]). The dissenting judgment raised critical points about the necessity of procedural fairness and constitutional compliance, highlighting broader implications for the application of justice in South Africa ([249]-[252]).

Overall, the case illustrates the delicate balance courts must navigate between ensuring individuals' rights are protected while maintaining the integrity and finality of judicial decisions. The outcome serves as a precedent reinforcing the existing legal framework for contempt proceedings and clarifying the thresholds for rescission applications within the South African legal landscape.
