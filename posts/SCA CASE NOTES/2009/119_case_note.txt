Source Folder: 2009

# Case Note: Kiepersol Poultry Farm (Pty) Ltd v Phasiya [2009] ZASCA 119

**FIRAC Method**  

**F – Facts**  
The appeal originates from the judgment of the Land Claims Court (Bam JP), which dismissed Kiepersol Poultry Farm (Pty) Ltd’s (the appellant) application for the eviction of Gideon Phasiya (the respondent) from a house situated on the Zandspruit farm ([para 1]). The appellant, which operates a poultry farming and processing business, leases the farm from Sojordi Beleggings (Edms) Bpk ([para 2]). The respondent's father, Sam Phasiya, an employee of the appellant, occupied the premises with his family, including the respondent who remained there following certain changes in their living arrangements.

The dispute centers around the alleged abandonment of the premises by Sam Phasiya, who reportedly moved to live with his son, Martin, after sustaining injuries from a motor vehicle accident in 2004. The appellant claims that Sam vacated the house in early 2004, while the respondent contends that Sam remained an occupier in accordance with section 8(4) of the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (the Act) ([paras 3-5]).

On 31 January 2007, the Land Claims Court allowed for evidence regarding the respondent's claim that he occupied the premises under his father's continued rights as an occupier. After considering testimonies from both parties, the lower court found in favor of the respondent, determining that Sam never abandoned his home on the farm but had been temporarily residing with his son ([paras 6-18]).

**I – Issues**  
The primary issue to determine in this appeal was whether Sam Phasiya continued to occupy the premises on Zandspruit at the time eviction proceedings were initiated, thus whether Gideon Phasiya could claim any right of residence under Sam’s occupancy ([para 7]). Additionally, the court considered whether the termination of the respondent's right of residence was just and equitable under the provisions of sections 8(1) and 11 of the Act.

The case raises questions regarding the definitions of 'occupier' and 'residence' under the Act and whether the actions of both the appellant and the respondent constituted abandonment of rights to the premises ([paras 7-24]).

**R – Rules**  
The relevant legal framework includes the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997, particularly sections 1, 8, and 9, which define an "occupier," delineate the rights and limitations of occupancy, and specify grounds for eviction ([paras 7, 25]).  

- Section 1 provides definitions including "occupier" as a person residing on land with consent.
- Section 8(4) protects elderly or incapacitated occupiers from eviction unless specific conditions are met ([para 7]).
- Section 9 outlines that a court can issue an eviction order only if certain procedural requirements are met, including notice and compliance with the just and equitable test outlined in section 8(1) ([para 25]).

The law seeks to balance the rights of occupiers—especially vulnerable individuals against unjust eviction practices.

**A – Application**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) dissected both the factual and procedural matters arising from the case. The court scrutinized evidence from the appellant's managing director, Du Plessis, and the respondent regarding the status of Sam's occupation and presence at the premises ([paras 19-24]).

The evidence for the appellant was found to be credible, including that of witnesses who confirmed Sam's limited presence on the farm post-accident. Importantly, the SCA noted that Du Plessis's testimony was unchallenged and highlighted inconsistencies in the respondent’s claims regarding Sam's rotating arrangement between living with his sons and the farm ([paras 22-23]).

The evaluation of the term "resides" indicated that it embodies a concept of a permanent home rather than occasional or temporary stays, thus tipping the balance towards the appellant's interpretation of events ([para 9]). There was substantial analysis of the procedural compliance as both sections 9(2) and 11 were addressed, affirming that appellant duly met the statutory requirements for eviction ([para 27]).

Furthermore, the court ruled that terminating the respondent's right of residence was justified based on substantial evidence of the respondent's non-compliance with the tenancy agreement and the appellant's need to house permanent employees on the farm.

**C – Conclusion**  
The SCA overturned the judgment of the Land Claims Court and concluded that Sam Phasiya was no longer resident on the premises, effectively denying any claim of occupation by the respondent ([paras 34]). The court ordered the respondent to vacate by January 31, 2010. Additionally, the court authorized the sheriff to assist in the eviction should the respondent refuse to comply, thereby reinstating the appellant's rights under the Act while underlining the importance of lawful eviction practices ([paras 34-34]). 

The decision underscores the critical balance between property rights and occupancy rights in the context of South African land reform law, reinforcing the necessity for proper adherence to eviction procedures as mandated by the Act.
