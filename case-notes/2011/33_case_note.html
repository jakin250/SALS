City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality v Blue Moonlight Properties 39 (Pty) Ltd and Others [2011] ZACC 33

In June 2005, Blue Moonlight issued eviction notices, initiating eviction proceedings under the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE) by May 2006.

Constitutional Court 2011 [2011] ZACC 33

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

This case arises from the unlawful occupation of a property located at Saratoga Avenue in Berea, Johannesburg, by 86 individuals, labeled "the Occupiers," including adults and children, many of whom have marginal incomes and limited employment. The property, previously owned by a company that allowed occupation, was bought by Blue Moonlight Properties 39 (Pty) Ltd in 2004 with the intent to redevelop it ([1]).

In June 2005, Blue Moonlight issued eviction notices, initiating eviction proceedings under the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE) by May 2006. The Occupiers opposed the eviction on the grounds that it would render them homeless and applied to join the City of Johannesburg, claiming the city's constitutional duties regarding housing ([6]–[11]).

The High Court granted the eviction, ordered the City to provide temporary accommodation or financial relief to the Occupiers, and found the City's housing policy unconstitutional for discriminating against those facing eviction from private property ([12]–[14]). The Supreme Court of Appeal set aside the High Court order but also declared the City’s housing policy unconstitutional ([13]).

I – Issues

The key legal questions revolve around:

  1. Whether the eviction of the Occupiers was just and equitable under the PIE, considering their socio-economic circumstances and the potential homelessness that would follow ([30]).
  2. The City’s obligations to provide accommodation for the Occupiers upon eviction, given their housing policy and capacity to fulfill such obligations ([42]).

R – Rules

The court's analysis is primarily guided by the following legal norms:

  1. Section 25(1) of the Constitution: Prohibits arbitrary deprivation of property.
  2. Section 26(1) and (3) of the Constitution: Guarantees the right to have access to adequate housing and articulates that no eviction can occur without a court order considering relevant circumstances ([18]).
  3. PIE: Particularly sections regarding just and equitable eviction, where courts must weigh the rights and needs of vulnerable groups against property rights ([30], [29]).

The case also references prior judgments, including Port Elizabeth Municipality v Various Occupiers [2004] ZACC 7, which highlight the balance that must be struck between property rights and the need for housing ([34]).

A – Application

The court analyzed several aspects to determine if eviction was just and equitable:

  • Social History: Examined the long-term occupation by the Occupiers and their socioeconomic backgrounds, noting that many have lived on the property for extended periods and that eviction could lead to homelessness ([39]).
  • City’s Housing Policy: The City claimed it was unable to provide accommodation due to financial constraints; however, the court scrutinized this claim against the City's fiscal reports and obligations outlined in Chapter 12 of the National Housing Code, which address emergency housing situations ([50], [68]).
  • Resources and Obligations: Despite the City’s assertions of limited resources, the court found no substantiated proof that the City was incapable of fulfilling its obligations. The City had, historically, operated with surplus finances and could have provided emergency housing if it had budgeted correctly ([69], [71]).

The differentiation within the City’s housing policy—allowing assistance for relocations by the City itself versus private evictions—was found unconstitutional, as it failed to provide equal protection for the vulnerable groups that it aimed to serve, particularly under section 9(1) of the Constitution ([84]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately upheld the Supreme Court of Appeal's declarations:

  1. The eviction of the Occupiers was deemed lawful, recognizing Blue Moonlight’s property rights, but the execution of the order was contingent on the City providing temporary accommodation.
  2. The City was mandated to ensure that accommodation was available by a specified date, ensuring that the Occupiers would not be rendered homeless ([99]–[101]).
  3. The court emphasized the need for balancing property rights and human dignity, holding that eviction cannot occur without a provision for adequate housing ([97]).

Thus, this case serves as a critical examination of how municipal policies must align with constitutional obligations while balancing property rights with the imperative to provide housing for vulnerable populations. The ruling affirms the principles of dignity and equality in the context of housing and eviction rights within South Africa's constitutional framework.
