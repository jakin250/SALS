Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: NK obo ZK v MEC for Health, Gauteng

**Neutral Citation**: NK obo ZK v MEC for Health, Gauteng (216/17) [2018] ZASCA 13 (15 March 2018)

### F – Facts
The appellant, NK, acted in her capacity as the parent and natural guardian of her minor son, ZK, who suffered severe brain damage during birth due to alleged medical negligence at the Chris Hani Baragwanath Hospital ([paras 2–4]). The Gauteng Provincial Government, responsible for the hospital's management, was the respondent in this matter ([para 3]). 

ZK, born on 25 May 2008, experienced a hypoxic-ischaemic incident that resulted in spastic cerebral palsy, quadriplegia, and various cognitive and physical impairments ([paras 2–3]). After establishing the merits of negligence in a prior ruling ([para 4]), the case proceeded to the assessment of quantum. The appellant sought damages under several heads: past and future medical expenses, future loss of earnings, and general damages. The high court awarded R200,000 for general damages and applied a 35% contingency deduction on the agreed R1,159,642 for loss of future earnings ([paras 8–10]). The appellant challenged both amounts, seeking R1,800,000 in general damages and a 20% contingency deduction ([para 1]).

### I – Issues
1. What is the appropriate amount to be awarded for general damages given the evidence of ZK's suffering?
2. What is the proper contingency deduction to apply to the loss of future earnings?

([paras 1–6])

### R – Rules
In determining the compensation for general damages, the court relied on established precedents in South African tort law, particularly in cases involving severe brain injury ([para 7]). The court noted that awards for general damages must be flexible and context-specific, with reference to past awards only serving as a guide for consistency and reliability in the application of the law ([para 9–13]). 

For contingency deductions, the established principle from *Bailey* underlined that such deductions must account for life's uncertainties, including potential periods of unemployment and health deterioration ([para 15]). The deduction’s evaluation should avoid arbitrary or median-based assessments ([para 16–19]).

### A – Application
#### General Damages
The court found the initial award inadequate, recognizing ZK’s “twilight” awareness state, which justified compensation for pain and suffering ([para 7]). The comparison with previous cases demonstrated a striking disparity between the high court's ruling and the amounts awarded in similar cases, leading the appellate court to determine that R1,800,000 was a suitable award ([paras 12–13]).

#### Contingency Deduction
Regarding the contingency deduction, the high court applied a 35% figure as a compromise. Still, the appellate court criticized this method as lacking a rational basis. The court leaned towards the appellant’s argument favoring 20%, indicating that there were no compelling reasons to deviate from this standard in ZK’s case ([paras 14–19]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, increasing the general damages to R1,800,000 and adjusting the contingency deduction for future loss of earnings to 20%. The total amount to be awarded was set at R19,048,291, reflecting a clear acknowledgment of ZK’s suffering and the need for equitable compensation ensuring no duplication with medical expense awards ([paras 19]). This decision emphasizes the importance of consistent, fair compensatory awards in medical negligence cases, reaffirming the court's commitment to addressing disparities in damages awarded in similar cases across the country ([paras 12–13]).

### Implications
This ruling not only rectified the prior court's inadequate compensation decision but also set a precedent for future cases involving significant injuries due to medical negligence, emphasizing a rigorous but fair assessment for damages that encapsulates both the economic and emotional ramifications of such injuries.
