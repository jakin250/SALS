# FIRAC Case Note: Sethosa v The State

**Case Title:** Sethosa v The State  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Judgment Date:** 12 December 2024  
**Neutral Citation:** Sethosa v The State (679/2023) [2024] ZASCA 172  
**Coram:** Nicholls, Molefe, Kgoele JJA and Koen and Dolamo AJJA  
**Type of Case:** Criminal Procedure – Appeal against conviction and sentence  

---

### F – Facts

The appellant, Mr. Phenyo Sethosa, was convicted of rape under s 3 of the Criminal Law (Sexual Offences and Related Matters) Amendment Act 32 of 2007 by the Regional Court in Johannesburg. As a result, he was sentenced to ten years' imprisonment, with the regional court determining that no substantial and compelling circumstances warranted a lesser sentence ([1]).

After his conviction, Sethosa sought leave to appeal both the conviction and the sentence, but this application was dismissed by the regional court. Subsequently, he submitted a petition under s 309C of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 to the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which also denied relief ([1]).

This matter was elevated to the Supreme Court of Appeal after special leave was granted against the dismissal of his petition by the High Court ([1]). During the proceedings, it emerged that the appeal record was incomplete, specifically missing transcriptions from several key court appearances, including testimonies and applications crucial to the appeal ([2]).

The Supreme Court, in its assessment, found that the importance of a complete record is integral to the right to a fair trial and the ability to conduct an informed appeal and subsequently ordered the reconstruction of the missing parts of the record ([5], [6]). 

### I – Issues

The main legal issue in this case is whether the Supreme Court should permit the appeal given the incompleteness of the record of the proceedings from the trial court, which could impact the appellant’s right to a fair trial and an equitable appeal ([2], [4]). Additional concerns included whether the High Court's prior decision disregarded necessary parts of the transcribed record and if the incomplete records compromised the right to an adequate appeal ([4], [8]).

### R – Rules

The legal principles relevant to this case include:

1. **Right to a Fair Trial:** This encompasses the right to appeal, which is supported by established jurisprudence affirming the necessity of an adequate record of trial proceedings for both the trial and appellate processes ([5]).
2. **Duties of the Appellant:** The appellant has the onus to ensure that a complete record of appeal is provided. This responsibility lies primarily with the appellant and their legal representative ([6]).
3. **Judicial Precedents:** The significance of a complete record was emphasized in cases such as *S v Schoombee and Another* and *S v Chabedi*, which articulate that the absence of a complete record undermines the integrity of the appeal process ([5], [6]).

### A – Application

In its application of the rules to the circumstances of this case, the Supreme Court noted that the absence of complete records impeded the court's ability to assess whether the High Court had considered all necessary evidence when evaluating the petition ([4], [7]). 

The court directed that reconstruction efforts be initiated promptly, specifically identifying missing transcripts from several critical dates (16 February 2021, 19 July 2021, 5 November 2021) in consultation with relevant stakeholders, including the regional magistrate ([9]). The Court emphasized the importance of complete documentation in evaluating the merits of both the conviction and sentence, contending it would be improper and against the interests of justice to base decisions on records not previously before the High Court ([7], [8]).

Moreover, the Supreme Court’s directives required the legal representatives to provide systematic affidavits explaining the reconstruction efforts and the measures taken to address the incomplete record ([9]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the appeal against the dismissal of the petition by the High Court should succeed to the extent of ordering the reconstruction of the missing portions of the record ([9]). The High Court's decision was set aside, imposing a responsibility on the appellant’s legal team to ensure that reconstruction was carried out effectively, with deadlines specified for finalization ([9]).

This ruling underlines the critical nature of a complete and accurate record in the justice system, reinforcing the principle that the right to a fair trial—a foundational tenet of criminal jurisprudence—requires that all parties have access to the complete factual backdrop regarding both convictions and associated penalties. The case thus clarifies the procedural duties of appellants and affirms the court's commitment to maintaining integrity in judicial proceedings. 

The implications of this decision are significant, establishing clear guidelines for the reconstruction of court records and emphasizing the necessity of these records for the integrity of the legal process, particularly in appeals concerning serious convictions like rape ([9]).

This judgment serves as a pivotal reference point for future cases concerning the substantive requirements of appeal processes in South Africa, encapsulating essential doctrines of fairness and judicial accountability.
