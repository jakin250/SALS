# Case Note: FIRAC Method for Brossy v Brossy (602/11) [2012] ZASCA 151

**F - Facts**  
The appeal in *Brossy v Brossy* ([1]) arises from a protracted dispute over the maintenance obligations of Mr. Martin Brossy (the Appellant) to his ex-wife, Ms. Deborah Brossy (the Respondent), regarding their two children following their divorce in February 1998. Initially settled via a consent order for R1,500 per child per month, the maintenance payments were modified in 2000 to R2,000 for one child and R1,800 for another, along with obligations for educational costs ([3], [4]).

From around 2005, the Respondent’s repeated demands for increased maintenance culminated in complaints filed under section 6(1)(b) of the Maintenance Act 99 of 1998, with the most recent being a request for R25,000 per child per month ([2], [6]). The accompanying financial burden included arrears exceeding R2 million ([6]). The parties encountered notable disputes on school fees and extra-mural activities, particularly relating to the costs of their children’s attendance at Reddam School, which were disproportionately borne by Ms. Brossy ([7], [8]).

During the maintenance enquiry, serious procedural issues arose, notably a defective record due to malfunctioning recording equipment and incomplete documentation ([9], [10]). The High Court, after noting irregular conduct by the magistrate, referred the matter back to a new magistrate for further proceedings ([12]). No decision was made on the merits due to the incomplete record; thus, Ms. Brossy was denied a substantive ruling on her appeal pertaining to maintenance ([10]). 

**I - Issues**  
The primary legal issues for consideration included:  
1. Whether the High Court erred in not striking the appeal from the roll due to a defective record and instead opting to postpone the matter.
2. The legal implications concerning the rights of minor children in maintenance disputes, particularly regarding their representation and views being considered.
3. The overall fairness of the decision-making process concerning maintenance obligations as it affects children ([10], [12], [17]).

**R - Rules**  
The predominant statutory rule applicable to this case is Section 6(1)(b) of the Maintenance Act 99 of 1998, which regulates disputes concerning maintenance adjustments ([1]). The High Court had the authority to control its own proceedings, particularly under the principle that a complete record is essential for just consideration. In addition, constitutional provisions—such as section 28(2) of the Constitution granting children the right to legal representation in civil matters affecting them ([17])—played a critical role, along with international obligations under the United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC) ([17]).

Additionally, existing judicial precedents outline the duty of the magistrate to conduct proceedings impartially to ensure justice is perceived to be done ([13]). The obligation of a party in appellate procedures to present a complete record is also well established, aligning with the principles of procedural fairness ([10]).

**A - Application**  
In reviewing the High Court's decision ([12]), the Supreme Court of Appeal noted significant procedural missteps. The High Court's decision to not strike the appeal off the roll preserved the children's interests but inadvertently delayed substantive justice for them ([12], [14]). The Supreme Court underscored that the magistrate’s alleged bias, though serious, did not preclude the merits of the case as no complaints were raised at the inquiry level ([13]).

The court reasoned that the incomplete record could be rectified and reiterated a procedure that would allow for a full, fair hearing on the merits while safeguarding the interests of both children as well as the parties involved([14], [15], [16]). The need to balance the protection of children’s accrued rights in maintenance matters with the efficient resolution of disputes was emphasized. The possibility for Ms. Brossy to bring new applications for maintenance was also acknowledged in light of changing circumstances ([15]).

Furthermore, the judgment reinforced the principle that voices of children should be considered. This principle aligns with constitutional guarantees and international standards that affirm children’s rights to be heard in legal proceedings that affect them ([18], [19]). 

**C - Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court concluded that the appeal should succeed and set aside the High Court’s order, opting instead to postpone the appeal so that the record could be reconstructed, allowing for proper adjudication on the merits while reserving costs ([24]). The decision affirmed the importance of ensuring the rights of children in maintenance disputes are upheld and advocated for fair procedures to be followed in family law matters.

The judgment highlights the delicate nature of maintenance disputes and the necessity of both upholding legal standards while ensuring children's welfare is placed at the forefront. Further, it emphasizes the importance of the courts to remain vigilant against procedural deficiencies that can undermine the integrity of the judicial process, particularly in sensitive family and children’s matters.
