# Case Note: Transport and Allied Workers Union of South Africa v PUTCO Limited

**Court**: Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Case Number**: CCT 94/15  
**Judgment Date**: 8 March 2016  
**Judges**: Khampepe J (Moseneke DCJ, Cameron J, Jafta J, Khampepe J, Madlanga J, Nkabinde J, Van der Westhuizen J, Nugent AJ, and Zondo J concurring)  
**Neutral Citation**: [2016] ZACC 7

## FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts

The applicant, the Transport and Allied Workers Union of South Africa (TAWUSA), is a trade union representing 26% of the employees at the respondent, PUTCO Limited, a bus operating company. On 30 July 2002, TAWUSA and PUTCO entered into a recognition agreement, affirming the Bargaining Council's jurisdiction over wages and employment conditions in the bus industry. A collective agreement was made in April 2012, which TAWUSA did not sign. On 19 June 2012, this agreement was extended to all employees in the industry by the Minister of Labour.

TAWUSA's membership with the Bargaining Council was terminated in August 2012, and by February 2013, TAWUSA sought reinstatement. During wage negotiations in April 2013, TAWUSA informed PUTCO that its members would not participate in a strike called by other unions representing employees in the Bargaining Council. However, PUTCO issued a lock-out notice on 19 April 2013 against all employees in response to the strike notice, including TAWUSA’s members, despite TAWUSA not being a part of the Bargaining Council for the dispute.

TAWUSA sought an interdict from the Labour Court to prevent the lock-out, arguing that it could not be locked out without being part of the dispute. The Labour Court initially ruled in favor of TAWUSA, stating there could be no lawful lock-out without a demand directed to TAWUSA. PUTCO appealed this decision to the Labour Appeal Court, which held that TAWUSA was a party to the dispute due to its interest in the outcome. This led to the current appeal before the Constitutional Court.

### I – Issues

The key legal question in this case is whether the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA) allows an employer to lock out members of a trade union that is not a party to a bargaining council where a dispute arises and is subsequently referred for conciliation ([paras 1–2]).

### R – Rules

The relevant statutory provisions from the LRA include:

1. **Section 213**: Defines a lock-out as the exclusion of employees from the workplace to compel them to accept a demand regarding any mutual interests between the employer and employee.
2. **Section 64(1)**: Specifies circumstances under which an employer may lock out employees, which requires an issue in dispute to have been referred to a council or the Commission. Specifically, section 64(1)(c) stipulates that notice of a lock-out must be given to trade unions that are parties to the dispute ([paras 31–45]).

The interpretation of these provisions is crucial, especially concerning what constitutes a "party to the dispute" and whether secondary lock-outs against non-party unions are permissible.

### A – Application

The Court applied the statutory definitions to the facts of the case. It highlighted that a lock-out requires a substantive demand directed at the employees to be locked out. The Court reasoned that since TAWUSA was not a party to the Bargaining Council, no demand had been made to its members. Thus, the lock-out notice issued by PUTCO was ineffective in law.

Furthermore, the Court rejected the Labour Appeal Court's assertion that TAWUSA was implicitly a party to the dispute because of its interest in the wage negotiations. It underscored the necessity for there to be a concrete dispute involving the employees who are to be locked out, finding that TAWUSA's participation through interest or hope did not suffice.

The judgment emphasized that the lock-out of TAWUSA members could not lawfully occur unless PUTCO had first made a legitimate demand pertaining to a matter of mutual interest, which had not occurred in this case ([paras 34–40]). The Court concluded that a lock-out mechanism is inherently linked to the rights and duties of parties engaged in the negotiation process; therefore, TAWUSA could not be locked out merely because it was situated in the broader industry context ([paras 41–72]).

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court granted TAWUSA's appeal, overturning the Labour Appeal Court’s decision, which had upheld PUTCO’s right to execute a lock-out against TAWUSA members. The Court ruled that the lock-out was unlawful due to the absence of a prerequisite demand directed to TAWUSA and confirmed that TAWUSA was not a party to the dispute as defined under the LRA.

The Court's ruling highlights the importance of adhering to statutory protocols within the framework of collective bargaining and reinforces the notion that industrial actions, such as lock-outs, must have a clear foundation in mutual agreements and disputes. This decision accentuates the need for orderly collective bargaining and provides clarity on the rights of employers and unions under the LRA. The case may have significant implications for future industrial relations, specifically regarding the representation and rights of minority unions in collective bargaining processes ([paras 73–75]).
