Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF A
Source Folder: 1998

# FIRAC Case Note: Abertech Industries (Pty) Ltd v National Union of Metalworkers of South Africa and Others

## F – Facts
On 22 June 1990, Abertech Industries (the appellant) dismissed workers who participated in an illegal strike against their management. Following the dismissal, the National Union of Metalworkers of South Africa (NUMSA) and affected workers filed a complaint, leading the Industrial Court to rule the dismissals constituted an unfair labor practice under the Labour Relations Act 28 of 1956 (the Act). The appellant's appeal to the Labour Appeal Court (LAC) regarding this finding was also unsuccessful ([paras 2–3]).

The factual background presented included a strained relationship between the management and the newly elected shop stewards, particularly focusing on an incident involving Mr. Tsiliso Mokoena, a shop steward. After a series of disciplinary inquiries where Mokoena failed to attend, he faced dismissal on grounds of insubordination ([paras 4–5]). 

Subsequent to Mokoena’s dismissal, workers protested by stopping work, which escalated into the events leading to the dismissal of the permanent day shift employees. The appellant issued an ultimatum demanding the workers return to their jobs by a specific time, failing which they would face dismissal. The workers complied with the ultimatum initially, but tensions rose again due to further disciplinary measures taken against the shop stewards, leading to additional work stoppages and ultimately to dismissals by management due to continued refusal to work ([paras 6–14]).

The Industrial Court upheld that dismissals were unjust because management failed to effectively communicate with workers and involve their union in resolving the dispute ([paras 12–24]). Their decision was based on the perception that the management's disciplinary actions appeared to be harassment, creating a working environment characterized by hostility towards employee representatives ([para 15]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issue revolved around whether the dismissals of the striking workers constituted an unfair labor practice as defined within the framework of the Labour Relations Act, particularly considering the context of illegal strikes ([para 3]).

Further, the court had to assess managerial conduct leading up to the dismissals and determine if the failure to allow reasonable time for employees to confer with their union played a role in the assessment of the unfairness of the dismissals ([paras 20–24]).

## R – Rules
The judgment heavily relied on precedents established in labor law that govern the permissible responses by employers to work stoppages by employees. The Labour Relations Act, particularly section 17C(1)(a), stipulates that appeals against findings must respect facts established by the LAC ([paras 3–4]). Case law from *Vetsak Co-operative Ltd* and *Performing Arts Council* served as key references in assessing whether dismissals were justified, even in the context of illegal strikes ([paras 15–18]).

Importantly, the court emphasized that fairness in labor relations requires a balance of interests between employer and employee, and that good labor relations are achieved through effective communication and dispute resolution practices ([para 16]).

## A – Application
The court applied the principles of fairness and the expectations of reasonable conduct from both the employer and the employees. It acknowledged the mixed responses from workers and noted that while the strike on 22 June was illegal, this alone did not justify immediate dismissal. The dismissal needed to consider the conduct of the appellant in provoking the conflict, as well as their failure to allow sufficient time for consultation with the union ([paras 17–24]).

Specifically, the court found that the workers' perception of harassment aggravated their response; however, management’s reaction to this perception lacked critical engagement that was essential to resolving the disputes. Even though the workers were noted to have engaged previously in illegal strikes, the court could not ignore the circumstances that provoked the stoppage this time, which were linked to the disciplinary actions taken against their representatives ([para 19]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court ultimately upheld the findings of the LAC and the Industrial Court, concluding that the dismissals constituted an unfair labor practice. The ratio decidendi emphasized that the employer's failure to engage effectively with the employees and their union in the face of protest significantly contributed to the ruling ([paras 25]). 

This case underscores the necessity for employers to maintain open lines of communication and to appreciate the complexities of labor relations, especially when managing conflict. The court’s decision was accompanied by costs awarded against the appellant, reinforcing the importance of adherence to fair practices in employment disputes ([para 25]). 

In summary, Abertech Industries (Pty) Ltd v National Union of Metalworkers of South Africa serves as a pivotal case illustrating the implications of labor relations law in protecting employee rights, particularly concerning employer conduct amidst disputes of industrial action.
