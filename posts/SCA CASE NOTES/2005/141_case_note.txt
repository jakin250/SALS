Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2005

### FIRAC Case Note: Thugwana v Road Accident Fund

**F – Facts**  
The case of *Thugwana v Road Accident Fund* (SCA 530/04) arose from a claim for compensation following injuries sustained by the appellant, Samuel Feli Thugwana, in a motor vehicle accident. This claim was made under section 17(1)(b) of the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 ([1]). The Road Accident Fund (the Fund) responded with a special plea indicating that Thugwana failed to comply with the procedural requirements outlined in section 17(1)(b) and regulation 2(1)(c) of the Act ([2]). 

In his defense, Thugwana contended he had adhered to the requirements or, alternatively, had "substantially complied" with them; he also argued that the regulation was ultra vires the Act. Initially, the Pretoria High Court (De Vos J) dismissed the Fund's special plea but granted the Fund permission to appeal ([3]). The Supreme Court of Appeal later upheld the appeal and set aside the initial ruling ([4]).

The Fund's argument rested on the claim that the failure of the Fund to object to Thugwana's claim within 60 days—per section 24(5)—did not automatically render his claim valid, as non-compliance with regulation 2(1)(c) was seen as fatal ([5]-[8]). 

**I – Issues**  
The primary legal issues presented before the court were: 

1. Whether failure by the Fund to object to the validity of Thugwana's claim within the stipulated 60-day period under section 24(5) rendered his claim valid in all respects ([6]–[7]).
2. The interpretation of the relationship between section 24(5) and regulation 2(1)(c), particularly whether regulation 2(1)(c) imposes a substantive requirement that must be fulfilled for the Fund to be liable for damages ([8]).

**R – Rules**  
The relevant legal frameworks were section 17(1)(b) and section 24(5) of the Road Accident Fund Act, as well as regulation 2(1)(c). Section 17(1)(b) mandates the Fund to compensate for losses in cases of unidentified drivers or vehicles, conditional on the compliance with the regulations ([1]). 

Section 24(5) states that if the Fund does not object to the validity of a claim within 60 days of its submission, the claim shall be deemed valid in law in all respects ([4]). Meanwhile, regulation 2(1)(c) outlines specific procedural obligations, including the requirement for the claimant to submit an affidavit to the police detailing particulars of the accident within 14 days ([2]).  

**A – Application**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal found the appellant's argument insufficient. Mlambo JA clarified that the failure to meet the requirement in regulation 2(1)(c) was substantive and non-compliance therewith rendered the claim invalid ([8]). The court emphasized that section 24(5) served to afford the Fund adequate time to scrutinize claims prior to litigation, but it did not confer validity upon claims that had not been properly established in accordance with the substantive provisions of the regulations ([8]). 

The judges concurred with Krischke v Road Accident Fund, which reiterated that procedural sections such as 24(5) should not be construed to empower claims that failed to meet substantive legal requirements. Moreover, the rationale was that the procedural provisions merely allowed a window for examining claims, without altering the foundation of substantive entitlements outlined in the regulations ([6]–[9]).

**C – Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Thugwana's appeal, reaffirming that the claim was invalid due to failure to meet the substantive requirement outlined in regulation 2(1)(c). This holding underscores the necessity of compliance with both procedural and substantive legal standards within the claim-making processes under the Road Accident Fund Act ([9]). The implications of this case are critical for future claimants, highlighting the importance of adhering strictly to the prescribed statutory and regulatory frameworks to ensure the validity of compensation claims against the Fund.
