Lazarus Mbethe v United Manganese of Kalahari (503/2016) [2017] ZASCA 67

The case of Lazarus Mbethe v United Manganese of Kalahari (Pty) Ltd involved the appellant, Mr.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 67

F – Facts

The case of Lazarus Mbethe v United Manganese of Kalahari (Pty) Ltd involved the appellant, Mr. Lazarus Mbethe, who served as the chairperson and director of the respondent, a prominent manganese ore producer ([1]). In 2012, the appellant introduced the board to Zastrospace (Pty) Ltd, a mobile crushing and screening contractor, to help meet rising manganese ore demand ([2]). Zastrospace was promoted as beneficial to the local community, and the contract, profitable for the respondent, was established.

However, by 2014, the demand for manganese drastically decreased, prompting the respondent’s board to terminate its relationship with Zastrospace on 19 November 2014 ([3]). The appellant contested this termination, citing management issues, and sought leave from the Gauteng Local Division of the High Court to institute legal proceedings on behalf of the respondent under s 165(5) of the Companies Act 71 of 2008 (the Act) ([4]). The application was dismissed by Wentzel AJ, leading to the appeal that is the subject of this judgment ([4]).

In his written demands to the respondent, the appellant raised multiple allegations, mostly revolving around the governance and management structures, including assertions that the respondent's shareholders’ agreement contravened the Act and demanded various interdicts against company executives ([5]). The core of the appellant's claims involved a request for reinstatement of the lucrative contract with Zastrospace, alleged to benefit the local community and satisfy regulatory requirements ([27]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues revolved around the interpretation and application of section 165(5)(b) of the Companies Act. Specifically, the disputes addressed whether the appellant acted in good faith as required by the statute ([8]), whether there was a serious question of material consequence to the company, and if it was in the best interests of the company to grant the appellant leave to commence the proposed proceedings ([7]).

Central to the legal debate was the nature of the onus required to demonstrate good faith. Differing interpretations arose, particularly regarding whether the absence of a collateral purpose needed to be proven as part of the good faith requirement, and what constituted reasonable grounds for such a belief ([10], [12]).

R – Rules

The relevant statutory framework includes section 165 of the Companies Act 71 of 2008, which specifically sets forth the conditions under which a derivative action can be instituted by shareholders or directors ([6]). Section 165(5)(b) states that the court may grant leave only if the following conditions are satisfied:

  1. The applicant is acting in good faith;
  2. The proposed or continuing proceedings involve the trial of a serious question of material consequence to the company;
  3. It is in the best interests of the company that the proceedings be commenced or continued ([7]).

The concept of good faith is explored in past judgments, notably in Swansson v Pratt and Mouritzen v Greystones Enterprises. The appellant's burden is primarily to demonstrate a sincere purpose and not ulterior motives, a point which was contentious in this case ([9], [12]).

A – Application

In analyzing the appellant’s case, the Court first established that the appellant bore the onus of proving his good faith on a balance of probabilities ([8]). This requirement was assessed through both subjective and objective lenses, meaning the appellant's stated intentions were scrutinized against the backdrop of the evidence presented ([20]).

The Court highlighted several critical points regarding the appellant's claims:

  • Zastrospace's Termination: Evidence indicated that the need for Zastrospace diminished with market conditions, rendering the contract's retention impractical and financially irresponsible ([30a]). The Court found that claims about the community benefits were overstated and failed to demonstrate reasonable grounds for seeking reinstatement of the contract ([31]).
  • Motivation and Collateral Purpose: The Court noted the absence of credible evidence supporting the appellant's assertion of acting in good faith. Instead, the motives appeared self-serving, focused on the retention of the Zastrospace contract out of personal interest, diminishing the communal argument ([31]).
  • Best Interests of the Company: The Court concluded that it was not in the best interests of the respondent to pursue the proposed actions, considering alternative routes for achieving similar relief were available under sections 20(4) and 163 of the Act ([20], [33]).

The cumulative effect of these analyses led the Court to conclude that the appellant not only failed in proving a serious question of material consequence but also in substantiating a genuine good faith claim ([31]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal with costs, emphasizing that the appellant failed to satisfy the onus of proof required under section 165(5)(b) of the Companies Act ([40]). The court found no legitimate claim of good faith in the appellant’s actions, marking a vital distinction in the application of legislative intent regarding derivative actions. The judgment underscored the necessity of aligning actions with corporate governance principles, reinforcing that actions must genuinely protect the interests of the company rather than serve individual motives ([40]).

This case illustrates the critical nature of maintaining corporate integrity and accountability while navigating claims made under statutory derivative actions. The decision serves as a reminder of the judiciary's role in ensuring that corporate governance remains aligned with both legal standards and ethical considerations.
