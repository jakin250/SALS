S[…] NO v Maitin (311/13) [2014] ZASCA 156

In the case of S[…] NO v Maitin, the appellant, Mrs N[…] S[…] S[…] (hereafter referred to as "Mrs S[…]"), appealed against a decision by the KwaZulu-Natal High Court which had dismissed her claim for damages against Dr D P Maitin, an obstetrician.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2014 [2014] ZASCA 156

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–19])

In the case of S[…] NO v Maitin, the appellant, Mrs N[…] S[…] S[…] (hereafter referred to as "Mrs S[…]"), appealed against a decision by the KwaZulu-Natal High Court which had dismissed her claim for damages against Dr D P Maitin, an obstetrician. The claim arose from the delivery of her daughter, Y[…], who sustained serious injuries resulting in Erb’s palsy due to alleged medical negligence during childbirth.

Mrs S[…] alleged that Dr Maitin was negligent in several respects during the delivery, notably: misestimating the baby's weight (which he estimated at 4kg, while the actual weight was 4.68kg), failing to perform a Caesarean section in light of the baby's size and complications of shoulder dystocia, and inadequately executing the McRoberts' manoeuvre to alleviate shoulder dystocia. The delivery was characterized by significant complications, including challenges stemming from the baby's macrosomic condition and shoulder dystocia that ultimately resulted in a brachial plexus injury.

The initial high court ruling, presided over by Penzhorn AJ, held the absence of negligence on the part of Dr Maitin, leading to Mrs S[…] seeking redress in the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA). Notably, Mrs S[…] had also previously withdrawn her claim against the second defendant, St Augustine’s Hospital. The SCA was presented with evidence reflecting the medical practices and standards of care relevant to the case, alongside the particulars of the delivery process which included timelines and medical interventions undertaken.

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 2–22])

This case concentrated on two primary legal issues:

  1. Negligence: Did Dr Maitin breach his duty of care to Mrs S[…] and her child by failing to meet the expected medical standards during the delivery process? This encompasses specific allegations regarding the estimation of weight, failure to perform a Caesarean section, and execution of the McRoberts' manoeuvre.
  2. Informed consent: Should the common law be developed to require that medical practitioners disclose all potential risks associated with medical procedures, particularly those which a reasonable patient in similar circumstances might deem significant enough to affect their decision-making?

The court was tasked with determining whether the actions taken by Dr Maitin during the delivery constituted negligence and whether the legal principles surrounding informed consent warranted a modification in the common law relating to medical treatment.

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 45–52])

The SCA's judgment was framed within established legal principles surrounding medical negligence and informed consent. Key legal authorities referenced included:

  1. Aquilian Action: Liability for delict in South African law requires proof of negligence resulting in harm. Established in Van Wyk v Lewis 1924 AD 438, the plaintiff bears the onus of proving negligence by demonstrating that a reasonable practitioner in similar circumstances would have acted differently.
  2. Informed Consent Doctrine: The standard for determining whether a physician has adequately informed a patient of risks reflects established precedents such as in Castell v De Greef 1994 (4) SA 408 (C), which interrogated whether a doctor's actions align with the expectations of a reasonable person in the patient's position given the circumstances.
  3. Authority on Medical Practice: The ease of variability in estimating fetal weight and the recommendations of expert medical bodies (such as the Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynaecologists) underscored the context for evaluating medical negligence and informed consent regarding potential delivery risks.

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 23–52])

The court engaged in a detailed analysis of the evidence presented around both the alleged negligent acts and the broader implications for medical consent protocols.

Negligence: The SCA found that Dr Maitin had not acted below the standard of care expected from a reasonable obstetrician. Testimonies from multiple medical experts confirmed that weight estimation, particularly in the context of a macrosomic baby, is an imprecise measure and subject to significant variations. The court underscored that Dr Maitin reasonably estimated the weight within a generally accepted margin of error and correctly followed established medical practices in labor management.

Furthermore, the court emphasized that none of the indicators raised during labor necessarily warranted an urgent Caesarean section according to the perspectives of different expert witnesses. Crucially, by the time Dr Maitin arrived for the delivery, the head of the baby was in the final stages of descent, rendering any C-section impractical and potentially harmful.

Informed Consent: In addressing the proposed development of the common law surrounding informed consent, the court assessed whether Mrs S[…] had established a case for liability based on the potential failure to disclose risks. The court ruled against the necessity of modifying the current standards, as no negligent conduct had been established. The court contended that the informed consent requirement should only be assessed when there is an underlying finding of negligence in conduct.

The need for Mrs S[…] to provide evidence about what a reasonable patient would have done if informed of the risks was paramount. Mrs S[…] herself stated a principal reliance on the expert judgement of Dr Maitin in managing her care, thereby undermining her claims regarding the significance of the alleged risks.

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 53])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Mrs S[…]’s appeal, upholding the findings of the KwaZulu-Natal High Court. The ratio decidendi emphasized that Mrs S[…] did not establish that Dr Maitin’s conduct constituted negligence or that the breach of a duty owed led to the harm suffered. Consequently, as there was no proven negligence, the inquiry into informed consent failed to arise.

The ruling carries significant implications for future medical negligence claims within South Africa, particularly regarding the standards of care expected from practitioners during childbirth and reinforces the necessity for clear evidence when challenging medical decisions. Furthermore, the decision highlights the delicate balance between patient autonomy, medical judgement, and the complexities of clinical practice in obstetrics.

In summary, the judgment clarified the prevailing legal framework for medical negligence and the conditions under which informed consent must be evaluated, thereby providing guidance for both practitioners and patients in future encounters within the healthcare system.
