Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: Emfuleni Resorts v Mazizini Community

**Case Citation:** Emfuleni Resorts v Mazizini Community (655/2010) [2011] ZASCA 139 (23 September 2011)  

---

## F – Facts

The appeal originated from a ruling made by the Land Claims Court, which awarded the Mazizini Community the restoration of land encompassing the Fish River Sun Hotel Complex based on Section 35(1) of the Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994 (hereinafter "the Act") ([1]). This judgment allowed the Mazizini Community to obtain ownership of the land and instructed the third respondent, the Regional Land Claims Commissioner (hereinafter "the RLCC"), to include the community as a beneficiary of a State support programme for development, with no order as to costs.

The first and second appellants, the current landowners, appealed the judgment, with their argument focused solely on the appropriateness of the Land Claims Court's order ([2]). Meanwhile, an unrelated community, the Prudhoe Community, which had not been part of the initial proceedings, sought rescission of the Land Claims Court’s order, asserting that their competing claim required consideration ([3]). 

Prior to the hearing, the RLCC requested a postponement of the appeal to assess the Prudhoe Community's claim further, and the validity of their claim was brought into question ([4–6]). This request was contested by both the appellants and the Prudhoe Community, asserting that the postponement lacked a valid basis ([8]). 

The court acknowledged that the RLCC failed to take necessary steps to assist the Prudhoe Community with their claim and indicated that a claim could not be deemed invalid due to procedural deficiencies when the RLCC had a duty to assist claimants ([7]). 

Ultimately, the court was confronted with dual applications: the RLCC's request for postponement of the appeal and the Prudhoe Community's application for rescission. The third respondent had failed to disclose the Prudhoe Community's competing claim during the initial hearings, impacting the legality of the Land Claims Court’s order ([12–15]).  

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues in this case can be summarised as follows:

1. **Did the RLCC demonstrate sufficient grounds for the postponement of the appeal?** ([9])
2. **Is the Prudhoe Community entitled to rescind the previous judgment based on the existence of a competing land claim?** ([12, 13])

## R – Rules

The applicable rules stem from the Restitution of Land Rights Act, particularly Section 35(1), which allows for land restoration claims, and Section 6(1)(b), mandating that the RLCC shall assist claimants in lodging their claims ([7]). 

Additionally, the principles governing the granting of a postponement were discussed, particularly referencing precedent from the Constitutional Court in *National Police Service Union v Minister of Safety and Security* ([9]) and *McCarthy Retail Ltd v Shortdistance Carriers CC* ([10]). In essence, it is established that the applicant for postponement is required to present a "good and strong reason" for such relief.

Furthermore, Section 35(11) of the Act allows for the rescission of judgments or orders if parties have been adversely affected, which includes non-disclosure of competing claims ([14]).

## A – Application

The court approached the application for postponement with a critical lens, stating that the RLCC had not satisfactorily justified its request nor fulfilled the statutory obligations to assist the Prudhoe Community. The RLCC’s failure to act and facilitate the community’s claim over many years was deemed a dereliction of duty, which could not be used later as an excuse to dismiss the Prudhoe Community's claim ([7–9]). 

In deciding against the postponement, the court emphasised the public interest in avoiding further delays in litigation and the need for expedient resolution, especially given the decade-long duration of the existing claims. Indications were given that allowing further postponement would primarily serve to prolong litigation rather than serve justice ([9, 11]).

As for the Prudhoe Community's rescission request, the court acknowledged that it was not tasked with determining the validity of their claim at this juncture; rather, it noted that the community had a legitimate interest in the land and should be allowed to assert this in court amicably with the Mazizini Community. The discussion underscored that the RLCC's actions (or inactions) had deprived the Prudhoe Community of their right to be heard ([12–15]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the RLCC's application for postponement and upheld the Prudhoe Community's request for rescission of the Land Claims Court's prior judgment, reiterating the importance of addressing competing land claims during restitution processes ([18]). Consequently, the appeal was struck from the roll, placing the burden of costs on the RLCC, which was deemed responsible for the complications arising from its administrative failures.

This case highlights the critical nature of proper administrative conduct by public bodies, particularly the RLCC, in upholding the rights of claimants under the Restitution of Land Rights Act, as well as the law's capacity to rectify oversights that may affect the outcome of prolonged legal disputes over land restitution in South Africa ([16–18]).
