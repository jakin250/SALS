Supreme Court of Appeal

Werner v Florauna Kwekery BK (224/14) [2015] ZASCA 46

On 10 February 2010, the first respondent, Florauna Kwekery BK, initiated an application in the North Gauteng High Court against the appellant and other respondents, seeking to affirm the existence of a servitude right of way over Portion 374 ([para 2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 [2015] ZASCA 46

F – Facts

This case arises from an appeal concerning the issue of whether a servitude of right of way exists over a fixed property owned by the appellant, Willem Gerhardus Werner, who is the guardian of his two minor children. The property in question is Portion 374 of the farm Hartebeesthoek, which was allegedly involved in a prior agreement regarding access to a provincial road ([paras 1-2]).

On 10 February 2010, the first respondent, Florauna Kwekery BK, initiated an application in the North Gauteng High Court against the appellant and other respondents, seeking to affirm the existence of a servitude right of way over Portion 374 ([para 2]). This application was built upon a historical context in which both the appellant and the fourth respondent, Johannes Jacobus Horn, had entered into a development agreement during the mid-1990s, which required provisions for an access road to be built from the development area to the provincial road P106-1 ([paras 3-4]).

The servitude area, although indicated on a registered subdivisional diagram, was not recorded in the Deeds Office at the time of the appellant’s acquisition of the property in 2005 ([paras 5-6]). Subsequently, a dispute arose between Louw senior, as a representative of the first respondent, and the appellant over the access road, specifically concerning the appellant's intention to deny public access to the road ([para 5]).

The lower court dismissed the appellant's counter-application and confirmed the existence of the servitude. The appellant subsequently appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal, contesting both the servitude's existence and the conditions underpinning the development ([paras 6-8]).

I – Issues

The primary issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal were twofold:

  1. Existence of Servitude: Is there a legal foundation for the registration of a public right of way servitude over Portion 374, despite the absence of a notarial deed creating such a servitude ([para 1])?
  2. Defence of Prescription: If a servitude does exist, is the appellant entitled to claim a prescription defence, thereby arguing that the application for the servitude is time-barred ([para 1])?

R – Rules

The statutory framework and case law governing servitudes in South Africa are primarily encapsulated in the Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937 and related provisions. According to established legal principles, servitudes can arise through various means such as explicit grants, long-standing usage (vetustas), or necessity ([para 15]).

In this case, the court reiterated the principle that the burden of proof lies on the party asserting the existence of the servitude. The definitions provided under Ethekwini Municipality v Brooks ([2010] ZASCA 39) and the conditions under which servitudes may arise illustrated the nuances of servitude claims and the necessity for proper registration ([para 21]).

A – Application

In its analysis, the court examined both the historical context of the access road and the legal basis for asserting a servitude. Notably, it acknowledged that the servitude as depicted in the subdivisional diagram could not be interpreted as a binding obligation merely because it existed on paper ([para 21]).

The arguments regarding the cancellation of the servitude note were discussed, particularly concerning whether such cancellation was lawful. The court noted that the failure to register the servitude at the time of transfer to the appellant and the subsequent caveat raised implications about the rights of the current landowner ([paras 22-24]).

Ultimately, the court found that the necessity for the servitude was tied to prior conditions imposed upon Horn's business rights, which had lapsed due to his failure to execute the necessary developments ([para 21]). Thus, the purported servitude did not extend to serve the public or adjacent property owners, as it was contingent on conditions that were not fulfilled.

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, concluding that no valid servitude of right of way existed over Portion 374 that could benefit the public or adjacent landowners ([para 25]). The previous order from the lower court was set aside, and the first respondent was ordered to bear the costs of the appeal. This judgment reinforces the principle that for a servitude to exist, there must be clear legal foundations and proper registration, emphasizing property rights underpinned by compliance with statutory conditions ([para 25]).

The ruling has substantial implications for property law, particularly concerning the registration of rights and the enforceability of oral or informal agreements as they pertain to land use and access, ensuring that property owners maintain their rights within the framework of established law.
