Supreme Court of Appeal

Puma AG Rudolf Dassler Sport v Global Warming (Pty) Ltd

Case Citation: Puma AG Rudolf Dassler Sport v Global Warming (Pty) Ltd (408/08) [2009] ZASCA 89 (11 September 2009)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009 [2009] ZASCA 89 (11 September 2009) ## F - Facts The appellan

Case Citation: Puma AG Rudolf Dassler Sport v Global Warming (Pty) Ltd (408/08) [2009] ZASCA 89 (11 September 2009)

F - Facts

The appellant, Puma AG Rudolf Dassler Sport, initiated a trademark infringement action against the respondent, Global Warming (Pty) Ltd. The dispute revolved around two trademarks registered by Puma in Class 25, which pertain to footwear and clothing. The first mark (TM 1980/05551) is characterized by a tapering stripe with dotted lines described as resembling stitching, alongside the 'PUMA' name. The second mark (TM 1982/04607) features a similar stripe but differs in color and does not incorporate the PUMA name ([1]–[3]).

Global Warming manufactures shoes to order and marketed a product bearing what Puma alleged to be an infringing mark. The shoes feature "DT NEW YORK" prominently displayed, with a stripe running from the heel that tapers similarly to Puma's marks but includes significant design differences ([4]). The High Court dismissed Puma's claim, leading to this appeal ([1]).

I - Issues

The primary legal issues presented in this case included:

  1. Infringement determination: Whether Global Warming's use of its mark constituted infringing use under §34(1)(a) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, by being identical or so similar as to likely deceive or cause confusion ([2]–[8]).
  2. Assessment of trademark significance: Whether the stripe on Global's shoe served as a source identifier or merely as an ornamentation ([6]).
  3. Reliance on subjective intentions: Whether Puma could rely on the alleged subjective intentions of Global Warming in adopting its mark in arguing for infringement ([11]).
  4. Damages assessment: Whether Puma was entitled to claim damages in the form of notional royalties without establishing actual loss ([12]).

R - Rules

The relevant legal provisions and precedents include:

  1. Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, §34(1)(a): Defines trademark infringement based on unauthorized use of registered marks that are identical or confusingly similar ([2]).
  2. Cowbell AG v ICS Holdings Ltd: Establishes that the assessment of likelihood of confusion involves a value judgment that requires a global appreciation of overall impressions ([8]).
  3. Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd: Discusses the factors necessary for determining the probability of confusion ([8]).
  4. Cincinatti Car Co v New York Rapid Transport Corp: Identifies the reasonable royalty as a legitimate form of damages in trademark cases, indicating that proof of actual loss is not a necessary requirement for such claims ([12]).
  5. McCarthy on Trademarks and Unfair Competition: Emphasizes that in modern jurisprudence, the emphasis on consumer confusion supersedes the subjective intent of the defendant ([11]).

A - Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal primarily analyzed the perceived similarities and differences between the marks. The Court noted that the presence of "DT NEW YORK" as a significant part of Global's mark would likely direct consumer perception, suggesting that the aggregate impression diverged notably from Puma's marks ([5], [8]).

The Court found that the trial court followed the correct methodology by focusing on visual and conceptual differences, adhering to the principle that registered trademarks do not grant monopolies over ideas or concepts ([8], [10]). Consequently, it determined that the average consumer, considering the mark as a whole, would not likely be confused ([9]).

Moreover, the Court rejected Puma's argument that Global’s subjective intention to mirror the Puma design amounted to infringement. It observed that intention should not interfere with the objective assessment required in trademark law ([11]).

On the issue of damages, the Court clarified that the provision for notional royalties serves to facilitate compensation within intellectual property infringements, and as stated by the precedent, actual loss does not need to be demonstrated ([12]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the lower court's decision, dismissing Puma's appeal with costs. The judgment emphasized an objective approach to trademark comparison, ruling that Global Warming's mark was not significantly similar to Puma's trademarks to constitute infringement under the relevant legal framework ([13]).

This case reaffirms the principle that trademark owners must demonstrate a likelihood of confusion based on the overall impression of the marks in question, and that subjective intent plays a limited role in such determinations. The ruling also clarifies the standards applied in assessing claims for damages in cases of trademark infringement, highlighting the court's preference for objective assessments to protect consumer interests.
