# Case Note: Mthembu v The State (525/2019) [2019] ZASCA 160

## FIRAC Method Case Note

### F – Facts

The appellant, Joseph Tiamo Mthembu, was convicted by the Regional Court for various offences, including attempted murder and possession of an unlicensed firearm and ammunition ([1]). Specifically, on 31 January 2009, Mthembu was involved in an incident where police officers, Constable Morris Zitha and Sergeant K Phase Themane, were shot at during a routine traffic stop involving a red Jetta ([2]). The officers were unable to identify the assailants due to the rapid progression of events; however, Sergeant Themane was later adamant that Mthembu was his assailant despite earlier stating he could not identify anyone ([4]).

Several hours later, Mthembu was stopped at a police roadblock during which he was found in possession of a firearm connected to the shooting ([5]). A ballistic examination subsequently confirmed that the cartridges found at the scene of the shooting matched the firearm found in Mthembu's possession ([8]). After a trial, he received a 10-year sentence for attempted murder, a 15-year sentence for possession of an unlicensed firearm, and a 3-year sentence for possession of ammunition ([1]). His motions for appeal were initially denied, but he received special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal, focusing on the conviction and sentences ([1]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal were whether the evidence was sufficient to sustain the convictions of attempted murder and possession of an unlicensed firearm, as well as whether the sentences imposed were appropriate ([1], [13]). Additionally, the court needed to determine the credibility of the evidence presented during the trial and whether circumstantial evidence could support the conviction in the absence of direct identification of the appellant by the police officers ([12]).

### R – Rules

The court relied on established legal principles regarding the evaluation of circumstantial evidence. In *R v Blom* [1939] AD 188, the court articulated two cardinal rules concerning circumstantial evidence: (1) the inference must be consistent with all proved facts, and (2) the proved facts must exclude every reasonable inference save the one sought ([10]). The admissibility of ballistic evidence was deemed critical, as it linked the firearm to the shooting incident. The rules governing the credibility of testimony were also paramount, with *S v Essack & another* [1974] 1 SA 1 affirming that conclusions cannot be drawn without objective verified facts ([11]).

Regarding sentencing, *S v Snyders* [1982] 4 All SA 215 outlined that appellate courts rarely intervene in sentencing unless the sentence is grossly disproportionate or causes a sense of outrage ([13]). The *Minimum Sentences Act 105 of 1997* outlined mandatory sentences which were pertinent to the firearm possession charge advocated by the prosecution ([13]).

### A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal assessed the circumstantial evidence, noting that though identification by Sergeant Themane was unreliable, the ballistics evidence was sufficient to link Mthembu to the crime ([9], [12]). The convictions were founded on the cumulative weight of evidence, demonstrating that Mthembu's possession of the firearm was suspiciously proximate to the timing of the shooting ([9], [12]). 

In terms of credibility, the appellant’s explanations and evidence were scrutinized and found to lack reliability. His inconsistent accounts about the firearm's discovery and presence in the vehicle undermined his defense ([6], [7], [12]). As to the sentences, the court found that the Regional Court had appropriately considered various factors such as the severity of the offences against the backdrop of public safety ([14]).

Moreover, the court reaffirmed that the seriousness of shooting at police officers warranted significant penalties. Mthembu’s sentence for attempted murder was thus held as appropriate in light of the gravity of the crime and the need for a deterrent effect within the community ([14]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal formally dismissed Mthembu's appeal against both conviction and sentence, affirming the lower court's findings ([16]). The court underscored the importance of the circumstantial evidence linking the appellant to the crime, the credibility evaluation of testimonies, and the appropriateness of the sentences in relation to societal interests. The judgment conveys the message that acts of violence against law enforcement will not be tolerated and reflects the judicial commitment to upholding the rule of law. The court's steadfast approach reinforces the principles governing both substantive and procedural criminal law, emphasizing a careful balance between ensuring justice and protecting society at large ([16]).
