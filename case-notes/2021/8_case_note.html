Constitutional Court 2021 [2021] ZACC 8

Facts

In this matter, the late Mr. Louis John Druiff executed a Trust Deed on January 28, 1953, concerning the distribution of his estate. He had four children, one of whom, Ms. Dulcie Helena Harper, adopted children after his passing. The applicants, David Louis Ayscough Wilkinson and Amanda Bridget Truter, are the adopted children of Ms. Harper. Following the death of Mr. Druiff, Ms. Harper sought a declaratory relief in the High Court regarding the inclusion of adopted children in the definitions of "children," "descendants," "issue," and "legal descendants" in the Trust Deed. The High Court ruled in favor of the trustees, reflecting that the Trust Deed did not explicitly include adopted children as beneficiaries. The applicants then appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which upheld the High Court's decision. The majority reasoned that the ordinary meaning referenced in the Trust Deed strictly referred to biological descendants unless expressly stated otherwise ([1]–[12]).

Issues

  1. Does the wording of the Trust Deed exclude adopted children from receiving benefits under the Trust?
  2. If so, does this exclusion constitute unfair discrimination based on their status as adopted children, and is it contrary to public policy, thus rendering the exclusion unenforceable?
  3. Can the court amend the Trust Deed under section 13 of the Trust Property Control Act to include the adopted children as beneficiaries? ([23]–[30]).

Rules

The court referred to multiple statutes, including:

  1. Section 71(2) of the 1937 Children’s Act, which indicated that an adopted child shall not inherit unless explicitly stated in the relevant testamentary document, reinforcing the need for clarity in such provisions ([29]–[40]).
  2. The concept of freedom of testation was emphasized, which is the right to dictate the distribution of one's estate posthumously, suggesting a significant judicial reluctance to interfere absent public policy violations ([69]–[90]).
  3. The principles regarding unfair discrimination were derived from Section 9 of the Constitution, which includes considerations regarding equality, the right to dignity, and the historical discrimination faced by adopted children ([67]–[90]).

Application

The majority judgment focused heavily on the intention of Mr. Druiff at the time of drafting the Trust Deed, asserting that his failure to include explicit language concerning adopted children indicated a clear intention to exclude them. The court highlighted the traditional views towards adopted children's inheritance rights under the law prevailing at the time the Trust was drafted. The interpretation of terms like "descendants" was aligned with historical legal understandings which favored biological links.

In contrast, the dissenting opinions highlighted the evolution of public policy regarding adoption and argued that the exclusion of adopted children was discriminatory, especially in light of current constitutional imperatives favoring equality and dignity. They referenced international perspectives supporting the inclusion of adopted children in the estates of their adoptive parents and interpreted the term "legal descendants" in a broader context to encompass both biological and adopted children, applying contemporary understanding and public policy considerations ([61]–[66], [99]).

Conclusion

The Constitutional Court found that the terms used in the Trust Deed denied adopted children the right to inherit, amounting to unfair discrimination based on their adoptive status ([100]). The court concluded that the Trust Deed's exclusion of adopted children was contrary to public policy and, thus, unenforceable. As a result, the court ruled that the adopted children of Ms. Harper were entitled to benefit as beneficiaries of the Trust, declaring them capital beneficiaries of a quarter share of the trust capital ([100]–[101]). This case underscores the tension between historical legal interpretations in testamentary matters and modern principles of equality and non-discrimination, marking a significant development in South African trust law.
