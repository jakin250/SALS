Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

## Case Note: VJV and Another v Minister of Social Development and Another [2023] ZACC 21

### F – Facts

In **VJV and Another v Minister of Social Development and Another**, the applicants, VJV and RT, both women in a permanent life partnership, sought a declaration against the constitutionality of section 40 of the **Children’s Act 38 of 2005** which, at the time, excluded permanent life partners from the automatic acquisition of parental rights when children were conceived through artificial fertilisation. The applicants had undergone in vitro fertilisation (IVF), using VJV's gametes and those of a donor to conceive twins. Under the existing statutory framework, only RT, who gave birth, was recognized as a legal parent, limiting VJV’s rights, despite her biological contribution and involvement in the family setting ([paras 5–6]).

The High Court found section 40 to be unconstitutional based on discrimination against permanent life partners regarding marital status and sexual orientation. The applicants sought to have the words “or permanent life partner” read into the statute to rectify this exclusion. The Centre for Child Law (CCL) was permitted to participate as amicus curiae, primarily supporting the applicants while expressing concerns regarding the ambiguity in the proposed reading ([paras 4–12]).

### I – Issues

The key legal issues raised in this case revolved around:
1. Whether section 40 of the **Children’s Act**, which excludes permanent life partners from automatic parental rights concerning children conceived by artificial insemination, violates the rights to equality and dignity as enshrined in the Constitution, specifically sections 9 and 10 ([paras 40, 60]).
2. The implications of general discrimination against unmarried persons and those in permanent life partnerships in the domain of family law and parental rights ([paras 29, 32]).
3. The interpretation and application of the best interests of the child principle under section 28 of the Constitution in context to both parents ([paras 62–63]).

### R – Rules

The court relied on a host of constitutional and statutory provisions, including:
- **Section 9 of the Constitution** (right to equality), which prohibits unfair discrimination on grounds including marital status and sexual orientation.
- **Section 10 of the Constitution** (right to dignity), affirming the valuation and self-worth of individuals in familial contexts.
- **Section 28(2) of the Constitution** asserting the best interests of the child as paramount ([paras 40, 60]).
  
The judgement drew upon previous rulings such as **Harksen v Lane N.O.** and **Dawood v Minister of Home Affairs** which elucidated the framework for evaluating discrimination and affirmed the significance of rights to dignity and family life. The CCL’s input served to highlight the necessity of considering the evolving nature of family structures in contemporary South African society ([paras 24, 45, 57]).

### A – Application

The court analyzed the implications of section 40 extensively, highlighting its discriminatory impact. The court agreed with the High Court's finding that the law unfairly privileged married couples over those in permanent life partnerships. By imposing an artificial barrier that left the non-birthing partner without legal status as a parent, the provision devalued the lived realities of families formed by permanent life partnerships and undermined their dignity.

The court critiques the reliance on marriage as the exclusive determinant for parental rights, acknowledging the diverse forms of familial structure present in South Africa. It considered the unique plight of same-sex couples reliant on artificial insemination, arguing that applying the law in its current form perpetuated historical inequalities faced by queer families ([paras 27–30, 48, 55, 59]). 

The court emphasized that the legislature failed to present any legitimate governmental interest in perpetuating the exclusion embodied in section 40 of the Children’s Act, thus failing the test for justifiable discrimination ([paras 73–75]). The ruling articulated that the best interests of the child were not served by denying nuanced parental rights based on marital status or sexual orientation ([paras 60–64]).

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court's declaration of constitutional invalidity regarding section 40, ruling that it unjustifiably discriminates against permanent life partners based on marital status and sexual orientation. The judgment mandated a read-in of the term “permanent life partner” to ensure that all committed parental relationships are equitably recognized under the law. The order would take effect retroactively from July 1, 2007, with a suspension period of 24 months for Parliament to address the identified deficiencies, ensuring that the legal framework was updated in a coherent and inclusive manner ([paras 1–6, 83, 87]).

The court's decision underscores its commitment to recognizing the diversification of family structures in post-apartheid South Africa, setting a precedent for future interpretations of equality and family law ([paras 86, 90]). The applicants were also granted costs, further affirming the precedent’s impact on the acknowledgment of familial rights within the ambit of constitutional protections ([paras 96–96]). 

This judgment signals a pivotal step forward in addressing the legal invisibility faced by permanent life partners in matters concerning family law and parental rights, ultimately advocating for an inclusive approach in legislative processes to align with constitutional tenets of equality, dignity, and the best interests of children.
