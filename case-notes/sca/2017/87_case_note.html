Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 87

F – Facts ([1]-[12])

On March 1, 2016, the National Energy Regulator of South Africa (NERSA) approved an additional 1.4% increase in electricity tariffs for Eskom Holdings SOC Limited (Eskom) over an already authorized 8% increase for the 2013/2014 financial year. This decision followed an application based on Eskom's claim of significant revenue under-recovery due to various operational challenges, including higher energy costs and insufficient electricity sales. Eskom's request for a selective reopening of the Multi-Year Price Determination Methodology (MYPDM) was declined by NERSA, leading Eskom to submit a Regulatory Clearing Account (RCA) application for a total tariff adjustment of R22 billion.

The first to sixth respondents, comprised of various affected businesses within the Nelson Mandela Bay Metropolitan Municipality and represented by the Nelson Mandela Bay Business Chamber, contested NERSA’s decision in the Gauteng Division of the Pretoria High Court, arguing that NERSA failed to adhere to the statutory pricing methodology. The High Court found that NERSA did not comply with the MYPDM, ruling that its decision was irrational and procedurally unfair, thus setting it aside.

I – Issues ([1]-[12])

The primary legal questions addressed in this case included:

  1. Whether the decision by NERSA to grant Eskom a tariff increase was rational, given the regulatory framework and compliance with MYPDM.
  2. Whether NERSA's adjudication process constituted administrative action subject to review under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) and, therefore, whether it was immune from judicial scrutiny.
  3. The interpretation and application of the MYPDM and the extent to which Eskom’s efficiencies should impact the recovery of costs.

R – Rules ([4]-[12])

The court relied upon several legal frameworks and rules:

  • Energy Regulation Act 4 of 2006 (ERA), which delineates NERSA's powers and duties, including the regulation of tariffs and prices under conditions of licensees (s 14 and 15).
  • National Energy Regulator Act 40 of 2004 (NERA), establishing NERSA and mandating justifiable and transparent actions in its regulatory functions (s 9 and 10).
  • Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000, asserting the right of affected parties to judicial review regarding administrative actions by public bodies.
  • MYPDM, which guides the methodology for pricing determination, stipulating conditions and requirements for Eskom regarding tariff applications, including timelines for submissions and public participation processes.

A – Application ([18]-[121])

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) thoroughly analyzed whether NERSA had complied with statutory and procedural requirements outlined in the MYPDM. The court highlighted several critical observations:

  1. Adherence to MYPDM: The SCA noted that the requirement for quarterly updates by Eskom was both a procedural necessity and integral to the predictability of tariff adjustments for consumers, which NERSA had not appropriately enforced.
  2. Rationality of Decision: The court emphasized that rationality is a principal tenet of administrative action under PAJA. It examined NERSA's approvals of cost recoveries in light of Eskom’s historical inefficiencies and whether such costs align with the legislative framework's mandate that only efficient costs can be recovered.
  3. Judicial Review: The court asserted that NERSA's operations are subject to judicial oversight, countering claims from Eskom and NERSA regarding the separation of powers. Importantly, the court differentiated between policy decisions that should receive deference and the regulatory compliance that must meet legal standards.

Overall, the SCA held that NERSA's failure to comply with its own methodologies and to justify deviations was sufficient grounds for setting aside the decision on the tariff increase.

C – Conclusion ([92]-[121])

The SCA concluded by upholding the appeal, stating that the Gauteng Division, Pretoria's High Court had erred in its ruling. The SCA reinforced that the NERSA decision was rational and that the necessary public participation had occurred. It ruled that proper enforcement of MYPDM was critical for maintaining the balance between Eskom's financial sustainability and consumer protections.

The court ordered that the application for the RCA adjustment be dismissed, enabling Eskom to proceed with the approved tariff adjustments, albeit with the caveat that adherence to compliance with the MYPDM will be strictly enforced in future regulatory practices.

The ruling thus delineates a clear framework for the analysis of adjustable tariffs within the energy sector in South Africa, emphasizing accountability and structured regulatory practices, significant for the relationship between state-owned entities and consumer rights.
