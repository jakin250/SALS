# FIRAC Case Note: Cadac (Pty) Ltd v Weber-Stephen Products Co [2010] ZASCA 105

**F – Facts:**  
[1] The dispute arose between Cadac (Pty) Ltd ("Cadac"), a South African manufacturer of kettle grills, and Weber-Stephen Products Co ("Weber-Stephen"), the registered trademark owner of a barbeque grill design in the U.S. The second respondent, Galactex Outdoor (Pty) Ltd, serves as Weber-Stephen’s sole distributor in South Africa. Cadac launched a new model in November 2004, prompting Weber-Stephen to accuse Cadac of trademark infringement and subsequent counterfeiting, threatening to file a complaint under the **Counterfeit Goods Act 37 of 1997** ([2]).

[4] Weber-Stephen initiated proceedings based on allegations without Cadac being properly informed or allowed to respond. An inspector allegedly took action based on Weber-Stephen's claims, resulting in the seizure of Cadac's kettle grills under a search warrant executed during the Christmas season, which Cadac claimed caused significant losses ([3]).

Cadac responded by seeking to set aside the warrant, asserting that the seizure was wrongful and applying for a damages inquiry. The High Court ruled in favor of Cadac, deeming the seizure warrant obtained irregularly ([5]). However, Weber-Stephen attempted to appeal this decision, which met with failure ([5]).

[8] Following a significant delay of three years in prosecuting its damages claim, Cadac instituted an interlocutory application in March 2008 seeking directions for the damages inquiry, prompting Weber-Stephen to contest the claim's validity based on prescription ([8]).

---

**I – Issues:**  
The case presents several pivotal legal issues ([9]):

1. Whether the interlocutory application for damage inquiry was competent given that it was initially brought in motion proceedings which typically address legal questions rather than factual disputes.
  
2. Whether Cadac's claim for damages had prescribed under the **Prescription Act 68 of 1969**, particularly considering the delay in prosecution since the setting aside of the seizure warrant.

3. The fundamental question of whether a claim for damages can proceed without a concurrent determination on the counterfeit nature of the goods as per **sections 10(1)(c)** and **17(1)** of the Counterfeit Goods Act.

---

**R – Rules:**  
The court's decision drew upon several key legal principles ([10]):

1. **Motion vs Trial Proceedings**: Cadac’s approach sought directions for quantifying damages, contrasting the typical conditions governing illiquid claims, which are more suited for trial proceedings rather than motion applications (see *Room Hire Co (Pty) Ltd v Jeppe Street Mansions (Pty) Ltd* [1950] 3 SA 1155).

2. **Prescription**: Under **section 15** of the Prescription Act, prescription can be interrupted by the service of any process claiming payment of a debt. The court needed to determine whether Cadac’s claim had indeed become prescribed due to its inaction ([20]).

3. **Counterfeit Goods Act**: The applicable provisions govern scenarios where damages due to wrongful seizure are claimed, stating compensation is contingent upon the context of alleged infringement ([7]).

---

**A – Application:**  
In determining whether the procedures followed by Cadac were permissible, the court emphasized that while motions are generally not suited for determining factual matters, Cadac was not seeking relief on the merits of its claim but only directions for quantification ([11]). Cadac deserved an avenue to pursue its compensation as per the statutory framework, despite the procedural deviations.

The court then addressed the argument around prescription, concluding that Weber-Stephen's assertion about failure to prosecute the claim was unfounded since Cadac's claim was effectively interrupted upon instituting the initial application ([21]–[24]). It indicated that the passage of time under the Prescription Act should reflect the complexities and dynamics of the litigation process, rather than penalizing Cadac for delays predominantly influenced by Weber-Stephen’s actions.

---

**C – Conclusion:**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Cadac’s appeal, asserting that Weber-Stephen’s defenses concerning the legitimacy of the damages inquiry lacked merit ([25]). The court directed that the interlocutory application be maintained and laid down procedures for the subsequent damages inquiry. This decision underscores the judiciary's pursuit to facilitate fair recourse under the Counterfeit Goods Act and reaffirms that strategic delay or procedural arguments should not negate substantive rights to compensation in contexts where wrongful seizures have caused demonstrable harm.

Overall, this judgment elucidates the balance courts must maintain in ensuring that procedural technicalities do not obstruct a plaintiff's right to seek redress for unlawful actions, illustrating the inherent flexibility courts can exercise in managing litigation processes, guided by principles of fairness and justice.
