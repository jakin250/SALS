Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

### FIRAC Case Note: **Rathumbu v S (369/11) [2012] ZASCA 51**

#### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–7])

The appellant, Nthabeleni Daniel Rathumbu, was convicted of the murder of his wife, Khathutshelo Rathumbu, by the Limpopo High Court sitting in Thohoyandou. The conviction arose from a written sworn statement made by the appellant's sister, Ms. Johanna Rathumbu, which implicated the appellant in the stabbing of the deceased. Ms. Rathumbu reported to Constable David Mulaudzi that she had witnessed the appellant stabbing the deceased shortly before the police were summoned to the scene ([para 2]).

Following a detailed account of her testimony, it was revealed that Ms. Rathumbu disavowed her initial statement during her trial testimony, contradicting her earlier account. Despite her denial, the trial court admitted her statement into evidence, relying on it predominantly to establish the appellant's guilt. It is pertinent to note that the appellant did not testify in his defense throughout the trial ([paras 3-7]).

#### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 8–9])

The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was the admissibility of Ms. Rathumbu's statement given the fact that it was disavowed during trial, raising a question about its probative value, especially under the context of hearsay evidence. Specifically, the court needed to determine whether the trial court had materially misdirected itself by admitting evidence that essentially constituted hearsay, contrary to the provisions laid out in s 3(1) of the Law of Evidence Amendment Act 45 of 1988. The court also considered whether the interests of justice necessitated the admission of the disavowed statement ([paras 8-9]).

#### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 10–11])

The judgment relied heavily on s 3(1) of the Law of Evidence Amendment Act 45 of 1988, which governs the admissibility of hearsay evidence in South African law. The section stipulates that hearsay evidence is inadmissible unless certain conditions are met, including the agreement of parties or the court determining its admission is in the interests of justice.

The Supreme Court of Appeal referenced prior cases such as *S v Ndhlovu* 2002 (6) SA 305 (SCA) and *S v Shaik & others* 2007 (1) SA 240 (SCA), emphasizing that the probative value of hearsay evidence primarily depends on the credibility of the declarant at the time the statement was made. The purpose of these considerations is to ensure that the interests of justice are preserved whilst balancing the rights of the accused against the evidentiary needs of the case ([paras 10-11]).

#### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 12–14])

In analyzing whether Ms. Rathumbu's statement should be admitted, the court reviewed the totality of the circumstances under which the statement was made. The court concluded that there was substantial supporting evidence for the truthfulness of the statement beyond the witness’s later disavowal. Key corroborative evidence included:

1. Ms. Rathumbu's own actions that night aligned with her statement - her visit to the deceased’s home and the urgent calls she received supported her account of impending upheaval in the deceased's domestic life.
2. The corroboration from Constable Mulaudzi regarding Ms. Rathumbu's spontaneous identification of the appellant as the perpetrator further reinforced the reliability of her statement.
3. The court found it improbable that multiple police officers, independently corroborating her statement, could have fabricated details that would align with different testimonies provided during the trial ([paras 12-13]).

Furthermore, the appellant's conduct at the scene, where he failed to inquire about the police's presence or show concern for the deceased, was examined as a factor that indicated guilt. This behavior, combined with the lack of any rebuttal evidence from the appellant, created an inferential link to culpability that substantiated the trial court’s reliance on the disavowed statement ([para 14]).

#### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 15–16])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal against the appellant’s conviction, affirming that the trial court had acted correctly in admitting Ms. Rathumbu's statement into evidence. The court held that the evidentiary value of the disavowed statement, in light of corroborative evidence and its alignment with the circumstances of the case, was a legitimate conclusion under the interests of justice. The court emphasized the importance of an accused providing a response to credible evidence presented against them, citing previous decisions where silence or failure to rebut evidence is often implicated in determining guilt ([paras 15-16]).

The judgment clarifies the application of hearsay rules within the South African legal framework, particularly regarding the circumstances under which such evidence may be admitted. It reinforces the principle that the credibility of a witness is to be assessed in light of the entirety of the circumstances surrounding the case, rather than solely on later recantations or disavowals of earlier statements. This case thus contributes to the ongoing discourse on evidentiary standards and the interaction between hearsay rules and the notion of justice in criminal proceedings.
