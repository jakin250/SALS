### FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title:** Huysamen & another v Absa Bank Limited & others  
**Neutral Citation:** [2020] ZASCA 127  

---

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1-7])**

The case involves an application by the appellants, Wilhelm George Huysamen and Constantia Tonia Huysamen, for condonation of the late filing of their notice of appeal regarding the dismissal of their application to set aside a sale in execution of their immovable property. The North Gauteng High Court, Pretoria, had ruled against the appellants, asserting that their property sale was lawful despite their prior submission of a surrender notice under the Insolvency Act 24 of 1936 (the Act).

The appellants had defaulted on bond repayment to ABSA Bank, leading to a judgment against them for R1,691,958 and the execution of the property on 28 February 2013. They published their intention to surrender their estates on 14 June 2013, after receiving notice of the impending execution sale scheduled for 18 June 2013. Nonetheless, the property was sold to third parties, Kolman and Du Plessis, without the respondents being notified of the applicants’ surrender application prior to the sale.

The appellants' subsequent application to set aside the sale was dismissed on 14 August 2017. Although the High Court granted them leave to appeal, the notice of appeal was filed late on 13 June 2019.

---

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 7-9])**

The primary legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether to grant condonation for the late filing of the notice of appeal. This entails assessing the adequacy of the explanation provided by the appellants and their legal representatives for the delay, particularly their attorney's remissness.

Additionally, the court had to assess whether the sale in execution could be deemed unlawful under section 5(1) of the Insolvency Act based on the appellants' claim that their surrender notice effectively suspended the execution process.

---

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 10-16])**

The court anchored its analysis in the provisions of the Insolvency Act, specifically section 5(1), which provides that the sale of property becomes unlawful if it occurs after the publication of a notice of surrender. Furthermore, the court referenced established case law concerning the criteria for granting condonation, notably the factors outlined in *Dengetenge Holdings* [2013] ZASCA 5 and *Darries v Sheriff Magistrate’s Court, Wynberg* [1998] ZASCA 18.

The court noted that a full and reasonable explanation for any delay must be provided and emphasized the importance of a litigant's responsibility for the conduct of their attorney. The court also cited *Reinecke v Incorporated General Insurances Ltd* [1974] 2 SA 84 (A) concerning the unreliability of placing blame on an attorney's incompetence when the results of the delay have serious implications for the affected parties.

---

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 8-21])**

The court meticulously evaluated the explanation for the late filing, identifying significant deficiencies in the reasons provided by the appellants and their attorney. The court highlighted that reliance on a candidate attorney and failure to follow up on essential tasks demonstrated gross negligence. 

Moreover, the court scrutinized the circumstances surrounding the execution sale. It highlighted that the applicants' surrender notice was published after the execution sale had already been scheduled, leading to the conclusion that section 5(1) did not apply. The court also expressed concern over the applicants’ misleading statements regarding their indebtedness and the misrepresentation of information to ABSA.

Ultimately, the court addressed the implications of allowing the appeal to proceed considering the subsequent costs and the potential for extensive prejudice endured by the purchasers who had acquired the property. The court concluded that the applicants not only failed to substantiate their claims sufficiently but also exhibited mala fides in their actions, creating serious legal and ethical implications regarding their appeal.

---

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 21-21])**

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the application for condonation of the late filing of the notice of appeal, emphasizing the importance of adhering to procedural rules and the responsibility of litigants for their legal representation. The court's ruling affirmed the validity of the sale in execution and underscored the necessity for full and honest disclosure during insolvency proceedings. 

The implications of this case extend beyond the appellants, reinforcing the criticality of following legal processes and maintaining transparency with all involved parties, thereby upholding the integrity of the judicial system.
