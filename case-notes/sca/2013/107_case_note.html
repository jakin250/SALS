Competition Commission v Yara (SA)(Pty) Ltd (784/12) [2013] ZASCA 107

This case originates from the Competition Commission's (the "Commission") initiative to amend a complaint referral against three entities: Yara (South Africa)(Pty) Ltd (the "First Respondent"), Omnia Fertilizer Ltd (the "Second Respondent"), and Sasol Chemical Industries Ltd...

Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 107

F – Facts

This case originates from the Competition Commission's (the "Commission") initiative to amend a complaint referral against three entities: Yara (South Africa)(Pty) Ltd (the "First Respondent"), Omnia Fertilizer Ltd (the "Second Respondent"), and Sasol Chemical Industries Ltd (the "Third Respondent"). The Commission had initially received a complaint from Nutri-Flo CC and Nutri-Fertiliser CC (collectively referred to as "Nutri-Flo") in November 2003, alleging that Sasol had imposed excessive prices on raw materials crucial for fertilization, constituting anti-competitive conduct under Sections 8 and 9 of the Competition Act 89 of 1998 (the "Act") ([paras 1–5]).

The Commission's investigation led to a complaint referral on May 4, 2005, alleging prohibited practices like collusive dealings between Sasol, Omnia, and Yara, also claiming exclusionary pricing practices by Sasol ([paras 8–9]). Subsequently, in May 2009, the Commission reached a consent and settlement agreement with Sasol, admitting the allegations and unveiling collusive pricing agreements with the other respondents ([paras 9–10]).

The Commission's attempt to amend the referral followed an assertion from Omnia that the proposed amendments to the complaint went outside the original scope lodged by Nutri-Flo and served as the basis for a counter-application to declare the referral invalid ([paras 4–19]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue for determination was whether the Commission's referral to the Tribunal and its proposed amendments complied with the requirements of the Act—specifically, whether the Commission initiated its complaint according to Sections 49B and 50 of the Act. This inquiry included examining whether the referral accurately corresponded to the original complaints lodged by the private complainant, Nutri-Flo ([paras 3, 11–12]).

Furthermore, the appeal raised a question regarding the interpretation of the so-called "referral rule," which posits that a referral must not exceed or deviate in substance from the initial complaints submitted by the complainant ([paras 11–12]).

R – Rules

The relevant provisions of the Competition Act include:

  1. Section 49B: This section provides the legal framework for initiating complaints. It allows the Commissioner to initiate a complaint (s 49B(1)), and also permits individuals to submit complaints (s 49B(2)(b)) ([paras 10–11]).
  2. Section 50: This section governs the outcome of complaints and stipulates that the Commission may refer its complaint to the Tribunal at any time after initiation. It mandates that for complaints lodged by individuals (such as Nutri-Flo), the Commission must refer them within one year of submission if a prohibited practice is established (s 50(2)) ([paras 10–11]).
  3. Referral Rule: Established jurisprudence from the Competition Appeal Court (CAC) outlined that a referral to the Tribunal must reflect the specific complaints initially presented, thereby serving as a control mechanism against unsubstantiated expansions of the original complaint ([paras 11–12]).

A – Application

Upon review, the CAC held that the referral by the Commission substantially exceeded the original complaint lodged by Nutri-Flo. The undertakings by Nutri-Flo suggested that its primary complaint was directed solely against Sasol and did not inherently encompass Yara and Omnia. Consequently, the CAC concluded that the expanded complaints against Yara and Omnia lacked a legal foundation as the Commission had not initiated complaints against those entities in line with the original submission ([paras 12–17]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), however, critiqued the CAC's interpretation of the referral rule, asserting that it unduly conflated the difference between complaints initiated by the Commission and those submitted by private complainants. The SCA reasoned that the Commission's initiation does not necessitate formalities per the provisions of the Act. As such, the decision to initiate complaints could indeed be informal, allowing for the inclusion of newly discovered evidence or conduct ([paras 18–30]).

The SCA explicitly disagreed with the CAC's interpretation that dictated the referral must mirror the original complaint. The court identified that the Commission had reasonably inferred to initiate additional complaints based on the evidence uncovered during the investigation ([paras 30–34]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA ultimately ruled in favor of the Commission, reversing the CAC's decision, and reinstated the Tribunal's original order allowing the Commission to amend its referral ([paras 31–36]). The judgment emphasized that the initiation of a complaint by the Commission could be informal and that valid complaints need not strictly align with initial submissions. The ruling suggests a more flexible and pragmatic approach to handling anti-competitive complaints, encouraging the Commission's activities in preserving competition law rather than adhering rigidly to procedural constraints.

This case reinforces the broader implications for how complaints within the ambit of competition law are construed and the latitude afforded to regulatory bodies in investigating and prosecuting interests against anti-competitive practices.
