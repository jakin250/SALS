Supreme Court of Appeal

Gumbi v The State (414/2017) [2018] ZASCA 125

[1] The appellants, Langstone Rexson Gumbi and three co-appellants, faced multiple charges before the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 125

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

[1] The appellants, Langstone Rexson Gumbi and three co-appellants, faced multiple charges before the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria. The offences in question were alleged to have occurred on 4 April 2007, but the trial did not commence until August 2014, seven years later, before Judge Webster. During the proceedings, the evidence of many witnesses was presented along with two admissibility trials. Judge Webster provisionally admitted statements made by two of the appellants as evidence. After the appellants had given testimonies and both sides had submitted written arguments, the trial was adjourned for the judge to consider his verdict. However, prior to rendering a decision, Judge Webster became incapacitated due to illness, leaving the case unresolved.

[2] Following this development, the prosecutor submitted an “application for special review” to the high court. This application sought guidance on whether another judge could issue a judgment without hearing the case anew or having the parties re-argue. In response, Judges Jordaan and Potterill recognized that review judges could not serve as trial judges and thus could not issue a judgment based on evidence previously heard in court.

[3] Consequently, Judge Potterill ruled that the trial must commence de novo, meaning it had to start afresh with the same parties. The parties agreed that the trial would start anew, and Judge Potterill indicated that the original record could be utilized in the new trial proceedings.

[4] On 7 April 2016, after considering the cases, Judge Potterill ultimately convicted the appellants of serious charges, including three counts of murder, two of robbery with aggravating circumstances, and other related offences. Before sentencing could occur, new legal representation applied for permission to appeal against the convictions.

I – Issues

The principal legal issue in this case was whether the trial court (Potterill J) acted appropriately in conducting the trial on the record of the prior proceedings without starting afresh in compliance with the provisions of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([para 9]). Specifically, the court had to ascertain whether the original indictment was void and whether a new trial could proceed without violating the accused's right to a fair trial.

R – Rules

The relevant legal principles derive primarily from the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, specifically sections 214 and 215 ([para 8]). Section 215 allows for the admissibility of a witness's testimony from a previous trial under certain conditions outlined in section 214. These conditions detail scenarios in which a witness may be unable to give testimony at a subsequent trial, naming situations such as death, illness, or avoidance by the accused.

Additionally, the South African jurisprudence establishes that a trial must follow established procedural rules to safeguard the rights of the accused and ensure justice. In S v Moodie 1961 (4) SA 752 (A) and S v Nzuza 1963 (3) SA 631 (A), the courts reaffirmed that procedural irregularities compromising the fairness of a trial warrant a finding of a miscarriage of justice.

A – Application

The Court of Appeal, led by Ponnan JA, concluded that Potterill J had misconstrued the application of sections 214 and 215. It was established that the trial under Potterill J effectively did not commence de novo, as required, when she merely continued with proceedings once led by Judge Webster without conducting the necessary inquiry stipulated in section 215 ([para 6]).

The judgment reflected a significant departure from procedure; the failure to adhere to the statutory mandates and the lack of consideration about the admissibility of evidence undermined the integrity of the subsequent trial. Additionally, the Court noted that each witness's admissibility must be evaluated separately, and counsel should have been afforded opportunities to address the court regarding the admissibility of each witness's evidence. This lack of procedural diligence meant that no viable evidence existed upon which to base convictions against the appellants ([para 14]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, nullifying the convictions and sentences issued by the Gauteng High Court ([para 15]). The reasoning reiterated that the original proceedings' procedural irregularities led to a miscarriage of justice, affirming that the inequities present in the trial could not be overlooked. The case underscores the critical importance of adhering to established procedural frameworks in criminal trials, emphasizing that the right to a fair trial is paramount ([para 8]). Consequently, the state was afforded the option to re-indict the appellants and proceed with a new trial if deemed appropriate, though this would not be within the remit of the appellate court’s powers.

The judgment serves as a reminder of the essential safeguards required to ensure that the integrity of criminal proceedings is maintained and that the rights of the accused are not compromised through procedural lapses.
