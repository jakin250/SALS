Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# Case Note: Magwabeni v Liomba (198/13) [2015] ZASCA 117

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

The case revolves around the malicious prosecution claim by George Magwabeni (the plaintiff) against Christopher Liomba (the defendant). The facts are as follows:

1. **Background and Conflict**: Magwabeni, an electrical contractor, was working for Liomba, who operated a hotel business in Thohoyandou. The relationship soured when Liomba terminated Magwabeni's services on 13 December 2008, demanding he vacate his rented premises by 15 December 2008. Magwabeni contested this termination, asserting he had not been compensated for his services ([paras 1–3]).

2. **Incident Leading to Prosecution**: On 17 December 2008, police accompanied Liomba to evict Magwabeni, accusing him of illegally occupying the premises. During this encounter, Magwabeni was arrested and charged with malicious injury to property related to damage to Liomba's electrical distribution box and water pump. He spent a week in detention before the charges were withdrawn ([paras 2, 3, 4]).

3. **Subsequent Legal Actions**: After the withdrawal of charges, Magwabeni initiated two separate actions: one against Liomba for malicious prosecution and another against the Minister of Police for unlawful arrest. The claim against the Minister was dismissed and is not subject to appeal. The appeal concerns only the findings regarding Liomba ([paras 2, 6]).

4. **Evidentiary Disputes**: The trial court found that Liomba had lacked reasonable and probable cause to initiate prosecution, primarily relying on statements from his employee, which were found inadmissible due to her non-appearance as a witness ([paras 8, 9]).

### I – Issues

The core legal issues in this appeal are:

1. Did the appellant (Liomba) set the law in motion against the respondent (Magwabeni) maliciously?
2. Did the initiation of prosecution lack reasonable and probable cause?
3. Did the defendant exhibit malice in pursuing the prosecution? ([paras 8, 9]).

### R – Rules

The legal requirements to establish a claim for malicious prosecution, as reiterated by several precedents, are as follows:

1. The defendant must have set the law in motion (instigated or instituted the proceedings).
2. The defendant acted without reasonable and probable cause.
3. The defendant acted with malice (or animo injuriandi).
4. The prosecution failed ([paras 9, 12]).

In the context of this case, the test for "absence of reasonable and probable cause" incorporates both subjective and objective elements, meaning that the defendant must genuinely believe the plaintiff is guilty, and that belief must also be reasonable under the circumstances ([paras 10, 11]).

### A – Application

In evaluating the appeal, the court applied the legal standards for establishing malicious prosecution:

1. **Analysis of Liomba's Intent**: The court weighed Liomba's belief regarding the allegations he made against Magwabeni. It was determined that while Liomba sought police assistance to evict Magwabeni, he also genuinely believed, based on the information at hand, that the latter was responsible for damages to his property ([paras 16, 19]).

2. **Evidentiary Considerations**: The trial court had previously dismissed the defendant's claims based on insufficient evidence and inadmissible statements from his employee. However, the appeal court noted that while the statements from the employee were crucial, the evidence still pointed to Liomba's honest, albeit mistaken, belief in the veracity of his accusations against Magwabeni ([paras 8, 19]).

3. **Nature of the Dispute**: Central to the court's evaluation was the understanding that the underlying conflict between the parties was civil in nature. Liomba misapprehended the role of law enforcement in resolving a civil dispute regarding eviction. Nevertheless, this misunderstanding did not undermine his belief in the legitimacy of his claims against Magwabeni ([paras 17, 19]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, finding that the evidence did not substantiate the trial court's conclusions regarding malice or lack of reasonable cause on the part of Liomba. The court concluded that Liomba had an honest belief in the truth of his allegations, based on the conditions surrounding the incident. 

Therefore, the order of the court a quo was set aside, and Magwabeni's claim was dismissed with costs, underscoring the importance of a defendant's subjective belief in the context of malicious prosecution claims. This case highlights the balance between the rights of individuals to seek legal recourse and the implications of instigating criminal proceedings based on misunderstandings or civil disputes ([paras 22]). 

This ruling reiterates pivotal aspects of malicious prosecution law, stressing the necessity for clear evidence of malice and lack of reasonable ground, while upholding the integrity of an individual's right to seek help from law enforcement when faced with disputes, provided their actions stem from genuine belief in their claims.
