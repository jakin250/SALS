Supreme Court of Appeal

MEC for Health, Eastern Cape v N H obo A [2022] ZASCA 181

Upon receipt of the summons, the appellant filed a special plea, asserting that the respondent's claim had prescribed.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 181

F – Facts
The case involved an action initiated by N H, the respondent, against the MEC for Health, Eastern Cape, the appellant. The respondent was the mother of a boy born on 11 May 2012 at St Barnabas Hospital, Libode, Eastern Cape. She claimed damages of R29,106,761 for alleged hospital negligence leading to her child's cerebral palsy. In addition, she sought R500,000 for emotional trauma ([1]).

Upon receipt of the summons, the appellant filed a special plea, asserting that the respondent's claim had prescribed. The appellant argued that the respondent's notice of intent to sue, dated 20 February 2018, was untimely, as the action should have been initiated within six months from the date her cause of action arose, which was a date around the child's birth ([2]). The respondent contended she lacked knowledge of the facts necessary for her claim until she consulted a legal practitioner on 1 February 2018 ([11]).

The High Court dismissed the appellant's special plea, leading to the current appeal, where the respondent did not oppose the appeal but chose to abide by the Court’s decision ([3]).

I – Issues
The primary issue at appellate level was determining when the prescription period began for the respondent's claim. Specifically, it involved evaluating when she acquired the requisite knowledge of the facts that constituted her claim. This included assessing whether the claimant only acquired such knowledge upon consulting a legal practitioner ([4]–[5]).

R – Rules
The relevant legal framework includes the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, particularly section 12, which states that a debt becomes due when the creditor has knowledge of the debtor's identity and the facts giving rise to the debt. Section 12(3) further stipulates that a creditor is deemed to have such knowledge if they could have acquired it through reasonable care ([4]).

The court cited precedent from Mtokonya v Minister of Police, which clarified that knowledge of the bare facts is required, while knowledge of wrongfulness and causation is not necessary to trigger the prescription period ([14]). The court also referenced critical interpretations from Links v Department of Health, Northern Province, reinforcing the structures around professional negligence claims and the requisite knowledge for prescription to commence ([17]).

A – Application
In its analysis, the Court found the respondent had limited education and was unaware of the potential negligence associated with her child's birth until her consultation with a lawyer. The High Court relied on affidavits without oral evidence and identified a two-pronged inquiry: the knowledge acquired by the respondent regarding the identity of the debtor and the facts leading to the debt. The High Court concluded that the initial circumstances surrounding the birth—marked by a complicated labor and the baby not crying—did not provide sufficient grounds for the respondent to suspect negligence ([6]–[10]).

The factual history established that no legal or medical indications were presented to the respondent that suggested negligence until she consulted her attorney. The High Court upheld that there had been no reasonable grounds for the respondent to suspect fault before that consultation, thereby affirming that her claim had not prescribed as the appellant could not demonstrate that she had acquired the requisite knowledge prior to 1 February 2018 ([21]).

The respondent's testimony indicated that her understanding was shaped by a perception that the complications experienced were unavoidable. The appellant's argument that knowledge of facts should be effective from the child's birth was not supported by evidence in the record of the case ([12]–[19]).

C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal and upheld the High Court's finding that the respondent's claim had not prescribed ([26]). The Court emphasized the established legal principles from previous cases and determined that the evaluation of knowledge regarding negligence must consider not only the circumstances but also the claimant's limitations in understanding medical realities. This ruling reinforces protections for claimants in situations of alleged medical negligence, recognizing that laypersons may require professional guidance to fully appreciate the implications of their circumstances and potential claims.

The implications of this decision are significant for future cases involving claims against state entities, establishing necessary thresholds for knowledge and awareness in prescription matters, particularly for vulnerable parties ([25]).
