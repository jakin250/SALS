# Case Note: AAA Investments (Pty) Ltd v Micro Finance Regulatory Council (CCT 51/05)

### FIRAC Case Note

**F - Facts**  
The case revolves around the regulation of micro-lenders in South Africa. The applicant, AAA Investments (Pty) Ltd ("AAA Investments"), challenges the validity of the Rules established by the Micro Finance Regulatory Council ("the Council"). The Council was created as a regulatory institution under the Exemption Notice issued by the Minister of Trade and Industry in June 1999, which allowed certain categories of money-lending transactions to be exempt from the Usury Act 73 of 1968. This regulatory framework was developed to address the exploitation of borrowers, particularly poor individuals who needed small loans but were often charged exorbitant interest rates under standard lending practices.

AAA Investments, operating as a micro-lender, asserts that the Rules promulgated by the Council represent an unlawful usurpation of legislative authority. The High Court ruled in favor of AAA Investments, declaring the Rules unconstitutional. Conversely, the Supreme Court of Appeal ("SCA") held that the Rules were valid, characterizing the Council as a "private regulator" rather than an "organ of state", thereby concluding that the constitutional protections did not apply.

The constitutional validity of the Rules has been debated across various levels of the judicial system, exposing deep divides on the constitutional application of the Rules and the delegation of powers under the Exemption Notice ([paras 1–4], [21]).

**I - Issues**  
The case raises the following key issues:

1. Whether the Rules made by the Council fall within the scope of public power, thus binding them to constitutional standards ([para 25]).
2. If the Council exercises public power, whether that power is legislative in nature ([para 25]).
3. Whether the delegation of rule-making powers to the Council by the Minister is lawful under the Usury Act and the Exemption Notice ([para 25]).
4. Specific constitutional concerns regarding the right to privacy as implicated by certain Rules ([para 25]).

**R - Rules**  
The case invokes various constitutional principles, particularly the rule of law and the legality requirement ([para 26]). The definition of "organ of state" under Section 239 of the South African Constitution, which applies to all institutions that perform public functions, is central to these considerations ([para 30]). The relevant statutory provisions include:

- Section 8(1) of the Constitution, which affirms the applicability of the Bill of Rights to all law.
- Section 15A of the Usury Act, allowing the Minister to exempt categories of money-lending transactions under specific conditions.

Key case law includes relevant precedents, such as **Fedsure Life Assurance Ltd v Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council** and **Pharmaceutical Manufacturers Association of South Africa v President of the Republic of South Africa**, which elaborate on the requirements for lawful delegation and the nature of regulatory powers ([paras 31–32], [43]).

**A - Application**  
The Constitutional Court analyzed whether the Council's rule-making function should be viewed as public or private in nature. The court concluded that the Council operates as an "organ of state" because its functions involved regulating a public industry deeply tied to legislative intent, namely protecting vulnerable borrowers ([para 39]).

The ruling focused on the delegation of powers conferred upon the Council by the Exemption Notice, asserting that it must be interpreted to facilitate the Council's effective regulatory function. The court noted that the Minister retains oversight and ultimately decides on regulatory matters, thereby establishing a framework for compliance with constitutional mandates ([paras 124–132]).

Despite this, the court also contended that certain provisions of the Rules imposed additional conditions that extended beyond the scope of the delegation and were thus invalid ([para 140]).

**C - Conclusion**  
The court upheld the SCA's order, affirming the validity of the Rules while recognizing that the Council had overstepped its authority concerning specific provisions that imposed additional burdens on lenders beyond those enumerated in the Exemption Notice. The ruling underscores the importance of constitutional compliance for regulatory institutions and clarifies the delineation between permissible and impermissible rule-making, allowing for future challenges to specific rules under the privacy rights protected by the Bill of Rights ([para 64]).

This decision holds profound implications for the regulation of the micro-lending industry, balancing the need for oversight with the necessity to protect the rights of both lenders and borrowers. The path forward is clearer for both borrowers and lenders, allowing for scrutiny of both institutional regulations and individual lending practices under the new landscape established by the National Credit Act.
