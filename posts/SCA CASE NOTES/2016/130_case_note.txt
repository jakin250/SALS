Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: The Registrar of Pension Funds v British American Tobacco Pension Fund (664/2015) [2016] ZASCA 130 

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–20])**

The case arises from the interpretation of the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956 (the Act) concerning the allocation and management of surplus funds within a pension fund. The appellant, the Registrar of Pension Funds, challenged the decision of the Gauteng Division of the High Court of South Africa, which had overturned a ruling by the Financial Services Board’s Appeal Board that rejected the British American Tobacco Pension Fund's (the Fund) actuarial valuation report dated 30 September 2007.

Initially, on 1 February 2006, the Fund submitted a surplus apportionment scheme to the registrar for approval, showing an actuarial surplus of R238,259,000 as at 31 March 2002. The registrar approved this scheme on 28 November 2006. However, subsequent actuarial valuations indicated deficits within the Fund, prompting debates over the allowable use of surpluses post-approval. 

On 6 September 2007, the Fund submitted an actuarial report indicating no deficit by utilizing credits from the member surplus account (MSA) to offset deficits reported in prior evaluations. The registrar countered that this interpretation of section 15H(1) of the Act misapplied the accounting of surplus funds, as these should have been allocated as per the approved scheme outlined in section 15D. The matter, which involves complex statutory interpretations, eventually culminated in an appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal.

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 21–22])**

The principal legal issue revolves around whether section 15H(1) of the Pension Funds Act permits the Fund to utilize surplus allocations to settle reported deficits after the surplus apportionment scheme has been approved. The Registrar contended that section 15D(2) requires strict adherence to the allocation as specified in the approved scheme, implying that the rights to such apportionment remain with the beneficiaries of the Fund and cannot be reassigned to mitigate deficits.

The court also needed to determine if the Fund’s interpretation and reliance on section 15H(1) were justified under the circumstances surrounding the actuarial deficits, particularly considering the legislative intent behind these provisions.

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 19–20])**

The case primarily references various sections of the Pension Funds Act:

- **Section 15A** establishes rights concerning actuarial surpluses, mandating that once apportionment is made, these funds belong to the Fund with specific stipulations on usage.
- **Section 15B** addresses the requirement for funds to submit apportionment schemes to the registrar.
- **Section 15D(2)** mandates that credit balances in member surplus accounts must be used as specified in the approved apportionment scheme.
- **Section 15H(1)** states that if a fund has credits in the surplus accounts, these can be employed to cover deficits only after evaluating the surplus as part of a statutory requirements framework.

Judicial interpretation from past cases, particularly concerning statutory construction and beneficiaries’ rights, is also essential in evaluating the application of these sections.

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 21–33])**

The court examined the chronological events leading to the Fund’s valuation contention and the registrar's objections. It found that the approval of the surplus apportionment scheme on 28 November 2006 retroactively affected valuations as of 31 March 2002, indicating that the Fund could not use the credit balance in the MSA for deficit reduction at a subsequent date.

By arguing that beneficiaries’ rights to the surplus were effectively initialized with the approval of the scheme, the court emphasized that the actuarial surplus would typically be used as specified in the approved scheme at the surplus apportionment date. Therefore, invoking section 15H(1) was deemed improper unless the scheme permitted such adjustments post-approval, which was not the case here.

The court also addressed the Fund's assertion that the survival of credit balances in the MSA is lawful under sections 15H and 15D, agreeing that the provisions work harmoniously but must operate in their respective contexts—one under normal conditions and the other after a statutory scheme has taken effect.

Thus, the court upheld the registrar’s initial rejection of the valuation report, concluding that the Appeal Board had correctly determined that the Fund had not adhered to the statutory requirements specified in sections 15D(2) and 15A regarding how surpluses should be utilized once apportioned.

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 33])**

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the registrar’s appeal, reversing the lower court’s decision. The ruling clarified that the Fund cannot invoke section 15H(1) to offset deficits with surplus allocations post-approval of an apportionment scheme without infringing on beneficiaries' rights established under prior provisions of the Act.

The ratio decidendi reflects the importance of adherence to statutory guidelines for pension fund operations, particularly regarding members' rights to surpluses. The court's decision reinforces the need for clarity and strict compliance with surplus allocation processes, aiming to protect beneficiaries and ensure they receive entitlements without undue alteration by fund management to offset financial discrepancies.

This ruling has broad implications for the management of pension funds in South Africa, emphasizing rigorous compliance with legislative frameworks and safeguarding the financial interests of pension beneficiaries, ensuring that their accrued rights are recognized and enforced against arbitrary usage by fund administrators. 

Overall, the decision reiterates the rigorous schemes established under the Pension Funds Act, safeguarding the rights and expectations of members and former members in pension fund structures.
