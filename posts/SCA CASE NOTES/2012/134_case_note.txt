Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note: Agri Wire (Pty) Ltd v The Competition Commissioner [2012] ZASCA 134 (660/2011) 

#### F – Facts 

The case concerns the appellants, Agri Wire (Pty) Ltd and Agri Wire Upington (Pty) Ltd, who challenged a referral made by the Competition Commissioner. The referral pertained to an alleged cartel within the wire and wire-related products sector, arising from a complaint provided by Consolidated Wire Industries (Pty) Ltd (CWI), which had approached the Commissioner under the Corporate Leniency Policy (CLP) ([paras 2–3]).

CWI disclosed its participation in cartel activities during an internal audit and was subsequently granted conditional leniency by the Competition Commission. This leniency allowed the Commission to conduct an investigation and ultimately resulted in a referral to the Competition Tribunal for a ruling against Agri Wire and various other respondents ([paras 3–4]). Agri Wire contested the legality of this referral based on claims that the CLP lacked lawful authority under the Competition Act 89 of 1998 ([para 5]).

The appellants argued that the evidence obtained from CWI was unlawfully acquired due to the alleged unlawfulness of the CLP itself, thus affecting the legitimacy of the subsequent referral to the Tribunal. The application to the North Gauteng High Court seeking to review and set aside the referral was dismissed, leading to this appeal with permission granted ([paras 4–5]).

#### I – Issues 

The legal questions at the core of the case are twofold:

1. Whether the Competition Commission, under the provisions of the Competition Act, has the authority to adopt the corporate leniency policy, which includes granting conditional immunity to cartel participants.
2. Whether the evidence obtained through the CLP is admissible and affects the lawfulness of the referral to the Competition Tribunal ([para 11]).

These issues raise substantial questions regarding the powers vested in the Competition Commission and the implications of the corporate leniency framework on competitive practices.

#### R – Rules 

The case primarily concerns the interpretation and application of the provisions found in the Competition Act 89 of 1998, particularly sections:

- **Section 4(1)**: addresses restrictive practices that constitute a cartel.
- **Section 51**: lays out the referral process for complaints to the Tribunal.
- **Section 59(3)**: pertains to considerations the Tribunal must take into account when determining penalties, which indicates that leniency could inform judicial decisions regarding penalties ([paras 4, 11, 22]).

Additionally, the Corporate Leniency Policy, published by the Competition Commission, outlines the procedure and conditions under which conditional immunity may be granted for cartel participation, serving as a framework to incentivize cooperation ([para 6]).

#### A – Application 

The court thoroughly analyzed the legality and operation of the CLP, asserting that the Competition Commission was indeed empowered to adopt such a policy based on the general objectives of the Competition Act, which aims to promote market transparency and combat anti-competitive practices ([paras 21–23]). The court opined that effective mechanisms, such as the CLP, were necessary for the Commission to fulfill its mandate of investigating and curtailing cartel behavior.

The appellants' argument—claiming that the Commission could not selectively grant leniency or immunity without involving the Tribunal—was dismissed. The court found that the provision allowing the Commission to refer either all or some members of a cartel was consistent with the statutory framework. Moreover, the argument that conditional immunity undermined the Tribunal's authority to impose penalties was flawed, since the immunity granted was within the Commission’s discretion rather than dependent on the Tribunal's final decision ([paras 24–26]).

Consequently, the court ruled that the evidence obtained under the CLP remained admissible and did not taint the referral to the Tribunal. The necessity for the willingness of cartel members to reveal their involvement justified the Commission's procedures, reinforcing the role of the CLP in the broader context of competition law enforcement ([paras 22–26]).

#### C – Conclusion 

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming that the Competition Commission had the requisite authority to implement the Corporate Leniency Policy, which included granting conditional immunity to whistleblowers involved in cartel conduct. The evidence obtained through this avenue was deemed admissible, thereby upholding the referral to the Competition Tribunal as lawful ([paras 27]).

This decision has significant implications for the enforcement of competition law in South Africa, reinforcing the viability of leniency policies as effective tools against cartel behavior while ensuring that the Commission retains its competence to investigate and pursue anti-competitive practices.

The judgment serves as a crucial precedent, clarifying the interplay between the Competition Commission and the Tribunal in terms of authority and procedural integrity while further emphasizing the importance of robust competition policies in maintaining market fairness.
