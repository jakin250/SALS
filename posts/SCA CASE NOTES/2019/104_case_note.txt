# Case Note: Afgri Grain Marketing (Pty) Ltd v Trustees for the Time Being of Copenship Bulkers A/S (in Liquidation) and Others (797/2018) [2019] ZASCA 104

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–8])

The case arose from a complex series of maritime and insolvency disputes involving the MV 'Fonarun Naree' and funds held by the Appellant, Afgri Grain Marketing (Pty) Ltd. The initial matter was heard in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, where an order was granted on 14 March 2018 for the arrest of funds in accounts owned by Afgri. This arrest was confirmed in subsequent proceedings within the same court, resulting in an interlude of legal interpretations focusing on the applicability of various sections of the **Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013** and the **Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983** ([para 3], [para 4]).

Following the confirmation of the arrest order, the Respondents, collectively referred to as Copenship, sought clarification regarding the terms under which the funds were to remain under arrest. They filed an application under section 18(3) of the Superior Courts Act, advocating for the continuity of the arrest despite an appeal being filed by the Appellant ([para 3]).

On 21 June 2019, the Respondents submitted an application for an order to amend the previous costs order, particularly seeking to annul the stipulation regarding costs linked to the application under s 18(3) ([para 2]). They argued that the inclusion of this language in the earlier order constituted an error. The Appellant chose not to oppose this amendment and abided by the court's decision ([para 2], [para 3]).

This case progressed without a traditional hearing, invoking the provisions of section 19(a) of the Superior Courts Act, indicative of the court’s consideration of procedural expediency ([para 1]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 2, 5, 6])

The primary issue at hand revolved around whether the inclusion of a specific provision regarding costs in the court's earlier order was premised on an erroneous understanding. Specifically, it addressed the legitimacy of including costs associated with the application for reconsideration under section 18(3) of the Superior Courts Act when the court's jurisdiction to decide on those costs had not been properly invoked by the Appellant ([para 5], [para 6]).

Another issue was the court's authority to amend its own prior costs order, given that the Appellant did not challenge specific parts of the previous orders regarding the arrest of funds ([para 5]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 4, 6, 8])

The court's rulings were underpinned by several statutory frameworks including:

1. **Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013**: This act clarifies the powers of the courts in terms of appeals, specifically addressing procedural rights concerning costs associated with applications under section 18(3) and 18(4)(ii), which governs the appeal rights concerning interim orders ([paras 4, 6]).
  
2. **Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983**: This act governs the jurisdictional aspects of maritime claims and was sprung in the context of applying costs related to maritime applications in court ([para 1]).

The court’s application of section 173 of the Constitution was also relevant, as it allows court discretion in terms of procedure, reinforcing the authority of the judiciary to amend orders identified as erroneous ([para 4]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 6, 8])

The Supreme Court of Appeal applied the relevant statutory frameworks to determine the legitimacy of the error claimed by Copenship in their request to amend the costs order. It underscored the principle that courts must only consider the issues explicitly raised by the parties and established that without a formal appeal or application concerning the specific cost orders associated with the section 18(3) application, the extension of the costs award to that application was unwarranted.

Given this lack of procedural clips surrounding the amber of the costs, the court deemed the original inclusion in the order concerning section 18(3) as erroneous and thus susceptible to amendment. The justices, led by Wallis JA, reached a consensus that it was necessary to amend paragraph 2(b) of the previously issued order to ensure adherence to correct judicial practice and statutory authority ([para 6], [para 8]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([para 8])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the inclusion of costs pertaining to the application under section 18(3) of the Superior Courts Act in the prior order was erroneous and thus required amendment. The court amended the order to delete the contested phrase, ensuring clarity and compliance with the statutory provisions governing such applications.

This decision underscores the court's commitment to procedural integrity and highlights the importance of adhering closely to the prescribed jurisdictional frameworks. Such rulings resonate with the principles of judicial restraint and clarity, reinforcing the judiciary's responsibility to rectify its orders when procedural missteps occur. The implications of this ruling delineate the boundaries of costs awarded in judicial proceedings, especially under specialized statutory regimes such as those governing admiralty law, and reaffirm the necessity for clear communication and procedural fidelity in legal orders.
