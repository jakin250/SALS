# Case Note: United Manganese of Kalahari (Pty) Ltd v Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service

### FIRAC Method

**F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–6])**

In this matter, the appellant, United Manganese of Kalahari (Pty) Ltd (UMK), challenges additional assessments levied by the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (SARS) regarding the income tax years 2011, 2012, and 2013. On 24 March 2017, SARS announced an audit of UMK’s financial affairs for the specified tax years. Following a series of interactions, including requests for information and witness interviews, SARS issued a letter of audit findings on the basis of section 42(2)(b) of the Tax Administration Act 28 of 2011 (TAA). 

UMK was provided 21 business days to respond to the audit findings, which was subsequently extended to 30 August 2019, given the complexity of the case. After receiving responses from UMK, SARS concluded the audit five months later on 31 January 2020. The findings resulted in considerable adjustments to UMK’s taxable income, leading to substantial tax liabilities for the years in question, combined with interest and penalties. 

On 17 February 2020, UMK issued a notice regarding its intention to initiate legal proceedings against SARS, seeking to have the additional assessments reviewed and set aside, while also challenging the interpretation of critical tax law provisions. UMK’s application raised issues surrounding constitutional law related to jurisdiction and proper procedure in tax disputes.

**I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 7–12])**

The core legal issues presented in this appeal revolve around the jurisdiction of the High Court regarding tax disputes, specifically whether UMK was entitled to bypass the Tax Court in contesting the additional assessments. The following questions emerge:

1. Did UMK provide sufficient grounds for the High Court to assume jurisdiction over the dispute, as required by section 105 of the TAA?
2. What are the implications of the jurisdictional restrictions prescribed by section 105 of the TAA on the right to fair administrative action under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA)?
3. Was there any deviation from legislative requirements in the application that would justify the Court’s intervention as per UMK's claims?

**R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 10–11])**

The primary legal authority governing appeals of tax assessments in this case is section 105 of the TAA, which stipulates that a taxpayer may only dispute an assessment via the objection and appeal procedures unless the High Court expressly permits otherwise. This emphasizes that the Tax Court is the default jurisdiction for tax disputes.

Additionally, precedents such as *Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service v Rappa Resources (Pty) Ltd* were pertinent, reinforcing that section 105 of the TAA reflects a clear legislative intention to streamline tax disputes through the Tax Court system and that appeals to the High Court should only occur under exceptional circumstances.

**A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 11–12])**

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) upheld the findings of the Gauteng Division of the High Court, arguing that UMK failed to establish a basis for the High Court to deviate from the established jurisdictional path set by section 105 of the TAA. The court reiterated that the High Court's entitlement to hear tax disputes is not automatic; it requires a proper pleading and justification for circumstantial deviation from the prescribed tribunal.

Moreover, the SCA examined the wording of section 105 and the relevant amendments, emphasizing that its purpose was to ensure that internal remedies be exhausted and disputes be resolved at the Tax Court level. The court ruled that UMK's assertions did not merit the exceptional justification necessary for High Court intervention, thereby affirming the principle delineated in prior case law that the legislative framework aims to preserve the integrity and separation of functions between the Tax Court and Higher Courts.

**C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 11–12])**

The court dismissed the appeal, highlighting that UMK did not present adequate grounds to justify a deviation from the normal jurisdictional requirements set forth by the TAA. UMK’s failure to properly plead a case for the High Court to take jurisdiction meant that the appeal could not succeed. The ratio decidendi underscores that taxpayers must follow prescribed internal processes regarding disputes, aligning with the broader legislative intent to limit High Court involvement in tax affairs.

This judgment not only affirms the procedural strictures under the TAA but also subtly reinforces the importance of adherence to statutory frameworks to promote efficiency and resolve disputes effectively at the appropriate legal levels. The ruling has significant implications for taxonomy in tax disputes and highlights the importance of navigating administrative law within the constraints of established procedures. 

In conclusion, the directive from section 105 is clear: taxpayers must utilize the Tax Court for disputes regarding assessments unless a compelling case is presented to warrant High Court involvement, thus serving to consolidate tax matters within specialized forums designed to handle them effectively.
