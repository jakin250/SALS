Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# FIRAC Case Note: The Member of the Executive Council, Department of Health, North West v NAM obo TN (035/2020) [2021] ZASCA 105

## F – Facts

In this case, the appeal arises from a claim initiated by the plaintiff, acting on behalf of her minor child, TN, for delictual damages against the Member of the Executive Council (MEC) for Health in the North West Province, based on alleged medical negligence ([para 1]). The incident in question occurred on 17 December 2003, when the plaintiff, at full term of her pregnancy, experienced labour pains around 03:00. She arrived at the Makgobistad clinic in a hired vehicle at approximately 07:50, only to find it closed until 08:00, contrary to its supposed 24-hour operation ([paras 1–8]).

Upon her admission, the plaintiff alleges that inadequate and negligent care from the nursing staff caused TN to suffer a hypoxic-ischaemic injury at birth, leading to cerebral palsy. The plaintiff claimed the nursing staff failed to adequately attend to her during and after delivery, including not administering oxygen or securing necessary medical assistance ([paras 6–10],[25]). The nursing staff present were a qualified midwifery sister and an assistant nurse, neither of whom were assigned to provide specialized obstetric care ([paras 8–9]).

In her initial claim, the High Court ruled in favor of the MEC on the grounds of insufficient evidence to prove negligence and causation, which the Full Court later overturned ([paras 7–8]). The MEC subsequently sought leave to appeal this decision, leading to the instant appeal ([paras 7–8]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issue in this case centers on whether the nursing staff's actions during the delivery constituted medical negligence, thereby making the MEC vicariously liable for TN's subsequent injuries ([paras 1, 25]). More specifically, the court was tasked with determining:

1. Were the nursing staff negligent in their treatment of the plaintiff and TN?
2. Did any negligence on the part of the nursing staff cause TN’s hypoxic-ischaemic injury leading to cerebral palsy?

Additionally, procedural issues arose regarding the late filing of the appeal record, and whether such delays warranted condonation ([paras 2–5]).

## R – Rules

The court relied on established principles of delictual liability concerning medical negligence, asserting that:

1. A medical professional must adhere to the standard of care expected within their profession ([para 20]), as per *Van Wyk v Lewis* 1924 AD 438 and *Mitchell v Dixon* 1914 AD 519.
2. The burden of proof lies with the plaintiff to establish that the defendant's alleged negligence was the cause of the injuries sustained by the minor child.
3. Condonation for late filing may be granted at the court's discretion based on factors such as the reason for the delay and the importance of the case ([para 2]).

Additionally, issues of record-keeping for medical cases were examined, reflecting on the responsibilities of healthcare providers to maintain accurate records ([para 25]).

## A – Application

In applying these principles, the Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized both the actions of the nursing staff and the evidence presented. The court noted:

- The nursing staff indicated they provided adequate immediate post-delivery care, monitoring TN for several hours before discharge, and found no evidence of abnormalities at that time ([paras 17-20]).
- The expert testimony suggested that the hypoxic event likely occurred at birth and that immediate interventions post-birth were not warranted given TN's stable condition after resuscitation ([paras 14-16]).
- Notably, the loss of critical medical records complicated the case, making it challenging for the plaintiff to establish a clear link between any alleged negligence and the resulting injuries ([para 20]).
- The court also highlighted that both parties' experts ultimately agreed on the uncertainties surrounding the timing and specifics of the alleged injuries, making it difficult to assert negligence ([para 19]).

Ultimately, the court determined that the plaintiff had failed to meet the burden of proof required to establish negligence by the nursing staff ([para 24]). As such, there was no need to consider causation further ([para 24]).

Regarding the issue of late filing, the court acknowledged the disruption caused by the national lockdown during the COVID-19 pandemic but found that the delay was satisfactorily explained. Thus, condonation for the late filing was granted ([para 5]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the MEC’s appeal and dismissed the plaintiff's claims. The court concluded that the nursing staff did not breach the standard of care required of them, thus absolving the MEC of vicarious liability for TN's injuries ([paras 26]). The case emphasizes the critical importance of providing comprehensive medical care while adhering to established protocols, especially in cases involving the delicate nature of maternal and neonatal health.

The ruling also carries implications for healthcare providers on the necessity of proper record-keeping to ensure that future claims can be accurately assessed and handled ([paras 25, 26]). Additionally, it reinforces the notion that the burden of proof in negligence cases, particularly those involving complex medical issues, remains firmly on the claimant.
