# Case Note: Bliss Brands (Pty) Ltd v Advertising Regulatory Board NPC and Others [2023] ZACC 19

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–12])
In **Bliss Brands (Pty) Ltd v Advertising Regulatory Board NPC and Others**, the applicant, Bliss Brands, is a company engaged in the manufacturing of cleaning and hygiene products ([para 1]). The case revolves around the authority of the Advertising Regulatory Board NPC (the ARB), a non-statutory body, to regulate advertising for firms that are not its members. Bliss Brands is notably a non-member of the ARB ([para 3]). 

The core function of the ARB, funded by its members and some non-members, is to ensure that its members' advertising practices comply with its established Code of Advertising Practice, designed to protect consumers and maintain professionalism ([para 3]). Despite being a non-member, Bliss Brands participated in the ARB proceedings after complaints were lodged by its competitors, Colgate-Palmolive ([paras 6–7]). The ARB’s ruling ultimately directed Bliss Brands to cease distribution of its Securex product due to alleged breaches of the Code regarding packaging imitation ([para 8]).

Bliss Brands subsequently filed a review application in the Gauteng Division of the High Court challenging the final decision of the ARB's Final Appeal Committee (FAC). The High Court initially ruled in favor of Bliss Brands, declaring the ARB’s jurisdiction over non-members unconstitutional. However, the Supreme Court of Appeal reversed this decision, establishing that Bliss Brands had effectively submitted to the ARB’s jurisdiction through its participation in the proceedings ([para 12]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 13–14])
The central legal issue in this matter is whether Bliss Brands can be deemed to have consented to the jurisdiction of the ARB, despite its status as a non-member ([para 15]). If Bliss Brands has submitted to the ARB's jurisdiction, it negates the need to address the various constitutional claims raised regarding the validity of the ARB's actions and the implications for non-members. 

Other issues include the interpretation of the ARB's authority over non-members and the broader implications of this authority within the framework of constitutional and administrative law ([para 14]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 12–20])
The court relied upon several legal principles when examining the submission to jurisdiction. The doctrine of submission to jurisdiction, as established in **Purser v Sales**, posits that a party participating in a judicial process without objection cannot later contest that court's authority ([para 16]). The Supreme Court of Appeal emphasized that participation in the jurisdictional proceedings showcases inherent acceptance of jurisdiction ([para 20]).

Furthermore, the constitutional implications of the case were significant. Bliss Brands argued that the ARB’s procedures violated its right to procedural fairness under Section 34 of the Constitution, given that the non-member could not adequately participate in the arbitration process without facing adverse consequences. However, the court underscored the ARB’s role as a facilitator for its members to regulate advertising standards, thereby framing the constitutional rights of the ARB's members as justifiable ([para 14]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 15–20])
The court, in its analysis, maintained that because Bliss Brands actively participated in the ARB proceedings without contesting its jurisdiction, it effectively consented to the ARB's authority over it ([para 15]). By engaging in the merits of the complaint and seeking remittal to the ARB for further consideration, Bliss Brands's conduct signified clear acceptance of jurisdiction ([para 16]).

Moreover, the argument positing coercion was dismissed. The court reasoned that while non-members might face adverse implications from ARB’s directives, their participation could stem from a voluntary choice. The court concluded that Bliss Brands's claims of coercion lacked substantial proof, as other non-members could hypothetically choose to abstain from ARB processes altogether without being prevented from advertising through other means ([paras 17–20]). 

The predominant conclusion drawn by the court was reflective of a broader principle in administrative law—the necessity for entities wishing to contest jurisdiction must do so explicitly, particularly when their lack of objection results in their engagement within proceedings ([para 20]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 23–23])
The Constitutional Court concluded that Bliss Brands had indeed consented to the jurisdiction of the ARB, rendering the appeal’s other grounds moot. As a result, it refused leave to appeal with costs, including the costs of two counsel ([para 23]). This decision underscores the importance of clear procedural adherence and the recognition of implicit acceptance of jurisdiction through participation within regulatory frameworks.

The ruling emphasizes the need for businesses, particularly non-members of regulatory organizations, to be aware of the implications of engaging with industry disputes within established bodies. This precedent offers guidance on jurisdictional matters in future cases, reinforcing the doctrine of submission and the broader context of regulatory authority within South Africa's advertising industry.
