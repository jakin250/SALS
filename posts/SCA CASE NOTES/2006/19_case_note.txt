Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2006

# Case Note: Road Accident Fund v G S O Guedes [2006] SCA 18 (RSA)

## F – Facts
The appeal before the Supreme Court of Appeal concerns an award of R3,119,048 for future loss of earnings granted by the Johannesburg High Court (Boruchowitz J) to the respondent, G S O Guedes, following a motor vehicle accident that occurred on November 12, 2000. The respondent, born on February 20, 1978, sustained serious bodily injuries resulting in her claiming damages from the appellant, the Road Accident Fund. The appellant conceded the merits of the claim; however, they contested the High Court's award regarding future loss of earning capacity ([1]–[2]).

The calculation for potential loss was based on an actuarial projection, establishing that the respondent would have earned R7,954,150 over her career without the accident. Post-accident projections indicated an earning capacity of R5,770,981. The actuary recommended applying a contingency deduction of 10% in the 'but for scenario' and 40% in the 'having regard to scenario' to account for unforeseen contingencies, which was partially accepted by the High Court. The High Court confirmed the 10% deduction for the 'but for scenario' but adjusted the second figure to 30%, resulting in the aforementioned award ([3]).

The appellant contended that the judgment was erroneous, advocating for a lesser deduction (40% in the 'but for scenario' and 20% in the 'having regard to scenario'). They further posited that the trial court misdirected itself in assessing the respondent’s potential and the resultant contingency deductions ([4]).

## I – Issues
The key legal issues on appeal were:
1. Whether the trial court misdirected itself in the contingency deductions applied in calculating the respondent's future loss of earnings ([5]–[6]).
2. Whether the amount awarded is excessively different from what an appellate court would consider appropriate, thereby justifying intervention on appeal ([5]).

## R – Rules
The court reviewed legal principles applicable to the assessment of damages for loss of earnings, as articulated in case law such as *Southern Insurance Association v Bailey NO* and others which establish that the calculation of damages, particularly for future loss of earning capacity, is inherently speculative and estimates may not conform to precise mathematical calculations. The appellate court will interfere primarily if there has been a material misdirection or striking disparity in the award made ([8]).

In terms of contingency deductions, decisions in previous cases indicate a sliding scale approach that may guide the court's discretion in these matters. The judgment referenced considerations from *Goodall v President Insurance*, affirming that deductions are to be made to reflect the uncertainties of life ([9]).

## A – Application
Following the facts and governing rules, the appellate court scrutinized the findings of the trial court critically. Notably, it considered an error in applying a 10% deduction for the 'but for scenario', as this figure mischaracterized the respondent's age and circumstances per statistical guidelines. The court acknowledged that the appropriate deduction should have been 20% based on the actuary’s advice and further contextual evaluations ([10]).

Moreover, the appellate court confirmed that evidence supported the trial court's assumption regarding the respondent's potential for promotions and future earnings. The court upheld the notion that the respondent would have likely attained high positions in her field based on her prior achievements, despite challenges arising from her injuries ([12]).

In relation to the deduction of 30% in the 'having regard to scenario', the court found the evidence sufficiently substantiated this figure, especially given the consensus from medical experts regarding the respondent’s ongoing impairments. The appellate court ultimately concluded that there was no misjudgment regarding this assessment and affirmed the stability of the High Court’s adjusted amount in this respect ([17]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal altered the trial court's award by adjusting the contingency deduction in the 'but for scenario' from 10% to 20%, modifying the total amount awarded from R3,119,048 to R2,323,633. The court emphasized that the appeal was allowed partly due to misdirection rather than a stark contrast in the amounts considered appropriate ([21]).

This outcome highlights the court's stance on the importance of precise calculations in damage awards and affirms the principle that the discretionary powers of trial courts in compensation matters should be safeguarded unless clear misdirections warrant appellate interference. The implications of this judgment resonate in personal injury law, particularly in how future loss of income claims are computed and evaluated through actuarial methods and expert testimony.
