# Case Note: United Democratic Movement v The President of the Republic of South Africa [2002] CCT 23/02

## FIRAC Method

### Facts
[1-10] The case originated from a significant political realignment in South Africa following the 1999 elections. The Democratic Party (DP), Federal Alliance (FA), and New National Party (NNP) contested elections separately but later formed the Democratic Alliance (DA). In November 2001, the NNP withdrew from the DA, which left local government representatives in a predicament: they could not change their party allegiance without losing their legislative seats due to prior anti-defection clauses introduced in the interim Constitution. As a response, Parliament enacted several laws in June 2002, notably the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa Amendment Act 18 of 2002 (First Amendment Act), the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa Second Amendment Act 21 of 2002 (Second Amendment Act), the Local Government: Municipal Structures Amendment Act 20 of 2002 (Local Government Amendment Act), and the Loss or Retention of Membership of National and Provincial Legislatures Act 22 of 2002 (Membership Act), allowing for limited party-switching or "floor crossing."

[8-9] Leading the challenge against these Acts was the United Democratic Movement (UDM), which claimed that these legislative changes were unconstitutional. The Cape High Court initially dealt with the matter and suspended the implementation of the Acts pending the outcome of the constitutional challenge raised by the UDM. The matter was then heard by the Constitutional Court in July 2002.

### Issues
[11-12] The central issue before the Constitutional Court was whether the amended provisions allowing floor crossing were constitutional. The Court emphasized that its inquiry was not to assess the merits of political strategies but to determine the constitutionality of the legislation in question. Key legal questions included:
1. Do these amendments undermine the basic structure of the Constitution?
2. Are the amendments consistent with the founding values of the Constitution, particularly concerning democratic governance and proportional representation?
3. Do they infringe upon citizens’ electoral rights as prescribed by the Bill of Rights?

### Rules
[12-14] The Court referred to several rules and legal authorities:
- Amendments to the Constitution must comply with the procedures set forth in section 74, which stipulates necessary majorities for different types of amendments.
- The Constitution, as amended, cannot be challenged on grounds of inconsistency unless such amendments do not follow the prescribed processes. The basic structure doctrine, recognized in *Premier of KwaZulu-Natal v President of the Republic of South Africa* [1996] (1) SA 769 (CC); which posits that certain core features of a constitutional order should be immutable unless passed by proper means.
- Founding values, as embodied in section 1 of the Constitution, provide the framework for interpreting laws and assessing their validity in regard to democratic governance.

### Application
[15-72] The Constitutional Court evaluated the arguments presented by both the UDM and the respondents concerning the floor-crossing legislation. The Court found that the amendments did not violate the basic structure of the Constitution.
- The Basic Structure Doctrine: The Court held that changes to the electoral system associated with floor crossing do not fundamentally alter or destroy the Constitution’s basic framework. 
- The Founding Values: The Court interpreted the founding values of democracy to encompass various forms of governance. While proportional representation is essential, the absence of an anti-defection clause does not necessarily breach multi-party democracy nor invalidate the democratic process.
- Voter Rights: The court assessed section 19 of the Constitution, concluding that voters could hold representatives accountable only at the polls and not retain control over representative conduct between elections. The amendments had not infringed any existing rights.

### Conclusion
[115-121] Ultimately, the Constitutional Court found that the amendments allowing floor-crossing were constitutional except for the Membership Act, which was declared invalid for being passed outside the reasonable timeframe stipulated by transitional provisions within the Constitution. The ruling underscored the importance of procedural adherence in constitutional amendments and reinforced the legislative powers of Parliament, affirming that electoral system changes, as long as they are properly enacted, do not infringe upon the basic principles of the South African Constitution. 

### Implications
This judgment reflects the evolving landscape of South African politics, particularly the tension between stable party representation and the dynamic nature of political affiliations. It clarifies the boundaries within which Parliament can operate regarding electoral laws, signifying a delicate balance between promoting inclusive governance and preserving the stability of democracy.
