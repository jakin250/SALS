# Case Note: Minister of Mineral Resources of the RSA v Sishen Iron Ore (2013)

**Neutral Citation:** Minister of Mineral Resources of the RSA v Sishen Iron Ore (394/12) [2013] ZASCA 50 (28 March 2013)

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

---

### FIRAC Case Note

**F - Facts**  
The disputes in this case arose from the application of the provisions of the **Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA)**, specifically regarding the transition of mineral rights post-MPRDA. Sishen Iron Ore Company (Pty) Ltd (SIOC) and ArcelorMittal South Africa Limited (AMSA) were co-holders of an 'old order mining right' concerning iron ore on certain properties in the Sishen Mine, Northern Cape ([para 1]). 

After the MPRDA came into effect, SIOC successfully converted its old order mining right before the designated five-year period expired; however, AMSA failed to do so. In response, the Deputy Director General (Deputy DG) of the Department of Mineral Resources purportedly granted a prospecting right to Imperial Crown Trading 289 (Pty) Ltd (ICT) concerning the properties relevant to AMSA's old order right ([para 1]). SIOC challenged this decision, initiating legal proceedings against the Minister of Mineral Resources and others ([paras 4-6]).

The court held that SIOC was the sole holder of the mining right after its conversion, which asserted that AMSA’s failure to convert its share meant it ceased to hold any rights to the old order mining right ([paras 3-4],[54-55]). The court a quo’s decisions were upheld on appeal, confirming SIOC's exclusive rights to the relevant properties ([para 2]).

**I - Issues**  
The legal issues before the court included:

1. **The effect of SIOC’s conversion of its old order right:** What happens to SIOC’s old order mining right upon conversion under Item 7 of Schedule II of the MPRDA, particularly involving the co-holder AMSA’s failure to convert its share? ([para 2])
2. **Validity of ministerial decisions:** What is the status of the minister's sanctioning if the conversion was incorrectly granted and what impact does this have on any subsequent rights granted to ICT? ([para 2])
3. **Rights post non-conversion:** What are the implications of AMSA not lodging for conversion before the identified deadline ([paras 2-3])?

**R - Rules**  
The case was adjudicated primarily on the provisions articulated in the following statutory frameworks:

1. **Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA).**
   - Particularly, **Item 7 of Schedule II** outlining the transitional arrangements concerning old order mining rights explains the process of conversion ([para 26]).
   - Item 7(8) specifically states that failure to lodge the right for conversion results in the cessation of the old order mining right ([paras 54-55]).

2. **Case law precedents:**
   - **Oudekraal Estates (Pty) Ltd v City of Cape Town** 2004 (6) SA 222 (SCA), which articulates that an administrative decision stands until challenged or set aside ([para 51]).
   - Relevant judgments providing context to the implication of the MPRDA and transitional rights, especially **Holcim SA (Pty) Ltd v Prudent Investors (Pty) Ltd** [2011] 1 All SA 364 (SCA) and **Xstrata South Africa (Pty) Ltd v SFF Association** [2012] (5) SA 60 (SCA) ([para 25]).

**A - Application**  
The court embarked on a detailed examination of the facts vis-à-vis the applicable legal principles and the MPRDA’s intention to facilitate existing mining operations while transitioning to a new legal framework. It found that SIOC's timely conversion effectively resulted in SIOC holding an exclusive right to mine the properties in question as of the conversion date, regardless of subsequent actions taken by AMSA or the DG ([paras 50-55]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal corroborated the action and interpretation of the MPRDA by emphasizing the force of the transitional provisions. The court illustrated that AMSA's failure to convert its rights before the expiry rendered its rights non-existent as per Item 7(8), effectively eliminating any grounds for ICT's subsequent claim to rights on the same properties ([paras 53-54]).

Moreover, the court dismissed the argument that AMSA's old order right should have been used to grant ICT a prospecting right, reinforcing that subsequent actions based on AMSA's lapsed rights could not be valid ([paras 53-54]). The court thus affirmed the legal framework under the MPRDA, asserting that SIOC became the sole right holder given the proper application of the law ([paras 55–56]).

**C - Conclusion**  
The court concluded that as a matter of law, SIOC emerged as the exclusive holder of the mining rights concerning the Table I and II properties, ceasing AMSA's 21.4% share following its non-compliance with conversion protocols. The judgments against the Minister and other state appellants were upheld, affirming AMSA and ICT's challenges as invalid. This ruling significantly clarifies the implications of the MPRDA's transitional provisions, particularly for co-holder situations failing to comply with conversion requirements.

The resulting orders confirm SIOC's exclusive rights and the dismissal of any rights supposedly granted to ICT, reinforcing compliance with the MPRDA's directives and protecting SIOC’s extensive operational interests ([paras 61]). 

This case serves as a pivotal reference on the handling of mineral rights amidst transitioning legal frameworks, ensuring that parties involved in co-holding arrangements understand the rigorous compliance required under the MPRDA. Future stakeholders will need to navigate these processes with vigilance to secure their mining interests effectively.
