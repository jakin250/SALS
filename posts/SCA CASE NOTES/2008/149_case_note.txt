Source Folder: 2008

# FIRAC Case Note: Matsabu v S (186/08) [2008] ZASCA 149

## F – Facts
The appellant, T P Matsabu, was a law enforcement officer employed by the Maqhaka Traffic Department. On **18 December 2003**, he was arrested during an anti-corruption operation conducted by a designated unit of the Free State Provincial Administration, aimed at investigating fraud and corruption ([para 1]). He faced charges under section 1(1)(b) of the **Corruption Act 94 of 1992**, specifically for allegedly accepting a **R300** bribe from Inspector Wilbers in exchange for not issuing a traffic summons following her excessive speeding ([para 1]).

At his trial in the magistrate's court, Matsabu pleaded not guilty. His counsel acknowledged that he had been operating a speed trap on the day of the incident, stopping Wilbers’ vehicle and refusing her initial request to avoid prosecution. Wilbers then allegedly pushed **R300** into Matsabu’s pocket, following which he was arrested ([para 2]). The prosecution presented Wilbers and three other witnesses involved in the operation, while Matsabu testified in his defense. Ultimately, he was convicted and sentenced to two years’ imprisonment ([para 3]).

An appeal was made to the Full Court of the Free State Provincial Division against his conviction, which was dismissed ([para 4]). Leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal was subsequently granted ([para 4]).

## I – Issues
The principal legal questions arose concerning the admissibility of evidence obtained through the undercover operation, particularly in light of section **252A of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977**. Two key issues were identified:

1. Whether the evidence obtained through the “trap” constituted entrapment such that it should be ruled inadmissible at trial because it exceeded merely providing an opportunity to commit an offence ([paras 6-7]).
2. Whether the appellant received a fair trial, given the concerns related to the conduct of the undercover operation ([para 7]).

## R – Rules
The relevant statutory framework is provided by **section 252A** of the Criminal Procedure Act. It permits law enforcement officials to use traps and undercover operations for the purpose of preventing and investigating crimes, with specifics addressing the admissibility of evidence obtained through such means ([para 6]). The law stipulates that while a trap can be used to provide an opportunity for official misconduct, it must not go beyond that role to the extent that it induces the commission of an offence.

Subsection (2) of section 252A lists various factors to be considered when determining if the conduct exceeded merely providing an opportunity ([para 7]). These factors include:
- Prior approval for the operation ([para 6]).
- The nature of the offence involved.
- The conduct’s influence on the accused’s voluntary commission of the crime ([paras 6-9]).

## A – Application
In assessing the facts against the legal standards outlined in section **252A**, the court noted that Matsabu's defense relied on the claim that Wilbers's conduct amounted to more than merely providing an opportunity to commit a corrupt act. Ultimately, however, the court found:

1. **No Entrapment Established**: The evidence indicated that Wilbers did not solicit the bribe nor tempt Matsabu into criminal conduct. Instead, her actions were deemed neutral (i.e., she did not engage in deceptive behaviour specifically aimed at inducing Matsabu to accept the bribe). The court stressed that Matsabu had the discretion to issue or withhold a ticket, which he failed to exercise ([para 16]).

2. **Fair Trial Considerations**: Matsabu's counsel contended that there was a trial-within-a-trial issue regarding the admissibility of the trap evidence. However, the court upheld that the magistrate did not err in declining a separate hearing on this matter, noting that Matsabu's legal representative did not sufficiently challenge the admissibility of evidence on grounds beyond mere noncompliance with procedural guidelines ([paras 8-9],[10]).

3. **Assessment of Evidence**: The court determined that even if there was a deviation from prescribed conduct in the operation's execution, this did not have a causal effect on the commission of the crime by Matsabu ([paras 12-13]). The concerns raised about the alleged unfairness were deemed not significant enough to undermine the trial's integrity ([paras 11-14]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Matsabu's appeal, holding that the evidence obtained through the undercover operation was admissible and that the operation did not exceed the bounds set by legislative standards for entrapment ([paras 18]). The court maintained that the conduct of the complainant did not induce the appellant to commit the offence beyond providing an opportunity, affirming the integrity of the trial process as fair ([par 16]). This decision illustrates the balance between the need for effective law enforcement through undercover operations and the protection of individual rights against entrapment, reaffirming the established legal standards under section **252A**. 

The ruling carries implications for how undercover operations may be conducted and reviewed in future cases, reinforcing the need for compliance with statutory guidelines while providing law enforcement the latitude to engage in proactive measures against corruption.
