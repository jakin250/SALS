Nkabinde and Another v Judicial Service Commission and Others [2016] ZACC 25

The case centered on an application for rescission submitted by Justices Baatise Elizabeth Nkabinde and Christopher Nyaole Jafta, both of whom were applicants and Justices of the Constitutional Court of South Africa ([paras 1–2]).

Constitutional Court 2016 [2016] ZACC 25

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case centered on an application for rescission submitted by Justices Baatise Elizabeth Nkabinde and Christopher Nyaole Jafta, both of whom were applicants and Justices of the Constitutional Court of South Africa ([paras 1–2]). The application sought to rescind an order made by the Constitutional Court on 16 May 2016, which dismissed their earlier application for leave to appeal against a Supreme Court of Appeal decision ([para 1]). This dismissal stemmed from the incapacity of the Court to adjudicate on the merits due to several justices being disqualified because of conflicts of interest. Specifically, the origins of the conflict dated back to a complaint made by the applicants, among others, against Judge President John Hlophe, which had been a matter of significant public interest ([paras 2–3]).

The applicants had initially brought the complaint against Hlophe in 2008. Following a protracted legal process involving the Judicial Service Commission and various courts, including the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal, the case reached the Constitutional Court ([para 2]). However, due to disqualifications among the justices—some having been complainants or involved in mediating the matter—the Court lacked the required quorum to hear the application ([para 10]). Following deliberation, the Court dismissed the applicants’ application for leave to appeal, applying the principle established in Hlophe v Freedom Under Law regarding the incapacity of courts due to conflicts ([paras 12–13]).

I – Issues

The key constitutional issues raised in this case pertained to the procedural fairness and the right of access to court under Section 34 of the Constitution. The applicants contended:

  1. Whether the Court erroneously dismissed their application without inviting them to submit additional arguments, thereby undermining their right to a fair hearing ([para 15].
  2. Whether the disqualification of several justices, who nonetheless participated in deliberating the dismissive order, constituted an irregularity that warrants rescission of the earlier order ([para 15]).

R – Rules

The Court grounded its analysis chiefly in the procedural rules outlined in the Constitution and the Rules of the Constitutional Court. Notably, Rule 42(1)(a) of the Uniform Rules allows for rescission of orders that were erroneously granted in the absence of a party ([para 14]). Rule 19 of the Constitutional Court Rules regulates applications for leave to appeal and permits summary dismissal without oral or additional arguments ([paras 4, 6, 21]).

Further, the precedent set in Hlophe was pivotal. The Supreme Court of Appeal's decision and the procedural practices it inspired informed how the court viewed its obligation to maintain a quorum and be mindful of constitutional principles ([paras 10, 12]). The Court also cited previous cases [(Democratic Party; Pennington; Mphahlele)] that confirmed the summary dismissal practice as consistent with the interests of justice and constitutional norms ([para 8]).

A – Application

The Court found the applicants' arguments for rescission unconvincing. Specifically, it noted that:

  1. Right to a Fair Hearing: The claim that their right to make representations was undermined was rejected. The Court elaborated that its established practice permitted summary dismissal without oral argument, a procedure the applicants were well familiar with ([para 21]). The absence of rebuttal or additional argument from the applicants in their founding affidavits indicated they did not proactively engage with the Court's summation process as they should have ([paras 23–24]).
  2. Disqualification of Justices: The claim that the participation of disqualified Justices in the decision was an irregularity was also rejected. While acknowledging that disqualification led to quorum issues, the Court maintained that it adhered to established practices when determining the applicants' case ([para 26]). The Court noted that all relevant justices were well aware of their disqualifications and did not deviate from the established procedures, which had already been upheld in previous cases ([para 24]).

The Court emphasized the importance of resolving the longstanding complaint against Judge Hlophe, asserting that undue delays would not serve justice ([para 29]). The lengthy history of the case and its implications informed the Court’s decision to dismiss the rescission application primarily due to the procedural limits and the absence of a substantive legal basis for the claims made by the applicants.

C – Conclusion

In a unanimous judgment, the Constitutional Court dismissed the application for rescission. The Court affirmed that the procedural safeguards in place during the initial decision-making process were consistent with the Constitution, and the dismissal of the application for leave to appeal on the grounds of lack of quorum and procedural rigidity was valid. The Court underscored the necessity of bringing judicial complaints to resolution promptly, citing concerns about the ongoing delays ([para 30]).

The case serves as a critical reminder of the interplay between procedural justice, the management of judicial conflicts, and the need for efficient resolution of complaints within the judicial system. It reinforces the significance of familiarizing oneself with procedural norms and the potential ramifications of disqualification in judicial contexts, especially within high courts dealing with sensitive matters.
