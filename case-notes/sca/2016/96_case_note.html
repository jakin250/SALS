Supreme Court of Appeal

S v Motloung (182/15) [2016] ZASCA 96

Motloung was on parole at the time of the murder, having previously been convicted of armed robbery and other related firearm offenses ([4]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 96

F – Facts

The case of S v Motloung ([1]) concerns an appeal by the State against a sentence imposed on Oupa Motloung (the respondent) by the Gauteng Local Division, Johannesburg ([1]). Motloung was convicted of murder, as well as unlawful possession of a firearm and ammunition, under the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 and the Firearms Control Act 60 of 2000 respectively. Initially, he was sentenced to 14 years’ imprisonment for the murder, with 6 years suspended for 5 years, and 6 years for the unlawful possession of a firearm, with half suspended for the same period. The sentences were ordered to run concurrently, resulting in an effective sentence of 8 years ([2]).

Motloung was on parole at the time of the murder, having previously been convicted of armed robbery and other related firearm offenses ([4]). The murder occurred during a physical altercation in which Motloung shot the deceased, Sandile Caleb Madalane, following a heated argument regarding Madalane's romantic advances toward Motloung's companion. The argument escalated, culminating in Motloung retrieving a firearm and fatally shooting Madalane multiple times, even after he had fallen to the ground ([3]).

I – Issues

The appeal raised several key issues:

  1. Whether the sentence imposed for murder was appropriate, considering the nature of the crime and prior convictions ([1]).
  2. Whether the court erred in directing the parole board how to handle the unexpired portion of a previous sentence ([1]).
  3. The validity of the implied repeal of certain provisions of the Criminal Law Amendment Act by the Firearms Control Act ([1]).
  4. The appropriateness of the costs order made by the lower court ([1]).

R – Rules

The rules governing the appellate court's intervention in sentencing are outlined in several precedents:

  1. An appellate court may interfere with a lower court's sentence if it perceives a material misdirection ([6]). The standard established in S v Malgas emphasizes that the objective seriousness of the crime and public interest must be taken into account ([6]–[8]).
  2. In S v Sadler and S v Cwele, the courts reiterated that a discernible disparity between the imposed sentence and what an appellate court would consider appropriate may justify interference, even absent a material misdirection ([8]).
  3. Section 280 of the Criminal Procedure Act (CPA) permits courts to impose concurrent sentences; however, courts cannot dictate parole board procedures ([16]).
  4. Regarding the relationship between the Criminal Law Amendment Act and Firearms Control Act, Khumalo established that later statutes do not repeal earlier ones unless explicitly indicated ([22]).
  5. In criminal cases, cost orders are generally uncommon and should be determined based on arguments presented by both parties ([24]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) found that the sentence for murder was indeed startlingly inappropriate in light of the significant violence involved, particularly since Motloung was a second offender who committed murder while out on parole ([11]).

Misdirections by the Court a quo

The SCA identified several misdirections in the lower court's reasoning:

  1. The lower court improperly emphasized mitigating factors, including a misjudgment regarding the deceased's location at the tavern ([10]).
  2. Mischaracterization of Motloung's mental state during the altercation, suggesting a "snap" reaction to an immediate provocation, failed to recognize the premeditated aspects of fetching the firearm ([10]).

These misdirections, combined with a dismal assessment of aggravating factors, such as Motloung's prior convictions and the calculated nature of the murder, indicated that the lower court had incorrectly balanced the relevant considerations ([12]).

Parole Board Direction

The SCA clarified that the lower court's directive to the parole board regarding the execution of sentences constituted an impermissible interference with executive functions. The court’s suggestion that the parole should coincide with the current offence failed to consider the complexities surrounding parole determinations and improperly dictated matters outside judicial purview ([14]–[18]).

Firearms Control Act and Criminal Law Amendment Act

The court found that the Firearms Control Act did not implicitly repeal the relevant provisions of the Criminal Law Amendment Act; rather, both statutes could coexist. The SCA recognized that the legislature intended to impose tougher penalties for firearm-related offenses and that there was no clear intention to replace or undermine the earlier statute ([19]–[23]).

Costs Order

Regarding the costs order imposed on the State, the SCA noted that since the issue had not been argued by both parties and Motloung was represented by the Legal Aid Board, no costs had actually been incurred. Consequently, the costs order was set aside due to procedural impropriety ([24]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA upheld the appeal against the original sentence, setting aside the 8-year effective sentence and imposing a new sentence of 15 years’ imprisonment, backdated to February 9, 2012. The direction concerning the concurrent implementation of parole and the costs order was also invalidated ([25]).

This decision reiterates the principle that courts must balance mitigating and aggravating factors effectively, with particular attention to public safety and the seriousness of violent crimes. The judgment also emphasizes the distinct roles of the judiciary and executive in matters of sentencing and parole, preserving the integrity of judicial decision-making while respecting the separation of powers inherent in South Africa’s constitutional framework.
