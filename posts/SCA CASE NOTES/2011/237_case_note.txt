Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: Gauteng Province Driving School Association & others v Amaryllis Investments (Pty) Ltd & another (006/11) [2011] ZASCA 237

## F – Facts

The dispute in *Gauteng Province Driving School Association & others v Amaryllis Investments (Pty) Ltd & another* revolves around the unlawful occupation of a parking area. The City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality (Second Respondent) granted the First Respondent, Amaryllis Investments (Pty) Ltd (Amaryllis), the use of certain properties (Erven 849, 851, and a portion of 847 Ferndale, Randburg) on September 12, 2007. Amaryllis occupied the parking area undisturbed until May 23, 2009, when individuals allegedly representing the Gauteng Province Driving School Association (First Appellant, GPDSA) forcibly entered the premises by cutting the lock and chain securing the gate ([1]).

In response, Amaryllis applied for urgent relief, culminating in an order from Phatudi J on September 2, 2009, restoring Amaryllis's possession of the parking area, mandating GPDSA to remove all locks, and ordering GPDSA to pay costs ([1]). Following a series of unsuccessful applications for leave to appeal, GPDSA noted an appeal on May 5, 2010, which temporarily suspended the execution of the spoliation order ([3], [4]).

Two days after the notice of appeal, Amaryllis sought leave to execute the spoliation order under Rule 49(11) of the Uniform Rules. This application led to an execution order on May 11, 2010, which GPDSA opposed ([4]). Both parties later sought leave to appeal against aspects of this execution order, with conflicting outcomes ([5]). By July 2010, despite being notified of the execution order, GPDSA reoccupied the parking area, prompting Amaryllis to apply for a contempt of court ruling ([6]).

The contempt application resulted in a court order on July 28, 2010, declaring GPDSA and its directors in contempt of court for failing to comply with prior orders ([8]). The High Court ratified this contempt order on November 12, 2010, granting GPDSA leave to appeal ([10]). GPDSA's two directors, Masinga and Mathina, also appealed.

## I – Issues

The case raises several pertinent legal issues, including:

1. Whether GPDSA, as a corporate entity, and its directors, Masinga and Mathina, were validly held in contempt of court for disobeying the execution order of May 11, 2010 ([6]).
2. The proper interpretation and application of Rule 49(11) regarding the suspension of the execution of a judgment pending appeal ([12]).
3. Whether the High Court acted within its discretion to grant leave for execution despite the noted appeal and the implications of this action ([13], [19], [20]).

## R – Rules

The relevant rules and legal principles referenced in this case include:

1. **Mandament van Spolie**: This legal remedy aims to restore possession to a party wrongfully deprived of it, without delving into the merits of property rights ([2]).
2. **Rule 49(11) of the Uniform Rules**: This rule stipulates that the operation and execution of a judgment shall be suspended upon the filing of an appeal, unless the court orders otherwise ([12]).
3. **Contempt of Court**: To establish civil contempt, the party must demonstrate that a court order was issued, served, and not complied with, alongside evidence establishing that the non-compliance was willful and mala fide ([19]).
4. **Discretion of Courts**: Courts are granted discretion under Rule 49(11) to determine the conditions under which a judgment may be executed despite an appeal ([13]).

## A – Application

In examining GPDSA’s contempt and appeal, the Court evaluated the factual basis for contempt against both the organizational entity and the individual directors. The judges emphasized that because the contempt provisions require a clear display of non-compliance coupled with willfulness, mere failure to obey an order does not suffice without establishing intent ([17], [19]).

The Court reiterated that the essence of the High Court's decision lay within its discretion under the Uniform Rules. It found that the High Court appropriately concluded that Amaryllis established all necessary factors for leave to execute the order. Notably, it held that an order allowing for execution pending appeal is an interlocutory decision and thus not subject to appeal, supporting the idea that the High Court's discretion is paramount ([13], [15], [20]).

Moreover, GPDSA's arguments focusing on the doctrine of *stare decisis* and the prospects of success on appeal were found to be misplaced. The Court clarified that an order allowing execution pending appeal remains subject to the High Court's discretion regardless of a prior appellate court's ruling about potential success ([16], [18]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ultimately dismissed GPDSA's appeal, upholding the contempt ruling against it. The appeal by the second and third appellants, Masinga and Mathina, succeeded, as the Court found insufficient grounds for their involvement in the contempt ruling due to a lack of evidence proving they acted willfully in disobeying the court's order. They were not adequately joined in the contempt proceedings initially and could not be held liable without specific allegations directed toward their conduct ([20]).

The implications of this ruling underscored the importance of adherence to court orders and the authority of the judiciary over compliance matters. It affirmed that not only is compliance with court orders a legal obligation but also central to the functioning of the rule of law in South Africa. The case highlights the balance courts must maintain between enforcing judicial authority and ensuring that individuals are given due process in contempt proceedings. The dismissals were ordered with costs, clarifying that GPDSA's actions warranted financial repercussions for its non-compliance and contempt ([21]).

This case illustrates the complexities surrounding judicial orders, the relevance of procedural compliance, and the critical nature of judicial authority in safeguarding the rule of law.
