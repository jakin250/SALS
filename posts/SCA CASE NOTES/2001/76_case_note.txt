# FIRAC Case Note: Nestlé (South Africa) Pty Limited v Mars Incorporated

## F – Facts

The present case centers on a dispute between Nestlé (South Africa) Pty Limited (the "Appellant") and Mars Incorporated (the "Respondent"), both renowned manufacturers within the confectionery industry ([1]–[3]). The controversy arose following Mars' introduction of its product, M&M’s, into South Africa in 1993. Nestlé, already established in the market with its Smarties product, allegedly appropriated Mars' branding by creating a plastic dispenser resembling a "little round man" similar to Mars' unique advertising character ([4]–[5]). 

In June 1995, Mars applied to register its brand character as trademarks under the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, which was publicised in late 1997. Nestlé's Swiss principal, seeking to oppose these applications, was granted extensions for this purpose ([6]). 

In May 1998, Mars lodged a complaint with the Advertising Standards Authority of South Africa (ASA), contending that Nestlé had breached Clause 9.1 of the ASA's Code of Advertising Practice by imitating its advertisement ([7]). The ASA's response indicated its belief that trademark issues fell outside its purview and thus declined to consider the complaint, prompting Mars to seek judicial intervention ([8]–[10]). 

The decision by the ASA was further questioned regarding its legitimacy, as Nestlé argued procedural impropriety in the absence of a formal resolution from Mars authorising the complaint ([11]). Nevertheless, the court found that Mars had effectively ratified the proceedings. The ASA, obligated to address complaints per its governing Code, did not oppose Mars’ application for review, leading to proceedings in the Witwatersrand Local Division, which ruled in favour of Mars ([12]–[13]).

## I – Issues

The key issue arose from the ASA's refusal to rule on Mars' complaint, pivoting on two primary legal concerns:

1. Whether the ASA's failure to consider the complaint constituted a breach of its obligations under the Code of Advertising Practice ([12]).
2. Whether the matter was validly before the ASA, given that the copyright dispute was simultaneously under consideration by the Trade Marks Registrar, thus implicating the principle of *lis alibi pendens* insofar as both bodies had concurrent jurisdiction over aspects of the trademark issue ([15], [17]).

## R – Rules

The ASA operates under the auspices of a self-regulatory framework established by its own Code of Advertising Practice, which dictates its responsibility to manage and adjudicate complaints ([1], [2]). Specifically, Clause 9.1 prohibits advertisers from copying existing advertisements in a manner that is discernible, regardless of whether confusion could occur. 

The court also considered the principles of *lis alibi pendens*—which precludes concurrent suits for the same matter—and *res judicata*, which seeks finality in judicial proceedings ([16], [17]). These doctrines underscore the necessity for courts and applicable tribunals to refrain from adjudicating matters simultaneously presented to different forums of competent jurisdiction.

## A – Application

Nestlé's argument regarding *lis alibi pendens* was systematically dismantled by the court. The ruling clarified that while both ASA and the Trade Marks Registrar may deal with similar themes regarding Nestlé's alleged copying, they would engage distinct inquiries—one focusing on the alleged infringement of advertising standards and the other on the registrability of trademarks under statutory law ([18]). 

The court elucidated that the standards at play between the ASA and the Trade Marks Registrar neither overlapped nor were mutually exclusive, thus negating the application of *lis alibi pendens* in this context ([16], [19]). 

Further, the ASA's rationale for abstaining from addressing the complaint—presumably to await the outcome of the Trade Marks Registrar—constituted a misinterpretation of its obligations under its Code ([21], [22]). The court reiterated that the ASA had an independent duty to examine and rule upon complaints made to it, independent of the existence of concurrent proceedings in another competent forum ([23]).

## C – Conclusion

The Appeal Court concluded that the ASA’s refusal to engage with Mars’ complaint was a violation of its procedural obligations and constituted an unlawful abdication of its responsibilities. Consequently, the appeal was dismissed, upholding the initial ruling from the Witwatersrand Local Division that mandated the ASA to reconsider and hear the complaint. 

The judgment underlines the crucial distinction between the functions of self-regulatory bodies such as the ASA and statutory bodies like the Trade Marks Registrar—each bearing distinct responsibilities with no authority to defer duties toward one another. As such, the ruling serves as a clarion call for the respect of regulatory frameworks and highlights the independence required for proper adjudication in matters of commercial and advertising standards. This case potentially impacts the landscape of advertising regulation, with considerable implications for the obligations of self-regulatory bodies across similar contexts ([10], [15]).
