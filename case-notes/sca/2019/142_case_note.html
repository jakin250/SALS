Supreme Court of Appeal

Liberty Group Ltd v Mall Space Management CC t/a Mall Space Management (644/18) [2019] ZASCA 142

The case arises from a contractual dispute between Liberty Group Limited (the first to fourth appellants) and Mall Space Management CC (the respondent).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 142

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–12])

The case arises from a contractual dispute between Liberty Group Limited (the first to fourth appellants) and Mall Space Management CC (the respondent). Liberty Group consists of several shopping malls, while Mall Space acted as an agent responsible for leasing exhibition space within these malls from 2013 until its services were terminated on September 4, 2017. Notably, the parties did not enter into a written agreement formalizing their relationship, although Mall Space claims it operated according to a draft agreement prepared in 2013, which included provisions allowing it to manage promotional events at the shopping centres ([para 6]).

On August 29, 2017, JHI Retail, acting on behalf of Liberty Group, notified Mall Space that its services would no longer be needed, effective immediately, which Mall Space contested, arguing it was entitled to a notice period of six months to terminate the agreement. Following the termination, Mall Space filed an application in the Gauteng Division of the High Court seeking interdictory relief against Liberty Group and the fifth appellant, Excellerate, alleging that the termination constituted unlawful competition ([paras 10–12]).

The high court ruled in favor of Mall Space, emphasizing the principles of Ubuntu and fairness in its decision, thereby granting the interdict ([para 21]). Liberty Group and Excellerate appealed this decision on the grounds that the common law principles allowed for immediate termination of the mandate without a notice period ([para 22]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–3, 17–20])

The appeal raised two primary issues:

  1. Termination of Mandate: Was Liberty Group required under the terms of the mandate to provide Mall Space with a six-month notice before terminating their relationship, or could Liberty Group terminate the mandate unilaterally with immediate effect?
  2. Unlawful Competition: Did Excellerate’s actions in assuming responsibilities previously held by Mall Space after the termination of the mandate constitute unlawful competition as alleged by Mall Space?

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 18–20, 25–29, 32–36])

The court referenced several key principles and precedents relevant to the resolution of these issues:

  • The common law principle of agency, which generally allows a principal to terminate a mandate at will, without the necessity for a notice period ([para 25]).
  • The requirement for a final interdict, which mandates that the applicant must show a clear right, actual or apprehended injury, and the absence of similar protection through another remedy ([para 22]).
  • Ubuntu and fairness have been invoked in previous cases to influence decisions regarding contractual obligations, but the court noted that these values should not override established contractual principles ([paras 28–30]). Previous cases such as Mohamed’s Leisure Holdings (Pty) Ltd and Roazar CC were discussed, articulating that abstract values like good faith do not independently override established contract law ([paras 27–29]).
  • Furthermore, in Schultz v Butt, it was established that lawful competition does not equate with unlawful interference unless it infringes upon another’s legal rights as a trader ([para 33]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 24–39])

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) critically evaluated the high court's reliance on Ubuntu and fairness as primary considerations for the termination of the mandate. The SCA argued that the high court's approach was misplaced, noting that the common law principle that allows for immediate revocation of a mandate was being unjustly overridden by the application of constitutional values ([paras 28–36]).

The court outlined that Mall Space had not demonstrated an enforceable right to a six-month notice period, as the absence of such a term in the mandate did not establish an implied obligation for Liberty Group to provide notice before termination ([para 25]). The SCA focused on the legitimate reasons Liberty Group had for terminating the mandate, including the failure of Mall Space to account fully for rental income, thus justifying the immediate termination without a notice period ([para 32]).

Regarding the claim of unlawful competition, the SCA found that Mall Space's mandate did not confer exclusive rights over the management of exhibition spaces. Consequently, Excellerate's subsequent actions in taking over Mall Space's roles were lawful, as Liberty Group had the right to appoint it following the termination of Mall Space ([paras 33–36]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 39])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appeal by Liberty Group and Excellerate should be upheld, setting aside the high court's order and dismissing Mall Space's application with costs, including the costs of two counsel where applicable ([para 39]). The judgment underscored the principle that while Ubuntu and fairness are important tenets of South African law, they cannot be applied in a manner that contravenes established legal principles in the realm of contract law. Furthermore, the ruling affirms the importance of contractual clarity and the discretion principals maintain to terminate agency agreements without undue restrictions. This case illustrates the tension between common law contract principles and constitutional values, paving the way for future considerations in similar disputes.
