Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1998

# FIRAC Case Note: Amod v Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund, CCT 4/98 

## F – Facts: 
The applicant, Hafiza Ismail Amod (born Peer), sought permission to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court of South Africa following a decision in the Durban and Coast High Court ([1]). Amod claimed damages for loss of support due to the death of her husband in a motor vehicle collision in 1993. The case was considered under the special case provisions of the Uniform Rules of Court ([1]). 

The essential facts included that Amod and her deceased were married under Islamic law on 18 April 1987, but this marriage was not registered according to the Marriage Act ([2]). Under Islamic law, the husband had a duty to maintain his wife, which he fulfilled until his demise on 25 July 1993, caused by the negligence of another driver ([2])—a liability ultimately rooted in the provisions of the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund Act (“MVA Act”) ([2]).

The High Court examined whether the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund (respondent) was liable to compensate Amod for her loss of support under these circumstances. Liability under the MVA Act was contingent upon whether the negligent driver would have been liable at common law if the MVA Act had not been in force ([3]). The High Court cited precedent from *Suid-Afrikaanse Nasionale Trust en Assuransie Maatskappy Bpk v Fondo* wherein it was determined that claims for loss of support were only recognized if the marriage at issue was lawful under common law ([4]). As Islamic marriages are potentially polygamous and thus unlawful under common law according to previous court decisions, the respondent was not liable, leading to Amod's contention that these precedents no longer reflected the evolution of public policy and constitutional principles ([7]).

## I – Issues: 
The primary issues before the Constitutional Court were ([11]):
1. Did the Constitutional Court have jurisdiction to hear this appeal, and did the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) also hold jurisdiction over the appeal?
2. If both courts had jurisdiction, should the appeal be directed to the Constitutional Court instead of the SCA?
3. Given the circumstances of the accident occurring before the interim Constitution, does section 39(2) of the 1996 Constitution apply to this matter, and does it allow for the common law to recognize a right of action for loss of support by a widow married according to Islamic law?

## R – Rules: 
The relevant legal frameworks referred to in the decision included:
- The Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund Act, Act 93 of 1989 ([2]).
- The Marriage Act, Act 25 of 1961 ([2]).
- The interim Constitution of 1993, specifically section 35(3), which provided for the development of common law ([7]).
- The 1996 Constitution, particularly section 39(2) which obligates courts to promote the Bill of Rights in the interpretation of laws, and section 8(3) granting courts the power to develop common law to realize rights under the Bill of Rights ([8]).
- Precedents such as *Suid-Afrikaanse Nasionale Trust en Assuransie Maatskappy Bpk v Fondo*, *Nkabinde v SA Motor & General Insurance Co Ltd*, and *Ismail v Ismail* which established the legal principles concerning support claims in the context of various marital statuses ([4], [6], [8]).

## A – Application: 
In considering the facts, the Constitutional Court focused on whether the High Court had erred in its interpretation and application of the common law regarding Amod’s claim. The court analyzed whether the prior rulings excluding customary and Islamic marriages from liability in loss of support cases retained validity under evolving public policy and constitutional mandates that emphasized equality and redress ([7], [8]).

1. First, it affirmed that common law could be developed under constitutional provisions; however, it remained bound to previous judicial interpretations regarding the legitimacy of Islamic marriages.
2. It determined that the SCA had adequate jurisdiction to examine the common law developments in light of constitutional principles of justice and equality, asserting that addressing these issues in the SCA would be in the interests of justice ([11], [34]).
3. The court concluded that while the 1996 Constitution applies to actions arising after its enactment, there was no indication that the precedents excluding Islamic marriages were inconsistent with constitutional mandates, suggesting a need for legislative action rather than judicial intervention to rectify the common law ([18], [20]).

## C – Conclusion: 
The Constitutional Court dismissed Amod's application for leave to appeal directly from the High Court, concluding that the matter should initially be dealt with by the SCA before any potential escalation to the Constitutional Court ([34]). The court emphasized the importance of allowing the SCA to assess the implications of common law development fully. The ruling signals that questions regarding the legal validity of marriages under Islamic law and potential claims for damages based on public policy require thorough deliberation and cannot be premature in advancing through judicial channels ([34]). 

Overall, while the decision left open the potential for future claims following suitable developments in common law, it underscored the complexities of addressing historical injustices and the necessity for legislative clarity on recognition and rights associated with Islamic marriages in South African law.
