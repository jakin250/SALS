# FIRAC Case Note

### Case Title: On-Line Lottery Services (Pty) Ltd v National Lotteries Board and Others
Neutral Citation: On-Line Lottery Services v National Lotteries Board (536/08) [2009] ZASCA 86 (7 September 2009)

### F – Facts
The case stems from an appeal regarding the registered trade mark "LOTTO" owned by the National Lotteries Board (the "Board") and its authorized operator Uthingo Management (Pty) Ltd ("Uthingo"). The first respondent, the Board, is the registered proprietor of the trade mark "Lotto" for services in connection with financial transactions and lotteries, with registrations dating from April 17, 1991, under the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 ([paras 2–3]).

On-Line Lottery Services (Pty) Ltd (“On-Line”) was established on February 14, 2000. Initially named LottoFun (Pty) Ltd, it later changed its name to On-Line Lottery Services but continued to trade under "LottoFun." The appellant aimed to provide online services for purchasing National Lottery tickets but failed to secure authorization from the Board ([paras 5–6]).

In June 2004, the Board initiated legal proceedings against On-Line, asserting trade mark infringement, passing off, and unlawful competition. The relief sought included an interdict against On-Line's use of "LottoFun" ([paras 9–10]). On-Line counterclaimed for the expungement of the "Lotto" trade mark, arguing that it was wrongly registered on the grounds of non-distinctiveness and non-use ([paras 12–13]).

### I – Issues
The core legal issues presented to the court include:
1. Should the registered trade mark "LOTTO" be removed from the trade mark register due to it being wrongly entered and wrongfully remaining on the register as per s 24(1) of the Trade Marks Act?
2. Did On-Line's business operations constitute passing off or conduct that misled the public into believing a connection with the National Lotteries?
3. Did On-Line's actions contravene sections 56 and 57 of the Lotteries Act 57 of 1997? ([paras 1–2]).

### R – Rules
The court relied on several legal principles, notably:
- The definition and requirements for trade mark distinctiveness under s 9 of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, which mandates that a trade mark must be capable of distinguishing the services of the owner from others ([para 13]).
- The legal framework governing passing off claims, specifically that a trader must prove that their goods or services are likely to be confused with those of another due to misrepresentation ([para 37]).
- Provisions of the Lotteries Act 57 of 1997 that govern the operations of lotteries in South Africa, which restrict unauthorized persons from conducting lottery-related businesses ([para 36]).

### A – Application
The Court undertook a detailed analysis of each of the above issues:

1. **Trade Mark Distinctiveness**: The Court found that the trade mark “LOTTO” lacked inherent distinctiveness, as it was descriptive of lottery services rather than identifying the source of such services. The evidence presented showed that the term was already in common use and did not signify the Board as the sole provider. The Court also referenced case law indicating that registration does not confer exclusive rights over a generic term ([paras 13–32]).

2. **Passing Off**: On-Line's operations were examined in light of passing off. The evidence indicated that customers understood that they were purchasing an agency service, not tickets directly from the National Lottery. Customers had access to disclaimers on the site, which clarified On-Line's independence from the official lottery ([paras 40–56]).

3. **Compliance with Lotteries Act**: On-Line argued that its model provided a permissible agency service to facilitate ticket orders rather than unauthorized sale or gambling. The court accepted this reasoning, concluding that their business model did not contravene applicable lottery laws or engage in prohibited activities as defined by the Lotteries Act ([paras 56–57]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that:
- The registrations of the trade marks "LOTTO" were wrongly placed on the trade mark register and ordered the expungement of these registrations as they lacked distinctiveness and did not identify the services specifically with the Board ([paras 34–36]).
- On-Line did not commit passing off, nor did it act unlawfully under the Lotteries Act in its operations, leading to a dismissal of claims against it for trademark infringement and misrepresentation ([paras 54–56]).

The Court ordered that On-Line's appeal be successful, including costs, but excluded costs related to specific preparatory work on part of the record ([para 58]). The implications of this ruling underscore the necessity for enforceable and distinguishable trade marks, especially in sectors involving public laws and consumer protection.
