Source Folder: 2009

# Case Note: City of Johannesburg v Gauteng Development Tribunal (335/08) [2009] ZASCA 106

## FIRAC Analysis

**F – Facts**  
The appellant in this case, the City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality ([para 1]), sought a declaratory order against the Gauteng Development Tribunal (first respondent) and the Gauteng Development Appeal Tribunal (second respondent) regarding the constitutionality of Chapters V and VI of the Development Facilitation Act 67 of 1995 (the "Act") ([para 2]). These chapters purportedly conferred authority upon provincial development tribunals to regulate land use, which the appellant contended conflicted with the exclusive powers assigned to local municipalities for land use regulation under the Town-Planning and Townships Ordinance 15 of 1986 ([paras 1, 4]).

After efforts to resolve these regulatory conflicts through discussions with other governmental tiers were unsuccessful ([para 2]), the municipality approached the South Gauteng High Court seeking to declare these provisions constitutionally invalid. The High Court, presided over by Gildenhuys J, dismissed the application, leading to the present appeal ([para 2], [para 3]).

**I – Issues**  
The primary legal issue in this case revolves around the constitutionality of Chapters V and VI of the Development Facilitation Act ([para 4]). Specifically, it considers whether land use regulation, as established under municipal authority, is an exclusive function of municipalities under the South African Constitution and therefore cannot be legislated to other tiers of government ([para 28]). The court also examined whether the powers granted to provincial tribunals under the Act infringe upon the constitutional mandate of municipal planning that falls within the jurisdiction of local authorities ([para 30]).

**R – Rules**  
The court's analysis began with an examination of various statutory instruments and precedents governing land use. Firstly, the Division noted the overarching constitutional framework (Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996) that delineates the powers of municipalities ([paras 25-28]). Specifically, section 156(1) reserves local government matters, including municipal planning, exclusively for municipalities ([para 28]).

Furthermore, the provisions within Chapters V and VI of the Development Facilitation Act conferring powers to development tribunals were highlighted. These provisions were argued to allow tribunals to approve land use applications that could contravene local municipal plans, thereby creating a conflict ([para 30]). The Act's declared purpose (as limited by its long title) was directed toward the swift implementation of development programs in line with national reconstruction objectives ([para 58]).

**A – Application**  
In applying the rules outlined above, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that the powers conferred upon the Gauteng Development Tribunal significantly overlapped with those reserved for municipalities by the Constitution ([para 44]). The court emphasized that the regulation and administration of land use are pivotal aspects of municipal planning, which could not be delegated to other bodies without infringing constitutional mandates ([paras 30, 31]).

The court also critiqued the interpretation by Gildenhuys J, which suggested that municipal planning might encompass only 'forward planning' and not the actual implementation of land use schemes ([para 43]). The Supreme Court rejected this view, stating that it would render the function of municipalities ineffective and meaningless if they could not implement or enforce planning decisions ([paras 41, 43]).

Nugent JA expressed concern over the chaos that could ensue from having multiple layers of authority over land use regulations, highlighting the potential for conflicting decisions ([para 12]). The imposition of parallel authority would likely disrupt the orderly planning efforts of municipalities ([para 12]). As a remedy, the court ruled to declare the invalidity of Chapters V and VI but suspended the declaration for 18 months to prevent immediate disruption in land development processes ([paras 50, 44]).

**C – Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appellant's appeal in part, declaring Chapters V and VI of the Development Facilitation Act to be constitutionally invalid but suspending the effect of this declaration for 18 months. The suspension is set with the intention of providing Parliament time to replace the conflicting provisions ([paras 50-46]). The court recognized the necessity for clarity in governmental powers concerning land use to uphold the principles of effective local governance ([para 60]). 

Ultimately, this ruling reinforces the constitutional principle that local governments possess exclusive rights to manage municipal planning, thus affirming the constitutional limits of provincial authority in land use regulation. The decision serves to provide critical guidance on the separation of powers within South Africa's constitutional framework, particularly regarding urban and rural development.
