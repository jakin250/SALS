Supreme Court of Appeal

Naidoo v The State [2007] SCA 102 (RSA)

The appellant, Minneldhevi Iris Naidoo, a 35-year-old widow, was charged with attempted murder in the Regional Court at Verulam, Kwa-Zulu Natal.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2007

F – Facts

The appellant, Minneldhevi Iris Naidoo, a 35-year-old widow, was charged with attempted murder in the Regional Court at Verulam, Kwa-Zulu Natal. The charge alleged that she incited a man named Bonginkosi to shoot her sister's husband, Selvin Pillay (also referred to as Roland Chetty), intending to kill him. Naidoo pleaded guilty and provided a detailed statement in terms of section 112(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, admitting to coordinating with Bonginkosi to commit the crime for a fee of R10,000. She further took out a life insurance policy on Pillay, named her sister as the beneficiary, and paid the first premium.

During the sentencing phase, evidence emerged suggesting Naidoo may have had a valid defense, prompting the regional magistrate to consider whether to invoke section 113(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act, which would allow her to change the plea to not guilty if it appeared that there was a valid defense. However, the magistrate chose to convict her and subsequently sentenced her to five years’ imprisonment under section 276(1)(i) of the Act. Naidoo appealed her conviction and sentence to the full bench of the Natal Provincial Division, which was dismissed, leading her to this appeal ([paras 1–4]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions at stake are:

  1. Whether the regional magistrate erred in failing to invoke section 113(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act, considering the new evidence that suggested Naidoo may have had a valid defense ([paras 5–10]).
  2. Whether the sentence imposed was inappropriate in light of the alleged misdirections by the magistrate ([paras 11–13]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal provisions and precedents include:

  • Section 112(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which allows an accused to plead guilty by admitting the essential elements of an offense.
  • Section 113(1) of the Act, permitting a court to record a plea of not guilty if it appears that there may be a valid defense.
  • Section 276(1)(i) and 276(1)(h) provide guidelines for sentencing options, focusing on the intention behind the crime and its implications for societal safety ([paras 4, 5, 11]).

A – Application

In addressing the appeal, the court scrutinized Naidoo's statement and the subsequent evidence presented. The court noted that while the probation officer's report and testimony from Naidoo's sister indicated that there might have been a consensus to withdraw from the conspiracy shortly before the crime, there was little credible evidence to support this claim ([paras 6–9]).

Specifically, Naidoo's s 112(2) statement contained explicit admissions of her intentions and actions, including taking out the insurance policy and coordinating with Bonginkosi. The court emphasized that these actions qualified as a completed attempt to murder by incitement and procurement, which precluded any genuine withdrawal from the common purpose ([paras 9–10]).

On the matter of sentencing, the court analyzed the alleged misdirections claimed by Naidoo's counsel. It found that the magistrate had appropriately considered the circumstances of the crime and held that the facts warranted a harsher sentence. The aggravating factors, including the premeditated nature of the crime—hiring an assassin and insuring the victim’s life—were significant enough to override the mitigating circumstances of Naidoo’s personal circumstances ([paras 11–12]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld both the conviction and sentence, finding that the magistrate did not err in neglecting to invoke section 113(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act. The court concluded that Naidoo’s actions constituted a deliberate and grave offense, meriting a conviction. The appeal was ultimately dismissed on both the grounds of conviction and sentencing, reaffirming the seriousness of her conduct in attempting to contract a murder and the appropriate judicial response ([paras 13]).

In summary, this case underscores the stringent application of legal principles regarding plea admissions, the responsibilities of legal representatives in criminal proceedings, and the judicial perspective on sentencing in cases involving serious crimes such as attempted murder.
