Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

### Case Note: South African Human Rights Commission v Standard Bank of South Africa Ltd and Others [2022] ZACC 43

#### F – Facts
The South African Human Rights Commission (SAHRC) initiated an application focused on whether the High Court has the discretion to decline jurisdiction over matters that fall within the concurrent jurisdiction of the Magistrates' Court ([para 1]). The case arose from 13 proceedings brought before the Gauteng Division of the High Court by three banks—Standard Bank, Nedbank, and FirstRand Bank—against various natural persons who defaulted on their mortgage or vehicle loan payments ([para 3]). 

The amounts in these claims predominantly fell within the monetary jurisdiction of the Magistrates' Court, implying that they could have been pursued there. Despite the banks seeking default judgments and declarations of properties being specially executable, the defendants did not participate in any of the hearings ([para 4]).

The High Court noted the growing trend of litigants enrolling cases in the High Court despite their monetary value falling within the Magistrates’ Courts' jurisdiction and highlighted the need for courts to consider the access to justice implications concerning financially distressed individuals ([para 5]). The Full Court of the High Court ruled that it is an abuse of process to initiate claims in the High Court when they could be effectively dealt with in the Magistrates' Court, and embedded its decision within a wider context of judicial resource management and access to justice for impoverished debtors ([para 6]).

An appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) reversed this decision, stating it was obligatory for the High Court to consider and hear cases over which it had concurrent jurisdiction, affirming the so-called "mandatory jurisdiction principle" ([paras 6–7]).

The SAHRC then sought leave to appeal before the Constitutional Court, challenging the SCA's interpretation and asserting that the exercise of judicial discretion should not mandate the High Court to hear all matters arising under its jurisdiction, particularly those pertaining to impoverished individuals ([para 9]).

#### I – Issues
The key legal questions before the Constitutional Court included:
1. **Does a High Court have the discretion to refuse jurisdiction over cases that fall within the concurrent jurisdiction of the Magistrates' Courts?**
2. **Is it appropriate for a provincial division of the High Court to determine whether to entertain a matter if it falls within the jurisdiction of a local division?** ([para 2]).

#### R – Rules
The rules that framed the judgment included:
- **Section 169(1) of the Constitution**, which states that the High Court “may” decide any matter within its jurisdiction, indicating a legislative intent for a discretionary approach rather than a mandatory one.
- **Sections 21 and 27 of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013**, which delineate the jurisdictional competencies of different courts, and the principles surrounding the mandatory jurisdiction must be interpreted in light of the Constitution's emphasis on individual rights and access to court.
- **Legal precedents** such as *Goldberg v Goldberg* and *Agri Wire (Pty) Ltd v Commissioner of the Competition Commission*, which emphasized that courts generally cannot refuse to entertain properly brought proceedings within their jurisdiction unless such proceedings amount to an abuse of process ([paras 12–13]).

#### A – Application
The Constitutional Court, led by Madlanga J, analyzed the interpretive frameworks around judicial discretion provided by the aforementioned statutes and precedents. It acknowledged that while the High Court might have the competence to hear matters, it is not an absolute obligation to entertain claims that can be efficiently and effectively resolved in lower courts—especially in matters involving financial distress like the cases at hand ([paras 14–20]).

The Court noted the validity of the SAHRC's concern regarding the implications of procedural justice for impoverished debtors, affirming that access to court is indeed a constitutional right under section 34 of the Constitution. However, it concluded that the automatic reduction of the High Court's jurisdiction based on the relief sought in the Magistrates' Court introduces complications that might hinder the efficacy and independent discretion accorded to the court ([paras 21–24]).

Furthermore, the determination that the High Court could not automatically classify claims as an abuse of process without substantive evidence or considerations depicted both the flexibility and constraints of judicial discretion—enabling the courts to be efficient without disregarding the rights of parties ([paras 25–30]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the appeal, holding that while the High Court has a duty to manage its case rolls judiciously, it does not possess an unfettered right to refuse to hear matters within its jurisdiction based on the location of the claim’s original venue ([paras 31–32]). The Court emphasized the need for High Courts to apply discretion methodically and within the context of enhancing access to justice, rather than dismissing claims that fall within their jurisdiction simply because they could be taken elsewhere.

This ruling underscores the delicate balance that must be managed by courts regarding jurisdiction, emphasizing the Court's recognition of socio-economic realities as integral to the functioning of the legal system. The Court also called for further legislative efforts to address the backlog and access issues raised by the SAHRC, highlighting an ongoing need for refinement in the legal framework regarding lower courts ([paras 33–34]). 

The judgment set a significant precedent for jurisdictional competences between courts in South Africa by reaffirming that the High Court should exercise its jurisdiction, without imposing blanket restrictions based on monetary thresholds, and ultimately fostering a system conducive to equitable access to justice.
