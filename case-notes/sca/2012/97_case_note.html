Case Note: Raubenheimer v Raubenheimer (560/2011) [2012] ZASCA 97

F – Facts

The case involves a dispute regarding the validity of a last will and testament signed by Dr. S P Raubenheimer on 30 March 2006 ([para 2]). Dr. Raubenheimer, a medical practitioner, signed the will with the intention of replacing an earlier will from 2002 and appointed Jan Hendrik Hagen as the estate administrator. The will was not properly witnessed, as the two individuals who signed as witnesses had not actually done so when they should have. This breach of statutory formalities arose from the testator's refusal to sign the will in a restaurant and Mr. Hagen’s decision to have his employees witness the document after the fact, despite the absence of proper witnessing ([paras 3-4]).

Upon the testator's death in 2009, the Master of the North Gauteng High Court accepted the will as valid, leading to objections from the deceased's children, Gerda and Stephanus Raubenheimer ([paras 5-6]). They contested the will on several grounds, primarily arguing that it was void for vagueness due to the absence of a requisite list of specific bequests ([para 7]). The High Court ruled in their favor, declaring the will void and that the deceased had died intestate ([para 6]).

I – Issues

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) addressed key issues concerning:

  1. Whether Dr. Raubenheimer intended for the document to be his last will in accordance with section 2(3) of the Wills Act 7 of 1953 ([para 10]).
  2. Whether the absence of a list of specific bequests rendered the will ambiguous or void for vagueness, particularly in reference to the provisions concerning the common home ([para 14]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal framework includes:

  • Wills Act 7 of 1953: Section 2(1)(a)(i)-(iii) prescribes the statutory requirements for a valid will, which includes the necessity for the testator’s signature to be attested by two witnesses ([para 4]). Section 2(3) provides a judicial remedy allowing a court to validate a will that does not meet all formalities if the intention to create a testamentary document can be established ([para 10]).

The SCA also employed principles from earlier case law regarding testamentary intent and the interpretation of potentially ambiguous bequests, emphasizing that the overarching goal is to ascertain the true intention of the testator ([para 23]).

A – Application

The SCA found that the testator had indeed intended for the document to serve as his last will, as he had engaged in detailed discussions about its contents with Mr. Hagen, who had drafted it. The fact that the testator expressed satisfaction with the will's provisions and deliberately signed it demonstrated his intent ([paras 11-12]).

Addressing the second issue, the court determined that the absence of a list of specific bequests did not invalidate the will's provisions for vagueness. It ruled that the testator likely intended to create a fideicommissum regarding the matrimonial home, conferring a right of usufruct to the appellant until her death or remarriage. The SCA also noted that any ambiguity arising from the wording in the will could be resolved through careful interpretation that considered the general structure and intention reflected in the will as a whole ([paras 19-24]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA overturned the lower court's decision, ruling that the will dated 30 March 2006 was valid and should be accepted by the Master of the High Court as the last will of the deceased. The court’s holding clarified that the absence of specific bequests did not imply a lack of testamentary intent, and it further established that the wording regarding the common home implied a fideicommissum without needing explicit identification of a beneficiary ([para 24]).

The implications of this judgment are significant in testamentary law, emphasizing judicial discretion in validating imperfect wills and the pragmatic approach courts should adopt in interpreting the intentions of testators, thereby preventing the unintended invalidation of wills that express clear testamentary intent, albeit through ambiguous phrasing. Additionally, the order emphasized the need for careful legal drafting in light of informal or untrained assistance often encountered in the preparation of wills ([paras 1, 25]).

This case should serve as a reminder of the profound importance of adhering to formal requirements in will execution while also retaining the flexibility for courts to uphold the testamentary wishes expressed by testators when the intentions can still be discerned despite statutory non-compliance.
