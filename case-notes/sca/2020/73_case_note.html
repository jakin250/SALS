Case Title: Tshaka NO & Others v Standard Bank of South Africa Limited & Another [2020] ZASCA 73, Case No: 141/2019.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 73, Case No: 141/2019. Court: Supreme Cour

Case Title:
Tshaka NO & Others v Standard Bank of South Africa Limited & Another [2020] ZASCA 73, Case No: 141/2019.

Court:
Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

Date Heard:
15 May 2020

Date Delivered:
25 June 2020

F – Facts

[1] This appeal arose from a dispute concerning a transfer of R9.5 million executed by officials of Standard Bank of South Africa Limited (the Bank) from the Bakubung-Ba-Ratheo Economic Development Trust (the Trust) to the Bakubung Economic Development Unit (the Unit). The appellants in this matter are four trustees of the Trust. Two trustees were appointed as of 12 August 2013, while the other two had been serving longer. The Trust was established to promote socio-economic development within the Bakubung community, following an initial donation of R10 million from Wesizwe Platinum Limited.

[2] The Trust Deed stipulates that the banking account for the Trust shall operate under certain conditions, allowing payments to be made by any one authorized signatory.

[3] In a resolution dated 23 July 2007, the trustees authorized two individuals to manage banking affairs, including opening accounts and maintaining electronic access. On 19 December 2007, the Bank approved an application to open an account for the Trust.

[4] A critical transfer of R5.5 million was executed on 26 July 2011, following authorization from two signatories. A complaint was lodged later by the third appellant, Jabulani Gumbi, who alleged the transfer lacked proper resolution from trustees, requesting the Bank to halt further transactions.

[5] The Bank subsequently received another instruction to transfer R4 million to the Unit, which it executed on 28 October 2011 after confirming with the authorized signatory for the Trust.

[6] On 16 July 2014, the appellants issued summons against the Bank, alleging negligence in executing the transfers without proper instructions. The High Court dismissed the claim, which led to this appeal.

I – Issues

[7] The central issues in this case are:

  1. Whether the Bank was negligent in executing the transfers in light of the alleged instructions from trustees.
  2. Whether the Bank's insistence on receiving written instructions from all trustees constituted a breach of duty to the Trust.

R – Rules

[8] The pertinent legal framework includes the Trust Property Act 57 of 1988, specifically emphasizing the duties and powers of trustees under trust law. It sets forth that trustees must generally act collectively and that a trust's activities must be conducted in accordance with its deed.

[9] As articulated in prior case law, including Lupacchini NO v Minister of Safety and Security [2010] ZASCA 108 and Land and Agricultural Bank of South Africa v Parker and Others [2005] ZASCA 77, the authority of trustees derives from the trust deed, which specifies how the trust must function.

A – Application

[10] The Supreme Court of Appeal confirmed the High Court's finding that the Bank acted properly when it processed the transactions based on instructions from authorized signatories. The court noted the third appellant's failure to present any evidence of fraud or improper conduct at the time of the first transfer.

[11] Furthermore, the court highlighted that the Bank had made it clear to the third appellant that a new resolution signed by all trustees was necessary to halt transactions following the second complaint. The appellants were unable to provide compelling reasons for delaying the issuance of such a resolution.

[12] In affirming the High Court's position, the Supreme Court underscored the legal expectations of trusteeship, emphasizing that the trustees must fulfill their obligations collectively to bind the trust in legal matters.

C – Conclusion

[13] The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court's judgment, concluding that the Bank was not liable for the unauthorized transfers. The ratio decidendi primarily focused on the trustees' collective duty of action and the Bank's reliance on instructions from its authorized signatories.

[14] The court also noted the implications for trust governance, reinforcing the necessity for timely and precise communication among trustees. The appeal was dismissed with costs, signifying that the appellants failed to prove negligence or breach of duty on part of the Bank ([paras 19–23]).
