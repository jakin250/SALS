# Case Note: Mamasedi v Chief of South African Defence Force and Others [2024] ZACC 17

## FIRAC Case Note

**F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 3–12])**

The applicant, Mr. Molefi Jonas Mamasedi, served in the Regular Force of the South African National Defence Force (SANDF) as a sergeant. He was absent from duty without permission from 29 November 2011 to 2 January 2012, a period exceeding 30 calendar days. His absence stemmed from his claim of being kidnapped and taken to an initiation school against his will, although this version was disputed by his father, who suggested it was voluntary. 

Upon his return, he was subjected to a Board of Inquiry initiated by Lt. General Yam to investigate the circumstances of his absence. This inquiry would later conclude with recommendations that led to the respondents treating him as dismissed or discharged based on ineffective absences exceeding 30 days. 

Mamasedi initially approached the Gauteng Division of the High Court, achieving partial relief as his reinstatement was ordered. However, this order was overturned by the Supreme Court of Appeal on procedural grounds, requiring a fresh decision be made regarding his reinstatement. A second Board of Inquiry upheld the first, reiterating that he was discharged due to his absence.

Concerned with these adverse findings, Mamasedi applied for a review of the second inquiry's decision, asserting that weekends and public holidays should not be counted towards the 30-day threshold specified in section 59(3) of the Defence Act 42 of 2002 ([paras 5-13]).

**I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 28-30])**

The central issue before the Constitutional Court was the interpretation of section 59(3) of the Defence Act, specifically:

1. Whether the "30 days" referred to in section 59(3) includes days on which a member of the Regular Force is not required to work, such as weekends and public holidays.

2. If weekends and public holidays are excluded from the 30-day calculation, did the applicant’s absence fall within the purview of section 59(3), thereby invalidating the dismissal or discharge declared by the respondents?

**R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 35-46])**

The Court primarily relied on section 59(3) of the Defence Act, which stipulates that a member absent without permission for a period exceeding 30 days is deemed dismissed or discharged ([para 1]). The interpretation of this section was anchored in principles of statutory interpretation as elaborated in existing precedents, such as:

1. **Hyundai**: Courts must interpret legislation to promote the spirit of the Bill of Rights ([para 35]).
2. **Mogapi**: Consideration of the Interpretation Act's definition of how to reckon days when specific actions are prescribed ([para 41]).

Based on these principles, the Court needed to ascertain the purpose of section 59(3) concerning discipline and misconduct and determine how it should be read in a manner consistent with constitutional values.

**A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 36-48])**

In its analysis, the Court found that section 59(3) should not include weekends and public holidays in its calculation of the 30 days because such days were not "official duty" days for the applicant, who was only obligated to work Monday through Friday. This interpretation aligns with the requirement that disciplinary action under the section constitutes a breach of duty, which could only apply if the member was contractually obligated to work on the given days.

The Court scrutinized the procedural fairness of the inquiries' findings, emphasizing that a dismissal or discharge must arise from a legitimate absence where a member was required to be on duty. The interpretation advanced by the respondents, who construed the days inclusively, was ruled to potentially result in disciplinary actions for members absent on days they were not required to report for duty, contrary to principles of fairness enshrined in the Constitution.

The Court established that the applicant did not exceed 30 "working" days of absence, thereby ruling that the conditions triggering section 59(3) had not been met. Consequently, he was not to be regarded as dismissed or discharged ([paras 38-48]).

**C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 49-54])**

The Constitutional Court held that the interpretation of "30 days" in section 59(3) refers solely to days on which a member of the Regular Force is obliged to be on official duty. The Court ruled accordingly, emphasizing that the applicant's absence did not exceed the stipulated threshold necessary for dismissal under the Act. The Court ordered the respondents to recognize the applicant's continued membership in the SANDF and mandated the payment of his remuneration from January 2012 onwards. 

The Court's decision has broader implications not only for the applicant but also for other SANDF members, reinforcing the necessity for fairness and clarity in interpreting statutory provisions related to military discipline and employment rights. This decision underscores the importance of protecting employees from unwarranted disciplinary actions based on ambiguous statutory language ([paras 52-54]).

In conclusion, the Constitutional Court's ruling in Mamasedi v Chief of South African Defence Force and Others addresses significant issues concerning statutory interpretation within military law while fostering adherence to constitutional values in public service governance. The decision may prompt a re-evaluation of related provisions and their applications, ensuring that military personnel are treated fairly under the law.
