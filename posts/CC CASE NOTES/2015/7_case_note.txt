Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note

## Case Title: AllPay Consolidated Investment Holdings (Pty) Ltd & Others v Chief Executive Officer of the South African Social Security Agency & Others [2015] ZACC 7

### F - Facts ([paras 1–16])

The matter arises from two prior judgments by the Constitutional Court of South Africa—AllPay 1 and AllPay 2—where the court annulled a tender awarded by the South African Social Security Agency (SASSA) to Cash Paymaster Services (Pty) Ltd (CPS) for the payment of social grants to approximately 15 million beneficiaries ([para 2]). The court's decision was premised on the tender being invalid, necessitating a rerun of the tender process, alongside a structural order to enhance monitoring and accountability of SASSA during this process ([para 3]).

Following the judgment in AllPay 2, CPS lodged a "Notice of Request by CPS for Ancillary Relief to Structural Interdict," challenging the validity of the Request for Proposals (RFP) issued by SASSA, claiming it did not align with the Court's previous directives ([paras 4–5]). The contested RFP became a point of legal contention, with CPS challenging multiple aspects of it ([para 5]).

On 19 March 2015, a hearing took place where concerns of the beneficiaries and the urgency of finalizing the tender process were highlighted, alongside the need for swift action regarding the RFP ([paras 9–10]). CPS’s objections were narrowed down to five key areas, thus forming the basis of the final hearing ([para 11]).

### I - Issues ([paras 11–14])

The principal legal issue concerned whether CPS could challenge the amended RFP under the framework of the previous court orders, specifically regarding the implications for the beneficiaries and the timeline of the tender finalization ([paras 11–12]). Additionally, there was the question of whether to include a provision that required parties to seek further relief from the Constitutional Court only when necessary to protect the beneficiaries' interests ([para 18]).

### R - Rules ([paras 1–16])

The court relied on previous jurisprudence established in AllPay 1 and AllPay 2, which articulated the constitutional obligations of public entities regarding the provision of social services ([para 14]). The rulings necessitated accountability from the service providers involved, particularly focused on ensuring a functional payment system was maintained during the transitional process ([para 15]). Critically, the judgments also established that any entities deriving benefits from a contract deemed unconstitutional could not continue inefficacious practices at the expense of beneficiaries ([para 15]).

### A - Application ([paras 13–18])

In applying the law, the Court acknowledged the urgency in finalizing the tender process, pointing out that any delays might inadvertently advantage CPS, with potential financial repercussions for beneficiaries ([para 12]). The necessity for a workable solution that aligned with constitutional obligations was underscored. While CPS sought further relief and challenged the amended RFP, the Court was mindful of the broader implications of prolonging this process for the beneficiaries.

The incorporation of a provision that required parties to seek further relief from the Constitutional Court, conditional on the necessity to protect beneficiary interests, was deliberated ([paras 17–18]). The Court, however, clarified that such requests should not hinder the access to justice or create barriers for parties needing to address future concerns.

### C - Conclusion ([paras 18–19])

The Court ultimately retained a supervisory jurisdiction to oversee the finalization of the tender process, allowing SASSA to amend the RFP within stipulated timelines and to ensure a new tender was awarded promptly ([para 19]). The decision underscored the urgency in protecting beneficiaries and minimizing further delays while clarifying Court procedures to address potential grievances in the future.

In its final order, the Court added a condition for the exclusive access to further relief for the parties involved, directly related to safeguarding beneficiaries’ interests. The Court's actions cemented its role in facilitating transparent and accountable governance in public service provision ([para 19]). 

This case not only illustrates the balance between legal remedy and constitutional obligations but also emphasizes the judiciary's role in ensuring that the rights of vulnerable populations, such as social grant beneficiaries, are prioritized and protected.
