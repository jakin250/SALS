Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

# FIRAC Case Note: SA Predator Breeders Association v Minister of Environmental Affairs (72/10) [2010] ZASCA 151

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])

This case arises from the legal challenge by the **SA Predator Breeders Association** and two individual breeders, **M C Mostert** and **D Cilliers** (hereafter referred to collectively as "the Appellants"), against certain regulations established under the National Environmental Management: Biodiversity Act, 10 of 2004, promulgated by the **Minister of Environmental Affairs and Tourism** (hereafter referred to as "the Respondent") to control the hunting of captive-bred lions.

The Appellants represent the interests of breeders and hunters of lions and are concerned with the regulatory framework that affects their activities. The Minister published the **Threatened or Protected Species Regulations** on **23 February 2007**, wherein the lion (Panthera leo) was classified as a “vulnerable species” and included within the definition of "listed large predator" under the regulations ([paras 1–4]).

The dispute escalated when the Appellants approached the **Free State High Court** on **4 May 2007**, seeking a judicial review of the regulations, arguing that they were invalid and alleging procedural deficiencies in the Minister's consultation process ([paras 5–6]). The High Court dismissed the application and the Appellants' subsequent request for leave to appeal was also denied, prompting them to seek intervention from the **Supreme Court of Appeal** ([paras 8–9]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 11–20])

The primary issues for resolution by the Supreme Court of Appeal were:
1. Whether the regulations enacted by the Minister, specifically regulation **24(2)** prohibiting the hunting of captive-bred lions (“‘put and take’ animals”), were rational and lawful.
2. Whether the Minister had followed the required consultative processes as mandated by Sections **97** and **100** of the Biodiversity Act during the formulation of the regulations, including the sufficiency of stakeholder representation ([paras 20–22]).
3. Whether the regulation's provision for a **24-month rehabilitation period** before a captive-bred lion could be hunted was rationally connected to the legislative purpose of the Act ([para 28]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 11–19])

The **National Environmental Management: Biodiversity Act 10 of 2004** forms the core legislative framework for this case. Key provisions include:
- **Section 2** outlines the objectives of biodiversity management, emphasizing sustainable utilization.
- **Section 56** grants the Minister the authority to list species as threatened or protected, specifically addressing the high risk of extinction ([paras 10–12]).
- **Sections 57 and 97** detail the Minister's regulatory powers and obligations to conduct appropriate consultative processes prior to the enactment of such regulations ([paras 12–19]).

In interpreting these sections, the court drew upon established precedents regarding the requirement of rationality in administrative decisions, citing cases such as **New National Party of South Africa v Government of the Republic of South Africa** and **Pharmaceutical Manufacturers Association of SA** which emphasize the need for a rational relationship between the means adopted and the objectives pursued ([paras 28–29]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 20–49])

The Supreme Court of Appeal reviewed the competing interests represented by the Appellants against the Minister's asserted need to regulate hunting for conservation purposes. The court found that the manner in which the Minister formulated the 24-month rehabilitation period was not grounded in any scientific evidence or rational basis that would warrant such a period. Expert testimonies suggested that no captive-bred lion had ever been successfully rehabilitated for hunting purposes, undermining the assumption that self-sustainability could be achieved within the stipulated timeframe ([paras 33–39]).

Furthermore, the court scrutinized the Minister's decision-making process, noting deficiencies in the consultative procedures followed prior to the enactment of the regulations. Specifically, it highlighted that the Minister appeared to have disregarded critical stakeholder input that would have informed a more reasonable regulatory framework ([paras 25–27, 46–49]). 

Ultimately, the court concluded that the rationale behind regulation **24(2)** was intrinsically flawed—it lacked a rational connection to the legislative purpose of conserving biodiversity and protecting threatened species, particularly in the context of captive-bred lions ([para 45]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 50–52])

The Supreme Court of Appeal allowed the appeal in part, declaring regulation **24(2)** invalid as it pertained to captive-bred lions defined as "put and take" animals ([paras 51–52]). The court emphasized the need for rationality and evidence-based policymaking in regulatory frameworks that affect both biodiversity conservation and the livelihoods tied to predator breeding and hunting.

The implications of the ruling reaffirm the necessity for government bodies to ensure that consultative processes are followed meticulously and that regulations are based on sound scientific evidence, balancing ecological sustainability with economic interests in the wildlife industry. This judgment underscores the importance of a cooperative approach in environmental governance, wherein the voices of all stakeholders—including those in the breeding and hunting sector—are integrated into decision-making processes. Thus, the ruling serves as a critical reminder of the need for equitable stakeholder engagement and scientific grounding in environmental legislation ([paras 50–52]).
