Supreme Court of Appeal

The Minister of Trade and Industry & Another v Murendi Properties and Building Supplies (Pty) Ltd [2021] ZASCA 53

Murendi, which operated retail outlets and employed 125 staff members, aimed to expand its tile manufacturing plant to enhance its capacity. It was a registered black-owned company seeking funds for purchasing machinery and vehicles, essential for operational expansion ([4]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 53

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case revolves around an appeal concerning the Black Industrialist Scheme (BIS), which is designed to empower Black-owned businesses within the manufacturing sector. The respondent, Murendi Properties and Building Supplies (Pty) Ltd (Murendi), applied for a grant of R14,210,953 under the BIS after having its business approved for co-funding by the Industrial Development Corporation (IDC) in a parallel loan agreement ([1]–[5]).

Murendi, which operated retail outlets and employed 125 staff members, aimed to expand its tile manufacturing plant to enhance its capacity. It was a registered black-owned company seeking funds for purchasing machinery and vehicles, essential for operational expansion ([4]).

Following conditional approval from the BIS adjudication committee, the respondent submitted its grant application in August 2016. The application was finally approved in October 2017 conditional upon specific stipulations, including the maintenance of 100% black ownership and submission of valid documentation ([6]–[8]).

Upon applying for payment, Murendi faced issues with its Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment (BBBEE) certificate; the initial certificate was recalled by its certifying agency, Muthelo (Pty) Ltd, due to misclassification under the incorrect sector codes. This recall led Murendi to challenge this decision, claiming that it operated under the correct classification ([9]–[12]).

The appellants, namely the Minister of Trade and Industry and the Director-General of the Department of Trade and Industry, denied payment on the grounds that Murendi's BBBEE certificate was no longer valid and that the project's completion conditions were unmet ([16]–[17]).

The matter ultimately went to the High Court, where it ruled in favor of Murendi, stating that the company had met the necessary requirements, resulting in the current appeal ([13][16]).

I – Issues

The key legal questions in this case revolved around:

  1. Whether Murendi met all the qualifying requirements for the BIS grant payment.
  2. Whether the recall of the BBBEE certificate impacted Murendi's compliance with the grant application guidelines.
  3. Whether the purported issues concerning the eligibility of the claims, chiefly the alleged failure to present a valid BBBEE certificate and the alleged lack of project completion, constituted sufficient reason to deny payment of the grant ([1][15][18]).

R – Rules

The legal framework comprises several statutes and policies, the most notable being:

  • The Black Industrialist Scheme Guidelines, which outline specific eligibility criteria for applicants, including requirements for BBBEE compliance and investment obligations ([3]–[4]).
  • The Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment Act 53 of 2003, as amended, which aims to level the economic playing field in South Africa ([6][12]).
  • Case precedents such as KwaZulu-Natal Joint Liaison Committee v MEC for Education ([16]), which establishes that a decision by a functionary confirming eligibility for a grant creates a binding obligation which cannot be revoked unilaterally without just cause ([16]).

A – Application

The court’s analysis highlighted that Murendi had substantially complied with the mandatory conditions set forth in the BIS guidelines. It reiterated that a valid BBBEE certificate was required at the claim submission stage, but also emphasized the principle of substantive compliance over mere formalities ([26]).

The court scrutinized the DTI's justifications for withholding payment, particularly regarding the need for a valid certification at the time the claim was submitted ([18][21]). It concluded that the BBBEE certificate’s validity was maintained until it was formally set aside and that the issues raised by SANAS did not negate Murendi's status as an eligible entity ([20][24]).

Moreover, the court addressed the DTI's claim that project completion was necessary for payment. It clarified that the grant scheme could allow for payment upon the incurrence of costs, not necessarily upon project completion, thus siding with Murendi's position that prepayments to suppliers were necessary for project facilitation ([30][31]).

The high court’s findings were therefore affirmed, with the jurisprudence emphasizing the need for fairness and equity in interpreting administrative processes and public policy aimed at empowering previously disadvantaged groups ([27][28]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, reaffirming the High Court's decision that Murendi had met its obligations under the BIS and was entitled to payment of the grant. The ruling underscored the importance of addressing substantive compliance in grant schemes driving economic empowerment and the necessity of integrity in BBBEE certification processes ([32]).

The outcomes of this case will have far-reaching implications for compliance with the BIS, especially regarding the interpretation of requirements in grant applications and the administration of BBBEE certifications. This decision reiterates a legal precedent that may guide future evaluations of eligibility under government incentive schemes, ensuring that substantive outcomes align with intended empowerment policies in South Africa ([32]).
