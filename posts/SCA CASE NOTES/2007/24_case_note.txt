Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2007

# FIRAC Case Note on Clipsal Australia (Pty) Ltd v Trust Electrical Wholesalers [2007] SCA 24 (RSA)

## F - Facts

The case of  **Clipsal Australia (Pty) Ltd v Trust Electrical Wholesalers** ([1]) involves an appeal by the proprietors of a registered design (the appellants) and a local exclusive licensee against the ruling of the High Court, which had dismissed their application for relief on the grounds of design infringement by the respondents. The High Court, presided over by Blieden J, determined that the design registration was invalid because it lacked novelty and originality. Furthermore, the court found no infringement of the design ([1]).

The design in question, registered under the Designs Act 195 of 1993, is classified as an aesthetic design and pertains to a set of electrical accessory plates—the application of which is strictly defined in the law ([2]). The appellants claimed that their design, identified by registration number **A96/0687**, comprises specific configurations of rectangular cover plates for electrical sockets, which are surrounded by a square plate with a convex slope ([3]).

The respondents, **Trust Electrical Wholesalers** and **Gap Distributor**, challenged the registration by claiming a lack of novelty and originality, asserting that their products—the Lear G-2000 series of electrical socket covers—were not substantially similar to the registered design ([5]). The definitive statement of the design registration was discussed extensively, indicating the scope of legal protection conferred to the design ([6]).

## I - Issues

The primary legal issues in this case revolve around the following points:

1. **Were the design's features sufficiently novel and original to warrant protection under the Designs Act 195 of 1993?** ([12])
2. **Did the respondents infringe the registered design of the appellants, and if so, to what extent can the appellants claim relief?** ([25])
3. **Is the determination of 'novelty' based on mosaic prior art valid in South African designs law?** ([13])

## R - Rules

The court relied on several legal principles and sections of the Designs Act 195 of 1993, specifically section 14(1)(a), which necessitates that a design must be both new and original for valid registration ([20]). Key definitions under this Act highlighted distinctions between aesthetic and functional designs, emphasizing that aesthetic designs must appeal to visual judgment ([2]).

Precedents cited included:

- ***Ocular Sciences Ltd v Aspect Vision Care Ltd*** [1997] RPC 289, emphasizing the importance of interpreting design protection through the perspective of the eye ([7]).
- ***Schultz v Butt*** 1986 (3) SA 667 (A), illustrating the standard for novelty wherein substantial identity is needed to affirm legal protection ([14]).
- High Court case law establishing that similarity should not be based on piecing together various prior art into a "mosaic" to negate novelty ([13]).

## A - Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) assessed the High Court's findings regarding originality and novelty of the design. The High Court had identified multiple older art pieces suggesting that the registered design was merely a trade variant, concluding that it lacked both novelty and original characteristics ([12]). 

The SCA found that the High Court’s reliance on the "mosaic" method of determining novelty was incorrect, stating that only direct comparisons with the totality of prior designs should govern validity ([13]). The SCA maintained that the appellant's design could not simply be invalidated by piecing together separate elements from various prior arts, refuting the High Court's approach.

Further, the analysis of the aesthetic perspective emphasized the need to evaluate the registered design based on how an ordinary, informed consumer would perceive it ([10]). The determining factor was whether the dominant aesthetic characteristics of the registered design would be recognized as distinct when compared with the alternatives presented by the respondents.

Upon comparing both designs, the SCA determined that the differences in the contours and switch placements were insufficiently substantial to negate infringement. Even with stepped slopes and switch positions being a secondary characteristic, the overall shape and configuration were adequately similar to infringe upon the registered design ([25]).

## C - Conclusion

The SCA concluded that the High Court erred in holding that the registered design lacked novelty and originality, overturning its decision based on incorrect legal premises regarding the evaluation of prior art ([17]). The court's ruling emphasized a nuanced understanding of originality vis-à-vis the Designs Act—clarifying that originality must not conflate with an absence of generality in art.

The SCA upheld the appeal with costs and ordered that the respondents be enjoined from infringing upon the registered design ([26]). Additionally, a monetary inquiry was mandated to assess damages suffered by the appellants due to the infringement. This case underscores the sophisticated interplay between design law and consumer perception, solidifying the protective framework surrounding aesthetic designs within the South African legal context.
