# Case Note: Minister of Home Affairs and Others v Johnson and Others; Minister of Home Affairs and Another v Delorie and Others [2015] ZACC 8

**FIRAC Method**

**F – Facts:**  
This case arose from an urgent application for leave to appeal against a High Court’s (Western Cape Division) order, granted on the basis of urgency by Yekiso J. The first and second applicants were the Minister of Home Affairs and the Director-General of Home Affairs, while the respondents in both applications were individuals who had previously overstayed their temporary residence permits: Louise Hendrickson Egedal-Johnson and David Ross Henderson ([1]–[3]).

The facts were similar for both applications. Before the new legal framework was applied, they could leave South Africa and apply for new permits from outside the country, with the only consequence being an administrative fine ([3]). However, the new legal regime declared them "undesirable persons" and materially impacted their ability to return to South Africa, especially considering their familial circumstances ([4]).

The High Court heard applications for relief, consisting of two parts: Part A sought urgent relief to allow the respondents to return to South Africa, while Part B requested the invalidation of the new legal instruments and a review of individual declarations of undesirability ([5], [6]). The High Court issued a temporary order allowing the respondents to return to South Africa, subject to conditions, while further legal questions were deferred to Part B ([6]).

**I – Issues:**  
The primary issue was whether there were sufficient grounds to permit an appeal against the High Court’s order that granted only temporary relief ([2], [5]). The applicants contended that the High Court’s order interfered with their executive authority, suggesting it undermined the provisions of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act and set a precedent for bypassing internal remedies under the Immigration Act ([1]).

Another significant issue arose regarding the nature of the relief granted: whether it was justifiable to suspend the declarations of undesirability and allow the individual respondents to return to South Africa ([6]).

**R – Rules:**  
The legal framework pertinent to this case included the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 and the Immigration Act 13 of 2002. Specifically, section 7(2) of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act outlines requirements for administrative decisions, while the Immigration Act governs declarations of undesirability ([1], [3]).

The court referenced the precedent set in *National Treasury and Others v Opposition to Urban Tolling Alliance and Others* (OUTA) where it established the conditions for considering appeals against temporary relief ([7], [8]).

**A – Application:**  
In deciding whether to grant leave to appeal, the Constitutional Court assessed whether the High Court's order effectively impeded the applicants' executive functions or created a precedent undermining the legislative framework of the Immigration Act ([2], [7]). The court concluded that the temporary nature of the relief provided did not fully resolve the substantive issues in dispute and thus did not meet the criteria for an appeal as articulated in *OUTA* ([6], [8]).

The Court also clarified that the High Court’s order was narrowly tailored to address the immediate situation of the specific respondents rather than imposing a blanket suspension of the new legal framework, indicating that the appeal lacked merit and, therefore, did not serve the interests of justice ([2], [6]).

**C – Conclusion:**  
The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the application for leave to appeal with costs, highlighting that the temporary relief did not generate permanent legal ramifications that warranted appeal ([8]). The decision reaffirms the importance of the distinction between temporary and permanent remedies in administrative law and underscores the principle that appeals should only proceed in circumstances where critical legal issues are at stake.

The court’s ruling elucidates the limitations of the executive's authority within the framework of administrative law, emphasizing adherence to procedural justice and the necessity for a clear basis upon which overriding institutional authority is challenged ([8]).
