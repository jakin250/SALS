Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note: Sithole v S [2024] ZACC 31

## F - Facts

The case of **Sithole v S [2024] ZACC 31** revolves around the applicant, **Mandlakayise Enos Sithole**, who sought to appeal against the imposition of a non-parole period fixed during his sentencing by the High Court of South Africa, KwaZulu-Natal Division, Pietermaritzburg. The background of the case dates back to **2 February 2006**, when Sithole, alongside three accomplices, executed an armed robbery at the Camperdown Post Office, stealing R110,000 and other items, while also engaging in a shootout with police. 

One accomplice was apprehended at the scene, and through an undercover operation by police, Sithole was subsequently arrested. He faced serious charges, specifically one count of robbery with aggravating circumstances and four counts of attempted murder. Despite pleading not guilty, the High Court found him convicted on all counts based on compelling witness testimony and circumstantial evidence, resulting in a total sentence of **30 years’ imprisonment**—20 years for robbery and 10 years for each attempted murder.

During sentencing on **27 August 2006**, without providing opportunity for representations from either party, the High Court imposed a non-parole period, stipulating that Sithole would be ineligible for parole until he had served **20 years** of his sentence. After subsequent appeals to the Full Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal—which were dismissed—Sithole filed an application to the Constitutional Court for leave to appeal against the non-parole order, arguing that he was not afforded a chance to address the court on this matter and that exceptional circumstances for the non-parole order were not established.

The respondent, the State, conceded during the proceedings that the imposition of the non-parole period was flawed owing to improper procedure and the absence of exceptional circumstances ([paras 1-8]).

## I - Issues

The principal legal issues raised before the Constitutional Court included:

1. Whether the court has jurisdiction over the matter, specifically concerning the non-parole period stipulated by the High Court.
2. Whether the appellant's application for leave to appeal should be granted.
3. Whether the appeal concerning the non-parole order should be upheld.
4. Whether the High Court acted appropriately in imposing the non-parole order.
5. The appropriate remedy for the identified errors ([paras 16-17]).

## R - Rules

The legal framework guiding this case primarily involves **section 276B of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA)**, which allows a court to impose a non-parole period as part of a sentence of imprisonment, conditional upon two stipulations: 
1. The existence of "exceptional circumstances" justifying such a decision.
2. The requirement that the parties involved must be given the opportunity to submit representations prior to fixing a non-parole period ([paras 23-27]).

The Court referenced **S v Jimmale** ([2016] ZACC 27) and **S v Mhlongo** ([2016] ZASCA 152), which established that failure to provide opportunities for representation and lack of justification regarding the imposition of non-parole periods constitute significant procedural misdirections ([paras 25-29]).

## A - Application

In deliberating the case, the Constitutional Court under Judge Mhlantla noted that the High Court did not allow for submissions regarding the non-parole period when it issued its judgment. Rather, it rendered the decision unilaterally. This constituted a breach of the judicial tenet of fair trial rights as outlined in **section 12(1)(a)** of the Constitution of South Africa, which upholds the principle against arbitrary deprivation of liberty. The Court further clarified that the imposition of a non-parole period without adhering to the procedural requirements under the CPA constitutes a material misdirection.

The Court found that the High Court’s order was also problematic because it was inconsistent with the legislative intent of providing a non-parole period that reflects the seriousness of the crime balanced against opportunities for rehabilitation. Notably, despite the High Court's recommendation, there were no arguments presented by either party during sentencing regarding the need for imposing a non-parole period, and the applicant maintained that he would have been eligible for parole after serving half of his sentence without such a directive ([paras 30-31]).

The respondent’s concession further indicated shared recognition of the procedural error, which supported the notion that the application for leave to appeal should be granted ([para 32]).

## C - Conclusion

In conclusion, the Constitutional Court ruled in favor of Sithole by granting leave to appeal specifically against the imposition of the non-parole order. It determined that the High Court had indeed misdirected itself by imposing a non-parole period without allowing for argumentation and failing to establish exceptional circumstances to justify such a decision. The Court subsequently set aside the non-parole order, restoring Sithole's eligibility for parole after serving potentially half of his sentence. Conversely, leave to appeal regarding the convictions and other sentences was denied. 

The case illustrates paramount issues surrounding judicial fairness, procedural justice, and the necessity for courts to observe established legal principles when crafting sentences that restrict individual freedoms ([paras 34-35]).
