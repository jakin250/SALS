# FIRAC Case Note: Blakes Maphanga Inc v Outsurance Insurance Company Limited (144/09) [2010] ZASCA 19

### F – Facts
This case arose out of disputes between Blakes Maphanga Incorporated (the appellant), a legal firm, and Outsurance Insurance Company Limited (the respondent), an insurance provider. The respondent terminated its mandate with the appellant on 4 March 2005 and demanded the payment of certain sums collected on its behalf by the appellant ([2]). The core issue was whether the appellant could offset claimed legal fees against the amounts collected for the respondent.

The relationship of the parties hinged on an oral agreement that permitted remuneration in accordance with a provided fee structure and tariff. The appellant claimed fees, while the respondent contended that the fees owed were disputed and not liquidated, necessitating taxation before any set-off could occur ([3]–[4]). The appellant asserted that a formula for set-off existed within the terms of their agreement, while the respondent argued that all amounts collected were to be remitted without deduction ([3]–[4]).

After several communications, including a claim by the appellant of a closing fee of R150 per file, disputes escalated regarding the amount owed, leading to the respondent initiating proceedings to recover R233,676.56 from the appellant’s trust account. The issue was further complicated by disputes over the alleged arreas and the relevance of taxation in determining the validity of the fees claimed by the appellant ([7]–[11]).

The trial court, under Van Rooyen AJ, concluded that the claims for fees were indeed disputable and required taxation to achieve the necessary legal clarity for any potential set-off ([12]). The full court upheld this decision, which resulted in the appellant's appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([13]).

### I – Issues
The central legal issue was whether the appellant could assert a right of set-off against amounts collected on behalf of the respondent for fees that were disputed and not yet taxed ([11]). More specifically:
- Could the appellant's claims for fees be considered liquidated without prior taxation?
- What effect does the lack of taxation have on the ability to invoke set-off in the context of legal fees?
- Could the terms of the oral agreement, specifically regarding the prohibition of set-off, be interpreted to allow the appellant’s claims to stand against the respondent’s amounts held in trust?

### R – Rules
The judgment relied on established principles of law surrounding set-off, taxation of legal fees, and the attorney-client relationship ([12]–[19]). The courts consistently held that:
1. **Set-Off Principles**: Set-off can only occur when there are two liquidated claims, as established in various precedents ([14]–[15]).
2. **Taxation Requirement**: Claims by attorneys for their fees become liquidated only upon taxation. An untaxed fee is not a liquidated amount, nor is it capable of easy proof, and thus does not permit set-off ([17]).
3. **Attorney-Client Agreement**: The attorney's right to enforce payment is predicated upon the terms outlined in the mandate, encompassing the necessity for timely payments and adherence to agreed tariffs ([3]–[4],[12]).

The court also referenced several cases illustrating that without consensus on the fees due and while disputes linger, taxation is essential before a determination of liquidated claims can be made ([17]–[19]).

### A – Application
In its examination, the court found that:
- The appellant's claims for fees were disputed, and thus, according to the law, these claims were not liquidated, rendering the appellant's assertion of set-off premature ([13],[19]).
- The unclear terms of the engagements, especially regarding the timeliness of payment, did not eliminate the need for taxation to validate the fees claimed ([12],[19]).
- Legal precedent dictated that an attorney's fees must be taxed to establish their validity. Therefore, the basis for the appellant's claim of a right to set-off collapsed since the fees had not completed the required legal processes ([18],[19]).

In dismissing the appeal, the court underscored that the appellant had consented to the possible taxation of its fees prior to invoking set-off, acknowledging a recognized dispute regarding the amounts ([18]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court's ruling that the appellant was not entitled to set off the claimed fees against the collected amounts held in trust ([19]). The judgment reaffirmed that without taxation, the appellant's claims remained indeterminate and thus unenforceable as liquidated debts. This decision has significant implications for legal practitioners, reaffirming the necessity of compliance with taxation as a prerequisite for asserting claims upon collected funds in trust scenarios. The court also imposed costs on the appellant, including the costs for two counsels, which signals the gravity with which the court viewed the procedural integrity of fee claims ([1]), ([19]). 

This case underscores the importance of clear contractual terms and the adherence to legal processes in attorney-client relationships, particularly regarding the collection and settlement of fees.
