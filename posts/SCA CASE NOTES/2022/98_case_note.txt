# FIRAC Case Note: Engen Petroleum Ltd v Flotank Transport (Pty) Ltd (876/20) [2022] ZASCA 98

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–8])
The case of **Engen Petroleum Ltd v Flotank Transport (Pty) Ltd** involved Engen Petroleum Ltd (hereafter "Engen" or "the applicant") appealing against a judgment from the Northern Cape Division of the High Court, which had dismissed its application to enforce a cession agreement against Flotank Transport (Pty) Ltd (hereafter "Flotank" or "the respondent"). This dispute centered around debts owed by Windsharp Trading (Pty) Limited (hereafter "Windsharp") to Engen amounting to over R5.5 million as of June 2014, for which two cession agreements were established in April 2012 and June 2014.

Engen initiated liquidation proceedings against Windsharp in November 2014, which culminated in a final liquidation order by January 2015 ([para 6]). Following this, Engen notified Flotank of its claimed cession of Windsharp's debts. Flotank, however, continued to make payments to Windsharp rather than Engen, asserting that the debts related to Windsharp’s cession would revert to its liquidators upon the latter's liquidation. After Flotank's continued payments to Windsharp, Engen sought the recovery of the amounts paid between December 2014 and January 2015, asserting its rights under the cession.

The High Court dismissed Engen's application, reasoning that the cession constituted a pledge rather than an outright cession, ultimately leading to this appeal ([paras 8–9]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 10–11])
The core legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was the interpretation of the second cession agreement established between Engen and Windsharp—specifically whether it constituted a pledge or an out-and-out cession incorporating a **pactum fiduciae**. Additional issues included:

1. Whether the interpretation of the cession could be considered a question of law which would allow Engen to argue its case afresh on appeal.
2. If the cession constituted an outright transfer of rights, whether Flotank was obligated to make payments to Engen instead of Windsharp after having received notice of the cession.

The SCA recorded, for the first time on appeal, Engen's argument that the second cession was indeed an out-and-out cession, contrary to the High Court’s interpretation ([paras 10–11]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 12–18])
The principles governing the interpretation of cessions in South African law hinge on the intention of the parties involved. The case draws upon established precedents to guide the interpretation, including the distinction between cessions in **securitatem debiti** and outright cessions.

The SCA cited **Grobler v Oosthuizen** (2009), highlighting the two prevailing theories regarding cession in securitatem debiti:

1. **Pledge Theory**: Involves the cedent retaining a reversionary interest in the ceded debts, suggesting only a limited right is given to the cessionary.
2. **Outright Cession Theory**: Posits that the ceded debts are entirely transferred to the cessionary with the intention of creating an outright transfer—resulting in the cedent retaining an enforceable personal right to reclaim upon discharge of the secured debt ([paras 12–14]).

The principles affirm that, while the pledge theory is typically regarded as the default position, parties may expressly structure a cession as an outright transfer, as was argued by Engen in this case ([paras 15–17]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 16–18])
The SCA interpreted the relevant clauses of the 2014 cession agreement, determining that the wording indicated an intention for an outright cession to Engen, inclusive of all reversionary rights Windsharp had to the debts ceded. The SCA reasoned that the explicit language employed in the cession signified an intention to transfer full title and not merely create a security interest ([para 17]).

The court established that Flotank had an obligation to honor payments to Engen following proper notice of the cession rather than to Windsharp, a consequence reflecting the error by the High Court in not recognizing the outright cession ([para 18]). The SCA concluded that Engen’s entitlement to the amounts paid was clear, given the questioned interpretation of the cession agreement, thereby establishing the precedent that parties can structure their commercial agreements in a manner reflecting their true intent.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 19])
The SCA ultimately found in favor of Engen, ordering that the appeal be upheld with costs and affirming that the 2014 cession constituted an out-and-out transfer of rights to Engen ([para 19]). The court’s ruling emphasized the importance of precise drafting and clarity of intention in commercial agreements and reiterates that the legal interpretation of such documents is a question of law reviewable on appeal.

The judgment underscores the legal reliance on cession agreements in securitatem debiti within commercial practice and serves as a critical reference for the treatment of out-and-out cessions versus pledges in South African law. This case will likely influence how similar agreements are structured in the future, reinforcing that explicit communication of parties’ intentions in contractual documents is vital to avoid disputes over such substantive interpretations.
