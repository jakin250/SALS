# FIRAC Case Note: **Berzack v Huntrex 277 (Pty) Ltd and Others** [2022] ZASCA 17 

## F – Facts
**Background and Procedural History** ([paras 1–8]):  
The case centers around a property dispute involving Margot Berzack (the applicant) and Huntrex 277 (Pty) Ltd (the first respondent), concerning the nature of a registered servitude associated with a garden on a property in Constantia, Cape Town. The servitude was established between Berzack and a third party, Mr. Wellens, for their mutual benefit, allowing Berzack continued access to and use of a garden area on the Huntrex property after a subdivision of her land. The High Court had earlier ruled that the servitude constituted a personal servitude of *usus* rather than a praedial servitude, which allowed Huntrex 277 to alter the servitude area. Berzack's counter-application for maintaining her rights based on acquired prescription was dismissed.

## I – Issues
**Legal Questions** ([paras 6–21]):  
1. Whether the servitude in question is a praedial servitude or a personal servitude of *usus*.
2. If deemed a praedial servitude, whether it is capable of registration under section 66 of the Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937.
3. The implications of the nature of the servitude for the parties involved, particularly regarding ownership rights and registration.

## R – Rules
**Legal Authorities** ([paras 22–28; 48–57]):  
1. **Definition of Servitudes**: A praedial servitude must benefit one property (the dominant tenement) while imposing a burden on another (the servient tenement), conferring real rights that attach to the land (City of Tshwane v Link Africa (Pty) Ltd and Others [2015] ZACC 29) and must meet the modal requirements for registration (section 66 of the Deeds Registries Act).
2. **Interpretation Principles**: The interpretation of servitudes must favor property law principles, including the presumption against servitudes (Willoughby's Consolidated Co Ltd v Copthall Stores Ltd [1918] AD 1) where ambiguities exist regarding servitude nature (Kruger v Joles Eiendom (Pty) Ltd and Another [2008] ZASCA 138).
3. **Utilitas Requirement**: Identified as a prerequisite for praedial servitudes, indicating that benefit cannot merely be personal pleasure but must provide a permanent advantage to the landholder.

## A – Application
**Court’s Reasoning** ([paras 29–50]):  
The court held that the original clause P represented a praedial servitude based upon several key points:
1. **Element of Utilitas**: The servitude afforded Berzack the right to garden, which enhanced the **economic value** of her retained property. The court emphasized that personal pleasure did not negate utilitas (para 24).
2. **Long-standing Nature of Use**: The servitude had enjoyed over thirty years of continuous usage by Berzack, thus supporting the interpretation of servitude as enduring and beneficial to the dominant tenement.
3. **Common Intent of Parties**: The original agreement was interpreted as clearly intending to confer rights that would extend to successors-in-title, reinforcing the notion of a praedial servitude rather than a transient personal one.
4. **Legislative Interpretation**: The court refuted the argument that the servitude was personal based on a narrow interpretation of its terms, asserting that such an interpretation disregarded essential context and intent (Endumeni Municipality principles).

## C – Conclusion
**Court’s Holding and Implications** ([paras 51–72]):  
The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the High Court judgment, recognizing the servitude as a praedial servitude—capable of lasting beyond Berzack's lifetime and thereby enforceable against successive owners of the servient property. This effectively would maintain the economic advantage of Berzack's property while preserving her personal gardening rights. The High Court's order allowing Huntrex 277 to demolish existing structures and alter the servitude area was set aside, reasserting Berzack's rights. The implications of this case extend to property law and the treatment of non-traditional servitudes, recognizing their legally ascertainable nature based on intent and established utility rather than strict adherence to conventional definitions.

This ruling marks a significant development in property rights, potentially influencing future cases concerning a broader interpretation of servitudes following the movement toward informal property enhancement rights.
