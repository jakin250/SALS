# FIRAC Case Note: Melamed & Hurwitz Incorporated v Goldberg

**Case Title**: Melamed & Hurwitz Incorporated v Goldberg  
**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal, South Africa  
**Case No**: 686/07  
**Neutral Citation**: Melamed & Hurwitz Inc v Goldberg (686/2007) [2009] ZASCA 15 (19 March 2009)  
**Judges**: Streicher, Mthiyane, Lewis, Mhlantla JJA, Leach AJA  
**Heard**: 2 March 2009  
**Delivered**: 19 March 2009  
**No Precedential Significance**

## F – Facts 

This case arises from a dispute over legal fees between Melamed & Hurwitz Incorporated (the Appellant) and Marlene Goldberg (the Respondent). In October 2004, Mrs. Goldberg sought the legal services of Mr. Stephen Melamed, a director at the appellant firm, during her divorce proceedings ([1]). Initially dissatisfied with a pre-existing settlement agreement established with her husband, she engaged Melamed to help negotiate a better deal. Over a span of seven months, Melamed provided legal advice that culminated in a more favorable settlement in April 2005, amounting to R5 million, alongside ongoing maintenance ([2], [4]).

The appellant later billed Goldberg R450,000 plus VAT for their services, arguing this was a previously agreed fee. Goldberg contested the validity of this fee on grounds that no agreement had been reached and that the amount claimed was excessive, constituting overreaching ([2]). The High Court, presided over by Bregman AJ, ultimately referred the fee claim to the Law Society for assessment due to the lack of clarity surrounding the agreement and the nature of the fees involved ([3], [5]).

## I – Issues 

The core legal questions before the Supreme Court of Appeal were: 
1. Did an agreement actually exist between Melamed and Goldberg over the fee?
2. Was the agreed fee of R450,000 excessive, constituting an overreaching of the client by the attorney?
3. Did the High Court err in referring the fee to the Law Society for assessment without explicit findings of overreaching or invalidity of the agreement? ([6], [7]).

## R – Rules 

The rules governing attorney-client fee agreements in South Africa dictate that attorneys are entitled to charge fees as agreed with their clients. However, if a client is found to have been overreached—meaning that the attorney extracted an unconscionable or exorbitant fee through undue advantage—the agreement may be rendered void ([3]).

Key precedents cited include:
- **Cape Law Society v Luyt** 1929 CPD 281, which illustrates that an attorney’s demand for an excessive bill could indicate misconduct.
- **Law Society of the Cape of Good Hope v Tobias** 1991 (1) SA 430 (C), establishing that clients have to be free agents in agreeing to fees.
- Definitions surrounding “overreach,” noting it involves attorneys circumventing or outwitting clients ([3]).

## A – Application 

The Supreme Court assessed the nature of the engagement between Melamed and Goldberg. Examining both parties' testimonies and the nature of their communications, the Court emphasized the conflicting interpretations of the fee agreement. Melamed claimed they discussed a fee structure based on performance, while Goldberg contended she was shocked by the quoted amount and felt little work had been done on her behalf ([8], [19]).

The Court noted that evidence from Melamed lacked detail, with no formal record of hours worked or precise tasks undertaken, which raised doubts about the propriety of the claimed fee ([11], [17]). Specifically, the suggested fee significantly exceeded the standard hourly rate multiplied by any plausible number of hours worked, making the figure seem not only extravagant but excessive as well ([18]). 

Goldberg's emotional state during the negotiation, her expressed reservations about the fee immediately following its suggestion, and her lack of familiarity with legal processes further illustrated the potential for undue influence by Melamed ([20]). Thus, the Court found Melamed had indeed overreached Goldberg, and the attorney-client relationship exhibited the signs of coercive disadvantage ([21]).

## C – Conclusion 

Ultimately, the Supreme Court concluded the agreement to pay R450,000 was void due to the extortionate nature of the fee. The court ruled that the fee should be referred to the Law Society of the Northern Provinces for assessment, allowing for a proper determination of reasonable legal costs ([22]). 

The appeal against the High Court's referral was dismissed with costs, affirming the lower court’s approach while rectifying procedural elements regarding the assessment process ([22]). The implications of this judgment emphasize the importance of clear fee agreements and the protections afforded to clients against potentially exploitative practices by attorneys in South Africa, reaffirming that unwarranted attorney fees can be contested effectively and must be justified in correlation with services rendered.
