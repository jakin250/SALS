# Case Note: Gilroy Clements McAlpine v Penny McAlpine N O and Stand 37 Anderbolt Extension 11 (Pty) Ltd Case No 299/95

**FIRAC Method**

### F – Facts

This case revolves around a dispute between brothers Gilroy and Ian McAlpine concerning their ownership shares in Stand 37 Anderbolt Extension 11 (Pty) Ltd, which owns immovable property. On 5 May 1981, the brothers entered into a written agreement (referred to as "agreement A") wherein Ian sold Gilroy a 50% shareholding in the company for R55,000. The agreement outlined that Gilroy would have exclusive use of a specified portion of the property (marked A B C D), while Ian would have exclusive use of the other portion (EFGH). 

Both agreements contained restrictions regarding the transfer of shares to third parties, and necessitated that any proposed sale of shares must first be offered to the other party (clauses 7 and 8 of agreement A) ([paras 2–4]).

Further, on 22 May 1981, the brothers signed an additional agreement (termed "agreement B") which aimed to clarify the rights of each brother in the context of the shareholding. Notably, it stated that, upon the death of either brother, the survivor would inherit the deceased's shares in the company at no cost ([paras 5–6]).

In June 1988, Ian passed away unexpectedly. Following his death, Gilroy sought the transfer of Ian's shares to himself based on the agreements in question. However, Ian's estate, represented by his widow Penny McAlpine, denied this claim, leading Gilroy to initiate legal action in the Witwatersrand Local Division ([paras 5–8]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal question before the court was whether the provisions within the two agreements constituted a “pactum successorium” (a contract regulating succession) and therefore rendered the agreements invalid under South African law ([paras 8–9]). A secondary issue involved whether the terms of the agreements could be interpreted as donations inter vivos or mortis causa, which would affect their enforceability ([para 18]).

### R – Rules

South African law recognizes that **pactum successoria** are typically invalid, as they interfere with testamentary freedom and circumvent required formalities for wills, as established in case law including *Borman en De Vos N N O en 'n Ander v Potgietersrusse Tabakkorporasie Bpk en 'n Ander* 1976 (3) SA 488 (A) ([paras 9–10]). The essence of a pactum successoria is that it involves an agreement that dictates the succession of property after death.

The court noted that the validity of agreements can hinge on the **vesting test**, determining whether the right vested in the beneficiary during the lifetime of the promisor or only after their death. If vesting occurs post-mortem, the agreement may be construed as a pactum successorium ([paras 21–22]).

### A – Application

The court scrutinized the peculiar wording and conditions of agreement B, emphasizing that the term "will get" and "will go," implied an intention for the shareholdings to transfer only upon the death of the first-dying brother. Consequently, the court held that both brothers entered into an arrangement closely resembling a pactum successorium, as the transfer of shares was contingent upon survival ([paras 29–31]).

The appellant contended that the agreements constituted inter vivos donations that were simply contingent on survivorship, thereby questioning their classification as a pactum successorium. However, the court sided with the arguments asserting that the agreements did indeed impose a future claim conditional upon the death of one party, thus fitting the description of a pactum successorium ([paras 17, 31-32]).

Additionally, the discussion around the validity and intent of the agreements offered no sufficient evidence to argue against the presumption that they aimed to arrange for posthumous distributions, ultimately emphasizing that the agreements fettered the freedom of testation of both brothers ([paras 19, 30]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of South Africa, led by Corbett CJ, dismissed the appeal, confirming the trial court's finding that the agreements constituted a pactum successorium and were therefore invalid. The judgment highlighted the importance of maintaining testamentary freedom and the requisite formalities surrounding dying declarations. 

The court's holding reinforced the necessity for clarity and adherence to established legal principles in drafting agreements that resolve ownership and succession issues, particularly within familial and commercial contexts. The outcome serves as a crucial reminder for parties engaging in similar agreements to consider the implications of their arrangements in regard to the laws governing property succession, and particularly the stringent measures surrounding pactum successoria ([paras 33–34]).
