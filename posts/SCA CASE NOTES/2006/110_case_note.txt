Source Folder: 2006

### Case Note: Pogostich Business Brokers (Pty) Ltd v Kenneth McLeod [2006] SCA 136 (RSA)

#### FIRAC Method

**F – Facts**

This case arises from the decision of the Johannesburg High Court (Mailula J) on 8 June 2004 ([para 2]). The court ordered the attachment of funds amounting to R1,263,358.62 in the account of Pogostich Business Brokers (Pty) Ltd, pending the finalisation of an action involving Patrick Mantle and Ozden Mantle ([para 2]). The fund attachment was sought by the respondents, Kenneth and Ryan McLeod, who alleged fraudulent misrepresentations by the Mantles that resulted in a loss of money ([para 3]).

The McLeods claimed that the money in Pogostich’s account was the proceeds of a sale involving the Mantles, whereas the applicants disputed this, asserting that the funds belonged to the second applicant’s siblings ([para 4]). The High Court upheld the respondents' claims, asserting the legality of the attachment while rejecting claims of improper procedure and consent ([paras 5-6]).

Following a refusal for leave to appeal by the High Court, the Supreme Court of Appeal granted permission on 29 July 2005 ([para 6]). An appeal was noted on 26 August 2005, but the record required by court rules was not lodged within the designated three-month period ([para 6-10]). Subsequently, because of disputes with their attorneys regarding fees and loss of faith in the appeal process, the applicants did not actively pursue the appeal ([paras 8-9]).

After consulting with new counsel on 6 April 2006, the applicants sought to revive the appeal, eventually lodging the record on 19 May 2006, over eight months after the initial appeal notice was filed ([para 10]). The Supreme Court of Appeal was asked to condone the late lodging of the record, giving rise to the current application ([para 1]).

**I – Issues**

The primary issue addressed by the court was whether the applicants could successfully apply for the reinstatement of their lapsed appeal based on a failure to lodge the necessary record within the time stipulated by court rules ([para 1]). This included consideration of whether sufficient cause existed to condone the late filing based on the circumstances surrounding the applicants' decisions and actions following the initial ruling of the High Court ([para 12]).

**R – Rules**

The court relied on the established precedent from *Cairn’s Executors v Gaarn* 1912 AD 180, which elaborated on the conditions required to grant relief from strict compliance with procedural rules ([para 11]). The court reiterated that failure to take steps toward an appeal for more than a year puts the onus on the applicant to demonstrate 'sufficient cause' to justify reopening the matter ([para 11]). Rule 8 of the court's procedural rules was also highlighted, with particular focus on subsection (3), stating that failure to lodge the appeal record within the prescribed timeframe results in the automatic lapsing of the appeal ([para 12]).

**A – Application**

The court examined the timeline of events in assessing the applicants' lack of action following the noting of the appeal. Notably, the court pointed out that there were substantial delays with no satisfactory explanation from the applicants about the failure to enforce their appeal rights after 26 August 2005 ([para 12]). The second applicant's documented communications indicated that he had chosen to abide by the adverse ruling, effectively admitting acceptance of the judgment ([para 9]).

Despite the apparent merits of the appeal, in which the second applicant believed they had strong prospects ([para 8]), the court stressed that the applicants’ previous inaction spoke volumes about their commitment to pursuing the appeal after accepting the judgment as final for several months ([para 12]). There was no indication that the applicants had made any attempts to address or challenge the issues surrounding their legal representation until after the substantial lapse in time, emphasizing the court’s reluctance to intervene in what had effectively become a settled matter ([para 13]).

Furthermore, the court reiterated the importance of finality in litigation, particularly where the successful party (the respondents) had vested interests in the judgment obtained ([para 11]). The absence of compelling evidence to support the applicants’ position further strained their request for the appeal to be reinstated ([para 13]).

**C – Conclusion**

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled against the restoration of the lapsed appeal, citing insufficient grounds to justify condonation for the lack of timely prosecution ([para 14]). The application for reinstatement was thus refused with costs. The ruling reinforces the principle that, in civil procedure, the onus lies on the parties to actively pursue appeals and engage with legal processes. The implications of this decision underline the importance of timely action following adverse judgments and highlight the judiciary’s commitment to preventing the prolongation of settled disputes ([para 14]).
