Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: Louw & Others v Davids & Others (783/2017) [2018] ZASCA 70

## FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts

The case arises from a contractual dispute involving a fishing quota and the sale of members’ interests in a close corporation, Meermin Viserye CC, granted under the Marine Living Resources Act 18 of 1998 (MLRA) ([1–2]). The appellants, ten women from Lamberts Bay who were members of the close corporation, sought to cancel a sale agreement concerning their members’ interests due to alleged breaches by the first and second respondents, Ashraf Davids and Shereen Mathir, who purchased the interests ([2]).

The initial agreement, reached on 8 July 2012, outlined a total sale price of R4 million, with payments linked to the effective date defined as the date on which the Minister would issue the annual small pelagic fishing permit for the 2013 fishing season ([5–6]). Amendments to the payment structure were made, and payments totaling between R18,000 and R30,465 were disbursed to the sellers before the final agreement was reached, including an addendum that altered the payment schedule to R50,000 per month ([10–11]). Disputed facts included whether an oral variation of the payment terms occurred, which the respondents asserted was valid despite the original agreement's non-variation clause ([17]).

The appellants experienced financial difficulties, requesting multiple variations and renegotiations, but eventually invoked cancellation on grounds of breach, claiming failure to pay the full agreed amounts in a timely manner ([18–20]).

### I – Issues

The central legal issues for determination are:

1. **Whether the cancellation of the agreement was justified.**
2. **Whether the sale of the members’ interests in Meermin was subject to a suspensive condition requiring approval from the Minister of Agriculture, Forestry, and Fisheries ([19–20]).**

### R – Rules

The rules for the application of contract law were primarily drawn from established legal principles regarding breach of contract, specifically surrounding the rights of an innocent party upon breach ([23–24]). Relevant case law allows for cancellation of contracts only if the breach is deemed material, encapsulating principles of fairness and proportionality ([24–27]). Notably, in the precedents cited, cancellation is treated as a last resort, necessitating that the breach goes "to the root of the contract" ([24–25]).

Furthermore, the terms of the original sale included significant stipulations regarding the completion of forms, compliance with regulatory conditions, and the express warranty that no other claims were outstanding against the quota ([32–34]).

### A – Application

The court found that while the appellants claimed the respondents had breached the agreement by failing to meet payment obligations as stipulated in the addendum, evidence indicated that the respondents had indeed made payments beyond the originally scheduled dates ([21–22], [27]). However, the appellants continued to accept these payments until the dispute arose. This conduct showed an implicit agreement to a revised payment schedule that did not necessitate a written confirmation per the original agreement's non-variation clause ([22], [28]).

Critical to the court's analysis was the fact that by the time of seeking cancellation, a significant portion of the agreed purchase price had been paid—approximately 96%, with only a small amount outstanding ([28–30]). The court expressed that cancellation would be inherently disproportionate given these circumstances, arguing that the appellants' request for cancellation, especially when they appeared unable to tender restitution for the payments they had received, was untenable ([29]).


In considering whether the need for ministerial approval constituted a condition precedent for the sale, the court pointed out that the agreement did not explicitly reflect such a contingency ([31–34]). The obligations imposed on the appellants to fulfill necessary accounts and documentation suggested that the acceptance of the sale had fully transferred the risks, thereby making the assertion of a suspensive condition baseless.

### C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, reaffirming the lower court's ruling that cancellation was not justified ([35–36]). The judgment reinforced the principle that parties must adhere to the agreed terms and must not unilaterally abandon their obligations under contractual agreements, especially when significant performance has already been rendered. The implications of this judgment serve to clarify the standards for contractual cancellation and emphasize the necessity for clear communication and documentation in the amendment of contractual obligations.

In conclusion, the court established that the principles governing contract law favor the performance of contractual obligations and only permit cancellation in cases of substantial and material breach, particularly when restitution becomes impractical. The decision underscores the duty of contracting parties to act in good faith and maintain consistent communication regarding agreed terms.
