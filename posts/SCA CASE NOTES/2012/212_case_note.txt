Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note: Agri Wire (Pty) Ltd v The Competition Commissioner [2012] ZASCA 134

## F – Facts

The case involved **Agri Wire (Pty) Ltd** and its affiliates (the first and second appellants) versus the **Competition Commissioner** and various respondents engaged in the wire manufacturing sector. The litigation arose from the **Competition Commission's** invocation of its **Corporate Leniency Policy (CLP)** after **Consolidated Wire Industries (Pty) Ltd (CWI)**, a group member, disclosed cartel involvement. Following an internal audit, CWI provided information regarding alleged anti-competitive practices implicating Agri Wire and other companies under the CLP, receiving conditional leniency in return ([2]–[4]).

Agri Wire challenged the legality of the referral to the **Competition Tribunal**, arguing that the evidence obtained from CWI was unlawfully secured under the CLP, which they contended had no lawful basis under the **Competition Act 89 of 1998** ([5]–[11]). They sought judicial review to set aside the referral, positing that the grant of conditional leniency improperly allowed CWI to evade penalties while imperilling the integrity of the competition enforcement process.

The inquiry into the legality of the CLP and its alignment with statutory powers occupied centre stage in the case ([21]–[26]).

## I – Issues

1. **Lawfulness of the CLP**: Is the Corporate Leniency Policy lawful, and does the Act empower the Competition Commission to grant conditional and total immunity to cartel participants?
2. **Review of the Tribunal Referral**: Can the Commission refer complaints to the Tribunal without including all members of the cartel, and does this violate Agri Wire's rights to a fair adjudication?
3. **Jurisdiction**: Did the North Gauteng High Court possess jurisdiction to review the Commission's actions regarding the referral based on the argument over legislative procedures ([11], [12], [20])?

## R – Rules

1. **Competition Act 89 of 1998**: The Act governs competition law in South Africa and establishes the Competition Commission and Tribunal. Relevant sections include:
   - **Section 4(1)**: prohibits certain anti-competitive conduct, such as collusion.
   - **Section 51**: governs the referral of matters by the Commission to the Tribunal.
   - **Section 59(3)(f)**: allows the Tribunal to take into account co-operation in penalties for anti-competitive conduct ([2], [4]).

2. **Corporate Leniency Policy (CLP)**: This policy is designed to encourage whistleblowing by offering immunity to cartel participants who disclose violations. It details procedures and conditions under which leniency may be granted. Significantly:
   - Clause 3.1 states that immunity is granted for full disclosure of cartel activity ([6]–[7]).
   - Clause 9.1 mentions that conditional immunity can be revoked, which means that participants are not fully absolved of liability until total immunity is conferred post-investigation ([7]).

3. **Jurisdictional Considerations**: Case law regarding jurisdiction, specifically regarding what constitutes a reviewable decision, was referenced, including the interpretations of powers within the Act ([12]–[20]).

## A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal applied the statutory provisions to assess the legality of the **Corporate Leniency Policy** and its implementation. The court determined that:

1. **Lawfulness of the CLP**: It affirmed that the **Competition Act** does permit the **Competition Commission** to implement a CLP aimed at combatting cartel conduct. This aligns with the Act's intention to promote competition and transparency ([21]–[22]). While the appellants argued that the Commission could not issue immunity, the court found no prohibition in the Act against such policies, emphasizing the necessity of tools to detect and deter cartel behaviour ([24]).

2. **Referral of Complaints**: The court concurred with the Commission's discretion to refer only certain members of a cartel to the Tribunal without implicating all participants. This interpretation reflects a pragmatic approach to enforcement, considering the complexities of cartel operations. Citing statutory provisions, the court rejected the notion that not including all members constituted a procedural unfairness ([25]).

3. **Jurisdiction**: The court rejected arguments against the High Court's jurisdiction to review the Commission’s referral as misconceived. The Competition Commission's reliance on **Section 27(1)(c)** to assert that the Tribunal held exclusive jurisdiction was found untenable, as the referral’s legality could be challenged at an earlier stage without needing adjudication from the Tribunal ([12]–[20]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming that the Corporate Leniency Policy is lawful and that the Competition Commission acted within its powers under the Competition Act when it granted conditional immunity to CWI. It upheld the legitimacy of the referral to the Tribunal and confirmed that the High Court had jurisdiction over the matter. The ruling elucidates the framework within which the Commission operates, reinforcing the efficacy and necessity of leniency programs in combatting anti-competitive practices while upholding legal standards of procedure and fairness ([26]).

The decision has significant implications for future competition law enforcement in South Africa, particularly the operational parameters of leniency policies and their integral role in dismantling cartels. As a primary tool for regulation, the ruling highlights the balance between promoting transparency and ensuring the individuals and entities within the market abide by statutory norms of competition. The court’s determination creates a precedent for similar cases involving cartel conduct and competition regulation in South Africa.
