Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: *Naidoo v Minister of Police* [2015] ZASCA 152

### F – Facts ([paras 1–5])

On the evening of 12 April 2010, Charmaine Naidoo (appellant), a victim of domestic violence, was assaulted by her ex-husband, leading to her hospitalization. Seeking police assistance, Ms. Naidoo went to the Lenasia South Police Station two days later, where she was incorrectly advised that she needed a protection order before laying charges against her assailant under the *Domestic Violence Act 116 of 1998* (the Act). Following further interactions with the police, Ms. Naidoo was subsequently arrested and detained after a counter-charge was laid against her by her ex-husband upon police instructions.

Ms. Naidoo was incorrectly handled by the officers involved, particularly by Inspector Molefe, who failed to follow proper procedures under the Act and subjected her to further trauma through excessive force during her transport to court. After charges against her were withdrawn the following day, she initiated a delictual action seeking damages from the Minister of Police along with several officers for wrongful arrest, assault, and a breach of statutory duties related to her treatment as a victim of domestic violence.

The Gauteng Local Division of the High Court initially dismissed her claims, asserting procedural errors regarding the citations of all parties involved and the legality of her arrest based on the police’s interpretation of domestic violence provisions.

### I – Issues ([paras 5–19])

The principal legal issues in this case revolved around:

1. **Negligence and Duty of Care**: Whether the South African Police Service (SAPS) owed a duty of care to Ms. Naidoo under the Act and whether they breached that duty.
2. **Legality of Arrest**: Determining if Ms. Naidoo's arrest was lawful under the provisions of the *Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977*, particularly under sections 40(1)(b) and (q) concerning acts of domestic violence.
3. **Vicarious Liability**: Whether the Minister of Police could be held vicariously liable for the actions of the police officers involved in Ms. Naidoo's arrest and detention.
4. **Psychological Harm and Secondary Victimization**: Assessing Ms. Naidoo's claims for psychological damage resulting from her experiences with the police after being subjected to domestic violence.

### R – Rules ([paras 20–40])

The court established the following legal principles and rules pertinent to the appellant's claim:

1. **Negligence Standard**: The *Kruger v Coetzee* test for negligence requires that a reasonable person foresee potential harm and take reasonable steps to prevent it. Police officers must abide by the directives in the *Domestic Violence Act* and its associated Regulations ([paras 22–23]).
  
2. **Legal Causation**: Following the *Carmichele v Minister of Safety and Security* precedent, the court confirmed that causation consists of factual causation, determined via the "but-for" test, as well as legal causation, involving considerations of policy.
  
3. **Lawful Arrest Criteria**: Section 40(1) of the *Criminal Procedure Act* permits police officers to arrest without a warrant under specific circumstances, particularly regarding acts classified as domestic violence ([para 38]).

4. **Applications of the Domestic Violence Act**: The Act mandates police to assist victims of domestic violence, outlining explicit duties and responsibilities, as detailed in its Regulations ([para 21]).

### A – Application ([paras 41–50])

The court analyzed whether the police officers' conduct constituted negligence through a thorough examination of the events leading up to Ms. Naidoo's arrest. There was a clear failure by Officer Molefe to uphold the provisions of the *Domestic Violence Act* and to ensure Ms. Naidoo's safety as a victim of domestic violence. His arbitrary decision to arrest her after only obtaining a counter-complaint lacked grounding in the law and constituted a breach of professional duty, underscoring negligence.

The claim to challenge the legality of her arrest was also applicable. The arrest under sections 40(1)(b) and (q) was deemed unlawful, as the circumstances surrounding the police response and consequent actions indicated a failure to exercise discretion appropriately. Notably, the subjective interpretation that an arrest should occur “as a matter of course” already undermined the rationality required for legal consistency.

Additionally, Ms. Naidoo's psychological suffering was recognized as a direct result of the secondary victimization she experienced at the hands of the police, exacerbating her initial trauma from domestic violence. The expert evidence from psychologist Mr. Louw, which was largely unchallenged by the respondents, established a clear link between her psychological condition and her negative experiences with the police.

### C – Conclusion ([paras 56–58])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Ms. Naidoo’s appeal, recognizing systemic failures within the SAPS to assist a victim of domestic violence adequately and legally. The court ordered the Minister of Police to pay damages for wrongful arrest, assault, and psychological harm, highlighting both the breaches of statutory duty under the *Domestic Violence Act* and the unlawful actions taken throughout the incident.

The decision serves as a crucial reminder of the obligations police officers have under South African law to protect victims of domestic violence and underscores the importance of adhering to established legal frameworks designed to prevent such victimization. Legal practitioners and law enforcement agencies are thus called upon to review and reaffirm their commitment to the rights and protections afforded under the Act, reinforcing procedural adherence to prevent future occurrences of neglect and harm. 

This judgment not only reaffirms the protective measures embodied in gender-based violence legislation but also elevates the discourse around accountability within law enforcement, strengthening the capacity for victims to seek effective recourse against state actors who fail in their duties.
