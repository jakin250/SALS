Supreme Court of Appeal

Manukha v Road Accident Fund (285/2016) [2017] ZASCA 21

The appellant claimed to have complied with the necessary lodging requirements under section 24 of the Act, including submitting the prescribed RAF1 claim form.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 21

FIRAC Method

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–4])

On 14 August 2008, Ntsieni Josephine Manukha (the appellant) was injured as a passenger in a vehicle that overturned due to alleged negligence by the driver of a bus. Subsequently, in July 2011, she instituted action against the Road Accident Fund (the respondent) for compensation under section 17(1) of the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 (the Act). Her claim included a sum of R200,000 for non-pecuniary damages as part of an overall claim of R700,000 for personal injuries which included a fracture and bruising.

The appellant claimed to have complied with the necessary lodging requirements under section 24 of the Act, including submitting the prescribed RAF1 claim form. However, her submission was incomplete; it did not include the serious injury assessment report required for her claim for non-pecuniary loss. The Road Accident Fund raised a special plea of prescription, asserting that the appellant's serious injury assessment report (the RAF4 form) was lodged well past the prescriptive period of five years, specifically on 14 August 2014—long after the expiry of the period for lodging under section 23(3) of the Act.

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 12–16])

The primary legal issue central to the appeal was whether the appellant’s claim for non-pecuniary loss had prescribed due to the late submission of the RAF4 form. The court needed to determine whether the prescriptive period was applicable to the overall claim, encompassing the non-pecuniary damages, or whether the claim for non-pecuniary loss was a distinct claim requiring its own submission within the prescribed time frame.

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 17–22])

The relevant provisions of the Road Accident Fund Act govern claims arising from motor vehicle accidents. Key provisions include:

  1. Section 17(1): Establishes the Fund’s obligation to compensate third parties for bodily injury caused by negligent driving and restricts compensation for non-pecuniary loss to serious injuries.
  2. Section 23(1): States that claims must be lodged within three years from the date of the cause of action but allows for a five-year prescription period for claims lodged in terms of sections 17(4)(a) or 24.
  3. Regulation 3(3)(b)(i): Allows for separate submission of the serious injury assessment report after lodging the initial claim, suggesting that the assessment does not constitute a separate claim against the Fund.

Past judgments, including Nonkwali v Road Accident Fund [2008] ZASCA 8, affirmed the interpretation that a claimant has a singular, indivisible cause of action and that components of the claim, including non-pecuniary loss, do not constitute distinct claims.

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 19–22])

In its findings, the court analyzed the provisions of the Road Accident Fund Act and concluded that the claims should not be viewed as separate entities. The appellant's submission of the RAF1 form within three years from the cause of action sufficed to initiate the claim. The court held that even though the RAF4 form for the serious injury assessment was submitted late, the prescription could not bar the claim since the original claim had been lodged in time and prescription would not apply to a component of that claim.

Specifically, the court emphasized that section 23(3) areas:

  • It prohibits the prescription of claims until five years after the cause of action arises, giving a broad scope that encompassed the entire claim.
  • The regulations permitting separate submissions signify that claims do not need to be lodged concurrently, preventing one component's delay from affecting the others.

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 23–24])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the special plea of prescription, reinstating the appellant's claim for non-pecuniary loss. The holding reinforced the understanding of claims under the Road Accident Fund Act as a singular claim with various components rather than distinct claims. The implications of this decision underscore the importance of the Act’s provisions allowing for flexibility in the submission of documentation supporting a claim, thereby preventing a strictly procedural disadvantage from barring substantial claims for damages.

The appeal was upheld with costs, and the order of the high court was set aside, leading to a more inclusive understanding of claims against the Road Accident Fund, particularly regarding the treatment of non-pecuniary loss. This case emphasizes that procedural delays in one aspect of a claim should not lead to the wholesale rejection of a claim where other components have been timely and adequately asserted.
