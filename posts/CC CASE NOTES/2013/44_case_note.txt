Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# Case Note on Minister of Communications v Phumla Ruth Patricia Ngewu and Others

**Citation:** CCT 117/11 [2013] ZACC 44

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts:

The case originates from a previous judgment by the Constitutional Court on 7 March 2013 that declared sections 10 to 10E of the Post Office Act 44 of 1958 invalid due to inconsistency with section 9(1) of the Constitution, which guarantees equality before the law. This declaration was suspended for eight months to allow Parliament to remedy the constitutional defect ([paras 1–2]). 

The defect arose because these provisions did not allow former spouses of members of the Post Office Retirement Fund, who had been awarded a portion of their former spouse’s pension interest as per section 7(8) of the Divorce Act 70 of 1979, to receive this payment upon divorce ([para 2]). 

As the eight-month period elapsed without any remedial action from Parliament, on 4 November 2013, the Minister of Communications filed an urgent application seeking to extend the suspension period by an additional six months. The only opposing party was Ms. Ngewu, who expressed dire financial straits due to the lack of access to her entitled pension benefits ([paras 4–5]). 

The Court had to ascertain whether the suspension period had indeed expired by the time of the hearing on 7 November 2013 ([para 5]).

### I – Issues:

The central legal issues identified by the Court included:

1. Whether the period of suspension for the declaration of invalidity had already expired as of 7 November 2013 ([paras 5–6]).
2. If the suspension had not expired, whether it would be just and equitable to extend this suspension given the circumstances surrounding the delay in legislative action by Parliament ([para 8]).

### R – Rules:

The applicable legal authorities included:

- **Constitution of South Africa, 1996** - especially section 9(1) concerning equality.
- **Post Office Act 44 of 1958** - the provisions that were found unconstitutional.
- **Divorce Act 70 of 1979** - particularly section 7(8), which allows for the assignment of pension interest upon divorce.
- Previous judgments, particularly *Zondi v MEC, Traditional and Local Government Affairs* ([2005] ZACC 18), which elaborated the conditions under which a court may extend the suspension period of its declaration of invalidity ([paras 5, 8]).
- The principle in *Mvumvu* ([2012] ZACC 20) regarding the conditions under which extensions may be granted ([para 8]).

### A – Application:

The Court first addressed the interpretation of the terms of the original order regarding the suspension. It concluded that while the order stated that the declaration was suspended for eight months, using civilian computation methods, the period expired at the end of 6 November 2013. Therefore, their authority to consider the extension remained valid when the application was heard ([paras 6–7]).

Upon assessing the factors outlined in *Mvumvu*, the Court found that the applicant failed to provide sufficient reasoning for the delay in legislative action and did not convincingly demonstrate how the extension would serve the public interest or prevent substantial prejudice ([para 9-11]). 

Ms. Ngewu's circumstances were particularly poignant; she faced severe financial hardship and relied on public assistance. The proposed extension lacked major administrative consequences—most notably, the distinction between the reading-in clause the court suggested and the new Bill proposed by the Minister was minor ([paras 12–13]).

Furthermore, the Court dismissed the argument about a separation of powers, emphasizing that the reading-in was a legitimate approach to remedying the constitutional defect. They noted that the prior government had been aware of the potential consequences should Parliament fail to act ([para 14]).

### C – Conclusion:

After weighing the pressing need for immediate rectification of the constitutional issue against the insufficient justification for extending the suspension, the Court held that it was neither just nor equitable to grant the requested extension. The application for extending the period of suspension was dismissed with costs on 7 November 2013 ([paras 1, 15–16]). 

The ruling showed a strong commitment to upholding constitutional norms pertaining to equality and highlighted the need for legislative bodies to act expediently in remedying constitutional defects identified by the judiciary. The decision also underscored the broader responsibility of government entities to avoid undue prejudice to individuals while navigating legislative processes.
