Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 180

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

[1] The appellant, Ms. Jennifer Emily Hutchinson Wild, is a seasoned advocate with 42 years of legal practice and a member of the Bisho Society of Advocates (BSA). She appeals against the Gauteng Division of the High Court of Pretoria’s decision, which dismissed her application to review an advisory note issued by the Legal Practice Council (LPC) regarding disciplinary proceedings against advocates. The LPC is a statutory body established under the Legal Practice Act 28 of 2014 (LPA) effective from November 1, 2018, and has jurisdiction over all legal practitioners ([para 2]).

[2] On September 26, 2017, the Eastern Cape Society of Advocates (ECSA) initiated an application for Ms. Wild's name to be struck off the roll of advocates due to alleged misconduct linked to past court judgments. In response, Ms. Wild sought a review of this process, which remained pending at the time of appeal. Following the enactment of the LPA, the LPC issued an advisory note on April 18, 2019, outlining transitional arrangements for disciplined members ([paras 3-5]).

[3] Ms. Wild subsequently sought to challenge the advisory note in the full court, arguing that it was issued without following the proper legal procedure as dictated by section 116(2) of the LPA, which pertains to ongoing striking-off applications. The full court dismissed her application, holding that the advisory note did not adversely affect her rights, and thus, lacked reviewable status ([paras 6-10]).

I – Issues

[1] This case raises significant legal questions regarding the following:

  • Whether the LPC's decision to issue the advisory note constitutes "administrative action" as defined under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) and is thus subject to review ([para 12]).
  • The interpretation of section 116(2) of the LPA, specifically whether it mandates the LPC to assume responsibility for ongoing striking-off applications initiated before the LPA took effect ([para 12]).

R – Rules

[1] The applicable legal framework includes the PAJA, which defines administrative action as decisions taken by an organ of state that adversely affect individuals' rights and have direct external legal effects ([para 14]). The LPA provides detailed guidelines for the regulation and governance of legal practitioners and their conduct, particularly regarding disciplinary proceedings ([paras 22-23]).

[2] Section 116(2) of the LPA stipulates that ongoing proceedings related to the suspension or removal of advocates previously instituted under repealed laws are to be continued as though those laws remain in effect ([para 23]). Additionally, the common law offers courts inherent jurisdiction over disciplinary matters involving advocates ([para 29]).

A – Application

[1] Upon examining whether the LPC’s advisory note constitutes administrative action under PAJA, the court determined that the advisory note, which reaffirmed existing procedures regarding disciplinary matters, did not introduce new rules or alter existing legal statuses. Instead, it simply aimed to clarify transitional arrangements related to the implementation of the LPA ([paras 16-17]). Thus, the court concluded that since the advisory note did not adversely impact Ms. Wild's rights, it could not constitute reviewable administrative action.

[2] Regarding the interpretation of section 116(2), the court noted that both the LPC and the Advocates’ Societies interpreted the provision differently concerning rights to continue disciplinary proceedings without LPC’s accreditation ([para 18]). The court emphasized that section 44 of the LPA preserves the power of the High Court to adjudicate matters concerning advocates and their professional conduct, asserting that Advocates’ Societies maintain standing to bring applications for the striking off of advocates ([para 29]).

[3] The court found that the advisory note's implications were secondary to the legislative framework established by the LPA and did not preclude Advocates’ Societies from participating in disciplinary processes related to their members. Therefore, the court confirmed that the common law principles allowing such standing existed prior to the enactment of the LPA and were preserved therein ([para 29]).

C – Conclusion

[1] The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) dismissed Ms. Wild's appeal, affirming that the advisory note issued by the LPC was not reviewable under PAJA, as it did not constitute administrative action due to lack of direct adverse effects on her rights. Furthermore, the court held that section 116(2) did not strip Advocates’ Societies of their common law standing to engage in disciplinary applications against advocates, thereby allowing the ECSA to continue its application against Ms. Wild ([paras 30-31]).

[2] This judgment reinforces the dual regulation of the legal profession by both the LPC and Advocates’ Societies, confirming the co-custodianship of the profession and the enduring common law principles that govern disciplinary actions against advocates. The court’s interpretations clarify the application of the LPA and signal the importance of upholding advocates' rights in disciplinary proceedings while maintaining compliance with the established legal framework.
