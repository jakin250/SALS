Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title:** Masemola v Special Pensions Appeal Board and Another [2019] ZACC 39  
**Neutral Citation:** CCT 260/18  
**Coram:** Mogoeng CJ, Cameron J, Froneman J, Jafta J, Khampepe J, Madlanga J, Mhlantla J, Theron J, Victor AJ  
**Judgment:** Mhlantla J (unanimous)  
**Heard on:** 6 August 2019  
**Decided on:** 15 October 2019  

---

## F - Facts

The applicant, Nathaniel Mashilo Masemola, a 92-year-old political activist and member of the African National Congress (ANC) since 1946, sought the restoration of his special pension. This pension had initially been awarded to him in December 1997 due to his contributions to the liberation struggle, as outlined in the Special Pensions Act 69 of 1996 ([paras 1–4]).

Masemola was convicted of fraud in 2001, leading to his disqualification from receiving the pension as per section 1(8)(b) of the Act ([paras 5–6]). In July 2011, he received a presidential pardon which expunged his conviction ([para 6]). Subsequently, Masemola requested the reinstatement of his special pension; however, the Government Pensions Administration Agency (GPAA) refused his request, arguing that the disqualification was not retroactively lifted by the pardon ([paras 7–8]). Following various procedural attempts to appeal and seek review in the courts, Masemola's efforts culminated in an action before the High Court, which ordered the reinstatement of his special pension effective from the date of the presidential pardon. The respondents appealed this decision to the Supreme Court of Appeal, which ruled against Masemola ([paras 12–15]).

## I - Issues

The primary legal questions before the Constitutional Court were:

1. Should leave to appeal be granted? ([par 17])
2. What is the effect of the presidential pardon on Masemola’s pension eligibility? ([par 17])
3. Is Masemola entitled to the restoration of his special pension benefits following the pardon? ([par 17])
4. Does the restoration require a fresh application or does it occur automatically upon the pardon? ([par 17])
5. What is the impact of section 6A of the Special Pensions Act on Masemola’s pension eligibility? ([par 17])
6. Do the Board and GPAA possess the authority to reinstate the special pension? ([par 17])

## R - Rules

The pertinent statutory provisions and judicial interpretations relied upon include:

1. **Special Pensions Act 69 of 1996**, particularly sections 1(1), 1(8)(b), and 6A, surrounding the qualifications and disqualifications for special pensions ([paras 4, 25]).
2. **Section 84(2)(j) of the Constitution**, which grants the President authority to issue pardons ([para 29]).
3. Case law, including **McBride** and **Du Toit v Minister for Safety and Security**, that discuss the implications of pardons and the nature of legal rights and disabilities following such pardons ([paras 28, 30, 34]).

## A - Application

The Court analyzed the implications of a presidential pardon on Masemola's previously suspended rights due to his conviction. It concluded that while the disqualification enacted by his 2001 conviction was valid at the time, the subsequent pardon—legally treating him as if he had never been convicted—revived his entitlement to the special pension. The Court addressed the respondents' argument regarding the lapsed provisions in section 6A, determining that this provision does not impede Masemola's entitlement because he had already been awarded the pension before the disqualification arose ([paras 41–46]). 

The Court held that the entitlement to the pension continued to exist post-pardon and that there existed no legitimate administrative barrier preventing the restoration of benefits, thus rejecting the notion that a fresh application was required ([paras 49–52]). 

## C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal and upheld Masemola’s appeal, restoring his special pension effective from July 21, 2011, and ordered that the GPAA pay this entitlement retroactively within 14 days. The Court emphasized the principle of legality whereby the revival of Masemola's pension entitlement was due to the operation of law following the presidential pardon ([paras 1–3, 53]). The decision not only reaffirmed the importance of recognizing the contributions of liberation struggle veterans but also clarified the legal effect of presidential pardons in restoring rights that had been temporarily suspended due to criminal convictions.
