FIRAC Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA (Case No: 524/2004)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2005

FIRAC Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA (Case No: 524/2004)

F - Facts

The case revolves around an appeal against a judgment by Chetty J in the Eastern Cape Division, concerned with the eviction of Lungiswa Snowy Kambule (the respondent) from a property owned by Burton Queenstown CC (the second appellant) and a claim related to an alleged customary marriage ([para 1]). The property in question was purchased by the second appellant, with the deceased, Burton Baltimore Zitha Baduza, being its sole member. The deceased passed away in June 2002, while Kambule had been residing in the property since September 2001 without any formal lease agreement or payment of rent.

Upon the deceased's death, the first appellant, as executor of the estate, sought to evict her based on her alleged unlawful occupation ([para 3]). Kambule claimed her right to occupy the property was derived from a customary marriage with the deceased, which the appellants contested, asserting any such claim was void ab initio due to the marriage's alleged non-eligibility under the Transkei Marriage Act. The appeal is made with leave from this court ([para 1]).

I - Issues

The main legal questions before the court are:

  1. Whether the court has the discretion under the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE) to ascertain a customary law wife's rights to occupy a property against her deceased husband’s estate ([para 1]).
  2. Whether a customary marriage confers a right to occupy property that is subject to a mortgage bond ([para 1]).

R - Rules

The relevant legal frameworks and authorities provide the following guidance:

  • The PIE Act governs eviction procedures and defines unlawful occupation. Key provisions include sections 1 (definition of an unlawful occupier), 4 (procedural and substantive obligations for eviction), and section 26(3) of the Constitution, which protects against unlawful eviction ([paras 10-11]).
  • The necessity of proving the validity of the customary marriage is based on the Transkei Marriage Act, with specific reference to the registration requirements as articulated in sections 33 and 37 of the Act ([paras 29, 36]).
  • Precedents like Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd ([1984] 3 SA 623 (A)) and Brisley v Drotsky ([2002] 4 SA 1 (SCA)) establish the need for just and equitable determinations in eviction actions ([para 11]).

A - Application

In the court’s analysis, it was held that the respondent did not hold any legal basis for her claim to occupation. While the court recognised that customary law may impose obligations on a husband to provide for his wife, it noted that customary law does not confer real rights in property ownership to wives or widows. Therefore, even assuming her marriage was valid, she only possessed a personal servitude of usage or habitation, which did not amount to a right to occupy property in the face of a contradictory mortgage arrangement ([para 13]).

The judge focused on the adequacy of the appellants' compliance with procedural requirements of PIE, observing that they had met necessary demands under sections 4(2) to (5), and highlighted that the issue at hand concerned whether eviction would be just and equitable ([para 9]). The court found that the respondent’s occupation lacked legal basis and that her claims did not sufficiently meet the threshold of need for protection under PIE, concluding she was an unlawful occupier ([para 15]).

On the declaratory order regarding marriage validity, the court reaffirmed the necessity for registration under the Transkei Marriage Act to ascertain the legitimacy of the customary marriage ([paras 25-29]). Since the judge acknowledged conflicting decisions on this point in previous Transkei cases, he asserted that registration was essential, and by failing to complete this, the marriage was deemed invalid ([para 36]).

C - Conclusion

Ultimately, the court ruled in favour of the appellants, holding that the respondent's claims regarding her right to occupy the property were invalid. The appeal against the eviction order was upheld, while the request for a declaratory order regarding the marriage validity was dismissed due to it being superfluous and unmerited in the context of eviction proceedings ([para 23.1]). The court ordered the respondent to vacate the premises within 12 months, while granting her the opportunity to pursue a maintenance claim contingent on proving actual marriage validity in a proper forum ([para 22]).

This case illustrates the complex interplay between customary law and statutory frameworks within South Africa, particularly emphasizing the need for adherence to legal formalities regarding marriage under the Transkei Marriage Act, alongside the protective measures afforded to unlawful occupiers by PIE.
