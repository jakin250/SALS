Supreme Court of Appeal

Mbhele v MEC for Health for the Gauteng Province (355/15)

The case centers on a claim brought by Delisile Mbhele against the Member of the Executive Council (MEC) for Health in the Gauteng Province.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016

F - Facts

The case centers on a claim brought by Delisile Mbhele against the Member of the Executive Council (MEC) for Health in the Gauteng Province. The action arises from events occurring on 18 August 2006, when Mbhele was admitted to Chris Hani Baragwanath Hospital (CHB) due to foetal distress ([para 1]). The appellant alleged that due to the negligence of the medical staff at CHB, her baby was stillborn. Specifically, she contended that there was a significant delay in her treatment, inadequate monitoring of her pregnancy, and a failure to act on alarming CTG scan results indicating foetal distress ([para 3]).

The claim initiated by Mbhele was dismissively dealt with by Tsoka J in the Gauteng Local Division, which concluded that her particulars did not disclose a cause of action for both her claim for emotional shock and for a constitutional damage claim based on the right to rear a child. The court's dismissal included a finding that her counsel had abandoned the emotional shock claim ([para 1]). Only Mbhele continued in the proceedings after her co-plaintiff, Themba Buthelezi, passed away ([para 2]).

The court's subsequent ruling on the emotional distress claims acknowledged a significant series of traumatic events suffered by Mbhele, including being placed in a ward with mothers who had newborns while side-by-side with her own empty cot ([para 4.3]) and the distress of identifying her deceased child ([para 4.4]). The appellant claimed that she experienced lasting emotional upset, depression, and anxiety following the tragedy ([para 4.6]).

I - Issues

The legal issues at the heart of this appeal can be distilled as follows:

  1. Whether a claim for damages for emotional shock was sufficiently established in the court of first instance ([para 1]).
  2. Whether South African law recognizes a claim for constitutional damages based on the right to rear a child, even when that child was stillborn ([para 1]).
  3. Whether the High Court erred in stating that the claim for emotional shock had been abandoned, and if so, whether Mbhele’s emotional distress was adequately proven to warrant damages ([para 10]).

R - Rules

The court relied on established principles of South African law regarding delictual claims. The legal standard for establishing negligence requires proof of a duty of care, a breach of that duty, causation, and resultant damages. The court referenced precedent cases such as Pinchin v Santam Insurance Co (Pty) Ltd regarding pre-natal injuries, emphasizing that while a claim exists for children later born alive, it is not applicable for those born still ([para 8]).

Concerning the emotional shock claim, the court evaluated it under the rubric of established precedent that recognizes emotional distress as a compensable harm, supported by earlier judgments including Majiet v Santam Limited and Allie v Road Accident Fund for quantum determination ([para 14–19]). Additionally, the court addressed procedural aspects under Uniform Rule 33 pertaining to a "stated case" method where clarity and specificity in laid facts are requisite for judicial consideration ([para 21]).

A - Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the issues about negligence had been conceded by the MEC, thus establishing the defendant's liability in the manner the appellant was treated at CHB ([para 4]). The court highlighted that while the emotional distress suffered by Mbhele was evident, the High Court erred in concluding that the claim was abandoned after examination of court transcripts revealed consistent references to emotional shock as central to the claim ([para 10]).

Moreover, the argument surrounding the right to rear a child was dismissed, affirming that no legal basis existed under South African law for a parent to claim damages for a child not born alive, and thus, that aspect of the claim was rightly rejected by the court of first instance. The court emphasized the procedural missteps regarding how the claims were articulated in prior proceedings and affirmed the importance of thorough factual presentation ([para 20–21]).

In terms of quantifying the damages for emotional shock, the court initially reviewed previous awards in comparable cases while allowing for inflationary adjustments, concluding finally that a sum of R100,000 was appropriate based on the specific psychological sequelae detailed by the appellant following her loss ([para 12–19]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruling overturned the High Court’s decision, acknowledging the appellant's suffering and awarding R100,000 for her claims of emotional shock. The court also highlighted the necessity of clear presentation in stated cases and criticized inadequate communication from the respondent’s counsel regarding procedural compliance ([para 22]).

Thus, this case is a significant affirmation within South African law regarding the recognition of emotional distress claims arising from negligence and sets a precedent defining the boundaries concerning claims for constitutional damages associated with the loss of a child prior to birth. Further implications of this ruling guide future litigants in how emotional distress claims substantiated through adequate factual narratives can achieve judicial recognition and relief ([para 23]).
