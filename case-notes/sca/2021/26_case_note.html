Solidarity and Another v Black First Land First and Others [2021] ZASCA 26

On 1 February 2019, a tragic incident occurred when a walkway bridge collapsed at Hoërskool Driehoek in Vanderbijlpark, resulting in the deaths of four learners, aged between 13 and 17, and injuring twenty others, all of whom were white ([para 1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 26

FIRAC Analysis

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–10])

On 1 February 2019, a tragic incident occurred when a walkway bridge collapsed at Hoërskool Driehoek in Vanderbijlpark, resulting in the deaths of four learners, aged between 13 and 17, and injuring twenty others, all of whom were white ([para 1]). Following the incident, Siyanda Gumede posted a controversial remark on Facebook, expressing a lack of sympathy for the victims due to their race ([para 2]). This post elicited supportive comments from Lindsay Maasdorp, spokesperson for the Black First Land First (BLF) political party, and Zwelakhe Dubasi, the party's deputy secretary, reinforcing incendiary sentiments regarding the deaths ([para 2]).

In response to the comments, Solidarity, a trade union primarily representing white members, initiated proceedings under the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000 (the Equality Act). Their application claimed that the statements constituted hate speech as defined under Sections 7, 10, and 11 of the Act, thereby violating human dignity and inciting discrimination against white individuals. They sought not only a declaration of hate speech but also damages for the bereaved families ([paras 4–5]).

After the initial hearing and while awaiting judgment, the presiding judge, Mokgoathleng J, concluded that in light of the Supreme Court of Appeal's decision in Qwelane v South African Human Rights Commission, the proceedings were declared a nullity, leading to widespread confusion and controversy regarding the decision's validity ([paras 6–8]). The court did not reach a substantive resolution on the case, instead opting to categorize the entire proceedings as void ([paras 11–13]), effectively leaving the dispute unresolved and requiring appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal.

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 4–9])

The primary legal issue arising from this case is whether the Equality Court's declaration of the proceedings as a nullity was competent, considering that it did not achieve the finality expected from judicial proceedings. The secondary issue involves the implications of the judgment in Qwelane on the application of Section 10 of the Equality Act, particularly regarding the balance between prohibiting hate speech and the constitutional right to freedom of speech as enshrined in Section 16 of the Constitution.

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 6–9])

The court relied heavily on the provisions of the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000, particularly Sections 7, 10, and 11, which provide a framework for addressing issues of hate speech and discrimination. The court also referenced the significant precedent set in Qwelane v South African Human Rights Commission, where the constitutionality of Section 10 was called into question. In that case, it was determined that the provision overly restricted freedom of expression, creating a context in which the Equality Court's role could be seen as problematic when dealing with hate speech claims ([para 9]).

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 10–14])

In its analysis, the Supreme Court emphasized the essential duty of a court to render decisions that resolve disputes definitively ([para 10]). The court criticized the Equality Court for having failed to fulfill this obligation. Instead of adjudicating the merits of the case, the lower court's declaration of nullity sidestepped the need to reach a conclusion on the substantive issues regarding the alleged hate speech.

The court articulated that the lower court, when faced with the implications of the Qwelane judgment, could have considered the parties' arguments and issued a ruling addressing the core issues raised in Solidarity's application ([para 12]). The Supreme Court underscored that while a court might lack jurisdiction or might face certain limitations, it must still provide a definitive ruling. By leaving the parties without any binding decision, the lower court neglected its basic duty to resolve the dispute ([paras 11–13]).

Furthermore, the Supreme Court indicated that despite the equality court’s misstep, the proceedings had advanced enough to merit remittance for resolution, whether by the initial presiding judge or another judge as directed by the Judge President ([para 14]). This remittance approach was deemed necessary to afford the parties appropriate legal recourse without undue delay.

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 15–17])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Solidarity’s appeal on the basis that the previous order declaring the proceedings a nullity could not stand as it failed to issue any substantive ruling on the merits. Consequently, the court set aside the Equality Court's decision and remitted the matter back to the same court for final resolution ([para 17]).

The case illustrates a critical balancing act between issues of hate speech, freedom of expression, and the courts' obligations to provide definitive rulings. The decision reinforces the principle that courts must actively engage in resolving disputes, especially within the context of sensitive societal issues such as race and discrimination. The implications of the ruling may set important precedents in future cases involving the interpretation of hate speech within the framework of the Equality Act, particularly in light of evolving constitutional interpretations relating to freedom of speech.

This comprehensive FIRAC analysis highlights the critical legal issues, statutory provisions, and judicial reasoning that ultimately drove the Supreme Court's decision regarding a complex case touching on themes of race, dignity, and legal accountability in South Africa.
