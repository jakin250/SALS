Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1998

# FIRAC Case Note: *Mistry v Interim National Medical and Dental Council of South Africa*

**CCT 13/97**  
**Date Heard:** 24 February 1998  
**Date Decided:** 29 May 1998  
**Judges:** Chaskalson P, Langa DP, Ackermann J, Goldstone J, Kriegler J, Madala J, Mokgoro J, O'Regan J, Sachs J

---

### F – Facts

The case arose from a complaint lodged against Ashok Rama Mistry, a registered medical practitioner, by a patient alleging fraudulent reimbursement claims for services not rendered. Following this complaint, the Interim National Medical and Dental Council of South Africa (the Council) initiated an inspection of the applicant’s surgery. This request was carried out by a team of inspectors, including J.P. Enslin, the second respondent, and D. Moodliar, the fifth respondent. They entered Mistry's surgery alongside medicine inspector Russell Coote, the fourth respondent, and conducted a search without Mistry's presence, seizing various items (including medications) based on section 28(1) of the Medicines and Related Substances Control Act 101 of 1965 (the Medicines Act).

Mistry sought a rule nisi from the Durban and Coast High Court, demanding the return of his possessions seized during this inspection and questioning the constitutionality of certain provisions of the Medicines Act and the Medical, Dental and Supplementary Health Service Professions Act 56 of 1974. The High Court partially upheld Mistry's application but refused to return certain items, leading to the referral of constitutional issues to the Constitutional Court.

The central legal concerns included whether these statutory provisions violated Mistry's right to privacy as enshrined in section 13 of the interim Constitution and whether the inspections conducted were lawful. The case combined a constitutional referral regarding the validity of the Medicines Act and an appeal against the High Court's decision denying the return of specific items ([paras 8-19]).

---

### I – Issues

The Constitutional Court identified the following key legal questions:

1. **Constitutionality of Section 28(1):** Does the authority granted by section 28(1) of the Medicines Act, which allows inspectors to enter private premises and seize items without a warrant, infringe the right to privacy guaranteed by section 13 of the interim Constitution ([para 8])?
   
2. **Nature and Justification of the Search:** Did the search and seizure actions of the inspectors constitute a violation of Mistry's constitutional privacy rights? Specifically, did the actions initiated by information disclosed in breach of statutory confidentiality under the Medical Act render the search unconstitutional ([paras 45-50])?

3. **Return of Seized Items:** Should Mistry be entitled to the return of the items seized during the inspection, particularly in light of any constitutional violations found ([paras 57-59])?

---

### R – Rules

The court’s examination revolved around several constitutional and statutory provisions:

1. **Section 13 of the Interim Constitution:** Guarantees the right to personal privacy, explicitly encompassing protections against searches and seizures of individuals’ property ([para 22]).

2. **Section 28(1) of the Medicines Act:** Grants inspectors broad powers of entry, inspection, and seizure without specific requirements for a warrant or prior judicial authorization ([para 16]).

3. **Section 33(1) of the Interim Constitution:** Allows for the limitation of rights under the Constitution, provided such limitations are reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society ([para 23]).

4. **Case Law Precedents:** The judgment also relied on principles from previous cases addressing privacy and searches, particularly *S v Makwanyane* regarding proportionality and the importance of balancing individual rights against public interest ([para 24]).

---

### A – Application

**Constitutionality of Section 28(1):**  
The court found that the provisions of section 28(1) of the Medicines Act were unconstitutional. The breadth of power granted to inspectors without any restriction was deemed excessive and incompatible with the right to privacy guaranteed by section 13. The lack of a warrant requirement was viewed as disproportionate to the legitimate public interest in regulating medicines ([para 30]).

**Nature of the Search:**  
The Constitutional Court examined the actions of the inspectors, concluding that the search initiated was not merely a routine inspection but rather one prompted by the disclosure of confidential information, further complicating its lawfulness. Mistry's privacy rights were implicated because the search was executed based on information shared in violation of statutory duties to maintain confidentiality ([paras 45-52]).

**Return of Seized Items:**  
Although the court acknowledged the public health objectives of the Medicines Act, it emphasized that these objectives must not come at the expense of constitutional rights. The failure to provide appropriate safeguards within the statutory framework meant that the search could not be justified. Thus, the court ruled that Mistry was entitled to the return of the items seized ([paras 58-59]).

---

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court held that section 28(1) of the Medicines and Related Substances Control Act 101 of 1965 is inconsistent with section 13 of the interim Constitution and thus declared it invalid. The provisions allowed unconstitutional levels of intrusion into personal privacy, demonstrating that legislative intent cannot overshadow constitutional rights. The appeal by Mistry for the return of seized items was dismissed, as the court ruled that while the inspections were unconstitutional, the material implications of those inspections did not warrant retroactive invalidation of all actions taken under the defective provisions ([paras 54-59]).

This case has implications for future legislative efforts in South Africa regarding the balance between regulatory power and individual rights, as well as the need for robust legal frameworks that respect constitutional protections while enabling effective public safety measures.
