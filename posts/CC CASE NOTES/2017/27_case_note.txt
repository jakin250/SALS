Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

### FIRAC Case Note: *Barlow v The State* [2017] ZACC 27

**F – Facts:**  
The applicant, Mr. Deshwin Barlow, was charged with several offenses, including murder, theft, attempted murder, and unlawful possession of firearms, stemming from an incident on 24 October 2009, during which Mr. Frederick Flagg was shot and killed. The circumstances surrounding the shooting were disputed; witnesses testified that the applicant shot Mr. Flagg during an altercation, while the applicant claimed he acted in self-defense after a struggle for a firearm occurred ([1]–[2]). Initially, Barlow faced eight charges but was acquitted of three at the close of the State's case ([21]). Ultimately, he was convicted of five counts ([25]). The trial judge's judgment contained inaccuracies regarding the specific charges, which raised concerns about the fairness of Barlow's trial and the clarity of his convictions. The High Court's Full Court then upheld the trial court's convictions, omitting any mention of the theft conviction, which led Barlow to apply for leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court ([11], [35]).

**I – Issues:**  
The case raised critical questions pertaining to Barlow's constitutional rights under Section 35 of the South African Constitution, specifically his right to a fair trial. The major issues included:
1. Whether Barlow's conviction suffered from procedural unfairness due to the inaccuracies in the trial court's judgment and appeal court's handling of his case.
2. The adequacy of the Full Court's consideration of Barlow's appeal against the theft conviction and the implications of failing to address this in their judgment ([19], [35]–[36]).
3. Whether the right to appeal under Section 35(3)(o) was violated by the Full Court's omission to consider all counts for which Barlow was convicted ([14], [36]).

**R – Rules:**  
The primary legal frameworks involved the South African Constitution, particularly Section 35, which guarantees the right to a fair trial and the right of appeal. Important case law, including *S v Boesak* [2000] ZACC 25, underlined the necessity of procedural fairness and detailed judgments to uphold the integrity of legal proceedings. Additionally, Section 146 of the Criminal Procedure Act, which mandates judges to provide reasons for their decisions, was relevant ([11], [35], [14]).

**A – Application:**  
Froneman J, writing for the majority, found that while there were inaccuracies in the judgments of both the trial court and the Full Court, these did not constitute a violation of Barlow's right to a fair trial. The majority opinion noted that the trial judge eventually addressed all charges against the applicant adequately and provided reasons for his conclusions notwithstanding initial misstatements ([1], [5]). The court emphasized that the right to a fair trial includes proper representation and the ability to navigate procedural complexities but ultimately found no new grounds for appeal. Zondo J’s dissent highlighted that the Full Court's failure to address the theft conviction constituted a significant breach of Barlow's right to appeal and undermined the integrity of the judicial process ([19], [40]–[46]).

**C – Conclusion:**  
The majority ruled that Barlow's application for leave to appeal was dismissed, concluding there were no prospects of success on appeal and it was not in the interests of justice to grant it ([18], [16]). Zondo J argued for remitting the case to the Full Court to address the unresolved appeal against the theft conviction, claiming the applicant’s rights were significantly compromised by the court's omissions ([46]). The case underscores the necessity for judicial diligence and clear reasoning in criminal trials, emphasizing that procedural oversights can impact the fundamental rights of the accused.
