Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: National Union of Public Service and Allied Workers obo Mani and Others v National Lotteries Board ([2013] ZASCA 63)

### F – Facts
This appeal arises from the dismissal of ten employees from the National Lotteries Board (NLB) on 25 August 2008, primarily for insubordination during a dispute surrounding the leadership of the NLB's CEO, Professor Vevek Ram ([1]). The employees, represented by the National Union of Public Service and Allied Workers Union (NUPSAWU), alleged that their dismissals were automatically unfair as they participated in union activities, including a petition demanding the removal of the CEO. Following unsuccessful conciliation efforts at the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation, and Arbitration (CCMA), the matter was escalated to the Labour Court, which upheld the dismissals as being both substantively and procedurally fair ([2], [5]). The petition involved complaints about Professor Ram's leadership style and the failure of governance policies, which the employees publicly asserted through letters to NLB management and the media ([11]).

The disciplinary proceedings, presided over by Professor André Van Niekerk, concluded with the finding of insubordination by the employees for supporting the petition against the CEO ([17]). Dismissals followed after some employees were given a chance to retract their support, which they did not pursue, leading to the appeal against the Labour Court's ruling ([20]).

### I – Issues
The central legal questions before the Court were:

1. Whether the dismissals of the ten employees constituted automatically unfair dismissals under section 187(1)(d) of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA), due to their involvement in lawful union activities ([2], [29]).
2. Alternatively, whether the dismissals were otherwise unfair, either on the grounds of severity of the punishment or inconsistency in disciplinary measures applied to other employees ([28], [33]).

### R – Rules
The relevant rules guiding the Court's decision included:

1. **Section 187(1)(d) of the LRA** - This section specifies that dismissals are automatically unfair if they are based on an employee's participation in lawful activities, such as exercising rights under the Act or participating in union proceedings ([2]).
  
2. **Definition of Insubordination** - Cited from John Grogan, insubordination occurs when an employee refuses to accept the authority of their employer ([17]). 

3. **Procedural Fairness** - The Labour Relations Act emphasizes that disciplinary actions ought to be proportionate, especially for less serious offenses, as suggested in the LRA Code of Good Practice ([33]).

4. **Case Law Precedents** - Reference to cases such as *Kroukam v SA Airlink (Pty) Ltd* and *SACWU v Afrox Ltd* was made, focusing on protecting trade union activities in line with constitutional rights ([25]).

### A – Application
The Court analyzed the context of the employees' statements and actions, asserting that while the petition was an exercise of their rights, its content expressed a definitive refusal to work with their CEO, which is legally categorized as insubordination rather than protected activity under the LRA ([30]). 

The judges emphasized that the issue was not merely their engagement in union activities but the specific language of insubordination employed in their correspondence. It was determined that the petition's threats to disregard the authority of the CEO constituted a legitimate cause for dismissal, emphasizing that employers are not required to tolerate insubordination to maintain workplace order ([29], [31]).

The Labour Court found that the dismissals were not disproportionate but a necessary response to maintain decorum and uphold the chain of command, based on the gravity of the misconduct illustrated by the petition ([26], [34]). Furthermore, the opportunity to retract their actions was noted as evidence of procedural fairness in the disciplinary process ([21]).

### C – Conclusion 
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the Labour Court's ruling, concluding that the dismissals were not automatically unfair under the LRA as the employees' actions constituted serious misconduct. Their continued assertion of innocence during the disciplinary procedures demonstrated an irretrievable breakdown of trust, justifying the decision to dismiss ([32], [38]). The Court dismissed the appeal with costs, reinforcing the principle that insubordination, even while couched in purported lawful activity, does not enjoy protection under the LRA ([38]).

This case underscores the delicate balance between the rights of employees to engage in union activities and the obligation to adhere to workplace authority, providing critical insights into the enforcement of disciplinary standards in labor relations.
