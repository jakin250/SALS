Premier of the Western Cape Province v The Public Protector & Another (771/2020) [2022] ZASCA 16

Zille admitted to making the tweets but argued that they were protected under the right to freedom of expression as enshrined in Section 16 of the Constitution ([9], [12]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 16

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The case stems from a series of tweets made by Helen Zille, the Premier of the Western Cape, on 15 March 2017, wherein she commented on the legacy of colonialism in South Africa ([1]). Following a complaint lodged by Mr. Magaxa, a member of the African National Congress, the Public Protector initiated an investigation, which led to a report asserting that Zille breached the Executive Ethics Code by her comments, notably under clauses that require members to act in a manner consistent with the integrity of their office ([3]-[8]). The Public Protector also recommended remedial action, which involved holding Zille accountable ([7]-[11]).

Zille admitted to making the tweets but argued that they were protected under the right to freedom of expression as enshrined in Section 16 of the Constitution ([9], [12]). After a series of procedural steps, including an apology from Zille acknowledging the offense caused by her tweets ([6]), the Gauteng High Court ruled against her, stating that even though she was no longer in office, the matter was moot, and it declined to review the Public Protector's findings ([13]-[15]). Zille appealed this decision, which culminated in the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) hearing the case on 22 November 2021.

I – Issues

The principal issues presented before the SCA were:

  1. Did Zille’s tweets violate the Executive Ethics Code as contended by the Public Protector? ([1]-[11])
  2. Did the Public Protector incorrectly interpret and apply Section 16 of the Constitution, specifically concerning the limitations on freedom of expression? ([16]-[20])

R – Rules

The relevant legal rules and authorities discussed include:

  • Section 10 of the Constitution: This section accords everyone the right to dignity and to have it respected and protected ([17]).
  • Section 16(1) of the Constitution: It enshrines the right to freedom of expression, while Section 16(2) delineates categories of expression that do not receive constitutional protection, such as incitement to violence and advocacy of hatred ([18],[19]).
  • The interpretations from significant cases such as S v Makwanyane and Islamic Unity v Broadcasting Complaints Commission, which emphasize the nuanced balance between free speech and dignity ([17]-[21]).

The SCA also considered the ethic codes stipulated in the Executive Members’ Ethics Act 82 of 1998, particularly concerning the integrity requirements imposed on public officials ([8]).

A – Application

In applying these legal principles, the SCA scrutinized the findings of the Public Protector and assessed whether Zille’s tweets could reasonably be interpreted as breaches of the Ethics Code. The Public Protector’s finding that Zille’s tweets were offensive and insensitive was critically evaluated. The Court noted that the Public Protector misunderstood the protections offered by Section 16 of the Constitution; while it recognized freedom of expression, it must balance this against the harm caused by speech that might provoke racial divisions ([25]-[40]).

The SCA concluded that the Public Protector had made several errors in her analysis which included:

  • Failing to apply an objective standard to determine the meaning of Zille's statements ([30]-[32]).
  • Not adequately considering whether Zille’s tweets constituted incitement to imminent violence, a critical requirement under Section 16(2)(b) ([33]-[34]).
  • Misinterpreting the scope of Section 16, which ultimately led her to the incorrect conclusion that Zille’s tweets were not protected by freedom of expression ([32]-[39]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA found that the findings and remedial actions posited by the Public Protector were fundamentally flawed and devoid of a rational connection to the evidence presented. Thus, the appeal was upheld; the Court set aside the Public Protector's findings and remedial actions, effectively restoring Zille's right to freedom of expression as protected under Section 16 of the Constitution. The implications of this ruling underscore the importance of safeguarding free speech within the bounds of responsible governance, particularly for public figures whose statements carry significant weight in societal discourse ([42]-[43]).

In conclusion, the SCA’s judgment serves as a pivotal affirmation of the principles of free expression and the necessity for due diligence in the interpretation of constitutional rights, especially where political discourse is concerned. The decision reflects the Court’s commitment to ensuring that freedom of expression is not unduly curtailed by subjective interpretations of offensiveness or insensitivity, reinforcing democratic values in the South African context.
