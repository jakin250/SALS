Stopforth Swanepoel & Brewis Incorporated v Royal Anthem Investments 129 (Pty) Ltd and Others [2014] ZACC 26

The case arises from a dispute involving a sale agreement of immovable property between Royal Anthem Investments 129 (Pty) Ltd ("Royal") and Yeun Fan Lau and Shun Cheng Liang (the "respondents").

Constitutional Court 2014 [2014] ZACC 26

F – Facts [1–14]

The case arises from a dispute involving a sale agreement of immovable property between Royal Anthem Investments 129 (Pty) Ltd ("Royal") and Yeun Fan Lau and Shun Cheng Liang (the "respondents"). The agreement stipulated that the respondents were to pay the sum of R3.6 million, including a R720,000 deposit and another amount for transfer duty of R264,723, which was to be held in trust by the applicant, Stopforth Swanepoel & Brewis Incorporated ("the attorneys"), acting as conveyancers. The attorneys were to invest these funds in an interest-bearing account as mandated by Section 78(2A) of the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979.

Following the respondents' failure to fulfill certain conditions of the agreement, the sale fell through. Consequently, disputes arose regarding the refunds owed to the respondents. Although the High Court ordered Royal to repay the funds plus interest, Royal appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), where the attorneys, though initially not parties to the proceedings, were ordered to pay the respondents the amounts owed along with accrued interest.

The attorneys subsequently sought leave to appeal this decision, arguing that they were not a party to the SCA proceedings and had been denied their right to a fair hearing under Section 34 of the Constitution of South Africa.

I – Issues [17–27]

Key issues for determination included:

  1. Whether the Supreme Court of Appeal's decision infringed the attorneys' right to a fair public hearing as guaranteed under Section 34 of the Constitution.
  2. Whether the SCA had the power to amend or correct its order post-decision, given the attorneys had claimed no prior opportunity to contest their liability.
  3. The propriety of the attorneys' basis for approaching the SCA, either through a letter to the Registrar or requiring a formal application.
  4. What constitutes appropriate relief under the circumstances.

R – Rules [19–30]

The applicable legal principles centered on the right to a fair and public hearing contained in Section 34 of the Constitution, which mandates procedural fairness in judicial processes. This includes the audi alteram partem principle, ensuring all parties are afforded an opportunity to present their case.

Furthermore, case law such as De Lange v Smuts NO and Others established the foregoing principles aimed at eliminating arbitrariness in judicial decisions. The Supreme Court of Appeal's original decision included a determination regarding attorneys' liability despite their absence from the proceedings, which raised questions about procedural fairness and the scope of their liability given their actions were taken on instructions from Royal.

A – Application [19–30]

The Constitutional Court analyzed whether the attorneys' right to fair hearing was violated by determining that the SCA had impermissibly imposed liability without allowing the attorneys to present their defense. The Court highlighted that the attorneys were only acting as custodians of the funds on Royal's instructions, and thus, they should not have been held liable for the amounts awarded by the SCA.

Despite recognizing the gravitas of the issues at hand, the SCA proceeded to make findings against the attorneys, thus breaching the fundamental principle of fairness. Key observations from the judgment clarified that the dispute on appeal did not extend to the attorneys and hence the attorneys, who had not been heard nor joined in the appeal, had unwarranted liability attributed to them. Consequently, the Court overturned the order of the SCA.

C – Conclusion [32]

The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal and successfully overturned the SCA's decision, which had effectively held the attorneys liable in proceedings where they were non-parties. The judgment concluded that the rightful liability belonged to Royal, affirming the attorneys’ right to fair judicial processes as outlined in the Constitution.

The implications of this ruling stress the importance of adherence to constitutional rights in legal proceedings, particularly for attorneys who may inadvertently find themselves as target parties in litigation. The judgment ultimately reinforced that all parties must be properly heard to ensure the avoidance of arbitrary judicial outcomes, thereby enhancing mechanisms of procedural fairness in South African law.
