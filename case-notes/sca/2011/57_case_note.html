Supreme Court of Appeal

CONSAWU v Nedlac (289/10) [2011] ZASCA 57

Initially, CONSAWU requested declarations regarding the constitutional validity of the Act and parts of NEDLAC’s constitution.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2011 [2011] ZASCA 57

F – Facts

The case before the Supreme Court of Appeal centered on the Confederation of South African Workers’ Unions (CONSAWU) seeking membership in the National Economic Development and Labour Council (NEDLAC), a body established under the National Economic Development and Labour Council Act 35 of 1994. CONSAWU's application for membership was declined, prompting it to seek judicial intervention from the North Gauteng High Court. The court dismissed CONSAWU’s application, leading to the appeal ([paras 1–2]).

Initially, CONSAWU requested declarations regarding the constitutional validity of the Act and parts of NEDLAC’s constitution. However, after the Minister of Labour was excluded from the proceedings, CONSAWU amended its application to challenge only the validity of clauses 9.3 to 9.6 of NEDLAC’s constitution, which outline the membership application process ([paras 3–4]).

NEDLAC’s constitution permitted only established federations to nominate representatives for the executive council and chambers. Specifically, it stipulated that a federation should represent at least 300,000 employed workers to gain admission. As CONSAWU’s membership numbers fell below this threshold, the request for membership was denied ([paras 4–8]).

Over the course of litigation, CONSAWU modified its legal stance, arguing that sections 4(b) and 4(d) of the Act explicitly prohibit any constituency from being a gatekeeper to its own membership and require the criteria for admission to be articulated within the constitution itself ([paras 14–15]).

I – Issues

The critical legal issues in this case pertain to:

  1. Whether the provisions of NEDLAC’s constitution concerning membership application are in compliance with sections 4(b) and 4(d) of the National Economic Development and Labour Council Act.
  2. Whether NEDLAC has the authority to delegate membership criteria determination to the labour constituency, effectively allowing them to act as gatekeepers ([paras 20–21]).

R – Rules

The pertinent legal rules found within the National Economic Development and Labour Council Act include:

  • Section 4(b): Mandates the constitution to provide the manner in which organized labour can nominate members.
  • Section 4(d): Requires the constitution to delineate the criteria and process for admitting trade union federations into the organized labour constituency ([paras 20–21]).

In terms of precedent, the court referred to the underlying principle that public authorities should not delegate powers unless explicitly stated by the legislation. This principle underlies the presumption against the delegation of legislative powers which was further substantiated by legal doctrines from established administrative law ([paras 42–44]).

A – Application

The application of the law commenced with an analysis of the specific clauses in NEDLAC’s constitution. The court examined whether these clauses sufficiently outlined the criteria for admitting federations into organized labour as specified in section 4(d).

The court concluded that while the constitution did mention that criteria could be determined by the relevant constituency, doing so effectively imparted gatekeeping power onto the labour constituency, which was inconsistent with the statutory mandate ([para 25]). Additionally, the court clarified that NEDLAC was created to ensure inclusive representation; hence, delegating the criteria-setting power could result in unfair exclusionary practices ([paras 41–45]).

Furthermore, the judges critically dissected CONSAWU's shifting argumentation and ultimately determined that the argument made by NEDLAC—that the membership criteria were sufficiently embodied within the existing constitution—failed because the requirement to explicitly state these criteria within the constitution was not met ([paras 50–51]).

C – Conclusion

The court upheld the position that NEDLAC's constitution did not adequately provide for the admission criteria as mandated by the Act, leading to the dismissal of the appeal. The implications of this ruling underscore the need for procedural clarity in membership admissions to public bodies, ensuring an equitable framework that prevents any single constituency from monopolizing representation ([paras 30, 48]).

Thus, the decision naturally emphasizes a larger constitutional principle about inclusivity and transparency in public governance, ensuring that labor bodies are not hindered by internal thresholds that may not reflect broader labor interests. The appeal was dismissed with costs, highlighting the necessity of adhering to procedural and statutory requirements in governing institutions ([order]).
