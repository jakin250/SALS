# FIRAC Case Note: **Sterklewies (Pty) Ltd v Msimanga & Others**

## F – Facts
In this case, **Sterklewies (Pty) Ltd** (the Appellant), operating a feedlot in Harrismith, sought the eviction of **Messrs Msimanga, Mqina, and Tsotetsi** (the Respondents), who were former employees residing in the company's hostel. Following the dismissal of the Respondents in 2004 and unsuccessful challenges to their dismissals under the **Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA)**, the Appellant purportedly terminated their right of residence on 16 January 2008, following notices served pursuant to **s 9(2)(d) of the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (the Act)**. The Respondents refused to vacate the premises, prompting the Appellant to initiate eviction proceedings in the **Magistrates’ Court** ([paras 1-2]).

The Magistrate ultimately granted the Appellant an eviction order, which was subject to automatic review by the **Land Claims Court**. However, on 25 November 2011, the orders made by the Magistrate were set aside, leading to the current appeal ([paras 2-3]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issue concerns whether the Appellant successfully proved that the Respondents' occupation of the hostel derived solely from their employment agreements, which had been terminated. Additionally, there are considerations on whether the statutory protections under the Extension of Security of Tenure Act were adequately addressed in the eviction proceedings ([para 5]).

## R – Rules
The relevant legal authorities drawn upon by the court include:

1. **Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997** – This Act provides protection against eviction for individuals occupying agricultural land:
   - **s 1** defines an "occupier" as someone residing on land with the owner’s consent.
   - **s 3(1)** indicates that consent to reside can be terminated only per **s 8**.
   - **s 8(2)** states that the right of residence of an occupier who is an employee and whose right arises solely from employment can be terminated upon dismissal ([paras 3-4]).

2. Precedent cases:
   - **Landbounavorsingsraad v Klaasen 2005 (3) SA 410 (LCC)**, clarifying that the consent does not necessarily stem from a formal agreement but can arise tacitly.
   - **Randfontein Estates Gold Mining Co Ltd v Forbes 1992 (1) SA 649 (W)**, which distinguished between lawful termination of employment and the associated right to occupation.

## A – Application
The court examined the nature of the Respondents’ right to reside within the hostel and concluded that their residence indeed depended on their employment status. The evidence presented by the Appellant showed that:
- The Respondents had been granted permission to reside in the hostel solely based on their employment, as outlined in their employment agreements.
- The former employees did not provide evidence to counter this assertion or establish any alternative basis for their right to occupy the premises ([paras 4-5]).

The court noted that while an explicit term in a written contract indicating residence duration may not exist, tacit agreements or policies communicated orally or through observed practices can suffice in these contexts ([para 21]).

The court further emphasized that the requirements under the Act had not only been followed but that the Respondents’ continued occupation post-termination effectively violated the conditions stipulated within their employment. With the dismissal upheld during LRA proceedings, the Appellant's termination of the Respondents' right of residence was deemed lawful ([paras 4-8]).

Regarding the character of s 8(1) and s 8(2), the court found that, while s 8(1) affirms a broader right to terminate residence on just and equitable grounds, s 8(2) provides a more specific condition for cases directly linked to employment termination. The court declined to rule definitively on the interplay of these sections due to issues not raised in prior argumentation, instead sticking to the narrow focus established during the trial ([paras 9-15]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the Appellant's appeal, concluding that the Respondents’ right of residence was validly terminated after their respective employments were dissolved. Consequently, it held that the orders of the Land Claims Court were improperly made, and it confirmed the original eviction order made by the Magistrates’ Court. 

The court’s ruling indicates a careful balance between employment law and property rights under the Act. Furthermore, it highlighted that eviction cases should focus on established legal principles regarding occupational rights stemming from employment and the necessity to comply with statutory procedures ([paras 27-28]).
