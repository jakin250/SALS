Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# Case Note: Wezizwe Feziwe Sigcau and Another v The President of the Republic of South Africa and Others (961/2020) [2022] ZASCA 121

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
This case involves an appeal against a decision of the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, which upheld the 2010 determination of the Commission on Traditional Leadership Disputes and Claims (the Commission). The Commission had found Zanozuko Tyelovuyo Sigcau (Zanozuko) to be the rightful successor to the throne of amaMpondo aseQaukeni. This dispute originates from the death of King Mandlonke in 1937, which led to a longstanding succession conflict primarily between descendants of Mandlonke’s wives. Following Mandlonke's death, his sons, Nelson and Botha, along with their respective descendants, asserted competing claims to the throne. The complexities of the amaMpondo succession system, particularly the significance of the Great Wife and customary rules surrounding lineage and leadership, form the heart of this case.

In 2010, Zanozuko’s claim to the throne was recognized by the Commission, which was established under the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003. The Commission’s determination was based partly on its interpretation of amaMpondo customary law regarding male primogeniture and the significant role of public participation in the determination of leadership positions. The appellants, Wezizwe Feziwe Sigcau and Lombekiso Makhosatsini Masobhuza Sigcau, challenged this determination, arguing it was flawed and did not represent the true practices and beliefs of amaMpondo customary law ([paras 1–15]).

### I – Issues
The appeal raised several key legal issues:

1. **Whether the Commission misapplied the principles of customary law** in determining rightful kingship, particularly in its reliance on genealogical succession over community preferences and fitness for leadership ([paras 28–34]).
  
2. **The adequacy of public participation** in the Commission's investigation and the degree to which the Commission considered the fitness of the candidates ([paras 29–40]).
   
3. **The implications of the appointments and claims made between 1937 and 1979**, including the significance of the 1979 election that appointed Mpondombini and the Commission’s findings about the nature of the amaMpondo kingship ([paras 36–48]).

4. **Procedural matters related to undue delay** in the prosecution of the review application and its impact on the substantive merits of the appeal ([paras 24–27]).

### R – Rules
The court examined several legal frameworks and principles relevant to this case:

1. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: Provides guidelines for reviewing administrative action, stipulating that actions must not be arbitrary or unreasonable and that affected parties deserve fair process ([para 1]).
   
2. **Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003**: Endows the Commission with the authority to investigate traditional leadership disputes and reinforces the importance of customary law in determinations of kingship ([paras 16–18]).
   
3. **Case law**: The court referenced previous judgments that underscore the role of public participation in customary law determinations (e.g., Shilubana v Nwamitwa [2009] ZACC 9 and Public Protector v Mail and Guardian [2011] ZASCA 108) to reinforce the need to adhere to evolving customary practices ([paras 35–40]).

4. **Constitutional Guarantees**: The necessity for traditional leadership structures to align with constitutional imperatives, including equality and democratic participation ([para 41]).

### A – Application
In applying these rules, the court scrutinized the Commission's procedural conduct. It found that the Commission failed to effectively engage with relevant community customs and neglected to consider public opinion and the fitness for leadership of the candidates. The Commission's approach was described as overly rigid and genealogical, rather than reflective of the fluid and evolving nature of amaMpondo customary law ([paras 29–36]).

Furthermore, the court noted that the process adopted by the Commission during the 2010 determination lacked robustness and public engagement, which led to a flawed outcome. The evidence presented had not been adequately tested through an investigatory framework conducive to establishing the living customary law of amaMpondo ([paras 33–35]).

The court also emphasized the historical context of the appointment processes, particularly regarding the election in 1979 and how it underscored a shift in community preferences away from strictly genealogical succession ([paras 36–47]).

The delay argument was also addressed; while the respondents claimed prejudice due to the eight-year delay in challenging the Commission’s determination, the court found that the need for such a significant societal decision to reflect contemporary values outweighed concerns over procedural delay ([paras 24–27]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the appellants, overturning the decision of the high court. The Commission's determination that Zanozuko was the rightful king was set aside, along with the subsequent actions of the President in recognizing him as king. The court declared that the process for identifying the new leader should be conducted under the Traditional and Khoi-San Leadership Act 3 of 2019 or applicable laws that govern such determinations ([paras 53]). 

The case reinforces the necessity for traditional leadership disputes to be approached with a modern understanding of customary practices, emphasizing public involvement and the need for fitness for office alongside genealogical claims. The judgment clarifies that traditional leadership structures must evolve to remain credible and legitimate in the context of current democratic norms ([para 53]). The implications of this case are profound, as it sets a precedent for how traditional leadership disputes might be adjudicated in South Africa, firmly anchoring them within both customary law and the constitutional framework ([para 53]).
