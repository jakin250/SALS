Harrielall v University of KwaZulu-Natal (493/2016) [2017] ZASCA 25

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 25

F – Facts

The appellant, Ms. Niekara Harrielall, aimed to qualify as a medical doctor and applied to the University of KwaZulu-Natal (UKZN) for admission to its MBChB degree program in 2015 but was denied admission. To improve her prospects, she enrolled in a Bachelor of Medical Science (Anatomy) program at UKZN in 2015. Unfortunately, her application for the MBChB degree for the 2016 academic year as a "mature student" was similarly rejected. Harrielall contested this decision by applying to the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court for a review, arguing that UKZN did not adhere to its own admissions policy regarding mature students ([1]–[3]).

In her High Court application, she claimed that the decision was arbitrary and not in line with the institution’s admissions policy, as outlined in Section 37(3) of the Higher Education Act 101 of 1997, which mandates that admission policies include measures to redress past inequalities. The court dismissed her application, noting that she failed to demonstrate that UKZN acted capriciously nor that it had deviated from its policy in a manner that warrants judicial intervention ([3]–[4]).

The admissions policy allowed for various categories of mature students, stipulating that 25% of places would be allocated to “open” candidates ([4]). Harrielall asserted that UKZN had considered only those who had completed their degrees for admission into the mature student category, effectively excluding her. On appeal, her argument originally centered on this exclusion but shifted focus to the need for UKZN to explain the admissions criteria in more detail. However, new arguments were neither introduced in her founding affidavit nor narrowed sufficiently for the court's examination, resulting in challenges to her claims of procedural unfairness ([5]–[8]).

I – Issues

The case primarily revolved around the following legal issues:

  1. Whether the University of KwaZulu-Natal properly followed its admissions policy in rejecting Harrielall's application for the MBChB degree for 2016.
  2. Whether the declaration of a ranking system for mature applicants by UKZN infringed upon the principles of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA), particularly regarding procedural fairness and rationality.
  3. The appropriateness of costs orders in relation to the conduct of the appeal and the underlying merits of the case, particularly in light of potential implications for access to justice ([7], [9]).

R – Rules

The legal framework surrounding this case includes:

  1. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000: This Act aims to ensure that administrative actions are lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair. Section 3 establishes a right to fair administrative action ([5]).
  2. Higher Education Act 101 of 1997: Section 37(3) mandates that the admission policies of public higher education institutions must contain measures to redress past inequalities ([4]).
  3. Principles of Judicial Review: These principles mandate that administrative bodies must act within their prescribed authority and comply with principles of procedural fairness. This encompasses the requirement of providing reasons for decisions made ([3]).

A – Application

The court, in reviewing the case, found that the appellant could not demonstrate that UKZN failed to consider her application properly or acted arbitrarily. The university's admissions policy outlined a clear distinction among different categories of mature students—including provisions for those who had completed their degrees and those who had not. Although Harrielall contested that she was unfairly evaluated against completed degree applicants, her legal arguments evolved in a manner that was not evidence-based or consistent across proceedings ([5]–[8]).

On appeal, the appellant’s counsel acknowledged that the distinction she initially sought to assert—that degree incomplete and degree complete students should not compete directly—was no longer central to her case. Instead, they argued a need for UKZN to clarify how points were allocated among applicants within the same category. The court maintained that new allegations made in the heads of argument were not sufficiently substantiated in her founding affidavit, thus undermining her appeal ([8]).

The majority judgment emphasized that the appellant should have initiated a Rule 53 inquiry earlier in the process to obtain documentation for the admissions criteria, thereby allowing her to structure her challenges with greater clarity. This procedural misstep contributed directly to her unsuccessful appeal ([9]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, emphasizing the need for deference in matters where administrative discretion is exercised unless arbitrary actions are conclusively demonstrated. The court underscored that Harrielall’s continual modifications to her challenge weakened her legal position, ultimately leading to the dismissal of her appeal with costs ([11]).

Notably, the dissenting judgment by Molemela AJA argued for a more lenient approach to costs due to the initial ambiguity in the admissions policy, understanding the appellant's pursuit of educational opportunities as a significant factor worthy of consideration. This divergence illustrates an ongoing tension between procedural rigor and equitable access to justice, especially in educational contexts where public interest may often intersect with administrative discretion ([29]–[37]).

Thus, the ruling served as a reminder of the critical need for clarity in admissions policies and the rigorous application of legal principles governing administrative actions, particularly in educational settings where the implications for individual aspirants can be profound.
