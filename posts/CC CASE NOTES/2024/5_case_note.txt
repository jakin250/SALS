Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# Case Note: President of the Republic of South Africa and Another v Tembani and Others [2024] ZACC 5

**FIRAC Method**

## F – Facts
The case revolves around a group of 25 plaintiffs, predominantly comprising Zimbabwean farmers and companies, who had their land confiscated without compensation under Zimbabwe's land redistribution program. This program was sanctioned by a constitutional amendment (section 16B) enacted on 16 September 2005, which barred legal redress for such confiscations within Zimbabwe’s jurisdiction. Consequently, the plaintiffs sought remedies against the South African President and Government for damages arising from alleged unlawful conduct, claiming that the actions of the South African authorities facilitated these violations against their rights ([1-6]).

The plaintiffs issued a legal claim in April 2019, concurrently serving an application for condonation for not having timeously served the required notice as per the Institution of Legal Proceedings Against Certain Organs of State Act ([2-3]). The High Court acknowledged their claims but partially upheld exceptions raised by the defendants concerning the lack of legal duty and causation. Following this, the case proceeded through various appeals, with the plaintiffs contending that their claims had not prescribed because they had only become due upon the Constitutional Court's confirmation of the unconstitutionality of the President's actions ([4-6]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal questions addressed by the Court included:
1. **Prescription**: When did the plaintiffs' claims prescribe, and did the service of the review application interrupt the prescription period?
2. **Condonation**: Was the High Court correct in its dismissal of the condonation application based on the alleged lack of compliance with the notice requirement?
3. **Delictual Liability**: Did the plaintiffs’ particulars of claim sufficiently allege that the defendants owed them a legal duty, and was there sufficient causal connection between the defendants' actions and the harm suffered? ([41-60]).

## R – Rules
The case involved several statutory and constitutional provisions, most notably:
- **Institution of Legal Proceedings Against Certain Organs of State Act 40 of 2002**: Mandates that legal proceedings against state organs require prior notice, served within six months of the debt becoming due ([66-69]).
- **Prescription Act 68 of 1969**: Establishes that a debt becomes due as soon as the creditor has knowledge of the debtor and the facts giving rise to the claim, with a three-year limitation period for delictual claims ([67-70]).
- South African constitutional provisions stipulating the obligations of the President, specifically concerning international agreements and the prohibition against unconstitutional actions under sections 167(4)(e) and 172(2)(a) ([28-29]).

Precedents influencing the decision included the **Biowatch principle**, which allows for protection from costs in constitutional matters where the action, although unsuccessful, was of public interest and significant constitutional concern ([127]).

## A – Application
In applying these rules, the Court engaged in a detailed analysis of prescription and its interruption. The majority concluded that because the plaintiffs' claims arose from alleged unlawful acts of the President occurring on or before 18 August 2014, the debts had become due at that point ([84-92]). The plaintiffs argued that their claims could not mature until the Constitutional Court confirmed the unconstitutionality of the President's conduct, a position the Court ultimately rejected, asserting that wrongdoing is evaluated against the constitutional framework at the time of the act, not contingent on later judicial pronouncements.

The Court found that prescription had begun on the dates of the President's relevant actions, and the plaintiffs had knowledge of the facts by July 2015, leading to the conclusion that their claims had indeed prescribed ([103-106]). The claim to have the service of the application interrupt the prescription was also rejected, as the initial legal proceedings did not constitute a formal claim for damages necessary to interrupt prescription under the Prescription Act ([110-120]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court, therefore, ruled in favor of the defendants, setting aside the orders of the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal. The Court dismissed the plaintiffs' application for condonation and subsequently their action for damages, citing that their claims had prescribed ([129]). The ruling not only clarified aspects of procedural law concerning prescription and the execution of constitutional duties but also affirmed the importance of timely legal action in the pursuit of justice, highlighting the balance between legal frameworks and the protection of constitutional rights.

This decision carries significant implications for future claims involving state organs and delictual liability, specifically concerning claims that may intertwine with constitutional matters and international treaties. It affirms the need for claimants to assert their rights promptly and to comprehend the intricate nature of the evolving legal landscape influenced by both national and international jurisprudence.
