Supreme Court of Appeal

Gcam-Gcam v The State (1034/13) [2015] ZASCA 42

The appellant, Alfred ZweliDumile Gcam-Gcam, was charged alongside several co-accused with multiple counts, including conspiracy to commit robbery, robbery, five counts of murder, and unlawful possession of firearms.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 [2015] ZASCA 42

F – Facts

The appellant, Alfred ZweliDumile Gcam-Gcam, was charged alongside several co-accused with multiple counts, including conspiracy to commit robbery, robbery, five counts of murder, and unlawful possession of firearms. The events underpinning these charges emerged from an armed robbery of a social-grant pay-point in Willowvale on 4 June 2009, where the robbers absconded with R509,970. Tragically, both a police officer and four robbers were killed in a subsequent shootout with the police ([1]–[2]).

The police's case primarily relied on confessions made by the appellant and an accomplice, Vuyisa Mayisela. The appellant contended that these confessions were coerced and that his alibi as an ambulance driver on the night preceding the robbery was not given proper consideration. The trial court found the confessions admissible despite claims of police misconduct, including physical assaults, which the appellant alleged undermined the validity of his statements ([3]–[4]). The evidence showed that a conspiracy was established among ten conspirators, including the appellant, to rob a pay-point, culminating in multiple fatalities ([6]–[11]).

I – Issues

The appellate court was tasked with evaluating several critical issues:

  1. Whether the evidence, particularly the confessions, was obtained in violation of the appellant's constitutional rights and therefore inadmissible ([39]');
  2. The appropriateness of forming a common purpose based on the abandonment of the original robbery plan and the subsequent robbery of a different pay-point without the appellant's knowledge ([25]–[28]);
  3. Whether the appellant could be convicted as an accessory after the fact with respect to his actions post-robbery ([32]–[36]);
  4. The validity of the conspiracy charge, given that the robbery and conspiracy may merge under South African law, resulting in issues of duplicity in sentencing ([37]).

R – Rules

The legal framework primarily rested on the provisions of the South African Constitution, specifically Section 35 regarding the rights of arrested individuals, which underscores the necessity of informing suspects about their rights to fair trial provisions and the right against self-incrimination ([39]). Relevant case law included:

  • S v Morgan & Others 1993 (2) SACR 134 (A), which provides guidance on what constitutes knowledge and intention in establishing accessory culpability ([34]);
  • S v Mbuli 2003 (1) SACR 97, outlining the necessity for evidence of chain possession in firearms cases ([30]);
  • The "common purpose" doctrine as elaborated in C R Snyman, Criminal Law 5th ed (2008), which explains legal accountability in collaborative criminal efforts ([37]).

A – Application

The court rigorously assessed the conditions under which the appellant's confessions were obtained, applying a stringent standard of scrutiny due to allegations of coercion. It was noted that the appellant had not been adequately informed of his rights, which, under Section 35(5) of the Constitution, might justify excluding the confession despite the presiding judge’s original determination of its admissibility ([39]–[52]).

Regarding the common purpose doctrine, the court rejected the prosecution's assertion that the second robbery fell within the ambit of the initial conspiracy. The details revealed that the initial plan to rob the first pay-point was abandoned, and therefore, the liability for the subsequent robbery could not justifiably apply to the appellant, who had no knowledge of or participation in that act ([25]–[27]).

Additionally, the notion of accessory liability post-commission of a crime was evaluated, with the court ultimately concluding that the appellant lacked requisite knowledge of the alleged robbery and murders when he assisted the fleeing robbers ([36]).

In terms of conspiracy, the court emphasized that a person cannot be charged with both conspiracy to commit a crime and the crime itself, as they merge. Consequently, because the robbery conviction was annulled, it prompted a re-evaluation of the conspiracy charge ([37]–[38]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded by upholding the appellant's appeal. It set aside all convictions and sentences of the Eastern Cape High Court, emphasizing the inadequacies in the prosecution’s case, particularly regarding evidence collection and interrogation integrity ([63]).

The ruling serves as a pivotal reminder of the essential safeguards inherent in criminal proceedings, especially regarding the treatment of confession evidence obtained under questionable circumstances. The court's order highlights both the nuances of the common purpose doctrine and the principle against duplicating convictions in conspiracy and robbery cases. Furthermore, it underscores the overarching necessity for rigorous adherence to constitutional protections securing fair trial rights against arbitrary state action ([68]).
