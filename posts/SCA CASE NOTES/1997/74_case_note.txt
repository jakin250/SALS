Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 1997

# Case Note: Mlungisi David Lange & Others v The State (Case No 406/95)

**FIRAC Methodology**

---

### F - Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 2–5])

The case involves the tragic murders of Louwrens Vorster and his wife, Anna Fransina Vorster, on their farm Dwaalfontein near Burgersdorp, South Africa, on September 19, 1991. Mr. Vorster was 57 years old, while his wife was 53. 

On the day in question, Mrs. Vorster left home around 8 a.m. and returned at 5 p.m., when her body was found shortly afterward, shot in the back of the head and abdomen. Mr. Vorster’s body was later discovered submerged in a dam, showing signs of strangulation and multiple stab wounds ([para 2]).

Three men—Mlungisi David Lange (the first appellant), Mahlomola Enoch Mokhoabane (the second appellant), and Abraham Mosanyane Moshapane (the third appellant)—were arrested three days later on September 22, 1991, and charged with murder and robbery with aggravating circumstances. They were initially convicted and sentenced to death for the murders, alongside a ten-year sentence for robbery.

The appellants appealed their convictions but were granted leave only concerning the murder convictions, not the sentences for robbery. The State’s case relied on the testimony of a key witness, Bhambushe Mathimba, whose credibility was central to the judgment ([para 5]).

---

### I - Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 4–7])

The primary issues raised in the appeal include:

1. **Credibility of Witnesses**: Whether the court could rely on the testimony of Bhambushe Mathimba, who served as the primary witness for the prosecution and whose status as an accomplice was contested by the appellants ([paras 4–7]).
   
2. **Corroborative Evidence**: The admissibility and probative value of various pieces of corroborative evidence, including forensic findings and spontaneous declarations made by the second appellant while in custody ([paras 8–10]).

3. **Alibi Defenses**: The validity of the alibi defenses presented by the appellants and whether they were properly discredited by the trial court ([para 11]).

4. **Constitutional Issues Surrounding Sentencing**: Given that the death penalty had been declared unconstitutional, the implications for the death sentences imposed on the appellants ([para 13]).

---

### R - Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 3, 11, 12])

The court relied upon several legal principles established in South African law, including:

- **Principles of Evidence**: The need for corroboration in cases involving accomplices and the criteria for assessing the credibility of witnesses, particularly those involved in criminal acts ([paras 6, 8–10]).
  
- **Murder and Robbery Statutes**: Application of relevant provisions of the Criminal Procedure Act, particularly regarding the gravity of the charges and the applicable penalties ([para 5]).

- **Constitutional Jurisprudence**: Reference to the constitutional declaration rendering the death penalty impermissible, thereby necessitating a remittal for sentence reconsideration ([para 13]).

The judgment cites the need for a sound assessment of evidential reliability in criminal proceedings, as established in earlier case law ([para 11]).

---

### A - Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 6–12])

The court upheld the credibility of Mathimba, the primary state witness. Despite the appellants’ contention that he was an accomplice and therefore unreliable, the court found no sufficient contra-indications to undermine his testimony. Mathimba’s evidence was critical as it placed the appellants at the crime scene and linked them to the goods taken from the Vorsters’ farm ([para 6]).

Forensic evidence, including hair strands from Mrs. Vorster found on the third appellant's swimming trunks, supported Mathimba’s claims and corroborated the events leading to the murders. The court ruled that the presence of such direct evidence weighed heavily against the appellants ([paras 8–10]).

The spontaneous comments made by the second appellant in custody were deemed admissible as they were responses to legitimate inquiries rather than contrived police interrogation tactics. Thus, they were not subject to exclusion under common evidential standards, serving to further implicate him in the crimes ([para 10]).

The alibi defenses presented by the first and second appellants were deemed unconvincing and were rightfully dismissed by the court. Their attempts at misdirection or variance in testimonies were interpreted as dishonesty, thereby substantiating their guilt ([paras 11–12]).

---

### C - Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 13–13])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appellants were correctly convicted on all counts relating to murder and robbery and upheld the trial court’s findings regarding their guilt ([para 12]). The court, however, overturned the sentences of death due to the unconstitutionality of the death penalty, requiring the matter to be remitted for sentencing under the current legal framework ([para 13]).

The implications of this judgment reaffirm the necessity for robust evidentiary standards in criminal cases while highlighting the evolving nature of capital punishment jurisprudence in South Africa. The court’s approach demonstrated a careful balancing of witness credibility, corroborative evidence, and adherence to constitutional norms, underscoring the continuous development of South African law in alignment with constitutional mandates.
