Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

### FIRAC Case Note: Competition Commission of South Africa v Senwes Limited ([2012] ZACC 6)

---

#### F – Facts 
The case at hand arises from an application for leave to appeal by the Competition Commission of South Africa (the Commission) concerning a ruling by the Supreme Court of Appeal. The latter had set aside a judgment by the Competition Tribunal which found Senwes Limited (Senwes) guilty of contravening section 8(c) of the Competition Act 89 of 1998 (the Act), by engaging in an exclusionary practice known as a “margin squeeze” ([11]–[12]). 

Historically, Senwes held a dominant position in the grain storage market with a market share of over 80% ([32]). The Commission's complaints against Senwes stemmed from its 2003 introduction of a "differential tariff" for storage fees, which effectively penalized traders while favoring farmers, thereby diminishing the competitors' ability to operate competitively ([34]–[36]). Upon referral of the matter from the Commission to the Tribunal, Senwes contested the Tribunal's jurisdiction on two alternative grounds: first, that the margin squeeze was not included in the complaint referred to the Tribunal, and second, that the essential elements of such a squeeze were not proven ([12]).

---

#### I – Issues 
The primary issues for determination are:
1. Did the Tribunal exceed its statutory power in adjudicating the margin squeeze complaint?
2. Was the margin squeeze complaint included in the referral to the Tribunal, or could the Tribunal examine issues beyond what was referred? ([21]–[22]).

---

#### R – Rules 
The legal framework is provided by the Competition Act 89 of 1998, particularly sections 8(c) and 52. Section 8(c) prohibits dominant firms from engaging in exclusionary acts that have an anti-competitive effect ([26]). Section 52 outlines the Tribunal's obligations to conduct hearings into matters that are referred to it, stipulating that the Tribunals must adhere to the rules and procedural requirements established under the Act ([52]).

Additionally, prior case law, including Netstar (Pty) Ltd v Competition Commission, reinforced that the Tribunal's authority is confined to the matters specified in the referral, which must align with the provisions of the Act ([14]). 

---

#### A – Application 
The Constitutional Court analyzed the arguments in light of the statutory provisions and the facts presented. It examined whether the complaint surrounding the margin squeeze fell within the boundaries of the allegations referred to the Tribunal. Notably, it concluded that the complaint referenced exclusionary practices related to differential fees, which succinctly aligns with the prohibitions laid out under section 8(c) of the Act ([39]).

Notably, the Tribunal's classification of the violation as a "margin squeeze" introduced ambiguity. However, the underlying conduct—the charging of differential tariffs—nevertheless constituted a contravention of section 8(c), supporting the Tribunal's overall ruling despite its terminological error ([43]–[45]). 

The Supreme Court of Appeal had misinterpreted the scope of the referral, asserting the need for rigid adherence to the technical language of the referral. Instead, the main judgment guided by the Constitutional Court highlighted that the Tribunal’s scope for inquiry remains expansive, provided it pertains to matters arising during the hearing ([50]).

In addressing procedural fairness, while acknowledging that Senwes raised specific objections regarding the margin squeeze, the Court ultimately found that the Tribunal's failure to rule on these objections did not amount to a violation of procedural rights ([51]–[52]). Senwes had ample opportunity to rebut the allegations presented against it, showing no actual prejudice despite the Tribunal’s procedural errors.

---

#### C – Conclusion 
The Constitutional Court ultimately awarded leave to appeal, upheld the Tribunal's ruling, and set aside the order of the Supreme Court of Appeal. It concluded that Senwes had contravened section 8(c) of the Act through its exclusionary practices, stressing the importance of interpreting the Act expansively to fulfill its objectives of promoting fair competition and preventing abuse of dominant market positions ([58]). 

The implications of this ruling underscore the necessity for clarity in procedural matters, noting that while the Commission holds specific powers to refer cases, the Tribunal retains the authority to evaluate related anti-competitive conduct emerging during hearings, thus safeguarding competitive markets in South Africa's socio-economic landscape ([57]–[58]).

---

This case establishes significant precedent in defining the powers of the Tribunal while also emphasizing the necessity of comprehensive interpretations of competition-related legislation to promote equitable market participation across diverse socio-economic divides.
