Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2001

# FIRAC Case Note: *Moise v Transitional Local Council of Greater Germiston* CCT 54/00

### F – Facts
The plaintiff, Sias Moise, initiated legal proceedings against the Transitional Local Council of Greater Germiston for damages due to injuries sustained by his minor daughter, Faith Moise, on 28 April 1998. At the time of the incident, Faith was eight years old, and she was injured attempting to board a bus driven by an employee of the defendant during the course of his employment ([1]). 

The case was brought before the Witwatersrand High Court, where the defendant filed a special plea claiming that the action was barred due to the plaintiff’s failure to comply with section 2(1)(a) of the Limitation of Legal Proceedings (Provincial and Local Authorities) Act 94 of 1970. This provision mandated that legal proceedings regarding claims against local authorities could not be initiated unless the claimant delivered a written notice within ninety days from the date on which the debt (in this case, the claim for damages) arose ([2]). The plaintiff contested the constitutionality of section 2(1)(a) on the grounds that it infringed upon section 34 of the South African Constitution, which guarantees the right to access to courts ([2]).

The High Court ruled in favor of the plaintiff, dismissing the defendant’s special plea and declaring section 2(1)(a) unconstitutional. The court referred this decision to the Constitutional Court for confirmation under section 167(5) of the Constitution ([3]). Subsequently, the parties reached an agreement and expressed lack of further interest; however, the Constitutional Court sought views from interested parties, including the Minister of Justice and the Women’s Legal Centre as amicus curiae ([4]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal question before the Court was whether section 2(1)(a) of the Limitation of Legal Proceedings Act constituted a limitation of the right to access to courts as guaranteed by section 34 of the Constitution. If it did, the further inquiry was whether such a limitation was reasonable and justifiable under the provisions of section 36 of the Constitution ([6], [17]). 

Additionally, the Court examined whether the legislative provision effectively imposed a significant barrier to justice, particularly for a claimant in the position of the plaintiff, who argued that the strict timeframe afforded by the Act was excessively limiting, especially considering the socioeconomic factors of potential litigants ([16], [24]). 

### R – Rules
The relevant legal framework stems from:
1. **Section 34 of the Constitution**, which asserts that every individual has the right to have disputes resolved by the application of law in a fair public hearing.
2. **Section 2(1)(a) of the Limitation of Legal Proceedings Act**, which establishes specific notice requirements for initiating claims against local authorities.
3. **Section 36(1) of the Constitution**, which permits the limitation of rights but mandates that such limitations must be reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society ([17]).

The court also referenced previous judgments where similar notice requirements had been criticized for undermining access to justice, such as *Mohlomi v Minister of Defence* ([12]).

### A – Application
The Court began by establishing that the requirement of a written notice within ninety days indeed limited access to courts, particularly for individuals like the plaintiff who might lack legal literacy or resources to comply with such stringent procedural requirements ([16]). The time frame of which the claimants must deliver notice was deemed by the Court to be unreasonably short, further compounded by socioeconomic factors impacting potential claimants' awareness of their rights and the means necessary to pursue their claims ([14]).

In examining the justification for the limitation under section 36, the Court weighed the state’s interest in efficient administration of justice and the respective rights of claimants. The government entity in question failed to provide robust justification for the necessity and proportionality of the limitation imposed by section 2(1)(a), which further complicated its defense ([20], [22]). 

Moreover, the Court noted the Minister’s acknowledgment of the inadequacies within the existing legislation and the proposal of a new Bill aimed at reforming this area of law, which evidentially suggested that even the legislature viewed the current framework as outdated and overly restrictive ([21]). 

Ultimately, the Court concluded that the limitation imposed by section 2(1)(a) was not justifiable within the context of a democratic society that prioritizes human dignity and equality. The right of access to the courts was said to be of cardinal importance, and the overwhelming interests of justice favored the invalidation of such legally entrenched barriers to litigants' access ([24]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court’s order declaring section 2(1)(a) of the Limitation of Legal Proceedings (Provincial and Local Authorities) Act 94 of 1970 unconstitutional ([26]). It emphasized that legislative provisions that create substantial obstacles to court access need to be carefully scrutinized and justifiable, especially in light of the Constitution’s commitment to fundamental rights. The decision highlighted the imperative of removing barriers to justice and ensuring equitable access for all individuals, particularly those from disadvantaged backgrounds ([24], [25]). This ruling sets a pivotal precedent for future legislative reforms concerning claims against public authorities in South Africa.
