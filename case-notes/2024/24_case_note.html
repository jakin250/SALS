Rademeyer v Ferreira [2024] ZACC 24

The case of Rademeyer v Ferreira concerns the enforcement of a sale agreement and subsequent claims relating to its breach.

Constitutional Court 2024 [2024] ZACC 24

F – Facts

The case of Rademeyer v Ferreira concerns the enforcement of a sale agreement and subsequent claims relating to its breach. The applicant, Dion Rademeyer, contested a claim brought by Thomas Ferreira, the respondent, which originated from the cancellation of a sale agreement pertaining to a subdivided property (Erf 4097, Theescombe) due to Rademeyer’s failure to comply with the agreement ([1]–[8]).

Ferreira, the registered owner of the property in question, aimed to develop it but faced non-compliance from Rademeyer, who had failed to sign necessary transfer documents and pay required amounts as per the sale agreement. This culminated in Ferreira filing an application in the High Court for specific performance, which resulted in a court order (the “Pickering J order”) mandating Rademeyer to fulfill his obligations. After Rademeyer’s continued non-compliance, Ferreira cancelled the agreement and sought damages ([1]–[8]).

The legal history of the case shows Rademeyer raising a special plea of prescription, arguing that Ferreira’s claim had prescribed since it was not pursued within three years from the date of service of the Pickering J order ([14]–[56]). Ferreira countered this by claiming the service of his initial application interrupted prescription under the Prescription Act ([56]).

I – Issues

This appeal raises critical legal questions:

  1. Whether the basis of Ferreira's claim in the 2012 application was the same as the claim in the current proceedings regarding damages.
  2. Whether the application proceedings constituted a legal step in enforcing a claim for the payment of a debt, thereby interrupting the prescription period for Ferreira's damages claim.
  3. Whether the previous application for specific performance directly influenced Ferreira's subsequent claim for damages, and if service on the application initiated the judicial interruption of prescription as per section 15(1) of the Prescription Act ([11]–[15]).

R – Rules

The court’s analysis hinges on the provisions of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, particularly sections 11(d) and 15. Section 11(d) addresses the prescription of debts, while section 15(1) outlines that prescription is interrupted upon the service of a process claiming payment of a debt. The interpretation of ‘debt’ within these sections was guided by precedents such as Allianz Insurance Co Ltd v Cape Town Municipality and Cadac (Pty) Ltd v Weber-Stephen Products Company, where the courts explored the nature of claims and the validity and timing of prescription ([9]–[16]).

The case hinged on determining whether Ferreira's legal recourse related to a singular cause of action, encompassing both the specific performance claim and the damages claim arising from Rademeyer's breach ([43]–[51]).

A – Application

In applying the law to the facts, the majority found that both the claim for specific performance and the subsequent claim for damages arose from the same set of facts and mutual obligations under the sale agreement. It was held that Ferreira's initial step in seeking specific performance constituted a ‘process’ interrupting the prescription period for his later damage claim. This led the court to conclude that Ferreira's right to damages was intrinsically linked to Rademeyer’s breach of the contract as well as the non-compliance with the Pickering J order ([32]–[56]).

The court rejected the applicant's argument that responding to the first application created a new, distinct cause of action. The majority contended that the case presented an integrated progression of legal claims centered on the same contractual obligation ([43]–[51]).

In contrast, the minority judgment underscored that Ferreira had not suffered damages at the time of filing for specific performance; thus, his damages claim was premature. This distinction was pivotal since a claim for damages can only arise once the breach has occurred and the cancellation executed. Thus, prescription would commence from the cancellation date, which was determined to be five days after the service of the Pickering J order, rather than during the earlier application ([52]–[94]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal and upheld Ferreira’s claim against Rademeyer on the basis that the initial proceedings effectively interrupted the prescription period for the damages claim, concluding they were part and parcel of the same legal process ([1]–[56]). This landmark ruling established that both the application for specific performance and the subsequent claim for damages formed a continuous legal framework under the same cause of action, thereby reinforcing the principle that the running of prescription is effectively interrupted by initiating appropriate and timely legal proceedings ([32]–[56]).

Consequently, the Supreme Court of Appeal’s decision was overturned, validating Ferreira's right to damages which had not prescribed within the time limits defined by the act. Given the constitutional implications concerning access to courts under section 34, the judgement also highlighted the need for clarity and consistency in the application of prescription law, calling into question how extinguishment of rights must be interpreted in light of fundamental rights ([57]–[94]).
