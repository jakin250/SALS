14 Case Note

Case Title: Farouk Jasat v Natal Law Society Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa Case No: 78/98 Judges: Grosskopf, Nienaber, Marais, Scott, Zulman JJA Date Heard: 20 March 2000 Date Delivered: 28 March 2000

Supreme Court of Appeal 2000 1997 (1) SACR 489

Case Title: Farouk Jasat v Natal Law Society
Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Case No: 78/98
Judges: Grosskopf, Nienaber, Marais, Scott, Zulman JJA
Date Heard: 20 March 2000
Date Delivered: 28 March 2000

F – Facts ([paras 1–10])

The appellant, Farouk Jasat, is an attorney based in Pietermaritzburg. He is appealing against an order from the Natal Provincial Division that struck his name off the roll of attorneys due to his conduct during criminal proceedings. In July 1993, Jasat was charged with housebreaking with intent to steal and theft, resulting in a conviction and a fine. His conviction was later appealed but ultimately confirmed in S v Jasat 1997 (1) SACR 489 (SCA).

Following his conviction, the Natal Law Society initiated proceedings to have him struck off the roll based not only on the conviction but also on his conduct during the trial, particularly for what the Society characterized as a “specious alibi defence” ([para 3]). The events that led to his criminal charges involved the departure of attorney Mr. Baboo Akoo from South Africa, who instructed his clerk to report any attempt by Jasat to take files from his office. Jasat falsely claimed he had permission to retrieve his files following Akoo’s abrupt exit, but evidence indicated he unlawfully entered Akoo’s office, supported by false statements and suborning a witness ([paras 2, 4–10]).

In his answering affidavit in the striking-off proceedings, Jasat admitted for the first time that he had lied under oath during the criminal trial and attempted to explain his actions by arguing that he believed it was justified to recover his files ([para 6]). Despite these explanations, he maintained that he was still a fit and proper person to practice as an attorney ([para 7]).

I – Issues ([paras 9–10, 12])

The key legal issues raised in this case include:

  1. Whether Farouk Jasat is a fit and proper person to continue practicing as an attorney, given his conduct in the criminal proceedings.
  2. Whether the discretion exercised by the Court a quo regarding the severity of the sanction (striking off versus suspension) was appropriate.
  3. The interpretation of the standards required for an attorney concerning honesty and integrity as stipulated in s 22(1) of the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979 ([para 10]).

R – Rules ([paras 10–13])

Legal authorities and rules relevant to this case include:

  • Attorneys Act 53 of 1979: Specifically, s 22(1), which empowers the court to strike off or suspend an attorney based on whether the practitioner is deemed not to be a fit and proper person ([para 10]). The Act establishes that this evaluation entails three stages, namely, the determination of wrongful conduct, assessing if the attorney is unfit to practice, and deciding the appropriate sanction.
  • Judicial Precedents:
  • Reyneke v Wetsgenootskap van die Kaap die Goeie Hoop 1994 (1) SA 359 (A): This case emphasized a twofold inquiry which has effectively evolved into a three-staged analysis as outlined in the current proceedings, focusing on misconduct, determination of unfitness, and appropriate punitive measures.
  • Vassen v Law Society of the Cape of Good Hope 1998 (4) SA 532 (SCA): This ruling reinforced the necessity for complete honesty, integrity, and reliability from attorneys, alongside emphasizing that not every instance of untruthfulness renders an attorney unfit.

A – Application ([paras 11–13])

In determining Jasat’s fitness to remain in practice, the Court considered his conduct during the criminal proceedings as well as his attempt to fabricate an alibi with false evidence. The Court acknowledged that while the appellant's conduct was a serious infraction, it held that not every untruthfulness equates to a lack of fitness to practice ([para 12]).

Critically, the Court noted that Jasat’s conduct was not merely a case of an impulsive lie. Instead, it involved a prolonged and calculated attempt to deceive, which also included suborning a second witness to support his false claims ([para 13]). The Court examined the extent to which such actions should reflect on his character and whether they indicated an unworthiness to continue in the honorable profession of law.

Despite the appellant’s arguments suggesting that the law society had overemphasized the critical importance of honesty, the Court ultimately determined that the totality of his actions demonstrated a fundamental unfitness to practice, supportive of the striking-off action initiated by the Law Society ([paras 12–17]).

C – Conclusion ([paras 14–18])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Jasat's appeal for the following reasons:

  1. The Court affirmed that the appellant had indeed demonstrated a lack of fitness through conduct that not only constituted perjury but also involved soliciting false testimony from an accomplice.
  2. The decision of the Court a quo to strike Jasat off the roll was ultimately deemed appropriate, given the nature of the misconduct and the need to uphold the integrity of the profession ([paras 13, 17]).
  3. The Court also ruled on procedural points, noting that the criminal record should have been included in the appeal record, which reinforced the justification for dismissing the appeal with costs, including the cost of producing the criminal record ([para 18]).

This case serves as a critical precedent regarding the standards required for attorneys in terms of conduct, honesty, and integrity, emphasizing that breaches in these standards warrant serious consequences, including striking off from the roll of attorneys.
