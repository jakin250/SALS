Case Note: REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2004

# FIRAC Case Note: Republic of South Africa, Road Accident Fund v Zwelakhe Jerome Mkhize (Case No. 557/03)

## F – Facts

In this case, the Supreme Court of Appeal encountered an issue regarding the application of section 18(1) of the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996. The respondent, Zwelakhe Jerome Mkhize, sustained injuries while seated in the cab of a tanker being towed by a tow truck. The tanker capsized due to the negligence of the tow truck driver, W J Lehmkuhl ([1]–[2]). The appellant, the Road Accident Fund (the “Fund”), contended that Mkhize's claim should be limited to R25,000 as prescribed by section 18(1) of the Act, arguing that he was a passenger in or on a vehicle at the time of the accident and thus within the statutory limitations ([1]).

The claim was first raised before Jappie J in the court of first instance, who ruled in favor of Mkhize, determining that the limitation did not apply ([1]). Consequently, the Fund appealed, seeking a declaration that Mkhize’s claim was indeed capped ([1]).

## I – Issues

The principal legal issue is whether section 18(1) of the Road Accident Fund Act limits the claim of Mkhize to R25,000 since he was injured while being towed in a motor vehicle at the time of the incident ([1]–[2]). Specifically, it must be determined whether the tanker, in addition to the tow truck, was considered a motor vehicle under the Act and whether Mkhize's status as a passenger affects the applicability of the limitation ([4]).

## R – Rules

The court referenced several key provisions of the Road Accident Fund Act:

1. **Section 17(1)**: It establishes an unlimited claim against the Fund for loss or damage resulting from the death of or bodily injury to individuals caused by the negligence of a vehicle operator ([2]).

2. **Section 18(1)**: It limits claims for compensation to R25,000 for passengers being conveyed in or on the motor vehicle involved, under certain conditions, including operating for reward or while engaged in lawful business or employment ([2–3]).

3. **Section 20(1)**: This section provides that a person in control of a vehicle propelled by mechanical power is deemed to be its driver ([4]). In this case, it establishes that the driver of the tow truck, Lehmkuhl, was also deemed the driver of the tanker.

Case law such as *Wells and another v Shield Insurance Co Ltd* and *Santam Versekeringsmaatskappy Bpk v Kemp* was cited to support the interpretations of "driver" and the notion of being "conveyed in or on" a motor vehicle ([4], [5]).

## A – Application

The court applied the definitions and statutory provisions to the facts of the case. It determined that since the tow truck driver, Lehmkuhl, had control over the tanker at the time of the incident, he was deemed to be driving both vehicles under section 20(1). Therefore, the court concluded that Mkhize was being "conveyed" by Lehmkuhl, regardless of whether he was on the tow truck or the tanker at the time of the accident ([4]–[8]).

Importantly, the judgment affirmed that Mkhize was not a “social passenger.” He was an employee of the owner of the truck, which means he could fall under the categories specified in section 18(1)(a) and potentially limit his claim to R25,000 ([9]). The court found no merit in Mkhize’s argument that he was entitled to the unlimited claim under section 17(1) given the provisions of section 18(1) applied to his situation ([8]–[9]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the limitation on the claim under section 18(1) of the Road Accident Fund Act is applicable in this case. The appeal by the Road Accident Fund was successful, and the original order of the court below was replaced with a declaration that Mkhize's claim is limited to R25,000. He was also ordered to pay the costs of the appeal ([10]).

The ratio decidendi emphasizes the interpretation of statutory provisions regarding passenger claims and the deemed driving status of individuals controlling multiple vehicles, demonstrating the court's commitment to upholding statutory limitations within the context of passenger injuries in road accidents. This case serves as a crucial precedent in defining the parameters of liability and compensation in the realm of the Road Accident Fund, reinforcing the distinction between unlimited and limited claims as articulated in the Act.
