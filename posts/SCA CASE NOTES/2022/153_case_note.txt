Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

### FIRAC Case Note: CSARS v The Thistle Trust (516/2021) [2022] ZASCA 153

**F – Facts**  
The present case involves an appeal from a decision by the Gauteng Tax Court (the Tax Court) regarding an additional assessment raised against the Thistle Trust by the South African Revenue Service (SARS). The Thistle Trust is one of the beneficiaries of the Zenprop Group, which consists of various Tier 1 Trusts conducting property business. During the 2014, 2015, and 2016 tax periods, the Tier 1 Trusts disposed of certain capital assets, realising capital gains which were then distributed to the Thistle Trust. The Thistle Trust subsequently distributed these amounts to its beneficiaries within the same tax periods, treating them as taxable income for the beneficiaries ([paras 1–2]).

On 21 September 2018, SARS issued an additional assessment regarding these distributions, treating the gains as taxable income in the hands of the Thistle Trust rather than the beneficiaries. Furthermore, SARS imposed an understatement penalty and interest on this assessed liability ([paras 3–4]). The Thistle Trust objected to this assessment, arguing that under Section 25B of the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962 (the ITA) and paragraph 80(2) of the Eighth Schedule, the gains should be taxable in the hands of its beneficiaries. SARS rejected this objection, leading the Thistle Trust to appeal to the Tax Court ([para 5]) which ruled in favor of the Thistle Trust, setting aside the additional assessments. This prompted SARS to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) with the leave of the Tax Court.

**I – Issues**  
The legal questions presented in this appeal are twofold: 
1. Whether the capital gains from the disposal of capital assets by the Tier 1 Trusts are taxable in the hands of the Thistle Trust or in the hands of the beneficiaries to whom those gains were subsequently distributed ([para 6]).
2. If the capital gains are determined to be taxable in the Thistle Trust, whether SARS was justified in imposing the understatement penalty stemming from the Thistle Trust's tax treatment of the distributions ([para 6]).

**R – Rules**  
The applicable legal provisions in this case stem from the ITA and the Eighth Schedule, particularly:
- **Section 25B of the ITA**: Establishes the taxation framework for amounts accruing to trusts and beneficiaries, stipulating that only amounts derived for the benefit of ascertained beneficiaries with vested rights are considered taxable income. It further specifies exclusions for amounts of a capital nature ([para 9]).
- **Paragraph 80 of the Eighth Schedule**: Describes the tax implications for capital gains attributed to beneficiaries of a trust due to asset vesting. Specifically, it outlines that capital gains should be disregarded for trust calculations and attributed to beneficiaries when they have vested rights ([para 11]).

The foundational cases reviewed include:
- **CIR v Friedman**: Established that trusts were not legal entities and that taxable income was to be attributed to individuals ([para 7]).
- **Secretary for Inland Revenue v Rosen and Armstrong v Commissioner of Inland Revenue**: These cases elucidate the conduit-pipe principle, where funds passed through a trust to beneficiaries are considered as accruing to the beneficiaries ([para 25]).

**A – Application**  
In addressing the first issue, the SCA interpreted Sections 25B and 80 of the ITA cohesively. It concluded that Section 25B applies specifically to income and does not encompass capital gains, as demonstrated through the statutory context. The analysis highlighted that Section 25B was enacted before capital gains tax was legislated and was not intended to apply to such gains; thus, taxable income as defined does not include capital gains ([paras 19–20]).

For the second issue regarding the understatement penalty, the court considered the grounds on which SARS imposed the penalty. Initially, SARS contended that the Thistle Trust acted without reasonable grounds in its tax treatment. However, counsel for SARS conceded during the hearing that the Thistle Trust acted in good faith and erred inadvertently, having relied on a legal opinion regarding the applicable tax treatment, thereby qualifying for relief from the penalty originally imposed ([paras 27–29]).

**C – Conclusion**  
The SCA subsequently held that the capital gains accrued from the Tier 1 Trusts' disposals were indeed taxable in the hands of the Thistle Trust, not its beneficiaries, thereby reversing the Tax Court's ruling regarding the tax treatment of these distributions ([paras 26]). The SCA set aside the understatement penalty originally levied by SARS, acknowledging that the Thistle Trust’s misinterpretation of the relevant legal provisions was a bona fide error ([paras 31]).

### Implications  
This case underscores the importance of precise legal interpretations in tax law, particularly regarding the distinction between capital gains and regular income taxation. It also reaffirms the application of the conduit-pipe principle cautiously, recommending that each case be evaluated on its specific facts before determining its applicability. This ruling clarifies the taxation approach for trusts in South Africa, highlighting a crucial boundary between capital gains and income taxation within trust structures. 

The final order from the SCA resulted in the appeal by SARS being upheld in part, specifically regarding the substantiated tax assessments while rejecting the basis for the understatement penalty, thus remaining a significant precedent in South African tax jurisprudence.
