Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# Case Note: Sewnarain v S (877/12) [2013] ZASCA 27

**FIRAC Method**

## F – Facts
The matter before the Supreme Court of Appeal concerned an appeal against the refusal of bail following the appellant, Rajiv Sewnarain, being sentenced to life imprisonment for the murder of his wife. On 10 December 2010, Sewnarain’s wife was shot and killed, after which he confessed to a magistrate on 20 December 2010 that he had arranged for her murder. Subsequently, he pleaded guilty to the homicide and was sentenced on the same day ([paras 2-3]).

Approximately one year from his conviction, Sewnarain sought to have his guilty plea and sentence reviewed, claiming that his confession was not made freely and voluntarily due to influence from law enforcement and personal conditions affecting his mental state. He further alleged assault and duress during the interrogation process. To support his claims, he submitted expert witness reports ([para 3]).

The court a quo (High Court in Pietermaritzburg) accepted his application for the review to be heard with oral evidence, which indicated a bona fide dispute regarding his mental state at the time of the confession. Subsequently, Sewnarain applied for bail while awaiting the outcome of his review application, which was dismissed on 23 October 2012. His appeal against this refusal is the focus of this case ([paras 4-5]).

## I – Issues
The central legal question addressed by the court was whether Sewnarain could demonstrate "exceptional circumstances" that warranted his release on bail, as mandated by Section 60(11)(a) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977. Specifically, the court needed to ascertain whether his circumstances justified reconsideration of his bail status despite his life sentence and ongoing review ([paras 6-9]).

## R – Rules
Under Section 60(11)(a) of the Criminal Procedure Act, the court must consider whether exceptional circumstances exist that justify the granting of bail, particularly for matters concerning convictions involving serious offenses like murder. Previous cases establish that a reasonable prospect of success in an appeal or review does not automatically qualify a defendant for bail; rather, other compelling circumstances must also be shown ([para 8]).

The Founding Principles of South African Bail Law emphasize the high threshold for proving exceptional circumstances. A ruling from *S v Dlamini; S v Dladla; S v Janyani* (2000) highlighted that such circumstances are assessed on a case-by-case basis, requiring thorough factual substantiation by the applicant ([para 7]).

## A – Application
In assessing the appellant's claim of exceptional circumstances, the court noted that the only factor he presented was his alleged financial incapacity to retain legal counsel and expert witnesses, contingent upon his ability to restart his bus building and repair business, which had been shuttered since the incident ([para 9]). 

However, the court found this argument unconvincing due to its lack of factual basis. It noted that reestablishing the business would take significant time, effort, and money, including construction of appropriate facilities and undergoing bureaucratic processes for resuming operations. Furthermore, the court pointed out that Sewnarain possessed sufficient assets to undertake his legal proceedings without necessitating his immediate release ([para 10]). The appellant's claims regarding recovering debt owed to him and the value of stored equipment contradicted his argument for financial necessity, establishing that he was not without resources ([para 10]).

The court ultimately decided that the dismissal of his bail application was justified, as no compelling evidence was presented to demonstrate that the financial constraints constituted exceptional circumstances as defined by law ([para 11]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the decision of the court a quo and dismissed Sewnarain's appeal against the refusal of bail. The court reaffirmed that the appellant had failed to establish exceptional circumstances justifying his release, which aligns with established precedents regarding bail in serious criminal matters. The decision underscores the importance of substantiating claims for bail with factual evidence and emphasizes that mere potential for review success does not invoke an automatic right to bail. The implications of this case reinforce the threshold requirements for bail applications in South African law, especially for applicants with serious convictions ([paras 8-11]).
