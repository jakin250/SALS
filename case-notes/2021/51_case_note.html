Constitutional Court 2021

F – Facts:
The case involves the applicant, Jane Bwanya, who challenged the constitutionality of the definitions of "survivor" and "spouse" under the Maintenance of Surviving Spouses Act 27 of 1990 and the Intestate Succession Act 81 of 1987 ([1–10]). Bwanya was in a permanent life partnership with the deceased, Anthony Ruch, from 2014 until his death on 23 April 2016. Their relationship exhibited characteristics akin to marriage; they shared a home, had mutual financial support, and were preparing for marriage before Ruch's untimely death ([3–4],[7]). Subsequent to his death, Bwanya sought maintenance under the Maintenance of Surviving Spouses Act and inheritance under the Intestate Succession Act but was denied these claims based on the statutory definitions that excluded partners in permanent life partnerships. After a High Court settlement of R3 million, Bwanya continued to seek a declaration that certain sections of both Acts were unconstitutional ([10–11]).

I – Issues:
The principal legal issues in this case are:

  1. Whether the exclusion of permanent life partners from maintenance benefits under the Maintenance of Surviving Spouses Act constitutes unfair discrimination based on marital status ([1–11]).
  2. Whether the definition of "survivor" in the Intestate Succession Act is constitutionally valid if it excludes surviving partners of permanent life partnerships ([11–12]).

R – Rules:
The court primarily relied on sections 9 (equality) and 10 (dignity) of the South African Constitution and previous case law, notably Volks NO v Robinson, where the Court ruled that the exclusion of permanent life partners from the Act did not constitute unfair discrimination. The test for discrimination involves assessing if the exclusion limits rights in a way that suffers from unfairness ([1–2],[38],[39]).

A – Application:
In the application of these principles, the majority held that while the exclusion does constitute discrimination, it was not unfair in the context of South African society and its legal framework regarding marriage. The majority noted the historical and legal significance of marriage as a recognized institution and that reciprocal duties of support enshrined in marriage do not extend to life partnerships by operation of law. The judgment drew heavily on the principles articulated in Volks, suggesting that while reforms are necessary for protecting vulnerable individuals in permanent life partnerships, the current legal structure appropriately differentiates between marriage and other intimate relationships due to the societal implications and expectations associated with the institution of marriage ([36–56],[74–120]).

In contrast, Justice Madlanga, in the dissenting opinion, argued that the existing definitions were outdated and that the law must evolve to reflect changing societal structures, given that many South Africans live in permanent life partnerships akin to marriage ([151–201]). He asserted that those in such partnerships who undertake mutual obligations should not be left without protections against financial destitution upon the death of a partner.

C – Conclusion:
The Constitutional Court ultimately confirmed the High Court's declaration that certain definitions within the Maintenance of Surviving Spouses Act and Intestate Succession Act are unconstitutional. It ruled the omissions from these definitions discriminatory against surviving partners in life partnerships. This ruling has immediate implications for those in such relationships by necessitating legislative reform to recognize and protect their rights. The court suspended the implementation of this declaration for 18 months to allow Parliament time to enact the necessary changes ([1–12]).

This case highlights the evolving nature of family law in South Africa, emphasizing the balancing act between historical legal structures and modern societal realities regarding intimate relationships.
