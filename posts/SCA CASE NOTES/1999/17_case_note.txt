Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 1999

# FIRAC Case Note: **Engen Petroleum Limited & Others v Commissioner for Customs and Excise & Another** (Case No: 171/97)

## F – Facts
The case concerns the rebate of excise duty and fuel levy applicable to distillate fuel supplied to foreign fishing vessels. Relevant procedural history begins with an amendment made in October 1993 to Schedule 6 of the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 ([1–2]). Historically, rebates were allowed for fuel supplied to foreign-going ships; however, the amendment extended these rebates to fishing vessels not recognized as South African nationals under the Merchant Shipping Act, 1951 ([2]).

The appellants—Engen Petroleum Limited, BP Southern Africa (Pty) Limited, Caltex Oil SA (Pty) Limited, and Shell Oil SA (Pty) Limited—supplied diesel oil to Namibian-registered fishing vessels. Initially, these supplies were not eligible for rebates. Nevertheless, after the amendment came into force, the appellants claimed these rebates under items 603.02.01 and 640.06 ([3]).

The first respondent, Commissioner for Customs and Excise, contested this claim, leading the appellants to seek relief in the Transvaal Provincial Division. The unopposed aspects of their claim were granted, whereas the further claims were rejected, prompting the appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([4]).

## I – Issues
The primary issue was whether the appellants were entitled to rebates of excise duty and fuel levy on distillate fuel supplied to foreign fishing vessels that were non-South African in nationality, particularly focusing on provision 1.1(b)(i) of the amended notice of motion. Specifically, the dispute revolved around the interpretation and application of the relevant rebate items in Schedule 6 of the Act, their requirements, and intended scope ([5–6]).

## R – Rules
The legal framework is grounded in the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964. Key provisions include Section 20(4), which governs the removal of goods from customs and excise warehouses and distinguishes between home consumption and export, and Section 75, which allows rebates subject to specifics outlined in Schedule 6 ([6–9]). 

Notably, rebate items 603.02.01 and 640.06 specify that rebates are applicable to excisable goods supplied as stores to foreign fishing vessels not recognized as South African vessels. The Court noted confusion stemming from the interpretive clarification of these statutory provisions, specifically attributing significance to distinctions drawn between excise duty and export duty ([10–13]).

The Court emphasized that rebates for exported goods clash with the regulatory framework governing excise duty. The sections clearly stipulate that rebates are merely for goods entered for home consumption, not exports ([12–13]).

## A – Application
The Court's analysis highlighted that the justification offered by the respondents—that the rebate applications pertained solely to exports—did not align with statutory provisions. The confusion had arisen from the notation of rebates in Schedule 6 alongside provisions for exports. The majority opinion emphasized that Items 603.02.01 and 640.06 should adhere to the overarching principle of the Act, which stipulates that such rebates can only be granted on goods intended for home consumption ([16]).

The respondents relied on the notion that the amendment was intended to clarify uncertainties regarding foreign fishing vessels and restrict unfair advantages over South African vessels. However, this reasoning lacked sufficient evidence to support the position that the rebate was solely relevant to a particular category of vessels ([15–16]). The Court asserted that the ambiguous intention behind legislative amendments shouldn't negate the core principles established within the Act that govern rebates ([14–15]).

Due to the clear distinction incorporated into the framework of the Act, the court concluded that the appellants were wrongly denied rebates based on the misconstrued assumption that their focus was on exported goods. The court’s interpretation reconciled the language of the relevant legislative provisions, establishing that the appellants were indeed entitled to the rebates sought under the clarified interpretation of items 603.02.01 and 640.06 ([17]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the appellants, concluding that they were entitled to rebates under rebate items 603.02.01 and 640.06 for distillate fuel supplied to fishing vessels not recognized as South African ships, irrespective of the use of that fuel for catching fish intended for return to South Africa ([18]). 

This decision underscores the importance of clear statutory interpretation and highlights the necessity of aligning administrative decisions with legislative intent. The ruling also has implications for how customs and excise law applies to various categories of vessels, ensuring equitable treatment under the law. The appellants were awarded costs, confirming the Court's stance on their entitlement to the rebates ([18]).
