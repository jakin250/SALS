Source Folder: 2001

# FIRAC Case Note: Grace Chrissie Nkosi and Joseph Mandla Nkosi v Gideon Wilhelmus Bührmann

### F - Facts
The case at hand, **Nkosi v Bührmann**, centers around a dispute involving the burial of Petros Nkosi, the son of the first appellant, Grace Chrissie Nkosi, on the farm owned by the respondent, Gideon Wilhelm Bührmann. The appellants assert that, as occupants of the land under the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 ("the Act"), they have the right to bury their family members on the property where they reside and have historically buried other family members ([1]–[5]).

Grace Nkosi and her family first occupied the respondent's farm in 1966 under the ownership of Bührmann’s late father, who designated an area on the farm for family burials—a claim supported by cultural and religious practices that view such sites as spiritually significant ([5]–[7]). After working for the respondent from 1970 until 1981, the family left but returned in 1987. Despite having buried multiple family members previously with apparent consent, the respondent now denies permission for the burial of Petros Nkosi ([8]–[11]). The High Court initially ruled in favor of the appellants, affirming their right based on an unregistered servitude claimed to have been granted by Bührmann’s father. However, on appeal, the majority ruled against it, which led to the current appeal ([12]–[14]).

### I - Issues
The key legal questions presented before the Supreme Court of Appeal include:

1. Does the appellant, as an occupier under the Act, have the right to bury a family member on the owner’s land without the owner's consent?
2. How do the rights to religious freedom and the property rights of landowners intersect in this context?
3. What is the applicability of sections 5(d) and 6(1) of the Act with respect to the claimed burial right? ([1]–[14], [15]–[24]).

### R - Rules
The relevant legal framework includes the **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996**, which guarantees the rights to human dignity (s 10), freedom of religion (s 15), and property rights (s 25). The provisions of the **Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997** govern the relationship and rights of occupiers and landowners. Specifically, section 5(d) articulates the right to freedom of religion, while section 6 defines the rights of occupiers, emphasizing consent and the owner's fiduciary powers over their land ([15]–[30]). 

Further, the Act underscores that rights of occupiers are subject to "limitations which are reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society" ([17]), and rights may not be exercised in a manner that dispossesses a landowner without compensation or lawful authority ([16]).

### A - Application
The Court analyzed the interactive provisions of the Act and the Constitution in determining the appellants' claim. The majority opinion found that the assertion of a right to bury a family member on the land without owner consent constituted an infringement of the owner's property rights ([49]–[50]). Although the appellants argued that the act of burial is intrinsically linked to religious and cultural practices, the Court maintained that such rights do not grant individuals the liberty to despoil another's land without consent or legal basis ([27]–[36]).

Arguments were made for a broader interpretation of the term "use" under section 6(1) of the Act to include burial practices; however, the majority found that this interpretation would imply an unjustifiable limitation on the landowner's rights ([40]–[55]). The burden of proof regarding the history of consent was also addressed, revealing deficiencies in the appellants' argument; they could not demonstrate that the respondent's father had established an unbroken agreement permitting burials without permission ([29]–[30]).

In terms of balancing rights, the Court highlighted that religious practices did not, under the current statutory framework, construe an automatic entitlement to expropriate land for burials ([46]–[49]). The majority further concluded that establishing additional gravesites would substantially diminish the landowner's rights and could not be permitted without formal consent ([49]–[55]).

### C - Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal ruled against the appellants, affirming the Full Court's judgment that the appellant had no inherent right, as an occupier, to bury their deceased family member on the respondent's farm without explicit consent from the landowner. The case underlines the delicate balancing act between the constitutional rights of individuals and the property rights of landowners, emphasizing that while freedom of religion is protected, it does not extend to encroachment on another's property rights without lawful authority ([58]). 

This holding reiterates the principles of property law as established under the Act and the Constitution, stressing the need for consent in matters involving land use and respect for ownership rights. The appeal was dismissed with costs, and a directive was issued to address outdated municipal regulations concerning burials in the area ([58]).
