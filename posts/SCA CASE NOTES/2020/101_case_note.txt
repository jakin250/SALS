Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

### Case Note: Moodliar and Others v Recycling and Economic Development Initiative of South Africa NPC (Case No: 977/2019) [2020] ZASCA 101

#### F – Facts
In the consolidated appeals before the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa, the plaintiffs, Sivalutchmee Moodliar, Trevor Philip Glaum, and Keitumetse Taunyane (the "Liquidators"), appealed against the orders of the Western Cape Division of the High Court which directed the return of funds held in trust by Bowman Gilfillan attorneys back to the companies, Recycling and Economic Development Initiative of South Africa NPC ("Redisa") and Kusaga Taka Consulting (Pty) Ltd ("KTC") ([1-6]).

The Minister of Environmental Affairs obtained provisional winding-up orders against Redisa and KTC, alleging misappropriation of public funds by undisclosed relationships between the companies' directors and KTC. Upon these companies being placed under provisional liquidation in June 2017, the Liquidators were appointed, gaining control over the companies' assets, including significant cash reserves ([3-4]). Following further court proceedings, the final winding-up orders were set aside, and both companies were discharged from liquidation on January 24, 2019 ([6]). 

Prior to the appeal hearing, the Liquidators transferred funds into the trust account of Bowman Gilfillan, amounting to R20 million from Redisa and R2 million from KTC, purportedly to cover their anticipated fees. Upon discharge from liquidation, the Liquidators sought to retain these funds pending the resolution of their remuneration, while the companies sought the return of the funds ([8-10]).

#### I – Issues
The core legal issue in this matter revolves around whether the former Liquidators were entitled to retain funds for payment of their fees upon discharge from provisional liquidation. Specifically, the questions raised include:
1. Were the Liquidators entitled to withhold payment of their fees until their remuneration was confirmed through taxation?
2. Did the liquidation legal framework permit the Liquidators to retain control of company assets to secure their claims for remuneration after discharge from liquidation? ([1-2], [10-12]).

#### R – Rules
The legal rules governing this case are grounded in the provisions of the Companies Act 61 of 1973, specifically:
- **Section 342**, concerning the application of assets during winding-up.
- **Section 384**, detailing the entitlement of liquidators to remuneration, which must be taxed and confirmed.
- **Regulations**, particularly Regulation 24 and Annexure CM104, providing guidance on the conduct of liquidators in relation to their remuneration and asset management during liquidation ([12-15]).

Precedent cases cited include:
- **In re Insolvent Estate Paruk**: Upholding the premise that a liquidator cannot retain fees before they are taxed and confirmed.
- **Van Eck v Meyer**: Confirming that upon discharge from liquidation, assets must be returned to the insolvents.
- **Rose v Kemp**: Establishing that the duty to account for assets continues post-discharge, with asset control reverting to the original entities ([17-20]).

#### A – Application
The Court, in applying the applicable law to the facts, noted that the common law principle preventing trustees from claiming remuneration before the taxation and confirmation of their accounts applies to liquidators. The salient point made by the Court was that as the companies were discharged from liquidation, the power of Liquidators ceased, and they were required to return control of all company assets, including the funds held in Bowman's trust account, to the companies ([28-30]).

Despite the Liquidators' assertion of their entitlement to retain funds to secure their future claims for remuneration, the Court found no legal basis supporting this stance. The Liquidators' argument, reliant on securing their remuneration via retained assets pending confirmation, directly contradicted the clear provisions of the Companies Act and the Regulations, which necessitate full restitution of assets post-discharge ([32-33]).

The Court further examined the implications of the prior cases cited by the Liquidators, concluding that they did not provide legal grounds for withholding the funds as claimed. Instead, the interpretations and principles emerging from these cases reinforced the position that the Liquidators must complete the winding-up process and return all assets to the respective companies, thereby affirming the High Court’s ruling ([28-30]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court ruled that the Liquidators were not entitled to retain control of the disputed funds following the discharge of the winding-up order. Consequently, the appeal was dismissed with costs, and the order of the court below was amended to reflect the proper return of the funds to Redisa and KTC. The Court enshrined the principle that liquidators may not withhold assets or funds from companies post-liquidation for the purpose of securing remuneration until properly taxed and confirmed ([35-36]). 

This case clarifies the limitations on liquidators' rights post-discharge and emphasizes the necessity for adherence to procedural statutes regarding asset management, thereby ensuring accountability and protecting the interests of creditors and stakeholders within the corporate structure.
