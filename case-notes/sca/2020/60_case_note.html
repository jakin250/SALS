Case Note – FIRAC for Fraai Uitzicht 1798 Farm (Pty) Ltd v McCullough and Others (118/2019) [2020] ZASCA 60

F – Facts

The case centers on an appeal against a decision made by the Western Cape Division of the High Court regarding the rescission of a court order granting a right of way of necessity through Fraai Uitzicht 1798 Farm (Pty) Ltd (Appellant) for the benefit of three landlocked properties owned by the First to Third Respondents ([1]–[2]). Initially, in November 2011, a court order was issued permitting the Respondents access via a disputed road traversing the Appellant's property after they had sought urgent relief following flooding that damaged a vital bridge ([4]).

The dispute arose from claims that the rights granted were induced by fraudulent misrepresentations made by the Respondents, particularly regarding a deproclamation request by the former owner of Kranskop. The Appellant alleged that crucial documents were initially missing but later discovered, revealing an inherent fraud within the representations made to the court at the time of the 2011 order ([5]–[11]). Various vital documents were claimed to have come to light post-judgment, prompting the Appellant to seek rescission based on fraud or justus error ([11]–[12]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the court revolved around:

  1. The circumstances under which a judgment can be rescinded on the grounds of fraud, or alternatively, justus error ([1]–[2]).
  2. Whether there was sufficient evidence to establish that the Respondents were aware of fraudulent misrepresentation when they initiated the earlier application ([14]).
  3. The legal question of whether said misrepresentations, if established, would have altered the original court's decision had they been known ([19]).

R – Rules

In assessing the grounds for rescission due to fraud, the court cited well-established principles that necessitate proof of three requirements ([16]):

  1. Incorrect evidence was presented at the original trial.
  2. Such evidence was provided fraudulently, intended to mislead the court.
  3. The misrepresentation had such a divergence from the truth that it would have likely resulted in a different judgment had the court been aware of the true facts.

Sound legal precedent was maintained through references to Childerley Estate Stores v Standard Bank of SA Ltd (1924), which continues to influence perspectives on judgments recovered through fraud ([16]).

Conversely, for justus error, particularly regarding newly discovered documents, the court reiterated that rescission can only be sought in exceptional circumstances, as established in prior rulings, such as those of Moraitis Investments (Pty) Ltd v Montic Diary (Pty) Ltd ([20]–[21]).

A – Application

The court's analysis focused significantly on the evidence presented. It examined whether the newly found documents substantiated the Appellant's claims of deceit and whether such claims established the necessary fraud to rescind the earlier order. The findings demonstrated a clear disconnect between the alleged misrepresentations and the Respondents’ alleged knowledge or complicity in any fraudulent conduct ([14],[18]).

In rejecting the Appellant’s claim, the court emphasized that the Respondents acted on information provided by Mr T D Smit, a witness whose reliability was questioned but whose credibility alone could not implicate the Respondents in any wrongdoing or fraud ([18]).

The court also scrutinized the importance of the newly revealed documents, concluding that even if they had been presented at the initial hearing, they would not have materially affected the outcome, as the disputed road was the most feasible access for the landlocked properties ([19]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, asserting that the Appellant failed to demonstrate that the trial court's previous judgment would have changed even with the knowledge of the alleged fraud or based on the existence of new documents ([24]–[25]). The court reiterated the principle that fraud must be established beyond mere assertion and that successful claims of justus error require significant evidence of impact on judgment, which the Appellant did not furnish.

The decision underscores the high burden of proof necessary to rescind judgments in South African law, particularly emphasizing the evidentiary standards regarding fraud and mistake. Consequently, the ruling affirms the original judicial determination, highlighting the resilience of rights of way engaged through necessity, even amidst claims of misrepresentation and error in procedural contexts. The order to dismiss the appeal was accompanied by costs awarded against the Appellant, indicating that the court viewed the pursuit of rescission as lacking a substantive basis ([25]).
