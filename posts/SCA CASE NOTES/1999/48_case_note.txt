Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 1999

# FIRAC Case Note: Premier Trading Company (Pty) Limited v Sportopia (Pty) Ltd

**Case Title:** Premier Trading Company (Pty) Limited v Sportopia (Pty) Ltd  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Case No:** 218/97  
**Date of Judgment:** 1 June 1999  
**Judges:** Nienaber JA, Hefer JA, Schutz JA, Plewman JA, Farlam AJA  

### F - Facts

The case revolves around a dispute regarding the passing-off of a trademark and the rights of two competing companies in the in-line roller skate market ([1]–[10]).

1. **Parties Involved:**
   - Premier Trading Company (Pty) Ltd (First Appellant)
   - GDK Sports (Pty) Ltd (Second Appellant)
   - Sportopia (Pty) Ltd (Respondent)

2. **Background:**
   - Premier Trading (the appellant) applied for an interdict to prevent Sportopia (the respondent) from using the trademark "Bladeline" for roller skates, claiming this led to product confusion and undermined their business reputation ([1]–[10]).
   - The appellants contend that they have been in the market since 1993, having acquired prior rights via Jokari, a company that sold roller skates, which included the name "Bladeline." They argue that they used the trademark before Sportopia's entry into the market in December 1994 ([2]–[9]).
   - The respondent obtained a distribution agreement with the original trademark holder, Skateworks, for exclusive rights to the name "Bladeline" in South Africa ([3]).

3. **Nature of the Products:**
   - The products in question are in-line roller skates, which differ from traditional roller skates by having wheels arranged in a line. The appellants sold Taiwanese-manufactured skates, while the respondent sold Australian-manufactured ones ([1]–[5]).

4. **Procedural History:**
   - The initial application for an interdict was dismissed, leading to this appeal ([8]–[10]). The second appellant's acquisition of certain assets and rights further complicated the case as it shifted the litigation to involve multiple parties ([10]).

### I - Issues

1. **Reputation and Rights:**  
   Did the appellants, at the relevant time and place, possess sufficient reputation in the trademark "Bladeline" to substantiate their claim of passing-off against the respondent ([14]).

2. **Confusion:**  
Did the respondent’s use of the trademark and associated branding create a likelihood of confusion among consumers, thereby supporting a claim of passing-off ([14]–[25])?

### R - Rules

1. **Passing-Off Definition:**  
Passing-off is a legal wrong involving a false representation made by one trader (the defendant) that leads consumers to confuse goods of one trader with those of another (the plaintiff) ([11]–[12]).

2. **Requirements for Passing-Off:**
   - The plaintiff must demonstrate their own reputation regarding the trademark.
   - There must be evidence of consumer confusion or deception regarding the goods ([12]–[14]).

3. **Relevant Case Law:**
   - The court referenced multiple precedents concerning passing-off, including *Caterham Car Sales & Coachworks Ltd v Birkin Cars (Pty) Ltd and Another* and *Reckitt & Colman SA (Pty) Ltd v S C Johnson & Sons SA (Pty) Ltd*, which underscored the necessity of established reputation and the likelihood of deception ([12]–[13]).

### A - Application

1. **Lack of Established Reputation:**
   - The appellants failed to produce compelling evidence of their established reputation in the "Bladeline" mark at the relevant time in Durban, where the respondent launched its product ([15]–[29]).
   - Evidence was largely based on nationwide sales data but did not demonstrate localized brand recognition or customer familiarity with "Bladeline" in Durban, suggested to be crucial given the nature of passing-off claims ([15]–[29]).

2. **Consumer Confusion:**
   - The court assessed the design and marketing differences between the products, indicating significant distinctions that would likely prevent consumer confusion ([31]–[32]).
   - The design of the products and the branding strategies employed by both parties pointed to limited overlap in the consumer marketplace, especially given the discerning nature of the target demographic ([31]).

3. **Role of the Word Marks:**
   - The appellants argued that the conjoined use of "Jokari" and "Bladeline" should be considered as a composite mark. The court found this proposition unproven and noted that the perception of "Bladeline" differed substantially depending on whether presented alongside "Jokari" or marketed independently ([26]–[27]).

### C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, concluding that the appellants had not successfully established the requisite reputation in "Bladeline" to support their passing-off claim. Additionally, there was insufficient evidence to demonstrate that Sportopia's use of the trademark created a likelihood of confusion among consumers ([30]–[32]).

**Order:** The appeal was dismissed with costs, including those incurred for the use of two counsel. The implications of the judgment suggest a stringent approach to trademark disputes where the evidentiary burden remains squarely on the claimant to establish both reputation and consumer confusion ([32]).

This judgment reinforces the significant threshold that must be met for establishing passing-off claims in South Africa, specifically emphasizing the need for clear evidence of consumer recognition and confusion in the marketplace.
