# Case Note: Shaik v The State (1) [2006] SCA 134 (RSA)

---

## F – Facts

**Introduction to the Case**  
The appeal before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) derives from a lengthy criminal trial in the Durban High Court, presided over by Judge Squires and assessors. The trial involved Schabir Shaik, a businessman, and several corporate entities under his control, charged with various offenses related to corruption and fraud allegedly linked to then Deputy President Jacob Zuma ([1]).

**Background**  
Between October 1995 and September 2002, Shaik and the corporate appellants made multiple payments to Zuma, totalling approximately R1.2 million. The payments were alleged to have been made to influence Zuma in various official capacities, capitalizing on his political influence to benefit Shaik's business ventures ([2]).

**The Charges**  
The indictment included the following:
1. **Corruption**: Under section 1(1)(a)(i) and (ii) of the Corruption Act 94 of 1992 (CA), for alleged payments intended to corruptly influence Zuma.
2. **Fraud**: Concerns the irregular write-off of R1,282,027.63 in the annual financial statements of the Nkobi group ([5]).
3. **Bribery and Conspiracy**: Related to the alleged bribery to secure benefits from government contracts ([4], [6]).

**Trial Court Findings**  
The trial court concluded that there was a "corrupt relationship" between Shaik and Zuma characterized by payments made to influence Zuma's performance of his duties ([27]). The trial judge found Shaik and several corporate entities guilty on multiple counts and sentenced Shaik to 15 years’ imprisonment for each of the counts of corruption and bribery, coupled with various financial penalties on corporate appellants ([49]).

## I – Issues

The case raised several pivotal legal issues:
1. Whether Shaik and the corporate appellants had indeed acted with corrupt intent as required by the CA.
2. Whether the trial court's admission of the 'encrypted fax' was proper under the evidential rules, particularly concerning hearsay evidence and its implications on due process ([60]).
3. Whether Shaik's actions constituted fraud concerning the financial misreporting of the Nkobi group ([38]).
4. The appropriateness of the sentences imposed on Shaik and the corporate appellants, especially in light of the minimum sentence provisions ([49]).

## R – Rules

**Legal Framework**  
1. **Corruption Act 94 of 1992**: Specifically, section 1(1)(a)(i) and (ii), which criminalizes offering benefits to influence public officials ([64]).
2. **Law of Evidence Amendment Act 45 of 1988**: This outlines exceptions to hearsay rules, notably, section 3 which permits hearsay evidence if it serves the interests of justice ([180]).
3. **Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (POCA)**: Relevant for count 3 concerning the proceeds of corrupt activities ([208]).

**Judicial Precedent**  
- In *S v Malgas*, the SCA discussed the prescribed minimum sentences and the need to justify departures from these standards ([226]).
- The admissibility of co-conspirators' statements was previously established in cases like *R v Miller* and *R v Mayet*, guiding the court's approach to statements made in furtherance of a common purpose ([168]).

## A – Application

**Corruption and Intent**  
The court assessed the cumulative evidence against Shaik and the corporate entities, determining that the payments made to Zuma were directly linked to the corrupt intent to leverage political influence for business advantage. The court emphasized that the intention underpinning the payments was a key aspect of the legislation, despite claims from Shaik that the payments were made out of friendship or were intended as loans ([75], [132]).

**Admissibility of Evidence**  
The trial court's ruling on the encrypted fax was examined under the criteria set forth in the Law of Evidence Amendment Act. The court found that the fax's content supported the State’s allegations and therefore justified its admission. The claim by the appellants that the evidence was prejudicial due to the lack of cross-examination opportunities was rejected, as the court found the evidence highly relevant and necessary for establishing intent ([170]).

**Fraudulent Misrepresentation**  
The examination of the Nkobi group’s financial statements revealed irregularities that directly linked Shaik to the fraud charges. The trial court supported this claim based on circumstantial evidence, including documentation and testimonies supporting the assertion that Shaik was complicit in the outcome of the misleading financial disclosures ([139], [166]).

**Sentencing**  
The court affirmed the trial judge’s sentences, considering the gravity of the offenses and the necessity of deterring similar crimes. The inconsistencies presented in Shaik's testimonies further highlighted the calculated nature of his fraudulent actions. The public's interest in punishing high-level corruption, especially involving a senior political figure, warranted the maximum sentences as outlined in the applicable statutes ([224], [228]).

## C – Conclusion

The SCA upheld the convictions and sentences handed down by the trial court, emphasizing the serious nature of the offenses involving public trust and corruption. The court affirmed the probative value of the evidence presented, including the encrypted fax, and established that substantial, compelling reasons did not exist to deviate from the minimum sentencing provisions ([231]). The implications of this case underscore the judiciary's commitment to addressing corruption in South Africa’s democratic landscape while ensuring the integrity of public officials remains intact.
