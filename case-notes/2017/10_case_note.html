Hotz and Others v University of Cape Town [2017] ZACC 10

Constitutional Court 2017 [2017] ZACC 10

F – Facts

In an urgent application brought on 12 April 2017, the Constitutional Court of South Africa deliberated on the background of the "#FeesMustFall" movement, which had garnered national attention due to widespread student protests across various South African universities, including the University of Cape Town (UCT). This movement sought the realization of a demand for free education ([1]). The applicants, comprising five affiliated students involved in a protest dubbed “Shackville,” erected unauthorised structures on campus to highlight issues regarding fees and accommodation, which disproportionately affected black students ([2], [5]).

The University responded by obtaining a final interdict from the High Court against the applicants, prohibiting them from entering university premises and participating in unlawful actions ([6]). This order mandated the applicants to jointly pay UCT’s legal costs, confirmed by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ([7]). The applicants subsequently appealed on the grounds that the High Court and SCA had improperly exercised their discretion in awarding costs against them ([10], [11]).

I – Issues

The primary issues for determination included:

  1. Whether the urgent application’s circumstances warranted judicial intervention regarding cost awards following the High Court’s final interdict.
  2. The legitimacy and appropriateness of the SCA’s endorsement of the High Court's costs order.
  3. Whether the applicants could introduce further evidence via a replying affidavit ([4]).

R – Rules

The Constitutional Court operated under the principles of constitutional law as stipulated in Section 29(1) of the Constitution, which guarantees the right to further education, and Section 17, which affirms the right to assemble, demonstrate, and petition peacefully ([14], [30]).

The precedential framework encompassed:

  • The Biowatch principle, which states that unsuccessful litigants in constitutional matters should not generally be liable for costs against the state to avoid a chilling effect on the enforcement of rights ([21]).
  • The aspects of judicial discretion in cost awards outlined in Trencon Construction (Pty) Ltd v Industrial Development Corporation of South Africa and the necessity for such discretion to be exercised judiciously ([25], [26]).
  • The Affordable Medicines Trust v Minister of Health case, which reinforced the notion that costs should reflect considerations related to the promotion of constitutional justice ([24]).

A – Application

The Court scrutinized the lower courts' decisions, recognizing the delicate balance between safeguarding constitutional rights and responding to unlawful conduct. While acknowledging the applicants’ engagement in social justice protests, the Court emphasized that such engagement does not excuse unlawful behavior, particularly when property damage and violence ensue during protests ([32]).

The High Court’s costs order was described as excessively broad, effectively sidelining the applicants from university premises, thereby infringing on their freedom of movement and association ([39]). This excessive breadth indicated that the High Court had failed to exercise its discretion judiciously by not accounting for the constitutional implications of its order ([35]).

In reviewing the merits, the Court found the SCA's affirmation of the High Court's costs order lacked sufficient reasoned analysis, particularly given that the costs could disproportionately impact students hoping to assert their constitutional rights ([38]). The reversal of the costs order was deemed necessary to uphold the very essence of constitutional jurisprudence and prevent the chilling effects that unjust costs awards could impose ([40]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately set aside the costs order from the High Court as confirmed by the SCA, with each party to bear their own legal costs. This ruling reflected critical reasoning that, although the applicants engaged in unlawful actions during their protests, their broader social justice efforts warranted special consideration in the context of constitutional rights.

The decision underscored that constitutional litigants, even those who face adverse outcomes, should remain shielded from the financial burdens that could inhibit their ability to seek redress for legitimate grievances ([42]). This ruling contributes significantly to the evolving landscape of constitutional law in South Africa, articulating a delicate balance between protecting public order during lawful protests and recognizing the imperative of access to justice for those challenging institutional injustices.
