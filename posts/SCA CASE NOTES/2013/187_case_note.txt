Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: Kingswood Golf Estate (Pty) Ltd v Witts-Hewinson & others (223/13) [2013] ZASCA 187

## F – Facts  
The case concerns a dispute arising from a deed of sale between Kingswood Golf Estate (Pty) Ltd (the appellant) and Jonathan Mark Witts-Hewinson and Michelle Susan Witts-Hewinson (the first and second respondents, respectively). On 10 March 2004, the respondents purchased property at Kingswood Golf Estate, relying on representations made by the appellant regarding the development of an upscale clubhouse ([1], [3]). An addendum attached to the deed of sale included clause 1.2.4, stipulating that the appellant would construct and furnish a clubhouse “duly furnished in accordance with the upmarket quality and nature of the proposed development.” 

Subsequently, a post-contractual newsletter issued in November 2004 outlined the envisioned clubhouse features ([4], [11]). The appellant constructed a clubhouse at the Gatehouse Complex, which the respondents contended did not meet the specifications agreed upon in clause 1.2.4. The respondents alleged that the clubhouse, built outside the terms of their agreement, constituted a breach, initiating legal proceedings against the appellant ([5], [6]).

The Western Cape High Court ruled in favor of the respondents, ordering the appellant to construct the clubhouse in accordance with the newsletter's specifications and awarded costs against the appellant ([2], [28]). The appellant appealed this decision ([2]).

## I – Issues
The key legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal involved:

1. Whether clause 1.2.4 of the addendum was so vague as to be void for vagueness and non-enforceable ([10], [13]).
2. Whether post-contractual documents, specifically the November 2004 newsletter, could be admitted as extrinsic evidence to clarify the vague terms of this clause ([12], [21]).
3. Whether the court had erred in ordering specific performance based on the contents of the newsletter and in interpreting the terms of the agreement ([20], [28]).

## R – Rules
The primary rules relevant to this case emanate from contract law principles, particularly regarding:

- **Vagueness and Enforceability**: A contract's terms must be sufficiently clear for enforceability; vague clauses may be deemed void ([11]).
- **Parol Evidence Rule**: This rule posits that when a contract is memorialized in writing, its terms cannot be contradicted, altered, added to, or varied by oral or extrinsic evidence ([21]).
- **Integration Doctrine**: A written contract is considered the final and complete expression of the parties' agreement, preventing the introduction of external documents to modify or clarify the contract's terms ([22]).
  
The distinction between "vague" clauses and those that provide a degree of discretion in performance was also considered, guided by the principle of **arbitrio boni viri**, which allows for discretion in executing contractual obligations, provided that such discretion is expressly granted ([24]).

## A – Application
The court conducted a thorough analysis of the contractual parameters defined by clause 1.2.4. It concluded that the clause lacked substantive detail regarding what constituted the clubhouse, failing to delineate any specific requirements or characteristics that could guide the parties' performance ([11], [16]). Consequently, the vagueness rendered the clause potentially unenforceable.

Regarding the­ post-contractual newsletter, the court clarified that its inclusion would contravene the parol evidence rule, which precludes the introduction of documents that are not signed and part of the original contract ([21], [22]). The court further stated that the newsletter's acceptance by the lower court constituted an error, as it could not legally modify the contract terms per the established legal framework.

Moreover, the respondents' argument that clause 1.2.4 provided the appellant with a “fettered discretion” to determine what constituted an appropriate clubhouse was dismissed. The court found that no such discretion had been explicitly granted in the original agreement, thus reinforcing that the appellant was bound by the contract's terms as written ([25]).

As for the specifics of the clubhouse's construction in the Gatehouse Complex, the court noted a lack of empirical evidence demonstrating that the existing facilities met the nebulous expectations set forth in the addendum ([26]). Consequently, the court indicated that it would not be feasible to monitor or enforce an order of specific performance awarded to the respondents, as it remained unclear what specifications were mandated within the terms of the contract ([28]).

## C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the lower court's decision, upholding the appellant’s claims regarding the vagueness of clause 1.2.4 and the inadmissibility of the November newsletter as extrinsic evidence ([28]). The court concluded that the lower court erred in invoking the newsletter to impose obligations not supported by the written deed of sale and in assuming that the appellant possessed discretionary latitude that was not contractually sanctioned.

The appeal was accordingly upheld, with the respondents ordered to bear the costs of the appeal. The implications of this verdict reaffirm the doctrine that written contracts must stand as exclusive evidence of the terms mutually agreed upon, emphasizing the necessity for clarity in contractual obligations and the inadmissibility of extrinsic materials as supportive documentation for ambiguities within a formal agreement ([28]).
