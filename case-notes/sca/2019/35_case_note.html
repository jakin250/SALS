Segwati v The State (620/2018) [2019] ZASCA 35

In Segwati v The State ([1]), Ishmael Blessing Segwati (the appellant) was convicted by the Regional Court on charges of theft and attempted theft.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 35

F – Facts

In Segwati v The State ([1]), Ishmael Blessing Segwati (the appellant) was convicted by the Regional Court on charges of theft and attempted theft. The offences were committed against his employer, the Emperor’s Palace Casino in Kempton Park. The appellant was accused of unlawfully appropriating R800,000 and attempting to steal an additional R200,000, both occurring simultaneously. At the time of these offences, Segwati had a history of prior petty convictions for theft and fraud, which dated back over 14 years. The trial court classified him as a third offender under section 51(2)(a)(iii) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, resulting in a minimum sentence of 25 years' imprisonment being imposed ([1]).

Following the sentencing, Segwati’s application under section 309B of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA) for leave to appeal was initially denied by the trial court. Subsequently, he applied to the Gauteng Division of the High Court for leave to appeal under section 309C of the CPA, which was also dismissed ([1]). This refusal set the stage for a special leave petition to the Supreme Court of Appeal, which was granted specifically concerning the sentence imposed on him.

I – Issues

The key legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal revolved around whether the imposition of the prescribed minimum sentence of 25 years was valid, particularly in light of the appellant’s previous convictions which had "fallen away" due to the time elapsed since they occurred ([5]). The court had to ascertain whether the trial court erred in its interpretation of the relevant statutes governing previous convictions and sentencing guidelines, especially considering section 271A of the CPA, which states that certain older convictions shall not be counted as prior convictions for sentencing purposes.

A secondary issue was whether the Gauteng High Court erred in dismissing Segwati's application for leave to appeal, which ultimately impacted his right to contest the legitimacy of the sentence upon which he was sentenced ([5]).

R – Rules

The ruling was heavily informed by various statutory provisions, particularly:

  1. Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997: This Act sets out the framework for sentencing, particularly for serious offences, including the classification and treatment of repeat offenders under section 51(2)(a)(iii) ([1]).
  2. Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977: Specifically, sections 271A and 309B/C, which detail the conditions under which previous convictions are considered when awarding sentences and the procedural prerequisites for appealing decisions ([1]).
  3. Precedent Case Law: This case drew on precedents set in earlier cases, notably Van Wyk v The State and Galela v The State, highlighting that when seeking special leave after a high court has refused an application under section 309C, the focus is on reasonable prospects of success in the appeal ([2]).

A – Application

The court dissected the application of the law to the facts presented. It noted that both lower courts failed to recognize that the appellant’s prior convictions, due to their age (over 14 years), "shall fall away" as per section 271A of the CPA, thus not warranting the classification of Segwati as a third offender subject to the 25-year minimum sentence ([4]). The absence of recent criminal activity negated the justification for the harsher penalty imposed.

Moreover, the court acknowledged that the respondent had conceded that the appeal must succeed based on these misunderstandings, indicating a clear legal misapplication by the lower courts that justified the appellant's claims ([4]).

The judgment highlighted procedural constraints as well. While the appellant sought to address the merits of his sentence on appeal, the Supreme Court clarified that its current role was limited to reviewing the decision of the high court regarding leave to appeal. Therefore, it could not directly impose a new sentence—a point emphasized in relation to prior case law like S v Matshona and S v Tonkin ([6]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld Segwati's appeal ([7]). It set aside the order made by the high court and granted leave to appeal against the sentence but did not impose a new sentence because the merits of such an appeal would have to be litigated in the Gauteng Division of the High Court. The ruling signified an important affirmation of principles surrounding the eligibility for sentencing reductions under the Criminal Procedure Act, as well as procedural standards regarding the application for leave to appeal against sentences. This decision has implications for the treatment of historical offences in sentencing discourses and reinforces the need for meticulous adherence to legislative frameworks in determining appropriate sanctions, ultimately preserving the rights of defendants against unjust sentencing outcomes in South Africa’s criminal justice system.
