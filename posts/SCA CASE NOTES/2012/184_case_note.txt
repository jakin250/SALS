Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note: Steyn v Ronald Bobroff & Partners (025/12) [2012] ZASCA 184

## F – Facts
The appellant, Elizabeth Catherina Steyn, instructed the respondent, Ronald Bobroff & Partners, on March 17, 2006, to institute a third-party claim against the Road Accident Fund (RAF) for injuries her minor son Micah sustained in a motor vehicle accident on August 28, 2005 ([paras 5–6]). The appellant believed the respondent to be "pre-eminent specialists" in personal injury claims and entered into three written agreements detailing the scope of the respondent's mandate ([paras 9–10]).

The claim was initially lodged with the RAF on February 27, 2007; however, after receiving no response, the respondent issued summons against the RAF on December 12, 2007 ([para 5]). The dispute arose when the appellant alleged that the respondent had failed to act with the requisite diligence, resulting in a delay of 14½ months in finalizing the claim, which she claimed led to financial loss in the form of mora interest on the damages awarded to her son, totaling R479,485.20 ([paras 8–10]) 

The trial commenced on February 1, 2010, and judgment was rendered in favor of the appellant on February 11, 2010, awarding her R500,000 for general damages and R2,060,099 for future loss of earnings ([para 7]). The appellant subsequently sought damages from the respondent, alleging that their negligence warranted a claim for interest ([paras 8-9]). 

The South Gauteng High Court (Tsoka J) dismissed her claims, prompting Steyn to appeal, contending that the respondent's failure to process her claim expeditiously constituted a breach of contract and negligence ([paras 4, 31]).

## I – Issues
The appeal raised several critical legal questions:
1. Did the respondent breach their mandate by failing to process and finalize the claim with the required diligence, care, and skill? ([paras 4, 19])
2. Can the appellant legitimately claim mora interest as damages when the respondent did not owe her a debt? ([paras 4, 23])
3. Is the appellant's claim rooted in delict or contract? ([paras 11–13])

## R – Rules
The legal principles applicable to this case include:
- **Standard of Care for Attorneys**: An attorney must exercise the care, skill, and diligence expected from a reasonable practitioner in their field ([paras 1–2]).
- **Liability in Contract Law**: An attorney's failure to adhere to their contractual obligations can result in liability for damages ([para 13]). This necessitates evidence of negligence aligned with the reasonable conduct expected under the attorneys' fiduciary duty to their client ([para 16]).
- **Mora Interest Principles**: Mora interest is typically awarded to compensate a creditor for loss incurred due to delayed payments. It is prominent in obligations where a creditor suffered financial loss due to the delay of a debtor ([paras 33-36]). However, a prerequisite is the establishment of a principal debt owed by the debtor, which was contested in this case ([para 34]).

## A – Application
The court applied these rules to assess the respondent's conduct against the expected standard for personal injury attorneys. It was argued that the delay of 14½ months was excessive, particularly given that the appellant required timely medical attention for her son ([paras 19, 21]). However, the court emphasized that the determination of negligence required evidence of what a reasonable attorney would have done under similar circumstances ([para 26]). 

The appellant's case faltered due to a lack of expert testimony; she alone testified, and her evidence did not establish that the respondent's delay was negligent ([para 29]). The absence of specific timeframes in their agreements and the nebulous nature of terms such as “as soon as possible” further complicated her claim ([paras 28, 36]). 

Regarding the mora interest claim, the court highlighted the absence of a principal debt owed by the attorneys to the appellant. Since the respondent was not liable for the damages awarded against the RAF, any claim for interest could not stand, as it pertained to a breach of their professional duty, not a financial obligation owed by them ([para 34]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, concluding that the appellant had not established a prima facie case of negligence against the respondent. The court found that the standard of care was not rigidly defined and that the respondent's actions fell within acceptable limits given the complexities of the case ([paras 30, 31]). Additionally, the absence of a debt owed to the appellant negated her claim for mora interest, highlighting the distinction between contractual obligations and claims arising from duties of care ([paras 33–38]).

The implications of this ruling underscore the necessity for claimants in similar circumstances to furnish clear evidence, including expert testimony, to establish that the attorney's conduct fell short of professional norms and to support claims for damages based on delay or negligence in professional services.
