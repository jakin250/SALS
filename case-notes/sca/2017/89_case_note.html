Supreme Court of Appeal

Goosen v The Mont Chevaux Trust [2017] ZASCA 89

The magistrate's court granted an eviction order on March 31, 2014, which was confirmed in automatic review by the Land Claims Court (LCC) on May 22, 2014 ([para 5]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 89

F – Facts

The case concerns a dispute about the eviction of the first to sixteenth appellants, who were residing on the Silver Oaks farm in Wellington, Western Cape, owned by The Mont Chevaux Trust. The trust acquired the property on July 13, 2012, for personal use by the Austin family, who intended to operate a high-care thoroughbred stud facility ([para 2]). Appellants were allegedly on the property without any formal agreement, and their living conditions were described as deplorable ([para 13]). The eviction process began when the trust filed an application in the Wellington magistrate's court on February 28, 2013, invoking the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA) ([para 3]).

The magistrate's court granted an eviction order on March 31, 2014, which was confirmed in automatic review by the Land Claims Court (LCC) on May 22, 2014 ([para 5]). The appellants sought to appeal the LCC’s confirmation of the eviction order, arguing procedural violations and substantive injustices, leading to this appeal ([para 6]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) were:

  1. Was the LCC justified in confirming the eviction order from the magistrate’s court?
  2. Did the trust comply with the procedural requirements set out in sections 9 and 10 of ESTA for lawful eviction ([para 6])?
  3. What constitutional obligations did the Drakenstein Municipality have in relation to providing emergency housing to the appellants upon eviction ([paras 31–35])?

R – Rules

The relevant legal authorities include:

  1. Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA) - Specifically sections 9 and 10, which set the procedural requirements for eviction and termination of rights of residence ([para 14]). Under section 9(2), a court may grant an eviction order only after the conditions therein are met, including proper notice and justification for eviction. Section 10 provides circumstances under which eviction can occur based on the behaviour of the occupiers ([para 14]).
  2. Constitutional Obligations: The case also invokes constitutional principles established in Government of the Republic of South Africa & others v Grootboom & others [2001] ZACC 19, regarding the state’s obligation to provide suitable housing to those in dire conditions ([para 31]).
  3. Legal Precedents: The court revisited the principles stated in Snyders & others v De Jager [2016] ZACC 55 regarding the powers of the LCC in reviewing eviction cases ([para 16]).

A – Application

The SCA began by reviewing the history of the ownership and tenant rights on the property, noting the previous eviction efforts initiated by Corpclo 109 CC and the absence of compliance with ESTA’s provisions ([paras 7, 22]). Critical to the appellants’ argument was the claim that they had not been adequately informed of the termination of their rights of residence in accordance with section 9(2)(d) ([paras 14, 24]). The LCC had justified its ruling by asserting that strict compliance was not necessary as there was no real prejudice to the appellants. The SCA found this reasoning flawed, stating that statutory provisions must be adhered to, regardless of perceived injustices ([para 26]).

Furthermore, the SCA addressed the municipality’s obligations to provide emergency housing, concluding that an eviction would render the appellants homeless but that it was ultimately the municipality's duty to ensure adequate housing arrangements prior to executing the eviction order ([paras 31–35]). The court emphasized that even though the appellants lived in poor conditions, the trust could not be expected to indefinitely accommodate them and that a balance must be struck between individual rights and property rights ([para 33]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA ultimately confirmed the eviction order against the first to sixteenth appellants but suspended its execution for 90 days, mandating that the Drakenstein Municipality provide temporary emergency accommodation within 75 days of the order ([para 4]). This judgment underscores the necessity for adherence to procedural requirements in eviction cases under ESTA and the state’s obligation to ensure that those evicted have access to emergency housing, impacting future landlord-tenant disputes where human dignity and rights to housing are concerned ([para 37]).

Thus, the ratio decidendi emphasizes the importance of procedural justice and the fundamental right to adequate housing as paramount considerations in eviction cases within South African law.
