Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note: Loch Logan Waterfront v Bentel Associates International (147/2015) [2017] ZASCA 135

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History** [1–4]

In *Loch Logan Waterfront v Bentel Associates International*, the appellants, Loch Logan Waterfront (Pty) Limited and The Trustees of the N Georgiou Trust, were engaged in a contractual dispute with the respondent, Bentel Associates International, a firm of architects. The conflict arose from the architectural services provided by Bentel for a shopping centre in Bloemfontein, commissioned in 2003, with contractual developments continuing until 2006. By December 2014, after trial proceedings presided by Kruger J in the Free State Division of the High Court, judgment was rendered in favour of Bentel, albeit with significant errors, notably a miscalculation in fees and interest rates awarded.

The appellants contended that they were aggrieved by multiple aspects of this judgment, including the trial court's decision on fees and who was liable under the contract. Furthermore, they sought to appeal the judgment but failed to file the requisite appeal record on time, leading to the appeal lapsing by early 2016 ([5][22]).

The complexities increased as Bentel pursued a cross-appeal concerning the miscalculation of fees and the legal validity of the parties involved in the contract, specifically whether the company or the trust held locus standi in the matter ([30][52]).

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions** [5]

The core issues addressed by the Supreme Court of Appeal were as follows:

1. **Condonation for Late Filing**: Should the court grant condonation for the late filing of the appeal record and the reinstatement of the appellants' appeal?
2. **Miscalculation of Fees**: Did the trial court err in calculating the amount due to Bentel, specifically regarding the fees and allowable interest?
3. **Locus Standi**: Which party—the company or the trust—was legally bound by the contract with Bentel?
4. **Costs Award**: What were the appropriate costs to be awarded to Bentel for its claims?

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon** [25–28]

The court referenced established principles of law guiding the practice of condonation, especially in instances of excessive and unexplained delays. Key decisions cited include:

- *Mtshali & others v Buffalo Conservation 97 (Pty) Ltd* (2017) where principles regarding the discretion to grant or refuse condonation were discussed ([26]).
- *Darries v Sheriff, Magistrate’s Court, Wynberg & another* (1998) elaborated on the necessity for a responsible argument for delays ([26]).
- *Tshivhase Royal Council & another v Tshivhase & another* (1992) outlined that flagrant breaches of court rules, devoid of acceptable explanations, may result in the refusal of condonation ([27]).

Additionally, the court examined the contractual obligations emanating from the engagement between the trust and Bentel, particularly concerning clauses around variations of the contract and assignment of obligations.

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law** [29–55]

1. **Condonation Analysis**: The appellants’ attempt to obtain relief through condonation was thoroughly scrutinized. The court deemed the failure to comply with the rules as "flagrant and inexplicable." The lack of substantial communication or action from the appellants for several months was considered particularly detrimental to their position. The court highlighted that the prospects of success, while a noteworthy factor, were insufficient to override the critical deficiencies in the appellants' explanations for their delays ([29][22]).

2. **Miscalculation of Fees**: Kruger J's initial calculations were found to be incorrectly executed, notably by omitting VAT from the sum awarded. The Supreme Court rectified this, determining that the proper fee was R2,496,265.30, reinforcing the notion that such costs were demonstrable and thus justified. The appeal court mandated the correct calculation, reflecting both the trial court's missteps and the requirement under the Prescribed Rate of Interest Act 55 of 1975 for appropriate interest to be applied to the amounts owed ([52]).

3. **Locus Standi Discussion**: A compelling argument arose over whether the company had taken over the trust's obligations under the architectural contract. The correspondence and transactional documents indicated no formal delegation or variation that would allow the company to replace the trust as a contracting party. The appeal court affirmed that Bentel’s invoicing to the company did not constitute an agreement to delegate obligations without the necessary written consent as espoused in the contract ([40][45]).

4. **Cost Determinations**: The appellate court found that due to the significant errors in the trial court's judgment, Bentel was entitled to its costs, underscoring a miscalculation that had adversely affected the legal outcomes regarding costs determination [56].

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications** [57]

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appellants’ application for condonation and reinstatement of their appeal due to the excessive delays and lack of sufficient justification for their inaction. The court upheld Bentel's cross-appeal, establishing that the N Georgiou Trust was liable for the correctly calculated sum of R2,496,265.30 along with interest at 15.5% from 19 May 2009 until payment. The implications of this judgment reaffirm the necessity for strict adherence to procedural rules in appellate processes and clarify contractual obligations concerning changes in the parties to contracts. This case serves as a poignant reminder of the judicial insistence on procedural integrity and the contractual obligations’ primacy in civil disputes.
