Botha v Road Accident Fund (463/2015) [2016] ZASCA 97

Subsequent to the settlement, it was discovered that the actual past medical expenses amounted to R784,278.78, with the appellant’s attorney failing to present all documents during negotiations, leading to an incomplete settlement ([4]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 97

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The appellant, Roelof Ernst Botha, and his wife sustained serious bodily injuries in a motorcycle accident, leading to the initiation of separate actions against the Road Accident Fund (the respondent) for damages incurred due to their injuries ([2]). In early March 2014, the respondent conceded liability for damages, and discussions ensued regarding Botha's claim, which was to be heard before Judge Molefe. The parties agreed on R1 million for general damages and R236,922.70 for past hospital and medical expenses, despite the appellant's original claim being R150,000. The respondent accepted a higher amount, influenced by representations made by Botha’s attorney ([3]).

Subsequent to the settlement, it was discovered that the actual past medical expenses amounted to R784,278.78, with the appellant’s attorney failing to present all documents during negotiations, leading to an incomplete settlement ([4]). The appellant’s attorney wrote to the respondent proposing a rescission of the court order and a revision of the settlement amount. However, the respondent objected, citing the res judicata effect of the existing order ([5]).

The appellant applied for rescission or variation of the order under Uniform Rule 42(1)(c), arguing that a mutual mistake had occurred ([6]). The respondent opposed the application, claiming that the appellant's attorney had misrepresented the facts ([7]).

I – Issues

The primary legal question was whether Botha was bound by the settlement agreement, despite claiming it arose from a misrepresentation by his attorney regarding material facts ([1]). This included determining the nature of the mistake—whether it was mutual or unilateral—and the validity of the agreement under the principles governing rescission and the application of Uniform Rule 42(1)(c) ([8]).

R – Rules

The court examined the principles surrounding mutual versus unilateral mistakes in contract law. In Tshivhase Royal Council & another v Tshivhase & another ([1992] ZASCA 185), the court defined common mistakes as mutual misunderstandings that undermine an agreement. In contrast, the principles articulated in Sonap Petroleum (SA) (Pty) Ltd v Pappadogianis ([1992] ZASCA 56) outlined that a unilateral mistake affects the consensus—significantly, if the opposing party relied on the mistaken impression as though it were true ([9], [10]).

Additionally, Van Reenen Steel (Pty) Ltd v Smith NO & another ([2002] ZASCA 12) clarified that the mistaken party would not be permitted to escape an agreement if their mistake was due to their own fault ([12]).

A – Application

The court contrasted the case at hand with Tshivhase, clarifying that Botha’s case did not involve mutual error. Instead, the mistake was termed unilateral, instigated by his attorney’s misrepresentation ([9]). The court upheld that liability under the reliance theory meant the respondent reasonably relied on the attorney’s representations, thus binding the appellant to the agreement ([10]).

The appellant attempted to frame the issue as a mutual mistaken assumption regarding the relevant documents. However, the court found this argument insufficient since no evidence suggested that both parties had agreed on the material expense figures presented ([12]). Ultimately, the court ruled that Botha's misrepresentation led to a binding settlement, which could not be rescinded under Rule 42(1)(c) since the agreement accurately reflected the parties' consensus at the time of settlement ([13]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Botha's appeal, confirming the High Court's decision to uphold the settlement agreement as binding. The court elucidated that unilateral mistakes primarily deal with the fault of the mistaken party and emphasized that plaintiffs cannot evade contracts based on errors stemming from their own carelessness or lack of diligence ([14]). The implications of this ruling reinforce the necessity for thorough investigation and diligence during negotiations to avoid reliance on erroneous representations when entering contractual agreements. The appeal was dismissed with costs including the fees for two counsel, underscoring the resolution of the contractual liabilities and the importance of accurate legal representation in settlement agreements.
