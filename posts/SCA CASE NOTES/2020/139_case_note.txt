Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# Case Note: Mgijima v The Premier of the Eastern Cape Province & others (949/2018) [2020] ZASCA 139

## F – Facts
The matter arises from a review of a traditional leadership dispute concerning the chieftainship of the Zulu Traditional Council in Sheshegu, Eastern Cape, involving the appellant, Mhlangabezi Raymond Mgijima, and the fourth respondent, Xabiso Zulu. The applicable legal framework is the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003, specifically sections related to the Commission on Traditional Leadership Claims and Disputes (the Committee) established to resolve such disputes.

The appellant, Mr. Mgijima, claimed hereditary rights to the chieftainship on the basis of ancestry, asserting that his lineage, derived from Elijah Mgijima, entitled him to succeed as chief. The dispute intensified following the appointment of Mr. Zulu, son of Mzwandile Zulu, as chief. The Committee held hearings where historical evidence and genealogies were presented by witnesses from both sides.

Ultimately, the Committee recommended that Mr. Zulu's claim be upheld, concluding the Mgijima line did not possess the same traditional authority as the Zulu line. Subsequently, the Premier issued a decision to dismiss Mr. Mgijima's claim. After Mr. Mgijima's death during the proceedings, his son, Dumalisile Mgijima, substituted him as the appellant ([paras 1–2]).

## I – Issues
The appeal raised several key legal issues:
1. Whether the decision of the Committee and the ensuing ruling by the Premier were procedurally and substantively valid under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA).
2. The legality of the Premier's written decision, specifically concerning counter-signature requirements under section 140(2) of the South African Constitution.
3. If the Committee's investigation and factual conclusions were irrational and unjustifiable under administrative law principles ([paras 12–18]).

## R – Rules
The court relied on several statutes and case law relevant to administrative law and traditional leadership rights:
- The Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003, which outlines the establishment and functions of the Committee.
- The Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000, which mandates lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair administrative action.
- Section 140(2) of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, requiring written decisions by the Premier to be counter-signed by the MEC when concerning a function assigned to that member.

Relevant case law included principles from *Zuma v DA* and other administrative law precedents, highlighting the distinction between reviewing a decision for legality versus its correctness ([paras 20–30]).

## A – Application
The court examined whether the Premier's decision adhered to the necessary administrative standards. It was determined that the Committee conducted a comprehensive investigation, gathering evidence from both parties and considering historical documentation. The Premier's decision to endorse the Committee's recommendation was found to be rational and based on substantive evidence presented during the hearings.

Regarding procedural adherence, the court addressed the argument pertaining to the need for the MEC's counter-signature. It clarified that the Premier acted within her powers under the Framework Act and the provincial legislation, and thus, the failure to obtain a counter-signature did not invalidate the decision. The court emphasized that the substantive rights of parties were preserved, as the appellant's claims had been fully ventilated before the Committee ([paras 16–27]).

Furthermore, the court articulated that the essence of several allegations raised by Mr. Mgijima revolved around factual determinations rather than issues of legality, reinforcing that an administrative review is not an appeal on the merits of factual findings ([paras 29-31]).

## C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, ruling that no grounds for review were established. The court held that both the Committee and Premier acted within their lawful capacities, made rational decisions based on adequate evidence, and conducted the proceedings fairly.

The decision has significant implications for traditional leadership disputes in South Africa, reaffirming the autonomy of the Committee and the authority of the Premier in such matters. The ruling also underscores the necessity for traditional claimants to substantiate their claims with robust historical evidence and to navigate the procedural requirements outlined in applicable statutes ([paras 32–34]).
