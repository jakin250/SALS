The Director of Public Prosecutions: Gauteng Division, Pretoria v Buthelezi (142/18) [2019] ZASCA 170

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 170

F – Facts

The case concerns an appeal by the Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP) against a decision of the Gauteng Division of the High Court wherein the respondent, Lucky Anthony Buthelezi, had his sentence reduced for the crime of raping a minor. Initially, Buthelezi was arraigned in the Regional Court of Vereeniging, where he pleaded guilty to raping his 13-year-old niece on 28 March 2011, resulting in multiple genital injuries ([2]–[3]). He was sentenced to life imprisonment. Aggrieved by this ruling, Buthelezi appealed to the High Court, arguing that the life sentence was inappropriate as it resulted from a typographical error in the charge sheet which referenced the incorrect legislation.

The High Court found in favor of Buthelezi, reasoning that since the charge sheet incorrectly referred to the Criminal Law (Sentencing) Amendment Act 38 of 2007 instead of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, which stipulated a minimum life sentence for child rape, the life sentence was invalid. Consequently, the High Court substituted the original sentence with one of 15 years’ imprisonment ([4]).

The DPP then appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal under section 311(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, asserting that the High Court’s interpretations constituted legal errors ([5]).

I – Issues

The principal legal issues addressed in this appeal include:

  1. Whether the High Court erred in determining that the incorrect reference to the Criminal Law (Sentencing) Amendment Act invalidated the life sentence.
  2. Whether the provisions of section 51(1) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 applied to Buthelezi's case, given the incorrect citation.
  3. The implications of the DPP's right of appeal as established by section 311 of the Criminal Procedure Act, particularly regarding questions of law decided against the prosecution and the accused’s right to a fair trial ([7]–[10]).

R – Rules

The following legal principles and statutes are relevant to this case:

  1. Section 311(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977: This section allows the DPP to appeal decisions made in favor of the accused on points of law ([6]).
  2. Section 51(1) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997: Establishes that individuals convicted of specified serious offenses (including child rape) must face a minimum sentence of life imprisonment unless compelling circumstances exist ([12]).
  3. Fair Trial Rights under Section 35(3) of the Constitution: Ensures access to a fair trial, which includes the accused's right to be informed of the charges against them ([11]).
  4. Case Precedents:
  • S v Legoa [2003] (i.e., the requirement for informing an accused of the minimum sentence).
  • S v Ndlovu [2017] (where the court found that sentence imposed misplaced due to miscommunications regarding applicable laws).
  • S v Khoza & another [2019] (clarified the requirements for informing accused about minimum sentencing laws) ([13]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court applied the principles outlined in the relevant statutes and case law to the specifics of the case.

  1. Typographical Error in Charge Sheet: The Supreme Court held that the typographical error in referencing the incorrect legislation did not impede Buthelezi's understanding of the charges and potential consequences, as all parties involved were cognizant of the applicable law regarding the minimum sentence ([14]–[15]).
  2. Jurisdiction of the Regional Court: The court analyzed the relevant provisions, determining that amendments made by the Criminal Law Amendment Act 38 of 2007 permitted regional courts to impose life sentences upon conviction for specified offenses. It illustrated that when Buthelezi was sentenced, the legislation allowing the regional court such jurisdiction was already in force ([16]).
  3. **Inapplicability of *Ndlovu***: The respondent’s arguments, based on S v Ndlovu, were dismissed as the circumstances in Buthelezi’s case differed significantly, particularly concerning the procedural clarity around the applicable statute. The fact that all stakeholders had mistakenly referenced the wrong statute did not prejudice Buthelezi's fair trial rights ([17]).

In reviewing the DPP's grounds for appeal, the court concluded that the High Court had erred by disregarding the applicable law and misinterpreting the implications of the charge sheet’s wording ([18]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held the DPP’s appeal and found that the High Court had made significant legal errors in its judgment. The court reinstated the original life sentence imposed by the Regional Court, citing the absence of any compelling circumstances justifying a lesser sentence. It emphasized the severity of Buthelezi's offenses against a vulnerable victim and the need to impose a sentence that reflects the gravity of the crime to act as both retribution and deterrence.

Consequently, the appeal was upheld, and the High Court's order was set aside, reinforcing the principle that the interpretation of law must adhere strictly to statutory provisions without compromising substantive justice ([20]). This case reaffirms the stringent application of minimum sentencing laws for serious offenses in South Africa's legal framework and clarifies the parameters governing the DPP's appeals on legal interpretations.
