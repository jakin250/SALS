Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# Case Note: MK Nkomo v The State (979/2013) [2014] ZASCA 186

## FIRAC

### F – Facts
The appellant, M K Nkomo, was convicted of the rape of a minor, Esta Pontsho Thwala, on 30 August 2010, in the Regional Magistrate’s Court at Bethlehem ([para 1]). Nkomo, a teacher and close family friend of the complainant, was sentenced to 15 years’ imprisonment after being accused of sexually assaulting Thwala on 23 November 2007 ([para 1]). The evidence against him primarily came from Thwala's testimony, along with corroboration from her sister, parents, and a medico-legal report prepared by Dr. Leboko, who had unfortunately passed away before the trial ([para 4]).

Following his conviction, Nkomo applied for leave to appeal to the Free State High Court, which was granted ([para 1]). Before the appeal was heard, Nkomo sought to introduce new evidence by way of a letter from Thwala. In this letter, dated 15 May 2011, she recanted her earlier testimony, alleging coercion from her stepmother and a court interpreter, Khambule, who had purportedly encouraged her to fabricate the charges ([para 16]).

The Free State High Court dismissed both Nkomo's appeal and his request to submit further evidence on the grounds that such applications are generally only granted under exceptional circumstances ([para 2]). This decision led Nkomo to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 2]).

### I – Issues
The primary issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal were:
1. Whether there were exceptional circumstances warranting the remittal of Nkomo's case to the trial court for the introduction of new evidence ([para 18]).
2. The role and credibility of recanting testimony in criminal proceedings ([paras 18, 19]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework relevant to this case includes section 22 of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959, which allows appellate courts to receive further evidence or remit matters to a trial court when necessary ([para 17]). The court discussed various precedents establishing that:
- Remittal for further evidence should be conducted sparingly and only in special or exceptional circumstances due to the risks of witness coercion or fabrication of testimony ([para 18]).
- A recantation of evidence does not usually justify a reopening of a concluded trial unless there is a prima facie credibility to the new claims ([para 19]).
- The requirements for successful applications for remittal include providing a coherent explanation for the new evidence, demonstrating a prima facie likelihood of its truth, and establishing its material relevance ([para 20]).

### A – Application
The court reviewed the contextual elements surrounding the recantation. It found that Nkomo had satisfied the criteria for remittal:
- The new evidence was unavailable at the time of the original trial since he received the letter after his conviction ([para 21]).
- There were significant questions surrounding the truthfulness of the initial testimony, with the letter calling into question the motivations behind Thwala's original claims ([para 22]). Particularly notable was the evidence that Thwala had not only faced coercion but had also previously expressed suicidal thoughts related to her home situation, which was crucial in assessing her credibility ([para 22]).
- The court also highlighted the alleged involvement of Khambule, reinforcing the concerns regarding the integrity of the initial proceedings and suggesting that a significant miscarriage of justice may have occurred ([para 24]).

Additionally, the simplicity and sincerity evident in Thwala's letter hinted that it was likely a candid attempt to confess rather than a manipulative fabrication ([para 26]). Hence, the court found there were exceptional circumstances justifying the remittal of the case ([para 27]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of Nkomo, upholding his appeal, and set aside the High Court's order. The matter was remitted to the original trial court for the admission of Thwala's letter and the re-opening of both the state’s and defense cases ([paras 28]). Importantly, the ruling emphasized the necessity for a thorough and careful reconsideration of the case to uphold justice ([para 25]).

This decision has significant implications for the treatment of recanting witnesses in South African criminal law, marking a pivotal position on how courts may address claims of coerced testimony and wrongful conviction in the pursuit of equitable justice.
