Teresa's decision to place her husband in nursing care was influenced by alarming behaviors, including a perceived suicide attempt.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2000 [1990] 1 SA 680 (A) and *Standard Chartered Bank of Canada v

FIRAC Case Note: The Road Accident Fund v. Teresa Doris Russell

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–9])

On 16 August 1989, Michael Henry Russell (the deceased), a roofing contractor in Durban, sustained severe injuries, including multiple fractures and brain damage, following a motor vehicle collision. This incident incapacitated him, leading to significant changes in his behavior and personality traits, as described by his wife, Teresa Doris Russell (the respondent). He was initially hospitalized at King Edward Hospital, remained in a comatose state for about a month, and was subsequently transferred to Addington Hospital until 22 January 1990. Although he returned home briefly, he was eventually admitted to Morningside Nursing Home in November 1990, where he tragically committed suicide in January 1991 ([paras 1–7]).

Teresa's decision to place her husband in nursing care was influenced by alarming behaviors, including a perceived suicide attempt. Despite caring for him at home with professional assistance, she concluded that he required better supervision. After his admission, she noted no significant improvement in his mental state, and on the day of his suicide, he had undergone a medical assessment indicating no prospects of recovery, leading to the grim decision he made later that day ([paras 2–6]).

Following his death, the respondent initiated two claims against the Road Accident Fund (the appellant). The appeal in question arose from the second equitable claim brought on behalf of their children, focusing on the merits of causation related to the deceased's suicide ([paras 8–9]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 9–11])

The primary legal issue at hand is whether the deceased's suicide constituted a novus actus interveniens that would sever the causal link between the negligence of the insured driver and the deceased's death. The trial court ruled in favor of the respondent, asserting that the suicide was causally connected to the injuries sustained in the collision, thereby establishing legal liability ([paras 9, 16]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 17–26])

In determining legal causation in delict, the Supreme Court of Appeal referenced key principles established in prior case law, particularly International Shipping Co (Pty) Ltd v Bentley [1990] 1 SA 680 (A) and Standard Chartered Bank of Canada v Nedperm Bank Ltd [1994] 4 SA 747 (A). These cases elucidate the dual inquiries of factual and legal causation, stressing that while factual causation is typically evaluated using the “but-for” test, legal causation requires an assessment of the proximity and foreseeability of the loss (including policy considerations) ([paras 17–18]).

The court also examined the House of Lords’ decision in Reeves v Commissioner of Police of the Metropolis [1999] 3 All ER 897, which distinguished cases based on the mental state of the individual committing suicide. While this case addressed a scenario involving a mentally sound individual, the current matter involved a deceased whose faculties were significantly impaired by injury and depression ([paras 20–22]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 25–26])

The court applied the established principles of legal causation, concluding that Michael Russell's suicide, while deliberate, was not an act of a person with unimpaired judgment. The evidence indicated that the deceased's brain injury and resultant psychological state significantly influenced his decision-making capabilities. Even though the suicide was voluntary in a literal sense, the court found that the mental impairment caused by the accident diminished his ability to make a rational decision ([paras 25–26]).

Consequently, the court concluded that the causal chain was not severed by the suicide, affirming that the inherent consequences of the accident, such as the resultant depression and inability to function normally, were direct results of the negligent conduct leading to the collision ([paras 24–26]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 26–27])

The Supreme Court of Appeal found that there was sufficient causal connection between the negligence of the insured driver and the deceased's subsequent suicide, ruling that the suicide did not represent a novus actus interveniens. The court emphasized the need for a flexible test of legal causation, affirming the trial court’s decision that the appellant was liable to compensate the respondent for the proven damages as they arose from the collision ([paras 26–27]).

In dismissing the appeal with costs, the court underscored the legal principle that when an individual's cognitive functions are impaired due to a negligent act, any resulting actions, even those that appear deliberate, remain within the ambit of liability arising from the initial wrongful conduct ([para 27]). This case reinforces the necessity of considering the mental health impacts of injuries in claims for damages resulting from negligence, and sets significant precedent for future cases involving similar factual matrices.
