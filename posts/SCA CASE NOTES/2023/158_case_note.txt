Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of *Polokwane Municipality v Double Four Properties and Another* (879/2022) and *Broadlands Home Owners Association NPC v Double Four Properties and Another* (913/2022) [2023] 158

## F – Facts

The conflict originates from a property ownership and sewage disposal issue involving Polokwane Municipality (the Municipality), Double Four Properties (Pty) Ltd (Double Four), and Broadlands Home Owners Association NPC (Broadlands). Double Four owns an office park adjacent to Broadlands Estate, under the maintenance of Broadlands. In September 2018, Double Four was notified by its tenant about a blockage in the drainage pipe, leading to the discovery that the office park's drainage was connected to Broadlands' sewage disposal system.

Attempts at negotiation failed as Broadlands demanded a fee to accept the waste from the office park, which Double Four disputed. Broadlands then declared that Double Four was unlawfully using its system and encroaching on Broadlands' property. Broadlands subsequently refused to reconnect the sewer line to the office park.

Double Four sought interim relief from the High Court to force the Municipality to provide a sewer connection and to compel Broadlands to reconnect the sewer system. Broadlands countered with a request to remove the encroachments. Initially, the application was dismissed, but upon appeal to a full High Court bench, the order was reversed, requiring the Municipality to provide the sewer connection and Broadlands to reconnect its system as an interim measure.

Both Broadlands and the Municipality subsequently applied for special leave to appeal against this order ([paras 1–4]).

## I – Issues

The key legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal include:

1. **Appealability of Interim Orders**: Whether the High Court's interim order compelling the Municipality to connect the sewage system was final and therefore appealable.
2. **Criteria for Granting Special Leave**: Whether the applicants demonstrated a reasonable prospect of success in their appeals and satisfied the threshold for granting special leave, considering the existence of special circumstances ([paras 5–6]).

## R – Rules

The principles guiding this case include:

1. **Criteria for Special Leave**: An application for special leave to appeal must demonstrate a reasonable prospect of success and the existence of special circumstances, which can include substantial points of law or a manifest denial of justice, as established in *Cook v Morrison* [2019] ZASCA 51.
2. **Appealability**: The appealability of an interim interdict is primarily determined by the considerations from *Zweni v Minister of Law and Order* [1993] ZASCA 61, where it was noted that interim orders are typically not appealable unless they have final and definitive consequences.
3. **Constitutional Considerations**: The appealability must also align with public interest considerations as guided by judgments from the Constitutional Court, notably *United Democratic Movement v Lebashe* [2023] ZACC 1, which emphasizes the necessity of reflecting upon the interests of justice ([paras 7–8]).

## A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed the implications of granting special leave in light of the existing orders from the High Court. Key findings included:

1. **Municipality's Argument**: The Municipality argued that the requirement to provide a sewer connection contravened its by-laws, specifically Section 4, which mandates that consumers apply for such services. However, the court found that the order did not absolve Double Four from complying with this requirement; rather, it merely enforced its right to infrastructure provision.
2. **Broadlands' Position**: Broadlands contended that the High Court's order imposed a legal duty to reconnect without a justifiable basis, asserting a final order on the issue of compensation. The court countered that the order was interim and aimed at public health needs, restoring a previously existing arrangement rather than imposing a new obligation.
3. **Legal Basis for Compensation**: While Broadlands raised concerns over compensation for the sewage service, the court emphasized that this was a matter for resolution in the impending trial action, where all rights and duties would be settled.

Ultimately, the court found that neither the Municipality nor Broadlands satisfied the thresholds for special leave, as the High Court's orders were not final and did not manifestly deny justice. The orders were viewed as necessary interim measures resolving immediate public interest concerns ([paras 9–16]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the applications for special leave to appeal, asserting that the orders made by the High Court were interlocutory and not appealable. The court underscored the importance of maintaining an interim regime to address the pressing issue of sewage disposal, which concerned public health. The applicants failed to demonstrate that the conditions for granting special leave were met, particularly in light of the comprehensive examination of the merits by the High Court ([paras 17]). 

The implications of this decision affirm the importance of procedural compliance while balancing immediate remedial needs—a crucial consideration in municipal law matters dealing with public services and health concerns. It reinforces the judicial principle that interim measures should not hinder administrative processes but rather aid in achieving just outcomes until a final resolution in court.
