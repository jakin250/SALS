Case Title: The South African Medical Association Trade Union v The South African Medical Association NPC and Another (Case no 490/2022) [2023] ZASCA 71 (24 May 2023)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 71 (24 May 2023) ### F – Facts This appeal arose

Case Title: The South African Medical Association Trade Union v The South African Medical Association NPC and Another (Case no 490/2022) [2023] ZASCA 71 (24 May 2023)

F – Facts

This appeal arose from the Gauteng Division of the High Court's dismissal of an application for a final winding-up order sought by the South African Medical Association Trade Union (SAMATU), the appellant, against the South African Medical Association NPC (SAMA), the first respondent ([para 1]). Established in 1927, SAMA is a non-profit company serving medical professionals, including public sector employees, while SAMATU was established in 1996 to represent these workers. It is undisputed that SAMATU did not function as a separate entity but was operated as a division within SAMA, lacking independent financial records ([paras 1–2]).

On October 10, 2019, SAMATU was placed under administration due to operational dysfunction ([para 2]). An administrator was appointed to restore its operational integrity. A central issue arose concerning the PERSAL deductions, a payroll system used for processing payments for state employees, which had been deposited into SAMA's account instead of SAMATU's ([para 3]). The administrator sought to recover R307 million from SAMA, claiming that these funds represented owed member dues, while SAMA countered that they were membership fees owed to it, leading to a significant legal dispute ([paras 4–6]).

In a prior Labour Court order, it was declared that deductions from public sector employees were to benefit SAMATU, which SAMA contested ([para 5]). The High Court rejected the winding-up application, affirming that SAMA's potential indebtedness was disputed and that it could not be wound up on this basis ([para 9]).

I – Issues

Two primary legal issues emerged for the Supreme Court of Appeal's determination:

  1. Indebtedness: Whether SAMATU could substantiate its claims that SAMA was indebted to it and unable to pay its debts ([para 10]).
  2. Just and Equitable Grounds: If not, whether it was just and equitable to order SAMA's winding up ([para 10]).

R – Rules

The rules applicable to this case primarily stem from the Companies Act 61 of 1973, particularly sections 344 and 345, which govern the conditions under which a company may be wound up ([para 7]). Specifically:

  • Section 345(1)(c) defines a company as "unable to pay its debts" when it cannot satisfy creditors ([para 7]).
  • Section 344(h) allows for winding-up on "just and equitable" grounds ([para 8]).

The court also considered the interpretation of its earlier orders and the implications of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995, especially concerning trade union membership and financial transactions ([para 14]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal, led by Gorven JA, critically analyzed the High Court's conclusions regarding SAMATU's claim of indebtedness. The court found that the financial statements did not provide undisputed evidence of SAMA's obligation to pay, and substantial evidence indicated that SAMATU had accepted costs incurred by SAMA on its behalf, complicating the issue of clear indebtedness ([paras 18–20]).

Moreover, SAMATU's argument regarding SAMA’s inability to pay its debts failed on two fronts:

  1. Indebtedness was genuinely disputed and not established as undisputed ([para 22]).
  2. SAMA presented evidence of its operational solvency and capability to meet any valid debts, undermining SAMATU's claim of insolvency ([para 21]).

In assessing the "just and equitable" grounds for winding up, the court recognized SAMA's prolonged operational history and considerable membership as evidence that argued against the dissolution. The test for whether a company should be wound up on these grounds was assessed against criteria established in jurisprudential precedence, notably the absence of a "substratum" or analogous extreme circumstances ([paras 25–31]).

SAMA's alleged unlawful conduct in managing SAMATU was also weighed. The court found insufficient grounds to classify the actions as equating to the total illegality which would justify winding-up, especially considering the complexities surrounding the relationship between SAMA and SAMATU ([paras 27–31]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming that SAMATU failed to establish both the alleged indebtedness of SAMA and a just and equitable basis for winding up the organization ([paras 9, 36]). The judgment underscored the necessity for clear, incontrovertible evidence in claims of financial obligation and emphasized that remedies short of liquidation should have been considered viable given the plaintiffs' circumstances.

In conclusion, the ruling contributes vital jurisprudential clarity on corporate indebtedness and the interpretation of statutory provisions relating to winding up, reflecting the judiciary's cautious approach to corporate dissolution in South Africa.
