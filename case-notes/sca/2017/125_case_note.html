Pather v Financial Services Board (866/2016) [2017] ZASCA 125

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 125

F – Facts

The Supreme Court of Appeal in South Africa heard the case of Pather v Financial Services Board on September 28, 2017, regarding an appeal by Mr. Maslamony Theegarajan Pather and Ah-Vest Limited against penalties imposed by the Enforcement Committee (the EC) under the Securities Services Act 36 of 2004 ([para 1]).

In 2005, the Directorate of Market Abuse (DMA) conducted an investigation into Pather and Ah-Vest Limited’s alleged contraventions of Section 76 of the Act. The investigation revealed that false representations regarding financial transactions were made to auditors and subsequently published within financial statements ([paras 1–2]). The EC found that Pather had participated in the manipulation of these financial records and imposed administrative penalties totaling R1.5 million against him and the company ([paras 3–4]).

Following the EC’s decision, which the Board of Appeal confirmed in August 2010, Pather sought relief from the North Gauteng High Court ([para 4]). His application was dismissed, allowing him to appeal further ([paras 4–5]). The appeal raised several legal issues concerning the nature of administrative penalties and the jurisdiction of the EC.

I – Issues

The principal issues before the court were:

  1. Standard of Proof: Whether the civil standard of proof was appropriate for administrative proceedings conducted by the EC, as opposed to the criminal standard being argued by the appellants ([para 5]).
  2. Jurisdiction: Whether the EC had jurisdiction to determine the contraventions under Section 76 of the Act, given prohibitions outlined in Section 79 regarding criminal offences ([para 5]).
  3. Constitutionality: The constitutionality of Sections 102 to 105 of the Act in light of the appellants' argument that findings akin to a criminal conviction were made without a requisite fair trial ([paras 37–38]).

R – Rules

The court considered several key legal provisions, including:

  1. Securities Services Act 36 of 2004: The Act governs the regulation of financial markets and outlines the powers of the DMA and the EC ([paras 1, 9]).
  2. Standard of Proof: The distinction between civil and criminal standards of proof was pivotal. The court analyzed both statutory provisions and relevant case law indicating that the civil standard of proof applies in administrative proceedings ([para 30]).
  3. Constitution of South Africa: The protections under Section 33 concerning administrative action were examined, alongside Section 35, which provides rights for individuals involved in criminal proceedings ([para 36]).

A – Application

In addressing the standard of proof, the court upheld the civil standard, asserting that administrative penalties do not equate to criminal sanctions. It distinguished between the capacity of the EC's proceedings and the formal nature of criminal proceedings, emphasizing that administrative penalties are intended for regulatory enforcement rather than punishment ([paras 25, 32]).

On the matter of jurisdiction, the court clarified that the EC operates under an administrative framework distinct from the criminal justice system. It affirmed that Section 76 violations, while serious, did not preclude the EC from exercising its jurisdiction to impose administrative sanctions. The court interpreted the legislation to allow for concurrent jurisdictions of administrative and criminal enforcement, thus reinforcing the EC's role without encroaching on criminal prosecution mechanisms ([paras 39, 40]).

Regarding constitutionality, the court found that the administrative penalties were within the bounds of constitutional mandates governing administrative conduct rather than criminality. The legal protections available under Section 33 provided sufficient safeguards against abuses, negating claims for application under Section 35 ([paras 37, 38]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, confirming that the penalties imposed by the EC were valid under the Securities Services Act 36 of 2004. The court affirmed that the civil standard of proof was appropriate for the proceedings before the EC, maintaining that they were not criminal in character and did not infringe on constitutional rights to a fair trial as articulated in Section 35 ([paras 41]).

In essence, the ruling reinforced the regulatory framework of the Securities Services Act, underscoring the purpose of administrative penalties within a broader context of market regulation to promote confidence and integrity in the South African financial markets without the punitive connotations associated with criminal law. The case serves as a pertinent reference for similar regulatory matters in the future, establishing important distinctions between civil and criminal processes within financial regulation.
