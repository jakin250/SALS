Black Sash Trust v Minister of Social Development

Case Citation: Black Sash Trust (Freedom Under Law Intervening) v Minister of Social Development and Others [2018] ZACC 36

Constitutional Court 2018 [2018] ZACC 36 Court: Constitutional Court of South Afri

Case Citation: Black Sash Trust (Freedom Under Law Intervening) v Minister of Social Development and Others [2018] ZACC 36

Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa

Coram: Mogoeng CJ, Zondo DCJ, Basson AJ, Cameron J, Dlodlo AJ, Froneman J, Goliath AJ, Khampepe J, Mhlantla J, Petse AJ, Theron J

Decided on: 27 September 2018

F – Facts

The case arises from a previous judgment (Black Sash 1) concerning the handling of social grants by the South African Social Security Agency (SASSA) and its then Minister, Bathabile Olive Dlamini (Minister Dlamini). Costs were reserved in the earlier judgment, directing Minister Dlamini to explain why she should not be held personally liable for costs arising from the proceedings, due to alleged gross negligence and conflict over parallel processes within her department ([1]-[2]).

The subsequent inquiry into Minister Dlamini's actions found that she had indeed appointed individuals to lead parallel "work streams" reporting directly to her, violating governance protocols. These work streams were crucial to addressing the socio-economic needs related to social grants, especially during a crisis in March 2017 ([3]-[5]). The Inquiry Report highlighted her failure to disclose essential information which led to significant delays and confusion in SASSA's operational mandates ([6]-[8]).

Minister Dlamini failed to address critical findings against her, claiming that holding her liable would breach the principle of separation of powers ([7�]-[8]). Conversely, the applicants, Black Sash Trust and Freedom Under Law, argued that Dlamini's non-disclosure represented bad faith and a dereliction of duty, warranting a personal costs order against her ([9]-[10]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions addressed by the Court included:

  1. Whether Minister Dlamini should be held personally liable for the costs associated with the proceedings, given her alleged gross negligence and bad faith in non-disclosure of material information ([1]-[10]).
  2. Whether the principle of separation of powers would be violated by imposing personal cost liability on a Minister ([7]-[10]).

R – Rules

The Court's analysis drew substantially from established principles concerning costs orders against public officials. According to previous rulings, particularly in Black Sash 2, public officials may be held liable for costs when their conduct reflects gross negligence or bad faith ([10]). The interaction of these rules with constitutional duties emphasizes transparency and accountability in governance. It mandates that public officials exercise their powers in a manner consistent with the Constitution and related statutes, including the provisions of the Superior Courts Act ([4], [10]).

Furthermore, the findings of the Inquiry Report served as significant evidential support for the conclusion reached regarding Minister Dlamini’s liability ([3]).

A – Application

The Court emphasized that Minister Dlamini's argument regarding the separation of powers lacked merit. The principles governing costs are applied not as a form of political accountability but as a mechanism to protect the court's integrity and the litigants' rights ([10]).

Froneman J investigated the implications of Dlamini’s failure to disclose critical information, which amounted to a reckless disregard for her duties and responsibilities ([11]-[12]). The Inquiry findings yielded uncontroverted evidence that Dlamini's deliberate non-disclosure was motivated by a fear of being held accountable, further cementing the characterization of her actions as being in bad faith and irresponsible.

The assessment of her conduct was critical in determining the extent of the personal liability ordered by the Court. Although ordinarily public officials do not bear personal liability for the actions taken in good faith while performing their duties, the circumstances of this case were exceptional and grave ([16]-[17]).

The order specified that Minister Dlamini pay a percentage of the costs personally and elucidated the gravity of her misleading the Court during a critical period of social service delivery failures. Simultaneously, the larger body of respondents was ordered to cover a more significant portion of the costs due to their participatory role in the crisis ([18]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded that Minister Dlamini's failure to act transparently warranted personal liability for the costs involved in the proceedings. Thus, the Court ordered her to personally pay 20% of the costs, reflecting her role in the events that had unfolded. The remaining 80% was assigned to the other respondents involved ([18]).

The case underscored the importance of accountability among public officials, particularly those in high government positions. It raised significant implications for governance, ethical conduct in public administration, and the role of independent bodies like the South African Constitutional Court to maintain the rule of law while holding public officials accountable for their misconduct. The judgment also indicated possible further action concerning perjury against Minister Dlamini, thereby emphasizing the gravity of her misrepresentations to the Court ([16]-[18]).
