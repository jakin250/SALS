Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note: *Rugnanan v The State* [2020] ZASCA 166

**F – Facts:**  
In *Rugnanan v State*, the applicant, Anesh Rugnanan, was convicted by the regional court in Madadeni, KwaZulu-Natal for two counts of rape, assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm, and compelling a complainant to engage in an act of masturbation, resulting in a sentence of 14 years' imprisonment ([1]). The trial court had found Rugnanan guilty based on the testimony of a single witness, the complainant, who detailed incidents of manipulation and sexual assault that occurred when she accompanied Rugnanan and another woman, later identified as Desiree, on various occasions ([9]–[12]).

Rugnanan's conviction was subsequently upheld by the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court, which rejected his petition for leave to appeal ([1]). His application for special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal was dismissed on 29 June 2015 on the ground of no special circumstances ([1]). This case was brought back to the Supreme Court for reconsideration, as the court was tasked to determine if exceptional circumstances merited an appeal ([1]).

Prior to engaging with the merits of the case, the Supreme Court addressed preliminary matters related to two applications for condonation for the late filing of certain documents, which were granted ([2]). The main issues emerged from allegations surrounding the trial court's decision not to allow cross-examination of the complainant regarding her previous sexual history and the failure of the prosecution to call a crucial witness, Desiree ([5], [8]).

**I – Issues:**  
The legal questions at stake included:

1. Whether the evidence of the complainant, as a single witness, was credible and adequately supported by the circumstances of the case ([1]).
2. Whether the prosecution's failure to call Desiree as a witness justified drawing an adverse inference against the State ([8]).
3. Whether the trial court erred in refusing to allow cross-examination of the complainant under section 227 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 concerning her previous sexual history ([25]).

These issues were critical for determining Rugnanan's prospects of success on appeal and the validity of the earlier denials for leave to appeal ([1]).

**R – Rules:**  
The court relied on several legal principles, notably:

1. **Exceptional Circumstances (Section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013):** This provision allows the President of the Supreme Court to refer decisions for reconsideration in exceptional circumstances ([3]–[4]). The court articulated that such circumstances must indicate potential grave injustices or risks to the administration of justice ([6]–[7]).
  
2. **Credibility of a Single Witness:** It is established that a conviction can rely solely on the evidence of a competent witness, though such evidence should be approached with caution ([23]). The credibility and reliability of the single witness’s testimony become pivotal findings ([20]).

3. **Cross-Examination and Prior Sexual History:** Under section 227 of the Criminal Procedure Act, cross-examination regarding a complainant's previous sexual experiences is permissible only if the court finds the evidence relevant to the case ([25]).

**A – Application:**  
In the application of law to facts, the Supreme Court evaluated the evidence presented at the trial, including the complainant's credible testimony and corroborating evidence from third parties, which included physical injuries corroborated by a medical examination ([14], [24]). The magistrate had found the complainant’s testimony to be coherent, consistent, and straightforward, which the Supreme Court recognized as a proper evaluation of credibility ([20]).

The applicant’s argument regarding the credibility of the complainant was dismissed, as the magistrate had approached her evidence with the necessary caution, confirming that her testimony was substantially satisfactory ([23]). The court emphasized that the initial magistrate had validly concluded that Desiree's absence did not undermine the State's case, particularly since the defence had the opportunity to consult with her later ([24]). This reflected a robust reasoning against the claims of the prosecution's failure to meet its evidentiary burdens.

Regarding cross-examination concerning prior sexual history, the Supreme Court found that the applicant had failed to establish that such history was relevant to his claim of consent; thus the magistrate's refusal was justified ([25]).

**C – Conclusion:**  
The Supreme Court, upholding the decisions of the previous courts, ultimately dismissed the application for reconsideration of the special leave to appeal due to a lack of identifiable exceptional circumstances ([28]). Rugnanan's conviction was deemed to have been upheld properly based on the evidence where the complainant's version consistently demonstrated her credibility against Rugnanan's unreliable testimony. By concluding that the State's case was formidable and Rugnanan’s appeals did not reflect a reasonable prospect of success, the court confirmed the integrity of the previous judicial findings and the established legal principles regarding the evaluation of witness credibility and evidentiary standards in sexual offences.
