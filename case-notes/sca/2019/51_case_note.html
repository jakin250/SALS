Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 51

F - Facts

The case arises from a series of convictions against Nceba Tyhulu in the Regional Court Paarl. He was found guilty of two counts under the Prevention and Combating of Corrupt Activities Act of 2004 (“POCCA”) and two counts concerning the Drugs and Drugs Trafficking Act 40 of 1992 ([2]). Tyhulu was sentenced on 13 July 2015 to a cumulative term of 13 years’ imprisonment, which included 10 years for the first two counts and 3 years for the latter ([2]).

After his convictions, Tyhulu sought leave to appeal the verdicts and sentences. His initial application was refused by the magistrate, leading him to petition the Judge President of the Western Cape High Court under section 309B of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977. This petition was also dismissed ([3]). A subsequent appeal against this dismissal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was granted special leave ([4]).

I - Issues

The principal issue to be decided was whether the appellant had a reasonable prospect of success on appeal, which is a criterion distinct from the merits of the underlying case ([5]). Specifically, the court examined whether Tyhulu could demonstrate realistic chances of succeeding on appeal concerning the convictions for counts 3 and 4, where the charge was dealing in drugs, as opposed to simple possession ([5]).

R - Rules

In determining the appeal, the court relied on established legal principles regarding the granting of leave to appeal. It was emphasized that the inquiry was not about the strength of the appeal itself but whether there exists a reasonable prospect of success ([6]). The case referenced precedents including S v Khoasana and S v Matshona, which affirm that the grounds for appeal must not be remote but should imply a blunt chance of yielding a different result ([4]).

Further, the court noted that the burden rested on the appellant to convince it that there are adequate grounds indicative of a likelihood of success ([5]).

A - Application

In its deliberation, the SCA carefully evaluated the merits presented by Tyhulu, particularly his claim with regard to coercion by undercover police agents and financial inducements ([6]). The court expressed its view that there was little prospect of another court reaching a different conclusion on the corruption counts due to the overwhelming evidence against Tyhulu, hence dismissing the appeal concerning counts 1 and 2 ([7]).

Conversely, for counts 3 and 4, the court found that the evidence might not have sufficiently established that Tyhulu dealt in drugs beyond a reasonable doubt. The contention was raised that the evidence may better align with a conviction for possession instead of dealing, hence finding reasonable grounds to allow for an appeal on those counts ([7]). The potential for a re-evaluation of the sentence imposed on those counts was also noted, as differing conclusions about the nature of the conviction could influence the sentence ([7]).

C - Conclusion

The court ultimately upheld Tyhulu's appeal regarding counts 3 and 4, as well as the associated sentences, recognizing that there were reasonable prospects that another court could reverse the convictions based on the evidence. Conversely, the appeal concerning counts 1 and 2 was dismissed. The SCA provided a new order allowing Tyhulu to appeal to the Western Cape High Court regarding the convictions and sentences related to counts 3 and 4, thus underlining the procedural significance of the application for leave to appeal being granted based on merit evaluated at this stage ([8]).

The implications of this ruling reiterate the judicial system's commitment to ensuring that accusers have reasonable avenues for appeal available, particularly where questions of evidence and legal interpretation arise. The case emphasizes the necessity for courts to maintain a balanced approach in impartially adjudicating petitions for appeal, guarding against the finality of convictions in light of potential judicial errors.
