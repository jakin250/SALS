Constitutional Court

M Fredericks and 47 Others v MEC for Education and Training, Eastern Cape

After receiving a positive certificate under rule 18, the High Court acknowledged that the case involved substantial constitutional matters warranting the Constitutional Court's intervention ([2]).

Constitutional Court 2001

F - Facts

In M Fredericks and 47 Others v MEC for Education and Training, the applicants, a group of 48 teachers employed by the Eastern Cape Department of Education, sought to challenge the refusal of their applications for voluntary retrenchment submitted in late 1996 ([1]). Initially, these applications were granted, but from December 1996 onwards, the department altered its approval process, leading to numerous refusals without providing reasons ([6]–[7]). The applicants claimed this constituted a breach of their constitutional rights under sections 9 (equality) and 33 (administrative justice) of the South African Constitution ([11]). They filed a complaint in the Eastern Cape High Court, which ultimately ruled that it lacked jurisdiction to address the matter, determining that the Labour Relations Act, 66 of 1995 had ousted its authority on the issue ([1][2]).

After receiving a positive certificate under rule 18, the High Court acknowledged that the case involved substantial constitutional matters warranting the Constitutional Court's intervention ([2]). The dispute involved the interpretation of the Labour Relations Act and its interaction with the Constitution's jurisdictional provisions.

I - Issues

The case presents two principal issues for determination ([4]):

  1. Does the claim of the applicants raise a "constitutional matter" under sections 167 and 169 of the Constitution?
  2. If so, does the High Court possess jurisdiction to adjudicate this constitutional matter?

The issues arise from the contention that the applicants' claims, based on alleged constitutional violations, may have been improperly categorized by the High Court as purely labour disputes ([4]).

R - Rules

The following constitutional provisions are central to the case:

  • Section 169 of the Constitution, which provides that High Courts may decide any constitutional matter except those exclusively assigned to the Constitutional Court or assigned to another court with similar status.
  • Section 167(3) states that the Constitutional Court is the highest court for constitutional matters and determines whether a matter is constitutional ([3]).

Additionally, the Labour Relations Act, 66 of 1995 plays a crucial role, specifically:

  • Section 24, which deals with the jurisdiction over disputes arising from collective agreements and requires that such disputes be conciliated by the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA) ([13]).

A - Application

The court examined whether the applicants' claims constituted a constitutional matter and whether the High Court's jurisdiction was restricted by the Labour Relations Act.

The court held that the claims indeed raised constitutional matters since they involved rights protected under sections 9 and 33 of the Constitution ([32]). The court emphasized that the applicants were not merely asserting contractual rights under Resolution 3, but rather alleging violations of constitutional rights due to the administrative decisions of the State ([11], [33]).

It clarified the High Court's jurisdiction by reinforcing that the referral of disputes to the CCMA under section 24 does not itself exclude the High Court's jurisdiction over constitutional matters related to employment ([29]–[30]). The High Court previously concluded that section 24 ousted its jurisdiction based on the erroneous premise that the CCMA functions as a court of equivalent status to the High Court [[32]–[33]].

The court also analyzed the transitional provisions of the Labour Relations Act, suggesting that the dispute arose under the new Act, thereby necessitating adherence to its procedures but not restricting High Court jurisdiction for constitutional issues ([34]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded that the High Court was incorrect in determining it lacked jurisdiction over the dispute. The appeal was upheld, and the matter was referred back to the High Court for a decision on the merits of the applicants' claims ([45]).

The ruling underscores the independence of constitutional jurisdiction and clarifies the interplay between administrative actions of state bodies and constitutional rights, specifically highlighting that legislative provisions should not limit access to the High Court for constitutional matters. The entitlement to fair administrative procedures and equality before the law was affirmed, reflecting broader implications for employment law and administrative justice in South Africa.
