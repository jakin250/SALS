Supreme Court of Appeal

Minister of Police and Others v Samuel Molokwane (730/2021) [2022] ZASCA 111

This appeal arose from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, concerning a dispute initiated by Samuel Molokwane against the Minister of Police and three officers (the second, third, and fourth appellants).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 111

F – Facts

This appeal arose from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, concerning a dispute initiated by Samuel Molokwane against the Minister of Police and three officers (the second, third, and fourth appellants). Molokwane alleged he was wrongfully arrested and assaulted on February 8, 2014, and subsequently instituted action on December 2, 2015, claiming damages ([1]).

The core issue relevant to the appeal was the validity of the summons served on the Minister of Police. While all three police officers were served with the summons, the Minister was only served at his office, and not per the statutory requirement imposed by s 2(2) of the State Liability Act 20 of 1957 (the State Liability Act), which mandated service upon the State Attorney within seven days of the summons being issued ([2]–[4]).

Despite the non-compliance, a default judgment in favor of Molokwane was granted on March 4, 2016, and the Minister was informed of this order on March 22, 2016. Subsequently, the State Attorney entered the proceedings, raising special pleas regarding the validity of the summons based on non-service, and the alleged expiration of the claim due to prescription, as the claim had not been served on the State Attorney within three years post-cause of action ([5]–[7]).

The High Court dismissed these special pleas, reasoning that while service on the State Attorney is procedurally significant, its absence did not render the summons a nullity. Instead, it constituted an irregular step that was moot since the State Attorney subsequently engaged in the proceedings ([9]).

I – Issues

The appeal presented two central legal issues:

  1. Validity of the summons: Whether the failure to serve the summons on the State Attorney invalidated the summons despite it being served on the Minister ([1]).
  2. Prescription: Whether the respondent's claim had prescribed by the time it was served, thus extinguishing the claim ([7]).

R – Rules

The case prominently involved the interpretation of s 2(2) of the State Liability Act, which mandates that a copy of any summons against the Minister must be served within seven days on the State Attorney ([3]):

  • Section 2(2) of the State Liability Act: Specifies the procedure for service upon members of the national executive, asserting mandatory compliance.
  • Prescription Act 68 of 1969: Specifically s 11(d), which notes that claims prescribed after three years if not enforced, and s 15(1), which states that prescription is interrupted by service of process on the debtor ([22]).

In previous judgments, the courts have laid foundational principles around compliance, noting a shift from strict interpretation of "mandatory" versus "directory" provisions toward a more purposive approach in interpretation, seeking to achieve the underlying legislative intent ([11]–[16]).

A – Application

The court embarked on a comprehensive analysis of the legislative intent behind the requirements of serving the summons. It established that the purpose of s 2(2) is to ensure the Minister receives appropriate legal representation and that service on the Minister sufficed for this purpose since the Minister did not show any demonstrated prejudice from the non-service on the State Attorney ([19]). The court underscored that the State Attorney subsequently represented the Minister effectively, filing documentation and entering appearances on behalf of the Minister.

The appellants' claim of prescription was also dismissed. The court noted that service on the Minister was sufficient to interrupt prescription, citing previous jurisprudence affirming that even if service does not adhere strictly to statutory requirements, if the state is aware and can respond adequately, the purpose of the legislation has been satisfied ([24]).

The judicial interpretation emphasized a disregard for rigid formalism in procedural compliance, aligning instead with a broad constitutional interpretation that fosters access to justice, particularly through the lens of the right to fair trial as encompassed in s 34 of the Constitution ([17]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court's decision, dismissing the appeal with costs. The ruling reaffirmed that the failure to serve the summons on the State Attorney did not nullify the action initiated against the Minister, and that the respondent's claim had not prescribed.

This judgment underscores an evolving approach in South African jurisprudence, favoring substantive justice and access to courts over procedural technicalities. It elucidates the necessity for legal representatives and litigants to engage meaningfully within procedural constraints but without elevating compliance over the fundamental right to seek redress in a court of law ([25]).

The implications of this ruling are significant, reinforcing the need for courts to adopt a purposive approach when interpreting legislative provisions, particularly in matters involving the State's liability, thereby promoting access to justice while balancing procedural rigor.
