Supreme Court of Appeal

Dos Santos and another v The State (726/07) [2010] ZASCA 73 (27 May 2010)

The case involved two appellants, Antonio Cesar Alves Dos Santos and Sikhonjwa Derick Mbatha, implicated in illicit diamond trading activities in Port Nolloth, South Africa.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2010 [2010] ZASCA 73 (27 May 2010)

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–7])

The case involved two appellants, Antonio Cesar Alves Dos Santos and Sikhonjwa Derick Mbatha, implicated in illicit diamond trading activities in Port Nolloth, South Africa. The area was identified as a hotspot for illegal diamond dealing due to its proximity to diamond mines ([1]). The first appellant, Dos Santos, was a business manager at Tony's Auto Spares, which served as the focal point for the alleged criminal activities. A task force from the now-disbanded Directorate of Special Operations initiated surveillance on Dos Santos in early 2003, using sophisticated video equipment to monitor his transactions involving unpolished diamonds ([2–3]).

On January 22, 2003, the South African Police Service (SAPS), backed by a search warrant, seized numerous unpolished diamonds, cash, and other paraphernalia associated with diamond dealing from Dos Santos's premises ([3]). Dos Santos faced 61 counts under the Diamonds Act along with charges under the Riotous Assemblies Act and the Prevention of Organised Crime Act (POCA). His associate, Mbatha, was arrested later and charged with similar offenses ([5–6]).

During the trial, the State relied heavily on the testimonies of accomplices, particularly Tim and Aubrey Basson, who linked the appellants to the illicit diamond transactions. The trial judge ultimately convicted both appellants, resulting in an eight-year sentence for Dos Santos and a fine with conditional suspension for Mbatha ([7]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 8–12])

The primary legal issues in the case revolved around the following:

  1. Admissibility of Evidence: Whether evidence obtained through a search warrant, which was technically defective under the Constitution, could be admissible under s 35(5) of the Constitution. This section prohibits the admission of evidence gained through violations of rights if it would render the trial unfair or undermine the administration of justice ([20–21]).
  2. Validity of Racketeering Charges: Whether the convictions under POCA and the Diamonds Act constituted improper splitting of charges that could lead to duplicative convictions, given the evidence presented for both the racketeering and diamond dealing offenses ([40–43]).
  3. Cautionary Rule for Accomplice Testimony: The court needed to evaluate the reliability of the accomplices' testimonies, given that their motivations and culpability were under scrutiny ([18]).
  4. Disparity in Sentencing: The appropriateness of the sentences imposed on the two appellants, particularly whether the sentence for Mbatha was grossly divergent compared to that of Dos Santos for similar offenses ([47]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 19–24])

  1. Constitutional Framework: Section 35(5) of the South African Constitution stipulates the criteria under which evidence obtained unconstitutionally may be excluded, notably emphasizing the necessity for fairness in trials ([20]).
  2. Prevention of Organised Crime Act (POCA): The statutory requirement for establishing a “pattern of racketeering activity” necessitates proof of ongoing illegal activities. The prosecution must demonstrate that the defendant was involved in the management of the enterprise ([36–38]).
  3. Cautionary Rule Regarding Accomplices: Established in case law, this rule requires that the testimony of accomplices be scrutinized with particular care due to potential self-interest and deceit. The need for corroboration is paramount in evaluating such testimonies ([18–19]).
  4. Sentencing Principles: The court must exercise discretion in sentencing while ensuring fairness and proportionality when evaluating the conduct and culpability of each defendant, as outlined in prior cases regarding sentencing criteria ([47]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 24–35])

  1. Admissibility of Evidence: The court reviewed whether the search warrant, despite being technically flawed, justified the admission of evidence collected during the search. The trial court's finding that the evidence did not render the trial unfair was upheld, given that the police acted in good faith and sought judicial approval for their actions ([21–23]).
  2. Racketeering Convictions: The court concluded that the prosecution properly established that Dos Santos's activities constituted a "pattern of racketeering" under POCA. The connection between the various offenses and the management of the illicit diamond enterprise was sufficiently demonstrated through corroborative testimony and physical evidence, thus dismissing claims of improper splitting of charges ([36–41]).
  3. Cautionary Approach to Accomplice Testimony: The court acknowledged the necessity of a cautionary outlook regarding the Bassons' testimonies. However, it ultimately found their accounts credible when corroborated by video evidence and other documents, which collectively lent weight to their statements ([25–28]).
  4. Disparity in Sentencing: The court found that the sentence imposed on Mbatha was disturbingly inappropriate compared to Dos Santos's, considering both were equally culpable. The appellate court adjusted Mbatha's sentence, seeking to align it with the nature and gravity of the offenses committed by both appellants ([47]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 48–48])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately altered the convictions of both appellants to reflect the appropriate sections of the Diamonds Act. The court upheld the trial court's convictions but corrected the misapplication of sections for both Dos Santos and Mbatha. It found that the significant evidence against the appellants, corroborated by reliable testimonies and the integrity of the investigative procedures employed by law enforcement, justified the convictions. The court's decision regarding the admission of evidence reinforced the balance between fairness to the accused and the public interest in prosecuting criminal conduct adequately.

The case has significant implications for the interpretation of admissibility rules under constitutional provisions, the prosecution of organized crime, and standards for evaluating accomplice testimony. It illustrates a robust application of legal principles in upholding the rule of law while addressing the challenges posed by organized crime in South Africa.
