Source Folder: 2006

# Case Note: Nwamitwa Shilubana v Nwamitwa [2006] SCA 174 (RSA)

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts ([1]–[56])

The case involves a succession dispute among the Valoyi tribe in Limpopo, South Africa, following the death of the chief, Hosi Mahlathini Richard Nwamitwa ([1]). The first appellant, TLP Nwamitwa Shilubana, and the respondent, Sidwell Nwamitwa, are cousins vying for the chieftaincy. The genealogical background indicates that the chieftaincy has traditionally descended patrilineally based on male primogeniture for five generations ([5]).

Following the death of Hosi Richard in October 2001, the second appellant was appointed Acting Hosi while discussions ensued regarding succession. The premier and traditional authorities supported the first appellant's claim for appointment based on a perceived change in customs and the constitutional framework that allows women to hold leadership positions. Conversely, the respondent argued he was the rightful successor per prior custom and based on a letter from his father indicating his disinterest in transferring the chieftaincy to his niece ([7], [9]).

The court a quo found in favor of the respondent, declaring him the rightful heir and invalidating the first appellant's claim as Hosi. This ruling led to the present appeal ([1], [2]).

### I – Issues ([17]–[56])

The primary legal issue revolves around the constitutionality of succession practices based on customary law. Specifically, the court had to address:
1. Whether the traditional understanding of the chieftainship could legally accommodate a female successor post-1994 ([19]); 
2. Whether the appointments and customs instituted by the Royal Family duly conformed to the traditional customs and the contemporary constitutional framework ([20]–[25]);
3. Whether the ruling of the court a quo complied with section 9 of the South African Constitution, which guarantees equality and prohibits discrimination based on gender ([36]).

### R – Rules ([19]–[51])

The court primarily relied on:
1. **Customary Law**: It reiterated the traditional norms regarding succession in the Valoyi tribe, affirming the historical precedence of male primogeniture as the operational standard until challenged by newer constitutional interpretations ([19], [46]).
2. **South African Constitution**: The court examined section 9, which stipulates the right to equality, assessing whether the customary rule barring female succession had evolved or could be interpreted to allow for gender inclusiveness ([36]–[39]).
3. **Promulgation of Administrative Justice**: Under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act, the court considered the appropriateness of its orders against the administrative powers retained by traditional authorities ([53]).


### A – Application ([47]–[52])

In applying the law to the facts, the court found that:
1. The concession by the first appellant that historically, a female could not become Hosi aligned with the traditional practices ([20]).
2. The Royal Family’s attempts to appoint the first appellant as Hosi did not adhere to established customs, which typically required that a Hosi be born rather than elected, thereby constituting an overreach of their powers ([50]).
3. The court rejected the notion that historical precedents could be dismissed or retroactively altered due to the principles established in the new Constitution. It reinforced that the customs surrounding succession were inherently steeped in tradition, requiring not just a formal acknowledgment but adherence to the longstanding practice ([51]).

Thus, the court determined that even if a gender-equal succession principle had been informally adapted, the specific appointment of the first appellant did not meet the traditional requirements for legitimacy in terms of lineage and custom ([50], [51]).

### C – Conclusion ([56])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the findings of the court a quo, concluding that the appointment of the first appellant did not conform to the customs and traditions of the Valoyi tribe, thus affirming that the respondent remains the rightful chieftain. It emphasized that while constitutional principles support gender equality, the essence of customary law must be respected unless laws are clearly articulated to evolve with contemporary societal norms. The court’s holding indicates a complex interplay between constitutional imperatives and traditional customs, inviting future considerations on how customary laws should adapt to align with modern legal standards. The appeal was dismissed with costs ([56]).
