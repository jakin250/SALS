Supreme Court of Appeal

EB Steam Company (Pty) Ltd v Eskom Holdings Soc Ltd (979/2012) [2013] ZASCA 167

The winding-up applications were contested solely on the grounds of improper service to the employees of the companies, as mandated by section 346(4A) of the 1973 Act.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 167

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

This appeal arises from final winding-up orders issued by Tsoka J concerning twenty companies that are subsidiaries of EB Steam Holdings (Pty) Ltd ([1]). The winding-up was prompted by the subsidiaries' failure to settle arbitration awards totaling various amounts on November 1, 2011. Subsequent to these awards, a notice was sent under section 345(1)(a) of the Companies Act 61 of 1973 to each company, but it failed to elicit any payment. Consequently, a winding-up application was made under sections 346(1) and 344(f) of the 1973 Act. Notably, although the 1973 Act was repealed, sections relevant to this case were preserved under section 9 of Schedule 5 of the Companies Act 71 of 2008.

The winding-up applications were contested solely on the grounds of improper service to the employees of the companies, as mandated by section 346(4A) of the 1973 Act. This provision explicitly requires that a copy of the application be furnished to every registered trade union representing employees, as well as directly to the employees themselves, among others ([2]). The applications were served on the companies at their registered office, but the affidavit detailing service was insufficient regarding the employees' involvement ([3]).

The appellant companies argued that service was defective since the sheriff merely affixed the application papers to the door of the registered office, which did not adequately meet statutory requirements. The absence of specified employees' information in the founding affidavit further complicated the case ([4]–[5]).

I – Issues

The primary legal question concerns whether the appellant companies properly complied with the service requirements outlined in section 346(4A) of the Companies Act 61 of 1973, specifically focusing on the nature of service to employees ([2], [5]). Additionally, the merits of the claim related to the grounds for winding-up—whether compliance with section 346(4A) is a peremptory requirement that invalidates a winding-up application if not met—remain at the forefront ([4]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal provisions can be extracted from:

  • Companies Act 61 of 1973, as amended (specifically sections 345 and 346).
  • Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995, which introduced provisions ensuring employees are informed and can protect their interests.
  • Section 346(4A), which requires applications to be served on employees either through a trade union or through alternative methods detailed in the Act ([2], [5]).

The case discusses the intention behind the legislative provisions and their purpose: to enhance transparency for employees facing potential insolvency and ensure they have a voice in the winding-up process ([7], [23]).

A – Application

The court scrutinized the appropriateness of the service methods employed. It acknowledged that while the requirement to furnish application papers to employees and other specified parties is peremptory, the varied methods prescribed for doing so could be interpreted as directory rather than strictly mandatory ([19]). The court emphasized that the primary objective of these provisions is to ensure that employees are informed of proceedings that directly affect them, rather than to impose a rigid procedural bar ([20], [23]).

It was found that the sheriff's method of service—affixing papers to the front door—did not adequately fulfill the aim of reaching all employees, given that the facts indicated the companies operated across various locations without employee representation at the registered office ([27]). The court indicated that a failure of service that left doubt concerning employees’ awareness necessitated a provisional winding-up order to address the statutory purpose effectively ([25]).

Moreover, if the methods prescribed were impractical, the court concluded that it had the authority to direct alternative service methods, allowing a flexible approach that aligns with the statutory intent ([21], [27]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately set aside the final winding-up orders and substituted them with provisional orders, requiring the appellants to furnish the application papers to employees within a stipulated timeframe. This decision emphasizes the court's commitment to ensuring that employees are adequately informed in winding-up procedures while allowing a mechanism for the efficient continuation of the process ([29]). This case underscores the balance between procedural compliance and the overarching aim to protect employee interests in insolvency situations, reinforcing the necessity of adhering to statutory obligations while providing courts the discretion to address practical challenges.

The outcome serves as an important precedent for future cases involving winding-up applications, highlighting the necessity for applicants to meticulously comply with statutory disclosure requirements and for courts to exercise their inherent powers to supervise the adequacy of service in the interest of fairness ([24], [28]).
