Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

### Case Note: Premier: Limpopo Province v Speaker: Limpopo Provincial Legislature & Others

#### FIRAC Structure

**F - Facts:**
The case concerns an application made by the Premier of Limpopo Province (the Premier) in relation to the Financial Management of the Limpopo Provincial Legislature Bill of 2009 (the Bill). The Bill was passed by the Limpopo Provincial Legislature (the Provincial Legislature) on November 24, 2009, and was submitted to the Premier for assent on December 8, 2009 ([2]–[3]). The Premier expressed reservations regarding the constitutionality of the Bill, particularly questioning the authority of the Provincial Legislature to legislate on financial management matters ([10]). The Premier subsequently referred the Bill back to the Provincial Legislature for reconsideration, but upon its return without amendments, the Premier referred the matter to the Constitutional Court in January 2010 ([4]).

The Bill is fundamentally aimed at regulating the financial management of the Provincial Legislature, ensuring efficient and transparent management of revenue, expenditure, assets, and liabilities ([7]). The Premier, alongside the Speakers of the National Assembly and National Council of Provinces, as well as the Minister for Finance, filed affidavits contending that the Bill was unconstitutional, given that the financial management of provincial legislatures was not expressly assigned to them by national legislation or included in the functional areas specified in the constitution ([5]–[6]).

**I - Issues:**
The key issues to be determined include:
1. Whether the Provincial Legislature has the authority to legislate on matters concerning its own financial management as per the constitutional provisions ([30]).
2. Whether the Section 3 of the Financial Management of Parliament Act, 2009 (FMPA) expressly assigns the power to regulate financial management to provincial legislatures as stipulated under Section 104(1)(b)(iii) of the Constitution ([32]).
3. Whether financial management of provincial legislatures can be considered a matter for which the Constitution envisages the enactment of provincial legislation as provided in Section 104(1)(b)(iv) ([32]).

**R - Rules:**
The relevant legal provisions include:
- **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa**: Particularly Section 104(1) regarding the legislative authority of provinces, and Sections 213 to 222 addressing general financial matters ([20]–[25]).
- **Financial Management of Parliament Act, 2009**: Specifically Sections 2(e) and 3, addressing the obligations of provincial legislatures with respect to financial management norms and standards ([27]–[28]).

**A - Application:**
The Court undertook a detailed analysis of the powers assigned to provincial legislatures under the South African Constitution, emphasizing that such authority must be clearly articulated either through specific constitutional provisions or express assignment through national legislation. 

1. The analysis established that financial management does not fall under the functional areas listed in Schedules 4 and 5 of the Constitution. Therefore, for the Provincial Legislature to legislate on financial matters, such power must be expressly assigned by national legislation or envisaged in the Constitution ([30]–[31]). 
   
2. It was ultimately determined that the wording of the FMPA, especially Section 3 concerning adherence to norms and standards, does not constitute an express delegation of power to provincial legislatures to independently enact financial management legislation ([43]–[49]). 

3. The Court further evaluated whether provisions of Sections 195, 215, and 216 of the Constitution might allow provincial legislatures to legislate on financial management. It concluded that these sections do not authorise provincial legislation in the manner contemplated by Section 104(1)(b)(iv) since they do not express a need for provincial law on financial management ([51]–[59]).

**C - Conclusion:**
The Constitutional Court ruled that the Financial Management of the Limpopo Provincial Legislature Bill, 2009, is unconstitutional due to the Limpopo Provincial Legislature's lack of authority to legislate on financial management matters ([60]). However, the Court addressed broader implications by recognizing similar legislation passed by other provinces, ultimately deciding to retain the matter regarding those statutes for further examination, acknowledging the potential need for provincial legislatures to regulate their financial management processes in the future ([60]–[66]).

The ruling sets a crucial precedent regarding the limits of provincial legislative power and reinforces the necessity for clarity in the assignment of legislative competences within South Africa’s constitutional framework. The decision highlights that while the provincial legislatures are indeed organs of state, their legislative powers are confined to those explicitly stated or prescribed in national legislation and the Constitution, thus upholding the constitutional principle of separation of powers.
