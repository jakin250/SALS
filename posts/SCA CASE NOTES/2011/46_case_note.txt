Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: The State v Romer (412/10) [2011] ZASCA 46

### F – Facts
In **The State v Romer (412/10)**, Mr. Stephen Romer was convicted of one count of murder and two counts of attempted murder by Jansen J in the Eastern Cape High Court, Port Elizabeth. The incidents in question occurred on 17 October 2007, when Romer shot three individuals in Port Elizabeth, resulting in the death of Mr. G du Mordt and serious injuries to Ms. K Heuer and Mr. E G Janse ([1]–[2]). The court found that at the time of the shootings, Romer was in a state of diminished responsibility, although not acting as an automaton, and subsequently imposed a sentence of ten years’ imprisonment, wholly suspended for five years, along with three years of correctional supervision under section 276(1)(h) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([1]).

The state appealed against this sentence, arguing that it was disturbingly lenient given the severity of Romer’s actions ([3]). Before examining the appeal's merits, the court addressed a preliminary issue regarding Romer’s application to introduce further evidence on appeal, aimed at contesting the sentence. This evidence included affidavits from Ms. A Ferreira, a social worker monitoring Romer’s community service, and Dr. Y Lucire, a psychiatrist, asserting that Romer had been suffering from insane automatism at the time of the offenses ([4]–[6]).

The court found the proposed evidence insufficiently relevant to the sentencing question, as it pertained to Romer's conduct after sentencing, rather than the appropriateness of the sentence imposed initially ([10]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues in this case included:
1. Whether the court's discretion in imposing the sentence was exercised appropriately given Romer’s state of diminished responsibility and the serious nature of the offenses ([18]–[20]).
2. Whether the trial court misdirected itself, leading to a disturbingly lenient sentence that warranted appellate intervention ([23]–[24]).
3. The relevance and admissibility of further evidence offered by Romer pertinent to the appeal ([6]–[10]).

### R – Rules
The court relied on several key legal principles, including:
- The discretion of trial courts to impose sentences is recognized, with appellate courts only intervening if the sentence is disturbingly inappropriate, disproportionate, or based on a material misdirection ([22]–[23]).
- The provisions of section 22(a) of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959 grant the appellate court the power to receive further evidence, but this is subject to stringent criteria, ensuring fairness and relevance to the appeal ([8]–[10]).
- The doctrine of diminished responsibility was central to Romer's case, influencing how courts view the gravity of crimes committed under conditions that impair full culpability ([18]–[20]).

### A – Application
In applying these rules to the facts of the case, the court first validated the trial court's emphasis on Romer’s diminished responsibility as a critical factor in determining the appropriateness of the sentence. The court considered the recommendations from probation officers and the evidence presented, noting Romer’s contrition, his history of mental illness, and efforts towards rehabilitation ([20]).

The court highlighted that while Romer's actions were objectively horrific, differentiating his mental state from that of an individual lacking diminished capacity was crucial. The absence of prior violent behavior and Romer’s significant personal struggles contributed to the trial judge's decision to impose a non-custodial sentence, which the appellate court found within reasonable bounds ([26]–[32]).

In dismissing the application to introduce further evidence, the court noted that the evidence sought to be adduced was either irrelevant or incapable of being adequately tested at the appellate level. The court emphasized that evidence addressing Romer's post-sentencing integration into society did not pertain to the initial sentencing decision ([10]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the trial court's sentencing decision, concluding that the trial judge had not committed a misdirection and that the sentence, while lenient, was not disturbingly so. The court reasoned that Romer's circumstances and diminished responsibility merited a more rehabilitative approach rather than punitive imprisonment ([32]). This case reinforces the principles of individualized sentencing, particularly in instances where mental health considerations significantly influence culpability and risk of reoffending.

Both the application for leave to introduce further evidence and the State's appeal against Romer’s sentence were dismissed, affirming the trial court's discretion in this sensitive matter ([32]). The implications of this ruling highlight the judiciary's commitment to balancing public safety against the principles of rehabilitation and justice for those with mental health issues.
