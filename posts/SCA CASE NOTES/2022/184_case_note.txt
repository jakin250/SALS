Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: MEC for the Department of Public Works, Eastern Cape and Another v Ikamva Architects CC (544/2021)

## F – Facts

The case stems from a series of legal proceedings involving the MEC for the Department of Public Works and the MEC for the Department of Health of the Eastern Cape (hereinafter referred to as "the Departments") and Ikamva Architects CC (hereinafter referred to as "Ikamva"). The Departments sought relief from the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court regarding a contract between them and Ikamva that had been concluded in September 2003 for architectural services related to the Frere Hospital project ([paras 4–5]). 

The Departments alleged that the appointment of Ikamva was invalid, citing that the procurement process violated constitutional requirements of fairness, equity, transparency, and competitiveness, as mandated by s 217 of the Constitution and the Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act, 2000. Following a series of procedural missteps, Ikamva sued for damages after the Departments abandoned the contract, leading to the granting of a default judgment in favor of Ikamva for R41,031,279.58 ([paras 7–9]).

The Departments subsequently sought to appeal the default judgment and rescind it, arguments that were consistently rejected. In September 2019, they initiated a self-review application to set aside the impugned decisions that led to Ikamva's appointment and the default judgment ([paras 8–10]). Further, their application for leave to appeal was met with judicial scrutiny regarding its legal basis and procedural adherence, culminating in their appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 1–4]).

## I – Issues 

The core legal issues presented in the appeal revolved around: 

1. **The validity of the default judgment**—Whether the default judgment was competent, valid, and binding ([para 9]).
   
2. **Discretion under s 172(1)(b) of the Constitution**—Whether the Departments could successfully argue for a just and equitable order that would effectively prohibit Ikamva from executing the default judgment ([para 14]).
   
3. **Procedural Compliance**—If the Departments' failure to follow procedural rules warranted dismissal of their application for leave to appeal ([para 2]).

## R – Rules

The Supreme Court of Appeal relied on several pertinent legal principles including:

1. **Section 172 of the Constitution**—This section dictates that a court must declare laws or conduct inconsistent with the Constitution invalid (s 172(1)(a)) and has discretion to make just and equitable orders (s 172(1)(b)) ([para 12]).

2. **Precedents on Just and Equitable Relief**—The court referenced *Bengwenyama Minerals (Pty) Ltd v Genorah Resources (Pty) Ltd* and *State Information Technology Agency SOC Limited v Gijima Holdings (Pty) Limited* to outline the discretionary nature of just and equitable orders ([paras 15-16]).

3. **Enforceability of Court Orders**—Section 165(5) of the Constitution mandates that court orders must be complied with until set aside, establishing a clear principle against interfering with the enforcement of valid and binding judgments ([paras 27-28]).

## A – Application

The court evaluated the Departments' arguments against established legal principles. They contended that an order under s 172(1)(b) was justified to avoid further payments to Ikamva under a contract they claimed was invalid. However, the court found no compelling legal authority to support their request to neutralize an existing valid judgment ([paras 19–20]). 

In assessing the Departments' arguments, the court noted that the requested order would essentially negate a binding judgment without due process for setting it aside ([paras 26–28]). The court underscored the necessity for courts to uphold their authority and the finality of judgments, indicating that lawful judicial decisions should not be rendered nugatory by subsequent orders unless through recognized legal channels (rescission or appeal) ([paras 30–34]).

Furthermore, the court clarified that the Departments' case for just and equitable relief was undercut by the absence of any precedents wherein courts granted an order that would contradict a previously issued valid court order. Consequently, they deemed the Departments’ failure to demonstrate a reasonable prospect of success pivotal to their application, confirming that no such relief could exist.

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the application for leave to appeal with costs, affirming the validity and binding nature of the default judgment granted in favor of Ikamva. The court emphasized the foundational constitutional tenets that underlie the enforceability of court orders, declaring that the requested order would contravene these principles ([paras 36–37]). The judgment reaffirmed the importance of judicial finality, the authority of judicial orders, and the necessity for compliance—vital components for upholding the rule of law in South Africa.
