Supreme Court of Appeal

City of Cape Town v Mtyido (Case no. 1272/2022) [2023] ZASCA 163

Relevant precedents in this case include: Mashongwa v Passenger Rail Agency of South Africa [2015] ZACC 36 regarding the standard for reversing factual findings by a trial court ([23]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 163

F – Facts

The respondent, Nqulelwa Mtyido, sustained injuries when she fell into an open manhole on Khwezi Street, Mfuleni, on 17 October 2013. Mtyido was walking along the street to fetch water, unaware of the manhole’s presence, as it was dark and she was unfamiliar with the area ([1]). The City of Cape Town, as the appellant, was sued for damages resulting from this incident. The lower courts found the appellant liable due to the negligent maintenance of municipal infrastructure ([1]-[2]). The appellant appealed the full court's decision, which upheld the trial court’s ruling ([2]).

I – Issues

The principal legal issues involved in this case include:

  1. Whether the appellant was negligent in failing to maintain the manhole sufficiently, thus causing injury to the respondent ([3]).
  2. Whether the trial court correctly evaluated the evidence presented regarding the open manhole and the circumstances surrounding the injury ([2], [22]).
  3. The determination of wrongfulness and the appellant’s duty of care towards members of the public ([32]-[34]).

R – Rules

In establishing negligence under the delictual framework:

  • The plaintiff must show the existence of a legal duty, a breach of that duty, causation, and damages ([3], [45]).
  • Wrongfulness arises from the breach of a legal duty, considering the community’s legal convictions ([36]-[38]).
  • The test for whether an actor's conduct can be deemed negligent is set out in Kruger v Coetzee 1966 (2) SA 428 (A), relating to foreseeability and the duty to take reasonable care ([45]).

Relevant precedents in this case include:

  • Mashongwa v Passenger Rail Agency of South Africa [2015] ZACC 36 regarding the standard for reversing factual findings by a trial court ([23]).
  • Stellenbosch Farmers Winery Group Ltd v Martell Et Cie 2003 (1) SA 11 (SCA) concerning the appropriate standards for resolving factual disputes ([22]).

A – Application

The court meticulously assessed the evidence presented at trial. The respondent's account, supported by Mr. Xwayi’s testimony about reporting the open manhole before the incident, was deemed credible and was accepted in full by both the trial court and the full court ([4]-[19]). The testimonies highlighted significant discrepancies regarding the condition of the manhole, bolstering the respondent's claims against the appellant ([19], [25]).

The appellant's witnesses provided an alternative narrative, asserting that the manhole cover had never been missing. However, their arguments relied on procedural inaccuracies and assumptions rather than compelling evidence ([19]-[20]). The trial court concluded that the appellant had not provided the documentation that could corroborate its claims of maintenance and oversight ([20]). These failures pointed toward a lack of due diligence, which constituted negligence.

In discussing wrongfulness, the court noted that merely having control over municipal infrastructure does not automatically establish legal duty; rather, prior knowledge of the dangerous condition of the manhole played a pivotal role in this case ([32]-[40]). The community’s expectations for municipal accountability in safeguarding public safety further reinforced the finding of wrongfulness resulting from the appellant’s inaction once the hazard was reported.

C – Conclusion

The appeal by the City of Cape Town was dismissed with costs, including the costs of two counsel. The courts reaffirmed that the respondent had met her burden of proof regarding negligence, establishing a breach of duty on the part of the appellant ([48]-[49]). The decision underscored the importance of municipalities maintaining safe public infrastructure, emphasizing the necessity of prompt action in response to reported hazards. The implications of this ruling may extend to increasing scrutiny on municipal responsibilities and the procedural integrity surrounding infrastructure management, ensuring that taxpayer safety and welfare remain a priority in public service operations.
