Supreme Court of Appeal

Van der Merwe v National Director of Public Prosecutions [2010] ZASCA 129

The appellant, Gary Walter Van der Merwe, was charged with contravening Exchange Control Regulations and defeating the ends of justice.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2010 [2010] ZASCA 129

FIRAC Method

F - Facts

The appellant, Gary Walter Van der Merwe, was charged with contravening Exchange Control Regulations and defeating the ends of justice. These allegations stemmed from events in July 2004 when he was arrested at Cape Town International Airport while attempting to expatriate a substantial amount of foreign currency ([1]). Initially, the authorities dismissed his application to retrieve the confiscated currency, leading to a series of unsuccessful appeals through the Cape High Court and ultimately the Constitutional Court ([1]).

On 9 June 2008, as his trial was set to commence in the Regional Court, Van der Merwe filed an urgent application for a declaratory order against the Directorate of Special Operations (DSO), arguing that their investigation had exceeded the statutory mandate outlined in the National Prosecuting Authority Act 32 of 1998, particularly regarding serious and organized crime, to which the alleged offences did not pertain ([2]). This application aimed to declare the DSO's conduct unlawful, invalid, and unconstitutional. The appellant's assertion focused on the claim that the DSO’s involvement constituted a deviation from constitutional norms, thereby threatening the fairness of his upcoming trial ([2][4]).

The application for a declaratory order was ultimately dismissed by the High Court, which indicated that the legal issues raised were better suited for resolution during the trial itself, without making a costs order ([5]). This dismissal was then subject to an appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([5]).

I - Issues

The key legal issues presented in this case revolve around:

  1. Whether the DSO operated within its statutory mandate while conducting the investigation against Van der Merwe, and consequently, whether its actions amounted to violations of constitutional rights, particularly the right to a fair trial ([21], [27]).
  2. Whether the court had an obligation under Section 172(1) of the Constitution to issue a declaratory order if it found that the conduct of the DSO was unconstitutional, even in the absence of consequential relief ([21], [28]).
  3. The implications of encouraging preliminary litigation in terms of its impact on the prosecution of criminal cases ([32]).

R - Rules

The applicable legal rules and principles arise from both statutory provisions and important case law interpretations:

  1. Section 172(1) of the Constitution mandates that a court must declare any law or conduct inconsistent with the Constitution as invalid. However, jurisprudence indicates that this does not impose an absolute obligation to issue a declaratory order without consideration of the case's facts ([28], [30]).
  2. The NPA Act outlines the mandate and scope of operations for the Directorate of Special Operations, particularly emphasizing limitations based on the definition of serious and organized crime ([7], [21]).
  3. Precedents highlight that courts should discourage preliminary litigation that serves primarily to delay or complicate criminal proceedings. This includes the rulings in National Director of Public Prosecutions v King and Thint (Pty) Ltd v National Director of Public Prosecutions, which stress that accused individuals should not be allowed to interrupt or delay trials through tactical legal challenges ([32]).

A - Application

In its ruling, the Supreme Court of Appeal examined the evidence concerning the DSO's involvement in Van der Merwe’s case. The court was initially inclined to accept for the sake of argument that Van der Merwe had made out a case for the conduct of the DSO exceeding its statutory authority ([27], [31]). However, despite this assumption, the court emphasized that a declaratory order is not automatically mandated under Section 172(1) if the case does not present concrete outcomes beyond a mere declaration ([28], [32]).

Furthermore, the court highlighted the principle that trial courts are best positioned to determine issues of relevance, such as the admissibility of evidence and constitutional rights, within the full factual context of a trial. In effect, decisions related to alleged irregularities should be made by the trial court rather than being pre-empted through separate constitutional litigation ([27], [32], [34]). The court found that permitting such preliminary challenges would not only undermine judicial efficiency but also complicate the prosecution's ability to conduct timely trials ([32][34]).

The court ruled that allowing the declaratory order would set a precedent for further preliminary litigation, adversely affecting the criminal justice process ([33]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court's decision, confirming the dismissal of Van der Merwe's application for a declaratory order. The court concluded that it was not compelled to issue a declaratory order under Section 172(1) of the Constitution and emphasized the discouragement of preliminary litigation designed to avoid trial proceedings.

The ruling underscored the balancing act between ensuring the individual's right to a fair trial and the public interest in prosecuting criminal offences effectively. Consequently, the application and cross-appeal were both dismissed, with costs awarded to the respondents ([42]). The implications of this case reaffirm the need for efficient judicial processes in criminal matters and the judiciary's reluctance to allow constitutional claims that stall the progress of significant cases.
