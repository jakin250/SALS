Supreme Court of Appeal

Manong & Associates (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Public Works

The appellant sought an interim interdict, claiming a prima facie case for the register’s inconsistency with constitutional rights and additional remedies, including an audit of the respondents' procurement policies ([para 1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009

F – Facts

The case arose from an ambitious application made by Manong & Associates (Pty) Ltd ("the appellant") against the Minister of Public Works and the Director-General of the Department of Public Works ("the respondents"). The appellant, a company specializing in civil and structural engineering, challenged the replacement of an existing roster system for procurement with a new Professional Services Supplier Register, arguing that this change would undermine their constitutional rights under sections 9 and 217 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, and contravened various provisions of the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000 (the Equality Act) ([paras 1-2]).

The appellant sought an interim interdict, claiming a prima facie case for the register’s inconsistency with constitutional rights and additional remedies, including an audit of the respondents' procurement policies ([para 1]). The application was initiated after a letter sent by Mr. Manong, the managing director, sought assurance that the new policy would not be implemented without further consultation, following the decision to abolish the existing roster system ([paras 21-22]).

The Equality Court, under Botha J, dismissed the appellant's application for an interim interdict, prompting the present appeal ([para 2]).

I – Issues

The main legal issues to be determined in this case revolved around:

  1. Right of Audience: Whether Mr. Manong had the right to represent his incorporated company in the appellate court ([paras 3-8]).
  2. Prima Facie Case: Did the appellant successfully establish a prima facie case for an interim interdict against the implementation of the new Professional Services Supplier Register? ([para 22]).

R – Rules

The rules governing these issues include:

  1. The rule that a company cannot represent itself in court unless through a qualified legal practitioner as established in Yates Investments (Pty) Ltd v Commissioner for Inland Revenue ([para 4]).
  2. The principles governing the grant of an interim interdict, particularly the necessity to establish a prima facie right ([paras 22]).
  3. Relevant provisions within the Constitution, particularly section 9 concerning equality and section 217 relating to the procurement of goods and services ([paras 17-18]).
  4. The Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act and its prohibitions on unfair discrimination ([para 24]).

A – Application

The court began by addressing Mr. Manong’s representation of the company, holding that while he lacked a general right of audience, there existed a residual discretion for the court to allow non-professional representation in exceptional circumstances, especially given that he had previously represented the company in court without objection ([paras 3-12]).

On the merits of the case, the court found that the appellant did not establish a prima facie case. The representations made by Mr. Manong were found to be speculative rather than grounded in factual evidence. The respondents had clearly articulated the rationale for the change in procurement policy, explaining that the existing roster was not compliant with several statutory frameworks and that the new register conformed to the legal requirements ([paras 25-30]).

The judges noted that the appellant’s assertions that the new register perpetuated discriminatory practices lacked evidentiary support, characterizing them as conjectural perceptions rather than substantial claims ([paras 27-30]). Because the register was aligned with national legislation aimed at ensuring fair and equitable procurement, the court concluded that the respondents could not be shown to be acting in violation of the equality provisions of the Constitution ([paras 29-30]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the judgment of the lower court, dismissing the appeal with costs. The judgment reaffirmed the principle that a party seeking an interim interdict must establish a prima facie case, which the appellant failed to do. Furthermore, the ruling clarified the rigor with which courts will assess claims of unfair discrimination in procurement practices, especially where a new policy is introduced to align with statutory obligations ([paras 30-31]).

The implications of this case emphasize the need for parties engaging in litigation, particularly those represented by non-professionals, to adequately prepare and substantiate their claims with concrete evidence, ensuring that allegations of discrimination are well-grounded in fact rather than assertion.
