Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: Danny Joseph Sibiya and Others v Road Accident Fund (1067/2022) [2023] ZASCA 171

## F – Facts
The case involves an appeal by Danny Joseph Sibiya (the first appellant), Du Toit-Smuts Attorneys (the second appellant), and Reuben Jado Krige (the third appellant) against a judgment delivered by the Mpumalanga Division of the High Court. On 4 March 2014, Mr. Sibiya retained D-S Attorneys to lodge a claim against the Road Accident Fund (RAF) for damages following a motor vehicle accident on 16 February 2014. He signed a fee agreement with D-S Attorneys, which stated that their fees would be paid on an attorney-and-client basis. By 8 October 2021, the RAF conceded the case's merits and agreed to pay costs on a party-and-party scale, leading to the setting of a taxation hearing for the bill of costs on 3 February 2022 ([paras 1–3]).

Prior to the taxation, Mr. Krige filed an affidavit asserting that no contingency fee agreement existed between Mr. Sibiya and D-S Attorneys. The taxing master then raised several questions regarding the fee agreement and subsequently adjourned the proceedings to inquire about the might-be existence of a contingency fee, a measure specified under Rule 70(5A)(d)(ii) of the Uniform Rules of Court. The taxing master referred the matter to the Judge President for directions ([paras 4–5]).

On 2 June 2022, the High Court ruled that the fee agreement was illegal, rendering it unenforceable. The court issued directives for the Legal Practice Council to investigate Mr. Krige’s conduct and suggest that Mr. Sibiya consider appointing another attorney to complete his case ([paras 7–8]). Dissatisfied with this ruling, the appellants sought to challenge the High Court's order ([para 2]).

## I – Issues
The appeal raised several legal and procedural questions:
1. **Procedural fairness**: Did the High Court grant its judgment without affording the appellants an opportunity to be heard, contravening principles of procedural fairness?
2. **Validity of the fee agreement**: Was the High Court justified in declaring the fee agreement between Mr. Sibiya and D-S Attorneys illegal?
3. **Misapplication of Rule 70(5A)(d)(ii)**: Did the referral process under Rule 70(5A)(d)(ii) apply correctly, or was it misused to question the legality of the fee arrangement?

These issues are critically assessed across the context of both substantive and procedural law principles ([paras 8–9]).

## R – Rules
The key legal provisions and precedents relevant to this case include:
- **Contingency Fees Act 66 of 1997**: This governs the parameters under which contingency fee agreements may be validly entered and executed.
- **Uniform Rules of Court, Rule 70(5A)(d)(ii)**: It details the protocol regarding referrals to a judge in chambers, primarily concerning circumstances of misbehaviour during the taxation process.
- **Constitutional principles**: The right to a fair hearing as guaranteed by Section 34 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, which incorporates the common law principle of **audi alteram partem**, ensuring that all parties involved in a legal process have an opportunity to participate and be heard ([paras 14–15]).

## A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal critically evaluated the procedural grounds on which the High Court issued its order. The judgment identified that the High Court did not provide the appellants an opportunity to present their arguments on the legality of the fee arrangement before making its determination. This lack of an adversarial hearing constituted a violation of foundational principles of judicial fairness ([paras 12–14]).

Regarding the application of Rule 70(5A)(d)(ii), the court found that the High Court had misconstrued its purpose. The rule is not designed for evaluating fee agreements but for addressing misbehaviour by parties or their representatives in the taxation process. The Supreme Court noted that there was no proof of misbehaviour in this case, which rendered the High Court’s reliance on this rule procedurally flawed and ungrounded ([paras 10–11]).

Additionally, the court observed that the judicial proceedings had lacked transparency, infringing upon both the right to access a fair process and the necessity for public oversight in legal matters ([paras 13–14]). The High Court's decision had cast aspersions on Mr. Krige’s professional conduct without affording him any opportunity to defend against the allegations made against him. This raised substantial concerns about the defensibility of the legal findings against him.

## C – Conclusion
The appeal was upheld, and the order of the High Court was set aside. The Supreme Court concluded that the High Court had erred in its methods by not including all interested parties in its deliberations and by acting beyond its procedural powers. The court emphasized that acts of judicial bodies must not detract from the rights of litigants, demonstrating a profound commitment to procedural justice ([paras 17–18]). 

Moreover, it was underscored that the High Court's decision to question the legality of the fee agreement constituted an overreach, creating an improper precedent that could deter attorneys from representing clients under valid agreements due to fear of judicial scrutiny without due procedural process. Ultimately, the matter was returned, upholding the fundamental principles governing the lawful conduct of judicial procedures and ensuring that parties enjoy fair representation in accordance with applicable legal standards. The Court ruled that no costs were awarded, recognizing the RAF did not participate in the appeal ([paras 18]).
