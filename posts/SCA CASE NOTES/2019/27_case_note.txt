Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: Atwealth (Pty) Ltd & others v Kernick & others (116/2018) [2019] ZASCA 27

### F – Facts

The case revolves around the actions of Andrea Moolman, a financial advisor who at various times was employed by Atwealth (Pty) Ltd and Vaidro 172 CC. Between 2009 and 2012, she provided financial advice to the Kernicks—Alan and Fiona, a married couple alongside their close corporation Kernick Consulting Ltd. The Kernicks alleged that Moolman had recommended high-risk investment products, specifically investments in the Relative Value Arbitrage Fund (RVAF) and associated entities, promising lucrative returns. Contrary to these promises, the financial products did not generate the expected returns, leading the Kernicks to suffer significant financial losses ([1]–[5]).

Moolman’s advisory role began amidst claims made during a presentation that assured the Kernicks of high returns. Investments were made based on these assurances, culminating in various transactions reviewed at trial ([4]). The Kernicks pressed charges against the appellants, arguing that Moolman had acted negligently and breached her statutory obligations under the Financial Advisory and Intermediary Services Act 37 of 2002 (FAIS Act). The High Court initially favored the Kernicks, awarding them damages, which led to this appeal ([5]).

### I – Issues

The legal questions presented in this case concerned:

1. What constitutes "financial advice" under the FAIS Act, and whether Moolman’s actions fell under this definition?
2. Whether Moolman breached her legal obligations and acted negligently, therefore causing loss to the Kernicks?
3. The appropriateness of the evidence presented regarding the nature and timing of Moolman’s advice compared to standard practices of reasonable financial advisors ([23]–[24]).

### R – Rules

The relevant legal principles and statutory provisions include:

1. **Definition of Financial Advice**: Under the FAIS Act, financial advice is defined as any recommendation made concerning financial products ([28]).
2. **Legal Duties and Standards of Care**: Section 3 of the Code of Conduct requires that financial advisors provide services honestly and fairly, do so with due skill and care, and avoid misleading clients. The common law standard outlined in *Durr v Absa Bank Ltd* indicates that a financial advisor must possess a level of skill typical of their profession and must not conduct themselves in a manner that endangers clients ([26]–[37]).
3. **Negligence**: As per *Mukheiber v Raath*, the standard for negligence requires showing that a reasonable person would foresee harm, understand the causal sequence of events, and take necessary precautionary steps ([45]).

### A – Application

The court meticulously examined the propriety of Moolman's advice during the critical meetings and how it aligned with the requirements set forth in the FAIS Act. Feedback from the Kernicks indicated reliance on Moolman's assurances which they deemed an explicit form of financial advice. However, the court found crucial gaps in the evidence regarding the content of the presentation Moolman made and whether her recommendations indeed constituted actionable advice as defined under the FAIS Act ([30]).

The legal discussion also turned to the nature of the investments themselves and whether Moolman should have been aware of their associated risks. The appellants argued that financial advisors, in this case, would not commonly possess in-depth knowledge of hedge fund activities at that time. This point was substantial as the legislation regarding hedge funds had only subsequently imposed regulatory measures ([25]).

The court deemed that Moolman’s conduct did not evidently breach the standards expected of a financial advisor nor did it lead to demonstrable negligence. The lack of robust evidence regarding standard industry practices and the absence of testimony illustrating what a reasonable financial advisor would have recommended at the time hindered a finding against Moolman ([49]–[55]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the earlier ruling from the High Court, stating that the Kernicks failed to establish Moolman’s negligence. The ruling clarified that mere reliance on the FAIS Act's statutory provisions does not automatically satisfy a claim in delict. For negligence to be actionable, there must be substantive evidence suggesting that a reasonable financial advisor would have acted differently under similar circumstances, which was lacking in this case ([56]).

As a result, the appeal was upheld, and the earlier judgment was dismissed with costs, representing a victory for Atwealth and Moolman, who contested the grounds of their liability throughout the appeal ([1], [56]). This verdict underscores the complexities surrounding the definition of financial advice, the duties owed by financial advisors, and the evidential burdens plaintiffs must carry in negligence claims.
