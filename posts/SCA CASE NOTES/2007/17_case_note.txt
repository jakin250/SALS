# FIRAC Case Note: Wessels NO v Die Meester [2007] SCA 17 (RSA)

## F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–8])

This case concerns an appeal against a ruling by Van der Merwe R in the Bloemfontein High Court regarding the validity of a joint will executed by Frederick Johannes Theodorus Bezuidenhout (the testator) and his predeceased spouse (the testatrix). The testator passed away on 7 March 2004. Prior to her death, on 18 January 2002, a joint will was made, the provisions of which were somewhat ambiguous, occasionally allocating the same assets to more than one heir ([para 2]).

The essential provisions of the will operated on the assumption that the testatrix would outlive the testator. Following her death, the testator effectively acquired the entire joint estate, which included both his marital share and her share according to clause 8 of the joint will. Subsequently, the testator executed two codicils (on 7 May 2003 and 11 June 2003), aiming to modify certain aspects of the will. However, after the testator's death, the Master of the High Court refused to accept the third codicil due to its unavailability ([para 6]–[8]).

The appellant, as the executor of the estate, contended that the joint will remained valid, supplemented by the first and second codicils, whereas the Master argued that no valid will existed at the testator's death, necessitating intestate succession. The Bloemfontein court rejected the appellant's application, upholding the Master's position ([paras 7–8]).

## I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 1, 9–10, 11])

The primary legal question relates to whether the joint will executed by the testator and testatrix became void upon the testatrix's death and, if so, whether the testator could reinstate it via the codicils executed afterward. The immediate issues are:

1. **Validity of the Joint Will**: Did the will lapse upon the death of the testatrix?
2. **Possibility of Revival**: Could the testator have manifested an intention to reinstate the lapsed will through subsequent documents?
3. **Legal Implications of Non-Execution of a New Will**: What happens when the testator dies without executing a new will after the death of the testatrix? ([para 10]).

## R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 9, 10, 11])

The court referenced established principles that govern the interpretation and execution of wills, focusing on the assumption that a joint will, despite its describe format, operates as two distinct wills concerning each testator's assets. Thus, the surviving spouse is free to create a new will after the death of the first spouse, reflecting the individual nature of testamentary rights ([para 9]).

The applicable legal principles include:

- A joint will may lapse if its terms explicitly depend on the death of one spouse, whereby the surviving spouse can create a new testament ([paras 10–11]).
- Citing *De Klerk v Estate De Klerk* and *Estate Coaton v The Master*, the court reiterated that a testator's shared assets remain in effect unless expressly stated otherwise, and that intestate succession arises if a valid will is not forthcoming at death ([para 10]).

## A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 11–30])

The court assessed whether the joint will lapsed under specific conditions following the testatrix's death. It concluded that the language used in the will demonstrated a clear dependence on the testatrix surviving the testator, which meant that the will automatically became void upon her death ([para 11]). Furthermore, it determined that the codicils did not express an intention on the part of the testator to revive the lapsed will. Instead, the terminology used in the codicils suggested he believed the joint will remained valid ([paras 25–26]).

The court's reliance on precedents such as *Moses v Abinader* helped underscore the necessity of evidencing a testator's intent to revive a previous will or portion thereof. The failure to prove such intent in the current case led the court to endorse the lower court's conclusion, effectively ruling that the testator's estate had to be distributed intestate due to the lack of a valid will ([paras 24–26]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 30])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appellant’s case, confirming that the joint will had lapsed upon the testatrix's death, and that there was insufficient evidence to demonstrate the testator’s intention to reinstate the will via the codicils. The court's judgment underscored the broader legal principle that the intentions of a testator must be expressed with clarity in a valid testamentary document to be actionable ([paras 30]).

The implications of this ruling reinforce the necessity for clarity in testamentary dispositions and the critical nature of adhering to formal requirements associated with the execution of wills. Furthermore, the case established a precedent regarding the revival of wills, highlighting that mere reference or perceived validity does not suffice to reinstate a testamentary document that has lapsed or been revoked.
