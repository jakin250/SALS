Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# Case Note: King Price Insurance Company Ltd v Concise Consulting Services (Pty) Ltd

**Neutral Citation:** King Price Insurance Company Ltd v Concise Consulting Services (Pty) Ltd (1067/2019) [2021] ZASCA 42 (13 April 2021)  
**Judges:** Mbha JA, Zondi and Molemela JJA, Kgoele and Rogers AJJA

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–9])

The appeal arises from a claim for indemnification under a contract of insurance by Concise Consulting Services (Pty) Ltd (the respondent), following damage to its motor vehicle on 1 January 2014. The appellant, King Price Insurance Company Ltd, had issued a policy covering the vehicle, a 2008 Toyota Yaris. After the incident, concerning which Mr. Sibusiso Ngobese, an employee of the respondent and the regular driver of the insured vehicle, was involved, the respondent lodged a claim with the insurer.

Initially, Mr. Ngobese provided information about the accident during telephonic interviews with the appellant's representatives. However, on 17 January 2014, the appellant repudiated the claim, citing that Mr. Ngobese had submitted false and misleading information—asserting the right to cancel the insurance contract retrospectively from the date of the accident.

The respondent subsequently issued a summons against the appellant for the damages incurred, leading to a trial. The initial judgment by the magistrate dismissed the respondent’s claim based on findings of dishonesty by Mr. Ngobese. On appeal to the full bench of the High Court, the verdict was reversed, instigating the current appeal by the appellant ([paras 1–8]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 10–15])

The primary issues for the Supreme Court of Appeal were twofold:
1. Whether Mr. Ngobese acted "on behalf" of the respondent when he provided the false information to the insurer, thereby implicating the respondent in his dishonest actions.
2. Whether the false statements given by Mr. Ngobese were of a material nature that would warrant the repudiation of the insurance claim and the retrospective cancellation of the insurance contract ([paras 10–16]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 16–23])

The legal principles pertinent to this case revolve around insurance contracts, privilege associated with the duty of disclosure, and the standard of proof required in proving fraud or dishonesty in insurance claims. Precedents such as *Van Zyl NO v Kiln Non-Marine Syndicate NO 510 of Lloyds of London* ([2002] ZASCA 120) and *South African Warehousing Services (Pty) Ltd v South British Insurance Co Ltd* ([1971] 3 SA 10 (A)) provided context regarding the obligations of both insurers and insured parties. Furthermore, established tenets of agency law were explored to determine whether Mr. Ngobese could be seen as acting on behalf of the respondent ([paras 16–23]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 23–24])

In its decision, the Supreme Court of Appeal highlighted the clear distinction between the act of lodging an insurance claim and the subsequent validation process conducted by the insurer. The Court emphasized that Mr. Ngobese's misleading statements were provided during the validation process, not as part of the original claim submission. It was noted that he was not acting on behalf of the respondent at that time, but rather answering inquiries from the insurer as an eyewitness.

Evidence revealed that Mr. Ngobese essentially acted solely in his interest, attempting to deflect potential blame for the accident, and did not have the necessary authority or role as an agent of the respondent. Thus, it was determined that the statements made could not be attributed to the respondent nor could they materially affect the insurance claim. Consequently, the Court concluded that the appellant had failed to prove the elements necessary for repudiation of the claim and cancellation of the policy ([paras 24–25]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 25–25])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, including the costs of two counsels, affirming the full bench's decision. The key takeaway from the judgment is the affirmation that an insurer cannot repudiate a claim based on misrepresentations made by an individual who is not acting within the scope of their authority to represent the insured. The Court further clarified that any ambiguity regarding the terms of the insurance contract should be resolved in favor of the insured, reinforcing the protections for policyholders against unfair repudiation of claims based on potentially misleading statements by uninvolved parties.

This case underscores the importance of proper definitions of agency within insurance contracts and sets a critical precedent regarding the standards insurers must meet to justify repudiation of claims. It illustrates the necessity for insurers to provide a concrete demonstration of materiality and intent in instances of alleged dishonesty, ensuring that insured parties are adequately protected from unjustified claims of fraud.
