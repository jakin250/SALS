Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2003

### Case Note: Xinwa and 1335 Others v Volkswagen of South Africa (Pty) Ltd, CCT 3/03

#### FIRAC Case Note

**F – Facts**: 
The applicants, comprising 1,336 former employees of Volkswagen (Pty) Ltd (Volkswagen), were dismissed on 3 February 2000 after participating in a strike action surrounding the suspension of NUMSA shop stewards ([1]). The National Union of Mineworkers of South Africa (NUMSA) represented the applicants, asserting that their dismissal was procedurally unfair and seeking compensation and re-employment per the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA) ([1]-[2]). 

The strike began on 20 January 2000, driven by demands to lift the suspension of NUMSA shop stewards. Management and NUMSA deemed this strike illegal, leading to warnings issued to workers that continued participation could lead to disciplinary action, including dismissal ([3]). When the workers, including the applicants, failed to return to work as mandated by a subsequent ultimatum, dismissal was executed ([5-6]). The ensuing arbitration found the dismissals substantively fair but procedurally unfair, leading to the Commissioner ordering reinstatement, which Volkswagen contested ([7-9]).

The Labour Court affirmed that the dismissals were substantively fair but held they were procedurally unfair, later ruling against the Commissioner’s reinstatement order. Upon appeal, the Labour Appeal Court (LAC) concluded the dismissals were procedurally fair, prompting the present case ([8]-[9]).

**I – Issues**: 
1. Were the dismissals of the applicants procedurally unfair under the Labour Relations Act?
2. Did the Commissioner exceed his powers in ordering reinstatement for procedural unfairness?
3. Should the application for leave to appeal and for condonation be granted? ([12]-[17])

**R – Rules**: 
The relevant rules include Section 193 of the LRA, stating that if a dismissal is found to be unfair, the employer may be ordered to reinstate or re-employ the employee unless specific exceptions apply. Section 145 outlines the grounds for reviewing arbitration awards, including misconduct and the exceeding of powers by the Commissioner ([6]-[8]).

Precedents such as the Labour Appeal Court’s decision in Mzeku & Others v Volkswagen SA (Pty) Ltd set important benchmarks for understanding procedures under the LRA ([2]). Case law mandates a generous, contextual interpretation of pleadings prepared by laypersons, as reflected in statutes like Haines v Kerner and others ([13]).

**A – Application**: 
The Constitutional Court reviewed the findings that the applicants had no merit in claiming procedural unfairness based on the LAC’s ruling that both NUMSA and the applicants had adequate opportunities to represent their case prior to dismissal. The Court emphasized that management had engaged in numerous attempts to resolve the strike, including issuing ultimatums and warnings, thus meeting procedural fairness requirements ([15]-[17]).

The Court noted the significance of the strikers' refusal to return to work despite clear warnings and communications regarding the consequences of their actions, fundamentally undermining their claims of procedural unfairness. Regarding the reinstatement order by the Commissioner, the LAC found that he misconstrued the enquiry related to the audi principle, thus ruling that reinstatement was an inappropriate remedy for procedural unfairness ([9]).

**C – Conclusion**: 
Ultimately, the Constitutional Court held that the applicants had no prospects of success, reaffirming the LAC's conclusion that their dismissal was procedurally fair. Consequently, the application for leave to appeal was dismissed, highlighting the Court's stance that the circumstances surrounding the dismissals favored Volkswagen and reflected an absence of substantive legal grounds for the applicants' claims ([17]-[18]). 

This judgment reiterates the courts’ emphasis on the procedural rigour expected from both employees and employers within the framework of the LRA and clarifies the limitations on remedies available in cases of procedural unfairness. The implications for future disputes underline the necessity for clear communication and adherence to procedural norms by all parties involved in labour relations.
