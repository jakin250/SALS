Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Minister of Safety & Security v Booysen (35/2016) [2016] ZASCA 201 (9 December 2016)

## F – Facts
The Supreme Court of Appeal in South Africa considered the case of *Minister of Safety & Security v Booysen*, where the facts revolved around an incident that occurred on March 22, 2013, involving a police reservist, Mr. Johannes Mongo. The deceased, a police reservist employed by the South African Police Service (SAPS), had been on duty and was wearing his police uniform when he visited the home of his partner, Ms. Elsa Booysen, in Pearston, Eastern Cape. Following a social visit for dinner, during which he purchased soft drinks for Ms. Booysen’s family, he unexpectedly shot her in the face before committing suicide.

The trial regarding Ms. Booysen’s claim for damages established that the only legal question was the Minister’s vicarious liability for the actions of the deceased. Initially, the High Court found in favor of Ms. Booysen, holding the Minister vicariously liable, which prompted the Minister to appeal.

The factual backdrop included the nature of Mr. Mongo’s duties, his possession of a service pistol issued by SAPS, and the immediate circumstances leading to the shooting, including Mr. Mongo’s preceding remark, which indicated a vendetta rather than a work-related grievance. Notably, despite being in uniform and on duty, there was no evidence that he was performing official police duties at the time of the incident ([1]–[4]).

## I – Issues
The key issue addressed by the court was whether the Minister of Safety and Security could be held vicariously liable for the damages suffered by Ms. Booysen as a result of the deceased’s wrongful conduct. Specifically, the court had to evaluate whether there existed a sufficiently close connection between the deceased’s wrongful act and his employment with SAPS, given the precedent of cases dealing with vicarious liability in similar contexts ([1], [5], [8], [14]).

## R – Rules
The legal framework for vicarious liability in South African law was primarily built on the principles established in *Feldman (Pty) Ltd v Mall* 1945 AD 733 and *Minister of Police v Rabie* 1986 (1) SA 117 (A). The court applied a two-pronged test for establishing vicarious liability, as adapted by the Constitutional Court in *K v Minister of Safety and Security* 2005 (6) SA 419 (CC):

1. **Subjective Test**: Whether the wrongful acts were done solely for the purposes of the employee, which is a factual inquiry.
2. **Objective Test**: If the first question is answered affirmatively, a further assessment is required to determine whether there is a sufficiently close link between the acts and the employer’s business.

The court reiterated the need to consider constitutional norms and the extent to which the employer’s business is engaged in the wrongdoing committed by the employee ([6]–[10]).

## A – Application
In its analysis, the court scrutinized both prongs of the test. It found that Mr. Mongo’s visit to Ms. Booysen’s home was for personal reasons and lacked an official police function. The deceased’s actions were characterized as a deviation from his employment duties, which indicated he was not acting within the scope of his employment at the time of the incident. This conclusion was drawn from the nature of their relationship—where he was seen as a partner rather than a police officer—and the absence of any forewarning or indication of potential wrongdoing that would have made the SAPS liable.

Despite recognizing that Mr. Mongo was on duty when he visited Ms. Booysen, the court stressed that his actions were purely social, and thus, did not present a sufficiently close link to his employment that could trigger vicarious liability ([14]–[20]).

The court further distinguished this case from *Pehlani v Minister of Police*, where the wrongful act was inextricably linked to the duties performed as a police officer. In *K* and *F*, where liability was found, the officers’ actions were closely connected to their roles as public protectors, a relationship which did not exist in the present matter. The court determined that the absence of trust—an essential factor in previous similar judgments—further negated the possibility of linking the shooting to Mr. Mongo's employment with SAPS ([22]–[24]).

## C – Conclusion
In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal by the Minister of Safety and Security, ruling that the evidence did not substantiate a sufficiently close link between the deceased’s misconduct and his role as a police reservist. The vicarious liability was rejected, based on the findings that Mr. Mongo acted in a personal capacity, without performing any duties for his employer at the time of the incident. The High Court's judgment was set aside, and the court’s order was amended to dismiss Ms. Booysen's claim with costs ([1], [34]).

This case emphasizes the critical distinction between personal and professional conduct for police officers and the boundaries of vicarious liability when improper acts occur outside the scope of employment. It affirms the necessity for a clear link to the employer’s business in assessing vicarious liability claims, particularly in sensitive contexts involving law enforcement ([35], [36]).
