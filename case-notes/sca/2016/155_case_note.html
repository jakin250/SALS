Supreme Court of Appeal

Okah v S (19/2014) [2016] ZASCA 155

[3-4] Claassen J in the South Gauteng High Court posed the question of the appropriateness of trying Okah in South Africa due to international conventions targeting terrorism, to which the Act seemingly provides a basis for extra-territorial prosecution.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 155

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history
[1] The case involves Mr. Henry Emomotimi Okah, a Nigerian citizen with permanent residency in South Africa, who was prosecuted in South Africa for his alleged involvement in bombings in Nigeria, resulting in twelve fatalities and numerous injuries ([1], [2]). Following his residency grant in March 2007, Okah was arrested on October 2, 2010, and charged with thirteen counts under the Protection of Constitutional Democracy Against Terrorist and Related Activities Act 33 of 2004 (the Act). The first twelve counts pertained to the bombings in Warri on March 15, 2010, and Abuja on October 1, 2010, while the thirteenth count involved threatening South African commercial entities in Nigeria ([2], [3]).

[3-4] Claassen J in the South Gauteng High Court posed the question of the appropriateness of trying Okah in South Africa due to international conventions targeting terrorism, to which the Act seemingly provides a basis for extra-territorial prosecution. This was substantiated by Claassen J's analysis of the Act, resulting in the conclusion that the court had the necessary jurisdiction to adjudicate on the counts ([3], [4]).

[5-6] The trial established that Okah was a leader of the Movement for the Emancipation of the Niger Delta (MEND) and was actively involved in orchestrating the bombings, which included planning logistics, providing materials such as explosives, and issuing threats through electronic communications. The court gleaned substantial evidence from various witnesses who were part of the operations ([5], [6]).

[24] Following his conviction on counts 1-12, which included various forms of terroristic engagement, and the securing of a sentence totaling 24 years’ imprisonment, Okah appealed against the convictions and sentences, arguing a lack of jurisdiction concerning the alleged acts in Nigeria, and asserting potential duplicity in the charges ([24]).

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions
[24] The primary issues on appeal included:

  1. The legal jurisdiction of South African courts over acts of terrorism committed outside its borders, specifically whether the High Court correctly asserted jurisdiction per the parameters set in the Act.
  2. Whether the evidence presented justified conviction for count 13, concerning threats made against South African interests in Nigeria ([25], [26]).

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon
[27] The principle of jurisdiction traditionally emphasizes that states exercise authority within their territorial boundaries, as recognized in Kaunda & others v President of the Republic of South Africa & others 2005 (4) SA 235 (CC) where it was articulated that territorial jurisdiction is a fundamental aspect of state sovereignty ([27]).

[30] The Act extends the jurisdictional reach of South African courts, particularly through sections 15(1) to (4), which outline when extra-territorial jurisdiction may be exercised, including cases where an individual is found in the Republic or if their actions are deemed to affect South African entities or citizens ([35], [36]). The long title and preamble of the Act emphasize compliance with international obligations concerning terrorism, aiming to empower South African courts to adjudicate such offenses ([32]).

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law
[41] In applying the law, the court required a nuanced interpretation of section 15 and the definitions therein, particularly concerning what constitutes a "specified offence." The court paid careful attention to the overlapping definitions of terrorist activities within the broader legislative context ([36], [37]).

[45] The court dismissed Okah’s claims regarding the jurisdiction over counts 1-7, asserting that the critical actions underpinning these charges occurred outside the South African territory. The convictions for counts 9 and 11, which involved the financing and logistical support for the bombings, were upheld, legitimizing the jurisdiction claim based on Okah's activities in South Africa ([46], [47]). Additional counts relating to the Abuja bombing were validated as they stemmed from actions orchestrated while Okah was present in South Africa ([48]).

[50] Lastly, with respect to count 13, the court found insufficient evidence linking Okah to threats made against South African interests, leading to the conviction being overturned due to a failure to substantiate a direct connection to the actions presented in the indictment ([49]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications
[55] The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal in part, affirming the convictions for counts 2, 4, 6, 8, 9, 10, 11, and 12 while overturning the convictions associated with counts 1 to 7 and 13. Consequently, Okah was sentenced to a total of 20 years’ imprisonment, reflecting a recalibration of penalties based on legal interpretations of jurisdiction and the evidence established during the trial ([55]).

The implications of this judgment clarify the extent to which South African courts can exercise jurisdiction over acts associated with terrorism and related offences occurring outside its borders. This case underlines the need for clear legislative guidelines to navigate the legal complexities introduced by transnational crimes and terrorism, illuminating the broader issues of international law and state sovereignty in the context of domestic legislation.
