Supreme Court of Appeal

Kebana v The State (161/09) [2009] ZASCA 100

The appellant, V Kebana, was charged with attempted murder and other crimes related to an incident involving his ex-wife on June 23, 2002.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009 [2009] ZASCA 100

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The appellant, V Kebana, was charged with attempted murder and other crimes related to an incident involving his ex-wife on June 23, 2002. A year post-divorce, Kebana unexpectedly visited the complainant at her residence, offering her a lift to work. However, he diverted to a secluded area near Coega, where he allegedly attacked her, inflicting serious injuries: he beat her, attempted to cut her throat, and stabbed her multiple times with a sharp object. The complainant lost consciousness during the brutal assault but later regained awareness and was subsequently found bleeding profusely on the roadside, eventually taken to St. George’s Hospital.

During the original trial in the regional court, medical evidence corroborated the complainant’s account, revealing three distinct categories of injuries. The appellant was convicted of attempted murder and possessing a dangerous weapon, receiving a cumulative sentence of 16 years' imprisonment. Upon appeal to the High Court, the convictions for rape and dangerous weapon were overturned; however, the attempted murder conviction was upheld. Leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal was granted concerning both the rape and attempted murder charges.

Both lower courts found the complainant credible and rejected Kebana’s defense, primarily on the grounds that he failed to produce a satisfactory explanation for the injuries sustained by the complainant, and the substantial medical evidence contradicted his defense narrative ([paras 1–12]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:

  1. Whether the evidence was sufficient to uphold the conviction for attempted murder, despite the High Court's decision to overturn the rape conviction, which raised questions about the credibility of the complainant's testimony.
  2. Whether the original sentence of ten years' imprisonment for attempted murder was appropriate under the circumstances outlined at trial ([paras 2, 9, 12]).

R – Rules

The court primarily relied on established legal principles concerning the assessment of credibility, the standard of proof in criminal cases, and substantive legal rules on attempted murder under South African law.

  • Credibility Assessment: The SCA noted the importance of the magistrate's role in assessing the credibility of witnesses and the presumption that the magistrate's factual conclusions were correct unless proved otherwise ([para 12]).
  • Standard of Proof: The principle that in criminal matters, the state must prove the accused's guilt beyond a reasonable doubt, as iterated in S v Van Aswegen 2001 (2) SACR 97 (SCA) and R v Dhlumayo 1948 (2) SA 677 (A) ([paras 11, 12]).
  • Assessment of Medical Evidence: The relevance of expert medical testimony to establish the nature and extent of injuries sustained by the complainant and its implications for the attempted murder conviction [[paras 5, 6]].

A – Application

In applying these legal rules to the facts of the case, the SCA conducted a holistic review of the evidence presented, particularly focusing on the reliability of the complainant's testimony and the corroborating medical evidence. The court determined that:

  1. The conviction for attempted murder was justified as the medical evidence aligned with the complainant's account of the attack. The serious nature of the injuries and the circumstances surrounding them indicated that Kebana's actions were deliberate and constituted attempted murder.
  2. The SCA addressed the appellant's claims of inconsistencies in the complainant’s testimony, emphasizing that such discrepancies did not undermine the overall reliability of her account when weighed against substantial medical findings.
  3. The SCA found Kebana's challenges to the previous rulings unconvincing, particularly given the magistrate’s notable assessment of the complainant's demeanor and the corroborative nature of the medical evidence, which the magistrate had evaluated with regard to the totality of evidence ([paras 6–11]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Kebana’s appeal against the conviction and the sentence. The court reaffirmed that the magistrate’s evaluation of the credibility of both the complainant and the appellant was sound and supported by the medical evidence presented. The ten-year sentence was deemed appropriate, taking into account the planned nature of the attack and the severity of the injuries inflicted. The court underscored the importance of holding the appellant accountable for his actions, thus prioritizing public safety and justice over the appellant's personal circumstances. Consequently, this case not only reinforces the legal standards for attempted murder in South Africa but also exemplifies the careful consideration required when evaluating witness credibility in a criminal trial ([paras 12, 14]).

  • R v Dhlumayo 1948 (2) SA 677 (A).
