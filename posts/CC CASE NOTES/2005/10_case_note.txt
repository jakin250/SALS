Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2005

# FIRAC Case Note: The State v Basson (CCT 30/03)

## F – Facts

In 1999, Dr. Basson faced 67 charges in the Pretoria High Court, including murder, fraud, and conspiracy, largely related to alleged activities during his tenure in the South African Defence Force (SADF) concerning a chemical and biological warfare program known as Project Coast. Following a long trial, in April 2002, he was acquitted on all counts. The state proceeded with an application for leave to appeal against a judgment rendered by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which addressed three significant issues, including alleged bias from the trial judge, the admissibility of a bail record, and the quashing of part of the indictment ([1]–[2]).

The state alleged that the conduct of the trial judge, particularly during a recusal application, created a perception of bias, as he had made derogatory comments and intervention during the trial ([3]–[4]). Additionally, the issue arose regarding the admissibility of a bail record where the accused was questioned without legal representation and the exclusion of that record from the trial ([5]–[6]). Finally, the state contested the quashing of certain charges related to conspiracies to commit murder that allegedly occurred outside South Africa, arguing that the trial judge misinterpreted section 18(2) of the Riotous Assemblies Act in quashing those charges ([13]).

## I – Issues

1. **Bias of the Trial Judge:** Did the judge’s remarks and conduct during the trial lead to a reasonable apprehension of bias against the state ([19]–[22])?
2. **Admissibility of the Bail Record:** Was it lawful for the trial court to exclude the bail record and consider its admissibility prior to the trial commencing ([104]–[122])?
3. **Quashing of Charges:** Did the trial judge err in quashing charges under section 18(2) of the Riotous Assemblies Act, particularly regarding the criminality of conspiracies to commit murder occurring outside South Africa ([124]–[260])?

## R – Rules

### Rule on Judicial Bias
The Constitutional Court has established the legal test for bias, indicating that a judicial officer's impartiality is essential for a fair trial as mandated by Section 34 of the Constitution, which guarantees the right to a fair public hearing ([23]–[30]). Case law, such as **President of the Republic of South Africa v South African Rugby Football Union** ([20]), articulates that a reasonable and informed person must apprehend that the judge will not bring an impartial mind to the adjudication of the case.

### Admissibility of Bail Records
Section 60(11B)(c) of the Criminal Procedure Act indicates that a bail record can form part of the trial record but must be evaluated against the backdrop of fairness ([107]–[112]). **S v Dlamini** reinforces that courts must exclude evidence that would impair the fairness of the proceedings ([108]).

### Interpretation of Section 18(2) of the Riotous Assemblies Act
According to Section 18(2)(a) of the Riotous Assemblies Act, a conspiracy can be prosecuted if it qualifies as a crime in South Africa. The Adams rule states that questions of law can only be reserved after a conviction or acquittal ([135]–[139]), raising jurisdictional concerns about the prosecution of conspiracies to commit acts outside the country but initiated within South Africa ([206]–[227]).

## A – Application

### Bias of the Trial Judge
The Court analyzed numerous complaints regarding the trial judge's conduct, including remarks indicating impatience and sarcasm towards the state counsel. Cumulatively, the Court found that while certain remarks might have been ill-considered, they did not constitute a pattern that would displace the presumption of impartiality ([66]–[102]). Ultimately, the Court determined that the state did not successfully establish any reasonable apprehension of bias as the judge’s conduct could be contextualized within the high-pressure environment of lengthy trials.

### Admissibility of the Bail Record
The Court ruled that the trial court was justified in excluding the bail record due to procedural contexts and the unfairness that could arise from its inclusion. This ruling related to the prosecutor's conduct during the bail hearing which was deemed as having impaired the accused's right to a fair trial. The Court upheld that the timing of the consideration concerning this record did not negatively impact the trial ([104]–[122]).

### Quashing of Charges
In addressing the state’s appeal against the quashing of charges, the Court concluded that the trial judge erred by interpreting Section 18(2) too narrowly. The Court criticized the application of the Adams rule and established that the conspiracy charges concerning acts undertaken by SADF members were indeed prosecutable under South African law, given their conduct during the governing period and the existence of a legitimate jurisdictional nexus. The legal question regarding these charges was thus resolved in favor of the state ([261]–[264]).

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately held that the state’s appeal against the SCA’s ruling concerning the quashed charges succeeded. The ruling by Hartzenberg J, which quashed the charges on the grounds they did not disclose actionable offenses, was reversed, reinstating the charges against Dr. Basson. However, the Court rejected the claims of bias and the challenge to the admissibility of the bail record as not sufficiently detrimental to the fairness of the proceedings. The implications of this decision underscore important legal precedents around judicial conduct, evidentiary standards in criminal trials, and the contours of jurisdictional authority on transnational alleged crimes ([265]). 

The outcome not only affects the immediate pursuit of justice for serious allegations surrounding Dr. Basson’s actions but also reinforces broader principles regarding the conduct of trials and the rights of individuals amid serious charges against both state and persons ([261]–[265]).
