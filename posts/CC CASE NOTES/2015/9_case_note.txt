Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# Case Note: Coughlan N.O. v Road Accident Fund 2015 ZACC 10

### FIRAC Structure

---

## F – Facts 
The case involves an appeal brought by Wayne Coughlan N.O., who acts as curator ad litem for three minor children following the death of their mother, Noelle Margaret Beyers. The mother died in a motor vehicle accident in June 2002, leaving the children—Jeslin Shelaine Williams, Alfreda Kim Beyers, and Elton Jason Beyers—in the care of their maternal grandparents, who were granted foster parent status ([paras 1–5]). 

Upon obtaining this status, the grandparents became entitled to receive foster child grants from the state as per the Social Assistance Act ([para 8]). The Road Accident Fund (RAF) accepted liability for the damages but contended that the foster child grants should be deducted from the compensation for loss of support, arguing that failing to do so would result in double compensation ([paras 3–4]). 

The High Court ruled that the foster child grants were res inter alios acta—unrelated to the claims of the children—and therefore not deductible ([paras 10–11]). Conversely, the Supreme Court of Appeal overturned this decision, ruling in favor of the RAF, asserting that the grants did relate to the loss of support as they were derived from the mother’s death ([paras 19–20]).

The case raised significant constitutional issues regarding children’s rights under sections 27 and 28 of the Constitution, as well as the obligations of the state towards vulnerable children ([paras 22–25]).

---

## I – Issues 
The primary legal question at stake is whether the foster child grants payable to foster parents are deductible from the compensation payable for loss of support to the foster children. The central issues can be summarized as follows:

1. **Causal Link**: Whether the foster child grants provided to the grandparents are connected to the mother’s death, thereby making them deductible from the award of damages ([para 29]).
2. **Res Inter Alios Acta**: Whether the foster child grants are to be considered as res inter alios acta, meaning that they do not impact the children’s claims against the RAF for loss of support ([para 11]).
3. **Double Compensation**: Whether allowing both the foster child grants and compensation for loss of support constitutes double compensation ([para 30]).

---

## R – Rules 
The court relied upon several key legal principles and rights pertaining to children enshrined in the South African Constitution, as well as relevant legislation:

1. **Constitutional Rights**: Sections 27 and 28 deal with the right to social security and protections for children’s welfare ([paras 34–36]). 
2. **Social Assistance Act**: Provides the framework for foster child grants and outlines their intended purpose—to ensure children in need of care receive adequate support ([para 6]).
3. **Previous Case Law**:
   - **Makhuvela v Road Accident Fund** established that foster child grants are intended to fulfill the constitutional rights of the child and are not dependent on the death of a parent ([para 11]).
   - **Zysset & Others v Santam Limited** highlighted the policy considerations of not allowing double compensation while also emphasizing that courts should strike a fair balance between a dependent's support needs and protecting the financial liability of entities like the RAF ([paras 12–13]).
   - **Timis**, concerning child support grants and their deductibility, further framed the debate but was ultimately reviewed critically by the court for being inapplicable to the facts of this case ([paras 18, 57]).

---

## A – Application 
The Constitutional Court analyzed the nature and purpose of foster child grants versus compensation for loss of support. Key points included:

1. **Nature of Foster Care Grants**: The Court noted that these grants are not directly tied to the death of a parent, as they aim to support children in need regardless of the cause of that need ([para 48]). Hence, they serve a broader social objective that cannot be equated to the compensatory intent of loss of support damages.
  
2. **Res Inter Alios Acta Doctrine**: The court reaffirmed that the doctrine is pertinent here because the grants are made to the foster parents and not directly to the children. Therefore, payments to grandparents who were already responsible for the children's welfare cannot be factored into the compensation calculation ([paras 45, 51]).

3. **Double Compensation**: The court rejected the RAF's argument that allowing both forms of support would lead to double compensation, emphasizing that the purposes of these payments are fundamentally different. The RAF’s compensation is aimed at remedying the economic loss incurred by the loss of a parental figure, while foster care grants serve to provide baseline support for children's welfare needs ([paras 38, 51–60]).

4. **Financial Implications for Children**: The court also considered broader implications of its ruling, particularly regarding vulnerable children and how their needs are addressed by both state grants and compensatory damages ([paras 25, 60]).

---

## C – Conclusion 
The Constitutional Court found in favor of the appellant, concluding that foster child grants should not be deducted from the award for loss of support. The court emphasized the distinct and non-comparable purposes of foster care grants and compensation damages, notably clarifying that the latter addresses specific financial loss resulting from the death of a parent. The ruling vacated the decision of the Supreme Court of Appeal and reinstated the High Court's determination regarding the grants being res inter alios acta ([paras 61–62]). 

The implications of this ruling underscore the protection of vulnerable children's rights and the necessity of maintaining adequate support mechanisms that do not penalize them for being in foster care. The Court imposed a duty on the state to ensure that its social security framework does not infringe upon the children's rights to a full measure of support necessary for their development ([paras 28, 34–35]).
