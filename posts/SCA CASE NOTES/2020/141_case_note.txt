Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note: **Hortors Pension Fund v Financial Sector Conduct Authority and Another** ([2020] ZASCA 141)

## F – Facts
The case arose from an appeal by the Hortors Pension Fund (the Fund) against a decision of the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which dismissed the Fund's application to declare Regulation 35(4) of the Pension Fund Regulations invalid and unenforceable ([paras 1–2]). The regulation required pension funds to allocate its actuarial surplus for enhancements due to former members who could not be traced into a contingency reserve account, which could not release funds except for payments to those members or public funds, such as the Guardian's Fund ([paras 4, 18]).

The Hortors Staff Pension Fund, established on November 1, 1950, transitioned from a defined benefit to a defined contribution fund in 1994, and faced issues with its record-keeping which impacted bonus distributions to members from 1994 to 2001 ([paras 6–10]). The Fund's trustees attempted to distribute a surplus to compensate for under-declared bonuses but later conceded that these distributions fell under the ambit of surplus legislation rather than just bonuses ([paras 11–12]).

Following regulatory challenges regarding the 2002 distribution, in which the Fund initially paid R86.7 million, the FSB (Financial Services Board) deemed it unlawful ([paras 11–12]). A tribunal later affirmed this view, necessitating compliance with the Pension Funds Act (PFA) regarding surpluses ([para 13]).

The Fund was compelled to submit a recovery plan due to financial instability, as determined by the Registrar of the FSCA ([paras 15–16]). The Fund contested the obligations imposed by Regulation 35(4) which, according to them, extended the Minister’s authority beyond what was legally permissible under the PFA. The Fund claimed that it undermined the board's discretion and adversely affected its financial operation ([paras 31–34]).

## I – Issues
The central legal question was whether Regulation 35(4) was ultra vires, meaning beyond the Minister's powers under the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956, and whether it contravened the principle of legality ([paras 2, 3, 40]). The issues specifically concerned:

1. The interpretation of the board’s discretion in creating contingency reserve accounts versus the Minister's prescribed regulations.
2. The implications of the lengthy delay in bringing the application before the court, particularly concerning a collateral challenge against the regulation ([paras 36–39]).
3. The legitimacy of the FSCA and Minister's interpretations of the relevant provisions in the PFA in relation to the contested regulation ([paras 35, 39]).

## R – Rules
The court examined several key pieces of legislation to inform its assessment:

1. **Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956**: This act outlines the authority of pension fund boards, specifically sections concerning surplus allocations and their management, notably sections 15B(4) and 15B(5) ([pars 57–59]).
2. **Regulation 35(4)**: It mandates that when a board calculates enhancements due to untraced former members, those amounts must be placed in a contingency reserve account with restricted access ([para 62]).
3. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: This act governs administrative actions, allowing for the review of decisions outside their scope of authority, as claimed by the Fund ([para 38]).

The court referred to the principles established in previous case law, such as **Tek Corporation Provident Fund and Others v Lorentz** which affirmed that the determination of surplus belongs to pension fund boards, emphasizing the need for prudent management of funds ([para 45]).

## A – Application
In its application for judicial review, the Fund argued that Regulation 35(4) imposed an undue restriction on the board's discretion by compelling the allocation of the full amount due into a contingency reserve account, undermining the established principles within the PFA ([paras 31–32]). The court agreed that the regulation usurped the board’s role, pointing out that a board should retain discretion over such funds and that the interpretation of the PFA provided a framework for such management ([para 64]).

The FSCA's stance that the regulation relates to liabilities of the fund, rather than contingent liabilities, was rejected. The court clarified that if benefits owed could be calculated and were due, these should not be treated as mere potentials but as established liabilities of the Fund's obligation to its members ([paras 35–38]). Additionally, the court considered that the principle of legality supports the need for regulations to remain within the bounds set by enabling legislation ([para 64]).

Regarding the delay in litigation, the court acknowledged that both the FSCA and the Minister effectively conceded this issue, enabling the evaluation of the core merits of the case without excessive delay hindering the justice process ([paras 43–44]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, declaring Regulation 35(4) invalid and unenforceable due to its overreach of the powers set out in the PFA. The court found that the previous interpretation by the lower court did not adequately appreciate the discretion inherent in the boards of pension funds to manage surpluses ([paras 72–73]). The decision has significant implications for the operational autonomy of pension fund boards, reaffirming the necessity for regulatory frameworks to align with the empowering statutes and the principles of good governance and legal compliance ([para 64]).

The outcome not only reinstated the board's discretion in managing funds but also illuminated the critical balance between regulatory authority and legislative intent in matters pertaining to pension fund management.
