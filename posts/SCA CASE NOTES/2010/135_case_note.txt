Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

### FIRAC Case Note: **CSARS v Plasmaview Technologies (Pty) Ltd** [2010] ZASCA 135

**Facts**:  
The case concerns an appeal by the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service ("SARS") against a judgment by the North Gauteng High Court, which had reviewed and set aside an alleged tariff determination related to the classification of fully assembled television sets imported by Plasmaview Technologies (Pty) Ltd ("Plasmaview"). The dispute originated from a series of imports of television components by Plasmaview from Korea in 2005–2006, specifically under tariff headings that would grant a full rebate under rebate item 460.16. Initially, parts were imported without tuners as 'input display units'; however, Plasmaview subsequently imported fully assembled television sets. The tariff determination by the SARS official, dubbed "Plasma 1," explicitly allowed certain classifications for the screens. On July 27, 2006, after inspections revealed underpayments in duties, the SARS issued new assessments based on an amended reasoning termed "Plasma 2," which Plasmaview claimed was improperly communicated. The High Court ruled in favor of Plasmaview, stating that the failure to communicate effectively and the presumed determination constituted unfair administrative action ([1–28]).

**Issues**:  
The Supreme Court of Appeal (“SCA”) was tasked with resolving whether the category of administrative action corresponded to the amended reason presented in "Plasma 2" and whether this constituted the proper basis for the subsequent duty assessments. Specifically, it questioned:
1. Was "Plasma 2" a legitimate determination under the Customs and Excise Act?
2. Did the communications and actions of SARS constitute administrative action that warranted review?
3. Were the subsequent duty assessments valid based solely on the schedules from SARS without reliance on "Plasma 2"? ([29–28]).

**Rules**:  
The legal framework for this dispute primarily revolves around the provisions of the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964, particularly:
- **Section 47(9)**: Outlines the process for determining and classifying imported goods.
- **Determinations and Tariff Classifications**: A tariff determination decisively categorizes an imported good under a specific tariff heading and provides the applicable duty rates ([30–31]).
- The principles governing administrative action established through relevant case law such as _Colgate Palmolive (Pty) Ltd v Commissioner, South African Revenue Service_ 2007 (1) SA 35 and related precedents ([28–29]).

**Application**:  
The SCA closely scrutinized the nature of "Plasma 2" in relation to statutory definitions of determination. It reasoned that "Plasma 1," which recognized specific tariff classifications for goods, had not been amended effectively by "Plasma 2." The essence of "Plasma 2" was merely an amendment to the reasoning of a classification that did not alter its core principles, thus failing to meet the statutory criteria for a tariff determination. The judges noted that while the altered reasoning suggested that plasma screens with tuners were ineligible for a rebate, there was no formal determination that conveyed this change until months after the assessments were made ([34–36]).

Moreover, the Court emphasized that the duties assessed were based on tangible schedules prepared by the SARS inspectors and not influenced by "Plasma 2". The nature of the goods' importation had changed from screens without tuners to fully assembled television sets, at which point the rebate classification under "Plasma 1" no longer applied. The timelines established in law allowed the original bureaucracy regarding tariff classification to stand, and any disputes regarding the schedules' correctness were unsubstantiated ([36–43]).

**Conclusion**:  
The Supreme Court of Appeal found in favor of the Commissioner, holding that "Plasma 2" was not a valid tariff determination and that the original determination "Plasma 1" remained applicable until the nature of the imports changed. Consequently, the assessments made by the SARS were legitimate, and the claim for underpaid duties was affirmed. The SCA thus set aside the order of the lower court, dismissing the application from Plasmaview with costs ([44]). This ruling reaffirms the importance of clear communication and the strict adherence to statutory requirements in tariff classifications, ultimately reinforcing the authority and procedural rigor of the South African Revenue Service in customs matters.
