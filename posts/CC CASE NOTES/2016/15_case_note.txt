Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

## FIRAC Case Note: **Electoral Commission v Mhlope and Others** [2016] ZACC 15

### F – Facts
The case arises from the Constitutional Court of South Africa where the **Electoral Commission (IEC)** appealed against the **Electoral Court’s order**, which postponed local government by-elections scheduled for February 24, 2016. The backdrop to this dispute was the ruling in **Kham and Others v Electoral Commission**, where it was determined that the IEC had an obligation to maintain a national common voters’ roll that must include voters’ physical addresses, particularly for municipal elections. The IEC failed to include 4,160 addresses, prompting a challenge from independent candidates.

After several administrative steps taken by the IEC to comply with the **Kham** order, an appeal was filed. The central issues revolved around whether the IEC had the obligation to provide addresses for voters registered before the December 2003 amendment to the **Electoral Act** and questions regarding the definition of "available" addresses. The **Electoral Court**, which found in favor of the independent candidates, ordered the IEC to provide the necessary information, set aside the voters’ roll certification, and postponed the elections.

The IEC, facing the looming deadline for the elections, sought direct access to the Constitutional Court and argued for a moratorium on its obligations concerning address recording.

### I – Issues
The pivotal legal and constitutional questions presented included:

1. Should leave to appeal be granted?
2. Does Section 16(3) of the Electoral Act require the IEC to provide addresses for voters registered before December 2003?
3. What is the prospective nature of the **Kham** order?
4. What constitutes "available" addresses under Section 16(3)?
5. Is there a basis for the IEC to appeal for direct access and a moratorium on compliance?
6. In the event of an appeal failure, what are the requisite measures to comply with the court’s determination?

### R – Rules
Key legal principles and rules invoked in the ruling included:

- **Section 19** of the South African Constitution, which guarantees the right to vote and to free, fair, and regular elections.
- **Section 1(d)** of the Constitution, establishing the rule of law as a foundational value.
- **Section 190** of the Constitution, mandating the IEC to manage elections and ensure their fairness.
- **Section 16(3)** of the Electoral Act, which requires the IEC to provide a voters' roll including addresses, where available.
- Precedent established in the **Kham** case, which emphasized the importance of maintaining an accurate voters' roll to ensure electoral integrity.

### A – Application
The court, after examining submissions, established that:

1. **Leave to Appeal**: The court concluded there were reasonable prospects of success on the constitutional issues raised, justifying granting leave to appeal.
   
2. **Obligation to Provide Addresses**: The court affirmed that the IEC must provide voters' addresses, even for those registered before December 2003, if those addresses were available. This interpretation aligned with the statute’s purpose and the overarching duty of the IEC to promote free and fair elections.

3. **Definition of "Available"**: The court favored a broad view of "available," stipulating it means addresses that the IEC could reasonably have obtained or recorded, not merely what was in existing records at the time of a request.

4. **Direct Access and Moratorium**: The court was hesitant to grant a blanket moratorium but recognized the IEC’s predicacy due to its prior failures. Consequently, it provided a structured remedy permitting time for the IEC to comply with address recording, specifically allowing until **June 30, 2018**, for collecting all addresses that were reasonably available.

5. **The Tlokwe Exception**: Notably, the court maintained that the resolution regarding the addresses was applicable to the Tlokwe elections, stressing the urgency to comply with prior court orders to rectify the irregularities that had surfaced.

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ruled as follows:

1. The IEC's appeal was dismissed based on its prior lapses in compliance with statutory obligations, compounded by its inability to record necessary voter information adequately.
2. The IEC was obliged to provide all potential voters' addresses for use in the upcoming elections, save for specific instances acknowledged in prior court orders related to the Tlokwe municipality.
3. Key timelines were established to enforce compliance and reporting mechanisms with the court to monitor the IEC’s progress in address collection.
4. Cost orders were made against the IEC, given its unsuccessful appeal and the procedural irregularities leading to this litigation.

The judgment reinforced the significance of maintaining a credible voter roll to ensure the integrity of elections, emphasizing that equality in voting rights cannot be compromised by administrative inefficiency.
