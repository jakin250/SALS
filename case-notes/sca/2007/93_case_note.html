Supreme Court of Appeal

Crossberg v S [2007] SCA 93 (RSA)

After being denied bail, Crossberg's legal representatives approached the Supreme Court of Appeal on 11 June 2007, and this court eventually granted him leave to appeal the convictions ([para 7]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2007

FIRAC Method Case Note

F – Facts

The appellant, Mr. Jewell Crossberg, was convicted on 5 April 2007 in the Transvaal Provincial Division on charges of murder and attempted murder, having been found guilty of shooting Mr. Jealous Dube and firing at four other persons ([paras 2–3]). Following his conviction, Crossberg received a sentence of 20 years’ imprisonment for murder and five years for each count of attempted murder, to run concurrently ([para 3]). He subsequently applied for leave to appeal the convictions, but the trial court denied this application. Simultaneously, he sought bail pending his appeal, which was also refused by the trial court on grounds that there were no reasonable prospects of success for his appeal ([paras 4–6]).

After being denied bail, Crossberg's legal representatives approached the Supreme Court of Appeal on 11 June 2007, and this court eventually granted him leave to appeal the convictions ([para 7]). The State’s counsel conceded at this new stage that, based on the appeal granting conditions, they would not oppose a fresh bail application; however, procedural complications, including delays in finalizing relevant judgments, led to further frustrations ([paras 8–9]).

The appellant ultimately pursued this appeal against the trial court's initial bail refusal rather than waiting for the fresh application, due to the significant delays and complications involved ([paras 10–11]).

I – Issues

The key legal questions before the Supreme Court of Appeal were:

  1. Does the trial court's refusal of bail pending appeal, based on the conclusion that there were no reasonable prospects of success, constitute a violation of Crossberg's right to liberty pending the resolution of his appeal?
  2. Should bail be granted in light of the change in circumstances after the appeal was granted, including the State’s non-opposition to a fresh bail application? ([paras 12–21])

R – Rules

The court relied on several legal principles and statutory rules:

  • Constitutional Rights: Section 12(1) of the Constitution of South Africa ensures the right to freedom and security of a person. This right is foundational in considerations regarding bail ([paras 17–19]).
  • Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977: Section 60 regulates bail decisions, specifically highlighting distinctions between bail pending trial and bail pending appeal ([para 14]).
  • Case Law: The court referenced pivotal cases, including S v Hlongwane and S v Van Wyk, elucidating the criteria for granting bail and the significance of the presumption of innocence until proven guilty ([paras 15–18]).

Additionally, it was emphasized that the prospects of success on appeal are fundamental in deciding bail applications, given that numerous applications might arise as dilatory tactics ([paras 15–16]).

A – Application

Applying the law to the facts, the court examined several factors:

  1. Change in Circumstances: The Supreme Court of Appeal noted that since it had granted leave to appeal, the basis on which the trial court had denied bail—that there were no reasonable prospects of success—had been undermined. The State's subsequent agreement to not oppose bail further weakened the position of the lower court ([paras 19–20]).
  2. Lack of Opposition by the State: Notably, the State did not file an affidavit contesting any of the facts put forth by Crossberg, thereby failing to establish a flight risk or show that granting bail would prejudice the interests of justice ([para 11]).
  3. Urgency in Bail Applications: The court underscored the urgency that surrounds bail applications given their impact on personal freedom. The court criticized the State for its delays and lack of timely communication regarding its stance on the bail application ([paras 16–24]).

Ultimately, the balance of interests favoured Crossberg, with the court concluding that deterrent measures could adequately address any concerns regarding his release pending appeal ([para 21]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal found in favour of Crossberg, granting him bail under specified conditions. The decision emphasized the importance of the appellant’s right to liberty pending appeal, especially given the changed circumstances whereby the State no longer opposed bail. The court’s order not only overturned the decision of the trial court but also highlighted procedural inefficiencies and the need for prompt action in bail matters ([paras 25(a)–(c)]). This case underscores the judiciary's role in safeguarding constitutional rights, emphasizing that even post-conviction, the principles of justice and fair treatment must be upheld.
