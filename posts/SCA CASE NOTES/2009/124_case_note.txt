Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2009

### FIRAC Case Note

**Selsley Farm Trust v Mhlongo (301/2008) [2009] ZASCA 124 (28 September 2009)**

---

#### F – Facts: 
The appellant, Selsley Farm Trust, is the current owner of the farm known as "Selsley," located in KwaZulu-Natal. The respondent, Simon Mhlongo, has resided on the farm since approximately 1953 and initiated proceedings against the appellant in the Land Claims Court. He sought a declaration under section 33(2A) of the Land Reform (Labour Tenants) Act 3 of 1996, claiming the status of a labour tenant ([1]-[2]). 

The historical background revealed that Mhlongo was born on the adjacent Maritzdal farm, where his parents worked for Mr. Guy Kimber. After Kimber relocated to Selsley in the 1950s, Mhlongo and his mother moved with him. Despite the farming operations changing hands over the years, Mhlongo remained on Selsley, continuing to provide labour and living there even after ceasing work due to health issues ([3]-[8]). 

Mhlongo maintained a simple home and was permitted to grow crops and graze cattle, while the appellant denied that these rights were contingent upon his labour ([8]-[9]). The respondent argued that he met the definition of a labor tenant according to the Act, which requires a person to reside on the farm, have rights to use farming land, and have family connections that meet specific criteria ([9]-[10]). 

During the trial, while the evidence suggested that Mhlongo's rights arose as a consequence of his and his mother's labour, the trial court found that the linkage was not sufficiently established but still declared him a labour tenant. The court based their decision on a presumption favoring Mhlongo, asserting the burden of proof lay with the appellant to prove Mhlongo was a farmworker ([11]-[13]). 

Subsequently, the appellant appealed against the trial court's finding ([14]-[15]).

---

#### I – Issues: 
The principal issue in this appeal was whether Mhlongo met the statutory definition of a "labour tenant" under the Land Reform (Labour Tenants) Act, specifically if he could establish the criteria outlined in the Act's definition ([17], [18]). The court was tasked with determining whether the respondent had proven that any rights to crop and graze were granted in consideration of labour provided to the current owner or lessee of Selsley, and whether he had established the necessary familial connections ([16]-[19]).

---

#### R – Rules: 
The rules pertinent to this case primarily arise from the Land Reform (Labour Tenants) Act 3 of 1996, which defines a "labour tenant" as one who resides on a farm and has rights to land use in consideration of their labour. The definition requires a conjunctive satisfaction of three conditions: residency, land use rights, and familial ties to past labour tenants ([9]-[10]). Importantly, the Act distinguishes labour tenants from farmworkers, with the presumption of labour tenancy arising only when the conditions of the definition are met ([9]-[12]). 

Additionally, the Deeds Registries Act of 1937 defines who qualifies as an "owner" for the purposes of the Act, thereby influencing the analysis concerning to whom labour must be provided ([18]).

---

#### A – Application: 
In applying the law, the court scrutinized whether Mhlongo satisfied the three criteria of the labour tenant definition. The first requirement of residency was satisfied, given his long-standing occupation of Selsley ([14]). The second requirement, regarding rights to farmland in exchange for labour, was more contentious. The trial court had found insufficient proof connecting the rights to crop and graze cattle with the labour provided by Mhlongo and his mother ([12]-[14]). 

However, the court underscored the error in the trial court's reasoning that presumed Mhlongo’s status as a labour tenant; it argued that unless the foundational criteria were met, such presumptions could not be applied ([13]). 

Most critically, the court addressed the third criterion concerning familial ties to labour tenants. Mhlongo’s assertion was based on the work of his mother under Guy Kimber, yet he failed to demonstrate that Guy Kimber was the legal owner at that time. Notably, Michael Kimber, the actual owner during Mhlongo’s mother's tenure, was identified only later, highlighting that the necessary labour linkage to the legal owner was not demonstrated ([18]-[21]). Thus, the court concluded that the failure to establish this link undermined Mhlongo’s claim as a labour tenant under the Act.

---

#### C – Conclusion: 
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal found in favour of the appellant, Selsley Farm Trust. The court set aside the orders of the Land Claims Court, ruling that Mhlongo had not proven that he was a labour tenant according to the definition in the Act. No order was made on the counter-application for eviction, reflecting the appellant's position. Thus, the decision clarified the burdens of proof and the necessity to establish concrete links in claims of labour tenancy in South Africa’s agricultural context ([22]). The implications of this ruling reinforce the statutory framework surrounding land reform and tenant rights in South Africa, emphasizing the need for precise adherence to defined legal criteria when pursuing such claims.
