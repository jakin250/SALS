Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: Le Roux v Nel (246/13) [2013] ZASCA 109

### F – Facts
This case involves an appeal by Pieter Paul Le Roux and Johanna Catharina Le Roux (the appellants) against the judgment of Williams J in the Northern Cape High Court. The appellants were seeking an order which would compel the first and second respondents (Paul Steenkamp Nel and Alwyn Jacobus Muller, the executor of the deceased's estate) to transfer ownership of a farm to them, based on an option to purchase that they claimed to have validly exercised ([1–3]).

The appellants obtained a written option from the deceased, Jan Harmse Steenkamp, to buy the farm located in Niewoudtville for R141,000. Notably, the option included a condition that it must be exercised within two months following the death of the deceased ([1]). The deceased died on 13 September 2003, and on 26 September 2003, the appellants instructed their attorney, Mr. D C Coetzee, to exercise this option. However, unbeknownst to them, the deceased had sold the farm to the first respondent on 8 July 2003, prior to his death, and the property had already transferred to the first respondent ([2]).

Upon discovering this situation, the appellants commenced legal action to enforce their alleged right to the property, asserting that their claim was based on the doctrine of notice and the validity of their option ([3–4]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal questions in this appeal include:

1. Whether the appellants' purported exercise of the option to purchase the land complied with the requirements of s 2(1) of the **Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981** ([9]).
2. Whether the appellants could successfully rely on the doctrine of notice, given that the first respondent was aware of the prior option when purchasing the property from the deceased ([4–5]).
3. If the exercise of the option was invalid, could the appellants invoke the doctrine of fictional fulfilment to effectively claim transfer of the property from the first respondent ([16–19])?

### R – Rules
The relevant legal framework includes:

- **Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981**, s 2(1), which requires that no alienation of land shall be of any force unless contained in a deed signed by the parties or their agents acting on written authority. This legislative provision aims to avoid uncertain disputes over the authority of agents ([10]).
- The **doctrine of notice**, where a prior option grants the holder equivalent standing as a prior purchaser, entitling them to assert rights over a subsequent purchaser with knowledge of the prior right ([5]).
- **Fictional fulfilment**, derived from cases like *MacDuff & Co (in liquidation) v Johannesburg Consolidated Investment Co* and expanded in *Du Plessis NO v Goldco Motor & Cycle Supplies (Pty) Ltd*, where a party may be deemed to have fulfilled a condition despite the non-compliance resulting from another party's interference ([17–19]).

### A – Application
In this case, the court analyzed the appeal based on the above issues. It first clarified that the instruction given to the attorney did not meet the written authority requirement of s 2(1) of the Alienation of Land Act. Although the appellants argued that the attorney's written letter could constitute an exercise of the option, the court noted that the letter did not reflect any written authority and was written based on oral instructions, which were insufficient ([9–14]).

The court also examined the appellants' reliance on the doctrine of notice. Although the first respondent had knowledge of the option when purchasing the property, the appellants needed to have a valid exercise of the option to claim rights against the first respondent. Since they failed to provide a valid exercise of the option, they could not invoke the doctrine of notice to succeed ([15]).

Further, the court rejected the argument invoking the doctrine of fictional fulfilment. Unlike in *Du Plessis*, where a party was actively prevented from fulfilling their obligations, in this case, the appellants were never hindered from exercising the option as they claimed to have done so. The court maintained that their inability to compel the transfer arose from their own attorney's failure to secure the necessary written authority ([18–20]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the decision of the court a quo, determining that the appellants had not validly exercised their option under the requisite formalities of the Alienation of Land Act. Accordingly, the application for reinstatement of the appeal was dismissed with costs, including the costs associated with the appeal and the amendment application ([21]). 

The ruling sets a significant precedent concerning the necessity for written authority in the execution of options for land sale, confirming the rigorous adherence to statutory requirements aimed at mitigating disputes over agency and contract enforcement in real estate transactions.
