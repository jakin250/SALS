Constitutional Court

Modjadji Florah Mayelane v Mphephu Maria Ngwenyama and Others

The primary issue arises from two women claiming to have valid customary marriages to the late Hlengani Dyson Moyana.

Constitutional Court 2013 [2013] ZACC 14 ## FIRAC Summary ### F – Facts: Relevant Bac

Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa
Case Number: CCT 57/12
Judgment Date: 30 May 2013
Citation: [2013] ZACC 14

FIRAC Summary

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])

The case involves Modjadji Florah Mayelane (Applicant) and Mphephu Maria Ngwenyama (First Respondent), with the Minister for Home Affairs (Second Respondent) and the Women's Legal Centre Trust, Commission for Gender Equality, and Rural Women’s Movement as amici curiae.

The primary issue arises from two women claiming to have valid customary marriages to the late Hlengani Dyson Moyana. Ms Mayelane asserts she married Moyana on 1 January 1984 under Xitsonga customary law, while Ms Ngwenyama claims a subsequent marriage on 26 January 2008 without Mayelane's consent. After Moyana's death in February 2009, both women sought registration of their respective marriages under the Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998 (Recognition Act), leading to disputes over validity.

The North Gauteng High Court declared Mayelane’s marriage valid but overturned the High Court's ruling regarding Ngwenyama’s marriage on appeal. The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that consent of the first wife was not required for the validity of subsequent customary marriages. Mayelane appealed this ruling.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 11–11])

  1. Should the Supreme Court of Appeal have determined the consent issue?
  2. Is the consent of a first wife necessary for the validity of her husband’s subsequent customary marriages?
  • (i) Does the Recognition Act require this consent?
  • (ii) Does the Xitsonga customary law impose such a requirement?
  1. If neither the Recognition Act nor Xitsonga customary law requires consent, does the Constitution necessitate the development of the law to include this requirement?

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 12–33])

The relevant legal framework includes:

  • Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998: Establishes the requirements for valid customary marriages and properties thereof ([paras 28–32]).
  • Constitution of South Africa: Sections affirming equality (s 9) and recognition of customary law (s 211). The court assesses whether customary laws align with constitutional values and whether development is warranted under section 39(2).
  • Judicial Precedents: Relevant cases including Bhe ([para 21]) and Gumede ([para 26]) were cited to emphasize the need for equality and dignity in marriages.

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 34–84])

The court outlined the shortcomings in prior judgments that failed to engage with the issue of consent grounded in Xitsonga custom. Upon evaluating customary practices, the court determined:

  • Recognition Act's Interpretation: No direct requirement in the Act for a first wife's consent for subsequent marriages; however, this does not preclude customary law from imposing additional requirements ([paras 34–40]).
  • Content of Xitsonga Customary Law: Evidence submitted reflected diverse perspectives on whether consent is necessary. The court determined that customary law, particularly Xitsonga, indeed requires the first wife’s consent for her husband to enter into further marriages ([paras 55–68]).
  • Evolution of Customary Law: The court emphasized the need for development of Xitsonga customary law to align with constitutional principles of dignity and equality, highlighting that denying a wife’s consent infringes on her rights ([paras 71–83]).
  • The court explicitly stated that marriages conducted without obtaining consent are invalid, thereby reinforcing the necessity of mutual agreement in marital relationships under transitional circumstances ([para 85]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 86–89])

The Constitutional Court:

  1. Granted leave to appeal and upheld Mayelane’s appeal against the Supreme Court of Appeal’s ruling.
  2. Declared Ngwenyama’s marriage to Moyana null and void for failing to procure Mayelane's consent, reinforcing the principle that consent is fundamental in customary marriages.
  3. The decision applies prospectively, ensuring protection for existing customary marriages while requiring future adherence to the consent requirement.

In summary, the court's ruling signals a vital shift in the protection of women's rights in polygynous marriages, mandating legal recognition of consent and establishing that customary law must evolve in alignment with constitutional tenets of equality and dignity.
