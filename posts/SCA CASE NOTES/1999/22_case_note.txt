# Case Note: Zwelake Ngcobo and Others v Salimba CC; Fikile Ngcobo v B.W.J. Van Rensburg

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Judgment Date:** 26 March 1999  
**Judges:** Mahomed CJ; Grosskopf and Olivier JJA; Farlam and Madlanga AJJA  
**Case Numbers:** 50/98; 631/97  

---

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The two appeals involve the interpretation of the definition of "labour tenant" under the Land Reform (Labour Tenants) Act 3 of 1996. The appeals, referred to as the Salimba and Van Rensburg appeals, arose from distinct claims for eviction by landowners against occupants asserting "labour tenant" status. 

In the Salimba appeal, the Respondent, Salimba CC, acquired a farm known as Rose Cottage in 1994. The Appellants, Zwelake Ngcobo, Bukiwe Ngcongo, Josephina Zungu, and Nomzinto Mthembu, asserted that they, along with their families, had a historical right to reside on the property and provide labour in exchange for land use. The initial court found them to be mere occupiers and ordered their eviction, leading to the present appeal ([2–3], [6]).

In the Van Rensburg appeal, Fikile Ngcobo contested a warrant for ejectment issued by the landowner, B.W.J. Van Rensburg. The lower court agreed that Ngcobo and another individual were not labour tenants as defined, primarily due to their failure to provide any labour to the landowner or his predecessors ([6]).

### I – Issues
The central issue in both appeals concerns the correct interpretation of the labour tenant definition as specified in Section 1 of the Act. Specifically, whether the definitions under subsections (a), (b), and (c) should be read conjunctively (requiring compliance with all three) or disjunctively (allowing for compliance with any combination). This interpretation affects the jurisdiction of the High Court versus the Land Claims Court in eviction proceedings ([3–5], [6], [9]).

Additionally, in the Salimba case, two further issues arose regarding:
1. Whether the "owner" referred to in paragraph (c) of the definition must be the same for the farm on which the Appellant currently resides and their parent's or grandparent's farm.
2. The burden of proof concerning whether the individuals were considered "farmworkers" under the Act ([5], [14]).

### R – Rules
The relevant legal framework is grounded in the Land Reform (Labour Tenants) Act 3 of 1996. The definition of "labour tenant" includes three components: 
- Paragraph (a) concerns the right of residence.
- Paragraph (b) addresses the provision of labour.
- Paragraph (c) involves historical residence and service of parents or grandparents on a farm.

The Act explicitly states that a "farmworker" is someone employed on a farm under a contract that primarily compensates in cash rather than in accommodation rights. The Act underscores the need for careful interpretation to avoid unjust outcomes, with the legislature's intent being to protect certain historical rights against arbitrary eviction as a legacy of the apartheid-era legal framework ([2–3], [6], [10]).

### A – Application
In determining whether the Appellants in both cases met the definition of "labour tenant," the court emphasized a conjunctive reading of the definitions. The majority opinion upheld the logic that to qualify as a labour tenant, all three elements must be satisfied to maintain consistency with the legislature's intent to protect genuine labour tenants ([4], [12]).

For the second and fourth Appellants in the Salimba appeal, it was clear that they could not prove compliance with paragraph (c) regarding service by their predecessors, rendering them ineligible for the protections of the Act ([4]).

Conversely, the first and third Appellants established parental occupation on a different farm while asserting historical continuity, leading to discussions about whether the relevant farms must be of the same ownership. The court ultimately diverged on this point, with findings supporting a wider interpretation for the first and third Appellants, allowing them to qualify as labour tenants ([12], [21]).

In the Van Rensburg appeal, the court adhered to the finding that the Appellant had failed to meet the requirement of proving his status as a labour tenant since he did not establish a record of providing labour to the current landowner ([6]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that:
1. The second and fourth Appellants in the Salimba appeal were not labour tenants and therefore, their appeal was dismissed.
2. The first and third Appellants were found to meet the definition of "labour tenants" and were entitled to protection under the Act, thus reversing their eviction order and dismissing Salimba's claim.
3. The Van Rensburg appeal was dismissed outright since the Appellant could not meet the criteria of a labour tenant.

This ruling underscores the importance of precise statutory interpretation and the balance the Act seeks to strike between tenant rights and landowner interests. The Act was fundamentally aimed at reforming historical inequities associated with land tenure under apartheid, thus any ambiguity in its application must favor the protected class to ensure that the Act serves its intended remedial purpose ([12], [30]). 

The overall implications of the decisions extend to the nature of rights and recognition of historical injustices, serving as a crucial precedent for future cases involving land rights in South Africa.
