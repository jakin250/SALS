Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

### FIRAC Case Note: South African Iron and Steel Institute and Others v Speaker of the National Assembly and Others [2023] ZACC 18

#### F – Facts
In the case of *South African Iron and Steel Institute and Others v Speaker of the National Assembly and Others*, the Applicants comprise various industry representatives, notably the South African Iron and Steel Institute (SAISI), the Fertilizer Association of Southern Africa (FERTASA), and ArcelorMittal South Africa Limited. They challenged the constitutionality of amendments made to the National Environmental Management Laws Amendment Act 2 of 2022 (NEMLA Act). The case arose due to allegations that Parliament failed to adhere to its constitutional obligations under sections 59(1)(a) and 72(1)(a) of the Constitution, which mandate public participation in the legislative process ([paras 1–3]).

The amendments in question involved a new definition of "waste" significant enough to impact both the steel and fertilizer industries, potentially altering the regulatory landscape without affording stakeholders the opportunity for further public participation ([paras 6–20]). The original Bill underwent multiple amendments, with critical changes introduced without public input after the "D" version, leading the Applicants to claim a breach of their rights to participate in legislation affecting their industries ([paras 3, 21, 32]).

#### I – Issues
The primary legal issue is whether the National Assembly (NA), the National Council of Provinces (NCOP), and Provincial Legislatures violated their constitutional mandate to facilitate public involvement during the legislative process, particularly concerning the materially amended provisions of the NEMLA Act ([paras 26–27]). Additionally, the court had to consider what relief should be granted in light of this constitutional breach, especially given that the amended Act had not yet come into force ([para 26]).

#### R – Rules
The constitutional provisions relevant to this case are sections 59(1)(a) and 72(1)(a), which require the Parliament and provincial legislatures to facilitate public involvement in their legislative processes. The Court referred to established precedents, particularly *Doctors for Life International v Speaker of the National Assembly*, which set the standard for public participation as a reasonable opportunity for stakeholders to engage in legislative processes ([para 29],[43]). The criteria for determining materiality in amendments include assessing the substantive impact of changes to the law, emphasizing that amendments with significant repercussions necessitate further public consultation ([para 32],[40]).

#### A – Application
The Court analyzed whether the changes made to the definition of "waste" constituted material amendments. It found that the revised definition substantially expanded the regulatory scope beyond previously established norms, thus requiring new public consultation ([paras 33–35]). The significant changes prompted potential responsibilities for a wider range of substances and added stringent compliance protocols, such as the requirement for waste management licenses, which could have dire economic consequences for the involved industries ([paras 36–40]).

The Court dismissed the Respondents' argument that ongoing engagements provided sufficient public involvement, noting that these did not extend to the critical amendments introduced after the earlier consultations had concluded. The Respondents mischaracterized their procedural adequacy by failing to account for the substantial nature of the changes made ([paras 45, 46]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court found that the Respondents had indeed failed to fulfill their obligation to facilitate public participation in the legislative process as mandated by the Constitution. Consequently, the Court declared the impugned provisions of the NEMLA Act invalid and unconstitutional without suspending the declaration of invalidity, as the provisions had not yet taken effect ([paras 50]). The ruling emphasizes the paramount importance of public participation in legislative processes, especially for amendments that significantly affect various stakeholders. The decision reinforces constitutional accountability in legislative activities and the necessity for transparent public engagement when addressing major regulatory reforms.

### Order
The Court ordered:
1. The declaration of failure by Parliament to facilitate public involvement concerning the amended definition of “waste,” “commercial value,” and “trade in” as well as the transitional provision in section 88 of the NEMLA Act ([Order 1]).
2. The invalidation of these provisions ([Order 2]).
3. The first and second Respondents to pay the Applicants’ costs ([Order 3]). 

---

This case highlights critical intersections between legislative processes and public participation rights in South Africa’s constitutional democracy, reinforcing the idea that substantial changes in law demand robust public scrutiny and involvement.
