Makatu v The State (612/12) [2013] ZASCA 149

The appellant, Humbulani Makatu, was charged with three counts before the Limpopo High Court: murder, rape (in terms of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997), and robbery.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 149

Facts

The appellant, Humbulani Makatu, was charged with three counts before the Limpopo High Court: murder, rape (in terms of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997), and robbery. During the proceedings, he pleaded not guilty to murder but guilty to rape and theft in respect of the robbery charge. A written plea explanation was submitted under section 112(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which the appellant confirmed ([1]-[5]).

The appellant's account indicated that he was intoxicated during the events leading to the murder of the deceased, Balanganani Tshavumbe Mukada, who was related to him. The appellant admitted to struggling with her when she resisted his sexual advances, which resulted in her death due to strangulation. This led to his conviction for murder based on dolus eventualis (the intention to cause death as a reasonable possibility) ([6]-[11]).

Earlier, on 9 June 2004, the trial court sentenced him to life imprisonment for murder, 20 years for rape, and a concurrent sentence of 6 months for theft. He sought to appeal both the convictions and the sentences, which was granted in June 2012 after an 8-year delay ([3]-[4]).

Issues

The case raised several legal questions concerning:

  1. Whether the written statement under section 112(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act sufficiently outlined the essential elements of rape and theft.
  2. Whether the evidence supported the charge of murder, specifically regarding the appellant's intent to kill ([6]-[9]).
  3. The appropriateness of the sentences imposed, particularly in relation to the minimum sentencing provisions of the Criminal Law Amendment Act, and whether substantial and compelling circumstances justified lesser sentences ([21]-[22], [35]-[36]).

Rules

The court's decision was rooted in various legal principles and statutory provisions:

  1. Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977: Sections 112(2) governs the plea of guilty and requires that the statement includes all essential elements of the crime charged.
  2. Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997: Governs sentencing for serious offenses such as murder and rape, stipulating minimum sentences unless substantial and compelling circumstances exist ([21]-[22]).
  3. Common Law Principles: Interpreting culpability, intention, and the distinctions between direct intent and dolus eventualis ([9]-[12]).

The guidelines from previous cases, such as S v Zinn and S v Malgas, also provided a framework for understanding the overall approach to sentencing and how court discretion should be applied ([35]).

Application

The court analyzed Makatu’s convictions and sentences:

Convictions

  1. Murder: The appellant's admissions indicated a struggle resulting in strangulation. The appellant contended that the evidence did not support an inference of intent to kill. However, the court found that the continuous physical assault demonstrated dolus eventualis. It concluded that the actual intent to kill was not necessary to uphold the murder conviction, as the appellant had foreseen the risk that his actions could lead to death ([11]-[12]).
  2. Rape: The court determined that while the appellant's plea explanation cited "sexual intercourse," it lacked specifics about penetration. However, it was held that the appellant, an adult, understood the term adequately. The distinction drawn with Nemavhola was critical, as the circumstances of the respondent's age were substantially different ([12]-[13]).
  3. Theft: Although the appellant contested this conviction, the court dismissed the claim against it, emphasizing that the act and the understanding expressed in the plea were sufficient to satisfy the definition of theft ([15]-[16]).

Sentencing

The trial court had imposed life sentences for murder, which was scrutinized given the absence of evidence supporting the claim that the murder was premeditated or met the necessary criteria under the Criminal Law Amendment Act. It was determined that both the murder and the rape sentences were exacerbated by significant misdirections from the trial judge, specifically the lack of justifications for the imposed life sentence. The court ruled that a 20-year effective sentence was more appropriate while still reflecting the seriousness of the crimes committed ([21]-[36]).

Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal against the convictions, affirming Makatu's guilt on all charges. However, it upheld the appeal against the sentences, significantly reducing them to 15 years for murder and 10 years for rape. The effective sentence was determined to be 20 years, reflecting a balance between retribution, deterrence, and the need for judicial accuracy and clarity in line with the Criminal Law Amendment Act ([1]-[3], [43]).

This case highlights the critical issues of judicial diligence in imposing sentences reflective of proper statutory interpretation and the importance of ensuring that legal representation comprehensively protects the appellant's rights, particularly in circumstances involving serious offenses that impact societal safety and justice ([30]-[41]). The identified systemic issues in the Limpopo area allude to broader discussions concerning the judicial process in South Africa, although not directly impacting this specific case’s outcome ([37]-[62]).
