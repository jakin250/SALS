Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

## Case Note: FIRAC Method - Liebenberg N.O. and Others v Bergrivier Municipality [2013] ZACC 16

**F - Facts:**

This case involves an application by 85 landowners, who are part of the Bergrivier Belastingbetalers-vereniging (BBV), against the Bergrivier Municipality regarding the validity of municipal rates imposed over several financial years. The Municipality, established on 5 December 2000, began imposing rates on property within its jurisdiction, which includes both urban and rural areas. Prior to the interim Constitution, rural landowners were not subject to municipal rates. However, this changed with the constitutional transition that mandated local governments to impose property rates.

The crux of the matter is that the landowners have not paid these rates for approximately eight years due to disputes over their legality. The Municipality sought declaratory orders from the High Court to confirm the lawfulness of these rates for the financial years 2001/2002 to 2008/2009. The High Court found that while some rates were valid, others were not due to non-compliance with statutory requirements.

The case eventually reached the Supreme Court of Appeal, which dismissed the applicants' challenges and upheld the Municipality's view, leading to the landowners applying to the Constitutional Court for leave to appeal against this decision, focusing primarily on the principle of legality regarding municipal rates ([paras 1-10]).

---

**I - Issues:**

The key issues in this case include:

1. Whether the Constitutional Court should grant leave to appeal based on the importance of the issues to local governance and the principle of legality.
2. Whether the applicants demonstrated valid grounds for challenging the rates imposed by the Municipality.
3. Whether the Municipality acted within its legislative authority when imposing rates during the relevant financial years.
4. Specific procedural challenges concerning notifications and the lawful promulgation of rates.
5. The implications of the repeal of section 10G(7) of the Local Government Transition Act by the Finance Act and its effect on the Municipality's authority ([paras 12-36]).

---

**R - Rules:**

The court relied on various statutory and constitutional provisions, including:

1. **Section 229 of the Constitution:** Grants municipalities the power to impose rates on property.
2. **Local Government Transition Act, 1993:** Provides the framework for the imposition of rates by municipalities including provisions on compliance and public participation.
3. **Municipal Finance Management Act:** Governs the financial accountability of municipalities, including budgeting and rate-setting processes.
4. **Rates Act, 2004:** Came into effect on 2 July 2005, regulating the imposition of rates and establishing specific procedures that municipalities must follow.
5. Case law, such as *Pretoria City Council v Walker* and *African Christian Democratic Party v Electoral Commission*, which provides guidance on compliance with statutory requirements and the principle of legality ([paras 22-41]).

---

**A - Application:**

The Constitutional Court critically examined whether the Municipality had acted within the bounds of statutory authority when imposing rates. The court noted that:

1. **Legality Principle:** The applicants argued that the imposition of certain rates was invalid because the Municipality had not complied with statutory requirements. The court emphasized that compliance with the statutory framework is vital for the validity of municipal rates.
   
2. **Section 10G(7) Repeal:** The critical issue rested on the interpretation of the repeal of section 10G(7) regarding its survival after the enactment of the Rates Act. The Majority opinion concluded that section 10G(7) remained applicable until the enactment of the relevant legislation as stipulated in section 179 of the Finance Act while the dissenting opinions contended it had been repealed when the Rates Act was enacted ([paras 52-78]).

3. **Substantial Compliance:** The court considered arguments of substantial compliance with statutory requirements. However, the majority held that failure to properly notify the public and comply with notice provisions constituted a significant breach of the obligatory processes for levying rates ([paras 153-166]).

4. The Municipalities failed to legally impose rates for the financial years 2006/2007 to 2008/2009 due to the non-fulfillment of promulgation requirements mandated by section 14 of the Rates Act ([para 146]).

---

**C - Conclusion:**

The Constitutional Court ultimately concluded that the applicants' challenges were valid, especially concerning the rates imposed for the 2002/03 financial year due to the Municipality's failure to comply with statutory requirements for public notice and participation. Consequently, the court dismissed the appeal [81].

The ruling reaffirms the crucial role municipal compliance with legal and procedural norms plays in the legitimacy of imposing rates. It reinforces the idea that local governments are accountable under constitutional law to ensure transparent governance and community participation in financial matters, establishing a precedent for future cases involving local government finance and property taxation ([paras 79-81]). 

**Ratio Decidendi:** The decision underscores the importance of adhering to procedural requirements in local governance, ensuring that municipalities operate within their legal frameworks when imposing rates, thus reinforcing the legitimacy of local governments in a constitutional democracy.
