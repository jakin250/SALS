Case Note: REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2006

# FIRAC Case Note: Prosch v Impala Water Users’ Association [2006] SCA 125 (RSA)

## F – Facts
The case concerns an appeal by six appellants, all farmers and water users, against judgments issued by the Natal Provincial Division of the High Court, which had ordered them to pay for water use charges imposed by the Impala Water Users’ Association, a water user association established under the National Water Act 36 of 1998. The orders included the right for the association to restrict water supply to the appellants until payment was made ([1]–[2]).

The dispute centers around the legality of a portion of the water charge associated with the financing of the Paris-Bivane dam, specifically termed the "dam financing component." This was not the first time the courts had addressed issues between the respondent association and its members; previous cases had established a backdrop for the current appeal ([4]–[5]). The appellants disputed a “properly compiled assessment roll,” which they contended was a prerequisite for imposing water use charges. Furthermore, they asserted that the assessment roll lacked essential details, such as payment due dates, rendering the claimed amounts unenforceable ([18]–[20]). 

Discretionary powers conferred upon the water user association's management committee under its constitution were cited by the appellants, arguing that prior authorization for loans, as delineated in clauses of the constitution, had not been duly observed, leading to their objection regarding the legitimacy of the charges ([21]–[23]).

## I – Issues
The key legal questions posed in this case revolve around whether:

1. A properly compiled assessment roll is a prerequisite to liability for water use charges imposed by the respondent.
2. The respondent has the authority under its constitution to levy charges that finance the dam, given the challenges to the legitimacy of the underlying loans incurred by the respondent ([2]–[3], [18]–[31]).

## R – Rules
The applicable legal framework includes the National Water Act 36 of 1998, particularly:

- **Section 57(1)**: Mandates that water use charges must conform to a pricing strategy set by the Minister ([12]).
- **Section 60(1)**: Asserts that charges based on section 57 are recoverable from the current landowner without releasing any other liable parties ([14]).
- **Section 93(1)**: Outlines the binding nature of the constitution adopted by water user associations, affecting members ([15]).
  
The respondent's constitution clauses pertinent to this matter include:

- **Clause 18**: Specifies the mechanism for assessing and recovering water use charges, outlining the necessity for an assessment roll ([16]).
- **Clause 17**: Requires proper consultation and compliance before the management committee can raise loans or incur certain types of financial obligations ([16]).

## A – Application
The court first addressed the appellants' assertions about the inadequacy of the assessment roll. It held that the failure to comply with certain procedural aspects of clause 18 did not nullify the respondent’s right to impose charges, as the provisions within the clause did not expressly state that such compliance was a pre-condition for liability or claims for payment ([25]).

Regarding the claim that charges could not be imposed since they were not overdue by 90 days (as derived from clause 18.5), the court found that the assertion lacked merit, considering that it was accepted by the appellants that charges were payable within 30 days. Therefore, the absence of a specified due date in the assessment roll did not detract from the respondent’s authority to recover the charges ([26]).

On the substantive issue concerning the legitimacy of the charges related to the dam financing component, the court emphasized that the management committee's ongoing obligation to repay the loan to First Rand Bank Ltd validated the obligation to collect water use charges to service that debt. It concluded that the financing charges incurred, even if related to future expenditures, fell within the ambit of lawful assessment under clause 18.1 of the constitution ([29]–[30]).

Lastly, the claim for damages made by the appellants was rejected, being deemed not readily ascertainable and insufficient to substantiate a claim against the respondent for the alleged breach of contractual duties as outlined in the constitution ([31]–[32]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeals of the appellants, reinforcing the authority of the Impala Water Users’ Association to raise water use charges for financing infrastructure like the Paris-Bivane dam. The court clarified that compliance with procedural aspects of the constitution, particularly regarding the assessment roll, is not an absolute barrier to enforcing financial obligations. It upheld the legitimacy of the water charges levied based on lawful expenditures incurred by the respondent, thus ensuring the viability of funds necessary for water management functions ([34]).

In addition, the court's recognition that the appellants could not set off their claims against the respondent signifies a continued emphasis on the binding nature of constitutions of water user associations. This outcome illustrates the delicate balance between the operational realities of water management and the legal obligations of individual members within cooperative structures ([35]).
