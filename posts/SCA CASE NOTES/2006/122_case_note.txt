# Case Note: South African Tourism v Pasis (PTY) LTD [2006] SCA 150 RSA

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The case arises from a contractual dispute between South African Tourism (the appellant) and Pasis (PTY) LTD (the respondent). The appellant is a state-funded organization tasked with promoting tourism within and to South Africa, while the respondent is the owner of a comprehensive tourism and business information system available in various formats ([1]). 

On 31 January 2000, the parties entered into an agreement that was to last until 30 January 2001. According to the agreement, the respondent committed to providing its information database for the appellant's promotional activities concerning tourism, while the appellant was obligated to market the respondent's products, including a website and a printed publication called Xplore ([1]).

On 27 January 2004, the respondent issued summons against the appellant in the Pretoria High Court, claiming that the appellant breached the contract by failing to promote and market its products, resulting in damages amounting to R23,222,321.31 ([2]). In response, the appellant filed a special plea arguing that the respondent's claim had prescribed, asserting that the claim accrued on or before 30 November 2000, which was more than three years before the summons was served ([3]). 

The special plea was determined without additional evidence. The lower court concluded that the appellant had not breached its contractual obligations by 30 November 2000, noting that the appellant still had until the expiration of the contract period on 30 January 2001 to fulfill its obligations. The appellant subsequently appealed this decision ([4]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issue in this case is whether the respondent's claim is barred by the prescription period established under South African law, particularly whether the appellant's alleged breach of contract occurred more than three years prior to the service of the summons ([5]).

Another related issue involves the interpretation of the contractual obligations stipulated in the agreement between the parties and whether those obligations necessitated actions to be performed before the end of the contract ([6]). 

### R – Rules
In South African law, the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 governs the prescription of debts. According to Section 11, a debt is prescribed after three years from when it becomes due. A creditor is considered to have knowledge, or should have knowledge, of the identity of the debtor and the facts from which the debt arises ([3]).

In this case, the court considered precedents on the interpretation of contractual obligations and related claims. It acknowledged that the determination of whether a breach of contract occurred largely revolves around the specific requirements laid out in the agreement between the parties ([4]).

### A – Application
In applying the law to the facts, the court analyzed the nature of the obligations imposed on the appellant under the contract. The appellant contended that by the end of November 2000, the respondent had already failed to fulfill various aspects of its contract, effectively classifying these as breaches ([5]). However, the court found that the interpretation of the agreement did not explicitly delineate a timeline for the appellant's obligations that would impose a breach prior to the end of the contract on 30 January 2001.

The inquiry focused on whether the duties outlined by the respondent in the pleadings were vague or subject to the interpretation that they could be performed until the end of the contractual term. The lower court's judgment concluded that because the obligations could still be performed before the expiration date, the claim did not fall due until that date ([6]).

Consequently, the court determined that since the respondent had not pled specific breaches that occurred before the three-year prescription period, the special plea should not be upheld. Thus, the appellant's arguments regarding prescription were dismissed, affirming that lawful performance under the contract remained viable up until 30 January 2001 ([4]-[6]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the lower court's decision, concluding that the appellant had not breached its contractual obligations before the expiration of the contract on 30 January 2001. Therefore, the respondent's claim, having been initiated more than three years after the alleged breaches, was not barred by prescription ([7]). The appeal was dismissed with costs, establishing important precedents regarding contract interpretation and the prescription of contractual claims in South African law.

This case reaffirms the necessity for precise allegations in claims related to contractual breaches and underscores the implications of the prescription period as it relates to claims arising from contractual relationships. The decision emphasizes the balance between protecting rights under contracts and respecting procedural timelines put forth by law.
