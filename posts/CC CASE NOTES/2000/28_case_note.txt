Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2000

### FIRAC Case Note: **Prince v President of the Law Society of the Cape of Good Hope** (CCT 36/00)

#### F – Facts
[1] Garreth Anver Prince, the appellant, is a practicing Rastafari who sought to register his contract of community service with the Law Society of the Cape of Good Hope, which refused to do so based on his prior convictions for cannabis possession and his intention to continue using cannabis as part of his religious beliefs ([paras 2–3]). The Law Society determined that anyone intending to break the law, particularly in regards to cannabis, could not be deemed a "fit and proper person" for admission as an attorney.

[2] Prince challenged this decision in the Cape of Good Hope High Court, asserting that the refusal infringed upon his rights to religious freedom, dignity, professional choice, and non-discrimination. The Law Society defended its position, citing that possession and use of cannabis remained illegal ([para 3]). 

[3] During proceedings, Prince also raised the constitutionality of the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act, 1992, which prohibits cannabis use ([para 4]). The Minister of Justice and the Attorney-General intervened in the case but asserted that the constitutionality of the Drugs Act was not an immediate issue ([para 5]).

[4] The High Court deemed the limitation on Prince’s religious freedom to be justifiable under section 36 of the South African Constitution but ultimately upheld the Law Society's decision ([para 6]). 

[5] After appealing to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) and obtaining leave for amendments, Prince asserted that the Drugs Act and related provisions were unconstitutional for failing to provide exemptions for bona fide religious practices ([paras 7–8]).

[6] The SCA dismissed his constitutional challenges, stating that allowing exemptions would undermine the legislative goal of drug abuse prevention and highlighted practical enforcement difficulties ([para 8]).

#### I – Issues
[1] The Constitutional Court was tasked with addressing several key issues:
   - Whether the refusal of the Law Society to register Prince’s community service contract constituted an infringement of his constitutional rights, specifically the right to freedom of religion.
   - The constitutionality of the prohibitions laid out in the Drugs Act and the Medicines Act, particularly regarding the absence of exemptions for religious use of cannabis ([paras 10–12]).
   - Whether further evidence regarding the Rastafari religious practices around cannabis use should be allowed in the appeal ([para 19]).

#### R – Rules
[1] Central to the case are the provisions of the South African Constitution concerning:
   - Section 15, which enshrines the right to freedom of religion ([para 26]).
   - Section 36, which allows for the limitation of rights if justifiable in an open and democratic society ([para 6]).
   - The Drugs Act and the Medicines Act, which govern cannabis possession and use, are also crucial to the Court's analysis ([paras 4–5]).

[2] Previous cases and jurisprudence were analyzed, especially the principles surrounding the constitutional rights in conflict with legislative acts ([paras 26–27]).

#### A – Application
[1] The Court comprehensively evaluated the constitutional implications of the refusal to register Prince's contract. The potential for restricting religious practices was weighed against public safety and the efficacy of law enforcement in regulating cannabis use within a minority religious context ([paras 12–14]).

[2] The Court recognized the relevance of additional evidence to delineate how the Rastafari community utilizes cannabis, who may possess it, and the extent of its religious significance. The failure to initially present this evidence precluded a robust assessment of the constitutional challenges ([para 18–21]).

[3] The Court noted that evidence presented by the Attorney-General, which claimed practical difficulties in enforcing a religious exemption, lacked substantive backing and relied on speculation rather than concrete facts. Thus, the assessment of limitations required more detailed factual framing ([paras 14–19]).

[4] The ruling of the SCA was scrutinized, particularly regarding its failure to constitute a quorum of eleven judges as mandated by the Supreme Court Act when addressing the constitutionality of legislative provisions ([paras 32–39]).

#### C – Conclusion
[1] The Constitutional Court concluded that dismissing Prince’s appeal without examining the necessity for religious exemptions for cannabis use would not serve justice or allow for a full exploration of the facts essential for an informed constitutional ruling. Therefore, the Court deemed it necessary to receive further evidence on these matters ([paras 30–31]).

[2] The judgment ultimately declared that the proceedings before the SCA were valid despite procedural critiques concerning quorum and that further evidence should be gathered to allow for a complete understanding of the Rastafari practices involving cannabis ([paras 41]). 

[3] The implications of this ruling extend beyond Prince’s immediate case; they resonate within broader discussions on the rights of minority religions, the scope of legislative powers to impose restrictions on such practices, and the overarching need for transparent and justifiable legal frameworks accommodating religious liberties in South Africa. 

[4] Consequently, the matter was remitted for further proceedings, allowing for an exploration of the necessary evidence to fully assess the constitutionality of the challenged provisions considering the context of the Rastafari religion ([para 41]).
