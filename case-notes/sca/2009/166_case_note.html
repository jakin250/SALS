Supreme Court of Appeal

Pricewaterhouse v Van Vollenhoven NO (261/2009)

This case comes before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) following a series of protracted legal battles between the parties, referred to as the appellants and respondents within the context of the main action.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–2])

This case comes before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) following a series of protracted legal battles between the parties, referred to as the appellants and respondents within the context of the main action. The appellants, comprising various entities associated with Pricewaterhouse Coopers, sought to review a decision made by the taxing master regarding the security for costs in litigation involving the second respondent, National Potato Co-operative Ltd ([para 1]).

On 5 December 2006, the trial judge ordered that the plaintiff (the second respondent) provide security for costs, a determination that later became the subject of an appeal ([para 2]). Following this, on 15 December 2006, the taxing master set the amount of security at R7.56 million but excluded fees for forensic auditors amounting to R5.46 million. It was this exclusion that initiated the defendants' application seeking review, which they filed on 6 June 2008, approximately 18 months post the taxing master’s decision.

The initial review application was dismissed by the North Gauteng High Court on 1 December 2008, leading to the current appeal ([para 2]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 4–6])

The primary issue in this appeal revolved around whether the defendants could successfully argue for an extension of the 180-day period prescribed under section 7 (1) of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA), after failing to bring their review application within the stipulated time frame ([para 4]). Specifically, the court needed to assess:

  1. Whether the high court erred in concluding that it would not be in the interests of justice to extend the review application time limit ([para 5]).
  2. The appropriate interpretation of "interests of justice" as outlined by the Constitutional Court in related cases ([paras 6], [7]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 3, 4, 5, 6, 8])

Relevant legal standards emerged from PAJA, particularly sections 7(1) and 9(2). Section 7(1) mandates that judicial review proceedings must be initiated within 180 days, while section 9(2) provides the court with the discretion to extend this time limit if it aligns with the interests of justice ([para 4]).

The court also referenced the standard for condonation articulated in Van Wyk v Unitas Hospital ([2008] ZACC 12), which emphasized that the determination of whether it is in the interests of justice to grant condonation must consider multiple factors, including:

  • The nature of the relief sought.
  • The extent and reason for the delay.
  • The impact of the delay on the administration of justice.
  • The reasonableness of the explanation for the delay.
  • The importance of the issue, and the prospects of success in the case to be reviewed ([para 6]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 8–12])

The court outlined that the appellants failed to adequately justify the delay in bringing their application. They posited that the delay was strategic; the attorney claimed they believed that if their appeal failed, no security would be required ([para 8]). However, the court found this rationale lacking:

  1. Lack of Timely Action: Compiling the founding affidavit was deemed a modest task that did not justify inaction for an extended time ([para 9]).
  2. Utilization of Resources: The defendants proceeded to secure the taxing master’s determination, indicating that they could have pursued the review concurrently ([para 9]).
  3. Failure to Communicate: The court noted a failure to communicate intentions to extend or review until it was too late, contributing to the perception of neglect ([para 9], [10]).

Ultimately, the reasoning of the lower court was upheld. The justifications presented for the delay were insufficient to meet the tests applied in Van Wyk v Unitas Hospital, particularly in respect to the requirements of interests of justice, leaving no basis for extending the 180-day review period as mandated by PAJA ([para 12]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 12, 13])

The SCA dismissed the appeal, supporting the findings of the lower court that the defendants had failed to prove that it was in the interests of justice to extend the time limit for their review application ([para 13]). The decision reaffirmed the strict adherence to PAJA's time frames while underlining the importance of timely engagement in judicial review processes.

The case has significant implications for future litigants contemplating review applications under PAJA. It emphasizes the necessity for prompt action in seeking judicial remedies, especially in administrative matters, and serves as a reminder that strategic delays can be detrimental to a party's legal standing. The final ruling clearly delineates the thresholds that parties must meet for establishing grounds of condonation in the context of administrative law.
