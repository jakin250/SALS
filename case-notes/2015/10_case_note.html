Pheko and Others v Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality (No 2) [2015] ZACC 10

The case at hand, Pheko and Others v Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality (No 2), arose from a previous ruling, Pheko I, which determined the unlawful removal of residents from the Bapsfontein Informal Settlement.

Constitutional Court 2015 [2015] ZACC 10

FIRAC Method Case Note

F – Facts

The case at hand, Pheko and Others v Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality (No 2), arose from a previous ruling, Pheko I, which determined the unlawful removal of residents from the Bapsfontein Informal Settlement. The applicants, Nthabiseng Pheko and others, were displaced due to safety concerns over the land they occupied, which was identified as dolomitic and deemed a disaster area by local authorities. This led to a court order issued on December 6, 2011, which mandated that the Municipality identify suitable land for the relocation of the affected residents and provide them with appropriate accommodation ([paras 3–4], [7]).

The Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality (the Municipality), responsible for enforcing these orders, was criticized for failing to submit several progress reports as mandated by the court ([paras 11–12]). Despite engagement attempts with residents, the Municipality did not comply with all the directives from the previous court rulings. Applicants alleged that this non-compliance amounted to contempt of court, prompting the current proceedings ([paras 12, 34]).

I – Issues

The principal legal issues presented by the case included:

  1. Whether the Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality demonstrated good cause not to be held in contempt for failing to comply with previous court orders ([para 24(a)]).
  2. Whether the attorney representing the Municipality, Mr. Bongani Khoza, should be held in contempt and subject to costs from his own pocket for his inaction in communicating the court's directives to the Municipality ([para 24(b), (c)]).
  3. The appropriateness of joining the Executive Mayor, Municipal Manager, and the Member of the Executive Council for Human Settlements (MEC) as parties to the contempt proceedings.

R – Rules

The court's analysis rested on several legal principles surrounding contempt of court. The leading case discussed was Fakie NO v CCII Systems (Pty) Ltd, which established that for a finding of contempt, certain elements must be met including:

  • Existence of a court order.
  • Proper service of the order to the alleged contemnor.
  • Evidence of non-compliance with the order which must be wilful and mala fide ([paras 25–35]).

The Constitution of South Africa, particularly section 165, emphasizes the authority of courts and binds all parties, including state organs, to comply with court orders ([paras 26–27]). Furthermore, local government obligations encompass ensuring adequate housing, executing the directives of court orders, and adhering to the principles of good governance as outlined in the Constitution and the Municipal Systems Act [54].

A – Application

  1. Municipality's Compliance: The court found that while the Municipality failed to comply with directives, it was not shown that it was served with the relevant orders or that non-compliance was wilful. The Municipality's attorney claimed he was unaware of the orders due to a change in contact details, thus mitigating the blame of contempt. The court determined that the failure to serve the orders negated findings of wilfulness or mala fides ([para 43]).
  2. Attorney's Liability: Mr. Khoza was not found guilty of contempt as the evidence did not support a conclusion of deliberate non-compliance or negligence ([paras 50–52]). However, the court expressed dissatisfaction with his professional responsibilities, leading to an order for him to bear a portion of the costs incurred in the contempt proceedings ([para 55]).
  3. Joinder of Officials: The court ruled that while the Mayor and Municipal Manager did not need to be joined in contempt proceedings, their joinder for the purpose of ensuring compliance with the court's supervisory orders was appropriate. Their statutory responsibilities required that they maintain oversight of the execution of the court’s obligations ([para 59]). The MEC was also joined to ensure that the Province would assist the Municipality in executing its obligations as outlined under the Housing Act ([para 60]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court affirmed that the Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality and Mr. Khoza were not held in contempt of court due to insufficient evidence of wilful disobedience and adequate explanation of their actions ([paras 1, 2, 68]). However, it stressed the importance of compliance with court orders as foundational to the rule of law, representing an institutional obligation of the government to respect judicial authority. The case underscored local government obligations to provide adequate housing and prompted the court to join key officials to facilitate compliance with prior orders, thereby reinforcing accountability within municipal governance ([para 64, 66]).

This outcome has significant implications for how municipal authorities engage with the judiciary and ensure the enforcement of court orders involving socio-economic rights, particularly concerning the provision of housing for vulnerable communities. The court's insistence on accountability at every administrative level reflects a commitment to uphold constitutional values, emphasizing the courts' role in supervising state actions in line with judicial directives.
