# Case Note: Adendorffs Boerderye (Pty) Ltd v Shabalala & Others (997/15) [2017] ZASCA 37

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10]) 

This case revolves around an appeal by Adendorffs Boerderye (Pty) Ltd (the appellant), the registered owner of a farm in Newcastle, KwaZulu-Natal, against an order from the Land Claims Court (LCC) regarding the removal of livestock belonging to the first and second respondents, Fayindolo Shabalala and the now-deceased Timothy Shabalala. The appellant contended that the respondents breached a grazing agreement, which permitted a limited number of livestock to graze on the land. Due to what the appellant claimed were excessive livestock numbers and resultant overgrazing, rehabilitation of the land had become necessary, incurring estimated costs between R35,000 and R60,000 ([paras 1–2], [4]).

Originally, the respondents had grazing rights under an agreement established with a previous owner of the farm. However, over time, their allowed grazing area was reduced, leading to significant disputes regarding the conditions of the land and the livestock ([paras 7–9]). The LCC found that while the grazed area needed rehabilitation, it issued orders without the appellant’s original requests and failed to give the parties adequate opportunity to submit further evidence or arguments ([paras 2, 11]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 16–24]) 

The core legal issue revolved around whether the LCC had the authority to grant orders not expressly requested by the parties, particularly without allowing them an opportunity to make supplementary submissions. The court also needed to determine the proper interpretation of the relevant provisions of the Conservation of Agricultural Resources Act 43 of 1983 (CARA) in relation to land ownership and grazing rights ([para 20], [22]).

Additionally, the court addressed procedural concerns regarding the failure of the respondents and their legal representatives to adhere to court rules throughout the proceedings, raising questions about the implications of such negligence in the broader context of access to justice and fair trial rights ([para 39]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 25–30]) 

The court relied on several legal principles, including those articulated in *Molusi & Others v Voges & Others NO* [2016] ZACC 6, which emphasized the importance of pleadings in defining the parameters of disputes between parties ([para 24]). The court also referred to CARA, particularly Regulation 9, which outlines the obligations of land users and landowners in managing agricultural land responsibly to prevent overgrazing and land degradation ([para 26]).

Further emphasis was placed on constitutional rights under s 24 of the South African Constitution, which guarantees an environment that is not harmful to health and wellbeing ([para 31]). The court highlighted the dual obligation imposed on both landowners and land users as defined in CARA, asserting that both parties bear responsibility for managing grazing lands ([para 30]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 16–37]) 

The SCA evaluated the LCC’s decision critically, noting that the court had overstepped its bounds in issuing orders that were neither requested nor argued by the parties. The appeal court held that a fundamental principle of fair trial necessitates that parties be informed of the case they must meet ([paras 24, 25]). The SCA highlighted that the LCC's approach violated the procedural rights of the parties, particularly since it did not provide them with an opportunity to present evidence or arguments regarding any alterations to the orders sought in the motion ([para 23]).

Moreover, the SCA clarified misunderstandings in the interpretation of CARA, asserting that the LCC's presumption about the appellant's obligations to intervene and manage the land solely placed the onus unfairly on the landowner, thus misaligning with CARA’s framework, which imposes shared responsibilities ([paras 26–28]). The court rejected the notion that the appellant had to wait for regulatory enforcement of grazing codes before acting and reinforced the right of landowners to pursue civil remedies in case of violations ([para 30]).

The appeal court further addressed the cross-appeal by the third respondent, the Minister of Rural Development and Land Reform, invalidating an order regarding assistance in securing alternative grazing. The court found that such an obligation fell outside the Minister's regulatory authority and was improperly directed by the LCC ([paras 35–38]).

Finally, the conduct of the respondents’ legal representation during the proceedings warranted scrutiny. Their consistent failure to comply with court rules led the SCA to impose costs on the attorneys personally (de bonis propriis), emphasizing the importance of professional responsibility within legal proceedings ([paras 39–46]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 1, 2, 39–46]) 

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the appellant, setting aside the LCC’s orders and replacing them with a directive for the respondents to remove their livestock within 30 days and prohibiting their return until the land was rehabilitated. The ruling underscored that proper procedural conduct must be adhered to by all parties and noted that legal practitioners have a duty to uphold the integrity of the court process ([paras 1, 2]).

The judgment reiterates the necessity for courts to provide equitable opportunities for all parties involved in litigation while defining the scope of responsibilities regarding agricultural resource management under CARA. The ruling serves as a significant precedent reinforcing the need for clarity and adherence to procedural rules in the South African legal context, particularly in matters involving land use and rights ([paras 20, 37]). The implications for legal practice are substantial, emphasizing the consequences faced by legal representatives for non-compliance and the need for diligence and responsibility in all filings and presentations to the court.
