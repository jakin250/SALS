Supreme Court of Appeal

Jongisile Fonjana v Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accident Fund and Road Accident Fund

Facts: The appeal centers on the interpretation of the term "military service" in Article 46 of the Motor Vehicle Accident Fund Act 93 of 1989 (‘the 1989 Act’) related to the appellant, Jongisile Fonjana.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2004

Citation:
THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA (2004) Case No. 19/03.

Facts:
The appeal centers on the interpretation of the term "military service" in Article 46 of the Motor Vehicle Accident Fund Act 93 of 1989 (‘the 1989 Act’) related to the appellant, Jongisile Fonjana. Fonjana was injured on 1 September 1996 while a passenger in a vehicle that overturned. He sought damages from the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accident Fund (first respondent) and, alternately, the Road Accident Fund (second respondent), based on claims arising under the 1989 Act, which was repealed in 1997 by the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 (‘the 1996 Act’). The second respondent, without denying liability, limited their obligation to R25,000, asserting that Fonjana was a passenger, excepted under a 46 only covered individuals "rendering military service". The key question arose regarding whether his status as a permanent member of the South African National Defence Force (SANDF) at the time of the accident constituted “rendering military service”.

Upon hearing that the court a quo ruled against the appellant—determining he was not "rendering military service"—Fonjana appealed on the grounds that his military membership sufficed for the exception under a 46 ([1]–[3]).

Issues:
The core issues present in this case are:

  1. What constitutes “rendering military service” under a 46 of the schedule to the 1989 Act?
  2. Does being a permanent member of the SANDF constitute “rendering military service” such that an exemption from the R25,000 limitation applies?

Rules:
The interpretation of "military service" is critical here. Article 46 of the 1989 Act stipulates exceptions for claims by passengers undergoing military service. The relevant legislation does not define “military service” directly. However, the Defence Act 44 of 1957 offers definitions relevant to “service in defence of the Republic.” The court referenced interpretations from both the 1989 Act and comparable provisions under the 1996 Act ([8]–[13]).

The court also noted previous cases that interpreted military service, in particular comparing its interpretation in Du Preez v Road Accident Fund ([10]–[12]), highlighting the lack of consensus on whether mere membership in the military is sufficient for the exemption.

Application:
The court assessed the definition of "military service" through the lens of legislative intent and historical context. The court a quo had held that "military service" referred only to actual active or combat service. However, the appeal court proposed a broader interpretation based on:

  1. The literal wording of the exception which did not explicitly limit military service to active duty.
  2. Legislative history that indicated an intention to protect members of the military (as seen in previous iterations of the exception) without restriction to combat service ([16]–[28]).

In its decision, the court highlighted that Fonjana, as a permanent member of the SANDF, was subject to military discipline and thus was engaged in military service at the time of the accident, despite not being in active duty or combat ([28]).

The court also refuted the respondent's arguments regarding irrational discrimination. It clarified that the exception in a 46 aims to facilitate transport for active members of the military and reinforced the notion that the limitation should not unduly burden those rendering military service.

Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that Fonjana was indeed "rendering military service" as per the requirements of Article 46 of the 1989 Act, thus entitling him to the exemption from the R25,000 limitation on claims. The court upheld the appeal, overturning the previous ruling of the court a quo and mandated the second respondent to cover costs, thus emphasizing the protective legislative intent towards military personnel ([29]).

This decision reinforces the importance of legislative context in statutory interpretation and suggests that membership alone can reflect a requisite status tied to rights and protections afforded under specific statutes.
