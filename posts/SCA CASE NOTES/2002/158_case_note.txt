Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2002

### Case Note: Road Accident Fund v Mongalo and Nkabinde

**FIRAC Method**

#### F – Facts

The case at hand involves two appeals heard concurrently; the first is Road Accident Fund v Mongalo, and the second is Road Accident Fund v Nkabinde. The common legal issue arises from the interpretation of a statutory provision regarding certificates issued to establish the existence of customary unions under the Black Laws Amendment Act 76 of 1963. The cases revolve around plaintiffs claiming damages for loss of support, alleging they were partners in customary unions with deceased individuals killed in traffic accidents ([1]–[3]).

In Mongalo, the Fund admitted liability but contested the plaintiff’s claim to be recognized as the deceased's customary partner, questioning the integrity of the certificate issued to her. The Court had to determine the legal status of this certificate, seeking a ruling on whether it barred the admission of evidence that the certificate was fraudulently obtained ([4]).

In Nkabinde, the plaintiff also claimed to be in a customary union with the deceased. The trial court allowed parties to present evidence concerning the authenticity of the relationship, concluding that the certificate was indeed obtained through fraudulent means. The trial resulted in a ruling against the plaintiff, leading to an appeal ([5]).

#### I – Issues

The central legal issues raised by these appeals are:

1. **Does a statutory certificate of customary union constitute absolute and conclusive proof against claims of fraud?**
2. **Can the admissibility of evidence regarding the fraudulent procurement of such a certificate be permitted in court?** ([1]–[6], [10]).

#### R – Rules

The stipulation under scrutiny is Section 31(2A) of the Black Laws Amendment Act 76 of 1963, which states that a certificate issued by a Commissioner shall be accepted as conclusive proof of the existence of a customary union. However, case law indicates that statutory provisions regarding conclusive proof are subject to challenge if fraud is alleged. This principle has foundational precedent, illustrated by cases like *African and European Investment Co Ltd v Warren and Others* where the court emphasized that no document is wholly unimpeachable, especially when fraud is involved ([6]–[9]).

Further, the Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998 introduces the nature of customary unions into discussions, establishing that a customary marriage must be recognized under law, and certificates serve to provide prima facie evidence thereof ([4]).

#### A – Application

In Mongalo, the Court concluded that the concept of ‘conclusive proof’ as stated in Section 31(2A) does not negate the possibility of proving that the certificate was obtained through fraudulent means. The court recognized that allowing the certificate to stand unchallenged despite potential fraudulent procurement would violate the principles of justice and equity under the law. The ruling of Lewis J, which had prohibited evoking evidence of fraud, was deemed incorrect ([10]–[12]).

Conversely, in Nkabinde, the court found that Snyders J incorrectly allowed the certificate's status to prevail despite evidence suggesting its fraudulent acquisition. The attempts made by the Fund to prove fraud, particularly via the father of the deceased's testimony, were assessed. The Court surmised that the trial court had erred by dismissing the plaintiff's credibility without a more comprehensive evaluation of the evidence presented ([18]–[20]).

The Court underscored that the obligation to ascertain the validity of the certificate must be ensured, particularly to prevent injustices arising from the fraudulent actions of one party ([19]).

#### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the appeals in both cases must succeed, reaffirming the legal standing that a certificate issued under Section 31 may be contested on grounds of fraud. The ruling clarified that such statutory certificates, while treated with presumptive validity, do not immunize themselves from attacks based on deceit ([22]).

In the Mongalo appeal, the initial trial ruling was set aside, and the Fund was authorized to dispute the validity of the plaintiff’s certificate based on alleged fraudulent collection, ensuring a fair re-examination of evidence. In Nkabinde, the decision to grant absolution from the instance was also overturned, restoring the plaintiff's right to claim for damages pending the validation of the customary union ([23]). 

This decision not only clarifies the interpretation of conclusive proof in legislative contexts but also reinforces the fundamental principle that the law disallows the advancement of any claim or defense rooted in fraud ([12]–[21]).
