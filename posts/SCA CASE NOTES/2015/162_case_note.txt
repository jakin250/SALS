# Case Note: City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality v Uniqon Wonings (Pty) Ltd (20771/2014) [2015] ZASCA 162

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The case arises from a dispute between the City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality (the "City") and Uniqon Wonings (Pty) Ltd ("Uniqon"), a property developer. In 2003, Uniqon purchased farmland located within the jurisdiction of the defunct Kungwini Local Municipality for the purpose of establishing a township named Six Fountains Estate, which ultimately comprises 200 erven approved through general plans. Following Kungwini's dissolution in 2011, its jurisdiction was assumed by the City ([1]).

By June 2012, the City initiated legal proceedings against Uniqon for unpaid municipal rates associated with the township property. Uniqon later attempted to obtain clearance certificates—required by section 118(1) of the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 (the "Systems Act")—to facilitate the transfer of certain erven to potential purchasers. The City denied this request, demanding the settlement of all unpaid arrear rates for the entire township rather than per erf. Faced with this refusal, Uniqon sought an urgent application in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, challenging the City’s position ([2]).

Ebersohn AJ of the High Court ruled in favor of Uniqon, asserting that the City must issue the clearance certificates upon receipt of an application fee and that it could not levy rates against untransferred erven. The City appealed this ruling ([7], [8]). 

### I – Issues
The appeal raised several pivotal legal questions, including:
1. **Interpretation of Section 118(1)** of the Systems Act: Does it mandate that municipal rates and charges must be paid for the entire township before transferring individual erven, or can these be applied solely to the specific erf being transferred?
2. **Employer's Obligation to Issue Clearance Certificates**: Is the requirement for clearance certificates per erf contingent on the payment of rates applicable to each erf or the entire township?
3. **Legal Precedents and Their Applicability**: How do past judicial interpretations, particularly regarding municipal property ratings, influence the current dispute? ([3]–[4]).

### R – Rules
Key statutes and legal authorities influencing this case include:
1. **Section 118(1) of the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000**: This section necessitates that prior to the registration of property transfer, a clearance certificate must be provided by the municipality, indicating that all dues for municipal services have been settled ([3]).
2. **Local Government: Municipal Property Rates Act 6 of 2004**: Outlines obligations for municipalities regarding the assessment and collection of rates on property.
3. **Precedents**: Notable cases discussed include:
   - *Estate Breet v Peri-Urban Areas Health Board*,
   - *Florida Hills Township Ltd v Roodepoort-Maraisburg Town Council*,
   - *Mooikloof Estates (Edms) Bpk v Die Stadsraad van Tshwane*,
   which address the principles underlying municipal property ratings and the conditions under which property becomes liable for rates ([9]–[22]).

### A – Application
The court found that clarity was required regarding the nature of property as described in the Systems Act. Ebersohn AJ had earlier determined that individual erven do not come into existence as separate entities until sold and transferred, thereby impacting their rateability. The court reasoned that the outstanding rates should be assessed against the erf intended for transfer, rather than the entirety of the township ([18]–[22]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal noted that the provision “in connection with that property” in section 118(1) implies that only rates related to the specific erf to be transferred, accrued over the prior two years, must be settled for the clearance certificate to be issued. The City’s previous practice of applying rates across the entire township structure was deemed inconsistent with the legal framework, as it failed to recognize the separate legal identity of the individual erven until the transfer process was initiated ([25]–[27]).

The court further reinforced that the City is mandated to assess amounts due in relation to each erf prior to the issuance of a clearance certificate, thereby establishing a procedure to ensure fair dealings between the municipality and property developers ([30]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld certain aspects of Uniqon's appeal, determining that the City must:
1. **Issue Clearance Certificates**: It is authorized to issue these certificates contingent on the payment of rates attributable to the specific erf intended for transfer rather than the entire township ([28]–[30]).
2. **Revise its Rating Framework**: The City must adjust its practices to facilitate the valuation and assessment of municipal rates applicable to individual erven rather than imposing blanket assessments of the entire township, reflecting a landmark interpretation of property law that balances municipal revenue needs with developers' rights ([26]–[30]).

In conclusion, the SCA's ruling affirms that the established principle must be that municipal rates and charges are attributable directly to respective erven during transfer, rather than to the remaining extent of the township, promoting operational clarity and fairness in property transactions ([30]).
