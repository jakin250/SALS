# FIRAC Case Note: Ex Parte: Christopher Lance Mercer and Beverley Pervan

## Facts
In September 2001, Christopher Lance Mercer and Beverley Pervan (the applicants) were convicted in the Kuruman Magistrates’ Court for contravening provisions of the Nature and Environmental Conservation Ordinance 1974, which prohibits the harboring of certain animal species without a permit ([1]). The specific species involved were three Northern Cape lynx, for which the applicants had repeatedly been denied permits. The basis for these denials stemmed from the status of these animals as "potential problem animals" under the Problem Animal Control Ordinance 1957 ([1]). 

Subsequently, the applicants sought to challenge both their convictions and the constitutionality of the Ordinance in the Northern Cape High Court. Initially set for a hearing on September 9, 2002, the case was postponed to March 3, 2003. In light of this, the applicants applied directly to the Constitutional Court, requesting that it hear the matter without waiting for the High Court's decision on their appeal ([2]).

## Issues
The Constitutional Court was tasked with examining various constitutional issues, primarily whether the Problem Animal Control Ordinance 1957 infringed upon the applicants' rights under section 9 (the right to equality) and section 24 (the right to an environment that is not harmful to health or well-being) of the South African Constitution. The central legal question was thus whether the Ordinance, as applied to the applicants, was unconstitutional and if the Constitutional Court had jurisdiction to intervene before the High Court had rendered its decision on the appeal ([3]).

## Rules
The key legal authorities relevant to this case included the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, particularly sections 9 and 24, which respectively deal with equality before the law and the right to a healthy environment. Furthermore, the court also considered procedural rules regarding its jurisdiction and the appropriateness of direct applications as opposed to awaiting lower court judgments ([3]).

## Application
In evaluating the application, the Constitutional Court emphasized the principle of proper legal process, recognizing the importance of allowing the High Court to first address the matter before further escalation to the Constitutional Court. The delay in the High Court was acknowledged, but the Constitutional Court found it premature to intervene prior to the High Court's determination on the validity of the Ordinance ([3]). This restraint illustrated a deference to lower courts and upheld the structured process of appeals within the South African legal system.

Furthermore, while the court hinted at the potential merits of the applicants' claims regarding the Ordinance's constitutionality, it ultimately refrained from adjudicating those issues at this juncture, leaving room for the applicants to present a fuller case in the proper forum ([3]).

## Conclusion
The Constitutional Court refused the application from the applicants to hear their case directly, underscoring the necessity of allowing the High Court to first adjudicate the matter. The decision reaffirmed the hierarchical structure of the South African courts and the importance of judicial process. As such, the court's holding reflects a commitment to procedural propriety and the careful navigation of constitutional law ([4]). The implications of this case are significant, as it reinforces the precedent that challenges to legislation must first be thoroughly vetted at trial courts before reaching higher courts, ensuring that all arguments are fully explored and adjudicated at appropriate judicial levels.
