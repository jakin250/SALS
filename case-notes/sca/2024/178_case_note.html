Supreme Court of Appeal

in FIRAC Format: N M v The Central Authority for the Republic of South Africa and Another

The case involves an appeal from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg by NM against the return order issued for her son NEM (aged two years and eight months) to be returned to Australia, his country of habitual residence.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 178 (19 December 2024). --- ## F – Facts: Relev

Neutral Citation: N M v The Central Authority for the Republic of South Africa and Another (1078/2024) [2024] ZASCA 178 (19 December 2024).

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–4])

The case involves an appeal from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg by NM against the return order issued for her son NEM (aged two years and eight months) to be returned to Australia, his country of habitual residence. NM and MBM, NEM’s parents, entered into a long-distance relationship that culminated in marriage in December 2020. After NEM’s birth, the family resided in Australia until September 2022 when NM sought to visit her family in South Africa, allegedly with an intention to return. Contrary to her agreement with MBM, NM communicated intentions of permanent residency in South Africa around two weeks before the intended return date, leading to MBM initiating Hague Convention proceedings for NEM's return (para 1-5).

At the first hearing, the high court ordered the separation of NM's constitutional challenge of section 275 of the Children’s Act and required her to return NEM to Australia. NM claimed a grave risk to NEM's welfare under Article 13(b) of the Hague Convention, arguing exposure to psychological harm due to alleged domestic violence and lack of support from MBM (para 4, 6-8).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 9–40])

The primary legal issues presented for determination were:

  1. Whether NM satisfactorily established a defense under Article 13(b) of the Hague Convention by showing that NEM's return to Australia would expose him to grave risk of harm.
  2. Whether the high court erred in separating NM's constitutional challenge against section 275 of the Children’s Act from the main application for NEM’s return (para 9).

Further considerations involved the procedural aspects, such as the high court's decision-making process regarding NM’s counterapplication and its impact on the immediate welfare and custody proceedings concerning NEM (paras 35–40).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 13–19, 21–28])

The court invoked provisions of the Hague Convention, specifically Article 12 and Article 13(b). Article 12 mandates the immediate return of a child wrongfully removed or retained unless a year has elapsed since the wrongful act, which was undisputed in this case (paras 13-14). Article 13(b) articulates exceptions regarding the return order if grave risk of physical or psychological harm exists (paras 14-15).

Moreover, the court referenced the precedent set in Koch and Sonderup, establishing the gravity of the standard required to invoke defenses under Article 13(b), which necessitates a high threshold of harm beyond mere discomfort or natural distress related to a court-ordered return (paras 15-19).

Importantly, the court reiterated that circumstances existing at the time of the return matter should be distinct from post-return evaluations in further custody proceedings, aligning with the interpretation established in earlier cases (paras 19-20).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 21–34])

The court found that NM had failed to demonstrate that the return of NEM would expose him to grave risk, refuting claims based on Ms. Keeve’s social worker report, which lacked a balanced assessment and relied on NM's uncorroborated accounts. The report did not account for the available support services in Australia that could mitigate potential disruptions to the child’s wellbeing (para 21-24).

While NM asserted a history of abuse and lack of family support in Australia, the court underscored that allegations of domestic violence did not inherently correlate with a grave risk of harm to NEM in this context, emphasizing that NM’s decision to unlawfully retain NEM was a pivotal factor (para 25–28). The court acknowledged the constitutional challenge NM made against section 275, but held that separating the challenges allowed for expedient justice, critical under international treaty obligations (para 35-36).

The court thus concluded that the high court's ruling on the return of NEM was not erroneous, and NM's defenses under Article 13(b) were insufficient to meet the required evidentiary burden (para 27).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 48])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld in part the high court's order requiring NEM’s return, emphasizing the guidelines provided by the Hague Convention that demand the return of children wrongfully retained, underlining that any risks of harm alleged must be sufficiently evidenced and assessed within the context of the return application (paras 30-48).

The court also reinforced the necessity for protective measures to be evaluated and potentially incorporated within the return order to mitigate any perceived harsh outcomes associated with the return. The involvement of the Central Authority throughout was noted as crucial for compliance with the Hague Convention, signifying the collective responsibilities under international child abduction treaties (para 44).

The judgment signals a firm adherence to international standards concerning child welfare, with implications that future proceedings must prioritize children’s immediate return to their habitual residence, while taking into account the broader context of familial and jurisdictional complexities (paras 40-47).
