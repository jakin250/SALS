Supreme Court of Appeal

Themba Yende and Another v Felani Yende and Another (1128/19) [2020] ZASCA 179

The case stems from a dispute regarding the rightful senior traditional leader of the amaYende asoGenyaneni traditional community in South Africa.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 179

F – Facts

The case stems from a dispute regarding the rightful senior traditional leader of the amaYende asoGenyaneni traditional community in South Africa. On 23 October 2012, the Premier of Mpumalanga officially recognized Mr. Themba Yende as the senior traditional leader following a recommendation from the Commission on Traditional Leadership Disputes and Claims (the Commission) ([paras 2–3]). Felani Yende and his siblings challenged this decision, arguing it did not comply with customary laws and procedures relating to the amaYende, specifically citing their exclusion from the process ([paras 4–10]).

The background indicates that Felani and Themba are half-brothers. Felani was born from the marriage of the late chief Leonard Yende with Ms. Maria Mnisi (the Great Wife), whereas Themba was born out of wedlock to a different mother ([paras 8–9]). Notably, Themba had assumed Yende’s surname only after his father’s death in 1997. The controversy centered around the legitimacy of Themba's recognition as the leader, especially since Felani claimed to be the rightful heir as the first male from the Great House.

Felani contended that the customary practices favored his recognition, claiming that he and his siblings had raised objections against Themba’s nomination at a public meeting but were not afforded a subsequent opportunity to represent their interests before the decision was made ([paras 12–14]). The matter ultimately escalated to the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which initially dismissed Felani’s review application ([paras 3, 4]). The Full Court later reversed this decision, highlighting procedural irregularities in the prior recognition process ([paras 4, 30]).

I – Issues

The key legal questions in this dispute are:

  1. Were the relevant members of the Royal Family afforded the opportunity to make representations regarding the nomination of a senior traditional leader? ([para 10])
  2. Was the Premier's decision in compliance with the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003 (the Framework Act)? ([para 10])
  3. Was there sufficient proof of the living amaYende customary law to substantiate the claims made by either party regarding succession? ([para 10])

These questions encompass both procedural fairness in the administrative decision-making processes and compliance with customary laws, raising broader implications for traditional leadership in South Africa.

R – Rules

The relevant legal authorities include:

  1. Constitution of the Republic of South Africa: Section 211 recognizes the institution, status, and role of traditional leadership, while Section 211(3) requires courts to apply customary law that is in accordance with constitutional values ([para 5]).
  2. Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003: Particularly, § 11 concerning the identification and recognition of senior traditional leaders, and § 22 detailing the functions and duties of the Commission ([paras 5, 6, 7]).
  3. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000: This governs the legality and fairness of administrative actions ([para 3]).
  4. Judicial Precedents: Cases such as Mphephu v Mphephu-Ramabulana ([para 11]) and Bapedi Marota Mamone v Commission of Traditional Leadership Disputes and Claims provide interpretations of the responsibilities of the Royal Family and the Commission under the Framework Act ([paras 12, 27]).

A – Application

In addressing the issues, the court underscored procedural fairness, emphasizing the failure of the Commission to adequately involve Felani and his siblings as members of the Royal Family. The court noted that the Commission did not provide them the opportunity to represent their interests, which constituted a significant procedural irregularity that violated the principles of natural justice, particularly the audi alteram partem rule ([paras 17–18]).

The court found that the Commission's recommendations did not thoroughly consider the actual living customary laws of the amaYende and stressed the importance of evidencing customary practices, which must be derived from the community itself rather than assumptions or uncorroborated claims by individuals ([paras 18–28]). Moreover, the court highlighted that such customary laws are not static and can evolve, yet no compelling evidence was presented to validate Themba's claims to have deviated from inherited customs ([paras 24–26]).

The ruling of the Full Court was thus upheld as it appropriately recognized the procedural shortcomings in the Premier’s decision-making process and how these flaws highlighted the failure to respect the customary structures as dictated by the Framework Act ([paras 19, 29]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, affirming that the Premier's recognition of Themba as the senior traditional leader was procedurally improper and not in line with the Framework Act due to the exclusion of the legitimate members of the Royal Family ([paras 30–32]). The judgment underlined the need for due consideration of customary law and the necessity for transparency and participation in processes affecting communal leadership.

The implications of this case reaffirm the judiciary's role in safeguarding compliance with both administrative justice and customary law, emphasizing the significance of collective family participation in affirming traditional leadership roles ([para 32]). This ruling serves as a critical marker for how traditional leadership disputes should be approached within the framework of contemporary legal standards in South Africa.
