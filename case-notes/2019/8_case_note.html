Case Title: Trustees of the Simcha Trust v Da Cruz and Others; City of Cape Town v Da Cruz and Others [2018] ZACC 8

Constitutional Court 2019 [2018] ZACC 8 --- ### F - Facts: The case arises from two

Case Title:

Trustees of the Simcha Trust v Da Cruz and Others; City of Cape Town v Da Cruz and Others [2018] ZACC 8

F - Facts:

The case arises from two consolidated applications before the Constitutional Court related to a building development in Cape Town, specifically regarding the interpretation of section 7(1)(b)(ii) of the National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act 103 of 1977 (the Act) ([1]). The applicants in CCT 125/18 are the Trustees of the Simcha Trust, who owned the property on Buitenkant Street adjacent to a sectional title scheme called Four Seasons, owned by the first respondent, Joao Jose Ribeiro Da Cruz. The City of Cape Town, as the third respondent, granted building approval to the Trust for the construction of additional stories up to the boundary line shared with Four Seasons ([4]-[7]).

In CCT 128/18, the City of Cape Town challenges the approval given by the Municipality. The High Court had set aside the development approval due to apparent procedural flaws and errors in law, noting that the decision-makers had failed to consider all relevant disqualifying factors as required under the Act ([9]-[12]).

The appeals follow the High Court's conclusion that the local authority had erred in applying the relevant tests. The Full Court confirmed that the decision-makers incorrectly assumed that compliance with zoning laws negated the possibility of disqualifying factors being present ([13]-[14]).

I - Issues:

The principal legal issue is whether the legitimate expectations test, which is traditionally applied to claims of derogation in value, should extend to the other disqualifying factors set out in section 7(1)(b)(ii) of the Act. Specifically, the court needed to ascertain whether these factors should be evaluated collectively or separately and how the legitimate expectations of neighboring property owners should factor into this assessment ([21]).

R - Rules:

The primary legal texts referenced include section 7(1)(b)(ii)(aa) of the Act, which delineates the conditions under which a local authority must refuse a building application, namely if the building is projected to:

  1. Disfigure the area (subsection (aaa)),
  2. Be unsightly or objectionable (subsection (bbb)),
  3. Derogate from the value of neighboring properties (subsection (ccc)).

The ruling relies heavily on the principles established in Walele v City of Cape Town ([2008] ZACC 11) and Camps Bay Ratepayers and Residents Association v Harrison ([2010] ZACC 19), particularly regarding the requirement that decision-makers must be positively satisfied that no disqualifying factors are present before approving a building application ([22]-[28]).

A - Application:

The court established that the legitimate expectations test is applicable across all disqualifying factors outlined in section 7(1)(b)(ii). By applying this criterion, it necessitates that local authorities substantiate their determinations regarding a building’s impact on neighboring properties, not just from a perspective of aesthetic disfigurement but also concerning the potential derogation of property values and general community expectations ([29]-[31]).

In this context, the Constitutional Court concurred with the Full Court that the decision-makers had used an incorrect test for evaluating these disqualifying factors. They deduced that a mere compliance with zoning regulations does not automatically negate the presence of disqualifying factors. Rather, each aspect must be evaluated in isolation to ensure that all possible neighborhood impacts are considered ([22]-[24], [33]).

The court further highlighted that the test respects the need for objectivity, emphasizing a balance between the interests of property owners and the community's collective welfare, a necessity in promoting harmonious urban development ([30]-[31]).

C - Conclusion:

The Constitutional Court dismissed the appeals in both cases (CCT 125/18 and CCT 128/18) and ordered the applicants (Trustees of the Simcha Trust and City of Cape Town) to pay the costs of the respondents, including the costs of two counsel where applicable. The court affirmed the proposition that the legitimate expectations test applies broadly to all disqualifying factors under section 7(1)(b)(ii) ([35]-[37]). This ruling underscores the importance of ascertaining the community's expectations and the potential impacts on neighboring properties as integral to responsible and lawful administrative decision-making in urban planning.

Thus, the decision highlights the necessity for local authorities to ensure that their evaluations of building applications align with both statutory obligations and established legal principles, ensuring that such approvals do not adversely affect the rights and expectations of neighbouring property owners ([36]-[37]).
