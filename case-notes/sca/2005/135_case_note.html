Supreme Court of Appeal

Republic of South Africa v Formalito (Pty) Ltd

In Republic of South Africa v Formalito (Pty) Ltd [2005] SCA 328/04, the case revolves around Formalito, a licensed dealer under the Arms and Ammunitions Act 75 of 1969, which has been importing firearms and ammunition into South Africa since 1964.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2005 1964 ([para 5]). 2

F – Facts:

In Republic of South Africa v Formalito (Pty) Ltd [2005] SCA 328/04, the case revolves around Formalito, a licensed dealer under the Arms and Ammunitions Act 75 of 1969, which has been importing firearms and ammunition into South Africa since 1964. In November 2000, the South African Revenue Service (SARS), represented by the appellant, received information from a former employee of Formalito suggesting that the company had under-declared the value of imported goods ([para 1]).

Upon investigation by Ms Denyssen from the Special Investigations Unit of SARS, it was determined that Formalito had underpaid customs duties, ad valorem duties, and VAT amounting to R696,652.95. Consequently, SARS advised Formalito that the goods were liable for forfeiture under section 87(1) of the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964. Alternatively, in case the goods could not be found, SARS was entitled to collect an amount equivalent to their value, which was assessed to be R3,792,912 ([para 2]).

On 22 January 2002, Denyssen demanded payment. Following negotiations, on 26 September 2002, it was decided that Formalito would be liable for 50% of the goods' value, amounting to R1,896,456 ([para 3]). Formalito then sought judicial review, and the High Court set aside the SARS decisions, ordering SARS to pay the costs ([para 4]). The case was subsequently appealed by SARS ([para 4]).

I – Issues:

The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal were:

  1. Whether Formalito's declaration was false as per the requirements under s 44(11)(a) of the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 ([para 5]).
  2. Whether the penalty imposed on Formalito was reasonable in light of SARS's own guidelines ([para 12]).

R – Rules:

The applicable legal statutes and principles included:

  1. Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964: Particularly, section 39(1), which mandates the submission of a bill of entry with full particulars of imported goods, and section 44(11)(a) regarding false declarations ([paras 6-7]).
  2. Definition of 'False': The term was interpreted based on the Concise Oxford English Dictionary and previous rulings, notably R v Mahomed ([para 8]). The court concluded that "false" in the context of s 44(11)(a)(i) referred to declarations that were untrue to the knowledge of the person making them.
  3. Customs Offences and Penalty Policy: This policy outlines the guidelines for setting penalties for customs offences. Under SARS policy, the prescribed penalty for a similar contravention would typically involve 50% of the underpayment, with a minimum of R500 ([para 12]).

A – Application:

In reviewing the evidence presented, the court focused on the key question of whether Formalito’s submissions constituted false declarations:

  • False Declaration: The court noted there was no evidence of a genuine error in the application of tariff codes that would support Formalito's claim. The managing director, Mr Engelbrecht, displayed ignorance about tariff matters but nonetheless provided incorrect tariff codes to the clearing agents, which was ultimately driven by the intention to benefit the company financially ([paras 9-10]). The court concluded that the consistent use of incorrect tariff codes over several years demonstrated a conscious effort to mislead SARS, affirming that the declarations were false based on the knowledge of Formalito ([para 11]).
  • Reasonableness of the Penalty: The court found that Ms Denyssen, in determining the penalty, failed to follow SARS's own guidelines, which would have significantly reduced the penalty imposed. The court remarked that ignoring these guidelines was unreasonably contrary to the intended framework for penalties ([para 12]). Accordingly, the court ruled that this deviation warranted a referral back to SARS for reconsideration ([para 12]).

C – Conclusion:

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately ruled in favor of SARS, stating that:

  1. The appeal was successful and the High Court's order was set aside ([para 13]).
  2. Formalito's obligation to pay the customs duties and VAT was affirmed, while the decision regarding the forfeiture amount was set aside for reconsideration, highlighting the unreasonableness of the imposed penalty ([para 13]).

The court's interpretation of "false" set a precedent for future cases involving declarations under the Customs and Excise Act. Additionally, it underscored the importance of adhering to internal guidelines for determining penalties, ensuring consistency and fairness in enforcement by SARS.

This decision serves to reinforce the obligation of accuracy in customs declarations and affirms SARs’ authority to levy penalties for non-compliance, provided that due processes and established guidelines are followed.
