Hunter v Financial Sector Conduct Authority and Others [2018] ZACC 31

Constitutional Court 2018 [2018] ZACC 31

F - Facts

The case arises from an application for leave to appeal against the judgments of the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissing Ms. Rosemary Thérésé Hunter's request for the Financial Sector Conduct Authority (FSCA) to investigate alleged irregularities linked to the "cancellations project." During this project, over 6,700 pension funds were cancelled by the registrar, often without properly constituted boards, raising concerns regarding compliance with statutory and constitutional duties ([1]–[2]).

Ms. Hunter, former deputy registrar of pension funds and deputy executive officer at the FSCA, initiated the application in the public interest. She alleged that the cancellations were unlawful and potential financial prejudice existed regarding affected pension funds ([4]–[5], [10]). The FSCA conducted several investigations into the project, invoking Justice O’Regan's recommendations and engaging KPMG and Mr. Mort for further scrutiny ([17]–[18], [19]). However, Ms. Hunter maintained that these investigations were inadequate and urged the court to compel a more thorough examination of the cancellations ([1]–[24]).

The High Court ruled that Ms. Hunter lacked the necessary standing to claim the relief sought, concluding that the application did not possess genuine constitutional importance, leading to the dismissal of her request ([20]–[21]). This decision was upheld by the Supreme Court of Appeal, prompting Ms. Hunter's appeal to the Constitutional Court ([22]).

I - Issues

  1. Standing: Does Ms. Hunter possess the necessary legal standing to compel FSCA investigations regarding the cancellations project?
  2. Duty to Investigate: Should public functionaries be obligated to investigate allegations of irregularities in the execution of their duties?
  3. Appropriateness of Review Mechanism: Is the appropriate legal mechanism for challenging alleged irregularities through the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA) review, or should it be addressed through a legality review?
  4. Ministerial Intervention: Did the Minister have a duty to intervene in the governance complaints made by Ms. Hunter?

R - Rules

  1. Standing: Section 38(d) of the Constitution permits individuals to advocate for the protection of a right in the public interest, thus providing Ms. Hunter with standing ([30]).
  2. Duty to Investigate: The Constitution does not impose a general duty on public functionaries to investigate irregularities; however, the court has acknowledged that officials must investigate allegations of potential unlawfulness when brought to their attention, as articulated in Khumalo v MEC for Education KwaZulu-Natal ([40]–[41]).
  3. Review Mechanism: The relevance of the PAJA arises directly from its provisions ensuring lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair administrative action. However, decisions not constituting “administrative action” fall under legality review ([48]–[49]).
  4. Minister’s Obligations: The Minister's obligation is contextualized within the Public Finance Management Act, requiring an investigation into allegations of financial misconduct ([14]).

A - Application

Standing

The court held that Ms. Hunter had standing under Section 38(d) of the Constitution, emphasizing her experience and role in the public service which aimed at safeguarding the interests of pension beneficiaries ([30]).

Duty to Investigate

The majority opinion stated that although the Constitution does not impose an absolute duty to investigate, public functionaries must fulfill their obligations towards accountability and transparency. The FSCA had conducted several investigations showing an acknowledgment of this duty ([37]–[46]).

Review Mechanism

The court ruled that the PAJA does apply concerning administrative action regarding fund cancellations. Nevertheless, since Ms. Hunter was a private party contending that the FSCA had failed to act upon compelling allegations, her case could also fit under legality challenge rather than PAJA ([49]–[106]).

Minister’s Obligations

The Minister's role, although pivotal, did not include a supervisory duty over internal investigations already undertaken by the FSCA. The refusal to intervene was deemed appropriate given the circumstances of ongoing investigations ([26]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal, dismissing Ms. Hunter's appeal and maintaining that the FSCA had adequately engaged in investigations, thereby discharging its constitutional duty. The court found no evidence of systemic irregularities requiring immediate further inquiry beyond what had been conducted. Consequently, the principle of no costs was applied, reflecting the constitutional significance of the matter and Ms. Hunter's public interest standing ([61]–[62], [59]–[60]).

Implications

This decision reinforces the boundaries within which public functionaries operate, establishing a legal precedent regarding their obligations to investigate alleged irregularities. It emphasizes the importance of upholding standards in administrative actions impacting the public, particularly in sensitive areas such as pension fund management. Additionally, the case strengthens the nuance of standing for public interest litigants, indicating a broader interpretation that aligns with the Constitution's spirit in safeguarding vulnerable societal members' interests.
