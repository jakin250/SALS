# FIRAC Case Note: Valerie Wolfaardt and Derek Wolfaardt v The State A26/2002

## F – Facts ([paras 1–18])
The respondents, Valerie Wolfaardt (First Appellant) and Derek Wolfaardt (Second Appellant), were found guilty of three counts of rape in a Regional Court and sentenced to 18 years of effective imprisonment each on 30 October 2000. Both appellants sought leave to appeal against the guilty verdicts, which was initially denied on 30 November 2000. Subsequently, their application for a late petition under section 309C of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 was further complicated due to the documents apparently going missing. The appellants then applied for condonation which the state did not oppose, leading to the appeal being heard.

The core factual underpinning revolves around a complex and unusual situation where the appellants were a married couple. The complainants included two younger sisters of the First Appellant and a friend of the younger sister, all minors at the time of the alleged incidents. The First Appellant facilitated situations whereby the complainants were persuaded to come to their residence under false pretenses related to the couple’s supposed fertility issues, leading to sexual encounters initiated and conducted by the Second Appellant.

The testimonies revealed a consistent modus operandi where D.K. (the friend) was 13 years old when introduced to the scenario, and both siblings, René, aged 13, and Sabriena, aged 9, faced similar treatment. Testimonies included split emotional responses, especially from the First Appellant's sisters, who experienced distress in testifying against a family member, highlighting the familial and psychological dynamics at play.

The trial was lengthy, extending over a year, with the witnesses displaying significant emotional responses, indicating the traumatic nature of their experiences.

## I – Issues ([paras 17–29])
The critical legal issue arose from the nature of the evidence given during the prosecution and the ensuing question of consent. Specifically, whether the complainants had valid legal consent to sexual acts under the duress of deception employed by the appellants when they were minors. 

The Court needed to determine two key questions:
1. **Determination of Consent:** Whether the consent given by the complainants, obtained through fraudulent misrepresentation regarding the purpose of the sexual acts, amounted to valid consent in the eyes of the law.
2. **Capacity and Volition:** Whether the complainants were capable of giving consent given their age (under 16) and the circumstances under which the consent was obtained.

## R – Rules ([paras 30–39])
The legal framework applicable to the case primarily included the South African Criminal Law regarding rape and consent, specifically Section 261(1)(e) of the Criminal Procedure Act and relevant sections from the Sexual Offences Act 23 of 1957. 

In defining rape, the common law and statutory law indicate that valid consent must be informed, voluntary, and made by a person with the capacity to consent. Specific precedents such as *R v M* 1953 (4) SA 394 (A) and *R v Z* 1960 (1) SA 739 (A) reiterated that mere agreement obtained under duress, especially when the complainant is a minor, cannot constitute legal consent.

The court also drew on insights from *Snyman's Strafreg*, addressing the necessity for the individual granting consent to possess the cognitive ability to understand the nature of the act and its implications.

## A – Application ([paras 40–50])
In applying the law to the facts, the court observed the following:
- **On Consent:** The majority of the testimonies indicated that the complainants were misled regarding the purpose of engaging in sexual intercourse under the guise of assisting with supposed fertility issues. The court elaborated that even if verbal consent was given, it was fundamentally flawed and invalid given the coercive circumstances created by the First Appellant.
- **On the Capacity to Consent:** The evaluative analysis of the witnesses’ ages—D.K. and René both being 13, and Sabriena only 9—impacted the legal interpretation of their capacity to give meaningful consent. The court established that minors cannot provide legally valid consent under such circumstances, particularly when the combination of emotional manipulation and familial relationships created an inherently coercive environment. The court underscored that consent given by the complainants could not withstand scrutiny due to their inability to fully comprehend the implications of their actions.

The court critically assessed the credibility of the evidence presented, siding with the testimonies of the complainants, especially as they consistently maintained their accounts throughout the trial despite significant emotional distress. 

## C – Conclusion ([paras 51–58])
In conclusion, the appeals against the convictions for rape were upheld, and the findings on counts one and two were set aside. It was concluded that while the actions of the Second Appellant fell under a violation of the Sexual Offences Act (specifically Article 14(1)(a)), the subsequent repercussions did not equate to a conviction for rape due to the invalidation of consent.

The court reaffirmed that both appellants would be appropriately convicted of lesser charges under the Sexual Offences Act. Notably, the sentences were modified to enable both appellants to serve concurrent sentences of three years for their respective offenses.

The conviction regarding the third charge, involving the youngest complainant, was upheld given the established legal presumption that a child under the age of 12 cannot consent.

In essence, the case underscored the vulnerable position of minors in sexual abuse allegations and affirmed judicial commitments to protect minors in the face of familial and societal pressures. The outcome embodied critical legal principles surrounding consent in sexual offenses, especially in cases involving minors exploited through deceitful methodologies.
