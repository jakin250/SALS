Supreme Court of Appeal

Adlem v Arlow (782/11) [2013] ZASCA 7

Following the appeal, the appellants were granted leave to make submissions regarding the provisional costs order made in the High Court (para 1).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 7

F – Facts

This case arises from a dispute between the appellants, Eugene Adlem and Joseph Adlem (collectively referred to as “the appellants”), and the respondent, Nestor Algemus Arlow (para 1). The matter was previously adjudicated in the North West High Court, where an initial costs order was made by Leeuw JP. The appellants sought to appeal this costs order, specifically looking to vary it. The matter of costs stemmed from the argument presented at the High Court, which revolved around the proceedings relating to a counterclaim made by the appellants alongside the respondent's claim. The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) held hearings on 6 November 2012 and delivered its judgment on 14 March 2013 (para 1).

Following the appeal, the appellants were granted leave to make submissions regarding the provisional costs order made in the High Court (para 1). The order explicitly allowed them to argue the costs incurred from the High Court's decision, but it limited the scope by not permitting submissions regarding the costs of the appeal itself (para 1).

I – Issues

The central legal issue in the case pertained to the appropriate costs that should be incurred by the parties as a result of the High Court's order (para 2). More specifically, the question was whether, given the circumstances of the appeal and the successful argument presented by the appellants, the costs should fall on the respondent, as opposed to being allocated across the entire proceedings (para 2). Another pertinent issue was the implication of varying the initial costs order and whether it would unduly complicate the ongoing litigation process between the parties (para 2).

R – Rules

The court's determination was guided by principles of civil procedure regarding costs. The leading authorities on costs allocation in South African law emphasize the principle that costs follow the event, meaning that the party who is successful in an argument typically has their costs borne by the losing party. This principle applies unless there are circumstances to justify a departure from this norm (para 2). Furthermore, the court considered previous judgments and the legal framework surrounding costs in an appeal context, solidifying the premise that costs related to a specific argument should be considered independently of broader proceedings (para 2).

A – Application

In applying the rules to the case at hand, the SCA unequivocally concluded that the appellants prevailed in their argument concerning the question addressed in the High Court (para 2). The Court outlined that the order related to costs was not supposed to cover all aspects of the case but was confined to the costs arising specifically from the argument on the question before the High Court. The court acknowledged that while the response to the posed question could have significant ramifications for subsequent claims and counterclaims in the matter, that was not a determinant for the costs at this juncture (para 2). Thus, it reaffirmed the usual rule that costs should follow the victory in argument while also distinguishing this specific argument from the wider implications of the original case (para 2, 3).

The SCA, therefore, upheld the High Court's order regarding the costs and made an additional order that the respondent was to cover the appellants' costs incurred in the attempts to vary the initial costs decision (para 3). This decision signaled a reaffirmation of legal costs principles while navigating the complexities introduced by the ongoing litigation between the disputing parties.

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal confirmed the costs order from the High Court, thereby ruling in favor of the appellants’ request for costs associated with their successful argument. The court concluded that it was appropriate for the respondent to bear these costs, as the appellants were successful in their assertions regarding the cost allocations (para 3). The case underscores the judicial principle that costs generally follow the event, aligning with established legal precepts of fairness in litigation (para 2). This ruling not only provided resolution to the specific costs matter at hand but also highlighted the court's intent to manage complex proceedings without delving into uncertainties regarding counterclaims, which remained to be addressed by the High Court in subsequent applications (para 2, 3). The implications of this decision resonate throughout litigation practices, ensuring parties are held accountable for costs in accordance with their litigation successes, thereby promoting judicial efficiency and equity.
