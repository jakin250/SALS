# FIRAC Case Note: Hoosen N.O. and Others v Deedat and Others [1999] ZASCA 22 (16 July 1999)

## F – Facts

The case involves the Islamic Propagation Centre International ("the Trust"), established through a Notarial Deed of Trust in April 1985 and registered with the Master of the Supreme Court on 19 June 1985. The third respondent is a co-founder and one of the original trustees ([1]). The Trust manages significant assets, including income-producing properties, but has dealt with factionalism among the trustees causing management issues ([2]).

In 1996, the third respondent suffered a debilitating stroke, impairing his ability to act as a trustee. In light of this, he granted a Special Power of Attorney to his daughter-in-law, the first respondent, on 10 March 1997, allowing her to vote and act on his behalf during meetings of the Trust ([3], [4]). Following this, the appellants sought to declare the Power of Attorney null and void, arguing it constituted an impermissible delegation of trustee powers ([4], [5]). 

The initial application arose amidst a backdrop of ongoing tensions among trustees and related litigation surrounding trustee appointments ([5], [6]).

The court a quo (Magid J) ruled in favor of the respondents, validating the Power of Attorney and allowing the first respondent to act as a trustee, prompting the current appeal ([7]).

## I – Issues

The key legal issue for the appeal is whether the third respondent was legally permitted to delegate his trustee duties through the Power of Attorney granted to the first respondent ([8-9]). This involves interpreting the Trust Deed and determining whether it expressly or impliedly allows such delegation.

## R – Rules

The applicable legal principles center around the definition and role of trustees as articulated in the Trust Deed. The law indicates that trustees, unlike agents, exercise their powers personally and do not have the right to delegate those powers without express authorization ([21], [23]). 

Relevant clauses within the Trust Deed specify that the management and control lie with the trustees collectively ([16-17]). Provisions for delegation exist but strictly limit it to collective action by the trustees, indicating that individual delegation is not permitted without consent or express provision ([23-24]). Furthermore, the principle of “delegatus non potest delegare” (a delegate cannot further delegate) serves as a foundational rule, affirming that managers must act in unison ([21], [22]).

## A – Application

In interpreting the terms of the Power of Attorney, the court found that it effectively conveyed the third respondent's responsibilities and powers, enabling the first respondent to act with a degree of independence not permissible under the Trust’s governance ([10-12]).

The court emphasized that the relevant clauses of the Trust Deed indicated no express provision for individual delegation of powers, reflecting a clear intent for collective function among the trustees ([23]). The delegation, as contended by the appellants, fundamentally altered the decision-making process that is to repose in the collective judgment of the trustees, something that cannot be delegated to a non-trustee without legal justification ([14-28]).

The court concluded that the Power of Attorney led to a temporary abdication of the third respondent's functions to the first respondent, which was found to lack legal validity, as it did not stem from any express or implied authorization found in the Trust Deed ([29]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appellants were justified in seeking an order declaring the Power of Attorney legally void. The delegation of trustee powers to the first respondent was impermissible based on both the express provisions of the Trust Deed and the principles of trust law governing trustee behavior. 

Furthermore, the court ruled that the appellants were entitled to their costs, thereby setting aside the lower court's ruling that had imposed costs against them. The court thus affirmed the need for clarity and adherence to the terms of the Trust Deed, emphasizing the importance of the individual attributes of trustees in upholding the Trust's objectives. 

The final order was that the Power of Attorney was declared null and void and that the first respondent was interdicted from acting under it. The costs of the proceedings were to be borne by the Islamic Propagation Centre International, emphasizing accountability among the trustees going forward ([30-37]).
