Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# Case Note: Alex Roux v Ryand Karel Hattingh (636/11) [2012] ZASCA 132

### FIRAC Method

## F - Facts
The appellant, Alex Roux, and the respondent, Ryand Karel Hattingh, were involved in a rugby match on 30 July 2005 between Laborie High School and Stellenbosch High School ([1]–[2]). Ryand, playing as the hooker for Laborie, sustained serious neck injuries during a scrum which Alex illegally maneuvered ([3]–[10]). Ryand testified that Alex shouted "jack-knife," a term that he alleged indicated a deliberate attempt to obstruct his position in the scrum, leading to his injuries. In contrast, Alex and his teammates claimed that "jack-knife" was merely a signal to wheel the scrum, asserting that Alex did not deviate from the rules ([4]–[5]).

The trial court, presided over by Fourie J, found that Alex intentionally blocked Ryand's entry channel in the scrum, leading directly to Ryand’s injuries. The court opined that while rugby players accept the risk of injury inherent in the game, Ryand did not consent to the extreme risk posed by Alex’s actions ([10]–[11]). Following the trial, Fourie J ruled in favor of Ryand, prompting Alex to appeal the decision ([29]).

## I - Issues
The central issues on appeal are:
1. Whether Fourie J's findings regarding the credibility of witnesses and factual circumstances could be contested ([11]).
2. Whether all of Ryand's injuries were inflicted solely by Alex's actions ([11]).
3. Whether Alex's conduct during the game could be categorically labeled as wrongful under the law ([11]).

## R - Rules
The appeal court reaffirmed established legal principles concerning delictual liability:
- The factual findings of a trial judge are deemed correct unless proven otherwise ([12]).
- Fault in the context of delict requires either intention or negligence to establish liability ([26]).
- Wrongfulness is determined by the societal and legal acceptability of the conduct in question, particularly whether it contravenes public policy standards ([27]–[44]). Specifically, the concept of volenti non fit iniuria applies, which posits that consent to participate in a sporting event entails acceptance of normal risks associated; however, conduct that blatantly violates the rules and intentionally causes harm steps outside this boundary ([41]–[42]).

## A - Application
Fourie J’s findings were based on the credibility of Ryand’s testimony, which he found forthright and consistent, particularly when corroborated by other witnesses and objective evidence, including expert testimonies ([9]–[10], [13]). In determining the credibility and veracity of the "jack-knife" maneuver, the court assessed the implications and anticipated ethical conduct in rugby, rejecting Alex’s assertion of defensive maneuvering ([15]–[18]).

The court underscored that the nature of Alex’s actions constituted a deliberate contravention of the rugby rules and an intent to cause harm. Despite expert witnesses asserting differing opinions regarding the mechanics of the scrum and possible injuries, the court prioritized direct witness evidence over speculative expert testimony ([19]–[20], [24]).

Additionally, Fourie J concluded that Alex's actions fell outside the realm of socially acceptable behavior in sports and were egregiously dangerous, thus constituting wrongful conduct under the law ([27]–[28]). The assessments made by expert witnesses were secondary to the direct testimonies, leading the court to maintain the ruling that Alex had acted both intentionally and wrongfully, resulting in Ryand’s injuries ([28]–[30]).

## C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Alex’s appeal, upholding the trial court's findings that Alex deliberately injured Ryand during the rugby match. It confirmed that Alex's conduct was wrongful, constituting a serious breach of rugby regulations and public policy ([29]). The appeal court reiterated that the situations leading to injuries in sports must be evaluated contextually; if behavior indicates a flagrant disregard for established rules and poses unreasonable risks of injury, such behavior will attract legal liability ([36]–[44]).

The implications of this ruling reflect a commitment to ensuring safety in contact sports, emphasizing that the acceptance of risk does not encompass intentional acts of harm underlining the boundaries of acceptable conduct in sports ([27], [44]).
