# Case Note: Nortje v Die Staat [2007] SCA 140 (RSA)

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([1] to [10])

In the matter of **Nortje v Die Staat**, the appellant, Lukas Jacobus Jurie Nortje, faced charges of **sexual assault and rape** stemming from an incident on **23 February 2000**. The complainant, a married woman in her thirties with two young daughters, recounted waking to a knock at her door and encountering the appellant, a family friend ([1]). Despite his earlier declarations to her about wanting to engage sexually ([3]), she permitted him entry under the impression he sought coffee. Subsequently, the appellant assaulted her and forced her into non-consensual sexual intercourse while she attempted to resist ([4] - [6]).

Nortje pled **not guilty**, asserting an **alibi**, claiming he was conducting business elsewhere at the time of the alleged crimes. Both the trial court and the High Court dismissed this defense as non-viable ([2], [12] - [18]). The trial court sentenced him to **ten years for rape** and **six months for sexual assault**, to run concurrently. His appeal against both the convictions and sentences was permitted to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([1], [28]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([2] to [22])

The principal **issues** raised on appeal included:

1. Whether the **alibi defense** sufficiently created reasonable doubt regarding the appellant's presence at the complainant's home at the time of the offenses ([18] - [21]).
2. The introduction of a **consent argument** at the appellate level, where for the first time, the appellant contended that the complainant may have consented to the sexual act, raising questions as to the prosecution's burden to prove the absence of consent ([22] - [27]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([22] to [27])

The court relied on the legal principle stated by Howie AR in **S v York** [2002] (1) SASV 111 (HHA), which emphasizes that the responsibility lies with the prosecution to establish the absence of consent ([22]). Furthermore, the court referenced **S v M** [2006] (1) SASV 135 (HHA), affirming that a defense of consent can prevail even in cases where the accused denies sexual intercourse, provided reasonable grounds for such consent exist. 

The evaluation of the appellant's alibi was subject to rigorous scrutiny under the law, noting that an alibi must merely present a reasonable possibility of innocence, rather than be “water-tight” ([18]). Both trial and appellate courts stressed that an alibi must be assessed in light of all existing evidence to ascertain if it generates reasonable doubt ([19]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([17] to [27])

In addressing the alibi, the court determined that while the claim of the appellant's presence at his workplace was substantiated by a **security slip** indicating his arrival and departure times, there were inconsistencies that rendered the alibi unreliable ([13] - [16]). The evidence presented indicated that the security measures could be manipulated, creating a substantial risk of false verification ([15], [21]).

Moreover, in evaluating the consent claim raised at the appellate stage, the court held that such a defense must not only arise from speculation but must have evidentiary support. The complainant's actions leading to opening her door did not constitute valid consent to engage in sexual acts ([22] - [27]). The court underscored that the absence of immediate acknowledgment of consent was pivotal, particularly given the established history between the parties and the implicit expectations regarding boundaries of familial friendship ([25], [27]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([28] - [29])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal **dismissed the appeal** against the convictions and sentences, affirming the decisions of the lower courts. The court concluded that the alibi was insufficient to overcome the presumption of guilt given the overwhelming evidence against Nortje and the inherent unreliability of both his testimony and the accompanying documentary evidence. Furthermore, the court rejected the consent argument outright, noting its speculative nature without a firm basis in evidence ([28], [29]).

This decision reinforces the judicial stance on the necessity for credible evidence in disputes concerning consent and clarity in alibi defenses. The implications of this case highlight the courts' commitment to rigorously assessing the burden of proof in sexual offences, while maintaining a nuanced understanding of consent within the context of relational dynamics.
