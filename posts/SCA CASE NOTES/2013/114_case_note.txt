Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# Case Note: Bayerische Motoren Werke Aktiengesellschaft v Grandmark International (Pty) Ltd [2013] ZASCA 114

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

The appellant, Bayerische Motoren Werke Aktiengesellschaft (BMW), is a German corporation engaged in the design and manufacture of motor vehicles, holding various registered designs under the Designs Act 195 of 1993 and trade marks under the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993. The specific designs relevant to this appeal encompass components of certain BMW vehicle models, namely a bonnet, grille, headlight assembly, and front fender, registered as ‘aesthetic designs’ ([1–2]).

The first respondent, Grandmark International (Pty) Ltd, imports and distributes motor vehicle components, with the acknowledgment that it among its imports are components that precisely embody BMW’s designs. BMW sought relief from the North Gauteng High Court, claiming relief for alleged design infringements and related interdicts ([3–4]). Notably, as the designs were registered until they expired during litigation, the appeal now primarily sought a declaration of infringement to support recovered royalties ([4]).

In response, Grandmark counter-claimed for the revocation of BMW's design registrations, arguing that they did not qualify for registration. It additionally proposed to refer the matter to the Competition Tribunal for alleged abuse of dominance by BMW. The court concluded that BMW's designs were indeed invalid and dismissed the claim of trade mark infringement, allowing BMW to appeal ([5]).

### I – Issues

The central legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:
1. Whether the designs of the components constituted ‘aesthetic designs’ as per the Designs Act, thus qualifying for protection.
2. Whether there was any infringement of BMW’s trade mark, specifically concerning Grandmark's use of the letters 'BM' on packaging ([6–17]).

### R – Rules

The court structure of the Designs Act underlines the need to distinguish ‘aesthetic designs’ from ‘functional designs’. An aesthetic design is identified as appealing solely to the eye, regardless of its functional use ([6–7]). The Designs Act makes no exclusive requirement that the design must be purely aesthetic, permitting some functional features, although those do not attract protection. Furthermore, the court interpreted relevant jurisprudence, particularly insights from Homecraft Steel Industries (Pty) Ltd v SM Hare & Son, emphasizing the distinction and meaning of 'design' in the context of consumer perception ([8]).

In examining trade mark infringement claims, the court referred to prior decisions where it was established that for infringement to occur, the consumer must interpret the mark as designating the origin of goods, necessitating an analysis of the mark through the consumer’s perspective ([19]).

### A – Application

In applying the law to the facts, the court critically evaluated whether BMW's designs aligned with the legal definition of aesthetic designs. It noted that individual components designed to replace parts of vehicles are principally selected by consumers for their functional utility rather than their aesthetic appeal ([13–14]). Thus, although BMW argued that its designs significantly impacted customer choice, the fact that customers predominantly purchase these components based on their functional role rather than their design undermined the claim that they were aesthetic in nature.

Consequently, the court ruled that the designs were primarily functional, leading to their conclusion that the claims made by BMW did not warrant protection under the Designs Act, as they fundamentally did not meet the criteria for aesthetic design registration ([16]).

Regarding the trade mark infringement allegations, the court analyzed the consumer’s perception of the ‘BM’ mark on Grandmark’s packaging. It found that the mark lacked the necessary distinctive nature to be considered a badge of origin. Instead, the letters were contextualized among various other identifiers, suggesting use in a descriptive manner rather than as a trade mark ([20]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed BMW's appeal, thereby affirming the lower court's rulings regarding both the designs and trade mark issues. The court underscored that the designs in question, characterized by their functional role as replacement components, did not qualify for protection as aesthetic designs under the statutory framework. Furthermore, claims of trade mark infringement failed as the marks were not used in a manner that suggested consumer association with BMW as the source ([22]).

The implications of this ruling extend to the understanding of design and trade mark protections in the automotive industry, clarifying the boundaries for claims involving component parts and emphasizing the need for clear aesthetic value to warrant such protections. This judgment also reflects the court's willingness to consider consumer perceptions critically, aligning with broader judicial trends that prioritize market dynamics in intellectual property disputes. 

Ultimately, the court’s decision reinforces the necessity for design owners to substantiate claims with a compelling demonstration of aesthetic appeal and consumer recognition to achieve the protective measures offered under relevant laws.
