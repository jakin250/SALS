Constitutional Court

Harold Bernstein and Others v L. Von Wielligh Bester NO and Others

Following a significant financial collapse which left creditors owed nearly R400 million, the company was placed under liquidation on 13 January 1993.

Constitutional Court 1996

F – Facts

The case arose from a referral pursuant to section 102(1) of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa Act 200 of 1993. The applicants, Harold Bernstein and others, partners and employees of Kessel Feinstein (a partnership of chartered accountants), contested the validity of the respondents, liquidators of Tollgate Holdings Limited, to continue proceedings as stipulated under sections 417 and 418 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973, asserting that these sections are inconsistent with the Constitution ([1]).

Following a significant financial collapse which left creditors owed nearly R400 million, the company was placed under liquidation on 13 January 1993. Subsequent to this, the liquidators sought to summon the applicants for examination concerning the company's affairs ([6]–[9]). The applicants initially cooperated but later contested the constitutionality of the inquiry, citing various violations of their constitutional rights. Notably, on 31 March 1995, the applicants sought relief from the Cape Provincial Division concerning the summonses issued against them, leading to the referral ordered by Fagan DJP for determination by the Constitutional Court ([10]).

I – Issues

The principal legal questions for the court's determination were:

  1. Are sections 417 and 418 of the Companies Act inconsistent with the rights entrenched in the Constitution, particularly regarding the right to freedom and security of the person (section 11), the right to privacy (section 13), the right to fair administrative action (section 24), and the implied rights to fairness in civil litigation and equality (section 8) ([12], [104])?
  2. Does the mechanism employed under these sections place undue burdens on witnesses, violating their constitutional protections ([43])?

R – Rules

The relevant legal provisions that the court utilized included:

  • The Constitution of the Republic of South Africa (Act 200 of 1993), particularly sections 11, 13, 24, and 8.
  • Companies Act 61 of 1973 (as amended), which governs the powers and duties of liquidators in winding-up processes, including summoning individuals for examinations ([3], [4]).
  • Prior case law, particularly Ferreira v Levin NO, where the court had previously found a part of section 417(2)(b) unconstitutional, establishing a precedent for the consideration of self-incrimination ([4], [13]).

The court also discussed the importance of judicial scrutiny in preventing the oppressive use of the examination process, consistent with comparative jurisprudence from foreign jurisdictions, particularly the UK and Australia ([17]–[35]).

A – Application

Applying the law, the Constitutional Court systematically addressed each ground of constitutional invalidity raised by the applicants. Firstly, on the issue of freedom and security of the person, the court determined that while the enquiry may impose burdens on individuals, these were justified in the context of public interest in the proper conduct of company affairs ([11], [46]).

Regarding the right to privacy, the Court acknowledged the tension between individual rights and social responsibilities. However, it ruled that the nature of the proceedings under the Companies Act did not constitute a violation of privacy rights as these pertain to corporate affairs—a domain with diminished expectations of privacy due to the public and financial interests involved ([56]–[81]).

On the matter of fair administrative action and procedural fairness, the court indicated that if applicants were subjected to unfair treatment during the proceedings, adequate remedies existed within the legal framework of South Africa to address such grievances ([90]–[101]). The statutory framework was considered a legitimate mechanism to facilitate corporate accountability and creditor recovery.

Finally, the court reinforced the notion that the provisions of sections 417 and 418 of the Companies Act were necessary tools for liquidators to carry out their duties effectively, arguing that the provisions, when properly applied, did not infringe upon the constitutional rights of those summoned ([124]).

C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the court held that sections 417 and 418 of the Companies Act were not unconstitutional as they facilitated necessary inquiries into the affairs of companies in liquidation, thus serving public interest goals of transparency and accountability. The court reaffirmed the validity of these sections, except where previously found invalid concerning self-incrimination, as established in Ferreira v Levin ([125]). Furthermore, all parties were directed to bear their own costs, recognizing the complexities of the case and the nature of legal procedures involved ([124], [125]).

This ruling establishes a precedent for balancing individual rights with corporate accountability in South Africa, reinforcing the necessity of these statutory mechanisms in maintaining financial integrity and trust in corporate governance.
