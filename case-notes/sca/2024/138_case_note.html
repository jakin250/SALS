Case Title: Jooste and Another v Member of the Executive Council for Local Government Environmental Affairs & Development Planning: Western Cape and Others (637/2023) [2024] ZASCA 138 (11 October 2024)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 138 (11 October 2024) ## F – Facts The appeal

Case Title: Jooste and Another v Member of the Executive Council for Local Government Environmental Affairs & Development Planning: Western Cape and Others (637/2023) [2024] ZASCA 138 (11 October 2024)

F – Facts

The appeal arose from a dispute involving a composting facility operated by the South African Farm Assured Meat Group CC (SAFAM) on a farm in Robertson, leased from the Reben Trust ([paras 1–3]). The facility was established in February 2017 following advice from local authorities that SAFAM could no longer dispose of its abattoir waste at municipal sites ([paras 5–6]). The facility's operation allegedly produced foul odours and flies that adversely affected the tourism activities of the appellants, who owned adjacent properties within a protected nature reserve ([para 5]).

The process leading to the appeal began when SAFAM applied for environmental authorisation under the National Environmental Management Act 107 of 1998 (NEMA) in January 2018 ([para 6]). The appellants participated as affected individuals, arguing that SAFAM had unlawfully begun the listed activities without proper authorisation as mandated by NEMA ([para 7]). Upon receiving adverse decisions, SAFAM sought to review these dismissals in the high court ([para 9]). A settlement was reached between SAFAM and the Department of Environmental Affairs which resulted in a court order on 18 October 2019, effectively withdrawing previous decisions and authorising the project under specific conditions ([paras 12–14]). The appellants learned of the settlement only shortly before it was finalized but took no legal action to contest it ([para 17]).

The high court subsequently dismissed the appellants' review application, prompting the present appeal ([para 1]).

I – Issues

The core issues for determination in this case included:

  1. Whether the MEC’s decision to condone SAFAM's failure to submit a Basic Assessment Report was procedurally fair, given that no notice was provided to the appellants or other affected parties ([para 14(a)]).
  2. Whether SAFAM had unlawfully commenced activities in contravention of section 24F of NEMA ([paras 14(c)–(d)]).
  3. The applicability of res judicata, considering the settlement order and its implications on the continued viability of the appellants' claims against SAFAM ([para 15]).
  4. The question of costs, particularly the application of the principles set forth in Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources in light of the appellants' conduct ([para 20]).

R – Rules

The constitutional and statutory frameworks relevant to this case include:

  1. National Environmental Management Act 107 of 1998 (NEMA): Particularly sections regarding the commencement of listed activities (s 24F), retrospective applications (s 24G), and procedural requirements for environmental authorisation ([paras 6–8]).
  2. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000: Provides procedural fairness protections for individuals affected by administrative decisions.
  3. Res Judicata Principle: As derived from common law, which precludes parties from relitigating issues that have been conclusively adjudicated in earlier proceedings.
  4. Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources [2009] ZACC 14: Establishes that costs may not be awarded against individuals acting in the public interest when they seek to protect environmental rights.

A – Application

The court applied these rules to assess the efficacy of the appellants’ claims ([paras 15–19]). The settlement order from October 2019 had legally substantive effects, rendering previous decisions by the Department null and void, and brought finality to the disputes between SAFAM and the State respondents. As such, the court interpreted the settlement order not merely as an administrative decision but as substantive relief granted by the court, thus preventing the appellants from re-arguing matters resolved by that order ([paras 15–16]).

The procedural fairness argument raised by the appellants was deemed unfounded, primarily because they had been aware of the proceedings leading to the settlement but chose not to intervene, therefore forfeiting their chance to challenge any decision prior to settlement ([para 17]). Moreover, any allegations of irregularities in the settlement were viewed as lacking in merit and insufficient to undermine the order ([para 18]).

The court further emphasized that the appellants could not utilize review proceedings to circumvent the effects of the settlement order, acknowledging that their case relied on arguments that were already encompassed by the resolved issues ([para 19]).

With respect to costs, the court found the appellants’ allegations against the State respondents to have been intemperate and the appeal ultimately deemed frivolous, which justified the award of costs against them ([para 22]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the validity of the High Court's decision to reject the appellants’ application for review ([para 1]). The court reiterated that the settlement order was binding and resolved all relevant issues, rendering further litigation regarding the same matters impermissible under the doctrine of res judicata ([paras 15–19]). The appellants were ultimately ordered to bear the costs of the appeal, including that of two counsel, which reflects the court's stance on the frivolity of the appeal and the seriousness of the allegations made ([para 22]).

This case thus reinforces the importance of finality in judicial proceedings and the principle that parties must act diligently to protect their interests in legal disputes, especially in environmental matters where third-party rights may be affected.
