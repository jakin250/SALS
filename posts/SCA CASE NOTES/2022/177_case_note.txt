Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: Eden Crescent Share Block Ltd v Olive Marketing CC and Others (1075/2020) [2022] ZASCA 177

---

## F – Facts
In the case of **Eden Crescent Share Block Ltd v Olive Marketing CC and Others** ([1]), the primary parties involved are Eden Crescent Share Block Ltd (the applicant) and Olive Marketing CC (the first respondent), with the Ethekwini Municipality as the second respondent and Shepstone & Wylie as the third party. This dispute arose from a servitude agreement concerning parking obligations related to two adjacent properties in Durban: Eden's property (erf 11496) and Olive's property (erf 12424), which both had previously been owned by the Ethekwini Municipality.

The original deed of sale between Eden and Ethekwini specified that a parking servitude of at least 250 spaces would be established for the benefit of Olive’s property. However, Eden refused to provide the parking spaces, asserting that the servitude was invalid due to vagueness. Olive, having benefitted from the servitude since its inception in the 1960s, sought enforcement. After the High Court ruled in favour of Olive, dismissing Eden's claims of invalidity, both Eden and Olive, along with Ethekwini, collectively sought leave to appeal ([1]–[3]).

## I – Issues
The core legal questions before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:
1. **Validity of the Servitude**: Was the servitude invalidated by vagueness concerning the specific number and location of parking spaces?
2. **Nature of the Servitude**: Should it be classified as a specific servitude, which would necessitate precise identification of parameters, or a general servitude, providing more flexibility?
3. **Compliance with Regulatory Framework**: Did the servitude require approvals pursuant to the Share Blocks Control Act 59 of 1980 and the Housing Development Schemes for Retired Persons Act 65 of 1988 for it to be valid? ([2]–[3]).

## R – Rules
The court's analysis relied upon several statutory provisions and legal precedents:
- **Servitude Law**: The distinction between specific and general servitudes, as clarified in *Tshwane City v Link Africa* ([17]) and *Nach Investments Ltd v Yaldai Investments* ([18]).
- **Share Blocks Control Act**: Section 8(1)(c) stipulates that a share block company requires a special resolution for any alienation of immovable property.
- **Housing Development Schemes for Retired Persons Act**: Section 4B mandates consent to alienation of land by at least 75% of holders of rights of occupation in a housing development scheme ([24][25]).
  
The court determined that, under the established legal framework, for an agreement to constitute a specific servitude, essential terms must be explicitly agreed upon ([19][20]).

## A – Application
In its application of the law, the court first addressed the vagueness claim made by Eden. It concluded that the servitude was a general servitude that allowed Olive to select the exact locations of parking spaces without specifying them in advance. The court referenced past decisions indicating that vagueness does not necessarily invalidate a servitude if the intent can be discerned from the conveyance context ([21]).

The court then examined the statutory requirements under the Share Blocks Act and the Retired Persons Act. It determined that the servitude's creation did not represent an alienation of property since Eden's obligations had always been tied to the property regardless of the servitude's registration timeline. Hence, the servitude did not require the approvals stipulated in those Acts due to Eden’s pre-existing obligations and the nature of the rights involved ([31]–[33]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal found that Eden's application for leave to appeal should be dismissed. With the servitude deemed valid, Olive's claims against Ethekwini and Eden stand, and the appeals from Olive and Ethekwini were also dismissed ([38]). The court reinforced the understanding that an unregistered servitude, formed through mutually acknowledged rights despite delayed formal registration, did not constitute illegal alienation ([30]). This ruling has broad implications for contractual relations regarding servitudes, especially in property law, emphasizing the importance of intent and the ongoing practical obligations of property owners.

---

In summary, this case solidified existing legal interpretations surrounding servitude agreements, ensuring that practical realities over legal formalities defined the balance of rights and obligations between adjacent landowners.
