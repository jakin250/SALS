# Case Note for Saaknommer: 452/94 (Toseph Mmabuti Raselebana v Freddy Sebi Makgoka & Die Staat)

## FIRAC Method Case Note

### F – Facts
The appellants, Toseph Mmabuti Raselebana and Freddy Sebi Makgoka, were charged with murder, aggravated robbery, and illegal possession of a firearm in the Local Division of the Northern Circuit District at Pietersburg ([para 1]). They pleaded not guilty to the murder charge but guilty to the other two charges. The court found them guilty on all counts, holding that they acted with direct intent to kill the deceased, an 85-year-old man, whom they assaulted during a burglary ([para 2]). Both appellants were sentenced to death for murder, along with additional sentences for the other charges, to be served concurrently ([para 3]).

The state’s case relied primarily on confessions made by the appellants before magistrates, admissions made during police interrogations, and their written statements under section 112(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([para 4]). The key issue at trial was whether the state had proven that both appellants had the requisite intent to kill the deceased ([para 4]).

Medical testimony indicated that the primary cause of death was asphyxiation due to strangulation, leading to a heart attack. The forensic evidence supported the conclusion that strangulation was sufficient to cause death, further solidifying the basis for the murder charge ([para 5]). Despite their admissions of causing the deceased's death unlawfully and negligently, the appellants denied having the intent to kill ([para 6]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issue was whether the appellants possessed the intent (dolus) required for murder, specifically whether their actions constituted either dolus directus or dolus eventualis ([para 4]). This encompassed the determination of whether the intent to kill could be inferred from their actions and statements during the trial. 

Secondary issues included:
1. The admissibility of the confessions and their probative value, although this was not contested ([para 4]).
2. The appropriate penalty for murder considering the constitutional implications raised by the death penalty, particularly following the precedent set in *S v Makwanyane and Another* ([para 12]).

### R – Rules
The legal standards guiding this case included:
- The definition of intent in South African law, particularly the distinctions between dolus directus and dolus eventualis as articulated in previous case law, including *R v Lewis* 1958 (3) SA 107 (A) and other precedents ([para 6]).
- The assessment of circumstantial evidence and confessions in establishing intent, as delineated in case law ([para 7]).
- The constitutional provision prohibiting the death penalty post-*S v Makwanyane* which rendered all death sentences invalid after June 6, 1995 ([para 12]).

### A – Application
The court meticulously examined the evidence roundly. The medical expert's testimony indicated that the deceased's death was predominantly due to strangulation, and the bruises on his neck supported the assertion of foul play ([para 5]). The appellants acknowledged that they had unlawfully and negligently caused the deceased's death but contended that they lacked the intent to kill.

The court rejected their defense, noting that the admissions made clearly suggested the appellants recognized the fatal potential of their actions, qualifying as dolus eventualis. The court leaned on precedent to establish that the appellants had foreseen the potential of their actions to cause death and nonetheless proceeded ([paras 6-7]).

The analysis further diverged between the two appellants concerning their specific actions. In particular, the court noted the first appellant’s statements regarding pressing the bedding over the deceased's face to prevent identification did not combat the inference of intent since this act directly related to the deceased's demise ([para 9]). Conversely, the second appellant’s participation was assessed against his claims of non-involvement in the act of strangulation; however, this was insufficient to exonerate him from responsibility ([para 10]).

### C – Conclusion
The court found both appellants guilty of murder, concluding they acted with at least potential awareness of the lethal outcome of their actions, thereby meeting the criterion for dolus eventualis ([para 12]). However, the death sentences imposed were rendered unconstitutional under the new constitutional framework, as determined in *S v Makwanyane*, mandating their annulment ([para 12]). The court ordered the case to be remitted to the trial court for the imposition of an appropriate sentence other than the death penalty. 

This case underscores the ongoing tension between legal definitions of intent and evolving constitutional mandates regarding permissible penalties in South Africa ([para 13]).
