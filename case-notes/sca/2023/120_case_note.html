FIRAC Analysis of Kunene Rampala Inc. v North West Province Department of Education and Sport Development (460/2022)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 120 (15 September 2023) Court: Supreme Cour

Neutral Citation: Kunene Rampala Inc. v North West Province Department of Education and Sport Development (460/2022) [2023] ZASCA 120 (15 September 2023)
Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Coram: Mbatha, Mothle, Hughes and Matojane JJA and Mali AJA
Heard: 11 May 2023
Delivered: 15 September 2023

F – Facts ([paras 1–27])

This case arises from a public procurement dispute involving Kunene Rampala Inc. (KR Inc.) and the North West Province Department of Education and Sport Development (the Department). On 28 September 2015, the Department announced a tender (EDU 04/15 NW) for various educational service delivery requirements, which KR Inc. successfully bid on. A Service Level Agreement (SLA) was formalized on 9 October 2015, stipulating a 12-month service period at a total contract price of R1,243,215.60 ([paras 1–2]).

Three days later, on 12 October 2015, a controversial addendum to the SLA was executed. This addendum sought to extend the SLA’s terms to include a new scope of services involving the provision and delivery of stationery, originally awarded under a different tender (EDU 01/15 NW) that had been invalidated by a court ruling ([paras 4–5]). The addendum increased the duration of engagement to three years and expanded the scope of work, enabling KR Inc. to charge a significantly higher fee ((up to R46,650,000.00) based on the budget for the services rendered ([paras 7–8]).

Discrepancies soon emerged, and on 22 March 2017, the Department issued a cancellation notice claiming the addendum was invalid as it embraced new work and terms not originally included within the SLA ([paras 10–12]). KR Inc. subsequently initiated legal proceedings following the cancellation, alleging improper repudiation of the contract.

The high court ruled against KR Inc., concluding the addendum contravened public procurement principles enshrined in section 217 of the Constitution and related statutory frameworks ([paras 13–14]). The court maintained that the addendum represented a clear breach of operational mandates established for public procurement, warranting its dismissal ([para 28]).

I – Issues ([paras 14–22])

The key issues presented in this appeal include:

  1. Validity of the Addendum: Whether the addendum to the SLA was valid given its circumvention of the required public procurement processes and compliance with relevant legal standards, particularly section 217 of the Constitution, the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA), and the associated Treasury Regulations ([paras 15–16]).
  2. Impact of Collateral Challenge: Whether the Department could successfully assert the invalidity of the addendum without a formal counter-application seeking to set it aside, thus determining whether the erroneous execution of the addendum could be contested in the absence of direct procedural challenge ([para 20]).
  3. Emergency Justification for Single Source Procurement: Whether KR Inc.’s assertion of emergency circumstances justified the unilateral extension of the SLA through the addendum without adhering to statutory procurement processes ([paras 19–22]).

R – Rules ([paras 15–16])

The appeal is governed by established legal rules concerning public procurement. Section 217 of the Constitution mandates that public contracts be procured in a fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective manner. Furthermore, compliance with the PFMA and accompanying Treasury Regulations is crucial for lawful public procurement practices, underscoring the imperative of competitive bidding processes where transaction values exceed statutory thresholds ([para 15]).

In Valor IT v Premier, North West Province and Others, the importance of these rules was reinforced, emphasizing that non-compliance would result in the invalidity of public contracts ([para 16]). Similarly, in Gobela Consulting CC v Makhado Municipality, the court ruled on matters regarding the ability to challenge the validity of administrative actions reactively, affirming that these challenges remain permissible based on the context of injustice presented ([para 20]).

A – Application ([paras 17–24])

The SCA scrutinized the validity of the addendum, reaffirming that its conclusion, executed without following an open tender process, constituted a breach of section 217 of the Constitution. The court highlighted that the transaction value far exceeded the stipulated threshold requiring a competitive bidding process, thereby rendering the addendum invalid ([paras 17–18]).

KR Inc. contended that the additional work specified in the addendum flowed directly from existing contractual obligations under the SLA. However, the SCA differentiated between the initial contractual responsibilities and the extensive new commitments introduced via the addendum, ultimately finding that the amendment represented a significant scope expansion such that it would necessitate separate procurement processes ([para 18]).

In addressing the issue of whether a collateral challenge could be entertained by the Department, the SCA determined that prior judicial interpretations allow for the declaration of invalidity without requiring additional formal proceedings, particularly where public interest and justice necessitate such a declaration. The court underscored the fact that the Department's assertion of the addendum's invalidity had been duly documented in earlier correspondence and court filings, thereby justifying the absence of a counter-application ([para 23]).

C – Conclusion ([paras 25–28])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed KR Inc.’s appeal, confirming the high court's ruling that the addendum was unlawful and invalid due to non-compliance with public procurement principles. The court found that allowing KR Inc. to pursue claims based on the addendum would contradict the essential principles of legality and accountability underpinning public sector procurement ([paras 25–27]).

The SCA's declaration that the addendum was invalid underscores the judicial commitment to upholding the tenets of public procurement law and preventing the potential misuse of state resources. The decision further clarifies the boundaries within which public entities must operate regarding procurement, enhancing transparency, accountability, and the rule of law in public administration ([para 28]).

This ruling serves as a critical precedent in reinforcing compliance with procurement regulations and positing a clear stance on collateral challenges in public contract disputes, thereby contributing significantly to the jurisprudence surrounding public procurement in South Africa.
