South African Airways (Pty) Limited v Aviation Union of South Africa

Case Citation: SAA v Aviation Union of SA (123/10) [2011] ZASCA 1 (11 January 2011)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2010 [2011] ZASCA 1 (11 January 2011) Court: Supreme Court of

Case Citation: SAA v Aviation Union of SA (123/10) [2011] ZASCA 1 (11 January 2011)

Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

Coram: Mpati P, Lewis JA, Mhlantla JA, Shongwe JA, Ebrahim AJA

F – Facts

The appeal arose from a dispute concerning the interpretation of section 197 of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA), which regulates the protection of employee rights during the transfer of a business. The appellant, South African Airways (Pty) Ltd (SAA), entered into an outsourcing agreement with the 66th respondent, LGM South Africa Facility Managers and Engineers (Pty) Ltd (LGM), effectively transferring its infrastructure and support service department to LGM on April 1, 2000. This transfer included the employment contracts of numerous employees ([1]–[5]).

By June 2007, following a change in ownership of LGM, SAA terminated the outsourcing agreement, leading to the potential displacement of the employees initially transferred to LGM. The 64 affected employees, represented by the Aviation Union of South Africa (AUSA), sought a declaration from the Labour Court asserting that the termination of the agreement mandated their transfer back to SAA or to any subsequent service provider in accordance with section 197 of the LRA ([5]–[10]).

The Labour Court ruled that the termination did not imply a "second generation outsourcing" transfer of employees back to SAA under section 197, and hence dismissed the application. This judgment was appealed to the Labour Appeal Court, which reversed the Labour Court's decision, asserting that second-generation transfers could fall within the ambit of section 197 ([12]–[16]).

SAA subsequently appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal on the grounds that the Labour Appeal Court misinterpreted section 197 both in language and application ([20]–[21]).

I – Issues

  1. Interpretation of Section 197: The primary issue was whether a second or subsequent transfer of business as a going concern occurred, thus invoking the protections afforded to employees under section 197 of the Labour Relations Act.
  2. Application to Second-Generation Outsourcing: The court needed to ascertain if the language could accommodate scenarios where a business similarly transitioned to different contractors beyond the initial transfer.

R – Rules

The relevant provision stipulated in section 197 of the LRA asserts that upon transferring a business, existing contracts of employment automatically transfer to the new employer, and the old employer is substituted by the new employer in respect to those employees. This section aims to protect employees during business transfers and ensure job security and continuity of employment ([3]–[4]).

Legal precedents emphasize a purposive interpretation of such statutes to align with constitutional mandates, particularly Fair Labour Practices (section 23 of the Constitution) and Equality (section 9) ([24]).

The Labour Appeal Court's interpretation was influenced by earlier judgments, particularly in Cosawu v Zikhethele Trade (Pty) Ltd, which contemplated a broader interpretation of transfers under section 197, thereby accommodating potential second-generation outsourcing scenarios ([16]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal critiqued the Labour Appeal Court's approach for misapprehending the plain language of section 197. The literal interpretation demanded the existence of a proactive transfer by the old employer to the new employer as a prerequisite for invoking section 197 protections. The court articulated that interpreting "by" to also mean "from" was not linguistically justified and distortive of legislative intent ([20]–[33]).

The court scrutinized whether SAA’s prior transfer of services constituted a "going concern," emphasizing that factual evidence showcasing active business continuation, handed back to SAA or transited to a new provider, was essential to affirm any subsequent transfer. The court concluded that a tangible transfer of business assets and operational continuity had not been demonstrably fulfilled ([30]–[41]).

The Supreme Court ultimately sided with SAA, asserting that the numerous speculative future contractual relationships did not meet the solid requirements set forth by section 197 regarding the transfer of business as a going concern, thus reaffirming the Labour Court's original dismissal of the union's claims ([41]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal by SAA, setting aside the Labour Appeal Court's ruling. The court firmly held that the absence of evidence establishing a completed transfer of business as a going concern negated the applicability of section 197. The judgment emphasized the need for clear legislative language and endorsed the principle that judicial interpretation should remain faithful to legislative intent without retroactively altering statutory protections ([42]).

The ruling underscored the tensions within employment law regarding outsourcing and affirmed the protection of employee rights, while reiterating the clear delineation necessary in statutory interpretations. The implications of this ruling reinforce the careful construction required in contracts and the inherent obligations accompanying business transfers under South African labour legislation.
