Supreme Court of Appeal

C E Van Wetten and Another v K A Bosch and Others

The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa Case No: 402/2002 Judgment Delivered: 19 September 2003

Supreme Court of Appeal 2003

The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Case No: 402/2002
Judgment Delivered: 19 September 2003

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The primary facts of the case revolve around the contested will of Tertius Bosch, who passed away on 14 February 2000. The central question is whether a document written by Bosch in September 1997 was intended as his final will or simply as instructions for an attorney to draft a will ([1]). The first appellant, C E Van Wetten, who is Bosch's sister, sought recognition of this document as his will after discovering it in Bosch's widow Karen-Anne Bosch's home ([2]).

The contested will was found within a sealed envelope that Bosch had given to a friend, Mr. Jan van der Westhuizen, to be opened only in certain circumstances, presumedly indicating Bosch's precarious mental state around the time of writing ([7], [8]). The couple's tumultuous relationship, characterized by allegations of infidelity and emotional distress, further complicated the matter ([14], [17]).

Van Wetten alleged that she had acted out of concern for her nephew Corbin Bosch, who would be the sole heir under the contested will ([6]). At the time of its composition, Bosch expressed discontent with his marriage and appeared to be contemplating a future that would not involve his wife ([17]).

Following Bosch's death, proceedings were initiated by Van Wetten to have the contested will recognized. However, the High Court declined her request, which prompted the current appeal ([2], [3]). The second appellant was subsequently appointed as curator ad litem for Corbin Bosch ([3], [28]).

I – Issues

The key legal issues in this case include:

  1. Whether Tertius Bosch intended the document he wrote in September 1997 to be his final will in accordance with section 2(3) of the Wills Act 7 of 1953, despite it lacking formal execution ([1], [14]).
  2. Whether Van Wetten had the requisite locus standi to bring forth the application for the recognition of the contested will ([2], [28]).
  3. The question of costs in the context of Van Wetten's standing and her conduct in the initial application ([29]).

R – Rules

The case is principally governed by section 2(3) of the Wills Act 7 of 1953, which allows a court to accept a document as a valid will if satisfied that it reflects the deceased's intent, even if not executed in accordance with statutory formalities ([1]).

The courts have established that to determine the validity of a contested will, it is necessary to ascertain the deceased's intention at the time of drafting ([15], [26]). Precedents such as Schnetler NO v Die Meester en andere [1999] 4 SA 1250 (C) and Delmas Milling Co Ltd v Du Plessis [1955] 3 SA 447 (A) further contextualize the interpretative principles relevant to will drafting and intentions ([15], [21]).

A – Application

The court undertook a comprehensive analysis of Bosch’s intent surrounding the contested will. The language and structure of the document indicated explicit intentions, articulated in phrases such as “I have made the following decisions” and “as of today 5th September this is my will to be followed” ([11], [26]). The court noted the significance of the personal turmoil Bosch was experiencing during this time, suggesting that his emotional state contributed to his resolve in providing for Corbin ([17], [19]).

Furthermore, despite arguments that subsequent reconciliatory actions by Bosch negated the original will's intent ([20]), the court emphasized that the relevant inquiry must focus on Bosch's mindset at the time of drafting the will. It was established that the fact he entrusted the document to Van der Westhuizen indicated that this was indeed intended as a will that should come into effect should he die ([19], [27]).

Regarding Van Wetten's locus standi, while her interest as Corbin's aunt warranted recognition, her failure to establish a direct claim to the estate in the absence of a valid will underpinned the ruling that she lacked sufficient standing ([28], [29]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the document written by Bosch on 5 September 1997 was intended to reflect his last will and testament, thereby directing the Master of the High Court to accept it in line with section 2(3) of the Wills Act 7 of 1953 ([26], [33]). The appeal was granted, marking a significant point regarding the interpretation of testamentary intention, particularly in the context of non-traditional wills. However, Van Wetten's lack of standing led to the court dismissing her application for costs, highlighting the importance of procedural adherence in legal proceedings ([31], [33]).

Implications

This case underscores the judiciary's commitment to honoring testamentary intent even in the absence of formal compliance with statutory provisions. It illuminates broader principles regarding legal capacity, emotional distress, and the evolving understanding of wills within South Africa’s legal framework.

The court's judgement notably addresses issues pertinent to social relationships, probation of wills, and the varying nuances of personal intent, marking an important precedent for future cases surrounding testamentary intentions and the circumstances of will writing.
