# Case Note using the FIRAC Method for **Electoral Commission of South Africa v Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs and Others [2021] ZACC 29**

### F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–20])

The Electoral Commission of South Africa (IEC) initiated an urgent application for direct access to the Constitutional Court, seeking to postpone the local government elections scheduled for 27 October 2021. The Commission claimed that it was unable to conduct a free and fair election due to constraints imposed by COVID-19 lockdown regulations and its failure to hold a voter registration weekend prior to the Minister's proclamation. This proclamation had significant implications, as it triggered the timeline for voter registration and the election process under the relevant constitutional and statutory frameworks, including the Municipal Structures Act 117 of 1998 and the Electoral Act 73 of 1998.

The COVID-19 pandemic had necessitated restrictions on public gatherings, which played a crucial role in the Commission's assertion that elections held under such conditions would not fulfill the constitutional mandates of conduct and fairness. The term for municipal councils had expired on 3 August 2021, necessitating elections within a 90-day timeframe. The Commission commissioned the Moseneke Inquiry to assess whether these elections could be held under such conditions, which ultimately recommended a postponement to allow for a safer electoral environment.

The applicant named multiple respondents, including the Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs and various provincial executive councils. Several political parties, including the African National Congress (ANC) and the Democratic Alliance (DA), intervened with varying stances on the application.

### I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 22–31])

The central issue at hand was whether the IEC could be permitted to postpone the scheduled elections outside of the 90-day constitutional limitation, as set out in section 159 of the Constitution. This required addressing the following legal questions:

1. **Impossibility of conducting a free and fair election**: Did the intersection of lockdown restrictions and the absence of a voter registration weekend amount to a legal impossibility that would allow the IEC to conduct elections outside the stated constitutional timeframe?
   
2. **Government duties and voters’ rights**: What is the extent of the IEC's responsibility to ensure that elections are conducted under conditions that align with the constitutional requirements for free and fair elections versus the exigencies of the ongoing global pandemic?
  
3. **Remedial Powers of the Court**: What authority does the court have to grant an order that would effectively alter the predetermined electoral timetable set by national legislation?

### R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 13–19, 155–161])

The court relied upon several key statutory and constitutional provisions to address the issues at hand:

1. **Section 159(2) of the Constitution**: Dictates that municipal elections must be held within 90 days after the end of a municipal council's term ([paras 18, 155]).
   
2. **Local Government: Municipal Structures Act 117 of 1998**: Defines the responsibilities and timelines for proclaiming elections ([para 24]).
  
3. **Electoral Act 73 of 1998**: Underpins the statutory framework within which elections must be managed, explicitly stating voter registration requirements ([para 159]).
  
4. **Precedents from Earlier Constitutional Court Rulings**: Cases such as *Kham v Electoral Commission* and *Electoral Commission v Mhlope* underscore the essential nature of regular, free, and fair elections ([paras 25, 36, 51]).

The court also engaged with general doctrines such as the impossibility maxim, which traditionally excuses parties from fulfilling obligations they cannot perform, and discussed its applicability in constitutional contexts.

### A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 37–42, 206–218, 224–226])

In its analysis, the Court evaluated the arguments presented by both the IEC and the respondents. The majority judgment emphasized the importance of adhering to the legal framework dictating regular elections while acknowledging the context imposed by the COVID-19 pandemic. However, the court found that the IEC’s assertion that elections could not be held in a manner compliant with constitutional standards failed to sufficiently establish that it was impossible to conduct free and fair elections within the 90-day framework.

Significantly, the Court highlighted that just because certain conditions (e.g., absence of a voter registration weekend) posed logistical challenges did not automatically equate to the elections being fundamentally compromised. It pointed out that a voter registration weekend had historically proven to enhance electoral participation but was not legally mandated under the current electoral framework. 

Ultimately, the court concluded that while conditions surrounding the pandemic posed challenges, they did not fundamentally preclude the risk of holding elections and that any ensuing irregularities could be fundamentally reviewed in ex post facto challenges.

### C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 233–250])

The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the IEC's primary application to hold elections in February 2022, declaring the Minister's proclamation unconstitutional and invalid due to the timing issues related to voter registration. However, the court also indicated that the Commission should conduct the elections by 1 November 2021, thereby fulfilling its constitutional obligations to ensure a regular electoral process.

The ruling carried significant implications: it enforced the primacy of constitutional deadlines for elections while balancing the need for electoral integrity and fairness against public health considerations. It served as a reminder that the electoral body must be proactive in addressing potential challenges to voter participation, particularly in an evolving public health crisis, thereby ensuring that every eligible voter's rights are safeguarded.

Furthermore, the Court's ruling clarified the bounds of public authority during health emergencies and underscored a significant aspect of democratic governance: the necessity for timely elections and the importance of public accountability even amid unforeseen circumstances. The case stands as a landmark decision that reaffirms the intersection of constitutional mandates and public health imperatives in South Africa's electoral landscape.
