Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2002

# FIRAC Case Note: Logbro Properties CC v SA Bedderson NO and Others

**Case Citation**: Logbro Properties CC v SA Bedderson NO and Others, Case No: 372/2001 (SCA) [2002] ZASCA 95

## F – Facts

In 1995, the KwaZulu-Natal provincial government awarded a tender for the sale of a property in Richards Bay to one Naidoo, rejecting an earlier bid from Logbro Properties CC (the appellant). Logbro successfully challenged Naidoo's bid in the High Court, leading to an order by McLaren J in February 1997 for the provincial tender committee to reconsider compliant tenders, excluding non-compliant ones like Naidoo's ([1–2]).

By late 1997, upon reconsideration, Logbro's tender was the highest; however, the committee recommended a fresh tender process due to an increase in property values since 1995, a decision made by a 3 to 1 vote ([3–4]). Logbro contested this decision in the Natal Provincial Division, which upheld the committee's action, interpreting McLaren J’s order as allowing consideration of new factors such as the increase in property value ([5–4]).

Logbro's appeal centered on whether the committee could consider the rise in property values when deciding on the tender, and whether it was required to afford Logbro a chance to make representations regarding this new consideration ([5]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues presented in this appeal include:

1. **Could the tender committee lawfully consider the increase in property values when reconsidering the tender?** ([4–5])
2. **Did the committee have an obligation to afford Logbro an opportunity to make representations concerning the significance of the price rise and its implications?** ([5–6])

## R – Rules

1. The tender process constitutes "administrative action" under the Constitution of South Africa, entitled to lawful, procedurally fair processes if rights are affected, as per section 33 of the 1996 Constitution ([5–6]). This includes adherence to principles of administrative justice, consolidating the contentions that administrators must act fairly and transparently during the process.
   
2. The principles from previous case law indicate that the government is bound to consider fairness when exercising its powers in the realm of public authority. Notable cases relevant here include *Umfolozi Transport (Edms) Bpk v Minister van Vervoer* [1997] 2 All SA 548, which stipulates that fairness governs public authorities' actions ([5–8]).

3. The *Cape Metropolitan Council v Metro Inspection Services* case establishes that where a public authority exercises contractual rights, it may still be bound by principles of fairness depending on the context ([9–10]).

## A – Application

The court began its analysis by reiterating that the tender process constituted administrative action, thus implying that fairness and procedural justice were indeed applicable. It addressed the argument by the province asserting their contractual right to withdraw tenders without administrative scrutiny. The court rejected this argument, aligning with principles of administrative fairness which, it ruled, transcended mere contractual frameworks: the province would be obliged to act lawfully and fairly by considering public interests and the implications of its administrative actions ([5–12]).

It was further acknowledged that the increase in property values was an established fact, and the committee's mandate to act in the public interest justified their consideration of this increase. The court found that the committee's recommendation for new tenders did not violate Logbro's rights, particularly since it had to weigh public benefits against individual claims. This broader perspective on public interest bolstered the argument for allowing the committee to act on changing circumstances ([18–20]).

Regarding the audi alteram partem principle, the court noted that the committee failed to provide Logbro a chance to present its case concerning the fresh circumstances of rising property values. While the court recognized the necessity of administrative flexibility, it concluded that failing to grant Logbro an opportunity to make representations violated procedural fairness, necessitating a remedy ([23–25]).

## C – Conclusion

The court concluded that while the tender committee was justified in considering the rising property values in their decision to seek new tenders, it ultimately erred by not providing Logbro with an opportunity for input regarding these factors. Consequently, the 1997 decision was set aside, with a directive for the committee to reconvene and allow affected bidders to make formal representations regarding the relevant circumstances during the reconsideration process ([25–28]).

The judgment reinforces the significance of adhering to principles of administrative justice and fairness in public tender processes, ensuring that even substantial administrative bodies must account for their duties towards affected parties. The decision also emphasizes the importance of allowing spaces for representation, fostering transparency and accountability within government decision-making.
