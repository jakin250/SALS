Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: Budge & Others v Glyn-Cuthbert & Others (1046/16) [2018] ZASCA 18

## F – Facts

The litigation at hand arises from a protracted dispute between Jonathon Stuart Budge (the first appellant) and Russell Glyn-Cuthbert (the first respondent), who were business partners in real estate from 2001 until their dissolution in 2007. Following a mutually agreed dissolution of their partnership on 26 November 2007, they entered into a memorandum of agreement outlining the division of their assets, which included several property holdings. The agreement stipulated that the proceeds from the sale of properties would be split equally between the parties following the dissolution [1–9].

The underlying conflict emerged primarily from the interpretation and execution of this agreement. The first appellant alleged that the first respondent had repudiated the agreement by failing to incorporate a new company, Rusco (Pty) Ltd, and misappropriated funds from a management fee [18–20]. While the first appellant asserted that his business partner's actions constituted a repudiation entitling him to damages, the court a quo dismissed this claim, finding no valid repudiation. The appellants subsequently appealed this decision, resulting in a complex dispute adjudicated by the Supreme Court of Appeal [2–3, 29–30].

## I – Issues

The central legal questions in this case were as follows:

1. Did the court a quo err in ruling that the first respondent had not repudiated the partnership dissolution agreement?
2. Were the claims for unjust enrichment and other ancillary reliefs appropriately considered by the court a quo?
3. Were costs awarded in relation to the claims and appeals justly determined by the court a quo?
4. Was the first appellant's application to amend the quantum of damages in claim B supported by the evidentiary record provided at trial? [32–34].

## R – Rules

In determining the outcome, the court applied several key legal principles:

1. **Repudiation**: The court referenced the test set in **Datacolour International (Pty) Ltd v Intamarket (Pty) Ltd** which elucidates that repudiation primarily focuses on the actions' perceptions rather than intentions. A party's conduct is assessed in the totality of circumstances to decide if it constitutes a repudiation that justifies cancellation [31].

2. **Unjust Enrichment**: The claims for unjust enrichment were based on the legal principles that prohibit a party from benefiting at another's expense without a valid basis. Statutory provisions under the **Close Corporations Act** further informed the court’s view on payment obligations between corporate entities and their members [a.s. of claim C].

3. **Cost Orders**: The court recognized that costs typically follow the result unless other compelling circumstances suggest otherwise. The absence of a costs order regarding claim B in the court a quo was deemed an error [40].

4. **Evidentiary Burdens**: The court closely evaluated the evidentiary submissions made by both parties, considering the agreement's written provisions and the parties' actions in interpreting contract terms.

## A – Application

In its judgment, the Supreme Court of Appeal affirmed the lower court's findings regarding repudiation, noting substantial evidence that indicated neither party acted in a manner that implied an outright repudiation of the agreement [32–39]. The appellants had failed to demonstrate a prima facie case of repudiation that warranted the cancellation of the agreement.

With respect to claims for unjust enrichment, the court emphasized that payments made to a third party (i.e., REM, rather than Rusco) had been acquiesced by the first appellant, undermining the basis for his enrichment claims. The court rejected the appeal against the claims relating to management fees on these grounds, upholding the rationale of the court a quo that the payments had been understood and agreed upon between the parties, despite subsequent disagreements [34–36].

Notably, the implications of the failed claims led the court to conclude that the original costs order concerning claim B needed rectification, highlighting issues of procedural justice in cost allocation [40].

The court further clarified the legal obligations regarding the amended quantum of damages sought, ruling that the appellant had failed to substantiate this amendment request reliably due to lack of relevant evidence identified during the trial [5–6].

## C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming that the court a quo's interpretation of the repudiation allegation was correct and that claimed enrichment based on managerial fees failed. The cross-appeal was partially granted, with adjustments made to earlier orders related to costs in the lower court’s dismissed claims [41–42]. The ruling underscored the stringent evidentiary requirements in establishing claims of repudiation and unjust enrichment, underscoring the necessity for a clear legislative framework in evaluating partnership agreements and associated claims following dissolution.

The decision has significant implications for future partnership disputes, clarifying that cessation of a business relationship necessitates explicit conduct to support claims of repudiation and that the reasonable expectations of profit-sharing must align with documented agreements. Thus, parties involved in similar partnerships should meticulously adhere to contract terms and maintain comprehensive records of agreements and financial transactions to mitigate potential disputes.
