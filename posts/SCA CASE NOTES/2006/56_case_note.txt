Source Folder: 2006

# CASE NOTE: Zanner v Director of Public Prosecutions [2006] SCA 56 (RSA)

## F: Facts
The case of **Frank Peter Zanner (Appellant) v The Director of Public Prosecutions, Johannesburg (Respondent)** arises from an incident that occurred on **12 March 1992**, when Zanner fatally injured his colleague, Samuel Tumisang Segaetso, with a vernier gauge during a disputed altercation at a factory in Krugersdorp. Witness accounts differ regarding whether the injury was accidental or deliberate, but it resulted in the deceased suffering a penetrating head injury ([2–3]).

Following the incident, an inquest was scheduled for **23 November 1992** but was not conducted due to witness unavailability. An informal inquest held on **8 March 1993** failed to determine liability due to insufficient evidence ([5–6]). Subsequently, the case was paused for over ten years until Zanner was indicted on **2 counts of murder** on **21 April 2004**—the same year investigations were revived after the killing of Zanner's wife ([7]).

Zanner sought a **permanent stay of prosecution** under **s 35(3)(d)** of the Constitution, arguing that the lengthy delay had resulted in trial-related prejudice, specifically the unavailability of key witnesses and the deterioration of evidence ([8]). The Johannesburg High Court dismissed his application, highlighting a lack of demonstrated prejudice ([9–10]).

## I: Issues
The primary legal question in this case is whether the lengthy delay in the prosecution of Zanner constituted a breach of his right to a fair trial under **s 35(3)(d)** of the Constitution, specifically if it caused trial-related prejudice warranting a permanent stay of prosecution ([8–9]). Additionally, the court considered whether the appellant could show extraordinary circumstances justifying such a stay ([8]).

## R: Rules
The decision hinged on several legal standards and precedents that address the right to a fair trial within a reasonable time, as enshrined in **s 35(3)(d)** of the South African Constitution, which guarantees every accused person the right to a trial without unreasonable delay ([11]). Relevant cases cited included:

- **Sanderson v Attorney-General, Eastern Cape** (1998) 2 SA 38 (CC), which discussed the stringent nature of a permanent stay and the necessity of demonstrating significant trial-related prejudice.
- **Fose v Minister of Safety and Security** (1997) 3 SA 786 (CC), providing a framework for judicial relief while considering infringement of constitutional rights ([9]).
- The principles established in **R v Morin** (1992) and **Barker v Wingo** (1972) regarding the impact of delayed trials on the rights to personal security, liberty, and a fair trial were also applied ([12]).

The court underlined that a permanent stay of prosecution is a drastic and infrequently granted remedy, suitable only when the accused can demonstrate clear and compelling evidence of actual trial-related prejudice ([10]).

## A: Application
The court meticulously assessed the circumstances surrounding the delay, which commenced in **August 1993** when Zanner was first charged and continued until the 2004 indictment. It determined that the mere presence of delay does not inherently violate constitutional protections; instead, the determination must be context-dependent, weighing various factors including the accused's assertion of rights and any resultant prejudice ([14–15]).

Regarding prejudice, the court found that Zanner had failed to establish that he would suffer any significant trial-related difficulties due to witness unavailability or memory deterioration. In fact:
1. Zanner had **nearly six months** to prepare a defense before the original trial date.
2. Key witnesses whom the appellant alleged were unavailable, particularly **Picota**, remained traceable, and the appellant did not adequately identify any others who were crucial to his defense ([17–20]).
3. It was speculated whether the memories of the witnesses had materially faded, but the court pointed out that any such erosion would likely burden the prosecution more severely than the defense, thus not establishing the requisite prejudice ([19–21]).

Ultimately, the court ruled that Zanner had not successfully demonstrated significant trial-related prejudice and failed to present extraordinary circumstances justifying a stay of prosecution ([22]).

## C: Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Zanner's appeal against the previous ruling of the Johannesburg High Court, affirming that the evidence presented did not substantiate his claims of prejudice linked to the lengthy prosecution delay. This ruling underscores the necessity for accused persons to provide concrete evidence when asserting a right to a fair trial has been compromised due to delays, distinguishing between mere assertions and demonstrable harm ([22–23]).

The importance of public interest in ensuring justice, particularly regarding serious offenses such as murder, was also highlighted, maintaining a balance between individual rights and societal demands for accountability. The appeal was thus concluded with no order as to costs, as the nature of the proceedings related to enforcing constitutional rights was acknowledged ([22–23]). The overarching implication remains that while rights to a speedy trial must be protected, claims of prejudice must be substantiated with tangible evidence in the context of serious criminal allegations.
