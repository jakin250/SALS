Source Folder: 2009

# Case Note: Lufuno Mphaphuli & Associates (Pty) Ltd v Nigel Athol Andrews and Bopanang Construction CC

**Court:** Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Case Number:** CCT 97/07  
**Judgment Date:** 20 March 2009  
**Judge:** Kroon AJ (with O'Regan ADCJ, Ngcobo J concurring)

## FIRAC Case Note

### F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([2]-[20])

The case revolves around a contractual dispute between Lufuno Mphaphuli & Associates (Pty) Ltd ("Mphaphuli"), the applicant, and Bopanang Construction CC ("Bopanang"), the second respondent. Mphaphuli, an electrical infrastructure contractor, entered into a subcontract with Bopanang for work related to an Eskom electrification project in Limpopo. 

Disputes arose regarding payments and performance, leading Bopanang to initiate legal proceedings to claim approximately R650,000 for work done. The parties subsequently agreed to refer their dispute to arbitration, appointing Nigel Athol Andrews (the first respondent) as the arbitrator.

Following the arbitration process, the arbitrator issued an award on 23 August 2004, ordering Mphaphuli to pay Bopanang R339,998.83 plus interest from 6 October 2002. Mphaphuli sought to have the arbitrator's award set aside, arguing procedural unfairness, including allegations of bias and misconduct by the arbitrator. These claims were initially rejected by the High Court and later upheld by the Supreme Court of Appeal, leading to Mphaphuli’s application for leave to appeal at the Constitutional Court.

### I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([22]-[23])

The following constitutional and legal issues were presented for determination:

1. **Judicial Control over Arbitration Awards**: To what extent are courts entitled to exercise control over arbitration awards before adopting them as court orders?
   
2. **Waiver of Constitutional Rights**: By entering into an arbitration agreement, do parties waive fundamental aspects of their right to a fair hearing as per s 34 of the Constitution, and under what conditions?

3. **Grounds for Review Under the Arbitration Act**: What is the correct approach to grounds of review in terms of s 33 of the Arbitration Act, in light of the right to a fair hearing as stated in s 34 of the Constitution?

### R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([24]-[36])

The Court relied on several key legal principles:

- **Constitutional Framework**: Section 34 of the Constitution ensures the right to have any dispute resolved in a fair public hearing before an independent tribunal ([34]). This right emphasizes procedural fairness, which the Court determined should apply to arbitration proceedings as well.
  
- **Judicial Precedents**: The judgment referenced the principles from cases like _Telcordia Technologies Inc v Telkom SA Ltd_ [2006] ZASCA 112, which established that while private arbitration is consensual and offers some waivers, fundamental rights to a fair hearing cannot be entirely relinquished ([35]).

- **Arbitration Act**: The provisions of sections 31 and 33 of the Arbitration Act 42 of 1965 were examined, focusing on the conditions under which courts can set aside arbitration awards, namely misconduct and gross irregularities ([33]).

### A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([67]-[176])

1. **Ex Parte Meetings**: The Court scrutinized the arbitrator's conduct, identifying three meetings held only with Bopanang. It found that these communications, not accessible to Mphaphuli, constituted a breach of the audi alteram partem rule (the right to be heard) as the arbitrator's engagement fortified Bopanang’s claims at the expense of Mphaphuli’s right to respond.

2. **Bias and Fair Hearing**: The judgment evaluated claims of bias and concluded that the circumstances surrounding the arbitrator’s interactions with Bopanang raised a reasonable apprehension of bias. The Court ruled that the arbitrary conduct potentially deprived Mphaphuli of a fair hearing, undermining the integrity of the award ([180]-[185]).

3. **Mandate of the Arbitrator**: The Court held that the arbitrator’s divergence from the original pleadings and awards in excess of claimed amounts signified a gross irregularity, violating the stipulations of the Arbitration Act. The arbitrator was deemed to have misconstrued his mandate, leading to the possibility of unfair outcomes ([175]-[177]).

### C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([187]-[304])

The Constitutional Court, in its majority judgment, ultimately determined that Mphaphuli was indeed denied a fair hearing. The arbitrator's procedural injustices were substantial enough to set aside the arbitration award. The implications of this decision underscored the necessity for consistency in maintaining the constitutional right to a fair trial in arbitration contexts. 

O'Regan ADCJ, in her minority judgment, contended that section 34 does not apply to private arbitration, advocating instead for an informal and investigative approach to arbitration as supported by the parties' agreement. The dichotomy in the judgments reflects ongoing debates about balancing party autonomy in arbitration with the constitutional rights enshrined in the South African Bill of Rights ([289]-[300]).

Thus, the Court provided essential jurisprudential guidance on navigating the intersection of arbitration, constitutional law, and the doctrine of procedural fairness, recognizing that arbitration agreements must inherently respect principles of fairness and justice. The decision stands as a pivotal reference for future arbitration proceedings, reinforcing the necessity of transparency and equal treatment within the arbitration framework.
