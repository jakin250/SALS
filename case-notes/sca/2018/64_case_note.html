Novartis AG v Cipla Medpro (Pty) Ltd

The dispute in Novartis AG v Cipla Medpro (Pty) Ltd revolves around the trade mark application for "CURIDA" made by Cipla Medpro (the first respondent) and Novartis AG's (the appellant's) earlier registration of the trade mark "CURITAZ".

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 64 Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal of Sou

Case Title: Novartis AG v Cipla Medpro (Pty) Ltd (728/17) [2018] ZASCA 64

Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

Date Delivered: 24 May 2018

Coram: Lewis, Ponnan, Leach, Saldulker, Mathopo JJA

F – Facts

The dispute in Novartis AG v Cipla Medpro (Pty) Ltd revolves around the trade mark application for "CURIDA" made by Cipla Medpro (the first respondent) and Novartis AG's (the appellant's) earlier registration of the trade mark "CURITAZ". The appellant, a Swiss pharmaceutical company, applied to register CURITAZ on 2 July 2008, which was accepted and granted on 23 August 2010, specifically for pharmaceutical preparations containing piperacillin and tazobactam ([paras 2–4]).

In contrast, Cipla Medpro applied for the CURIDA mark on 18 November 2009, intended for a broader range of products in Class 5, including pharmaceutical and veterinary preparations amongst others. Upon learning of this application, Novartis opposed it based on Section 10(14) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, arguing that CURIDA was too similar to CURITAZ, thus likely to cause confusion ([paras 2–5]). The Gauteng Division of the High Court ruled in favor of Cipla Medpro, finding insufficient likelihood of confusion, and allowed the CURIDA mark's registration ([para 6]).

I – Issues

The main legal question was whether the trade marks CURIDA and CURITAZ were sufficiently similar to create a likelihood of confusion among consumers, as stipulated by Section 10(14) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 ([paras 1, 9]). The court needed to determine if the first respondent had adequately disproven the appellant's objections based on this foundation ([para 6]).

R – Rules

The court interpreted Section 10(14) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, which states that a mark cannot be registered if it is identical or too similar to an existing registered mark, thereby likely to deceive or cause confusion in the marketplace. Previous case law, including the criteria established in J W Jagger and Co Ltd v Furnishers Cape (Pty) Ltd, clarified that the onus lies on the applicant to prove no likelihood of confusion exists once an objection has been raised ([paras 4, 6]).

The court also referenced the principles set forth in prior rulings:

  1. Confusion arises not only when all interested parties may be deceived but when a significant number are likely to do so ([para 11a]).
  2. The assessment of confusion requires a holistic comparison of the marks, considering their visual, aural, and conceptual similarities ([para 10]).
  3. The initial impression made by the first syllable of a mark is the most impactful ([para 14]).

A – Application

The court critically examined the two marks in question, noting the self-evident similarity in their prefixes and concluding that the difference created by the suffixes ("TAZ" vs "DA") is less impactful when considering how they would be perceived aurally ([para 12]). It argued that the common prefix "CURI" is distinctive and memorable, which could mislead consumers regarding the origin of the products ([paras 14–15]).

Furthermore, the court rejected the lower court's assertion that the regulatory environment and specialized nature of the pharmaceutical market would mitigate the risk of confusion amongst trained healthcare professionals. It emphasized the importance of accounting for patients’ roles in discussing medications and making informed choices ([paras 16, 20]). The comparison ultimately viewed CURIDA and CURITAZ as likely to cause initial confusion, even if clarity could follow upon more detailed examination ([para 20]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the private interests of the appellant outweighed the potential market confusion posed by the registration of CURIDA. The lower court erred in assessing the likelihood of confusion; thus, the appeal was upheld with costs awarded in favor of the appellant. The court ordered the refusal of the CURIDA application, recognizing the legal principles and market realities that contributed to the risks of consumer deception ([paras 21–22]).

This case underscores the critical role of the onus on trademark applicants to demonstrate the absence of confusion when a prior mark is contested, particularly in sectors where product safety and efficacy are paramount concerns.
