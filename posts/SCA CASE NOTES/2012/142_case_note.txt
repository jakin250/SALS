Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

### FIRAC Case Note: Azwihangwisi Mmboi v The State (167/12) [2012] ZASCA 142

#### F – Facts
The case under consideration presents a scenario that arose on 21 December 2003, when the two appellants, Azwihangwisi Robert Mmboi (First Appellant) and Andries Ndishavhelafi Mudau (Second Appellant), faced charges of murder and robbery stemming from an incident in Lwamondo village, Limpopo. Alongside three other individuals, they were accused of planning to rob the deceased after observing his consumption of expensive liquor at a bar ([1]–[2]). The first appellant was indicted as one of the principal participants while the second appellant was also implicated as an accused but primarily engaged in the robbery ([3]).

The trial itself took place in the Limpopo High Court, which ultimately sentenced both appellants to lengthy terms of imprisonment—twenty years for murder and eighteen for robbery—with the sentences ordered to run consecutively, leading to an effective sentence of thirty-eight years for each appellant ([4]). Following the trial, the first appellant appealed against both his conviction and sentence, while the second appellant appealed only against his sentence ([5]).

#### I – Issues
The primary issues to be determined in this appeal included:

1. Whether the first appellant possessed the requisite mens rea for murder based on his alleged participation in a common purpose with his co-accused, particularly given the lack of evidence proving a prior agreement among the accused to carry out the robbery ([12], [16]).
2. Whether the high court appropriately assessed the second appellant's sentence, particularly in regard to the cumulative effect of the sentences imposed and whether concurrent sentences should have been applied ([23]).

#### R – Rules
The court considered several legal principles pertinent to the adjudication of the case:

- **Common Purpose Doctrine**: As articulated in *S v Mgedezi & others* 1989 (1) SA 687 (A), a group of individuals may be held liable for a crime committed if they agree to commit a crime or are involved in a joint unlawful enterprise. However, all participants must exhibit a shared intention or express conduct that might demonstrate common purpose, as underscored by established jurisprudence ([12], [18]).
- **Evaluation of Accomplice Testimony**: In *R v Ncanana* 1948 (4) SA 399 (A), it was stressed that the testimony of accomplices should be scrutinized with caution because of their inherent motivations to potentially deceive. To convict based on an accomplice's testimony, corroboration implicating the accused is also necessary ([14], [15]).
- **Sentencing Discretion**: The appellate court may intervene in sentence judgments if there is evidence of material misdirection or when the imposed sentence is disturbingly inappropriate. This principle was reaffirmed in *S v Malgas* 2001 (2) SA 1222 (SCA) ([24], [26]).

#### A – Application
In analyzing the first appellant’s claim, the majority decision found no cogent evidence supporting the existence of a prior agreement indicative of a common purpose to murder. The testimonies of the two accomplices conflicted significantly, particularly regarding the first appellant's actual participation, which deviated from the expected conduct needed to establish liability ([16], [21]). The court concluded that the first appellant’s conduct was not indicative of active association but instead revealed him to be a passive bystander, thus leading to the finding that the conviction could not stand ([21]).

Regarding the second appellant’s appeal against his sentence, the court recognized that even though the individual sentences did not seem disproportionately severe on their own, the cumulative effect of a thirty-eight-year imprisonment sentence warranted consideration ([29]). Conceding that the state had not sufficiently accounted for the concurrent nature of the robbery with murder, the court found grounds for modifying the imposed sentences and ordered the sentences to run concurrently, resulting in a total term of twenty years ([28], [30]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded by partially allowing the second appellant's appeal against his sentence, ordering that his sentences for robbery and murder run concurrently, resulting in an effective term of twenty years’ imprisonment. Conversely, the first appellant’s appeal was upheld, leading to the complete reversal of his conviction and the associated sentences due to insufficient evidence of mens rea and active participation ([1], [35]).

This case underscored significant legal concepts in South African criminal law related to the common purpose doctrine, the treatment of accomplice evidence, and sentencing practices. It highlights the court's careful evaluation of the evidentiary basis for criminal liability, demonstrating the critical importance of diligent legal standards in maintaining fairness within the criminal justice system.
