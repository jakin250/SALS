Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# Case Note: Strata International (Pty) Ltd v Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality (79/2014) [2015] ZASCA 47 

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The facts of this case revolve around the Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality (the respondent) and the attempted transaction involving immovable property located in Selcourt Extension 4. The first appellant, Strata International (Pty) Ltd, and the second appellant, the Garden Estate for Small and Medium Enterprises (Pty) Ltd, sought to assert rights regarding this property ([1]).

The property, encompassing around 103 hectares, had been designated as a residential township since 1992 but remained undeveloped ([2]). In March 2000, Mr. Johnson proposed developing the property into an industrial park, leading to several draft agreements with the municipality ([2]–[3]). 

The Corporate Affairs Committee of the municipality was given the authority to approve disposals of municipal land, with specific adherence to legal stipulations outlined in the Local Government Ordinance 17 of 1939 and other related legislative frameworks ([3]–[4]). In January 2004, the committee took a resolution to approve the concept of a Land Availability Agreement with Mr. Johnson, which sought to establish the terms for developing the property.

However, it was later revealed that by the time the agreement was signed in September 2004, no public notification or engagement occurred regarding the disposal of the property ([9], [12]). The agreement was initially upheld by municipal officials but fell into question when large-scale irregularities came to light by July 2006 ([15]).

In December 2004, the second appellant accepted the agreement, and subsequently transferred its rights and obligations to the first appellant ([14]). After the municipality refused to execute the agreement, the appellants sought a declaratory order from the court regarding their claims as the contracting party and to ensure the municipality complied with statutory provisions before the properties could be disposed of ([16], [18]).

### I – Issues
The central issues in this case involved whether the resolutions passed by the municipality's committee could confer 'public law rights' as claimed by the appellants and whether these resolutions were valid given their lack of public participation and transparency as mandated by relevant legislation ([17]). Additionally, the court examined whether the resolutions improperly circumvented statutory requirements in both the Local Government Ordinance and the Municipal Finance Management Act ([20]–[21]).

### R – Rules
The court's analysis relied heavily on several key legislative provisions:

1. **Local Government Ordinance 17 of 1939 (LGO)**: Specifically, sections 79(18)(a) and 79(18)(b) outline requirements for the alienation of municipal property, mandating public notice and the opportunity for objections ([5]–[6]).
  
2. **Municipal Finance Management Act 56 of 2003 (MFMA)**: Sections 14(1) and (2) restrict a municipality from disposing of capital assets unless certain conditions have been satisfied, including ensuring that the asset is not needed for public services and assessing its fair market value ([10]).

3. **Constitution of South Africa**: Relevant provisions include Section 152(1)(a), which emphasizes accountability and responsiveness in local government, and Section 195(1)(f) and (g), which mandate transparency and accountability in public administration ([21]).

### A – Application
In applying these rules to the facts, the court noted that the resolutions passed by the municipality's committee breached both statutory and constitutional requirements relating to public participation and transparency ([20]–[21]). Specifically, there was no public notice of the committee's meeting that approved the agreement, nor was there any opportunity afforded to the public to object as required under section 79(18) of the LGO ([12]).

The committee’s actions fell short of the requirements of the MFMA as well, particularly concerning the obligations to ensure the asset in question was not essential for municipal services and to consider its fair market value before disposal ([10], [21]). Consequently, the agreement could not validly confer rights to the appellants nor obligate the municipality to follow through with the agreement due to these significant legal deficiencies. 

Further, the court dismissed the appellants' argument that public law rights had arisen from the resolutions—highlighting that due to the failure to comply with constitutional imperatives concerning transparency, such rights could not exist ([22]).

### C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the findings of the court a quo, concluding that the resolutions of the Corporate Affairs Committee were invalid and incapable of creating enforceable rights. The lack of compliance with necessary statutory frameworks and the absence of public participation rendered both the resolutions and the subsequent agreement fundamentally flawed ([17], [23]). 

As a result, the appeal was dismissed with costs, underscoring the implications for local government accountability and transparency in the disposal of municipal assets. This case reinforces the critical necessity for adherence to legislative and constitutional mandates in governance and public administration ([21]–[23]).
