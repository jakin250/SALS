Supreme Court of Appeal

Minister of Mineral Resources and Energy and Others v Sustaining the Wild Coast NPC and Others

However, when Shell began to conduct seismic surveys in December 2021, the first to seventh respondents, including environmental organizations and local communities, approached the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court for interdictory relief.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024

F – Facts

The genesis of this appeal lies in the granting of an exploration right on 29 April 2014 by the first appellant, the Minister of Mineral Resources and Energy ("the Minister"), to the third appellant, Impact Africa Limited ("Impact"), for oil and gas exploration along South Africa's Wild Coast to be exercised by the second appellant, Shell Exploration and Production South Africa B.V. ("Shell"), and the fourth appellant, BG International Limited ("BG") ([1]). Impact subsequently sought and received renewals of this exploration right on 20 December 2017 and 30 July 2021 ([1]).

However, when Shell began to conduct seismic surveys in December 2021, the first to seventh respondents, including environmental organizations and local communities, approached the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court for interdictory relief. On 28 December 2021, the court granted an interim interdict against Impact and Shell, preventing them from conducting seismic operations while the case was considered ([2]).

In subsequent proceedings, the high court reviewed and set aside the ministerial decisions granting the original and renewed exploration rights, ordering the appellants to pay costs. Notably, the high court did not address the requirement of environmental authorization under the National Environmental Management Act 107 of 1998 (NEMA), as it believed that the review's success would render that query moot. The appellants appealed this decision, and the respondents cross-appealed, challenging the high court's refusal to consider the necessity of environmental authorization ([3], [4]).

I – Issues

The key legal issues in this appeal included:

  1. Delay in Review Application: Did the high court err in ruling that the respondents timely initiated their review application within the 180-day period mandated by section 7(1) of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)? The appellants contended that the respondents delayed action unreasonably ([6] to [10]).
  2. Exhaustion of Internal Remedies: Did the high court err in exempting the respondents from the requirement to exhaust internal remedies prior to pursuing judicial review of the ministerial decisions related to the exploration right, based on claims of bias and urgency? ([15], [18]).
  3. Procedural Fairness: Did the Minister and Impact comply with the procedural fairness requirements mandated under section 33 of the Bill of Rights and PAJA in consulting with affected communities, particularly concerning the seismic surveys? The adequacy of public engagement concerning the seismic exploration’s impact on local communities was also under scrutiny ([19] to [25]).
  4. Remedial Discretion under Section 172(1): Did the high court err by failing to consider just and equitable relief under section 172 when setting aside the exploration rights? Should the court have evaluated the feasibility of a remedy without an outright setting aside of the rights? ([26] to [30]).

R – Rules

The court relied on several legal rules and principles, including:

  1. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA): Particularly sections regarding delays in initiating review applications (section 7(1)) and the requirement to exhaust internal remedies (section 7(2)(c)) ([10], [18]).
  2. Environmental Legislation and Authorities: The court considered the National Environmental Management Act 107 of 1998 (NEMA) and the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA), particularly the stipulations regarding consultation and environmental impact assessments ([4], [19], [20]).
  3. Principles of Procedural Fairness: These were drawn from case law, including the Constitutional Court’s decision in Bengwenyama Minerals (Pty) Ltd v Genorah Resources (Pty) Ltd ([20], [22]).
  4. Rights Under Section 172 of the Constitution: The remedy considerations arising upon a finding of unlawfulness required assessing both the just and equitable nature of the relief ([26], [27]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal addressed the issues systematically:

  1. Delay in Review Application: The court found that the delay in launching the review application was not unreasonable, as the respondents became aware of the exploration right's grant only in late October 2021. The court rejected the appellants' argument that the public's general awareness of the exploration application sufficed. As such, the 180-day period under PAJA was deemed to have commenced in October 2021, affirming the high court's ruling ([7] to [14]).
  2. Exhaustion of Internal Remedies: The court acknowledged the respondents' valid concerns about bias and urgency, which justified their bypassing of internal remedies. The lack of notification about the decisions to the public suggested that internal remedies would not have been effective ([15] to [18]).
  3. Procedural Fairness: The court concluded that Impact failed to adequately consult with affected communities, particularly by utilizing English and Afrikaans for public notices instead of local languages, thus rendering their consultation efforts ineffective. The analysis indicated a significant failure to address essential factors affecting community rights and livelihoods ([22] to [25]).
  4. Remedial Discretion: The court critiqued the high court for not considering just and equitable remedies following a declaration of unlawfulness. It emphasized the need for courts to ensure that economic and social implications are factored into the outcome of administrative decisions—highlighted by the significant investment made by the appellants based on the granted rights ([26] to [30]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal in part, maintaining the high court's finding on procedural fairness, while also addressing the necessity of incorporating just and equitable remedial measures concerning the exploration rights.

The court notably added that the setting aside of the exploration rights was suspended pending a further application for renewal submitted on 21 July 2023, allowing the appellants the opportunity to address public participation shortcomings identified in the previous process. The judgment underscored the importance of balancing environmental concerns, community rights, and administrative certainty, reinforcing the constitutional commitment to just administrative processes. The decision is significant in clarifying the procedural requirements under PAJA and the MPRDA, emphasizing the necessity of effective community engagement in administrative processes affecting their rights ([31], [32]).
