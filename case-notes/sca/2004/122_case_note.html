Supreme Court of Appeal

Pharmaceutical Society of South Africa and Others v The Minister of Health and Another (SCA)

The dispute arose post-promulgation, leading the PSSA and other pharmacy stakeholders to apply for a judicial review in the Cape High Court.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2004

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–7])

The case in question involves the Pharmaceutical Society of South Africa (PSSA) and New Clicks South Africa (Pty) Ltd, both appellants, challenging the validity of the “Regulations relating to a Transparent Pricing System for Medicines and Scheduled Substances,” issued on 30 April 2004 under section 22G of the Medicines and Related Substances Act 101 of 1965 (hereinafter referred to as "the Medicines Act"). The ministerial regulations aim to establish a pricing system that mandates manufacturers to charge a single exit price for medicines while prohibiting discounts. A dispensing fee is also set based on the prescription status and price of the medicine ([para 1]).

The dispute arose post-promulgation, leading the PSSA and other pharmacy stakeholders to apply for a judicial review in the Cape High Court. The High Court dismissed their applications, prompting the appellants to seek leave to appeal ([paras 2–4]). Critical to the case were procedural complexities regarding the timing of the leave to appeal application, as the High Court had not promptly ruled on the appeal ([para 8]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 9–38])

Three main legal issues were identified for determination in the Supreme Court of Appeal:

  1. Urgency: Should the application for leave to appeal, despite the non-ruling of the lower court, be treated with urgency? The appellants contended that urgency persisted due to the implications for public health ([para 10]).
  2. Jurisdiction: Could the Supreme Court of Appeal exercise jurisdiction to grant leave to appeal without a ruling from the High Court? The respondents argued that absence of a High Court ruling rendered the application a nullity ([paras 18–19]).
  3. Reasonable Prospects of Success: Do the appellants show reasonable prospects of success on appeal concerning the legality of the regulations in question? This also involved scrutinizing the concept of "appropriateness" regarding the dispensing fees ([paras 24–29]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 40–65])

The court relied primarily on the Medicines Act and principles of administrative justice as established under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 2 of 2000.

Key legal tenets included:

  • The principle of legality, which mandates that all actions taken by state organs must fall within the powers conferred to them by law ([para 41]).
  • Section 22G of the Medicines Act serves as the regime within which the minister may regulate pricing, which must align with the Act's goals concerning public health ([para 48]).
  • The ruling emphasized earlier case laws that infer that regulations exceeding the statutory power conferred (ultra vires) are invalid ([para 41]).

Furthermore, the judgment underscored the constitutional mandate for the right to access health care, found in section 27 of the Constitution of South Africa ([para 42]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 66–92])

The court's scrutiny revealed that the single exit price regulation placed unreasonable restrictions on the pricing of medicines, leading to a conflict with the Medicines Act’s provisions. The minister was found to have exceeded her statutory powers by incorporating provisions advocating for a logistics fee, which negated the single exit system’s transparency ([paras 50–55]).

The court further discussed the implications of the dispensing fee, pointing out that while fees could be set, they must adhere to the criterion of appropriateness, which addresses both affordability for the public and the economic viability of pharmacies ([paras 72-86]). The assessments made regarding fee viability were corroborated by economic expert testimony demonstrating that the prescribed fees could threaten the operational capacity of pharmacies, defeating the accessibility goals of the Medicines Act ([paras 87-90]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 93–96])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeals, declaring the regulations invalid under the Medicines Act due to their ultra vires nature and failure to satisfy principles of legality. The judgment emphasized the essential harmony between the Medicines Act and the constitutional right to access healthcare, reinforcing the need for regulations that truly serve public interest without diminishing the viability of service providers ([paras 95–96]).

Accordingly, not only did the decision reject the regulations, but it also solidified a robust legal framework under which future legislation and regulations must conform to constitutional standards and public welfare considerations ([para 96]).

This case note reflects the legal analysis and implications of Pharmaceutical Society of South Africa and Others v The Minister of Health, illustrating the court's approach to the legality of regulatory powers within a constitutional democracy. The broader implications for health policy and pharmacy practice in South Africa were also acknowledged, stressing the delicate balance between access to medicine and maintaining the economic viability of healthcare providers.
