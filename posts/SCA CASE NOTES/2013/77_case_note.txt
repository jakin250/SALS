# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of Robinson v State (472/12)

**Neutral Citation**: Robinson v State (472/12) [2013] ZASCA 77 (30 May 2013)

**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Coram**: Ponnan, Tshiqi JJA, Erasmus, Plasket, and Mbha AJJA  
**Heard**: 22 May 2013  
**Delivered**: 30 May 2013

---

## F – Facts

The appellant, Trevor Brian Robinson, alongside a juristic entity he represented, was charged with committing 31 counts of fraud in the Oberholzer Regional Court ([1]). The complainant in this case was Goldfields International Mining South Africa (Pty) Ltd, which operates the Driefontein mine ([2]). The appellant's company, Oberholzer Timber and Hardware CC (Timber and Hardware), submitted two quotations on 3 June 2009 for urgent plumbing work needed at the mining site. The first quotation was for R94,801.66 for 515 meters of cladding piping, and the second for R111,524.35 for 630 meters. Each quotation contained a caution stating that "only an estimate can be given, a bill will be given on completion of application" ([2]).

After these quotations were accepted, the work was completed by a subcontractor. The appellant signed on behalf of Timber and Hardware, while Goldfields' Unit Manager Procurement, Mr. Patel, signed for the complainant ([3]). In the subsequent months, invoices reflecting the quoted amounts were submitted for payment. Notably, these invoices did not reference the meterage nor the additional charges for 'bends', 'tees', 'reducers', and 'taps' that were initially included in the quotes. Payment was approved despite Buys, an internal employee, being informed that work was not yet complete ([4]). 

Diverging measurements revealed that the meterage presented in the quotes did not match the completed work, triggering complaints. However, the evidence in the case showed no direct proof linking the appellant to any fraudulent representations or the preparation of these invoices ([5], [6]). 

The Oberholzer Regional Court convicted the appellant on two counts of fraud, but following appeals, the North Gauteng High Court found in favor of Timber and Hardware, allowing its appeal entirely. The present appeal addressed only the two counts affecting Robinson ([1]).

---

## I – Issues

The core legal question in this case revolved around whether the appellant had been correctly convicted of fraud based on the allegations that he made false representations to the complainant ([1]). Specifically, the issues for determination included:

1. **Did the trial court err in finding that the appellant made fraudulent representations which induced the complainant to act to its detriment?**
2. **Was there sufficient evidence showing that the appellant had submitted the allegedly fraudulent invoices or demanded payment thereof?** ([6]).

---

## R – Rules

The legal framework underpinning the court's decision draws upon established principles of South African fraud law, operational under the Crime Prevention and Prohibition Act (South Africa). The elements of fraud require:

1. A representation made that is false.
2. The knowledge or belief that the representation is false or made with reckless indifference to the truth.
3. The intent to deceive and induce another party to act on the representation to their detriment.

Furthermore, the court referred to previous case laws which establish the necessity for a clear link between the wrongful act and criminal liability, emphasizing that mere submission of invoices does not inherently constitute fraud if sufficient evidence does not support intent or misrepresentation ([6]).

---

## A – Application

In applying the aforementioned principles to the facts at hand, the court undertook a detailed evidential analysis. The crux of the decision was founded on the absence of any material evidence that the appellant had directly participated in the preparation or submission of the invoices in question, nor did he make any false representation to induce the complainant's action ([6]).

The court noted that although the invoices were approved for payment, there was no wrongdoing attributable to Robinson. Moreover, the complainant's own employee corroborated that there was an understanding that outstanding work on the project remained before any final payment should be made ([4]).

The discrepancies noticed only surfaced after the submission of further invoices, which were not pertinent to the charges before the court ([5]). Consequently, the findings of the court a quo, which led to the appellant's conviction, failed to meet the requisite burden of proof that should accompany allegations of fraudulent activity ([6]).

---

## C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appellant's conviction could not stand due to the lack of evidence demonstrating fraudulent intent or misrepresentation attributed directly to him. Therefore, the appeal was upheld, and the convictions and related sentences were set aside ([7]). 

The ruling not only established significant precedent regarding the need for clear evidence of intent in fraud cases but also underscored the importance of evaluating the roles of all parties involved before attributing criminal liability. The implications of this case highlight the necessity for precision in legal assessments concerning financial transactions and representations in corporate and contracting contexts within South Africa's legal system.
