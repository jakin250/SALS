# Case Note: FIRAC Method - Food and Allied Workers Union v Ngcobo N.O. and Mkhize

### CCT 50/13 
[2013] ZACC 36 

## Facts 
The matter concerns a legal dispute involving the Food and Allied Workers Union (the "Union") and two former employees, Mandla Ndlela and Michael Mkhize, who were dismissed by Nestlé South Africa (Pty) Ltd after 20 years of service ([paras 1–8]). Following their dismissals in May 2002, the employees sought assistance from the Union, which agreed to represent them in their claims of unfair dismissal. The Union initially referred the dispute to the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA), but its involvement ceased after the conciliation process failed ([paras 3–4]). 

The Union informed the employees it would refer their claims to the Labour Court but missed the 90-day statutory deadline to do so, without ever applying for the necessary condonation ([paras 4–6]). After failing to obtain timely representation, the employees engaged independent legal advice and subsequently sought damages from the Union for breach of contract after it had effectively abandoned their case ([paras 8–9]).

The High Court ruled in favor of the employees, as did the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), both finding the Union had a contractual obligation to represent them which it breached by failing to take necessary legal action. 

## Issues 
The primary issue before the Constitutional Court was whether a trade union could invoke its constitutional right to determine its administration to defend against claims for damages due to its failure to fulfill its obligations to its members ([paras 1, 24]). Specifically, the Union argued it enjoyed special constitutional protection, which exempted it from liability for breach of contract arising from the representation failure ([paras 18–24]).

## Rules 
The relevant legal provisions included:

1. **Section 23(4)(a) of the South African Constitution**, which establishes that trade unions have the right to determine their own administration, programmes, and activities.
2. **Section 200 of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA)**, which allows trade unions to act in three capacities: in their own interest, on behalf of their members, or in the interest of any of their members ([paras 18–20]). 
3. **Common Law Contract of Mandate**, which requires a mandatary (the Union here) to perform faithfully, honestly, and with diligence, particularly when a contractual obligation has been accepted ([paras 12–14]).

## Application 
In its analysis, the Court critically examined the Union's constitutional argument, determining that its right to self-administer did not exempt it from fulfilling its obligations to its members ([paras 26–28]). The Union's reliance on its constitution's clause 5.11 — allowing for legal assistance provision "where it deems it in the interest of the Union" — was found insufficient to imply a blanket exemption from liability for neglecting representation ([paras 32–36]).

The Court concluded that the Union's failure to lodge a claim within the stipulated 90-day period significantly, and detrimentally, affected the employees' legal standing ([paras 41–42]). Consistent with the decisions of the lower courts, the Union's argument that an implied tacit term entitled it to withdraw from its mandate at any time was rejected as contrary to the parties' reasonable expectations and understanding of their agreement ([paras 36–39]).

## Conclusion 
Ultimately, the Constitutional Court dismissed the Union's application for leave to appeal, upholding the findings of the previous courts regarding the Union’s breach of contract due to its mishandling of the employees' claims. The court noted that the Union could not escape contractual obligations through its constitutional rights ([paras 46–47]). 

This case underscores the binding nature of the obligations imposed on trade unions towards their members and illustrates that the constitutional protections afforded to unions cannot serve as a shield against liability for actions detrimental to their members' rights. The ruling emphasizes the importance of diligence and accountability in union representation in the context of labor disputes.
