Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: Minister of Police v SA Metal and Machinery Company (Pty) Ltd

## F - Facts

The case arises from the seizure of certain scrap metal belonging to the respondent, SA Metal and Machinery Company (Pty) Ltd (SA Metal), by members of the South African Police Service (SAPS) on June 13, 2008. The goods included various quantities of scrap materials, specifically 142 kilograms of scrap brass water meters, 20 kilograms of mixed copper scrap, 31 kilograms of greasy copper wire, 40 kilograms of scrap lead, and a scrap cast iron drain cover ([1]).

In April 2010, SA Metal initiated motion proceedings in the Western Cape High Court against the Minister of Police and the Officer Commanding of the Elsies River Police Station. Initially, they sought the return of the seized goods. However, prior to the appellants filing opposing papers, they informed SA Metal's attorneys that the goods had already been disposed of under Section 31 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA) ([2]). Consequently, SA Metal amended its motion to seek damages amounting to R8,611.50, representing the value of the disposed goods plus interest ([2]).

The High Court ruled in favor of SA Metal, granting it the alternative relief sought. The appellants, on the other hand, contended that they were entitled to dispose of the goods based on Section 31(1) of the CPA and that SA Metal could not recover damages since it was neither the owner nor lawful possessor of the goods ([3], [5]).

## I - Issues

The central legal questions in this case are:

1. Whether SAPS was justified in disposing of the goods seized from SA Metal.
2. Whether SA Metal could successfully recover damages under the actio ad exhibendum given the circumstances surrounding the seizure and subsequent disposal of its goods ([3]).

## R - Rules

The legal framework pertinent to this case includes:

- **Section 20 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA)**: This allows the State to seize items suspected to be involved in the commission of an offence ([3]).
- **Section 31 of the CPA**: If no criminal proceedings are initiated, the article must be returned to the lawful possessor, unless it cannot be lawfully possessed ([6]).
- **Actio ad exhibendum**: This delictual claim allows an owner to seek compensation when another party has wrongfully disposed of their property ([11]).
- **Legal Principles from Previous Judgments**: Previous cases such as *Minister van Wet en Orde en ‘n ander v Datnis Motors* dictate that the burden of proof rests with the State to show that the possession was unlawful ([7]).

## A - Application

In analyzing the application of law, the court noted that SA Metal needed to establish that it was in possession of the goods during the seizure, and that no criminal proceedings were ongoing against it ([7]). Importantly, the SAPS had not provided evidence to assert that SA Metal could not lawfully possess the goods, which was critical to justify their disposal of the items under Section 31(1)(b) of the CPA ([8]). 

The court dismissed the argument from SAPS that SA Metal’s failure to keep a register under the now-repealed Second-Hand Goods Act rendered its possession illegal. The court found that mere failure to adhere to the registration requirements did not equate to unlawful possession ([10]).

Regarding the claim for damages under actio ad exhibendum, the court reiterated that the disposition of goods occurred before litis contestatio, establishing SAPS's awareness of SA Metal's claim to the materials ([16]). This construct enabled SA Metal to successfully argue for damages, as they could demonstrate ownership and the wrongful alienation of their goods.

### Evaluation of Key Elements for Actio Ad Exhibendum

1. **Ownership**: The court concluded that SA Metal acted as the owner, asserting that it conducted due diligence in its acquisition of the scrap ([17]).
2. **Wrongful Disposition**: The disposal of the goods without proper legal basis constituted wrongful alienation, making SAPS liable for damages.
3. **Patrimonial Loss**: SA Metal suffered an identifiable loss due to the alienation of its property, enabling it to seek compensation ([17]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal of the appellants, upholding the High Court's ruling that SAPS wrongfully disposed of the goods without just cause. The court reinforced the legal principles surrounding the actio ad exhibendum and clarified the onus of proof under Section 31 of the CPA ([18]). The ruling not only confirmed SA Metal’s entitlement to damages (R8,611.50) but also reinforced the need for law enforcement agencies to adhere to proper legal processes when handling property seized under suspicion of criminal activity. This case highlights the balance between law enforcement's powers and individual rights to property, while also ensuring there are clear legal justifications for the disposal of seized items. 

The implications of this ruling are significant for both law enforcement practice and the rights of individuals in possession of seized property, as it reaffirms the importance of lawful possession and due process in criminal investigations. 

Thus, the judgment provides a critical reference point for future cases involving seizure and disposal of property, clarifying the obligations of SAPS and upholding the property rights of individuals against wrongful deprivation.
