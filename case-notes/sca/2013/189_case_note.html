Supreme Court of Appeal

Kievits Kroon Country Estate Pty Ltd v Mmoledi (875/12) [2013] ZASCA 189

The case involves Kievits Kroon Country Estate (Pty) Ltd (the appellant), a company providing conference and leisure facilities, and its employee, Johanna Mmoledi (the first respondent).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 189

F – Facts

The case involves Kievits Kroon Country Estate (Pty) Ltd (the appellant), a company providing conference and leisure facilities, and its employee, Johanna Mmoledi (the first respondent). Mmoledi, employed as a Chef de Partie since March 2005, was charged with misconduct for allegedly disobeying a directive to report for duty and for being absent without permission ([para 1–2]).

The sequence of events began in May 2007 when Mmoledi requested an extended period of unpaid leave to attend a traditional healer’s training course, citing visions of her ancestors as a "calling" to become a traditional healer. Her request was partially accommodated by allowing a week off, but the appellant refused her request for an extended absence due to operational needs ([para 4–6]). After further communication with the human resources manager, Mmoledi submitted a letter from her traditional healer as justification for her absence. Despite this, her request for further leave was denied, prompting her to fail to report for duty ([para 8–10]).

A disciplinary hearing led to her dismissal on 16 June 2007, after which she referred the matter to the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (the CCMA). The CCMA found her dismissal substantively unfair, as it held that her absence was due to "circumstances beyond her control," specifically her cultural beliefs relating to her health ([para 12–16]). This conclusion was subsequently upheld by the Labour Court and the Labour Appeal Court before reaching the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 1, 16]).

I – Issues

The core legal issues of the case include:

  1. Whether Mmoledi's reliance on a “calling from ancestors” provides a justifiable reason for her disobedience towards her employer's instruction to report for duty ([para 2, 17]).
  2. Whether a certificate from a traditional healer can be equated with a medical certificate for the purposes of justifying absence from work due to health issues ([para 8, 22]).
  3. The implications of cultural contexts in employment disputes concerning absence from work due to traditional beliefs ([para 15, 22]).

R – Rules

The decision relied on several legal principles and precedents:

  1. Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995, Section 145(2)(a)(ii), concerning review of arbitration awards due to gross irregularities ([para 19]).
  2. Basic Conditions of Employment Act 75 of 1997 which delineates provisions regarding sick leave and the procedural requirements for justifying absence due to ill health ([para 12]).
  3. Jurisprudential principles concerning the recognition of cultural and religious beliefs, as established in cases such as Department of Correctional Services v Popcru [2013] 4 SA 176, where dismissals related to cultural practices were deemed automatically unfair ([para 23–24]).
  4. Discussions concerning the nature of illness in various cultural contexts, contrasting traditional and empirical perceptions of health ([para 26–29]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal evaluated the arguments presented by both the appellant and the respondent. The appellant contended that the CCMA commissioner mischaracterized the inquiry by focusing improperly on whether Mmoledi was justified in failing to report for duty rather than whether the employer appropriately evaluated her request for leave. The appellant highlighted the lack of a medical basis for her absence as a primary reason for the fairness of her dismissal ([para 17–19, 31]).

However, the court recognized that the CCMA had correctly understood the respondent's cultural beliefs and experiences. The commissioner found Mmoledi’s sincerely held belief that failing to attend her traditional healer course posed severe health implications. The evidence from both Mmoledi and her traditional healer was largely unchallenged and formed the basis for the commissioner’s determination that the respondent's absence was necessitated by "circumstances beyond her control" ([para 15, 22, 30]).

The court further noted that Mmoledi's traditional healer’s certificate was not merely a request for leave but an expression of a legitimate cultural belief system regarding her health, which deserved consideration under South African labour relations principles. Acknowledging the importance of accommodating such beliefs in the workplace, especially given South Africa's diverse societal fabric, the court upheld the CCMA’s findings ([para 24–29]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal by the appellant, affirming the decisions of the CCMA, Labour Court, and Labour Appeal Court, noting that Mmoledi was justified in her non-compliance with the employer's directive based on her cultural beliefs ([para 34]). The ruling underscores the need for employers to respect and accommodate the cultural practices and beliefs of their employees, especially regarding matters of health and wellbeing.

The court’s approach highlighted a much-needed sensitivity towards traditional healing practices as valid expressions of cultural identity, thus contributing to the ongoing discourse regarding cultural rights and employment law in South Africa. The court ordered the appellant to bear the costs of the appeal, indicating a recognition of the merit in the respondent's case and the importance of fairness in the workplace ([para 34]).

This case serves as a seminal reference for future employment disputes involving cultural beliefs and the intersection between traditional practices and employer expectations in South Africa.
