Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# Case Note: Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service v Stepney Investments (20192/14) [2015] ZASCA 138

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts:

The appeal in this case arose following the Tax Court's decision which set aside additional assessments raised by the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (SARS) against Stepney Investments (Pty) Ltd regarding the 2002 and 2003 tax years. The core issue involved the determination of the base cost of shares disposed of by Stepney, comprising 4.37% of its shares in Emanzini Leisure Resorts (Pty) Ltd (ELR) ([1]).

Capital gains tax was introduced in South Africa on 1 October 2001, with the Eighth Schedule to the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962 establishing the framework for calculating tax liabilities on disposals of assets acquired prior to this date ([1]). The specific shares in question were classified as “pre-valuation date assets,” and Stepney elected to determine their value based on the market value on the valuation date, as permitted by paragraph 26(1)(a) of the Schedule ([2]).

The valuation of the shares was contested after the Commissioner adjusted their assessed value to nil, leading to assessments of a capital gain of R2 million in 2002 and R2.2 million in 2003. Stepney contended that the aggregate base cost of the shares exceeded the disposal proceeds, thus incurring a loss rather than a gain ([3]).

The dispute necessitated an examination of the Bridge Capital Services valuation, which employed the Discount Cash Flow (DCF) method. The Commissioner, while conceding the inappropriate use of alternative valuation methods, maintained that Stepney failed to prove the reliability of its figures and predictions ([5], [6]).

### I – Issues:

The primary legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the Tax Court was correct in setting aside the additional assessments imposed by SARS on Stepney ([1]). Specifically, the following legal questions arose:

1. Did Stepney substantiate the base cost of the shares it disposed of, as required under the Income Tax Act and the Eighth Schedule?
2. Was the market value of the shares determined by Stepney reasonable, given the method employed and the evidence presented?
3. Did the Commissioner impermissibly change the grounds of assessment during the appeal process?
4. What is the appropriate standard of review regarding findings of the Tax Court, especially concerning expert evidence on valuations ([12], [14])?

### R – Rules:

The Court based its decision on several critical legal principles:

1. **Income Tax Act 58 of 1962**: This Act outlines the procedures and methods for calculating capital gains tax and provides the legal basis for the valuation of assets as per the Eighth Schedule.
2. **Expert Evidence Standards**: Jurisprudence demonstrates that an expert’s opinion must be reasoned and based on sound methodology ([16]). The nature of expert testimony requires courts to scrutinize not just the conclusions but also the reasoning and reliability of the underlying data.
3. **Standard of Review**: The court noted that it possessed a full right of appeal over decisions made by the Tax Court concerning facts or law, given the provisions of section 86A of the Income Tax Act ([13]).
4. **Burden of Proof**: Stepney bore the onus of proving the accuracy and validity of its valuation under section 102(1)(e) of the Tax Administration Act 28 of 2011 and other applicable legal frameworks ([5]).

### A – Application:

The appellate court critically assessed both the methodologies used for valuation and the evidence presented by both parties. Stepney’s reliance on the DCF method was scrutinized in light of several deficiencies identified in the valuation by Bridge Capital Services. Key issues included:

1. **Future Cash Flow Projections**: The court highlighted significant discrepancies between predicted revenues and actual performances, noting that the projections used were based on outdated and unrealistic assumptions ([17]).
2. **Validation of Assumptions**: It was determined that the valuation did not adequately consider certain market conditions and historical performance data, which the valuer failed to incorporate into the analysis ([19]–[23]).
3. **Discount Rate Application**: The application of a uniform discount rate across different operations showed a lack of specificity regarding the risk profile of Stepney’s asset ([25]).
4. **Terminal Value Calculations**: The valuation’s terminal value failed to account for established risks regarding lease renewals and operational sustainability ([24]).

The court concluded that these failures invalidated the findings of the previous assessments. It emphasized that while the criticisms leveled against the DCF method were valid, Stepney had also failed to provide conclusive evidence supporting its valuation methodology within the context of the expectations set by the market conditions at the relevant time.

### C – Conclusion:

Ultimately, the Court held that the Tax Court erred in upholding the initial Bridge valuation, which was fundamentally flawed. The Commissioner conceded that a valuation of nil was unreasonable, recognizing the intrinsic value of the casino license held by ELR ([28]). Thus, the order of the Tax Court was substituted with an upheld assessment taken back to the Commissioner for further consideration and re-assessment of the tax liabilities, addressing the shortcomings illustrated in the original valuation process.

The implications of this ruling signal that taxpayers must substantiate their valuations with robust evidence and accurate methodologies in accordance with established tax law. Furthermore, the decision clarifies the nature of appeal rights under tax law, re-establishing the judicial scrutiny afforded to both fact and law by higher courts in South Africa.

In summary, the SCA set aside the additional assessments and directed the Commissioner to further investigate and reassess the market value of the shares in question, ruling in favor of Stepney with costs awarded {[29].
