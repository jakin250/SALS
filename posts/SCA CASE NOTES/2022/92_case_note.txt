Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# Case Note: Rademeyer v Ferreira (343/2021) [2022] ZASCA 92 (17 June 2022)

### FIRAC Method

#### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–7])

The appeal in question arises from a judgment of the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, Port Elizabeth, where the appellant's special plea of prescription was dismissed. The appellant, Dion Rademeyer, entered into a written agreement of sale on 27 August 2008 with the respondent, Thomas Ignatius Ferreira, for the purchase of immovable property valued at R950,000, of which R190,000 was paid as a deposit ([para 2]). Rademeyer failed to comply with the contractual obligations, including signing transfer documents.

In 2012, Ferreira applied for rectification of the deed of sale and to compel Rademeyer to execute necessary documents; alternatively, he sought cancellation of the agreement and damages if Rademeyer failed to comply within five days. The High Court, under Pickering J, granted the application, specifying that non-compliance would enable Ferreira to cancel the agreement and claim damages ([paras 3–4]).

Rademeyer’s non-compliance led Ferreira to apply for amended relief for damages in the same case. However, due to Rademeyer’s objection that the original order was final, Ferreira withdrew and instituted fresh summons in March 2016 under a new case number, seeking R854,182.20 as damages due to Rademeyer’s failure to comply with the 2012 order ([paras 5–6]). Rademeyer countered with a special plea, stating that the claim had become prescribed as the action was not instituted within three years from the date of the initial order ([paras 7–8]).

#### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 8–14])

The main issues to be resolved in this appeal are two-fold. Firstly, did the service of the notice of motion in 2012 constitute "a process whereby the creditor claims payment of the debt" under Section 15(1) of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 (the Act)? Secondly, did the issuing of summons under a different case number amount to the prosecution of "the process in question" as meant in Section 15(4) of the Act ([para 14])? The determination revolves around whether Ferreira's initial application interrupted the running of prescription for his damages claim.

#### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 10–15])

The relevant statutory provisions include Section 10 of the Prescription Act, which states that a debt shall expire after a specified period, and Section 11(d), which specifies a three-year prescription period for any other debt. Importantly, Section 15 focuses on judicial interruption—a process that may halt the running of prescription through the service of a process that demands payment of a debt ([paras 10–12]).

Key case law includes *Cape Town Municipality v Allianz Insurance Co Ltd* ([para 15]), which elucidated the importance of the service of a process in interrupting prescription, emphasizing that both the process and the subsequent judgment must form part of the same action. This principle is supported by earlier rulings in *Du Bruyn v Joubert* and *Neon and Cold Cathode Illuminations (Pty) Ltd v Ephron* ([para 15]).

#### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 21–23])

The court determined that the fundamental issue of whether the service of the initial notice of motion interrupted prescription hinged on the relationship between the original application and the subsequent actions for damages. The original application sought not only a declaratory order but also damages contingent upon Rademeyer’s non-compliance, thus anchoring it as a step in the enforcement of a claim for payment of a debt ([paras 21–22]).

The court affirmed that multiplying legal actions did not negate the original claim's spirit; the enforcement mechanisms were linked through the same cause of action. Given that the judgment ordering compliance was never abandoned, prescription remained interrupted ([para 23]). This conclusion built on the court's interpretation of how the declaratory judgment’s resolution of liability for damages reinforced the legal basis for the subsequent damages claim.

#### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 24–25])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the notice of motion served in 2012 constituted an effective interruption of prescription regarding Ferreira’s claim for damages. Thus, the appeal against the lower court's decision was dismissed with costs. The court emphasized that the legal rationale established confirms that calls for payment of damages arising from non-compliance with judicial orders must be treated holistically, reinforcing the interconnectedness of procedural steps in legal claims ([paras 24–25]). 

The implications of this ruling are significant as they clarify the thresholds for interruption of prescription within civil proceedings in South Africa, illustrating that procedural distinctions should not hinder valid claims arising from the same factual matrix.
