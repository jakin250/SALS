Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2007

# FIRAC Case Note: *Road Accident Fund v Grobler* [2007] SCA 78 (RSA)

## F – Facts: 
The case concerns a road accident that occurred on September 4, 1999, near Hammanskraal, involving a Nissan Skyline driven by Mr. SR Matseke (the insured driver) and a Yamaha motorcycle operated by Jason King Grobler (the respondent). Prior knowledge among the parties established that the insured driver overtook a stationary Isuzu bakkie and failed to return to his designated lane, entering the incorrect lane where Grobler was traveling ([para 3]). 

An eyewitness, Ronald Basson, estimated the distance between the motorcycle and the Skyline to be between 50 and 100 metres at the moment of emergency ([para 5]). The respondent was confronted with a sudden emergency upon realizing the insured driver’s negligence. Grobler attempted to evade a collision by swerving to the right, which ultimately resulted in a head-on collision. Grobler sustained severe injuries, including paralysis ([para 16]). 

The respondent, a minor at the time, sought compensation from the Road Accident Fund under the Road Accident Fund Act No 56 of 1996. The court a quo ruled in favor of Grobler, attributing sole negligence to the insured driver, and awarded damages totaling R3,931,461 ([para 17]). The Fund appealed the ruling, disputing the lack of contributory negligence on Grobler's part.

## I – Issues: 
1. **Contributory Negligence**: Whether there was contributory negligence on the part of Grobler that would impact the liability of the Road Accident Fund.
2. **Standard of Care**: What constitutes reasonable conduct in an emergency situation regarding motor vehicle operation under the circumstances presented ([paras 24-26]).

## R – Rules: 
The court relied upon principles from previous case law that described the standard of reasonable behavior expected of drivers, especially when confronted with a sudden emergency not of their own making. Critical judgments cited include:
- *Rodrigues v SA Mutual & General Insurance Company Ltd* [1981] 2 SA 274 (A) ([para 9]).
- *Ntsala v Mutual & Federal Insurance Company Ltd* [1996] 2 SA 184 (T) ([para 10]).
- *South African Railways and Harbours v Symington* [1935] AD 37 ([para 11]).
  
Additionally, the concept of “error of judgment” is essential in evaluating whether Grobler’s actions fell below the reasonable standard where a split-second decision is required ([para 12]).

## A – Application: 
In examining the actions of Grobler, the court noted that the emergency situation arose due to the insured driver’s negligent behavior, and Grobler had only a split second to react. The court criticized the idea of analyzing his decision-making through hindsight. It was found that a reasonable individual might similarly have chosen to swerve rather than maintain a straight course, given the imminent collision ([para 9],[para 10]).

The court highlighted Grobler's limited options in light of the unexpected nature of the emergency. The hazardous terrain to the left, comprising gravel, trees, and other obstructions, made it a perilous choice to swerve left. Given the computed times to impact (ranging from one to two seconds), the court concluded that Grobler acted reasonably under pressure ([paras 7-9]).

The appellant’s assertion that Grobler negligently failed to keep a lookout and could have averted the accident disregarded the immediate context of the emergency and the nature of his response, which was not seen as an actionable failure. Per earlier case precedents, the emphasis must focus on each driver's opportunity to avert danger prior to the emergency, which Grobler did not neglect ([paras 24-30]).

## C – Conclusion: 
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal from the Road Accident Fund, affirming that no contributory negligence could be established on Grobler's part. The court held that the actions taken by Grobler reflected reasonable conduct in the face of a sudden threat and did not invoke negligence. The broader implications of the ruling illustrated the necessity for courts to assess driver behavior not just from the moment an incident occurs but also to consider preemptive actions taken prior to the emergent scenario ([paras 15, 14]).

### Final Order:
The appeal was dismissed with costs, reiterating the original judgment that the Road Accident Fund was liable for the damages awarded to Grobler, which included future medical expenses and incurred costs related to the accident ([para 15]). This decision has precedent value for similar cases involving sudden emergencies and the expectation of reasonable action from drivers under stress.
