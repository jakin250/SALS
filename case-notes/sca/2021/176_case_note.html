The Chairperson of the North West Gambling Board & Another v Sun International (SA) Limited (1214/2019) [2021] ZASCA 176

The Board faced significant procedural issues, notably failing to file the record of appeal and heads of argument on time, which resulted in the appeal lapsing.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 176

F – Facts:

The case involves an appeal by the Chairperson of the North West Gambling Board and the North West Gambling Board (collectively, the "Board") against Sun International (SA) Limited ("SISA") concerning the exclusion or inclusion of Free Play credits in the calculation of gross gaming revenue for the purposes of gambling levies. The North West High Court had previously ruled in favor of SISA ([paras 2–3]).

The Board faced significant procedural issues, notably failing to file the record of appeal and heads of argument on time, which resulted in the appeal lapsing. The Board's delay stemmed from various administrative failures and reliance on the COVID-19 lockdown as a reason for non-compliance. Despite these delays, the Board submitted applications for condonation to reinstate their appeal ([paras 6–19]).

I – Issues:

The central legal issues to be resolved were:

  1. Whether the Board showed good cause for the late filing of the appeal record and heads of argument, thus justifying condonation ([para 1]).
  2. Consideration of whether the appeal would be reinstated despite the Board's delays in following procedural rules ([para 2]).

R – Rules:

The applicable rules governing appeal processes, particularly Rules 8 and 10 of the Supreme Court of Appeal's Rules, require the lodgment of appeal records and heads of argument within specified timeframes; failing to do so results in the lapse of the appeal. Rule 12 allows for applications for condonation ([paras 4–5]).

In SA Express Ltd v Bagport (Pty) Ltd, the court emphasized that in applications for condonation, applicants must provide adequate explanations for delays and that negligence by the applicant or their legal representatives can impact the outcome ([para 20]). Other relevant case law noted that prospects of success, while relevant, do not automatically justify condonation where there has been flagrant disregard for procedural requirements ([paras 20–23]).

A – Application:

The court critically assessed the Board's explanations for its procedural delays. Key points included:

  • The Board had a consistent pattern of delay, including the late filing of its application for leave to appeal and subsequent documents ([paras 7–19]).
  • The assertion that the COVID-19 lockdown interfered with filing was insufficient, given that the Board's attorney was based in Bloemfontein and could have filed the necessary documents electronically ([para 10]).
  • The court highlighted that the Board failed to communicate with SISA or the Registrar regarding its delays and did not seek extensions timely ([paras 11–12]).

Despite the Board claiming good prospects of success on appeal, the court found that the continuous and blatant disregard for court rules and the significant period of inactivity exhibited by the Board undermined their request for condonation and reinstatement ([paras 24–31]).

C – Conclusion:

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed both applications for condonation and reinstatement of the appeal ([para 30]). The Board's failure to comply with procedural rules was considered severe enough to warrant such a dismissal, reflecting the court's determination to uphold the integrity of the judicial process. This case serves as a pertinent reminder of the importance of timely compliance with procedural rules and the weight such compliance carries in the appellate context, even where prospects of success may appear strong ([paras 31–32]).

This case note effectively captures the critical aspects of the ruling and analyses the consequences of procedural neglect in the context of appellate jurisprudence.
