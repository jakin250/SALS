Izak Andreas Geldenhuys v National Director of Public Prosecutions

CCT 26/08 [2008] ZACC 21

FIRAC Analysis

F – Facts ([paras 1–9])

The applicant, Izak Andreas Geldenhuys, a 53-year-old dentist, applied for the confirmation of an order by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) which declared sections 14(1)(b) and 14(3)(b) of the erstwhile Sexual Offences Act 23 of 1957 unconstitutional. The impugned sections established a legal age of consent for “immoral or indecent acts” at 19 years for same-sex relationships, while the age was set at 16 years for opposite-sex relationships. The applicant had previously been convicted of indecency involving minors and was sentenced to 11 years in prison, following which he sought to appeal his conviction.

In March 2008, the SCA ruled these provisions to violate the Constitution's equality clause and ordered them to be read as providing for a uniform age of 16 years. The legislation had already been repealed by the Criminal Law (Sexual Offences and Related Matters) Amendment Act 32 of 2007. Geldenhuys applied to the Constitutional Court for confirmation, basing his argument on the right to equality outlined in section 9 of the Constitution.

I – Issues ([paras 22–24])

The central issue before the Constitutional Court was whether the differentiation in the age of consent for same-sex and opposite-sex acts infringed the right to equality in terms of section 9 of the Constitution. Specifically, it examined whether the law's setting of a higher age of consent for same-sex acts constituted unjustifiable discrimination, as prohibited under sections 9(1) and 9(3) of the Constitution. Furthermore, the Court scrutinized whether such discrimination could be justified under the general limitations clause (section 36) of the Constitution.

R – Rules ([paras 28–34])

The Court relied on established jurisprudential standards concerning equality and discrimination. Under section 9(1) of the Constitution, any provision differentiating between people must bear a rational connection to a legitimate governmental purpose. Under section 9(3), discrimination based on sexual orientation is presumed to be unfair unless proven otherwise.

Previous cases, such as Harksen v Lane NO [1998] ZACC 12 and National Coalition for Gay and Lesbian Equality v Minister of Justice [1999] ZACC 15, provided precedents to evaluate the fairness of legislative classifications. The Court affirmed that the differentiation made by sections 14(1)(b) and 14(3)(b) was based on a ground specified in section 9(3) and thus created a presumption of unfairness.

A – Application ([paras 34–38])

In its analysis, the Court noted there was no rational justification for the different ages set for same-sex and opposite-sex acts. It observed that the statutes clearly imposed a higher age for same-sex relations, amounting to discrimination against individuals based on sexual orientation. No evidence was presented to demonstrate that this differentiation served a legitimate government interest.

The Court reinforced the damaging stereotypes that the differential age perpetuated, highlighting that such a distinction demeans members of the LGBTQ+ community by framing their sexual identities as less valuable. The Court concluded that the disproportionate age of consent not only violated section 9 but also conflicted with the broader constitutional values of dignity and equality.

C – Conclusion ([paras 39–45])

The Constitutional Court confirmed the SCA's order, declaring sections 14(1)(b) and 14(3)(b) of the Sexual Offences Act unconstitutional due to the unjustifiable differentiation in ages of consent. It ruled that a uniform age of 16 years was to apply to both same-sex and opposite-sex acts. The Court reasoned that striking down the provisions was essential to ensure that no convictions were invalidated based on the unconstitutional standards, allowing for an equitable remedy while upholding legal certainty in the realm of constitutional rights.

The decision further established an important precedent for future cases regarding discrimination based on sexual orientation, reaffirming the commitment to ensuring equality in the face of historical legislative discrimination. Costs were not awarded against any party, emphasizing the Court's view of this as a significant public interest issue.
