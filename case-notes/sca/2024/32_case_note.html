Cyril and Another v The Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service [2024] ZASCA 32

This case arises from an application for intervention in a review to set aside a magistrate's ruling that admitted certain evidence into a criminal trial ([1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 32

F – Facts

This case arises from an application for intervention in a review to set aside a magistrate's ruling that admitted certain evidence into a criminal trial ([1]). The appellants, Walter Eleazar Cyril and Letisha Cyril (hereafter "the Cyrils"), face criminal charges including 41 counts of fraud and contraventions under the Customs and Excise Act 9 of 1964 (the "CEA") for allegedly diverting goods without payment of duties ([1]). The South African Revenue Service ("SARS"), as the respondent and complainant, sought to intervene in the review application, which the Gauteng Division of the High Court granted ([4]).

The contested evidence was obtained during a search conducted on the premises of Tish Maritime CC, a corporation controlled by the Cyrils, prior to the Constitutional Court’s ruling in Gaertner and Others v Minister of Finance ([2]). That ruling deemed certain provisions of the CEA unconstitutional but suspended its declaration to allow Parliament time to amend the Act ([3]). Following the ruling, the Cyrils contended that the evidence obtained via the unconstitutional searches should be rendered inadmissible ([3]). Despite their challenges, the magistrate ruled the evidence admissible, prompting the Cyrils to initiate a review ([4]).

I – Issues

The key legal question in this case centers on the appealability of the lower court's order granting leave for SARS to intervene in the Cyrils’ review application while the underlying criminal trial was ongoing ([1]). Notably, the Cyrils argued that the ruling adversely affected their rights to a fair trial by allowing a non-party to participate in the review process, hence questioning whether this ruling meets the legal criteria for appealability as established in Zweni v Minister of Law and Order and subsequent cases ([6], [9]) and the relevant provisions of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 ([5], [6]).

Another issue pertains to whether the interests of justice necessitate treating the order as appealable, despite it being interlocutory in nature ([10]).

R – Rules

The South African law regarding appealability is primarily governed by the Zweni criteria, which stipulate that an order must be final, definitive of rights, and should dispose of a substantial portion of the relief sought in main proceedings for it to be appealable. Furthermore, section 17(1) of the Superior Courts Act outlines specific conditions under which leave to appeal may be granted ([5], [6]). Additionally, developments in case law have established that an order may still be appealable if it serves the interests of justice, notwithstanding noncompliance with the Zweni criteria ([7], [8], [10]).

A – Application

The court analyzed the appealability of the decision in light of the Zweni requirements. While the granting of intervention was final and could not be reconsidered by the lower court ([9]), it did not dispose of any portion of the relief claimed in the review application or definitively determine the rights of the parties involved, thereby failing two of the three criteria ([9], [10]).

Furthermore, the court highlighted the judicial reluctance to engage in piecemeal litigation, underscoring that the criminal trial judges have the authority to assess the admissibility of evidence within the context of the ongoing trial, rendering the review application itself potentially an instance of fragmented judicial proceedings ([10]). The court declined to classify the order as appealable on the basis of the interests of justice, distinguishing the precedent set in Nedbank Limited v Survé and other similar cases, explaining that no prejudicial order had been made against the Cyrils ([11], [12], [13]).

The Cyrils’ argument regarding the potential impact of SARS’ intervention on their rights to a fair trial was examined but found lacking. The court concluded that allowing SARS to intervene did not infringe upon the Cyrils' rights to conduct their defense effectively since they still retained the ability to contest the admissibility of evidence in the trial ([16], [17]).

C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that the order granting SARS leave to intervene in the review application did not meet the criteria for appealability outlined in Zweni. Consequently, the appeal was struck from the roll, with the court adjudging that it lacked the jurisdiction to hear the matter due to the absence of a final and definitive effect on the rights of the parties as well as concerns over piecemeal litigation ([18], [19]). Additionally, the court mandated that the costs of the appeal, including those incurred from the application for leave to appeal, be borne by the Cyrils as the losing party, thereby including the costs associated with the employment of two counsel ([19]).

This judgment underscores the commitment of the South African judiciary to uphold procedural integrity and fairness, particularly in matters where the stakes involve rights of individuals facing serious criminal allegations.
