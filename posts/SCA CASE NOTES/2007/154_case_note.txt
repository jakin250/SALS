# Case Note: Cecil Nurse (Pty) Ltd v Nkola [2007] SCA 154 (RSA) 

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts ([paras 1–20])

The appeal arose from a judgment of the Port Elizabeth High Court, which upheld the respondent, Bongile Nkola's, appeal against a prior ruling by the Magistrate's Court in East London. The cases involve a dispute regarding suretyship arising from a credit facility and subsequent debt.

Cecil Nurse (Pty) Ltd (the appellant) is a manufacturer and supplier of office furniture with branches in Port Elizabeth and East London. Bongile Nkola was the sole director of FMMC Holdings (Pty) Ltd ("FMMC"), which purchased goods from the appellant. 

Initially, FMMC purchased furniture on a cash-on-delivery basis from April 2000. Following an agreement to establish a showroom assisted by the appellant, a credit facility of R50,000 was granted, for which Nkola agreed to act as surety. On August 18, 2000, the appellant provided a credit application and a deed of suretyship, both executed and returned by Nkola on August 23, 2000. 

However, complications arose when FMMC accumulated a debt of R145,911.54, leading to a lawsuit initiated by the appellant to recover this amount based on the original suretyship agreement, which Nkola acknowledged. The respondent later contested this, presenting an "amended suretyship" document allegedly negotiated with the appellant, claiming it limited Nkola’s liability.

The key legal contention revolved around the validity of the original and amended suretyship documents, primarily focusing on whether the appellant had accepted any changes and the effectiveness of clauses stipulating that modifications must be made in writing. 

### I – Issues ([paras 5–14])

The primary legal issue was whether the appellant could enforce the original suretyship agreement against Nkola, given his claims about the existence and validity of the amended suretyship document. Specifically, the court needed to determine:

1. Which of the two suretyship agreements—original or amended—was binding on Nkola?
2. Did the appellant's conduct, particularly the correspondence from Lindsay, imply an acceptance of the amended suretyship despite the formal requirements of the original document stating that amendments must be made in writing and agreed to by the creditor?

### R – Rules ([paras 15–18])

The court primarily relied on established contract principles, particularly concerning the binding nature of executed agreements and the necessity for written amendments. Key legal principles highlighted include:

- The requirement that alterations to written contracts must be consented to in writing as outlined in the original suretyship agreement (clause 6) ([para 17]).
- The doctrine of misrepresentation, as articulated in *Smith v Hughes*, emphasizes that parties are bound by their conduct if it creates an expectation in the mind of the other party ([para 15]).
- The party invoking the terms of a contract must bear the burden of proof regarding the contract's terms and its acceptance ([para 14]).

Case law reference included *Sonap Petroleum* for clarifying the test for determining actual versus declared intention in contract formation ([para 15]).

### A – Application ([paras 19–20])

In its analysis, the court examined whether Nkola's actions in presenting the amended document constituted an amendment to the original suretyship. The court concluded that the appellant maintained that Lindsay lacked the authority to accept changes to the suretyship document. Despite Nkola's claims that an agreement had been reached to limit his liability, the court found that no written consent was given, which was a condition for amendment as per the original agreement. 

The appellant's correspondence, particularly Lindsay's letter warning that no order could be processed until the credit application and suretyship were completely executed, supported the conclusion that there was no binding acceptance of any amendments ([para 19]). Nkola's assertion that a valid amendment had been reached was deemed insufficient in the absence of the formal written consent required.

### C – Conclusion ([paras 20])

The Supreme Court of Appeal allowed the appeal, concluding that the original suretyship agreement was binding on Nkola. The court determined that its findings rested on the lack of any evidence demonstrating that the appellant had accepted Nkola's proposed amendments. The judgment of the court below was thus set aside, and the original ruling in favor of the appellant was upheld with costs. 

This case underscores the importance of adherence to formal contract requirements, particularly in relation to suretyships, signalling to parties involved in such agreements that modifications must be compliant with the stipulated formalities to be enforceable.
