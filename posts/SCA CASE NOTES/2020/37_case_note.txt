Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note: Quad Africa Energy (Pty) Ltd v The Sugarless Company (Pty) Ltd and Another ([2020] ZASCA 37)

## F – Facts
The case concerns a trademark dispute between Quad Africa Energy (Pty) Ltd (QAE) and The Sugarless Company (Pty) Ltd (TSC). TSC, an Australian company, applied for trademark registration of the S SUGARLESS logo in South Africa on September 16, 2015, which included a wide range of confectionery products ([para 2]). Subsequently, QAE was appointed the exclusive distributor for TSC's products in South Africa.

On April 16, 2018, QAE notified TSC of its intent to terminate the distribution agreement. Shortly after, TSC learned that QAE had launched a competing confectionery brand, SUGARLEAN, whose packaging closely resembled TSC’s ([para 3]). Despite QAE's claims of transitioning to new non-infringing packaging, TSC expressed continued concern, leading to a series of demands and legal actions starting in July 2018 ([para 5]).

TSC filed a motion seeking various remedies including declarations of trademark infringement, copyright infringement, and passing off, while QAE initiated a counter-application for a disclaimer regarding the exclusive use of the word "sugarless" in TSC’s trademark ([paras 5-8]). The High Court granted several of TSC's requests, which prompted QAE's appeal and TSC's cross-appeal, leading to a review by the Supreme Court of Appeal.

## I – Issues
The core legal questions involved in this appeal include:

1. **Trademark Validity**: Whether the trademark "SUGARLESS" is inherently capable of distinguishing the goods of TSC and whether QAE's use of "SUGARLEAN" infringed on this trademark ([paras 42-44]).
2. **Copyright Infringement**: Whether QAE's packaging constitutes an unauthorized adaptation of TSC’s protected works ([paras 19-31]).
3. **Passing Off**: Whether QAE’s actions constituted unfair competition by misleading consumers regarding the origin of its products ([paras 34-39]).
4. **Counterfeit Goods**: Whether the goods in question can be classified as counterfeit under the Counterfeit Goods Act, considering both trademark and copyright infringement ([paras 48-50]).

## R – Rules
The case relied upon several relevant statutory provisions and judicial precedents:

- **Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 (TMA)**: Particularly, Section 34(1)(a) regarding infringement due to confusing similarity, and Section 15 concerning the endorsement of trade marks with disclaimers ([paras 12, 42]).
- **Copyright Act 98 of 1978 (CRA)**: Sections 7 and 23, which denote the rights of copyright owners over original works and define acts constituting copyright infringement ([paras 20-22]).
- **Case Law**: The court referenced previous cases, such as *Cadbury v Beacon Sweets* for trademark disclaimers, and *Baker v Toad* regarding the concept of passing off and consumer confusion ([paras 16, 34]).

## A – Application
The Court analyzed the evidence relating to each of the issues:

### 1. Trademark Validity
The court determined that "sugarless" is inherently descriptive and therefore incapable of distinguishing TSC's goods as per Section 9 of the TMA. The court found merit in QAE's counter-application for a disclaimer of the exclusive rights related to this descriptor ([paras 12-18]).

### 2. Copyright Infringement
On the copyright front, the court scrutinized the artistic works embodied in the packaging. It ruled that QAE's new packaging did not sufficiently resemble TSC’s original packaging to constitute an infringement, thereby rejecting TSC's claim ([paras 19-33]).

### 3. Passing Off
As for passing off, the court emphasized that consumer confusion must be likely and should be evaluated based on the overall presentation of the products. It found that the differences, particularly in the use of the word "LEAN," diminished potential confusion amongst consumers, which led to the appeal’s success on this issue as well ([paras 34-39]).

### 4. Counterfeit Goods
The court reiterated that the legal definition of counterfeiting requires more than mere trademark or copyright infringement; it requires intent to deceive. As TSC’s claims did not satisfy the criteria for counterfeiting under the CGA, this part of TSC’s appeal was also dismissed ([paras 48-50]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld QAE's appeal, primarily ruling in favor of QAE on all substantive issues, including the counter-application for the disclaimer of the "sugarless" mark ([para 51]). It set aside the High Court’s orders and replaced them with a decision reflecting these conclusions. This ruling not only strengthens the parameters within which trademarks can be protected but also emphasizes the necessity for distinctiveness in trademark registration. The decision highlights the balance courts strive to achieve in protecting intellectual property rights without granting monopolies over descriptive language. Ultimately, the implications of this case reiterate the cautious approach that must be taken in trademark law to ensure fair competition remains paramount.
