# Case Note: Emalahleni Local Municipality v Lehlaka Property Development (Pty) Ltd (600/2022) [2023] ZASCA 138

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The case relates to an appeal by the Emalahleni Local Municipality (the Municipality) against a High Court judgment granting declaratory and interdictory relief to Lehlaka Property Development (Pty) Ltd (Lehlaka) regarding the termination of an electricity supply agreement ([1]–[2]). The background involves the mining village of Rietspruit, initially developed by the Rietspruit Colliery Mine to house miners. Following the mine's closure in 2002, the village was handed over to the municipality and formalized as Rietspruit Township in 2004 ([2]–[4]).

Lehlaka took ownership of several properties, maintaining a consumer agreement with the Municipality for the supply of electricity ([5]–[7]). By February 2020, Lehlaka provided notice to terminate this agreement, leading to ongoing disputes regarding payment for electricity consumed by unlawful occupiers of the properties ([8]–[10]). Despite Lehlaka arguing that it was not responsible for the debts incurred for electricity consumed after termination on 15 May 2020, the Municipality contended that it had discretion in accepting the termination and insisted that a relocation plan for unlawful occupiers be developed first ([12]–[13]).

The High Court ruled in favor of Lehlaka, leading to the Municipality's appeal ([14]–[15]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal questions raised in the appeal include:
1. Whether the High Court erred in dismissing the non-joinder argument concerning the unlawful occupiers ([14]–[15]).
2. Whether the relationship between the Municipality, Lehlaka, and the unlawful occupiers constitutes a "special cluster of relationships" requiring the application of public law principles in the termination of a private contract ([24]–[30]).

### R – Rules
Key legal principles involved include:
1. The nature of joinder under South African law, emphasizing that parties with a direct and substantial interest in litigation must be joined to ensure their rights are protected ([16]–[20]).
2. The distinction between public law obligations imposed on municipalities and private law contracts between entities, which was discussed across various precedents, including *Joseph and Others v City of Johannesburg* ([23]–[24]).
3. Administrative principles established under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) and the Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000, which dictate the Municipality's duties regarding service provision ([25]–[26]).

### A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) conducted an analysis of whether the non-joinder of unlawful occupiers was appropriately handled by the High Court. The SCA reiterated the inherent requirement that no adverse findings should be made against parties not present in the proceedings, establishing that the rights of unlawful occupiers needed examination given their implied interest in municipal services ([16]–[18]). The Court emphasized that principles from *Matjhabeng Local Municipality v Eskom Holdings* should apply, noting the necessity for direct interests in any adjudicated matters ([16]).

The SCA also contended that the special cluster relationship characteristic involves public law obligations, asserting that the unlawful occupiers hold rights extending beyond typical contractual arrangements. Therefore, the Court recognized that the unlawful occupiers’ rights were tied to the broader constitutional obligations borne by the Municipality to provide basic services, implicating PAJA’s provisions ([28]–[32]).

### C – Conclusion
The SCA upheld the appeal, ruling that the High Court had erred in dismissing the non-joinder point, acknowledging that the unlawful occupiers had a significant interest in the ruling ([33]). Consequently, the order granted by the High Court was set aside with costs awarded to the Municipality, who must ensure all necessary parties are appropriately joined in future proceedings to safeguard constitutional rights and obligations ([44]).

The implications of this decision highlight the critical intersection of private and public law in municipal service provision, reinforcing that private landowners are not solely responsible for the obligations associated with unlawful occupiers, thus clarifying the legal framework surrounding municipal duties in South Africa ([44]).
