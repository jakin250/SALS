Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of Bakgaka – Ba – Mothapo Traditional Council v Tshepo Mathule Mothapo & Others

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History
The case concerns a dispute filed by the **Bakgaka – Ba – Mothapo Traditional Council** (“the Council”) and **Kgoshigadi Madipoane Refiloe Maremadi Mothapo** (“Kgoshigadi”) against several respondents, whom they accused of unlawfully selling and allocating residential and business sites on tribal land, specifically portions of **Syferkuil 921 LS** and **Majeebaskraal 1005 LS** ([1]–[2]). The appellants claimed that such actions were contrary to customary law, which stipulates that land held in trust by traditional leaders cannot be sold or allocated without proper authority ([2]). 

Initially, the action was lodged in the Limpopo Division of the High Court, where the respondents challenged the appellants’ **locus standi**. They contended that neither Kgoshigadi nor the Council had the standing to take action against them due to non-compliance with the **Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003** (“the Framework Act”) and the **Limpopo Traditional Leadership and Institutions Act 6 of 2005** ([4]–[5]). The High Court found that while Kgoshigadi did possess requisite standing, the Council itself did not ([5]). 

Dissatisfied with the ruling regarding the Council's standing, the Council applied for leave to appeal, which was granted ([6]–[7]). 

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions
The pertinent issues to be adjudicated by the Supreme Court of Appeal included:

1. Whether the **Bakgaka – Ba – Mothapo Traditional Council** had **locus standi** to institute an action on behalf of the traditional community.
2. Whether Kgoshigadi had locus standi to act despite deriving authority from a Council that lacked standing, and whether the Court’s interpretation of relevant statutory requirements was adequate ([1], [4]-[5], [16]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon
The legal framework governing traditional leadership and the role of traditional councils is encapsulated in both the **Framework Act** and the **Limpopo Traditional Leadership Act**. Key provisions relevant to this case include:

- **Section 4 of the Limpopo Traditional Leadership Act** stipulates the composition and authority necessary for the recognition of a Traditional Council, mandating compliance with procedures prescribed therein ([8]–[9]).
- **Section 33(5)** of the Limpopo Act provides that Traditional Councils existing before the Act's commencement had to comply with its provisions within a specified timeframe ([7]).
- The **judgment of the Constitutional Court** in **Pilane and another v Pilane and another** asserts the necessity of adherence to statutory requirements as part of lawful governance of traditional communities ([14]).
- The **Sigcau v Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs** ruling emphasizes the statutory framework over customary practices regarding traditional leadership disputes ([15]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law
The Court undertook a meticulous analysis to determine "locus standi" based on statutory compliance. The findings elucidated that the Traditional Council had failed to adhere to the stipulated requirements for recognition, particularly non-compliance with gender representation and membership criteria, leading to the determination that it lacked the necessary standing to proceed with the case ([10]–[12]).

The Court observed that the Council did not constitute a legally recognized entity according to the Framework Act and the Limpopo Act, and thus could not bring action against the respondents ([13]). The Court also highlighted the implications of a lack of compliance, stating: "The provisions regulating the composition and recognition of traditional councils are clear, unambiguous, and consistent," thus emphasizing the statutory necessity for governance ([12]).

In considering Kgoshigadi's locus standi, the Court ruled that since her authority was derived explicitly from the Council, which itself was found to lack standing, her locus standi could not be successfully argued. The Court stated that it was perplexing for the High Court to grant Kgoshigadi standing when her authority came from a body lacking legitimacy ([16]) . 

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming the High Court's ruling that the **Bakgaka – Ba – Mothapo Traditional Council** did not possess the required locus standi to institute the action ([17]). The Court reinforced the legal principle that adherence to statutory structures governing traditional authority is imperative, effectively nullifying any attempts to derive authority solely from customary law in a manner that circumvents established legal frameworks. 

This judgment underscores the critical importance of compliance with legal provisions governing traditional councils, thereby providing clarity on the jurisdictional boundaries within which traditional leadership must operate. It conveys a clear message that traditional councils must remedy deficiencies in their formation according to statutory requirements if they wish to maintain the legitimacy necessary to institute legal proceedings ([12]–[17]). 

This decision may have significant future implications for how traditional councils operate in South Africa and their interactions with formal legal systems, ensuring that traditional governance is aligned with contemporary legal standards.
