Case Note: CASE NUMBER: 232/95
Source Folder: 1996

**Case Note: Luster Products Inc v Magic Style Sales CC**

**FIRAC Method**

### F – Facts
Luster Products Incorporated (Appellant) is a corporation registered in Illinois, USA, and the owner of South African Trade Mark 84/6404, registered on 18 July 1984 in Class 3 for specific hair care and cosmetic products ([para 2]). Since 1980, Luster has marketed its products in South Africa, relying on its trademark, initially registered in the U.S. and then locally in 1984; its brand primarily services the black community ([para 2]). 

Magic Style Sales CC (Respondent), a South African close corporation established in 1988, began marketing hair care products similar to Luster's. Tensions arose when Luster discovered that Magic Style was using a mark it believed infringed its registered rights, which led to a letter of demand on 9 December 1990, requesting Magic Style to cease the infringing actions ([para 3]).

After the Respondent refused the demands, Luster sought an interdict against the Respondent's use of the mark on 9 December 1992. The Respondent countered with an application filed on 9 February 1993 for the expungement of Luster's mark and an order directing a disclaimer on the exclusive use right to "S-CURL" ([para 4]). The counter-application prompted the joinder of the Registrar of Trade Marks, who remained neutral throughout the proceedings ([para 4]). The main application sought an interdict against infringement, and the learned judge of the lower court ruled against Luster on this issue while granting a disclaimer pursuant to the Respondent's application ([para 5]).

### I – Issues
The core legal issues revolve around the validity of Luster's trade mark and whether the Respondent's actions constituted infringement under sections 44 and 16 of the Trade Marks Act 62 of 1963 ([para 16]). Key questions include:
1. Whether the Respondent's use of the mark "S-CURL" infringes upon Luster's registered trademark and whether it is likely to cause confusion ([para 30]).
2. The legitimacy of the Respondent's counter-application seeking to expunge Luster's trademark ([para 16]).
3. The application and interpretation of sections 16, 33, and 42 of the Trade Marks Act regarding the likelihood of deception or confusion ([para 15]).

### R – Rules
The applicable legal framework includes:
- **Trade Marks Act 62 of 1963**:
  - **Section 16**: Prohibits registration of marks likely to deceive or cause confusion ([para 15]).
  - **Section 33(1)**: Provides for application to court for expungement based on insufficient grounds for existing entries ([para 15]).
  - **Section 42**: Validates marks after seven years of registration unless proven fraudulent or against sections 16 or 41 ([para 15]).
  - **Section 44**: Defines trade mark infringement, focusing on unauthorized use that is likely to confuse consumers ([para 30]).

### A – Application
The Court meticulously analyzed the use of the mark "S-CURL" and its resemblance to Luster's registered trademark. The Respondent used a bold "S" alongside the word "curl" in a manner that the Court deemed to likely deceive or confuse consumers ([para 30]). The Court found that the Respondent's depiction of "S-CURL" was in direct violation of Luster's registered rights under section 44(1). 

Furthermore, the Court arrived at the conclusion that the Respondent's post-registration evidence to argue for expungement based on “generic” use was insufficient, as any such transformation of the term’s distinctive character arose after Luster’s mark registration, aligning with previous findings in cases such as *Adcock-Ingram Laboratories Ltd v SA Druggists Ltd* ([para 16]), which limited the weight of post-registration evidence unless proved blameworthy.

The argument concerning the disclaimer was evaluated based on section 18 of the Trade Marks Act, which allows for disclaimers but was ultimately rejected as inappropriate by the Court, establishing that such disclaimers would negate the protection offered by the trademark registrations ([paras 28-29]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of South Africa upheld Luster’s appeal against the lower court’s decision to deny the interdict and affirm the entry of the disclaimer. The orders from the lower court regarding the expungement of Luster's trademark and the grant of a disclaimer were set aside. The ruling reinforced the exclusive rights of trademark holders, indicating that a trademark duly registered would be assumed valid after seven years, barring specific exceptions such as fraud or effective prior common use that could have prevented registration in the first place ([para 35]). 

The implications of this decision are substantial for trademark law in South Africa, stressing that unauthorized use of a registered mark, even when defended on grounds of descriptive or generic claims, may still result in infringement, particularly where the mark retains its distinctiveness and the alleged infringer fails to demonstrate blameworthiness in the trademark's loss of distinctiveness ([paras 30-35]).  

This case reaffirms the protective framework surrounding trademarks and the judicial reluctance to permit the dilution of a brand's goodwill through unauthorized use, thus strengthening consumers’ recognition and the trust associated with trademarked goods in the marketplace.
