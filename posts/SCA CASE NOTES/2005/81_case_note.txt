# Case Note: FIRAC Method

**Case Title:** Dr Henk Douw Louwrens v James Peter Oldwage  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Case No:** 181/2004  
**Judgment Delivered:** 21 September 2005  
**Coram:** Mpati DP, Streich, Mthiyane, Lewis, Ponnan JJA

---

## F - Facts

The present appeal revolves around allegations of medical negligence against Dr Henk Douw Louwrens (the appellant), a vascular surgeon, by his patient (the respondent), James Peter Oldwage. The case arose from the events following the respondent's severe pain in his right leg, prompting consultations with his general practitioner, Dr George Simons, and subsequently the appellant ([1]–[3]).

On June 5, 2000, the respondent experienced intense pain in his right leg and was referred to the appellant, who, upon examination, suspected poor blood flow due to chronic arterial blockage. An angiogram conducted on June 7, 2000, confirmed severe ischaemia, leading to urgent surgical intervention through an iliac bi-femoral bypass performed on June 8, 2000. Despite the surgery, the respondent reported persistent pain, and further consultation revealed additional problems related to his spinal condition, which necessitated later surgery ([2], [3]).

The central factual dispute at trial concerned whether the symptoms presented to the appellant were of vascular or neurological origin. The respondent contended that he had suffered from lower back pain resulting from a fall, while the appellant and Dr Simons asserted that the pain was strictly vascular, rooted in ischaemia ([4], [6]).

## I - Issues

The appellate court was tasked with determining several interrelated issues ([5]):
1. Did the appellant misdiagnose the respondent's condition as primarily vascular rather than neurological?
2. Did the appellant adhere to the standard of care expected of a vascular surgeon in light of the symptoms presented?
3. Was informed consent properly obtained for the surgical intervention performed?
4. Is the current condition of claudication in the respondent's left leg attributable to the surgical intervention conducted by the appellant?

## R - Rules

To resolve the above issues, the court relied on various legal precedents and medical principles. A pivotal case referenced was *Mitchell v Dixon*, which articulated that a practitioner could only be deemed negligent if their diagnosis is palpably wrong, thus demonstrating a lack of reasonable skill and care ([19]). 

The concept of informed consent required that a patient understands the nature and risks of the proposed treatment. This standard was discussed in *Castell v De Greef*, which specified that patients must be aware of the extent of risks involved ([22]).

Regarding expert medical testimony, the court underscored the significance of credibility assessments in cases with conflicting medical opinions. The principle from *Michael v Linksfield Park Clinic* highlighted that judicial determinations must engage with expert testimony critically and logically ([27]).

## A - Application

In applying the established rules, the appellate court engaged in a detailed reassessment of the evidence presented. The court found that the trial judge had not adequately evaluated the credibility of witnesses, leading to a misapprehension of the facts ([15]). The assessment pointed to multiple inconsistencies in the respondent's accounts, contrasted with medical evidence from the appellant and Dr Simons, which corroborated the existence of a vascular issue ([16]–[19]).

The discussion regarding informed consent revealed that the distinction between the iliac bi-femoral bypass and the 'fem-fem' procedure was largely semantic and that the respondent was adequately informed about the surgery and its necessity ([21]). Further, the risk associated with the surgical procedure was assessed using expert testimonies; it was concluded that the likelihood of complications like 'steal syndrome' was negligible, aligning with the standard practices of medical professionals ([22]–[25]).

The appeal highlighted that the appellant acted within the bounds of accepted medical standards, as corroborated by expert opinions which suggested the selected surgical procedure was appropriate based on the presenting symptoms. The comprehensive evidence supported a finding of no misdiagnosis or negligence on the part of the appellant ([20], [24]).

## C - Conclusion

The court overturned the original decision of the trial court, concluding that the allegations of negligence against Dr Louwrens were unfounded. The ruling emphasized the need for a careful reconsideration of witness credibility and factual context when attributing fault in medical negligence cases.

The appeal was upheld, and the judgment of the court a quo was set aside, leading to a dismissal of the plaintiff's action. The case underscores the critical importance of precise medical evaluation, effective patient communication, and adherence to professional standards in medical practice ([28]).
