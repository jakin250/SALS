Casino Association of South Africa and Others v Member of the Executive Council for Economic Development Environment Conservation and Tourism and Others

Case Title: Casino Association of South Africa and Others v Member of the Executive Council for Economic Development, Environment, Conservation and Tourism and Others Neutral Citation: [2023] ZACC 39 Case No: CCT 322/22 Date Heard: 11 May 2023 Date Decided: 29 November 2023...

Constitutional Court 2023 [2023] ZACC 39 Case No: CCT 322/22 Date Heard: 11

Case Title: Casino Association of South Africa and Others v Member of the Executive Council for Economic Development, Environment, Conservation and Tourism and Others
Neutral Citation: [2023] ZACC 39
Case No: CCT 322/22
Date Heard: 11 May 2023
Date Decided: 29 November 2023
Coram: Zondo CJ, Kollapen J, Madlanga J, Majiedt J, Makgoka AJ, Potterill AJ, Rogers J, Theron J and Van Zyl AJ
Judgement: Makgoka AJ (unanimous)

F – Facts

The case arose from an application for confirmation of an order of constitutional invalidity issued by the High Court of South Africa, North West Division ([1]). The High Court declared sections 84(1)(e), 87(1)(a), and 87(3) of the North West Gambling Act 2 of 2001 (NW Gambling Act) unconstitutional and invalid, following challenges from the Casino Association of South Africa (CASA) and its members, Peermont Global (North West) (Pty) Limited and Sun International (South Africa) Limited ([1–3]). The controversy stemmed from an amendment to regulation 73(1) of the North West Gambling Regulations 2002, which significantly increased gambling levies payable by casino operators in the North West Province ([5–10]).

Regulation 73(1) was initially framed to impose levies based on gross gaming revenue. However, the amendment shifted the thresholds and rates, thereby substantially raising the fees for casino operators ([5–6]). The amendment was promulgated by the MEC for Tourism on 24 January 2020, following a consultation process initiated in 2018 ([9–10]).

CASA, representing 36 out of 38 licensed casinos in South Africa, alongside Peermont and Sun International, argued that the amendment unlawfully imposed taxes without legislative authority ([3–4]). The respondents included various state officials associated with economic development and finance, with the Minister of Finance not participating in the proceedings ([4]).

I – Issues

The central legal questions revolved around whether:

  1. The empowering provisions of the NW Gambling Act allowed the imposition of taxes or levies contrary to section 228(1) of the Constitution ([34]).
  2. The High Court's declaration of constitutional invalidity should be confirmed by the Constitutional Court ([34]).
  3. The amendment constituted an unconstitutional delegation of legislative power from the Legislature to the Executive ([34]).
  4. The levy’s imposition and the MEC's decision were reviewable under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA) or the principle of legality ([34]).
  5. The applicants were entitled to repayment of excessive levies paid in compliance with the unlawful regulations ([34]).

R – Rules

The court referred primarily to the following legal standards:

  1. Section 228(1)(a) of the Constitution: Grants provincial legislatures the authority to impose taxes, levies, and duties, contingent on compliance with national legislative frameworks ([11]).
  2. Provincial Tax Regulation Process Act 53 of 2001: Implementing section 228(2)(b), this Act delineates the procedures required for the imposition of provincial taxes ([12]).
  3. Case Law Precedents:
  • Shuttleworth v South African Reserve Bank: Established the "dominant purpose test" to determine whether a charge is classified as a tax or regulatory fee ([24]).
  • Fedsure Life Assurance Ltd v Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council: Reiterated that taxation powers reside with democratically elected bodies, emphasizing that the Executive cannot independently impose taxes ([42]).
  • Applicable jurisprudence from Canada: Examined how charges align with regulatory schemes to ascertain their true nature ([50]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court began its analysis by affirming the High Court's finding that the impugned provisions of the NW Gambling Act, including the regulation imposing substantial gambling levies, violated section 228(1) of the Constitution. The MEC’s authority to levies taxes was deemed unconstitutional since it usurped legislative powers reserved for provincial legislatures ([65–66]).

The court applied the dominant purpose test from Shuttleworth, assessing whether the levies were primarily intended to raise revenue rather than regulate gambling activities. The analysis revealed that the gambling levies were fundamentally a means to raise funds for the provincial treasury rather than serving a regulatory purpose ([62–64]).

In addressing the respondents' contention that the provisions facilitated a regulatory framework, the court distinguished between revenue-generating activities and genuine regulatory impositions, concluding that the levies lacked the necessary nexus to a regulatory scheme outlined in the NW Gambling Act ([55–61]).

Consequently, the court ruled that the amendment was invalid and not salvaged by any notion of regulatory authority, warranting affirmation of the High Court’s order ([66]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court's order, holding that sections 84(1)(e), 87(1)(a), and 87(3) of the NW Gambling Act unlawfully empowered the MEC to impose a gambling levy classified as a tax under section 228(1)(a) of the Constitution ([71, 2]). The ruling emphasized the imperative of legislative integrity, underscoring the principle that taxing authority rests exclusively with elected representatives in a democratic state ([43]).

The court also sanctioned the applicants' right to repayment for the gambling levies paid under protest, establishing a just and equitable remedy based on the principle of condictio indebiti. This remedy mandates the return of any payments made that exceeded the legally mandated levies prior to the amendment ([66–68]).

The decision thus reinforces constitutional norms regarding the separation of powers, affirming the Legislature's exclusive role in taxation and ensuring accountability within the provincial governance framework. The order highlights the need for stringent adherence to procedural requirements in tax imposition and the importance of lawful governance in the exercise of executive authority.
