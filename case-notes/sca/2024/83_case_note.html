Goedverwachting Farm (Pty) Ltd v Roux and Others (641/2023)

Citation: Goedverwachting Farm (Pty) Ltd v Roux and Others (641/2023) [2024] ZASCA 83 (31 May 2024)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 83 (31 May 2024) Court: The Supreme Court of

Citation: Goedverwachting Farm (Pty) Ltd v Roux and Others (641/2023) [2024] ZASCA 83 (31 May 2024)

Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

Judges: Nicholls, Meyer, Matojane JJA, Coppin and Mbhele AJJA

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–9])

This case arises from an appeal against a decision of the Land Claims Court (LCC), which dismissed an eviction application by Goedverwachting Farm (Pty) Ltd (the appellant) against Mr. Adriaan Johannes Roux and any unlawful occupiers (the respondents) of a property situated on portion 17 of the farm Goedverwachting in Gauteng Province ([para 1]). Goedverwachting is the registered owner of the property, having acquired it from Barend Frederik Keet, the previous owner, on 9 December 2020 ([para 7]).

The respondents have occupied the farm since 2017 under a conditional consent granted by Mr Keet, which was later contested and ultimately revoked due to a breach of the sale agreement with another entity, Deneys Swiss Dairy (Pty) Ltd ([paras 5–6]). After the property was transferred to the appellant in June 2021, the respondents refused to vacate, leading to the appellant's eviction application filed on 15 September 2022 ([paras 8]).

The legal framework governing this eviction application is the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA), which defines an “occupier” and details the grounds upon which an occupant's rights can be terminated ([para 4]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 10–22])

The central issue is whether Mr. Roux falls within the legal definition of an “occupier” under ESTA, which is critical for determining the jurisdiction of the LCC. This hinges on whether he was engaged in commercial farming on the land, a factor which would exclude him from ESTA's definition of an occupier ([para 2]). The LCC's reliance on the probation officer's report—which indicated Mr. Roux was conducting commercial farming—without sufficient evidence from the parties, raises significant questions about the admissibility of such evidence and the division of responsibilities between the court’s discretion and the report’s findings ([paras 4, 19]).

Furthermore, the respondents claim an indigenous title to the land through customary rights, complicating the issue of whether they have lawful occupancy or have rights to contest the eviction ([paras 10–12]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 23–25])

Key statutory provisions examined include:

  • Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA), particularly the definitions of “occupier” and the requirements for eviction outlined in sections 1 and 9, which establish the criteria that must be satisfied for lawful evictions ([para 2] and [para 19]).
  • Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996, which mandates that evictions must consider the constitutional rights of affected persons, as well as the just and equitable nature of the eviction ([para 15]).
  • Case law such as Valley Packers Co-operative v Dietloff and Nederburg Wines (Pty) Ltd v Nero, which clarify the necessity of considering both factual and legal sufficiency when determining occupancy ([para 15]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 17–22])

In adjudicating the appeal, the Supreme Court scrutinized the evidence presented, especially the reliance on the probation officer's report, which the LCC used to determine that Mr. Roux was engaged in commercial farming. The SCA found that the report was not evidence per se because it was not made under oath and composed factual inaccuracies, such as misrepresentations about Mr. Roux’s status and lack of documentation supporting a successful business operation ([para 19]).

The court emphasized that the LCC had overstepped its jurisdiction by accepting the probation report at face value without requiring adequate proof of the commercial farming allegation—this was a pivotal point, as the nature and scale of Mr. Roux's activities were not substantiated by direct evidence during the proceedings ([paras 20–21]).

Moreover, the appellants’ claims to aboriginal title brought forth by the respondents were not adequately substantiated, leading the court to issue an eviction order based on a failure to provide any persuasive and comprehensive challenge to the appellant’s claims ([paras 10-12]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 24–25])

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the LCC erred in its finding that the respondents were excluded from ESTA’s definition of occupiers based solely on the probation officer’s report, which the court deemed inadequate for establishing the facts necessary for such a conclusion ([para 22]). The SCA overturned the LCC’s decision, granting an eviction order. This ruling underscored the need for both parties to properly define their disputes within the legal framework and emphasized the judiciary’s role in ensuring decisions are founded on solid evidentiary bases.

The implications of this judgment extend beyond the immediate parties, reaffirming the importance of a rigorous examination of the evidence in eviction proceedings, particularly where claims of commercial activities are asserted without solid backing, and the necessity for courts to remain vigilant against infringing on rightful occupancy without proper cause. The court also held that no costs order was warranted due to the unique nature of land claims involving vulnerable persons ([paras 23–25]).

This case contributes to the ongoing discourse surrounding land tenure rights, particularly the intersection of statutory regulations under ESTA with customary land rights and the broader implications for land reform in South Africa.
