Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# Case Note: City of Johannesburg v Dladla (403/15) [2016] ZASCA 66

## FIRAC Structure

### F – Facts

The case involves an appeal by the City of Johannesburg (the Appellant) against a ruling by the Gauteng Local Division of the High Court (the court a quo) regarding the “Ekuthuleni Overnight/Decant Shelter House Rules” ([1]). The respondents, Ellen Dladla and 33 others (the First and Second to 34th Respondents), were temporary residents at the Ekuthuleni Shelter, which provided emergency accommodation after they were evicted from a dilapidated building known as Saratoga. Their eviction was ordered in a preceding case referred to as "Blue Moonlight" ([2]).

The Ekuthuleni Shelter, operated by Metropolitan Evangelical Services (MES), had specific rules governing entry and egress, particularly requiring residents to enter by 20h00 and to vacate by certain times ([8]). Rules 3 and 4, which the court a quo found to infringe upon residents' constitutional rights, were challenged on the grounds that they violated their rights to dignity, freedom, and privacy as enshrined in sections 10, 12, and 14 of the South African Constitution. The court a quo had issued an order that prevented the City and MES from enforcing these rules against the respondents ([1]–[3]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issues pertain to whether the rules of the Ekuthuleni Shelter constitute an unjustifiable infringement on the residents' constitutional rights, thereby raising questions about the balance between individual rights and the operational necessities of a shelter meant for emergency temporary accommodation ([1], [8], [23]). Specifically, the issues include:

1. Do the rules limiting access and requiring residents to vacate the premises infringe upon the respondents' rights to dignity and privacy?
2. Is there a distinction between the rights applicable in permanent housing situations versus those in temporary emergency accommodations that should be considered?

### R – Rules

The legal authority for evaluating the constitutionality of the rules lies primarily within the framework provided by the South African Constitution, specifically the rights enshrined in:

- Section 10: Right to human dignity.
- Section 12: Right to freedom and security of the person.
- Section 14: Right to privacy.
- Section 36: Limitations clause, which allows for the lawful limitation of rights under specific reasonable and justifiable circumstances.

Key case precedents include:

- **Bernstein & others v Bester & others NNO**: The court emphasized a high level of protection for individual dignity and privacy ([16]).
- **Dawood & another v Minister of Home Affairs**: Recognized cohabitation as central to the dignity inherent in marriage ([17]).
- **Blue Moonlight Properties Case**: Established that rights in a situation of emergency accommodation could differ from those in permanent housing ([19]).

### A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal revisited the context in which the Eviction and housing issues arose, recognizing that while residents have rights to dignity and privacy, these need to be interpreted within the confines of the temporary shelter situation. The court emphasized that emergency accommodation inevitably entails limitations on rights due to operational constraints ([19], [20]).

In assessing whether the limitations imposed by the shelter rules were reasonable, the court acknowledged that:

- The core purpose of the rules was to ensure safety, encourage independence, and manage limited resources effectively ([7], [8], [23]).
- The communal nature of the shelter required that some reasonable compromises be made regarding privacy and familial cohabitation ([10]).
- Given the stark realities of housing shortages and the need for emergency accommodations, the court underscored the difference between being housed temporarily during a crisis versus achieving the ideal of permanent housing ([15], [20]).

The court systematically dismantled the reasoning put forth by the court a quo, which had found the rules unconstitutional, highlighting that the respondents did not prove how the temporary nature of their accommodation should equate their rights with those of permanent residents ([24]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal by the City of Johannesburg, concluding that the rules of the Ekuthuleni Shelter did not unjustifiably infringe on the constitutional rights of the residents as claimed in the lower court. The ruling underscored the importance of recognizing the differentiated legal and social contexts governing temporary accommodations versus permanent residences ([1], [3], [25]).

The implications of this decision are significant for local authorities in South Africa when developing and implementing housing policies and regulations for temporary accommodations, affirming that while constitutional rights are fundamental, they may be reasonably limited in the context of emergency shelters ([24], [25]). The court’s decision illustrates a nuanced understanding of constitutional rights, balancing the essential need for dignity and privacy against the overlay of practical realities faced by municipalities in emergency situations. The court’s ruling may impact future policies related to temporary housing assistance and the associated rights of individuals who find themselves in similar situations of dispossession.
