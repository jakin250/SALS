Supreme Court of Appeal

African Transformation Movement v Speaker of the National Assembly and Others [2021] ZASCA 164

The case involves an appeal by the African Transformation Movement (ATM) against the Speaker of the National Assembly, along with other respondents including the President of South Africa and several political parties ([1]-[2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 164

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case involves an appeal by the African Transformation Movement (ATM) against the Speaker of the National Assembly, along with other respondents including the President of South Africa and several political parties ([1]-[2]). The ATM tabled a motion of no confidence against President Cyril Ramaphosa on 11 February 2020, citing grievances such as the collapse of State Owned Entities and misleading Parliament regarding load-shedding ([1]). Following the tabling of the motion, on 24 February 2020, the ATM requested that the vote be conducted by secret ballot, citing precedents and principles from a previous case decided by the Constitutional Court—United Democratic Movement v Speaker and Others ([2]). They argued that the decision on whether the ballot should be secret must be guided by rationality and accountability factors, including the atmosphere in which the vote would take place ([2]).

On 26 November 2020, after an initial delay, the Speaker announced the motion would be debated on 3 December 2021, but subsequently declined the ATM's request for a secret ballot in a letter dated 5 March 2020, claiming that the ATM had not provided sufficient evidence of a "toxified" atmosphere ([3]). Following her refusal, the ATM sought urgent relief from the High Court, which was ultimately dismissed with costs on 26 March 2021 ([5]). The appeal was subsequently lodged by the ATM with leave granted by the High Court ([6]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue in this case is whether the Speaker of the National Assembly exercised her discretion appropriately in declining the ATM's request for a secret ballot for the motion of no confidence against the President ([6]-[9]). Specifically, the court was tasked with determining if there was a sound basis for the Speaker’s decision and whether it adhered to the principles of rationality required in the exercise of her official duties ([9]-[11]). The case also examined the question of whether the requesting party (the ATM) had any onus to show the necessity for a secret ballot and, if not, whether this misunderstanding constituted a material irrationality ([8]-[20]).

R – Rules

The court employed principles derived from previous judicial decisions, most notably the UDM case and Albutt v Centre for the Study of Violence and Reconciliation, which emphasized that a rationality review is necessary to ensure decisions made by public officials are consistent with constitutional principles ([6]-[11]).

  1. Rationality Requirement: The decision of the Speaker must be rationally connected to the objective sought to be achieved, which in this instance was ensuring a proper oversight mechanism for Parliament ([6],[14]).
  2. No Onus on Requesting Party: The court held that a party requesting a secret ballot does not bear an onus to prove the need for said procedure, reaffirming the Speaker's discretionary power ([8],[21]).
  3. Constitutional Accountability: Members of Parliament have a constitutional duty to act in the public interest, which should guide procedures regarding voting, especially in sensitive situations like a motion of no confidence ([15]-[16]).

A – Application

In applying these rules to the facts of the case, the court found that the Speaker failed to properly understand her discretionary authority. Specifically, her requirement that the ATM provide "compelling reasons" for a secret ballot was fundamentally flawed as it misconstrued the nature of her decision-making power ([20]-[22]). The court emphasized that the Speaker should have considered whether the secret ballot would facilitate effective oversight and engagement of Members of Parliament in a politically charged climate, rather than defaulting to the notion that the ATM needed to substantiate its request with evidence of a toxic environment ([20]).

Moreover, the decision was vitiated by irrationality because the Speaker did not approach the matter as one requiring a fresh consideration based on the circumstances at hand. By imposing an incorrect burden on the ATM, she limited her own discretion unjustifiably, suggesting that absent evidence of a compromised voting environment, a secret ballot would not be warranted. This demonstrated a misunderstanding of both the context and importance of effective parliamentary oversight mechanisms ([21]-[22]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the ATM's appeal, concluding that the Speaker's refusal to conduct the no confidence vote by secret ballot was reviewable under the principles of rationality. The court set aside the High Court's decision and ordered a fresh assessment by the Speaker of the ATM's request, emphasizing the importance of proper discretion in parliamentary procedures ([24]). The implications of the ruling highlight the necessity for Parliament's leadership to exercise discretion grounded in accountability, particularly in situations that could affect governance such as a motion of no confidence. The case serves as a critical affirmation of the principles governing legislative processes and the types of scrutiny required to ensure the integrity of parliamentary democracy in South Africa.

This ruling signifies not merely a procedural victory for the ATM but underscores the broader constitutional mandate that requires the interplay of accountability and rationality in parliamentary decision-making processes.
