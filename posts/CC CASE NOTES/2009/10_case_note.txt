Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2009

# FIRAC Case Note: Netherburn Engineering CC t/a Netherburn Ceramics v Mudau N.O. & Others

## F – Facts
The case originated from the dismissal of Ms Jane Moabelo on 26 October 1998 by Netherburn Engineering CC (the applicant). Following her dismissal, Moabelo contested the fairness of the dismissal by referring the matter to the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA), with the arbitration hearing set for 17 May 2000. During the proceedings, Moabelo was represented by a trade union official. The applicant sought to have its own legal representation, which was objected to under section 140(1) of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995. The CCMA commissioner, Mr Mudau, denied this request, leading the applicant to withdraw from the arbitration. Subsequently, the CCMA found in favour of Moabelo, ruling that her dismissal was unfair, mandating reinstatement and compensation.

Following this, the applicant sought to review the award in the Labour Court, alleging that the commissioner’s refusal of legal representation was irrational, biased, and violated the applicant’s constitutional rights. The Labour Court, in its judgment on 31 August 2003, upheld the commissioner’s decision but identified a misdirection regarding the denial of postponement for legal preparation.

The applicant subsequently appealed to the Labour Appeal Court, which dismissed its constitutional arguments regarding legal representation in a judgment delivered on 5 December 2008. The case ultimately reached the Constitutional Court, where the applicant sought leave to appeal against the Labour Appeal Court's decision, specifically focusing on the constitutionality of section 140(1) and its relevance given that it had been repealed.

## I – Issues
The principal legal question arose from the constitutionality of section 140(1) of the Labour Relations Act concerning legal representation in CCMA arbitration proceedings ([para 10]). Additionally, the court had to consider whether it was in the interests of justice to adjudicate the appeal, given the significant lapse of time since Moabelo's dismissal and the repeal of the contested provision ([para 9]).

## R – Rules
**Legal Framework:**
1. **Section 140(1) of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995** - governs the rights of parties concerning legal representation in arbitration hearings. It allows for legal representation only with the commissioner's consent or under specific circumstances ([para 2]).
2. **Rule 25(1) of the CCMA Rules** - While replacing the repealed section 140(1), it addresses representation objections but does not carry identical provisions ([para 4]).
3. **Section 34 of the Constitution** - asserts that everyone has the right to have disputes resolved in a fair public hearing, raising the question of whether this right extends to legal representation in modern tribunals around unfair dismissals ([para 6]).

The judgments from the Labour Court and Labour Appeal Court elaborated on these legal provisions, particularly assessing the relationship between statutory frameworks and constitutional rights concerning legal representation.

## A – Application
The Constitutional Court began its analysis by stating that the appeal sought to question the constitutionality of a provision (section 140(1)) that has not been in effect for several years ([para 10]). The court inferred that it was unlikely that such a determination would have practical implications due to the distance in time since the provision's repeal. The Court noted the potential inadequacy of the current CCMA Rules (specifically Rule 25(1)) to provide a direct comparison or remedy ([para 10]).

Furthermore, the Court highlighted that over a decade had passed since the dismissal, raising concerns about whether a live dispute still existed between the parties ([para 11]). It found that neither Moabelo nor her union had sought representation in the Labour Appeal Court, underlining the probable resolution of the matter without the need for constitutional clarification regarding section 140(1) ([para 11]). The suggestion was made that if Moabelo wished to pursue her claims anew, she could approach the CCMA for arbitration based on the Labour Court’s earlier ruling ([para 11]).

Although the Court expressed concern about the lengthy process of resolving the unfair dismissal case, it ultimately decided that it would not serve any meaningful judicial purpose to delve into the constitutionality of section 140(1) due to the probable absence of an active dispute ([paras 12–13]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court concluded that the application for leave to appeal was dismissed. The Court found that determining the constitutional status of section 140(1) was not warranted in the present circumstances, primarily because the provision was no longer in effect, and no active dispute was present between the parties ([paras 10–14]). The implications of this decision indicate a judicial tendency to prioritize relevant, live disputes in constitutional adjudication, while also recognizing the potential impact of procedural delays on justice and the handling of labour disputes in South Africa.

Overall, while the Constitutional Court refrained from addressing the broader constitutional questions regarding legal representation in AMC arbitration, it underscored the need for timely resolutions in employment disputes to maintain justice and fairness in the workplace ([paras 12–13]).
