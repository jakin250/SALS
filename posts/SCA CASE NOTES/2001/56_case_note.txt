# FIRAC Case Note: Cape Metropolitan Council v Metro Inspection Services

## F – Facts

The case concerns a dispute between the Cape Metropolitan Council (the Appellant) and Metro Inspection Services (Western Cape) CC (the First Respondent), among others. The Appellant, established as a transitional metropolitan council under the Local Government Transition Act 209 of 1993, entered a contract with the First Respondent for levy collection services. In late 1998, after internal investigations revealed alleged fraudulent claims by the First Respondent, the Appellant summarily terminated their contract, claiming a material breach due to substantial fraudulent claims ([1]–[8]).

The First Respondent applied to the court, asserting that the cancellation of the contract constituted "administrative action" under section 33 of the South African Constitution, thus entitling it to procedural fairness and reasons for the Appellant's decision. The Appellant countered that its termination did not constitute administrative action, asserting it had acted lawfully in canceling the contract based on the alleged fraud ([9]).

The court a quo sided with the First Respondent, declaring the termination unlawful and ordering the Appellant to disclose documents related to the alleged fraud ([1]–[10]). The implications of this case raise significant questions regarding the classification of administrative actions within the context of public contracts.

## I – Issues

1. **Did the Appellant's cancellation of the First Respondent's contract constitute "administrative action" under section 33 of the Constitution, thereby requiring adherence to principles of procedural fairness?** ([1]).
   
2. **Was the First Respondent entitled to access to information under section 32 of the Constitution, necessary for the protection of its rights?** ([22]–[29]).

## R – Rules

### Legislative Framework:

1. **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa Act 108 of 1996**:
   - **Section 33** provides the right to lawful and procedurally fair administrative action when a person's rights are affected ([16]).
   - **Section 32** ensures the right of access to information held by any organ of state, facilitating the exercise or protection of rights ([26]).

### Precedents:

1. **Administrator, Transvaal v Zenzile**: The court emphasized that administrative decisions made by public bodies that involve public power require adherence to fair procedures ([10]).
   
2. **Pharmaceutical Manufacturers Association of SA v President of RSA**: Reinforced that the category of 'administrative action' is broad in scope and tied to the performance of public authority duties ([18]).

3. **Ramburan v Minister of Housing**: Established that decisions impacting 'rights' can be classified as administrative actions and thus invoke the audi alteram partem rule ([15]).

## A – Application

**Issue of Administrative Action**: The Appellant argued that its cancellation was based on the terms of the contract rather than public law, thus not constituting administrative action. The court summarized that the nature of the power exercised is pivotal. Since the decision stemmed from common law rather than public duty, the Appellant did not exercise a public power but acted in its capacity as a contracting party ([18]).

The court a quo referenced the influence exerted by competitors on the Appellant as a factor enhancing the First Respondent's entitlement to procedural fairness, yet the appellate court clarified that such influence did not transform a commercial act into an administrative decision. The distinction between commercial contracts and agreements involving public services is crucial; administrative actions often stem from the exercise of public power, whereas the relationship in question was governed by a contractual obligation ([14]–[22]).

**Access to Document**: The Appellant did not deny the existence of the requested documents; however, it claimed the First Respondent failed to demonstrate why those documents were essential for exercising its rights. The court highlighted that the First Respondent needed specifics on the allegations to safeguard its reputation, and therefore ruled in favor of granting access to the documents ([29]).

## C – Conclusion

The appeal partly succeeded. The Court held that the cancellation of the First Respondent's contract did not constitute administrative action. Consequently, the First Respondent was not entitled to procedural fairness as dictated by section 33 of the Constitution. The appellate court overturned the court a quo's finding regarding the unlawful termination and its various orders ([30]).

However, the court affirmed the First Respondent’s right to access specific documents relevant for the protection of its rights under section 32 of the Constitution. Thus, both parties were ordered to share 75% of the legal costs of the case, factoring justice and procedural equity ([32]).

**Implications**: This decision underscores the nuances in distinguishing between administrative actions and private contractual dealings involving public bodies. It clarifies the external regulatory boundaries when public authorities enter contractual relationships, indicating that unless actions are demonstrably tied to the execution of public duties, they may fall outside the ambit of administrative action protections.
