Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: Hlongwa v The State (1380/2018) ZASCA 156

### F – Facts
The appellant, Mzomisi Hlongwa, was convicted in the Regional Court of Westonaria on multiple serious charges: four counts of robbery with aggravating circumstances, one count of attempted murder, two counts of possession of an unlicensed firearm, and possession of live ammunition, in contravention of the Arms and Ammunition Act 75 of 1969 ([1]). He received a cumulative sentence of 33 years, comprising 20 years for each robbery count running concurrently, 10 years for attempted murder, and an additional 3 years for the firearm-related offences ([1]). Following his conviction, the Regional Court denied him leave to appeal, as did the Gauteng Division of the High Court when he petitioned them for relief ([1]). Consequently, he sought special leave to appeal from the Supreme Court of Appeal ([1]).

### I – Issues
The primary issue before the Supreme Court was whether the high court had erred in refusing to grant Hlongwa leave to appeal against his sentences ([3]). The court also considered what constitutes "reasonable prospects of success" for allowing such appeals, specifically focusing on whether a different court may impose a different sentence ([4]).

### R – Rules
The applicable legal framework is grounded in the **Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977**, particularly section 309(1)(a), which stipulates that appeals from lower courts should be directed to the High Court ([3]). The standard for granting leave to appeal is based on the test of "reasonable prospects of success", which necessitates a dispassionate evaluation of the facts and law, establishing that a different court may reasonably arrive at a different conclusion ([4], Smith v S [2011] ZASCA 15). The court must also consider principles surrounding cumulative sentences, as established in **S v Young [1977] 1 All SA 654 (A)**, which emphasizes the need for proportionality and fairness in sentencing ([5]).

### A – Application
In applying these rules to the facts, the Supreme Court underscored that the Regional Court failed to fully consider the cumulative effect of the sentences and the personal circumstances of the appellant—such as the considerable time spent in custody and the lack of physical harm to the complainants ([6]). The court noted that the sentence of 33 years appeared excessive given these contexts ([6]). Further, the court acknowledged the possibility that another court, particularly the High Court, could reinterpret the sentences more leniently, thereby establishing reasonable prospects for success on appeal. Those factors are pertinent for determining whether Hlongwa should be granted leave to appeal against his sentences ([8]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that there were reasonable prospects that a different court, particularly the High Court, might impose a different sentence. The decision of the high court was consequently set aside, and leave to appeal was granted to the Gauteng Division of the High Court ([8]). This case illustrates the court's adherence to procedural norms and its commitment to ensuring that sentences reflect not only the gravity of the offences but also the unique circumstances of the accused. The implications of this ruling reinforce the checks in place regarding sentencing practices and the avenues available for appealing judicial decisions within the South African legal framework. 

This case serves as a significant reminder of the importance of proportionality in sentencing and the role of higher courts in rectifying potential injustices arising from lower court rulings.
