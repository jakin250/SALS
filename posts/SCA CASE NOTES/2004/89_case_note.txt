# FIRAC Case Note: Michael Weare v The Commissioner for South African Revenue Service

**Case Title:**  
Michael Weare v The Commissioner for South African Revenue Service  

**Court:**  
Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  

**Case No:**  
401/2003  

**Coram:**  
Scott, Farlam, Mthiyane, Cloete JJA et Erasmus AJA  

**Dates:**  
Hearing: 13 September 2004  
Delivery: 29 September 2004  

---

## F – Facts ([1–16])

The appellant, Michael Weare, engaged in the business of bookmaking and was a registered vendor under the Value-Added Tax Act 89 of 1991 (hereafter "the Act"). He overpaid a total of R1,432,038.83 in VAT from the commencement of the Act in September 1991 until the tax period of June to July 1996. Following the payment of a tax amount from this period, he sought a refund of R1,417,018.99. 

Weare claimed entitlement to a refund under §44(1) of the Act, which would allow for recovery of overpayments made within a five-year window. Conversely, the respondent, the Commissioner for South African Revenue Service (SARS), asserted that any refund should adhere to §44(2)(a), which would limit recovery to a six-month period unless the Commissioner deemed the payments made were consistent with domestic practice as of that date. The Commissioner concluded that Weare’s payments complied with prevailing practices, thus limiting his refund claim to R336,259.93.

The appellant contested this through legal channels, which included the Natal Income Tax Special Court before advancing to the Supreme Court of Appeal.

## I – Issues ([1–16])

Two principal legal issues were presented for determination:

1. Whether the appellant was entitled to a refund of VAT under §44(1) or §44(2)(a) of the Act.
2. Whether the overpayment was consistent with the prevailing practice at the time of payment, impacting the refund eligibility under §44(2)(a).

## R – Rules ([1–6], [14–15])

The relevant legal provisions from the Act include:

- **§44(1)** provides for refunds of tax amounts if the vendor's tax deductions exceed the tax payable in a given tax period, allowing recovery within five years if not set off against unpaid taxes.
  
- **§44(2)(a)** mandates the refunding of excess tax amounts paid but is constrained by specific timelines and conditions outlined in §44(3), which imposes a six-month limitation on refunds unless the Commissioner is satisfied that the payment was made in accordance with prevailing practices.

- **§16(3)** allows vendors to deduct input tax from output tax. Specifically, the input and output tax calculations define the refund calculations.

## A – Application ([11–16])

The court scrutinized the appellant's tax calculation method. Weare utilized a ‘netting off’ approach where he calculated output tax from total bets received, deducting take-back winnings while accounting for input tax on winning payouts. This method diverged from what the Act and SARS guidelines prescribed.

The appellant argued that his calculation under the prevailing understanding warranted a refund under §44(1) since the overpayment stemmed from an under deduction of input tax. However, the court clarified that the appellant's approach led to inflated output tax as he included take-back winnings, overcharging himself for VAT.

The court examined the nature of the overpayment in relation to common practice. SARS presented evidence indicating most bookmakers adhered to a practice of treating all take-back winnings as responsible for output tax calculations, thereby establishing a standard that Weare did not successfully dispute.

Consequently, the court concluded that Weare's overpayment correctly fell under §44(2)(a) with the six-month limitation applicable since the overpayments were indeed consistent with established practices of the time.

## C – Conclusion ([16])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the respondent's application of tax law as it pertained to the appellant's refund claim. The court held that Weare was not entitled to recover the full overpaid VAT under §44(1) due to his erroneous calculation method. Instead, the refund was limited by §44(2)(a) as his payments conformed to the prevailing practice despite his unique method of accounting.

This case underscores the consequences of improper tax calculation methods and highlights the importance of understanding statutory frameworks and applicable guidelines for calculating VAT, especially for specialized professions such as bookmaking.
