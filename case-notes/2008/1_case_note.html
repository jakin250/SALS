Occupiers of 51 Olivia Road v City of Johannesburg and Others

The High Court had issued an order that prevented the eviction until it was demonstrated that adequate alternative housing was provided to the occupiers.

CCT 24/07

[2008] ZACC 1

F – Facts

The case involved more than 400 occupiers of two buildings in Johannesburg who sought leave to appeal the decision from the Supreme Court of Appeal that sanctioned their eviction by the City of Johannesburg. The eviction was authorized based on findings that the buildings were unsafe and unhealthy, with an order directing the City to provide temporary relocation for those in desperate need of housing ([1]–[2]).

The High Court had issued an order that prevented the eviction until it was demonstrated that adequate alternative housing was provided to the occupiers. The city’s housing program was found to be inadequate for the urgent requirements of the inner-city populace ([2]). The appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal contested whether the eviction orders should have been granted without a pre-eviction hearing or suitable accommodation ([3]).

During the proceedings, the court highlighted the dire circumstances of urban homelessness and questioned whether the City had fulfilled its constitutional obligations regarding housing ([3]–[4]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues raised in this case included:

  1. Whether the Supreme Court of Appeal had erred in granting an eviction order without ensuring meaningful engagement with the occupiers.
  2. The constitutionality of the procedure leading to eviction under section 12 of the National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act, particularly its compliance with section 26 of the Constitution, which protects against arbitrary eviction ([7]–[8]).
  3. The adequacy of the City's housing policy to accommodate vulnerable urban populations, and whether the constitutional rights of the applicants had been infringed by the City's decisions ([6]).

The court also considered whether the City had taken into account the potential for homelessness resulting from the evictions, particularly in light of the procedural and substantive aspects of justice and equity ([9]–[10]).

R – Rules

The legal framework was grounded in various constitutional provisions:

  • Section 26 of the Constitution, which states that everyone has the right to have access to adequate housing and outlines the duties of municipalities in this regard.
  • Relevant portions of the Health Act relating to local authority obligations to maintain sanitary conditions ([3], [11]).
  • Section 12(4)(b) of the National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act, allowing local authorities to evict occupants without prior court orders, which was contested for its potential to circumvent constitutional protections ([39]).

The Grootboom case was cited for establishing the importance of human dignity in housing matters, emphasizing that state actions must be reasonable and considerate of affected individuals’ dignity ([10]).

A – Application

The court’s analysis emphasized that meaningful engagement between the City and the occupiers was vital before any decision on eviction could be made. It criticized the absence of any attempt by the City to engage with the occupiers despite the awareness of likely homelessness resulting from the eviction ([14]–[22]).

The judgment pointed out that the decision taken by the City to evict was executed in isolation, disregarding the constitutional imperative to consider the housing needs of those affected. The City was found to have failed to balance its obligations regarding health and safety to the occupants with its responsibilities to provide adequate housing solutions ([43], [44]).

The court underscored that avoiding meaningful engagement could constitute a breach of constitutional obligations, particularly in the context of eviction proceedings, which could lead to homelessness. The Supreme Court of Appeal had failed to appreciate this interplay, leading to its erroneous decision to enforce evictions without adequate preparations ([38]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately found that the Supreme Court of Appeal should not have approved the eviction in the absence of meaningful engagement with the occupiers. It declared section 12(6) of the Act unconstitutional as it permitted eviction under a criminal sanction without a court order. Instead, it ordered that section 12(6) be read to apply only post-court order ([54]).

The court's ruling emphasized the necessity of engaging vulnerable populations in matters of eviction and housing rights, thus reinforcing the interdependence of health, safety, and housing obligations on municipalities. Additionally, the City was ordered to pay the costs of the applicants, as the initial engagement and consideration of housing needs had been neglected from the outset ([53], [54]).

This landmark judgment not only rectified the immediate situation for the occupiers but also established essential precedents for the treatment of similar cases, advocating for the protection of human dignity and constitutional rights in housing matters.
