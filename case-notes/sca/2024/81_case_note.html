Supreme Court of Appeal

City of Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality v Tshepo Gugu Trading CC and Another (1054/2022) [2024] ZASCA 81

Following the installation of the billboard, the municipality sought an order for its removal in August 2016, claiming it violated their regulations.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 81

FIRAC Analysis

F – Facts ([paras 1–7])

The case involves an appeal by the City of Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality (the appellant) against a decision of the Gauteng Division of the High Court (the full court) regarding the removal of a billboard owned by Tshepo Gugu Trading CC (the first respondent). The billboard, installed on municipal land in March 2016, was alleged to have been erected in contravention of municipal by-laws governing outdoor advertising (specifically the Billboards and Display of Advertisements By-laws) and the Local Government Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 ([paras 2–3]).

Following the installation of the billboard, the municipality sought an order for its removal in August 2016, claiming it violated their regulations. After a protracted legal series that included a settlement agreement (the Victor J order) which allowed the respondent to regularize the billboard, the respondent failed to comply with the required fee payments and did not submit a compliant application. Consequently, the municipality acted to dismantle the billboard on August 20, 2020, prompting the respondent to assert that removal was unlawful and constituted a spoliation, bringing an application for a mandament van spolie ([paras 10–11]).

The court of first instance ruled against the respondent, finding that the restoration of the billboard was moot since it had been dismantled. The full court subsequently overturned this decision, leading the municipality to apply for special leave to appeal, which was granted ([paras 12–14]).

I – Issues ([paras 18–22])

The primary legal issue pertains to whether the municipality unlawfully deprived the respondent of its possession of the billboard and whether the remedy of mandament van spolie applies in this case. Specifically, the court needed to determine:

  1. Whether the respondent was in peaceful and undisturbed possession of the billboard at the time of its removal.
  2. Whether the removal and dismantling of the billboard by the municipality was justified under the terms of the Victor J order and the relevant by-laws governing its installation.

Additionally, the court addressed the constitutional implications of the respondent's claim regarding the right to possess property and whether such possession was lawful given the billboard's non-compliance with statutory regulations ([paras 18–30]).

R – Rules ([paras 19–24])

The legal framework governing this case hinges on principles deriving from the common law remedy of a mandament van spolie. Established precedents dictate that:

  • A party claiming spoliation must show it was in peaceful possession and that such possession was unlawfully deprived ([para 22]).
  • Relevant case law, including Nino Bonino v De Lange and Tswelopele Non-Profit Organisation and Others v City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality, underscores that the essence of the mandament is the restoration of possession before delving into conflicting property rights ([paras 20–21]).

The relevant by-laws also played a significant role, notably provisions requiring fees for applications, which were not complied with by the respondent, thus rendering its initial claim of lawful possession questionable ([paras 25–29]).

A – Application ([paras 30–39])

In its judgment, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) applied the aforementioned rules to analyze the case's facts. The court underscored that the municipality had a statutory obligation to enforce by-law compliance and noted that the respondent's failure to pay requisite fees for the regularization application indicated acknowledgment of its prior unlawful possession of the billboard ([para 30]).

Ultimately, the court found that:

  • The respondent's possession was not lawful, as it stemmed from the erection of an illegal structure that violated the by-laws ([paras 31–33]).
  • The municipality was justified in dismantling the billboard based on the provisions of the Victor J order which allowed removal upon the respondent's failure to comply with statutory requirements ([para 34]).
  • The court also highlighted the public interest in enforcing compliance with laws designed to maintain public order and safety ([para 39]).

C – Conclusion ([paras 40–40])

The SCA concluded that the appeal was valid, asserting that the municipality acted lawfully in removing the billboard, and it reinstated the original order of the full court. The court additionally awarded costs for the appeal, including those for two counsels due to the complexity and significance of the issues at stake ([para 40]).

This ruling clarifies the legal standing regarding spoliation in cases where a possessory claim is juxtaposed against violations of statutory regulations, reinforcing the notion that unlawful possession, regardless of prior agreements, does not afford protection under the mandament van spolie remedy. The decision has significant implications for both municipal governance and property rights, emphasizing the necessity for compliance with existing laws to maintain lawful possession.
