Supreme Court of Appeal

South African National Parks v MTO Forestry (Pty) Ltd & Another [2018] ZASCA 59

In 2005, a lease agreement was established between MTO and the government, allowing MTO to clear-fell approximately 600 hectares of tree plantations over 20 years.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 59

F – Facts ([paras 1–9])

The case revolves around the South African National Parks (SANParks), which was appellant, and MTO Forestry (Pty) Ltd, the first respondent, alongside ParksCape, the second respondent. The key issue arose from SANParks allowing MTO to change a previously agreed-upon tree felling schedule for the Tokai Forest, which was part of the Table Mountain National Park.

In 2005, a lease agreement was established between MTO and the government, allowing MTO to clear-fell approximately 600 hectares of tree plantations over 20 years. Following a devastating fire in March 2015, MTO requested a revision to the felling schedule, accelerating the felling process due to concerns about tree infestation. SANParks publicly announced this acceleration in August 2016, leading to considerable community protests.

ParksCape, a non-profit organization advocating for biodiversity in urban parks, raised the alarm about this acceleration, asserting that the public, particularly those affected by the changes, had a right to be consulted, given the public interest in the management of Tokai forest. The Western Cape High Court agreed with ParksCape, deeming SANParks’ approval of the accelerated felling as procedurally unfair due to lack of consultation. This decision was subsequently appealed by SANParks.

I – Issues ([paras 1, 10, 18])

The primary legal issue at hand concerns whether SANParks’ decision to permit MTO to vary the tree-felling schedule constituted administrative action under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA). This poses questions of:

  1. Whether SANParks had an obligation to consult affected stakeholders before implementing the revised tree-felling program.
  2. Whether the nature of SANParks' decision, framed as a contract adjustment, fell within the realm of public administrative action, requiring adherence to principles of administrative justice.

R – Rules ([paras 19–22])

Relevant legal principles can be summarized as follows:

  • Administrative Action: Defined under section 1 of PAJA, an administrative action includes decisions by an organ of state that affect the rights of individuals and are taken while exercising public power.
  • Legitimate Expectation: This principle asserts that individuals may have an expectation to be consulted based on previous conduct or commitments made by public bodies.
  • Public Law vs. Private Law: The distinction between actions arising from private contractual obligations and public duties is crucial, especially regarding whether SANParks acted within private contract law or in pursuit of its statutory responsibilities under the National Environmental Management: Protected Areas Act (NEMPAA) and National Forests Act (NFA).

A – Application ([paras 30–31])

The court analyzed whether SANParks’ decision to allow the accelerated felling constituted an exercise of public power. Dambuza JA emphasized that SANParks had engaged in extensive public consultation prior to the original management framework's adoption, establishing a precedent for stakeholder consultation regarding park management.

The court also stressed that SANParks' commitments as articulated in the management framework and subsequent management plan created a legitimate expectation of consultation for those affected by the decision. Therefore, the approval to change the felling schedule was deemed not merely an exercise of contractual rights, but an administrative act requiring compliance with public law obligations.

Conversely, Rogers AJ, dissenting, contended that the decision emerged from the contract, presenting it solely as a private contractual adjustment not encompassed by the definitions of administrative action in PAJA. In determining the public impact, he suggested that there were no coercive public law duties triggered by SANParks' private contract framework.

C – Conclusion ([paras 32–33])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed SANParks’ appeal, upholding the High Court's ruling that SANParks had failed to consult the public and therefore acted in a procedurally unfair manner. The ruling reaffirmed the legal position that public authorities must maintain transparency and uphold open participation, particularly in decisions affecting public lands and environmental management.

The implications of this case underscore the necessity for public entities to recognize their public law obligations even when acting under seemingly private contractual frameworks. The court's decision enhances the doctrine of legitimate expectation and ensures that public participation in administrative processes remains a fundamental tenet of administrative justice in South Africa.

Overall, the case serves as a critical reminder of the intersection between administrative law and contractual obligations, emphasizing public accountability and the need for ongoing consultation in matters impacting communities and environments managed by state entities.
