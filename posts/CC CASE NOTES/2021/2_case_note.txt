Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# FIRAC Case Note: Secretary of the Judicial Commission of Inquiry into Allegations of State Capture, Corruption, and Fraud in the Public Sector Including Organs of State v Jacob Gedleyihlekisa Zuma [2021] ZACC 2

## F – Facts ([paras 1-30])

The case arose from an urgent application initiated by the Judicial Commission of Inquiry into Allegations of State Capture, Corruption and Fraud in the Public Sector (the Commission) to compel the former President, Jacob Gedleyihlekisa Zuma (the Respondent), to appear before it and testify. The Commission was set up under Proclamation R3 GG 41403 of 25 January 2018, pursuant to section 84(2)(f) of the Constitution of South Africa, to investigate serious allegations concerning state capture and corruption.

The urgency of the Commission's application was underscored by its impending dissolution on 31 March 2021, which was exacerbated by delays due to the COVID-19 pandemic. Despite repeated requests and a prior agreement regarding his participation, the Respondent had failed to submit an affidavit or attend subsequent hearings. This culminated in the issuance of a summons compelling the Respondent to appear for his testimony, which he ultimately rejected, leading the Commission to seek relief in the Constitutional Court.

## I – Issues ([paras 1-5],[80-82])

The central legal issues before the Court involved:

1. Whether the Constitutional Court should grant direct access to the urgent application from the Commission ([para 52]).
2. Whether the Respondent could be compelled to appear and testify before the Commission in light of claims regarding the right to remain silent and other privileges ([paras 80-84]).
3. The interpretation of section 3(4) of the Commissions Act concerning the privilege against self-incrimination and its applicability to the Respondent’s situation ([paras 94-96]).

## R – Rules ([paras 6-19],[72-109])

The case primarily applied the constitutional provisions regarding commissions of inquiry, specifically section 84(2)(f) of the Constitution, which grants the President the authority to establish commissions, and the legal frameworks provided by the Commissions Act of 1947, especially sections 3 and 6, which detail the powers and limitations of commissions regarding witness testimonies.

Notably, section 3 of the Commissions Act empowers a commission to summon witnesses and demand evidence, while section 6 imposes penal sanctions for failure to comply. Furthermore, section 3(4) extends certain privileges of witnesses in criminal trials to those appearing before a commission, particularly the privilege against self-incrimination.

The role and function of a commission were explained through landmark cases and judicial interpretations, stressing that commissions serve vital public interests and that the right to remain silent is reserved for accused persons and does not extend to witnesses in commission hearings ([paras 90-92]).

## A – Application ([paras 53-69],[80-107])

The Court addressed the urgency of granting direct access, emphasizing the necessity to protect the public interest by ensuring that allegations of serious wrongdoing are thoroughly investigated. The Commission had rationally focused on direct access given the time constraints tied to its winding-up.

In its assessment, the Court considered the diligence of the Commission's lawyers and noted previous failures on their part to invoke timely summons. However, it balanced this against the pressing need for evidence given the severity of the allegations against the Respondent, thus concluding that the public interest outweighed procedural shortcomings ([paras 66-71]).

On the matter of whether the Respondent could be compelled to testify, the Court concluded that the Respondent, as a witness, did not hold a right to remain silent, underscoring the statutory obligation to comply with the summons. The Court determined that the privilege against self-incrimination applies only in cases where a witness can demonstrate that answering a question may lead to self-incrimination, which the Respondent had not established satisfactorily ([paras 92-100]).

The Respondent's conduct was characterized as undermining the constitutional authority of the Commission and reflecting a disregard for the rule of law, thereby justifying the Commission's necessity to compel his attendance ([paras 106-113]).

## C – Conclusion ([paras 68-115])

The Constitutional Court granted the application for direct access, compelling the Respondent to obey all lawful summonses and directives issued by the Commission. The Court declared that the Respondent does not have a right to remain silent in the proceedings and clarified that he is entitled to the protections afforded under section 3(4) of the Commissions Act. The Respondent was ordered to pay the legal costs incurred by the Commission, establishing a precedent that emphasizes accountability, the responsibilities of state officials, and the overarching principle that no one is above the law.

This case underscores the critical role of commissions in upholding democracy and accountability, particularly when investigating allegations of significant public interest. The Court's reaffirmation of the necessity for compliance with lawful processes represents a robust affirmation of the rule of law in South Africa, reinforcing the constitutional faith in accountability mechanisms amidst significant political scrutiny.
