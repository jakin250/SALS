Mpumelelo Obed Mbatha v University of Zululand [2013] ZACC 43

Constitutional Court 2013 [2013] ZACC 43

F – Facts

The applicant, Mr. Mpumelelo Obed Mbatha, sought leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court of South Africa following a dismissal of his claim for unpaid wages by the Labour Appeal Court. Mr. Mbatha was employed by the University of Zululand (Unizul) as a researcher from September 1984 and was mandated to join specific employee benefit schemes. In September 2002, a tripartite agreement involving PanSALB and Isikhungo Sesichazamazwi SesiZulu (ISS) was established, allowing Unizul to support the development of an isiZulu dictionary. This agreement stipulated that ISS would eventually assume the employment of Unizul staff seconded to it, including Mr. Mbatha, by 31 December 2005 ([paras 1-10]).

From 2002, Unizul seconded Mr. Mbatha to ISS as CEO, yet he remained an employee of Unizul. However, in mid-2005, PanSALB suspended funding due to administrative issues, ultimately affecting salary payments. Despite this, Unizul continued to pay Mbatha until June 2008, when payments ceased, leading him to approach the Labour Court for an order compelling Unizul to pay his salary ([paras 10-25]).

I – Issues

The primary legal question in this case was whether Mr. Mbatha remained an employee of Unizul, which would entitle him to claim unpaid wages under the Basic Conditions of Employment Act, or if he had become an employee of ISS following the tripartite agreement. Specifically, the case examined whether there was an implicit transfer of his employment under the agreement and, if so, how that transfer occurred ([paras 36, 38-39, 85]).

R – Rules

Key legal principles governing this case arise from the Basic Conditions of Employment Act (BCEA) and the Labour Relations Act. Section 23(1) of the South African Constitution guarantees the right to fair labor practices, which extends to definitions and obligations outlined in the BCEA. Moreover, the BCEA provisions concerning remuneration (Section 32) impose a statutory duty on employers to pay employees their due wages within the designated period ([paras 44, 148]).

The tripartite agreement itself served as a critical legal framework, dictating conditions under which Unizul would transfer staffing obligations to ISS. Specifically, clauses 2.4 and 2.5 stipulated conditions that needed to be satisfied for such a transfer to occur, emphasizing that proper appointments made by ISS's Board were essential for staff transitions ([paras 7-8, 182-186]).

A – Application

The Labour Court initially found that Mr. Mbatha ceased to be an employee of Unizul on 31 December 2005. This conclusion was broadly based on the assumption that his role as CEO at ISS implied a definitive transfer of employment. This view was upheld by the Labour Appeal Court. However, the Constitutional Court, led by Zondo J, highlighted several critical misjudgments in both lower courts’ disregard for the implications of secondment as outlined in the tripartite agreement, asserting that mere control and supervision by ISS did not negate his employment status with Unizul ([paras 36-40, 84]).

Zondo J further critiqued the position of both lower courts for neglecting evidential concerns surrounding the essential requirement for appointment to ISS as an employee, which remained unfulfilled. The absence of documented appointment evidence supported the notion that Mr. Mbatha remained an employee of Unizul throughout the relevant period, despite ISS’s operational involvement ([paras 72, 89-117]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately ruled in favor of Mr. Mbatha, granting him restitution of his salaries owed. The Court concluded that he maintained his employment with Unizul and that nothing in the tripartite agreement validly terminated that relationship. Thus, Unizul was held liable for failing to compensate him, reflecting broader implications of employment law in the context of public sector contracts and the necessity for adherence to established procedural protocols ([paras 154-214]).

The case underscores the critical examination of employment relationships governed by statutory frameworks, emphasizing how administrative agreements must adhere to procedural law to effectuate valid employment transfers. The judgment also notes the significance of ensuring that employee rights under the Constitution are upheld, reinforcing the notion that contractual obligations should not trammel individual rights to fair labor practices ([paras 55, 188-189]).

References

  • Basic Conditions of Employment Act 75 of 1997
  • Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995
  • Constitution of the Republic of South Africa (1996)

This case sets an important precedent in understanding the functional dynamics between secondment, employment relationships, and the statutory protections afforded to employees in South Africa's constitutional framework.
