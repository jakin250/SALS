S.O.S Support Public Broadcasting Coalition and Others v South African Broadcasting Corporation (SOC) Limited and Others [2018] ZACC 37

Constitutional Court 2018 [2018] ZACC 37

F – Facts

The case arises from an application for leave to appeal against a decision made by the Competition Appeal Court concerning the interpretation of an earlier order regarding the powers of the Competition Commission (Commission) related to the investigation of a contentious agreement between the South African Broadcasting Corporation (SABC) and MultiChoice (Pty) Limited. The applicants — the S.O.S Support Public Broadcasting Coalition, Trustees for the Time Being of the Media Monitoring Project Benefit Trust, and Caxton and CTP Publishers and Printers Limited — sought the appeal based on the Competition Appeal Court's order of April 28, 2017, which clarified the June 24, 2016 order that had set aside the Tribunal's ruling regarding whether the agreement constituted a notifiable merger under the Competition Act 89 of 1998.

The SABC and MultiChoice entered into a five-year agreement, which became public following its leak. This agreement included an entertainment channel produced by the SABC for MultiChoice with financial commitments exceeding R500 million. Following initial rulings at the Competition Tribunal, where it was argued that the agreement constituted a notifiable merger, the Tribunal dismissed the applicants' application. This dismissal led the applicants to appeal to the Competition Appeal Court, which in turn set aside the Tribunal's decision, prompting the current case focused on the Commission's investigatory powers ([paras 1–8]).

I – Issues

  1. Does the Competition Act authorize the Commission to exercise its investigatory powers to determine whether the agreement constitutes a notifiable merger?
  2. What is the proper interpretation of the June 2016 order by the Competition Appeal Court regarding the Commission's investigative powers?
  3. If the June 2016 order doesn't authorize the investigative powers claimed by the applicants, should the Competition Appeal Court have varied the order or issued a new order allowing such investigations? ([paras 23–27]).

The broader context addresses the significant public interest surrounding the agreement between the public broadcaster and MultiChoice, which necessitates scrutiny under the Competition Act ([para 2]).

R – Rules

The legal framework applicable to this case is primarily derived from various provisions of the Competition Act 89 of 1998, particularly sections relevant to merger control and the powers of the Commission (sections 12, 13A, 13B, and 59) ([paras 30–36]).

  • Section 12(1) defines a merger and establishes the parameters for determining when a transaction becomes notifiable.
  • Section 13A obligates parties to notify the Commission of a proposed merger if specific conditions are met, alongside penalties for failing to notify.
  • Part B of Chapter 5 lays out the scope of the Commission's investigatory powers, enabling it to conduct inquiries and summons related documents or individuals integral to determining the legality of a merger ([para 42]).

Legal precedents such as Senwes and Bulmer illuminate the interpretational scope of the Commission's authority and the statutory approach toward the promotion of competition ([paras 30–41]).

A – Application

In his judgment, Kathree-Setiloane AJ extensively analyzed the interpretation of the orders from the Competition Appeal Court. The Court emphasized the need for the Commission to engage fully with its investigatory powers to determine the nature of the agreement in question effectively. The order from June 2016 was intended as a remedy for the Tribunal's prior shortcomings and aimed at ensuring public interest considerations were fully incorporated into the investigation of the agreement ([paras 51–65]).

The judgment confirmed that the Commission is vested with significant investigative authority, including both non-coercive and coercive methods, which are essential for a comprehensive review of whether the agreement constitutes a notifiable merger. The appeal was a necessary mechanism to clarify the Commission's role and empower it effectively to conduct inquiries expected in complex merger assessments ([paras 51–64]).

Contrasting views from the SABC and MultiChoice posited that the Commission should limit itself to documentation currently available, which the Court rejected, recognizing that such limitations could impede meaningful investigation ([paras 45–52]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal and upheld the applicants' appeal against the Competition Appeal Court, thereby clarifying that the June 2016 order does not preclude the Competition Commission from exercising its full investigatory powers under the Competition Act. It emphasized the critical public interest inherent in overseeing agreements that relate to the public broadcaster, stating that the Commission must file its reports based on thorough investigations that may include coercive methods ([paras 89, 90]).

The judgment reestablishes the necessity for comprehensive scrutiny in merger cases, especially those concerning significant public interest, thereby ensuring stronger enforcement mechanisms within South Africa’s competition law framework. The Court’s decision is fundamental not only for the present case but also for future merger assessments involving public entities and private enterprises, thus reinforcing the integrity of the merger regulatory system and the overarching goal of promoting competition within the economy ([paras 86–88]).

This ruling reaffirms the importance of statutory interpretation in the context of the Competition Act, maintaining robust oversight of mergers and ensuring compliance with the legal standards designed to protect public interest ([paras 48–60]).
