Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: Mngomezulu v Ethekwini Metropolitan Municipality (079/2018)

## F – Facts
[1] The appellant, Mr. Nkosinathi Wiseman Mngomezulu, challenges a judgment from the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court that dismissed his delictual claims against the Ethekwini Metropolitan Municipality (the Municipality) for alleged unlawful demolition of his informal dwelling and for being shot by officials of the Municipality during the demolition process. This appeal is made with leave from the High Court.

[2] The conflict arose on 28 March 2013, when an interdict was granted by the High Court permitting the Municipality, alongside the South African Police Service (SAPS), to take measures to prevent illegal settlements on certain properties, specifically targeting lands including portions of Erf 960 in the Cato Crest informal settlement. This order enabled the demolition of unauthorized structures on these sites.

[3] Following a contentious series of demolitions that began under this interdict, on 21 September 2013, Mngomezulu confronted Mr. Gavin Le Cordier, a member of the Municipality's land invasion unit (LIU), while armed with a sharpened metal object described as a spear and a shield. The highlighted confrontation culminated in Mngomezulu sustaining four gunshot wounds while Le Cordier sustained a stab wound.

[6] Mngomezulu subsequently instituted legal proceedings on 27 October 2014 against the Municipality and the Minister of Safety and Security, claiming damages for the unlawful demolition of his dwelling, assault, and unlawful arrest. The Municipality, in turn, asserted that the actions taken were in self-defense, as Mngomezulu had allegedly attacked Le Cordier.

[10] The High Court found that Mngomezulu failed to prove the necessary elements of his claims, primarily, that his dwelling had either existed or had been unlawfully demolished. It also ruled in favor of the Municipality’s self-defense claim, concluding that Mngomezulu’s conduct warranted such a response.

## I – Issues
[11] The case raises several pivotal legal questions:
- Whether Mngomezulu demonstrated that his dwelling was unlawfully demolished by the Municipality.
- Whether the Municipality's use of force through gunfire was legally justified under the self-defense doctrine.
- The implications of Mngomezulu's actions and whether they constituted reasonable defensive action against an alleged imminent threat.

## R – Rules
[13] The legal framework governing intonations of self-defense includes several principles:
1. The burden of proof lies with the claimant (Mngomezulu) to establish unlawful conduct by the Municipality.
2. Self-defense is justified if the defensive action is reasonable and proportional to an imminent threat, which is governed by common law principles as well as constitutional norms aimed at protecting rights such as the right to life and property.
3. The necessity of immediate danger to justify defensive actions is also critical. Prior case law has underscored that retrospective threats or fears do not substantiate claims for self-defense (S v Mogohlwane [1982] 3 All SA 483).

[17] The law is clear: an individual may defend their property, but the action must be objectively reasonable and not excessive in relation to the threat posed.

## A – Application
[14] The court meticulously analyzed the evidence presented:
- Mngomezulu's claims faltered on credibility grounds. It was elucidated that he had not convincingly shown that he possessed a dwelling outside the designated demolition zones nor substantiated the unlawful destruction thereof.
- The court accepted the Municipality's version that only non-occupied, incomplete structures were demolished, and emphasized the importance of complying with the court's interim orders in light of a broader context of unlawful evictions affecting other residents.
- It was determined that Mngomezulu's actions on the day of the incident, including allegedly escalating to an armed confrontation, depicted him as the aggressor rather than a victim acting in self-defense of property. His persistence in approaching armed officials, despite repeated warnings, did not align with notions of reasonable defensive action under imminent threat.

[16] The court concluded that while Mngomezulu had the legal right to protect property, his actions lacked justification. The situation did not demonstrate a scenario where he acted against an immediate and real threat; thus, the defending actions could not be classified as proportionate or reasonable within the circumstances.

## C – Conclusion
[21] The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming the High Court's finding that Mngomezulu had not met his burden of proof regarding the unlawful demolition of his property and further recognized the Municipality's actions as justified self-defense. The judgment reiterates key principles regarding self-defense, particularly emphasizing the requirement for imminent threat and proportionality. The implications underline the complexities surrounding evictions, property rights, and lawful resistance in the context of municipal authority actions, further shaping the spectrum of constitutional rights concerning housing and property. 

In dissent, Justice Molemela contended that the case engaged constitutional issues, advocating for the application of the Biowatch principle, which traditionally safeguards against adverse costs orders in constitutional litigation. The divisions in opinion around costs reflect ongoing debates concerning the intersection of legal processes with constitutional obligations, particularly in sensitive contexts such as housing rights and community evictions.

This case exemplifies the intricate dynamics between individual property rights and the actions of state authorities attempting to enforce orders for public policy, emphasizing continued judicial vigilance in ensuring balance and fairness.
