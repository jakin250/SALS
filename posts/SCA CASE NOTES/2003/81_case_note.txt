# FIRAC Case Note for Goodenough NO v Road Accident Fund

**Case Title**: Goodenough NO v Road Accident Fund  
**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Case Number**: 441/2002  
**Date Heard**: 15 September 2003  
**Date Delivered**: 15 September 2003  
**Coram**: Harms, Brand JJA and Motata AJA  

---

### F - Facts
[1] The appellant, Doris Goodenough, was appointed as the curatrix ad litem for Mr. Norman Modise, who was incapacitated due to brain injuries sustained in an incident on 3 December 2000. Modise, a pedestrian, alleged that he was struck from behind by an unidentified motor vehicle while crossing a street.

[2] Goodenough initiated legal proceedings against the respondent, the Road Accident Fund, in the Witwatersrand Local Division under the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996. The trial court determined the issue of liability first, finding in favor of the respondent, which prompted the current appeal.

[3] The respondent denied that Modise had been struck by a motor vehicle, leading to two pivotal questions for determination: (a) Was Modise struck by an unidentified motor vehicle, as alleged? and (b) Was the accident caused by the negligence of the driver of that unidentified vehicle?

[4] The record indicates that the respondent did not call any witnesses. The primary evidence concerning the accident came from Modise himself, who, due to his condition, exhibited severe confusion and memory issues. As such, the trial court placed little reliance on his testimony.

[5] Goodenough’s argument was largely based on circumstantial evidence and hearsay. A friend of Modise, Zwandele Dube, testified that he had been with Modise shortly before the accident. Dube noted hearing braking sounds but did not initially associate them with Modise.

[6] Both Dube and Modise's sister, Ntede Modise, provided hearsay accounts of statements made by Modise after the accident, asserting that he had been struck by a motor vehicle. Goodenough attempted to leverage Modise's hospital records indicating that he described himself as "a pedestrian hit from behind."

[7] The lower court found the hearsay statements admissible under section 3(1)(b) of the Law of Evidence Amendment Act 45 of 1988, citing that Modise had been called as a witness despite his inability to confirm his statements. This interpretation was noted as conflicting with prior judgments.

### I - Issues
[8] The central legal questions are:
1. Was Modise indeed struck by an unidentified motor vehicle?
2. Can the accident be attributed to negligence on the part of the driver?

### R - Rules
[9] The court relied upon the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 and the Law of Evidence Amendment Act 45 of 1988, particularly section 3(1) permitting the admissibility of hearsay under specific circumstances. The legal principle of **res ipsa loquitur** was also invoked, suggesting that established facts speak for themselves, often used to infer negligence based on the nature of an accident.

[10] Precedents were cited, including **S v Ndlovu** [2002] 2 SACR 325 (SCA) and **Motor Vehicle Assurance Fund v Dubuzane** [1984] 1 SA 700 (A), highlighting the nuances involved in interpreting negligence and the admissibility of evidence.

### A - Application
[11] The court accepted, for argument's sake, that Modise had been struck by a motor vehicle, addressing the first question in favor of the appellant. However, the court scrutinized the necessity of establishing negligence from this accepted fact. 

[12] The appellant's reliance on **res ipsa loquitur** was critically evaluated. While Modise was indeed struck while crossing the street in daylight, the court conjectured that alternative explanations could exist. The possibility that Modise entered the street without due caution was considered, undermining a conclusion of driver negligence.

[13] Likewise, the alleged fact that the driver failed to stop or offer assistance was deemed unproven beyond speculation. The court indicated that this behavior could support various conclusions, potentially absolving the driver of negligence.

[14] Ultimately, the court found multiple plausible inferences that could detract from establishing negligence. Thus, even assuming Modise's account of being struck was true, the evidentiary basis for attributing negligence to the unidentified driver was insufficient.

### C - Conclusion
[15] The appeal was dismissed with costs, reinforcing the lower court's finding of no liability. The court emphasized that the burden of proof concerning negligence in civil cases must be met by establishing factual circumstances from which negligence can be reasonably inferred.

[16] This judgment provides critical insights into the complexities surrounding hearsay evidence and the application of circumstantial evidence in negligence claims within the context of traffic accidents, specifically reinforcing the necessity for clear evidence to substantiate claims of negligence.
