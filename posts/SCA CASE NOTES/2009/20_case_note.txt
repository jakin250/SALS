Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2009

# FIRAC Case Note: City of Johannesburg v L Mazibuko (489/08) [2009] ZASCA 20

## F – Facts
This case involves the City of Johannesburg and Johannesburg Water (Pty) Ltd, who are the appellants, against residents of Phiri Township, including Lindiwe Mazibuko and others, as respondents. The central issue arose from the implementation of a prepayment water meter system that limited the free basic water supply to 25 litres per person per day or 6 kilolitres per household per month ([1]-[4]). Prior to 2004, residents had access to an unlimited supply of water that was unmetered and charged based on a deemed consumption of 20 kilolitres per month. Following financial pressures and a shift in policy, the City transitioned to a prepayment model which was contested by residents, who argued that the amount was insufficient to meet their basic needs as defined under section 27 of the South African Constitution ([4]-[5]).

In essence, the respondents claimed a constitutional right to sufficient water and submitted that the City’s limitation on the free basic supply was not only inadequate but unconstitutional. The Johannesburg High Court originally ruled in favor of the respondents, mandating a supply of 50 litres per person per day, and declared the prepayment system unconstitutional ([2]). The City and Johannesburg Water then appealed the ruling to the Supreme Court of Appeal.

## I – Issues
The Supreme Court of Appeal was tasked with determining two primary issues:
1. Whether the City and Johannesburg Water are constitutionally obliged to provide free water to residents of Phiri who cannot afford to pay for it ([1]).
2. Whether the use of prepayment water meters by the City constituted a restriction on access to water, thereby infringing the residents' rights under the Constitution ([1]).

## R – Rules
The legal framework involved includes:
- **Section 27 of the Constitution**: stipulates the right of access to sufficient water as a fundamental human right. The section encompasses a duty on the state to take reasonable legislative measures to achieve the progressive realization of this right ([5]).
- **Water Services Act 108 of 1997**: envisions a basic water supply of 25 litres per person per day or 6 kilolitres per household per month. It commands water service authorities to take reasonable measures to ensure equitable access to water services ([6]-[9]).
- **Bylaws and Regulations**: relevant municipal bylaws must align with national legislation, detailing conditions for service provision ([9]-[10]).

The court primarily referred to the need for a purposive interpretation of laws to uphold the dignity of residents and ensure their access to basic needs ([16]-[20]).

## A – Application
The court analyzed the sufficiency of water provision, determining that 42 litres per person per day was necessary for dignified living conditions, especially considering that many residents relied on waterborne sanitation ([24]-[25]). The court rejected the City’s argument that the Water Services Act effectively limited the residents' rights under section 27 of the Constitution, clarifying that the Act did not intend to exhaust the constitutional provision but rather aimed to progressively realize it ([13]-[14]). 

The court also addressed the City’s financial constraints, emphasizing that while the right to access sufficient water cannot be absolute, it must be progressively fulfilled where feasible. Notably, the City’s policy of limiting free supply to 25 litres was found to fall short of reasonable actions under its constitutional obligations ([27]-[30]).

Regarding the prepayment meters, the court echoed findings from the High Court that indicated these were unlawful under municipal bylaws and misconstrued service level classifications ([50]-[59]). It pointed out that the meters effectively cut off water supply in violation of residents' access and did not align with the intended purpose of ensuring equitable water services.

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, amended the High Court's order, and mandated that the City must provide 42 litres of free water per person per day to the residents of Phiri who cannot afford to pay. It challenged the City to reformulate its free water policy within a reasonable timeframe and suspended the ruling on prepayment meters for two years to allow the City to potentially legalize their use ([62]).

The court’s decision reaffirmed the constitutional right to access sufficient water, highlighting the state and local authority's obligations to not only meet these rights but to ensure that any limitations are justifiable and in the interest of social equity. This case underscores the balance that must be struck between legislative frameworks and the intrinsic rights guaranteed by the Constitution, promoting accountability among public authorities in delivering essential services.
