Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# Case Note: Justice Alliance of South Africa and Others v President of the Republic of South Africa and Others [2011] ZACC 23

## F - Facts

[1] The Constitutional Court of South Africa adjudicated three applications for direct access arising from a decision by the President of the Republic to extend the term of office of Chief Justice Sandile Ngcobo for five years. These challenges were brought during the court recess, indicating the urgency of the matter given the impending expiration of the Chief Justice's term on 14 August 2011 ([1]).

[2] Prior to an amendment in 2001, section 176 of the Constitution provided a non-renewable term of twelve years for Constitutional Court judges, mandating retirement at age seventy. The 2001 amendment modified this to allow Parliament to extend a judge's term via legislation ([2]). In conjunction, section 4 of the Judges’ Remuneration and Conditions of Employment Act stipulates the continuation of service for judges nearing the completion of their term, given they meet certain criteria — 15 years of active service or reaching the age of 75 ([3]-[4]).

[5] Notably, section 8(a) of the Act uniquely grants the President power to extend the Chief Justice's term at the request of the incumbent, making it distinct from the terms of other judges ([5]). Chief Justice Ngcobo was eligible for discharge from active service on 15 August 2011, and the President formally requested an extension via letter dated 11 April 2011. Ngcobo agreed to this extension on 2 June 2011, leading to the President's formal announcement of the extension later that day ([7]-[10]).

[11] In response to the extension, three applications were filed: 
1. Justice Alliance of South Africa (JASA) on 20 June 2011.
2. Freedom Under Law (FUL) on the same day.
3. Centre for Applied Legal Studies (CALS) and the Council for the Advancement of the South African Constitution (CASAC) jointly applied shortly thereafter ([11]).

[12] The above organizations sought to challenge the constitutionality of the law allowing for the extension of the Chief Justice's term, asserting it was ultra vires the powers conferred by section 176 of the Constitution and infringing on judicial independence. The President, the Minister for Justice, and Justice Ngcobo were named as respondents ([13]).

## I - Issues

[41] The primary issue for determination was whether section 8(a) of the Act contravenes section 176(1) of the Constitution, particularly with respect to:
- (a) the validity of the delegation of power to extend the Chief Justice's term to the President;
- (b) the constitutional permissibility of differentiating the terms of office for judges of the Constitutional Court; 
- (c) whether the President is required to consult with the Judicial Service Commission (JSC) and political entities prior to granting such an extension; 
- (d) the appropriate remedy if the court finds in favor of the applicants ([41]).

## R - Rules

[20] The rules governing this case are grounded in the South African Constitution, specifically sections 1 (values of human dignity, equality, and the rule of law), 2 (supremacy of the Constitution), and 176(1) (regarding judicial tenure). The separation of powers doctrine outlined in Chapter 8 of the Constitution delineates the functions and powers of the legislature, executive, and judiciary. 

Section 176(1) dictates that a Constitutional Court judge holds office for a non-renewable term of twelve years unless otherwise extended by an Act of Parliament, which must remain a legislative power of Parliament as emphasized in previous judgments concerning undefined delegations ([24]-[26]). 

Additional statutes relevant to this case include:
- The Judges' Remuneration and Conditions of Employment Act, particularly sections 4 (extension of judges' terms post-retirement) and 8(a) (authorizing the President to extend the term of the Chief Justice) ([3]-[4]).

## A - Application

[41] The Court employed a purposive interpretation of the provisions to assess the extension. The applicants contended that section 8(a) resulted in impermissible delegation of legislative powers to the President, thus violating the constitutional mandate. They argued that the explicit wording of section 176(1), which specifies that only an Act of Parliament can extend a judge's tenure, excluded the possibility of delegation or differentiation ([42]-[43]).

[50] The court ultimately agreed with the applicants that section 8(a) does grant the President significant discretion regarding the extension of the Chief Justice’s term—deeming it inconsistent with section 176 as it effectively transferred legislative powers held solely by Parliament to the executive ([52]). Moreover, the Court noted the critical importance of maintaining judicial independence, indicating an open-ended executive discretion could lead to perceptions of external influence ([68]).

The Court further dissected whether section 176(1) allows for differentiation among Constitutional Court judges ([70]). Upon examining the constitutional framework, the Court found that the language does not permit Parliament to single out a judge for term extension, thereby reinforcing the principles of impartiality and equal treatment among judges ([72]-[89]).

## C - Conclusion

[99] The Court concluded, unanimously, that section 8(a) of the Judges' Remuneration and Conditions of Employment Act is inconsistent with section 176(1) of the Constitution and thus invalid. The decision of the President to extend Chief Justice Ngcobo's term was also declared invalid and of no force ([116]).

[115] Costs were awarded to the applicants, as they were materially successful, although the requests for additional counsel were denied due to the legal complexities not warranting such ([115]-[116]). 

This case has significant implications for the integrity of judicial independence in South Africa, affirming the necessity of legislative authority in the appointment and retention of judicial leaders without undue executive influence. The ruling underscores the importance of maintaining the delicate balance between the branches of government mandated by the Constitution, thereby reinforcing public confidence in the judiciary as an impartial arbiter.
