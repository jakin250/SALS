# Case Note: Mngomezulu v Van den Heever [2006] SCA 149 (RSA)

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts ([paras 1–8])
On 17 September 2004, an ex parte provisional restraint order was granted by the Johannesburg High Court against appellants Ngengelezi Zaccheus Mngomezulu and Nontando Mngomezulu under the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (POCA). The order restrained the appellants from dealing with their assets, including their residential home, designating Theodor Wilhelm van den Heever as the curator bonis responsible for managing these assets ([para 1]).

Following the issuance of the restraint order, the first appellant voluntarily surrendered possession and control of the assets to the curator bonis ([para 3]). Debates ensued regarding the conditions under which the appellants could reoccupy their residential property. The first appellant, after consulting with legal counsel, argued that they were entitled to occupy their home without prior negotiations with the curator bonis, which the curator opposed, citing issues concerning the property’s condition and security ([paras 5–6]).

An urgent application by the appellants for a declarator that they were entitled to occupy the property pending proceedings was initially upheld, asserting that the curator could not impose conditions on their reoccupation ([para 8]). However, this decision was appealed, leading to the current case.

### I – Issues ([paras 10–12])
The primary legal issue before the Court was whether the restraint order effectively resulted in an eviction of the appellants from their home, given the conditions imposed by the curator bonis for their reoccupation. The appellants contended that their constitutional right under Section 26(3) of the Constitution, which protects against arbitrary eviction, was violated. Additionally, they argued that the curator's actions contravened the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE) ([para 10]).

The Court was tasked with interpreting the term "surrender" as utilized in Section 28(1)(b) of POCA and whether it implied a complete transfer of possession to the curator bonis, thereby necessitating an order for the appellants to vacate their home ([paras 9–12]).

### R – Rules ([paras 9–11])
The legal framework guiding this case stems from the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998, specifically Chapter 5, which governs the restraint and confiscation of property connected to unlawful activities. Section 14 defines “realisable property,” while Section 28 outlines the powers of the High Court concerning restraint orders, including the appointment of curators bonis and the requirement for the defendant to surrender property to the curator ([paras 9, 11]).

In determining occupancy rights of the appellants against the backdrop of restraint orders, relevant precedents, including *National Director of Public Prosecutions v Phillips* ([2005] SCA 265), suggest that unless explicitly mandated by an order under Section 28(1)(b), a defendant may continue to occupy based on the assessment of risk and preservation duties of the curator bonis ([para 12]).

### A – Application ([paras 13–18])
In applying the law to the facts, the Court observed that the appellants had voluntarily surrendered their assets, including the residential property, to the curator bonis. It clarified that this surrender did not amount to eviction as they had not contested the surrender's voluntary nature at any stage ([para 11]). Thus, the curator bonis possessed legitimate authority to seek conditions ensuring the preservation of the property, citing concerns over its recent history of vandalism and previous removals of valuable assets by the appellants ([paras 15–17]).

The Court concluded that the conditions imposed by the curator bonis, including fulfilling obligations regarding property maintenance, security measures, and ensuring no further removals of assets, were not unreasonable. It asserted that these steps were integral to enabling the curator to fulfill his duties as outlined by the court order and maintain the property’s value and security ([para 18]). 

### C – Conclusion ([paras 19])
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, holding that the curator bonis was within his rights to impose conditions for the reoccupation of the property. The Court reaffirmed that the restraint order remained valid and that the appellants' claims regarding their right to unconditioned occupancy were misplaced, given their voluntary surrender of the property ([para 19]). This ruling underscores the balance between the state’s interest in preserving property under suspicion of being linked to organized crime and the rights of individuals residing in such properties, highlighting the necessity for judicial oversight in the imposition of conditions governing property management under restraint orders.
