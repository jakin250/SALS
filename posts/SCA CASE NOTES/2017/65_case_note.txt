# FIRAC Case Note: Magubane & Another v Twin City Developers (Pty) Ltd & Others

**Case Title**: Magubane & Another v Twin City Developers (Pty) Ltd & Others  
**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Neutral Citation**: [2017] ZASCA 65  
**Case Number**: 981/16  
**Date of Judgment**: 30 May 2017  
**Judges**: Ponnan, Mbha, Dambuza, Van der Merwe JJA, and Fourie AJA  

---

## F – Facts

The case involves an appeal concerning the eviction of the appellants, July Joseph Magubane and Gweje Khumalo, from farm land owned by the first respondent, Twin City Developers (Pty) Ltd, where they had been residing, along with their family members, since 1975 and 1980, respectively ([para 2]). The relationship between the appellants and the respondents deteriorated, resulting in the termination of the appellants' right of residence as of July 2, 2013, under section 10 of the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (the Act) ([para 2]). The appellants opposed their eviction, leading the Land Claims Court (LCC) to adjudicate the matter, and ultimately issue an eviction order on January 13, 2016. 

Significantly, the court based its decision without having considered a probation officer’s report requested under section 9(3) of the Act, which outlined the implications of the eviction and the availability of alternative accommodation ([para 6]) and ([para 7]). The probation officer’s report was submitted six weeks before the judgment but was inadvertently overlooked at the hearing ([para 9]). 

The LCC found that the appellants’ conduct had caused irreparable damage to the respondents’ property, thereby justifying the eviction ([para 3]). Following the ruling, the appellants sought to appeal that decision, with the leave of the LCC, arguing that its failure to consider the probation officer’s report constituted a material irregularity ([para 1]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issue on appeal was whether the LCC was required to consider the probation officer's report before ordering the eviction and whether its failure to do so constituted a material irregularity that warranted the setting aside of the eviction order ([para 5]). The appeal further raised the question of what the appropriate remedy should be, whether to remit the matter to the LCC for reconsideration or for the Supreme Court of Appeal to determine the legality of the eviction based on the report ([para 10]).

## R – Rules

The judgment chiefly pertains to the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997. Specifically, section 9(3) mandates that before an eviction order can be granted, a report must be obtained from a probation officer covering several considerations to protect the constitutional rights of the occupiers, including their right to housing, education, and the avoidance of undue hardship ([para 5]). 

Furthermore, the court considered the principle that the interests of justice and convenience guide whether a court of appeal should finalize a matter or remit it for further consideration ([para 10]). Various precedents were referenced, including *Glen Elgin Trust v Titus* [2001] 2 All SA 86 (LCC) and *Theewaterskloof Holdings (Edms) Bpk v Jacobs* [2002] 3 SA 401 (LCC), which address the obligations of courts in eviction matters and the necessity of considering the constitutional rights of occupants ([para 12]).

## A – Application

The court found that the LCC's failure to consider the probation officer’s report preceding its eviction order constituted a material misdirection. Since the report was available and had been filed in accordance with the statutory requirements, the eviction order should not have been issued without consideration of its contents ([para 9]). 

However, upon examining the report, the Supreme Court of Appeal noted that both parties conceded the report adequately addressed the statutory considerations and complied with section 9(3) of the Act. The appellants’ counsel conceded that they could not point to any aspects within the report that would substantiate a claim against the eviction, which undermined their argument for interference with the LCC’s order ([para 13]).

Additionally, the court considered the overarching constitutional obligations that required any eviction to be executed with compassion, yet it noted that the Act does not place the responsibility of providing housing on landowners ([para 12]). Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal deemed it just and equitable to uphold the eviction following an assessment of both the report and the surrounding circumstances, concluding that remitting the matter would lead to unnecessary delays and costs ([para 11]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the eviction order, dismissing the appeal without costs ([para 16]). The court determined that the eviction's procedural irregularities did not warrant overturning the LCC’s ruling since the probation officer's report did not counter the eviction order effectively. The judgment underscored the necessity of balancing the rights and interests of landowners against those of occupants while also highlighting the importance of statutory compliance in eviction processes under the Extension of Security of Tenure Act. The case serves as a critical reference point for future eviction cases, especially regarding the procedural prerequisites mandated by the Act and the implications of constitutional rights in eviction proceedings.
