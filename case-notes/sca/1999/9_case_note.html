Supreme Court of Appeal

The Director : Mineral Development, Gauteng Region v Save the Vaal Environment & Others

The appeal revolves around an application made by Sasol Mining (Pty) Ltd for a mining licence under Section 9 of the Minerals Act 50 of 1991.

Supreme Court of Appeal 1999

Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Case Number: 133/98
Judgment Date: 12 March 1999
Judges: Mahomed CJ, Howie, Marais, Olivier JJA, and Madlanga AJA

F – Facts

The appeal revolves around an application made by Sasol Mining (Pty) Ltd for a mining licence under Section 9 of the Minerals Act 50 of 1991. The mining operations in question were proposed to take place in a region adjacent to the Vaal River, with significant environmental implications ([para 1]). The appellants are the Director of Mineral Development, Gauteng (first appellant), and Sasol Mining (second appellant), while the respondents include Save the Vaal Environment and other concerned property owners, who are primarily focused on environmental integrity ([paras 2-4]).

Sasol Mining's application for the mining licence was submitted in May 1996, amidst urgent demands for the expansion of coal mining. However, the Director declined to hear objections raised by Save, asserting that it would be premature to consider such environmental concerns at that stage, ultimately issuing the mining licence in May 1997 ([paras 5-6]). The environmental concerns articulated by Save included potential destruction of the Rietspruit wetland, threats to local fauna and flora, pollution, water quality degradation, and depreciation of property values ([para 6]).

The legal status of Save was also brought into question; Sasol Mining argued that it was an "illegal association" under Section 30(1) of the Companies Act 61 of 1973, which governs the formation of associations for business purposes ([para 7]).

The legal proceedings followed, leading to a review in the High Court where it was established that the Director had erred by not allowing the respondents to raise their environmental objections ([paras 1, 5]).

I – Issues

The primary legal question concerns whether interested parties like Save are entitled to raise environmental objections and be heard by the Director before a mining licence is granted ([para 1]). Specific issues include:

  1. Does the audi alteram partem rule apply in this context, obligating the Director to allow for public hearing or objections regarding the application?
  2. Does Section 30(1) of the Companies Act preclude Save from legitimately opposing the mining licence, thereby affecting its legal standing in this matter?
  3. What are the implications of issuing the mining licence on environmental rights guaranteed under the South African Constitution ([para 9])?

R – Rules

The relevant legal framework encompasses:

  1. Audi Alteram Partem Rule: A principle of natural justice requiring that individuals affected by a decision be given an opportunity to be heard. This rule applies when a public official's decision can adversely affect someone's rights or legitimate expectations ([para 9]).
  2. Minerals Act 50 of 1991: Particularly Section 9, which governs the granting of mining licences and includes provisions relating to environmental rehabilitation ([para 11]).
  3. Companies Act 61 of 1973, Section 30(1): This section prohibits the formation of unregistered associations with more than 20 members that intend to conduct business for profit, which was invoked by Sasol Mining to challenge Save’s standing ([para 7]).
  4. Constitution of South Africa (1996): Section 24, which enshrines the right to an environment that is not harmful to health or well-being ([para 9b]).

A – Application

The court applied the audi alteram partem rule by analyzing the Director's responsibilities under the Minerals Act. It held that specific provisions of Section 9 (particularly paragraphs (b) regarding rehabilitation requirements) necessitate consideration of environmental matters. This implies that stakeholders raising legitimate concerns about potential environmental degradation ought to be granted an opportunity to voice their objections before a licence is issued ([para 20]).

Regarding Save's legality, the court determined that because Save was not established with a profit motive and did not conduct business in the sense intended under Section 30(1) of the Companies Act, the objection raised by Sasol Mining was baseless. Thus, Save's concerns were deemed valid and its standing to oppose the mining licence was affirmed ([para 8]).

The court also dismissed arguments suggesting that the audi rule was unnecessary because the Director could consider objections during the later environmental management approval phase. It noted that an initial adverse decision at the mining licence stage would impact stakeholders, thereby compelling the Director to allow for public input regarding the potential consequences on environmental rights, as evidenced by concerns raised about the impacts on local ecosystems ([paras 16-19]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the decision by the High Court which had ruled that the Director must allow interested parties the opportunity for input before issuing a mining licence. The judgment reinforced the principles of natural justice concerning environmental rights, reinforcing the need for effective public participation in decisions impacting ecological well-being. The ruling underscored that environmental considerations must be integrated into the administrative processes of the state, reflecting the constitutional commitment to safeguarding the environment for current and future generations ([para 20]).

The implications of this decision resonate not just within the confines of mining operations but also reinforce the need for a comprehensive approach to environmental governance where public interests are duly considered in the balancing of developmental needs against ecological imperatives.
