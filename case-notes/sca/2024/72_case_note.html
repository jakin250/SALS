Supreme Court of Appeal

Maritz v The State (81/2023) [2024] ZASCA 72

This case concerns Jan Gysbert Maritz (the appellant), a practicing attorney charged with 18 counts of sexual assault and statutory rape.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 72

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

This case concerns Jan Gysbert Maritz (the appellant), a practicing attorney charged with 18 counts of sexual assault and statutory rape. The trial commenced in the Free State Division of the High Court, Bloemfontein, where Maritz, represented by two senior counsel, initially pleaded not guilty to all charges ([paras 1–2]). However, on 21 May 2021, during the testimony of the first state witness, he expressed a desire to change his plea to guilty and made several admissions under section 220 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (the “Act”). Following these admissions, he was convicted on counts 1 to 16, while the state discontinued proceedings on counts 17 and 18 ([para 2]).

Subsequent to his conviction, Maritz was released on bail, pending sentencing scheduled between 14 and 17 September 2021. However, following a bereavement, the presiding Judge Naidoo was absent for a bail variation hearing, which was instead conducted by Judge Daniso. On 14 September 2021, Maritz’s new legal representatives withdrew their services, and the court revoked his bail, remanding him into custody, a decision which was later overturned by the full court on 5 November 2021 ([paras 3–5]).

On 29 November 2021, Maritz filed an application for the recusal of Naidoo J, asserting that he had a reasonable apprehension of bias against her, which he based on four grounds: her alleged unilateral revocation of his bail, her status as a Gender-Based Violence (GBV) activist, her request for a victim impact report, and her communication with Judge Daniso regarding his case ([para 6]). His application was dismissed by the high court, leading to this appeal ([paras 6–8]).

I – Issues

The primary issues raised in this appeal revolve around:

  1. Whether Judge Naidoo's refusal to recuse herself constituted a reasonable apprehension of bias against the appellant.
  2. Whether the procedural and legal grounds cited by Maritz were sufficient to establish a basis for recusal ([paras 6, 12, 32]).

R – Rules

The legal framework surrounding recusal is grounded in the right to a fair trial as enshrined in section 35(3) of the Constitution of South Africa, which guarantees every accused individual the right to a fair trial and access to an impartial tribunal ([para 8]).

Key principles established in judicial precedence highlight the presumption of judicial impartiality and the criteria for evaluating bias is found in President of the Republic of South Africa v South African Rugby Football Union ([1999] ZACC 9) which stipulates that the test for bias is whether a reasonable, objective, and informed person would apprehend that the judicial officer has failed to maintain an impartial mind ([para 11]). The court also reaffirmed the need for a cumulative examination of the complaints against the judge while ensuring that judicial experience and the oath taken by judges are taken into account ([paras 10, 32]).

A – Application

The court analyzed the allegations made against Judge Naidoo rigorously.

  1. Revocation of Bail: Maritz argued that the unilateral revocation of his bail was irrational and reflected bias. However, the court noted that such a revocation can be appealed and that misapplication of the law does not equate to bias. The presiding judge’s authority to revoke bail was upheld, negating Maritz’s claims of unfair treatment ([paras 14–16]).
  2. Gender-Based Violence Activism: Maritz’s assertion regarding the judge being a GBV activist was dismissed. The court clarified that a judge's involvement in social issues does not inherently imply bias. Testimonies regarding the presence of a teddy bear, symbolizing support for GBV causes, were deemed inadequate to substantiate claims of bias ([paras 17–20]).
  3. Request for Victim Impact Report: The request for a victim impact report was described as procedurally normal. The high court held that such reports are typically requested by the prosecution, and Naidoo J’s alleged request was not substantiated by credible evidence ([paras 21–27]).
  4. Communication with Daniso J: The court found that the communication between Naidoo J and Daniso J was appropriate and part of judicial protocol, especially in light of a family emergency that prevented Naidoo J from hearing the case herself. This did not indicate any bias or impropriety ([paras 28–30]).

Ultimately, the court concluded that the cumulative effect of the appellant’s complaints lacked the required merit to substantiate a reasonable apprehension of bias ([paras 32–33]). The court reiterated the need for individual scrutiny of each complaint rather than a collective assessment.

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal, by dismissing Maritz’s appeal, upheld the lower court's decision as there was insufficient evidence to establish a reasonable apprehension of bias against Judge Naidoo. The court’s judgment reaffirmed the principles of the presumption of impartiality of judges and the high threshold required to prove bias. The ruling underscores the importance of maintaining judicial integrity and the processes that protect it, thereby fostering public confidence in the judicial system.

In summary, the appeal was dismissed as unmeritorious, with implications for future recusal applications requiring clear and objective evidence of bias ([para 34]).
