Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note  
**Case Title:** Fourie v FirstRand Bank Ltd (578/2012) [2012] ZASCA 119 (18 September 2012)  
**Coram:** Brand, Lewis, Bosielo, Shongwe, and Theron JJA  
**Heard:** 27 August 2012  
**Delivered:** 18 September 2012  

### F – Facts  
[1] The appellant, Conrad Fourie, is an accountant who worked for Mr. Francois du Preez, an auditor based in Polokwane. The first respondent, FirstRand Bank Ltd, trading as Wesbank, initiated legal proceedings against both Fourie and Du Preez in the North Gauteng High Court, claiming approximately R10 million, based on Section 424 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973 as well as the actio legis Aquiliae ([1]–[2]). 

[2] Following du Preez's tragic death during the trial, Jacobus Spangenberg NOS was appointed as the executor of his estate and substituted as the second respondent for the action. The trial concluded with Southwood J finding Fourie liable under Section 424 for the debts of the vast, but insolvent, entity known as Supreme Car ([1]–[2]).

[3] The claim arose from Fourie's actions while acting as a financial manager of XHRS Investments 71 (Pty) Ltd, operating as Supreme Car, from August 2001 to April 2004. The company entered a Used Car Floor Plan Agreement with FirstRand to finance automotive purchases, which were to be sold back to the bank within a week. However, discrepancies were uncovered leading to the eventual cancellation of the agreement ([4]–[5]).

[4] The company was placed in provisional liquidation, revealing that substantial vehicles financed under the agreement were missing from the premises. Subsequently, Fourie was found to have prepared fraudulent financial statements asserting the financial health of Supreme Car which misled FirstRand into granting increasing credit facilities ([5]–[7]).

[5] Ultimately, Fourie was found responsible under Section 424 without requiring proof of a causal link between fraudulent conduct and the company's inability to pay ([6]).

### I – Issues  
[1] The primary issue in this case revolves around whether Fourie could be held personally liable for the debts of Supreme Car in accordance with Section 424 of the Companies Act, specifically:

1. Did Fourie's actions constitute fraudulent misrepresentation and recklessness in the conduct of the company's business ([5]–[6])?  
2. Was FirstRand required to establish a causal link between the fraudulent conduct and Supreme Car's inability to pay ([23]–[31])?  
3. Should the executor (Spangenberg) of du Preez be held liable in delict for the damages incurred by FirstRand, despite the assertion of a lack of causation between the misrepresentations and the incurred damages ([34]–[36])?

### R – Rules  
[1] Section 424(1) of the Companies Act 61 of 1973 stipulates that individuals who engage in reckless or fraudulent conduct while managing a company's business may be held personally responsible for its debts, regardless of formal corporate protections ([3]). The judgement references the principles of delictual liability, emphasizing the necessity of establishing factual and legal causation for damages incurred ([35]–[37]).

[2] The court examines the traditional requirement of causation in claims stemming from fraud, elaborating on case law that has historically set precedents for holding individuals accountable for the conduct of corporate entities ([23]–[31]).

### A – Application  
[1] Fourie was charged with fraudulent misconduct for preparing misleading financial statements of Supreme Car which led to the bank approving substantial credit increases. The High Court found him to have knowingly issued false representations, constituting a violation under Section 424 ([6]–[14]). Fourie contended his role was limited to cash flow management, but the court enhanced his liability by recognizing the scale of his actions in facilitating fraudulent activities ([8]–[12]).

[2] The Supreme Court clarifies that it is unnecessary to demonstrate a direct causal relationship between misrepresentations and the company’s insolvency under Section 424, as this provision is designed to protect creditors from the adverse effects of such conduct ([23]–[30]). The court supported this position by highlighting that the fraudulent financial information directly enabled increased credit to Supreme Car, contributing significantly to its ultimate insolvency ([39]).

[3] On appeal, it was established that Fourie's statements misled FirstRand and that this was sufficient for liability under both Section 424 and delict ([38]–[39]). The court reinforced that criminal acts causing loss similarly demand accountability, and aligning with precedent cases, it illustrated that the ultimate financial decisions made by FirstRand were induced by these nefarious actions ([15]–[23]).

### C – Conclusion  
[1] The Supreme Court of Appeal affirmed the decision of the court a quo, dismissing Fourie’s appeal with costs. It upheld FirstRand's cross-appeal against the executor, Spangenberg, determining he was jointly and severally liable. The culmination of £7,340,229.73 and interest was affirmed in the ruling ([41]).

[2] The ruling underlines the stringent accountability measures embedded within Section 424 of the Companies Act, illustrating that even without a direct causation requirement, individuals engaging in deceitful conduct in corporate governance may be held fully liable for the financial harm inflicted upon creditors. The implications of this case reinforce the significance of transparent financial practices in corporate governance and heighten the discourse surrounding personal liability in cases involving corporate fraud and recklessness ([30]–[31]). 

This case serves as a potent reminder of the judiciary's steadfast resolve to uphold corporate accountability and protect stakeholders from misleading actions affecting their financial interests.
