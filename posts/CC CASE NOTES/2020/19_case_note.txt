# FIRAC Case Note: Van der Walt v S [2020] ZACC 19

### F - Facts
[1] This case concerns an application for leave to appeal against the judgment and order of the High Court of South Africa, Mpumalanga Division. The applicant, Dr. Danie Van der Walt, an obstetrician and gynaecologist, was convicted in 2016 by the eMalahleni Regional Court of culpable homicide following the death of his patient, Ms. Pamela Noni Daweti, after childbirth. He was sentenced to five years' imprisonment. His initial appeal to the High Court was dismissed, and his subsequent request for special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal was rejected.

[2] In the Constitutional Court, Dr. Van der Walt contended that his conviction violated his fair trial rights, specifically under section 35(3)(i) of the Constitution, which guarantees the right to adduce and challenge evidence. He argued that the Regional Magistrate made critical rulings regarding the admissibility of evidence only after he had closed his case, leaving him uninformed about the evidence against him. The State’s case included expert testimony and various exhibits, with the Regional Magistrate admitting some but not others, impacting the trial’s fairness.

[3] The applicant also challenged the sentence imposed, contending that it was "shockingly inappropriate" and infringed his rights under section 12(1)(a) of the Constitution, which protects individuals from arbitrary deprivation of freedom. He argued for a differential approach in sentencing healthcare professionals compared to other offenders, citing their societal role in providing healthcare.

### I - Issues
[4] The primary legal questions raised before the Constitutional Court included:

1. Did the Regional Magistrate’s late ruling on the admissibility of evidence infringe the applicant’s right to a fair trial as protected under the Constitution?
2. Did the reliance on medical literature not presented as evidence further violate the applicant's right to adduce and challenge evidence?
3. Was the sentence imposed on the applicant constitutionally disproportionate or unjust?

### R - Rules
[5] The legal frameworks relevant to this case include provisions from the South African Constitution:

- **Section 35(3)(i)**: Guarantees every accused person the right to a fair trial, including the right to adduce and challenge evidence.
- **Section 12(1)(a)**: Prohibits arbitrary deprivation of freedom and mandates just cause for any such deprivation.

[6] Precedent established in cases like *S v Molimi* [2008] ZACC 2 and *S v Ndhlovu* [2002] ZASCA 70 emphasizes the necessity for timely rulings on evidence admissibility to ensure that an accused is aware of the case they must meet. The concept of a fair trial extends beyond formal compliance with procedural norms, reflecting substantive fairness.

### A - Application
[7] The court analyzed whether the Regional Magistrate’s decisions regarding the admissibility of evidence and reliance on extrajudicial sources constituted procedural irregularities that undermined the applicant's fair trial rights as described in constitutional jurisprudence.

[8] It was determined that the timing of the admissibility ruling left the applicant without knowledge of the full scope of the evidence against him. The court noted that an untimely ruling could hinder an accused's ability to make informed decisions regarding the presentation of their defense, paralleling findings in *Ndhlovu*, where the late admission of hearsay evidence was deemed prejudicial.

[9] Moreover, the Constitutional Court ruled that reliance on medical texts not presented during the trial deprived the applicant of the opportunity to interrogate or contest the information, further compromising his right to a fair trial as stipulated in section 35(3)(i). The court reasoned that this reliance constituted an infringement on the applicant's rights, aligning this case with the principles established in *Molimi*, where similarly late admissions and reliance on external material without the opportunity for contestation were deemed prejudicial.

[10] Regarding the question of an appropriate sentence, the court declined to engage with the specifics of sentencing for health professionals, asserting that a valid conviction must precede any penalty. The court reinforced that while healthcare practitioners hold a significant societal role, they are also held to a higher standard of care.

### C - Conclusion
[11] The Constitutional Court concluded that both the late determination of the admissibility of evidence and the reliance on unproven medical literature constituted serious breaches of the applicant’s fair trial rights, warranting the setting aside of the conviction.

[12] The court ordered:

1. Leave to appeal was granted.
2. The appeal was upheld, the High Court’s order was set aside, and Dr. Van der Walt’s conviction and sentence were annulled.  
3. The matter was referred to the Director of Public Prosecutions, Mpumalanga, for potential re-arraignment before a different Regional Magistrate.

[13] The judgment underscores the essential nature of procedural fairness in the judicial process and provides essential guidance for future cases involving admissibility of evidence and the rights of accused individuals within the South African legal framework.
