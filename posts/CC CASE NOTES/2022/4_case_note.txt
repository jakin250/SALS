Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# Minister of Finance v Afribusiness NPC [2022] ZACC 4 - FIRAC Case Note

## F – Facts
The case before the Constitutional Court of South Africa originated from an application by the Minister of Finance to appeal a ruling by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which had declared the Preferential Procurement Regulations of 2017 invalid. The applicant, the Minister, contended that the Supreme Court of Appeal erred in its findings regarding his powers to enact the regulations under the Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act 5 of 2000 (the Procurement Act) and section 217 of the Constitution ([1]–[12]).

The respondent, Afribusiness NPC, a non-profit organization representing the interests of businesses, argued that the Minister's regulations acted ultra vires—beyond his powers—as they subordinated a previously established scoring system embodied in section 2 of the Procurement Act to new pre-qualification criteria laid out in the regulations ([11]–[14]). Afribusiness sought a judicial review of the Minister’s conduct, claiming that the regulations disadvantaged their members. The High Court initially ruled in favor of Afribusiness, stating the regulations were invalid due to their inconsistency with existing statutory frameworks ([12]).

The SCA reversed this ruling, asserting the Minister had overstepped his authority by enacting regulations that contradicted the Procurement Act's framework, specifically by allowing pre-qualification criteria that disqualified tenderers without evaluation ([15]–[17]).

## I – Issues
The core issues examined by the Constitutional Court pertained to:

1. Jurisdiction: Whether the Court had the authority to review the order of the SCA ([18]).
2. The lawfulness of the 2017 Procurement Regulations: Whether the Minister acted within the scope of his regulatory powers as granted by the Procurement Act and the Constitution ([18]).
3. The validity of the pre-qualification criteria outlined in the 2017 Regulations, particularly whether they align with the requirements set forth in section 2 of the Procurement Act and section 217 of the Constitution ([18]).

## R – Rules
The following legal principles and provisions were central to the Court's analysis:

1. **Section 217 of the Constitution**: Mandates a procurement system that is fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective while allowing for preferences that protect or advance historically disadvantaged persons ([60]).
   
2. **Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act 5 of 2000**: Especially section 5, which authorizes the Minister to make regulations necessary to achieve the objects of the Procurement Act ([38]).
   
3. **Ultra vires Doctrine**: Public officials must operate within the bounds of the power granted to them by legislation. Any action taken beyond this scope is deemed unlawful ([39]-[40]).

4. **Legitimacy of Legislative Delegation**: Courts interpret legislation by considering the intention of the Legislature, ensuring that subordinate legislation (like the regulations) remains consistent with the enabling statute ([43]).

## A – Application
The Court examined whether the Minister's regulations contravened the existing statutory and constitutional frameworks regarding public procurement. In evaluating the Minister's regulatory authority, it determined the limits imposed by sections 2 and 5 of the Procurement Act. 

### Minister's Powers
The Court held that while the Minister possessed wide-ranging powers to create regulations to achieve the objectives of the Procurement Act, the enactment of pre-qualification criteria violated the established framework. It was emphasized that the regulatory intent must align with the statutory guidance provided in section 2 of the Procurement Act, which requires that all tenders be evaluated based on a specified scoring system before applying any pre-qualification ([46]-[76]).

The Minister's claim that the regulations were necessary or expedient for correcting past economic imbalances was not sufficient to justify the deviations from the established points system outlined in section 2 of the procurement process. The introduction of the 2017 Regulations was viewed as fundamentally altering the intended scoring mechanism and placing undue emphasis on race and ownership pre-qualifications, which was inconsistent with the principles set forth in the Procurement Act and Constitution ([79] - [87]).

### Interpretation of the Regulations
The Court assessed the specific provisions of the impugned regulations. It concluded they did indeed create a preliminary disqualification process that was inconsistent with the legislative framework as outlined in section 2 of the Procurement Act. This deviation not only undermined the intent of existing legislation but also contradicted the procurement system's foundation that prioritizes fair competition and equal opportunity for all bidders ([86]-[92]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court concluded that the 2017 Preferential Procurement Regulations were indeed invalid as they contradicted both the Procurement Act and section 217 of the Constitution. The Minister acted ultra vires by promulgating regulations that allowed for pre-qualification criteria to disqualify bidders without subjecting their tenders to the established points system.

Consequently, the application by Fidelity Services Group and the South African National Security Employers Association for leave to intervene was dismissed, along with their application for direct access. The appeal was upheld and the order of the Supreme Court of Appeal was set aside. Each party was directed to bear its own costs. The ruling reinforces the importance of adhering to established legal frameworks and the need for transparency and fairness in public procurement ([94] - [125]).
