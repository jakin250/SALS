Somali Association of South Africa v Limpopo Department of Economic Development, Environment and Tourism (48/2014) ZASCA 143 (26 September 2014)

It was noted that many of these individuals, unable to secure employment due to various barriers, such as language difficulties and xenophobia, relied on self-employment to survive ([5]–[6]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2014

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

In the case of Somali Association of South Africa v Limpopo Department of Economic Development, Environment and Tourism ([1]), the appellant parties, primarily representing Somali and Ethiopian nationals in South Africa, challenged the decisions of several government entities regarding their ability to engage in self-employment as spaza or tuck-shop traders. The appellants, including registered non-profit organizations and recognized refugees/asylum seekers, claimed that their Constitutional rights were infringed upon due to police operations (notably Operation Hardstick) that targeted their businesses. The appellants argued that they had been treated unequally compared to South African citizens, specifically being denied licenses necessary for trading because of their refugee or asylum seeker status ([1]–[2]).

It was noted that many of these individuals, unable to secure employment due to various barriers, such as language difficulties and xenophobia, relied on self-employment to survive ([5]–[6]). The appellants initially sought a declaratory order that would affirm their rights to apply for and renew business licenses and prevent authorities from shutting down their operations based on discriminatory grounds ([3]–[4]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue centers on whether asylum seekers and refugees in South Africa have the right to apply for trading licenses in a manner equivalent to that available to South African citizens, particularly in regard to the interpretation of their rights under the Constitution and relevant legislation ([1]–[4]). The specific issues to be considered include:

  1. Does the Constitution permit the denial of trading licenses to asylum seekers and refugees based on their status?
  2. Are the actions taken under Operation Hardstick, which involved the confiscation of property and the closure of businesses operated by refugees and asylum seekers, lawful?
  3. What are the implications of international obligations and domestic law concerning the rights and dignities of refugees and asylum seekers in South Africa? ([21]–[28]).

R – Rules

The Court relied on several key legal principles, including:

  • Section 22 of the South African Constitution, which grants citizens the right to choose their trade, occupation, or profession freely but was interpreted by the High Court as limiting this right to South African citizens only ([21]).
  • Section 10 of the Constitution, which addresses the right to dignity and its intersection with the rights of refugees and asylum seekers ([22]).
  • Refugees Act 130 of 1998, particularly Section 27(f), which permits refugees to seek employment but does not explicitly extend these rights to self-employment ([26]).
  • Prior case law, notably Minister of Home Affairs & others v Watchenuka ([25]), which recognized the importance of not rendering asylum seekers destitute by denying them the right to work or self-support.

The Constitutional Court's judgments in cases of Union of Refugee Women ([24]) and Watchenuka were significant in shaping the interpretation of refugee rights and the applicable legal framework.

A – Application

In determining the case, the Supreme Court of Appeal critically analyzed the implications of constitutional rights juxtaposed against statutory restrictions. The Court observed that:

  1. Dignity and Survival: The Court emphasized that denying refugees the right to self-employment fundamentally impacts their dignity and ability to survive. It noted that if refugees are rendered destitute through a lack of opportunities to support themselves, their dignity is compromised ([33]–[43]).
  2. Legal Interpretation: The Court found that the prior ruling by the North Gauteng High Court had an overly restrictive interpretation of both the Constitution and the Refugees Act. It revisited the classifications in the legislation concerning licenses, affirming that there was no explicit prohibition on refugees and asylum seekers obtaining licenses or permits ([42]).
  3. International Obligations: The Court noted South Africa's commitments under various international agreements, underscoring that refugees lawfully in the country should not be denied rights akin to those enjoyed by citizens. This analysis included interpretations of articles from the 1951 United Nations Convention on Refugees, particularly regarding fair treatment in employment ([37]–[38]).

The ruling underscored that the legislative framework did not support a blanket ban on licenses for asylum seekers and that such an interpretation neglected the realities these individuals face in their plight for survival ([43]).

C – Conclusion

In a unanimous decision, the Supreme Court of Appeal set aside the High Court's order. It declared that asylum seekers and refugees are entitled to:

  1. Apply for new business licenses under relevant statutes.
  2. Renew existing licenses and operate tuck-shops or spaza shops ([46]).

Additionally, the Court deemed the closure of businesses operated by refugees as unlawful and invalid, underscoring the jurisdictional responsibilities of the State in addressing the rights of vulnerable populations amidst its regulatory actions. The Respondents were ordered to pay the costs of the appeal, signaling a substantial commitment to uphold the rights of asylum seekers and refugees within the legal framework of South Africa ([46]). The decision places significant emphasis on the protective nature of the Constitution and affirms the necessity of compassion and humanity in addressing the challenges faced by those seeking refuge and a better life.
