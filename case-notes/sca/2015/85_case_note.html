Supreme Court of Appeal

Merafong City Local Municipality v AngloGold Ashanti Ltd (20265/2014) [2015] ZASCA 85

AngloGold operates three mines—Tautona, Mponeng, and Savuka—since the mid-1940s, necessitating large volumes of water for both industrial and domestic purposes.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 [2015] ZASCA 85

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case revolves around a dispute between Merafong City Local Municipality (Appellant) and AngloGold Ashanti Ltd (Respondent) concerning a ruling made by the Minister of Water Affairs and Forestry under section 8(9) of the Water Services Act 108 of 1997 (the Act). The Minister's ruling prevented Merafong from levying incremental surcharges on water supplied for industrial use to mines operated by AngloGold within Merafong's jurisdiction. The Minister mandated negotiations between Merafong, AngloGold, and Rand Water to establish a reasonable tariff for domestic water supply ([1]).

AngloGold operates three mines—Tautona, Mponeng, and Savuka—since the mid-1940s, necessitating large volumes of water for both industrial and domestic purposes. Historically, AngloGold has sourced its water from Rand Water, which is a state organ mandated to provide water services. The dispute mechanism prescribed by the Act requires municipalities to approve water services for industrial use ([2]–[5]).

In December 1997, Parliament enacted the Water Services Act to structure water service provision in alignment with constitutional mandates. Section 6 of the Act prohibits the provision of water services from anyone other than a designated water service provider, and section 7 sets forth similar restrictions for industrial use ([4]–[5]). Merafong received water authority status in July 2003 and subsequently requested mines to apply for water supply approval under the Act. However, when Merafong proposed tariffs exceeding those of Rand Water, AngloGold appealed to the Minister, who ruled in favor of AngloGold by asserting that the significant tariff increase was unjustified ([6]–[8]).

Over the following years, Merafong contested the validity of the Minister's ruling without pursuing formal judicial review and continued to impose surcharges despite a clear directive from the Minister ([9]–[10]). Eventually, AngloGold implemented legal action to compel compliance with the Minister's ruling, leading to the High Court granting AngloGold the relief sought ([12]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues in this case include:

  1. Whether Merafong was entitled to disregard the Minister’s ruling on the tariff scheme set forth under the Water Services Act.
  2. The validity of the Minister’s order under section 8(9) of the Water Services Act and whether it bound Merafong as a local authority.
  3. The appropriateness of the procedural actions taken by both Merafong and AngloGold in respect to the compliance with administrative rulings and disputes ([1]–[17]).

R – Rules

Key legal provisions and precedents that informed the court's decision include:

  1. Water Services Act 108 of 1997: Sections 6 and 7 impose requirements and limitations for water services via designated providers, including the necessity for municipal approval for both general and industrial water services. Section 8 grants the Minister the authority to override municipal decisions if deemed unreasonable ([5]–[6]).
  2. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA): Under section 6, parties aggrieved by administrative decisions—including but not limited to tariff setting—are entitled to seek judicial review ([12]).
  3. Legal Principles Established in Judicial Precedents: The court referenced the case of MEC for Health, Eastern Cape v Kirland Investments (Pty) Ltd to underscore that administrative decisions, even if deemed unlawful, remain effective until overturned by a competent authority ([15]).
  4. Principle of Legality: The ruling emphasized that an organ of state cannot unilaterally disregard another’s administrative action without proper legal recourse, reinforcing the principle of legality in administrative affairs ([17]).

A – Application

The court analyzed the interplay between Merafong’s local authority powers and the Minister's oversight role under the Act. It established that Merafong failed to properly challenge the Minister’s ruling through judicial review, effectively breaching the principle of legality by choosing to disregard the ruling rather than seek a formal annulment ([15]–[17]).

The court ruled that the Minister's decision, even if deemed ultra vires, had substantive legal consequences that Merafong was obligated to recognize. The claims by Merafong that the Minister exceeded her authority were insufficient in light of the established validity of her ruling until it was formally revoked or set aside by a court ([15]).

Furthermore, the court addressed the argument raised by Merafong regarding the alleged lack of jurisdictional grounds for the Minister’s ruling, affirming that the absence of jurisdictional facts does not render an administrative act null. Instead, it is a matter of reviewability ([16]–[17]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the lower court’s ruling, reinforcing the principle that governmental entities must adhere to legal decisions rendered by statutory authorities. Merafong’s appeal was dismissed, and it was held that the Minister’s ruling was binding until overturned by court proceedings. This decision underscores the necessity for municipalities to operate within the confines of statutory authority and respect for administrative law as set forth by legislative frameworks ([18]).

References

  • Water Services Act 108 of 1997.
  • Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000.
  • MEC for Health, Eastern Cape v Kirland Investments (Pty) Ltd 2014 (3) SA 481 (CC).
  • Oudekraal Estates (Pty) Ltd v City of Cape Town & others 2004 (6) SA 222 (SCA).
  • City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality v Cable City (Pty) Ltd 2010 (3) SA 589 (SCA).

This case serves as a critical reminder of the necessity for municipal adherence to legislative directives and the implications of failing to respect administrative processes.
