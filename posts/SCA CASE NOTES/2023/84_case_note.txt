Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title:** The Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service v Free State Development Corporation (1222/2021) [2023] ZASCA 84 (31 May 2023)

**Coram:** Dambuza AP, Zondi JA, Weiner JA, Mali AJJA, Unterhalter AJJA

**Heard:** 22 February 2023

**Delivered:** 31 May 2023

---

## F – Facts

The case revolves around an appeal concerning the jurisdiction of the Free State Tax Court, which granted the Free State Development Corporation (the taxpayer) the permission to withdraw its statement of grounds of appeal and submit an amended statement in response to additional assessments imposed by the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (SARS) ([1]–[2]). The Tax Court allowed the taxpayer to amend its grounds of appeal within a specified timeframe, which SARS contested on the grounds that the amendment introduced a new objection that breached Tax Court Rule (TCR) 10(3), prohibiting the introduction of grounds not raised in the original objection ([3]).

The dispute pertains to VAT liabilities related to funding agreements between the taxpayer and the Department of Trade and Industry (DTI), which allocated substantial sums for the establishment of the Maluti-a-Phofung Special Economic Zone (SEZ) ([12], [19], [26]). The taxpayer had claimed that certain supplies were zero-rated in its VAT returns, asserting that it acted merely as a conduit for funds from DTI ([20], [24], [27]). SARS contested this characterization, arguing that the payments received from DTI were indeed taxable supplies subject to standard VAT rates ([21]).

The taxpayer had initially filed an objection to the assessments, asserting that the transactions were zero-rated under sections of the VAT Act. After receiving a second legal opinion suggesting that the transactions did not constitute a taxable supply, the taxpayer sought to amend its grounds of appeal ([32]–[33]). Ultimately, the Tax Court found that the amended grounds were sufficiently foreshadowed in the original objection and granted the amendment, prompting SARS to appeal the decision ([41]).

---

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues presented in this case include:

1. **Appealability of the Tax Court's order**: Is the order of the Tax Court granting permission for the taxpayer to amend its statement of grounds of appeal considered appealable under the applicable legal framework? ([5]).

2. **Jurisdiction of the Tax Court**: Did the Tax Court possess the jurisdiction to allow an amendment to the statement of grounds of appeal, considering whether the amended ground constituted a new ground of objection not previously raised? ([12]).

3. **Nature of the amended grounds**: Do the amended grounds of appeal constitute matters that were adequately foreshadowed in the initial objections filed by the taxpayer? ([41]).

---

## R – Rules

The applicable legal principles in this case stem from the following statutes and rulings:

1. **Tax Administration Act 28 of 2011 (TAA)**: Sections 107, 117(3), and 129 outline the powers of the Tax Court concerning disputes and procedural matters ([4]).

2. **Tax Court Rules (TCR)**: Specifically, Rule 10(3) which prohibits a taxpayer from introducing new grounds of objection in amended statements that were not raised in the original notice of objection ([3],[10]).

3. **Case Law Precedents**:
   - *Macsteel Tube and Pipe v Vowles* ([5]) outlines the principles of appealability concerning interlocutory orders.
   - *The Commissioner for South African Revenue Services v Airports Company for South Africa* ([7]), which discusses the limitations of amendments in tax objections within the context of the TAA.
   - *Zweni v Minister of Law and Order* ([6]) establishes criteria for determining whether a judicial decision is definitive and thus appealable.

---

## A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed whether the Tax Court's decision to grant the amendment was indeed appealable and whether the Tax Court had acted within its jurisdiction. The court distinguished between decisions based on jurisdictional competence and those that do not bear such finality ([10],[11]).

1. **On Appealability**: 
   The court determined that the Tax Court's order was indeed appealable due to the competence of the Tax Court being questioned, aligning with principles established in *TWK Agriculture Holdings v Hoogveld Boerderybeleggings* ([10]). The decision was considered definitive because if the Tax Court had overstepped its jurisdiction, it could render its actions null ([10]).

2. **Jurisdictional Authority of the Tax Court**: 
   The court evaluated whether allowing the amendment would contravene TCR 10(3) and if the amended grounds were effectively new. It's critical to note that the basis of the taxpayer's objection had always pertained to the character of the transactions as either taxable supplies or zero-rated supplies ([42],[40]). The amendment, therefore, was seen as a clarification of previously raised issues rather than the introduction of entirely new grounds ([41]).

3. **Nature of the Amended Grounds**: 
   The Supreme Court emphasized that legal conclusions could evolve over the course of litigation, especially in response to new legal opinions. The taxpayer’s assertion that funds received were not tied to any supply was a repositioning of the inquiry around the same set of facts and did not contravene TCR 10(3), as it fell within the scope of the original objection's substance ([32],[46],[48]).

---

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal by SARS, affirming the Tax Court's decision to grant the amendment of the statement of grounds of appeal. The ruling emphasized that amendments of this nature are essential for the resolution of legal disputes, ensuring that properly assessed VAT liabilities align with the legislative intent of tax law ([49]). The court concluded that allowing such amendments would serve the interests of justice over technical adherence to procedural rules where no substantive prejudice would arise from the allowance ([41], [48]). Consequently, the taxpayer's ability to clarify its stance on classification under VAT law was upheld, reinforcing a principle that legal interpretations should reflect actual economic realities rather than merely procedural formality. 

The order included a directive for SARS to bear the costs of the appeal, reflecting the court's recognition of the taxpayer's legitimate right to clarify its legal position effectively ([49]).
