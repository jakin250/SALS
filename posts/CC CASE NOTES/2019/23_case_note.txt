# FIRAC Case Note: General Council of the Bar of South Africa v Jiba and Others

**Court**: Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Case Number**: CCT 192/18  
**Neutral Citation**: General Council of the Bar of South Africa v Jiba and Others [2019] ZACC 23  
**Judgment**: Jafta J (unanimous)  
**Heard on**: 14 March 2019  
**Decided on**: 27 June 2019  

---

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–17])

The case concerns the fitness of three advocates—Ms Nomgcobo Jiba, Mr Lawrence Mrwebi, and Mr Sibongile Mzinyathi—to practice law in South Africa. The General Council of the Bar of South Africa (GCB), the applicant, instituted disciplinary proceedings against them based on their alleged unfitness stemming from misconduct in several high-profile litigations.

1. **Initial Proceedings** ([paras 6–12]): Ms Jiba and Mr Mrwebi's conduct came under scrutiny due to their handling of cases that involved the withdrawal of charges against General Richard Mdluli and the authorization of charges against Major General Johan Booysen. Both advocates were criticized for failing to comply with court rules, resulting in court delays and adverse findings against them for dishonesty and misconduct.
  
2. **High Court Findings** ([paras 18–24]): The GCB sought to have the respondents' names struck from the roll of advocates based on the High Court’s findings which proved misconduct by Jiba and Mrwebi, but not by Mzinyathi. The High Court held that Jiba and Mrwebi had acted dishonestly and should be struck from the roll, while Mzinyathi was found not guilty of misconduct.

3. **Appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal** ([paras 26–29]): Jiba and Mrwebi appealed the High Court’s ruling. The SCA overturned the decision against Jiba, finding that misconduct was not established, while it upheld findings against Mr Mrwebi but modified the order from striking him off to suspending him. Mzinyathi’s favorable ruling was not contested.

4. **GCB's Application to the Constitutional Court** ([paras 30–32]): The GCB's application sought leave to appeal primarily regarding the costs orders and challenges to the SCA's judgments which it deemed flawed. The application also posited that significant legal and constitutional questions were invoked regarding the standards for advocates.

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 33–38])

The main legal issues that the Court addressed were:

1. **Jurisdiction**: Whether the Constitutional Court had jurisdiction to hear the case based on the GCB's claims and the nature of the appeal ([paras 35–38]).
  
2. **Standard of Misconduct**: The implications of varying standards for fitness and propriety of advocates in differing judicial assessments, especially in light of the conflicting decisions from the High Court and SCA on Jiba's and Mrwebi’s conduct ([paras 54–56]).

3. **Cost Orders**: The implications of the costs orders against the GCB, which traditionally cannot be held liable for costs due to their role in upholding the integrity of the profession, unless they acted irresponsibly ([paras 63–67]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 39–49])

1. **Admission of Advocates Act 74 of 1964**: This legislation outlines the conditions under which an advocate can be admitted to practice, including the standards of honesty and integrity ([para 3]).

2. **Case Law**:
   - **Kekana v Society of Advocates of South Africa** ([para 1]): Establishes the high ethical standards expected from legal practitioners.
   - **Ex parte Swain** ([para 2]): Highlights the requirement for courts to rely implicitly on the truthfulness of advocates.
   - **Gcaba v Minister for Safety and Security** ([para 39]): Outlines the approach to determine jurisdiction based on pleadings.
   - **Biowatch Trust v Registrar Genetic Resources** ([para 66]): Distinguishes costs in public interest litigation involving state actors.

3. **Three-Stage Test for Fitness**: Established in previous case law, which assesses an advocate's conduct through weighing evidence of misconduct, determining fitness for practice, and deciding the appropriate disciplinary action ([para 20]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 50–60])

The Constitutional Court examined the factual record carefully:

1. **Jurisdictional Analysis**: The Court confirmed that the GCB’s case did not present a constitutional issue under section 167(3)(b) of the Constitution. It emphasized that simply enforcing the Admission Act's provisions does not engage constitutional principles that would grant it jurisdiction ([paras 60–67]).
  
2. **Evaluation of Misconduct**: The court determined that the discrepancies in the judgments reflected differing factual assessments rather than constitutional points of law; hence, the Court declined to intervene in the fact-finding or merit assessments made by the lower courts ([paras 50–59]).

3. **Costs Orders Reassessment**: The Court ruled that the GCB should not bear costs as its actions were taken in the public interest, aligning with established understandings of advocacy in legal and constitutional contexts ([paras 62–68]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 69–70])

1. **Holding**: The application for leave to appeal regarding the merits was dismissed, with the Constitutional Court confirming that it did not have jurisdiction over the procedural decisions of the lower courts regarding factual assessments. However, it granted leave concerning the costs orders, overthrowing the High Court’s and SCA's previous costs rulings.

2. **Ratio Decidendi**: The court reinforced the principle that the integrity of the legal profession must be upheld while delineating the boundaries of the jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court, emphasizing the importance of established ethical standards without delving into mere factual disagreements from lower courts ([paras 50–60]).

3. **Implications**: This case reinforces the standards that advocates must uphold in South Africa while also clarifying the landscape of judicial discretion about costs in disciplinary proceedings against practitioners. Further, it showcases the limitations of the Constitutional Court’s jurisdiction when it pertains strictly to issues grounded in the interpretation of legislative provisions rather than constitutional issues.

---

The case underscores the importance of maintaining the integrity and ethical standards of the legal profession while delineating the jurisdictional boundaries of South African courts, particularly in the context of appellate review concerning factual determinations.
