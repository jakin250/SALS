Supreme Court of Appeal

Gawa Cassiem v The State [2001] ZASCA 28 (SA)

On 24 July 1994, Sergeant John King of the South African Police Services discovered Cassiem selling new clothing at a local flea market.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2001 [2001] ZASCA 28 (SA)

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The appellant, Gawa Cassiem, was convicted on nine counts of theft in the Wynberg Regional Court and sentenced to five years' imprisonment under section 276(1)(i) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977. An additional sentence of two years was suspended for four years on the condition that she not be convicted of theft during the suspension period. The magistrate recommended that Cassiem not be released under correctional supervision until she had served half of her sentence ([1]).

On 24 July 1994, Sergeant John King of the South African Police Services discovered Cassiem selling new clothing at a local flea market. Each item bore tags from prominent stores such as Woolworths, Edgars, and Foschini, raising suspicions as to their origin. Upon questioning, Cassiem initially claimed the clothing belonged to her daughter but later changed her story multiple times, stating they belonged to her deceased husband ([2]–[4]).

After the police acquired a warrant based on her evasive responses, they searched her residence and discovered a significant amount of newly-tagged clothing valued at R59,831.52 ([3]–[6]). This clothing was presented in different locations within her home, alongside other valuable items, and was confirmed by store complainants as belonging to their respective stores ([6]).

I – Issues

The case presented several critical legal issues:

  1. Whether the state met its burden of proof in establishing that theft had occurred.
  2. Whether the appellant was aware that the clothing in her possession was stolen.
  3. The alternative question regarding whether the appellant could be convicted under sections 36 and 37 of the General Law Amendment Act 62 of 1955, especially concerning the reverse onus issue highlighted by previous case law ([7]).

R – Rules

The legal framework for this case primarily revolves around the definition and elements of the crime of theft, particularly the concept of "continuing crime" where theft persists as long as the stolen property remains in the possession of the thief or an accomplice ([8]). The burden of proof lies with the state, and the inferences regarding knowledge of the stolen nature of goods can be drawn from circumstantial evidence ([8]).

Importantly, prior rulings such as S v Manamela ([2000] ZACC 5) are pertinent to the application of reverse onus provisions in relation to the sufficiency of proof in theft cases ([7]).

A – Application

In addressing the first issue, the court determined that the State sufficiently demonstrated theft through the evidence of the clothing’s tags and Cassiem's inconsistent statements, concluding that the items were likely stolen from the respective stores, even if specific complainants could not precisely trace the stolen goods to their particular outlets ([9]).

Regarding whether Cassiem was aware the clothing was stolen, the court found substantial incriminating circumstantial evidence. Cassiem's multiple changes in her claims about ownership and her initial non-cooperation with the police were viewed as strong indicators of her awareness. The court observed that it would be implausible that a person receiving numerous bags of new clothing from her husband would not suspect their origin, particularly when her husband advised her not to inquire further about them ([10]).

The alternative provisions of sections 36 and 37 were deemed unnecessary for consideration, with the court emphasizing that if theft was proven directly, those sections need not apply ([7]).

On the matter of sentencing under section 276(1)(i), the court noted that the magistrate had incorrectly incorporated the suspended sentence into the overall length of incarceration, which should not exceed five years. This procedural misstep rendered the sentence inappropriate ([11]–[12]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal unanimously dismissed the appeal against the convictions, affirming that the State had successfully proven theft and that Cassiem was aware of the stolen nature of the clothing in her possession. However, the appeal against the sentence was successful due to the magistrate's misinterpretation of the statutory framework, specifically regarding the totality of imprisonment. Consequently, the sentence was modified to five years of imprisonment without the additional suspended sentence, thereby aligning with the legal provisions stipulated in section 276(1)(i) ([14]).

This case highlights essential principles in South African theft law, particularly regarding possession, knowledge, and statutory sentence limitations, contributing significantly to both procedural understandings and substantive criminal jurisprudence.
