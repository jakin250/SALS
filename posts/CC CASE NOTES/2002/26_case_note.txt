Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2002

# Case Note: CCT 19/02 PHOEBUS APOLLO AVIATION CC v THE MINISTER OF SAFETY AND SECURITY

## FIRAC Structure

### F – Facts

The case originates from a robbery on a November night in 1998, during which the appellant, Phoebus Apollo Aviation CC, lost a significant sum of money. The robbery involved an armed gang that gained access to the home of the appellant’s managing member. Following this incident, police investigations led to the father of two of the robbers, where part of the stolen money was traced. However, it was discovered that three police officers, who were not involved in the original investigation and were stationed elsewhere, had unlawfully taken the money the day before the investigating officer arrived ([paras 1-2]).

Despite the initial High Court ruling in favor of the appellant, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) reversed the decision. The appellant sought to have the original award reinstated, grounding its argument in constitutional provisions regarding property rights and the obligations of the police service under the South African Constitution ([para 3]).

### I – Issues

The central issue presented to the Constitutional Court was whether the Minister of Safety and Security could be held vicariously liable for the actions of the three police officers, who had engaged in theft under the guise of their police duties. This raised significant questions about the interplay between criminal acts conducted by police officers and the constitutional and common law principles governing vicarious liability ([paras 1, 6]).

### R – Rules

The Court’s deliberations were heavily influenced by the constitutional protections afforded to property rights under section 25 of the South African Constitution, which prohibits arbitrary deprivation of property. It also referenced section 39(2), which mandates courts to promote the Bill of Rights’ spirit in developing common law, and section 205(3), outlining the police's obligations to prevent crime and protect property ([paras 3-4, 7]).

The jurisprudence surrounding vicarious liability, primarily established in cases such as *Minister of Police v Rabie* and *Minister of Law and Order v Ngobo*, served to delineate the standards under which the state could be held accountable for the actions of its employees, notably distinguishing between acts performed within the scope of employment and those executed for personal gain ([para 8]).

### A – Application

The Court found that the appellant's reliance on its constitutional property rights was misplaced, as its rights were never directly infringed by the state; the loss originated from criminal acts by private individuals—the robbers. The subsequent misappropriation of funds by the police officers did not constitute an act of state that could engage liability under constitutional protections ([paras 4-6]).

The distinction between the police officers’ alleged misconduct and their official duties was pivotal. The officers acted outside their designated roles, solely pursuing personal goals disguised as police work. The Court determined that their actions did not take place "in the course of their employment," thereby absolving the Minister of Safety and Security from vicarious liability ([paras 7-8]) Thus, the appellant’s argument that constitutional duties imposed by the law necessitated a finding of liability was unsubstantiated, as no evidence indicated that the officers were acting with official intentions during the theft ([para 10]).

Furthermore, the Court clarified that even if the SCA had misapplied the test for vicarious liability, that would not typically invoke constitutional jurisdiction unless a broader constitutional matter was at stake. The crux of the appellant’s complaint rested on factual disputes rather than constitutional interpretations or rights ([para 9]).

### C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Constitutional Court dismissed the appeal, confirming that the appellant could not invoke constitutional grounds for vicarious liability claims against the state due to the independent criminal acts of the police officers. The Court expressed empathy for the appellant’s plight, reflecting on systemic failures in the criminal justice system, yet ultimately held that these observations did not alter the existing legal principles concerning the liability of the state ([paras 10-12]).

The ruling solidified the boundaries of state liability in complex cases involving police misconduct, reinforcing the delineation between public duty and individual wrongful acts, and emphasized that claims of negligence or wrongful conduct must be grounded in constitutional or statutory mandates to possess any significant legal weight.
