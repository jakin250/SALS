Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# Case Note: City of Johannesburg v Ad Outpost (Pty) Ltd ([2012] ZASCA 40)

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts

In **City of Johannesburg v Ad Outpost (Pty) Ltd**, case no 55/11, the respondent, Ad Outpost (Pty) Ltd, applied for permission to continue using two outdoor advertising billboards situated alongside major roadways in Gauteng (the **'Sandown billboard'** and **'Kelvin View billboard'**) which had been in use for over ten years ([1]–[2]). The original permission granted in 2001 lapsed in 2004 due to non-renewal, but the respondent continued to operate the billboards without objection from the City of Johannesburg (the **first appellant**) until correspondence in 2006 indicated that the City recognized the lapse and requested removal of the billboards unless a new application was submitted ([1]–[5]).

Subsequently, applications for renewal were made in March 2007 under the **2001 Advertising Signs and Hoardings By-laws**, which replaced the earlier **1999 by-laws** that initially governed billboard usage ([6]–[10]). The applications were ultimately rejected, citing a requirement that billboards must be at least 200 meters away from overhead traffic signs, a stipulation not included in the 2001 by-laws ([8]–[9]). An appeal to the City Manager (the **second appellant**) was dismissed, leading the respondent to seek judicial review in the South Gauteng High Court, where it won a decision granting the requested permission retroactively ([2], [10]).

### I – Issues

The core issue on appeal revolved around whether the City of Johannesburg's (first appellant’s) decisions to refuse permission were valid given that the 2001 by-laws had been repealed by the 2008 by-laws during the proceedings. The appellants contended that the decisions were correctly made under the existing regulations, while the respondent argued that its applications should be reconsidered under the more permissive terms of the 2001 by-laws ([12], [10], [16]). An ancillary issue also arose regarding the interpretation of the applicability of the subsequent 2009 by-laws ([12]–[22]).

### R – Rules

The legal framework for this case includes:

1. **The 1999 By-laws**: Initially governed billboard placement which prohibited billboards within specific distances from traffic signs unless certain conditions were met ([3]).

2. **The 2001 By-laws**, which removed the explicit distance requirement, allowing discretion to approve billboards while considering traffic safety ([6]).

3. **The 2008 By-laws**, which introduced stricter prohibitions consistent with previous regulations but without the explicit distance requirement found in the original framework ([10], [16]).

4. **The 2009 By-laws**, which reasserted prohibitions on billboard placements near ramps and signs with an overarching discretion provision still present ([13], [22]).

Relevant case law guiding the court's decision included **Noah v Union National South British Insurance Co Ltd (1979)** and **Oudekraal Estates (Pty) Ltd v City of Cape Town (2004)**, affirming that once a decision is declared invalid, it is treated as if it never existed ([19]–[22]).

### A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed both the procedural and substantive aspects of the case. The court recognized that, due to the passage of time and repeals in laws, the decisions made by the City of Johannesburg with regard to the respond to the applications were invalid ([10]). Specifically, the court emphasized that the 2001 by-laws would need to be applied for reconsideration as the permits had lapsed before the 2008 and later by-laws came into effect, which was a critical point in the court's analysis of the doctrine of legality in administrative actions ([16], [18]).

However, the court also concluded that any authority to grant the requested permissions was curtailed by the eventual enactment of the 2009 by-laws that reaffirmed prohibitions which the billboards in question contravened ([22]). Thus, while the respondent argued that its applications should be reviewed under the older, more permissive by-laws, these arguments failed when considering the absolute prohibitions under the 2009 regulations, extinguishing any residual discretion the first appellant might have had to grant the request ([21]).

### C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the court held that the appeal by the City of Johannesburg was to be granted, as the permissions granted by the High Court were set aside—recognizing the absence of any lawful basis for the initial decisions kept under review. This ruling underscored the importance of strict adherence to by-law stipulations while highlighting complications arising from changes in regulatory frameworks over time ([24]).

Thus, the consequences of the ruling articulated that even if decisions taken regarding applications could initially appear to be wrongful, the overarching legal context dictates that clarity in regulation and adherence to legislative requirements in the matters of urban planning and safety are paramount ([23]–[24]). The judgement henceforth demands rigorous compliance with municipal power exercised under the context of legally defined frameworks, ensuring that such powers cannot be arbitrarily extended or distorted beyond their designation.
