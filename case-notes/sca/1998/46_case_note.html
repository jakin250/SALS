S v Accused No 1 (Case No 385/97)

Supreme Court of Appeal 1998

FIRAC Method

F – Facts ([paras 2-19])

The appellant, referred to as accused No 1, was convicted in the Giyani Magistrates Court of theft and violation of Section 36 of Act No. 62 of 1955, concerning stolen property. This case arose from an incident on December 19, 1992, at the Smart Centre, a shopping venue in Giyani. The appellant, along with two companions described as students, was alleged to have stolen a handbag and clothing from the store.

Witnesses presented conflicting testimonies regarding the appellant’s actions. Miss Anna Mokoena, a saleslady, reported observing the three women acting suspiciously and stated that accused No 3 handed accused No 1 a handbag, who then concealed several items, including a lipstick, in it before attempting to leave the store. Security personnel intervened, subsequently conducting a search which allegedly uncovered clothing concealed under accused No 1's attire.

In a separate charge, Sergeant Rodney Ngobeni testified to discovering multiple items, including electronic goods, in a BMW belonging to the accused following their arrest. The accused maintained that these items were purchased in Durban, but could not provide adequate receipts or supporting evidence. Both the theft and statutory charge were disputed by the defence, arguing that the entire case was a fabrication.

Both the Giyani Magistrates Court and the Transvaal Provincial Division dismissed the appeal brought by the appellant, leading to this Supreme Court appeal concerning both the conviction and sentence.

I – Issues ([paras 2-19])

The appeal raised critical legal questions, primarily concerning:

  1. Whether the evidence presented by the state was sufficient to sustain convictions for theft and contravention of Section 36 of Act No. 62 of 1955.
  2. The adequacy of the procedure followed in the search and discovery of the alleged stolen items.
  3. The right of the accused to challenge the credibility and admissibility of the evidence, particularly the eyewitness accounts.

R – Rules ([paras 12-15])

In assessing the case, the court referred to principles of evidence law and the legal standards governing theft and statutory offences. The critical rules considered included:

  • Burden of Proof: The state bears the burden of proving the accused’s guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. Evidence must be compelling and reliable to secure a conviction.
  • Possession and Suspicion: According to S v Wilson 1962 (2) SA 619 (A), the distinction between ‘found in possession’ and ‘in possession’ was pertinent to evaluating the sufficiency of the state’s evidence. The court scrutinized whether the police had reasonable grounds to suspect the items were stolen.
  • Credibility and Reliability of Evidence: The testimony of witnesses must be corroborated and assessed for credibility. The absence of certain witnesses and discrepancies in testimonies were significant.

A – Application ([paras 10-18])

In its analysis, the court scrutinized the prosecution's case against the appellant.

For the theft charge, the court noted significant gaps in the state’s evidence. Although Miss Mokoena's observations were ostensibly supportive, her conclusions about the appellant's actions were deemed subjective and largely uncorroborated. The critical failure was the absence of testimony from the security officer, Margaret, who conducted the search, which undermined the prosecution's claims about the findings of clothing on accused No 1. The court highlighted this absence as undermining the prosecution's case because it left the evidence presented by Miss Mokoena in a vacuum, devoid of critical validation.

Regarding the contravention of Section 36, the court acknowledged the initial suspicion of Sergeant Ngobeni but ultimately found the cumulative evidence insufficient to hold the accused accountable. Although Sergeant Ngobeni's reactions were anchored in observed behavior and the quantity of goods found, the court identified that the prosecution failed to present definitive proof that the items were indeed stolen. The accused's explanations for their possession of the goods, while lacking substantial corroboration, introduced reasonable doubt.

The court conducted a careful examination of the credibility of the witnesses and highlighted the implausibilities in the accused's defence. The inconsistencies in recalling details, such as accommodations while in Durban or the names of auctioneers, culminated in the rejection of their explanations.

C – Conclusion ([paras 18-19])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the evidence failed to establish the theft of the handbag and clothing beyond a reasonable doubt. Consequently, the appeal against the theft conviction was upheld, and the conviction and sentence were set aside. However, the court sustained the conviction for contravening Section 36 of Act No. 62 of 1955, affirming that the evidence presented sufficiently met the legal threshold despite the complexities surrounding the accused's explanations.

The appeal against the sentence of 12 months imprisonment for contravening Section 36 of Act No. 62 of 1955 was dismissed, reflecting judicial deference to lower courts' sentencing discretion, considering the appellant's prior convictions.

The implications of this ruling underscore the judiciary’s commitment to upholding evidentiary standards and the fundamental principle of ‘innocent until proven guilty’ within the context of criminal law in South Africa. This case sets a significant precedent for how evidence and credibility are evaluated in future theft and possession cases.
