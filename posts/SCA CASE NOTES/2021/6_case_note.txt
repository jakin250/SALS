Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# FIRAC Case Note

## Case Title: 
**The Health Professions Council of South Africa and Others v Grieve** (1356/2019) [2021] ZASCA 06 (15 January 2021)

### F – Facts: 
The pivotal facts of this case revolve around Dr. David Grieve, a general medical practitioner from Centurion, Gauteng, who faced allegations of unprofessional conduct. The Health Professions Council of South Africa (the Council) charged him for allegedly persuading patients to invest in a financially distressed company of which he was a director, and for transferring funds from that company to his private bank account ([1]–[2]). 

Initially, on 25 November 2014, Dr. Grieve appeared before the Council's Professional Conduct Committee, where he raised a **point in limine** arguing that the Council lacked jurisdiction based on the charging allegations not constituting unprofessional conduct as defined in the Health Professions Act 56 of 1974 (the Act) ([4]–[5]). The committee dismissed this point, leading Dr. Grieve to appeal to the Gauteng High Court, where Khumalo J ruled in his favor, concluding that the Council had no jurisdiction to institute the disciplinary proceedings against him ([6]).

### I – Issues: 
The fundamental legal issues presented in this case included:

1. Whether the Health Professions Council had the jurisdiction to institute disciplinary proceedings against Dr. Grieve concerning allegations of unprofessional conduct that did not appear to pertain directly to the health services rendered to patients ([2]).
2. Whether the conduct alleged constituted unprofessional conduct as understood under the Act ([5]–[6]).

### R – Rules: 
The determination of this case relied extensively on the provisions in the Health Professions Act. Key statutory provisions include:

- **Section 41(1)** of the Act, which allows the Council to institute inquiries into any complaints or charges regarding unprofessional conduct by registered practitioners ([9]).
- The definition of “unprofessional conduct” as per **Section 1** of the Act, encompassing conduct that is “improper or disgraceful or dishonourable” ([10]).
- Additionally, the overarching legislative intent of the Council as established in various provisions within the Act, including its responsibilities toward upholding moral standards within the health profession ([11]).

The principles established in previous cases, such as **De Beer v Health Professions Council of South Africa** and **Preddy v Health Professions Council of South Africa**, underscored the necessity of the Council acting as the ethical guardian for practitioners ([12]–[13]).

### A – Application: 
The court applied these rules as follows:

1. **Jurisdictional Authority:** The court emphasized that the conduct alleged, if proven, fell within the purview of the Council’s disciplinary authority as it didn’t solely relate to medical malpractice but rather addressed ethical standards arising from the doctor-patient relationship ([9]–[10]).

2. **Broad Definition of Unprofessional Conduct:** The definition was interpreted broadly, signalling that the Council was not merely a ‘watchdog’ for medical malpractice but a protector of public interest against unethical behaviour, which could arise outside direct patient treatment ([12]–[14]). 

3. **Nature of Conduct:** The court underscored that the allegations related to the misuse of the doctor-patient relationship for personal financial gain, thus asserting that such actions could be deemed unprofessional conduct ([13]–[18]). The court further reinforced that the Council had not erred in acting without waiting for a criminal conviction but could initiate disciplinary action based on the alleged ethical breaches ([14]).

### C – Conclusion: 
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that the appeal by the Health Professions Council succeeded. The High Court's decision was set aside, and the matter was remitted to the Professional Conduct Committee to proceed with the disciplinary inquiry ([1], [19]). The judgment reinforced the Council's broader supervisory role encompassing ethical standards beyond mundane malpractice, confirming its mandate to uphold the integrity and moral compass of the health profession in South Africa. The court’s ruling thus clarified the boundaries of the Council's jurisdiction and its requisite actions in cases of alleged unprofessional conduct ([19]).

### Implications: 
This judgment serves as a significant precedent affirming the Health Professions Council's extensive powers to address ethical breaches among registered practitioners. It elucidates the responsibilities of medical professionals in adequately separating personal interests from professional obligations to safeguard the welfare of patients, thereby reinforcing ethical standards within the healthcare sector.
