# FIRAC Case Note: Morudi and Others v NC Housing Services and Development Co Limited and Others [2018] ZACC 32

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–41])

In the matter of Morudi and Others v NC Housing Services and Development Co Limited and Others, the dispute arose out of a conflict among the shareholders of NC Housing Services and Development Co Limited (the "Company"), initiated by the 1st applicant, Mosalasuping Morudi, and 70 others ([paras 1–4]). Established in the Northern Cape in 1997 for the benefit of black economic empowerment, the Company experienced years of mismanagement, leading to its deregistration in 2007. It was later re-registered for the purpose of selling its substantial asset, shares in NWC Manganese (Pty) Ltd, valued at R250 million ([paras 2–3]).

The primary disagreement arose regarding the entitlement and proportion of shareholding among the applicants and the second and third respondents, Scholtz Babuseng and Seodi Mongwaketsi ([paras 4–6]). The applicants contended that every contributor was entitled to an equal amount of shares, while the respondents insisted on significant disparities in ownership ([para 6]).

Legal battles ensued, culminating in the High Court issuing an interim order that allowed shares to be issued to individuals listed in an annexure. However, crucially, the fifth to 71st applicants were not cited in this main application and consequently did not have the opportunity to contest the proceedings ([paras 8–11]). When they later sought rescission of this order under rule 42(1)(a) of the Uniform Rules of Court, the High Court rejected their application. The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld this order, citing that the applicants were present during the proceedings and thus not entitled to rescission ([paras 18–20]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 21–23])

The critical issue before the Constitutional Court was whether the High Court's denial of rescission and leave to intervene infringed upon the applicants' right of access to court as provided under section 34 of the Constitution. Specifically, the Court needed to examine whether the fifth to 71st applicants were "parties affected" under rule 42(1)(a) and whether the High Court had committed any procedural irregularities ([paras 22–23]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 24–41])

The court referenced several principles of law concerning rescission. Central to their determination was rule 42(1)(a) of the Uniform Rules of Court, which allows for rescission of orders granted in the absence of affected parties ([para 18]). The court also considered the necessity of joinder, as outlined in case law such as *Cape Bar Council* and *Amalgamated Engineering*, establishing that parties with direct and substantial interests must be joined to prevent prejudicial effects ([paras 29–30]).

The court further emphasized that a fundamental aspect of the right to access to justice is the guarantee of a fair hearing under section 34 of the Constitution. Consequently, a failure to allow interested parties to voice their positions or contest an order leads to a serious procedural irregularity ([paras 33–34]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 24–39])

The court concluded that the applicants were indeed parties affected by the order due to their financial interests and potential shareholdings as recorded in annexure "M". It determined that the High Court had failed to properly assess the implications of the interests of these parties when granting orders without their participation ([para 39]). 

Madlanga J's judgment critiqued the High Court's dismissal of the applicants' rights, arguing that it had committed serious procedural errors by not affording the applicants a fair opportunity to present their case. The majority in the Supreme Court of Appeal had erred in interpreting the applicants' presence in court as indicative of acquiescence to the proceedings. Instead, there was a clear indication of an intent to contest the decision ([para 35]).

The court argued that the interpretation of "absence" under rule 42(1)(a) should reflect the realities of access to justice, especially when the applicants were essentially barred from participating. The failure by the High Court to allow for the joinder of necessary parties amounted to a miscarriage of justice that warranted rescission of the order made in the main application ([paras 37–39]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 41–43])

The Constitutional Court ultimately granted leave to appeal, upheld the appeal against the previous orders, and ordered the rescission of the High Court's decision. It emphasized the necessity of allowing all potentially affected parties to intervene in the main proceedings, effectively granting the fifth to 71st applicants leave to intervene in the case ([paras 41–43]).

The court's holding reaffirmed the principle that procedural fairness is critical in judicial processes, and highlighted the importance of the right of access to courts as enshrined in the Constitution. This case illustrates the judiciary's duty to protect this right robustly, ensuring that all parties with a direct interest in legal proceedings are heard, thereby reinforcing the rule of law in South Africa.
