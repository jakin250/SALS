Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: Dengetenge Holdings (Pty) Ltd v Southern Sphere Mining and Development Company Ltd and Others

## F - Facts
This case arises from an application by Dengetenge Holdings (Pty) Ltd (hereinafter "Dengetenge") for leave to appeal against the decision of the Supreme Court of Appeal, which dismissed its request for condonation for the late submission of its written heads of argument as well as for the reinstatement of its appeal against a judgment from the North Gauteng High Court that set aside the grant of prospecting rights to Dengetenge by the Minister of Mineral Resources ([1]).

In the underlying matter, Southern Sphere Mining and Development Company Ltd (hereinafter "Southern Sphere") sought to have the grant of certain prospecting rights to Dengetenge and Abrina 1998 (Pty) Ltd (hereinafter "Abrina") set aside. The dispute arose concerning prospecting for platinum and associated minerals over certain farms in Limpopo Province ([5], [18]). Southern Sphere’s application was subsequently supported by Rhodium Reefs Ltd (hereinafter "Rhodium"), who also sought to challenge the legality of Dengetenge's prospecting rights ([4], [19]).

Dengetenge had filed its application for prospecting rights on 7 February 2006, which was not granted in full, as only part of its application was executed. The backdrop includes a series of administrative actions by the Department of Mineral Resources and a previous High Court interdict which prevented the Minister and her delegates from granting prospecting rights during its operation ([18], [25]). The confusion regarding overlapping mining rights and uncommunicated decisions created a complex legal dispute among the involved parties ([20]).

Dengetenge's efforts to appeal were hampered by procedural delays, leading the Supreme Court of Appeal to deem its application lapsed and requiring it to seek reinstatement for delayed filings. Southern Sphere and Rhodium had moved forward with exploratory activities, claiming loss and investments based on the substantive grants of their prospecting rights ([39], [41]).

## I - Issues
The main legal questions before the Constitutional Court included:

1. Whether the Supreme Court of Appeal erred in dismissing Dengetenge’s application for condonation, thereby raising issues of procedural fairness and potential bias in the judicial process ([46]).
2. Whether the High Court had jurisdiction to hear Southern Sphere's review application given the alleged failure to exhaust internal remedies as stipulated in section 96 of the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA) and the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([44], [52]).

## R - Rules
The legal framework guiding this case includes:

1. **Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA)**: Section 96 mandates exhaustion of internal remedies before judicial review based on administrative decisions made under the act ([63]).
2. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: Parallel provisions in section 7 require that any review application must be initiated within 180 days from knowledge of the administrative action challenged ([66]). 

Previous case law, including rulings from the Supreme Court of Appeal and this Court, emphasizes the need for exhaustion of remedies and raises substantive aspects involving bias and reasonable impartiality within the judiciary ([42], [48],[49]).

## A - Application
The Court applied the aforementioned rules and legal standards to the facts of the case:

1. The Court agreed with Dengetenge's assertion that procedural fairness should govern the process, notably in terms of whether the Supreme Court of Appeal exhibited any bias when juxtaposing the grounds of condonation for Dengetenge compared to the other parties ([46], [49]). However, the Court highlighted that the rationale for treating the applications differently stemmed from discrepancies in the timely compliance with procedural rules by Dengetenge as opposed to the compliance by Southern Sphere and Rhodium.

2. Regarding the exhaustion of internal remedies, the Court noted that when a party invokes PAJA and MPRDA requirements, it is essential that any internal remedies, as stipulated, be fully engaged before judicial review. In this case, the High Court directly processed claims without the requirement for exhausting internal remedies, as the state functionaries voluntarily allowed the Court to adjudicate, effectively waiving this necessity ([88]).

3. The fundamental concession made by Dengetenge in the High Court, acknowledging the unlawfulness of the prospecting rights granted under the interdict, essentially reflected a forfeiture of its claim to oppose Southern Sphere's motions ([31]). Given this precedent, the Court reasoned that to entertain Dengetenge’s appeal would contradict the process they originally endorsed and complicate already resolved disputes ([54], [62]).

## C - Conclusion
The Constitutional Court dismissed Dengetenge's appeal on the basis that there were valid procedural considerations which rendered the appeal without prospects of success. Notably, the perceived bias in the Supreme Court of Appeal was found to lack substance as the distinctions drawn by the judges concerning the condonation applications were legitimate, given Dengetenge’s excessive non-compliance with procedural timelines. Additionally, the Court exercised its jurisdiction without the need for all parties to exhaust internal remedies considering the administrative decisions were pending around the same speculative determinations by the respective parties ([110]).

The Court established that, while the manner of the Supreme Court of Appeal’s adjudication was justifiable, the concession from Dengetenge severely undermined its position, leading to the conclusion that the High Court's judgments should stand ([108]). Costs were ordered against Dengetenge, reflecting the commercial nature of these proceedings and ensuring that justice followed results in these rights disputes ([109]).

--- 

This case illustrates the complexities involved when administrative and procedural laws intersect within the context of mining rights and the obligations purportedly owed by parties, not only to each other but equally to judicial processes intended to ensure fairness and clarity in rights assignments.
