# FIRAC Case Note: R K and Others v Minister of Basic Education and Others (754/2018 and 1051/2018)

### F – Facts

[1] On 20 January 2014, S K (S), a five-year-old student at Mahlodumela Lower Primary School in Limpopo, tragically died after falling into a pit latrine at his school. This event sparked a lawsuit by his family against various educational authorities, alleging damages related to S's death and the emotional trauma experienced by his family members.

[2] The appellants, comprising S's parents and siblings, filed claims for damages, including emotional shock and grief arising from S's death. The Limpopo Division of the High Court initially recognized some damages but dismissed the majority of the claims, prompting the appellants to appeal.

[9-11] The conditions surrounding S's demise were deplorable. Complaints about the sanitation facilities had been made over the years without any effective response from the provincial education authorities. A local handyman had attempted to remedy the toilet's structural issues, but by January 2014, the facilities were in dire condition, ultimately leading to the tragic incident.

[12-14] The death affected the entire family profoundly, causing various mental health issues, including post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD), insomnia, nightmares, and severe emotional distress. Expert testimonies during the trial corroborated the family's suffering and the psychological impact of the incident.

[17] The claims filed by the appellants were categorized as follows:
(a) Claim A pertained to emotional trauma.
(b) Claim B was related to grief compensation.
(c) Claim C comprised future medical expenses for psychological treatment.
(d) Claim D was for funeral costs.
(e) Claim E concerned loss of earnings for Mrs K.
(f) The appellants sought a declaratory order regarding the alleged constitutional obligations breached by the respondents ([15-16]).

### I – Issues

[22] The key legal queries addressed by the court included:
1. The legitimacy of the claims for emotional shock (Claim A) and grief (Claim B).
2. Whether the common law should be developed to compensate for grief without an accompanying psychiatric condition.
3. The appropriateness of awarding constitutional damages in this case ([22],[41]).

### R – Rules

[24-29] The court reviewed common law regarding claims for emotional shock and nervous disorders, utilizing precedents established in notable cases such as *Bourhill v Young* and *Bester v Commercial Union*, which recognize claims stemming from detectable psychiatric injuries due to traumatic events. It highlighted that in South Africa, unlike in other jurisdictions, the common law does not recognize claims for grief absent psychiatric injury.

[26] Development of the common law must align with the spirit of the Constitution, specifically section 39, which encourages adaptation where adequate protections and remedies under common law are absent ([41]). 

### A – Application

[46] The court reasoned that the claims for emotional shock do have a basis in law as long as they are linked to recognized psychiatric injuries, confirming that the appellants had demonstrated sufficient evidence of psychological harm. Despite the incorrect frame in the initial claim, the court established that the family's grief was tied to their psychiatric trauma and therefore compensable under Claim A.

[49-50] The issue concerning the claims for grief (Claim B) indicated the necessity to realign the claims since the psychiatric injuries were already accounted for in Claim A. The court advocated for a remedy that accounts for all forms of acknowledged emotional suffering without categorically dismissing the family’s trauma due to procedural misalignments.

[57-63] On the question of constitutional damages, the court thoroughly analyzed cases that had previously set parameters, determining that the appellants' claims were already satisfied through the relief granted under the established claims. 

### C – Conclusion

[73] The Supreme Court of Appeal modified the lower court's order, recognizing the claims for emotional shock and related grievances suffered by the appellants. It ordered substantial monetary awards compensating the family and mandated the payment of future medical expenses for the children affected. The appeal regarding constitutional damages was dismissed, asserting that the existing legal and financial redress was adequate and that further compensation was unwarranted. 

This case underscores the need for legal clarity surrounding claims of emotional trauma arising from negligent deaths and the necessity for educational authorities to maintain safe conditions for children, reinforcing constitutional rights to dignity and life in the educational context. 

**Implications**: This ruling contributes to the evolving understanding of emotional shock claims within the legal framework of South Africa, highlighting the interplay between constitutional principles and common law while outlining the courts' cautious approach toward expanding legal remedies in emotional distress cases.
