Case Note: RAPPORTEERBAAR
Source Folder: 2001

# FIRAC Case Note on *Rapporeteerbaar* (211/2000)

**F – Facts:**

[1] The case revolves around an incident during a production auction where a Brahman bull, owned by the first appellant, Loriza Brahman, injured the respondent, Casper Dippenaar. The incident occurred when the bull charged at Dippenaar, resulting in fractures to his leg.

[2] Loriza, operated under Tommiesrus (Edms) Bpk, was engaged in Brahman cattle breeding near Nietverdiend in North West Province, with Kobus Naudé as the herd manager. Dippenaar, a part-time farmer and accountant from Centurion, Gauteng, attended the auction intending to purchase several bulls.

[3] On May 22, 1997, prior to the auction, Dippenaar informed Naudé of his purchasing intentions. Naudé offered to recommend suitable animals. Accompanied by others, they entered a pen for inspection.

[4] The pen housed approximately 25 Brahman bulls and auction attendees. While examining the catalog, an incident occurred where the bull, Alicia, charged towards Dippenaar, causing the injuries that led to this litigation.

[5] Dippenaar’s leg injuries were severe enough to necessitate legal action. He pursued claims against Loriza via actio de pauperie and against Naudé as an alternative via actio legis Aquiliae.

[10] The court found in favor of Dippenaar, holding Loriza primarily liable for damages, and ordered both Loriza and Naudé to cover legal costs incurred during the proceedings.

**I – Issues:**

[12] The core legal issue is whether the actio de pauperie, a claim entailing strict liability for damage caused by animals, remains applicable under South African law, especially in light of its historical context and possible evolution into negligence-based liability.

[14] A secondary issue concerns whether the bull acted contra naturam, meaning in a manner inconsistent with its nature as a domesticated animal, a requirement necessary for establishing liability under actio de pauperie.

**R – Rules:**

[12-13] The actio de pauperie originates from Roman law, allowing a plaintiff to claim damages for injuries inflicted by a domesticated animal. Liability under this action requires proof that the animal acted in a manner contrary to its nature (contra naturam) and that such action was spontaneous (sponte feritate commota), unprovoked by external factors.

[19] The act requires an analysis of whether the animal's actions were instinctual or resulted from provocation. If an animal displays behavior indicative of wildness, such conditions could lead to liability.

**A – Application:**

[12-19] The court affirmed the validity of the actio de pauperie, rejecting arguments that it had become obsolete in contemporary law. The judges emphasized that, despite critiques regarding its strict liability approach, this legal avenue plays a crucial role in compensating injured parties.

[21] In assessing whether Alicia acted contra naturam, the court concluded that the evidence did not establish any extenuating external provocation. Testimony indicated that Alicia's behavior was indicative of inner nervousness or fear rather than an anticipated reaction to outside stimuli.

[23] The court determined that Dippenaar had established a prima facie case for liability, as no adequate rebuttal was presented by Loriza to negate the claim that the bull acted in a manner contrary to its nature. The absence of identified provocations further supported the view of spontaneous agency in the bull’s actions.

[24] Concerning the defense of assumption of risk, the court upheld that Dippenaar was unaware of both the presence of the cattle in the pen and the risks involved therein, affirming that the defense of consent or assumption of risk had not been sufficiently established.

**C – Conclusion:**

[25] The court ultimately upheld the order of the lower court, affirming that Loriza bears liability for the damages sustained by Dippenaar due to the action of the bull. The finding reinforces the doctrinal precedent of actio de pauperie in contemporary South African law.

[26] The legal implications of this decision stretch beyond the immediate parties, affirming the continued relevance of strict liability and encouraging the cautious management of domestic animals, particularly in public settings. The complexities surrounding risk assessment and liability standards remain critical to both litigants and their legal representatives in future cases involving animal-related injuries.
