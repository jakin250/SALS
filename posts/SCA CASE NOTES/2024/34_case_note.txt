Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note: Centaur Mining South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Cloete Murray N O and Others ([2024] ZASCA 34)

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–6, 9–18])

In *Centaur Mining South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Cloete Murray N O and Others*, the appellant, Centaur Mining South Africa (Pty) Ltd (CMSA), appealed against a ruling from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg, which dismissed its application for rescission of a judgment. This judgment related to a claim against Trillian Management Consulting (Pty) Ltd (TMC) and others, ordered to repay a significant debt to Eskom. The Trillian group, consisting of multiple companies including Trillian Capital Partners (Pty) Ltd and others, was implicated in complex financial misconduct involving fraudulent transactions orchestrated by its former CEO, Eric Anthony Wood. The South African Revenue Services (SARS) initiated a preservation application against TMC due to significant tax evasion and the alleged abuse of corporate structures for fraud.

Following TMC's liquidation in early 2020, the appointed joint provisional liquidators applied to the high court under s 20(9) of the Companies Act 71 of 2008, seeking to "pierce the corporate veil" of various Trillian entities, effectively consolidating their liabilities. On 20 January 2021, the high court confirmed the rule nisi, declaring the Trillian companies to be distinct for legal purposes, thereby facilitating a composite liquidation. Subsequently, CMSA approached the court for rescission, arguing that the ruling to consolidate the Trillian companies was erroneous.

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 19–22])

The primary legal question before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether the high court erred in dismissing CMSA's application for rescission. Specifically, the issues included:

1. Did the high court have the jurisdiction and authority to grant the application under s 20(9) of the Companies Act and rule 42(1)(a) of the Uniform Rules of Court?
2. Were CMSA's arguments for rescission based on an alleged error in the application of the law sufficient to warrant the overturning of the high court’s decisions?
3. Is seeking rescission under the grounds provided in rule 42(1)(a) permissible in light of the fact that the underlying judgment was not erroneous in the traditional sense of having been granted without proper notice?

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 12–15, 21])

The court referred to several legal principles and statutory provisions:

1. **Section 20(9) of the Companies Act** allows a court to disregard the separate legal personality of a company where it is established that there has been an "unconscionable abuse" of that personality, facilitating the piercing of the corporate veil, a power upheld in *City Capital SA Property Holdings Limited v Chavonnes Badenhorst St Clair Cooper NO and Others* [2017] ZASCA 177.
2. The common law principle of "piercing the corporate veil," as established in *Cape Pacific Ltd v Lubner Controlling Investments (Pty) Ltd and Others*, dictates that a court may lift the veil in cases of fraud or misconduct.
3. **Rule 42(1)(a)** of the Uniform Rules of Court outlines provisions for the rescission of an order erroneously granted in the absence of an affected party, a principle applied in *Lodhi 2 Properties Investments CC and Another v Bondev Development (Pty) Ltd* [2007] ZASCA 85.

The high court also cited *Zuma v Secretary of the Judicial Commission of Inquiry into Allegations of State Capture* [2021] ZACC 28 regarding principles governing rescission.

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 20–26])

The SCA upheld the high court’s reasoning, determining that CMSA failed to demonstrate that the prior rulings were erroneous as required under rule 42(1)(a). The court stated that the judgments sought to be rescinded were not erroneous but rather validly obtained, as the liquidators had the entitlement to seek the interim and final orders under s 20(9) of the Act.

The SCA also emphasized that CMSA's arguments effectively amounted to a disguised appeal against the substantive ruling under s 20(9), for which no right to appeal existed. The lack of opposition from the subject companies in the initial proceedings and the advanced stage of the winding-up process further weakened CMSA's case.

Significantly, the SCA highlighted the need for finality in judicial proceedings, noting that the composite winding-up had progressed to an advanced stage, involving substantial costs and resource investment. This consideration effectively overshadowed the merits of CMSA's rescission application.

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 24–26])

The SCA dismissed CMSA's appeal, affirming the high court's dismissal of the rescission application. The ratio decidendi underscored the principles of finality in litigation and the court's reluctance to permit rescission when the originally obtained judgment was not proven to be erroneous. This judgment sets a precedent reinforcing the efficacy of s 20(9) in addressing corporate misconduct and extends the application of corporate veil piercing to cases of fraudulent practice within corporate groups.

The implications of the decision resonate within corporate law, underscoring the judiciary's commitment to preventing the misuse of corporate structures for fraudulent purposes. The ruling validates the application of s 20(9) and reinforces the operational integrity of corporate governance, urging stakeholders in the corporate arena to engage responsibly with the law and ethical standards.
