# Case Note: Makgoba & Others v Ledwaba NO & Others (054/2018) [2018] ZASCA 181

**FIRAC Structure**

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–18])

The case follows an appeal by the first to the twelfth appellants against the decisions made by the Gauteng Division of the High Court concerning the Mamphoku Makgoba Community Trust (the Trust), the first respondent being Maponya Lazarus Ledwaba, along with other parties ([para 1]). The appellants were members of the Board of Trustees until their term ended in June 2013 per the Trust Deed, yet they continued to act in their roles without holding the prescribed meetings or duly accounting to the beneficiaries ([paras 2–3]). 

The Trust was established in 2004 following land claims made by the Makgoba community, intending to manage various properties on behalf of beneficiaries. The Master of the High Court, unsatisfied with the trustees' management, removed them on February 22, 2017, leading to a high court application by the appellants to restore their positions, alleging improper removal ([paras 3–8]). 

In response, the high court set aside the Master's decision to remove the trustees but mandated their immediate resignation upon their reinstatement. The appellants appealed this substitutional order ([paras 10–11]). The issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) revolved around the legitimacy and authority of the terminated trustees to continue their functions and the need for appointing new trustees ([paras 1, 10–18]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–18])

The primary legal questions considered in this case included:

1. Whether the previous order reinstating the appellants as trustees was appropriate when they had already exceeded their term limits as stipulated in the Trust Deed, which called for resignation upon term expiry ([paras 2, 16]).
2. The legal necessity for a verification meeting of beneficiaries prior to the appointment of new trustees and if the SCA had the authority to enforce such a mechanism in light of the circumstances ([paras 12–14, 18]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 1–18])

The court considered several legal frameworks and previous rulings to justify its decision:

1. **Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988** — relevant provisions, such as section 20(2)(e), empower the Master to remove trustees and are essential in determining the validity of trustee actions ([paras 7, 12]).
2. **Trust Deed Provisions** — particularly clauses relating to the terms of office set a definitive three-year limit, requiring a clear transition process to ensure compliance with the governance principles of the Trust ([paras 4, 14]).
3. **Case Law** — previous court orders, including a ruling by Mabuse J in November 2015 regarding the expiration of the trustees’ terms, underlined the duty of the appellants to relinquish their offices at the end of their terms ([paras 6, 18]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 1–18])

The SCA critically assessed the factual context and the overarching legal principles governing trust administration. The court recognized the appellants' acknowledgment that they had overstayed their terms and thus had no legal basis for challenging their removal. This acknowledgment was crucial in affirming that the appellants’ continuous tenure was contrary to the Trust Deed, which mandated their expiration of office ([paras 10, 16]).

Furthermore, the court emphasized that ensuring the Trust’s administrative legitimacy and beneficiary welfare were paramount. The rulings dictated that a thorough and transparent reappointment process of trustees was vital following the dysfunctionality experienced under the appellants ([paras 12, 18]). The court concluded that the inclusion of verification processes for potential beneficiaries was essential in upholding the integrity of the trust's objectives ([para 14]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 1–18])

The SCA upheld the appeal, determining that the appellants' term officially ceased in June 2013, thereby setting aside any letters of authority issued thereafter ([paras 2–8]). It was mandated that new trustees be appointed within 60 days, excluding the appellants from eligibility. The court’s directive emphasized the necessity of involving eligible beneficiaries in the decision-making process, reinforcing accountability within trust administration ([paras 3–7, 18]).

The implications of this ruling resonate with wider governance practices concerning trust management, especially in light of community-administered trusts where beneficiaries' active involvement is critical. This case illustrates judicial prerogative to ensure statutory compliance while promoting communal welfare through active governance frameworks. The ruling sends a clear message about fiduciary duties and the importance of adhering to established governance mechanisms within trusts.
