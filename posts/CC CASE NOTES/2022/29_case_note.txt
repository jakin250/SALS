Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

### Case Note: FIRAC Method - Rafoneke and Others v Minister of Justice and Correctional Services and Others (Makombe Intervening) [2022] ZACC 29

---

#### F - Facts

The case involves several applicants, primarily non-citizens who seek admission as legal practitioners in South Africa under the Legal Practice Act 28 of 2014 (LPA). The main applicants are Relebohile Cecilia Rafoneke and Sefoboko Philip Tsuinyane, both citizens of Lesotho who studied law in South Africa but were denied admission because they are neither South African citizens nor permanent residents ([1]–[9]). 

The main issue pertains to the constitutionality of section 24(2)(b) of the LPA, which states that only South African citizens or permanent residents can be admitted and enrolled as legal practitioners. This provision effectively excludes foreign nationals like the applicants who possess valid work or study visas, resulting in their inability to practice law despite meeting all other qualifications ([36]–[38]).

Additionally, Bruce Chakanyuka, Nyasha James Nyamugure, and Dennis Tatenda Chadya, all Zimbabwean nationals, bring similar claims, asserting the unconstitutionality of the same provision. The High Court of South Africa, Free State Division, initially declared section 24(2)(b) unconstitutional but only regarding non-practising legal practitioners, offering interim relief for non-citizens to be admitted under restricted terms ([39]).

Ultimately, the case was consolidated into three appeals (CCT 315/21, CCT 321/21, and CCT 06/22) before the Constitutional Court, all challenging the High Court's limited declaration and seeking a broader interpretation to allow non-citizens who possess legal qualifications to practice law in South Africa comfortably ([2]–[4]).

---

#### I - Issues

The key legal issue before the Constitutional Court is whether the provisions of section 24(2)(b) of the LPA are unconstitutional as they prohibit individuals who are neither South African citizens nor permanent residents from being admitted as legal practitioners. Specifically, the inquiries boil down to:

1. Does this differential treatment based on citizenship and residency amount to unfair discrimination as outlined in section 9 of the Constitution? ([41]–[83])
2. If deemed discriminatory, is such discrimination unjustifiable under section 36 of the Constitution, which allows for reasonable limitations on rights? ([96]–[102])

---

#### R - Rules

The relevant legal frameworks considered in this case include:

1. **The South African Constitution, 1996**:
   - Section 9: Ensures equality before the law and prohibits unfair discrimination on various grounds, including social origin and citizenship ([69]–[70]).
   - Section 22: Provides citizens with the right to choose their trade or profession freely, which does not extend to non-citizens ([72]).

2. **Legal Practice Act 28 of 2014**:
   - Section 24(2)(b): Limits the admission of legal practitioners to South African citizens and permanent residents ([41]).

3. **Precedent Cases**:
   - **Harksen v Lane**: Provides the test for assessing whether differentiation amounts to discrimination and if it is unfair ([70]).
   - **Larbi-Odam v Member of the Executive Council for Education**: Discusses the treatment of non-citizens and confirms that not all rights available to citizens extend to non-citizens ([93]).

---

#### A - Application

The Court engaged in a detailed analysis to determine whether there is a rational connection between the legislation and a legitimate governmental purpose. The respondents argued that the legislation aims to protect citizens' employment opportunities and ensure the integrity of the legal profession. However, the applicants contested that the prohibitory nature of section 24(2)(b) creates an unjust barrier despite their qualifications and lawful presence in the country ([44]–[55]).

Evaluating this issue under the Harksen test, the Court determined that while the provision does create a differentiation based on citizenship, it does not cause unfair discrimination since:

1. Non-citizens do not enjoy the same rights as citizens regarding the profession, thus mitigating the impact on their dignity ([97]–[102]). 
2. The distinction reflects government policy that legally legitimizes discrimination in certain professions regarding citizenship status, validating the government's rationale of protecting citizens' work interests ([72]–[78]).

While some testimonies indicated that international best practices do not mandate citizenship as a precondition for legal practice, the Court held that South Africa’s regulatory framework remains valid and aligns with the rights enunciated in the Constitution ([86]).

---

#### C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court upheld the appeal against the High Court's order, concluding that section 24(2)(b) of the LPA is not unconstitutional under the equality clause. The Court ruled that the differentiation based on citizenship is justified within the rational framework of protecting employment for citizens and maintaining professional integrity in the legal sector. 

The Court’s holding emphasizes the legislature's prerogative to regulate professions while cautioning that any future amendments may need to consider broader implications for non-citizens entering various fields, including law ([100]–[104]). Ultimately, the ruling balances the complexities of citizenship, professional regulation, and the constitutional landscape, affirming the restrictions as not in violation of constitutional mandates.

---

This analysis highlights the intricate relationship between legislation, individual rights, and governmental purpose, providing insight into constitutional protections afforded in South Africa, especially concerning non-citizens in professional fields.
