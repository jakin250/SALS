Source Folder: 2003

# FIRAC Case Notes: Republic of South Africa - Costa da Oura Restaurant (Pty) Ltd t/a Umdloti Bush Tavern v Anthony Reddy (Case No. 486/01)

## F – Facts

1. In November 1999, the respondent, Anthony Reddy, initiated legal action against the appellant, Costa da Oura Restaurant (Proprietary) Limited, in the Durban and Coast Local Division of the High Court. The claim was for damages amounting to R122,536.00, arising from an alleged assault by Goldie, a part-time barman employed by the appellant at the Umdloti Bush Tavern, on the evening of 31 October 1997 ([1]).

2. Reddy and his girlfriend visited the tavern for drinks and observed that Goldie was not serving them while attending to other patrons ([1]–[2]). Reddy, after some time, made a comment to another barman about Goldie's service, which Goldie overheard, leading to a confrontation in which Goldie reportedly felt provoked and agitated ([28]–[32]).

3. The altercation escalated after Reddy left the bar, where Goldie attacked him outside the premises, resulting in physical injuries ([38]–[41]). Following this incident, Goldie was summarily dismissed by the tavern’s management.

4. The High Court found the appellant vicariously liable for Goldie’s actions, leading to the present appeal ([2]).

5. Prior to the appeal, the parties agreed on a factual statement to facilitate the proceedings, which noted the operational details and conduct policies at the Umdloti Bush Tavern and the events that transpired on the night of the incident ([3]).

## I – Issues

1. The legal question before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the appellant, Costa da Oura Restaurant, could be held vicariously liable for the actions of its employee, Goldie, during the assault on Reddy ([43]). 

2. It required consideration of whether Goldie's actions were sufficiently connected to his employment duties to justify holding the employer liable under the principles of vicarious liability, specifically whether the assault can be considered within the course and scope of his employment, or a personal frolic, as argued by the appellant ([44]).

## R – Rules

1. The principles of vicarious liability dictate that an employer may be held liable for the tortious acts of an employee if those acts were performed in the course of the employee's duties ([5]). The key factors include whether the wrongful act occurred during the course of employment and whether it was incidental to the business of the employer.

2. The standard of whether an employee’s actions were a personal journey versus the business’s interests has been articulated in various precedents, notably the case of **Feldman (Pty) Ltd v Mall** 1945 AD 731, where the court emphasized that the crucial inquiry is the nature of the act performed in relation to the employment ([5]).

3. Reference was also made to **Minister of Law and Order v Ngobo** 1992 (4) SA 822 (A), which reaffirms the need for a close relationship between the employee’s act and their work responsibilities ([5]).

4. The court examined the notion of the "frolic of one's own," noting that if the employee was acting solely in a private capacity, then the employer would typically not be held liable ([5]).

## A – Application

1. The court began by assessing the proximity of Goldie’s assault to his duties as a barman. It acknowledged that the incident followed directly after Reddy made a comment regarding Goldie’s service while he was still engaged in his role ([44]).

2. Arguments were made by the respondent that the altercation was a direct result of Goldie's engagement at work, occurring shortly after he was required to serve Reddy and in an area closely associated with his employment ([44]).

3. Conversely, the appellant contended that Goldie's actions were an act of personal vengeance, performed after the customer had exited the bar. They claimed Goldie's behavior constituted a departure from his duties and a breach of the employer's instructions ([44]).

4. The court noted that Goldie's aggression was provoked as a result of his engagement with Reddy during his employment but critically found that the assault transpired outside the premises and shortly after a significant effort by the employer's management to de-escalate any potential conflict. This indicated that Goldie had effectively abandoned his role in acting out the ensuing aggression ([6]–[8]).

5. The court concluded that Goldie's actions were personal and not conducted during the proper exercise of his duties, aligning more closely with the example in **Deatons (Pty) Ltd v Flew**, which underlined that not every misguided action stemming from an employee's employment context translates to vicarious liability for the employer ([6]).

## C – Conclusion

1. The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the taankar “The attack by Goldie on the respondent outside the tavern occurred after he had abandoned his duties” was pivotal. The actions were deemed personal, not furthering the employer's interests, nor carried out within the obligations bestowed upon him as an employee ([8]).

2. Therefore, the court ruled in favor of the appellant, overturning the High Court’s ruling, declaring the appellant not vicariously liable for Goldie's actions during the assault on Reddy. As a consequence, the appeal was upheld with costs, effectively dismissing Reddy’s claim and reinforcing the lawful parameters surrounding employer liability in cases of employee misconduct ([8]). 

3. The decision elucidates the nuances of employee behavior in relation to vicarious liability, establishing a precedent for future cases involving similar challenges in delineating personal acts from those grounded in an employee's work responsibilities.
