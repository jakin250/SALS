Dlanjwa v The Minister of Safety and Security (20217/2014) [2015] ZASCA 147

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 [2015] ZASCA 147

F – Facts

This case involves an appeal by Sanela Dlanjwa (the appellant) against a judgment of the Full Court of the Eastern Cape Local Division, Mthatha, which overturned a previous decision holding the Minister of Safety and Security liable for the damages she sustained due to a violent incident involving her deceased husband, Sergeant Thandikhaya Dlanjwa (the deceased) ([1]–[2]). The incident occurred on April 19, 2006, when the deceased shot his wife with his service firearm before turning the weapon on himself, committing suicide. The appellant alleged that the shooting resulted from the negligence of the South African Police Services (SAPS), particularly regarding their failure to respond adequately to her previous reports concerning her husband's violent behavior and threats ([2]–[3]).

In her summons, the appellant claimed damages for general damages, medical expenses, loss of earnings, and loss of support, asserting that the police failed to act despite her reports of her husband’s abusive behavior, which included physical assaults and threats of suicide ([3]–[4]). The police did not take appropriate action after being notified of the deceased’s violent conduct, which included incidents where he threatened his wife with a firearm.

During the trial, the appellant's testimony highlighted a pattern of abuse, including incidents where the deceased brandished a firearm and physically assaulted her. Although she was inconsistent in her recollection of certain details during cross-examination, she was firm in her assertion of the violence she suffered ([4]–[5]). Conversely, the police witnesses contended that the appellant had not adequately reported the severity of her situation and did not mention the weapon during her previous visits to the station ([8]–[9]).

The trial court found in favor of the appellant, but this decision was reversed by the Full Court, which deemed her testimony unreliable and concluded that the police had no duty to act as the facts did not support her allegations ([11]–[12]). The appellant appealed this decision, raising concerns about the credibility assessment made by the Full Court and the handling of her claims ([13]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue in this case revolves around whether the police owed a duty of care to the appellant and whether they breached that duty through negligence leading to the harm she suffered ([30]). Specifically, the court needed to assess:

  1. Whether the appellant had sufficiently informed the police about the deceased's violent behavior and his possession of a firearm, thus triggering an obligation for police intervention.
  2. If the police's failure to respond appropriately constituted negligence and resulted in a breach of duty that directly caused the appellant's injuries and loss ([30]).

R – Rules

In determining the existence of negligence and a duty of care, the court relied on established principles of delictual liability as articulated in cases such as Minister of Safety and Security v Van Duivenboden [2002] ZASCA 79, where it held that a legal duty arises when police officers are aware of a threat to an individual’s safety and fail to take necessary action ([25]). The basic elements for establishing negligence require the plaintiff to demonstrate:

  1. The existence of a legal duty owed by the police to the appellant;
  2. A breach of that duty due to negligent conduct; and
  3. A direct causal link between the negligent act and the injury sustained by the plaintiff ([30]).

Further guidance was taken from the South African Constitution, especially Section 12(1)(c), which guarantees individuals the right to freedom and security, including the right to be free from violence ([24]).

A – Application

Upon reevaluation of the evidence, it became apparent that the Full Court had overemphasized inconsistencies in the appellant’s testimony while neglecting the systemic failures of the police to respond to her reports adequately. The majority of the Full Court had disregarded key aspects of the trial court's findings, which recognized the severity of the appellant’s allegations involving threats with a firearm ([10]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal highlighted the flaws in the police’s approach to handling the appellant's reports. Crucially, the police officers failed to take formal statements from the appellant during her visits, a violation of procedure which undermined their defense. The testimony from police officers displayed significant inconsistencies and led to the inference that their recollections were unreliable due to the passage of time ([15]–[17]).

Moreover, the court noted that even if the appellant had inconsistencies in some minor details, the essential elements of her versions—her fear of the deceased and his violent conduct—remained consistent and credible ([19]). The court also emphasized that the police had a constitutional obligation to protect citizens from domestic violence, which they failed to uphold when they did not act on her reports ([24]).

The evidence indicated that the police were aware of the protection order obtained by the appellant before the shooting incident but had not acted upon it, further showcasing their negligence and failure to perform their statutory obligations ([27]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the Full Court's ruling, reinstating the trial court’s findings that the police were liable for failing to protect the appellant adequately. The justices determined that the police's negligence directly contributed to the harm the appellant suffered, reinforcing the statutory duty imposed on law enforcement to act in matters involving domestic violence ([30]–[32]).

The appeal was upheld with costs, confirming that accountability exists for police inaction in domestic violence cases, emphasizing their duty to take all necessary steps to ensure the safety of individuals who report threats ([32]). This case serves as a critical reminder of the responsibilities of law enforcement agencies in protecting vulnerable citizens against domestic violence and the legal recourse available for victims.
