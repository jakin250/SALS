# FIRAC Case Note 

**Case Title:** Michelin Tyre Company (South Africa) (Pty) Ltd v F Janse van Rensburg & Others  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Case No:** 198/2001  
**Coram:** Hefer AP, Zulman, Brand, Nugent, JA, Lewis AJA  
**Heard:** 16 May 2002  
**Delivered:** 29 May 2002  
**Summary:** Companies Act 61 of 1973 - s 417 enquiry - only available in winding-up by the court.

---

## F – Facts:

The legal matter before the Supreme Court of Appeal revolved around an application by Michelin Tyre Company (South Africa) (Pty) Ltd (the Appellant) challenging a decision made by the Master regarding an enquiry under section 417 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973. This enquiry was initially sought in respect of a company that was undergoing a voluntary winding-up process ([para 1]). 

The appellant contested the ruling of Soggot AJ from the High Court in the Witwatersrand Local Division stating that section 417 does not apply in the context of voluntary winding-ups. The judge ruled in favor of the respondents, thereby denying the requested enquiry. The Appellant subsequently filed an appeal ([para 1]).

In essence, the argument presented by the appellant proposed two main submissions: first, that the language of section 417 should encompass both compulsory and voluntary winding-ups; and second, that a purposive interpretation of the provision should be employed to avert unintended consequences ([para 2]).

## I – Issues:

The primary legal issue was whether an enquiry under section 417 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973 could be conducted in the context of a company being wound up voluntarily ([para 1]). The specific questions included:

1. Does the wording of section 417 extend to voluntary winding-ups?
2. Can a purposive interpretation reconcile the legislative intent without contravening its clear language? ([para 2]).

## R – Rules:

The legal rules relevant to this case stem from the Companies Act 61 of 1973, particularly the provisions articulated in section 417. The section states that inquiries can be initiated following a winding-up order made by the court when the company is unable to pay its debts ([para 3]).

The court needed to consider the interpretations previously established in jurisprudential context, particularly cases such as *South African Philips (Pty) Ltd and Others v The Master and Others* 2000 (2) SA 841 (N), which expressed that the language of section 417 is unequivocal and limits its applicability ([para 4]).

## A – Application:

Upon examining the arguments, the court first addressed the appellant’s primary submission, indicating that the language of section 417 does not support the idea of it being applicable to voluntary winding-ups. The court noted that the Act contains explicit provisions for both compulsory and voluntary winding-ups and that section 417 cannot be logically placed into either category ([para 3]).

Furthermore, the court articulated that while the introductory words of section 417 seem broad, the subsequent text clearly indicates a limitation to compulsory winding-up scenarios. Soggot AJ's analysis of the section’s wording, distinguishing between compulsory and voluntary procedures, was affirmed as correct ([para 3]). 

The court addressed the notion of purposive interpretation, asserting that allowing such interpretation would counteract the clear legislative intent expressed in the statute. It was highlighted that there are alternative legal pathways for obtaining a section 417 enquiry in cases of voluntary winding-up, including converting the process into a court-ordered winding-up or applying for leave to convene an enquiry, as outlined under sections 346(1)(e) and 388 of the Companies Act ([para 4][5]).

## C – Conclusion:

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, thereby upholding the decision of the High Court. The court’s decision affirmed that section 417 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973 does not extend to enquiries in voluntary winding-up scenarios, and the legislative framework necessitates a clear distinction between the two types of windings-up ([para 5]). 

The ratio decidendi emphasized the necessity of adhering strictly to the statutory language to preserve the prescribed legislative intent and ensure consistency in the application of the law. The implications of this case reinforce the need for clarity in statutory interpretation and safeguard the procedural integrity of winding-up processes under South African company law.
