Supreme Court of Appeal

National Association of Broadcasters v South African Music Performance Rights Association (119/2013) [2014] ZASCA 10

The dispute focuses primarily on the formula used to calculate the royalties owed by NAB to SAMPRA.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2014 [2014] ZASCA 10

F – Facts

The case of National Association of Broadcasters v South African Music Performance Rights Association arises from a dispute concerning the determination of royalty rates payable by commercial and public radio stations under the Copyright Act 98 of 1978. The controversy originated after legislative amendments in 2002 that mandated royalty payments to the music industry for the use of sound recordings during broadcasts ([1]–[2]). The appellant, the National Association of Broadcasters (NAB), represents over 80 members involved in the South African Broadcasting Industry, including all major television broadcasters and a significant number of community radio stations ([2]). The first respondent, the South African Music Performance Rights Association (SAMPRA), acts as an accredited collecting society for sound recording royalties on behalf of the Recording Industry of South Africa (RISA) ([3]).

The dispute focuses primarily on the formula used to calculate the royalties owed by NAB to SAMPRA. SAMPRA initially proposed a formula that would significantly increase the royalties, while NAB countered with a formula that would lower the rates payable. The Copyright Tribunal rendered a decision on the matter, which NAB contested, leading to the appeal ([8]–[10]). Notably, the tribunal's determination raised questions regarding its jurisdiction, evidential foundations, and the legislative structure governing the royalty calculations, as there were no predefined statutes outlining the procedures for determination ([21]–[22]).

I – Issues

Several legal issues arose from this case, including:

  1. Whether the Copyright Tribunal appropriately exercised its jurisdiction in determining the royalty rate for NAB to pay to SAMPRA ([1]–[2]).
  2. The correctness of the respective royalty rates proposed by NAB and SAMPRA and whether the Tribunal's determination overlooked relevant evidence and factors critical to making such a decision ([7]–[8]).
  3. The legal implications surrounding the definition of “editorial content” in the calculation of royalties, especially concerning what elements can be included or excluded from broadcasting time ([69]–[70]).
  4. The question of whether the Tribunal has the authority to set the effective date from which royalties would be applicable ([77]).

R – Rules

The primary legal framework for this case is derived from the Copyright Act 98 of 1978, which establishes the basis for the payment of royalties for the broadcast of sound recordings. Relevant provisions include:

  • Section 9A: It mandates that no person may broadcast sound recordings without paying royalties, establishing that rates should ideally be determined through agreement or, in the absence thereof, by the Copyright Tribunal ([7]).
  • Regulations under the Copyright Act: These were established to provide for the accreditation of collecting societies but were noted to lack clarity and specificity regarding procedural guidelines for the Tribunal ([22]).
  • Judicial Precedent: Reference is made to the judgment of Oakdene Square Properties (Pty) Ltd v Farm Bothasfontein (Kyalami) (Pty) Ltd ([72]), which indicates that discretion in the Tribunal's determination should not overlook statutory obligations or relevant evidence.

A – Application

The court found that the Tribunal's decision, rendered by Sapire AJ, failed to adequately consider essential evidence, thereby breaching principles of rationality and legality in administrative decision-making. The court noted the following:

  1. Evidence Assessment: There was a consensus among witnesses that a more accurate reflection of royalties should be derived from the financial statements of the radio stations rather than extrapolated from SAMPRA's calculations based on estimated discounts ([60]).
  2. Methodological Flaws: The court criticized the Tribunal's reliance on arbitrary assessments of how royalty rates should be determined without adequately integrating substantial evidence or the economic realities surrounding the broadcasting and music industries ([54]–[59]).
  3. Discretion and Jurisdiction: It ruled that while the Tribunal had certain discretionary powers, its failures in assessing crucial evidence rendered its conclusions baseless. The discretion should be exercised with regard to prevailing evidence and established benchmarks ([75]–[73]).
  4. Final Formula Determination: The appellate court ultimately substituted Sapire AJ’s formula for a recalibrated formula reflecting a maximum royalty rate of 3%. The new formula considered the broadcasting time and verifiable revenue figures, excluding advertising time from the definition of editorial content, prompting broadcasters to be fairly charged only for music used in programming ([76], [78]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal by NAB, significantly altering the royalty determination and substituted the Tribunal's formula with its own, establishing that a reasonable maximum royalty rate should be 3% of net revenue. Additionally, the concern of excessive currency outflows from South Africa as a consequence of high royalty rates was considered, highlighting the balance between fair remuneration for artists and the financial viability of broadcasters ([75], [66], [70]). Ultimately, the judgment emphasized the necessity for a clear procedural framework and reasoned evidence-based decision-making within the context of copyright royalties, calling for legislative attention to rectify existing ambiguities in the law ([58]). This case underscores the intricate interplay between copyright law, economic realities, and the imperatives of a vibrant broadcasting sector in South Africa.
