# Case Note: Vhengani v The State [2007] SCA 76 (RSA)

## F – Facts
The appellant, N E Vhengani, was convicted of rape in the Venda High Court and sentenced to twenty years’ imprisonment. He appealed against the conviction on grounds of the credibility and reliability of the prosecution's primary witness, the complainant ([1], [2]).

The complainant testified that on March 29, 2001, while returning home from a neighbor's place, she was attacked by four men on a footpath. One of the men, later identified as the appellant, raped her after she was gagged and undressed ([2]). Following the assault, the complainant escaped and later reported the incident to her mother the next day, supported by a medical examination that confirmed sexual penetration ([4]).

The appellant denied the allegations, providing an alibi substantiated by his mother. However, the trial court rejected this defence, stating that the rejection was based on probabilities without convincing elaboration ([6]). The primary evidence against the appellant pivoted on the complainant, who was a single witness ([7]).

## I – Issues
The key legal questions in this appeal pertained to the reliability and credibility of the complainant as the sole witness in the case. Specific issues included:
1. Whether the trial court erred in its handling of the single witness rule.
2. Whether the complainant's testimony met the requisite standards of credibility and reliability given the inconsistencies in her account and the circumstances of the identification of the appellant ([1], [19], [21]).

## R – Rules
The legal rule regarding the cautionary approach to evidence given by a single witness is pivotal in this case. According to the South African case law, particularly from *S v Sauls* 1981 (3) SA 170(A), the trial judge is required to critically assess the credibility and reliability of a single witness’s evidence. 

Furthermore, in *S v Mthetwa* 1972 (3) SA 766(A), it was established that factors such as visibility, prior knowledge of the accused, and opportunities for accurate observation must be considered when a witness identifies the accused ([8], [15]). 

The importance of a cautious approach in cases of sexual offences was reaffirmed in the *S v Jackson* 1998 (1) SACR 470 (SCA), which indicated that while a cautionary rule existed, it should not stereotype complainants as inherently unreliable ([9]).

## A – Application
In examining the application of these rules, the court assessed the complainant's testimony and noted several inconsistencies that significantly undermined her credibility. For instance, there were discrepancies in her timeline: the complainant alleged she reached home at 22:00 after escaping at around 19:30, without adequately accounting for the time lapse of 2.5 hours ([10], [11]). 

Additionally, her initial falsehood about being chased raised questions regarding her reliability as a witness. The court highlighted her failure to report the rape incident to a neighbor, which she contradicted by offering her reasons for not requesting immediate assistance ([11], [12]). The trial court failed to apply the cautionary approach effectively, as it inadequately scrutinized the complainant's credibility and did not appropriately address the fallibility of human observation in identification ([20]).

As for the identification of the appellant, the complainant claimed to recognize his face and voice despite poor visibility and the lack of prior direct interactions, creating doubts regarding her ability to make an accurate identification ([15], [19]). 

Overall, the inconsistencies in the complainant's testimony—including her accounts of the attack, her emotional state after the event, and the details surrounding her identification of the appellant—exemplified significant flaws that should have mandated a more cautious approach by the trial court ([21]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the credible concerns arising from the complainant's evidence warranted the allowance of reasonable doubt regarding the appellant's identity as the assailant. It concluded that the trial court erred in its finding of the complainant's credibility without a thorough evaluative process of her evidence and failed to consider the various shortcomings that collectively undermined the prosecution's case ([21]).

Consequently, the appeal was upheld, resulting in the conviction and sentence being set aside. The implications of this judgment reaffirm the necessity for rigorous scrutiny of the reliability of single witness testimonies, particularly in serious allegations such as rape, thereby protecting the rights of the accused against wrongful conviction while ensuring the integrity of the judicial process.
