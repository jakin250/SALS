Supreme Court of Appeal

Minister of Police v SA Metal and Machinery Company (Pty) Ltd [2014] ZASCA 95

On 4 June 2010, before filing their defense, the appellants notified SA Metal’s attorneys that the goods had already been disposed of under the auspices of section 31 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA) ([2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2014 [2014] ZASCA 95

F – Facts
In April 2010, the respondent, SA Metal and Machinery Company (Pty) Ltd (hereafter referred to as “SA Metal”), initiated motion proceedings against the appellants, the Minister of Police and the Officer Commanding of the Elsies River Police Station, seeking the return of certain items seized by the South African Police Service (SAPS) from its premises on 13 June 2008 ([1]). The seized items included objects like brass water meters, mixed copper scrap, and various other scrap metal items ([1]).

On 4 June 2010, before filing their defense, the appellants notified SA Metal’s attorneys that the goods had already been disposed of under the auspices of section 31 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA) ([2]). Consequently, SA Metal amended its notice of motion to seek damages amounting to R8 611.50 for the value of the scrapped goods under the actio ad exhibendum, a legal action for the recovery of goods ([2]).

The High Court (Traverso DJP) initially granted this alternative relief, leading to the current appeal by the Minister of Police ([2]). The critical point of contention is whether SAPS had the authority to dispose of the goods and whether SA Metal could recover damages under the mentioned legal action ([3]).

I – Issues
The principal issues in this appeal are: (1) whether SAPS was justified in disposing of the seized goods and (2) whether SA Metal could successfully pursue a claim for damages under the actio ad exhibendum despite the disposal of the goods ([3]).

R – Rules
Relevant to this case are the provisions of the Criminal Procedure Act, specifically sections 20 and 31. Section 20 permits the seizure of items believed to be connected to criminal activity, while section 31 delineates the protocol for the return of items if no criminal proceedings are instituted ([3], [6]). The actio ad exhibendum requires proof of ownership, wrongful disposition of goods before litis contestatio, and resultant patrimonial loss as a basis for recovering damages ([15]).

Court precedent, notably in Minister van Wet en Orde en ‘n ander v Datnis Motors (Midlands) (Edms) Bpk [1989] 1 SA 926 (A) ([7]), establishes that the onus rests on the State to demonstrate unlawful possession of seized goods when claims are made for their return. Additionally, the Cadac (Pty) Ltd v Weber-Stephen Products Co judgment elucidates that motion proceedings may still be appropriate for illiquid claims when the legal status and liability can be determined without delving into significant factual disputes ([12]).

A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal found that SAPS failed to prove that SA Metal could not lawfully possess the goods ([9]). Consequently, the disposal of the goods lacked legal justification. The court emphasized that it was SAPS's responsibility to demonstrate that SA Metal's possession was unlawful, which they failed to do (1). Despite SAPS's claims regarding the illegality of SA Metal's possession due to alleged violations of the former Second-Hand Goods Act, the court did not agree, stressing that mere non-compliance does not equate to unlawful possession ([10]).

Additionally, the court noted that SAPS’s arguments regarding the need for litigation to resolve matters surrounding ownership were unsubstantiated. They concluded that the goods were not lawfully disposed of, establishing the basis for SA Metal’s damages claim ([14], [15]). The court held that the fact that SAPS had prior knowledge of SA Metal’s claims to the goods reinforced the assertion of wrongful alienation ([16]).

As a result, the appeal court sanctioned SA Metal's position on the entitlement under the actio ad exhibendum, asserting that the disposal of the goods did not preclude motion proceedings since the damages could be quantified later ([12], [18]).

C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal brought by the Minister of Police and held that SAPS wrongfully disposed of the goods, entitling SA Metal to damages under the actio ad exhibendum for the quantifiable value of the disposed goods ([18]). This case reiterates the principles governing the onus of proof regarding possession and the lawful authority for the police to dispose of seized items while simultaneously affirming significant procedural rights for individuals regarding property law. The implications of this judgment highlight the courts' endorsement of legal protections against unlawful seizures and the emphasis on procedural safeguards in police conduct, which is crucial for reinforcing constitutional rights and principles of justice within South African law.
