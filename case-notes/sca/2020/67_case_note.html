Case Title: Le Roux NO and Others v Botha NO and Others (Case no 427/19) [2020] ZASCA 67 (17 June 2020)

F - Facts
This case concerns a dispute over water servitudes connected to agricultural land situated near Worcester. The river in question is the Wolvenkloofstroom, which flows through a farm that was owned by Andries Abraham Le Roux and his brother, Schalk Johannes Le Roux. In 1983, both acquired the farm equally, which was subdivided into four properties by 1990, along with established servitudes allowing water usage from the river ([1]–[2]).

The dominant properties are owned by the trustees of the Goudyn Plase Trust (the "Goudyn Trust"), while the servient property is owned by the TC Botha Trust (the "Botha Trust"). Disagreements arose when the Goudyn Trust blocked the furrow that historically supplied water from the original weir on the servient farm to Dam 3, claiming the servient farm had no entitlement to that water ([4]–[5]). This led to a claim by the Goudyn Trust for spoliation, ultimately resulting in a legal battle over rights to access water from the river ([5]).

The dispute intensified after a flood in 2013 washed away the original weir and extraction pipe. The Botha Trust erected a new weir without environmental authorisation, which connected to a new furrow for water distribution. This construction was seen as unlawful by the Goudyn Trust, leading them to seek a court declaration to affirm their servitudal rights ([6]–[10]).

Initially, the High Court dismissed the Goudyn Trust's application and denied the appeal for court consideration, prompting the Goudyn Trust to seek leave for that appeal ([12]–[13]). The trusts were open to negotiation and proposed solutions during the appeal, leading to an in-principle settlement ([15]).

I - Issues
The key legal issues pertained to the interpretation and enforcement of water servitudes following the construction of the new weir and whether the Botha Trust had the legal right to alter existing water extraction arrangements without prior consent from the relevant authorities or the Goudyn Trust. This included questioning if the Goudyn Trust had the right to water abstraction at the previous weir location and the ramifications of these actions under the National Water Act 36 of 1998 (NWA) and the National Environmental Management Act 107 of 1998 (NEMA) ([10]–[14]).

R - Rules
The court relied on the principles of property law regarding servitudes, specifically the servitudes of aquaehaustus and aquaeductus. The National Water Act 36 of 1998 sets out the rights and responsibilities regarding water use in South Africa, emphasizing sustainable and equitable distribution. Additionally, the National Environmental Management Act 107 of 1998 requires environmental authorisation for any significant alterations to water courses ([10]). Precedents concerning spoliation and the rights of servitude holders in respect of water extraction were also applied ([1]–[14]).

A - Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal highlighted the need for a practical and peaceful resolution, considering the historical context of the water use agreements and past behaviours of both trusts. The court found that the disagreements stemmed from misunderstandings exacerbated by the lack of dialogue and cooperation. The established servitudes were intended to facilitate water extraction for agricultural needs, and while the Goudyn Trust believed it was within its rights to restrict water access, the Botha Trust's modifications were undertaken under pressing circumstances following the flood. The court encouraged both parties to negotiate and reach a consensual agreement that would adhere to legislative requirements while resolving their disputes practically ([15]).

The consent order reached during the appeal addressed immediate water supply issues, established a mechanism for equitable division of water, and provided for the installation of new infrastructure according to professional standards. This arrangement aimed for a ratio of 60% for the Botha Trust and 40% for the Goudyn Trust while ensuring existing water turns were respected, and the need for authorisation for ongoing works was highlighted ([21]).

C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the original order from the High Court should be set aside. It issued a new order allowing a framework for practical cooperation between the trusts regarding water extraction and the construction of necessary installations. In a significant shift from adversarial litigation to cooperative negotiations, the court did not impose costs on either party in the interests of fostering a constructive relationship between the neighbouring trusts. This case thus underscores the importance of dialogue, negotiation, and compliance with environmental legislation in disputes concerning water rights within agricultural contexts ([20]–[21]).
