Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: *Nhlapo v The State*

---

## F – Facts

The appellant, Banele Bafokeng Nhlapo, along with his co-accused Boy Lebyane, was convicted on June 8, 2011, by the Regional Court for robbery with aggravating circumstances and attempted murder ([1]). Nhlapo received a sentence of 17 years for robbery and 5 years for attempted murder, with 2 years of the latter running concurrently with the first, resulting in an effective sentence of 20 years ([1]). His appeal against both conviction and sentence was initially dismissed by a Gauteng High Court, with leave granted solely regarding sentencing ([1]). 

The incident took place on October 3, 2010, near a tavern in Etwatwa, Gauteng, where Nhlapo and his associates attacked the complainant, Mr. Ntuli, and his friend, Mr. Dlamini, after they left the tavern. Nhlapo stabbed Ntuli multiple times and proceeded to rob him of his belongings, including a cellular phone and cash ([3]). The attack escalated as Ntuli attempted to seek refuge in the yard of a bystander, Mr. Ndala, yet was pursued and further assaulted, resulting in severe injuries that required hospitalization ([4]).

The trial court found Nhlapo's defense of mistaken identity unconvincing, given the recognition by the victims and the evidence presented ([5]). The court also noted the heinous nature of the attack, emphasizing that it exceeded standard robbery circumstances and warranted a more severe sentence given the violence displayed ([6]).

## I – Issues

The case raises multiple legal questions:

1. Did the High Court err in confirming the sentencing decision of the trial court that exceeded the statutory minimum as outlined in section 51(2) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 (CLA)? ([2])
2. Was it appropriate for only part of the sentence for attempted murder to run concurrently with the sentence for robbery? ([2])

## R – Rules

The court relied on several legal principles and statutory provisions:

1. The significance of the *discretionary powers* of the sentencing court was explored, referencing *S v Rabie*, which emphasizes that appellate interference is merited only in cases of misdirection or when a sentence is disturbingly inappropriate ([8]). The appeal must not erode the trial court's sentencing discretion.
  
2. The framework established in section 51(2) of the CLA mandates a minimum sentence of 15 years for robbery with aggravating circumstances unless substantial and compelling circumstances justify a lesser sentence ([12]). The court also noted that section 280(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act generally mandates that sentences be served consecutively unless explicitly stated to run concurrently ([13]).

3. The *aggravating factors* of the crime and the *interests of the community* weigh heavily in sentencing considerations, as highlighted in various precedential cases such as *S v Matyityi* and *S v Kgosimore* ([10]).

## A – Application

In examining the trial court's sentence, the Supreme Court of Appeal found no critical misdirection or unjustified deviation from the minimum sentencing framework established by the CLA. The court examined whether the trial court took adequate account of Nhlapo’s personal circumstances but concluded that they were overshadowed by the gravity of the crimes committed.

The trial court’s rationale for ordering two years of the attempted murder sentence to run concurrently with the robbery sentence was understood within the context of its discretion. The court determined that the overall violence and the repeated nature of the attack effectively warranted a serious consequence and ruled that allowing the entire sentence for attempted murder to run concurrently would undermine the severity of the crime ([14]).

Nhlapo's argument that he should have received a reduced sentence focused on his youth, first offender status, and limited personal circumstances (unemployment, completion of standard 10); however, the court noted that this argument lacked substantial merit in light of the violent nature of the crime ([9])[15]. 

Furthermore, the appellant’s claim regarding the trial court's need to provide more explicit reasoning for exceeding the minimum was dismissed, as precedent holder cases clarified that no requirement exists for justifying upward deviations from minimum sentences. The trial court’s explanation of the harshness of the attack and the risks presented to the complainant was deemed sufficient ([18]) 

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Nhlapo's appeal, affirming the trial court's finding that the sentence of 20 years was not manifestly unjust and appropriately reflected the nature and gravity of the offences ([20]). The court underscored the principle that the interests of the community and the seriousness of the crime must guide sentencing, particularly in violent cases like this one. 

This ruling reinforces the judiciary’s commitment to ensuring that victims of violent crime are taken into serious consideration during sentencing and emphasizes that both personal circumstances and the nature of the crime must be weighed accurately. The decision serves as a critical reminder of the limited scope for appellate courts to interfere with sentencing judgements made by trial courts unless significant misdirection is apparent.
