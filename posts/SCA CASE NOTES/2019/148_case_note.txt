Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

## Case Note: Africa Cash & Carry (Pty) Ltd v The Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service [2019] ZASCA 148

### F – Facts
In the case of *Africa Cash & Carry (Pty) Ltd v The Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service* ([1–10]), the appellant, Africa Cash and Carry, operates a cash and carry business and was subject to estimated assessments for additional income tax and value-added tax (VAT) by the South African Revenue Service (SARS) amounting to approximately R600 million, covering the financial years from 2003 to 2009. The assessments were issued following an investigation by SARS, which revealed that the taxpayer had fraudulently suppressed sales figures, leading to a significant under-declaration of tax liabilities. The taxpayer’s own experts acknowledged a tax liability of at least R68 million.

After SARS disallowed the taxpayer's objections to the assessments, the taxpayer appealed to the Tax Court, raising defenses including an argument of prescription based on the fraudulent understatement of income. The Tax Court ultimately dismissed this appeal, altered the assessments under Section 129(2)(b) of the Tax Administration Act 28 of 2011, and refused to remit interest under Section 89quat of the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962 ([1–10, 30, 31]).

### I – Issues
The appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal raised several key legal questions:

1. Whether SARS was bound by its original assessments, which the taxpayer argued were formal determinations of tax liability that could not be altered without a formal amendment.
2. Whether the Tax Court acted beyond its jurisdiction by altering the assessments rather than remitting the matter back to SARS.
3. Whether SARS had sufficiently proven the reasonableness of the assessments, particularly after revising the estimated tax liability.
4. Whether the imposition of interest under Section 89quat should have been remitted ([30, 31]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework applicable to this matter is primarily derived from the following statutes:

1. **Tax Administration Act 28 of 2011**: Particularly Section 129(2), which empowers the Tax Court to confirm, alter, or remit assessments ([41, 42]).
2. **Income Tax Act 58 of 1962**: Specifically Section 89quat, which deals with interest charges on unpaid tax liabilities ([131, 132]).
3. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: Provides guidelines on the fairness of administrative decisions impacting taxpayers. 

The interpretation of “assessment” under Section 1 of the Act further underscores the requirement for tax liability determinations to be formally recorded by SARS ([32]).

### A – Application
The court analyzed the way in which SARS conducted estimated assessments based on the limited records provided by the taxpayer, which were found to be inadequate and misleading ([11–12]). The evidence presented included an expert report from Ms. Khoosal, which underscored the reasonableness of the methodologies used by SARS in arriving at estimated tax liabilities based upon a gross profit margin calculation. 

Regarding the taxpayer's argument that SARS was bound by the original assessments, the court noted that formal assessments can be altered when validated by credible evidence that establishes the tax liability’s basis, particularly when evidence is presented showing fraud or deceit by the taxpayer, as was established in this case ([31, 41–57]). 

The court also addressed the taxpayer's due process rights under PAJA. It found that adequate notice was given and that the taxpayer had every opportunity to present its case against SARS' calculations ([55, 56, 60]). This included significant cross-examination and full disclosure of evidence regarding altered sales figures and financial manipulations. 

The reasoning for the refusal to remit interest under Section 89quat was also scrutinized, wherein the court emphasized that the original assessments imposed significant penalties that were justified under the circumstances of non-disclosure ([131–136]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the Tax Court’s ruling. The court held that SARS did not err in its assessments and that the methodologies used to establish the taxable amounts were reasonable and justified given the context of the fraudulent actions by the taxpayer ([137]). The decision confirms the authority of the Tax Court to adjust assessments in the face of compelling evidence while maintaining due adherence to statutory guidelines, thus preserving the integrity of tax compliance in South Africa. The implications of this ruling are significant in reinforcing the mechanisms by which tax authorities can enforce compliance amid evidence of wrongdoing and the rigorous standards required to overturn administrative decisions in tax law.
