FIRAC Case Note

Case Title: Deltamune (Pty) Ltd and Others v Tiger Brands Limited and Others

Neutral Citation: Deltamune (Pty) Ltd and Others v Tiger Brands Limited and Others (Case no 847/2020) [2022] ZASCA 15 (4 February 2022)

Coram: Zondi, Makgoka, Mokgohloa, Gorven JJA and Meyer AJA

F – Facts

Background and Procedural History
The case stemmed from a listeriosis outbreak in South Africa between January 2017 and September 2018, linked to ready-to-eat meat products produced by Tiger Brands ([para 2]). The National Institute for Communicable Diseases (NICD) identified these products as the source of the outbreak, prompting a class action filed by 18 individuals against Tiger Brands ([paras 2–3]).

Tiger Brands, which produces various processed meat products, instituted subpoenas against several third-party appellants (including Deltamune and Federated Meats) to compel the production of documents relevant to the impending class action ([paras 1, 3]). These subpoenas were challenged in the Gauteng High Court, contending that they were overly broad and irrelevant to the class action, leading to a consolidated hearing of four applications ([para 18]).

The High Court dismissed the applications to set aside the subpoenas, prompting the appellants to appeal ([paras 17, 19]). The case was ultimately elevated to the Supreme Court of Appeal, where the key legal issues revolved around the relevance and specificity of the subpoenas issued ([paras 14–15]).

I – Issues

  1. Relevance of Subpoenas: Were the subpoenas issued against the third-party appellants relevant to the underlying class action regarding the listeriosis outbreak ([para 19])?
  2. Specificity of Subpoenas: Did the subpoenas adhere to the requisite standards of specificity as mandated by the Uniform Rules of Court and the Superior Courts Act ([para 57])?
  3. Potential Abuse of Process: Did the broad scope of the subpoenas constitute an abuse of process or amount to a "fishing expedition" ([para 13])?

R – Rules

The court relied on several legal standards, primarily:

  • Section 35(1) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013: This section governs the issuance of subpoenas, allowing any party to procure the attendance of witnesses or the production of documents for court proceedings ([para 7]).
  • Rule 38 of the Uniform Rules of Court: This rule outlines the procedure for issuing subpoenas duces tecum. It necessitates that documents requested must be sufficiently specified ([paras 58-59]).
  • Case Precedent: The court referenced past judgments regarding the relevance of subpoenas and the necessity for specificity, including Beinash v Wixley 1997 (3) SA 721 (SCA) and Sher and Others v Sadowitz 1970 (1) SA 193 (C) to stress that subpoenas must not be too broad or vague ([para 59]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal critically analyzed the High Court's reasoning and the objectivity of the subpoenas:

  • Relevance: The appellants argued that despite Tiger Brands’ amended subpoenas, the requests were still overly broad and lacked direct relevance to the allegations set forth in the class action ([para 19]). The court reiterated that relevance should be framed by the specific claims made within the particulars of claim for the class action, echoing the practice in Helen Suzman Foundation v Judicial Service Commission 2018 (4) SA 1 (CC) ([para 20]).
  • Specificity: The Court agreed that the subpoenas were egregiously vague and had not sufficiently specified the documents required, infringing upon Rule 38's standards. The flaws included the indiscriminate bundling of unrelated documents and insufficiently precise requests ([paras 59–61]).
  • Scope and Abuse of Process: The court recognized the risk that the subpoenas constituted a "fishing expedition," reflecting concerns raised by the appellants regarding the extensive and irrelevant nature of the demands. This approach runs counter to judicial efficiency and the interests of justice, which advocate for focused and pertinent document requests ([paras 13–14]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the subpoenas issued by Tiger Brands were excessively broad, lacked the required specificity, and thus infringed upon the rights of third-party appellants. Accordingly, it set aside the High Court's judgment, concluding that the subpoenas would not yield pertinent evidence for the determination of the issues underlying the class action ([para 63]).

This decision underscores the necessity for judicial processes to be equipped with tools that ensure the protection of third-party rights while maintaining the efficiency of court proceedings. The court ordered that the appellants be awarded costs in these proceedings ([paras 63, 64]).

The implications of this case extend to subsequent subpoenas, highlighting the standards that must be adhered to regarding relevance and specificity within litigation processes and potentially influencing future judicial approaches to third-party disclosures in class actions.

References: Deltamune (Pty) Ltd and Others v Tiger Brands Limited and Others [2022] ZASCA 15.
