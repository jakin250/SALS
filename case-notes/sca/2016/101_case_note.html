Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 101 #### F – Facts The dispute in *Gowar v Gowar

FIRAC Case Note: Gowar v Gowar (149/2015) [2016] ZASCA 101

F – Facts

The dispute in Gowar v Gowar arises from family feuds surrounding the control of multiple family trusts established by the late Reginald Denver Gowar, father of the parties involved ([1–2]). The parties to the appeal include the appellants Johanna Dorothea Gowar and Francois Peter Gowar, and the respondents Reginald David Gowar and Tertius Nicolaas van der Walt, who are their respective family members and trustees of various trusts, including the David Gowar Trust and the Rietfontein Trust ([2–3]). The trusts collectively manage significant farming assets, including several farms and livestock in Kensington and Somerset East ([3–5]).

Following the death of the trust founder in 2007, significant conflicts arose, particularly between the Gowar brothers regarding trust operations and fiduciary responsibilities. The second appellant, Francois, accused the first respondent, Reginald, of misconduct in managing the trusts, including misappropriation of trust assets ([6–8], [14–18]). The appellants sought relief in the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court to have the respondents removed as trustees, which was ultimately denied following an application dismissing both their requests and the counter-applications ([9–10], [11]).

I – Issues

The core issues in this appeal revolve around two principal questions:

  1. Under what circumstances may a court remove a trustee from office, either under the common law or per Section 20(1) of the Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988 (the Act) ([1]).
  2. Have the appellants adequately demonstrated the need to terminate the trusts under Section 13 of the Act, given the ongoing familial strife ([1]).

R – Rules

The legal framework addressing the removal of trustees stems both from common law principles and statutory provisions under the Act.

  • Common Law Principles: The court has inherent jurisdiction to remove a trustee if their continued office is detrimental to the welfare of the beneficiaries and the proper administration of the trust ([27–28]). The removal does not necessitate a showing of mala fides or misconduct alone, rather focusing on whether the trustee’s conduct risks the trust property ([30–31]).
  • Trust Property Control Act: Section 20(1) allows for removal if the court finds it serves the interests of the trust ([27]). Section 13 permits termination if provisions of the trust impede its objectives or harm beneficiaries ([33–34]).

A – Application

The court undertook to evaluate both parties’ claims in light of the legal standards established. In assessing the removal of the respondents, the court found that no evidence of misconduct had been satisfactorily proven by the appellants, and the alleged improprieties were contested, forming part of substantial factual disputes ([9], [37]). The court adhered to the precedent in Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd, which emphasized accepting the respondents’ accounts given the conflicting narratives between the parties involved ([36]).

In analyzing the termination of the trust, the court determined that the appellants failed to meet the statutory requirements of Section 13 of the Act. The allegations concerning the degradation of relations and assertions of impropriety were not sufficient to support a conclusion that the terms of the trust led to consequences unforeseen by the founder ([47]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that both the appeal and the cross-appeal were void of merit and upheld the decision of the Eastern Cape Division ([54]). The judgment highlighted that mere conflicts and grievances between trustees or beneficiaries do not suffice to establish grounds for removal under the governance of trust law. Consequently, the court affirmed the necessity of proving significant impairments to the trust’s objectives or the welfare of beneficiaries before considering either removal or termination of trust, which were not substantiated in this case.

Implications

The decision serves as a critical reinforcement of the judicial approach to trust management and the high threshold required to alter trustee appointments or terminate established trusts. The ruling underlines the importance of transparent evidence and the courts’ role in mediating internal family disputes over trust administration, emphasizing their circumspection before intervening in such conflicts.
