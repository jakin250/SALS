Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

# Case Note: Curators Ad Litem to Certain Beneficiaries of Emma Smith Educational Fund v The University of KwaZulu-Natal (510/09) [2010] ZASCA 136

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

The case centers around the *Emma Smith Educational Fund* established under the will of Sir Charles George Smith, a notable industrialist and politician in South Africa ([1]). His will, executed on 21 July 1938, dedicated a portion of his estate to support the higher education of specific categories of South African women, specifically those who were white and had resided in "Durban" for at least three years ([8]).

The fund was administered by the University of KwaZulu-Natal ("the University"), a public institution primarily funded by the government ([14]). As time progressed, issues arose regarding the racially exclusive nature of the fund, which conflicted with contemporary legal principles on equality and non-discrimination, as enshrined in the South African Constitution ([22]). The lower court ordered the removal of the racially restrictive provisions in the will via section 13 of the Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988 ("the Act") and proposed to change the geographic limitation from "Durban" to "the Ethekwini Municipality" ([22]).

The appellants, the curators ad litem ("the Appellants"), representing potential beneficiaries of the Fund, appealed against parts of the lower court's decision, particularly regarding the substitution of geographical descriptions, asserting that such amendments were improperly made and violated the freedom of testation ([20]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal questions posed in this case revolve around:

1. Whether the racially restrictive bequest in the will, limiting educational benefits to white South African women, is against public policy as per contemporary constitutional standards ([34]).
2. Whether the provisions of the Trust Property Control Act apply to the fund and permit the deletion of racially restrictive clauses and the amendment of geographic descriptions of eligibility ([25], [34]). 

Additionally, the case examines the implications of amending clauses concerning eligibility and the definitions of beneficiaries in light of the right to freedom of testation versus the constitutional imperatives of equality and non-discrimination ([42]).

### R – Rules

The following key legal principles and statutory provisions were employed by the court to resolve the issues:

1. **Section 13 of the Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988** - This section empowers the court to vary trust provisions that result in consequences not foreseen by the trust founder and which hamper the achievement of the trust’s objects or conflict with public interest ([24]).
  
2. **Constitutional Provisions** - The court analyzed the case with reference to the equality clause within Section 9 of the South African Constitution, which guarantees the right to equal protection and benefit of the law, and prohibits unfair discrimination on various grounds, including race ([35]).

3. **Case Law Precedents** - The court alluded to *Minister of Education & another v Syfrets Trust Ltd NO & another* ([34]), which established that provisions contrary to public policy, especially those promoting racial discrimination, could be judicially amended or removed.

### A – Application

The court assessed the racially exclusive stipulations of the will against modern constitutional requirements and noted that the University – as a public entity obliged to serve all sections of society – could not administer a fund that discriminated based on race ([14], [42]). This led to the conclusion that the original provisions of the fund were not only outdated but also constituted a violation of the principles of equality and fairness as mandated by the Constitution.

The application of section 13 of the Act was upheld, illustrating that the restrictive provisions hindered the actualization of the trust’s intended objectives, namely supporting education ([40]). The court deemed that removing the racially discriminatory language was essential for aligning the fund's purposes with contemporary public policy and ensuring accessibility to all eligible South African women ([38]). 

Conversely, the court scrutinized the request to amend the geographical description. It concluded that there was no evidence indicating that referencing "Durban," in the context of the modern-day expanded municipality, would sufficiently affect the university's ability to pursue the fund's objectives or that it would attract potential beneficiaries ([48]). 

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled unfavorably against the Appellants regarding the racially discriminatory provisions, affirming the decision of the lower court to delete the terms "European," "British," and "Dutch South African" from the will. However, it set aside the amendment that changed "Durban" to "the Ethekwini Municipality," on grounds of insufficient evidence to demonstrate that such a change would facilitate the Fund's administration ([50]).

The broader implications of this judgment are significant, as it reinforces the essential values of the South African Constitution, promoting equality and opposing racial discrimination in charitable settings. While the freedom of testation remains a fundamental tenet, the court has established that such freedom must be exercised within the bounds of contemporary public policy and constitutional mandates ([42]). 

Moreover, this ruling may establish precedent for future cases concerning the administration of trusts and charitable funds in light of prevalent equality and non-discrimination norms, thus ensuring that all individuals have equal access to educational opportunities regardless of their racial or ethnic background.
