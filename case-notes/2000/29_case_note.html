Jürgen Harksen v The President of the Republic of South Africa

Case Citation: Jürgen Harksen v The President of the Republic of South Africa CCT 41/99, Constitutional Court of South Africa, decided on 30 March 2000.

Constitutional Court 2000 CCT 41/99

Case Citation: Jürgen Harksen v The President of the Republic of South Africa CCT 41/99, Constitutional Court of South Africa, decided on 30 March 2000.

F: Facts

The case arises from a request for extradition made by the Federal Republic of Germany (FRG) to the South African government on 8 March 1994 concerning the appellant, Jürgen Harksen, who is alleged to have committed serious fraud in Germany and is living in South Africa ([1]). Harksen has engaged in multiple legal proceedings to thwart the extradition process ([2]).

Historically, South Africa possessed very few extradition treaties primarily due to its apartheid policies, influencing its extradition practices during and post-1994 ([3]). South Africa had no existing treaty with FRG at the time of Harksen's extradition request. However, international law allows extraditions based on reciprocity and comity ([3]).

The domestic legal framework governing extradition in South Africa is outlined in the Extradition Act, 1962. Amendments introduced post-1996 provided additional grounds for extradition, thereby affecting the interpretation of pending cases ([5], [6]). Harksen's extradition was initiated under section 3(2) of the Act, necessitating the South African President’s consent, which was duly granted in May 1995 ([7]).

Harksen subsequently filed three interrelated applications in the Cape Provincial Division of the High Court, questioning the constitutionality of section 3(2) and seeking to challenge the committal ordered by the magistrate ([8]). The High Court dismissed his constitutional claims but set aside the committal order on technical grounds, remitting the matter for re-evaluation.

Following the High Court’s decision, Harksen obtained a certificate to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court ([9]).

I: Issues

The primary constitutional issues raised by Harksen in the Constitutional Court were:

  1. Whether section 3(2) of the Extradition Act is inconsistent with section 231 of the South African Constitution ([10a]).
  2. Whether the President’s consent under section 3(2) contravenes sections 231(2) and (4), rendering it invalid and of no effect ([10b]).

Additionally, the respondents raised a plea of res judicata, contending that the issues had already been determined in previously adjudicated proceedings ([11]).

R: Rules

The relevant legal principles underpinning this case include:

  • The Extradition Act, 1962 (specifically, sections 3(2), 5(1), and 10), which governs domestic extradition proceedings in South Africa ([4], [5],[14]).
  • Section 231 of the South African Constitution, which regulates the negotiation, conclusion, and domestic enactment of international agreements, stipulating that such agreements require parliamentary approval unless specified otherwise ([16]).
  • The international legal principle of reciprocity and comity, allowing for extradition in the absence of treaties ([3]).

A: Application

The Constitutional Court analyzed the nature of the President’s consent under section 3(2), concluding that it operated solely within the purview of South African domestic law without creating any international obligations ([14], [22]). The Court emphasized that section 3(2) merely facilitated the extradition process according to domestic law which is governed by the Extradition Act ([15]).

Regarding the argument that the Presidential consent amounted to an international agreement requiring legislative approval, the Court outlined that no such agreement existed solely because consent was granted under the Act. The President's consent did not establish an enforceable international obligation recognizable in international law— it was fundamentally a domestic procedural step ([21], [22]).

Harksen’s assertions that violations of the Constitution arose due to a failure to follow procedures mandated by section 231 were declared unfounded. The Court maintained that the Constitution does not necessitate express references in legislation to ensure compatibility with its provisions, as all laws must inherently align with constitutional mandates ([18], [19]).

The appeal was ultimately considered on its merits without needing to resolve the res judicata objection, as the challenges were deemed insufficient ([29]).

C: Conclusion

The Constitutional Court dismissed Harksen's appeal, affirming that the Presidential consent did not contravene the Constitution or render the extradition proceedings invalid ([30]). The ruling reestablished the domestic scope of the Extradition Act in handling extradition requests devoid of international treaty obligations. This judgment reinforces the primacy of domestic legal frameworks in extradition matters, ensuring protections for individuals facing extradition.

The Court chose not to impose a costs order, reflecting a recognition of the genuine constitutional concerns raised even in their dismissal ([30]). This case illustrates the delicate balance between adhering to international obligations and ensuring compliance with domestic constitutional requirements, thereby shaping future extradition practices in South Africa.
