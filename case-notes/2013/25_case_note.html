Constitutional Court

Head of Department, Department of Education, Free State Province v Welkom High School and Harmony High School

In 2008 and 2009, both schools adopted controversial pregnancy policies stating that any learner who fell pregnant would be automatically excluded from school for the remainder of that academic year.

Constitutional Court 2013 [2013] ZACC 25 Date of Hearing: 5 March 2013 Date o

Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa
Case Number: CCT 103/12
Judgment: [2013] ZACC 25
Date of Hearing: 5 March 2013
Date of Decision: 10 July 2013

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The case involves the Head of the Free State Department of Education (Free State HOD) as the applicant and two public schools—Welkom High School and Harmony High School—along with their respective governing bodies, as respondents.

In 2008 and 2009, both schools adopted controversial pregnancy policies stating that any learner who fell pregnant would be automatically excluded from school for the remainder of that academic year. This led to significant legal proceedings after a 16-year-old learner from Harmony was informed that she could not return to school after giving birth, which would have impeded her ability to complete her education. Similarly, a learner at Welkom faced mandatory exclusion under identical circumstances ([6]–[11]).

In response to these actions, the governing bodies sought interdictory relief from the Free State High Court, asserting that the HOD lacked the legal authority to instruct principals to ignore their policies. Both the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the governing bodies, holding that the HOD’s conduct was contrary to the principles of legality and the South African Schools Act ([22]–[24]). This case ultimately reached the Constitutional Court for an appeal against the ruling of the Supreme Court of Appeal.

I – Issues

The main legal issues to be determined were:

  1. Whether the Free State HOD had the lawful authority to instruct the principals of public schools to disregard the pregnancy policies established by the governing bodies, given his powers under the South African Schools Act and constitutional duties ([28]).
  2. The extent to which the Court could engage with the underlying concerns regarding the constitutionality of the pregnancy policies, despite the respondents not directly challenging their legality in lower courts ([29]).

R – Rules

Several key legal rules and principles were invoked throughout the legal discussions, including:

  • Principle of Legality: Established in AAA Investments (Pty) Ltd v Micro Finance Regulatory Council and Another [2006] ZACC 9, it posits that state functionaries must act within the law's remit.
  • Constitutional Rights: The case implicates various rights protected by the South African Constitution, including the right to basic education (section 29), equality (section 9), and protection from discrimination based on pregnancy (section 9(3)).
  • South African Schools Act: This Act provides the framework governing the management of schools, placing governance in the hands of school governing bodies but simultaneously granting the HOD supervisory authority ([16]-[32]).

A – Application

The Court analyzed the intersection between the authority of the Free State HOD and the autonomy of school governing bodies. It was determined that:

  • The HOD’s instruction, compelling school principals to disregard the pregnancy policies, was founded upon the need to protect learners’ constitutional rights, particularly their right to education and protection against discrimination.
  • The Court noted the procedural shortcomings in the HOD's intervention, as he did not follow the proper procedures outlined in the Schools Act for withdrawing functions from governing bodies ([80]-[88]).

The Court discussed precedential implications from the Hoërskool Ermelo case to clarify that even though governing bodies possess powers of governance, these are limited by the overarching constitutional and legislative framework.

Additionally, the pregnancy policies were evaluated on their face for inconsistencies with the Constitution, and it was concluded that they potentially violated learners' rights to education, dignity, and equality. The inflexible nature of the policies was highlighted as problematic ([110]-[119]).

C – Conclusion

The Court ultimately ruled that the Free State HOD acted beyond his powers in instructing the principals to disregard the governing bodies' policies. However, significant concerns were raised regarding the constitutionality of the policies themselves, which were deemed to infringe upon the rights of pregnant learners.

The Court did not invalidate the policies outright but mandated that the governing bodies review their pregnancy policies in consultation with the Free State HOD, reflecting principles of cooperative governance embedded in the Schools Act. Furthermore, it required that reports on revised policies be submitted to the Court by a specified date. There was no order made as to costs given the constitutional importance of the issues involved ([128]–[134]).

With this decision, the Court underscored the necessity for public authorities to operate within their legal confines while upholding and protecting constitutional rights, thereby emphasizing the need for collaborative governance in addressing sensitive issues in educational policy.
