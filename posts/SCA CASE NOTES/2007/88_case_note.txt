Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2007

# Case Note: Central Authority v B.H. [2007] SCA 88 (RSA)

### FIRAC Method Case Note

---

### F – Facts
This case revolves around the minor child, N, who was born on May 1, 2002, in Zandvoort, Netherlands. N was brought to South Africa by his mother, B.H. (the respondent), in September 2003 for what the father believed was an agreed holiday of no more than three months. After approximately four months, the mother informed the father that she intended to permanently remain in South Africa with N ([paras 1–6]). 

The father, a citizen of the Netherlands, argued that the mother had ‘wrongfully retained’ N in South Africa after the agreed holiday had expired. He initiated proceedings in the Pretoria High Court under the Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction (1980), as incorporated into South African law by the Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction Act 72 of 1996 ([paras 2, 4, 8]). The Pretoria High Court found that the father's consent to taking N to South Africa did not equate to consent for permanent retention, which led to the appeal ([para 26]).

---

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues raised in this appeal concern:
1. **Whether the retention of N in South Africa constituted a ‘wrongful retention’ under the terms of the Hague Convention** ([paras 8, 12]).
2. **Whether the mother's defense based on 'consent' under Article 13(a) of the Convention was valid** ([para 25]).
3. **The procedural aspect relating to the timeliness of proceedings and delays experienced throughout the process** ([para 28]).

---

### R – Rules
The relevant legal framework includes:
1. **Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction (1980)**: Articles 3 and 4 stipulate that a removal or retention of a child is considered ‘wrongful’ if it violates custody rights from the child's habitual residence ([para 9]).
2. **Article 12** mandates the immediate return of a child if the retention was wrongful and no exceptions apply, such as demonstrating consent under Article 13(a) ([para 17]).
3. **Case law**: The court referred to previous cases (e.g., **Sonderup v Tondelli; Pennello v Pennello**) to highlight the standards for determining custody rights and the obligation on the resisting party to produce evidence of consent in order to succeed in an Article 13(a) defense ([paras 16, 19]).

---

### A – Application
In applying these rules to the facts:
1. The court found that N was habitually resident in the Netherlands at the time of retention, making the father’s application valid under the Convention ([para 9]).
2. For the mother's defense of ‘consent’ to be valid, she needed to prove, on the balance of probabilities, that both parties had agreed to the permanent relocation of N to South Africa ([para 20]).
3. The court examined the evidence presented, concluding that the mother did not establish a genuine dispute of fact regarding consent. Despite her assertions that there had been discussions about permanent relocation, the evidence indicated contradictions in her narrative ([paras 22–25]). The mother's attempt to demonstrate consent through a change of address form was insufficient due to discrepancies in her statements and the nature of the arrangements made prior to travelling to South Africa ([paras 23, 24]).
4. The court emphasized the requisite expeditiousness in Convention proceedings, highlighting the extended time frame of three and a half years from the alleged wrongful retention until the case was decided, viewing this as a systemic failure ([paras 28, 30]).

---

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appellant’s (Central Authority's) appeal, setting aside the Pretoria High Court's order due to the misinterpretation of the Convention's provisions. The court mandated that N be returned to the Netherlands. The findings established the importance of timely proceedings in international child abduction cases and clarified that the mother did not substantiate the defense of consent under Article 13(a) ([paras 26, 36]). The decision reinforces the principle that expeditiousness is vital in protecting children's interests in international custody disputes and emphasizes the need for South African authorities to comply with international obligations under the Hague Convention.

--- 

This case serves as a significant reference for future matters involving the Hague Convention on child abduction, particularly in relation to establishing custody rights and the burdens of proof for defenses against wrongful retention claims.
