Constitutional Court

National Commissioner of the South African Police Service v Southern African Human Rights Litigation Centre and Another [2014] ZACC 30

The case centers on the National Commissioner of the South African Police Service (SAPS) being compelled to investigate allegations of torture that occurred in Zimbabwe.

Constitutional Court 2014 [2014] ZACC 30

F – Facts

The case centers on the National Commissioner of the South African Police Service (SAPS) being compelled to investigate allegations of torture that occurred in Zimbabwe. The first respondent, the Southern African Human Rights Litigation Centre (SALC), together with the second respondent, the Zimbabwe Exiles’ Forum (ZEF), challenged the SAPS's refusal to undertake such an investigation following a torture docket compiled by SALC, which documented instances of torture against Zimbabwean nationals by the Zimbabwean police in March 2007 ([1]–[11]).

SALC presented detailed corroborative evidence—including 23 sworn statements detailing torture and corroborated by medical and legal experts—to the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA) and subsequently sought action from the SAPS to investigate these complaints in accordance with the Implementation of the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court Act 27 of 2002 (ICC Act) ([8], [12]). The SAPS refused to act, citing insufficient evidence, impracticality, and concerns over potential diplomatic consequences ([15], [64], [70]) This refusal led to the High Court, followed by the Supreme Court of Appeal, declaring that the SAPS was obligated to investigate these allegations under the constitutional duty enshrined in Section 205(3) ([16]–[18]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues revolve around:

  1. Does the SAPS have a duty to investigate international crimes, including torture, occurring outside South African territory under the South African Constitution and ICC Act? ([19]–[20]).
  2. Is the presence of the alleged perpetrators within South Africa a requirement to trigger the SAPS's duty to investigate? ([41]).
  3. What are the implications of universal jurisdiction in this context? ([61]).

R – Rules

Several legal rules and precedents were the basis for the Court's decision:

  1. Constitutional Provisions: Section 205(3) of the Constitution establishes the SAPS’s duty to prevent, combat, and investigate crime ([50]). The court relied on the interpretative principles found in Glenister II, which emphasize the necessity of aligning national law with international human rights standards ([22]–[24]).
  2. ICC Act: The ICC Act gives South African authorities jurisdiction to prosecute international crimes, codifying obligations to take action against crimes against humanity, including torture, regardless of whether perpetrators are present in South Africa ([32]–[39]).
  3. Principles of International Law: The case invoked principles of universal jurisdiction and emphasized that certain international crimes, like torture, necessitate due investigation without requiring the suspect's physical presence within domestic territory ([28]–[30]).
  4. Complementarity and Subsidiarity Principles: These principles dictate that domestic jurisdictions should be exercised primarily when the state is unable or unwilling to prosecute crimes, which was a crucial factor in determining South Africa's obligations in this case ([61]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court applied the law as follows:

  • Duty to Investigate: The Court concluded that SAPS has an unequivocal constitutional duty to investigate crimes against humanity, including torture. It emphasized that this duty is not merely permissive, but obligatory under both domestic and international law, referencing the ICC Act ([55], [80]).
  • Interpretation of Presence Requirement: The Court held that presence of the suspects in South Africa was not a precondition for initiating investigations. It interpreted the ICC Act to mean that investigations could commence based on the available evidence, irrespective of the suspects’ geographical location ([43], [48]).
  • Universal Jurisdiction: The Court affirmed that under customary international law, the prohibition against torture commands a response from any state, thus requiring South Africa to engage in a thorough investigation to uphold its international responsibilities, especially in the absence of action from Zimbabwean authorities ([27], [56]).
  • Consequence of Inaction: The Court critically reviewed the reasons provided by SAPS for its inaction and deemed them legally insufficient, highlighting the need for an investigation to address allegations supported by substantial evidence presented by SALC ([71], [77]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded that the SAPS's refusal to investigate was unlawful and required correction. The Court dismissed the appeal from the National Commissioner, ordered that the decision not to investigate be set aside, and mandated an investigation into the allegations of torture by the SAPS ([84]). This case underscores significant implications for South Africa's commitment to uphold international law obligations, reflecting a decisive stance against torture and reinforcing the importance of investigating human rights violations while asserting the necessity of preemptive measures against impunity for such crimes ([80]).

By establishing clear guidelines regarding the obligation to investigate international crimes, this ruling not only foregrounds the responsibility of South African authorities but also situates South Africa within the broader international human rights framework, shifting the paradigm towards accountability and justice.
