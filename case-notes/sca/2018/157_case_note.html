Supreme Court of Appeal

Reno Moyo v The State (848/18) [2018] ZASCA 157

The appellant, Reno Moyo, was convicted on 10 January 2012 by the Specialised Commercial Crimes Court in Johannesburg for 14 counts of theft, with the total amount embezzled amounting to R1,358,912.50 ([para 1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 157

F – Facts

The appellant, Reno Moyo, was convicted on 10 January 2012 by the Specialised Commercial Crimes Court in Johannesburg for 14 counts of theft, with the total amount embezzled amounting to R1,358,912.50 ([para 1]). For the purpose of sentencing, the court imposed a 7-year imprisonment for counts 1 to 10, and counts 11, 12, and 14 also garnered a 7-year sentence. The most significant count, count 13—where the amount involved exceeded R500,000—resulted in a 15-year imprisonment sentence, with a stipulation that Moyo would not be eligible for parole until he had served two-thirds of that sentence ([para 2]).

After the trial court dismissed Moyo's application for leave to appeal against the sentence on 11 December 2012, he sought recourse to the Gauteng Division of the High Court under section 309C of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which was similarly refused by two judges ([paras 3–4]). Subsequently, Moyo petitioned the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) for special leave to appeal the high court's refusal under section 16(1)(b) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 ([para 4]).

The SCA considered the precedent in S v Khoasasa, establishing that the refusal of leave to appeal by a high court is a judicious issue for further appeal, necessitating special leave for review ([paras 5–7]).

I – Issues

The principal issue for determination before the SCA was whether the High Court's refusal to grant Moyo leave to appeal against his sentence constituted grounds for a further appeal ([para 8]). Specifically, the Court needed to ascertain if there were sufficiently special circumstances that warranted the grant of special leave, alongside the requisite reasonable prospects of success on the merits ([para 9]).

R – Rules

The SCA relied on established jurisprudence concerning applications for special leave to appeal. The court asserted that an applicant must demonstrate not only reasonable prospects of success but also that special circumstances exist that merit further appeal ([para 10]). The Court noted pivotal cases such as Westinghouse Brake & Equipment v Bilger Engineering and Van Wyk v S, Galela v S, which delineated the types of 'special circumstances' that might arise ([para 10]).

Furthermore, the SCA referenced precedent that outlined common misdirections which could substantiate ‘special circumstances’, notably, the failure of the lower court to provide the accused an opportunity to address the court before imposing a non-parole period ([para 11]).

A – Application

In applying these rules to Moyo’s circumstances, the SCA determined that the trial court had committed a critical misdirection by not allowing Moyo an opportunity to address it prior to setting the non-parole period under section 276B(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act ([para 11]). The Court also highlighted the absence of adequate reasons given for the imposition of this non-parole period, which further substantiated the claim for special circumstances to exist in Moyo’s case ([para 11]).

Consequently, the Court concluded that there was a reasonable prospect of success on the merits of the appeal regarding count 13, particularly as the concurrent sentences imposed for the other counts were contingent upon this significant count ([para 12]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the SCA upheld Moyo's appeal, setting aside the High Court's order that had refused him leave to appeal, and allowed the application for leave to be granted regarding the effective sentence imposed ([para 12]). This ruling signifies both the importance of procedural fairness in sentencing matters, as well as the courts' recognition of the potential impacts of misdirections on appeals. Moyo's case underlines the judiciary's duty to ensure that defendants are afforded all procedural rights, crucially impacting the legitimacy of sentencing outcomes in South African law.

In light of the above ruling, the SCA indicated that the merits of the appeal should be returned to the High Court for consideration without prejudice to the parties involved, thus preserving multiple avenues for redress in matters of significant sentencing decisions ([para 12]).

Implications

The implications of this case extend to the necessity for transparency and accountability in sentencing procedures while reinforcing the judicial acknowledgment of potentially unequal power dynamics inherent in criminal proceedings. The decision enhances the appellate court's role in safeguarding against potential miscarriages of justice stemming from procedural oversights.
