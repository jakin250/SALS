Century City Apartments Property Services CC v The Registrar of Companies and Close Corporations — FIRAC

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009 [2009] ZASCA 157 (27 November 2009) Court: The Supreme C

Neutral Citation: Century City Apartments v Century City Property Owners (57/09) [2009] ZASCA 157 (27 November 2009)

Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal, Republic of South Africa

Coram: Harms DP, Lewis, Mhlantla JJA, Hurt, and Wallis AJJA

Heard: 6 November 2009

Delivered: 27 November 2009

F – Facts

The core of this appeal pertains to trade mark infringement involving the respondent, Century City Property Owners’ Association (a section 21 company), and the first appellant, Century City Apartments Property Services CC, together with the second appellant, the Registrar of Companies and Close Corporations. The lower court had ruled in favor of the Association, granting it all relief sought concerning trade mark infringement related to the name "Century City" and its corporate identity.

Century City, a substantial commercial and residential development on 300 hectares near Cape Town, began in 1997 with plans for an integrated operation to provide diverse services within its precinct ([para 2]). The trade marks in question, including various registrations featuring the name "Century City," were assigned to the Association by the original developer. Several trademarks, registered under different classes, were allegedly infringed by the Appellant's use of “Century City Apartments” in its business activities ([paras 3-5]).

The Association claimed that the appellants’ designation of "Century City Apartments" infringed upon its registered trade marks, thus constituting both trade mark infringement and passing-off ([paras 1, 7-8]). The court below accepted this assertion without fully distinguishing between the name and device marks, nor addressing the nature of the services the appellant provided ([para 9]).

I – Issues

  1. Legal Standing: Did the appellant's use of "Century City" infringe on the trade marks owned by the respondent as per Section 34 of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993?
  2. Similarity of Marks: Are "Century City" and "Century City Apartments" identical or confusingly similar, leading to a likelihood of deception or confusion among consumers?
  3. Geographical Indication: Can the registration of the trade marks be deemed invalid under Section 10(2)(b) of the Trade Marks Act, which prohibits the registration of marks that serve to designate the geographical origin of services?
  4. Passing-off: Does the appellant's use of "Century City" constitute passing-off, given its descriptive nature as relating to the geographical location?
  5. Undesirability of Corporate Name: Is the appellant’s corporate name "Century City Apartments Property Services CC" undesirable under Section 20 of the Close Corporations Act due to its association with the respondent's trade marks?

R – Rules

The legal framework pertinent to this case involves:

  1. Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, specifically sections dealing with trade mark infringement ([s 34]) and the stipulations regarding geographical indications ([s 10(2)(b)]).
  2. Common Law Principles: The passing-off doctrine, which protects an unregistered trade mark or goodwill from misrepresentation as to the source of goods or services ([para 54]).
  3. Close Corporations Act 69 of 1984, specifically Section 20, which provides for applications concerning undesirable corporate names based on potential confusion or harm to existing trade mark rights.

Previous case law, such as Verimark (Pty) Ltd v BMW AG and Commercial Auto Glass (Pty) Ltd v BMW AG, outline the nature of trade mark use and infringement ([para 10]). The courts have consistently highlighted the importance of consumer perception in assessing the likelihood of confusion [see Compass Publishing BV v Compass Logistics Ltd; LTJ Diffusion SA v Sadas Vertbaudet SA].

A – Application

Trade Mark Infringement:

  1. Identification of the Marks: The court first identified the alleged infringing mark, emphasizing the legal standard that allows for descriptive use which does not amount to trade mark use ([para 10]). The Appellant’s utilization of "Century City" was accepted as trade mark usage in relation to its services ([para 11]).
  2. Comparison of Marks: The court addressed whether the marks were identical or confusingly similar. While the addition of "Apartments" denotes some distinction, the court found that the appellant's mark was confusingly similar to the registered mark as it could lead consumers to believe both entities were connected ([para 12-14]).
  3. Confusion Likelihood: The average consumer test was applied, focusing on the overall impression created by the marks. Given the services were in proximity, the likelihood of confusion was considered significant ([para 13]).

Geographical Indication:

In evaluating the counter-application under Section 10(2)(b), the court assessed whether the name "Century City" had become a geographical designation, determining that it had indeed developed such significance through its prominent use ([para 50]). Therefore, it ruled that registrations of marks designating geographical origin were subject to revocation.

Passing-off:

The appellant’s use of "Century City" in its descriptive capacity failed to support a passing-off claim due to the inherent descriptiveness concerning the location of services provided ([para 54]). This correspondence reduced the claim's validity concerning the trade mark designation.

Undesirability of Corporate Name:

The court concluded that the lower court erred in deeming the corporate name undesirable based solely on the resemblance to the respondent's marks. Fundamental distinctions between the entities were sufficient to negate any claims of potential consumer confusion ([paras 51-53]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal from the High Court with costs. The registration of the trade marks in classes 35, 36, and 42 was revoked, while the registration in class 35 was rectified to omit "management services." Moreover, the court found that the corporate name of the appellant was not undesirable, thus reversing the prior findings regarding the same.

Implications

This decision reaffirmed the standards for establishing trade mark infringement and clarified the application of geographical terms within trade marks. Notably, it delineated the boundary between descriptive and trade mark use, emphasizing the need for careful examination of consumer perception in trade mark law ([paras 58-59]). The ruling serves as a precedent for future cases involving trade marks that reflect geographical origins and clarifies the conditions under which a trade mark might be deemed invalid on the grounds of becoming a geographical indication.

This case highlights the balancing act courts must perform in protecting brand identities while allowing legitimate descriptive usage, thus reinforcing the importance of context in interpreting trade mark law.
