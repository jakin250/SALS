Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

**CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA**  
**Case CCT 385/21**  
**AmaBhungane Centre for Investigative Journalism NPC v President of the Republic of South Africa**  
**Neutral citation:** [2022] ZACC 31  
**Judgment:** Majiedt J  

---

### 1. Facts
The matter involves the **AmaBhungane Centre for Investigative Journalism NPC** ("amaBhungane") challenging the constitutionality of the **Executive Ethics Code** (the "Code") enacted in 2000 under **Proclamation No. R41 of 2000**. The primary question is whether the Code requires the disclosure of donations made to internal political party campaigns, specifically donations tied to the **"CR17 campaign"** associated with the then-President's election to the leadership of the **African National Congress (ANC)** ([1]–[2]).

AmaBhungane, a non-profit investigative journalism entity, intervened after the **Gauteng High Court** ruled on related matters involving the Public Protector's findings against the President regarding these donations. The High Court had found that the President breached the ethics obligations by not disclosing such donations. However, amaBhungane's original challenge to the Code's constitutionality was not initially adjudicated, leading to further court proceedings ([4]–[10]).

Following several procedural steps, the Full Court upheld amaBhungane's challenge, declaring the Code unconstitutional to the extent it did not mandate the disclosure of such campaign donations and issued an order of constitutional invalidity which the Constitutional Court was called to confirm ([11]–[12]).

### 2. Issues
The core legal issues presented to the Constitutional Court include: 
1. Whether the Code, as interpreted, imposes a duty of disclosure regarding donations to political party campaigns.
2. Whether the current form of the Code is constitutional given the broader implications for transparency, accountability, and the fight against corruption ([13]–[14], [50]).

### 3. Rules
The relevant legal frameworks involved in the case are:
- **Section 96(1)** of the Constitution, which mandates that Cabinet members act in accordance with a prescribed Code of Ethics.
- **The Executive Members' Ethics Act 82 of 1998**, specifically:
  - **Sections 2(2)(c)** which outlines the need to disclose all financial interests when assuming office and any interests acquired thereafter.
- **International obligations**, such as the **United Nations Convention Against Corruption**, which mandate transparency concerning political financing ([31]–[42], [66]).

### 4. Application
In its judgment, the Court grapples with the interpretations of “financial interest” within the Ethics Act. The applicant contends that the Code's current wording allows members to avoid disclosure by structuring contributions such that they fall outside the "personal benefit" requirement outlined by previous judgments ([63]).

The Court found that the Code fails to comply with the Ethics Act and, consequently, the Constitution. It emphasized the necessity of transparency to combat corruption, noting that excluding internal party campaign contributions from disclosure undermines democratic accountability and the public's right to information ([66]–[70]). The Court reiterated that the legislation must be interpreted in a way that aligns with constitutional imperatives of openness and accountability ([57]–[66]).

### 5. Conclusion
The Court confirmed the **Full Court’s** declaration of invalidity regarding the Code, ruling that it is unconstitutional for not requiring disclosure of donations to internal political party campaigns ([74]–[75]). The confirmation of the High Court's order indicates a move towards greater transparency in political financing and affirms the responsibility of the executive to act openly in accordance with constitutional principles. Furthermore, the order's prospective application suspends the declaration of invalidity for 12 months, allowing the President time to amend the Code accordingly, while costs were awarded in favor of amaBhungane ([75]). 

This judgement reinforces the necessity of robust measures against corruption and ensures that the ethical guidelines surrounding political financing are aligned with constitutional standards, contributing to a more informed electorate and democratic process.
