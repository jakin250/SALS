Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

### FIRAC Case Note on *Moosa and Others v Minister of Justice and Correctional Services and Others* [2018] ZACC 19

#### F – Facts
[1] The case originated from a ruling by the High Court of South Africa, Western Cape Division, which declared section 2C(1) of the Wills Act 7 of 1953 constitutionally invalid due to its exclusion of polygamous Muslim marriages from the definition of "surviving spouse." The claimants, Dr. Fareed Moosa (First Applicant), Amina Harneker (Second Applicant), and Farieda Harneker (Third Applicant), sought confirmation of the High Court's order. The deceased, Osman Harneker, had two wives under Muslim law, and the wills of the deceased did not acknowledge the right of the third applicant (Farieda Harneker) to inherit under the Wills Act due to its narrow definition of "surviving spouse" ([1–4]).

[2] The deceased had two marriages recognized under Islamic rites, and the wills’ distribution was supposed to be in accordance with Islamic law. Following the death of Harneker, his executor sought to apply section 2C(1) to distribute the will's benefits to both wives, but the Master of the High Court recognized only one wife due to the Act's restrictive language. This interpretation led to a challenge by the third applicant against the Registrar of Deeds' ruling ([5–9]).

[3] The High Court found in favor of the applicants, citing violations of the right to equality and dignity as enshrined in sections 9 and 10 of the Constitution. The court acknowledged that the historical context of the Wills Act's language excluded multiple surviving spouses, manifesting unfair discrimination against Islamic marriages. The respondents did not oppose the High Court's order ([10–12]).

#### I – Issues
[4] The central issue addressed by the Constitutional Court revolved around the interpretation of the term "surviving spouse" in section 2C(1) of the Wills Act. Specifically, whether the exclusion of surviving spouses from polygamous Muslim marriages from benefiting under the Wills Act constitutes a violation of their rights to equality and dignity under the Constitution ([12–13]).

[5] Additional concerns arose regarding whether the High Court's reasoning sufficiently addressed dignity violations specifically attributed to the non-recognition of the Muslim marriage in inheritance contexts. The issues required balancing fairness in testate succession against the principles of freedom of testation ([16]).

#### R – Rules
[6] The primary legal provisions implicated in this case involve sections 9 and 10 of the South African Constitution, which articulate the rights to equality and dignity. Section 9 prohibits unfair discrimination and mandates that everyone is equal before the law. Section 10 further guarantees the inherent dignity of every person ([10–13]).

[7] Judicial precedent cited includes the decisions in *Hassam v Jacobs N.O.* and *Daniels v Campbell N.O.*, which emphasize the judiciary's role in recognizing and remedying inequalities stemming from outdated legal frameworks. The High Court also referenced international obligations under the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women, which requires the elimination of discriminatory practices against women ([10–12]).

#### A – Application
[8] Upon review, the Constitutional Court agreed with the High Court's reasoning that the term "surviving spouse" historically stems from a monogamous context and therefore does not encompass those in polygamous Muslim marriages. The court noted that the Wills Act must evolve to align with constitutional values that promote equality and dignity ([12–13]).

[9] The Court acknowledged that the application of the term "surviving spouse" resulted in a direct violation of the rights of the third applicant. It argued that the definition neglects significant social realities in contemporary South African society, particularly regarding the rights of individuals in Muslim marriages, thereby reinforcing systemic discrimination. As such, it concluded that merely limiting the effects of this enforcement temporally was insufficient; an immediate rectification is mandated to align legislation with constitutional values ([13–20]).

[10] The judgment examined the implications of past injustices and recognized the need for a retrospective application of the remedy to extend to those affected by the legal framework dating back to 27 April 1994, effectively necessitating the amendment of section 2C(1) to include "every husband and wife of a monogamous and polygamous Muslim marriage" under its definition of "surviving spouse" ([20–21]).

#### C – Conclusion
[11] The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court's order, declaring section 2C(1) of the Wills Act unconstitutional. The ruling expands the definition of "surviving spouse" to include spouses from polygamous Muslim marriages, correcting injustices faced by these individuals and reaffirming their rights to equality and dignity ([20–21]).

[12] By reinforcing the backward compatibility of this ruling, the court directed the legal system towards a more inclusive interpretation of marriage that respects diverse cultural practices in contemporary South Africa. Ultimately, the judgment serves as a significant milestone in constitutional jurisprudence regarding the intersection of traditional marriage customs and formal legal recognition in the realm of inheritance and estate law ([20–21]).

This case highlights the necessity for legal frameworks to adapt continually in recognition of the evolving social fabric while ensuring that the principles of equality and dignity are uniformly applied, reflecting South Africa's constitutional commitment to substantive justice for all its citizens.
