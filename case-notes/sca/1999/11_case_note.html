Supreme Court of Appeal

Denver Joshua Francis v The State

The appellant, Denver Joshua Francis, was involved in a series of violent events that unfolded on the night of 27 February 1995.

Supreme Court of Appeal 1999

Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

Case No: 253/97

Judges: Schutz JA, Melunsky AJA, Madlanga AJA

Heard: 9 March 1999

Delivered: 18 March 1999

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The appellant, Denver Joshua Francis, was involved in a series of violent events that unfolded on the night of 27 February 1995. The sequence began when he fired two shots at Allan Gobey, the father of his estranged partner Melanie Gobey. The first bullet narrowly missed Gobey, while the second shot struck him in the left elbow. Hours later, Francis killed Melanie Gobey. The case centers around Francis's assertion of non-pathological criminal incapacity, specifically "sane automatism," claiming he was unable to distinguish right from wrong or control his actions due to alcohol impairment and emotional distress stemming from his possessive feelings for Melanie ([paras 1–2]).

The trial court, presided over by Shearer J, found Francis guilty of murder with dolus eventualis (indirect intent) for killing Melanie Gobey and assault with intent to cause grievous bodily harm towards Allan Gobey for the first shot. The court sentenced him to 14 years imprisonment for murder and 6 months for assault. Francis appealed against both his conviction and sentence, arguing instead for diminished responsibility ([paras 1–2], [paras 31–34]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues in this appeal included:

  1. Whether the appellant acted in a state of non-pathological criminal incapacity—specifically, if he could not appreciate the wrongfulness of his actions at the time of the shooting ([paras 2–4]).
  2. Whether the trial court properly assessed the nature of the appellant's intent (dolus eventualis versus dolus directus) regarding both the shooting of Gobey and the murder of his daughter, Melanie ([paras 30–34]).
  3. The appropriateness of the sentences imposed on the appellant after considering the facts of the case, including his background and mental state at the time of the offenses ([paras 31–34]).

R – Rules

Key legal principles invoked in the judgment include:

  1. Criminal Incapacity: The appellant's defense of non-pathological incapacity necessitates establishing a reasonable doubt regarding his criminal responsibility. In instances of "sane automatism," the onus is on the State to demonstrate the voluntariness of the accused's actions beyond a reasonable doubt (S v Cunningham 1996 (1) SACR 631 (A) at 635 I) ([para 4]).
  2. Intent: The distinction between dolus eventualis and dolus directus was crucial, with dolus eventualis requiring proof that the accused foresaw the possibility of their actions leading to death or serious harm ([paras 30–32]).
  3. Assessment of Evidence: The trial court's credibility determinations of the witnesses, particularly those providing expert testimony regarding intoxication and the appellant’s mental state, were paramount in determining the outcome ([paras 25–26, 29]).

A – Application

The court extensively analyzed the evidence, including testimonies regarding the appellant's mental state. Evidence indicated that Francis had consumed a significant amount of alcohol, rendering his memory of the events fragmented ([paras 10–12]). However, the court found that despite his high blood alcohol level (0.28g per 100ml), he exhibited actions consistent with deliberate intent, such as driving to the Gobey residence and carrying a loaded firearm ([paras 26–28]).

The judges noted that Francis had articulated various threats and clear intentions in earlier discussions, particularly concerning his relationship with Melanie and his views on others she involved herself with, indicating a premeditated plan to take violent action ([paras 27–30]). The decision underscored the importance of intent in evaluating the severity of his actions, particularly regarding the shooting of Gobey and his daughter.

Moreover, the court critically evaluated the testimonies of both the appellant and the witnesses, finding that the appellant displayed a pattern of behavior that was neither impulsively violent nor entirely unprogrammed. For example, his actions post-shooting—engaging in coherent conversations and demonstrating an understanding of the consequences of his actions (as noted in communications with Schnoor and police)—contradicted claims of complete incapacitation due to alcohol ([paras 15–28], [paras 30–32]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the murder conviction and sentence based on the presence of dolus eventualis, emphasizing the appellant’s calculated actions, motives, and the progression of events leading to the murder ([paras 30–35]). The ruling recognized the appellant's poor mental health and background but reasoned that such factors did not exculpate him from responsibility for his actions. On count one, the appeal court amended the conviction from assault to attempted murder, highlighting the gravity of firing at Gobey and increasing the sentence from six months to three years, with two years running concurrently. The effective sentence was thus 15 years imprisonment ([paras 32–34]).

This ruling serves to underscore the judicial recognition of accountability in cases involving both psychological distress and substance abuse while reaffirming the criteria for assessing intent in violent crimes.
