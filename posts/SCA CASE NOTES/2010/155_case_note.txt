Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA

# FIRAC Case Note

## Case Title: **Els v Weideman and Others**  
Neutral citation: Els v Weideman (512/09) [2010] ZASCA 155 (30 November 2010)

### F – Facts ([paras 1–26])
The case involves an appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa concerning the publication of an article by the respondents, which the appellant, Christiaan Jurie Els, claimed breached an interim interdict granted by the South Gauteng High Court. 

The appellant is a singer and well-known personality who, during the events leading to this litigation, was residing in New Zealand ([para 3]). The first respondent, Esmaré Weideman, is the editor of the magazines *Huisgenoot* and *You*, published by the second respondent, Media 24 Limited, while the third respondent, Izelle Venter, is the editorial head in Johannesburg ([paras 4–5]). 

On February 11, 2008, the newspaper *Beeld* published an article wherein Robbie Klay, a young actor, alleged sexual molestation by an unnamed established figure in the entertainment industry over several years ([paras 6–7]). This mention prompted a series of communications between Els and the respondents, culminating in a request for an interdict against the publication of Klay's allegations, which was heard and granted by Sutherland AJ ([paras 15–16]).

The interim interdict prohibited the publication of the article while allowing for the identity of the interdict applicant (Els) to be disclosed ([para 16]), but did not explicitly cover the publication of the proceedings prior to the final interdict. The respondents proceeded to publish the article on February 21, 2008, which included details that indicated the identity of Els, despite the granted interdict ([paras 19–22]).

In response, Els filed contempt proceedings against Weideman, Media 24, and Venter on February 25, 2008, alleging that their actions disregarded the court order ([para 25]). The Western Cape High Court dismissed these proceedings based on jurisdictional grounds and merits, concluding the offending publications were not covered by the order ([para 26]).

### I – Issues ([paras 27–39])
The central legal issues as emerged in this case are:
1. **Jurisdiction**: Whether the Western Cape High Court had the jurisdiction to enforce an order made by the South Gauteng High Court regarding contempt of court.
2. **Interpretation of the Interdict**: Whether the publication of the article—even in a truncated form—constituted a breach of the interdict.
3. **Intention and Bad Faith**: Whether the respondents acted with the requisite intention and mala fides in potentially contravening the court order.

### R – Rules ([paras 29–39])
The relevant legal rules considered by the court include provisions from:
- **The Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959**, which states that judgments and orders of one High Court are enforceable in all other jurisdictions ([para 29(a)]).
- **-section 19(1) of the Act**, which outlines the jurisdictional parameters of the High Courts in terms of their ability to adjudicate actions involving parties located within their territories ([para 29(b)]).
- **Fakie NO v CCII Systems (Pty) Ltd 2006 (4) SA 326 (SCA)**, which discusses the necessary mens rea for contempt of court, requiring deliberate disregard and mala fides when a party violates a court order ([paras 52-53]).

### A – Application ([paras 40–68])
In its analysis, the court identified that:
1. **Jurisdiction**: It ruled that the Western Cape High Court indeed had the jurisdiction to hear the contempt application, countering the findings made by Dlodlo J in the court a quo. The court noted that the enforcement of court orders extends throughout South Africa and affirmed that no legal principle necessitates adjudication by the court issuing the original order alone ([paras 27-39]).

2. **Breach of Interdict**: The court concluded that the scope of the interdict was clear and prohibitively broad, encompassing any form of publication that reflected the substance of the article ([paras 40–42]). The respondents' actions—through editorial content, articles, and covers—were deemed to breach the order since they engaged in acts that led readers to infer the identity of the appellant, despite efforts to redact specific terms ([paras 41-66]).

3. **Intention and Mala Fides**: The court scrutinized the state of mind of the respondents, establishing that they acted with mala fides in their disregard for the interdict. The editorial reflected a conscious strategy to circumvent the order while maintaining the appeal of public interest in the alleged molestation claims. Their conduct indicated a brazen defiance to the authority of the court, signifying that negligence was not a sufficient defense against contempt ([paras 51-68]).

### C – Conclusion ([paras 70–85])
The Supreme Court of Appeal found the following key outcomes:
1. The appeal was successful concerning the first and second respondents, with both convicted of contempt of court.
2. The third respondent, Venter, was exonerated of the charges as insufficient direct connection to the contempt was established ([para 71]).
3. The order of the court a quo was set aside, thus requiring the matter to be remitted for further consideration regarding appropriate sanctions and costs ([para 72]).

The implications of this judgment affirm the court's position on the sanctity of its orders and the necessity for all individuals and entities—including media organizations—to adhere to judicial directives. The ruling underscores the importance of accountability in situations where the balancing of free expression is contested against the integrity of lawful court processes.
