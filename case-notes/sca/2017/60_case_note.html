Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 60

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case, Lourens v Premier of the Free State Province & another (526/16) [2017] ZASCA 60, involves an appeal by Cornelius Johannes Alexander Lourens (the appellant) against the dismissal of his application for a court order holding the Premier of the Free State Province in contempt of court ([1]). The original issue arose from a declaratory order granted in 2013, wherein the Premier was mandated to regulate and monitor the use of official languages via legislative measures by 31 March 2014 ([2]).

Subsequent to the granting of this order, the Premier acted by directing the Member of the Executive Council for Sports, Arts, Culture, and Recreation to draft a language policy and a bill, both of which were submitted to the Free State Legislature ([3]). However, due to the general elections in 2014, the proposed legislation lapsed, prompting the appellant to claim that the Premier had failed to comply with the order. On 22 April 2014, the appellant was informed that the legislative process was beyond the Premier's direct control as legislative authority lies within the provincial legislature under section 104(1) of the Constitution ([4]).

Despite various efforts to revive the initiative, including the re-publication of the Language Bill in November 2014 and submissions to the executive council and the portfolio committee, the appellant initiated contempt proceedings against the Premier in June 2015, claiming deliberate non-compliance with the court order ([5]-[7]). Ultimately, the High Court ruled against the contempt application, leading to the present appeal ([8]).

By the time the appeal was under consideration, the Free State Legislature had enacted the Use of Free State Official Languages Act, 2017 ([9]). The appellant conceded that the appeal had no practical effect due to this legislative development, which led to a focus on the issue of costs ([10]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue addressed in the appeal is whether the Premier had willfully disobeyed the initial court order regarding the regulation and monitoring of official languages in the province, and consequently, whether the appellant’s claim for contempt of court should succeed ([1]-[8]). This encompasses the necessity of establishing proof of non-compliance, wilfulness, and mala fides in relation to contempt proceedings, as well as the practical implications following the enactment of the Languages Act that may render the appellant’s application moot ([9]-[10]).

R – Rules

The legal principles governing contempt of court proceedings require the applicant to prove the following elements beyond a reasonable doubt:

  1. The existence of a court order.
  2. Proper service or notice of the order.
  3. Non-compliance with the order.
  4. The respondent's acts were willful.
  5. The acts were committed mala fide ([12]).

If these elements are established, the burden then shifts to the respondent to demonstrate that the non-compliance was not willful or mala fide ([13]). Notably, the judgments in Fakie NO v CCII Systems (Pty) Ltd and Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd are pivotal in delineating the evidential burdens concerning contempt of court claims ([12]-[13]).

Moreover, the principles outlined in Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources & others play a crucial role in determining cost implications in constitutional litigation, particularly the notion that generally, if the state is found liable, they bear the costs, unless the application is deemed vexatious or frivolous ([15]).

A – Application

In applying these legal rules to the facts, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) critically assessed whether the Premier complied with the court's order to regulate and monitor the official languages as mandated. The court noted that the Premier had indeed taken steps in good faith to comply with the order, including expediting the drafting of the language policy and Language Bill, which were subsequently published and introduced to the legislature ([13]).

The court firmly established that since the legislative authority rested with the Free State Legislature, the Premier was not in breach of the order simply because the Language Bill did not progress due to circumstances beyond his control, namely the dissolution of the legislature due to elections ([4]-[5]). Further, the court acknowledged that the appellant failed to provide clear evidence substantiating the claim of wilfulness and mala fides on the part of the Premier ([14]).

Regarding the issue of costs, the court determined that the appellant's contempt application was inappropriate under the circumstances, considering he was advised of the Premier's compliance efforts. The application was viewed as a fruitless pursuit, prompting the court to conclude that fairness mandated costs to be awarded against the appellant, akin to the principles of Biowatch ([15]-[16]).

C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the SCA upheld the dismissal of the contempt application, finding no evidence of willful non-compliance or mala fides on the part of the Premier. Furthermore, the enactment of the Use of Free State Official Languages Act rendered the appeal moot, thereby dismissing it with costs ([17]). This case illustrates the stringent requirements for establishing contempt of court, the critical role of clear jurisdictional boundaries in legislative processes, and the potential consequences for litigants who pursue claims perceived as unwarranted in constitutional litigation.

The judgment emphasizes the principle that public officials should not be unjustly penalized for acting within their constitutional mandates, and underscored the accountability mechanisms inherent in both the legislative and executive branches of government in South Africa.
