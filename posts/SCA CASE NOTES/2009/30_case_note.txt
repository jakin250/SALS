Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2009

**Case Note: South African Broadcasting Corporation Ltd v Coop & Others (178/08) [2009] ZASCA 30**

---

### FIRAC Method

**F – Facts:**  
This case concerns a dispute between the South African Broadcasting Corporation (SABC), the appellant, and former employees, referred to as the respondents, Fred Peter Coop & Others. The procedural history spans approximately eight years, beginning when the respondents, who had left the SABC after receiving full actuarial pension values between 1993 and 2000, continued as members of the SABC's medical scheme, benefiting from a 60% subsidy on their contributions. The SABC had earlier notified the respondents of its intention to withdraw these subsidies in 2001, asserting that since these employees were no longer entitled to benefits as they were not considered retirees under SABC policy, the payments were made without authorization.

After unsuccessful negotiations, the respondents instituted motion proceedings in the Johannesburg High Court seeking the reinstatement of their subsidies, resulting in a 2004 judgment by Blieden J that mandated the SABC to restore these benefits. On appeal, the SCA upheld Blieden J's order on different grounds, establishing that SABC was estopped from denying the authority of its officials' decisions regarding the subsidies. Subsequently, nearly a year later, SABC attempted to phase out these subsidies. This prompted the respondents to seek further legal intervention, asserting that such actions were in contempt of the previous court order. The High Court sided with the respondents, stating that the SABC misinterpreted the earlier judgment, leading SABC to appeal the decision ([paras 1–7]).

---

**I – Issues:**  
The core legal issue revolves around whether the SABC's decision to phase out the subsidies for the respondents contravened the previously issued court order by Blieden J. Two principal legal questions emerged:
1. Does the SABC have the authority to unilaterally withdraw or reduce the subsidies despite a prior order stipulating that the respondents were to continue receiving them?
2. Is the legal basis for the respondents' entitlement to these subsidies extinguished, thereby allowing SABC to assert its withdrawal of obligations? The appeal challenged the interpretation of Blieden J's order and the continuing obligation for SABC to uphold it ([paras 6–10]).

---

**R – Rules:**  
The applicable rules derive from contract law principles and the doctrine of estoppel within the context of employment benefits. The SCA relied on precedents regarding continuing obligations imposed by judicial orders, noting that a party cannot evade compliance by merely claiming a change in circumstances without lawful justification. The legal framework involved considerations of whether the respondents’ previous contracts created an enforceable right to the subsidies and whether those rights had been legally expunged ([paras 5–10]).

---

**A – Application:**  
The SCA assessed whether the SABC could legitimately claim to withdraw the subsidies based on their understanding that they were phasing out benefits related to bona fide pensioners in a manner congruent with Blieden J's order. Mlambo JA opined that the essential finding of Blieden J indicated that the respondents had a legal right to the subsidies, which must remain effective unless their underlying rights were lawfully terminated. The SABC's contention that it could escape the obligations stemming from the earlier order through unilateral decision-making was rejected. 

The court further detailed that the SABC's assertion of modifying its obligations required a more substantive basis. The interpretation of the previous judge's ruling, as held by Mogagabe AJ, was found valid, reinforcing that the SABC's obligations were not discharged by its intention to treat all former employees equally, as they failed to establish that the conditions fundamentally underpinning Blieden J's order had changed ([paras 8–10]).

---

**C – Conclusion:**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the SABC's appeal, affirming the decision of the High Court which ordered the SABC to comply with Blieden J's ruling. The ratio decidendi emphasized that an order remains in effect until the legal rights that formed its basis are dismantled through lawful means, which the SABC failed to demonstrate. This case underscores the significance of judicial authority and the binding nature of court orders on parties, reinforcing the principle that parties cannot unilaterally alter obligations established by the courts without appropriate legal justification. The implications of this ruling reaffirm the necessity for entities such as SABC to adhere to the terms of employment benefits and legal obligations despite shifting corporate governance policies ([paras 12–12]).
