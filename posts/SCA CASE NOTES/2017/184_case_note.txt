Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: The Chemical Industries National Provident Fund v Tristar Investments (Pty) Ltd [2017] ZASCA 184

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts

The appellant in this case, **Chemical Industries National Provident Fund (the Fund)**, is a pension fund catering to employees within the South African chemical industry, managed by a Board of Trustees ([1]), while the respondent, **Tristar Investments (Pty) Ltd** (Tristar), is an investment consulting firm. The escalation of the case began when the Fund entered into an investment consulting agreement with Tristar on **19 December 2007**. This agreement designated Tristar as the consultant to the Fund for a duration of three years, from **1 January 2008** ([3]).

After working for the Fund and receiving R2,722,207.44 for services rendered ([4]), on **17 April 2008**, the Fund rescinded their engagement with Tristar, arguing the invalidity of the agreement due to an alleged lack of authority from the Fund's signatories to finalize it and the agreement being ultra vires the Fund's governing rules ([4]). In response, Tristar accepted the Fund's repudiation of the agreement and instigated legal proceedings, asserting their entitlement to accrued income and damages stemming from the unlawful termination of the agreement ([5]).

The case was adjudicated in the **Gauteng Local Division of the High Court, Johannesburg**, where the trial court found the agreement valid, thereby dismissing the Fund’s claim while awarding Tristar R20,139,810.96 for accrued income ([6]). This judgment led to appeals and cross-appeals from both parties ([6]).

### I – Issues

The appeal presented three predominant legal questions:

1. **Authority of Signatories**: Whether the individuals who signed the investment consulting agreement on behalf of the Fund were authorized to do so.
2. **Ultra Vires Claims**: Whether the agreement was ultra vires the Fund's rules, particularly concerning the Fund's authority to enter binding commitments for fixed terms.
3. **Counter-Claim for Accrued Income**: The validity of Tristar’s counter-claim for accrued income and damages regarding the unfulfilled agreement ([14]).

### R – Rules

The determination of the authority of the Fund's representatives revolved around **rule 13.6.8** of the Fund's rules, stipulating that all trustee decisions necessitate a two-thirds majority of both employer and employee trustees for validity. Notably, no formal vote was conducted for Tristar's appointment, as historically, decisions had been made consensually among trustees ([15], [18]). 

Furthermore, the **rule 13.7.5** allows trustees to appoint consultants "on such terms as they may determine," which the Fund contended invalidated the agreement’s fixed-term nature ([22]).

The authority of judicial precedent was also invoked, especially referencing the case **Numsa v Cosatu**, which emphasized that decisions must be taken in adherence to stipulated procedural norms ([15]).

### A – Application

#### Authority of Signatories

The court found that although no formal vote was taken, the prevailing practice among trustees allowed for decision-making through consensus, which included recognition of a two-thirds majority without explicit voting. This consensus-based approach was accepted as valid when the Fund appointed Tristar, resonating with past practices where decisions had been ratified in similar manners ([18], [20]). The absence of formal voting was considered insufficient to undermine the authority of the signatories, since they had established the requisite majority through informal consensus, which was in line with customary decision-making at the Fund.

#### Ultra Vires Claims

On the claim of ultra vires, the court opined that the rule allowing for termination of contracts did not implicitly invalidate the contract's terms when agreed upon with a fixed duration. It held that such a provision empowered the Fund to legally terminate an agreement but did not equate to granting authority to void or invalidate contracts retroactively ([24]). In this interpretation, the court emphasized pragmatism in contract enforcement to avoid absurd business repercussions arising from strict adherence to restrictive interpretations ([24]).


#### Counter-Claim for Accrued Income

The discussion surrounding Tristar’s counter-claim for accrued income revealed the challenges posed by calculating speculative damages, exacerbated by the termination of the contract before the stipulated time frame could yield demonstrable savings. The court recognized inherent difficulties in quantifying potential income loss and maintained that the evidence, albeit speculative, supported the claim for accrued income based on past performances and ongoing negotiations that reflected Tristar's efficacy ([28], [31]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the trial court’s findings, dismissing the Fund’s appeal and validifying Tristar's contract as legal and effective. This culminated in an acknowledgement of the Board’s historical decision-making practices, thereby affirming Tristar’s entitlements to accrued income under their consulting agreement ([35]). The ruling reinforces the significance of customary practices in the governance of trusts, establishing a precedent that may serve as a reference point for future disputes regarding contractual authority and management in similar contexts. 

The implications of this ruling extend not only to the parties involved but also offer clarity on the interpretation of authority norms and contractual enforceability within the realm of trust management in South Africa, highlighting the necessity for judicious administration and adherence to established decision-making protocols among trustees.
