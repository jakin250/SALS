# Case Note: Philani-Ma-Afrika & Others v W M Mailula & Others

**Neutral citation**: Philani-Ma-Afrika & Others v W M Mailula & Others (674/08) [2009] ZASCA 115 (25 September 2009)

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The case concerns Philani-Ma-Afrika (hereinafter "Philani"), incorporated under the Companies Act 61 of 1973, which serves as a corporate vehicle for the Gauteng Provincial Housing Board to facilitate housing acquisition for low-income residents ([para 1]). Philani sought to set aside a sale of the property known as Angus Mansions, located at Erf no 4562, Johannesburg, which had purportedly been sold to W M Mailula ([para 1]).

The appellants included the residents of Angus Mansions, numbering from the second to sixty-eighth, who were facing eviction due to Mailula's application for eviction against unlawful occupiers ([para 2]). The case was complicated by claims of lack of proper authorisation for the sale, as the documentation presented for the sale was questionable ([paras 10–12]). The previous High Court decision by Judge Willis found against Philani, granting the eviction application to Mailula and allowing him to execute against certain respondents ([para 3]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issue was whether the sale of Angus Mansions was validly executed and whether the transfer of property was legally authorised ([para 17]). Additionally, the appeal raised questions about the constitutionality of the eviction order and the propriety of the High Court's execution order ([para 4]).

The applicability of the par delictum rule was also an essential legal question, specifically whether Philani could secure relief given that it had some complicity in the fraudulent actions leading to the contested sale ([para 18]).

### R – Rules
The court relied on various legal principles, including the importance of adherence to procedural validity in property transactions governed by the Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937. The court emphasized that a deed of sale must reflect effective authority for such a transaction to be valid ([paras 10–12]). The principles established in earlier cases, such as S v Western Areas 2005 (5) SA 214 (SCA), highlighted the necessity of a fair trial and the importance of justice in determining whether decisions are appealable ([para 20]).

Additionally, it was necessary to consider whether the absence of any valid and coherent resolution of the board of directors of Philani, and hence the invalid appointment of directors, rendered the sale void ab initio ([paras 10–12]).

### A – Application
The court found that the procedural requirements for the sale of Angus Mansions were not met. The documentation, including a purported power of attorney, lacked authenticity and failed to prove that the signatory had valid authority to sell the property on behalf of Philani ([paras 10–12]). The so-called "Madisha document," presented as a board resolution, was deemed invalid as it did not meet the requirements for a legally binding decision by valid directors ([para 11]).

Counsel for Mailula conceded the documentation's deficiencies and suggested that a referral back to the High Court for further evidence gathering may be warranted. However, the Supreme Court of Appeal deemed this request untimely and unnecessary, finding that Mailula had previously consented to the resolution of issues based on the existing evidence ([paras 17–18]).

As for the par delictum rule, the court noted that Philani was not complicit in the fraud and thus could not be barred from seeking relief on those grounds ([para 18]). The court found that it had sufficient grounds to overturn the High Court's decision and rule in favour of Philani, as the sale's invalidity resulted from fundamental procedural failures ([paras 19]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal allowed the appeal, overturning the High Court's orders and finding that the sale of Angus Mansions was invalid ([para 21]). The court ordered the cancellation of the relevant deed of transfer and declared Philani as the legitimate owner of the property. The appeal's success implied significant implications for housing policy administration, particularly concerning the rights of vulnerable communities and the need for transparent management in corporate entities serving such clientele ([para 21]). 

By restoring ownership to Philani, the court underscored the importance of protecting communal interests against fraudulent transactions that exploit vulnerable populations, while also reaffirming judicial oversight in property disputes. The ruling offered a robust reflection of the courts' role in safeguarding the rights and interests of marginalized individuals in South Africa.
