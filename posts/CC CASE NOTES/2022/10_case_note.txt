Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note for Baloyi N.O. and Others v Pawn Stars CC and Another [2022] ZACC 10

### F – Facts

This case arises from the proceedings initiated by Andrew Tuee Baloyi N.O. and Eunice Mhaka Baloyi N.O. (the applicants) against Pawn Stars CC (the first respondent) and the Sheriff of the High Court (the second respondent). The genesis of the dispute can be traced back to a loan agreement entered into in February 2016, under which Pawn Stars advanced R870,000 to the Navuyeriwa Business Trust (the Trust), represented by the applicants, with an obligation to repay within three months. The third and fourth applicants stood as sureties for the loan, and a mortgage bond secured the loan against a property owned by the Trust ([paras 1-2]).

After the Trust defaulted on the repayment, Pawn Stars applied to the High Court for a judgment to declare the property specially executable. A default judgment was granted on 18 April 2017, which the applicants thereafter sought to rescind, claiming they were unaware of the application and contending that the penalty clauses were contrary to public policy ([paras 3-4]). Following unsuccessful settlement discussions, a consent order was established on 17 April 2018, which detailed the repayment conditions and stipulated that failure to meet these conditions would result in the property becoming specially executable [[para 5]].

Contrary to the terms of the consent order, the applicants defaulted, leading Pawn Stars to issue a warrant of execution on 25 July 2018. Subsequently, the applicants sought urgent relief from the High Court to interdict the property sale, arguing that the consent order was incorrectly issued without applying Rule 46A of the Uniform Rules of Court, which mandates judicial oversight in cases involving the execution of residential property of judgment debtors. The High Court, however, rejected the applicants' requests, leading them to appeal to the Constitutional Court ([paras 6-12]).

### I – Issues

The Court was tasked with addressing the principal issue: whether Rule 46A applies to properties owned by juristic persons or trusts when determining the validity of the consent order issued in this case ([paras 13-14]). The applicants argued that the High Court’s failure to apply the rule violated their constitutional rights, effectively raising a constitutional issue as defined by Section 167(3)(b) of the Constitution, which allows intervention from the Constitutional Court in certain definitive constitutional matters ([para 15]).

### R – Rules

The relevant legal framework for this case stems from Rule 46A of the Uniform Rules of Court, which governs the execution against the residential properties of judgment debtors and mandates judicial oversight prior to such executions being allowed to occur. Specifically, it introduces a requirement for the court to consider various factors such as the interests of the judgment debtor and whether the property serves as their primary residence ([para 16]) and clarifies the conditions under which such a sale can be executed ([paras 17-19]).

Judicial precedents, especially the decision in *First Rand Bank Ltd v Folscher*, are also pertinent to this case. This earlier ruling concluded that the provisions of former Rule 46(1)(a)(ii) did not extend to properties owned by juristic persons, a principle that the applicants sought to challenge ([paras 20-21]).

### A – Application

The Court scrutinized the arguments surrounding whether the applicability of Rule 46A to trust-owned properties warranted its engagement. Notably, the Constitutional Court clarified that the matter did not reach the Rule 46A issue for several reasons:

1. **Judgment Independence**: The High Court had found that the applicability of Rule 46A was not significant in its decision as it dismissed the application on grounds unrelated to Rule 46A ([para 22]).

2. **Consent Order and Judicial Oversight**: The fact that the consent order was agreed upon by both parties, represented by legal counsel, indicated that there had been judicial oversight, despite the applicants’ contentions to the contrary. Consequently, they failed to demonstrate that any potential application of Rule 46A would have altered the outcome of the consent order ([para 23]).

3. **Lack of Jurisdictional Basis**: The Court also asserted that the mere failure of the High Court to strictly apply the rule does not grant grounds for variation or rescission without meeting requisite legal thresholds. The applicants did not articulate why the consent order ought to be overturned, thereby weakly substantiating their claim ([para 24]).

4. **Failure to Indicate Potential Harm**: The court highlighted the applicants’ inability to express how the enforcement of the consent order would leave them vulnerable to homelessness or how a different application of the rule might have produced a different result ([para 25]).

Ultimately, the Court ruled that since the jurisdiction was inappropriately pegged solely to the applicability of Rule 46A—one that had not properly engaged in the lower courts—the application for leave to appeal was dismissed due to lack of jurisdiction ([para 26]).

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court dismissed the application for leave to appeal based on the conclusion that the applicants failed to establish a constitutional issue necessary for its jurisdiction. They were deemed liable for the costs of the first respondent. The case reinforced the importance of due diligence and legal representation during settlements, indicating that consent orders backed by legal oversight are generally upheld unless substantiated errors or issues are adequately demonstrated. This decision further emphasizes the often complicated interplay between contractual obligations, statutory regulations, and the responsibilities of juristic entities in South African law ([paras 27-28]). 

This case embodies a reaffirmation of existing legal principles as applicable to trusts and juristic persons, while illustrating the necessity for precise legal arguments when invoking constitutional issues in appellate contexts.
