Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: Wilkinson v The Law Society of the Northern Provinces (783/2016)

## FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts ([paras 2–3])

The appellant, Joseph Joshua Wilkinson, is an admitted attorney and conveyancer who has been practicing independently since 2011. On June 20, 2014, the Law Society of the Northern Provinces instituted motion proceedings against him in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, seeking to have his name struck from the roll of attorneys and conveyancers. Two primary complaints prompted this action: misappropriation of trust funds and failure to account to clients as required by Rule 68 of the Law Society Rules. This action was brought in accordance with section 22(1)(d) of the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979, which allows for such sanctions when an attorney is deemed unfit to practice.

The high court's ruling supported the respondent's claims, concluding that Wilkinson was unfit to continue practicing due to provable violations of professional conduct. The high court not only ordered that his name be struck from the roll of attorneys but also appointed a curator bonis to manage his accounting records and trust accounts ([paras 2–4]).

### I – Issues ([paras 1, 4, 15, 18])

The central issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the high court had exercised its discretion judicially when ordering Wilkinson's removal from the roll of attorneys. Additional legal questions revolved around the nature of the alleged wrongdoing and whether Wilkinson's conduct warranted such a severe penalty as striking off, rather than a more lenient suspension. Specifically, the court needed to evaluate the process undertaken by the high court and the factual basis underlying its decision, including the allegations of dishonesty associated with Wilkinson's handling of the trust money ([paras 1, 4, 15]).

### R – Rules ([paras 4, 15, 18, 22])

In adjudicating such cases, the court follows a three-stage inquiry:

1. Establish whether the alleged unethical or improper conduct occurred on a balance of probabilities.
2. Determine if the attorney is a 'fit and proper person' to practice, which requires a value judgment regarding the seriousness of the misconduct.
3. Decide the appropriate sanction, whether striking off or suspension, taking into account public interest, the nature of the transgressions, and the character of the attorney ([paras 4, 15, 18]).

The principles governing these inquiries are established in cases such as *Malan & Another v Law Society Northern Provinces* (2009) and *Summerley v Law Society, Northern Provinces* (2006). The standard applied allows for a limited appeal against the high court’s discretion unless such discretion is exercised capriciously or without due consideration of the facts ([paras 4, 22]).

### A – Application ([paras 10–21])

The court meticulously reviewed the evidence surrounding Wilkinson's conduct, which included a failure to account for a considerable amount of money—R28.8 million—which had been entrusted to him for investment. The findings reflected ongoing issues with his trust account, including a reported deficit of over R10 million. Despite claims from Wilkinson that his accounting records were compromised due to negligence and external factors, the court found these assertions to lack credibility.

In evaluating whether Wilkinson was a fit and proper person, the court noted his repeated failures to justify his actions and account for the funds accurately. Notably, Wilkinson's explanations for the deficit were deemed implausible, undermined by clear evidence of unauthorized withdrawals for personal gain, which amounted to acts of dishonesty. The court emphasized that while the high court did not explicitly label Wilkinson's conduct as dishonest, the actions he took could intrinsically be characterized as theft of trust money. Thus, the serious nature of his misconduct warranted the extreme sanction of striking off ([paras 10–21]).

### C – Conclusion ([paras 23])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the decision of the high court, concluding that it had not misdirected itself in its exercise of discretion when ordering Wilkinson’s name to be struck from the roll of attorneys. The findings of serious misconduct confirmed that Wilkinson was no longer a fit and proper person to practice law. The appeal was dismissed with costs, taxed on the scale of attorney and client, reinforcing the principle that attorneys owe a paramount duty to maintain integrity and trust in their professional conduct ([para 23]).

---

This case serves as a significant precedent on the standards of conduct required of legal practitioners and illustrates the judiciary’s commitment to upholding the integrity of the profession in South Africa.
