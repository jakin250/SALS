Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: **National Director of Public Prosecutions v Freedom Under Law** ([2014] ZASCA 58)

### **F – Facts** ([paras 1–17])
This case arose from the actions and decisions of several officials within the South African law enforcement and prosecutorial agencies regarding criminal charges against Lieutenant General Richard Mdluli, Head of Crime Intelligence. Mdluli faced numerous charges including murder and fraud stemming from incidents related to a personal dispute and alleged misconduct involving police resources.

On 31 March 2011, Mdluli was arrested and charged with 18 criminal offenses, including serious charges of murder, intimidation, and corruption ([para 5-10]). Following his arrest, several actions were taken against him, including suspension and initiation of disciplinary proceedings by the Commissioner of Police, General Bheki Cele ([para 7-8]). Over time, conflicting decisions emerged from various prosecutorial figures, primarily Advocate Lawrence Mrwebi (the Head of the Specialised Commercial Crime Unit) and Advocate K M A Chauke (the DPP South Gauteng), leading to the withdrawal of both criminal charges and disciplinary proceedings against Mdluli ([para 12–15]).

The respondent, a public interest organisation named Freedom Under Law (FUL), sought to challenge these withdrawals, believing they represented a denial of justice and a failure to uphold the rule of law. FUL initiated a review application, which the North Gauteng High Court granted, leading to the appeal by the appellants ([paras 16-17]).

### **I – Issues** ([paras 18–20])
The main issues in the case revolve around the legality and reviewability of prosecutorial decisions. Specifically:

1. Whether decisions to withdraw criminal charges should be reviewed under the principle of legality rather than the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA).
2. The extent to which the high court could intervene to compel the reinstatement of disciplinary proceedings and prosecutions.
3. Consideration of the manner in which FUL could challenge the decisions regarding Mdluli’s charges, including standing and the adequacy of internal remedies before seeking judicial intervention.

### **R – Rules** ([paras 19-36])
The judgment hinges on constitutional and statutory principles, notably:
- **Principle of Legality**: The exercise of public power must be lawful, rational, and consistent with the Constitution ([para 28]).
- **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act**: PAJA defines and regulates what constitutes administrative action and the processes involved in its review ([para 20]).
- **Constitution of South Africa, Section 179**: Provides the structure for the National Prosecuting Authority, including the powers related to prosecution and disciplinary actions ([paras 30-33]).

The court also looked into the necessity of exhausting internal remedies when applicable, particularly under PAJA’s provisions ([para 36]).

### **A – Application** ([paras 38–49])
The court critically evaluated the trending decisions of Mrwebi and Chauke to withdraw charges, focusing on procedural integrity and adherence to statutory requirements. 

- **Decision to Withdraw Charges**: The court found that Mrwebi’s failure to consult appropriately with the DPP led to an improper withdrawal of charges. His claims of consultation were contradicted by evidence and thus rendered his decision unsustainable ([para 41]).
- **Chauke’s Decision**: Although Chauke argued that his withdrawal of the murder charges was pending an inquest, this was deemed rational in the context he provided. Nevertheless, the failure to take action post-inquest raised concerns, but the court concluded that this was not subject to the immediate review ([para 44]).
  
Furthermore, the court addressed jurisdictional issues regarding the Commissioner of Police's decisions. It ruled that the high court had the jurisdiction to review disciplinary actions taken by the Commissioner, emphasizing that such powers were pervaded by public law principles and not purely internal labor disputes ([para 46-49]).

### **C – Conclusion** ([paras 51–54])
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded by upholding part of the High Court's order while overturning others. It set aside the decisions made by Mrwebi to withdraw fraud and corruption charges and the Commissioner’s termination of disciplinary proceedings, affirming the legality principle’s application. However, the court refrained from enforcing mandatory interdicts compelling prosecution, citing separation of powers within the Constitution. Notably, the NDPP committed to reviewing the charges and reporting back within a specified timeframe.

Ultimately, the appeal was partially upheld to the extent that the court recognized the need for the prosecution and disciplinary components to be revisited while preserving judicial restraint and respect for executive authority. The ruling indicates a commitment to maintaining the rule of law while acknowledging the complexities of prosecutorial discretion. The court also determined that no costs would be assigned regarding the appeal, acknowledging the broader public interest involved and the nature of constitutional litigation ([paras 53-54]).
