Lead HV (Pty) Ltd and Another v HV Test (Pty) Ltd [2024] ZASCA 36

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 36

FIRAC Structure

F – Facts

This appeal involved Lead HV (Pty) Ltd (the first appellant) and Barry Lombaard (the second appellant), both suppliers in the competitive electrical engineering market, against HV Test (Pty) Ltd (the respondent) [1]. The dispute arose after two former employees of the respondent, Ms. Sheik and Mr. Ledwaba, who were under a restraint of trade agreement, obtained employment with the first appellant in 2020. Subsequently, an Anton Piller order was granted against the former employees and the appellants on 10 September 2020, wherein confidential information belonging to the respondent was found in their possession.

The respondent alleged misuse of this information to the detriment of its business, leading to an urgent application for an interdict to prevent further dealings with the confidential information and enforcement of the restraint of trade agreement against the former employees [2]. While a default order was made against Sheik and Ledwaba on 13 November 2020, the application against the appellants was struck off the urgent roll due to a lack of urgency [3]. The matter progressed to a hearing on 14 April 2021, resulting in the granting of a final interdict on 12 August 2021, restraining the appellants from using any of the respondent's confidential information [4].

I – Issues

The primary legal questions arising from this case were:

  1. Did the High Court misdirect itself in its factual and legal analysis leading to the granting of the interdict against the appellants?
  2. Did a live dispute remain between the parties at the time of the appeal, or had the passage of time rendered the matter moot? [5][6].

R – Rules

The court relied on the principles governing interdicts, particularly the urgent application procedures and the enforcement of restraint of trade agreements. Key legal principles addressed included the need for a live dispute in order for a court to entertain an appeal, as established in Police and Prisons Civil Rights Union v South African Correctional Services Workers' Union and Others ([2018] ZACC 24), which articulates that courts do not adjudicate matters that no longer present an existing controversy [6].

A – Application

In addressing the first issue, the court found no factual or legal misdirection by the High Court. The court underscored that the possession of confidential information by the former employees, who were in breach of their restraint of trade agreements, created a solid basis for the interdict against the appellants. The concession by the appellants' counsel that no grounds for interference existed substantively reinforced this finding [5].

On the second issue, the court assessed the elapsed time since the relevant incidents—involving events from 2020 and a final order in August 2021. It concluded that the issues raised had become academic, indicating that the harm to the respondent and any rights the appellants sought to protect were no longer relevant. The spatio-temporal lapse rendered the dispute moot, as the dynamics of the competitive landscape changed post-2021, thus constituting an insurmountable obstacle for the appeal [6].

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal. The dismissal was grounded in the conclusion that the High Court had not erred in its decision, and additionally, that the matter had lost any urgency or relevant dispute due to the effluxion of time [7]. This case reinforces the complications of disputes revolving around confidentiality and restraint of trade agreements, while also reaffirming the necessity of a live dispute for the pursuance of legal appeals, underlining that courts are barred from adjudicating moot issues.
