Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

## Case Note: FIRAC Method

### Case Title
**South African Legal Practice Council v Mokhele (1138/2022)** [2023] ZASCA 177 (14 December 2023)

### F – Facts
This case involves an appeal by the South African Legal Practice Council (the LPC), a regulatory body overseeing legal practitioners in South Africa, against Lebohang Michael Mokhele (Mr Mokhele), concerning his suspension from the roll of legal practitioners ([1]). The LPC initiated legal proceedings in the Free State Division of the High Court, Bloemfontein, requesting Mr Mokhele's suspension pending a disciplinary inquiry, based on prima facie evidence of trust shortages following complaints from the public ([1]). 

The high court, consisting of Mthimunye AJ and Mathebula J, dismissed the LPC's urgent application, mandating that the LPC complete its disciplinary hearing before pursuing suspension ([2]). The LPC appealed this decision after leave to appeal was granted, asserting its need to clarify the criteria for suspensions that are not punitive but precautionary in nature ([2]–[3]). 

It is relevant to note that while the investigations indicated serious misconduct, including trust shortages in at least two matters, the high court concluded that the LPC must wait until the disciplinary process was finalized before seeking suspension ([4]–[5]). As proceedings progressed, the high court eventually provided an interim suspension for Mr Mokhele but the appeal before the Supreme Court of Appeal was conducted under the premise that the central issue may have become moot, as the circumstances shifted post the high court's ruling ([6]).

### I – Issues
The principal legal issues for determination were ([7]):
1. Whether the SCA should hear an appeal that has ostensibly become moot following the high court’s decisions and interim orders.
2. The applicability and interpretation of section 43 of the Legal Practice Act 28 of 2014 regarding the suspension of legal practitioners pending disciplinary proceedings.
3. The importance of clarity for matters affecting third parties not before the Court and the institutional interpretation of law.

### R – Rules
The interpretation of section 43 of the Legal Practice Act is pivotal, which mandates that if a legal practitioner is found to have misappropriated trust funds or committed serious misconduct, the LPC must take urgent legal action for suspension pending inquiry ([3]). 

Mootness, while commonly viewed as a barrier to hearing a dispute, may still allow for judicial review if the interests of justice so dictate. Relevant precedents outlined factors for consideration, such as the potential practical effects of any court order, the importance and complexity of the issue, and the potential conflicts in judicial interpretation ([8]). 

The Constitutional Court has addressed the concept of mootness in various rulings, emphasizing that a matter may be justiciable despite the absence of a live dispute if it implicates broader interests ([8]).

### A – Application
The SCA, led by Nicholls JA and supported by concurring justices, acknowledged the complexities of interpreting section 43 and the pressing need for uniform application across jurisdictions ([7]). It was noted that while the appeal appeared moot due to the high court's interim orders, the LPC advanced its request to clarify the interpretative ambiguities that could affect future disciplinary processes and potentially impact the broader legal community ([7]).

However, the court highlighted the procedural shortcomings in the LPC's approach, specifically noting that the notice of motion did not seek a declaratory order concerning section 43. Judicial prudence dictated that any determinations made in this context by the SCA would have to involve all interested parties, including members of the legal profession and potentially the Minister of Justice ([9]).

The conduct of Mr Mokhele and his legal representative raised concerns regarding procedural integrity. Mr Mokhele’s late appearance and lack of proper arguments undermined his position, reflecting poorly on his responsibilities as a legal practitioner ([10]). This context framed the appeal in a manner that underscored the ethical obligations of legal practitioners to maintain decorum in court proceedings.

### C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the SCA ruled to strike the matter from its roll, with no order as to costs, reflecting the court’s reluctance to hear a matter lacking the engagement of all pertinent stakeholders ([11]). The decision underscores the necessity for procedural and substantive clarity in matters of legal practice, particularly concerning statutory interpretations involving disciplinary action against lawyers. The judgment implicitly calls for the LPC to seek further legal remedies in the appropriate forum, ensuring that broader implications, particularly for the legal profession at large, are adequately considered ([11]).

In sum, this case reiterates the significant legal principles surrounding the interpretation of statutory provisions related to the professional conduct of legal practitioners and the inherent need for procedural compliance in legal adjudication. Future matters may establish critical precedents concerning the treatment of legal practitioners under scrutiny pending disciplinary review, affecting not only the LPC’s regulatory authority but also the foundational standards of professional integrity within South Africa's legal framework.
