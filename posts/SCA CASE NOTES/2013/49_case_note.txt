Case Note: SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: Mafoho v The State (149/12) [2012] ZASCA 49 

**F – Facts**  
The appellant, Michael Mafoho, appealed against a judgment from the North Gauteng High Court, which upheld a sentence of 275 years' imprisonment imposed by the trial court ([1]). Mafoho was convicted on 60 counts, including robbery with aggravating circumstances, attempted murder, kidnapping, rape, attempted rape, and pointing a firearm, after pleading guilty ([2]). The offence series occurred between May 1998 and May 2000, where the appellant led a gang targeting heavily laden vehicles, primarily belonging to foreigners, presenting themselves as law enforcement officers and committing serious violent crimes ([5]).

The trial court found no substantial compelling circumstances to impose a lesser sentence, resulting in the cumulative effect of 275 years, with some sentences running concurrently ([6]). Mafoho contended that he was incarcerated under provisions of the old Correctional Services Act 8 of 1959, claiming eligibility for parole after serving half of his sentence, and argued that the long period of incarceration constituted cruel and inhuman punishment ([4]). He sought to replace his sentence with life imprisonment, which would enable him, according to new regulations, to apply for parole after 20 years of imprisonment.

**I – Issues**  
The appeal primarily raised the issue of the appropriateness of the sentence and the applicability of parole eligibility provisions under the old and new Correctional Services Acts. Specifically, the determination of when Mafoho would be eligible for parole if his sentence were not altered emerged as a crucial question ([3], [10]). Additionally, the court had to consider the legitimacy of his request to submit new evidence related to his character and rehabilitation while incarcerated ([22]).

**R – Rules**  
The relevant legal framework includes:
- **Section 65(4)** of the old Correctional Services Act 8 of 1959, which stipulates that a prisoner serving a determinate sentence may only be considered for parole after serving half of their sentence ([9]).
- **Section 136** of the new Correctional Services Act 111 of 1998, which clarifies that offenders whose sentences commenced prior to the new Act's enactment are governed by the old Act's terms but can earn credits impacting parole eligibility ([12]).
- **Section 73(6)(a)** of the new Act establishes a 25-year review period for parole eligibility for those serving determinate sentences, effective from 1 October 2004 ([11]). 
- The Constitutional Court’s decision in **Van Vuren v Minister of Correctional Services** (2012), which addressed the implications of parole policy changes on prisoners sentenced before the new Act's implementation, was also instrumental in resolving retrospective rights ([18], [20]).

**A – Application**  
The court analyzed how Mafoho's prior conviction and sentencing fitted within the framework laid out by the relevant sections of both the old and new Acts. It emphasized that while Mafoho was sentenced under the older Act, the concurrent application of the new Act's provisions, particularly with respect to his parole eligibility following amendments, had substantial implications for his appeal ([9], [14]). Upon reviewing legislative history, the court determined that the amendments established by the Parole and Correctional Supervision Amendment Act 87 of 1997 allowed prisoners, including Mafoho, to be considered for parole after serving 25 years, thereby significantly altering his situation despite his substantial sentence ([17], [21]). 

The court concluded that while it would not interfere with the original sentence due to the strict legality surrounding the handling of sentences, Mafoho would now be eligible for reconsideration for parole after serving a lesser period than originally imposed due to these amendments ([21]) and that the nature of his crimes, while serious, was better suited for reform rather than indefinite incarceration.

**C – Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Mafoho's appeal against the sentence ([24]). The findings reinforced that while Mafoho had received a lengthy sentence of 275 years, the legislative framework in light of amendments meant he could be considered for parole after serving 25 years, placing a clear limit on his incarceration despite the heavy nature of his crimes. This ruling highlights a critical movement toward aligning legal principles concerning human rights with the developed standards of punishments, thus underscoring a evolving consensus on the treatment and rehabilitation of offenders in South Africa. While the appeal's outcome and its thorough examination of the legal principles reaffirm the existing sentences, the court's recognition of the amendments considerably ameliorates the impact of such extensive sentencing ([21]).
