Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: The Advertising Standards Authority v Herbex (Pty) Ltd (902/16) [2017] ZASCA 132

**FIRAC Method**

### F – Facts 
The case revolves around a dispute between the Advertising Standards Authority of South Africa (the "ASA") and Herbex (Pty) Ltd ("Herbex"). Herbex, a company engaged in marketing complementary medicines, is not a member of the ASA. The central issue emerged when Herbex filed a motion in the Gauteng Local Division of the High Court, challenging the ASA's jurisdiction over non-members. The court granted a declaratory order affirming that the ASA lacks jurisdiction over non-members, rendering its rulings against Herbex void ([1]–[9]).

The ASA, as a voluntary association established under the Companies Act 61 of 1973, serves as a self-regulatory body for the advertising industry. It aims to uphold advertising standards through the Advertising Code and adjudicates complaints from members. The ASA's rulings, published online, do not hold legal authority over non-members, which became a point of contention when Herbex contested the legitimacy of certain actions taken against it by the ASA ([3]–[6]).

Notably, the ASA had previously issued rulings against Herbex, which included extracting appeal fees and publishing adverse rulings on its website. Herbex argued that the ASA misled it into participating in proceedings by means of non-disclosure and misleading representation about its jurisdiction and authority ([7]–[9]).

The High Court's ruling, against which the ASA appealed, laid out conditions about the ASA's jurisdiction, effectiveness of rulings on non-members, and the necessity of an explicit agreement for appeal fees ([9]–[10]).

### I – Issues 
The key legal issue in this appeal was whether the ASA possesses the jurisdiction to govern entities or persons who are not its members and who have not explicitly consented to its jurisdiction ([1]–[2], [11]). The implications of this jurisdiction question raised constitutional considerations regarding the rights of non-members under the framework of self-regulation in the advertising industry ([7]).

Additional issues arose regarding the cost implications of the appeal, especially in light of the substantial success Herbex had in the court a quo and subsequent settlement discussions between the parties concerning costs ([2], [15]–[16]).

### R – Rules 
The governing rules and principles in this case emanate primarily from the legal definitions of jurisdiction with respect to voluntary associations and the specific statutory framework under which the ASA operates. The ASA is fundamentally a self-regulatory body whose powers are conditional upon participation and membership ([4]–[5]). 

Moreover, the Electronic Communications Act 36 of 2005 (ECA) intersects with this case, as it mandates broadcasting service licensees to adhere to the Advertising Code, while simultaneously requiring a framework wherein the ASA adjudicates relevant complaints ([11], [17]). Legal principles surrounding costs, particularly those concerning judicial discretion, were also significant, with case law dictating that costs follow the event unless factors peculiar to the case justify a different approach ([16]).

### A – Application 
The court examined the ASA's claims of jurisdiction over non-members in detail. The ASA's position was that it should be able to regulate the industry effectively, including non-members, particularly concerning advertisements published in media owned by members ([10], [11]). However, the court upheld the earlier ruling that the ASA’s authority does not extend to non-members without their submission to its jurisdiction. It recognized that a non-member's participation is not compulsory and that the ASA must provide clear communication to non-members about their non-obligatory status regarding the adjudication processes ([8]–[9]).

The application of statutory frameworks, including the ECA, was re-evaluated in light of the court's previous rulings. The Court conceded the importance of the ASA’s function but emphasized the need to clarify its limits to ensure fairness and due process in segmenting its authority effectively ([18]).

Ultimately, the court directed that the ASA amend its standard letters of complaint to reflect the reality of non-member rights appropriately and allowed the ASA to rule on advertisements submitted by non-members only for the benefit of its members ([17], [18]).

### C – Conclusion 
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal regarding certain aspects of the High Court’s ruling, particularly the jurisdiction of the ASA over non-members. It reiterated that the ASA does not possess jurisdiction over individuals or entities not consenting to its governance. The court's final order consequently clarified the domain of the ASA’s authority while ensuring that non-members could not be compelled to engage with its processes unless they voluntarily chose to do so ([18]).

The ruling underscores the balance of power between self-regulatory frameworks and the rights of non-members in the context of advertising standards and consumer protection, setting a significant precedent for future disputes involving voluntary associations. Importantly, the court's decision on costs manifested a judicious approach to fairness, acknowledging the substantial success of both parties in their respective claims ([17]–[18]). 

This case stands as a reference point for issues surrounding the jurisdictional authority of self-regulatory bodies, the clarity required in communications directed at non-members, and the essential principles of fairness in adjudicative processes within private law frameworks.
