Source Folder: 2002

# Case Note: Road Accident Fund v Patrick Norman Arendse NO [2002] ZASCA 30; Case No. 030/2002

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The case concerns an appeal by the Road Accident Fund (RAF) against a declaratory order made by Msimang AJ in the court a quo regarding the extent of the RAF's liability under an undertaking provided to compensate for the costs of future medical care for a minor, Cindy Patricia Arendse, who was injured in a road accident in 1991 ([paras 1-4]). The respondent, Patrick Norman Arendse, acted on behalf of his daughter and initially claimed damages from the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund (MMF), which was later transitioned to the RAF under the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 ([paras 1-6]).

In an agreement, the MMF's appointed agent, South African Eagle Insurance Company Limited (SA Eagle), provided an undertaking to compensate for future accommodation in a hospital or nursing home, and other medical treatments ([paras 5-6]). The respondent later decided against the appointment of a curator bonis to administer the damages, opting instead to form a trust ([paras 4-6]). The costs associated with the administration of this trust were then sought to be claimed from the RAF, but were not included in the original particulars of claim ([paras 10-12]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal question before the court was whether the costs incurred from the administration of the undertaking provided by SA Eagle could be claimed from the RAF, despite not being included in the respondent's original damages claim ([para 8]). Another ancillary issue was whether such costs could justifiably be classified as "loss or damage" claimable under the RAF’s obligations ([para 11]).

### R – Rules
The key legal framework revolves around the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 and its procedural predecessor, the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund Act 93 of 1989. Notably, Article 40 of the MMF Act mandated the MMF to compensate for loss or damage due to bodily injury resulting from motor vehicle accidents ([paras 2-3]). 

Article 43(a) of the MMF Act allowed for compensation of future costs of accommodation, medical treatment, and other services, which were to be claimed after they had been incurred ([paras 3-4]). This provision was mirrored in section 17 of the RAFA. The case also referenced the ‘once and for all principle’ in claims for damages and the implications of the introduction of section 21(1C) of the Compulsory Motor Vehicle Insurance Act 56 of 1972, which aimed to relieve the uncertainty surrounding future medical expenses by permitting compensation as costs were incurred rather than as a lump sum award ([paras 8-9]).

### A – Application
The court meticulously examined the nature of the undertaking provided by SA Eagle and determined that it only pertained to costs explicitly specified in Article 43(a) of the MMF Act ([paras 10-11]). The court highlighted that the costs of attorney services in administering the trust had never formed part of the original claim. Therefore, the RAF's liability remained strictly limited to the terms of the undertaking ([paras 10-12]).

The court also pointed out the incongruity in the respondent's position: had he followed through with appointing a curator bonis, all relevant costs, including those for administration, would have been automatically accounted for. Instead, by not doing so, and thereby setting the trust, he could not retroactively claim costs that were not included in his original agreement ([paras 12-13]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the costs of administering the undertaking did not fall under the RAF’s liability as defined in Article 43(a). The appeal by the RAF was upheld, and the previous order of the court a quo was set aside, effectively dismissing the claim for additional costs incurred in administering the undertaking ([paras 13-14]). This decision clarifies the boundaries of the RAF's obligations in terms of compensation for road accident damages and emphasizes the importance of specificity in claims related to legal and administrative costs.

### Implications
This ruling signifies a critical standpoint in the interpretation of the RAF's responsibilities under the applicable legislation, emphasizing that claims for reimbursement of administrative costs must be explicitly included within the confines of the original undertaking. Moreover, it underscores the necessity for claimants to carefully articulate and justify all anticipated costs at the outset of the claims process to avoid limitations in recoverability, thus guiding future practices.
