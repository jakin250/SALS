Member of the Executive Council for Development Planning and Local Government in the Provincial Government of Gauteng v Democratic Party [1998] ZACC 17

This case arises from a budget dispute within the Eastern Metropolitan Substructure of the Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council (the “Council”).

Constitutional Court 1998 [1998] ZACC 17

FIRAC Method Analysis

F – Facts

This case arises from a budget dispute within the Eastern Metropolitan Substructure of the Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council (the “Council”). On 31 May 1997, a disagreement arose concerning the voting majority required for budget approval—the appellant contended that a simple majority sufficed, while other members insisted on a two-thirds majority ([2]).

The Council, represented by the appellant, launched an urgent application in the Witwatersrand High Court, seeking an order to declare that a simple majority was adequate for budget approval. Central to this dispute were conflicting interpretations of the 1996 Constitution and the Local Government Transition Act (the “LGTA”) ([3]). The High Court ruled against the appellant, indicating that certain provisions in the LGTA would not be subject to constitutional scrutiny until 30 April 1999 ([3]), leading to the dismissal of the application with costs ([4]).

The appellant sought leave to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court, circumventing the Supreme Court of Appeal (the “SCA”). However, the existing legislative framework and rules of court were unclear regarding the procedure for appeals of this nature ([4]-[5]). Subsequently, it became apparent that the procedures framed under sections 166 and 167 of the new 1996 Constitution provided an avenue for such appeals, specifically accommodating direct appeals from High Court decisions on constitutional matters ([9]).

The parties involved were as follows: the appellant was the Member of the Executive Council for Development Planning and Local Government in Gauteng, the first respondent was the Democratic Party, and the second and third respondents were local politicians, Ivor Blumenthal and Elizabeth Clogg ([1]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue before the court was the constitutionality of section 16(5) of the LGTA, which required a two-thirds majority for budget approvals, conflicting with sections 160(2)(b) and 160(3)(b) of the 1996 Constitution that stipulated a simple majority for municipal council decisions ([34]).

Further, the procedural legitimacy of the application for leave to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court raised additional questions, particularly whether the framework under which the appeal was sought adhered to the newly established constitutional norms ([19]-[22]).

R – Rules

The constitutional provisions relevant to this case include:

  1. Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996: Sections 160(2)(b) and 160(3)(b) govern municipal council voting procedures.
  2. Local Government Transition Act, 1993 (Act 209 of 1993): Specifically, section 16(5), which mandates a two-thirds majority for budget approvals ([34]).
  3. Constitutional Court Rules: Rule 18 outlines the procedure for appealing from judgments of the High Court on constitutional matters ([11]).

The court also referenced its previous judgment in S v Pennington, which established that interim procedures would apply until definitive rules were created under the new Constitution ([15]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court had to navigate the transitional provisions laid out in item 26 of Schedule 6 of the 1996 Constitution. The appellant argued that despite being operational until 30 April 1999, section 16(5) should not be absolved from constitutional scrutiny and was inconsistent with the more democratic provisions of the 1996 Constitution regarding municipal governance ([40]-[44]).

The court analyzed the interplay between section 16(5) and sections 160(2) and 160(3), ultimately determining that while section 16(5) indeed conflicted with the constitutional framework, it was nonetheless valid and could not be repealed before the stipulated transitional date ([54]-[58]). The necessity of such a provision was recognized as a mechanism to prevent potential governance deadlocks within municipal frameworks, emphasizing that allowing a two-thirds majority was not antithetical to democratic principles ([58]).

The procedural issues were addressed by clarifying that the appeal from the High Court was valid under the rules of the Constitutional Court. The court determined that the appeal, despite the previous negative certificate from the High Court, was legitimate because it addressed substantial constitutional matters that required urgent resolution ([20]-[23]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court held that section 16(5) of the LGTA, while conflicting with sections 160(2)(b) and 160(3)(b) of the 1996 Constitution, remained valid until 30 April 1999 and was not shielded from constitutional review. The appeal was granted, leading to the dismissal of the matter, and the court concluded that allowing such appeals was imperative for the swift resolution of constitutional matters affecting local governance ([59]-[68]).

The judgment emphasizes the evolving nature of South African constitutional jurisprudence, wherein transitional provisions must align with democratic principles while allowing for the practical governance needed during periods of constitutional transformation. This case underscores the importance of interpreting both the Constitution and transitional laws holistically to foster effective democratic governance.

Court’s Holding: The Constitutional Court ruled the appeal legitimate and relevant constitutional principles required that issues concerning the two-thirds majority be resolved. The case sets vital precedents regarding the intersection of transitional provisions and constitutional review, encouraging prompt judicial intervention in matters of local governance. The appeal was dismissed with costs awarded against the appellant.
