African Congress for Transformation v Electoral Commission of South Africa; Labour Party of South Africa v Electoral Commission of South Africa and Others; Afrikan Alliance of Social Democrats v Electoral Commission of South Africa [2024] ZACC 7

The case comprises three separate applications heard by the Constitutional Court of South Africa pertaining to the Electoral Commission of South Africa's (the Commission) management of candidate submissions for the upcoming elections scheduled for 29 May 2024.

Constitutional Court 2024 [2024] ZACC 7

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–20])

The case comprises three separate applications heard by the Constitutional Court of South Africa pertaining to the Electoral Commission of South Africa's (the Commission) management of candidate submissions for the upcoming elections scheduled for 29 May 2024. The applicants—concerned newly registered political parties—sought to appeal against earlier decisions made by the Electoral Court, which had dismissed their claims challenging the Commission's refusal to accept late submissions of candidate lists due to alleged issues with its online submission portal.

The applicants in these cases included the African Congress for Transformation (ACT), the Labour Party of South Africa, and the Afrikan Alliance of Social Democrats (AASD). The critical factual backdrop was that all three parties had failed to meet the submission deadline of 8 March 2024 due to complaints regarding the dysfunctionality of the Commission's Online Candidate Nomination System (OCNS). The Commission maintained that the OCNS had operated effectively, asserting that other political parties managed to submit their nominations successfully.

The applicants claimed difficulties such as system failures, unresponsiveness, and lack of adequate training on using the OCNS, hindered their compliance with the submission process ([paras 3–30]). In light of this, they challenged the Commission's strict adherence to the Election Timetable and sought to present their lists beyond the prescribed deadlines, invoking sections 18 and 19 of the Constitution which safeguard the right to freedom of association and political participation.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–8])

The core issues addressed in the case were:

  1. Whether the Commission's insistence on absolute adherence to the Election Timetable contravened the constitutional rights to free and fair elections as stipulated under sections 19(1) and (3) of the Constitution.
  2. Whether the EC’s refusal to entertain late submissions due to alleged dysfunctionality of the OCNS impaired the political rights of the applicants and rendered the upcoming elections less free and fair.
  3. The appropriateness of the Commission's administrative conduct in refusing to amend the timetable despite multiple complaints about its online submission system.

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 8–12])

The Court grounded its analysis in several legal authorities, primarily:

  • Section 190 of the Constitution, which mandates the Commission to ensure free and fair elections.
  • Electoral Act 73 of 1998, particularly sections 20 and 27 which outline the procedures for submission of candidate lists and the Commission's powers in relation to amending the Election Timetable ([paras 8–10]).
  • Promulgation of electoral rights under sections 18 and 19 of the Constitution, which affirm the right to form political parties and participate in elections.
  • The concept of administrative justice under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA), which emphasizes the need for responsive governance by state officials and the accountability expected in electoral processes.
  • Precedent cases such as Inkatha Freedom Party and Liberal Party, which have addressed the necessity for strict compliance with election timelines in the context of fairness and transparency ([paras 9–12]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 30–108])

The Court held that the Commission's conduct did not violate constitutional rights or the Electoral Act. In assessing the functionality of the OCNS, the majority opinion emphasized the importance of accountability and the strict adherence to regulations governing electoral processes. Applying the Plascon-Evans principle, the Court ruled that the version presented by the Commission—asserting that the OCNS was operational and that other parties successfully submitted their documentation—should prevail over the claims of malfunction by the applicants.

The findings included:

  • ACT's operational failures were largely self-inflicted, demonstrating a lack of proper time management and awareness of the OCNS’s functionalities ([paras 30–40]).
  • The Labour Party's procrastination and last-minute submission efforts were noted, which were detrimental to their case for relief. The Court drew support from objective evidence that demonstrated the portal's overall functionality, as many parties had met the deadlines ([paras 40–60]).
  • AASD also faced a similar predicament, critiqued for failing to utilize alternative submission methods available to them at the Commission’s office ([paras 60–80]).

The majority ruling concluded that allowing exceptions to the deadlines would undermine the integrity and reliability of the electoral process, compromising fairness to other parties who complied ([paras 75–109]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 116–167])

The Constitutional Court dismissed all three claims primarily on the grounds that the applicants failed to demonstrate that the Commission's refusal to amend the Election Timetable was irrational. Furthermore, the applicants’ non-compliance was viewed as self-imposed rather than a result of systemic failure of the OCNS.

The ratio decidendi synthesized key points:

  • The right to free and fair elections must be balanced against the necessity of operational adherence to established procedures and regulations by all political entities ([paras 116–138]).
  • The Court underscored the lack of substantive grounds for the applicants’ complaints regarding the OCNS, which had functioned in accordance with the Commission's claims.
  • The importance of upholding electoral timelines was reiterated, with the court noting that flexibility could not come at the cost of undermining the electoral system's integrity ([paras 158–167]).

The judgments contained dissenting opinions that highlighted concerns about responsive governance, potential digital inequalities, and the need for greater consideration of complaints raised by all political parties regarding the electoral processes ([para 117–193]).

The decisions have significant implications for how electoral processes are managed in South Africa, setting a precedent about the rigor of compliance expected from political parties and the limits of the Commission’s regulatory powers.
