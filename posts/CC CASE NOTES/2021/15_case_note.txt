# Case Note: Mkhatshwa and Others v Mkhatshwa and Others [2021] ZACC 15 (FIRAC Analysis)

## F – Facts

This case arose from a series of legal disputes involving the Mawewe Communal Property Association (MCPA) and allegations of corruption, theft, and fraud that emerged within its operational framework. The first applicant, Mr. Siphiwe Happy Mkhatshwa, along with the second and third applicants, pursued legal action against the first and second respondents—Ms. Evah Simangele Mkhatshwa and the Mawewe Tribal Authority—following an Anton Piller order and a temporary interdict issued by the High Court of South Africa, Mpumalanga Division, Mbombela, in March 2020 ([1]). The Anton Piller order allowed for urgent search and seizure operations to preserve vital evidence pertinent to the alleged misuse of MCPA resources and control.

The first respondent, Ms. Evah Simangele Mkhatshwa, who is the Acting Chieftainess of the Mawewe Tribe, sought this relief on the grounds of protecting the integrity of the MCPA from its purported hijackers ([1], [2]). The applicants contended that the orders were obtained unlawfully and that subsequent decisions from the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissing their appeals were patently flawed ([7], [8]).

## I – Issues

1. **Whether the orders granted by the High Court were legally justified and could be appealed against.**
   
2. **Whether the conduct of the applicants warranted a punitive costs order, considering their allegations against the judiciary and the proceedings leading to their application.**

3. **The applicability of the Biowatch principle regarding costs in private litigations and whether it shields the applicants from comprehensive cost penalties due to their unsuccessful appeal ([17]).**

## R – Rules

The legal framework revolves around the application of the Communal Property Associations Act, 28 of 1996, which governs the operations of the MCPA, and Section 25(1) of the Constitution, which protects property rights ([8]). The Court further considered precedents regarding costs orders, particularly those outlined in Biowatch Trust v Registrar: Genetic Resources ([18]), which pertain to constitutional challenges against state actions, and established principles for awarding punitive costs if a litigant behaves in a vexatious manner ([21]).

The Court referred to historic cases that delineated the boundaries for punitive cost awards, emphasizing that such awards are exceptional and should only be applied when litigants demonstrate clear and unambiguous acts of vexatious behavior ([22], [23]). The judgment also noted the significance of maintaining the integrity of the judiciary against unfounded allegations ([25], [24]).

## A – Application

Khampepe J analyzed the applicants' claims against the substantiated background laid before them. The Court emphasized that the appeals did not hold reasonable prospects of success, particularly given the significant lack of substantiation for the allegations against the presiding officers involved in the High Court ([9]). The applicants’ assertions were characterized as defamatory and vexatious, culminating in an evaluation of the appropriateness of a punitive cost order ([26]).

Amidst affirmations of the applicants’ stance throughout the litigation, Khampepe J underscored that punitive costs served not only as a deterrent to vexatious litigation but also to safeguard judicial integrity. The applicants repeatedly failed to retract false allegations made against members of the judiciary, despite clear responses from the Judge President that discredited their claims ([23], [24]). The Court emphasized that the applicants' choice to perpetuate these claims, alongside their representation by experienced legal counsel, heightened the severity of their actions ([27]).

In rejecting the application for leave to appeal, the Court articulated that the applicants had not approached the matter with the necessary integrity mandated of them as litigants ([28]). Thus, the absence of grounds for applying the Biowatch principle in this instance further justified the punitive costs, reinforcing that such measures were warranted given the circumstances ([19]).

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately concluded with a refusal of the application for leave to appeal and mandated that the applicants pay the costs of the first and second respondents on an attorney-and-client scale, acknowledging the vexatious nature of their submissions ([28]). The ratio decidendi of the Court centers on the imperative to uphold judicial integrity and the implications of unchecked allegations against judicial officers. This outcome serves as a clarion call against litigants who resort to undermining the judicial process through scurrilous and unsubstantiated claims, emphasizing that the courts will not tolerate such behavior.

This ruling has significant implications for the conduct of future litigants, reiterating the notion that repeated unfounded allegations against judges can lead to severely punitive financial consequences. It serves as an educational juncture regarding the relationship between judicial officers and the integrity of the legal system that is predicated on mutual respect and legal propriety ([26], [27]).
