# FIRAC Case Note: Groupe LFE (SA) (Pty) Ltd v Swartland Winery Ltd & Others

**Neutral Citation:** Groupe LFE v Swartland Winery (467/09) [2011] ZASCA 4 (4 March 2011)  
**Coram:** Harms DP, Heher, Ponnan, Malan and Tshiqi JJA  
**Heard:** 16 February 2011  
**Delivered:** 4 March 2011  

## F - Facts

On 4 March 2011, the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa delivered a judgment regarding a trademark dispute between **Groupe LFE (SA) (Pty) Ltd ('LFE')** and **Swartland Winery Ltd ('the Winery')**. The Winery had a registered trademark for the term "Swartland" under class 33, effective from 13 December 1994 ([para 1]). Initially established as a wine co-operative named "Die Swartlandse Koöperatiewe Wynkelder Bpk" in 1948, the Winery has since evolved into a prominent public company, renowned in the wine industry for its award-winning products ([para 2]).

The central legal issues revolved around the Winery's claim that LFE infringed its trademark by using the name "Swartland" in a manner similar to trademark use for its wines, which were primarily intended for export ([para 11]). This claim was substantiated by evidence showcasing the Winery's long-standing use of "Swartland" and the public's association of the name with its quality wines ([para 4]).

LFE countered with an application for the expungement of the trademark, asserting that "Swartland" is geographically descriptive and hence unregistrable under trade mark law, citing sections 10(2)(b) and 10(12) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 ([paras 13-14]). However, the Winery insisted that its established use of the mark qualified it for protection under a savings provision ([para 15]). 

## I - Issues

The major issues of law that the Court needed to address were:

1. Whether LFE's usage of "Swartland" constituted trademark infringement under section 34(1)(a) of the Trade Marks Act ([paras 5-7]).
2. Whether LFE's argument for the expungement of the Winery's trademark registration was meritorious according to sections 10(2)(b) and 10(12) of the Act ([paras 13-14], [17]).

## R - Rules

The key legal authorities and provisions applicable to this case included:

1. **Section 34(1)(a) of the Trade Marks Act, 1993:** This section provides that the rights acquired through registration of a trademark are infringed by unauthorized use of an identical mark in relation to goods for which the trademark is registered ([para 5]).
2. **Section 10(2)(b):** Explains that a mark cannot be registered if it serves to designate the geographic origin of the goods ([para 14]).
3. **Section 10(12):** Prohibits the registration of a trademark that is contrary to law ([para 17]).
4. **Section 11(6) of the Liquor Products Act 60 of 1989:** Contains a savings provision allowing for the use of a legally established trademark as of 1 July 1990 to continue, regardless of geographic restrictions ([para 18]).

Precedent from *Century City Apartments Property Services CC v Century City Property Owners’ Association* 2010, which elaborated on the nature of trademark versus geographical indications ([para 6]).

## A - Application

The Court focused on LFE's claims, analyzing whether its use of "Swartland" constituted bona fide descriptive use or violated the trademark rights held by the Winery ([paras 7-8]). The Court noted that LFE's labeling emphasized the name "Swartland" prominently alongside "wine of origin," indicating that the name was being used as a trademark rather than simply as a geographical designation ([paras 8-10]).

The Court dismissed LFE's argument regarding the necessity for proof of financial loss to justify the interdict, clarifying that apprehension of harm isn't a requirement for granting such relief. Furthermore, it negated LFE's claim of forum shopping, asserting that South African courts had jurisdiction over local trademark infringements ([paras 11-12]).

Regarding the counter-application to expunge the trademark, the Court upheld the findings of the lower court, asserting that the Winery’s long-established use of "Swartland" had indeed imbued it with distinctiveness, allowing it protection under the savings provision in section 11(6) of the Liquor Products Act ([paras 15-19]). This affirmed that the trademark was valid and sustainable despite the geographic nature of the name.

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed LFE's appeal with costs, including the costs for two counsel, thereby affirming the decision of the Western Cape High Court. The ruling reinforced the importance of maintaining the integrity of established trademarks and clarified the balance between geographical indications and trademark rights under South African law. The implications of this judgment underscored the necessity for companies operating in the wine industry to respect registered trademarks and the legal complexities surrounding geographical indications in trademark law ([para 20]). 

This case serves as an essential reference point for understanding the intricacies of trade mark law, geographic indicators, and the legal protections that established brands enjoy in South Africa.
