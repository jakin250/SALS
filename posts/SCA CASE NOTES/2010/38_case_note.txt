Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH
Source Folder: 2010

# FIRAC Case Note: Leketi v Tladi (117/09) [2010] ZASCA 38

## F – Facts 
The case in question, **Leketi v Tladi** ([1]), revolves around a claim by the appellant, Stanley Elias Leketi, against the estate of his grandfather, the late Albert Mogale, for the recovery of immovable property known as Nooitgedacht No. 287. The appellant contended that this property rightfully belonged to his late father, George Mogale, who died on 5 January 1966. The dispute traces back to 25 June 1969, when Albert Mogale allegedly committed fraud by representing himself as the sole male heir of George to secure the fraudulent transfer and registration of the property into his name. This transfer was executed without disclosing that George had other surviving children, including the appellant and his sisters ([2]).

Initially, on 18 June 1974, Albert executed a will bequeathing the identified property to the second and third respondents, thereby excluding the appellant and his siblings entirely. Notably, the appellant only instituted his claim in February 2004—nearly 35 years after the fraudulent transfer—when summons was served on the respondents. The fourth respondent, the Registrar of Deeds, was served later, on 20 July 2005 ([3]–[4]). It was during these proceedings that the first respondent raised a special plea of prescription, asserting that the appellant's claim had prescribed due to the significant lapse of time since the basis of the fraud arose.

## I – Issues
The primary issue facing the court was whether the appellant's claim had become prescribed. Specifically, the inquiry was focused on the application of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 as it pertains to claims of fraud. This led to the critical question: Could the appellant have obtained the necessary knowledge of the fraud (and thus the requisite facts for instituting his claim) earlier than August 2003, thereby triggering the three-year prescription period ([7]–[8]).

Additionally, a secondary issue arose concerning the argument presented by the appellant that the fraud constituted a continuing wrong, which would negate the prescription of his claim. This aspect examined whether the nature of the fraudulent act could extend the period before the claim could be considered prescribed ([20]).

## R – Rules
The court's decision primarily hinged on the interpretation of the **Prescription Act 68 of 1969**. Specifically, sections pertinent to the case included:

- **Section 11(d)**, which stipulates that the ordinary period of prescription for a debt is three years from the date it becomes due.
- **Section 12(3)**, which provides that a debt shall not be deemed to be due until the creditor has the knowledge of the identity of the debtor and the facts from which the debt arises. The Act further clarified that a creditor is deemed to have knowledge if they could have acquired it by exercising reasonable care.
- **Section 13(1)(a)**, which concerns the extension of prescription for minors, indicating that the period does not complete until a year after the minor reaches the age of 21 ([8]).

The court also referenced precedents regarding the distinction between a continuing wrong and a once-off fraudulent act, particularly in relation to property disputes ([21]).

## A – Application
The court meticulously analyzed the circumstances surrounding the appellant's knowledge and actions leading to the institution of his claim. It noted that the appellant had failed to take reasonable steps to investigate his entitlement to the property and the identity of the defendants. By examining the appellant's actions—or lack thereof—over the decades since the transfer, the court established that he and his siblings likely possessed knowledge or were aware of their claims well before August 2003. Evidence suggested that the appellant visited the property and engaged with family members who were involved in its management ([10]–[19]).

The trial judge concluded that the appellant had the ability to have discovered the necessary information regarding the fraudulent nature of the transfer long before he did. Therefore, his claim for a debt arising from misappropriation by fraud should have been instituted considerably earlier. The court emphasized that the appellant's approach was marked by dilatoriness, as he failed to prioritize or assess the ownership of the property until many years after he had attained the legal capacity to act ([19]).

Concerning the claim of a continuing wrong, the court firmly rejected this argument, asserting that the single act of fraud occurred at the time of the transfer, which constitutes the actionable wrong, thereby allowing for prescription to apply after three years from that date ([20]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the decision of the North Gauteng High Court, concluding that the appellant's claim had indeed prescribed. The judgement ruled against the appellant on all grounds. The central holding was that the appellant had capabilities and opportunities to pursue his claim significantly earlier, but his inaction and reasonable care failures led to the lapse of prescription. Consequently, the court dismissed the appeal with costs, establishing that claims arising from historical fraud must be pursued diligently and without undue delay ([22]).

### Implications
This decision reinforces the doctrine of prescription in South African law, particularly in cases involving allegations of fraud and property rights. It underscores the emphasis on the need for claimants to actively pursue their interests and make timely inquiries into potential claims or fraud, especially when the law provides them the means to protect their rights. The ruling also clarifies the non-continuity of unauthorized acts of fraud in terms of their legal implications for prescription, thereby providing clear insights into how similar claims may be adjudicated in the future.
