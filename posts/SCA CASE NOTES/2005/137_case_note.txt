Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH
Source Folder: 2005

# Case Note: Andre De Reszke v Czeslaw Maras and Others

## F – Facts
The case concerns the determination of whether a document, referred to as "Annexure A," constitutes the last will of William Maras de Reszke (the deceased) under the Wills Act 7 of 1953 ([para 1]). The appellant, Andre de Reszke, is the deceased's son, who challenged a decision by the Master of the High Court, relating to a prior will dated 4 October 1999 ("the first will") that favorably included other beneficiaries ([para 2]). 

Following various interactions, the deceased expressed dissatisfaction with his attorney, Julian Weil, and instructed his confidante, Jienie-Michelle Dreyer, to prepare a new will ([para 3]). Dreyer typed a document embodying the deceased's instructions, which underwent various amendments and was finalized on 15 October 2001 and subsequently signed and witnessed on 18 October 2001 ([para 5]). This document purported to revoke any prior wills ([para 10]). 

However, at a meeting on 21 October 2001, the deceased reaffirmed to his neighbor and attorney, Wynand Albert Barnard, his desire for a new will, indicating that he still sought formal legal assistance ([para 6]). Despite affirming his intent on multiple occasions that he wanted a new will drafted, the deceased later signed Annexure A as witnessed by his caregiver ([para 7]). 

The deceased died shortly thereafter, on 7 November 2001, leading to disputes about the validity of Annexure A as his last will ([para 9]).

## I – Issues
The central legal issue before the court was whether Annexure A was intended by the deceased as his last will according to the stipulations of the Wills Act, particularly focusing on Section 2(3). This section allows for documents that do not meet formal execution requirements to be accepted as wills if it can be established that the deceased intended the document to be his last will ([paras 11-12]).

## R – Rules
The legal framework governing this matter is rooted primarily in the Wills Act 7 of 1953, particularly Section 2(3), which provides that a court may recognize a non-compliant will if it can ascertain that the deceased intended the document to function as such at the time of its drafting or execution ([para 11]). 

Relevant case law includes Harlow v Becker NO and Others 1998 (4) SA 639 (D), which discusses that the requisite intent must coexist with either drafting or executing the document ([para 11]). The Supreme Court of Appeal also referenced Van Wetten and Another v Bosch and Others 2004 (1) SA 348 (SCA), particularly regarding the nature of the deceased's intention in assessing whether a document can be deemed a valid will ([para 12]).

## A – Application
In applying these legal principles to the facts, the court emphasized that while Annexure A was drafted based on the deceased's instructions, it was initially intended merely as a set of instructions to an attorney and not as a final will ([para 13]). The appellant's counsel conceded that the initial intention of the deceased was to have a formal will drafted by Burger, thereby undermining his argument that Annexure A was intended as the final will at that moment ([para 13]).

The court considered the deceased's conduct following the signing of Annexure A and ultimately found that the deceased's continuing reliance on legal counsel and discussions with Barnard indicated a persistent intention to formalize a will rather than rely on Annexure A as a standalone document ([paras 15-17]). The deceased’s actions reflected a clear understanding of will execution requirements, contributing to the court's conclusion that he could not have intended Annexure A to serve as his will ([para 17]).

Lastly, the court addressed the issue of costs, deciding against allowing the expenses related to the appeal to be covered by the estate, given that the appellant had disregarded the previous court's indication of the document’s non-validity as a will ([para 19]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, concluding that the appellant failed to establish that the deceased intended Annexure A to be his last will in compliance with the Wills Act ([para 20]). The court held that both the evidence surrounding the execution of Annexure A and the deceased's subsequent actions were inconsistent with an intention to treat the document as a valid will. Consequently, the ruling reinforces the stringent requirements surrounding will execution and the necessity for clear intent, offering critical insights for future cases involving document interpretation within the realm of testamentary dispositions.
