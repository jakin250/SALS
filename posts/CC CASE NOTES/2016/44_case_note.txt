Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# Case Note: Laubscher N.O. v Duplan and Another [2016] ZACC 44

### FIRAC Method

---

### F - Facts

**Background**  
The case involves the intestate succession rights of unmarried same-sex partners, specifically examining whether the respondent, Eric Duplan, has the right to inherit from the estate of the deceased, Cornelius Daniel Laubscher, despite their partnership not being formally registered as per the provisions of the Civil Union Act (CUA) ([1]–[4]). The applicant, Rasmus Elardus Erasmus Laubscher, the deceased's brother and executor of the estate, contests that Duplan, having lived with the deceased in a permanent same-sex partnership since 2003, is not entitled to inherit given that their partnership was neither solemnized nor registered under the CUA, which grants rights only to formally recognized unions ([1]–[3]).

**Procedural History**  
The High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Division, Pretoria, was initially tasked with determining this issue based on a stated case agreement ([5]). The judgment determined Duplan's entitlement citing the precedent set in *Gory v Kolver NO* ([8]). The applicant contended that the enacting of the CUA meant that only partners who registered their unions would qualify as "spouses" under the intestate provisions of the Intestate Succession Act (ISA) ([6]–[9]).

---

### I - Issues

The primary legal issues of the case include:
1. Whether the "reading-in" remedy established by the Constitutional Court in the *Gory* case is an interim measure that has lapsed with the enactment of the CUA.
2. The interplay between the *Gory* order and the CUA concerning intestate succession rights for same-sex partners who are not formally married.
3. Whether the precedent set in *Volks NO v Robinson*, which delineates the rights of permanent life partners regarding intestate rights, applies in this context ([19]).

---

### R - Rules

**Constitutional Provisions**  
- **Section 1(1) of the Intestate Succession Act (ISA)**: Details the succession rights of a surviving spouse and descendants ([5]).
- **Section 13(2)(b) of the Civil Union Act (CUA)**: States that a partner in a civil union is also deemed a "spouse" for the purposes of many laws ([11]).

**Judicial Precedents**  
- **Gory v Kolver NO** ([4]): The court declared the exclusion of same-sex partners from intestate succession provisions unconstitutional, prompting a reading-in remedy.
- **Volks NO v Robinson**: The court held that the Maintenance of Surviving Spouses Act does not extend rights to partners in non-solemnized unions, highlighting the legal status difference between married and unmarried partners ([43]–[52]).

---

### A - Application

The Constitutional Court primarily evaluated whether the *Gory* ruling remained applicable following the enactment of the CUA. The majority opinion, authored by Mbha AJ, held that the reading-in provision was not an interim measure subject to expiration with the enactment of subsequent legislation ([21]–[25]). The opinion reasoned that the law allowing for civil unions did not repeal the *Gory* order; rather, both legal frameworks coexist, where each provides distinct rights and protections ([55]–[56]).

Significantly, the court reasoned that the evidence indicates a legislative intent to protect same-sex partners in permanent relationships who have not opted for formal registration under the CUA, reinforcing the applicability of the reading-in remedy ([37]–[42]). The court further delineated that while legislation can define marriage-like relationships, it did not mandate that non-married partners be excluded from the protections provided to same-sex life partners under the *Gory* precedent ([25]–[34]).

---

### C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court dismissed the appeal, affirming the decision of the High Court and declaring that the respondent, Eric Duplan, possesses intestate succession rights to inherit from the deceased’s estate as a result of the *Gory* reading-in provision. The court ruled that the enactment of the CUA did not invalidate the rights established in *Gory*, and in light of the ongoing discrimination faced by unmarried same-sex partners, it would be improper to strip them of existing protections ([57]). The ruling exemplifies the court's commitment to upholding equality and human dignity in the context of evolving social norms and legal interpretations concerning same-sex relationships.

---

This case underscores both the dynamic legal landscape in South Africa regarding same-sex partnerships and the delicate balance of legislative intent and judicial interpretation in safeguarding individual rights under constitutional law.
