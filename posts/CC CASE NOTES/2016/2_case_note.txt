Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Tronox KZN Sands (Pty) Ltd v KwaZulu-Natal Planning and Development Appeal Tribunal and Others [2016] ZACC 2

## F - Facts
This case arises from the application brought by **Tronox KZN Sands (Pty) Ltd** (Tronox) against the **KwaZulu-Natal Planning and Development Appeal Tribunal** (First Respondent) and other parties ([paras 6-8]). Tronox, a major producer of titanium ore and titanium dioxide, applied to **Umlalazi Municipality** (Fourth Respondent) for land use rights to conduct mining operations in an area outside a designated scheme ([para 7]). The municipality granted this application on February 19, 2014; however, the second and third respondents, **Mtunzini Conservancy** and **Mtunzini Fish Farm (Pty) Ltd**, lodged appeals to the Appeal Tribunal under section 45 of the **KwaZulu-Natal Planning and Development Act 6 of 2008** (PDA) ([para 8]).

Dissatisfied with the legislative scheme allowing provincial appeals on municipal decisions, Tronox initiated proceedings in the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court, seeking a declaration that section 45 and Chapter 10 of the PDA were unconstitutional and interfering with municipal powers ([paras 9-10]). The High Court ruled in Tronox's favor, declaring section 45 constitutionally invalid, requiring confirmation from the Constitutional Court ([para 12]).

## I - Issues
The primary issue in this case is whether section 45 of the PDA is constitutionally valid, particularly in the context of local government autonomy as allocated by the Constitution of South Africa ([para 11]). Further issues include:

1. If section 45 infringes on municipalities' executive authority by allowing external appeals.
2. Whether the Court should declare Chapter 10 of the PDA unconstitutional if section 45 is invalid.
3. The implications of declaring section 45 invalid for the ongoing appeals and whether the invalidity will have retrospective effect ([para 4]).

## R - Rules
The case hinges on several constitutional provisions:

1. **Section 156(1)** of the Constitution: This provision grants municipalities the executive authority to administer local government matters, including municipal planning.
2. **Sections 40(1) and 41(1)**: These mandate that different spheres of government must respect each other's powers and functions ([para 19]).
3. **Precedents** from cases such as **Gauteng Development Tribunal** and **Habitat Council**, which emphasize the autonomy of municipal decision-making in planning matters, affirming that provinces cannot assume authority over municipal planning ([paras 20-24]).

## A - Application
The Court analyzed whether section 45 interfered with the constitutionally guaranteed powers of municipalities. It referenced previous precedents emphasizing that municipal planning falls exclusively under municipal authority, and external oversight by provincial bodies constitutes significant interference ([paras 25-30]). The Court found that provisions within the PDA disappointed the constitutional design, as they created an appellate authority (the Appeal Tribunal) without the municipalities' consent, which was deemed unconstitutional ([para 27]).

Despite the MEC (Fifth Respondent) portraying the Appeal Tribunal as independent from provincial control, the court underscored that such perceived autonomy does not negate the infringement on municipal powers ([para 28]). The discussion of possible legislative weaknesses (reading-in vs. reading-down) failed when considerable constitutional contradictions were identified in the suggestions put forth for modification to section 45 ([paras 32-39]).

The Court also determined that suspending the declaration of invalidity could not be justified based on the MEC's arguments regarding municipal capacity limitations ([para 49]).

## C - Conclusion
The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court’s declaration that section 45 of the KwaZulu-Natal Planning and Development Act 6 of 2008 was constitutionally invalid ([paras 61-63]). The Court ruled that:

1. The declaration does not have retrospective effect and does not affect finalised appeals.
2. All pending appeals under section 45 may continue until finalised, ensuring municipalities' integrated development plans are upheld in these processes.
3. The MEC must pay the costs incurred by Tronox due to opposition to the confirmation application ([para 62]).

This ruling reaffirms the inviolable nature of municipal powers concerning planning decisions, clarifying the constitutional limits on provincial interference and solidifying the hierarchical autonomy between South Africa's governmental tiers.
