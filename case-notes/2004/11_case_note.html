Constitutional Court

MOKGORO J

In both matters, the applicants sought social assistance due to their destitution, qualifying for social grants under the Act, barring the citizenship requirement.

Constitutional Court 2004

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history

The cases of Khosa and Others v Minister of Social Development and Mahlaule and Another v Minister of Social Development were consolidated before the Constitutional Court of South Africa to address constitutional challenges against provisions of the Social Assistance Act 59 of 1992. The applicants (permanent residents of South Africa, primarily from Mozambique) contest the constitutionality of various sections of the Act that reserve social grants for South African citizens only.

In both matters, the applicants sought social assistance due to their destitution, qualifying for social grants under the Act, barring the citizenship requirement. Specifically, in the Khosa case, the challenge was aimed at section 3(c) of the Act, while in Mahlaule, sections 4(b)(ii) and 4B(b)(ii) were under scrutiny ([paras 1–4]). The applicants had successfully argued in the High Court that these provisions violated their rights to equality, human dignity, and access to social security as entrenched in the Constitution ([paras 5–10]).

Procedurally, the High Court found these sections unconstitutional, leading to confirmation proceedings in the Constitutional Court ([paras 18–19]). During the confirmation hearings on 13 and 30 May 2003, the respondents initially failed to appear and later contested the High Court's decision, leading to a need for further arguments on the matter ([paras 13-16]).

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions

  1. Exclusion from social assistance: Whether sections 3(c), 4(b)(ii), and 4B(b)(ii), which limit access to social grants to South African citizens, are unconstitutional as they exclude permanent residents.
  2. Interpretation of constitutional rights: Does the exclusion infringe upon the rights to equality, dignity, and access to social security as guaranteed by the Constitution?
  3. Implications of government policies: Whether the legislative intent justifies the citizenship requirement amidst broader claims of socio-economic rights ([paras 1–4], [38–40]).

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon

The judgment substantively relied on various constitutional provisions, including:

  • Section 9 (Equality Clause) of the Constitution, which bars unfair discrimination ([para 9]).
  • Section 10, which establishes the right to dignity, and Section 27, outlining the right to access social security for "everyone" ([parcel 38]).
  • Precedents such as Dawood v Minister of Home Affairs and Grootboom, which explore the interplay between socio-economic rights and the limitation of such rights under the Constitution ([paras 39-43]).

Moreover, the judgment drew on international human rights principles, affirming that social security frameworks should be inclusive of all residents, especially vulnerable groups ([paras 40-44]).

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law

The court's analysis commenced with the premise that the exclusion of permanent residents from social grants is an affront to the constitutional guarantee of rights to social security for "everyone." Significant emphasis was placed on the interdependence of rights, particularly those of equality, dignity, and social assistance, which are meant to protect the most vulnerable ([paras 40–82]).

In dissecting the legislative justification for the citizenship requirement, the court found that while the state presented rational bases—such as budgetary constraints and the promotion of self-sufficiency among immigrants—these did not sufficiently outweigh the severe detriment to dignity and equality experienced by permanent residents. The court emphasized that the denial of grants relegates permanent residents to socio-economic marginalization and interferes fundamentally with their dignity ([paras 44–82]).

Furthermore, the respondents' reliance on comparative jurisprudence from other jurisdictions regarding citizenship and welfare eligibility was critiqued as inapplicable to South Africa's constitutional context, thus reinforcing the need for an inclusive social security framework ([paras 55-60]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications

The court ultimately ruled sections 3(c), 4(b)(ii), and 4B(b)(ii) unconstitutional, declaring that the exclusion of permanent residents from social assistance contradicts the values enshrined in the Constitution. The court held that the citizenship requirement failed the test of reasonableness as outlined in Section 36, thus striking down the provisions while mandating that the words "or permanent resident" be read into the relevant sections ([paras 80–92]).

This landmark decision carries significant implications, not only affirming the socioeconomic rights of permanent residents but also propelling broader discussions on inclusivity within legislative frameworks governing social assistance, encouraging the state to reassess its policies regarding non-citizens ([paras 85-98]). The Court also mandated that the legislature must ensure that ensuring human dignity is foundational to its social security policies moving forward ([para 97]).
