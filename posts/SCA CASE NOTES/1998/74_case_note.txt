Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 1998

# FIRAC Case Note: Syntheta (Pty) Ltd v Janssen Pharmaceutica N V & Another

## F - Facts

The case revolves around an application for a compulsory license under South Africa's Patents Act No 57 of 1978^[1]. The Appellant, Syntheta (Pty) Ltd, previously known as Delta G Scientific (Pty) Ltd, sought a license regarding South African Letters Patent No 75/7193, which covers an invention of "Triazole Derivatives"^[2]. The patent's inventor and proprietor is the first Respondent, Janssen Pharmaceutica N V, while the second Respondent, Novartis A G (formerly Ciba-Geigy A G), is the registered exclusive licensee^[3].

The patent in question had originally been granted under the 1952 Patents Act and had an extended term due to a lack of adequate remuneration during its initial duration^[4]. The extension expired in November 1996, creating a pressing timeline for Syntheta's application, filed in August 1995^[5].

Syntheta's appeal was predicated on two specific claims under Section 56 of the Act, which pertain to patent abuse: (2)(a) concerning non-working of the patented invention on a commercial scale, and (2)(d) relating to the patentee's refusal to grant a license on reasonable terms^[6]. Notably, Syntheta's proposal was to produce the fungicide propiconazole solely for export—without any intent to market it within South Africa^[7].

The original judgment by Streicher J found in favor of the Respondents, dismissing the application for a compulsory license, leading to Syntheta's appeal^[8].

## I - Issues

The primary legal questions addressed by the court included:

1. Whether Syntheta sufficiently established the "non-working" of the patented invention commercially within the Republic to satisfy Section 56(2)(a)^[9].
2. Whether the refusal to grant a license on terms proposed by Syntheta constituted an abuse of patent rights under Section 56(2)(d)^[10].

Additionally, the court considered whether the intended purpose of Syntheta's application aligned with the public interest underlying the provisions of the Patents Act^[11].

## R - Rules

The legal framework for this case is established primarily by Section 56 of the Patents Act. The relevant subsections are as follows:

1. **Section 56(1)** allows any interested party to apply for a compulsory license if they can demonstrate abuse of patent rights^[12].
2. **Section 56(2)** stipulates circumstances under which patent rights are deemed abused, notably:
   - **(a)** Non-working of the patented invention commercially or inadequately after specified periods^[13].
   - **(d)** Refusal to grant licenses that may harm trade, agriculture, or industry within the Republic, affecting public interest^[14].
3. Guidelines for determining license conditions are provided in **Section 56(7)**, requiring consideration of relevant facts, including risks to the licensee and customary terms in voluntary agreements^[15].

The broader philosophy underlying the Patents Act is to incentivize innovation by balancing reward for inventors against public benefits^[16].

## A - Application

The court's analysis revealed that Syntheta failed to demonstrate the required jurisdictional facts under both subsections of Section 56 it invoked. For **Section 56(2)(a)**, the court noted that Syntheta did not effectively show that the patent was not being worked commercially within South Africa. While Syntheta claimed that propiconazole was sold in South Africa, it conceded that it was being imported by Ciba and repackaged, which constituted a form of working within the meaning of the Act^[17]. The court emphasized that Syntheta's assertion regarding non-working amounted to a mere recitation of statutory language, lacking factual substantiation concerning the demand and feasibility of local production of propiconazole^[18].

In terms of **Section 56(2)(d)**, Syntheta's case was similarly weak, with the court noting the lack of essential facts regarding the proposed license's reasonableness. Syntheta merely stated that it offered a 6% royalty, but this claim was not substantiated by adequate evidence regarding comparative market practices or the cost structure necessary for determining a reasonable royalty^[19]. The Respondents rebutted this assertion by indicating that the terms offered were considered low in the context of the industry, particularly given their prior investment in research and development^[20].

Furthermore, the court considered whether Syntheta's real motivations for seeking the license—specifically, to address its financial difficulties in the export market—aligned with the public interest that the section aims to serve^[21]. The judgment noted that Syntheta's focus appeared self-serving rather than geared towards enhancing public access to the invention traded within South Africa^[22].

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that Syntheta failed to establish a case of patent abuse under both grounds for its compulsory license application. It found that Syntheta did not provide sufficient evidence to demonstrate that the patented invention was not being commercially worked or that the terms proposed for licensing were reasonable^[23].

Consequently, the appeal was dismissed, and costs were awarded to the Respondents, reflecting the Court's view that Syntheta's application was inadequately supported both factually and legally^[24]. The case underscores the necessity of clear factual grounding in applications for compulsory licenses and highlights the balance that patent law seeks to maintain between incentivizing innovation and serving public interests^[25].
