Supreme Court of Appeal

Padachie v The Body Corporate of Crystal Cove ([2016] ZASCA 145)

In July 2009, the Body Corporate initiated proceedings against the appellants in the Magistrate’s Court, claiming R9,891.83 for alleged arrear levies and related charges.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 145)

F – Facts

The present case concerns an appeal against the judgment of the KwaZulu-Natal Local Division of the High Court, Durban, where the high court (Nkosi J) dismissed the appellants’ application to review and set aside an arbitration award rendered by the second respondent, Mr Brian Agar, an attorney appointed as an arbitrator by the parties ([1]). The appellants, Krishna Soobramoney Padachie and Amanda Marcella Padachie, are legally married and co-own a unit in a residential estate governed by the first respondent, The Body Corporate of Crystal Cove. The Body Corporate is a sectional title body corporate as established under the Sectional Titles Act 95 of 1986 ([2]).

In July 2009, the Body Corporate initiated proceedings against the appellants in the Magistrate’s Court, claiming R9,891.83 for alleged arrear levies and related charges. The appellants defended this claim, asserting various defences, including extinction by prescription, before agreeing to arbitrate the disputes arising from the Magistrate’s Court proceedings. They were subject to five claims from the Body Corporate during arbitration, out of which two were subsequently abandoned, leaving three active claims ([5]).

During the arbitration process that began in November 2012, the arbitrator requested written arguments from both parties. The appellant’s attorneys raised several legal points, suggesting that certain questions of law should be referred to the court under section 20(1) of the Arbitration Act 42 of 1965. The arbitrator declined to make such a referral before publishing his award on December 13, 2012, which concluded that the appellants were liable for the amounts claimed ([10], [11]).

On April 4, 2013, the appellant sought judicial review of the arbitration award, arguing that the arbitrator had improperly restricted his ability to approach the court for legal opinions on the posed questions. The high court dismissed this application, establishing that the arbitrator had not committed any irregularity and had not denied the appellant the opportunity to engage the court ([11], [12]).

I – Issues

The primary issue for determination was whether the arbitrator had deprived the appellant of his right under section 20 of the Arbitration Act to obtain the court's opinion on designated legal questions ([3]). Secondary issues included:

  1. Whether any of the claims should rightly have been subject to referral to the court for an opinion.
  2. The permissibility of the appellant’s qualified request for referral to the court ([16], [20]).

R – Rules

The rules governing this case primarily derive from the Arbitration Act 42 of 1965. Specifically, section 20(1) permits an arbitration tribunal to refer any question of law arising during arbitration to the court at the request of a party. This rule was interpreted within the statutory and jurisprudential context, including case law such as Telcordia Technologies Inc v Telkom SA Ltd ([2006] ZASCA 112) which emphasized that section 20 is applicable only to legal questions that arise during arbitration, and not to those voluntarily submitted for arbitration by parties ([4], [17]).

The court also cited other significant legal principles, including comments from Government of the Republic of South Africa v Midkon (Pty) Ltd 1984 (3) SA 552 (T), which elaborated that a qualified request for issues to be sent to court is not permissible under South African arbitration law. The various precedents emphasize that once parties agree to submit a matter to arbitration, they are bound by that decision, and under section 20, they cannot circumvent the arbitral process on issues they willingly submitted ([22]).

A – Application

In its analysis, the court emphasized that the arbitrator had already indicated on multiple occasions that no points of law warranted referral to the court ([12], [13]). The arbitrator advised the appellant’s attorneys to provide written arguments by a specific deadline without indicating an intention to refer any questions to the court, thus demonstrating that he was prepared to finalize the decision without a referral ([7], [13]).

The appellant's argument for maintaining that he was wrongly deprived of the right to approach the court relied on the assertion that his requests for clarification were legally substantial and unambiguous ([12]). However, the court concluded that it was the appellant’s own negligence in formulating those questions that resulted in their deemed imprecision, ultimately rendering his claim of deprivation baseless. The ambiguity in his request was juxtaposed against the robust arguments he had provided within the arbitration, further undermining his assertion ([14], [20]).

As for the claims regarding prescription and Management rule 10, the court noted that these matters were integral to the arbitration process, and thus, did not arise as separate legal issues warranting judicial input ([19]). Accordingly, the appellant was deemed to have voluntarily submitted these questions to the arbitrator, thereby relinquishing the right to contest them later in court ([18], [20]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the ruling of the high court, concluding that the arbitrator had acted within the bounds of his authority and had not committed any gross irregularities that would necessitate the review of his decision. The appeal was dismissed, and costs were awarded to the respondents, with emphasis on the peculiar nature of litigation costs outweighing the capital amount in dispute ([28]).

The implication of this judgment reaffirms the bounds of arbitration under the Arbitration Act, emphasizing the finality of arbitral awards and the necessity for parties to be diligent in making proper legal assertions during arbitration. The case serves as a vital reminder of the importance of thorough legal representation and clarity in submissions within arbitral proceedings.
