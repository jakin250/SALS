Case Note: Samancor Chrome Limited v North West Chrome Mining (Pty) Ltd and Others

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 Samancor Chrome Ltd v North West Chrome Mining (Pty) Ltd and Others (Case no 30/20) [2021] ZASCA 183 (23 December 2021)

Case Note: Samancor Chrome Limited v North West Chrome Mining (Pty) Ltd and Others

Neutral Citation: Samancor Chrome Ltd v North West Chrome Mining (Pty) Ltd and Others (Case no 30/20) [2021] ZASCA 183 (23 December 2021)

Coram: Petse AP, Dambuza, Van der Merwe, Makgoka, and Mabindla-Boqwana JJA

FIRAC Method Case Note

F – Facts

The dispute arises from an urgent application initiated by Samancor Chrome Limited (hereinafter “Samancor”) on 10 December 2018 against North West Chrome Mining Proprietary Limited (the first respondent) and Monageng Family Mining Services Proprietary Limited (the second respondent) ([2]). Samancor sought an interdict to prevent the respondents from conducting mining activities on its designated prospecting area, as established by a prospecting right granted by the Deputy Director-General of the Department of Mineral Resources ([4]).

The prospecting right, initially issued on 11 December 2017 and finalized with notarial execution on 16 May 2018 ([5]), encompassed portions of the farm Tweelaagte 175 JP, covering an area of approximately 1089.4138 hectares. After discovering the respondents’ mining activities within this area, which Samancor claimed were unauthorized, it took legal action ([9]).

The respondents contested the validity of Samancor's prospecting right, asserting that it was void due to inaccuracies regarding the area description ([11]). They claimed valid mining permits for their activities, although later evidence indicated that two of the three permits had lapsed and that their mining activities occurred outside of any legal permit area ([18]). The North West Division of the High Court dismissed Samancor's application ([13]), prompting Samancor to appeal.

I – Issues

The main issues for determination included:

  1. Whether Samancor held a valid prospecting right and had a clear right to seek an interdict against the respondents based on their unlawful mining activities ([21]).
  2. Whether the Minister of Mineral Resources needed to be joined as a party to the proceedings due to the authority over prospecting rights ([26]).
  3. Whether the appeal should be granted based on the high court's perceived error in law regarding the validity of Samancor’s prospecting right ([19]).
  4. Whether Samancor could introduce further evidence on appeal to support its claims ([28]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal framework centres around the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA). Key sections include:

  • Section 17(1): Establishes criteria for the granting of prospecting rights, requiring technical ability, financial resources, and compliance with environmental regulations.
  • Section 47: Governs the Minister's authority to cancel mining rights, outlining the procedural requirements for such cancellation ([11]).
  • Uniform Rules of Court, Rule 6(12): Governs urgent applications in the High Court, including provisions for interim relief in matters of immediacy ([6]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal also considered precedents on the requirements for granting interdicts, determining that the applicant must show a clear right, irreparable harm, and the absence of an alternative remedy ([30]).

A – Application

The Court critically assessed the high court's dismissal of Samancor's application. It established that:

  • The high court erroneously questioned the validity of Samancor's prospecting right, which, although descriptively flawed, was legally granted and valid until set aside ([21]). The existence of valid mining permits for the respondents was overshadowed by their illegal mining activities, breaching Samancor’s rights ([24]).
  • As per the finding in [26], the high court misapplied the necessity of joining the Minister. It observed that both the Deputy Director-General and the Regional Manager acted within their delegated authority, thus negating the need for the Minister’s involvement in enforcing Samancor’s rights against unlawful mining ([26]).
  • On the issue of further evidence, the court underscored that the high court should have allowed the introduction of new evidence concerning the prospecting area’s rectification; however, such evidence must be shown to substantially affect the matter's outcome ([28]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal granted leave to appeal, overturning the high court's decision and recognizing Samancor’s entitlement to an interdict against the respondents. The importance of maintaining the integrity of prospecting rights under the MPRDA was reaffirmed, particularly the necessity for courts to uphold these rights against illegal operations ([31]).

Ultimately, the court ruled that Samancor demonstrated a clear right to the relief sought, necessitating the ruling in its favor. The decision not only rectified the procedural errors of the lower court but solidified the legal standing of prospecting rights in South Africa, with significant implications for future mining law disputes ([30]).

In conclusion, the Supreme Court’s ruling elaborated on the nuances of interdicts in mineral rights law, particularly the weight of accurately described boundaries and the substantive rights of prospectors amidst conflicting mining claims.
