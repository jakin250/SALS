# Case Note: Thorpe v Trittenwein [2006] SCA 30 (RSA)

## F - Facts

The case revolves around an application filed by the appellants, B E Thorpe, S Thorpe, and A E R Dixon (the trustees of the Brian Edward Thorpe Trust), in the High Court of Johannesburg. They sought a declaration that a sale agreement for an immovable property in Bedfordview was valid and enforceable ([1]).

On 8 December 2000, Mr. Brian Edward Thorpe signed a written offer to purchase the property in the name of the Brian Edward Thorpe Trust. Although described as an "offer," it was intended to constitute a written agreement of sale upon acceptance ([2]). The document detailed the purchase price of R2,520,000, which was to be paid by "Purchaser 1" (the trust) and "Purchaser 2," who was left blank. The first respondent accepted the offer the same day.

Subsequent to this, on 19 December 2000, an addendum was signed which identified a nominee as "second purchaser" but also included a clause allowing the second purchaser to find another nominee within 90 days ([3]). On 6 March 2001, another addendum titled "Addendum B" was signed, changing the second purchaser's liability and confirming the nominee's identity.

The agreement included a condition regarding the establishment of a township on the property, the fulfillment of which was essential for the sale to go forward ([5]). A deposit from the trust was paid shortly after the first addendum, while the second purchaser delayed payment. As difficulties arose with the township application, the first respondent moved to cancel the sale on 10 October 2002, but the appellants contended that the cancellation was invalid ([6]).

The appellants launched their application on 15 May 2003, asserting the validity of the sale agreement. The first respondent's defence was largely based on the assertion that the agreement did not comply with the requirements of the Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981 ([7]). The High Court dismissed the application, leading to the current appeal ([1]).

## I - Issues

The primary legal issue in this case concerned whether the sale agreement signed by one trustee, without the written authority of the other co-trustees, was valid under section 2(1) of the Alienation of Land Act ([8]). Specifically, the court needed to determine if Thorpe had acted as an agent for the other two trustees and whether that authority was required to be in writing, thus impacting the enforceability of the agreement.

## R - Rules

The court relied on section 2(1) of the Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981, which states that no alienation of land shall take effect unless it is contained in a deed of alienation signed by the parties or their agents, acting on written authority ([8]). Additionally, the court referenced case law establishing principles regarding the role of trustees, including *Hoosen and others NNO v Deedat and others* and *Nieuwoudt and another NNO v Vrystaat Mielies (Edms) Bpk*, which clarify that trustees must act jointly unless otherwise stated in the trust deed ([9][10]).

Moreover, the distinction between the authority status of a trustee and that of a partner or corporation was emphasized, highlighting that trustees are bound by the requirement of joint action as set forth in the trust deed and applicable statutes ([12]).

## A - Application

The court examined whether Thorpe’s signing of the agreement constituted him acting as an "agent" of the other trustees. While acknowledging that a trust is an accumulation of assets and liabilities and that trustees manage them, the judges asserted that one trustee cannot unilaterally bind the trust without written consent from the others ([9][10]).

Thorpe's defense was that he had obtained oral authority, which was subsequently ratified in writing. However, the court clarified that ratification could not validate a contract that was void ab initio, meaning that it lacked legal effect from the outset ([16]). In obtaining validation, the burden to prove written authority remained with the appellants, and the absence of such authority rendered the agreement void despite the somewhat unusual circumstances, including that Thorpe was also a beneficiary of the trust ([17]).

The judgment outlined that even if Thorpe had consent from the other trustees verbally, the law mandated that such authority should be documented to ensure transparency and compliance with the statutory requirements designed to reduce disputes over land transactions ([15]). Hence, the absence of written authority meant that the agreement was invalid under section 2(1) of the Act, and the appeal was dismissed ([18]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court's ruling that the sale agreement was void for failing to meet the statutory requirements of the Alienation of Land Act. The judgment underscored the necessity for trustees to act jointly and the imperative that authority to bind a trust must be documented in writing ([17][18]).

This case reinforces the legal distinction between trusts and other business entities in transactional authority and the necessity for compliance with statutory requirements, even when the parties involved do not suffer prejudice. The decision serves as a cautionary tale for trustees engaging in transactions and the need for proper governance in trust operations.
