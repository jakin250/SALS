Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: Van der Westhuizen v S [2011] ZASCA 36 (28 March 2011)

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–1])
On 28 July 2006, M Van der Westhuizen (the appellant) shot and killed his three children, resulting in his conviction for three counts of murder by the Western Cape High Court (Louw J), which sentenced him to 14 years of imprisonment for each count. Notably, nine years of the sentences on the second and third counts were ordered to run concurrently with the first count, resulting in an effective term of 24 years. Following this conviction, the appellant appealed against both the conviction and the sentence, focusing on issues of trial fairness and the informal admissions made under section 220 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977. The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) heard the case on 23 February 2011 and delivered its judgment on 28 March 2011.

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 1–1])
The court was tasked with addressing two prime issues in this appeal:
1. Did the appellant receive a fair trial, particularly considering the conduct of the prosecutor and the ruling on witness testimonies?
2. After consenting to admissions being formally recorded under section 220 of the Criminal Procedure Act, did the appellant retain the right to present evidence contrary to those admissions?

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 3–3])
Central to the appellant's claim was the interpretation of section 32 of the National Prosecuting Authority Act, which mandates that prosecutors act impartially, alongside section 179(4) of the Constitution, asserting that the prosecuting authority must exercise its functions without fear, favour, or prejudice. Moreover, the court leaned on its previous rulings concerning prosecutorial discretion and the fairness of trials, referencing established norms in South African and international legal contexts regarding the role and responsibility of prosecution.

Additionally, section 220 of the Criminal Procedure Act allows for formal admissions in criminal proceedings, which can serve as sufficient proofs of fact without the necessity for further evidence. The case also cited previous judgments pertaining to diminished responsibility and mental capacity grounds for legal reasoning surrounding an accused's admissibility of guilt.

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 5–5])
In analyzing the fairness of the trial, the SCA discredited the appellant’s claims regarding prosecutorial misconduct. It determined that the prosecutor’s actions in calling specific witnesses did not violate the principle of impartiality but were executed within the bounds of prosecutorial discretion aimed at presenting credible evidence to the court. It emphasized that a prosecutor's duty is not to present evidence favoring the defense but to ensure that credible evidence contributes to a just outcome.

The second issue regarding the admissions made under section 220 was assessed. The court clarified that while an accused could consent to the recording of admissions, this does not allow for the later introduction of evidence contradicting those admissions. The court reinforced the conclusion that such admissions constituted sufficient proof of the acknowledged facts, precluding the appellant from arguing against them.

Regarding claims of diminished responsibility, both the expert testimonies and the evidence presented during the trial were critically evaluated. The SCA underscored that despite the psychological assessments indicating some level of diminished capacity, it ultimately affirmed that the appellant was capable of appreciating the wrongfulness of his actions at the time of the murders. The ruling emphasized that the appellant’s acts were premeditated and carried out in a controlled manner, indicating a full understanding of his actions.

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 41–41])
The SCA ultimately dismissed the appeal, upholding the conviction and sentence imposed by the High Court. It concluded that the appellant had indeed received a fair trial, noting that no significant irregularities had occurred that would have damaged the integrity of the trial process. The court reaffirmed the principle that a prosecutor's obligation in presenting a case is not to garner a conviction but to ensure integrity and justice in proceedings.

Moreover, the court’s definitive stance on the implications of formal admissions under section 220 reinforced the importance of legal representation and informed decision-making during trial processes. The judgment served as an essential precedent in affirming the responsibilities of both prosecution and defense in criminal proceedings, particularly in managing and interpreting admissions and evidence regarding diminished responsibility defenses.
