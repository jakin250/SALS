Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2007

# FIRAC Case Note: Price v Minister of Correctional Services [2007] SCA 156 (RSA)

## F – Facts

The appellant, David Ashley Price, was sentenced to 15 years’ imprisonment for two counts of fraud on 21 December 2000. After serving four and a half years in St Albans Medium B Prison, he initiated proceedings in the High Court to review the decision made by officials of the Ministry of Correctional Services regarding his eligibility for placement under correctional supervision as per section 276A(3)(a)(ii) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (hereafter “the CP Act”). The central matter in dispute was the interpretation of “date of release” as articulated in that section ([paras 1–3]).

The Minister of Correctional Services contended that “date of release” referred specifically to the date of expiration of the sentence, minus any legal remission, while the appellant posited that it included the earliest date on which a prisoner could be considered for parole. It was established that following amendments, the provisions of the Correctional Services Act 1959, which had been repealed by the Correctional Services Act 111 of 1998, still applied to the appellant’s case given the transitional provisions ([paras 4–9]).

The Case Management Committee (CMC) of the prison determined that Price's “date of release” extended beyond five years based on the expiration of his initial sentence. This conclusion was premised on the interpretation that "date of release" did not account for the eligibility date for parole, thereby denying him a recommendation for correctional supervision. Subsequently, Price was released on parole on 2 October 2006, which led him to argue that this release did not preclude his application for correctional supervision under the provisions of the CP Act ([paras 6–16]).

## I – Issues

The key legal issue at hand was the interpretation of the phrase "date of release" within section 276A(3)(a)(ii) of the CP Act and its implications for correctional supervision. Specifically, the court needed to determine:

1. What constitutes the "date of release" within the meaning of section 276A(3)(a)(ii) for prisoners, particularly relating to those under the Correctional Services Act 1959 ([paras 3, 14]).
2. Whether a prisoner released on parole retains the right to seek reconsideration of their sentence under this provision ([paras 15–17]).

## R – Rules

The relevant statutory provisions are primarily contained in the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, specifically section 276A, which outlines the conditions under which correctional supervision can be imposed. Additionally, section 63 of the Correctional Services Act 1959 sets out guidelines regarding the consideration for placement under community corrections ([paras 2, 4–5, 9]).

The interpretation of statutory language is guided by legal precedents, most notably the case of **Steenkamp v Commissioner of Correctional Services**, where a distinction was made regarding the timing and process involved in correctional supervision ([para 6]). The Supreme Court of Appeal also considered how prior decisions had interpreted these statutes and any consequent anomalies that might arise from different interpretations ([paras 10–12]).

## A – Application

In its deliberation, the court assessed the complex interplay between the definitions provided in the CP Act and the 1959 Act. It acknowledged that while the section's wording could suggest a strict interpretation regarding the expiration of the sentence, context provided through the 1959 Act clarified that the “date of release” referred to the earliest date on which a prisoner could be considered for parole or the expiration of their sentence, whichever occurred first. This interpretation aligns with the legislative intent and accounted for transitional provisions still relevant to the appellant ([paras 4–14]).

The court further critically evaluated the implications of steering a strict interpretation based solely on sentence expiration, recognizing that such an approach could lead to absurd outcomes. It asserted that redefining "date of release" to encompass both parole eligibility and sentence expiration ensured coherence within the legal framework governing correctional supervision ([para 12]).

Regarding Price's release on parole, the court concluded that he could not seek reconsideration of his sentence while on parole since he no longer met the definition of a prisoner as required by section 276A(3)(a)(ii). Despite this, the court emphasized the need to clarify the definition of "date of release" for future implications and upheld a clear rationale behind correctional supervision processes ([paras 15–17]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the phrase "date of release" in section 276A(3)(a)(ii) includes the earliest date for parole consideration or the expiration of the sentence, whichever occurs first. This interpretation overruled the decision in **Steenkamp** and clarified ambiguities relating to correctional supervision eligibility ([paras 14–18]).

The court mandated that the Minister of Correctional Services reimburse the appellant for his legal costs. Furthermore, it specified that while on parole, Price cannot seek reconsideration under the CP Act, reinforcing the distinction between prisoners and parolees in terms of statutory rights and processes ([para 18]).

Overall, this case underscores the importance of statutory interpretation and its implications for the rights of incarcerated individuals regarding correctional supervision, drawing vital distinctions in the context of parole and sentence reconsideration within South African law.
