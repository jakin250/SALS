# FIRAC Case Note: The Road Accident Fund v Wayne Coughlan NO (702/2013) [2014] ZASCA 106

## F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–2])

The case arises from a road collision where the mother of two children was negligently killed by a driver, causing a subsequent claim for damages for loss of support from the Road Accident Fund (RAF) by the children’s foster parent. The Western Cape High Court, presided over by Judge Henney, held that any foster child grants received as a consequence of the mother's death were "res inter alios acta," implying that they could not affect the children’s entitlement to full damages. The RAF had admitted 100% liability for the collision.

Prior to the mother's death, she had temporarily placed her children in the care of her parents due to a brief imprisonment. Upon the mother's death, her mother (the children's grandmother) sought and obtained foster parent status, qualifying for foster child grants under the Social Assistance Act. The agreed quantum of damages for loss of support was R112,942, while the foster child grants received totaled R146,790, prompting the RAF to argue that the grants should be deducted from the damages award as they represented double compensation.

## I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 6–7], [20])

The primary legal question was whether the foster child grants issued to the grandmother should be deducted from the damages awarded to the children for loss of support. The RAF contended that allowing both the grants and damages to stand would result in an unjust double compensation scenario. Conversely, the curator for the children argued that the grants were not compensation for losses sustained due to the accident but rather forms of government assistance provided independently of the death.

## R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 9–15], [22])

The court relied heavily on two precedent cases: *Makhuvela v Road Accident Fund* and *The Road Accident Fund v N F Timis*. In *Timis*, it was found that social assistance grants were linked directly to the death of a breadwinner and should be deducted from the total award for damages. The court established that the compensatory nature of delictual damages mandates that a plaintiff should not receive double compensation for the same loss (a principle stemming from cases such as *Zysset & others v Santam Ltd*).

The *Social Assistance Act 13 of 2004* was central to the deliberation, as its provisions emphasize the need for social security and appropriate social assistance for those unable to support themselves and their dependants. The court also differentiated between compensatory allowances and purely governmental assistance, primarily focusing on whether the grants were intended as compensation for loss or as a form of social assistance.

## A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 18–24])

The court found no substantive difference between the foster child grants and social assistance grants in terms of their purpose—to provide financial support for the children—and determined that this support was directly linked to the mother’s death. The RAF’s argument hinged on the contention that allowing both compensations would lead to financial injustice against taxpayers who fund the grants.

The court also considered the rationale from *Makhuvela*, recognizing the importance of legislative protection for children's rights. However, it ultimately reached a conclusion aligned with *Timis*, asserting that the grants fundamentally aimed to replace the financial support lost due to the mother's death.

In doing so, the court reiterated that the essential inquiry in such cases requires a balance between ensuring that dependants do not receive double compensation and holding wrongdoers accountable for their actions. It arrived at the conclusion that the foster child grants effectively served the same purpose as the damages and thus should be deducted from any compensation awarded.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 25])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the RAF's appeal, finding that the foster child grants should indeed be taken into account when assessing damages for loss of support, and consequently, that no further payment would be made as the grants exceeded the agreed amount of damages. The judgment clarifies that dependants are not permitted to claim double compensation for the loss of a breadwinner and sets a significant precedent regarding the treatment of social assistance grants in claims against the RAF.

This ruling effectively underscores the principle that both the nature of the compensation received and the specific needs created by the loss of the breadwinner must be carefully analyzed to ensure equity and justice in compensatory systems. The decision reinforces the commitment to sound public policy principles, indicating that courts should weigh the impact of social support structures against the financial responsibilities of wrongdoers.
