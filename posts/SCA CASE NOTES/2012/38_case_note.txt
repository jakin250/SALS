Case Note: SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note: Andrew Lionel Phillips v South African Reserve Bank & Others (221/11) [2012] ZASCA 38

## F - Facts
In January 2009, Andrew Lionel Phillips (the Appellant) instituted proceedings against the South African Reserve Bank (1st Respondent), the Minister of Finance (2nd Respondent), and the President of the Republic of South Africa (3rd Respondent) in the North Gauteng High Court, Pretoria, seeking to review and set aside the Reserve Bank's refusal to return certain foreign currency seized at Oliver Tambo International Airport on 10 February 2008. He also sought declarations that the Exchange Control Regulations promulgated in Government Notice R 1111 of 1 December 1962, as amended, were inconsistent with the Constitution and thus invalid ([1–2]). 

The High Court, on 1 June 2010, postponed the application sine die, ordering Phillips to pay the respondents’ wasted costs due to non-compliance with Rule 16A of the Uniform Rules of Court, which mandates that parties raising constitutional issues notify the registrar prior to filing their affidavits ([3–4]). The appellant's failure to assure compliance with this Rule formed the basis of the High Court's decision ([6–10]).

During the proceedings, a Rule 16A notice was presented later, which bore the registrar's stamp but had no case number. The judge noted that this filing did not prima facie meet the rule's requirements for notifying interested parties ([11–14]). The Full Bench of the High Court denied Phillips’s subsequent application for leave to appeal against the costs order ([19]). Eventually, the Supreme Court of Appeal granted Phillips leave to appeal regarding the costs order and admitted the affidavits from the leave-to-appeal proceedings as evidence ([22]).

## I - Issues
The appeal posed several critical issues:

1. Whether the findings and costs order by Makgoba J in the court below were appealable ([23a]).
2. Whether the Rule 16A notice complied with the requirements set out therein ([23b]).
3. Whether the responsibility for ensuring compliance with Rule 16A fell on Phillips or the registrar ([23c]).
4. The appropriateness of the respondents raising the Rule 16A non-compliance argument at a late stage ([23d]).
5. Whether the general principle regarding costs in constitutional cases applied to ancillary orders anywhere within the dispute ([23f]).

## R - Rules
Rule 16A of the Uniform Rules of Court mandates that any person raising a constitutional issue must give notice to the registrar upon filing the relevant affidavit or pleading. The notice must contain:

- A clear and succinct description of the constitutional issue ([4]).
- A stipulation that the notice be placed on a designated notice board for a minimum of 20 days ([4]).

The court has noted in previous decisions, particularly in *Shaik v Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development* 2004 (3) SA 599 (CC), that the purpose of such a rule is to ensure that those who may be affected or have a legitimate interest are notified of the particularities of the constitutional challenge ([8]). Additionally, the principles governing the appealability of orders hinge on whether the order affects rights substantially and if correcting the order would lead to justice without undue delay or expense ([25–27]).

## A - Application
Upon reviewing the appeal:

1. **Appealability**: The Court determined that despite the order being interlocutory, it was appealable because it could create significant inconvenience and prejudice related to constitutional rights ([28–29]).

2. **Compliance with Rule 16A**: Phillips’ notice was deemed acceptable in that it identified the statutory provisions undermined. The Court noted that while the exact grounds for questioning the provisions did not need to be outlined, identifying the implicated provisions sufficed under the Rule's purpose ([30–44]).

3. **Responsibility for the Notice**: The Court held that the duty to ensure the notice was appropriately filed on the notice board lay with the registrar, informing the judgment that the appellant had a reasonable expectation that the registrar’s office would perform its duties correctly ([45–50]). 

4. **Raising the Non-Compliance Argument**: The Court criticized the respondents for introducing the non-compliance argument late in the proceedings, which impeded the appellant's ability to rectify the situation, particularly since the issue was raised when it could not be remedied ([51–54]).

5. **General Rule on Costs**: The Court reaffirmed the general principle that an unsuccessful litigant should not be ordered to pay costs in constitutional matters unless exceptional circumstances exist. Such principles extend to ancillary points, indicating the High Court should not have ordered costs against Phillips ([56–60]).

## C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the initial order of the High Court regarding costs was incorrect and should be set aside. The appeal was upheld, directing the first and second respondents to bear the costs, including those consequent from the engagement of two counsel ([61]). The Court established critical implications for future constitutional litigation regarding compliance with Rule 16A, suggesting a proactive approach by both applicants and state organs to ensure constitutional challenges are properly ventilated without undue obstruction ([55]). 

The case underscores the necessity of clarity in filing constitutional challenges and highlights the unfair burden on individuals if procedural rules are manipulated or inadequately adhered to by state respondents. The judgment serves as a reminder that courts must facilitate, not hinder, the enforcement of constitutional rights.
