Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1996

## Case Note: FIRAC Method
### IN THE SUPREME COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA (APPELLATE DIVISION)

**Case No:** 70/95  
**Parties:** SA Metal & Machinery Co (Pty) Ltd v Cape Town Iron & Steel Works (Pty) Ltd & Others  
**Judgment Date:** 26 September 1996  
**Coram:** Hefer, Vivier, Eksteen, Howie, Scott JJA

---

### F - Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 2–22])

The appellant, SA Metal & Machinery Co (Pty) Ltd (hereinafter "the plaintiff"), initiated two separate actions for damages against Cape Town Iron & Steel Works (Pty) Ltd (the first respondent, hereinafter "the first defendant") in the Cape Provincial Division. The first action (case no 14421/92) involved the joining of the second and third respondents, National Metal (Pty) Ltd and Chicks Scrap Metals SA (Pty) Ltd, without seeking relief against them, which rendered them non-defensive parties. The second action (case no 4411/93) was solely against the first defendant. Both actions were consolidated for trial ([para 2]).

During the proceedings, the first defendant raised a special defence asserting that the contract in question constituted "horizontal price collusion" and "horizontal collusion on conditions of supply" — definitions provided in Government Notice no 801, published pursuant to Section 14(5)(a) of the Maintenance and Promotion of Competition Act 98 of 1979 (hereinafter "the Act") ([para 3]). The trial judge, Berman J, upheld this special defence, rendering the contract unenforceable and ordering the plaintiff to bear the costs related to the special defence. Following this decision, the plaintiff sought to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 4]).

The core of the plaintiff's case rested upon a written agreement regulating the supply of scrap metal, concluded during October 1989 among suppliers and consumers of scrap metal in Cape Town. The contract's implementation saw subsequent management by different parties and included specifications about pricing, categorization, and conditions concerning the supply agreements that would be adhered to by all parties ([paras 5–8]).

### I - Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 9–20])

The pivotal legal issue addressed by the Supreme Court was whether the agreement constituted unlawful collusion under the Competitive Act — specifically, if it indeed involved "horizontal price collusion" and "horizontal collusion on conditions of supply" as defined in the Notice ([paras 9–12]). Additional inquiries raised involved the interpretation of the contract's terms vis-à-vis the claim for damages alleged by the plaintiff due to the first defendant's actions (specifically underpayments and contract breaches) ([paras 9–14]).

Furthermore, the court considered whether the onus of proving the illegality rested with the first defendant and whether there was sufficient evidence to suggest that the agreement operated within an anti-competitive environment ([paras 12–18]).

### R - Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 21–25])

The court's obligations were primarily framed under the Maintenance and Promotion of Competition Act 98 of 1979, particularly the definitions outlined in Government Notice No 801 which declared various arrangements as unlawful ([para 20]). The definition of "horizontal price collusion" included agreements among suppliers concerning uniform pricing, while "horizontal collusion on conditions of supply" encompassed arrangements regarding terms that suppliers might enforce together ([paras 11–12]).

Case law, particularly **National Home Products (Pty) Ltd v Vynide Ltd** 1988 (1) SA 60 (W), was cited, asserting that agreements among suppliers operating on the same horizontal level in the supply chain were defined as collusive under the Act ([paras 13–14]). The consequences of establishing “restrictive practices” included not only unlawful conduct but also measures suppressing competition that could elevate or stabilize prices ([para 25]).

### A - Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 26–40])

The Court, upon reviewing the contractual terms and the nature of the arrangement among suppliers, ultimately found that the contract did fit the definitions of both "horizontal price collusion" and "horizontal collusion on conditions of supply". The Judgement made it clear that regardless of the intent behind the formation of the contract — being aimed at ensuring a steady supply of scrap for local consumption — the recourse established reciprocal obligations that were binding exclusively among suppliers, which constituted collusion ([paras 29–31]).

The court refuted the plaintiff's argument that the contract merely amounted to separate individual contracts as it recognized that the uniformity imposed by the collective contract constrained the competitive freedoms of the suppliers by ensuring fixed pricing structures ([para 36]). The factual findings, buttressed by the lack of provision for variations to individual agreements unless supported by collective agreement among all parties, further underscored this conclusion of collusion ([paras 38–39]).

The court differentiated between the contractual obligations of the suppliers towards each other and their obligations towards the consumers, noting that supplier-to-supplier agreements still inflected on permissible competitive practices ([paras 39–40]).

### C - Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 41–56])

In conclusion, the Supreme Court upheld the special defence, reaffirming that the contract was indeed unlawful as it substantially fit the definition of restrictive practices concerning the competitive norms prescribed by the Act ([paras 41–44]). The plaintiff's appeal was dismissed with costs, including expenses for two counsel, thereby solidifying the judicial interpretation of competitive contracts and underscoring the necessity for absolute clarity in supplier agreements to avoid falling foul of anti-competitive legislative frameworks.

This judgment sets a significant precedent regarding the enforcement of contracts in competitive industries, highlighting the essentiality of safeguarding competition to support broader market health ([para 56]). The implications of this decision resonate beyond this specific case, dealing with how commercial relations among suppliers should be structured and the preventive measures needed to ensure that such arrangements do not contravene competition law.
