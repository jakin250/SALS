Source Folder: 2001

# Case Note: Simon Makondo v The State

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

The appellant, Simon Makondo, was convicted in the regional court for attempted murder and sentenced to ten years' imprisonment ([1]). This initial conviction arose from an incident where a collision occurred between a vehicle driven by the complainant, a police detective, and a vehicle in which Makondo was a passenger ([2]). Following the minor collision, the complainant pursued the other vehicle, attempting to effect a stop by flashing his headlights. After the other vehicle halted approximately 500 meters away, the complainant exited his vehicle and approached it while armed, leading to an altercation where he was shot multiple times by the appellant ([3]-[4]).

Both the complainant and his girlfriend, Mrs. Joyce Makola, testified that the complainant had drawn his firearm and had been shot while walking away. In contrast, Makondo claimed self-defence, arguing he and the driver of the other vehicle, Mr. Mashinini, were shot at first, leading him to return fire in a panic ([5]-[6]). The trial court admitted photographs showing damage to the other vehicle due to gunfire, which contradicted Makondo's version of events and supported the complainant's ([7]).

The trial court ultimately found the evidence against Makondo to be compelling and convicted him of attempted murder after deductive reasoning negated the appellant's self-defence claim ([8]-[9]). On appeal, Makondo contested both the conviction and the severity of the sentence, claiming a misdirection during sentencing that overemphasized community expectations ([14]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issues in this case can be categorized as follows:

1. **Was the trial court justified in dismissing the appellant's claim of self-defence based on the evidence presented?** ([8])
2. **Did the sentencing court err by considering community expectations in imposing a ten-year prison term?** ([15]-[16])

### R – Rules

The legal standards applied by the courts in adjudicating this case include principles of criminal law concerning self-defence, which allows for the use of reasonable force to protect oneself from imminent harm. The court must evaluate whether the accused had a genuine belief that their life was in danger, and if the response was proportional ([9]). Additionally, sentencing guidelines require that courts exercise their discretion judiciously, considering factors like the seriousness of the offence, the interests of society, and the personal circumstances of the defendant ([14]-[15]).

In appeals regarding sentences, the appellate court typically does not interfere unless a clear misdirection is found, such as a failure to consider mitigating factors ([16]).

### A – Application

#### Conviction

The court scrutinized the evidence submitted, particularly the accounts of the complainant and Mrs. Makola, whose testimonies were consistent and corroborated by physical evidence—specifically, images of the bullet damage to the complainant's vehicle ([7][9]). The appellate court acknowledged the improbability of the appellant's version of events, particularly the notion that the complainant would shoot at the other vehicle from a place of disadvantage, illuminated by headlights ([11]).

Further analyses revealed discrepancies in the appellant's narrative that were critical to the conviction. Notably, the absence of a clear and categorical denial of shooting the complainant in the back, despite the complainant being visible, was a pivotal aspect of the court's conclusions ([10]). 

#### Sentencing

In discussing sentencing, the trial court indicated that societal expectations could influence judicial outcomes ([15]). The appellate court noted that while community expectations are relevant, they must not overshadow individual case specifics ([12][18]). The appellant's personal circumstances—a first offender with family obligations—served as mitigating factors; however, the gravity of attempting to murder a police officer warranted a custodial sentence ([16][17]).

Despite these mitigating circumstances, the appellate court identified a substantial disparity between the initial sentence and what it considered appropriate, suggesting that a portion of the sentence should be suspended to reflect the appellant's personal context while ensuring accountability for his actions ([18]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Makondo's appeal against conviction, affirming the trial court's conclusion that the evidence supported a finding beyond reasonable doubt of attempted murder ([19]). However, it upheld his appeal against the sentence, modifying it to ten years' imprisonment, with three years suspended for five years, conditioned on good behavior concerning violent offences. This nuanced approach underscores the court's commitment to balance justice for both the individual and societal norms while accounting for the unique circumstances of each case ([19]).

In essence, this case illustrates the critical application of self-defence standards in criminal law and the importance of appropriate sentencing practices that take into account a defendant's background alongside the nature of their crime. The appeal's outcome serves as a significant precedent in the continued discourse surrounding the limits of self-defence in South African law.
