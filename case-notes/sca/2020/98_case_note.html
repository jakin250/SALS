Supreme Court of Appeal

Leshilo v The State (345/2019) [2020] ZASCA 98

The appellant, Moshidi Danny Leshilo, was convicted in the regional court of Pretoria for housebreaking with intent to rob, along with two counts of unlawful possession of a firearm and ammunition ([1]) on 11 June 2014.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 98

F – Facts

The appellant, Moshidi Danny Leshilo, was convicted in the regional court of Pretoria for housebreaking with intent to rob, along with two counts of unlawful possession of a firearm and ammunition ([1]) on 11 June 2014. The convinction was primarily related to an incident occurring in the early hours of 28 August 2013, when the complainants, Mr. Mahlangu and Ms. Maraba, were accosted in their home by two intruders—one of whom was accused 2. The intruder pointed a firearm at Mr. Mahlangu, leading to a physical struggle over the weapon, during which a shot was discharged ([4]-[6]).

It was established that while the appellant entered the home after the first assailant and engaged in a struggle over the firearm, he was never in actual possession of it ([5], [6]). The regional court initially convicted the appellant on all counts, sentencing him to 15 years imprisonment. A co-accused was acquitted due to insufficient identification ([2]). Subsequently, the appellant was granted leave to appeal, and his conviction was upheld by the Gauteng High Court, which led to further appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([3]).

I – Issues

The appellate court was tasked with determining:

  1. Whether the appellant was in joint possession of the illegal firearm used in the crime ([1], [10]).
  2. Whether the principles of common purpose applied to the appellant’s case regarding possession charges ([10], [14]).
  3. The validity of the imposed sentence in light of the modified findings on conviction ([16]-[21]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal principles regarding joint possession and common purpose are well established:

  1. Joint Possession: As confirmed in S v Nkosi, joint possession requires proof beyond a reasonable doubt that the accused intended to exercise control over the firearm through the actual possessor and that the actual possessor intended to hold it for the group. Mere participation in a crime does not suffice for joint possession ([11]).
  2. Common Purpose: The doctrine of common purpose applies when an accused is present at the scene, aware of the crime, and shows some act of association with others committing the crime. However, it does not apply in possession cases ([10]). Previous rulings such as S v Mbuli, notably overruled earlier applications of common purpose relating to unlawful possession of firearms, solidifying that knowledge of possession alone is insufficient for a conviction ([12], [13]).
  3. Sentencing Principles: Sentences for multiple convictions should ideally address each offense separately, avoiding globular sentences due to their complexities on appeal ([16]).

A – Application

The court critically analyzed the evidence presented. It found significant gaps regarding whether the appellant ever possessed the firearm. Testimonies confirmed that while the appellant may have been aware the first assailant had a firearm, he did not physically possess it himself ([15]). The regional court had erred by applying the principles of joint possession erroneously, as evidence only indicated the co-perpetrator's possession, and there was no inference drawn that the appellant intended to hold the firearm jointly. This misapplication of the joint possession standard led the appellate court to conclude that the convictions concerning the possession of the firearm and ammunition could not stand ([15]).

Regarding sentencing, the court acknowledged the appellant's youth (20 years at the time of the crime), his status as a first offender, and time served in custody awaiting trial. The original sentence of 15 years was deemed excessive, especially considering that he was not in possession of the firearm ([18], [19]). Instead, a more appropriate sentence of 5 years was warranted for housebreaking, reflecting the circumstances while maintaining public interest ([20]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal regarding the convictions on counts 2 and 3, thereby setting them aside based on insufficient evidence for joint possession. The ratio decidendi established that knowledge and acquiescence do not equate to possession involving the prosecution’s burden of proving intent ([15]). Furthermore, the original 15-year sentence was reduced to 5 years for housebreaking, reflecting a balanced consideration of aggravating and mitigating factors ([21]). This case emphasizes the importance of clearly establishing possession and intent in criminal law and the nuanced application of sentencing practices in South Africa.

This case underlines the critical boundary regarding joint versus actual possession, shaping future interpretations of possession-related offenses within a group criminal context, ensuring that the fundamental principles of criminal liability remain justly applied in South African law.
