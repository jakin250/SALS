Supreme Court of Appeal

Bennett Madala Mkhatswa v Minister of Defence

Facts [1] The case arises from an incident on 25 May 1992, when the appellant, Bennett Madala Mkhatswa, was forcibly removed from his home in Tamboville by off-duty members of the South African Defence Force (“the Defence Force”).

Supreme Court of Appeal 1999

Facts

[1] The case arises from an incident on 25 May 1992, when the appellant, Bennett Madala Mkhatswa, was forcibly removed from his home in Tamboville by off-duty members of the South African Defence Force (“the Defence Force”). The appellant was subjected to a severe assault resulting in the loss of his right eye ([1]).

[2] In response to the incident, Mkhatswa initiated legal proceedings against the Minister of Defence, claiming damages of R186,050. The case proceeded in the Witwatersrand Local Division, where the trial court initially ruled in favor of Mkhatswa, holding the Defence Force liable for damages ([2]).

[3] The Defence Force appealed the ruling, and the High Court overturned the decision, dismissing the claim and awarding costs to the respondent. The matter was subsequently escalated to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([3]).

[4-9] At the time of the incident, 21 SA Infantry Battalion was stationed at Lenz military base. Following unrest in the region, certain areas, including Tamboville, had been declared unrest areas, prompting soldiers to conduct patrols in collaboration with the South African Police ([4]-[6]). D and A companies, comprising experienced soldiers, were assigned to this task and were stationed at Apex military base. Due to operational needs, their duties had been extended, prompting dissatisfaction among the troops ([7]-[9]).

[10-12] While returning from a shebeen in Wattville after consuming alcohol, members of D Company encountered locals and a conflict ensued. Subsequent to this confrontation, a soldier named Lawerlot retrieved a military truck and firearms, launching a revenge attack that included the assault on Mkhatswa ([10]-[12]).

[13-14] The judgment indicates deficiencies in command and control at Apex base, notably the deplorable condition of the perimeter fence that allowed soldiers to leave without passing through the gate ([13]-[14]). The orders and protocols at the base were found to be vague, contributing to misinterpretations regarding authorized leave ([15]-[16]).

Issues

[17-18] The key legal question revolves around whether the Minister of Defence can be held vicariously liable for: (1) the alleged wrongful omissions of the military personnel in charge of Apex base, and (2) the actions of the sentries who were supposed to prevent the unauthorized removal of vehicles and firearms. The principles governing vicarious liability require proof that the Defence Force was negligent in preventing foreseeable harm to Mkhatswa.

Rules

[19-22] The applicable principles concerning negligence and vicarious liability are founded on established precedents in South African law. Notably, the tests originate from Kruger v Coetzee [1966] 2 SA 428 (A), which delineates the circumstances under which negligence arises. It posits that for liability to exist, (a) a reasonable person in the defendant's position must foresee the possibility of injuring another and take steps to avert such occurrences, and (b) a failure to take those steps must be established ([20]).

The jurisprudence also highlights that the precise mechanism of harm need not be foreseeable; rather, the general manner in which such harm could occur should be anticipated ([22]).

Application

[23-26] The court assessed the specific facts surrounding the chain of events leading to the assault on Mkhatswa. The judges articulated that while it was foreseeable that members of D Company might leave the base in search of liquor—thereby increasing the chance of conflict with locals—this did not extend to foreseeability regarding a soldier’s unlawful appropriation of a vehicle and weapons for a revenge attack. The Defence Force’s failure to foresee such specific conduct from disciplined soldiers was deemed unreasonable and not within the realm of typical culpable conduct ([23]).

In the second prong regarding the actions of the sentries, the court evaluated whether they were negligent in allowing Lawerlot to remove the military vehicle and firearms. It emphasized that the sentries had no reason to believe the soldiers leaving the base were acting improperly. Thus, even if they violated standing orders by permitting the vehicle to exit without proper clearance, this did not equate to negligent conduct that would attribute liability ([26]).

Conclusion

[39-41] Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming that the appellant failed to establish negligence on the part of those in command of Apex base, along with the sentries tasked with guarding it. The court held that the actions of the soldiers who assaulted Mkhatswa were not within the course and scope of their employment, thereby precluding the possibility of vicarious liability. Furthermore, the ruling emphasized that while the circumstances were regrettable, legal principles dictate that liability cannot be imposed without established negligence ([39]).

In concluding remarks, the court noted the socially unfortunate nature of the outcome, implying a call for consideration of ex gratia payments by the Defence Force but refrained from expanding liability beyond what the law permits ([40]). Thus, the appeal was dismissed with costs.
