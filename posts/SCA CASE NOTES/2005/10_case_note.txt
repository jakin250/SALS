# FIRAC Case Note: Littlewood v Minister of Home Affairs

## F – Facts

The case of *Littlewood v Minister of Home Affairs* concerns the Littlewood family, British citizens who had resided in South Africa for over two years. Nicholas Littlewood discovered, while attempting to renew his passport towards the end of 2000, that the permanent residency permits for himself and his wife, Heather, were not authentic ([1]). This situation rendered them in violation of section 23 of the *Aliens Control Act 96 of 1991*, which prohibited their presence in South Africa without valid permits ([1]). The Littlewoods had established their lives in South Africa—severing ties with the UK, establishing a home, and settling their children into school ([2]).

Faced with the prospect of being deported, the Littlewoods sought an exemption from the provisions of section 23, which allowed the Minister of Home Affairs to exempt individuals from this requirement if “special circumstances” were established ([3], [4]). Their application for exemption was made in 2001 but was ultimately rejected by the Minister, following departmental advice, citing reasons that centered around the alleged fraudulent nature of their permits and the obligation to adhere to the law ([5], [14]).

The Littlewoods subsequently challenged the Minister's refusal in a Pretoria High Court, which dismissed their application ([5]). The High Court judged that the Minister acted lawfully and reasonably. The Littlewoods appealed this decision ([5]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues in this case relate to:

1. Whether the Minister of Home Affairs exercised his discretion appropriately in considering whether special circumstances existed for granting an exemption to the Littlewoods ([16]).
2. Whether the reasons provided by the Minister in denying the Littlewoods’ application were sufficient to uphold the decision ([15]).

## R – Rules

The court considered relevant provisions of the *Aliens Control Act 96 of 1991*, specifically section 23, which prohibits the presence of individuals without valid permits, and section 28(2), which empowers the Minister to grant exemptions under special circumstances ([3]). The standard for reviewing the Minister's decision included principles of administrative law, which require that decisions be lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair ([15]).

Several precedents were cited, including the principle that a court should only exceptionally substitute its judgment for that of an official executing a discretionary power ([18]), highlighting the necessity of a thorough factual assessment before reaching a conclusion about the exercise of discretion ([18]).

## A – Application

The court’s analysis focused on the Minister's failure to genuinely consider whether special circumstances were present ([16]). The Minister’s decision was primarily based on the existence of fraudulent permits, which the court found did not directly address the critical question of whether special circumstances warranted granting the exemption. The judgment emphasized that the mere fact of possessing a fraudulent permit does not negate the potential for special circumstances ([16], [17]).

Moreover, the court noted that the underlying explanation provided by the Littlewoods regarding their circumstances in South Africa had not been scrutinized, indicating a lack of proper discretionary exercise on the part of the Minister ([16]). The High Court's ruling was subsequently criticized as it appeared to endorse a decision-making process that did not engage with the substantive merits of the Littlewoods' case ([15]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the Minister had failed to apply his mind properly to the question of special circumstances, violating the criteria established for lawful administrative action. Consequently, the court set aside the previous decision and remitted the matter back to the Minister for reconsideration based on the findings that a legitimate inquiry into circumstances—however defined—had not occurred ([23]).

The court held that the dismissal of the application without significant consideration of the Littlewoods’ explanation constituted a failure to exercise discretion as required by the Act. Thus, the court ruled in favor of the appellants, a decision with important implications for similar future cases involving administrative discretion in immigration matters. The costs of the application were to be borne by the respondents, reflecting the court's stance on the procedural improprieties involved in this case ([23]).
