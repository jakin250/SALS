# Case Note: Yair Shimansky v Browns the Diamond Store (9/2014) [2014] ZASCA 214

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

The case revolves around a trademark dispute between Yair Shimansky (the first appellant) and My Light Holdings (the second appellant) against Browns the Diamond Store (the respondent). The first trademark, "EVOLYM," is registered by Shimansky and is used on various jewellery products, including engagement rings and wedding bands. The second trademark, "EVOLVE," is utilized by Browns specifically on men’s wedding bands. The marks in question fall within Class 14 of the Trade Marks Act 94 of 1993, which covers precious metals, jewellery, and similar goods, thus covering both parties' products ([1–2]).

At the heart of the matter is an urgent application filed by Shimansky in the Western Cape High Court seeking an interdict against Browns for alleged trademark infringement under section 34(1)(a) of the Trade Marks Act. In addition to the interdict, Shimansky sought the removal of the "EVOLVE" mark from Browns' products, its destruction, and a damages enquiry or reasonable royalty determination under section 34(1)(c) of the Act. The urgency of the application followed Shimansky's discovery of an advertisement for Browns' products in a magazine while on a flight ([3–5]).

The High Court, presided over by Savage AJ, dismissed Shimansky's application on grounds that he failed to prove that Browns' use of the "EVOLVE" mark constituted a trademark infringement as specified in section 34(1)(a) of the Act ([5]).

### I – Issues

The primary issue addressed by the court was whether the two trademarks — "EVOLYM" and "EVOLVE" — were so similar that their usage by Browns was likely to cause deception or confusion among consumers, thereby constituting an infringement of Shimansky's registered trademark. This analysis necessitated a comprehensive examination of the visual, aural, and conceptual similarities and differences between the two marks ([1]-[20]).

### R – Rules

The legal framework applicable to trademark infringement is predominantly guided by section 34(1) of the Trade Marks Act, which delineates the rights acquired through the registration of a trademark and stipulates the conditions under which infringement occurs. Specifically, section 34(1)(a) contends that infringement arises from unauthorized use in trade of either an identical mark or one that so closely resembles a registered mark that it is likely to deceive or confuse the public ([6]).

The court relied heavily on established case law to guide its analysis, including *Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd* and *National Brands Ltd v Blue Lion Manufacturing (Pty) Ltd* which articulate the principles governing non-complex comparisons of trademarks, emphasizing the need for a holistic evaluation of the marks in question. Further references include *Sabel BV v Puma AG Rudolf Dassler Sport*, which underscores the need for a global appreciation of marks based on the overall impression they create, with particular focus on distinctive and dominant elements ([7]–[20]).

### A – Application

The court undertook a detailed comparative analysis of the trademarks "EVOLYM" and "EVOLVE." It noted that while both marks share the initial segment "EVOL," the remaining letters diverge significantly in both appearance and pronunciation. According to the defense, the aural distinction is clear, and even if "EVOLYM" could sound similar to "EVOLVE," the potential confusion was mitigated by the stark visual differences between the two marks ([15–19]).

The court dismissed Shimansky's argument regarding the likelihood of confusion based on a substantial number of customers, determining that the standard required would not be met in this case. The reasoning was grounded in the notion that the average consumer, particularly in the context of purchasing engagement and wedding rings, would engage in careful consideration while selecting these items in a jewellery store. As such, the court opined that it was unlikely a consumer would mistake goods from Browns for those affiliated with Shimansky, especially given the marked differences in the trademarks ([22–23]).

Furthermore, the court analyzed the conceptual aspects of the trademarks and noted that the meanings associated with "EVOLYM" (implying "my love" reversed) and "EVOLVE" (suggesting growth or progression) were notably different, complicating claims of potential confusion ([20–21]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, upholding the decision of the High Court and affirming that Shimansky had not established the requisite elements for demonstrating infringement under section 34 of the Trade Marks Act. The critical finding was that consumers would not likely be confused between the two marks based on their visual, aural, and conceptual characteristics. This case serves as a significant reference point in South African trademark law, particularly with regard to evaluating the likelihood of confusion among consumers, emphasizing the importance of holistic mark assessments and the diligence exercised by consumers in making purchasing decisions.

The appeal was therefore dismissed with costs, including those of two counsel, reinforcing the legal principle that trademark owners must provide compelling evidence of confusion to succeed in infringement claims ([24]).
