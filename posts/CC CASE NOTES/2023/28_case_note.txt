Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: Rand Refinery (Pty) Ltd v Sehunane N.O. and Others [2023] ZACC 28

## F – Facts
The case arises from an application for leave to appeal a judgment made by the Labour Court in Johannesburg, which set aside an arbitration award favoring Rand Refinery (Pty) Ltd. The dispute began when Wanda Maseko, an employee of Rand Refinery, was dismissed following alleged misconduct related to the theft of gold bars. Maseko challenged his unfair dismissal through arbitration at the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA), where the arbitrator found his dismissal fair ([1]–[2]). 

Maseko, with the support of the National Union of Metalworkers of South Africa (NUMSA), initiated a review application in March 2018, claiming that the arbitration award was improperly obtained because the arbitrator had relied on hearsay evidence and failed to follow proper procedures. In September 2021, prior to the hearing of the review application, Maseko sought to introduce new evidence from a separate High Court matter involving a former Rand Refinery employee, Phumudzo Sydney Mulafhi, who alleged that he had been coerced into providing false testimony during the arbitration ([3]–[5]).

The Labour Court ultimately ruled in Maseko's favor, concluding that Rand Refinery had failed to file an opposing affidavit that could respond to the serious allegations raised by Mulafhi, leading to a finding of gross irregularity and the setting aside of the original arbitration award ([9]–[10]).

## I – Issues
The principal issue before the Constitutional Court was whether the Labour Court had violated Rand Refinery's fundamental right of access to courts under section 34 of the Bill of Rights by failing to consider its opposing affidavit. Additionally, issues arose regarding the proper application and interpretation of section 145(2)(b) of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 concerning whether an arbitration award can be set aside for being “improperly obtained” ([19]).

## R – Rules
The legal framework relevant to this case includes:
1. **Section 34 of the Bill of Rights**: Provides the right to have disputes resolved by a court or tribunal in a fair public hearing.
2. **Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA)**: Particularly, section 145 outlines the grounds upon which an arbitration award can be reviewed. Section 145(2)(b) specifically states an award may be set aside if it has been improperly obtained ([1]–[2]).
3. **Case law**: The relevance of previous judgments, particularly *Mkhize v Department of Correctional Services*, was highlighted concerning the handling of witness credibility and evidence in arbitration ([26]–[29]).

## A – Application
The Court found that Rand Refinery had, in fact, filed its opposing affidavit prior to the Labour Court hearing, and the Labour Court had erroneously concluded that Rand Refinery had not provided a response ([19]–[21]). The Court held that disregarding an opposing affidavit constituted a violation of Rand Refinery’s rights under section 34 of the Bill of Rights, particularly the right to a fair hearing.

Moreover, the Constitutional Court critically evaluated the Labour Court's reliance on hearsay evidence and pointed out the procedural flaws in both the Labour Court and the initial arbitration process, emphasizing that the Labour Court's approach undermined the statutory requirement that any claimed "improper obtaining" of an award must be established on a balance of probabilities ([27]–[30]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court held that the Labour Court's order setting aside the arbitration award was invalid, as it failed to correctly engage with the evidence presented by Rand Refinery and did not properly apply the standards defined in section 145(2)(b) of the LRA. The Court's finding reinforced the necessity for a fair hearing that includes considering all relevant evidence, thereby remitting the case back to the Labour Court for a proper review of the applications ([34]). 

The implications of this judgment emphasize the importance of procedural fairness and the need for courts to meticulously engage with the evidence before them, particularly in labor disputes where an employee’s livelihood is at stake. The order granted leave to appeal, upheld Rand Refinery's position, and mandated the Labour Court to re-examine the review application comprehensively.
