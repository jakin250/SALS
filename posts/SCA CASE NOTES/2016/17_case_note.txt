Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# Detailed Case Note: The Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development v The Southern African Litigation Centre (867/15) [2016] ZASCA 17

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The Supreme Court of Appeal considered a case arising from the South African government's failure to arrest Sudanese President Omar al-Bashir during his visit to South Africa in June 2015, despite outstanding arrest warrants issued by the International Criminal Court (ICC) concerning serious international crimes (including genocide and crimes against humanity) ([1] – [4]). The Southern African Litigation Centre (SALC) brought an urgent application against the Minister of Justice and the Minister of Police, among others, arguing that this inaction was unconstitutional as it breached South Africa's obligations under the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court and associated domestic legislation ([4] – [5]). 

The High Court initially ordered that al-Bashir not leave the country until a final decision was made. However, by the time the matter was heard, al-Bashir had departed despite the court's order, leading to the High Court deeming the matter moot and refusing to grant leave to appeal ([8] – [9]). The SALC then petitioned the Supreme Court of Appeal, which prepared to resolve the legal questions raised ([8], [10]).

### I – Issues
1. **Mootness**: Did the departure of President al-Bashir render the issues moot? 
2. **Leave to Appeal**: Should the appeals be granted despite the High Court's findings?
3. **Immunity Claims**: Did the hosting agreement and the subsequent ministerial proclamation afford al-Bashir immunity from arrest under South African law and customary international law?
4. **Implementation Act Interpretation**: Do sections 4(2) and 10(9) of the Implementation Act preclude claims of immunity when complying with ICC arrest warrants?
5. **Costs**: What costs orders should be made concerning the appeal and the respective parties?

### R – Rules
The court relied on several legal authorities:
- **Rome Statute**: Establishing South Africa’s obligation under international law to cooperate with the ICC, particularly articles concerning the immunity of heads of state ([1], [54]).
- **Implementation of the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court Act 27 of 2002**: Sections 4(2) and 10(9) delineate obligations for arresting and surrendering individuals in line with ICC arrest warrants, overriding preceding statutes regarding immunity ([86] – [103]).
- **Customary International Law**: Engaged primarily concerning head of state immunity and its exceptions, particularly in the context of international crimes ([66] – [84]).
- **DIPA (Diplomatic Immunities and Privileges Act 37 of 2001)**: Section 4(1)(a) provides immunity to heads of state, a point contrasted against the Implementation Act’s provisions which negate this immunity when international crimes are concerned ([116]).

### A – Application
**Mootness**: The Supreme Court of Appeal determined that the issues were not moot even though al-Bashir had left South Africa, as the legal questions at hand had implications for future dealings with heads of state and South Africa’s obligations under international law ([19] – [21]).

**Leave to Appeal**: The Court granted leave to appeal, emphasizing the significance of resolving questions of public importance pertaining to international obligations and the application of domestic law concerning international crimes ([22] – [24]).

**Immunity**: The Government's argument that al-Bashir enjoyed immunity based on a hosting agreement and a ministerial proclamation was rejected. The Court held that while customary international law generally provides for immunity, the provisions of the Implementation Act explicitly negate any such immunity in the context of obligations to arrest individuals sought by the ICC ([49] – [105]).

**Implementation Act Interpretation**: The Court concluded that the Implementation Act intended to give effect to the Rome Statute, thereby emphasizing that no form of immunity could serve as a valid defense against arrest for international crimes. The ruling underscored that South Africa's obligations under international law took precedence, establishing a framework where immunity does not shield perpetrators of international crimes from legal accountability ([86] – [103]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately varied the High Court’s order and reinforced the notion that South Africa must fulfill its obligations under the Rome Statute. The Court held that the failure of the Government to arrest President al-Bashir was inconsistent with these obligations and thus unlawful. The applicants were ordered to bear the costs, which included the costs for the SALC’s legal team ([4]–[5], [107]). The judgment clarified the interplay between local and international law regarding the prosecution of international crimes, particularly those crimes perceived as affecting the global community on a moral and legal basis ([112] – [113]). 

This case serves as a critical landmark in constitutional law, international obligations, and the extent of state immunity in instances involving serious accusations of human rights violations.
