# FIRAC Case Note: PCL Consulting (Pty) Ltd v Tresso Trading 119 (Pty) Ltd [2007] SCA 9 (RSA)

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–2])

In this case, PCL Consulting (Pty) Ltd (the appellant) engaged in a legal dispute with Tresso Trading 119 (Pty) Ltd (the respondent) concerning payment due under a lease agreement. The respondent initiated proceedings in the Johannesburg High Court, seeking payment of R396,188.35, which it claimed was owed as per the written lease agreement. The appellant entered an appearance to defend, after which the respondent applied for summary judgment under the Uniform Rule 32. The High Court granted summary judgment, which included the claim amount, interest, and costs, prompting the appellant to appeal this decision. The full court upheld the initial ruling, leading to special leave being granted for further appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA).

The underlying factual dispute arose from the claim by the respondent that there had been a common error in the written lease, which misidentified the premises as "office, 6th floor, Fedsure Towers" instead of the intended "office, 4th floor, Fedsure Towers." Notably, during summary judgment proceedings, the appellant did not contest the merits of the claim for rectification but argued that such a claim was not acceptable under Rule 32.

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 2–4])

The primary legal issue at stake was whether the Supreme Court of Appeal could grant summary judgment for a claim based on a rectified lease when the terms of that lease, particularly the accurate identification of the premises, were not in dispute. Specifically, the court needed to determine whether rectification of a contract is a necessary precursor to resolving issues related to a claim for arrears owed under that contract, and whether the appellant had presented a bona fide defense to the claim.

Additionally, the court had to consider whether the presence of an arbitration agreement within the lease precluded civil proceedings in the absence of a disputed claim.

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 3–4, 6–9])

The applicable rules primarily stem from the Uniform Rules of Court, particularly Rule 32, which governs summary judgment. This rule specifies that a plaintiff may obtain summary judgment when the claim is based on a liquid document, encompasses a liquidated amount, or seeks delivery of specified property.

Further, case law such as *Malcomess Scania (Pty) Ltd v Vermaak* was referenced. The court agreed with aspects of this judgment in relation to the confines of claims for rectification while expressly disagreeing with its characterization of summary judgment as incompetent in circumstances where the parties agree on the misdescription of the contract.

The SCA also relied on the *Arbitration Act 42 of 1965* to clarify the legal framework regarding arbitration clauses, emphasizing that a lack of a genuine dispute precludes recourse to arbitration.

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 5–9])

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the essence of the claim was for payment of arrears based on the alleged rectified agreement. The rectification was necessary for the plaintiff to adequately present its case, but it was not part of the core claim itself. The court noted that there was no dispute regarding the actual terms of the agreement or the identity of the premises let.

It was further determined that the appellant's response to the summary judgment application failed to adequately disclose any bona fide defense or genuine dispute. The assertion of an arbitration agreement was insufficient as it did not align with the requirement of evidential disputes necessary for arbitration. The court noted that mere non-payment does not create a litigable dispute regarding liability.

The court underscored that the appellant’s failure to articulate a substantial dispute warranted the continuation of summary judgment proceedings. The absence of a bona fide defense directly informed the court's conclusion that the orders made by the courts of first instance and the full court were correct.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 10])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the orders of the lower courts. The decision reinforced the principle that when a party is seeking summary judgment based on a rectified claim that is not fundamentally disputed, such judgment can be appropriately granted. The court established that a misunderstanding of the contract terms requiring rectification does not bar summary judgment, provided that both parties agree on the core issues concerning liability.

This ruling carries significant implications for how claims involving disputed contract terms are handled moving forward, particularly underscoring the clarity needed in disputes regarding arbitration clauses versus undisputed claims for payment. The judgment clarifies the threshold for disputes to exist and emphasizes the need for parties to articulate bona fide defenses in proceedings under Rule 32.
