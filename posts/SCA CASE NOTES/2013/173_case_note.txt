Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note

## Case Title: Boschpoort Ondernemings (Pty) Ltd v Absa Bank Ltd  
**Neutral Citation:** Boschpoort Ondernemings (Pty) Ltd v Absa Bank Ltd (936/12) [2013] ZASCA 173 (28 November 2013)  
**Coram:** Cachalia, Petse, Willis JJA and Swain, Meyer AJJA  
**Heard:** 15 November 2013  
**Delivered:** 28 November 2013  

## F – Facts

This case arose from an appeal against an order of liquidation granted by the North Gauteng High Court, presided over by Bertelsmann J, on 15 June 2012 ([paras 2-3]). The Respondent, Absa Bank Limited, sought the winding-up of the Appellant, Boschpoort Ondernemings (Pty) Ltd, under Section 344(f) read with Section 345 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973 (the old Act) and alternatively under Section 81(1)(c)(ii) of the Companies Act 71 of 2008 (the new Act). The high court deemed it just and equitable to grant the liquidation order under the new Act ([para 2]).

At the time the application for liquidation was made, Boschpoort was in arrears concerning its debts to Absa amounting to over R29 million, with additional trade creditors owed in excess of R11 million, First Rand Bank Ltd owed approximately R9 million, and R2 million due to the South African Revenue Service ([paras 3–4]). The appellant acknowledged that it was commercially insolvent but argued that it was solvent per the technical definition of the new Act because its assets exceeded its liabilities ([paras 6–8]). 

## I – Issues

The principal legal issue for the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether the winding-up of the Appellant should be governed by the old Act or the new Act, specifically whether the criteria for declaring a company commercially insolvent applied. The Court had to determine the interpretation of 'solvent company' as per the new Act in the context of ongoing insolvency disputes ([para 2]).

Additionally, the case examined whether the court's decision to liquidate based on the new Act was appropriate given that the appellant was commercially insolvent ([paras 18–24]). 

## R – Rules

The relevant statutory provisions include:

1. **Companies Act 61 of 1973**:
   - **Section 344(f)**: Allows for court-ordered winding-up if the company is unable to pay its debts ([para 10]).
   - **Section 345**: Defines inability to pay debts and sets forth the relevant conditions for corporate insolvency ([para 10]).

2. **Companies Act 71 of 2008**:
   - **Section 81(1)(c)(ii)**: Provides that a company may be wound up if it is just and equitable to do so, allowing a broader interpretation compared to the old Act ([para 12]).
   - **Part G, Section 79**: Governs the winding-up of solvent companies and establishes that a corporate entity deemed 'solvent' cannot be wound up under certain provisions of the old Act ([para 11–13]).

3. **Judicial Precedents**:
   - In prior cases, including Johnson v Hirotec (Pty) Ltd, the distinction between 'factual insolvency' and 'commercial insolvency' has been crucial in determining the criteria for liquidation ([para 16]).

## A – Application

The SCA found that the appeal against the winding-up order should be dismissed despite the initial high court's reliance on Section 81 of the new Act. The SCA concluded that while the appellant's balance sheet indicated that its assets exceeded its liabilities—thus suggesting factual solvency—it was indeed commercially insolvent as it could not pay its debts ([paras 18–24]). 

The Court emphasized that judicial interpretations established prior to the new Act's enactment must be preserved unless explicitly altered by new statutory language. Given that the old Act’s definitions remained relevant, particularly regarding the inability to meet financial obligations, the winding-up parameters defined therein were again applicable despite claims to factual solvency ([paras 22–24]). 

Thus, the SCA reversed the high court's rationale by asserting that commercial insolvency justified liquidation under the old Act—the Court's reasoning underscores the importance of a business's practical ability to satisfy its debts rather than merely adhering to asset-liability calculations ([para 26]). 

## C – Conclusion

In concluding, the SCA upheld the decision of the high court to order liquidation due to the Appellant's inability to pay its debts, although establishment of the basis of the order under the old Act was incorrectly decided by the lower court. The recognition that a company could be deemed commercially insolvent despite being factually solvent supports a more nuanced understanding of corporate financial health. This case reinforces the legal principles regarding insolvency and indicates the necessity for clarity in legislative definitions surrounding solvency to avoid potential complications in future liquidation proceedings ([paras 26–28]). The Court affirmed that the confusion regarding definitions will not serve as a barrier for liquidators when pursuing justice in insolvent cases ([para 23]).

This judgment signifies a pivotal moment in understanding the operational landscape of corporate insolvency, highlighting the necessity for businesses to maintain not only a favorable asset balance but also to ensure liquid cash flow to meet obligations consistently. The importance of the appellant’s commercial insolvency delineates a potentially critical legal precedent for how companies should approach concerns of solvency going forward. The SCA's insistence that factual solvency is not a definitive shield against liquidation may well direct future judicial interpretations and legislative clarity surrounding insolvency matters ([para 20–26]).
