Country Cloud Trading CC v MEC, Department of Infrastructure Development, Gauteng [2014] ZACC 28

Constitutional Court 2014 [2014] ZACC 28

FIRAC Method Case Note

F – Facts

[1] This appeal stems from a delictual claim for pure economic loss. The appellant, Country Cloud Trading CC, brought forth the appeal against the respondent, the Member of the Executive Council (MEC) for the Gauteng Department of Infrastructure Development, following a judgment by the Supreme Court of Appeal, which dismissed an appeal against a decision from the South Gauteng High Court. The underlying dispute originated from a building contract awarded in May 2006 to a construction company, iLima Projects (Pty) Ltd, to construct a district hospital in Soweto.

[2] In March 2008, iLima’s three partners abandoned the project, leaving Mr. Buthelezi, the then Head of Department, to find a suitable contractor to complete the project. Despite a recommendation from the Departmental Acquisition Council (DAC) for a competitive tender process, Mr. Buthelezi decided to award the contract to iLima, based on the urgency of service delivery.

[3] Subsequently, the Department agreed to provide iLima with a R21.5 million remobilisation payment, necessary for completing the project. Country Cloud advanced R12 million to iLima, under conditions that included reliance on the remobilisation payment. However, due to media pressure regarding the Department's procurement procedures, Mr. Buthelezi cancelled the completion contract with iLima, claiming false representations concerning iLima’s tax clearance certificate and building accreditation, resulting in a total loss of R12 million to Country Cloud.

[4] The High Court held that Mr. Buthelezi had no authority to deviate from the tender process, rendering the completion contract void ab initio. The Supreme Court of Appeal, however, ruled Mr. Buthelezi had that authority, but ultimately dismissed Country Cloud's appeal on grounds of delictual wrongfulness.

I – Issues

[19] The core issue before the Constitutional Court was whether the Department should be held delictually liable for Country Cloud’s economic loss following the cancellation of the completion contract. More specifically, the inquiry focused on whether the Department’s conduct in cancelling the contract was wrongful vis-à-vis Country Cloud.

R – Rules

[20] The court's consideration of wrongfulness in delictual liability hinged on established principles regarding pure economic loss. The law generally distinguishes between claims for pure economic loss and those for physical harm, with the former requiring a more stringent demonstration of wrongfulness. This includes whether public policy recognizes the conduct as acceptable.

[22] The premise of wrongfulness was elaborated upon, noting that intentional interference with contractual relations constitutes a recognized category in which wrongfulness may be established if a party intentionally induces a breach of contract by a third party, impacting the plaintiff's rights.

A – Application

[26] The court conducted a thorough examination of the specifics surrounding the claim of intentional interference. Country Cloud argued that its claim was an extension of established principles pertaining to this recognized category, positing Mr. Buthelezi’s actions constituted intentional interference with its contractual arrangement with iLima.

[30] The court countered that while the Department's conduct could be seen as resulting in Country Cloud incurring loss, it did not meet the legal threshold for wrongful action as it did not actively induce iLima’s breach of contract with Country Cloud.

[32] Further, the court assessed public policy considerations, noting that allowing such liability in this case could lead to indeterminate liability for the state, aligning public policy with protecting governmental functions from overarching liability claims arising from contractual relationships.

[51] The question of vulnerability was also centered in the court's determination. The appellant failed to establish that it was particularly vulnerable to risk, given that it had contractual safeguards and could have pursued remedies directly against iLima or its surety.

C – Conclusion

[67] Ultimately, the court dismissed the appeal, concluding that Country Cloud had not sufficiently demonstrated that the Department’s conduct was wrongful under the established principles of delict. The court underscored the importance of maintaining a clear boundary in liability, particularly in cases involving public administration, to avoid potential overreach into the systemic workings of government and to respect existing contractual frameworks. Additionally, the court's ruling reinforced that economic loss claims, particularly those framed within complex contractual arrangements, must adhere strictly to principles of clear liability and established wrongdoing.

[69] The final order of the court was that the appeal was dismissed with no order as to costs, marking a significant ruling on the issues of state accountability, wrongfulness in delict, and the interplay between public policy and private contractual relations.
