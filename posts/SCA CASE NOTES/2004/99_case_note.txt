# Case Note: N.K. v The Minister of Safety and Security (2004) SCA 456/03

**FIRAC Method**

## F - Facts
[1] The appellant, a young woman in her early twenties, was sexually assaulted by three policemen in the early hours of 27 March 1999. After a disagreement with her male companion at an entertainment venue, she sought assistance at a petrol station to call her mother for a ride home. However, when the police arrived at the scene, one of the officers offered her a lift, which she accepted. Once in the vehicle, the situation escalated dramatically. The policemen deviated from their duty, abducted her, and subsequently raped her. Each of the three individuals was later convicted and sentenced to life imprisonment for rape, along with an additional ten years for kidnapping. The appellant abandoned her claim against the individual policemen and proceeded to sue the Minister of Safety and Security for damages based on vicarious liability for the actions of the officers ([1]-[2]).

## I - Issues
[3] The central legal question was whether the Minister of Safety and Security could be held vicariously liable for the criminal conduct of the three policemen, specifically whether their actions of rape fell within the course and scope of their employment ([1]-[4]). The appellant argued that because the officers were on duty while offering her a lift and, even in their deviation from this duty, their actions still implicated the state’s liability.

## R - Rules
[4] The legal principles governing vicarious liability indicate that an employer is liable for the delictual acts committed by an employee if they occur during the course and scope of employment. The determination often rests upon the “standard test,” which assesses whether the employee was performing duties or acting under employer instructions at the time of the commission of the act. The test functions to balance the necessity of holding employers accountable for their employees while avoiding unjust imposition of liability for wrongful acts committed outside the realm of employment ([4]-[6]).

### Precedents
- In **Feldman (Pty) Ltd v Mall** [1945] AD 733 at 774, it was established that deviation from duty must be assessed to determine if the employee was still executing the functions for which they were employed.
- **Minister van Polisie v Ewels** [1975] (3) SA 590 (A) was referenced concerning the duty of policemen to intervene in wrongful acts, although its applicability was found limited in this case.
- Previous cases like **Carmichele v Minister of Safety and Security** [2001] (4) SA 938 (CC) reiterated the distinction in liability standards when considering wrongful acts committed by police officers.

## A - Application
[5] The court acknowledged that a concession was made by the appellant's counsel that the actions constituting the rape did not meet the standard test for vicarious liability under these circumstances ([5]-[6]). It was agreed that the crime of rape was not performed in the pursuit of police duties but rather for personal gratification, representing a significant deviation from lawful duties of a police officer. 

[7] Counsel argued that the police officers' initial act of picking the appellant up for safety established a duty from which deviation occurred. However, the court found no legal precedent supporting the notion that mere deviation from a duty, without the act being connected to the performance of their duties, could impose vicarious liability on the respondent.

[8] A critical argument relied upon by the appellant was that during the assault, all policemen retained a duty to prevent crime. However, the court outlined that the actions undertaken during the rape—aggregation of criminal conduct—essentially removed the officers from acting within the course of their employment, thus negating the possibility of vicarious liability.

[9] The court also declined to entertain broader calls for the development of common law to impose strict liability on the State for actions of its officials conducted in clear violation of law. The precedent set in **Phoebus Apollo** confirmed the traditional stance—that vicarious liability requires adherence to the established principles of conduct in relation to the duties expected from an employee ([9]-[10]).

## C - Conclusion
[10] The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming that the Minister of Safety and Security was not vicariously liable for the actions of the policemen, as their conduct fell significantly outside the requisite duties of their employment. The judgment emphasized that while the court recognized the tragedy of the appellant's experience, it was up to the legislative authority to address compensation related to crimes of this nature, not the judiciary. The ruling reinforced the principle that for employers to be held liable, the employee’s actions must occur within the scope of their professional duties, a standard not met in this case ([10]-[11]). 

Ultimately, this case underscores the delicate balance between protecting individual rights against state actions and upholding the principles of vicarious liability as dictated by established precedents and legal standards in South Africa.
