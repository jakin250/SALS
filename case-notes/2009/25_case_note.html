Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development v Mqabukeni Chonco and 383 Others [2009] ZACC 25

The case revolves around Mqabukeni Chonco, who in 1989 was convicted of murder, robbery, unlawful possession of a firearm, and attempted murder, with a death sentence that was later commuted to life imprisonment following the abolition of the death penalty in South Africa ([1]).

Constitutional Court 2009 [2009] ZACC 25

F – Facts

The case revolves around Mqabukeni Chonco, who in 1989 was convicted of murder, robbery, unlawful possession of a firearm, and attempted murder, with a death sentence that was later commuted to life imprisonment following the abolition of the death penalty in South Africa ([1]). The context of his conviction involved political violence during the 1980s, where Chonco, a member of the Inkatha Freedom Party (IFP), claimed his actions were politically motivated ([2]).

In 2003, Chonco filed for a presidential pardon under section 84(2)(j) of the South African Constitution, part of a larger group of 384 prisoners seeking pardons for crimes committed in the politically violent context of that era, but notably did not apply for amnesty through the Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) process, which ended in 2003 ([3]–[4]). The Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development, as the applicant, failed to process these pardon applications for an extended period, prompting Chonco to initiate legal proceedings in the North Gauteng High Court arguing a breach of constitutional duty by the Minister ([5]–[6]).

The High Court ruled in favor of Chonco, stating that the Minister had a constitutional obligation to process the applications ([10]). The Minister appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal, arguing there was no obligation on her part to do so ([11]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions addressed in this case are as follows:

  1. Does the Minister for Justice have a constitutional obligation to process applications for presidential pardons, which are traditionally an executive function of the President?
  2. Is the Minister's failure to act subject to administrative review under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA)?
  3. Is the matter appropriately before the Constitutional Court, particularly regarding the President's role in the pardon process?

These issues arise from an interpretation of sections 84(2)(j) and 85(2)(e) of the Constitution, addressing the scope and nature of powers held by the President and the national executive ([14]–[16]).

R – Rules

The case relies on several key legal authorities:

  1. Constitution of South Africa - Sections 84(2)(j) and 85(2)(e) outline the powers of the President and the national executive. Section 84(2)(j) specifically addresses the President's power to pardon, while section 85(2)(e) pertains to the executive authority that includes performance of functions provided in the Constitution or legislation.
  2. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) - Under section 6(2)(g), it allows for the judicial review of administrative action, including failures to take decisions by state organs ([9]).
  3. Precedent - The court referenced its prior rulings on the role of the President and the national executive, including cases like S v Makwanyane ([1]) and Pharmaceutical Manufacturers Association of South Africa ([27]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court determined that:

  1. Obligations of the Minister: The Minister has a constitutional obligation to process pardon applications, as the President’s decision relies on preliminary steps generally performed by the Minister. Despite the President holding the exclusive final power to pardon, the Minister's inaction constituted a failure to fulfill a constitutional obligation as mandated by section 237 of the Constitution ([9]).
  2. Administrative Action: The court found that the Minister's failure to process the applications could indeed be reviewed under PAJA. However, a nuanced understanding emerged concerning the nature of the Minister's duties vis-à-vis the President's powers. It was determined that the President alone is responsible for the ultimate decision of a pardon. Hence, while the Minister's failure constituted poor governance, only the President can be held accountable for the decision-making regarding pardons ([40]–[42]).
  3. Jurisdictional Issues: The Minister’s appeal had initially raised questions about the jurisdiction of the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal in regard to the President's action or inaction. The court held that only the Constitutional Court had jurisdiction to determine presidential obligations under section 167(4)(e) of the Constitution, thus complicating the appeal's legitimacy without proper joinder of the President ([43]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court held that while the appeal by the Minister was upheld, Chonco's case against her was misplaced since the proper party (the President) was not effectively targeted in the proceedings. The Court affirmed the necessity for all constitutional obligations to be performed without delay and lamented the unacceptable passage of time without consideration of Chonco's application. This ruling implies a need for both the Minister and the President to fulfill their respective roles diligently, with the potential for scrutiny of Ministerial actions in future applications involving executive responsibilities concerning pardons ([49]).

  1. The application for leave to appeal was granted.
  2. The appeal was upheld, setting aside the Supreme Court of Appeal's order.
  3. The substantive issue related to Chonco's failure to name the correct party in his original proceedings, effectively nullifying his quest for relief against the wrongful party ([49]).

This case underscores the intricate balance of powers within South Africa's constitutional framework, particularly the delineation and fulfillment of executive functions.
