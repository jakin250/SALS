Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# FIRAC Case Note

### Case Title: Nwafor v The Minister of Home Affairs and Others (1363/2019) [2021] ZASCA 58 (12 May 2021)

---

### F – Facts

The applicant, Anthony Okey Nwafor, appealed for leave to appeal against a judgment by the High Court of Gauteng, which dismissed his application to review the decision made by the Minister of Home Affairs and the Director-General of the Department of Home Affairs (the respondents) regarding the deprivation of his and his minor children's South African citizenship ([paras 1–2]). 

Nwafor was born in Nigeria and acquired South African citizenship in October 2009 following the issuance of a naturalization certificate ([paras 4–5]). His citizenship was later called into question by the Department of Home Affairs when it was argued that he had acquired a permanent residence permit through false representation regarding his marital status ([paras 6–7]). Specifically, the Minister contended that Nwafor concealed his marriage to Ms. Amarachukwu Ebare Nwafor while entering into a new marriage with Ms. Gladys Vilankulo, who was allegedly a minor at the time of their marriage ([para 6]).

After receiving notification of the potential deprivation of citizenship, Nwafor submitted representations defending his case but was subsequently informed that his citizenship would be revoked due to alleged fraudulent activity in securing his residence permit ([paras 9–10]). The court a quo ultimately dismissed Nwafor's application, stating that the Minister had the proper authority to delegate the power to effect such a decision, and the allegations against him regarding fraud were substantiated ([paras 19–20]).

### I – Issues

The central issue in the appeal was whether leave to appeal should be granted on the ground that there were reasonable prospects of success concerning the prior ruling ([paras 26–29]). Specifically, Nwafor raised several points of law, including:

1. The legitimacy of the delegation of authority by the Minister to the Director-General, which was alleged to have violated procedural requirements.
2. The collective deprivation of citizenship of Nwafor’s wife and children, and whether they were considered in consideration of their citizenship.
3. The issues surrounding the abandonment of the prior court order, which led to the decision being made on papers rather than live evidence ([paras 22, 27]).

### R – Rules

The court's consideration was governed primarily by section 17(1) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which establishes the statutory prerequisites for leave to appeal — specifically, that there must be a reasonable prospect of success for the appeal to be heard ([paras 25–26]). Additionally, principles from the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA) concerning procedural fairness in administrative actions were also relevant ([paras 47]). 

The court relied upon previous judgments to articulate the standards for reviewing administrative action, particularly regarding allegations of fraud and delegated authority, grounding its interpretations in the statutory provisions of the Citizenship Act and relevant case law such as *Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd* ([para 21]).

### A – Application

In its analysis, the court examined whether the applicant had successful grounds for appeal based on the merits presented. The bench noted that the issues of marital status and the validity of the representations made were interlinked with questions of fact that had already been established ([paras 22, 34, 46]). 

On the matter of the delegation of authority, the court reaffirmed that the Minister had the power to delegate such authority under the provisions of the Citizenship Act ([para 32]). Furthermore, because factual disputes were evident, particularly regarding Nwafor’s marriage status and whether appropriate processes had been followed, the court found no merit in claims of procedural unfairness under PAJA, stating that adequate notice and opportunity to represent were provided ([paras 47–49]).

The court also addressed the issue of the collective deprivation of citizenship for Nwafor’s children, contending that this argument was newly raised and hence not fit for review at the appeal stage ([para 38]). Likewise, the argument surrounding the abandonment of the previous court's decision was dismissed, emphasizing that procedural compliance with Rule 41(2) was not applicable in cases where issues have not reached a conclusive stage ([paras 40–45]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the application for leave to appeal, concluding that Nwafor did not demonstrate reasonable prospects of success ([paras 49–50]). The court determined that the factual disputes surrounding Nwafor's citizenship status were substantiated by the respondents’ evidence, disallowing any claims that the deprivation of citizenship was undertaken arbitrarily or unlawfully. 

This judgment reinforces the significance of maintaining procedural integrity in administrative actions and highlights the overarching principles and safeguards surrounding the deprivation of citizenship. It also serves as a precedent impacting future considerations of similar jurisdictional disputes within South Africa ([para 26]).

The appeal was thus denied, with costs awarded against the applicant, establishing authority on the delegation of powers and procedural rights surrounding citizenship deprivation in administrative law ([para 50]).
