Road Accident Fund v Mohohlo (882/16) [2017] ZASCA 155

Mohohlo testified that she took Otshepeng in when he was a baby, raising him as her son in a context that did not involve formal adoption due to cultural practices ([8]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 155

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

In Road Accident Fund v Mohohlo ([1]), the matter arose from a claim for damages for loss of support by Rebecca Mohohlo, the aunt of Otshepeng Letshufi, who died on 27 October 2011 due to injuries sustained in a motor vehicle collision on 2 July 2011. The appellant, the Road Accident Fund (RAF), conceded the issue of negligence but contested Mohohlo’s right to damages regarding the loss of support. The central issues before the court were whether Otshepeng owed Mohohlo a legal duty of support and if she was sufficiently indigent to enforce that duty ([2]).

Mohohlo testified that she took Otshepeng in when he was a baby, raising him as her son in a context that did not involve formal adoption due to cultural practices ([8]). While she was a domestic worker, health issues forced her to give up steady employment in 2004, and she struggled with various health concerns ([9]). Otshepeng, who became a financial consultant, supported her financially during his life, contributing substantially to her living expenses ([9]).

I – Issues

The case presented two primary legal questions ([2]):

  1. Did Otshepeng owe Mohohlo a legal duty of support despite their uncle-nephew relationship?
  2. Did Mohohlo demonstrate sufficient indigence to enforce this duty?

R – Rules

The decision relied upon several legal principles and precedents. Traditionally, under South African common law, a legal duty of support was limited primarily to direct family relationships up to the second degree of consanguinity. This limitation meant that there was typically no duty of support between an uncle/aunt and their nephew/niece ([5]).

However, the court recognized that the legal convictions of society have evolved. Citing Paixão v Road Accident Fund 2012 (6) SA 377 (SCA), it indicated that the existence of a duty of support could be informed by societal values and the “boni mores” criterion—essentially, the morals and justice as perceived by the community ([10]).

Furthermore, the court placed significant weight on the constitutional value of ubuntu, which emphasizes communal support and relationships, reflecting the spirit of interconnectedness ([12]). Customary law, under section 211(3) of the Constitution, was also considered, with acknowledgment that its application might extend beyond traditional interpretations of legal duties of support ([13]).

A – Application

During the application of these legal principles to the facts, the court noted that while Otshepeng and Mohohlo did not share a traditional support obligation by law, their actual relationship mirrored that of mother and child due to the nature of their care and mutual support ([14]). Their interactions demonstrated a de facto adoption, reinforcing the understanding of familial relationships beyond mere blood ties.

The court also evaluated Mohohlo’s financial situation in light of the established duty of support. Testimony showed that, despite receiving a monthly pension and sporadic income from selling goods, her financial circumstances were inadequate to meet her basic needs ([23]–[27]). The court acknowledged this inadequacy, emphasizing that the support should reflect her status relative to the assistance she provided and the connection she had with Otshepeng, who had reached a stable economic status prior to his death.

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming that Otshepeng did have a legal duty to support Mohohlo based on their de facto relationship, which had cultural underpinnings affirming her position as a caregiver ([29]). The judgment reaffirmed the legal recognition of evolving familial structures and societal norms, suggesting a willingness to recognize support duties that arise from deep emotional and practical connections rather than strict biological lines. This ruling aids in reshaping the understanding of familial obligations within contemporary South African law, harmonizing traditional legal frameworks with modern societal values.

The decision ultimately reinforced the principle that denying support obligations rooted in deep familial ties would violate contemporary moral and justice standards, reflecting a necessary evolution of the law to accommodate the realities of societal relationships ([17]). The court’s willingness to broaden interpretations of duty of support beyond traditional norms signifies a move towards inclusivity in recognizing the varied forms of familial bonds present in society today.
