Economic Freedom Fighters v Gordhan and Others; Public Protector and Another v Gordhan and Others [2020] ZACC 10

The case involves two applications for direct leave to appeal from the High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Division, Pretoria.

Constitutional Court 2020 [2020] ZACC 10

F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])

The case involves two applications for direct leave to appeal from the High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Division, Pretoria. Both applications arise from a judgment by Potterill J, which allowed Mr. Pravin Gordhan to interdict the Public Protector's remedial actions following her reports concerning accusations against him. The High Court also ordered costs against the Economic Freedom Fighters (EFF) and the Public Protector, which the applicants contest as they assert their applications raise significant constitutional questions involving the Public Protector's role.

The first case (CCT 232/19) was brought by the EFF against Mr. Gordhan, the Public Protector, and several others, challenging the High Court's order granting an interim interdict against the Public Protector’s remedial actions. The EFF sought a stricter test for granting interim interdicts against the Public Protector, while the respondents argued that the standard set in OUTA was correctly applied.

In the second case (CCT 233/19), the Public Protector and Ms. Busisiwe Mkhwebane sought to appeal the High Court's costs orders against them, asserting the litigation was constitutional in nature and thus should involve the application of the Biowatch principle—protecting parties from costs in genuine constitutional litigation. Both appeals raise similar issues regarding the appropriate standard for interim interdicts against the Public Protector and the implications of costs orders issued in these proceedings.

I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 11–18])

The key legal issues revolve around:

  1. What is the appropriate test for the High Court to apply when granting interim interdicts against the Public Protector's remedial actions, particularly in the context of the OUTA ruling?
  2. Does the litigation constitute constitutional matters, thereby invoking the application of the Biowatch principle to protect the applicants from bearing costs?
  3. Should the courts permit appeals against costs orders in the absence of a final determination of the merits of the underlying disputes?
  4. How does the principle of separation of powers factor into the granting of interim interdicts against the Public Protector?

Each issue brings into focus the balance between judicial oversight of administrative actions and the protections afforded to constitutional offices like that of the Public Protector.

R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 19–30])

  1. OUTA (National Treasury v Opposition to Urban Tolling Alliance): The precedent sets forth the criteria for granting interim interdicts, which include the necessity for a prima facie case, real and imminent harm, the balance of convenience, and the absence of alternative remedies ([paras 19–22]).
  2. Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources: This case delineates the principle whereby costs orders should be resolved in favor of parties engaged in constitutional litigation, thereby impacting the funding of public interests ([paras 23–25]).
  3. Constitutional Provisions: Section 181 establishes the framework for the Public Protector, affirming its role in a constitutional democracy and the non-interference by other state organs in its operations ([para 3]).
  4. Precedents on Separation of Powers: These include various rulings asserting the hands-off approach the judiciary must take regarding executive functions, ensuring that interdicts do not impair the operations of constitutional bodies ([paras 27–30]).

A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 31–90])

The Court adopted a comprehensive approach in assessing the merits of the appeals against the High Court's ruling:

  1. Application of the OUTA Test: The Court found the High Court correctly applied the OUTA criteria for interim interdicts, emphasizing that the EFF's call for a stricter standard lacked merit. The ruling observed that there was no compelling reason to deviate from established tests ([paras 31–45]).
  2. Biowatch Principle Applications: The Court examined whether the context constituted constitutional litigation deserving protection. It concluded that, while the issues raised pertained to the functions of a constitutional office, the nature of the litigation did not meet the stringent criteria set out in Biowatch, thus upholding the High Court's costs order against the EFF ([paras 46–65]).
  3. Separation of Powers and Interim Relief: The Court reiterated the importance of judicial restraint regarding the Executive's functions while finding that the interdicts did not encroach upon the constitutional prerogatives of the Public Protector's office. The potential impact of interim interdicts on public administration was weighed heavily, with the Court favoring the preservation of individuals' rights pending reviews when well-justified ([paras 66–85]).
  4. Direct Appeals and Interests of Justice: The Court explored the necessity of allowing direct appeals, determining that the circumstances did not justify bypassing standard appellate procedures as the prospects of success were minimal. The potential resolution of the matters did not warrant the urgency claimed ([paras 86–90]).

C - Conclusion: Court's Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 91–102])

Ultimately, the Court dismissed the application for leave to appeal regarding the merits of the High Court's order but granted leave for the limited purpose of challenging the costs orders issued against the EFF and the Public Protector. The Court's judgment underscores several critical implications:

  1. Reaffirmation of OUTA Standards: The ruling confirmed the validity of using the established OUTA criteria when dealing with interim interdicts against constitutional bodies, thereby promoting judicial consistency.
  2. Care in Costs Decisions: The judgment highlights the necessity for courts to carefully consider the constitutional nature of the rights in question when deciding on costs, emphasizing a nuanced understanding of the relationship between litigation and public interest.
  3. Separation of Powers Diligence: The decision reiterates the prudence required when courts engage with cases involving the Executive, reaffirming that interdicts should be limited and only granted in clearest cases to avoid undermining the constitutional functions of the State.
  4. Civil Society and Legal Resources: The ruling indicates that while organizations like the EFF play an essential role in constitutional discourse, the courts will maintain a framework that encourages responsible engagement with legal proceedings, particularly concerning costs connected to constitutional litigation.

These case proceedings serve as a significant touchstone for future litigation involving the Public Protector and underline the delicate balance of power within South Africa’s constitutional framework.

Overall, the case reaffirms foundational principles of constitutional law, guiding the use and limits of interim relief in the contentious arena of state accountability and governance.
