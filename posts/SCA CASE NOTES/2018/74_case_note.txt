Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: *Mogale City Local Municipality v Black Tad Investments CC* (889/17) [2018] ZASCA 74

## F – Facts
The case involved an appeal brought by Mogale City Local Municipality (the appellant) against Black Tad Investments CC (the respondent) regarding an interlocutory structural interdict issued by the Gauteng Local Division. The core issue was the municipality's statutory and constitutional obligation to provide emergency accommodation to alleged illegal occupiers facing eviction from properties owned by the respondent ([1]-[2]).

The eviction proceedings were initiated by Black Tad Investments on 25 September 2013 against certain alleged illegal occupiers on their property, Holding No 48, Steynsvlei Agricultural Holdings. The municipality was cited but initially elected not to participate in the proceedings. After the respondent's application for an informational order relating to emergency accommodation, the court, in an unopposed order dated 7 November 2014, directed the municipality to provide a report on the availability of alternative accommodation within a set timeframe. The municipality consistently failed to comply with this order, ultimately delivering an insufficient report on 30 January 2015, which acknowledged the poor living conditions of the occupiers but was described as vague and unhelpful ([3]-[4]).

Despite acknowledging its lack of suitable accommodation, the municipality did not act to remedy the situation, prompting the respondent to seek a declaratory order establishing the municipality's obligation to the occupiers. The court a quo found that the municipality had failed in its duties, both statutory and constitutional, and was thus in breach of these obligations ([5]-[6]).

During the appeal, the municipality introduced a defense that the eviction should fall under the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA) rather than the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE), a point not previously raised. This defense was deemed improper as it had not been introduced during earlier proceedings ([7]).

## I – Issues
The main legal questions arising from the case included:
1. Whether the Mogale City Local Municipality owed a statutory and constitutional duty to the alleged unlawful occupiers to provide temporary emergency accommodation.
2. Whether the municipality's failure to comply with the orders of the court constituted a breach of its obligations under both statutory and constitutional law ([1], [5]).
3. The appropriateness of the application of PIE versus ESTA in the proceedings ([7]).

## R – Rules
The governing statutory provisions included:
1. **Section 26 of the Constitution**: This section obligates the state to take reasonable legislative and other measures to ensure access to adequate housing. It forms the constitutional basis for social obligations related to housing and evictions ([10]).
2. **Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE)**: This act lays down procedural requirements for evictions and dictates that the needs of unlawful occupiers must be considered, in conjunction with their rights and the obligations of the municipality ([8], [13]).
3. **Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA)**: This act provides for the rights of tenants and regulates the eviction process from certain types of land, particularly concerning occupiers on agricultural holdings ([7]).

In *Government of the RSA v Grootboom & others* 2001 (1) SA 46 (CC), the court emphasized that the state must devise and implement coherent programs to address housing needs ([11]). Additionally, prior judgments such as *City of Johannesburg v Changing Tides* 2012 (6) SA 294 (SCA) reinforced the obligations of local authorities to have plans to address the needs of the homeless ([11]).

## A – Application
In its application of the law, the court analyzed the municipality’s failure to act upon its constitutional obligations effectively. It focused on the municipality's history of non-compliance with court directives and its inadequate reporting regarding emergency accommodation. The court recognized the vagueness of the submitted report, viewing it as a clear admission of its failure to engage meaningfully with the situation of the occupiers ([5]-[6]). 

The municipality's new argument regarding the classification of the land under ESTA was dismissed as it did not follow proper procedural mechanisms by raising the point in its answering affidavit. The court reiterated that issues must be clearly delineated in pleadings to avoid ambush and ensure that all parties are aware of the claims they must meet ([7]). It was held that the local authority owed a duty under Section 26 of the Constitution, which mandates making contingency arrangements for those facing eviction, irrespective of the statutory framework employed.

Ultimately, the court upheld the decision of the court a quo to issue a structural interdict obligating the municipality to fulfill its responsibilities towards the occupiers, as it had demonstrated an indifference and lack of cooperation in the preceding proceedings ([8]-[13]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the Mogale City Local Municipality did indeed owe a statutory and constitutional duty to provide the occupiers with emergency accommodation due to their imminent eviction risks. The municipality's failures were seen as a breach of these obligations, and the ordering of a structural interdict was deemed appropriate and necessary to compel the municipality to comply with its duties ([13], [14]). The appeal was dismissed with costs, emphasizing that successful parties in litigation are entitled to their costs unless exceptional circumstances are presented ([14]).

This case reinforces the essential role of local government in addressing housing crises and ensuring that marginalised communities are not unjustly rendered homeless. The interplay between constitutional obligations and local governance is critically underscored, highlighting the necessity for municipalities to engage actively and responsibly in social housing matters.
