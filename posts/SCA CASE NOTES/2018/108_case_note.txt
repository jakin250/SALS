Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: Truworths Ltd v Primark Holdings (989/2017) 2018 ZASCA 108 

## F - Facts
The appellant, Truworths Ltd (Truworths), is a well-established fashion retailer in South Africa, whereas the respondent, Primark Holdings (Primark), is a subsidiary of Associated British Foods plc, based primarily in Ireland and with no retail presence in South Africa. Primark registered the trademark "PRIMARK" in South Africa in 1976 in respect of clothing and related goods, yet has never opened a store there. Truworths applied to expunge Primark's trademark from the register under sections 27(1)(a) and (b) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, citing failure to demonstrate bona fide use of the mark in South Africa. The High Court, led by Monama J, dismissed Truworths' application. Truworths subsequently appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 1-2]).

## I - Issues
The appeal raised the following legal issues:
1. Whether Truworths was entitled to invoke the provisions for expungement under sections 27(1)(a) and (b) of the Trade Marks Act, considering Primark claimed protection under section 35(1) of the Act as a well-known mark.
2. The definition of a "well-known" mark requiring evidence that such a mark is recognized within applicable sectors of the public in South Africa.
3. The necessity of “bona fide” use of the trademark “PRIMARK” in South Africa, particularly whether such use had occurred or was intended ([paras 3-4]).

## R - Rules
1. **Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993**:
   - Sections 27(1)(a) and (b): Allows for removal of a trademark from the register if there is no bona fide intention to use or no genuine use for a period of five years.
   - Section 27(5) provides that well-known trademarks under Section 35 are exempt from expungement for non-use ([para 4]).
   - **Section 35(1)** defines a "well-known mark" as one recognized by sectors of the public as significant and grants it protection against actions such as expungement ([para 5]).

2. **Precedents**: 
   - The judgment often referenced *McDonald’s Corporation v Joburgers Drive-Inn Restaurant* which clarified that recognition should be substantial, and the relevant public can consist of various sectors depending on the goods or services ([para 10]).

3. **Criteria from WIPO Guidelines**: The judgment referred to recommendations from the World Intellectual Property Organization (WIPO) concerning the evaluation of marks’ recognition within distinct sectors ([para 12-14]).

## A - Application
The court evaluated whether the PRIMARK mark gained well-known status among defined relevant sectors. Primark directed its argument towards sectors engaged in retail, fashion design, and related industries, while Truworths argued for a broader interpretation that included all local consumers interested in clothing. 

In examining both positions, the court noted that substantial evidence of PRIMARK's brand presence was shown primarily in retail circles rather than among the general public. Primark's marketing and international retail success provided a backdrop for its claim, including awards and industry ranking, particularly noting Primark’s visibility and the respect it commands in the international retail landscape.

Substantial evidence was provided through affidavits from South African retailers who were either familiar with or actively engaged with Primark due to international travel or industry interest ([paras 21-25]). 

Conversely, the court scrutinized the consumer survey commissioned by Truworths which found only a mere 3.5% recognition of PRIMARK among adults in major urban areas, significantly less than other local brands, indicating that general awareness among potential consumers was weak. This survey was thus deemed ineffective in establishing Primark's mark as well-known under South African law ([paras 34-40]).

The evidence cited by Primark about online discussions and media mentions was insufficient to demonstrate genuine usage or recognition among the broader South African market ([paras 61-63]). Ultimately, the court found that while PRIMARK had established its mark in fashion circles, it did not satisfy the criteria for being considered well-known among the broader consumer base in South Africa necessary for protection under section 35.

## C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that Primark's mark did not meet the threshold of being considered a well-known mark in the requisite South African market per the statutory guideline of Section 35(1). As a result, it ruled in favor of Truworths, allowing for the expungement of Primark's registration. The court highlighted that the essence of trademark protection is directly tied to its recognition and practical use in the corresponding market ([paras 66-74]). The appeal was successful, and the costs were awarded to Truworths, including those for employing two counsel, marking a definitive legal stance in balancing local rights against international trademark law nuances. 

This ruling exemplifies ongoing dynamics in trademark disputes, especially regarding the concept of "well-known" marks in a globalized commercial context and the importance of bona fide usage to uphold trademark rights in respective jurisdictions.
