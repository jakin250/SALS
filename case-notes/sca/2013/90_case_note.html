Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 90 (31 May 2013) Court: The Supreme Court Delivered 31 May 2013

Malgas v S (703/12) [2013] ZASCA 90 (31 May 2013)

Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Coram: Navsa and Majiedt JJA, Willis AJA
Heard: 22 May 2013
Delivered: 31 May 2013

F – Facts

The case concerns an appeal against the sentences imposed on the appellants—Henry Malgas, Shon Williams, Johan Beyers, and Andy Jansen—who were formerly police officers convicted of crimes involving housebreaking and theft of dagga from a police storeroom and a police vehicle at the Beaufort-West police station ([para 1]). The convictions occurred on 5 November 2002, with sentences subsequently imposed on 6 March 2003. The first two appellants received ten years' imprisonment, while the third and fourth received eight years and varying concurrent sentences ([para 2]).

Following various procedural developments, including their appeals being heard and sentenced by the Western Cape High Court on 3 June 2011, the third and fourth appellants' sentences remained unchanged, while the first and second appellants' sentences were reduced to eight years ([para 3]). A considerable delay also acknowledged the fact that, although the appellants were free on bail post-conviction, no clear explanation was provided for the long delay in proceedings ([para 4–9]).

The appeal now before the Supreme Court concerns the question of whether a significant delay in hearing their appeal—over eight years—constitutes an exceptional circumstance justifying a revision of their sentences ([para 13]). The appellants argued for a reduction based on the claim of trauma and disadvantage resulting from this delay, yet the state contended that the appellants were responsible for the tardiness ([para 12], [para 14]).

I – Issues

The central legal issue is whether the long delay in hearing the appellants' appeal, primarily the eight-year gap between the imposition of sentence and the hearing of the appeal, constitutes an exceptional circumstance that warrants interference with the sentences ([para 13]). The court specifically considered the appellants' responsibilities and actions during this period, and whether their failure to expedite the process disqualified them from relief ([para 20], [para 22]).

R – Rules

The court relied upon several precedents regarding delays in appeal hearings justified by exceptional circumstances. It referenced S v Karolia and S v Michele, wherein courts displayed a willingness to reconsider sentences affected by undue delays in the appeal process ([para 15–16]). In S v Jaftha, a specific ten-year delay was considered substantial enough to review a sentence ([para 17]).

Notably, the rules governing appeals in magistrate's courts place the ultimate responsibility for ensuring the timely preparation of records on the appellants ([para 18]). This responsibility extends to providing clear explanations when significant delays arise, which was lacking in this case ([para 19]).

A – Application

In applying these principles, the court evaluated the circumstances surrounding the delay and the appellants' actions thereafter. The justices noted that they were not in custody at the time, thus implying a degree of self-directed agency in the matter ([para 14]). Their apparent "supine attitude" towards expediting the appeal was emphasized, likened to hoping their troubles would dissipate without action ([para 21]).

Additionally, although the court acknowledged the potential for trauma due to delay, it found that the appellants had not actively sought to address the lack of progress in their case, nor did they offer support for their claims concerning difficulty in obtaining transcripts of proceedings ([para 20, 22]). Mere remoteness from the judicial process did not excuse their inaction, given that they benefited from legal representation and were not impecunious ([para 19]).

C – Conclusion

The appeal was ultimately dismissed, reinforcing the notion that significant delays do not automatically warrant interference with sentences, particularly when the responsibilities of the appellants are taken into account ([para 24]). The court underscored that granting relief under such circumstances might undermine the integrity of the justice system. The appellants were deemed responsible for the lack of finality in their legal standings, rendering their appeal unpersuasive in the eyes of the court, which sought to uphold law and order ([para 22–23]).

This case illustrates the paramount importance of diligence in prosecuting appeals and signifies the threshold of responsibility that rests with appellants, particularly when they enjoy the privilege of bail following conviction.
