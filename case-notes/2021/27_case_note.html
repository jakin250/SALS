RAiN Chartered Accountants Incorporated v South African Social Security Agency [2021] ZACC 27

The case before the Constitutional Court of South Africa (CCT) concerns RAiN Chartered Accountants Incorporated (RAiN), which was appointed by the South African Social Security Agency (SASSA) following an April 2021 court order.

Constitutional Court 2021 [2021] ZACC 27

F – Facts

The case before the Constitutional Court of South Africa (CCT) concerns RAiN Chartered Accountants Incorporated (RAiN), which was appointed by the South African Social Security Agency (SASSA) following an April 2021 court order. This order required RAiN to perform an audit verifying the profits of Cash Paymaster Services (Pty) Ltd (CPS) in relation to its earlier unlawful contract with SASSA ([paras 1-3]). The background of the matter traces back to earlier litigation known as the Allpay saga, wherein SASSA was mandated to account for CPS's profits after it was determined that CPS's contract was invalid ([para 1]).

RAiN encountered significant delays due to CPS’s failure to provide necessary documentation for the audit ([para 4]). This delay resulted not only in additional hours worked by RAiN beyond the contractual agreement with SASSA but also in complications surrounding the framing of their final invoice ([paras 6-16]). Initially, SASSA acknowledged its responsibility to pay RAiN for the work necessitated by the April 2021 order. However, it later reneged on this acceptance, prompting RAiN to seek a declaratory order confirming SASSA's responsibility for the fees incurred in compliance with the court order ([paras 13-14]).

I – Issues

The principal legal questions at issue in this case are:

  1. Who is liable for the payment of RAiN's fees stemming from the court's April 2021 order?
  2. Is SASSA obligated to consider appointing RAiN under a deviation process as provided in Treasury Regulation 16A.6.4?
  3. Is RAiN entitled to recover any legal fees incurred related to the current proceedings? ([paras 1-3, 5-18]).

R – Rules

The court considered several statutory regulations and principles while addressing these issues:

  1. Constitutional and Statutory Framework: Section 217 of the South African Constitution mandates that organs of state procure services in a manner that is fair, equitable, transparent, and competitive. The court noted that SASSA's obligation to pay RAiN could not contravene this statutory framework. Additionally, Treasury Regulation 16A.6.4 permits deviation from competitive bidding processes under specific circumstances ([paras 16, 29]).
  2. Principles of Contract Law: The court applied principles regarding contract interpretation to assess whether RAiN's subsequent services fell within the ambit of their original agreement with SASSA. The court also referred to established case law on estoppel, particularly concerning public authority conduct ([para 21]).
  3. Judicial Precedents: Citing previous judgments, especially within the Allpay litigation, the court highlighted its retained supervisory authority over compliance with its orders, affirming the relevance of equitable considerations in public procurements ([para 24]).

A – Application

In its application of the law, the court noted that SASSA had implicitly accepted RAiN's performance under their initial contract by paying the final invoice without any dispute over the quality of the service delivered. The court found that this acceptance triggered an obligation for SASSA to regard RAiN as having fulfilled its contractual obligations ([para 25]).

Furthermore, the court assessed the significant delays caused by CPS’s non-cooperation, which could reasonably be seen as a barrier to RAiN’s full compliance with the April 2021 order. The court thus accepted RAiN's counterclaims regarding the need for further payment as being rooted in SASSA's own failures to provide necessary documents in a timely manner ([paras 6-12]).

The court ruled that while section 217 provides a framework for procurement, RAIN's circumstance may encompass conditions justifying a deviation from standard procurement processes, allowing SASSA to appoint RAiN to fulfill the ongoing obligations under the court's directive ([paras 29-36]).

C – Conclusion

The court held that SASSA is responsible for paying RAiN reasonable fees for the work required by the April 2021 order ([paras 1-3]). Additionally, SASSA must incorporate RAiN under the Treasury Regulation 16A.6.4 deviation process, essentially allowing expedited procurement of services necessary for compliance with the court's directives ([paras 1-2]).

Moreover, the court determined that while RAiN's claims for legal fees were not substantiated adequately, it is entitled to costs due to its success in the application ([paras 39-40]). The implications of this ruling reinforce the need for public bodies to adhere closely to judicial orders and could influence future procurement practices within SASSA and similar public entities navigating their obligations under legal and constitutional frameworks.

In summary, the court's ruling solidifies the accountability of state entities for fulfilling their procurement responsibilities while respecting both contractual obligations and the constitutional imperatives governing public service procurement.
