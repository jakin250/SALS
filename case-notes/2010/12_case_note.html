The State v Kholekile Witness Thunzi and Siyabulela Mlonzi [2010] ZACC 12

Constitutional Court 2010 [2010] ZACC 12

F - Facts

The case, The State v Kholekile Witness Thunzi and Siyabulela Mlonzi, revolves around the application of the Dangerous Weapons Act (Transkei) Act 71 of 1968 (DWA (Tk)) in the context of two applicants, Mr. Thunzi and Mr. Mlonzi, both convicted under its provisions. Their trials were conducted in separate magistrate's courts where each received sentences exceeding the ordinary penal jurisdiction of the courts, which is capped at three years as per the Magistrates' Courts Act 32 of 1944 ([6]-[9]). The Mthatha High Court reviewed their convictions following discrepancies about judicial procedures and the application of sentencing laws ([10]-[15]).

The High Court identified a crucial anomaly in South African law regarding the existence of the DWA (Tk) alongside the national Dangerous Weapons Act (DWA (SA)), resulting from apartheid-era legislation that continued to apply differentially across regions post-transition ([4], [28]-[29]). The court ultimately ruled that the applicability of section 4 of DWA (Tk) was unconstitutional, creating an unjust distinction in sentencing based solely on geographical jurisdiction, thereby prompting this confirmation proceeding ([11]).

I - Issues

The primary issues requiring adjudication were:

  1. Confirmation of the High Court's findings regarding the unconstitutionality of the DWA (Tk) - specifically, whether the terms of the High Court's order were competent under sections 167(5) and 172(2)(a) of the Constitution, which govern confirmation of invalidity of legislative provisions ([30]-[31]).
  2. Whether the Court has the authority to amend the order of the High Court to extend invalidity to past cases not protected in the decision ([52]).
  3. The obligation of Parliament to rationalize duplicate dangerous weapons legislation which still exists post-1994, reflecting on the legislative scheme's constitutionality ([66]).

R - Rules

The Court relied on:

  • Section 167(5) of the Constitution: The requirement for confirmation of any order of constitutional invalidity from lower courts before it can take effect ([30]).
  • Section 172(1)(b) of the Constitution: Empowering the court to make orders that are just and equitable, suggesting a broad interpretative scope regarding remedies and constitutional duties ([112]).
  • Precedents highlighting the importance of ensuring equal treatment under the law and the principles governing legislative frameworks post-apartheid ([128]).

A - Application

The Constitutional Court navigated the ambiguity in the High Court's order. The High Court did not directly declare the provisions of the DWA (Tk) as invalid but instead deemed its applicability to violate the equality clause under the Constitution. Consequently, it limited the impact of its ruling, sparking questions about the equitability of this limitation as it left intact the oppressive structure for those already convicted or charged but whose cases were not delineated by the High Court's judgment ([38]-[43]).

The justices considered whether to invoke their inherent powers to ensure the interests of justice were served without further exacerbating the existing legal malaise. The constitutional validity of maintaining distinct laws governing dangerous weapons across jurisdictions was critically assessed, particularly the potential for continued discriminatory enforcement of the DWA (Tk) if left unaddressed ([66],[69]).

Ultimately, the Court determined that a broad remedy was warranted to rectify the existing legal inconsistencies. This meant not only invalidating the 1975 Notice that triggered the application of section 4 but also ensuring that all affected individuals, irrespective of the state of their judicial proceedings, would have recourse to challenge their sentences through appeals or reviews ([71]-[75]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court refrained from confirming the High Court’s order as it was insufficient to address the injustices perpetuated by the separate application of the DWA (Tk). Instead, it declared the 1975 ministerial notice invalid, opening the pathway for the affected individuals, explicitly those serving sentences or facing charges under this discriminatory framework, to realize their rights within the context of a unified legal system. The Court thus directed the DPP to withdraw all pending charges under the DWA (Tk) and mandated comprehensive reporting and consultation measures to ensure affected individuals received necessary legal support[, [71]-[73]].

The case illustrates ongoing challenges in reconciling legacy legislation with contemporary constitutional realities in South Africa, emphasising the necessity for continuous legal reform aligned with the principles of equality and fairness. The judgment serves as a clarion call for legislative action, compelling Parliament to rationalize antiquated laws and establish uniform legal standards across the newly unified territory of South Africa in adherence to the values enshrined in the Constitution.
