# Case Note: The Attorneys’ Fidelity Fund v Prevance Capital (Pty) Ltd (917/17) [2018] ZASCA 135

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History
[1] The appeal concerns the liability of the Attorneys Fidelity Fund Board of Control (the Board) for a theft loss incurred by Prevance Capital (Pty) Ltd (Prevance). These funds, amounting to R3 081 926,55, were deposited into the trust account of Mr. Robert Victor Weide, an attorney and conveyancer, under the guise of a bridging finance scheme. 

[2] Under Section 26(a) of the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979, the Fidelity Fund exists to reimburse individuals suffering pecuniary loss due to theft committed by an attorney or their personnel of any money entrusted to them. In this case, Prevance sought reimbursement following the theft, asserting that the funds had been entrusted to Mr. Weide as outlined by the legislative provision.

[3] The Board, however, denied liability, citing Section 47(1)(g) of the Act, which excludes the Fidelity Fund’s liability for losses suffered as a result of theft of money that an attorney was instructed to invest on behalf of the claimant. The critical issue before the court was whether the funds deposited by Prevance constituted “entrusted” money as envisioned in Section 26(a) and whether the criteria for exclusion under Section 47(1)(g) applied.

[4] Importantly, a scheme was established wherein Prevance would enter a financial agreement with Mr. Weide to provide funds required by a seller for a property transfer, with additional fees incorporated into the deal. Mr. Weide had misrepresented a series of transactions, presenting fictitious sales and parties involved, thereby committing theft when he appropriated the funds.

[5] The Gauteng Division of the High Court, led by Vorster AJ, ruled in favour of Prevance, indicating that the nature of the transaction was not an investment, as it was contingent upon the successful transfer of property. Consequently, the court ordered reimbursement to Prevance.

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions
[6] The principal issues hinged on:
1. Whether the funds deposited could be classified as having been "entrusted" to Mr. Weide under Section 26(a) of the Attorneys Act.
2. Whether the Board could successfully invoke the exclusionary provisions of Section 47(1)(g), which pertains to money an attorney was instructed to invest on behalf of another party.

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon
[7] The court's interpretation of the term "entrusted" was informed by previous jurisprudence, notably in *Industrial and Commercial Factors (Pty) Ltd v Attorneys Fidelity Fund Board of Control* 1997 (1) SA 136 (AD), which shaped the understanding that the term need not adhere to a strict trust definition. The case clarified the circumstances under which funds are considered entrusted, emphasizing the intent and context surrounding the transaction.

[8] The applicability of Section 47(1)(g) was scrutinized. The court assessed previous cases, including *King v Attorneys Fidelity Fund* 2010 (4) SA 185 (SCA), highlighting a distinction between scenarios where attorneys acted as conduits for investments versus more dubious scenarios involving fraudulent intent and misrepresentation.

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law
[9] The court determined that the funds deposited by Prevance were indeed entrusted to Mr. Weide. Despite the Board's attempts to argue otherwise, the circumstances unveiled by the facts indicated that Prevance was lured into a fraudulent scheme. The court underscored the intentionality behind Mr. Weide’s actions — to appropriate the funds for personal gain rather than to facilitate genuine transactions.

[10] The Board's reliance on Section 47(1)(g) was scrutinized, with the court concluding that Prevance did not merely instruct Mr. Weide to invest funds, which would have triggered the exclusion. On the contrary, the funds were acquired through deception, and Mr. Weide was indeed acting in a fiduciary capacity when he misappropriated the funds.

[11] The contrasting precedents recounted by the Board, such as *Claassens* and *Mettle Property Finance*, did not hold—the present case's unique aspects, notably the fraudulent backdrop and the nature of the agreements between Prevance and Mr. Weide, differentiated it from earlier rulings where investments were more forthrightly defined.

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications
[12] The court ultimately dismissed the Board’s appeal, reaffirming that Prevance's claim for reimbursement correctly fell within the ambit of Section 26(a) of the Attorneys Act. Furthermore, it delineated that the Board failed to successfully assert the exclusion under Section 47(1)(g).

[13] The judgement underscores the importance of honouring the sanctity of trust accounts operated by attorneys and the obligations imposed upon them. The court's decision elucidates that funds misappropriated under fraudulent circumstances do not escape the reach of the Fidelity Fund merely because they were presented as investments. This case reinforces the protective measures afforded to clients and the critical responsibilities of legal practitioners within the fiduciary realm.

[14] Thus, the implications of this ruling extend beyond mere financial liability for the Board; it exemplifies a reaffirmation of the ethical duties of attorneys and the mechanisms in place to protect individuals from professional misconduct within the legal sector.
