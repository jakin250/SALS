Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# Case Note: Competition Commission of South Africa v ArcelorMittal South Africa Ltd (680/12) [2013] ZASCA 84

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–19])

The matter of *Competition Commission of South Africa v ArcelorMittal South Africa Ltd* arose from a dispute concerning the production of documents related to an alleged steel cartel. The Competition Commission (the "Commission") accused the respondents—ArcelorMittal South Africa Limited (AMSA), Cape Gate (Pty) Ltd, and Scaw South Africa Pty Ltd—of engaging in prohibited practices that contravened the Competition Act 89 of 1998 (the "Act") ([para 3]). The Commission's investigation led to a request for documents pertinent to the complaint, which AMSA and Cape Gate argued were necessary to prepare their defenses before the Competition Tribunal ([paras 5–6]).

The Commission, however, refused to provide these documents, asserting they were privileged and included restricted information pursuant to its rules ([paras 3, 5]). Scaw, the third respondent, did not dispute the cartel involvement and provided information to the Commission under the Corporate Leniency Policy (CLP), which grants immunity from prosecution in exchange for full disclosure from cartel participants to facilitate the Commission’s investigations ([para 4]). Following a series of procedural applications, AMSA and Cape Gate appealed the tribunal's limited disclosure order to the Competition Appeal Court (CAC), which remitted confidentiality claims to the tribunal without addressing the substantive dispute ([paras 6–7]).

In essence, AMSA and Cape Gate sought access to materials that included the leniency application where Scaw confessed its role within the alleged cartel ([para 6]). The CAC’s failure to make a ruling on the specific complaints led AMSA and Cape Gate to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 8–9]). 

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–19])

The primary legal issues presented before the Supreme Court were:
1. Whether the Commission could properly claim litigation privilege over the leniency application ([para 19]).
2. Whether the Commission waived any privilege by referring to the leniency application in its referral affidavit ([paras 32–38]).
3. The implications of the confidentiality claims made by Scaw and whether these claims could prevent disclosure of otherwise discoverable evidence under the rules of the tribunal ([para 42]).

The court had to resolve whether producing the leniency application and other documents was justified for AMSA and Cape Gate to contest the allegations made against them and whether the Commission's grounds for withholding the material were valid under the statutory and regulatory framework of the Competition Act.

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 20–51])

The court relied on several legal principles, notable among them:
1. **Litigation Privilege**: Defined as protecting communications made for the purposes of pending or contemplated litigation ([para 20]). The requirements for claiming this privilege were discussed, highlighting that the intention behind the creation of the document is pivotal ([paras 21–22]).
   
2. **Corporate Leniency Policy (CLP)**: This policy encourages cartel members to disclose incriminating information in exchange for immunity from prosecution ([para 4]). The operational processes established by the CLP were referenced to clarify the nature of communications and their intended purpose concerning future litigation.
   
3. **Rules of Court**: The relevant rules included Rule 15 of the Commission’s rules, enabling parties to access Commission records and Rule 14 governing the categories of information that may be withheld ([paras 46–49]).
   
4. **Promotion of Access to Information Act (PAIA)**: Sections relating to the restriction of access to information under specific circumstances were highlighted, particularly regarding claims of confidentiality ([para 42]).

The court's analysis referenced both statutory provisions and case law pertaining to the requirements for litigation privilege and the broader implications of access to information in relation to competition law ([paras 22, 28]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 20–51])

The court’s reasoning emphasized that litigation privilege should not merely focus on the intention of the document’s author but rather on the procuring party’s intentions when the document was obtained ([para 27]). The Commission contended that the leniency application was obtained for prosecutorial purposes, whereas AMSA and Cape Gate argued that it was oriented towards granting immunity to Scaw. The court found that the privileges claimed by the Commission were indeed appropriate but deemed that the privilege had been waived by the Commission’s references in the referral affidavit ([paras 32–36]).

The court explicitly stated that mere references in legal pleadings can amount to an implied waiver of privilege, particularly when those references are more than casual mentions but rather substantive assertions that invite response from the opposing party ([paras 34–38]). Thus, the court concluded that the Commission had implicitly waived its privilege over the leniency application by referring to it in its foundational affidavit.

Regarding Scaw’s confidentiality claim, the court determined that until the tribunal could render a decision on the substance of its claim, the documents in question must remain confidential. However, it affirmed that confidentiality claims should not obstruct the legitimate pursuit of due process by AMSA and Cape Gate ([para 44]).

## C – Conclusion: Court's Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 51–52])

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the Commission's appeal while upholding the cross-appeals by AMSA and Cape Gate. The court ordered that the Commission must disclose the leniency application and certain specified documents to AMSA and Cape Gate, while remitting the confidentiality claims back to the tribunal for thorough adjudication ([para 52]). 

The ruling reinforces principles of transparency and accountability in competition law enforcement, balancing the need to protect confidential information with the right of affected parties to prepare adequately for litigation. This case illustrates the judiciary's commitment to ensuring that sufficient disclosure occurs to enable effective legal defenses within the oversight mechanisms of competition law. The implications of this decision extend beyond the specific parties involved, potentially impacting how investigations under the Competition Act are conducted and how information is treated under the lens of privilege and confidentiality in the future. 

This landmark decision thereby clarifies key principles regarding privileged communications, the treatment of leniency applications, and the broader applicability of access to documents in competition law, contributing significantly to the jurisprudence surrounding these issues in South Africa.
