Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: Sarrahwitz v Maritz N.O. and Another [2015] ZACC 14

## F – Facts

The case involves Virginia Sarrahwitz (the applicant) who, in 2002, entered into a sale agreement to purchase a residence in Port Elizabeth from Reynier Posthumus. Sarrahwitz paid the full purchase price of R40,000 upfront, occupying the property thereafter ([4]–[6]). Despite her efforts over the years, the property was never transferred into her name due to administrative shortcomings and Posthumus' insolvency, leading to the sequestration of his estate in 2006. The applicant faced the grim prospect of homelessness as she was unable to secure ownership of her home through the proceedings initiated against the trustee of Posthumus' estate ([8]–[10], [45]). 

Sarrahwitz sought relief from the Eastern Cape High Court, arguing that the trustee's refusal to transfer the property contravened her rights under various constitutional provisions, including her right of access to adequate housing. The High Court ruled in favor of the trustee, citing common law principles over the Alienation of Land Act, leading Sarrahwitz to appeal ([9]–[11], [18]).

The focus of the litigation shifted to the constitutionality of the Alienation of Land Act, which did not provide for the transfer of residential property from an insolvent estate to a purchaser who had paid in full within a year ([39]). This overlap with constitutional rights to equality (s 9) and access to housing (s 26) formed the basis for Sarrahwitz’s appeal, particularly in the context of the differentiation made between her situation and that of instalment purchasers.

## I – Issues

The key issues in this case are:

- Whether the exclusion of purchasers like Sarrahwitz, who pay the full purchase price within one year, from the benefits under sections 21 and 22 of the Alienation of Land Act constitutes a violation of her constitutional rights, specifically the rights to equality and access to adequate housing ([17], [19]).
- Whether the relevant provisions of the Alienation of Land Act are unconstitutional due to their under-inclusiveness, thereby failing to protect vulnerable purchasers from homelessness under the conditions of a seller’s insolvency ([20], [47]).

## R – Rules

The court examined various statutes and constitutional provisions relevant to the case:

1. **Constitution of South Africa, 1996**: Sections 9 (Equality), 26 (Housing), and 25 (Property) were central to the argument, specifically focusing on the rights to equality and access to adequate housing, as well as the implications of legislative decisions that may unjustly discriminate ([9],[10],[18],[50]).

2. **Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981**: Relevant sections 21 and 22 establish the rights and protections for purchasers under instalment sales, which the court must interpret in light of constitutional obligations ([39]).

3. **Legal Precedents**: The court referenced decisions where essential socio-economic rights have been enshrined, notably *Jaftha v Schoeman* and *Grootboom*, which underscore the importance of access to housing and the obligations of the State and individuals to preserve existing housing rights ([41], [42], [48]).

## A – Application

The court meticulously analyzed the relationship between the constitutional provisions and the legislative framework. It emphasized that section 26(1) aims to ensure not only future access to housing but also the protection of existing housing rights. By failing to extend protection to those who had paid their purchase price fully and promptly, the Alienation of Land Act effectively undermined this principle ([30], [67]).

Mogoeng CJ noted that the differentiation between vulnerable purchasers protected under the Act and those excluded (like Sarrahwitz) was arbitrary and devoid of a rational basis. The argument established that equally vulnerable individuals should not be subjected to a loss of housing due to an unjust classification based on payment methods ([48],[56],[66]).

The court ultimately concluded that the legislative exclusions created an under-inclusive framework that disproportionately affected poorer households, which was viewed as an unjustifiable limitation of rights protected under the Constitution ([68]).

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court granted Sarrahwitz's appeal, determining that the failure of the Alienation of Land Act to provide adequate protections for purchasers like her, who have fully paid for their homes, was unconstitutional. The court ordered the reading in of specific provisions that would ensure vulnerable cash-paying buyers are equally protected from the risk of homelessness in insolvency situations ([4]–[5],[72]).

The judgment represents a substantial victory for housing rights in South Africa, reaffirming the Constitution's commitment to equitable access to housing and setting a precedent that could influence similar cases in the future. The remedy ordered not only rectified the specific harm faced by Sarrahwitz but also aimed to enhance the legislative scheme to ensure broader protections against homelessness ([71],[78]). 

The court underscored the necessity of legislative action to address gaps that result in real-world consequences for individuals like Sarrahwitz, and invited the legislature to amend the provisions of the alienation law to align with constitutional standards ([66],[72]).
