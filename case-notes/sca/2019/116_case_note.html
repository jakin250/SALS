Supreme Court of Appeal

KwaZulu-Natal Bookmakers’ Society v Phumelela Gaming and Leisure Ltd (889/2018) [2019] ZASCA 116

The case revolves around a dispute concerning the legality of totalisator betting on sports other than horse racing.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 116

FIRAC Method

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–6])

The case revolves around a dispute concerning the legality of totalisator betting on sports other than horse racing. The KwaZulu-Natal Bookmakers’ Society (first appellant) and Gauteng Off-Course Bookmakers’ Association (second appellant), representing voluntary associations of licensed bookmakers, claimed that their understanding of the Lotteries Act 57 of 1997 (the Lotteries Act) prohibits totalisator betting on sports other than horse racing in the absence of a 'sports pool' licence. The appellants argued that Phumelela Gaming and Leisure Ltd (first respondent), along with Kenilworth Racing (Pty) Ltd (second respondent) and Gold Circle (Pty) Ltd (third respondent), were unlawfully operating totalisator betting on various sports without proper licensing.

The High Court, presided by Molopa-Sethosa J, dismissed their application, which led to the appeal in the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA). The SCA was tasked with interpreting the definitions of ‘sports pool’ and ‘totalisator betting’ as per the relevant legislation and assessing the legality of the operations of the tote respondents.

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 7–8])

The primary legal issue at hand was whether totalisator betting on sports other than horse racing falls under the definition of a ‘sports pool’ as articulated in the Lotteries Act, which would require a specific licence for operation. Furthermore, it needed to be determined whether the National Gambling Act 7 of 2004 (the National Gambling Act) or the Lotteries Act governed such betting activities, and to what extent the provinces had legislative power to regulate totalisator betting on sports.

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 8–22])

The court relied on interpretations of the Lotteries Act, the National Gambling Act, and constitutional provisions. Section 1 of the Lotteries Act defines 'sports pool,' prohibiting totalisator betting on horse racing without the requisite licence. The National Gambling Act includes provisions on totalisator betting and elucidates that totalisator licences can be granted by provincial authorities. The distinction made in Section 104(1)(b)(i) of the Constitution emphasizes that lotteries and sports pools are governed at a national level, while gambling, including betting (totalisator), is regulated at both national and provincial levels ([paras 21–22]).

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 23–30])

The court dissected the definitions provided in the legislation and considered historical legislative context. It established that totalisator betting and sports pools are fundamentally different. The interpretation was guided by the principle that statutory language must be given its ordinary meaning while preserving constitutional validity ([para 8]).

The appellants' argument that totalisator betting should be classified within the ambit of sports pools was assessed against the historical legislative context, which showed a consistent distinction between totalisator betting and sports pools. The court noted that the Lotteries Act did not repeal the prior legislative framework but aimed to clarify the scope of operations, allowing provinces to regulate totalisator betting, including on sports, and affirming that these rights had historically belonged to provincial authorities ([paras 27–30]).

The court rejected the appellants' interpretation that a sports pool includes totalisator betting. The distinction between betting, which involves wagering, versus schemes awarding prizes based on chance or predictions was emphasized. The interpretation of the term 'sports pool' as strictly confined to those who forecast outcomes for prizes was affirmed, thus confirming that totalisator betting did not fall under this definition.

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 31–40])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming that totalisator betting on sports is outside the definition of ‘sports pool’ as defined in the Lotteries Act, validating the provincial regulatory framework under the National Gambling Act. The ruling underscored provincial legislative competence to regulate totalisator betting, including on non-horse racing sports, establishing a clear distinction between totalisator and betting pools.

The implications of this case signify that provincial regulations on totalisator betting are valid and that the Lotteries Act does not restrict provinces from licensing such operations. Additionally, this ruling clarifies the rights of different stakeholders within the gambling industry, reaffirming the longstanding practice of provinces to manage various forms of betting and their licensing regimes, while also highlighting the need for explicit legislative questioning of policy decisions impacting this regulatory landscape.
