# Case Note Following FIRAC Method

**Case Title:** Provincial Minister for Local Government, Environmental Affairs and Development Planning, Western Cape v Municipal Council of the Oudtshoorn Municipality and Others [2015] ZACC 24

**Coram:** Mogoeng CJ, Moseneke DCJ, Cameron J, Froneman J, Jafta J, Khampepe J, Madlanga J, Molemela AJ, Nkabinde J, Theron AJ, and Tshiqi AJ

**Judgment:** Molemela AJ (unanimous)

**Heard on:** 21 May 2015

**Decided on:** 18 August 2015

---

## F - Facts

[1] This case concerns an application for confirmation of an order from the High Court of South Africa (Western Cape Division) that declared section 30(4) of the Local Government: Municipal Structures Act 117 of 1998 (the Structures Act) constitutionally invalid.

[2] The applicant was the Provincial Minister for Local Government, Environmental Affairs and Development Planning, Western Cape. The first respondent was the Municipal Council of the Oudtshoorn Municipality, and the second respondent was the Oudtshoorn Municipality itself. The third respondent was the National Minister of Co-operative Governance and Traditional Affairs, and the fourth was the South African Local Government Association (SALGA).

[4] The factual backdrop of the case involves a dispute between the Provincial Minister and the Municipality regarding the approval of the Municipality's budget for the 2014/15 financial year. On 28 May 2014, the vote on the budget resulted in a tie, with 12 councillors in favor and 12 against it. The Speaker, who had already voted in favor, cast a second vote using their casting vote as prescribed by section 30(4) of the Structures Act. 

[5] The Provincial Minister contended that this action breached section 160(3)(b) of the Constitution, which necessitates a majority support for such matters, while the Municipality argued that the Speaker's action was lawful under the Structures Act. 

[6] The Provincial Minister sought a declaration from the High Court to set aside the budget approval. Initially opposed, the Municipality later settled the matter, and the crux of the judicial enquiry became whether a municipal council Speaker could use a casting vote on budget approvals.

## I - Issues

[11] The court identified central issues for resolution: (a) Is section 30(4) of the Structures Act inconsistent with section 160(3)(b) of the Constitution, which requires majority support for the approval of the municipal budget? (b) If section 30(4) is found to be unconstitutional, what remedy is appropriate?

## R - Rules

[12] The court applied principles of statutory interpretation as previously established, emphasizing the need to consider the language, purpose, and context of the statute. The interplay between the Structures Act and the Constitution was particularly salient.

[19] According to section 160(3)(b) of the Constitution, motions concerning specific matters require a majority vote of the whole council, while section 30(4) of the Structures Act allows the presiding councillor to cast a vote in addition to their original vote.

## A - Application

[25] The court analyzed whether the wide language in section 30(4) conflicted with section 160(3)(b). It concluded that section 30(4) inherently permits casting votes, which undermines the constitutional requirement for a majority of council members to be present to approve budgetary decisions.

[30] The court considered the High Court's proposed remedy of "reading-in," which would insert the phrase "other than a matter mentioned in section 160(2) of the Constitution" into section 30(4). The court upheld that the reading-in was appropriate as it did not infringe upon the legislative power unduly and was necessary to ensure compliance with constitutional mandates.

## C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court's order declaring section 30(4) of the Structures Act invalid. The length of procedural history underscored the importance of legislative clarity in matters of local governance, especially concerning budgetary approvals. This ruling not only rectifies the inconsistency but also raises implications for the conduct of municipal councils across South Africa, reinforcing the need for adherence to constitutional principles in local governance.

---

**Order of the Court:**

1. The order granted by the High Court is confirmed.
2. There is no order as to costs. 

This case reiterates the supremacy of constitutional requirements over legislative provisions, ensuring that essential elements of local governance remain aligned with the tenets of democracy as espoused by the Constitution.
