Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

### Case Note: **Conradie v S** [2018] ZACC 12

#### F – Facts 
The applicant, Dane Conradie, was convicted of two counts of rape in the Wynberg Regional Magistrate’s Court, resulting in an 18-year sentence, coupled with a declaration of unfitness to possess a firearm ([1]). Conradie was granted leave to appeal his conviction and sentence to the High Court of South Africa, Western Cape Division, which upheld the convictions but reduced the sentence to 12 years ([1]). Following this, the applicant sought further special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal, which was denied ([1]). Conradie contended that he only became aware of the denial on 31 July 2017, and upon obtaining legal advice, lodged a fresh application for leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court on 5 September 2017, claiming the trial record was vital for a thorough consideration of his appeal ([4]).

#### I – Issues 
The central legal issue raised by Conradie was whether the trial record of proceedings was necessary for a fair appeal, specifically for the application to the Supreme Court of Appeal, thereby implicating his constitutional right to a fair trial under section 35(3)(o) of the South African Constitution ([3]). Further, the Court had to consider whether granting condonation for the late application was warranted, given the circumstances presented by the applicant ([4]).

#### R – Rules 
The primary legal rule was derived from section 35(3)(o) of the Constitution, guaranteeing every accused the right to appeal ([6]). The jurisprudence surrounding this provision suggests that while the right to appeal should enable adequate reappraisal of trial decisions, it does not mandate a uniform requirement to have the trial record available in all cases ([6], [10]). Precedent from cases like *S v Steyn* and *S v Twala* confirmed that while the necessity of trial records for appeals from lower courts is established, the same does not automatically apply to direct appeals from High Courts ([7], [9]).

#### A – Application 
The Court analyzed the constitutional implications of Conradie’s claims within the broader context of the right to a fair trial ([6], [8]). It affirmed that the test for assessing the adequacy of appeal procedures aims to safeguard against wrongful convictions while allowing courts the flexibility to adapt assessment standards to specific circumstances ([7], [11]). The judge categorized Conradie’s request as essentially an appeal grounded in factual matters rather than a legitimate challenge to the judicial process which would implicate constitutional rights. In this way, the findings and judgments from both the Regional Court and the High Court were considered sufficient to conclude the applicant's case without necessitating additional review of the trial record ([12]).

#### C – Conclusion 
The Constitutional Court dismissed the application for leave to appeal, affirming that no infringement of fair trial rights had occurred. The exhaustive prior judgments were deemed adequate for just and efficient legal processes, reinforcing the notion that not all appeals merit the same procedural considerations, particularly when they do not substantially advance the dialogue on issues of law ([13]). This case underscores the judiciary's commitment to maintaining a balance between the rigidity of procedural requirements and the need for fair and judicious legal outcomes, specifically in the context of criminal appeals ([13]).

Through this decision, the Constitutional Court clarified the contours of the right to appeal and the imperatives of the fair trial provision, potentially informing future applications that question the necessity of trial records in various contexts.
