Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: Head of Department, Mpumalanga Department of Education v Valozone 268 CC (837/2015) [2017] ZASCA 30 

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
[1] In August 2013, the Head of the Mpumalanga Department of Education invited bids for tender EDU/069/13/MP, aimed at implementing the National School Nutrition Programme. The goal was to ensure that needy school learners from disadvantaged communities received daily nutritional meals. Successful bidders would be appointed under three-year contracts, with an option to extend for two additional years, and were responsible for managing warehouses and supplying food to schools across eight municipalities in Mpumalanga. 

[2] The tender closed on 11 September 2013, with intended implementation by January 2014. However, implementation was delayed, leading to the controversy in this case centering on the decision taken by the appellant on 23 June 2014, to cancel and re-advertise the tender.

[3] The first to seventh respondents submitted bids but were unsuccessful, as the tender was awarded to seventeen others, including the eighth respondent. Discontented, the first to seventh respondents sought a review of the tender award, alleging various irregularities, such as non-compliance with compulsory documentation requirements by successful bidders and inconsistent treatment among the tenderers.

[4] On 26 May 2014, the High Court, led by Janse van Nieuwenhuizen J, found in favor of the respondents, setting aside the original tender award and remitting it for reconsideration. The appellant, subsequently reviewing the issue, initiated a cancellation of the tender in favor of re-advertisement, citing potential litigation risks as justification. 

[5] Following the cancellation, the respondents—the first to seventh—sought judicial review, which formed the basis of this appeal.

### I – Issues
[12] The primary legal questions before the court were:

1. Did the prior judgment require the appellant to adjudicate and award the tender, thereby disallowing its cancellation? 
2. Was the cancellation of the tender valid under regulation 8(4) of the Preferential Procurement Regulations, 2011?
3. Was the cancellation decision irrational under section 6(2)(f)(ii) of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000?

### R – Rules
[16] The court identified the critical legal framework surrounding the tender cancellation, particularly referencing regulation 8(4) of the Preferential Procurement Regulations. This regulation stipulates that an organ of state may cancel a tender prior to its award only under specific conditions: 

- a) changed circumstances eliminate the need for the procurement,
- b) funds are no longer available for the total anticipated expenditure, or
- c) no acceptable tenders have been submitted.

Additionally, the case hinged on the principles of administrative justice and fair procurement processes as mandated by section 217 of the Constitution, which requires such processes to be fair, equitable, transparent, and competitive.

### A – Application
[17] The court examined the appellant’s rationale for the tender cancellation. It established that the identified conditions under regulation 8(4) were not met, as the appellant acknowledged the existence of 522 acceptable bids, contradicting the need to cancel the tender. The court highlighted that the appellant did not claim changed circumstances or lack of funding, thus invalidating the grounds for cancellation as outlined in the regulations.

[18] The ruling also articulated that the decision was irrational due to a lack of logically connected reasoning. The potential for litigation cited by the appellant appeared unsubstantiated and speculative, failing to hold weight against the urgent need to serve the nutritional demands of local children and the procedural expectations from a significant public procurement process.

[19] Finally, the court found merit in the concerns about administrative inefficiencies. The implementation of the tender was critical, given the needs of poor children, and the fear of litigation did not justly outweigh the public interest inherent in ensuring proper evaluation and award of contracts.

### C – Conclusion
[20] The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal and upheld the High Court's order to set aside the appellant’s decision to cancel and re-advertise the tender. The court’s ruling reaffirmed that the decision did not comply with the procedural fairness required by law and demonstrated the necessity for agencies in similar positions to act within their regulatory frameworks when managing public contracts. 

[21] The case emphasizes the importance of transparency and accountability in public procurement processes and serves as a precedent for similar future disputes regarding the validity and rationality of cancellation decisions, underlining that the fear of litigation cannot substitute for proper adherence to established procurement rules. 

The implications of this ruling extend beyond this single case, reinforcing the requirement for governmental entities to comply with applicable regulations and follow through on their commitments until clear, lawful reasons justify a deviation from established procedures. The adjustments made in the order by the court ensured that all qualifying bids would now be reconsidered, enhancing fairness and rigor in future evaluations.
