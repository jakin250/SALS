Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2006

# Case Note: Oos Vrystaat Kaap Bedryf Beperk v Van Aswegen [2006] SCA 14 (RSA)

## F (Facts)

The case revolves around the interpretation of a contractual clause in a maize sales agreement. Oos Vrystaat Kaap Bedryf Beperk (OVK), a farmers' cooperative, intended to purchase 500 metric tons of maize from Isabella Fredrika Johanna van Aswegen, a farmer. The specific clause under scrutiny (Clause 21) stipulated that the agreement would only become effective once signed by both parties, with an indication that if the buyer (Van Aswegen) decided to sign the agreement, it must be done within 14 days of the seller's (OVK's) signature.

On 26 October 2000, Mr. Andries van Aswegen signed the document at OVK’s store, claiming to do so on behalf of his wife, Isabella. This document was later delivered to OVK, which signed it on 9 November 2000. However, Van Aswegen mailed a letter withdrawing her offer before OVK could accept it, asserting that the agreement had not been validly concluded due to the absence of her written authorization for her husband to sign on her behalf.

The Bloemfontein High Court ruled in favor of Van Aswegen, concluding that the clause did not represent an irrevocable offer and that it could be revoked before acceptance. The court found that while there was a valid clause for acceptance within 14 days, it did not preclude the seller from revoking the offer within that timeframe. OVK appealed this decision ([paras 2-12]).

## I (Issues)

The primary legal question posed in this appeal is whether Clause 21 of the document constituted an irrevocable offer by OVK to sell maize or merely implied an offer that could be revoked before acceptance. Specifically, did the clause entitle OVK to enforce the sale after the seller's unilateral agreement, or did it merely provide a timeframe during which acceptance could occur, allowing for the revocation of the offer within that window?

Ancillary issues included whether Van Aswegen’s husband had the authority to bind her and the credibility of her testimony regarding the alleged lack of authorization for his actions ([paras 2-8]).

## R (Rules)

In determining the outcome of this appeal, the court relied on established principles of contract law, particularly concerning the irrevocability of offers and the authority to conclude contracts. South African contract law generally posits that offers can be revoked at any time before acceptance, unless expressly stated otherwise. The court examined statutory and common law precedents, including Greenberg v Wheatcroft [1950 (2) PH A56 (W)], which indicated that ambiguity in contractual terms could be construed contra proferentem against the party presenting the contract.

Furthermore, the court referenced R H Christie's treatise, "The Law of Contract," discussing conditions under which unilateral declarations of irrevocability can become binding upon acceptance ([paras 3, 13, 14]).

## A (Application)

The court endorsed the lower court's finding that Clause 21 was ambiguous and did not constitute an irrevocable offer. It noted that the clause's wording implied that acceptance by Van Aswegen could occur within a 14-day period but failed to stipulate that OVK could not retract their offer before that acceptance took place. The court held that this ambiguity was critical because it aligned with the general principle that contracts must define obligations clearly to avoid misunderstandings.

The court further supported the contention that a prior interaction, wherein Van Aswegen's husband had authorized the sale, did not confer sufficient authority for subsequent transactions without explicit written consent from Van Aswegen herself. Additionally, the court found that Van Aswegen had communicated her withdrawal of the offer effectively—thereby negating any limits on OVK’s ability to accept the offer. The testimony provided by OVK’s former employee corroborated her intention to withdraw the offer before it was accepted, leading the court to find the original ruling sound ([paras 12-15]).

## C (Conclusion)

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed OVK's appeal, affirming the lower court's conclusion that the clause in question did not embody an irrevocable offer. The court highlighted the practical implications of requiring clear terms when delineating offer and acceptance in contracts. It upheld that despite the complexity of contract law, particularly regarding authority and the nuances of offer revocation, the facts of this case pointed to a straightforward conclusion: Van Aswegen had not effectively entered into a binding contract with OVK due to the allowable revocation before acceptance. As a result, it ordered the appeal to be dismissed with costs ([paras 16-17]).

This case underscores the necessity for clarity in contractual language, particularly within formal agreements involving multiple parties, and reiterates the principle that the authority of signatory parties should be expressly defined in commercial agreements.
