Supreme Court of Appeal

Malherbe v The State (829/18)

The present case involves Dawid Johannes Malherbe, the appellant, who sought to appeal a decision by the Western Cape Division of the High Court, which denied his application for leave to appeal against his convictions for fraud and money laundering ([para 1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–11])

The present case involves Dawid Johannes Malherbe, the appellant, who sought to appeal a decision by the Western Cape Division of the High Court, which denied his application for leave to appeal against his convictions for fraud and money laundering ([para 1]). The appellant was initially convicted by the Bellville Regional Court, functioning as a Specialised Commercial Crime Court, for fraud arising from misrepresentations made to Eskom, leading to significant financial loss, and for money laundering related to proceeds from that fraud ([para 2]).

The regional court sentenced him to ten years' imprisonment on both counts, with five years of the money laundering sentence running concurrently, culminating in an effective fifteen-year sentence ([para 1]). After the refusal of leave to appeal from both the regional court under section 309B of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 and the high court under section 309C, special leave to appeal was granted by the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 2]).

During the trial, it emerged that Malherbe, while serving as the managing director of a state entity and subsequently involved with a joint venture company wholly owned by Eskom, misrepresented his interests in a company that received contracts from Eskom ([para 5]). The company, Energy Utility Services (Pty) Ltd (EUS), was awarded lucrative contracts which the court held were based on fraudulent misrepresentation of its Black Economic Empowerment status, resulting in a profit of R10.2 million for which he was held accountable ([para 6–8]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–11])

The primary issue before the court was whether the appellant should have been granted leave to appeal against his convictions and sentence, grounded in the standard that there must be reasonable prospects of success on appeal ([para 4]). This encompassed two critical inquiries:

  1. Whether there were reasonable prospects that the original convictions were incorrect, particularly in light of contradictions presented by key witnesses ([paras 8–9]).
  2. Whether the sentence imposed reflected the substantial and compelling circumstances as found by the regional court ([para 10]).

The legal framework applicable to appeals from lower courts was also central to this case, particularly the interpretation of "reasonable prospects of success" in the context of both conviction and sentencing appeals under the Criminal Procedure Act ([para 4]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 1–11])

In evaluating this case, the court relied on several key legal principles:

  • The definition provided in S v Khoasasa, which established that a refusal to allow an appeal to the high court constitutes a "judgment or order," making it subject to appeal with special leave ([para 3]).
  • The standard for granting leave to appeal developed through precedent affirming that reasonable prospects of success are determined not by the final assessment of the merits but by the potential for prevailing upon appeal ([para 4]).
  • Relevant statutes included the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, particularly sections 309B and 309C regarding appeals and petitions, and the Minimum Sentences Act 105 of 1997, which governed sentencing guidelines ([para 10]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 1–11])

The Supreme Court of Appeal undertook a careful review of the lower court's findings. It scrutinised the witness testimony, particularly noting contradictions in the evidence provided by Mr Machinjike, a key witness whose credibility was challenged ([para 9]). This led the court to conclude that these contradictions might create reasonable doubt regarding whether the appellant had made the alleged misrepresentation with fraud intent.

The court also assessed the sentence imposed by the regional court. Despite acknowledging that substantial and compelling circumstances justified a deviation from minimum sentencing, it found that the sentence did not adequately reflect this deviation, thereby implying misapplication of discretion by the sentencing court ([para 10]).

By refraining from conclusively determining the merits of the case, the court established that reasonable prospects existed for the appellate court to reassess evidence and potentially overturn both conviction and sentence ([para 4–10]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 1–11])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal, setting aside the high court's refusal to grant leave to appeal, and granted the appellant leave to appeal against both his convictions and sentence ([para 11]).

This decision underscores the importance of the standard of "reasonable prospects of success" in the criminal appeal context, reiterating that appellate courts must ensure that defendants have a fair opportunity to contest convictions, particularly in complex fraud cases with significant repercussions. The case illustrates a pivotal moment in the judicial balance between ensuring accountability for financial crimes and safeguarding the rights of appellants in the criminal justice system. The implications extend to the need for lower courts to adequately address evidentiary conflicts and the judicial discretion applied in sentencing.
