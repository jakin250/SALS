Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note: Sanele Zondo v The State (541/11) [2012] ZASCA 27

## F – Facts

The case under consideration is an appeal against sentence only, originating from the conviction of Sanele Zondo in the Regional Court, Durban, on December 5, 2003, for one count of robbery with aggravating circumstances and a second count of attempted robbery ([1]–[2]). The appellant was sentenced to the minimum prescribed period of 15 years' imprisonment on the first count, as per Section 51(2) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, and an additional 10 years on count two, with 5 years of that sentence running concurrently with the first ([1]). Thus, the effective term of imprisonment amounted to 20 years.

The charges arose from an incident on September 7, 2003, during which Zondo, alongside his co-assailants, accosted and robbed two Philippine nationals who were en route to attend a conference. Zondo was armed with a knife, which he brandished during the robbery of the female complainant, carrying off her handbag. The male complainant successfully resisted the attempted robbery of his backpack. The incident was reported to the police shortly thereafter, leading to Zondo's apprehension ([3]). Along with one of his co-assailants, Zondo was pursued by police and subsequently arrested. The stolen handbag was recovered shortly after, containing personal belongings that were identified by the female victim ([4]).

Following the High Court's dismissal of Zondo's appeal against the original sentence, he appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal, seeking a reduction in the overall effective term of imprisonment, specifically requesting that the entirety of the sentence on the second count run concurrently with that on the first ([2]–[3]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issue revolves around whether the trial court exercised its sentencing discretion correctly, particularly regarding the balance of mitigating versus aggravating factors in Zondo’s case ([5]–[6]). The appellate court needed to ascertain whether there was a material misdirection by the trial court in imposing a sentence of 20 years, which Zondo contended was excessively harsh given the specifics of the incident ([6]).

## R – Rules

The legal principles guiding the court in this appeal include the discretionary power of a trial court to impose sentences, which the appellate court may only interfere with if it identifies a material misdirection ([5]). The court noted the importance of considering both mitigating and aggravating circumstances when sentencing, emphasizing that a balance between these factors is essential to achieve a just result ([6]). The precedent set forth in **S v Malgas** reiterates that a sentence must not be so severe as to be disproportionate to the crime committed and that certain sentences may be reserved for more heinous offenses ([6]).

Moreover, the court underscored that the robbery, characterized as handbag snatching with a knife and without physical injuries to the complainants, may not warrant an effective sentence of 20 years ([8]). Sentences imposed should align with the nature of the offense, as reiterated in **Muller v The State** and **S v Johaar**, emphasizing proportionality and the context of the crime ([9]).

## A – Application

In applying the law to the facts of the case, the Supreme Court of Appeal examined the specifics of the robbery for which Zondo had been convicted. The court noted that the complainant had suffered no physical injuries, and the crime was executed hastily and without the intent for severe harm. Consequently, this lack of long-term trauma or loss to the complainant constituted a mitigating factor that had not been appropriately weighed by the trial court against the aggravating circumstances, such as the use of a knife ([6]–[10]).

The court concluded that the original 20-year sentence was excessive, particularly given that the offenses were part of a continuous incident and the nature of the attempted robbery did not reflect a high level of criminal sophistication. As such, the court agreed to recast the sentence on count one to 12 years and confirmed the sentence on count two, directing that they run concurrently, thereby resulting in an effective term of 12 years' imprisonment ([11]).

## C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal held that the sentencing discretion was not exercised judiciously by the trial court, as it failed to balance mitigating and aggravating factors effectively. The appellate court thus intervened, reducing the sentence to 12 years' imprisonment for the robbery count while confirming the sentence for attempted robbery and ordering that the sentences run concurrently. The ruling serves as an important reminder of the necessity for courts to consider the totality of circumstances and maintain proportionality in sentencing ([11]). The case exemplifies the appellate court's role in ensuring that justice is appropriately served while upholding the principles of fair sentencing in South African law.
