Pick ’n Pay Retailers (Pty) Ltd v Pillay (900/2020) [2021] ZASCA 125

The case revolves around the injury sustained by the respondent, Cherylene Pillay, when she was struck by an automated boom gate (the "boom") at the Pick ’n Pay Hypermarket in Durban North on December 10, 2015.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 125

F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–9])

The case revolves around the injury sustained by the respondent, Cherylene Pillay, when she was struck by an automated boom gate (the "boom") at the Pick ’n Pay Hypermarket in Durban North on December 10, 2015. The boom was designed to regulate egress from a parking area intended for individuals with special needs and parents with small children. The boom, which comprised a three-meter aluminum pole, suddenly descended from a vertical position and hit Ms Pillay on the head, causing severe injuries including concussion and a cervical spine strain.

Ms Pillay, along with her colleague Geraldine Leach, had concluded their shopping and were walking towards Ms Leach's vehicle located in the shopping center's parking area. Unbeknownst to Ms Pillay, the boom was descending, as she had not been attentive to her surroundings. Following the incident, Ms Pillay initiated legal proceedings in the Durban Magistrate's Court against Pick ’n Pay, claiming that the company had been negligent in not providing appropriate safety measures around the boom.

The defendant denied negligence and argued that they had established reasonable systems for safety, further alleging that the plaintiff was contributorily negligent for her inattentiveness. The Magistrate's Court ruled in favor of Pick ’n Pay, stating that Ms Pillay had not adequately proven her injury or the defendant's negligence. However, the decision was appealed to the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court, which found that the defendant had failed to foresee the risk of harm and thus was liable, imposing a 60% contribution towards Ms Pillay's damages.

I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–9])

The primary issue at stake was whether Pick ’n Pay Retailers (Pty) Ltd was negligent in its operation of the boom that struck Ms Pillay. This encompasses several sub-issues:

  1. Was it reasonably foreseeable to Pick ’n Pay that the boom could descend unexpectedly and cause injury to pedestrians?
  2. Did the absence of adequate warnings or safety measures constitute negligence on the part of the defendant?
  3. Was Ms Pillay’s conduct, specifically her inattentive walking pattern, sufficient to warrant a finding of contributory negligence?

R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 13–15], [27])

The court examined the established principles of negligence, chiefly those outlined in Kruger v Coetzee 1966 (2) SA 428 (A), where it defined negligence as arising where:

  • A reasonable person in the defendant's position would foresee the possibility of their actions causing injury to others.
  • The reasonable person would take preventative measures against such harm, and if they failed to do so, they would be held liable.

In addition, the court noted Sea Harvest Corporation 2000 (1) SA 827 (SCA) for the proposition that while categorizing negligence may assist courts, the ultimate determination rests on whether the particular conduct did not meet the standard of a reasonable person in the circumstances of the case.

Furthermore, reliance was placed on the Australian decision in Livsey v Australian National Car Parks Pty Ltd, where the risk associated with boom gates was considered but determined not significant due to the nature of the boom's operation, although this was distinguished from the facts in the current case.

A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 10–26])

The court analyzed the circumstances surrounding the boom’s operation and the foreseeability of harm. Counsel for the defendant conceded that the risk of the boom striking a pedestrian was foreseeable, a significant acknowledgment that favored the plaintiff's stance. Ultimately, the court looked to the circumstances surrounding the incident, particularly focusing on the high pedestrian traffic in the vicinity of the boom, which was known to coincide with the route frequently taken by patrons.

Despite the arguments posed by Pick ’n Pay that the injury was negligible due to the boom's lightweight design, the court was not persuaded. Evidence emerged that prior to the incident, another individual had been struck by the boom, providing a historical context that underscored the potential risks associated with its operation. The absence of appropriate warning signage at the time of Ms Pillay's injury compounded the defendant's negligence, given that a cautionary measure sign had only been implemented after the prior incident.

Further, the court scrutinized the testimony of Mr. Shalendra Parbhoo, the project manager involved in the installation and servicing of the boom, indicating that the boom's design allowed it to reverse upon contact with a significant object, but did not test it under conditions that would be representative of actual pedestrian encounters. This oversight highlighted a failure to adopt adequate safety precautions in a context where predictably inattentive pedestrians were present.

The court ultimately concluded that a reasonable person in the situation of the defendant should have implemented enhanced measures to avert risk to the public, and the failure to do so constituted negligence, dismissing the appeal and upholding the High Court's decision.

C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 28])

The Supreme Court of Appeal unanimously dismissed Pick ’n Pay’s appeal, affirming the lower court’s conclusion that the defendant's conduct fell short of the standard expected of a reasonable person. The ratio decidendi centered on the foreseeability of harm in a public shopping context, emphasizing the need for adequate safety measures when operating potentially dangerous equipment.

The implications of this ruling extend beyond the immediate case, demonstrating the necessity for businesses operating in public spaces to proactively assess and mitigate risks that could result in injuries to patrons. In this instance, with the automatic descent of the boom posing a legitimate threat and the establishment of a history of related incidents, the court reinforced the underlying principle that commercial establishments owe a duty of care not only to mitigate risks but also to implement appropriate safety measures and signage to ensure the safety of patrons.

The judgment serves as a precedent emphasizing the balance between the convenience of automated systems in public spaces and the responsibility of operators to ensure public safety, indicating a robust stance on negligence in cases involving public interactions with potentially hazardous environments.
