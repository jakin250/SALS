Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2009

### FIRAC Case Note: Mostert v The State (338/2009) [2009] ZASCA 171

#### F – Facts
The appellants, Jacobus Petrus Christiaan Mostert Snr and Jacobus Petrus Christiaan Mostert Jnr, operated a sugarcane farming business on the farm "Dadelvlak" in Barberton. Their farm was adjacent to the Lomati River, from which they abstracted water for irrigation purposes. The farm was situated within the Lomati Irrigation District, created under the Water Act 54 of 1956, with the Lomati Irrigation Board overseeing water management ([1]–[2]).

The appellants registered one pump station (pump-station 46), required to measure the volume of water abstracted. However, they constructed an unregistered second pump station (pump-station 46.1) without a monitoring system. Investigations revealed alterations to the electrical system of the main pump, designed to bypass the water flow monitoring ([3]–[4]). In April 2006, the appellants faced seven criminal charges, including fraud and theft, related to unauthorized water abstraction and tampering with the monitoring system ([4]–[5]).

While the High Court overturned most convictions, it upheld the appellants' appeal concerning two convictions but allowed the State’s cross-appeal on the fraud and theft counts, leading to the current appeal ([6]–[7]).

#### I – Issues
The case presented several legal questions, notably:

1. Can the appellants be prosecuted for common law offences of fraud and theft in the light of the National Water Act of 1998, which provides statutory offences related to water management?
2. Was it legally tenable for the appellants to be charged under the National Water Act given that the Lomati Irrigation Board was still operational under the repealed 1956 Water Act at the time of the alleged offences?
3. Whether water abstracted from a public resource can be the subject of a theft charge due to its classification under Roman-Dutch law as res extra patrimonium ([6]–[24]).

#### R – Rules
The relevant legal frameworks include:

1. **Water Act 54 of 1956** - This Act allowed for the creation of irrigation boards and regulated public water usage for agricultural purposes. It established the jurisdiction of boards and their powers in relation to water management ([9]–[10]).

2. **National Water Act 36 of 1998** - This Act repealed the earlier Water Act, altering the legal framework for water resources: it prohibited the use of water without licensing and established a public trust over the country’s water resources ([10]–[11]).

3. **Common Law Principles (Fraud and Theft)** - The elements of fraud require misrepresentation, deliberate deception, and resultant prejudice. Theft under South African law requires the appropriation of property capable of being owned ([22]–[24]).

#### A – Application
Applying the law to the case, the Court observed the following:

1. **Prosecution Under the National Water Act** - The Court concluded that the continued operation of the Lomati Irrigation Board did not invalidate the applicability of the National Water Act. The preservation of the Board's functions did not equate to the 1956 Act remaining in force, allowing the State to prosecute for violations under the 1998 Act ([14]–[17]).

2. **Common Law Offences** - The Court found no legislative intent to exclude prosecutions for common law offences. The mere overlap of elements between statutory and common law offences did not preclude concurrent charges. The High Court’s earlier finding was determined to be incorrect ([18]–[21]).

3. **Theft of Water** - The Court acknowledged Roman law principles regarding the classification of public water as res communes, incapable of ownership. Hence, while the appellants committed statutory offences under the Act, they could not be charged with theft under common law. The legal argument that the 1998 Act's changes did not transform the classification of public water was accepted. Consequently, the theft conviction was found to be unsustainable, supporting the conclusion that the appellants could only be convicted of fraud ([22]–[24]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court's dismissal of the theft charge while reinstating the fraud conviction based on the deception over water abstraction figures, which was found to have caused prejudice to the complainant. The appellants were sentenced to a fine of R20,000 or a 12-month prison term, wholly suspended conditional on no further fraud convictions. The appeals related to convictions under counts 3 and 4 for tampering and unlawful abstraction were similarly dismissed ([36]).

The case highlights the tension between statutory and common law in relation to water resource management, cementing the relevant legislative framework while reinforcing principles of fraud against acts of misrepresentation that affect regulatory bodies ([36]). The implications extend to practices within agricultural sectors regarding water use and compliance with existing laws.
