Snowy Owl Properties 284 (Pty) Ltd v Celliers and Another (1295/2021) [2023] ZASCA 37

[1] The appellant, Snowy Owl Properties 284 (Pty) Ltd, was the registered owner of immovable properties known as the remainder of the Farm Fagolweni No 16156 and the Farm Ntabankosi No 14594, situated in KwaZulu-Natal.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 37

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

[1] The appellant, Snowy Owl Properties 284 (Pty) Ltd, was the registered owner of immovable properties known as the remainder of the Farm Fagolweni No 16156 and the Farm Ntabankosi No 14594, situated in KwaZulu-Natal. The first respondent, Mr. Norman Celliers, served as the chairman of the second respondent, Mziki Shareblock Limited, which was the registered owner of Portion 1 of Farm Fagolweni. The parties were bound by a notarial deed of servitude executed in 1990, granting reciprocal rights to traverse the land for the purpose of viewing wild game ([paras 2-4]).

[2] Following a series of disputes regarding the interpretation and enforcement of the servitude agreement, Snowy Owl Properties brought an application for an interdict against Mziki and its members to prevent them from traversing the properties during restricted hours as outlined in the servitude ([paras 5-6]).

[3] On 24 May 2019, the Full Court of the KwaZulu-Natal Division ruled in favor of the appellant, granting it an interdict preventing the respondents from traversing its lands outside of specified conditions. The High Court later dismissed an application for contempt of court launched by Snowy Owl Properties, which alleged that the respondents failed to comply with the Full Court’s order. Consequently, the appellant sought leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 7-8]).

I – Issues

[4] The central issue before the Supreme Court was whether the respondents, specifically Mr. Celliers and Mziki, were in contempt of the Full Court’s order, given the alleged non-compliance and communications that appeared to undermine that order ([paras 21-22]).

[5] A secondary issue presented was the procedural adequacy of the evidence related to the disparaging remarks made by Mr. Celliers about the judiciary, which purportedly aimed at scandalizing the court's authority ([paras 11-12]).

R – Rules

[6] The legal framework regarding contempt of court in South Africa, primarily established through cases like Fakie NO v CCII Systems (Pty) Ltd and Pheko and Others v Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality (No 2), indicates that the applicant must prove the existence of a court order, the alleged contemnor’s awareness of that order, and failure to comply ([paras 23-25]).

[7] The Supreme Court emphasized the distinction between coercive and punitive contempt of court orders. The standard of proof in civil matters is on a balance of probabilities, while a higher standard applies in criminal matters ([para 25]).

A – Application

[8] The Supreme Court determined that the respondents had indeed failed to comply with the Full Court’s order. The evidence presented showed that members of Mziki, including vehicles marked with Mziki logos, traversed the appellant’s land after sunset, contravening the order ([paras 26-27]).

[9] Furthermore, Mr. Celliers’ communications during a members’ meeting and subsequent messages demonstrated a deliberate intent to undermine court authority. His remarks indicated a lack of respect for the court’s order, effectively encouraging non-compliance among Mziki members ([paras 26-28]).

[10] The High Court's approach, as noted, was criticized for applying a criminal standard of proof, thereby misplacing the burden during contempt proceedings. The Supreme Court clarified that, once the appellant established the necessary elements of contempt, the burden shifted to the respondents to demonstrate reasonable doubt regarding their compliance and intentions ([paras 28-29]).

C – Conclusion

[11] The Supreme Court ruled in favor of Snowy Owl Properties, confirming that the respondents were in contempt of the Full Court's order. It emphasized that the integrity of court orders must be upheld to ensure the effective administration of justice ([para 29]).

[12] Consequently, the order of the High Court was set aside, and the Supreme Court mandated several remedial actions to ensure compliance, including the obligation on the respondents to implement rules preventing further non-compliance. Costs were awarded to the appellant on an attorney and client scale due to the respondents' wanton disregard for the court's authority ([paras 30-31]).

Significance: This case reinforces the importance of compliance with court orders and highlights the potential repercussions of actions that undermine judicial authority. It also illustrates the complexities surrounding the procedural treatment of contempt issues, clarifying the standards of proof and the responsibilities resting upon alleged contemnors.
