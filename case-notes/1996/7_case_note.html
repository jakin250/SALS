Constitutional Court

Case, Patrick and Inga; Curtis, Stephen Roy v. The Minister of Safety and Security and Others

Facts The case concerns the constitutional validity of section 2(1) of the Indecent or Obscene Photographic Matter Act, 37 of 1967 (the “Act”).

Constitutional Court 1996 CCT 20/95

FIRAC Method

Facts

The case concerns the constitutional validity of section 2(1) of the Indecent or Obscene Photographic Matter Act, 37 of 1967 (the “Act”). The Applicants, Patrick and Inga Case (CCT 20/95) and Stephen Roy Curtis (CCT 21/95), were charged in the Randburg Magistrates’ Court for possessing materials deemed obscene under this provision, specifically approximately 150 video cassettes in the Case household, and five cassettes with Curtis. Police seized these materials during various operations conducted in February 1993 ([1–2]).

Following their initial appearance on February 24, 1995, the Case Applicants applied for their proceedings to be postponed pending a determination of the constitutionality of section 2(1) of the Act, alleging it violated several constitutional rights under the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa Act, 200 of 1993 ([3]). The application was granted, leading to the referral to the Constitutional Court to assess the compatibility of the Act's provisions with constitutional safeguards ([4]).

Issues

Key issues before the court included:

  1. Whether section 2(1) of the 1967 Act conflicts with constitutional provisions concerning equality, privacy, freedom of expression, and the various intonations of these rights as enshrined in sections 8, 13, 15, 24, and 33 of the Constitution ([4–5]).
  2. The implications of the Act's broad definitions of indecent or obscene materials, including their consistency with the right to freedom of artistic creativity and expression ([4–5]).

Rules

The court was guided by significant legal precedents and constitutional provisions including:

  1. Section 15 of the Constitution protecting the right to freedom of expression.
  2. Section 33 outlining permissible limitations on fundamental rights, requiring that limitations be reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society.
  3. Previous cases establishing parameters for determining obscenity (e.g., Miller v California and R v Butler in international comparative law) and their relevance to the interpretation of domestic law ([6-20]).

Application

Mokgoro J addressed the intrinsic and extrinsic definitions of “indecent” and “obscene” as used in section 1 of the Act, recognizing that the legislative focus had been overly broad and encompassed constitutionally protected expressions. The court acknowledged historical complexities in defining obscenity, resulting in statutes that were vague, overbroad, and detrimentally expansive ([18-20]).

The examination of context shed light on the legislative intentions behind prohibiting materials considered offensive: a protection of morals that conflicted with free expression rights. Taking particular note of the chilling effect such overreaching legislation had on artistic expression, the court emphasized the necessity of a finely tuned balance between legislative objectives and constitutionally safeguarded rights ([61-79]).

Throughout the judgment, it was illustrated that the statutory definitions were not only overbroad but also vitiated by vagueness, leading the applicants to assert that the statute improperly encompassed a variety of innocuous material, thus violating their right to privacy and freedom of expression ([84-87]).

Didcott J, Langa J, and other justices highlighted practical implications, arguing that striking down section 2(1) would not create a legislative vacuum, as the 1974 Act would continue to address the broader issue of regulating sexually explicit content ([81-86]).

Conclusion

The court ultimately ruled that section 2(1) of the Indecent or Obscene Photographic Matter Act was unconstitutional. The judgment underscored the importance of maintaining a constitutional framework that respects the rights of individuals against unwarranted invasions stemming from overly broad legislative measures. The ruling reinforced foundational rights surrounding privacy and freedom of expression, implications that resonate throughout South African jurisprudence and set a precedent for the regulation of obscenity while safeguarding individual liberties ([89-91]).

Order: Section 2(1) of the Indecent or Obscene Photographic Matter Act is declared inconsistent with the Constitution and invalid from the date of the judgment ([89]).

This case note serves as a substantial outline of the critical aspects of the judgment, suitable for citation in academic and legal contexts and reflecting a synthesized understanding of constitutional implications regarding freedom of expression within the framework of South African law.
