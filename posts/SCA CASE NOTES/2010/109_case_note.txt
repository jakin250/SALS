Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

# FIRAC Case Note: Holcim (South Africa) (Pty) Ltd v Prudent Investors (Pty) Ltd [2010] ZASCA 109

## F - Facts
In May 2008, Holcim (South Africa) (Pty) Ltd ("the appellant") sought a declaration from the High Court regarding its entitlement to an "old order mining right" as defined in Schedule II of the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 ("the Act"). This came after the Act's implementation on May 1, 2004, which significantly impacted the mining licensing framework in South Africa ([paras 2-4]).

The appellant had held a mining licence (ML 3/1997) since 1997, allowing it to mine limestone across multiple farms, including Dudfield 35, Kalkfontein 77, Hibernia 52, and Bethlehem 75. Importantly, while the appellant conducted mining on Dudfield, it had not yet commenced operations on the properties owned by the first and second respondents (Prudent Investors (Pty) Ltd and Louis Hendrik Meyer) ([paras 5-10]).

The court a quo (High Court) held that the Transitional Arrangements within the Act did not afford the appellant the requisite security of tenure because it had not conducted mining operations on all the properties covered by its licence as of the date the Act took effect. Consequently, it found that the appellant possessed what was categorized as an "unused old order right," which had lapsed due to the failure to apply for a prospecting right within one year of the Act's implementation ([paras 3-12]).

## I - Issues
The primary legal issue in this appeal was whether the Transitional Arrangements in Schedule II of the Act secured an "old order mining right" for the appellant given that it did not conduct mining operations on all properties covered by its licence immediately before the Act took effect. The question further revolved around the interpretation of what constitutes ongoing mining operations under the definition provided in the Act ([para 1]).

## R - Rules
The relevant statutory framework consists predominantly of the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002, particularly Schedule II, which delineates transitional arrangements for existing mining operations. 

- **Definition**: An "old order mining right" is defined in Item 1 of Schedule II as any mining lease, license, or permission that was in force before the Act came into effect and for which mining operations were being conducted ([para 13]).

- **"Unused Old Order Right"**: Item 1 also defines this term, relevantly indicating that if no mining operations were being conducted before the Act took effect, rights were considered expired unless timely application was made for conversion ([para 11].

- **Security of Tenure**: The Act's fundamental objectives, as laid out in its preamble and in various sections, aim for equitable access to mineral resources and the protection of existing mining rights ([paras 28-30]).

## A - Application
In its judgment, the Supreme Court of Appeal highlighted that the interpretation of "old order mining rights" must focus not solely on the strict geographical limitations of separate legal entities but should also account for the realities of mining operations. The appellant argued that despite its non-engagement on certain properties covered by its licence, the ongoing operations on adjacent or contiguous properties constituted sufficient grounds to claim an "old order mining right" over the entire mining licence area ([paras 2-4, 29-30]).

The court took a purposive approach, distinguishing between "operations in respect of" the authorisation granted by the mining licence as opposed to physical operations conducted on each cadastral unit. It emphasized that as long as mining operations were being executed under the terms of a valid mining authorisation, section 4(1) warranted a broader interpretation that aligns with the Act’s objectives to ensure operational continuity and security of tenure ([paras 32-34]).

Furthermore, the court dismissed the notion that the absence of mining activities on the respondents' land prior to the Act's effective date rendered the appellant's rights invalid. The emphasis was placed on the continuation of operations across the wider mining area and the operational flexibility intrinsic to mining undertakings. It pointed out that practical considerations often dictate that mines commence operations on select areas while planning for future expansions ([paras 29-30, 42-43]).

## C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appellant was indeed entitled to an "old order mining right" that encompassed the properties belonging to the first and second respondents. The ruling affirmed that the respondents were directed to grant access to the appellant for mining purposes, invalidating the High Court’s earlier conclusions regarding the necessity of operations being conducted on every property area covered under a mining licence ([paras 41-45]). 

This decision serves as a pivotal interpretation of the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act, emphasizing the importance of context and the need for flexibility in operational frameworks, particularly relevant to the mining sector’s spatial dynamics. The ruling underscores the legislative intent to facilitate ongoing mining activities while transitioning to the new regulatory regime established by the Act, which can significantly impact mining operation sustainability in South Africa.
