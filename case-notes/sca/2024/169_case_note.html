FIRAC Case Note: The Body Corporate of San Sydney v Shivani Singh and Others (779/2023) [2024] ZASCA 169

F – Facts

This case concerns a sectional title scheme known as San Sydney, registered under the Sectional Titles Act 95 of 1986 (STA) and the Sectional Titles Schemes Management Act 8 of 2011 (STSMA). The appellant, The Body Corporate of San Sydney, sought to enforce an agreement made on May 11, 2018, to sell its right to extend the scheme to HF Property Investments (Pty) Ltd (HFP) ([paras 1–3]). The scheme initially comprised eight registered units, and the right to extend had been reserved to the developer, who eventually allowed this right to lapse ([paras 7–11]).

The appellant sought written consent from various owners, including the first respondent, Shivani Singh, before proceeding with the sale ([paras 4–5]). However, not all consents were granted, leading the appellant to initiate court proceedings to seek declarations against the respondents, who included other unit owners and mortgagees ([paras 5–6]). The KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court dismissed the appellant's application based on the interpretation that the agreement involved an alienation of common property requiring unanimous consent under the STSMA, thereby denying the appellant the remedy sought ([paras 5, 8]).

I – Issues

The legal questions arising from this case include:

  1. Whether the sale agreement constituted an alienation of common property, thus requiring consent under s 5(1)(a) of the STSMA and s 17(1) of the STA ([paras 8, 31]).
  2. Whether the consent required from the unit owners and mortgagees could be withheld without good cause in law, as stipulated by s 5(1)(b) of the STSMA ([paras 3, 16]).
  3. Whether the High Court correctly interpreted the laws governing the title scheme and the implications for the body corporate's authority to act ([paras 28, 32]).

R – Rules

The key legal rules applicable to this case derive from various provisions in the STA and STSMA:

  • Section 25(6) of the STA states that if the right to extend the scheme is not reserved by the developer, that right vests in the body corporate, which may then obtain a certificate of real right for extension ([para 10]).
  • Section 5(1)(b) of the STSMA mandates that any alienation of the right to extend the scheme requires the written consent of all owners and any mortgagees, who cannot unreasonably withhold such consent ([para 14]).
  • The Alienation of Land Act informs the criteria whereby common property can only be alienated or otherwise dealt with upon unanimous resolution of the members of the body corporate ([para 31]).

A – Application

In reviewing the appellant's claims, the Supreme Court of Appeal assessed whether the transfer of the right to extend the scheme constituted a sale of common property. The Court differentiated between the alienation of a part of common property and the alienation of the right to extend, concluding that the latter does not equate to an actual physical transfer of that property ([paras 31–43]).

The Court noted that the distinction is crucial: while the extension may dilute individual ownership shares in the common property, it does not involve their transfer as understood under common property laws. Hence, it determined that no unanimous resolution was necessary for the body corporate to cede its right of extension ([para 43]).

Additionally, the Court ruled that the withholding of consent by Singh and other respondents lacked good cause since the information provided to them prior to their decision significantly informed the context of their dissent. From the facts available, it appeared the body corporate could adequately justify the decision-making process — failing to present material facts and ongoing concerns to the shareholders properly ([paras 56–59]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal partially. The Court ruled that the respondents must provide the required consent for the body corporate to obtain the necessary certificate of real right to extend the scheme, consistent with the laws outlined in the STA and STSMA ([paras 60]).

The ruling highlights the importance of transparent communication between sectional title bodies and individual owners. Furthermore, the decision indicates that while it is vital for owners to be adequately informed about major decisions impacting their property interests, the applicability of legal requirements concerning consent must also flexibly reflect the circumstances, including previous compliance measures and existing legal interpretations ([paras 57–59]).

In conclusion, both the body corporate and the respondents were directed to bear their own costs, underscoring the absence of a clear prevailing party in this complex dispute ([paras 60]). The implications of this judgment reinforce the necessity of cooperation and open dialogues within sectional titles to preclude similar disputes in the future, thus enhancing the communal living experience impacted by statutory constraints.
