Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

# Case Note Using FIRAC Method: Scoin Trading (Pty) Ltd v Bernstein, Gillies Martin No [2010] ZASCA 160

---

## F – Facts

The appeal arises from a decision of the KwaZulu-Natal High Court, where Scoin Trading (Pty) Ltd ("the appellant") sought payment for the balance on a purchase of a rare ZAR Een Pond Overstamp gold coin from Gregory John Till ("the deceased") ([1]–[2]). The total purchase price was set at R1,950,000, of which the deceased paid a deposit of R200,000. A dispute emerged regarding whether the balance was to be paid by 31 December 2007, or contingent upon the sale of the deceased’s property ([3]).

Tragically, the deceased died on 16 November 2007, 15 days before the agreed payment date ([4]). The executor of the deceased's estate, represented by Bernstein, Gillies Martin No ("the respondent"), acknowledged the liability for the purchase price but contested the obligation to pay interest ([4]–[5]). The High Court granted the appellant the balance but refused to award interest, prompting the appeal ([5]).

The primary legal contention involves two aspects: the factual disagreement regarding the payment deadline and the legal implication of the deceased's death on the liability for interest ([6]).

## I – Issues

The main legal issues presented in this appeal are twofold:
1. Whether the balance of the purchase price was due by 31 December 2007, as claimed by the appellant, or only when the proceeds of the deceased's property sale became available ([6]).
2. The legal effect of the debtor's death—specifically, whether it absolves the estate from liability for mora interest owed as a consequence of the deceased being in default ([10]).

## R – Rules

The legal framework applicable to this case includes principles governing contractual obligations, as well as specific provisions under the Administration of Estates Act No. 66 of 1965 and the Prescribed Rate of Interest Act 55 of 1975.

1. **Mora Interest**: Defined as the delay in fulfilling a contractual obligation, which under South African law does not require proof of fault or wrongful conduct on the part of the debtor ([11]). Interest is designed to compensate the creditor for damages incurred due to default.
   
2. **Culpability in Mora**: The argument that mora interest arises only in cases of culpability relates to the notion that default must be ‘wrongful’ ([15]–[18]). However, in breach of contract claims, the creditor must show either that the debtor was in mora or that an express obligation to pay existed ([18]).

3. **Executor Obligations**: Per the Administration of Estates Act, an executor is responsible for settling the debts of the deceased only after the liquidation and distribution account is confirmed ([23]).

## A – Application

1. **Determining Payment Deadline**: The court assessed the evidence, concluding that the obligation to pay the balance was indeed due by 31 December 2007, anchoring this on the email exchanges which indicated a clear agreement to this effect. The deceased’s response, albeit mentioning expected funds from property sales, did not modify the clear payment term set ([7]–[10]).

2. **Death and Liability for Interest**: The core of the argument hinged on whether the death of the debtor negated the obligation to pay interest. The respondent contended that since the debtor died before the payment became due, there was no culpability and thus no mora interest should be owed ([10], [16]). The court rejected this, emphasizing that mora arises automatically under the agreed terms of payment once the due date arrives, regardless of the debtor's ability to pay due to unforeseen circumstances like death ([18]–[28]).

Previous judgments cited in the case, such as *RB Ranchers*, were distinguished based on their specific factual context, where liability for mora interest was predicated on differing conditions which did not apply here ([21]).

In analyzing the executor's position, the court noted that while the deceased’s death alone does not prevent the obligation to pay interest from existing, the claim for interest is automatic as part of the contractual obligations incurred prior to death. The respondent's position failed in logic since the acknowledged debt implied the obligation to also meet any accrued interest ([27]).

Therefore, the court concluded that liability for interest does not depend on wrongful conduct by the debtor but emerges as part of the contractual obligations upheld even after death ([18], [29]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, confirming the appellant’s right to receive both the principal sum and the interest from the estate of the deceased. The decision reinforced the principle that the death of a debtor does not extinguish liability for interest when the debt is clearly established and due ([1], [30]).

The court's findings reaffirm that contractual obligations persist beyond the parties' lives unless expressly stated otherwise. Ultimately, the appeal was successful, with costs awarded to the appellant, illustrating the importance of clarity in contractual timelines and obligations, and the enduring nature of contractual relationships in death scenarios ([30]).

---

This case serves as a critical reference point regarding the obligations of estates and the implications of debtor death on financial liabilities within South African law. The ruling clarifies that interests accrue as a part of the contractual landscape, emphasizing creditor protection during unforeseen debtor incapacitation.
