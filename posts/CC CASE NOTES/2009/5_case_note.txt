# Case Note: Johncom Media Investments Ltd v M & Others

**Court**: Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Case Number**: CCT 08/08  
**Judgment Date**: 17 March 2009  
**Citation**: [2009] ZACC 5  

## F – Facts  
The case presents an intricate conflict between the right to freedom of expression and the rights to privacy and dignity, framed within the context of a divorce proceeding. The crux of the dispute arose when Mr. D, after a marital separation from Ms. M since 1995, sought to contest the legitimacy of paternity regarding their son, PD. He initiated legal action in 2001 against them, claiming damages and requesting a declaration that he was not PD's biological father ([paras 3-5]).

The matter attracted media attention, specifically from The Sunday Times, owned by Johncom Media Investments Ltd (the applicant), which intended to publish an article based on the pending legal issues. However, an urgent interdict was sought by Ms. M and PD, who argued that publication would infringe upon their constitutional rights to privacy and dignity as outlined in Section 12 of the Divorce Act 70 of 1979 (the Divorce Act). This section prohibits public disclosure of particulars arising from divorce proceedings ([paras 6-7]) and was subsequently declared unconstitutional by the High Court on 11 February 2008, primarily on grounds of overbreadth concerning the right to freedom of expression protected by Section 16 of the South African Constitution ([paras 10-13]).

The High Court's decision to invalidate Section 12 necessitated the engagement of the Constitutional Court for confirmation, which would evaluate both the constitutional validity of the provision and the nature of the remedy ([para 15]).

## I – Issues  
The Constitutional Court was tasked with confirming whether Section 12 of the Divorce Act was unconstitutional and further, whether the order for constitutional invalidity was just and equitable as required under Section 172(1)(b) of the Constitution ([paras 15-16]).

## R – Rules  
The relevant legal principles arise from two sections of the South African Constitution:

1. **Section 16**: Defines the right to freedom of expression, encompassing various forms of communication and establishing parameters for limitations on that right ([para 20]).
   
2. **Section 36**: Provides the framework for justifiable limitations on rights enshrined in the Bill of Rights, stipulating that such limitations must be reasonable, necessary, and proportionate to the purpose served ([para 36]).

The judgment also refers to precedents and provisions regarding freedom of the press and public interest. The balancing of competing constitutional rights—freedom of expression versus privacy and dignity—is a critical aspect of this examination.

## A – Application  
The Court's analysis centered on whether Section 12 constituted an unjustifiable limitation on freedom of expression. The Court employed a two-stage test to ascertain:

1. **Does the provision limit a constitutional right?** The Court determined that Section 12 indeed limited media expression by prohibiting any publication concerning divorce proceedings, which could encompass non-sensitive information ([para 23]).

2. **Is the limitation reasonable and justifiable?** The Court employed a proportionality test to evaluate the necessity and appropriateness of the legislative measure in serving its intended purpose—protecting privacy and dignity in divorce proceedings. It concluded that the absolute prohibition imposed by Section 12 was overly broad and not sufficiently tailored to meet its legitimate aims. The Court posited that alternatives existed which could protect privacy without impinging excessively on freedom of expression, such as a focused prohibition on revealing identities rather than all proceedings ([paras 29-31]).

The amicus curiae, Media Monitoring Project, contributed substantive insights, emphasizing the broader implications for media freedom and public access to information, which further supported the case against the blanket prohibition found in Section 12.

## C – Conclusion  
The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court's declaration of invalidity regarding Section 12 of the Divorce Act, asserting that its provisions were unconstitutional due to their overbroad character and lack of a proportionate relationship to the protected rights. The Court determined that the limitation on freedom of expression could not be justified under Section 36.

The ruling requires that any publication regarding the identities of individuals involved in divorce proceedings is generally prohibited, endorsing a framework that seeks to balance confidentiality with the principle of openness in judicial proceedings. The Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development was directed to pay the applicant's costs in both instances, reflecting the legal principle that the applicant should not bear the burden of challenging the unconstitutional provision ([paras 44-45]).

This case underscores the delicate tension between two fundamental rights in the South African context and reiterates the judiciary's role in safeguarding constitutional freedoms while protecting vulnerable parties in sensitive legal matters.
