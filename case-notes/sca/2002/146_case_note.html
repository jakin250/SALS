Supreme Court of Appeal

Levack v Regional Magistrate, Wynberg

The initial application for review was dismissed by Davis J in June 1999, and subsequent leave to appeal was granted by the Supreme Court of Appeal in October 1999.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2002

F - Facts

The case involves an appeal from the decision of the Cape High Court regarding an order made by a Regional Magistrate under section 37 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977. Five accused individuals (the appellants), charged with drug-related offences in the Wynberg Regional Court, were required to provide voice samples for comparison with recorded conversations held by the State ([paras 1–2]). This order was made in March 1998, and the accused had not yet entered pleas due to subsequent legal challenges. The appellants sought review of this order in the High Court, arguing that it breached their rights concerning self-incrimination and was improperly granted ([paras 2–3]).

The initial application for review was dismissed by Davis J in June 1999, and subsequent leave to appeal was granted by the Supreme Court of Appeal in October 1999. Meanwhile, one of the accused had died, and two others were untraceable, resulting in their appeals being dismissed ([paras 3–4]). The court noted significant delays in the proceedings, citing that the case brought discredit to the justice system ([para 5]).

I - Issues

The primary legal issues presented before the court were:

  1. Whether the requirement to provide voice samples fell within the ambit of "characteristic or distinguishing feature" as articulated in section 37 of the Criminal Procedure Act [paras 7, 24].
  2. Whether the order for the compulsory submission of voice samples breached the appellants' right against self-incrimination and their right to a fair trial [paras 15–22].
  3. Whether the Magistrate had the authority to grant such an order under the relevant provisions of the law, specifically section 37(1) and section 37(3) [paras 24–26].

R - Rules

The pertinent statutory provision under discussion is Section 37 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which provides police and courts with broad authority to ascertain bodily features of arrested individuals. It specifies that any police official may take prints and photographs, make the accused available for identification, and ascertain if the body has distinguishing or identifying features ([paras 1, 7]).

The constitutional context includes protections against self-incrimination and the right to a fair trial as articulated in section 35 of the Constitution of South Africa, which guarantees that no person should be compelled to give self-incriminating evidence and everyone has the right to a fair trial ([paras 15, 18]).

Prior case law, including S v Zuma and Matemba, was referenced to clarify the legal standards concerning the compulsion to provide certain types of evidence ([paras 17–19]).

A - Application

The court began by establishing that the voice qualifies as a "characteristic or distinguishing feature" of a person, as defined by the Concise Oxford Dictionary. It maintained that the voice, being a sound produced by the human body, indisputably falls within the statutory provisions of section 37 ([paras 8–13]).

The appellants argued that the requirement to produce voice samples violated their rights against self-incrimination and fair trial rights. However, the court clarified that compelling an accused to provide a voice sample did not equate to self-incrimination. The right against self-incrimination protects individuals from being forced to provide testimonial evidence, while a voice sample is considered ‘autoptic evidence’ ([paras 15–24]).

On interpreting section 37, the court stated that it allows the police to require individuals to submit samples, and the court can supplement this by issuing a compliant order. The court clarified that both police and judicial orders coexist and reinforce each other ([paras 25–27]). The order by the Magistrate did not present a gross irregularity warranting intervention by the Supreme Court.

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appeal by the first two appellants was dismissed due to their lack of prosecution. The application for condonation by the remaining appellants was also dismissed. The court reaffirmed the Magistrate's authority to compel the submission of voice samples under section 37, maintaining that the voice constitutes an identifying feature and does not infringe upon the rights to silence or fair trial provisions.

The decision thus sets an important precedent regarding the extent of police powers under the Criminal Procedure Act and clarifies the legal boundaries concerning self-incrimination and fair trial rights ([paras 1, 24–27]). The judgment emphasizes the need for swift action in criminal proceedings to preserve the integrity of the justice system.
