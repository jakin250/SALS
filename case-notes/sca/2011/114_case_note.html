Levin v Levin (644/09) [2011] ZASCA 114

The contested will was lodged and accepted by the Master of the High Court, with the second, third, and fourth respondents nominated as co-executors ([paras 1–2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2011 [2011] ZASCA 114

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

This case revolves around the validity of a will executed by the deceased, Mrs. Minnie ‘Minna’ Breslawsky (referred to as the deceased), dated 4 August 2002, following her death on 19 October 2002 at the age of 107 ([paras 1–2]). The appellants, Larry Ivan Levin and Stephanie Levenberg, are children of Freida Levin, the first respondent, who was closely connected with the deceased during her life ([paras 1–2]). The deceased had executed previous wills, one of which dated 8 August 2001, which allocated her assets significantly differently compared to the disputed will. The latter divided her estate among her children and grandchildren ([paras 3–4]).

The contested will was lodged and accepted by the Master of the High Court, with the second, third, and fourth respondents nominated as co-executors ([paras 1–2]). The appellants challenged the will on two principal grounds: firstly, that the signature on the will was not that of the deceased, and secondly, that it did not comply with the witnessing requirements as per the Wills Act 7 of 1953 ([paras 4–5]). The High Court dismissed the challenge, concluding that the appellants failed to meet the burden of proof required to invalidate the will ([para 5]).

The findings involved examination of evidence from various witnesses concerning the execution and validity of the will along with testimonies regarding the deceased's mental capacity and physical condition ([para 7–9]). A handwriting expert's testimony suggested the disputed document might be a forgery, although conflicting evidence raised doubts about this claim ([para 24]).

I – Issues

The central issue to be determined by the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the disputed will was executed in a manner compliant with section 2(1)(a) of the Wills Act 7 of 1953, which outlines the formalities required for a valid will ([para 6]). The sub-issues included whether the signature on the will was authentic and whether the required witnessing conditions were satisfied. Additionally, tangential issues addressed the procedural conduct of the appellants in relation to the incurred costs of litigation ([paras 28–30]).

R – Rules

The pertinent provision under scrutiny was section 2(1)(a) of the Wills Act 7 of 1953, which states that:

  1. No will executed on or after January 1, 1954, shall be valid unless:
  • The will is signed at the end by the testator.
  • The signature is made in the presence of two competent witnesses present at the same time.
  • Such witnesses attest the will in the presence of the testator and of each other.

Additional legal principles derived from common law and previous case law standards also guided the court's interpretation of testamentary intentions, particularly the burden of proof in establishing validity claims ([paras 27–28]).

A – Application

In assessing the evidence presented, the court engaged critically with the testimonies of the witnesses regarding the execution of the will. Testimonies supported the position that conditions for signing in the presence of witnesses were met, despite contradictions surrounding the exact timeline of events ([para 18–26]). The credibility of the handwriting expert's findings was undermined by the subsequent presentation of evidence demonstrating substantial improvement in the deceased’s health and abilities, due to a cataract operation performed prior to the signing of the will ([para 24]).

The court found the will's disposition of the estate logical, intending to bring peace among the family, aligning with the deceased's purported wishes. Notably, the simplicity and fairness of the will's terms suggested a genuine testamentary intent that survived the challenge ([paras 22–26]).

The court also scrutinized the inconsistency displayed by the appellants throughout the litigation, particularly how their actions post-litigation initiation were influenced by their knowledge of the deceased's mental acuity and the likelihood of a valid will existing ([paras 28–30]). Consequently, the appellants' claim was deemed ill-founded as it did not withstand the scrutiny of existing laws concerning testamentary formalities.

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the validity of the disputed will despite the appellants' challenges ([para 27]). The costs were awarded against the appellants, given their conduct which the court characterized as unreasonable, thus not justifying the estate bearing the litigation expenses ([paras 28–31]). This decision underscored the broader implications concerning testamentary dispositions, the importance of adhering to legislative requirements for wills, and the consideration of familial relationships in the context of inheritance.

The ruling reaffirms the necessity of procedural integrity in the execution of wills and the challenges of evaluating testamentary intent amidst familial disputes, thereby reinforcing the standards and expectations set forth in the Wills Act.
