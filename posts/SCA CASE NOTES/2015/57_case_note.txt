Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

### FIRAC Case Note: Thulamela Municipality & another v T Tshivhase & others (78/2014)

#### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–4])

The appellants, Thulamela Municipality and its municipal manager, exercised executive jurisdiction in the Thohoyandou area ([1]). The first respondent, Chief Thovhele Midiavhathu Prince Kennedy Tshivhase, a traditional leader, along with the Tshivhase Traditional Council, contested the municipality’s decision to alienate certain properties. On 23 February 2012, the municipality sold properties known as erven 22 and 26 Thohoyandou IA to Valuline (Pty) Ltd for R579,150 each, with the transfer executed on 11 June 2012 ([2]). Subsequently, on 18 February 2013, Tshivhase and the council launched a main application in the Limpopo High Court, seeking judicial review of the municipality's decisions to alienate these properties. They argued that such decisions were unconstitutional, illegal, and taken without due consultation or authorization ([3]).

The municipality’s response was delayed, and when it ultimately filed its affidavits, it contested the standing of Tshivhase and the council. The municipality raised a point in limine regarding locus standi, asserting that the respondents failed to demonstrate their status as traditional leaders as required under the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003 and the Limpopo Traditional Leadership and Institutions Act 6 of 2005 ([4]). The matter was subsequently heard by Mpshe AJ, who dismissed the exception without costs. The municipality then appealed against this dismissal ([5]).

#### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 6–8])

The appeal raised several critical legal questions. Chief among them was whether the dismissal of the exception was appealable as an interlocutory order, particularly given that the municipality contended that the dismissal effectively determined the respondents’ ability to proceed with their application ([6]). This presented a nuanced examination of the nature of interlocutory orders in judicial practice, especially regarding exceptions.

Moreover, the appeal raised concerns about the practical implications of the main application. Specifically, the court needed to assess whether the relief sought by Tshivhase and the council regarding the review and setting aside of the alienation decisions would be effective, given that the properties had already been transferred to Valuline prior to the initiation of the legal action ([8]).

#### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 7–9])

The court primarily referenced established legal principles regarding the appealability of interlocutory orders, particularly the dismissal of exceptions that do not pertain to jurisdiction. It cited the precedent in *Maize Board v Tiger Oats Ltd* where it was established that generally, a dismissal of an exception does not dispose of the substantive rights in the matter and is thus not appealable unless it decides a critical issue related to the main dispute. This echoed the principles articulated in earlier cases like *Pretoria Garrison Institutes v Danish Variety Products (Pty) Ltd* and *Cronshaw v Fidelity Guards Holdings (Pty) Ltd* ([7]).

Furthermore, the court noted the implications of property law as it relates to the transfer of immovable properties, referencing *Legator McKenna Inc & another v Shea & others* to substantiate the abstract theory of transfer—that once transfer has occurred, the rights associated with the property are effectively severed from the alienating municipality, raising questions about the relevance of the review sought ([8]).

#### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 7–9])

The court applied the legal rules surrounding interlocutory appeals with careful consideration of the implications of the order dismissing the exception. It analyzed whether the dismissal led to any final determinations that would impact the rights of Tshivhase and the council. The court concluded that dismissing an exception does not equate to making a final determination on the merits of the application, thereby reinforcing the stance that such orders are interlocutory and not subject to appeal ([7]).

In examining the main application, the court highlighted that the relief sought was fundamentally flawed due to the already completed transfer of ownership to Valuline. Even if the decisions to alienate were declared unconstitutional, the substantive issue of ownership had effectively moved beyond the purview of the court. Thus, the court inferred that the main application could be rendered academic, further complicating the issue ([8]).

#### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 8–9])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appeal was not viable due to the nature of the order from the High Court being interlocutory and not final on the substantive rights of the parties involved. The court held that such procedural dismissals are not appealable unless they irreparably affect the outcome of the main action ([9]).

Moreover, the court declined to provide a costs order, settling on a ruling that each party would bear its own costs, reflecting the impracticality of the main application and the academic nature of the questions raised therein. This decision emphasized the judiciary's role in managing litigation effectively, particularly when the pursued legal remedies might no longer hold relevance due to changed circumstances.

The implications of this ruling suggest a reaffirmation of the principles governing locus standi and the limitations of judicial review in cases where the circumstances have fundamentally altered since the initiation of legal action. It also reflects a careful balancing of the rights of traditional leaders against procedural propriety and the realities of property transfer under South African law.
