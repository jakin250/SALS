Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 1998

# Case Note: FIRAC - Holly Ndukuzakhemvelase v The State

## F – Facts

The appellant, Holly Ndukuzakhemvelase, was convicted in a Regional Court of two counts: possession of an AK-47 rifle (count 1) and possession of 29 live rounds of AK-47 ammunition (count 2). The trial court sentenced him to an effective term of 6 years in prison. Following the dismissal of his appeal by the court a quo, which upheld both his convictions and sentence, the appellant sought redress from the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 2–3]).

Evidence against the appellant primarily came from two police officers, Sergeant Johnston and Constable Luthuli, whose testimonies contained discrepancies. Nevertheless, the trial magistrate concluded that, despite these inconsistencies, Sergeant Johnston's evidence was credible enough to conclude that the appellant possessed the AK-47 rifle and ammunition at the time of the incident ([paras 3–4]). The appellant, for his part, denied possession and provided a version of events considered implausible, with the court noting that he had falsely denied the presence of the weapon found at the scene—a fact that undermined his credibility further ([para 4]).

The magistrate acknowledged the discrepancies in police testimonies but determined that they did not significantly detract from the overall reliability of Johnston's evidence, especially given his opportunity to observe the witnesses firsthand. The court also noted that the appellant's hypothesis that he was framed by Johnston was speculative and lacked evidential support ([paras 4–5]).

In relation to sentencing, the magistrate imposed a 5-year sentence for possession of the firearm and a separate 2-year sentence for the ammunition, with one year of this latter sentence set to run concurrently with the firearm sentence ([paras 6–7]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:

1. Whether the trial court misdirected itself in its assessment of the credibility of witness testimonies, particularly regarding the possession of the AK-47 and ammunition.
2. Whether the sentence imposed for count 2 (ammunition possession) ran contrary to established legal precedents regarding concurrent sentencing for related offences ([paras 4–5][7–9]).

## R – Rules

The relevant rules derived from the case encompass the following:

1. The principles set forth in **S v Hadebe and Others** [1997] 2 SACR 641 (SCA) regarding the presumption of correctness of a trial court's factual findings unless a misdirection is evident ([para 4]).
2. The established judicial practice regarding concurrent sentencing for offences that are closely intertwined, particularly when a firearm and its associated ammunition are involved ([pars 8–9]).
3. The seriousness of possessing an AK-47 in South Africa, particularly in the context of the ongoing violence and crime related to such weapons ([para 7]).

## A – Application

In reviewing the evidence, the Supreme Court of Appeal applied the rules regarding the assessment of credibility and presumption of correctness in trial court findings. It upheld the magistrate's evaluation of Johnston's evidence despite the discrepancies, concluding that the inconsistencies did not warrant a disturbance of the factual findings. The court reiterated that it was unpersuaded by the appellant's arguments that suggested the police had planted the evidence against him. The court noted that any assertions made about the evidence being fabricated lacked substantiation and were rooted in speculation, thus failing to counter the direct evidence presented by the police ([paras 5–6]).

Regarding sentencing, the court found that the magistrate had erred in not ordering the full 2-year sentence for ammunition possession to run concurrently with the 5-year sentence for the firearm. The rationale underlying concurrent sentencing was grounded in the principle that the crimes were interconnected, and it would be unjust to treat them separately in sentencing ([para 9]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal concerning the convictions, affirming the trial court's factual findings and the severity of the offences given the nature of the weapon involved ([para 8]). However, the court granted the appeal concerning the sentence for the ammunition possession, ordering that the entirety of the 2-year sentence run concurrently with the firearm possession sentence. The effective sentence thus remained at 6 years, but with adjustments reflecting established sentencing practices ([para 9]).

The decision underscored the importance of reliability in police testimony and the judicial system's responsibility to impose just and proportionate sentences, especially in cases involving dangerous firearms, contributing to the broader principles of criminal law in South Africa.
