Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

### Case Note: Helen Suzman Foundation v The Speaker of the National Assembly and Others

**Neutral Citation:** Helen Suzman Foundation v The Speaker of the National Assembly and Others (484/2021) [2023] ZASCA 6 (03 February 2023)  

**Court:** The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Coram:** Dambuza, Plasket and Mabindla-Boqwana JJA, Basson and Chetty AJJA

---

#### F – Facts
This case involves an application by the Helen Suzman Foundation (HSF) under section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 for reconsideration of a costs order. This followed a dismissal of HSF’s petition for leave to appeal against an order from the Gauteng Division of the High Court ([1]).

HSF initiated an urgent application for a declaratory order contending that the government, through its various entities including the Speaker of Parliament and the President, failed to fulfill constitutional obligations regarding legislation related to the Covid-19 pandemic ([2]). The urgency was predicated on the assertion that the government's reliance on the Disaster Management Act 57 of 2002 (DMA) compromised democratic and accountable governance, arguing that the establishment of specific legislative measures for pandemic management was necessary to uphold constitutional responsibilities ([3]).

The full court of the High Court found that the DMA could serve as a proper framework for managing such emergencies and dismissed HSF’s application, concluding that there was no binding obligation on Parliament to legislate specifically for Covid-19 ([4]). Notably, the High Court had initially ruled that each party would bear its own costs, awarding HSF the benefit of the Biowatch principle, recognizing its public interest litigation ([5]). However, upon denial of HSF's leave to appeal, the SCA imposed a costs order against HSF without rationale about the Biowatch principle ([6]).

#### I – Issues
The primary legal issues that arose from this appeal included:

1. Whether the application for reconsideration of the costs order was justifiable under exceptional circumstances as required by section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act.
2. Whether the SCA adequately applied the Biowatch principle regarding costs in constitutional litigation, and whether HSF was entitled to its protections against costs orders due to its constitutional litigation efforts ([1–6]).

#### R – Rules
The significant legal principles relevant to this case include:

1. **Superior Courts Act**: Section 17(2)(f) permits the President of the Supreme Court of Appeal to refer a decision for reconsideration if there are exceptional circumstances, particularly where grave injustice might occur ([7]).
2. **Biowatch Principle**: This principle asserts that an unsuccessful private litigant challenging State actions on constitutional grounds should not generally be liable for costs, acknowledging the added risks faced in such litigation. This principle, however, is not absolute and can be overridden if the litigation is found to be frivolous or vexatious ([8]).
3. **Separation of Powers Doctrine**: The court is barred from directly mandating the legislature on law-making, aligning with constitutional constraints on judicial power ([11]).

#### A – Application
The court analyzed the application under the parameters set forth by the Biowatch principle and the exceptional circumstances provision in section 17(2)(f). Dambuza JA noted that there was no evidence that the earlier decision awarding costs against HSF disregarded established legal principles or lacked discretion from the judges ([14]). 

The application to reconsider was positioned against the backdrop of prior high-profile cases including *Women’s Legal Centre Trust* and relevant principles from *Biowatch Trust*. The court underscored that while it recognized the public interest in HSF's litigation, the absence of merit would negate the protections afforded by the Biowatch framework, especially regarding actions post the dismissal of an appeal ([11], [14]). The court emphasized that the entirety of the HSF's litigation must be assessed holistically, including whether it had acted reasonably in proceeding with their appeal after the *Women’s Legal Centre Trust* ruling ([13], [15]).

The respondents argued that the costs order was justified based on HSF’s decision to pursue litigation despite the unfavorable context established by existing judgments ([12], [13]). They maintained that had HSF acted prudently, it would not have faced adverse costs. 

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed HSF's application for reconsideration of the costs order, ruling that it could not demonstrate that grave injustice would arise from the costs awarded against it. The court affirmed that the prior costs order adhered to the principles surrounding constitutional litigation and underscored that the Biowatch principle does not provide blanket immunity against costs for litigation deemed unreasonable or lacking in merit after careful consideration of the context ([15]).

The implications of this decision emphasize the stringent application of the Biowatch principle and the realities of litigating constitutional issues against state action. It serves as a reminder that litigants must approach constitutional challenges with a thorough understanding of related precedents and the broader implications of their actions in court. Thus, moving forward, HSF and similar public interest litigants may need to refine their strategic approaches to avoid costs implications when advancing their cases in constitutional matters ([7–15]).
