Supreme Court of Appeal

Knox D'Arcy Ltd v Jamieson and Others [1996] 2 SA 579 (A)

The background reveals that Jamieson and Jones departed Knox D'Arcy in mid-1991 to establish a competing management consultancy, Krestahague, shortly after which Ottley also left Knox D'Arcy to join them ([para 2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 1996 [1996] 2 SA 579 (A)

F – Facts

This case, originating from a petition for leave to appeal against the judgment of Stegmann J, involves Knox D'Arcy Limited and its related companies (collectively referred to as "Knox D'Arcy") who claimed that their former employees, including Jamieson, Jones, and Ottley (the respondents), engaged in unlawful competition after leaving their employ ([paras 1–4]).

The background reveals that Jamieson and Jones departed Knox D'Arcy in mid-1991 to establish a competing management consultancy, Krestahague, shortly after which Ottley also left Knox D'Arcy to join them ([para 2]). On 19 July 1991, in response to concerns of unlawful competition, Knox D'Arcy sought an urgent interdict, resulting in a temporary order preventing the respondents from soliciting clients, including significant companies such as South African Breweries and South African Druggists ([paras 4–6]).

Upon subsequent findings of asset concealment, Knox D'Arcy applied for a more permanent interdict to restrain the respondents from dissipating their assets. On 18 April 1994, Stegmann J granted a “draconian” Mareva-style injunction which effectively froze the respondents’ assets, pending further developments in the case ([paras 7–9]). The substantive matter concerning the merits was ultimately dismissed by Stegmann on 9 December 1994, leading to the present appeal ([paras 11–12]).

I – Issues

The core legal issues presented for consideration in the appellate proceedings involve two significant inquiries:

  1. Whether the interlocutory order refusing the permanent injunction constitutes a "judgment or order" appealable under Section 20(1) of the Supreme Court Act, 59 of 1959 ([para 12]).
  2. Whether Knox D'Arcy established reasonable prospects of success on the merits of their claims for unlawful competition and the associated interdict ([para 24]).

The court also considered the appropriateness of the lower court’s reliance on discretionary factors when refusing to grant the requested interdicts ([paras 24–25]).

R – Rules

The legal framework applicable to this case is primarily grounded in the principles of interim interdicts, as established in prior South African case law. The requisites for the granting of an interim interdict are:

  1. A prima facie right: This does not need to be conclusive ([para 26]).
  2. A well-grounded apprehension of irreparable harm: The applicant must show that they would suffer harm if the interdict is not granted ([para 26]).
  3. Absence of an alternative remedy: It must be shown that without the interdict, the applicant has no satisfactory means for remedy ([para 26]).

The court also referred extensively to cases clarifying what constitutes an appealable "judgment or order" in this context, citing Zweni v Minister of Law and Order and associated precedents ([para 13]).

A – Application

In its analysis, the Supreme Court found that contrary to Stegmann J’s ruling, the decision to refuse the interim interdict should be deemed a final order. It established that the application for the interdict was to seek relief distinct from the main action, thereby granting it the nature of a definitive decision ([paras 19–21]).

The court further analysed whether Knox D'Arcy had established a prima facie case for unlawful competition. Key claims involved the alleged conduct of Jamieson and his approach to South African Druggists while still employed by Knox D'Arcy, suggesting a breach of fiduciary duty. However, the evidence primarily comprised hearsay and lacked corroboration from direct eyewitness accounts such as Van der Walt or Shawe — undermining the credibility of Knox D'Arcy's assertions ([paras 31–45]).

The mere assertion of claims regarding asset concealment, without tangible proof of malintent, was insufficient under the legal standards established for an interdict. The court found that the respondents’ business structure appeared to be legitimate and established for tax purposes rather than for the intention of thwarting judgments ([paras 68–79]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court concluded that the interlocutory order was indeed appealable but upheld the initial ruling of Stegmann J, concluding that Knox D'Arcy failed to demonstrate a reasonable prospect of success on the merits of its claims. The court dismissed the appeal with costs against Knox D'Arcy, emphasizing the necessity for plaintiffs seeking interdicts to substantiate claims with credible evidence rather than conjecture or hearsay ([paras 90–91]).

The implications of this decision underscore the rigorous standards required in interim interdict applications and clarify the parameters within which courts may exercise discretion, specifically regarding the balance of convenience and the necessity for a prima facie case demonstrating potential harm.
