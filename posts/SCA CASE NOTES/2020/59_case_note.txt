Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

## FIRAC Case Note: Minister of Safety and Security v Lincoln (SCA)

**Neutral citation**: *Minister of Safety and Security v Lincoln* (Case No. 682/19) [2020] ZASCA 59 (5 June 2020)  
**Coram**: Cachalia, Saldulker, Van der Merwe, Dlodlo JJA, and Eksteen AJA  
**Heard**: 4 May 2020  
**Delivered**: 5 June 2020  

### F – Facts  
The dispute involves Major-General André Edward Lincoln (the Respondent) and the Minister of Safety and Security (the Appellant). Lincoln was initially charged with 47 counts, predominantly fraud, in the Wynberg Regional Court, resulting in convictions countered by an appeal that led to the acquittal of all but two charges ([1]–[2]). Following this, Lincoln sought damages claiming malicious prosecution against the Minister, arguing that the police instigated charges without reasonable cause ([2], [14]). The trial court dismissed his claim, but the full court found in his favor concerning most charges, except two, prompting the appeal ([2]). 

Historically, Lincoln was integrated into the South African Police Service (SAPS) from the ANC post-apartheid and operated a covert investigative unit, the Presidential Investigative Task Unit (PITU) ([3]–[5]). The legal tension arose after allegations from Sergeant Abramn Smith, who indicated criminal conduct within PITU, leading to an investigation by SS Bouwer and Superintendent Knipe, followed by charges that Lincoln contended were overly supported by coerced witness statements ([6]–[10]).

### I – Issues  
The core legal questions in this case involve:
1. Did the police officials instigate the prosecution of Lincoln?
2. Did they possess reasonable and probable cause for doing so?
3. Was malice present in instigating these charges, implying an absence of honest belief in Lincoln’s guilt? ([1], [20]).

### R – Rules  
In determining a claim for malicious prosecution under South African law, the plaintiff must show:
1. The defendant set the law in motion (i.e., instigated the proceedings).
2. The defendant acted without reasonable and probable cause.
3. The defendant acted with malice or animus injuriandi.
4. The prosecution failed ([20], [21], [24]). 

The onus is on the plaintiff to establish each element distinctively ([21]). The standard for reasonable and probable cause combines both subjective beliefs of the instigators and an objective evaluation of the inherent facts ([44]).

### A – Application  
The court systematically evaluated whether the prosecution originated through actions by the involved police officials, particularly Knipe and Rossouw, or merely as an extrapolation from Smith's disclosures. The findings indicated that the evaluation team, including Knipe and Bouwer, did not solely rely on Smith's statements but conducted extensive investigations supporting the prosecution ([11], [30]–[32], [40]).

Lincoln's argument hinged on demonstrating that the statements which instigated the prosecution were procured through duress. However, the court observed a lack of direct evidence showing that the police had coerced witnesses to deliver false testimonies against him. Despite the suggestion of irregularities, the evidence yielded from the investigation presented reasonable grounds for prosecuting Lincoln ([51], [53]).

The judges further assessed Smith's allegations against Lincoln, positing they were assessed thoroughly by officials and were not irrecoverably harmful enough to displace an honest belief in the lawyers' conviction of Lincoln's potential malfeasance ([42]–[48]).

Ultimately, Lincoln failed to meet the burden of proof required to show that the police acted with malice or without reasonable grounds to initiate the prosecution, as indicated in various evaluations of the testimonies presented during the original prosecution phase ([49]–[50]).

### C – Conclusion  
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appeal by the Minister succeeded, with costs awarded to the Appellant, effectively reinstating the trial court's dismissal of Lincoln's claim for malicious prosecution. The court’s ruling underscored that the decision to prosecute stemmed from several factors that met both the subjective and objective standards for reasonable and probable cause, thus dismissing Lincoln's allegations of misconduct against the law enforcement officials ([54]).

The implications of this judgment affirm the necessity of establishing solid causal links and meet clear evidentiary thresholds when claiming for malicious prosecution, consistent with precedents in South African tort law concerning public and police duties ([20], [28]).
