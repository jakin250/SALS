Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

### FIRAC Case Note: MEC for the Department of Public Works, Roads and Transport v Botha (20811/2014) [2016] ZASCA 20

#### F – Facts ([1]–[6])

On 2 August 2006, Louis Bartholomeus Botha, driving on the R62 road during a severe storm characterized by heavy rain and strong winds, tragically collided with a fallen tree that obstructed the road. This incident led to his death due to a tree trunk penetrating the vehicle's cabin. Loretta Botha, the widow of the deceased, instituted legal proceedings in the Eastern Cape Local Division, Port Elizabeth against the MEC for the Department of Public Works, Roads and Transport (the appellant). Mrs. Botha claimed damages for loss of support suffered both personally and on behalf of their minor child, Bart Petrus Botha, alleging that the negligence of the appellant's employees was the direct cause of her husband's death. 

The trial court, under Judge Tshiki, determined liability as a preliminary issue, which resulted in a finding of negligence against the appellant. The court concluded that the appellant's employees failed to avert the risk presented by the tree, attributing wrongful conduct to them for not maintaining the road and not closing it in time. The appellant subsequently appealed the decision, and the matter was considered by the Supreme Court of Appeal.

#### I – Issues ([4]–[9])

The primary legal questions before the Supreme Court of Appeal were centered around the duty of care owed by the public authority in maintaining public roads. Specifically, the court sought to clarify whether the trial court had erred in finding that the appellant's employees had a generalised duty to maintain roads by removing potentially dangerous trees and close the road when a risk to life was evident. Moreover, it examined the adequacy and relevance of evidence presented concerning the negligence attributed to the appellant’s staff regarding the specific tree that fell.

#### R – Rules ([10]–[13])

The court turned to established principles of negligence articulated in **Kruger v Coetzee** 1966 (2) SA 428 (A) and referenced in **Minister of Safety and Security v Van Duivenboden** 2002 (6) SA 431 (SCA) to articulate the criteria for establishing the duty of care. This legal precedent implies that the duty of care is evaluated based on the foreseeability of harm and whether an individual could reasonably be expected to take precautionary measures under specific circumstances. The judgment emphasized that the imposition of an extensive duty upon public authorities must consider the availability of resources and the operational priorities they can manage.

In its analysis of the case, the Supreme Court of Appeal noted a lack of evidence presented by the appellant regarding the practicalities and costs associated with removing risks such as falling trees from public roads, and whether such measures were achievable or necessary.

#### A – Application ([6]–[13])

The Supreme Court of Appeal closely analyzed the factual basis of the trial court’s findings. While it affirmed the factual determinations regarding the presence of the tree and the actions of the appellant’s employees prior to the accident, it scrutinized the broader imposition of a general duty to maintain the highways rigorously. The evidence presented by the respondent suggested a profound responsibility on the part of the Roads Department to manage tree hazards, but the court countered that without the necessary evidence of resources or operational feasibility, the addition of such a generalized duty was unwarranted for the case's resolution.

The court concluded that the trial court's acceptance of generalized obligations needed a solid evidential grounding which was absent in this instance. While the specific liabilities concerning the tree were indeed valid, extending liability to the overarching duty to monitor and maintain all trees bordering roadways was inappropriately expansive given the lack of decisive evidence.

#### C – Conclusion ([14])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, effectively upholding the trial court’s finding regarding the failure of the appellant’s employees to act with the necessary care concerning the immediate tree hazard. However, it significantly recalibrated how far the duty of care extends for public authorities. The court underscored the necessity for concrete evidence when imposing wide-ranging duties to avoid placing undue demands on public entities lacking the resources to execute such broad mandates.

The ruling serves to clarify the limits of public authority liability regarding road user safety, advocating that while public authorities must ensure reasonable safety standards, these duties must be balanced with fiscal and operational realities. Consequently, the legal implications of this ruling may resonate widely across future negligence cases involving public entities, ensuring that their liability is distinctly defined and evidence-based.
