Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2002

# FIRAC Case Note: NEHAWU v University of Cape Town and Others

## Facts
The case originated from the University of Cape Town's (UCT) decision in 1997 to outsource certain non-core activities, namely cleaning, gardening, and sports ground maintenance, largely performed by members of the National Education, Health and Allied Workers Union (NEHAWU). UCT engaged four contractors (Supercare Cleaning Pty Ltd, Metro Cleaning Services CC, Turfmech CC, and Eco Environment Pty Ltd) to take over these services. This outsourcing decision led to the termination of approximately 267 workers’ employments, yet UCT indicated that retrenchment benefits would be paid. The affected workers sought to remain employed under UCT and transition to the contractors but were met with refusal from UCT, which stipulated that the workers should apply directly to the contractors for new positions ([paras 2–4]).

In response to the layoffs, NEHAWU filed an urgent application in the Labour Court, seeking declaratory relief. It contended that the outsourcing represented a transfer of business under section 197 of the Labour Relations Act (LRA), leading to automatic transfer of the workers' employment contracts to the new employers ([paras 5–6]).

The Labour Court (Mlambo J) dismissed the application, asserting that section 197 did not permit automatic transfer without a prior agreement between the transferor and transferee regarding the employees ([paras 6–7]). NEHAWU appealed to the Labour Appeal Court (LAC), which also ruled against their position, interpreting section 197 as requiring mutual consent between employers for the transfer to be valid ([paras 9–11]).

## Issues
The central issues raised in this case included:
1. Whether employees' contracts are automatically transferred under section 197 upon the transfer of a business as a going concern, without a prior agreement between employers.
2. The nature of the appeals from the LAC to the Constitutional Court, including jurisdictional questions pertaining to constitutional issues raised by NEHAWU ([paras 1, 12–18]).

## Rules
The relevant legal framework includes:
1. The Labour Relations Act (LRA), specifically section 197, which outlines the conditions under which a contract of employment may be transferred when a business is transferred as a going concern. Subsection (1)(a) states that an employment contract cannot be transferred without the employee's consent, except in specified circumstances ([para 44]).
2. Constitutional provisions, especially section 23(1) of the Constitution, which guarantees the right to fair labour practices, and section 39(2) requiring that legislation be interpreted in a manner that promotes the spirit of the Bill of Rights ([para 33]).
3. Comparisons to international instruments, such as the European Union's Acquired Rights Directive, which promote employee rights in employment transfers ([paras 47–50]).

## Application
The Constitutional Court, led by Ngcobo J, focused on three key aspects:
1. **Interpretation of "Going Concern":** The Court established that a business is transferred as a going concern if it remains operational and includes a substantial part of the workforce. The majority approach, requiring mutual consent for the transfer of employees deemed too restrictive, as it could bypass the statute's intent to protect jobs ([paras 54–58]).
  
2. **Protection of Workers:** The Court emphasized that the intention of section 197 is to protect workers’ rights and prevent unfair dismissals due to business transfers. The ruling from the LAC inadequately addressed the protection of employment that should be afforded under the LRA ([paras 46–50]).

3. **Procedural Considerations:** The judgment also navigated complexities surrounding procedural rules for appeals from the Labour Appeal Court to the Constitutional Court. It clarified that appeals based on constitutional matters do allow for direct access to the Constitutional Court under certain provisions of the Constitution ([paras 19–23]).

Ngcobo J concluded that the interpretation of section 197 should be consistent with the constitutional mandate to uphold fair labor practices and that the lack of prior agreement between UCT and the contractors should not preclude a transfer of employment contracts under section 197 ([paras 71–72]).

## Conclusion
The Constitutional Court granted NEHAWU leave to appeal, overturned the dismissal by the LAC, and concluded that upon the transfer of a business as a going concern under section 197(1)(a), workers' contracts are automatically transferred. The case was remitted to the LAC for further consideration of the facts in light of this judgment ([paras 73–74]). The judgment highlighted the importance of protecting worker rights amid business transitions and clarified procedural pathways for future appeals in labour law cases, thus reinforcing the judiciary's role in safeguarding constitutional rights in the employment context. The implications of this ruling are profound, ensuring that worker rights are reconsidered in outsourcing scenarios, thereby promoting job security and fair treatment in labour practices across South Africa. 

**Full Citation:** National Education, Health and Allied Workers Union v University of Cape Town and Others [2002] CCT 2/02 (CC) [2002] ZACC 8.
