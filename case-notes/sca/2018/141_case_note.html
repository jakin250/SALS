Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 141

F – Facts ([paras 1–9])

The Appellant, A. M., initiated delictual damages claims in the Eastern Cape High Court on behalf of her minor child, K. M., against the Member of the Executive Council for Health in the Eastern Cape Province (the MEC). This claim arose following K. M.'s diagnosis of cerebral palsy, which was attributed to an acute profound hypoxic ischaemic event during delivery ([para 1]). The High Court, presided by Nhlangulela DJP, dismissed the claim after determining that Ms M. failed to establish negligence and causation ([para 1–2]).

On the date of admission, 4 May 2010, Ms M. was admitted to the All Saints Hospital in Engcobo at around 12h40, being a first-time mother at 17 years and full-term pregnant. Upon admission, both Ms M.'s and the foetus's health parameters were noted as generally satisfactory with normal foetal heart rates ([paras 2–4]). Throughout the night, there were significant gaps in monitoring; the next recorded examination happened at 23h45, allowing a troubling time lapse before the next observation at 08h20 the following day. The delivery, which occurred at 10h00, was characterized by a face-to-pubis presentation, and the infant displayed alarming Agpar scores which were later altered by unknown individuals ([paras 5–7]).

The court records were later revealed to have been tampered with, calling into question their reliability. Experts, including Professor Van Toorn and Professor Andronikou, agreed that K.'s cerebral palsy was attributable to a hypoxic ischaemic injury, although a strained debate remained about causation and whether earlier monitoring could have changed the outcome ([para 8]).

I – Issues ([paras 10–12])

The legal issues at the trial pertained to the reliability of hospital records, the alleged negligence of nursing staff in monitoring and treating Ms M., and whether said negligence resulted in K.'s injury ([para 10]). Nhlangulela DJP determined that the hospital staff's actions did not constitute negligence, holding that Ms M. had not demonstrated a non-negligent monitoring outcome would have altered K.'s situation ([para 10–11]).

Furthermore, pivotal to the case was whether or not the alterations in the medical records constituted sufficient ground to establish negligence, as the Appellant needed to demonstrate that the medical staff's conduct deviated from accepted standards of care ([para 12]).

R – Rules ([paras 13–16])

The court ruled on the admissibility of hospital records, emphasizing provisions of the Law of Evidence Amendment Act 45 of 1988, specifically regarding hearsay evidence. The court understood the admissibility of records to depend on agreed circumstances and the factors listed in s 3(1)(c), primarily considering the credibility and reliability of the documents ([para 14]).

Additionally, to establish delictual liability, there must be a duty of care, a breach of that duty, the breach must cause harm, and the harm must not be too remote from the breach ([para 18]). The standard of care required from medical professionals was articulated to be commensurate with that expected from their professional peers ([paras 18–19]).

A – Application ([paras 19–43])

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) examined the foundational elements of negligence regarding the nursing staff's adherence to medical guidelines ([para 19]). Evidence—most notably from Professor Buchmann—pointed to inadequate monitoring and gap-filled periods during which Ms M. was not assessed as indicative of care significantly falling below expected standards ([para 29]).

K.'s injury was posited to have developed due to prolonged undetected hypoxia rather than as a non-causal abrupt event without warning signs. This established that, under the circumstances of the failure to monitor, the risk was significantly heightened ([paras 35–43]). The case Lee v Minister of Correctional Services was referred to, which illustrated a flexible approach to establishing causation in cases where direct evidence was scant due to deficient record-keeping ([para 41]).

However, the court also highlighted the MEC's contention: that K.'s acute injury was not a result of negligence but rather arose as a sudden, catastrophic event that could not have been anticipated or prevented by medical staff given the immediate nature of the harm ([para 66]).

C – Conclusion ([paras 44–66])

The SCA ultimately upheld the decision of the High Court, dismissing the appeal. President Justice Maijedt underscored the distortion of facts caused by the tampered medical records while also acknowledging that the absence of Ms M.'s testimony created significant evidentiary gaps affecting the clarity of the case ([paras 44–52]).

The judgment established that, irrespective of the admitted negligence in monitoring, this had not causally led to the cerebral palsy, given the nature of the event and the inability of hospital staff to effect timely interventions due to the acute nature of the injury sustained ([paras 56–66]).

Thus, the legal decision illuminates critical aspects of medical negligence, record-keeping integrity, and the perennial challenge of establishing causation in delictual claims in South Africa, reinforcing the need for coherent and reliable medical documentation in care settings.
