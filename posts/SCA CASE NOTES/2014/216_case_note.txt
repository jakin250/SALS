Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: Minister of Safety and Security NO v Schubach (437/13) [2014] ZASCA 216

## F – Facts
The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa heard an appeal regarding the case of **Minister of Safety and Security N.O. and Director of Public Prosecutions N.O. v Leonard Charles Schubach** ([1]). The respondent, Colonel Leonard Charles Schubach, previously a commanding officer in the South African Police Service (SAPS), was arrested on 14 March 2005 for unlawful possession of firearms and ammunition seized from a safe at the Escort Unit, which he controlled ([2]). Despite Schubach's assertion that the firearms were licensed and legally owned, he was detained until his bail release on the charges presented by the Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP), which included possession of prohibited firearms and explosives ([3–4]).

After making representations to the DPP, which resulted in the withdrawal of charges concerning his personal and service firearms, Schubach was nonetheless prosecuted for possession of explosives and other items. Ultimately, he was acquitted of all charges and subsequently filed a claim in the North Gauteng High Court against the Minister and the DPP for damages arising from alleged malicious prosecution ([8–10]).

The trial court found no reasonable and probable cause for prosecuting Schubach regarding his legitimate firearms and determined the prosecution was malicious, resulting in an award of **R120,000** for general damages and **R93,000** for legal costs incurred in his defence ([10–21]).

## I – Issues
The core legal questions in this case were: 
1. Whether the appellants (the Minister of Safety and Security and the DPP) acted within reasonable bounds of legal authority in prosecuting Schubach, thereby giving rise to a claim of malicious prosecution ([12–13]).
2. Whether the DPP's exercise of prosecutorial discretion in charging Schubach was conducted with malice or without reasonable and probable cause ([11–12]).
3. If the allegations of malicious prosecution were substantiated, what constituted an appropriate amount of damages in lieu of the wrongful prosecution ([21–23]).

## R – Rules
The elements required to establish a successful claim for malicious prosecution were reaffirmed from **Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development v Moleko** ([11]). For a claimant to succeed, they must demonstrate that:
- The defendants initiated legal proceedings.
- The defendants acted without reasonable and probable cause.
- The defendants acted with malice (or animo injuriandi).
- The prosecution was ultimately unsuccessful.

Additionally, the court referenced **Beckenstrater v Rottcher and Theunissen** to elaborate on the standard of 'absence of reasonable and probable cause,' which incorporates both subjective and objective elements ([14–15]).

Furthermore, the DPP also invoked **section 42 of the National Prosecuting Authority Act 32 of 1998**, which provides immunity for actions taken in good faith, contending that the prosecution was initiated in a bona fide manner ([19–20]).

## A – Application
The court carefully examined each charge against Schubach individually, ultimately concluding that there was no reasonable cause to proceed with charges of unlawful possession concerning the firearms registered to him and his wife, as these charges were explicitly ordered to be withdrawn by the DPP ([16]). The court recognized that the DPP's conduct reflected malicious intent, particularly in ignoring directives to forgo prosecution related to charges that had no basis ([18]). The court reasoned that if a prosecutor had both the capacity for belief in the accused's guilt and the requisite information, but chose to proceed with charges rooted in malice, the prosecution could not be defended under the principles established for good faith actions ([20]).

In contrast, the charges associated with the explosives and service pistol were reviewed with an understanding that they required a specific assessment based on available evidence. The evidence suggested a flawed understanding from the DPP regarding the nature and validity of the charges against Schubach, thus, the prosecution in these specific instances was declared to lack reasonable cause ([17–18]).

Regarding damages, the court found that the previously awarded damages were excessively high and incorrectly assessed. In light of the mixed verdict, the appellate court determined a more equitable compensation of **R10,000**, taking into account that the prosecution on some charges was justified ([23]).

## C – Conclusion
The appeal was upheld, resulting in a reduction of the damages awarded to Schubach for the malicious prosecution claim to **R10,000**, alongside the requirement to pay interest and costs of the appeal ([24]). The decision reaffirms essential legal standards regarding malicious prosecution and the obligations of prosecutorial discretion, highlighting the need for reasonable cause and the avoidance of malice in instigating legal proceedings. This judgment carries implications for future cases involving prosecutorial conduct and civil claims for damages arising from wrongful prosecutions, emphasizing the necessity for thorough evaluations of the evidence before pursuing charges. 

In essence, the **Minister of Safety and Security N.O. v Schubach** case illustrates the delicate balance required in the exercise of prosecutorial powers, reinforcing the importance of accountability within the legal framework governing criminal prosecutions.
