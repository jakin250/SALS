Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note: Limpopo Legal Solutions v Eskom Holdings Soc Limited [2017] ZACC 34

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–11])

**Background**  
The case concerns two applicants: Limpopo Legal Solutions, a non-profit organization established to promote and protect human rights, and Mr. Meshack Masingi, a resident of Malamulele, Limpopo. They sought leave to appeal against a High Court ruling pertaining to an order against Eskom Holdings, a state-owned entity responsible for electricity supply.

The dispute began on May 29, 2016, when Eskom received a complaint about a low-hanging electricity cable in Section C, Malamulele. Eskom responded immediately by sending a technician, who assessed the situation and determined that the cable was not a safety hazard due to its elevation and shielding. Despite this, the applicants filed an urgent application on May 30, 2016, requesting an order for Eskom to dispatch specialists to address the cable.

During the filing process, Eskom notified the applicants that the issue was being handled, providing evidence through communication and photographs. Nevertheless, the applicants proceeded with their application, resulting in an interim order issued by the High Court on May 31, 2016, in Eskom's absence.

Upon realizing the applicants had misled the court, the High Court found the application to be an abuse of process that exhibited dishonesty, leading to the discharge of the rule nisi and the imposition of punitive costs against the first applicant. The applicants sought leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court, contesting both the dismissal and the costs order.

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 16–17])

1. **Whether the High Court erred in discharging the rule nisi and dismissing the application without properly considering the constitutional issues raised.**
2. **Whether the punitive costs order imposed by the High Court was justified or appropriate in the context of constitutional litigation, particularly given the general legal principle that costs should not be awarded against private parties who litigate against a state entity in matters of genuine constitutional interest.**

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 19–27])

The case relies on various legal precedents and constitutional principles:

1. **Biowatch Principle**: This principle, established in *Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources* [2009] ZACC 14, stipulates that in constitutional litigation against state parties, costs should not ordinarily be awarded against unsuccessful private parties. Exceptions apply for cases deemed frivolous or vexatious ([paras 22-23]).

2. **Criteria for Conferring Costs**: The rules surrounding the discretionary authority of courts in awarding costs were reiterated. The exercise of discretion must be deemed judicial, devoid of misdirection or reliance on erroneous principles ([para 24]).

3. **Notable Cases**: The court referenced *Lawyers for Human Rights v Minister in the Presidency* [2016] ZACC 45, emphasizing the vital need to prevent discouragement of litigants seeking to enforce constitutional rights due to the fear of adverse costs.

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 18–39])

Upon examining the circumstances surrounding the initial application, the Constitutional Court agreed with the High Court's findings that the conduct of the first applicant was not only egregious but also amounted to an abuse of the court's process. The following points were highlighted:

1. **Misleading the Court**: The applicants proceeded with their application despite contemporaneous reassurances from Eskom regarding the handling of the complaint. The urgent nature of the application was thus found to be manufactured, rendering it manifestly inappropriate and vexatious ([paras 13–14]).

2. **Judicial Discretion**: The High Court exercised its discretion correctly by imposing a punitive costs order against the applicants due to their unreasonable conduct. This was justified as the applicants acted “irrationally, ill-thought, capriciously, and/or superfluously” by proceeding with the litigation despite knowledge of ongoing repairs ([paras 26–33]).

3. **Context of Constitutional Litigation**: Although the general rule from Biowatch protects litigants from adverse costs in constitutional matters, the court reaffirmed that those who engage in misleading actions cannot claim shelter under this doctrine. The distinction made in cases of genuine advocacy and those marked by impropriety is crucial ([paras 31-39]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 42–44])

The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the application for leave to appeal, affirming the High Court’s decision. The judgment encapsulated the following key points:

1. **Dismissal of Appeal**: There was no constitutional merit in the applicants' claims as per the ruling of the High Court. The High Court's decision to discharge the rule nisi was upheld as justified ([paras 43]).

2. **Cost Implications**: The Court found that the application did not merit a costs award against Eskom in the Constitutional Court; therefore, each party would bear its own costs therein. This signifies a return to the earlier respect for the Biowatch principle when it comes to costs related to constitutional litigation in the higher court ([paras 42].

3. **Broader Implications**: The judgment sends a strong message about the integrity required in litigation, particularly in constitutional matters. Abusing court processes or misleading the court will be met with serious repercussions, including punitive costs, serving as a discourse on ethical conduct in legal advocacy and the obligation to uphold the judicial process.
