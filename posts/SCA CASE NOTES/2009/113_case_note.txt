# Case Note: Nxumalo v The State (450/2008) [2009] ZASCA 113

## FIRAC Method

### F - Facts

[1] The appellant, Benzani Nxumalo, was one of 15 co-accused charged with multiple offenses, including robbery with aggravating circumstances and kidnapping, before the Free State High Court ([1]). After a lengthy trial, Nxumalo was convicted on ten counts, including four robberies and five kidnappings, ultimately sentenced to an effective 25 years imprisonment ([1]).

[3] Nxumalo and his co-accused were part of a criminal syndicate that specialized in the hijacking of heavy trucks for export purposes, primarily to Mozambique. The indictment covered more than twenty incidents occurring over a span of five-and-a-half years ([3]). Evidence against Nxumalo primarily involved cell phone records, which purportedly linked him to the crimes, specifically through the numbers ending in 8640, 7346, and 8852 ([4]).

[5-11] Two particular incidents were central to the appeal. In one, on July 16, 2004, the complainant was hijacked and kidnapped while driving an employer's truck. Circumstantial evidence included the cell phone linked to Nxumalo being used in the vicinity of Frankfort at the exact time of the incident. Furthermore, records showed calls made before and after the crime, allegedly linking Nxumalo to the criminal act, though he denied any association with the specific cell phone ([5]-[9]). 

[12-16] In another instance on July 27, 2004, another complainant was hijacked near Memel, but the court found no evidence directly tying Nxumalo to this incident beyond general cell phone contact, leading to concerns about whether the state could prove a conspiracy or cooperation with co-accused ([12]-[15]).

### I - Issues

[7] The primary issue before the court was whether the circumstantial evidence presented was sufficient to sustain Nxumalo's convictions for counts 19 and 20 related to the hijacking and kidnapping of Mr. Ernst Rasepedi Moepi, and counts 21 and 22 related to another hijacking. Further, the court needed to determine if any evidence linked Nxumalo to the crimes beyond circumstantial evidence, particularly for counts 21 and 22 ([7]-[14]).

### R - Rules

[10] The rules surrounding circumstantial evidence are well established in South African law. The court must determine whether the evidence presented creates a compelling inference of guilt, as endorsed in previous cases such as *R v De Villiers* (1944) and *S v Reddy & others* (1996) ([10]). The court reiterated that to convict on grounds other than direct evidence, there must be a strong circumstantial basis that aligns logically, leading to a conclusion of guilt that is the only reasonable inference.

### A - Application

[11] In evaluating the circumstantial evidence for counts 19 and 20, the court found that the appellant's cell phone records established a temporal and geographic connection to the scene of the crime, suggesting that he was present during the hijacking. The fact that the phone was linked to multiple calls in rapid succession near the crime scene and later had contact with a known associate raised the inference of Nxumalo's involvement ([10]-[11]).

[14] However, regarding counts 21 and 22, the legal representation for the state conceded that there was insufficient evidence linking Nxumalo to these offenses. The contact with a "guilty" cell phone the day prior did not fulfill the burden of establishing his guilt beyond reasonable doubt. The court was explicit in stating that without direct participation or a participatory agreement to commit the crime, Nxumalo could not be justifiably convicted on these counts ([14-15]).

### C - Conclusion

[18] The Supreme Court of Appeal partially upheld Nxumalo's appeal, setting aside the convictions and sentences for counts 21 and 22 due to the lack of sufficient evidence to implicate him directly in those offenses. The appeal was dismissed concerning counts 19 and 20 based on the conclusion that circumstantial evidence established that Nxumalo was at or near the scene of the crimes during the commission of those offenses. The judgment underscored the importance of both circumstantial evidence and the notion of collective criminality, noting that the failure to link Nxumalo to counts 21 and 22 did not significantly affect the overall sentence upon which he had been convicted for other robberies and kidnappings ([18]).

The case ultimately illustrates the court's adherence to strict evidentiary standards concerning convictions based on circumstantial evidence and the necessity of finding a clear link to the individual's actions in relation to the crime.
