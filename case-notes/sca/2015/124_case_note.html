Supreme Court of Appeal

Desmond Ettienne Dӧman v Kgabo Gabriel Selomo [2015] ZASCA 124

Mr Selomo argued that he had been a resident of the farm since 1948, establishing a familial claim to the land.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 [2015] ZASCA 124

F – Facts

The case revolves around the events following the death of Caroline Celia Selomo, daughter of the respondent, Kgabo Gabriel Selomo. On 23 February 2013, after Ms Selomo's passing, Mr Selomo sought permission from Dr Desmond Ettienne Dӧman, the registered owner of the farm Pennsylvania in Limpopo Province, to bury her there alongside family members. Dr Dӧman denied this request. Consequently, on 18 March 2013, Mr Selomo filed an urgent application in the Land Claims Court, invoking Section 6(2)(dA) of the Extension of the Security of Tenure Act (ESTA) for burial rights.

Mr Selomo argued that he had been a resident of the farm since 1948, establishing a familial claim to the land. He further asserted that a designated burial site had been allocated for residents and workers of the farm. Contrarily, Dr Dӧman acknowledged Mr Selomo's historical connection but contended that he had not been a resident since an agreement in January 2005 had supposedly led to Mr Selomo leaving, having accepted R8,000 in compensation for vacating the property. Mr Selomo denied the validity of this agreement.

The Land Claims Court, presided over by Spilg J, ruled in favor of Mr Selomo, permitting him to proceed with the burial, which occurred on 23 March 2013. Dr Dӧman then appealed this decision. However, two and a half years elapsed following the burial before the appeal was heard on 1 September 2015, raising mootness concerns regarding the practical effect of any ruling ([paras 1–10]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue in this case was whether the appeal brought forth by Dr Dӧman had any practical effect, given that the burial had already taken place, thereby rendering the original dispute seemingly moot. The court needed to determine whether the appeal could be dismissed under Section 16(2)(a)(i) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which allows for dismissal where a judgment would have no practical result. A secondary issue was the appropriate interpretation of the appellate powers concerning decisions that do not resolve a substantive legal issue ([paras 1, 2, 8, 11]).

R – Rules

The pivotal legal framework pertinent to this case is established by Section 16(2)(a)(i) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which states that an appeal may be dismissed if the decision sought would have no practical effect or result. This provision was crafted to prevent the judiciary from expending resources on moot issues, a practice that had been identified as problematic in previous cases, such as Radio Pretoria v Chairman, ICASA [2005] ZASCA 3.

The Land Reform (Labour Tenants) Act 3 of 1996 provides additional context, albeit not directly relied on by Mr Selomo during the original proceedings. It is significant, however, as it pertains to occupancy rights and land tenure issues which could have implications for the rights to burial site access ([paras 2–3, 9]).

A – Application

Upon analyzing the facts, the court recognized that the primary action sought by Dr Dӧman—preventing Mr Selomo from burying his daughter—had already been fulfilled, rendering the matter moot. The court highlighted that the appeal should not proceed simply due to concerns about potential precedential effects of the lower court's judgment, as such considerations do not override the mootness rule established in Section 16(2)(a)(i).

Dr Dӧman’s counsel attempted to surmount the mootness hurdle by arguing that a favorable outcome for Dӧman could lead to exhumation of the body. However, this argument was met with recognition of its impracticality and offensive nature, further supporting the dismissal of the appeal on mootness grounds. The court noted that both parties had acknowledged this point during oral arguments, illustrating mutual understanding that the merits of the underlying claim were irrelevant to the appeal's practical effects ([paras 8–10, 11]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Dr Dӧman's appeal based on Section 16(2)(a)(i) of the Superior Courts Act, indicating that the matter was moot and held no practical effect. No order as to costs was made, likely reflecting the recognition that neither party had effectively prevailed in an ongoing dispute.

The case underscores the importance of the mootness doctrine in judicial review, illustrating the appellate court's role in curtailing unnecessary litigation on issues that no longer produce substantive legal outcomes. The implications extend to future cases where the prospect of practical effect is absent; litigants should be cautious in pursuing appeals that might be dismissed on these grounds, emphasizing practical legal principles over theoretical disputes ([paras 1, 11]).
