Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title:** Eskom Holdings SOC Limited v Masinda (1225/2018) [2019] ZASCA 98 (18 June 2019)

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Coram:** Leach, Wallis, Mocumie JJA; Mokgohloa and Weiner AJJA

---

## F – Facts

This case arose from an appeal concerning the disconnecting of electricity supply to the residential property of the respondent, Ms. Masinda, located in Tsolo, Eastern Cape. Eskom Holdings SOC Limited ("Eskom"), as a public company wholly owned by the State, serves as the national generator and distributor of electricity in South Africa. Its inspection on August 8, 2017, revealed illegal connections to its grid, one of which was at Ms. Masinda's property. Eskom claimed defects in the electricity supply installation and disconnected the power due to safety concerns, stating that the installation posed an immediate danger to the public [para. 2–4].

Following this disconnection, Ms. Masinda launched urgent proceedings on September 1, 2017, seeking restoration of her electricity supply primarily based on a spoliation application and, secondarily, on the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA) [para. 6]. While her PAJA claim was abandoned during the proceedings, the spoliation claim was addressed in the court a quo, which ruled in her favor and ordered Eskom to reconnect the electricity supply [para. 7]. Eskom subsequently appealed this decision.

## I – Issues

The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether Ms. Masinda was entitled to a spoliation order, which necessitated determining if the disconnection of electricity constituted an unlawful dispossession that warranted restoration under the mandament van spolie [para. 1]. The critical sub-issues included:

1. Whether the electricity supply itself is a right protectable under the mandament van spolie.
2. The distinction between possessory rights and personal rights in the context of electricity supply.
3. Eskom's obligations concerning unlawful or dangerous installations [para. 11–14].

## R – Rules

The ruling involved various legal principles surrounding the mandament van spolie, a remedy which preserves individuals' possessory rights and enforces the restoration of possession irrespective of the merits of the primary dispute over rights. Key principles identified included:

1. The **mandament van spolie** serves to instantaneously restore possession when it is wrongfully taken without due process. It does not require the claimant to prove actual ownership of the property but only prior possession [para. 8].
2. The scope of protection covers not just corporeal property but can extend to certain **incorporeal rights**, provided these rights are classified as quasi-possessio and not solely personal rights stemming from a contractual basis [para. 14].
3. Previous case law (e.g., *Telkom v Xsinet* and *Firstrand v Scholtz*) emphasized that rights to utilities such as electricity and water may not automatically equate to incidents of possession unless linked to the property in a more substantial legal sense [paras. 21–22]. 

## A – Application

The Court engaged critically with the issues, focusing initially on whether Ms. Masinda's claim could fall under the mandament van spolie. It recognized that while the right to possess property is generally protected, the specific facts of the case showed Ms. Masinda's electricity supply arose from a prepaid system, wherein her right was merely a personal right dependent on her compliance with payment obligations through purchasing a prepaid card [para. 23]. 

Thus, her possession of electricity was not inherently tied to her possession of the immovable property but rather was transactional and contractual. The Court noted that spoliation would typically not apply where the claimed right does not emanate from property control but rather from a contract [paras. 25]. Furthermore, the analysis considered Eskom’s duty to ensure public safety against illegal and hazardous installations, suggesting that reinstating such a supply could compel Eskom to act unlawfully [para. 14–20].

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that Ms. Masinda's claim for a spoliation order was misplaced. In the absence of a recognized legal basis to assert that her claim to electricity constituted an incident of possession to the immovable property, and in light of the contractual nature of her relationship with Eskom (based on prepayments), her appeal for spoliation was surpassed by the need to maintain legal order and public safety. 

Consequently, the appeal was upheld, and the court a quo’s order was set aside with a substitute order dismissing Ms. Masinda’s application [para. 27]. The implications of this ruling underscore the distinction between possessory rights protectable by the mandament van spolie and mere personal rights arising from contractual relationships, reinforcing the necessity for lawful possession and compliance with safety regulations in utility management.
