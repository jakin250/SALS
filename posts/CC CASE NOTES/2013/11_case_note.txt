Source Folder: 2013

# Case Note: Olga Rademan v Moqhaka Local Municipality and Others

### CCT 41/12 [2013] ZACC 11

#### F – Facts

The applicant, Ms. Olga Rademan, a resident of Kroonstad, initiated proceedings against the Moqhaka Local Municipality after her electricity supply was disconnected due to her failure to pay the full amount owed to the Municipality. Ms. Rademan, dissatisfied with the local government's service delivery, decided to withhold payment for property rates while continuing to pay for electricity and other municipal services ([1]–[4]). 

Following her non-payment, the Municipality issued a notice for disconnection, and subsequently, Ms. Rademan's electricity was terminated ([5]). She sought an order from the Magistrate’s Court for the restoration of her electricity supply, citing three grounds: the absence of a court order for disconnection, a claim that her electricity account was not in arrears, and that none of the conditions outlined in section 21(5) of the Electricity Regulation Act 4 of 2006 (ERA) justifying disconnection were met ([6]). The Magistrate's Court ruled in her favor, restoring her electricity ([6]).

Upon appeal, the Free State High Court overturned the decision, asserting that the Municipality was empowered to disconnect services without a court order, even if her electricity account was not in arrears, as her rates account was overdue ([7]). The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld this judgment, concluding that the municipality’s powers to suspend services arise from statutory frameworks that support local governance, including the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 ([8]).

Rademan subsequently sought leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court, which was granted. The case raised constitutional issues regarding local authority powers in service delivery and the rights of residents ([10]–[11]).

#### I – Issues

The primary legal issues presented in this case were:

1. **Substantive Authority**: Did the Municipality have the legal authority to disconnect Ms. Rademan’s electricity supply solely based on her failure to pay property rates?
2. **Interpretation of Relevant Statutes**: Did the provisions of section 21(5) of the Electricity Regulation Act impose preconditions for the termination of electricity supply that were not satisfied in Ms. Rademan’s case?
3. **Constitutionality of Disconnection Powers**: How do the powers afforded to local government under municipal by-laws and the Systems Act reconcile with the provisions of the ERA regarding the disconnection of services for non-payment ([12]–[13])?

#### R – Rules

The legal frameworks relevant to the case included:

1. **Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000**: This Act provides for the administration of municipal services and outlines the financial provisions related to service fees, rates, and debt collection policies ([19]–[20]). 
2. **Electricity Regulation Act 4 of 2006**: Section 21(5) restricts the power of licensees (including municipalities) to terminate services unless certain conditions (a-c) are met ([35]).
3. **Municipal By-laws**: The Moqhaka Local Municipality's by-laws stipulated that a failure to pay accounts in full enabled the Municipality to disconnect services without prejudice to existing service agreements and conditions of payment ([21]–[26]).

#### A – Application

In the Constitutional Court, Zondo J examined the statutory bases for the Municipality’s action against Ms. Rademan's non-payment. The judge emphasized the integrated framework of municipal laws and their alignment with constitutional mandates, particularly regarding local governance and service delivery ([10]–[11]). 

- **Statutory Authority**: The court affirmed that the Municipality did not need a court order to disconnect services; the Municipal Systems Act and its by-laws collectively empowered the Municipality to enforce payment protocols. The consolidation of accounts (including rates and services) effectively redefined the customer’s obligations, meaning non-payment of rates breached her overall financial obligations to the Municipality ([34]).
  
- **Section 21(5) Analysis**: The court considered whether section 21(5) of the ERA imposed restrictions incompatible with local governance approaches. It clarified that the conditions listed (such as insolvency or non-compliance with payment conditions) need not necessarily conflict with the Municipality’s own regulations which govern service delivery practices ([38]). The court noted that Ms. Rademan’s failure to pay for rates constituted a violation of conditions set forth in the by-laws, thus justifying the disconnection [40].

- **Constitutional Interpretation**: The court also addressed the constitutional implications of service delivery and individual rights. The State’s obligation to provide services sustainably and effectively must align with residents’ obligations to compensate for such services ([15]).

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court dismissed Ms. Rademan's appeal, affirming the legality of the Municipality’s actions to disconnect her electricity supply under the relevant legal framework ([47]). The court found that the by-laws governing the Municipality provided a legitimate basis for the disconnection, which was reinforced by the collective obligations outlined in the Systems Act.

The judgment highlights the balance of power between local governance and resident obligations, affirming that while residents can invoke complaints about service delivery, they remain bound by statutory requirements to pay for services provided ([46]). This ruling lays foundational clarity on the power of municipalities to execute debt collection measures without adjudication, underscoring the necessity for residents to comply with local by-laws and the implications of failing to meet financial obligations in the realm of municipal service provision.
