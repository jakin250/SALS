Marion Smith v Mountain Oaks Winery (Pty) Ltd & another (1171/18) [2019] ZASCA 123

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 Marion Smith v Mountain Oaks Winery (Pty) Ltd & another (1171/18) [2019] ZASCA 123 (26 September 2019)

  • Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
  • Coram: Navsa, Swain, Zondi and Mokgohloa JJA and Dolamo AJ
  • Date Heard: 6 September 2019
  • Date Delivered: 26 September 2019

F – Facts

In this matter, Marion Smith (the appellant) operated a website aimed at promoting certified biodynamic and organic wine producers in South Africa ([1]). The first respondent, Mountain Oaks Winery (Pty) Ltd, and the second respondent, Eikenbosch Farm (Pty) Ltd, were involved in the organic wine production industry ([2]). The appellant published a statement on her website asserting that Mountain Oaks Winery was "no longer organic" ([6]). The first respondent, through its director Mark Stevens, claimed that this statement was factually incorrect and damaging to their reputation, alleging that they had maintained their certification as organic producers since 2005 ([7]).

The publication occurred after Stevens and Smith engaged in prior email correspondence regarding the certification status of Mountain Oaks. Stevens confirmed their organic certification and provided details for verification ([4]). Despite this, the appellant insisted that the respondents were not producing certified organic wine, referring to distinctions under EU regulations ([8]). This claim led the respondents to seek an interdict against the appellant to retract the statement and clarify the facts, which the court a quo initially granted ([10]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues addressed by the court included:

  1. Whether the statement made by Smith constituted injurious falsehood.
  2. The requirements to establish a claim of injurious falsehood, specifically whether the respondents demonstrated:
  • A false representation made by the appellant.
  • Knowledge on the part of the appellant that the representation was false.
  • Damages suffered by the respondents due to the representation.
  • A causal connection between the representation and the damages claimed ([13]).

R – Rules

The court's analysis was governed primarily by principles of defamation and injurious falsehood, as articulated in relevant precedents. Notably, the court referenced Geary & Son (Pty) Ltd v Gove 1964 (1) SA 434 (A) to outline the elements required to establish a claim of injurious falsehood, which necessitate proof of false representation, the claimant's awareness of falsity, demonstrable damages, and a direct causal link ([13]).

Additionally, the principle from Plascon-Evans Paints v Van Riebeeck Paints 1984 (3) SA 620 (AD) concerning the burden of proof in disputes of fact was applied, emphasizing that a party must substantiate claims with clear evidence ([16]).

A – Application

In its application of the law to the facts, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that the respondents failed to satisfy the essential elements of their claim of injurious falsehood.

  1. False Representation: The court examined the context of the certification debate. It concluded that the appellant's assertion that Mountain Oaks was "no longer organic" was not false given the context of changing EU regulations concerning organic wine certification ([16]). The court reiterated that distinctions between “organic wine” and “wine made from organic grapes” had substantial implications under updated standards.
  2. Knowledge of Falsity: There was insufficient evidence presented showing that the appellant had actual knowledge that her statement was false. The respondent's general denial was deemed inadequate to fulfill their burden of proof on this element ([17]).
  3. Damages: The responses from the respondents indicated speculation regarding damages without quantifiable proof, leading the court to conclude that they did not demonstrate sufficient harm attributable to the appellant's publication ([18]).

In light of these factors, the court decisively rejected the findings of the court a quo, determining that it had erred in its assessment of the facts and legal principles in granting the interdict ([19]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, thereby setting aside the order of the court a quo, which had granted the respondents the interdict they sought. Consequently, the court ordered that the application be dismissed with costs ([20]).

This ruling underscores the need for precision in establishing claims of injurious falsehood and emphasizes the court's commitment to protecting freedom of expression, particularly in matters concerning claims about the organic certification status in the highly regulated wine industry. The implications of this case extend to other similar claims of reputational damage in competitive commercial contexts, reinforcing that simply alleging harm is insufficient to succeed in litigation without robust evidentiary support.
