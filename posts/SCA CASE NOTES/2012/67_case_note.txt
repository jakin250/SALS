Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# Case Note: Lekup Prop Co No 4 (Pty) Ltd v Wright (286/11) [2012] ZASCA 67

## FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts

The case arises from a real estate transaction involving Lekup Prop Co No 4 (Pty) Ltd (the appellant) and John Colin Wright (the respondent). On 12 April 2004, the appellant sold a portion of erf 39 in Sandhurst to the respondent, with the agreement of sale containing a clause stipulating that the contract was subject to the formal approval and registration of a subdivision by 31 October 2004. Failure to register by this date would result in the agreement being cancelled ([2]). The parties subsequently executed several addenda, extending this deadline to 31 December 2005, but by this date, the subdivision had not been approved ([2]).

The appellant sought a declaratory order confirming that the agreement had lapsed due to this failure, initiating motion proceedings in the South Gauteng High Court. The respondent opposed the application, alleging that the appellant intentionally failed to procure the necessary subdivision and that the doctrine of fictional fulfilment should apply, deeming the condition to have been fulfilled despite its non-fulfilment ([3]).

After referral to trial, the trial court ruled in favor of the respondent, concluding that the agreement had not lapsed, and ordered the appellant to proceed with the transfer of the property ([5]). The appellant then appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([5]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issue in this appeal was whether the appellant's actions or inactions warranted the invocation of the doctrine of fictional fulfilment, requiring proof of the intention to prevent the fulfilment of the condition ([1]). Specifically, the court needed to ascertain whether dolus (fraudulent intent) could be attributed to the appellant, thus negating the effect of the unfulfilled condition ([6]).

### R – Rules

The doctrine of fictional fulfilment, rooted in Roman-Dutch law, allows a condition to be deemed fulfilled when a party intentionally prevents its fulfilment to escape contractual obligations ([6]). The doctrinal essence requires the proving of intentional actions or omissions aimed at thwarting the fulfilment of the condition, rather than mere negligence.

The court highlighted the necessity for the respondent to show that the appellant acted with dolus, as established in cases such as *Gowan v Bowern* and *MacDuff & Co Ltd v Johannesburg Consolidated Investment Co Ltd*, where it was clarified that mere negligence would not suffice to invoke this doctrine ([7]-[9]). The court affirmed that the distinction between intentional conduct and negligence is critical in assessing whether the doctrine applies ([8],[9]).

### A – Application

In analyzing the evidence, the court considered the testimonies of several witnesses, including Mr. John Alastair Legh, a director of the appellant, and the appointed town planner, Mr. Henry Nathanson. Legh testified that both he and his co-director had a strong interest in completing the subdivision, disputing any claims that they intended to evade their contractual obligations ([13],[15]). The evidence reflected their continued efforts to fulfill the requirements of the agreement, including multiple extensions sought in good faith ([14]).

Nathanson's account revealed procedural complications faced in obtaining the subdivision approval due to factors outside the appellant's control, notably the resignation of designated officials from the City of Johannesburg ([17],[18]). The court evaluated whether these delays constituted dolus and emphasized that the appellant’s actions demonstrated a lack of intention to frustrate the agreement, illustrated by their asking for extensions and seeking alternatives to expedite the process ([25]).

The court explicitly noted that the evidence failed to substantiate claims of dolus and observed that the appellant’s delay could not simply be interpreted as intentional conduct meant to evade their obligations. The resolutions made to extend deadlines were counter to the notion of intention to escape the contract, a standpoint reinforced by Mr. Jawitz's testimony regarding market conditions ([15],[25]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the court a quo had erred in its finding that the appellant had acted with the intention required to invoke the doctrine of fictional fulfilment. The appeal was allowed with costs, thus reinstating the initial claim that the agreement had indeed lapsed in accordance with the specified terms ([33]). The court set aside the order of the trial court and confirmed that the respondent’s counterclaims were dismissed ([33]). 

This case affirms the stringent standard for proving dolus in the context of the doctrine of fictional fulfilment, reasserting the need for intention over mere negligence when examining contractual obligations. The implications of this ruling serve as a guiding principle for future contractual disputes involving conditions precedent in South African law.
