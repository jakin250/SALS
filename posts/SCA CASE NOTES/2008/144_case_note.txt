Source Folder: 2008

## FIRAC Case Note: Legator McKenna Inc v Shea (143/2008) [2008] ZASCA 144

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History

[1] The first appellant, Legator McKenna Inc, is a firm of attorneys in Durban, represented by the second appellant, Mr. Michael McKenna, who was appointed as curator bonis for Clare Shea, the first respondent, after she was found incapable of managing her affairs due to brain injuries from a motor vehicle accident.

[2] On April 22, 2002, McKenna, in his capacity as curator bonis, purportedly sold Shea’s house to Jamie and Clare Erskine, the joint second respondents, for R540,000. This sale was contentious, as it raised questions about its validity due to McKenna's lack of formal letters of curatorship at the time of the sale.

[3] Following a recovery process, Shea was declared capable of managing her affairs by the Durban High Court on March 10, 2003. Subsequently, Shea initiated legal proceedings to reclaim her property on the grounds that the sale was invalid, citing that it had been executed before the issuance of letters of curatorship under section 72 of the Administration of Estates Act 66 of 1965.

[4] Shea's main claim sought both a declaration of the sale's invalidity and a restoration of her property against repayment of the purchase price. Additional claims for damages were also lodged against the appellants, but these were not the focus of the appeal.

[5] The trial court initially found in favor of Shea, declaring the sale null and void, and ordered the return of the house to Shea, thus prompting the appellants to appeal.

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions

[3] The primary legal issue revolves around the validity of the sale of Shea’s house, executed by McKenna as curator bonis without the requisite letters of curatorship. Specifically, the case examines:

1. Whether the sale constituted a contravention of section 71(1) of the Administration of Estates Act, thus rendering it void.
2. The implications of the abstract versus causal theory of property transfer in South African law concerning the sale's enforceability.
3. The potential availability of enrichment claims concerning the parties involved if the sale was deemed invalid.

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon

[11]-[12] The relevant statutes include:

- **Administration of Estates Act 66 of 1965**:
  - Section 71(1): Prohibits appointed curators from managing or administering property without proper authorization.
  - Section 72(1)(d): Outlines the process for the issuance of letters of curatorship.
  - Section 102(1)(g): Criminalizes non-compliance with the provisions of the Act.

- **Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981**:
  - Section 2(1): Stipulates that land sale agreements must be contained in a written deed, signed by both parties.

[20]-[21] The court also referenced legal principles underlying the abstract theory of transfer, which states that the validity of property transfer does not depend on the validity of the underlying transaction. Several case law precedents were cited to emphasize the application of this theory in both movable and immovable property, including Trust Bank van Afrika Bpk v Western Bank Bpk en Andere NNO 1978 (4) SA 281 (A).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law

[14]-[26] The Supreme Court of Appeal applied the relevant statutory and common law principles to the facts of the case:

1. **Validity of the Sale**: The court assessed whether McKenna's actions constituted a final sale or if they were conditional. It determined that McKenna's insistence on obtaining the Master’s approval rendered the agreement conditional rather than final at the time it was executed.

2. **Abstract vs. Causal Theory**: The appellants argued for the application of the abstract theory of transfer, suggesting that the sale was valid even if the original contractual agreement was not. The court agreed that principles from previous case law did support this theory; however, it ultimately determined that there was no valid agreement in the first place due to a lack of acceptance by the Erskines.

3. **Enrichment Claims**: The court considered the implications of performing under an invalid agreement and its relevance under the condictio ob turpem vel in iustam causam. Given that the sale did not meet the requirements for valid transfer, Shea could not successfully claim based on this argument.

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications

[32] The appeal was upheld, setting aside the lower court's ruling. The Supreme Court concluded that no valid sale had occurred between McKenna and the Erskines, affirming that the Erskines had never acquired ownership of Shea’s property due to the failure to meet legal requirements for a binding agreement.

1. **Holding**: The sale failed to satisfy the necessary legal standards under the Administration of Estates Act, thus reaffirming the role of statutory provisions in curatorial actions. The court specified that ownership could not pass where the initial agreement was void.

2. **Ratio Decidendi**: This case underscored the importance of statutory compliance in curatorship matters, reinforcing the principle that the actions of curators are closely regulated to protect the interests of those under their care. It also demonstrated the ongoing relevance of both the abstract and causal theories of property transfer within South African legal discourse.

3. **Implications**: The verdict has significant implications for practitioners in estate law and curatorship, reiterating the necessity for compliance with procedural regulations. This case serves as a cautionary tale on the importance of adherence to statutory obligations before taking actions affecting the property of those under curatorship, critically impacting future curatorial acts.

This case sets a precedent regarding curatorial transactions and highlights the requisite legal frameworks guiding estate management, which are essential for practitioners and legal scholars alike in South African law.
