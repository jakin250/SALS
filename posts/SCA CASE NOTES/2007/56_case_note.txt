# Case Note: Midi Television (Pty) Ltd v Director of Public Prosecutions [2007] SCA 56 (RSA)

**FIRAC Method**

## F – Facts

On June 15, 2005, a heinous crime in Cape Town involved the abduction and murder of a six-month-old child, which garnered significant media coverage as the investigation unfolded ([1]). The appellant, Midi Television (Pty) Ltd, broadcasting as 'e-tv', intended to air a documentary focusing on the impact of these events on the victim's family ([2]). Before broadcasting on August 2, 2005, the Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP) for the Western Cape requested access to the documentary, aiming to ensure its content did not jeopardize the impending trial of the accused ([3]). After negotiations failed, the DPP sought an urgent High Court interdict to prohibit the broadcast until he could review the documentary and decide on further action ([3]). The High Court granted the injunction, prompting e-tv to appeal ([3]).

However, by the time the appeal proceeded, the DPP had withdrawn his objection after the documentary was aired, leading the court to consider the broader legal questions raised by the case ([4]). 

## I – Issues

The principal legal question raised by this appeal was whether the imposition of a prior restraint on the freedom of expression, specifically the broadcasting of a documentary, was constitutionally permissible given the competing interests of protecting the integrity of judicial proceedings ([5]). This encompassed consideration of the limits placed on free expression under section 16 of the Constitution, as well as the requirements of section 36 regarding justifiable limitations on rights ([7]).

## R – Rules

In assessing the potential for limiting constitutionally protected rights, the court identified several critical principles based on the South African Constitution, especially sections 16 (freedom of expression), 34 (right to a fair trial), and 35 (rights of accused persons). Limitations on rights are permissible only if they stem from law of general application and are demonstrably reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society ([8], [9]).

The court also relied on comparative jurisprudence from beyond South Africa, noting the standards for prior restraints from Canada, the UK, and the USA, which stress that restrictions on free expression must not only demonstrate a significant risk of prejudice but also that there are no less restrictive means available to achieve the same objectives ([15], [16], [18]). 

## A – Application

The court's analysis began by acknowledging the integral role of a free press in a democratic society, emphasizing that limitations on press freedom are not easily justified and must be carefully scrutinized ([5], [11]). It was pointed out that the DPP did not seek an outright ban on the documentary but rather a conditional broadcast, contingent on review by the DPP. This requirement was viewed as an unconstitutional precondition, infringing upon e-tv's rights under the Constitution. 

The court examined the arguments presented by the DPP regarding the potential risks if the documentary were to air, such as witness safety and the potential for prejudicial coverage impacting judicial processes ([22]). However, the court found these concerns insufficient, noting that they were largely speculative and did not substantiate the need to impose such a significant restriction on freedom of expression, especially given that various safeguards against prejudicial effects were available during the trial process ([24], [25]).

Ultimately, the court found no legal entitlement that would require e-tv to submit its content to the DPP before broadcasting. The absence of a statutory basis for such a requirement reinforced the court's decision against the DPP's appeal ([25], [26]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld e-tv's right to broadcast the documentary, reversing the High Court’s order. The ruling underscored the fundamental principle that the freedom of expression is foundational to democratic society and that courts must rigorously protect this right, especially against speculative claims of prejudice without clear demonstrable risks. 

The implications of this judgment extend beyond this particular case, establishing important precedents regarding the balance necessary between protecting judicial integrity and safeguarding freedom of the press. The court emphasized that no publication could be prohibited without strong justification, thereby reinforcing the need for robust protections for the press in the face of governmental or prosecutorial requests ([28]).

The appeal was thus allowed, with the DPP ordered to bear costs, affirming the principle that restrictions on press freedom require stringent justification consistent with constitutional mandates ([28]).
