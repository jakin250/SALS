# Case Note: Gilbert Rabotshankga v The State [1997] ZASCA 22

## FIRAC Method Analysis

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 2–16])

The case originates from the conviction of Gilbert Rabotshankga, a 28-year-old male, for the murder of a domestic worker in Rembrandt Park, Johannesburg. Rabotshankga was sentenced to death along with additional convictions for assault, theft, and rape, leading to various prison sentences. The appeal was filed under section 316(A)(1) of Act 51 of 1977, contesting only the murder conviction.

On April 2, 1993, at approximately 4:45 PM, the body of the deceased was discovered by a family member of her employers. The deceased had been brutally killed, suffering multiple skull fractures consistent with being struck by a golf club, one of which was found at the crime scene alongside other evidence, including blood-stained clothing and personal items.

Witness testimony revealed that Miriam Ramohlolo, a friend of the deceased and former partner of Rabotshankga, had arrived at the scene and was assaulted by Rabotshankga during the incident. Following her account, Rabotshankga was arrested. Both Rabotshankga and Ramohlolo provided conflicting narratives regarding the events leading to the death of the deceased, with Ramohlolo presenting her version as a victim of Rabotshankga's violence.

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 8–12])

The primary legal issue revolves around whether the court a quo erred in accepting Ramohlolo's testimony over that of the appellant, Rabotshankga, which ultimately led to the conviction for murder. Additionally, the implications of the repeal of the death penalty as determined by the Constitutional Court in *S v Makwanyane* ([para 16]) raised another critical issue concerning the appropriateness of the sentenced penalty.

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 3, 14, 16])

The court relied heavily on the assessment of evidentiary credibility, particularly in weighing the testimonies of witnesses. Established legal principles concerning the onus of proof in criminal cases were implicit in the court's analysis. The decision referenced the significant ruling in *S v Makwanyane and Another* 1995 (2) SACR 1 (CC), which declared the death penalty unconstitutional in South Africa, establishing a precedent impacting sentencing in this case.

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 13–15])

The court critically evaluated the credibility of witnesses, finding Ramohlolo's accounts to be more coherent and consistent than those of Rabotshankga. Factors leading to the rejection of Rabotshankga's testimony included inconsistencies regarding his observational ability of the deceased's injuries and implausibilities in his account, such as the unlikely events of an armed confrontation and subsequent violence involving Ramohlolo. 

Moreover, the court noted that the arguments against the credibility of Ramohlolo, presented by the appellant, did not significantly undermine the overall coherence of her narrative. Consequently, the court upheld the trial court's finding that Rabotshankga’s version was riddled with improbabilities, supporting the conviction.

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 16–20])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Rabotshankga's appeal against the conviction for murder, affirming the trial court's findings based on the credible testimony of Ramohlolo. However, recognizing that the death sentence imposed was inconsistent with the constitutional mandates, the court set aside the death penalty and remitted the matter to the lower court for a suitable sentencing determination.

The implications of this ruling significantly extended beyond the individual case, reinforcing the unconstitutionality of the death penalty in post-apartheid South Africa and underscoring the importance of proper evidentiary standards and the protection of the rights of those convicted under the law.
