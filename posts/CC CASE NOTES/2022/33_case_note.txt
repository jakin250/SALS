# Case Note: Blind SA v Minister of Trade, Industry and Competition and Others [2022] ZACC 33 (FIRAC Method)

## F – Facts 
The matter at hand involves an application for confirmation of a constitutional invalidity declaration made by the High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Division, Pretoria. The applicant, Blind SA, a non-profit organization advocating for the interests of visually impaired individuals, challenged the constitutionality of the Copyright Act 98 of 1978 ([1]–[2]). The contention was that the Act restricts the ability of persons with visual and print disabilities to access copyright works, which persons without such disabilities can easily obtain. Specifically, this restriction arises from the requirement of copyright owner consent to convert works into formats suitable for these individuals ([4]–[5]).

The legislative history reveals that efforts to amend the Copyright Act for this purpose have been ongoing since 2015, culminating in the introduction of the Copyright Amendment Bill, which proposed new exceptions ([5]–[7]). However, the delay in the legislative process prompted Blind SA to pursue judicial relief, seeking a declaratory order that the existing provisions of the Act unjustifiably limit the rights of persons with disabilities as enshrined in the Constitution, particularly sections 9 (equality), 10 (human dignity), 16 (freedom of expression), 29 (education), and 30 (cultural life) ([6]–[8]).

The High Court ruled in favor of Blind SA, declaring relevant sections of the Copyright Act unconstitutional and recognizing the urgent need for reform to address the unjust limitations placed on these individuals ([9]–[12]). 

## I – Issues 
The central constitutional issues revolved around:
1. Whether the requirement of authorization from copyright owners constitutes an unjustifiable infringement of the rights of persons with visual and print disabilities ([8]–[9]).
2. Whether the limitations imposed by the Copyright Act are reconcilable with constitutional provisions ensuring equality, dignity, and access to information and education for disabled persons ([10]–[12]).
3. The appropriateness of the proposed legislative amendments, particularly the provisions of section 19D under the Copyright Amendment Bill, and their alignment with international treaties such as the Marrakesh Treaty ([14]–[15]).

## R – Rules 
The court's analysis referenced several guiding legal authorities and constitutional provisions:
1. **Section 9 of the Constitution**: Enshrining equality and prohibiting unfair discrimination ([10]).
2. **Section 10**: Human dignity as a cornerstone of constitutional rights ([10]).
3. **Section 16**: Right to freedom of expression, encompassing access to information ([10]).
4. **Sections 29(1)(a)(b)**: Rights to basic and further education ([10]).
5. **Marrakesh Treaty**: International obligations necessitating accessibility of published works for persons with print disabilities ([14]–[15]).

The High Court noted that the existing legislation failed to comply with these constitutional standards, particularly by not providing for necessary exceptions facilitating access to copyright works for disabled individuals ([12]–[13]).

## A – Application 
In deliberating on the constitutional implications, the court found that the exclusive rights of copyright holders, as established by sections 6 and 7 of the Copyright Act, unduly inhibit the rights of individuals with disabilities ([66]–[70]). The court emphasized that while copyright aims to protect the interests of creators, it should not create barriers to access for marginalized groups, thus resulting in unfair discrimination ([66]–[70]).

Blind SA's arguments were supported by substantial evidence showing that the inability to access literary works severely impacted the quality of life and educational opportunities for visually impaired individuals ([52]–[56]). This was deemed a violation of their rights as enshrined in the Constitution, leading to a conclusion that the copyright regime, as it stood, was indeed unconstitutional ([65]–[74]).

The court critically assessed the justifications provided for the limitations imposed by the Copyright Act and concluded that they did not meet the constitutional test of limitation under section 36, as they lacked necessity and proportionality ([67]–[74]). Consequently, the court ruled that blind and print-disabled persons deserve equal access to literature, supporting an urgency for legislative reform ([105]–[110]).

## C – Conclusion 
The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court's declaration of inconsistency with the Constitution and ruled that sections 6 and 7 of the Copyright Act violated the rights of persons with visual and print disabilities ([112]). A period of 24 months was allowed for Parliament to amend the Copyright Act to incorporate provisions facilitating access to accessible formats for disabled patrons, underlining the need for urgent remedial action ([112]). 

The judgment signifies a pivotal step towards addressing the legal barriers faced by individuals with visual and print disabilities in accessing literary works, reinforcing the imperative for legislative alignment with the rights provided in the Constitution and international treaties ([112]). This case stands as an example of how the law can evolve in function and scope to fulfill the promise of human rights against prevailing statutory limitations, fostering inclusivity and recognition of the unique needs of marginalized communities.

The court's decision holds significant implications for copyright reform, emphasizing a need for balance between protecting intellectual property and ensuring equitable access to knowledge and information in accessible formats. It concludes with costs awarded to Blind SA, emphasizing the state's responsibility in upholding constitutional rights ([112]).
