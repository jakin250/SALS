Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note: Emalahleni Local Municipality & another v Propark Association & another (089/12) [2012] ZASCA 177

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–24])

In this case, the appellants, Emalahleni Local Municipality (first appellant) and Witbank Muslim Jamaat (second appellant), sought to challenge the decisions made by the North Gauteng High Court regarding the alienation of public open space and its permanent closure. The legal proceedings stemmed from a review application by Propark Association (first respondent) under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) against resolutions of the first appellant. 

The background of the case involves the initial request from the second appellant in 2006 and 2007 to identify a site for constructing a mosque in Emalahleni, which led to the identification of Stand 2243, zoned as 'Public Open Space'. The council of the first appellant, in response to this request, resolved to invite tenders for alienating the property, and subsequently accepted the second appellant’s tender, involving a sale agreement dated 8 February 2008. However, the procedures mandated under the Transvaal Local Government Ordinance 17 of 1939 (LGO) and the Local Government: Municipal Finance Management Act 56 of 2003 (MFMA) were called into question, leading to a set of legal challenges by Propark Association ([paras 1–22]).

The first respondent argued that the first appellant failed to comply with statutory requirements related to the alienation and permanent closure of public space, notably that the council had not published notices calling for objections and had not considered any received objections before proceeding with the sale and closure. These assertions culminated in the High Court setting aside the council’s resolutions and declaring the deed of sale invalid, prompting the current appeal ([paras 3–24]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–2])

The legal questions in this appeal predominantly revolved around whether the Emalahleni Local Municipality had complied with the provisions of the LGO and the MFMA prior to alienating public open space and permanently closing it. Specifically, the issues included:

1. Did the first appellant comply with section 79(18)(b) of the LGO, which outlines the notice requirements before alienating municipal land?
2. Did the first appellant fulfill the obligations under section 14 of the MFMA, which governs the disposal of municipal capital assets?
3. Were the procedures followed by the first appellant in relation to the permanent closure of the property consistent with section 68 of the LGO and additional relevant statutory provisions? 

These questions highlight the intersection of municipal administrative actions with statutory oversight and the rights of the local community to object to public land alterations ([paras 1–2]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 24–31])

The court primarily referred to the following statutes relevant to the decision-making process of local councils regarding land use and alienation:

1. **Transvaal Local Government Ordinance 17 of 1939 (LGO)**: Sections 79(18) and 68 establish the requirements for the alienation of municipal land and conditions for the closure of public spaces, emphasizing the necessity of public notice and consideration of objections.
   
2. **Local Government: Municipal Finance Management Act 56 of 2003 (MFMA)**: Specifically, section 14 mandates that a municipality may transfer ownership of non-essential capital assets only after public deliberation and consideration of the asset's economic and social value.

3. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: This act governs the legal procedure for administrative decisions and reviews, reinforcing the need for fairness and transparency in local government decisions.

4. **Judicial Precedents**:
   - *Diggers Development (Pty) Ltd v City of Matlosana* held that compliance with notification requirements delineated in the LGO is a prerequisite for valid land transactions.
   - *Ferndale Crossroads Share Block (Pty) Ltd* reaffirmed that failure to notify and consider objections results in the invalidity of resolutions made by a council regarding land matters.

The court's reliance on these rules illustrates the fundamental legal framework governing municipal decisions pertaining to public land and the standards for administrative accountability ([paras 24–31]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 26–32])

The court's analysis emphasized the lack of compliance with both the LGO and the MFMA on the part of the first appellant. 

1. **Alienation of the Property**: The first appellant admitted non-compliance with section 79(18) of the LGO, which necessitates a public notice to allow residents to object before any decision regarding the alienation of public land is made. The court found that the statutory obligation to notify the public and consider objections was not met, thereby rendering the resolutions connected to the land alienation invalid. The court clarified that merely inviting tenders did not constitute the actual sale or alienation and emphasized that proper statutory procedures must be followed prior to such actions ([paras 26–28]).

2. **Compliance with the MFMA**: The court noted that the first appellant failed to demonstrate that it considered whether Stand 2243 was essential for providing minimum municipal services or evaluated its fair market and community value before the sale. The council had to substantiate its claim that the property was surplus to municipal needs, which was unproven in the presented evidence ([paras 27–28]).

3. **Closure of the Property**: The closure process also fell short of statutory requirements. The court highlighted that the necessary steps—such as public notifications, an open hearing for objections, and proper consideration of those objections—were not sufficiently followed according to sections 67 and 68 of the LGO. The failure to appropriately notify and consider input from residents invalidated the resolution to close the park permanently ([para 32]).

Thus, through assessing the factual landscape against the legal framework, the court systematically deconstructed the procedural defaults attributed to the first appellant ([paras 26–32]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 33–37])

The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the High Court's ruling, affirming the appeal in favor of the litigants regarding the tender invitations and resolutions to accept bids. However, the court upheld the High Court's finding of the invalidity of the land transfer due to procedural deficiencies. Consequently, the court ordered the first appellant to comply with the statutory provisions before proceeding with the sale.

The decision highlighted the critical importance of adherence to statutory requirements by local governments, reinforcing the need for transparent processes that allow for community involvement in matters impacting public assets. Furthermore, it emphasized the legal standing of local citizens and organizations to challenge governmental decisions when statutory provisions are not properly followed. 

The ruling is significant not only for its immediate context but also as a precedent underlining the accountability mechanisms that are essential in municipal governance, ensuring that local councils operate within the confines of the law while respecting the rights of their constituents ([paras 33–37]).
