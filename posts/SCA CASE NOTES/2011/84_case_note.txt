Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# Case Note: The MEC for the Department of Health v Denise Franks (329/10) [2011] ZASCA 84

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The case centers on an appeal by the MEC for the Department of Health (the appellant) against a decision by the KwaZulu-Natal High Court, which held that the appellant was liable for the transmission of the Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) to the respondent, Denise Franks, by paramedics employed by the appellant. The incident occurred on 31 August 2000 when Franks was a passenger in a vehicle that collided with a pedestrian, Mr Mthalane, who died as a result of the accident ([1]–[3]).

Franks sustained serious injuries, including skull fractures and lacerations, during the collision. She was treated by paramedics at the scene and subsequently transported to hospital, where she was discharged a few days later. Approximately six weeks post-accident, she was diagnosed with HIV, which she alleged was the result of contact with Mthalane's blood during her treatment by the paramedics ([3]–[5]).

Franks argued that she contracted HIV due to the negligent actions of the appellant’s employees, who had purportedly failed to follow proper medical protocols that would prevent the transmission of the virus. Her case relied on circumstantial evidence, including a notebook by Mthalane that listed an AIDS helpline number, and the high incidence of HIV in KwaZulu-Natal at the time ([5]–[8]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal question articulated in the appeal was whether the respondent could establish, on a balance of probabilities, that Mthalane was infected with HIV at the time of the accident, and consequently, whether the paramedics’ actions constituted negligent causation leading to her infection ([6]–[9]).

Furthermore, the court examined issues regarding the sufficiency of circumstantial evidence to satisfy the burden of proof in a civil case, and whether the trial court erred in its reliance on inference rather than direct evidence to establish HIV infection ([10]–[12]).

### R – Rules
The court relied on established principles of inferential reasoning in civil matters as articulated in landmark cases such as *R v Blom* ([10]) and *Ocean Accident and Guarantee Corporation Ltd v Koch* ([10]). These principles mandate that:

1. The inference sought must be consistent with all proved facts.
2. The proved facts must exclude every reasonable inference from them save the one sought to be drawn.

Furthermore, the court emphasized the burden of proof lies with the party making the allegation, which in this case was the respondent ([9]).

### A – Application
In its application of the law, the Supreme Court of Appeal meticulously analyzed the evidential foundations set by the trial court. The court found that the trial court had erred significantly by over-relying on inferences drawn from Mthalane’s diary, which merely noted an AIDS helpline without direct evidence supporting the assumption that he was HIV positive at the time of the accident ([9]–[11]).

The court questioned the sufficiency of Franks’ circumstantial evidence, concluding that mere possession of an AIDS helpline number does not logically imply infection. It highlighted the absence of supporting facts about Mthalane’s health or lifestyle that would make such an inference reasonable. The evidence presented did not adequately address key questions about the conditions under which the alleged transmission could have occurred, particularly given expert testimonies that indicated low likelihood of blood viability shortly after Mthalane's death ([12]–[14]).

Furthermore, the evidential failures regarding the exact treatment procedures administered by the paramedics were also critiqued. The court noted that the paramedics had followed standard procedures and had taken precautions to avoid bloodborne infections ([15]–[24]).

Overall, the court underscored that the respondent’s argument hinged on speculative reasoning that lacked solid factual underpinning, rendering the conclusion reached by the trial court unjustifiable ([10]–[17]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that the respondent had failed to prove, on a balance of probabilities, that Mthalane was infected with HIV at the time of the accident, and therefore, the alleged transmission through the actions of the paramedics could not be established. The appeal was upheld, and the trial court's ruling was set aside; thus, Franks' action was dismissed with costs ([27]).

The judgment reinforces the critical importance of establishing factual causation in negligence claims, particularly in complex medical contexts where inferring infection rests on tenuous circumstantial evidence. As such, this case serves as a precedent regarding the evidential burdens in civil cases, shaping the landscape for future claims related to medical negligence and communicable diseases in South Africa.
