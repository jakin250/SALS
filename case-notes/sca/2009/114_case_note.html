Supreme Court of Appeal

Mamase v The State (175/08) [2009] ZASCA 114

During pre-trial proceedings, an issue arose regarding the jurisdiction of the Bhisho High Court to hear the case, particularly concerning offences allegedly committed outside its geographic area.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009 [2009] ZASCA 114

FIRAC Method

F – Facts:
The appellants, Maxwell Mamase, Neo Moerane Mamase, and Quickvest 54 (Pty) Ltd, were implicated on multiple charges under the Prevention and Combating of Corrupt Activities Act 12 of 2004 and one count of fraud. The charges were associated with actions that transpired both within and beyond the jurisdiction of the Bhisho High Court ([1]).

During pre-trial proceedings, an issue arose regarding the jurisdiction of the Bhisho High Court to hear the case, particularly concerning offences allegedly committed outside its geographic area. The appellants filed a notice invoking Section 106(3) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, asserting that the court lacked jurisdiction ([1]).

In an attempt to mitigate this jurisdictional challenge, the State sought to amend the indictment through a direction from the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) on September 15, 2006, asserting that the criminal proceedings against the appellants should be held in the Bhisho jurisdiction ([1][2]). The trial court, presided over by Miller J, reviewed this direction and concluded it was not fatal to the jurisdictional question, as it effectively conferred jurisdiction, and ruled that the direction was valid ([2][3]).

The appellants applied for leave to appeal the ruling, arguing it was erroneously granted to the Bhisho High Court, as the Supreme Court of Appeal had exclusive jurisdiction to decide on questions of law under Section 315(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act ([3]). The petition for appeal was later supported by the State, and the matter was referred to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([4][6]).

I – Issues:
The primary legal issue before the Supreme Court was whether the direction by the NDPP was valid and conferred jurisdiction upon the Bhisho High Court to hear the case ([3][4]). A secondary issue concerned the appealability of the ruling made by the trial court regarding its jurisdiction prior to the appellants entering a formal plea ([5][6]).

R – Rules:
The court relied upon several legislative provisions, including:

  • Section 106(1)(f) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, permitting a plea of lack of jurisdiction when raised by an accused ([1]).
  • Section 22(3) of the National Prosecuting Authority Act 32 of 1998, allowing the NDPP to direct prosecution in a different geographical jurisdiction if deemed in the interest of justice ([11]).
  • Section 319(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act, which relates to points of law arising during a trial ([4]).
  • Relevant case law interpreting jurisdictional authority at certain procedural stages (e.g., Director of Public Prosecutions v Magidela 2000 (1) SACR 458 (SCA)) and precedents establishing the requirements for jurisdictional determinations ([10]).

A – Application:
The court scrutinized the procedural history and legislative framework to determine whether the direction effectively conferred jurisdiction upon the Bhisho High Court. It examined the timing of the NDPP’s direction relative to the commencement of proceedings as dictated by Section 76(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act ([12]).

The court concluded that jurisdiction is vested based on actions prior to, and including, the serving of the indictment. Since the direction was issued after the indictment was served—meaning that proceedings had already commenced according to the definitions established in the Act—the direction could not confer jurisdiction and was ineffective ([13][14]).

Furthermore, the court determined that Miller J’s assertion of jurisdiction was not permissible as it contravened the stipulated requirements of Section 22(3). The decision effectively undermined the principles governing jurisdiction and the authority of the prosecutorial office ([16][17]).

The appeal also raised procedural concerns regarding whether issues of jurisdiction could be ruled upon before the entering of a plea, which the court found to be unprecedented and without a solid foundational basis in existing procedural laws ([8]).

C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, ruling that the direction issued by the NDPP did not validly confer jurisdiction on the Bhisho High Court. The ruling of the court a quo was set aside, and the subsequent order was amended to state that "no order is made on the application" ([23]).

The implications of this ruling underscore the importance of strict adherence to jurisdictional protocols in criminal proceedings and alert legal practitioners to the necessity of timely procedural actions within defined statutory frameworks. This case illustrates the judiciary’s reluctance to concede jurisdictional authority post hoc, reinforcing the legal doctrine that questions of jurisdiction must be rooted in clearly articulated legislative provisions at the initiation of proceedings.
