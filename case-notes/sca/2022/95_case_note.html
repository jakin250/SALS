Supreme Court of Appeal

Hendricks v The Church of the Province of Southern Africa, Diocese of Free State (108/2021) [2022] ZASCA 95 (20 June 2022)

Hendricks appealed the Bishop's decision to the Archbishop, who upheld the decision.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 95 (20 June 2022)

F – Facts

The appellant, Lloyd Eugene Hendricks, was a priest within the Church of the Province of Southern Africa, Diocese of Free State. His licensing to perform clerical duties was revoked by the Church following his refusal to be relocated from St. Margaret’s Church, Bloemfontein. This request for relocation came from the Bishop, Bishop Dintoe, who argued it was warranted for pastoral reasons due to alleged disharmony in the parish stemming from prior allegations of financial mismanagement against the appellant ([1]-[2]).

Hendricks appealed the Bishop's decision to the Archbishop, who upheld the decision. He subsequently initiated legal proceedings in the Free State High Court, seeking a review of the Church's decision under section 6 of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) or alternatively, under common law principles ([2][5]). The High Court ruled that the Bishop's decision did not constitute administrative action under PAJA and thus was not reviewable ([3]). The court reasoned that the Church operated as a voluntary association with powers derived from its internal Canons ([4]). As the appeal did not raise any points pertaining to the Archbishop's decision, the High Court dismissed the application ([5]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue at this stage was whether the decision made by the Bishop was reviewable under PAJA or common law principles of administrative justice. Relatedly, whether the Bishop had adhered to the procedural requirements embodied within the Church’s Canons when revoking the appellant's licence ([6]-[7]). Another aspect revolved around the interpretation of Canon 25(6) regarding relocation of clerics and whether procedural fairness was afforded to the appellant in this context ([8]).

R – Rules

The court's analysis rested on Canon 25(6), which outlines the circumstances under which a Bishop may change a cleric's placement and mandates the involvement of other clergy unless consent is given. This Canon also establishes that if charges are related to the placement (as defined in Canon 37(1)), the Bishop should follow procedures outlined in Canon 39, which calls for a more formal inquiry before a decision can be made to revoke a cleric’s licence ([8]).

In addition, the court relied on precedent cases that believe in the autonomy of religious organizations, notably De Lange v Presiding Bishop and Calibre Clinical Consultants (Pty) Ltd v National Bargaining Council, which assert that courts should refrain from intervening in doctrinal matters unless absolutely necessary ([47], [48]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal deliberated on whether the procedural fairness requirements had been met by the Bishop in the context of the Canons. The majority opinion concluded that the Bishop had acted within his prerogatives under Canon 25 and that procedural fairness had been observed. The Court noted that Hendricks was given multiple opportunities to engage in the process, but he refused to comply with the directive for relocation, leading to the revocation of his licence in accordance with Canon 25(7) ([48]-[49]).

The position outlined by the judges contended that the Bishop’s decision stemmed primarily from the need for pastoral care and did not relate directly to any disciplinary issues warranting a separate inquiry under Canon 39. They criticized the appellant's grounds for appealing, stressing that he had not contested the behaviour leading to the relocation but simply the refusal to move ([40]-[41]).

In contrast, the dissenting opinion emphasized the failure of the Bishop to invoke the necessary procedural protections outlined in the Canons in light of the pre-existing allegations against Hendricks. It argued that these allegations directly informed the reasons for relocation and that compliance with Canon 39 should have been pursued, claiming the Bishop's actions were thus irrational due to the lack of proper inquiry ([54]-[85]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Hendricks's appeal, confirming the High Court's ruling that the Bishop acted within his canonical authority in revoking the appellant's licence. The majority held that due process was followed in accordance with the Church's Canons. Conversely, the dissent highlighted significant procedural irregularities, arguing that the failure to follow Canon 39 undermined the process, advocating for a reinstatement of due process. The implications of this ruling underscore the delicate balance courts must maintain when adjudicating matters involving ecclesiastical governance, particularly the enforcement of internal procedural rules against the backdrop of constitutional principles of fairness and justice in administrative decision-making ([50]-[86]).
