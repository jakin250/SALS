Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

# Case Note: Kabelo Betlane v Shelly Court CC [2010] ZACC 23 

### FIRAC Method

#### F – Facts
The applicant, Kabelo Betlane, engaged in a series of legal battles against the respondent, Shelly Court CC, concerning his residential lease and subsequent eviction ([paras 1–3]). In November 2004, the respondent initiated eviction proceedings against the applicant, claiming arrear rent of R1,100 ([para 6]). The matter progressed through various judicial avenues, culminating in a High Court order for eviction issued by Bhika AJ on 17 October 2007 ([para 9]). The applicant, dissatisfied with the judgment, filed for leave to appeal, which was ultimately denied with costs, leading to a restraining order that barred him from pursuing further legal actions unless he complied with certain cost requirements ([paras 10–13]).

By 2010, after multiple legal maneuvers, the respondent abandoned the restraining orders that impeded Betlane's ability to appeal ([paras 18–19]). The applicant then sought direct access to the Constitutional Court to contest these restraining orders on constitutional grounds, specifically, the infringement of his right of access to the courts guaranteed under section 34 of the Constitution ([para 4, 20]).

#### I – Issues
The matter raised several legal questions, primarily focusing on:
1. Whether the abandonment of the restraining orders rendered the application for direct access moot ([paras 20–23]).
2. The lawfulness of the writ of execution that led to the applicant's eviction and whether it was executed in contravention of the applicable court rules ([para 28]).
3. The broader implications for the applicant's right to access the courts and the proportionality of the costs imposed on him ([paras 10, 40–41]).

#### R – Rules
The Constitutional Court's determination hinged on several critical legal rules and constitutional principles, including:
- **Section 34 of the Constitution**, which guarantees the right to access courts for a just resolution of legal disputes ([para 4]).
- **Rule 49(11) of the Uniform Rules of Court**, stating that the execution of a court order is suspended pending an appeal, unless specifically directed otherwise by the court ([paras 34–35]).
- Judicial precedents, including *Jaftha v Schoeman* and *Machele v Mailula*, which establish that eviction inherently raises constitutional issues, implicating rights to housing and fair judicial processes ([para 31]).

#### A – Application
In addressing the issues, Mogoeng J first concluded that the abandonment of the restraining orders effectively eliminated the barriers preventing the applicant from appealing the eviction order, rendering the access application moot and thus dismissible ([paras 21–23]). However, the court recognized the potential merit in contesting the lawfulness of the writ of execution which led to the applicant's eviction while his leave to appeal was pending.

Upon analyzing the second substantive issue, the court found that the issuance of the writ of execution was unlawful since it contravened Rule 49(11) as the respondent executed the eviction order while the applicant's leave to appeal remained unresolved ([paras 33–34]). The court noted that this unlawful eviction amounted to spoliation, thereby entitling the applicant to restitution. However, the court acknowledged that reinstatement was impractical due to the current occupation of the premises by a bona fide third party, leading to the decision that setting aside the writ sufficed for the remedy ([paras 36–37]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the application for direct access while granting leave to appeal regarding the writ of execution, which it found to be unlawfully executed, thus setting it aside ([para 43(a), 43(c)]). The court mandated the respondent to bear the costs incurred by the Socio-Economic Rights Institute, which had provided pro bono representation to the applicant throughout the proceedings, highlighting the need for judicial recognition of legal assistance for unrepresented litigants ([para 41–43]).

The case exemplifies significant constitutional implications, reaffirming the inviolable nature of legal access and procedural fairness within eviction proceedings, particularly concerning vulnerable litigants. The ratio decidendi underscores the imperative for courts to safeguard such rights actively, whilst also emphasizing adherence to procedural rules that protect against arbitrary evictions and ensure due process.
