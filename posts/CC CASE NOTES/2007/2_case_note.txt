# Case Note: The Crown Restaurant CC v Gold Reef City Theme Park (Pty) Ltd [2007] ZACC 4

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The applicant, Crown Restaurant CC, a close corporation, entered into a written lease agreement with the respondent, Gold Reef City Theme Park (Pty) Ltd. Following the accrual of significant rental arrears exceeding R 71,000, Gold Reef City Theme Park cancelled the lease and sought an ejectment order and payment of R 8,743.82 in the Johannesburg High Court. The applicant opposed the application, claiming that the respondent had verbally agreed to grant an indulgence allowing time for settling the arrears. The applicant argued that this constituted a waiver of the respondent's right to cancel the lease agreement.

The respondent denied any verbal agreement, invoking a non-variation clause in the lease, which required any amendment, addition, or cancellation to be in writing and signed by both parties. Additionally, the lease contained a clause stating that granting an indulgence did not preclude either party from enforcing their rights under the agreement.

The High Court, presided by Msimeki AJ, sided with the respondent, recognizing its right to enforce these clauses. Subsequently, the applicant's attempt to appeal this decision to the Supreme Court of Appeal was unsuccessful, prompting the current application to the Constitutional Court. The applicant sought to introduce the doctrine of *exceptio doli generalis*, claiming this equitable remedy aligned with constitutional values, arguing that it would be unconscionable for the respondent to rely on the formal clauses of the lease after the alleged verbal agreement. Furthermore, the applicant alleged that the cancellation amounted to an arbitrary deprivation of property, affecting its dignity.

### I – Issues
The case presents several constitutional and legal questions:
1. Whether the applicant could successfully raise the defence of *exceptio doli generalis* regarding the alleged verbal agreement in light of the non-variation clause in the lease ([para 3]).
2. The potential application of constitutional principles regarding the arbitrary deprivation of property and respect for the applicant’s dignity in the context of contract law ([para 3]).
3. The appropriateness of the Constitutional Court hearing the case as a court of first and last instance, given that critical factual and legal issues were not fully explored in lower courts ([para 5]).

### R – Rules
The relevant legal principles include:
1. **Common Law Contractual Principles**: The non-variation clause in the lease agreement and the legal doctrine of waiver were central to the proceedings. The common law does not easily allow for oral modifications against explicit written agreements ([para 2]).
2. **Equitable Doctrines**: *Exceptio doli generalis*, which allows for the defense against the enforcement of unconscionable contracts, although historically rejected in South African law, was reintroduced as a potential argument ([para 3]).
3. **Constitutional Principles**: The application of constitutional values, especially regarding property rights and personal dignity, as affirmed in various judgments ([para 5]).
4. **Judicial Precedent**: The court reiterated the principle from *Fleecytex v Mavundla* that encourages the examination of issues by multiple courts to enhance the correctness of decisions ([para 5]).

### A – Application
The Constitutional Court emphasized the importance of adhering to established legal doctrines when considering new arguments, particularly those not previously raised in lower courts. The court ruled that Msimeki AJ was only solicited to consider the waiver defense, which he did competently, thus rendering the applicant's new reliance on the *exceptio doli generalis* inappropriate at this stage. The court indicated that it is typically undesired for it to act as a court of first and last instance due to the necessity for thorough consideration by lower courts, stating that the High Court and Supreme Court of Appeal play crucial roles in developing the common law of contract ([paras 4–6]).

By asserting that the further legal and factual complexities of the applicant's claims had not been adequately addressed in prior rulings, the Constitutional Court reasoned that it would undermine judicial efficiency and cause potential injustice to hear the appeal without prior exploration of the pertinent issues ([para 5]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court dismissed the application for leave to appeal, ruling it was not in the interests of justice to intervene as a court of first instance. The court emphasized the need for a robust examination of legal principles within the appropriate judicial context. Consequently, the High Court's original ruling was upheld. The implications of this decision reaffirm the necessity for parties in contractual agreements to adhere strictly to written terms and communicate effectively as per contractual stipulations, particularly in light of informal agreements that may not hold legal weight under existing contract law. The ruling underscores the reluctance of the Constitutional Court to diverge from traditional contractual doctrines without substantial justification and the importance of ensuring lower courts resolve disputes before the highest court intervenes ([paras 6–7]).
