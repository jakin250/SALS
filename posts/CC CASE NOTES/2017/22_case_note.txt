Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: Lawyers for Human Rights v Minister of Home Affairs and Others [2017] ZACC 22 

## F – Facts

This case arose from a challenge to the validity of certain provisions of the Immigration Act 13 of 2002. The applicant, Lawyers for Human Rights (LHR), a non-governmental organization dedicated to the promotion and enforcement of human rights, sought judicial redress against the Minister of Home Affairs and other state officials for provisions that allowed for the administrative detention of illegal foreigners pending deportation. The applicant contended that these provisions violated constitutional rights under sections 12(1) and 35(2)(d) of the South African Constitution by permitting detention without sufficient judicial oversight ([paras 4–12]).

The specifics of the provisions at issue included section 34(1)(b) and (d), which allowed for the detention of foreigners for up to 30 days without automatic judicial review, and extended detention beyond this period without the detainee's right to appear in court personally to challenge their detention ([paras 4–12]). The applicant indicated that in practice, these provisions led to violations including extended periods of detention beyond statutory limits, often occurring in conditions that subjected detainees to inadequate legal protections ([paras 10–12]).

The High Court found in favour of the LHR, determining that the provisions were inconsistent with constitutional guarantees and declaring them invalid. This prompted an appeal by the state, challenging the High Court's order ([paras 9–14]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the Constitutional Court centered on:

1. **Whether the provisions of section 34(1)(b) and (d) of the Immigration Act were inconsistent with the rights enshrined in sections 12(1) and 35(2)(d) of the Constitution**. This analysis involved determining the scope of rights granted to both South African citizens and illegal foreigners under the Constitution ([paras 4–12], [24–28]).

2. **Whether the limitations imposed by the impugned provisions could be justified under section 36 of the Constitution**, examining the grounds offered by the state regarding logistical practicalities and cost implications of ensuring judicial oversight of detainees ([paras 50–63]).

## R – Rules

The rules governing this case refer to the following legal authorities and constitutional provisions:

1. **Sections 12 and 35 of the Constitution**: These sections articulate the rights to freedom and security, as well as procedural safeguards for detained individuals, including the right to be informed of reasons for detention, the right to legal representation, and the right to challenge the lawfulness of detention ([paras 28–44]).

2. **Immigration Act 13 of 2002, particularly section 34(1)**: This section outlines the powers of immigration officers regarding the arrest and detention of illegal foreigners, detailing procedural requirements related to their deportation and detention ([paras 46–55]).

3. **Judicial precedents**: Previous judgments by the Constitutional Court, particularly regarding detention without trial, and the necessity of judicial oversight, played a critical role in shaping the court's analysis, notably the historic case De Lange v Smuts NO ([paras 3–4]).

4. **Section 36 of the Constitution**: Governing the justifiability of limitations placed on constitutional rights, assessing factors such as the nature and importance of the right, the extent of the limitation, and whether the means to achieve the purpose are the least restrictive ([paras 59–62]).

## A – Application

The court first established that the rights invoked by the applicant under sections 12(1) and 35(2)(d) of the Constitution applied to illegal foreigners in South Africa, thereby rendering section 34(1) of the Immigration Act inconsistent with these constitutional provisions ([paras 24–26], [58]).

In applying the law to the facts, the court scrutinized the lack of automatic judicial oversight in detentions under section 34(1)(b). The provisions permitted the detention of illegal foreigners without immediate recourse to courts, undermining the procedural safeguards intended to protect against arbitrary detention ([paras 52–54], [58]). 

The court addressed the state's justifications concerning cost and logistical challenges in implementing judicial reviews, reframing the argument that procedural justice is paramount even in times of resource constraints. The court cited previous judgments asserting that budgetary limitations cannot justify limitations on fundamental rights ([para 62]).

The court emphasized that the lack of explicit guidelines within the Immigration Act for immigration officers regarding when to detain individuals rendered the provision fundamentally flawed, echoing concerns raised in prior cases such as Dawood v Minister of Home Affairs ([paras 48–50], [59]).

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded that the provisions of section 34(1)(b) and (d) of the Immigration Act were unconstitutional as they did not comply with the rights guaranteed under sections 12(1) and 35(2)(d) of the Constitution. The declaration of invalidity was deemed necessary, and the court ordered that the invalidity of the provisions would be suspended for 24 months to allow Parliament to correct the deficiencies. During this period, provisions were established mandating that any illegal foreigner detained must be brought before a court within 48 hours of arrest ([paras 73–74]).

The court’s decision held significant implications for the protection of human rights in detention practices, stressing the necessity of judicial oversight and the importance of procedural rights for all individuals within South Africa's jurisdiction, reinforcing the principle that effective judicial review is essential in maintaining the rule of law ([paras 73–74]).

This case underscores the delicate balance between state interests in immigration enforcement and the protection of individual rights, illuminating the perpetual need for vigilance in safeguarding constitutional protections, particularly for vulnerable populations.
