Supreme Court of Appeal

Republic of South Africa (Case No. 381/04)

The appellant, S A Breweries Limited, sought to recover a debt from Gensam (Pty) Ltd, a company owned by the respondent, Pieter van Zyl, and others.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2005

F – Facts:

The appellant, S A Breweries Limited, sought to recover a debt from Gensam (Pty) Ltd, a company owned by the respondent, Pieter van Zyl, and others. On 7 July 1997, the respondent and three other directors entered into a Deed of Suretyship, becoming sureties for any debts owed by Gensam to South African Breweries Limited (SAB 69). Following the sale of SAB 69's business, which included a cession of debtors to Lexshell 159 Investment Holdings (Pty) Ltd (later named SAB 98), the company continued to extend credit to Gensam. By June 2000, Gensam owed R610,000, which reduced to R515,177.14 by February 2001.

After issuing summons against Gensam and its sureties, the court a quo dismissed the action, ruling that the sureties were not liable for debts incurred after the cession date, asserting that SAB 98 could not claim debts that had not been ceded. The court held that the cession of the principal debt transferred only the right to recover debts that had already accrued at the time of cession, not future debts ([paras 1–4]).

I – Issues:

The central legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether a surety is liable for debts of the principal debtor that accrued after a cession of those debts to a new creditor. Specifically, it questioned if a surety, through a Deed of Suretyship, could be held liable for future debts incurred by the principal debtor after the original creditor ceded its rights to another entity ([paras 1–5]).

R – Rules:

The court examined the principles surrounding cession and suretyship, relying on legal precedents such as Pizani and Another v First Consolidated Holdings (Pty) Ltd and Mignoel Properties (Pty) Ltd v Kneebone. It clarified that a cession allows the cessionary to take on the cedent's rights against both the principal debtor and the surety without the need for additional cession ([paras 6–7]).

However, the court emphasized that, in cessions, the creditor must hold rights to the debts at the time of cession. The rule “huur gaat voor koop”, which facilitates the automatic substitution of one party in a lease agreement, does not extend to cession of debts where a right of action must be explicitly ceded ([paras 8–9]).

A – Application:

In applying the aforementioned rules, the court determined that SAB 98 could only pursue debts that were owed to SAB 69 at the time of cession. The Deed of Suretyship specified the surety's liability as ancillary to the principal debtor's obligations, meaning the respondent's liability was limited to debts that existed as of 4 March 1999, the date of the cession. The court found no evidence of current debts owed to SAB 69 at the time of the cession, as subsequent dealings with Gensam post-cession had resulted in credits rather than debits. Thus, the court concluded that the surety's liability had effectively expired following the cession ([paras 9–10]).

C – Conclusion:

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the court a quo’s decision, affirming that the respondent was not liable for the debts incurred by Gensam after the cession to SAB 98. The appeal was dismissed with costs, reinforcing the notion that under suretyship, a surety is liable solely for amounts owed by the principal debtor at the point of cession, with no claims allowable for future debts not ceded ([paras 11–11]).

This ruling clarifies the scope of suretyship liability in cases where an original creditor ceded rights to a new entity and underscores critical distinctions between cession of debts and other forms of liability transfer in contractual relationships, aligning with established principles of contract and credit law.
