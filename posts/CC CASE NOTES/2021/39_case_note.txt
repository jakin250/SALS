# Case Note: Mtolo and Another v Lombard and Others [2021] ZACC 39

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

The case involves an application for leave to appeal concerning the rights of the first and second applicants, Alias and Maneheng Mtolo, who, along with their eight children, were rendered homeless due to the alleged unlawful removal of the roof and windows of their home at Plot 8, Ardenworld, Vanderbijlpark, by the first respondent, Theunis Christoffel Lombard, and the fourth respondent, Maria Helentje Lombard ([paras 1–3]). The matter arose after the applicants were notified on 16 June 2021 by the first respondent that their accommodation was to be vacated by reason of the property being sold. Following their refusal to leave, on 24 June 2021, police allegedly intimidated the applicants into vacating the premises ([paras 4–5]).

On 6 July 2021, fearing eviction, the applicants vacated the property but returned to find their home effectively demolished, with only the roof and windows having been removed ([paras 6–7]). The applicants subsequently sought urgent relief in the Gauteng Local Division of the High Court. This court initially ruled on 2 August 2021 that the demolition was unlawful, ordering the respondents to provide the applicants with suitable alternative accommodation, which was not fulfilled ([paras 8–9]).

The applicants then applied again for urgent enforcement of the High Court's order, but their application was struck from the roll by Judge Dippenaar on 25 August 2021 for lack of urgency ([paras 11–13]). The present appeal concerns whether the High Court correctly assessed the urgency of the application and whether the respondents complied with the previous orders ([paras 2–28]).

### I – Issues

The main legal issues before the Constitutional Court were: 

1. Whether the Gauteng Local Division of the High Court erred in striking the applicants' application from the urgent roll and thus failing to recognize the urgency of the applicants' plight ([para 13]).
2. Whether the respondents complied with the order of Antonie AJ requiring the restoration of the applicants’ home to a habitable condition ([para 2, 12, 26]).
3. The interpretation of Antonie AJ's order in relation to the scope of any compliance necessary beyond merely replacing the roof and windows ([paras 26, 34–37]).

### R – Rules

Key legal rules engaged in this case include:

- **Constitutional Rights:** The right to dignity ([section 10 of the Constitution]), access to adequate housing ([section 26 of the Constitution]), and the right to basic education ([section 29 of the Constitution]).
- **Urgency in Court Proceedings:** The High Court Practice Manual, which outlines protocols for urgent applications ([para 11]).
- **Past Precedents:** This Court’s decision in *Occupiers, Berea* regarding the stance that eviction from one's home always raises constitutional issues ([para 14]), and the principles derived from *Informal Traders*, emphasizing the need for urgent resolution in matters impacting fundamental rights ([para 24, 32]).

### A – Application

The Court began by analyzing the urgency of the matter in light of the severe consequences faced by the applicants, stressing the destitution and inhumane conditions imposed upon them due to the respondents' actions ([paras 29–30]). Given the history of earlier judicial engagement that acknowledged the urgency of the matter, the Court found it necessary to grant the direct appeal to prevent further infringement of the applicants' constitutional rights ([paras 28–33]).

As to compliance with the previous court's order, the Court noted the conflicting claims regarding whether the respondents had rectified the conditions of the home as mandated. The assertions by the respondents that the roof was secured were scrutinized through submitted photographs and described present conditions ([paras 39–42]). The Court pointed out that the absence of a suitable roof, unsafe conditions presented by leftover construction materials, and potential structural hazards undermined the claim of compliance ([paras 40–42]).

Interpretatively, the Court sided with the respondents on the narrower construction of Antonie AJ's order, indicating that the order specifically directed the replacement of the roof and windows but did not intend for broader structural repairs necessary for full habitability unless explicitly stated ([paras 34–37]). However, the Court clarified the necessity of ensuring overall safety tied integrally to the roof’s proper installation, thus reinforcing that the respondents had to identify and adequately address associated structural risks ([para 36]).

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court upheld the appeal, set aside the High Court's ruling regarding lack of urgency, and mandated the first and fourth respondents to repair the applicants' home to a state fit for human habitation within seven days ([paras 46(2)–(3)]). The ruling underscored the importance of protecting constitutional rights related to housing and dignity while emphasizing the need for courts to prioritize urgent matters impacting vulnerable populations ([paras 41, 42]). Additionally, the decision highlighted judicial oversight as a critical function to ensure compliance with court orders and safeguard the basic rights afforded by the Constitution. The Court ultimately remitted the matter for the supervision of the High Court to ensure adherence to the terms of its decision ([para 44]). 

This case illustrates the careful balancing required in urgent legal applications involving constitutional rights, setting a precedent for what constitutes adequate compliance in the context of housing disputes.
