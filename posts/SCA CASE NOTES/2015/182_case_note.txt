Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: Uniqon Wonings (Pty) Ltd v City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality [2014] ZASCA 182

## F – Facts
The case involved Uniqon Wonings (Pty) Ltd (the Appellant), a property development company that purchased farmland and converted it into the Six Fountains Residential Estate. The City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality (the Respondent) was responsible for levying property rates in its jurisdiction, including the Bronberg area, previously under the Kungwini Local Municipality, which was disbanded in 2011 and incorporated into Tshwane ([paras 3-4]).

In the 2004/2005 fiscal year, Kungwini had levied a property rate of 0.054 cents per rand value but the Appellant contended that this rate was invalid based on a prior ruling, which had deemed it unlawful ([paras 7-8]). The Appellant sought repayment of R788,282, arguing that there was no effective rate due to the invalidity of the levied rates ([paras 7-8]). The High Court ruled in favor of the Respondent, leading to the present appeal ([paras 8]).

The core issue revolved around whether the municipality was required, under section 10G(7) of the Local Government Transition Act 209 of 1993 (the Transition Act), to determine property rates annually and if such rates lapsed at the end of the financial year ([paras 1, 8, 30]).

## I – Issues
The fundamental question presented in this case was whether the municipality was obliged to determine property rates annually and whether such rates automatically lapsed at the end of the financial year in which they were levied, according to section 10G(7) of the Transition Act ([paras 1, 32]).

## R – Rules
The legal framework relevant to this dispute includes several statutes:

1. **Local Government Transition Act 209 of 1993**: Particularly section 10G, which allows municipalities to levy and recover property rates. The specific language of section 10G(7)(a)(i) allows a municipal council to levy rates without being subject to any other law, implying that such powers are "self-standing" ([paras 11, 19-20]).

2. **Local Authorities Rating Ordinance 11 of 1977**: Historically governed the levying of rates in municipalities, which stipulated that property rates must be determined annually ([paras 12, 25]).

3. **Municipal Property Rates Act 6 of 2004**: Instituted subsequent reforms that govern property ratings, including the requirement that rates must be levied annually and lapse at the end of the financial year ([paras 26, 30]).

4. **Case Law**: Notable precedents include *Liebenberg NO and Others v Bergrivier Municipality* and *CDA Boerdery v Nelson Mandela Metropolitan Municipality*, which elaborated on the independence of the rating powers conferred by section 10G of the Transition Act compared to prior ordinances ([paras 14, 19]).

## A – Application
The court analyzed the legislative history and context of the Transition Act, concluding that its provisions conferred a self-standing rating power to municipalities, independent of the obligations set by the pre-existing Ordinance ([paras 19-20]). The court highlighted that the intention of section 10G was to establish a flexible, efficient mechanism for municipalities to levy rates rather than be encumbered by outdated legislative requirements ([paras 12-14]).

In addressing the annual rate determination question, the court noted that although section 10G(7) did not explicitly require annual review or determination, it also did not suggest that rates would lapse at the end of each financial year. The lack of specific language in the Transition Act paralleling section 12 of the Municipal Property Rates Act indicated that the rate fixed under section 10G(7) remained in force until changed by resolution of the council ([paras 29-30]).

The court held that the High Court's conclusion, which inferred an obligation to comply with both statutes, was erroneous. This misinterpretation overlooked the legislative intent behind the Transition Act and its purpose to streamline municipal financial powers ([paras 22-23]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the Appellant's appeal, affirming that a municipality is not obliged to impose property rates annually under section 10G(7) of the Transition Act. The levied rate does not automatically lapse at the end of the financial year, continuing until a new resolution is passed by the municipal council to amend it. The decision clarifies the autonomous standing of municipal rating powers under the Transition Act and has significant implications for the fiscal management of municipalities, allowing flexibility in the levying of rates without the constraints of prior legislation ([paras 1, 32]). The court's interpretation emphasizes the autonomy and evolving nature of municipal governance post-transition, underscoring the need for coherent legislative frameworks to facilitate effective local governance.
