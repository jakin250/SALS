Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2006

# FIRAC Case Notes: Relyant Trading (Pty) Ltd v Shongwe [2006] SCA 111 RSA

## F – Facts 

The case at hand concerns an appeal by Relyant Trading (Pty) Ltd, trading as Geen and Richards (the "Appellant"), against the decision of Legodi J, which upheld claims for wrongful arrest and malicious prosecution made by Mr. Shongwe (the "Respondent"). The claims emanated from events that transpired after a person identifying himself as Mr. Makgabo purchased a computer from the Appellant on 30 May 2002 ([para 1]). 

Mr. Makgabo allegedly failed to make any installment payments and subsequently provided false employment and residential details. Consequently, the Appellant reported the matter to the police, leading to a fraud docket being opened on 24 August 2002 and a warrant for Makgabo's arrest issued ([para 3]). On 26 August 2002, Ms. Mahlangu, an employee of the Appellant, mistakenly identified Mr. Shongwe as Makgabo and reported him to the police. Following this report, Inspector du Plessis arrested Mr. Shongwe, leading to his prosecution for theft ([paras 3–4]).

Mr. Shongwe was detained, appeared in court, and ultimately had the charges against him withdrawn on 2 December 2002 without any connection to the Appellant's employees ([para 3]). The claims involved distinct elements of wrongful arrest and malicious prosecution, although the pleading regarding these claims was neither clear nor separated effectively ([para 2]).

## I – Issues 

At the heart of this appeal are two primary legal questions:

1. **Wrongful Arrest**: Did the Appellant or its agents effect the arrest of Mr. Shongwe, thereby leading to liability for wrongful arrest? ([paras 8–9])
    
2. **Malicious Prosecution**: Did the actions of the Appellant's employees constitute malicious prosecution, specifically focusing on the existence of reasonable and probable cause and the requisite animus iniuriandi? ([paras 9–14])

## R – Rules 

In addressing these issues, the court elaborated on the legal definitions and requirements for both wrongful arrest and malicious prosecution. 

1. **Wrongful Arrest**: For liability to arise from wrongful arrest, it is sufficient to show that the defendant, or someone acting on their behalf, deprived the plaintiff of liberty ([para 4]). Strict liability applies, meaning no conscious fault or awareness of the wrongful nature of the arrest is required ([para 4]).

2. **Malicious Prosecution**: The requirements for a successful claim include demonstrating that the prosecution was instigated without reasonable and probable cause and with malice (animo iniuriandi) ([paras 5–6]). Established case law, including *Beckenstrater v Rottcher and Theunissen* and *Heyns v Venter*, underscores that a genuine belief in the plaintiff's guilt, founded on reasonable grounds, negates malicious prosecution liability ([para 14]).

## A – Application 

The court conducted a meticulous analysis of the Appellant's liability regarding both claims. 

1. **Wrongful Arrest**: The court determined that while the Appellant's employees had identified Mr. Shongwe to the police, it was the police who executed the arrest. The Appellant's employees acted merely as informants. Consequently, the court held that the employees did not effectuate the arrest ([para 8]). Their actions were insufficient to impose liability for wrongful arrest since the police took independent action ([para 8]).

2. **Malicious Prosecution**: For the claim of malicious prosecution, the court explored the concept of "instigation" and concluded that the actions of Ms. Mahlangu did not amount to instigation in a legal sense. The employees provided a fair representation of the facts to the police, expressing their suspicions without harming Mr. Shongwe's dignity or privacy ([para 9]). The crucial point was whether Ms. Mahlangu acted with reasonable belief in Mr. Shongwe's guilt based on her prior knowledge of the case. The court noted that her identification of Mr. Shongwe was rooted in her personal interactions and observations ([paras 11–15]). Thus, her belief, combined with the totality of the evidence, suggested that reasonable and probable cause for prosecution was present, dismissing the malicious prosecution claim ([para 15]).

## C – Conclusion 

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the Appellant's appeal, effectively dismissing the claims of wrongful arrest and malicious prosecution against it. The judgment underlined the foundational principles of liability: the necessity for an arresting party to be directly responsible for the act of arrest and the requirement of reasonable and probable cause to negate malicious prosecution claims ([paras 17]). The outcome underscores the importance of precise pleading and the legal standards that govern personal liability in the context of arrest and prosecution, clarifying that lawful conduct on the part of an informant does not necessarily transfer culpability in the event of wrongful judicial action. Thus, the court’s decision not only vindicated the Appellant but also provided valuable legal precedents on the scope of liability in cases involving wrongful arrest and malicious prosecution.
