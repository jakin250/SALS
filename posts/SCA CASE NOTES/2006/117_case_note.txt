Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2006

# FIRAC Case Note: Stevens v Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service [2006] SCA 145 (RSA)

## F – Facts
The appellant, Charles Douglas Newman Stevens, was employed as the group company secretary of Safmarine and Rennies Holdings Limited (‘Safren’) and held share options under the company’s share incentive scheme ([1]). In 1998, he had acquired the right to purchase 51,000 Safren shares at R3.70 each. However, due to company developments—including an impending liquidation and the declaration of a special dividend of R1 per share—the options became worthless by the time the shares could have been exercised ([1]–[5]).

In February 2000, Safren’s board resolved to make ex gratia payments of 75 cents for each share option held by those unable to benefit from the options due to these developments. Stevens received a total of R38,250 as an ex gratia payment during the 2001 tax year ([1], [6]). The Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (CSARS) assessed this payment as falling within the definition of ‘gross income’ under section 1 of the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962, thus subjecting it to tax. Stevens' objection and subsequent appeal in the Cape Tax Court were unsuccessful, leading him to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([1], [2]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal question was whether the ex gratia payment of R38,250 received by Stevens constituted ‘gross income’ as defined by the Income Tax Act, specifically under paragraph (c) relating to amounts received in respect of services or employment ([2], [10]).

Secondary issues included:
1. Whether the ex gratia payment was essentially compensation for loss or recognition of past services.
2. The relevance of the link between the payment and Stevens’ employment status.

## R – Rules
The relevant legislative rule derives from section 1 of the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962, which outlines ‘gross income’ to include any amount received in respect of services rendered or to be rendered ([10]). The case also referenced previous judicial interpretations, notably *CIR v Shell Southern Africa Pension Fund* ([15]), which addressed causation and the factors determining tax obligations in regards to payments made outside a direct contractual obligation.

Other precedents, including *De Villiers v Commissioner for Inland Revenue* and *Stander v Commissioner for Inland Revenue*, were invoked to establish how causal relationships are evaluated under the definition of gross income ([20]). 

## A – Application
The Court examined the intention behind the payment. Although Stevens' counsel argued that the ex gratia payment was merely compensatory for a perceived unfairness rather than a reward for service, the Court found that the payment was indeed influenced by the prior employment relationship. The rationale behind the board’s decision to grant the payment stemmed from a recognition of the employees' contributions, particularly in light of their lost opportunity for profit due to the special dividend ([23]).

Despite the fact that the options remained unexercised, the Court focused on the board's acknowledgment of the employees’ past services, noting that the causative event behind the ex gratia payment (the special dividend and subsequent company liquidation) had created a direct loss for those option holders. Stevens and his fellow R3.70 option holders were specifically targeted because their options had become unexercisable or worthless, reinforcing the view that their employment status played a significant role in the decision to grant the payment ([22], [23]).

Moreover, it was clarified that the distinction between compensation for loss versus recognition of service was not determinative of taxability; the critical aspect was whether the receipt was in respect of the taxpayer's employment ([22]). Given that the board's resolution was made in its capacity as an employer and directly in context to the employees’ contributions, the Court found an unbroken link between the payment and the employment relationship ([23]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the Tax Court's decision, confirming that the ex gratia payment received by Stevens constituted gross income under the relevant provisions of the Income Tax Act. The Court concluded that the payment was fundamentally linked to Stevens' employment and past services rendered, regardless of the intention behind its determination ([24]). Thus, the appeal was dismissed with costs, reaffirming the broad interpretation of gross income and the causal relationship required between such payments and employment obligations ([24]).

---

In the context of tax law, this case highlights important principles concerning the interpretation of 'gross income' and the nuances in determining the nature of payments received by employees, illustrating the courts' commitment to recognizing the realities of employment relationships in tax assessments.
