# Case Note: Lungisa Grifhs v The State (433/2020) [2021] ZASCA 112

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts ([1]–[2])

The appellant, Mr. Lungisa Grifhs, was convicted along with two co-accused for the crime of murder on 28 November 2018 in the Regional Court for the Eastern Cape Region, Mthatha. The conviction stemmed from the singular testimony of a witness, Mr. Bavu, which the Regional Court deemed sufficient to establish guilt. The court recognized substantial and compelling circumstances permitting a sentence of 16 years imprisonment, which deviated from the minimum prescribed under the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997.

Following his sentencing, Grifhs sought leave to appeal both his conviction and the sentence. His initial application was rejected by the Regional Court, and a subsequent petition to the Judge President of the Eastern Cape Local Division of the High Court also failed. Consequently, he sought special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) under s 16(1)(b) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which was granted on 15 May 2020.

### I – Issues ([4])

The primary legal issue for determination was whether there were reasonable prospects of success regarding the appeal against the conviction and sentence of Grifhs. This question arose from the reliance on the testimony of a single witness, which introduced complexities regarding the standard of that evidence and its reliability.

### R – Rules ([4])

The judgment relies on the established precedent concerning the evidential weight attached to the testimony of a single witness. This is particularly elucidated in cases such as *Van Wyk v The State* and *Galela v The State* [2014] ZASCA 152, which assert that a conviction based on the evidence of a single witness can only be sustained if the evidence is “clear and satisfactory in all material respects.” The court evaluates the reliability of such evidence, especially in light of contradictions between oral testimony and prior written statements.

### A – Application ([4]–[5])

In assessing the case, Mbatha JA noted the substantial contradictions between Mr. Bavu’s oral testimony and his written statement to the police. These inconsistencies raised doubts about the reliability of the witness’s account, leading the court to determine that the appellant had reasonable prospects of success on appeal. The judgment reiterated the necessity that the evidence be dependable, especially when it constitutes the sole basis for conviction. By highlighting these discrepancies, the court underscored the importance of a thorough examination of witness credibility and the implications for a fair trial.

### C – Conclusion ([6])

The SCA upheld the appeal, reversing the lower court’s decision and granting Grifhs the right to appeal both his conviction and sentence. This ruling emphasizes the critical role of evidential integrity in criminal proceedings, particularly the reliance on witness testimony. The court’s decision reinforces the benchmark that convictions based on singular witness statements must meet stringent reliability standards, and sets a legal precedent that supports the right to a fair appeal process. Given the court's findings, the case serves as an important reference point for future cases involving the testimony of single witnesses and the thresholds for considering appeals under similar circumstances.

### Implications for Future Cases

This case is particularly significant in its affirmation of the principles governing the use of single witness testimony in criminal law, illustrating the court's vigilance against potential miscarriages of justice. The emphasis on "reasonable prospects of success" forms a critical consideration for appellants in seeking judicial recourse. This judgment could influence future appeals, particularly those involving the credibility of evidence, the handling of witness statements, and the broader parameters of how courts assess the merits of appeals arising from convictions based on limited evidential foundations.
