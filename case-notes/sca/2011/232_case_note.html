Supreme Court of Appeal

Equity Aviation Services (Pty) Ltd v South African Transport and Allied Workers Union (SATAWU) [2011] 232 ZASCA 478/09

On 13 November 2003, SATAWU referred a wage dispute to the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2011 [2011] 232 ZASCA 478/09

F – Facts

The appeal arises from the majority judgment of the Labour Appeal Court concerning the interpretation of s 64(1)(b) of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA) regarding the lawful right to strike ([paras 1–3]). The appellant, Equity Aviation Services (Pty) Ltd, an aviation logistics company, had a majority of its employees belonging to the first respondent, the South African Transport and Allied Workers Union (SATAWU). Out of 1,157 employees, 725 were union members, while the remaining employees were not ([para 4]).

On 13 November 2003, SATAWU referred a wage dispute to the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA). Following unsuccessful conciliation, the CCMA issued a certificate of non-resolution on 15 December 2003, on which day the union issued a strike notice indicating a strike would commence on 18 December 2003 ([para 5]). The strike initiated by the union members lasted for four months and was deemed lawful under s 64(1)(b) of the LRA. However, employees who were not union members participated in the strike without providing separate notice, leading to their dismissal for unauthorized absenteeism on 19 November 2004 ([para 6]).

The dismissed employees argued their dismissals were automatically unfair under s 187(1)(a) of the LRA, contending that they were entitled to participate in the strike without separate notice. The Labour Court initially agreed with this interpretation, ruling for their reinstatement ([para 7]). Upon appeal, the Labour Appeal Court found that non-union employees were required to provide a separate notice for their strike participation to be lawful ([para 9]). This led to the current appeal.

I – Issues

The central issue in this case is whether non-union employees who participated in a strike initiated by a union must provide separate notice of their intention to strike in order for their strike action to be considered lawful under s 64(1)(b) of the LRA ([paras 3, 4]). Specifically, the court needed to consider if the interpretation provided by the Labour Appeal Court was correct, and whether it unduly restricted the right to strike as provided for by the LRA ([para 11]).

R – Rules

The primary rule to consider is found in s 64(1)(b) of the Labour Relations Act, which mandates that at least 48 hours' notice must be given in writing to the employer of the intent to strike ([para 2]). The Act aims to promote orderly collective bargaining and effective resolution of labor disputes ([para 13]). Case law pertaining to the interpretation of strike notices, such as Ceramic Industries Ltd t/a Betta Sanitaryware & another v NCBAWU & others ([1997] 6 BLLR 697 (LAC)), supports the requirement of a proper strike notice to facilitate adequate preparations by employers ([para 14]). Additionally, jurisprudence, including Chirwa v Transnet Ltd & others ([2008] 4 SA 367 (CC)), affirms that statutory provisions should be interpreted in alignment with the objectives of the LRA ([para 12]).

A – Application

The court, particularly Khampepe ADJP in the majority judgment, noted that s 64(1) does not explicitly state who must refer a dispute to the CCMA or who must provide strike notice, necessitating an interpretation that aligns with the objectives of the LRA ([para 10]). The majority opined that requiring separate notices from non-union members would lead to excessive technicalities that undermine the purpose of the Act and ultimately limit workers' rights to strike ([para 16]).

Davis JA further supported that the strike notice given by a significant number of employees should suffice to ensure orderly industrial relations ([para 17]). The majority judgements found that the requirement for multiple notices would impose unnecessary obstacles to workers' rights within the structure of labor relations.

Opposing this view, Zondo JP in dissent argued that without the requirement for separate notices from non-union employees, disorderly strikes could ensue, and the principle of orderly industrial action would be compromised ([paras 19–21]). This demonstrated a significant divergence in interpretation; while the majority focused on promoting rights, the dissent emphasized the importance of practicalities in terms of maintaining order within labor relations.

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal by Equity Aviation Services, stating that non-union employees must provide separate strike notices for their actions to be lawful ([paras 28–30]). The court concluded that this requirement aligns with the LRA’s intent to foster orderly industrial relations and that failing to do so would lead to chaotic collective bargaining practices. The court's decision has significant implications for labor law in South Africa, reinforcing the necessity for compliance with the procedural requirements set forth in the LRA for all employees wishing to partake in strike actions ([paras 29–30]).

Moreover, the court noted the significant delays throughout the adjudicatory process, extending the case's time frame and highlighting systemic issues within the labor dispute resolution mechanisms ([para 29]). This ruling thus serves as an important precedent regarding the interpretation and practical application of the right to strike within South African labor law.
