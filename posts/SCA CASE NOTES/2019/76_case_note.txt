# Case Note: P V v E V (843/2018) ZASCA 76 (30 May 2019)

## FIRAC Method

**F – Facts**: 
The case arises from an appeal concerning an ante-nuptial contract signed by the appellant, Mr. P V, and the respondent, Mrs. E V, prior to their marriage on 8 January 2011 ([1]). Due to her concerns regarding the matrimonial property regime, the respondent expressed her preference for a marriage out of community of property with accrual ([2]). Despite her apprehensions, they signed a power of attorney authorizing Mr. Weyers to execute the ante-nuptial contract on their behalf. The respondent testified that she believed the contract could be amended after the honeymoon, following assurances from both the appellant and Mr. Weyers ([3], [4]). 

Post-marriage, marital discord arose, culminating in the appellant filing for divorce in October 2012, attaching the signed ante-nuptial contract ([7]). The respondent counterclaimed, seeking rectification of the contract to reflect the accrual system, asserting that it was the common intention of both parties to include it ([8]). The trial court, after some proceedings and admissions, found in favor of the respondent, ordering that the ante-nuptial contract should be rectified to include accrual ([9], [10]).

The appellant's subsequent appeal to the Full Court was dismissed, solidifying the trial court's decision. This appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal challenges both the findings of the Full Court concerning the existence of a common intention and the rectification of the ante-nuptial contract ([11]).

**I – Issues**: 
1. Whether the respondent could successfully invoke the remedy of rectification of the ante-nuptial contract based on a supposed common mistake or misunderstanding ([12]).
2. The validity and enforceability of the clear and unambiguous terms in Clause 3, which explicitly excluded the accrual system from their matrimonial property regime ([19]).
3. The implications of contractual capacity and the weighing of evidence regarding the parties' understanding and intentions at the time of signing the ante-nuptial contract ([17], [19]).

**R – Rules**: 
- Rectification is available where the written document does not accurately reflect the true intention of the parties, necessitating a common mistake or misunderstanding regarding the agreement ([13], [14]).
- The onus lies on the party seeking rectification to establish, on the balance of probabilities, that their version of the agreement is accurate ([13]).
- Courts have maintained that agreements between parties of full contractual capacity should be enforced strictly according to their terms, as per established contract law ([17]).

In regards to matrimonial property, Section 2 of the Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984 stipulates that unless expressly excluded, marriages out of community of property are subject to the accrual system ([18], [20]).

**A – Application**: 
The court evaluated whether rectification could apply despite the absence of a proven mutual intention regarding the accrual system. The evidence presented indicated a lack of consensus between the parties as the respondent did not show that she had a misunderstanding when signing the power of attorney, nor did the trial court adequately assess the credibility of the opposing testimonies ([13], [19]). The appeal court cited that merely expressing an intention to amend the contract post-nuptials does not provide a basis for rectification since there was no mutual agreement to exclude the accrual ([15], [20]).

Contrary to the Full Court's approach, which implied a misinterpretation of the appellant's statements regarding the future amendment of the contract, the Supreme Court highlighted that the parties were bound by the clear terms of their ante-nuptial contract as registered. The findings of the trial court were scrutinized, with particular attention on the lack of sworn credibility evaluations that led to concluding there was no common consensus on the terms of the marriage, which directly affected the alleged claims of rectification ([23]).

**C – Conclusion**: 
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, reversing the Full Court’s decision. The court concluded that the respondent did not discharge the burden of proving a common mistake or misunderstanding; hence, the effort to rectify the ante-nuptial contract was dismissed ([24]). This case reinforces contractual autonomy, emphasizing that unequivocal terms in an ante-nuptial contract must prevail unless substantiated claims of prior mutual intention are demonstrably substantiated. The ruling has broader implications for matrimonial agreements, establishing the imperative of clarity and mutual understanding prior to executing such contracts ([24]).

In light of the above, this case serves as a critical reminder of the importance of precise drafting in ante-nuptial contracts and the enforceability of agreed terms in family law contexts. The decision underlines the reluctance of the courts to alter the clear intentions of the parties unless adequately proven otherwise.
