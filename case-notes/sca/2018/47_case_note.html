Supreme Court of Appeal

Thakeli v S (231/2017) [2018] ZASCA 47

The pivotal event occurred when, after the defense had presented its case and the appellants had testified, the trial court amended the charge sheet to delete the reference to section 51(2).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 47

F – Facts

The appellants, Mr. Sente Joseph Thakeli (1st appellant) and Mr. Samuel Zambuk Marumo (2nd appellant), were charged with murder under the provisions of section 51(2) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 (the "Act") in the regional court at Welkom. Following a trial, they were convicted on 23 August 2011 and sentenced to 28 years' imprisonment under section 51(1) of the Act despite an initial charge alleging a lesser offense under section 51(2) ([paras 1–4]).

The pivotal event occurred when, after the defense had presented its case and the appellants had testified, the trial court amended the charge sheet to delete the reference to section 51(2). This amendment ostensibly took place under section 86 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, purportedly claiming that it would not prejudice the appellants ([para 4]). The trial court concluded that the appellants faced a charge of murder that warranted the harsher minimum sentence of life imprisonment, although it eventually found substantial and compelling circumstances justifying a lesser sentence of 28 years ([paras 4–5]).

The appellants' initial attempts to appeal their conviction and sentence were unsuccessful at the regional court level but were subsequently granted by the Free State High Court's full bench, which later dismissed the appeal ([para 1]). With special leave, the appeal focused solely on the sentence imposed.

I – Issues

The primary legal issue in this case is whether the trial court erred in amending the charge sheet after the appellants had already pleaded and testified, thereby increasing the potential sentence from 15 years (under section 51(2)) to 28 years (under section 51(1)) without allowing the appellants to address the amendment ([para 2]). This brings into consideration the implications of fair trial rights as afforded by the Constitution of South Africa.

R – Rules

The relevant legal principles are drawn from the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 and the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977. Specifically, section 51 of the Act stipulates minimum sentences for murder based on factors such as premeditation and the offender's criminal history. Section 51(1) sets a minimum sentence of life imprisonment for premeditated murder, while section 51(2) establishes a minimum of 15 years for first offenders ([para 3]).

The principles surrounding fair trial rights are also crucial. The court referenced the case of S v Makatu where it was emphasized that an accused must be aware from the outset of the charges they face and the consequences of those charges ([para 6]). The court asserted that amendments to indictments during a trial must be conducted carefully to avoid prejudicing the defendant.

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed the trial court's amendment of the charge sheet and determined that it detrimentally affected the appellants' understanding of their charges and the potential sentences. By altering the charge after the defense's testimony, the trial court not only failed to properly inform the appellants of the specific charges faced, thereby impeding their ability to prepare their defense, but also significantly increased their exposure to a harsher penalty ([paras 4–7]).

The court highlighted that this amendment, described by the lower court as a mere "curing of a typographical error," actually changed the substantive nature of the charge. The appellants were deprived of the opportunity to contest the amendment, which constituted a fundamental irregularity that violated their right to a fair trial ([paras 6–7]).

The court further considered whether any substantial and compelling reasons existed to justify a deviation from the 15-year sentence stipulated in section 51(2). The reviewing panel thoroughly examined the aggravating circumstances of the crime—particularly the brutal nature of the attack, which involved multiple stab wounds inflicted upon an unarmed victim—while taking into account factors related to the appellants, such as their youth and status as first offenders. Ultimately, the court found no compelling circumstances that would warrant a deviation from the minimum sentence ([paras 8–9]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal against the sentence imposed on the appellants, concluding that the amendment of the charge sheet violated their fair trial rights. It ruled that the correct application of the law would have resulted in a sentence of 15 years under section 51(2) of the Act, barring any substantial and compelling circumstances justifying a harsher sentence. As a result, the trial court's original sentence was set aside, and the appellants were each sentenced to 15 years' imprisonment ([para 9]). This case reinforces the principle that fair trial rights must be upheld rigorously to maintain the integrity of court proceedings and to protect the rights of the accused. The implications extend to the necessity for clarity in charges and the importance of defendants' rights to properly contest those charges throughout the judicial process.
