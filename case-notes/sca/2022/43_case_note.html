Songo v Minister of Police and Others (Case No. 220/2021) [2022] ZASCA 43

The first and second special pleas were based on alleged failures to comply with procedural requirements of the Uniform Rules of Courts, and were subsequently abandoned ([2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 43

F – Facts

The appellant, Mr. Simon Songo, was convicted of two counts of murder by the North-West Division of the High Court in November 2009, resulting in an 18-year prison sentence. On 15 October 2015, he successfully appealed this conviction in the Full Bench of the high court and was released ([1]). Subsequently, Songo initiated an action for damages against the three respondents: the Minister of Police, the National Director of Public Prosecutions, and the Minister of Justice and Correctional Services, in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria. Six special pleas were raised by the respondents, which consisted of procedural and substantive challenges to Songo's claim ([2]).

The first and second special pleas were based on alleged failures to comply with procedural requirements of the Uniform Rules of Courts, and were subsequently abandoned ([2]). The third special plea argued that Songo did not comply with the requirements of the Institution of Legal Proceedings Against Certain Organs of State Act 40 of 2002, but this plea was dismissed by the court ([5]). The fourth and fifth special pleas related to the assertion that Songo had no cause of action since his imprisonment was lawful, while the sixth special plea claimed misjoinder of the third respondent ([3]).

Despite being prompted to resolve these issues, the court, led by Sardiwalla J, failed to determine the fourth and fifth special pleas and ordered them to be adjudicated separately, leading to confusion regarding the appeal process ([6], [8]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions before the Supreme Court of Appeal were:

  1. Whether the court a quo was correct in ordering that the fourth and fifth special pleas be adjudicated separately and in upholding the sixth special plea of misjoinder ([2], [6]).
  2. Whether the court a quo discharged its duty sufficiently by not determining all relevant disputes, particularly those related to the fourth and fifth special pleas ([6]).
  3. Whether the Supreme Court of Appeal had jurisdiction to address the issues left undetermined by the court a quo, or if the matter should be remitted back for proper adjudication ([8]).

R – Rules

The court recognized that a definitive decision must meet specific criteria to be appealable. The principles established in Zweni v Minister of Law and Order, namely that the decision must:

  1. Be final in effect and not subject to alteration by the court of first instance,
  2. Be definitive of the rights of the parties,
  3. Dispose of a significant portion of the relief claimed in the main proceedings, were central to the deliberation ([9]).

Additionally, the necessity for courts to finalize disputes appropriately was emphasized, referencing Solidarity and Another v Black First Land First and Others where courts must bring clarity and enforcement capability to orders made ([10]).

A – Application

In applying these principles, the Supreme Court of Appeal determined that the order from the court a quo effectively postponed the determination of both the fourth and fifth special pleas, failing to deliver a conclusive ruling which would allow for meaningful appeal ([8], [11]). The sixth special plea concerning misjoinder was upheld despite the overarching issue of whether Songo possessed a cause of action, which remained unresolved.

Moreover, the court highlighted the importance of judicial economy and the potential injustice of treating the claims piecemeal. The Supreme Court emphasized that the resolution of the fourth and fifth special pleas could potentially develop the law concerning claims for damages following wrongful imprisonment, thus necessitating their adjudication ([11]). The ruling took into account the absence of judicial clarity and consistency, reiterating that the role of the appellate court is to correct legal errors made by the lower court.

The court ultimately dismissed the sixth special plea, finding it unwarranted given that the overarching cause of action was still in dispute ([12]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Songo's appeal, recognizing that the lower court erred in handling the special pleas. The Court set aside paragraphs 3 and 4 of the order from the court a quo, instead concluding that the dismissal of the sixth special plea was appropriate with costs. Crucially, the Court remitted the matter back to the high court for a proper determination of the fourth and fifth special pleas ([12]).

This judgment underscores the critical balance between procedural compliance and substantive justice, particularly in cases involving claims related to wrongful imprisonment. The implications extend beyond this case, potentially influencing future applications for damages in similar contexts and advancing discussions about the development of common law concerning the rights of individuals wrongfully convicted.
