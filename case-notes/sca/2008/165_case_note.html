Supreme Court of Appeal

Shunmugam and Others v National Democratic Convention ([2008] ZASCA 165)

The appellants challenged their expulsions in the Pietermaritzburg High Court, asserting that they were unlawfully expelled and, therefore, retained their status as councillors with the right to cross the floor.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2008 [2008] ZASCA 165)

F – Facts

The case involves multiple appellants (Matthew Shunmugam et al.) who were members of the National Democratic Convention (NADECO) and served as municipal councillors. In late August 2007, prior to the opening of a "floor crossing" window that allowed political representatives to change parties without losing their seats, the appellants were expelled from NADECO in a bid to preempt potential defections due to the impending floor crossing window ([paras 1–2]).

The appellants challenged their expulsions in the Pietermaritzburg High Court, asserting that they were unlawfully expelled and, therefore, retained their status as councillors with the right to cross the floor.

Due to inadequate notice of the urgency of their application, NADECO contested the appellants' ability to cross the floor amid their legal battle, leading to two intertwining applications before the High Court ([paras 4–5]). The first case (6883/07) sought to declare the expulsions unlawful, and the second case (7680/07) included a counter application by some appellants after they had crossed to other parties ([paras 7–12]).

The High Court eventually ruled against the appellants, holding that they had ceased being councillors due to lawful expulsions. The appellants subsequently appealed the decision ([paras 12–14]).

I – Issues

  1. Were the appellants lawfully expelled from NADECO? This hinged on the validity of the expulsions in the context of the Constitution's provisions on floor crossing and party membership ([paras 14, 16–17]).
  2. Did any court order prevent the appellants from exercising their right to cross the floor during the designated period? This issue addressed whether the 31 August interim order, which stated the appellants were to "remain suspended as members" of NADECO, implicitly prohibited their party switching ([paras 18–20]).

R – Rules

The case primarily involves the interpretation of Schedule 6B of the South African Constitution, which governs the floor crossing mechanism for political representatives. Key provisions included:

  • Item 1: A councillor ceases to be a member of a municipal council if they cease to be a member of the political party ([para 23]).
  • Item 4(2)(c): Prohibits political parties from suspending or terminating membership during a floor crossing period ([para 24]).
  • Legal precedents such as Oudekraal Estates (Pty) Ltd v City of Cape Town and Seale v Van Rooyen NO clarified the implications of invalid administrative acts that appeared effective until proven otherwise ([paras 14, 15]).

A – Application

The court analyzed whether the appellants' expulsions were indeed unlawful as contended, emphasizing that NADECO had conceded that the expulsions were not valid, which formed the crux of the decision about their party membership ([paras 16–17]). The court rejected NADECO's argument based on Oudekraal, asserting that the case did not involve subsequent acts reliant on prior invalidating acts, but rather a direct challenge to the vital question of membership and standing at the commencement of the floor crossing window.

Furthermore, the interpretation of the 31 August order came under scrutiny. NADECO's claim that the order "suspended" the appellants was deemed incorrect. The court opined that the wording was absurd, as the dispute concerned the validity of the expulsions—not suspension ([paras 19–20]). Ultimately, the court held that the order did not bar the appellants from exercising their right under Schedule 6B to cross the floor, particularly as their expulsions had not been legally valid.

C – Conclusion

The appeal by the appellants succeeded. The Supreme Court of Appeal set aside the High Court’s orders, ruling that since the expulsions had been unlawful, the appellants remained members of NADECO during the floor crossing window and were entitled to switch political allegiance without losing their municipal council positions ([paras 27–29]). This case reasserted the principles of legality in administrative action and clarified the interplay between party membership rights and the constitutional provisions governing floor crossing, underscoring the importance of procedural fairness and validity in political party governance.

The court concluded that the original High Court's interpretation was flawed and that the appellants had retained their privileges as councillors, reinforcing the constitutional provisions that protect the political rights of representatives ([paras 27–29]).

In terms of costs, the court ordered that NADECO must pay the appellants’ costs, including provisions for two counsel where applicable ([para 29]).

References

  1. Oudekraal Estates (Pty) Ltd v City of Cape Town 2004 (6) SA 222 (SCA).
  2. Seale v Van Rooyen NO; Provincial Government, North West Province v Van Rooyen NO 2008 (4) SA 43 (SCA).
  3. Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, Schedule 6B.
