Supreme Court of Appeal

TCT Leisure (Pty) Ltd v The Commissioner for the South African Revenue Services (59/09) [2010] ZASCA 10

The matter involves TCT Leisure (Pty) Ltd (hereinafter "the taxpayer"), which operated in the time-share accommodation market under an entity known as The Holiday Club ([1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2010 [2010] ZASCA 10

F – Facts

The matter involves TCT Leisure (Pty) Ltd (hereinafter "the taxpayer"), which operated in the time-share accommodation market under an entity known as The Holiday Club ([1]). Initially, VAT was paid on time-share sales. However, following the reorganization of the Holiday Club in 1995, the taxation status of such sales was called into question. Specifically, the court considered whether VAT was payable for the goods sold after the restructuring, particularly concerning the transfer of preference shares and associated rights ([2]).

During this reorganization, a new company called Leisure Holiday Club Ltd (LHC) emerged. The taxpayer transferred properties and use rights to LHC in exchange for preference shares and debentures, subsequently selling these shares to the public along with 'points rights' for holiday accommodation ([3]). An audit led the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Services (SARS) to issue revised assessments asserting that the taxpayer's sales constituted taxable supplies under Section 7(1) of the Value-Added Tax Act 89 of 1991 ([4]).

The taxpayer, arguing for an exemption under Section 12(a) of the VAT Act, contended that the rights sold were "financial services" in the form of "equity securities," which should be exempt from VAT ([5]). Specifically, the burden of proof regarding this exemption rested on the taxpayer, as outlined in Section 37 of the VAT Act ([4], [5]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue in this case was whether the transactions conducted by the taxpayer involved the supply of 'equity securities' which would exempt it from VAT under Section 12(a) of the VAT Act. This hinges on whether the occupation rights sold alongside shares were incorporated as integral components of the shares, thereby characterizing the transaction primarily as a sale of financial services rather than goods ([6], [10]).

Additionally, there was another questioning of whether the rights to accommodation were indeed part of the rights attached to the shares or whether they were separated and thus treated as distinct elements ([8], [11]).

R – Rules

The legal framework relevant to this case consists primarily of the Value-Added Tax Act 89 of 1991. The key sections being discussed are:

  1. Section 7(1) - Imposes VAT on supplies made in the course of an enterprise, defining 'goods' to include 'fixed property' ([4]).
  2. Section 12(a) - Provides that the supply of specified financial services, including the issuance of equity securities, shall be exempt from VAT ([5]).
  3. Section 37 - Places the burden of proof on a person claiming any exemption from VAT, with further stipulations regarding the decisions of the Commissioner ([5], [12]).
  4. Section 10(22) - Addresses composite supplies, stating that where a taxable supply is part of a larger consideration, it would be subject to VAT according to the proportion properly attributed to it ([12]).

A – Application

In assessing the application of these statutory rules, the court examined the structural changes made in 1995 to the time-share scheme. The amendment to the articles of association specifically removed any entitlement for shareholders to use the property, thus severing the connection between shareholding and occupancy rights ([7], [9]).

The court concluded that the shares sold did not convey the points rights that entitled members to occupation; rather, they represented separate, distinct transactions. The reasoning drew parallels to a previous case cited from New Zealand, where membership rights were considered incidental to share ownership through a club's structure ([10]). However, the court found the facts in the current case diverged significantly, as occupation rights were sold as separate entities ([11]).

Furthermore, it was determined that even if portions of the sale could encompass financial services, the burden remained on the taxpayer to demonstrate that the turnover from these sales was exempt under the statute. As the taxpayer conceded that rights to accommodation did not form a part of the shares sold, this rendered the entire transaction, including the shares, subject to VAT ([10], [12]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the taxpayer's appeal, ruling that the rights sold did not constitute the sale of equity securities exempt from VAT as claimed. The court confirmed that the sales made by the taxpayer were subject to VAT, as the specific rights bundled with the shares did not satisfy the legislative criteria for financial services under the VAT Act ([13]).

This ruling firmly establishes that the distinct separation of rights within corporate structures significantly affects tax obligations under VAT legislation, clarifying the parameters of what constitutes a financial service versus a taxable supply. The taxpayer was ordered to pay costs, reinforcing the court's stance that compliance with the VAT Act must be strictly adhered to in the context of complex service offerings like time-share arrangements.
