FIRAC Case Note: Certification of the Amended Text of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996

Constitutional Court 1996

FIRAC Case Note: Certification of the Amended Text of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996

F – Facts

The Constitutional Court of South Africa was called upon to certify the Amended Text (AT) of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa following the Constitutional Assembly's adoption of the new constitutional text in May 1996. This certification process was mandated under sections 71 and 73A of the Interim Constitution (IC), which required the court to assess compliance with Constitutional Principles (CPs) outlined in Schedule 4 of the IC ([paras 1–2]). The AT aimed to rectify non-compliance issues identified in the previous Certification Judgment (CJ), which had withheld certification of the initial New Text (NT) due to various reasons, including insufficient protections for fundamental rights and inadequate provincial powers and functions ([para 2]).

Following extensive deliberations, including public submissions and arguments heard from political parties and other stakeholders, the court meticulously examined the AT to determine its alignment with each CP ([paras 4–7]).

The CA satisfactorily addressed most previous deficiencies and complied with the majority of the requirements, leading the court to focus on specific contentious areas like the Bill of Rights, local governance structures, and the powers of the provinces ([paras 33–204]).

I – Issues

The primary legal question was whether the provisions of the AT complied with the CPs as required by the IC. Specific issues included:

  1. The adequacy of safeguards for the independence of key institutions like the Public Service Commission (PSC).
  2. The extent to which provincial powers were decreased in comparison to those established under the Interim Constitution.
  3. The certification of rights protections, particularly concerning collective bargaining and local government, which had previously been deemed insufficient ([paras 1–6]).

R – Rules

The court relied upon several constitutional provisions relevant to the certification:

  • Interim Constitution: Sections 71 and 73A, which delineate the grounds for certification and the necessity for compliance with CPs.
  • Constitutional Principles: Set forth in Schedule 4, these principles outline fundamental rights and the structure of government, emphasizing the protection of human rights and the autonomy of provinces.
  • Previous Judgments: The CJ provided critical benchmarks for assessing compliance, highlighting substantial inadequacies in the NT concerning rights protections and provincial powers ([paras 3–7].

A – Application

The court engaged in a thorough analysis of how the CA addressed previously identified shortcomings:

  • Bill of Rights: Compared to the NT, the AT enhanced equity in rights protections, incorporating clearer definitions and enforcement provisions that aligned with CP II ([paras 17–30]).
  • Provincial Powers: The amendments acknowledged concerns raised regarding substantial inferiority of provincial authority compared to the IC. The court found that while some areas remained constrained (e.g., tertiary education), others had seen enhanced powers, particularly with regard to police oversight and appointments ([paras 158–177]).
  • Independence of Institutions: The court noted that the AT provided increased safeguards for the Public Protector and the Auditor-General, coupled with a robust oversight commitment from the PSC, addressing previous inadequacies directly ([paras 128–139]).

C – Conclusion

The court ultimately concluded that although the powers of the provinces in the AT remained lower than those accorded by the IC, they were not "substantially less" as previously determined with respect to the NT. Furthermore, it certified that all provisions of the AT complied with the CPs, facilitating its enactment and validating the democratic foundation of the new Constitution. This holding affirmed the CA's capacity to craft a document that recognized and incorporated essential human rights while ensuring the functional autonomy of provincial governance ([paras 204–206]).

In sum, the court’s order to certify the AT marked a significant step in solidifying South Africa's constitutional democracy, demonstrating the essential balancing of human rights protections and provincial autonomy necessary for effective governance.
