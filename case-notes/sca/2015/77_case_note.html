Supreme Court of Appeal

FIRAC Analysis of African Exploration Mining and Finance Corporation (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Mineral Resources [2015] ZASCA 77

The core matter arises from a dispute regarding mining rights under the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (the Act).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 [2015] ZASCA 77

F – Facts

The core matter arises from a dispute regarding mining rights under the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (the Act). African Exploration Mining and Finance Corporation (Pty) Ltd (AFEX) and the Strategic Fuel Fund Association (SSF) (the appellants) contested the decision of the Minister of Mineral Resources to convert an old-order mining right owned by Tavistock Collieries (Pty) Ltd (Tavistock) into a mining right.

On 15 February 2010, AFEX applied for a coal prospecting right on certain portions of the farm Klippoortje 32 IS, but this application was denied by the Regional Manager of the Department of Mineral Resources on 10 March 2010 due to Tavistock's existing old order mining right over the same land ([paras 1–2]). Shortly after, on 29 March 2010, Tavistock's old order right was converted into a mining right by the Director-General of the Department of Mineral Resources, who had acted on Tavistock's application submitted on 6 December 2007 ([para 3]).

In March 2011, AFEX sought an order from the Gauteng Division of the High Court to annul both the conversion of Tavistock's right and the refusal of its prospecting right application. However, the High Court found that AFEX lacked standing to contest the conversion and failed to exhaust internal remedies under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 ([paras 4–5]).

The judgment in the court a quo primarily focused on procedural grounds, leaving the substantive issues unexamined, thus forming the appeal basis ([paras 6–7]).

I – Issues

The principal legal question for the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether the Minister's decision to convert Tavistock's old order mining right could be challenged by AFEX. This question inherently required an examination of whether Tavistock was exercising its mining right prior to the implementation of the Act, which was crucial for establishing AFEX's standing to apply for a prospecting right on the same land ([paras 1, 4, 7]).

Other subsidiary issues included:

  1. Whether AFEX's application to prospect for coal included a legitimate claim to prospect in situ, as opposed to merely on waste dumps ([para 6]).
  2. The substantive implications of AFEX's allegations that Tavistock had failed to meet its mining obligations under their lease agreement ([para 14]).

R – Rules

The legal principles applicable in this case drew heavily from the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act and case law surrounding old order mining rights. Specifically, the relevant provisions can be found within Schedule II to the Act, particularly item 7, which outlines the conditions under which old order mining rights persist and may be converted into new mining rights.

Item 7 provides:

  1. Old order mining rights continue for a maximum of five years and must be converted before they cease to exist.
  2. The Minister must convert an old order mining right to a mining right if the holder complies with specified requirements, including conducting mining operations ([paras 10–13]).

Judicial interpretations from prior cases, such as Xstrata South Africa (Pty) Ltd & others v SFF Association ([2012] ZASCA 20), were also cited to establish that the provisions of the Act do not simply preserve common law rights but rather create new statutory rights that incorporate previous entitlements ([para 20]).

A – Application

In its analysis, the SCA determined that AFEX's challenge to the conversion of Tavistock's right was fundamentally flawed. The court clarified that AFEX's case hinged on the assertion that Tavistock had failed to exercise its mining right effectively prior to the coming into effect of the Act ([paras 22–23]).

AFEX argued that based on clause 7.5 of the lease agreement between SSF and Tavistock, which mandated that mining begin within three years and maintain specified production levels, Tavistock's failure to mine at the required rate resulted in a loss of its rights ([paras 16–19]). However, the SCA found insufficient evidence to support the claim that Tavistock had not met its mining obligations, emphasizing the lack of definitive production figures before the dispute arose ([paras 24–25]).

The SCA examined the nature of old order rights and reinforced that only those who were actively mining before the enactment of the Act could claim old order rights and pursue conversion ([paras 22, 26]). The court concluded that Tavistock lawfully converted its old order right into a mining right, as the necessary conditions were satisfied, thereby upholding the Minister's decision ([para 27]).

Moreover, since AFEX had no standing to contest the conversion due to its failed application for a prospecting right on land that Tavistock had a legitimate claim over, the arguments concerning procedural fairness were rendered moot ([para 28]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA dismissed AFEX's appeal with costs, asserting that both the procedural and substantive challenges to the conversion of Tavistock’s mining right were unfounded ([para 28]). The court affirmed the principles governing the transition from old order rights to new mining rights under the Act, highlighting that statutory rights derived from the prior entitlements remained intact if the conditions for conversion were fulfilled.

This judgment clarifies the boundaries surrounding the rights of old order mining holders and underscores the importance of fulfilling operational obligations. The failure of AFEX to establish a legitimate basis for its claims not only underscores the significance of compliance under statutory law but also illustrates the complexities surrounding mining rights in South Africa's legal landscape.
