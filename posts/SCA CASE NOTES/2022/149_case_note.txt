# Case Note: FIRAC Method on **Siyangena Technologies (Pty) Ltd v PRASA and Others** (487/2021) [2022] ZASCA 149

## F – Facts

The case **Siyangena Technologies (Pty) Ltd v Passenger Rail Agency of South Africa (PRASA) and Others** involved a series of procurement contracts awarded by PRASA to Siyangena Technologies for the provision and maintenance of an integrated security access management system (ISAMS) at various train stations. The contracts, initially valued at approximately R5.5 billion, were challenged in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, and were declared constitutionally invalid due to irregularities in the procurement process ([1], [2]).

PRASA challenged these contracts based on evidence of maladministration and violations of the procurement processes under section 217 of the South African Constitution. Notably, it was found that PRASA failed to act in a manner that was "fair, equitable, transparent, competitive and cost-effective" in their dealings with Siyangena, leading the High Court to invalidate the contracts and set them aside under section 172(1)(a) of the Constitution ([2], [5]).

A significant aspect of this case is that PRASA, under a newly constituted Board, voluntarily sought to review its own decisions to award these contracts. The High Court acknowledged widespread corruption in PRASA under the leadership of former officials, particularly former Group Chief Executive Officer, Mr. Montana. Evidence indicated that the procurement processes had become riddled with irregularities, including bypassing established checks and validating contracts without proper scrutiny. Several contracts were extended to Siyangena without a necessary competitive bidding process ([5]–[10]). 

The High Court's remedial order mandated an independent engineer to assess the value of the works Siyangena performed, considering payments made to them by PRASA, which became a focal point of Siyangena's appeal ([2], [3]).

## I – Issues

This case raised several critical legal issues:

1. **Legitimacy of Procurement Process**: Did the contracts awarded to Siyangena comply with the procurement standards mandated by section 217 of the South African Constitution? ([2], [5]).
   
2. **Constitutional Invalidity**: Should the award of the contracts be declared unconstitutional, and what are the repercussions of such a declaration regarding the parties involved? ([2], [34]).

3. **Innocent Party Defense**: Is Siyangena Technologies an "innocent party" in the alleged corruption and irregularities surrounding the contract process? ([4], [36]).

4. **Remedy and Condonation for Delay**: Did the High Court correctly exercise its discretion in granting remedial relief, and should it have condoned the delay by PRASA in bringing the review application? ([31], [45]).

5. **Independent Engineer's Assessment**: Should the appointment of an independent engineer to assess the value of work performed under the contracts be upheld? ([37], [39]).

## R – Rules

The Supreme Court of Appeal relied on several legal principles and precedents:

1. **Section 217 of the Constitution**: Establishes the requirements for public procurement, emphasizing fairness, transparency, and competitiveness ([2], [5]).

2. **Section 172(1)(a) and (b) of the Constitution**: Permits courts to declare a law or conduct invalid if it is not compliant with the Constitution and allows for just and equitable remedies to be ordered, which must consider the consequences of such a declaration ([2], [39]).

3. **Legal Precedents on Procurement and Corruption**: Notable rulings such as *State Information Technology Agency SOC Ltd v Gijima* and *Buffalo City Metropolitan Municipality v Asla Construction* were discussed regarding the conditions under which procurement awards can be declared invalid for failing to meet constitutional standards ([3], [36]).

4. **Judicial Discretion**: Established through cases such as *Swifambo Rail Leasing (Pty) Ltd v Passenger Rail Agency of South Africa* and *Millennium Waste Management (Pty) Ltd v Chairperson, Tender Board: Limpopo Province*, specifying the courts’ broad powers to intervene in instances of corruption and maladministration in procurement ([34], [36]).

## A – Application

The Court applied these principles to assess the validity of the procurement contracts and the remedial action taken by the High Court. It found that the circumstances surrounding the contracts were tainted by a widespread culture of corruption and misconduct within PRASA. The High Court had properly exercised its discretion to declare the contracts invalid, primarily based on findings of inadequate adherence to procurement guidelines and the impropriety of the former administration's actions ([5]–[12]).

The Court acknowledged Siyangena's argument, claiming it was an innocent party unaware of the misconduct. However, it held that the existence of irregularities and the connections to corruption made it unreasonable to classify Siyangena as innocent. The Court ruled that the prima facie evidence of corruption sufficiently implicated Siyangena, supporting the High Court's conclusion that it was complicit in the decision-making processes which led to the contracts’ invalidity ([36]–[45]).

Regarding the High Court's remedial order of appointing an independent engineer, the Supreme Court endorsed this as a fair and equitable remedy. It was deemed necessary to ensure that any payments to Siyangena correlated with the actual value of the work performed, thereby protecting the public interest ([38], [47]).

The High Court's determination to condone PRASA's delay in pursuing the review application was also upheld. The Court found that the time taken to uncover the systemic issues was reasonable given the extensive corruption and administrative challenges faced by the new Board ([28]–[33]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Siyangena's appeal, affirming the High Court's findings regarding the invalidity of the procurement contracts and the appointment of an independent engineer to evaluate the works carried out. The judgment underscored the principle that even parties claiming to be innocent cannot benefit from contracts established through corruption and maladministration.

The decision holds significant implications for public procurement processes in South Africa, serving as a critical reminder of the necessity for compliance with constitutional principles and the enforcement of accountability in state contracts. It establishes a precedent for future cases involving procurement irregularities and sets a standard for remedial actions to ensure just outcomes in cases of systemic corruption ([52]).
