Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: Dipholo v The State

**Court:** The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Case No:** 094/2015  
**Neutral Citation:** Dipholo v The State (094/15) [2015] ZASCA 120 (16 September 2015)  
**Judgement Delivered By:** Bosielo JA (Petse JA and Van der Merwe AJA concurring)  
**Date Heard:** 02 September 2015  
**Date Delivered:** 16 September 2015  

---

## F - Facts

[1] The appellant, Steven Ofentse Dipholo, was convicted in the regional court of Lichtenburg on 17 March 2009 on multiple counts, including rape (15 years’ imprisonment), robbery with aggravating circumstances (15 years’ imprisonment), and sexual assault (10 years’ imprisonment). The regional magistrate imposed these sentences to run consecutively, resulting in a total of 40 years of direct imprisonment.

[2] Following his sentencing, the appellant applied for leave to appeal solely against the sentence to the regional court. This application was dismissed on 1 May 2009. Subsequently, a petition for leave to appeal to the North West Division of the High Court, Mahikeng, was also dismissed on 10 August 2012.

[3] On 16 January 2015, the Supreme Court of Appeal granted the appellant special leave to appeal against his sentence. The appeal thus reached the Supreme Court of Appeal, generating significant legal questions surrounding jurisdiction and the merits of the appeal itself.

---

## I - Issues

[4] The key issues presented for consideration by the Supreme Court of Appeal were:

1. Whether the Court had jurisdiction to hear appeals on the merits directly from the magistrates’ court, particularly regarding section 16(1)(b) of the Superior Court Act 10 of 2013.
2. If not, whether the High Court erred in its refusal to grant leave to appeal against the sentence imposed by the regional magistrate, given the appellant's claims of his sentence being disproportionate and shocking.

---

## R - Rules

[5] The legal framework applicable in this case is primarily encapsulated in the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA), particularly section 309, which states that appeals from magistrates’ courts must first be heard by a high court. The applicable legal principles surrounding appeals, particularly the essence of establishing reasonable prospects of success for leave to appeal, are derived from relevant case law including:

- *S v Khoasasa* (515/2001) [2002] ZASCA 113, where it was reaffirmed that the Supreme Court of Appeal lacks the jurisdiction to entertain merits of appeals directly from lower courts.
- *S v Tonkin* (938/12) [2013] ZASCA 179, which laid out procedural clarifications regarding appeals from magistrates’ courts.
- *S v Smith* 2012 (1) SACR 567 (SCA), which specifies that leave to appeal is granted only where there are reasonable prospects of success.

---

## A - Application

[6] The Court identified that the factual situation before it did not represent an appeal on the merits of the case—rather, it involved an appeal against the High Court's refusal to grant leave to appeal. The High Court had not engaged with the merits of Dipholo’s case; thus, the Supreme Court of Appeal acknowledged that it was not in a position to adjudicate such merits directly due to statutory limitations.

[7] The judges critically analyzed the appellant's contention that the regional magistrate’s decision to impose cumulative sentences without them running concurrently constituted a misstep. Counsel for both parties recognized that there existed jurisdictional issues concerning the categorical separation of roles between the courts according to the CPA. The respondent’s counsel conceded that the cumulative effect of the sentences was severe, prompting the Court to consider the reasonable prospects of success that another court might identify, should the matter be heard fully.

[8] The Court underscored the need for proportionality in sentencing, referencing preceding cases, urging a consistent approach to sentencing where offences occurring concurrently should not result in disproportionately severe penalties.

---

## C - Conclusion

[9] The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the High Court had indeed erred in its refusal to grant leave to appeal regarding the sentence imposed. The cumulative sentence of 40 years was deemed starkly disproportionate given the context of multiple concurrent offenses. Therefore, the Court ordered the appeal to be upheld, thereby reversing the previous decision. The new order granted leave to appeal against the sentence imposed by the regional magistrate to the North West Division of the High Court, Mahikeng.

The implications of this ruling reaffirmed the necessity for proper procedural adherence when managing appeals from lower courts, emphasizing judicial oversight to ensure sentences align with justice and proportionality principles. Furthermore, the case reaffirms the role of the appellate courts in scrutinizing sentencing practices, particularly against the backdrop of legislative mandates.
