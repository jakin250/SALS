Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

### FIRAC Case Note: Shongwe v The State (Case no 991/2019) [2024] ZASCA 127

#### F – Facts
In this case, the appellant, Lindokuhle Percy Shongwe, was convicted of murder, robbery with aggravating circumstances, and theft in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg ([1]). The deceased, Solomon Sinkenyani Ngodi, was found dead in his home on 10 December 2017, with evidence indicating that he had been asphyxiated ([2]). The home showed no signs of forced entry, but several neckties were used to bind the deceased’s limbs and to gag him, leading to the conclusion that he died from ligature strangulation ([2]).

Police investigations revealed that the deceased's bank card had been used at an ATM in KwaZulu-Natal, capturing the appellant on video withdrawing money ([3]). The deceased's vehicle and belongings, including a wristwatch and house key, were found in the appellant’s possession, alongside fingerprints lifted from the crime scene ([4]). The appellant claimed an alibi, asserting he had been given a lift and denied making any admissions to the police officer, Lieutenant Colonel Calvin Makamu ([5]).

The High Court rejected Shongwe’s alibi and, upon examining the evidence, found that the murder was premeditated and characterized the robbery as aggravated due to the violent means employed in the commission of the offences. The court subsequently sentenced Shongwe to life imprisonment for murder, 15 years for robbery with aggravating circumstances, and 2 years for theft, all to run concurrently ([6]).

The appeal focused on two main issues: the alleged lack of proof for premeditation in the murder conviction and the claim that intentions to steal arose only post-murder, which should have warranted a conviction for theft instead of robbery with aggravating circumstances ([7]).

#### I – Issues
1. **Premeditation of Murder**: Was the evidence sufficient to conclude that the murder was premeditated ([7])?
2. **Robbery with Aggravating Circumstances vs. Theft**: Did the appellant form the requisite intent to misappropriate the deceased’s belongings before or after the killing ([15])?

#### R – Rules
The court referred to established legal principles concerning premeditation, pointing out that it can be inferred from the facts of each case and does not necessarily require extensive prior planning. In *Kekana v The State*, it was emphasized that short periods of thought prior to committing an act could suffice for premeditation ([9]). 

Regarding robbery, the court noted that overcoming a victim's resistance is an essential element. The definition of robbery with aggravating circumstances includes the use of violence or intimidation during the commission of the crime ([16]).

#### A – Application
In assessing the premeditation issue, the court considered not only the actions taken during the assault — such as binding the victim and using a necktie to gag him — but also the overall context of these actions, which presented a calculated intent to kill ([11]). The court concluded that the manner of killing, including the care taken to disable the victim and the subsequent concealment of the act, indicated a deliberate plan rather than spontaneous action ([12]).

With respect to the robbery charge, the ruling noted the binding of the victim was a clear act of overcoming resistance, thus satisfying the criteria for robbery with aggravating circumstances. The evidence indicated that the appellant took the deceased's belongings while he was incapacitated, signaling that the intent to rob existed even if the physical act occurred post-murder ([16]). The court dismissed the appellant's suggestions that the intent formed only after the death of the victim, emphasizing that the violent circumstances surrounding the robbery established an inseparable link to the act of murder ([18]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appellant's appeal against both the murder and robbery convictions. The court upheld the findings of the lower court, agreeing that sufficient evidence established premeditated murder and robbery with aggravating circumstances based on the circumstances of the case. The ruling reaffirmed that clear intent and planning can be inferred from the evidence presented, thereby concluding that the appellant’s convictions were justified ([19]). 

The implications of this decision underscore the importance of careful analysis in determining intent and the connection between violent conduct and the resultant charges, thereby reinforcing established precedents in South African criminal law.
