Supreme Court of Appeal

Republic of South Africa v. SACCAWU & Others

In the court a quo, the appellants sought to review the tribunal's decisions, primarily alleging irregularities and bias among the tribunal's members ([4]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2000

F – Facts

The case concerns an appeal by the South African Commercial Catering and Allied Workers Union (SACCAWU) and 173 individuals previously employed by the Venda Sun Hotel and Casino (the second respondent) against a decision made by the Industrial Tribunal of Venda ([1]). The context of the dispute arose from the dismissal of 180 employees by the second respondent in January 1993, with some being dismissed for participating in an illegal strike and others for absence without leave ([3]). The Union and the dismissed employees subsequently sought relief from the tribunal, which ultimately ruled on 9 May 1997 that the dismissals of those who participated in the strike were not unfair, while classifying seven dismissals due to absence without leave as procedurally unfair ([3]).

In the court a quo, the appellants sought to review the tribunal's decisions, primarily alleging irregularities and bias among the tribunal's members ([4]). However, the High Court of Venda dismissed the application, prompting the appellants to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([2]). The substantive focus of the appeal centered on the claim of bias against the tribunal's members, particularly a member named Mr. Netshifhefhe, who allegedly showcased disrespect toward the appellants' attorney ([5]). The High Court had only considered affidavits since it did not receive the record of the tribunal’s proceedings at the first instance ([6]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue under consideration was whether the appellants successfully established claims of bias against the members of the tribunal ([4]). Secondary issues included procedural questions regarding the service of notices and the adequacy of the response from the tribunal’s president ([5]). The court also had to consider whether the appellants could effectively claim a denial of due process resulting from the alleged bias, impacting the fairness of the tribunal's proceedings ([7]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal principles include the standard for establishing apprehended bias. The Supreme Court of Appeal noted that the onus rests with the applicant to demonstrate a reasonable suspicion of bias ([10]). Legal precedents indicating that both actual bias and the appearance of bias can lead to the nullification of a decision made by a quasi-judicial entity were also highlighted ([11]). The court further referenced the procedural guidelines outlined in the Uniform Rules of Court, emphasizing the necessity for the applicant to furnish the record of the proceedings to support their claims of irregularity or bias ([7]).

A – Application

The court considered the testimonies presented, particularly those of Mr. Gelebe, asserting that the tribunal members, specifically Netshifhefhe, displayed bias and disrespect towards the appellants’ attorney ([5]). However, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that general allegations of bias made by the appellants lacked sufficient particularity and did not establish a strong case for bias beyond the ill-mannered remarks attributed to Netshifhefhe ([8]).

The critical focus was placed on the implications of Netshifhefhe's comments regarding the attorney's competence. The court concluded that such comments, while inappropriate, did not convincingly establish bias against the parties involved ([9] - [12]). The judgment asserted that while the tribunal members must act impartially, the evidence did not support the claims of bias as required by the legal standard ([10]).

The lack of the tribunal's record further hindered the appellants' ability to substantiate their claims, as they bore the burden of proof to demonstrate the factual basis for bias ([6] - [9]). Since the second respondent's response included denials of the allegations without significant elaboration that raised disputes of fact, the court deemed that the appellants had failed to discharge their onus on the claim of bias ([8]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the decision of the High Court of Venda, stating that the appellants had not established the necessary grounds for review based on bias ([12]). The ratio decidendi emphasized the necessity for applicants to furnish adequate evidence and the pertinent record of proceedings when claiming procedural irregularities or bias in administrative adjudications. Moreover, it highlighted the significance of maintaining the judicial impartiality required of tribunal members while also noting that perceived bias must withstand stringent scrutiny ([10]-[12]). The dismissal effectively reinforced the importance of procedural propriety and adequate substantiation of claims in appeals involving administrative bodies within South Africa's labour relations framework.
