Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2008

# Case Note (FIRAC Method)

## Case Title
Deliwe Muriel Njongi v Member of the Executive Council, Department of Welfare, Eastern Cape CCT 37/07 [2008] ZACC 4

---

### F – Facts

The applicant, Mrs. Deliwe Muriel Njongi, had been receiving a disability grant from the Eastern Cape Provincial Government since 1989. However, this grant was unlawfully terminated in November 1997 without any adequate explanation from governmental officials ([para 4]). Despite being advised to reapply, which she did in January 1999, she did not begin receiving payments again until July 2000. By that time, she was entitled to R15,200 as arrears for the period her grant was not paid, yet she received only R1,100 upon reinstatement and a subsequent R9,400 while the legal action was ongoing ([paras 5–6]).

Mrs. Njongi brought a case against the Provincial Government, arguing for the setting aside of the unlawful administrative decision and claiming the owed arrears. The High Court initially ruled in her favor, stating that the administrative action halting her payments was inconsistent with the Constitution and thus invalid ([para 7]). The court mandated reinstatement of her grant and ordered that back-pay be awarded to her with interest ([para 7]). However, the Provincial Government appealed, contending that the debt had prescribed, a claim upheld by the Full Court, which dismissed Mrs. Njongi's application ([para 6]).

---

### I – Issues

The primary legal questions presented in this case were:

1. **Does prescription run in favor of a provincial government against a person entitled to claim arrear disability grant payments while an unlawful administrative decision remains in effect?** ([paras 2, 39])
   
2. **Under what circumstances can the State legitimately rely upon the defense of prescription when claiming entitlement to social grants?** ([para 3]). 

3. **What constitutes lawful and fair administrative action in the cancellation of social grants?** ([para 8]).

---

### R – Rules

The court's considerations encompassed several legal principles and precedents, including:

1. **The principle of legality:** An unlawful administrative act is considered null and void ([para 39]). The ruling reiterated the necessity for public administration to adhere to constitutional norms of fairness and legality, particularly in the context of social grants ([para 9]).

2. **Prescription Act 68 of 1969:** Section 12 of this Act specifies that prescription runs when a debt becomes due and enforceable ([para 41]). 

3. **Constitutional provisions:** The court referenced Section 27 of the South African Constitution, which obligates the State to provide social assistance to those in need, ensuring dignity, and equality for all citizens ([para 1, 18]).

4. **Relevant case law:** The court drew on past judgments, such as Ngxuza and Others v Permanent Secretary, and Bushula and Others v Permanent Secretary, illustrating unlawful terminations of social grants ([para 9, 10]).

---

### A – Application

The Court, led by Yacoob J, conducted a thorough analysis of the arguments presented. It engaged with the historical context of systemic failures in the Eastern Cape regarding social grant management, emphasizing the bureaucratic ineptness that led to numerous unlawful cancellations of grants ([para 9–10]). 

Critically, it examined whether the Provincial Government's wording and application of prescription should lead to the diminution of Mrs. Njongi's rights. The Full Court's ruling on prescription was challenged, with Yacoob J arguing that the administrative action taken against Mrs. Njongi had not been disavowed and therefore could not be deemed an enforceable debt ready to prescribe ([para 47–59]). The ruling reinforced the idea that public authorities must uphold constitutional mandates, particularly concerning the rights of vulnerable populations ([para 81]).

The court also emphasized that prescription could not be invoked unless explicitly raised by the debtor; this precludes the possibility of a court raising the subject of prescription on its own accord ([para 33]).

---

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately ruled in favor of Mrs. Njongi, reversing the Full Court's judgment and reinstating the High Court's order that the Province's decision was invalid. It underscored that the State had a obligation to acknowledge the unlawful administrative actions and afford appropriate reparations to those affected. As a result, the Court mandated that the Provincial Government pay Mrs. Njongi her owed arrears with calculated interest, emphasizing the importance of timely and effective social assistance ([paras 92]). The implications of this ruling highlight a critical commitment to upholding the rights of socio-economically vulnerable individuals and ensuring administrative fairness and transparency. 

This case serves as a precedence that reinforces the obligation of the State to act justly and proactively in managing social grants, which is seen as a constitutional duty rather than merely a discretionary act. The ruling prevents the State from using prescription as a barrier to fulfilling its obligations towards its citizens, particularly the most vulnerable ([paras 41, 59]).
