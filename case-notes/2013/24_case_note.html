Constitutional Court of South Africa - CCT 34/13

Constitution of the Republic of South Africa: Section 167(4)(e) provides that the Constitutional Court may decide whether the President has failed to fulfill a constitutional obligation.

Constitutional Court 2013 CCT 34/13

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

Mr. Frederick Coenrad Daniel (the "Applicant") initiated a legal challenge against the President of the Republic of South Africa and the Government of the Republic of South Africa (the "Respondents") concerning the President's failure to appoint an independent Commission of Inquiry. This application followed a prior application (CCT 106/12), wherein Daniel sought a declaration that the President's inaction was inconsistent with the South Africa Constitution ([1–2]). The Court, on 31 January 2013, dismissed the application for direct access to the Constitutional Court, determining that it was not in the interests of justice to grant such access ([3]). Following this decision, Daniel sought a rescission of this order, alleging that it was erroneously granted because the application fell under the exclusive jurisdiction of the Court ([4–6]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue revolves around whether the President's inaction in appointing a Commission of Inquiry constitutes a failure to fulfill a constitutional obligation as outlined in section 167(4)(e) of the Constitution, which would grant the Constitutional Court exclusive jurisdiction over the matter ([9–10]). Additionally, the case touches upon the interpretation of sections 84(2)(f) and 167(4)(e) with regard to the discharge of the President's duties and prerogatives.

R – Rules

The legal framework encompasses several constitutional provisions and judicial precedents:

  1. Constitution of the Republic of South Africa:
  • Section 167(4)(e) provides that the Constitutional Court may decide whether the President has failed to fulfill a constitutional obligation.
  • Section 84(2)(f) grants the President the discretionary power to appoint Commissions of Inquiry.
  1. Uniform Rules of Court and Rule 42: This rule allows the court to rescind or vary orders erroneously granted, specifying particular grounds such as an order made in error due to lack of awareness of pertinent facts ([5–6]).
  2. Case Law:
  • Doctors for Life International v Speaker of the National Assembly: Interpreted the scope of "fulfilling a constitutional obligation" to mean specific duties imposed on the President.
  • Von Abo v President of the Republic of South Africa: Clarified that obligations under section 167(4)(e) must be construed narrowly to avoid conflicts with section 172, empowering other courts to review presidential conduct.
  • President of the Republic of South Africa and Others v South African Rugby Football Union: Affirmed that the High Courts retain jurisdiction over matters concerning presidential decisions on Commissions of Inquiry.

A – Application

The Court undertook a detailed examination of Daniel's claim. He argued that the nature of the inquiry into the President's inaction inherently fell within the exclusive jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court ([9–10]). However, the Respondents countered that the discretionary nature of the President's power under section 84(2)(f) indicates that his failure to appoint a Commission is not a violation of a constitutional duty ([10–12]).

In analyzing the definitions of "obligation" as used in the Constitution, the Court aligned its interpretation with prior judgments. It held that the President's powers were discretionary and thus did not impose a constitutional duty that would necessitate direct access to the Constitutional Court ([12–14]). The decision emphasized that a high court's jurisdiction encompasses the refusal to appoint inquiries as well, thus rejecting Daniel's assertion that only the Constitutional Court could address such failures ([13]).

Ultimately, the Court concluded that the initial order dismissing Daniel's application was not granted erroneously, as it correctly assessed the nature of the case and the applicable legal framework ([14]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court of South Africa dismissed Mr. Daniel's application for rescission of the previous order, affirming that the President’s decision was within his discretion and did not equate to a failure to fulfill a constitutional obligation. The ratio decidendi established that claims against presidential inaction regarding Commissions of Inquiry do not fall exclusively under the jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court, thus allowing for judicial review by lower courts ([15]). This ruling emphasizes the importance of delineating between discretionary powers and constitutional obligations within executive functions, reinforcing the checks and balances inherent in the South African legal framework.
