Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

### Case Note: FIRAC Method

### **Case Title**: Gongqose & others v Minister of Agriculture, Forestry & Fisheries and others; Gongqose & others v State & others  
**Neutral Citation**: (1340/16 & 287/17) [2018] ZASCA 87 (01 June 2018)  
**Court**: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Coram**: Maya P, Majiedt, Dambuza JJA, Plasket and Schippers AJJA  
**Heard**: 04 May 2018  
**Delivered**: 01 June 2018  

---

### **F** - Facts

[1] The appellants consisted of individuals from several communities (Hobeni, Mendwane, and Cwebe) adjacent to the Dwesa-Cwebe Marine Protected Area (MPA), declared under the Marine Living Resources Act 18 of 1998 (MLRA) on December 29, 2000, prohibiting all extraction activities, including fishing ([paras 2-4]). 

[2] Historically, these communities relied on marine resources for sustenance. After various legal prohibitions and forced removals dating back to the 19th century, the communities asserted customary rights to marine resources, which they claimed remained intact despite the imposition of the MPA and the MLRA’s provisions ([paras 5-12]).

[3] In September 2010, the appellants were arrested for attempting to fish within the MPA without a permit, leading to charges under several statutory provisions. Although convicted on one charge (fishing without permission), the appellants contended that their actions were justified by their customary rights ([paras 14-15]).

[4] The Eastern Cape Division of the High Court upheld their convictions despite recognizing their customary practices. Following the enactment of the Marine Living Resources Amendment Act 5 of 2014, which introduced provisions recognizing small-scale fishing rights, the appellants sought to appeal the decisions ([paras 17-18]).

### **I** - Issues

[1] The key legal questions before the court included:
- The status and applicability of customary law in the context of the MLRA.
- Whether the appellants could establish their customary rights of access to marine resources.
- Whether the MLRA and the Conservation Decree extinguished these customary rights.
- The legality of the appellants’ conduct given their assertion of customary rights amid statutory prohibitions ([paras 21-22]).

### **R** - Rules

[1] Section 211 of the Constitution of South Africa recognizes the validity and application of customary law, mandating its application by courts when relevant, while also being subject to the Constitution and specific legislation ([paras 22-23]).

[2] Precedents set by the Constitutional Court, including **Alexkor Ltd v The Richtersveld Community** and **Bhe & others v Khayelitsha Magistrate**, reinforced that customary law is an original and independent source of law that can confer rights, and it is to be interpreted in alignment with Constitutional values ([paras 24-25]).

[3] The MLRA was silent on whether unlawfulness is an element of the offences it outlined, leading to established principles that typically presuppose unlawfulness exists unless expressly outlined otherwise, which can include customary rights ([paras 61-62]).

### **A** - Application

[1] The court analyzed the appellant's historical dependence on fishing and community customs. Evidence indicated that fishing practices were governed by customary regulations specific to the Dwesa-Cwebe communities, thereby framing their actions as lawful instead of unlawful ([paras 38-39]).

[2] The court found that the customary rights to fish had not been expressly extinguished by prior legislation, with significant consideration given to the principles of interpretation of statutes, affirming that customary rights continued to exist despite the MLRA’s existence ([paras 40-51]).

[3] Notably, the court emphasized that regulatory measures alone (such as requiring permits) do not equate to the elimination of known customary rights, reinforcing that customary fishing practices can coexist with legal frameworks seeking to protect marine environments. The appellants successfully demonstrated their long-standing customary practices and the community's collective historical connection to the resources ([paras 54-58]).

[4] Finally, the judgment highlighted that the high court erred in concluding that the appellants were required to seek a permit, as their customary rights were sufficient to render their actions lawful ([paras 60-67]).

### **C** - Conclusion

[1] The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appellants’ customary rights to fish in the MPA were valid and had not been extinguished. Their conduct, therefore, was not unlawful as they were exercising protected customary rights as recognized under the Constitution. The appeal was upheld, resulting in the overturning of the convictions and sentences issued by the lower court ([paras 68-69]).

[2] The decision affirmed the critical role of customary law within South African jurisprudence, promoting a greater recognition and protection of indigenous practices in the face of statutory limitations. The court also signaled that customary law could engage with contemporaneous conservation efforts without being entirely overridden by statutory prohibitions ([paras 68-69]).

---

This case stands as a pivotal reaffirmation of the vitality of customary law in South Africa and its harmonization with contemporary legal instruments, specifically within the sphere of environmental law and community rights to resources.
