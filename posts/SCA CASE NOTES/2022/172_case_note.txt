Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: Wulffers v Boxer Dale Holdings (Pty) Ltd and Others (1224/2021) [2022] ZASCA 172

**F – Facts**
The matter pertains to a dispute regarding a servitude over property owned by **Martina Christina Catharina Wulffers** (Appellant) and the **Boxer Dale Holdings (Pty) Ltd** as well as individuals **Henry Anthony Klitsie** and **Anton Heinrich Genade** (Respondents) ([1]–[3]). The parties share ownership of portions of the **farm Goed Geloof 745**, which was subdivided in October 2010. Prior ownership was communal, and Ms Wulffers owns **Portion 233**, landlocked between portions owned by the Klitsies. The respondents claim a servitude that would allow them access to the Krom River, which necessitates traversing Ms Wulffers' property, specifically through a path delineated as ‘x-y’ in submitted sketches ([2]–[4]).

Following disputes over suitable routes, Ms Wulffers erected a fence along the ‘x-y’ path. This led the respondents to launch urgent proceedings in the high court for the removal of the fence and the establishment of a formal servitude route over her property ([5]–[6]). The high court ultimately sided with the Klitsies and ruled in favor of establishing a registered servitude, prompting appeals from both sides ([7]–[10]).

**I – Issues**
The central legal questions in this case concern:
1. Whether the respondents established their entitlement to a servitude right of way over Ms Wulffers' property based on existing agreements and prior servitude ([9]–[10]).
2. Whether a way of necessity should be granted to the Klitsies, given that their portion of the land is landlocked ([11]–[12]).
3. The appropriateness of the motion proceedings given the factual disputes surrounding the paths and their respective impacts on property rights ([14]).

**R – Rules**
The court’s determination relied upon principles concerning servitudes in property law. A servitude must be exercised civiliter modo, meaning it must be done with consideration of the servient owner’s rights ([12]). The existence of a general reciprocal praedial servitude must demonstrate a pre-established route, agreed upon by the involved parties ([8]–[9]). The legal premise for a way of necessity (via ex necessitate) was referenced, emphasizing that considerations on routes should minimize impact and prejudice to the servient property ([11]).

Further, established jurisprudence indicates that motion proceedings are ill-suited for resolving factual disputes, which are better adjudicated through trial ([14]).

**A – Application**
In addressing the appeal, the court described how the respondents failed to demonstrate a prior agreement on the path needed for the registered servitude as per the original 1993 servitude stipulations ([10]). Critical to the judgment was the fact that the owner of **Property One** was not consulted regarding the route’s impact on that property, which was essential in determining entitlement to a servitude, highlighting the complexities of property arrangements ([10]–[11]).

On the question of necessity, while acknowledging that the Klitsies may well have a legitimate claim due to the landlocked status of Part C, the court remarked that they did not present a robust case during motion proceedings as to why a specific route was necessary over Ms Wulffers' property ([11]). This decision was influenced by the producing disputes regarding the width and suitability of the paths, which could not be adequately addressed within the confines of motion proceedings.

The court ultimately ruled that since the respondents had not established their case for the servitude nor the ancillary relief from the urgent application ([14]), the high court’s decision to establish a route was erroneous, necessitating a dismissal with costs ([15]).

**C – Conclusion**
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal and dismissed the cross-appeal, finding that the respondents had failed to demonstrate entitlement to a servitude [15]. The high court’s order to register the servitude was set aside, concluding that given the disputes of fact and principles related to servitudes, the appropriate course was to dismiss the application outright. The judgment underscores the necessity for clarity and consensus in property rights disputes and emphasizes that motion proceedings cannot be used as a substitute for a full trial when there are substantial factual disagreements. This case serves as a crucial reminder of the complexities involved in property law, especially concerning servitudes and access rights.
