FIRAC Method - Zhongji Development Construction Engineering Company Limited v Kamoto Copper Company SARL

Supreme Court of Appeal 2014 [2014] ZASCA 160 (1 October 2014) ### F - Facts This case in

Neutral Citation: Zhongji Construction v Kamoto Copper Company (421/13) [2014] ZASCA 160 (1 October 2014)

F - Facts

This case involves an appeal from a decision of the South Gauteng High Court concerning the applicability of an arbitration agreement and the dispute resolution processes between Zhongji Development Construction Engineering Company Limited (the appellant) and Kamoto Copper Company SARL (the respondent). The appellant is a Chinese company that engaged in a contractual arrangement with DRC Copper and Cobalt Project SARL (DCP), a Congolese company, to undertake construction work in the Democratic Republic of Congo. A main agreement was established on 20 August 2008 under English law, stipulating that disputes would be resolved through arbitration under the Association of Arbitrators (Southern Africa) rules ([paras 1–4]).

Prior to the main agreement, an interim agreement was reached due to concerns raised about DCP's operations, which resulted in operational suspensions ([paras 7–12]). Disputes arose concerning payment for work performed under both agreements, notably with three invoices under the interim agreement remaining unpaid and additional claims related to the main agreement ([paras 14–16]). Following a merger between DCP and Kamoto in 2009, Kamoto took over DCP’s obligations but contended that it was not bound by the arbitration processes established in the main agreement ([paras 16–24]).

In the High Court, Zhongji sought a declaratory order affirming the arbitrability of its claims and the applicability of the arbitration agreement, which was ultimately dismissed due to jurisdictional concerns ([paras 28–29]).

I - Issues

The primary legal issues distilled from the case include:

  1. Jurisdiction: Whether the High Court had jurisdiction to determine the arbitrability of the disputes given that both parties are peregrini (foreign nationals) of South Africa, and whether the arbitration clause is applicable under the merger agreement with DCP.
  2. Arbitration Agreement: Whether Kamoto, as the successor in terms of the merger, is liable for any debts and whether it is bound by the arbitration provisions laid out in the main agreement with DCP.
  3. Declaratory Relief: Whether the High Court could grant declaratory relief regarding arbitration in an abstract manner when no genuine dispute existed.

These issues highlight the interplay between arbitrability under international arbitration laws and the specifics of contractual obligations subsequent to a merger ([paras 18–37]).

R - Rules

The legal rules applicable to this case include:

  • The Arbitration Act 42 of 1965 and Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards Act 40 of 1977, which govern arbitration proceedings and enforceability of arbitral awards in South Africa.
  • The New York Convention on the Recognition of Foreign Arbitral Awards, which promotes international arbitration and supports adherence to arbitration agreements ([paras 29–30]).
  • Case Law Precedents, including Lufuno Mphaphuli & Associates (Pty) Ltd v Andrews ([2009] 4 SA 529 (CC)) and Bank Mellat v Helsinki Techniki S.A ([1984] QB 291) affirming court’s duty to respect arbitration as a dispute resolution mechanism.
  • Additionally, the case referenced Fili Shipping Co Ltd v Premium Nafta Products ([2007] UKHL 40) concerning the construction of arbitration clauses, primarily focusing on the presumption of rational intent by parties to resolve disputes through arbitration.

A - Application

The court applied these legal principles by analyzing the jurisdictional claims in light of both parties being foreigners and the agreements being executed outside of South Africa. The court reinforced the notion that the arbitration tribunal possesses the "competence-competence" principle—that it can rule on its own jurisdiction ([paras 32–34]).

The judges emphasized that any discussion of whether the disputes arising from the interim and main agreements were subject to arbitration should first be left to the arbitration tribunal itself, aligning with the growing trend to minimize judicial interference in arbitration matters ([paras 35–38]). Kamoto’s argument against arbitration, based on its merger with DCP, was found unconvincing; it did not absolve Kamoto from inheriting DCP's obligations, including adherence to the arbitration agreement ([paras 41–52]).

The High Court's ruling was upheld, with the court concluding that Zhongji’s attempts to secure a declaratory order were premature as the arbitration process had yet to be honored ([paras 38–39]).

C - Conclusion

Therefore, the Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the application for a declaratory order made by Zhongji was inappropriate at this stage since the merits of the claims, including the applicability of the arbitration agreement, should be considered by the arbitration panel rather than the High Court. The appeal was dismissed with the understanding that respect for arbitral processes is paramount in international commercial dealings involving parties from different jurisdictions ([paras 40–60]). This case underscores the broader implications for international contract enforcement in South Africa and reinforces the judiciary's role in promoting arbitration as a resolution tool, elucidating that contractual parties' choices must be respected to encourage international commercial relationships and arbitration in South Africa.

Order: The appeal is dismissed.
