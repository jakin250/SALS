Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: *Nekokwane v Road Accident Fund* [2018] ZACC 11

### F – Facts
This case arises from the applicant, Ndishavhelafhi Thomas Nekokwane, seeking leave to appeal against the dismissal of his claim for damages related to personal injuries allegedly sustained in an accident on June 3, 2014 ([1]). The incident occurred at Tshavhalovhedzi, Limpopo, where the applicant was injured when the front loading basket of a Tractor-Loader-Backhoe (TLB) fell on his feet. At the time of the accident, the TLB was parked without a driver, and a significant aspect of the applicant's claim hinged upon the assertion that the vehicle’s ignition key was left in the "on" position, enabling the hydraulic system to function despite the engine being off. The trial judge in the High Court noted conflicting versions provided by the applicant regarding the circumstances of the incident, which contributed to a lack of credibility in his testimony ([3]).

The High Court framed the central issue for determination as whether the TLB “moved from where it was parked as a result of gravity,” as delineated by section 20(2) of the Road Accident Fund Act (RAF Act 56 of 1996) ([7]). The trial judge concluded that the mere dropping of the loading basket did not satisfy the movement criterion under the statute, holding that liability could not arise under the RAF Act in such circumstances ([8]). Following this ruling, the applicant’s subsequent appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal was also dismissed, prompting this application for leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court ([1]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issue before the Constitutional Court was whether the injuries sustained by the applicant fell within the ambit of sections 17 and 20 of the RAF Act ([1]). More specifically, the court had to determine if the circumstances of the accident could yield a constitutional issue, primarily focusing on the interpretation of legislative provisions and whether factual findings from the trial court could be contested on constitutional grounds ([5],[9]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework underpinning this case is the Road Accident Fund Act, particularly sections 17 and 20, which addresses liability for injuries sustained from motor vehicles under specified conditions ([7]). Section 20(2) states:

> "For the purposes of this Act a person who has placed or left a motor vehicle at any place shall be deemed to be driving that motor vehicle while it moves from that place as a result of gravity..."

Additionally, the Constitution of South Africa requires courts to promote the spirit, purport, and objects of the Bill of Rights during statutory interpretation ([8]). The landmark cases referenced in this judgment elucidate the principle that appeals based purely on factual disputes do not ordinarily constitute constitutional issues for the higher court to resolve ([2],[4]).

### A – Application
The court, led by Froneman J, applied the constitutional and statutory rules to assess the merits of the appeal. It articulated that the applicant attempted to assert a constitutional issue based on the interpretative approach mandated by section 39(2) of the Constitution, arguing that the trial court’s finding misapplied section 20(2) ([9]). However, the Constitutional Court held that the applicant's attempt to frame the appeal within a constitutional context was insufficient. Critically, the court noted that overturning the trial court’s factual findings would not fall under its jurisdiction, as those findings were factual determinations devoid of constitutional implications ([4],[10]).

The court resolved that the factual discrepancies in the applicant's testimony—specifically the contradictions regarding the hydraulic system and the alleged negligence of the insured—the basis for liability, negated any constitutional claim. Instead of focusing on legal principles, the appeal was fundamentally a matter of disputed facts that had already been evaluated by the trial tribunal ([10]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court unanimously dismissed the application for leave to appeal, affirming that the case remained within the realm of factual determinations rather than constitutional matters ([11]). The court underscored that the absence of a constitutional issue precluded it from exercising jurisdiction to review the factual findings made by the trial judge. The ruling illustrates the principle that not all disputes warrant a constitutional resolution and reiterates the boundaries of the court's jurisdiction in dealing with factual disagreements arising in lower courts. Consequently, there was no order as to costs, reflecting the court’s stance on the matter’s intrinsic merits and lack of procedural merit for the applicant ([11]). 

This case solidifies the precedent that courts must adhere to jurisdictional limits when addressing appeals, particularly where issues of fact are at play without an accompanying constitutional question.
