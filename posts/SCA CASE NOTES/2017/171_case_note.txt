Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note: Omega Risk Solutions (Pty) Ltd v De Witt (149/2017) [2017] ZASCA 171

## F – Facts [1–3]
The case before the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa concerned an appeal by Omega Risk Solutions (Pty) Ltd (the appellant) against Josias Alexander De Witt (the respondent). The appellant sought to establish claims arising from financial transactions managed by the respondent during his tenure as Chief Executive Officer of the Omega Group from March 2007 to August 2010. Specifically, the appellant alleged that the respondent breached his fiduciary duties by authorizing payments for which the appellant was not liable and misappropriating funds for personal purposes.

The appellant initiated the claims in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, where the respondent raised a special plea of prescription, which the lower court upheld, ruling that the claims had prescribed under the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 (the Act). The court held that it was accepted knowledge held by Mr. Philippus Smit, a finance manager of the appellant, that should determine whether claims had prescribed.

## I – Issues [1–3]
The legal issues presented in this appeal centered on the application of sections 12(1) and 12(3) of the Prescription Act, specifically whether the knowledge possessed by Mr. Smit could be attributed to the appellant and, if so, the timing of the commencement of prescription regarding the claims. The core questions revolved around:
- Did Mr. Smit's knowledge equate to that of the appellant under the provisions of the Act?
- Were the claims filed by the appellant still within the prescriptive period, given these circumstances?

## R – Rules [4–10]
The relevant legal framework is established in the Prescription Act, particularly section 12, which governs when prescription begins to run. Per section 12(1), prescription commences as soon as a debt is due, while subsection 12(3) stipulates that a debt is not deemed due until the creditor possesses knowledge of the debtor's identity and the facts from which the debt arises. Importantly, the creditor is attributed knowledge if they could have acquired it with reasonable care.

The court referred to established case law, including:
- **PriceWaterhouseCoopers v National Potato Co-operative** [2015] 2 All SA 403 (SCA), which clarified that the knowledge of corporate directors can be attributed to the company for purposes of prescription;
- **Macleod v Kweyiya** [2013] 6 SA 1 (SCA), which discussed constructive knowledge relating to reasonable care in acquiring knowledge about debts; and
- **Minister of Finance v Gore NO** [2007] 1 SA 111 (SCA), which highlighted the underlying policy of the Prescription Act to protect defendants from undue delay by claimants.

## A – Application [12–19]
The court carefully analyzed the responsibilities of Mr. Smit within the structures of Omega Risk Solutions. Mr. Smit was intimately involved in the financial management of the company, handling corporate oversight related to cash flow, budgets, and financial audits. The evidence indicated that the payments in question occurred over several periods, likely coming to the attention of the board via financial statements that required directors’ approval. 

The court concluded that a reasonable director, in light of the nature of the financial transactions and their oversight obligations, could reasonably have acquired knowledge of the claims. The counsel for the appellant admitted that these claims were essential for the company’s financial health, reinforcing the court's reasoning that the directors had a duty to be aware of significant financial transactions impacting the company. 

The court further dismissed the appellant's argument that the trial court had employed a "novel proximity test," clarifying that the attribution of knowledge techniques differed based on constructive knowledge as mandated by section 12(3) of the Act.

## C – Conclusion [19]
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, confirming the findings of the court a quo that the claims made by the appellant had indeed prescribed. The ruling underscored the significance of knowledge attribution within corporate entities and established a precedent on how the knowledge of finance officers may be charged to the corporation in prescriptive matters. The implications of this decision extend to corporate governance, establishing a clearer standard that directors must diligently act to be informed about their organization’s financial dealings.

This judgment emphasizes the critical role of the doctrine of prescription in protecting entities against stale claims while also delineating the factors that contribute to knowledge attribution among corporate boards, reinforcing the need for vigilance in corporate governance and fiduciary duties.
