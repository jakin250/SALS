Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: Road Accident Fund v MKM obo KM and Others [2023] ZASCA 50

**F – Facts**

The appellants, the Road Accident Fund (RAF), initially settled claims for loss of support brought on behalf of two separate groups of minor children (MKM obo KM and TM; NM obo CM and LM) whose parents had died in motor vehicle accidents ([5–6]). The claims, against the RAF, were established under South Africa's Contingency Fees Act 66 of 1997 ([1]). Due to the nature of claims involving minors, the claimants were represented by attorneys under contingency fee agreements, which require judicial approval for settlement ([8]).

In both cases, the RAF made offers of settlement that were ultimately accepted by the attorneys representing the children. However, the necessary judicial approval, as mandated by Section 4 of the Contingency Fees Act, was not sought during these settlements ([12–16]). As a result, both the Gauteng Division of the High Court declared the agreements invalid, necessitating the appeal ([2], [17]).

**I – Issues**

The central legal issues presented in this appeal include:
1. Whether the RAF had an obligation to ensure that the legal practitioners complied with Section 4 of the Contingency Fees Act before entering into settlement agreements ([1]).
2. Whether a settlement agreement that did not receive judicial approval could be considered lawful ([1], [32]).

**R – Rules**

The legal framework applicable in this case is primarily governed by the provisions of the Contingency Fees Act of 1997, particularly Section 4, which stipulates that:
- A legal practitioner must file an affidavit and obtain judicial approval before settling a claim if a contingency fee agreement is in place ([8]).
- The validation of settlement agreements under this act relies on the adherence to these procedural requirements ([4], [8]).

Additionally, relevant case law involves **Price Waterhouse Coopers v National Potato Co-operative** ([22]), which discusses the implications of non-compliance with statutory requirements regarding contingency fees, reinforcing that invalid agreements affect only the contractual relationship between client and practitioner, not the underlying settlement ([37]).

**A – Application**

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) assessed the high court's interpretation of the RAF's obligations and the consequence of non-compliance with the Contingency Fees Act. The court rejected the high court’s finding that the RAF had a duty to ensure compliance by legal practitioners, clarifying that:
- There is no statutory requirement obligating the RAF to verify adherence to the contingency fees procedure before concluding settlements ([20], [31]).
- The invalidity of the contingency fees agreements between the attorneys and clients does not inherently invalidate the settlement agreements with the RAF ([36]).

The SCA highlighted that the intention of Section 4 is to impose obligations on legal practitioners, rather than third parties, like the RAF. Thus, the court concluded that the inability of the attorneys to seek approval did not affect the settlement's validity with the RAF ([30], [39]).

The application of the rules led the SCA to overturn the high court's declarations of invalidity, positing that claims’ validity could remain intact despite non-compliance from legal representatives ([28], [46]).

**C – Conclusion**

The SCA held that the appeal succeeded, setting aside the high court’s orders declaring the settlement agreements invalid. The ruling established that the RAF was not culpable for the attorneys' failure to comply with statutory obligations and that the settlements made, despite irregularities, were enforceable ([70]). The ratio decidendi underscores the separation of roles between legal practitioners and bodies like the RAF, delineating that compliance with the Contingency Fees Act is the practitioner's responsibility—not that of the RAF ([24], [31]). 

The implications of this judgment are significant for ongoing and future cases involving the RAF and contingent fee arrangements, underscoring the necessity for legal representatives to adhere strictly to statutory provisions while removing undue burdens of compliance from third-party defendants like the RAF. Moreover, this decision implies a need to reevaluate advocacy benchmarks and safeguards in representing dependents in similar litigations to safeguard minors' interests properly while maintaining statutory compliance ([54–55]).
