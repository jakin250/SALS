# Case Note: Gent and Another v Du Plessis (1029/2019) [2020] ZASCA 184

## FIRAC Methodology

### F – Facts
The appeal in *Gent and Another v Du Plessis* arose from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, wherein the Full Court ordered Anita Julia Gent (the first appellant) to sell her majority shareholding in Bonnox (Pty) Ltd to Pieter Daniël Jacobs du Plessis (the respondent). The Full Court reached this decision despite determining that Mr du Plessis failed to establish the required elements under section 163(1) of the Companies Act 71 of 2008 (the Act) ([paras 1–2]).

Bonnox, originally established in 1957 by Gent’s father, evolved into a profitable fencing manufacturer. On share distribution, Gent received 80 shares, Du Plessis 45, while another 10 shares were given to an accountant, leading to Gent holding 53.33% and Du Plessis 46.67% of the shares ([para 4]). Disputes arose following mismanagement allegations against Du Plessis, who faced dismissal for misconduct ([paras 5–6]). Moreover, he sought to liquidate the company, claiming his rights as a minority shareholder were oppressed by Gent’s actions ([paras 8–10]).

Initially, the court held that Du Plessis's application for liquidation was not justified, primarily due to his own actions contributing to the breakdown of the relationship between the parties ([para 12]). The appeal then moved to the Full Court, which ruled that although Du Plessis did not meet the burden of proof under section 163(1), it nonetheless mandated a buyout of Gent’s shares to facilitate a "clean break" between the parties ([paras 14–15]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues examined by the Supreme Court of Appeal included:

1. Whether a respondent, like Mr du Plessis, could pursue variations of a court's order in the absence of a cross-appeal ([paras 2, 16]).
2. Whether the Full Court erred in mandating that Gent sell her shares, given that it found Du Plessis did not meet the necessary criteria for relief under section 163(1) of the Companies Act ([paras 2, 14, 19]).
3. The interpretation and application of "oppressive or unfairly prejudicial conduct" as described in section 163 of the Companies Act ([paras 10, 18]).

### R – Rules
The legal foundations determining the case principally fall under the Companies Act 71 of 2008, particularly section 163, which outlines provisions for seeking relief from oppressive conduct. Section 163(1) provides that a shareholder may seek relief for conduct that is oppressive or unfairly prejudicial. Furthermore, the Full Court's decision was governed by procedural norms regarding appeals, notably the necessity for a cross-appeal to modify lower court orders ([paras 2, 14]).

The principles from *Shatz Investments (Pty) Ltd v Kalovyrnas* establish that a respondent must lodge a cross-appeal to vary a decision, thus guiding the Court's understanding of appellate rights and obligations ([para 16]).

### A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed the Full Court's ruling that, while Du Plessis did not satisfy the prerequisites for relief under section 163(1), it nonetheless had the authority to craft a buyout order. The Court emphasized that once the statutory requirements were unmet, the Full Court’s jurisdiction to issue additional remedies under section 163(2) evaporated. Without valid grounds articulated under section 163(1), the court could not properly exercise discretion to mandate a buyout ([paras 2, 19]).

Critically, the Supreme Court evaluated the factual basis for Du Plessis’s claims of oppression, noting that Gent's decisions, including the removal of Du Plessis from directorship, were legitimate under proper corporate governance practices as prescribed by the Act. The court reaffirmed valid shareholder and director decisions made within the law must stand. Hence, Du Plessis had not demonstrated that generally accepted shareholder rights and practices were contravened ([paras 20–21]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the Full Court’s ruling, emphasizing that Du Plessis could neither compel Gent to sell her shares nor expect the court to engage in discretionary relief unauthorized by a substantial legal basis. The court's determination not only served to protect corporate governance principles but also reinforced the stringent requirements for relief under section 163 of the Companies Act. The ruling clarifies the operations of oppressive conduct in corporate law and affirms the necessity for respondents to maintain procedural rigor in appeals. Consequently, the order to compel Gent to sell her shares was declared invalid, and the appeal was upheld, reinstating the original judgment by Hughes J ([paras 22, 14]).

This case illustrates the limitations of judicial intervention in corporate disputes absent clear statutory grounds and emphasizes the importance of procedural integrity in appellate proceedings.
