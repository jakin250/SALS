Source Folder: 2003

### FIRAC Case Note: South African Veterinary Council v. GREG SZYMANSKI

**F – Facts**  
The precise details and procedural history are encapsulated from paragraphs [1] to [3]. The case emerged from an appeal against a decision by the Pretoria High Court, which had set aside the decision of the South African Veterinary Council (SAVC). The core of the dispute arose after the SAVC conducted a special examination in September 1998 to assess South African citizens and permanent residents with foreign veterinary qualifications. Dr. Greg Szymanski, a Polish national who immigrated to South Africa, sat for this examination and received a combined score of 45.25%, deemed a failure by the SAVC, which established a pass mark of 50%. Dr. Szymanski contended, however, that he had a legitimate expectation that the requisite pass mark was only 40% based on information from the SAVC, including letters and conversations with its members ([4]–[8]).

### I – Issues  
The critical legal questions stem from paragraphs [11] to [12]. The first issue is whether Dr. Szymanski established a reasonable case for his belief that the pass mark for the examination was 40% rather than 50%. The second issue concerns whether the High Court erred in granting relief given the factual disputes regarding the alleged communications and representations made by the SAVC's officials.

### R – Rules  
The applicable legal standards are distilled in paragraphs [12] and [19]. Firstly, the constitutional requirements for just administrative action, as enshrined in section 33 of the 1996 Constitution, necessitate that any action taken by a statutory body like the SAVC must be lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair. The court also referenced the doctrine of legitimate expectation, which necessitates a clear, unambiguous representation and assesses the reasonableness of such an expectation ([19]). Key cases cited include National Director of Public Prosecutions v. Phillips and Others [2002] 4 SA 60 (W) as well as principle considerations from De Smith, Woolf, and Jowell’s Judicial Review of Administrative Action.

### A – Application  
The Court's analysis, articulated in paragraphs [13] to [30], emphasized that Dr. Szymanski's case failed at multiple junctures. Primarily, the Court found that the documentation provided by the SAVC did not constitute a representation indicating that a pass mark of merely 40% was sufficient. The term "sub-minimum" indicated that a higher requirement existed, which Dr. Szymanski misunderstood ([16]–[18]). Thus, his expectations were neither clear nor reasonable under the circumstances. This fundamental flaw meant no legitimate expectation could be upheld in law.

Further, the findings highlighted the presence of genuine factual disputes relating to the discussions that allegedly took place between Dr. Szymanski and SAVC members ([23]–[30]). The SAVC provided detailed denials of the purported communications, and the Court emphasized that such disputes precluded resolution in motion proceedings without oral evidence.

### C – Conclusion  
In conclusion, as articulated in paragraphs [32] to [33], the appeal was upheld, and the order of the lower court was reversed. The Court found that Dr. Szymanski had not demonstrated a legitimate expectation regarding the pass mark, nor did he refute the factual disputes adequately raised by the SAVC. The Court dismissed the application with costs, reinforcing the principle that clarity and proper administrative processes are paramount in such cases.

This case underscores the necessity for clear communication from statutory bodies and the judiciary's role in maintaining fairness in administrative decision-making. It serves as an important precedent regarding the legitimate expectation doctrine within South African administrative law.
