Mphephu-Ramabulana and Another v Mphephu and Others [2021] ZACC 43

This matter involves a dispute regarding the leadership of the VhaVenḓa Community and the corresponding claims to the throne, which stem from the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003 ("Framework Act") and the Framework Amendment Act 23 of 2009.

Constitutional Court 2021 [2021] ZACC 43

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([3]–[10])

This matter involves a dispute regarding the leadership of the VhaVenḓa Community and the corresponding claims to the throne, which stem from the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003 ("Framework Act") and the Framework Amendment Act 23 of 2009.

The principal parties are Khosikhulu Mphephu-Ramabulana (First Applicant), who was recognized as King of the VhaVenḓa Community by a Recognition Notice issued by the President in 2012 ([4]), and Masindi Mphephu (First Respondent), a member of the Royal Family who contends that she is the rightful Queen. The Second Applicant is the Mphephu-Ramabulana Royal Family Council, which endorsed Mphephu-Ramabulana's identification as the successor ([5]).

The dispute reached the High Court when Ms Mphephu challenged both the Royal Family Council’s Identification Decision and the President's Recognition Decision, arguing that both were unconstitutional and invalid ([9]). The High Court initially ruled against her, leading to her appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([12]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld parts of Ms Mphephu's appeal, finding that there were procedural lapses in the recognition process, particularly concerning the lack of a ministerial recommendation, which is mandated under the amended Framework Act ([14]–[15]). Consequently, it remitted the matter back to the High Court for final adjudication on the merits, including issues of customary law ([18]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([2], [21])

The primary legal issues revolve around:

  1. Whether the Supreme Court of Appeal erred in remitting the matter for further adjudication after having set aside the Recognition Decision and the Identification Decision, particularly in light of findings that certain claims had prescribed.
  2. The validity of the High Court's procedural decisions and the scope of judicial authority over customary law disputes, especially as they relate to the Framework Act and PAJA (Promotion of Administrative Justice Act).
  3. Whether the applicants’ delayed application for condonation to appeal to the Constitutional Court was justifiable ([22], [40]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([6], [14], [43]")

The court's decision is grounded in several key legislative and constitutional provisions:

  • Customary Law: Section 211 of the Constitution grants courts authority to adjudicate disputes arising under customary law without requiring prior referral to the Commission on Traditional Leadership Disputes and Claims ([13]).
  • Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA): This statute governs the manner in which administrative decisions can be challenged and sets out standards for lawful administrative action ([14], [15]).
  • Biowatch Principle: This principle establishes that generally, the losing party in constitutional litigation involving the state should bear the costs, particularly where the litigant is challenging unlawful state conduct ([77]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([32]–[39], [76]–[80])

Application of the Condonation Standard

The court meticulously evaluated the applicants' request for condonation, noting the extended period of delay—over a year—combined with insufficient justification for the deferment in filing appeal. The court determined that the mere expectation of favorable outcomes from the House’s reports did not adequately justify the delay ([34]). The court emphasized that procedural compliance with timelines is crucial and found the argument presented to be bordering on abuse of court processes ([35]).

Judicial Review and Jurisdiction

With respect to the substantive claims and the Supreme Court of Appeal's remittal of the matter, the court reaffirmed its jurisdiction to review customary law matters. The applicants contended that the procedural lapses, including the issue of prescribed claims and the scope of judicial power, could preclude a return to the High Court. However, the court held that such complaints are not absolute barriers to the rightful adjudication of the claims ([39]).

Costs and the Biowatch Principle

The court also addressed the Supreme Court of Appeal’s costs orders, ruling that Ms Mphephu, having substantially succeeded in her initial claims, was entitled to costs. The Supreme Court of Appeal had incorrectly characterized some costs as "costs in the cause" rather than awarding them to Ms Mphephu, thereby failing to apply the principles set out in Biowatch regarding successful constitutional litigation ([78]–[79]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([85])

In conclusion, the Constitutional Court held the following:

  1. Condonation Application: The court refused condonation for the main application due to a significant delay without a sufficient explanation ([40]).
  2. Remittal and Review: The court upheld the procedural findings of the Supreme Court of Appeal on the need for further adjudication on the merits, affirming the courts' jurisdiction over customary law ([27],[39]).
  3. Costs Orders: The court set aside the costs orders of the Supreme Court of Appeal, reiterating that a substantial success in constitutional matters must lead to a corresponding costs award ([79]).
  4. Policy Implications: This case underscores the judicial branch's commitment to maintaining the principles of the rule of law and due process in disputes involving customary leadership, as well as the stringent adherence to procedural justice within the context of administrative review ([75],[80]).

Overall, the decision emphasizes the responsibility of state organs to act lawfully and transparently when exercising powers over traditional leadership structures under customary law, while also affirming the independence of the judiciary in managing such disputes.
