Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: CSARS v Amawele Joint Venture CC [2018] ZASCA 115

## F – Facts 
The respondent, Amawele Joint Venture CC (Amawele), is a contracting firm specializing in housing and infrastructure. They were engaged by the KwaZulu-Natal Provincial Department of Human Settlements for three projects between July 2008 and September 2010: the Umsunduzi Project, the Mooi River Project, and the Emnambithi Project. These projects involved substantial remedial work on previously constructed housing that was deemed structurally inadequate due to defective workmanship and storm damage ([1]–[3]).

Amawele contended that the services provided were exempt from Value Added Tax (VAT) under section 11(2)(s), in conjunction with section 8(23) of the Value-Added Tax Act 89 of 1991 (the VAT Act). It claimed that these services qualified for a zero tax rating due to their association with the Housing Subsidy Scheme ([2]).

Following an audit, the South African Revenue Services (SARS) issued an additional assessment demanding approximately R38 million in VAT. After an appeal to the Tax Court, Amawele's position was upheld, leading to SARS' appeal to the Full Court of the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which was later dismissed. SARS further appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal on the question of whether the services provided were indeed zero rated ([3], [30]).

## I – Issues 
The primary legal issue before the court was whether Amawele's services rendered under the described contracts were correctly classified as zero-rated supplies under the VAT Act. Specifically, the court needed to determine:

1. Whether the services rendered by Amawele could be interpreted as being part of the Housing Subsidy Scheme as referred to in sections 11(2)(s) and 8(23) of the VAT Act ([3], [30]).
2. How the criteria and requirements set out for the zero rating of services align with the nature of the projects undertaken ([10], [22]).

## R – Rules 
This case primarily concerns the interpretation and application of sections 11(2)(s) and 8(23) of the VAT Act. 

- **Section 11(2)(s)** stipulates that certain services provided to a public authority, deemed to be funded by a specific subsidy scheme, can be zero-rated.
- **Section 8(23)** defines the context under which services provided as part of a Housing Subsidy Scheme may be deemed to be zero-rated ([4], [6]).
  
Additionally, the court considered the legislative history surrounding these sections, noting that they were amended and introduced in different phases, including significant changes to the definitions of qualifying schemes in the Housing Act ([5], [8]). Relevant case law regarding statutory interpretation was also consulted, particularly regarding how changes in legislation might inform previous provisions ([10], [30]).

## A – Application 
In its analysis, the court undertook a detailed examination of the provisions and the legislative context surrounding the Housing Subsidy Scheme. Importantly, it noted that when the VAT Act was initially amended in 2003, the relevant Housing Subsidy Scheme did not include the Emergency Assistance Programme (EAP) or the Rectification and Repair Programme (RRP), which were introduced later ([22], [28]).

The court further found that neither the EAP nor the RRP, which were relevant to Amawele's projects, fell within the scope of the Housing Subsidy Scheme as outlined in the VAT Act during the period in question. As there was no evidence indicating that these programmes had been incorporated into the existing framework of the Housing Subsidy Scheme post-2003, Amawele's reliance on the zero-rating provisions was misplaced ([31]):

1. The EAP was structured as a separate programme with distinct operational criteria, and its beneficiaries were not subject to the same limitations as those of the housing subsidy framework ([24], [26]).
2. Similarly, the RRP addressed issues of rehabilitation and upgrading existing housing stock, which did not align with the Housing Subsidy Scheme's focus on providing new housing assistance to underserved populations ([27], [30]).

Thus, Amawele failed to demonstrate any legislative or interpretative basis that would allow their services to qualify for zero-rating under the VAT Act.

## C – Conclusion 
The Supreme Court of Appeal found in favor of SARS, ruling that the appeal should be upheld. The order of the High Court was set aside, and the Tax Court's decision to grant a refund was not supported by the relevant statutory provisions ([31]).

The court's decision reinforces the necessity for careful statutory interpretation, particularly concerning fiscal measures and their applicability to housing assistance programmes. It underscores the limits of zero-rating provisions under VAT, emphasizing that new programmes cannot be retroactively included under previously established fiscal frameworks, absent clear legislative intent or amendment ([30], [31]). 

This ruling has implications for future interpretations of VAT exemptions, particularly in sectors heavily dependent on public funds and governmental programmes, necessitating careful compliance with existing legislation and its amendments.
