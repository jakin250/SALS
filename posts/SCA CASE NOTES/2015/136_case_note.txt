Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# Case Note: Nube v The State (091/15) [2015] ZASCA 136

## F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–8])

The appellant, Madoda Dopla Nube, was tried alongside six others in the Eastern Cape Local Division, Port Elizabeth for several serious offences, including robbery with aggravating circumstances, attempted murder, and multiple counts of murder. The charges stemmed from a planned heist targeting Brink Security’s transport of liquid platinum on November 8, 2011 ([para 1]).

During the trial, it was established that the appellant played an integral role in a criminal syndicate that conspired to execute the heist. Evidence revealed that the group met multiple times to discuss the details of the operation, culminating in a plan that, albeit foiled by police intervention, resulted in violence and fatalities when the heist was attempted ([paras 2–5]). Nube claimed to have dissociated himself from the conspiracy on November 1, 2011, due to concerns about the use of explosives, yet he attended a crucial meeting on November 7, where the plan was finalized ([paras 7–8]).

## I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 12–14])

The critical legal question before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the trial court erred in concluding that Nube's actions did not effectively dissociate him from the common purpose of the heist ([paras 12–13]). This question encompassed the interpretation of dissociation from a conspiracy under South African criminal law, particularly regarding how far a participant must go to demonstrate a genuine withdrawal from a planned crime ([para 21]).

Additionally, the court had to consider whether the appellant's absence at the crime scene was adequate to prevent his conviction under the doctrine of common purpose, which implicates participants even when not physically present during the commission of an offence ([para 14]).

## R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 19–22])

The court relied on established legal principles regarding dissociation as articulated in *S v Nduli* 1993 (2) SACR 501 (A) ([para 21]) and further discussed in *S v Musingadi* 2005 (1) SACR 395 (SCA) ([para 22]). The key criteria for effective dissociation included the necessity for clear conduct demonstrating a withdrawal intention, particularly in light of the individual's prior substantial involvement in the crime ([para 21]).

The court also noted that dissociation requires a qualitative assessment based on the nature of the defendant's involvement, the stage of crime preparation, and the efforts made to prevent the crime's execution ([para 22]).

## A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 23–24])

In applying these principles, the Court reviewed the evidence surrounding Nube’s claims of withdrawal. It highlighted that his assertion of first learning about the bomb's use on November 1 contradicted the transcript from an earlier meeting, thus undermining his credibility ([para 23]). Additionally, his continued engagement in the planning process, including attending meetings post-asserted withdrawal and failing to inform members of the syndicate about his dissociation, pointed to a lack of credible effort to dissociate ([paras 23–24]).

The court further evaluated the phone call made by the appellant on the day before the heist, concluding that the vagueness of the information provided did not satisfy the requirement to assist law enforcement in preventing the crime, further reflecting continued complicity ([para 16]).

Ultimately, the cumulative evidence demonstrated that Nube's actions did not constitute an effective withdrawal from the conspiratorial agreement or the common purpose, affirming the trial court's findings ([para 23]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 24–26])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Nube's appeal against both the convictions and the sentences. The court upheld the trial court’s decisions, finding no grounds to interfere with its determinations. The key takeaway established by the court was that mere absence at a crime scene does not absolve a participant of liability when there remains substantial evidence of prior involvement and a failure to adequately dissociate from the criminal venture ([paras 24–26]).

This case illustrates the stringent requirements for a defendant claiming withdrawal from a conspiracy in South African law, emphasizing the necessity for demonstrable actions that convincingly reflect an intention to sever ties with the criminal enterprise. The judgment serves as a critical reference point for future cases involving complex dynamics of conspiracy and the doctrine of common purpose.
