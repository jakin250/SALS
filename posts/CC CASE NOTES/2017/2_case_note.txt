# Case Note: The Business Zone 1010 CC t/a Emmarentia Convenience Centre v Engen Petroleum Limited and Others [2017] ZACC 2

## FIRAC Method Case Note

### F – Facts
The matter before the Constitutional Court arose from a legal dispute between The Business Zone 1010 CC, operating as Emmarentia Convenience Centre (the "Applicant"), and Engen Petroleum Limited (the "First Respondent"). Business Zone is a licensed retailer of petroleum products, while Engen is a licensed wholesaler under the Petroleum Products Act 120 of 1977 (the "Act") ([1]–[4]).

In 2005, Business Zone purchased a fuel and service station business and entered into a three-year lease agreement with Engen for the operation of the service station ([3]). A second lease agreement was concluded in April 2008, followed by an addendum in February 2010 detailing specific obligations of Engen regarding site improvements, including adding access points ([4]–[5]).

By September 2010, Business Zone raised concerns regarding Engen's failure to fulfill its obligations under the addendum, which Engen subsequently countered by claiming Business Zone had breached the agreement due to unauthorized alterations to the premises ([6]–[8]). Engen then canceled the lease agreement, citing Business Zone's breach ([8]).

Disputed agreements led Business Zone to seek relief from the High Court ([12]) and filed a request for arbitration under section 12B of the Act, asserting that Engen's conduct constituted unfair and unreasonable contractual practices ([13]).

However, the Controller of Petroleum Products rejected Business Zone's referral, citing the absence of an ongoing contract, a decision upheld by the Minister ([19]–[20]). This led Business Zone to seek a review of these decisions, and the Pretoria High Court ordered the Controller to refer the matter to arbitration ([24]).

Engen appealed the High Court's ruling in the Supreme Court of Appeal, which ultimately sided with Engen, arguing that the Controller lacked jurisdiction to refer disputes following a contract's cancellation ([25]–[26]).

### I – Issues
The central legal questions identified by the Court were:
1. Do the decisions of the Controller and the Minister constitute administrative action subject to review under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)? ([37]) 
2. What constitutes proper interpretation of section 12B of the Act regarding the existence of a contractual practice in circumstances involving a single act of cancellation? ([36]) 
3. Can an act such as cancellation be characterized as a “contractual practice” under section 12B? ([36]) 
4. What are the implications of Engen’s so-called “second cancellation” on the Controller’s ability to refer the complaint under section 12B? ([36]) 

### R – Rules
The Court relied principally on the following legal authorities and precedents:
- **Petroleum Products Act 120 of 1977**: Particularly, section 12B, which gives the Controller authority to refer disputes to arbitration concerning unfair contractual practices ([45]).
- **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: Used to determine if the decisions of the Controller and the Minister could be reviewed as administrative actions ([38]).
- **Case Law**:
  - Viking Pony Africa Pumps (Pty) Ltd v Hidro-Tech Systems (Pty) Ltd, which addressed the nature of administrative action ([41]).
  - Maphango v Aengus Lifestyle Properties, which informed the interpretation of “practice” to include single acts within the ambit of contractual disputes ([71]).
  
### A – Application
The Court began by assessing whether the Controller's decisions were administrative actions under PAJA, determining that the decisions adversely affected Business Zone’s rights, particularly regarding access to arbitration, and thus qualified for review ([39]–[43]). 

On interpreting section 12B, the Court established that no requirement existed for an ongoing contract to make a referral possible; rather, the only threshold was alleging an unfair or unreasonable contractual practice. The Controller's refusal based on the absence of an existing agreement was deemed erroneous and materially influenced by an error of law ([82]–[83]).

Addressing the central issue of whether a single act, i.e., the cancellation of the contract, constituted a “contractual practice”, the Court concluded that, in context, it indeed qualifies as such. The interpretation of "practice” was expanded to include single acts, thus allowing arbitration proceedings related to these acts ([69]–[76]). 

Finally, concerning Engen's argument about the effect of its second purported cancellation on the referral, the Court emphasized the arbitrator's capacity to address the issues notwithstanding ongoing high court proceedings, noting that engaging with unresolved disputes through arbitration is essential to uphold statutory intentions ([94]–[96]).

### C – Conclusion
The Court granted leave to appeal, deciding in favor of Business Zone. The Supreme Court of Appeal's ruling was set aside, and the case was remitted for arbitration under section 12B of the Act. This decision underscored the importance of equitable standards in licensing and contractual dealings within the petroleum industry while clarifying the meaning of “contractual practice” in legal terms ([99]). The implications of this ruling extend to enhancing access to arbitration in disputes regarding unfair contractual practices, thereby promoting fairness and equity in the petroleum market. 

This case illustrates a pivotal judicial stance on administrative review procedures and the interpretation of contractual obligations in South Africa’s regulatory environment, fostering a clearer framework for future disputes under the Petroleum Products Act.
