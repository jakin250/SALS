Supreme Court of Appeal

Geldenhuys v The State (470/2007) [2008] ZASCA 47

In summary, the implications of this ruling extend beyond this case; it necessitates a reassessment of statutory sexual consent laws in South Africa, aligning them with constitutional principles of equality and protection under the law.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2008 [2008] ZASCA 47

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The appellant, Izak Andreas Geldenhuys, was charged in 2004 with 13 counts of indecent assault, involving two minors: L B, born on June 29, 1983, and A, L's younger brother. The State withdrew one charge before trial, with Geldenhuys pleading not guilty to the remaining counts. On February 9, 2005, he was convicted of ten counts of contravening section 14(1)(b) of the Sexual Offences Act 23 of 1957, relating to alleged indecent acts with the complainant. He was subsequently sentenced to a total of 11 years imprisonment, which was later reduced to 7 years by the Pretoria High Court on appeal. However, during the appeal, constitutional issues regarding the validity of section 14's stipulations on age of consent were raised by the appellant ([1]–[3]).

I – Issues

The primary constitutional challenges centered on the validity of sections 14(1)(b) and 14(3)(b) of the Sexual Offences Act, which established different ages of consent for heterosexual and same-sex sexual activities (16 and 19 years, respectively). The appellant contended that this distinction constituted unfair discrimination based on gender and sexual orientation, violating section 9(3) of the South African Constitution. The court was also tasked with determining whether the age difference constituted an unreasonable restriction, thus rendering it unconstitutional ([3]–[4]).

R – Rules

The court examined the provisions of sections 14(1)(b) and 14(3)(b) of the Sexual Offences Act, which criminalized acts between a boy under the age of 19 and a girl under 16. It also referenced the South African Constitution, specifically section 9, which enshrines equality and prohibits discrimination based on sexual orientation. Additionally, the court considered international obligations under the United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child to protect children from sexual exploitation ([54]–[60]).

A – Application

The court applied a holistic approach to the evidence presented and concluded that while some aspects of the complainant's testimony had inconsistencies, the overall credibility of the complainant was upheld by corroborating evidence, particularly through the psychologist's testimony regarding the impact of manipulation and the notion of consensuality in cases involving adults and minors. The court found that the appellant's arguments for discriminatory age limits based on sexual orientation were unfounded and that the law, as it stood, placed unfair restrictions on one group while privileging another. Ultimately, the court deemed the differences in age limits unconstitutional, recalling previous judicial affirmations of sexual orientation equality ([41]–[70]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal declared sections 14(1)(b) and 14(3)(b) of the Sexual Offences Act unconstitutional due to their discriminatory nature, mandating that both same-sex and heterosexual sexual acts have a uniform age of consent of 16. The court ordered that the appellant's convictions on counts relating to same-sex acts were to be set aside pending confirmation by the Constitutional Court. The findings also suggested a qualified retrospectivity, ensuring that convictions prior to the ruling could be contested within established legal frameworks, thereby protecting the integrity of previous legal proceedings while conforming to constitutional standards ([77]–[80]).

In summary, the implications of this ruling extend beyond this case; it necessitates a reassessment of statutory sexual consent laws in South Africa, aligning them with constitutional principles of equality and protection under the law. The legislative framework now mandated must also reflect a unified standard free from discriminatory practices in relation to sexual orientation.
