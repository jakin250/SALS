Supreme Court of Appeal

Spenmac (Pty) Ltd v Tatrim CC

In this case, Spenmac (Pty) Ltd (the Appellant) appealed against a ruling from the Eastern Cape High Court, which set aside a sale agreement between it and Tatrim CC (the Respondent) regarding the purchase of a unit in a sectional title property, Park Towers.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2014 Spenmac v Tatrim CC (216/2013) [2014] ZASCA 48 Delivered 1 April 2014

Neutral Citation: Spenmac v Tatrim CC (216/2013) [2014] ZASCA 48

Coram: Mthiyane DP, Lewis, Shongwe, Petse JJA, and Mocumie AJA

Heard: 28 February 2014

Delivered: 1 April 2014

Facts ([paras 1–32])

In this case, Spenmac (Pty) Ltd (the Appellant) appealed against a ruling from the Eastern Cape High Court, which set aside a sale agreement between it and Tatrim CC (the Respondent) regarding the purchase of a unit in a sectional title property, Park Towers. The agreement, concluded on 8 October 2010, was deemed void due to lack of consensus resulting from a fundamental mistake induced by a misrepresentation from the plaintiff’s representative, Mr. Spendley ([para 1]).

The dispute arose over whether the representations made by Spendley regarding the property’s subdivision rights were false and whether these representations led the purchaser to mistakenly believe that they were buying a unit subject to a right of veto over the subdivision of unit 2. The appellant claimed that its understanding of the property and its features was fundamentally flawed due to these misrepresentations, while Tatrim disputed this, insisting that the purchaser was aware of the relevant risks and circumstances surrounding the property ([paras 2–4], [12]).

A key aspect of the agreement included a "voetstoots" clause and an acknowledgment that the purchaser had not been induced to enter the agreement by any representations from the seller ([para 3]). The plaintiff's representative, Mr. Thompson, testified that he was assured that the owner of unit 2 could not subdivide without the owner of unit 1's consent ([paras 6–10]). Evidence revealed that prior consent for subdivision had been granted, thereby undermining the alleged representations and raising questions about the parties' shared intentions at the time of contract ([paras 9–15]).

Issues ([paras 2–8])

The primary legal issue in this case concerned whether a misrepresentation made by the seller’s representative resulted in a fundamental mistake by the purchaser which precluded the parties from reaching a consensus. Two specific questions dominated the inquiry:

  1. Was the alleged misrepresentation regarding the right of veto over the subdivision a fundamental error that would allow the purchaser to rescind the agreement?
  2. Did the exemption clause in the contract, which limited the liability of the seller to the purchaser's claims, preclude the plaintiff's ability to rescind the contract based on the misrepresentation?

Rules ([paras 21–24], [27–29])

The court referred to principles of contract law related to misrepresentation and mistake. Relevant case law, including Trollip v Jordaan and Allen v Sixteen Sterling Investments, provided important precedents concerning the liability of parties for misrepresentations, whether made fraudulently or innocently. Specifically, the rule from Allen suggested that a contract could be rendered void ab initio if a misrepresentation caused a fundamental mistake regarding the subject matter of the agreement ([paras 23–24]).

Additionally, the court recognized that both parties must have reached a mutual understanding (consensus ad idem) for a binding agreement to be formed. If a fundamental mistake exists due to misrepresentation, it can invalidate the contract entirely, including associated exemption clauses, as both parties operate under an erroneous understanding ([paras 27–29]).

Application ([paras 27–31])

In applying these principles, the court assessed whether the plaintiff’s belief in the nature of the property—namely, that it consisted of only two units with the corresponding right of veto—was reasonable and whether it undermined the consensus necessary for an enforceable agreement. The court found that:

  1. The misrepresentation regarding the unit’s subdivision was indeed significant and likely influenced the appellant's decision to conclude the sale. The misrepresentation not only misled the purchaser but also affected the essential character of the agreement itself.
  2. The “voetstoots” clause was analyzed concerning the claimed misrepresentation. The court concluded that the clause could not protect the seller in instances where a fundamental mistake vitiated the contract's foundation, supporting the reasoning that not only must the law protect parties from fraudulent intentions, but also from justifiable mistakes resulting from a perceived misrepresentation.

The court's analysis confirmed that both parties operated under the mistaken understanding that unit 1 governed the veto over the subdivision without knowledge of the prior approval for subdivision. Therefore, the agreement was determined to lack the necessary consensus and validity ([paras 29–30]).

Conclusion ([paras 31–32])

The Supreme Court of Appeal affirmed the High Court's ruling that the sale agreement was void due to the mutual mistake induced by the misrepresentation concerning the property’s characteristics. The appellant's appeal was dismissed with costs, reinforcing the notion that parties must engage in transactions with a clear and mutual understanding of their agreements, and that misrepresentation—whether innocent or fraudulent—must be addressed to protect the integrity of contractual agreements.

The implications of this ruling underscore the importance of accurate representations in real estate transactions, as well as the necessity for parties to be diligent in understanding both the nature of the contract and the underlying realities of the property being sold. The decision reinforces contractual fairness and discourages misrepresentative practices in dealings, ensuring that all parties operate with transparency and good faith in contractual negotiations.
