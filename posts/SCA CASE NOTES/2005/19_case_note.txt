Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2005

# FIRAC Case Note: Gauteng Gambling Board v Silverstar Development Limited

**Case Title:** Gauteng Gambling Board v Silverstar Development Limited & Others [2005] ZASCA 18  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Date Delivered:** 29 March 2005  
**Reportable:** Case No: 80/04

---

## F – Facts

The facts surrounding this case involve a dispute concerning the grant of a casino licence in the West Rand area of Gauteng. The Gauteng Gambling Board (the "Board") issued a public invitation for applications for up to six casino licences in April 1997, receiving 23 applications by June of the same year ([4]). The Board ranked applicants including Silverstar Development Limited ("Silverstar") and Rhino Hotel and Resort Limited ("Rhino") for the West Rand area, ultimately favoring Rhino due to its perceived advantages ([8],[10]).

The Board worked in conjunction with the Executive Council of the Province of Gauteng ("Exco"), despite disagreements over preferred applicants. Exco preferred Silverstar, while the Board maintained Rhino as the better candidate ([10],[12]). Following extensive evaluations, including a significant meeting on 20 April 1998, Exco concurred with the Board to issue a sixth licence to Rhino ([14]).

Subsequently, a series of events led to Rhino’s application being compromised by environmental regulations following a rejection of its site proposal in October 1999 ([16]). In a notable turn of events, both Silverstar and Rhino collaborated on a joint proposal by December 2000, consolidating their applications into a new entity, Rhino Resort Ltd ([17]).

Despite this collaboration, the Board later directed that it would deny Silverstar's original application in December 2002, asserting that it was not the preferred choice for the licence in the West Rand area ([22]). Silverstar contested this refusal, initiating judicial review procedures ([24]). 

The court a quo ultimately set aside the Board's purported refusal and directed the Board and Exco to award the licence to Silverstar, a decision that became the focus of the appeal by the Board ([25]). This court’s ruling was based on the view that Silverstar was the only remaining viable applicant and had previously complied with the franchise requirements of the Board ([27]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the court include:

1. Whether the court a quo was justified in substituting its decision for that of the Board and Exco by directly awarding the casino licence to Silverstar, instead of remitting the matter back to the Board for reconsideration ([1],[25]).
2. Whether Silverstar's application constituted a continuation of a pending administrative process after the changes brought by the Gauteng Gambling Amendment Act 6 of 2001, particularly concerning Exco’s concurrence ([26]).
3. The implications of procedural fairness and legitimate expectation surrounding the grant of licences ([36],[38]).

## R – Rules

The legal principles applicable in this case were drawn from several statutes and precedents:

1. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000:** This act outlines the legal basis for judicial review of administrative action and allows courts to substitute administrative decisions when exceptional circumstances warrant it, as seen in section 8(1)(c)(ii)(aa) ([28]).
  
2. **National Gambling Act 33 of 1996 and the Gauteng Gambling Act 4 of 1995:** These Acts stipulate the regulatory framework for licensing and the powers vested in the Board, including the requirement of concurrence from Exco for issuing licences ([2],[3]).

3. **Legal Precedents:** Cases such as *Unitrans Passenger (Pty) Ltd t/a Greyhound Coach Lines v Chairman, National Transport Commission* [1999] 4 SA 1 (SCA) and *Commissioner, Competition Commission v General Council of the Bar of South Africa* [2002] 6 SA 606 (SCA) were cited to establish the standards for judicial review and the scope for courts to intervene in the decision-making of administrative bodies ([26],[28]).

## A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal assessed the factual and legal complexities, determining that the Board had effectively treated Silverstar's application as a de facto refusal throughout the proceedings, particularly given the Board's frequent reiteration of its preference for Rhino. The court emphasized that the Board had ceased to evaluate Silverstar as a viable candidate as issues around Rhino's viability unfolded ([35],[39]).

The court was persuaded that substantial delays and the ineffectiveness of remitting the decision back to the Board would not serve fairness to either party. It found that there were no compelling reasons to believe that the Board would arrive at an adverse conclusion upon reconsideration of Silverstar's application ([38],[40]). Thus, the court underscored that:

1. Silverstar met all minimum requirements and that any objections previously recorded had been sufficiently addressed or were no longer applicable ([26],[39]).
   
2. The Board's objections had been resolved by previously concurrent evaluations and agreements concerning proposed developments that were subsequently accepted in a joint application ([38]).

In light of these observations, the court determined that the exceptional circumstances justified a direct award of the licence to Silverstar.

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the Board's appeal, affirming the order of the court a quo directing that the Board and Exco award a casino licence to Silverstar ([42]). The decision underlined the principle that courts could substitute administrative actions in exceptional circumstances where a remittal would serve no practical purpose. 

The ruling highlights significant implications for the future of administrative reviews, reinforcing the idea that substantive considerations of fairness and the avoidance of undue delays are critical in administrative law ([41]). It reflects the judiciary’s role in ensuring that administrative authorities adhere to the principles of legality, reasonableness, and procedural fairness ([28],[40]). The outcome also suggests that in cases of prolonged indecision or in the presence of legitimate expectations, the courts may choose to usurp the function of administrative bodies to ensure justice is served without further delay. 

--- 

This case is critical in providing a framework for understanding the interaction between judicial authority and administrative discretion in South Africa, particularly within the context of the gambling regulatory framework.
