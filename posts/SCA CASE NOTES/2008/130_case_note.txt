Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2008

# FIRAC Case Note: City of Cape Town v Reader (719/2007) [2008] ZASCA 130

## F – Facts
The appeal before the Supreme Court of Appeal arose from a dispute related to the Municipality of Cape Town's approval of building plans submitted by the third respondent, Julia Patricia Ikin. The first and second respondents, Marina Guilietta Reader and Ian Donald Peploe, owned properties adjacent to Ikin's and objected to the building plans which transitioned a single-storey house into a double-storey structure ([1–6]).

The approval, granted on 20 February 2003, was contested based on the applicants' concerns that the construction would obstruct their views, reduce property value by approximately R350,000, and compromise their privacy ([6–8]). The applicants sought judicial review under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) but were met with a procedural challenge from the municipality. The municipality contended in limine that the applicants had not exhausted internal remedies stipulated under section 62 of the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 (Systems Act) before initiating court proceedings. The High Court judge, Veldhuizen J, dismissed the application based solely on this procedural point ([2–3]).

On appeal to the Full Court, the decision was reversed. The Full Court determined that section 62 did not provide a viable internal remedy in cases of third parties who had their rights or legitimate expectations adversely affected by the decision ([3]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issue raised was whether the provisions of section 7(2) of PAJA precluded the applicants from seeking judicial review of the municipality’s decision regarding building plan approval without first exhausting the internal remedies outlined in section 62 of the Systems Act. This encapsulated questions regarding the definition of "internal remedies" and the nature of rights affected by municipal decisions ([1], [10]).

Additionally, the case raised significant interpretive issues regarding the extent of the rights granted under the Systems Act to third parties, notably whether interested neighbors could be considered "affected parties" entitled to appeal against local administrative decisions ([3], [27]).

## R – Rules
The relevant legal authorities include:

1. **PAJA**: Section 7(2) prohibits a court from reviewing an administrative action unless all available internal remedies have been exhausted, unless exceptional circumstances warrant exemption ([1], [12]).

2. **Systems Act, Section 62**: Provides internal appeal processes for parties whose rights are affected by municipal decisions, outlining the conditions under which someone may appeal a decision made by municipal authorities. Importantly, it excludes the rights of third parties not involved in the original decision-making process ([14]).

3. **Walele v The City of Cape Town**: This important Constitutional Court case established that individuals like the applicants, who were not parties to the approval process, held no rights to demand pre-approval hearings regarding decisions that did not directly affect their rights in the initial scope ([19]).

4. **Interpretation of Statutes**: General principles dictate that the intention of legislation must be determined through its language and the context, focusing on whether internal remedies provided effectively protect the rights or legitimate expectations of affected parties ([24]).

## A – Application
The Court applied these legal frameworks through a systematic analysis of section 62 of the Systems Act. The judgment highlighted that for a party to invoke the appeal process under section 62, they must show that the municipal decision directly affected their rights. The Supreme Court found that the applicants' objections were not to the approval itself but rather to the execution of the plans post-approval, which did not meet the threshold required to trigger section 62 remedies ([21–23]).

Furthermore, the decision in Walele was pivotal in affirming that the mere act of granting approval, as it pertained to the municipal process, did not inherently affect the applicants’ legal rights, thus reinforcing the notion that the applicants were not entitled to appeal under section 62. The judgment emphasized that the necessity for notification post-approval, as per section 62(1), was contingent upon an actual alteration of rights, which was absent in this circumstance ([23–24]).

The majority contended that if the applicants could not demonstrate that their rights were affected by the approval itself, then logically, they should not be entitled to invoke an appeal that would typically require the assertion of adverse impact. Thus, the failure to allege such an impact led to the conclusion that the application for judicial review was indeed premature ([26]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court dismissed the appeal, upholding the Full Court's decision. The judgment clarifies that the appeal route stipulated in section 62 of the Systems Act does not extend to third-party objectors who are not part of the original approval process. Hence, the judgments effectively delineate the boundaries of administrative redress available for aggrieved property owners and illustrate the strict procedural adherence required under PAJA ([10], [26–37]).

The implications of this ruling are significant for the balance of local governance and property rights, particularly in establishing that objectors cannot rely on internal remedies unless they possess a direct and legally cognizable interest in the municipal decision that materially impacts their rights. Furthermore, it sets a clear precedent regarding the interpretation of "affected rights" in municipal decision-making frameworks ([29–32]).
