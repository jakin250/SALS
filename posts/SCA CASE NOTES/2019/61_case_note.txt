Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

### Case Note: Crockery Gladstone Farm v Rainbow Farms (Pty) Ltd

**Neutral Citation:** Crockery Gladstone Farm v Rainbow Farms (Pty) Ltd (592/18) [2019] ZASCA 61 (20 May 2019)

**Court:** The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Coram:** Tshiqi, Mbha, Mathopo JJA, Davis and Weiner AJJA  
**Heard:** 20 May 2019  
**Delivered:** 20 May 2019  
**Case No:** 592/18  

#### F – Facts
The case arises from a default judgment that was granted by Phatudi J on 2 August 2016. This judgment was issued in response to a claim where the parties were engaged in settlement negotiations at the time ([para 2]). Both the appellant, Crockery Gladstone Farm, and the respondent, Rainbow Farms (Pty) Ltd, were involved in negotiations concerning the underlying issues of the dispute, which led to a lack of timely opposition to the judgment.

The respondent had signalled its intention to oppose the claim by serving a notice to oppose but had not filed the answering affidavit, anticipating that the settlement discussions would yield a resolution ([para 2]). Despite this, when the matter proceeded before Phatudi J, the respondent's advocates were rebuffed when they requested a postponement, and the court was not made aware of the ongoing negotiations, leading to a default judgment being granted against the respondent promissory ([para 2]).

Following this, the respondent sought rescission of the default judgment, which was initially denied by Muller J on the grounds that the judgment was not a default judgment ([para 2]). The Full Court subsequently reversed this decision, finding that the judgment had indeed been obtained erroneously due to a material non-disclosure of facts at the initial hearing ([para 3]).

#### I – Issues
The principal legal question before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the Full Court's order, which granted rescission of the default judgment and allowed the respondent an opportunity to file its answering affidavit, was appealable, specifically in light of whether the order was final, definitive of the parties' rights, or disposed of a substantial portion of the relief claimed ([para 1], [para 4]).

A secondary issue arose concerning the appealability of the costs order resulting from the rescission application and subsequent appeal, particularly whether this order had practical consequences and warranted appellate review ([para 6]).

#### R – Rules
The determination of appealability is governed by the criteria established in *Zweni v The Minister of Law and Order* 1993 (1) SA 523 (A), which stipulates that an order is non-appealable if it is (a) not final in effect, (b) does not definitively determine the rights of the parties, and (c) does not dispose of a substantial portion of the relief claimed ([para 4]).

Moreover, the principles surrounding the necessity for appeals to have practical consequences were underscored in *Radio Pretoria v Chairman, Independent Communications Authority of SA* 2005 (1) SA 47 (SCA), which states that appeals can be dismissed if they are of such a nature that the decision sought would have no practical effect or result ([para 6]).

#### A – Application
The court concluded that the Full Court’s order was not appealable due to its interlocutory nature; it did not result in any final determination on the substantive claim ([para 5]). The case highlights that the mere provision of an opportunity for the respondent to file any necessary opposing papers does not infringe upon the appellant’s rights, nor does it dispose of the substantive issues that had been raised by the initial claim ([para 5]). 

Consequently, the appellant's position remained intact, and the court emphasized that the absence of a final resolution allows the possibility for further litigation on the matter. Therefore, the order did not cause irreparable harm to the appellant, reinforcing its interlocutory character ([para 5]).

Regarding the costs order, the court noted the appellant’s contention that the Full Court had erred in its findings. However, the court held that the legal principles and context necessitated that the respondent's attorneys had acted appropriately and responsibly in seeking rescission given the circumstances. The apparent failure of the appellant’s attorneys to communicate effectively had significant implications for the procedural dynamic, leading the court to affirm that the Full Court's exercise of discretion was free from misdirection ([para 7]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming the Full Court's decision which had allowed the respondent the opportunity to oppose the claim ([para 8]). The judgment reflects a fundamental commitment to ensuring fairness in civil procedure, particularly where parties may be disadvantaged due to procedural mismanagement or miscommunication. 

The implications of this judgment underscore the importance of effective legal communication and the duty of legal representatives to ensure that the court is fully informed of relevant negotiations and the context when judgments are sought. This case thus contributes significantly to the understanding of the appealability of decisions relating to rescission of judgments and the broader principle of fair trial rights in civil proceedings.
