# Case Note: The South African Rugby Football Union v. The Commissioner for the South African Revenue Services (Case No. 90/98)

**Coram:** Hefer, Grosskopf, Zulman, JJA, Melunsky and Farlam AJJA  
**Heard:** 2 September 1999  
**Delivered:** 1 October 1999  

## F – Facts

The appellant, the South African Rugby Football Union (hereafter "Sarfu"), sought a refund for value-added tax (VAT) based on profits from ticket sales for the 1995 Rugby World Cup, which Sarfu hosted. This claim was initiated following the refusal of the South African Revenue Services (hereafter "the respondent") to refund the VAT paid under section 44 of the Value-Added Tax Act 89 of 1991. 

The Rugby World Cup was arranged under a contract involving Rugby World Cup Limited (the "Central Organiser"), Rugby World Cup Licensing BV, and Sarfu, who managed the event in South Africa ([1]–[2]). A notable aspect of the arrangement was that ticketing policy, including the categorization of tickets, was ultimately controlled by the Central Organiser. Up to 50% of tickets for the matches were allocated for sale to overseas buyers ([2]–[3]).

Sarfu claimed VAT on a gross amount of R13,192,900. Additionally, Sarfu's accountants reported that 109,050 tickets were sold, with only a portion sent directly to the Central Organiser in London ([4]–[5]). Sarfu contended that its supply of these tickets should either qualify for zero-rating under provisions of the VAT Act, specifically sections 11(1)(a), 11(2)(k), and 11(2)(l), as they were sold overseas ([6]–[8]).

## I – Issues

The legal issues at the heart of this case concern:

1. Whether Sarfu's supply of overseas tickets constituted a taxable supply under the VAT Act.
2. Whether those tickets should be zero-rated for VAT purposes per the relevant statutory provisions ([8]–[9]).

The court also contemplated whether the respondent's initial assessment of VAT was accurate and aligned with the agreed contractual obligations ([15]).

## R – Rules

The relevant statutory provisions from the Value-Added Tax Act 89 of 1991 include:

- **Section 7(1)(a)**: Imposes VAT on supplies of goods or services unless exempt or zero-rated.
- **Section 11(1)(a)**: Allows zero-rating for the supply of goods exported outside South Africa.
- **Section 11(2)(k)** and **(l)**: Provide for zero-rating services physically rendered outside the Republic and supplied to non-residents ([9]–[11]).

The legal definition of "supply" is critical here, particularly in interpreting whether Sarfu's action constituted a sale under VAT principles ([10]).

## A – Application

The court primarily analyzed Sarfu's classification of its supply of tickets. In determining whether a sale took place, the court highlighted that Sarfu did not sell the tickets to the Central Organiser; instead, they were merely arranged for delivery to the Central Organiser as part of an administrative obligation ([6], [10]). Thus, the court rejected Sarfu's argument that the supply of tickets should be zero-rated under section 11(1)(a) since a sale, as defined, had not occurred ([10]).

Additionally, for sections 11(2)(k) and (l) to apply, Sarfu needed to demonstrate that these tickets were provided as services to actors who were non-residents of the Republic at the time these services were executed; however, the court found sufficient basis to claim that these parties, including the Central Organiser, were engage in business activities within South Africa and thus classified as residents ([12]).

Although the court acknowledged that the tickets could, under certain definitions, be classified as services ([17]), it noted that VAT must be calculated based not merely on the gross sales price but rather on the value of the services rendered. Sarfu was unable to prove that it had received consideration relative to the total amount invoiced ([18]).

## C – Conclusion

The court ultimately held that Sarfu’s appeal should be allowed, focusing on the need for the Commissioner to reassess the VAT payable. The ruling emphasized that the prior VAT assessment was flawed due to misclassification of the ticket supply and the failure to align the tax amount appropriately with the true circumstances of the supply ([19]).

The court ordered the Commissioner to recalculate the Value-Added Tax on services regarding tickets sold overseas and mandated the reimbursing of the excess payment to Sarfu. The findings illustrate a nuanced understanding of the principles of supply under VAT law and affirm the need for precise adherence to the legislative framework when assessing tax liabilities in complex transactional arrangements, especially those crossing national boundaries ([19]).
