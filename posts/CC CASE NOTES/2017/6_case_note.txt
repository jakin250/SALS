Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: Mogaila v Coca Cola Fortune (Pty) Limited [2017] ZACC 6

## F - Facts
The applicant, **Maria Jane Mogaila**, was employed as a stock controller by **Coca Cola Fortune (Pty) Limited** from November 2001 until her dismissal on 26 November 2007 following a disciplinary hearing which found her guilty of assault. Displeased with her dismissal, she sought recourse through the **Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA)**. An arbitration held on 26 March 2008 resulted in a ruling on 29 April 2008, where the arbitrator deemed her dismissal procedurally fair but substantively unfair. Hence, Coca Cola was ordered to reinstate her effective from 2 June 2008 and pay her six months’ back pay amounting to R27,899.40 ([paras 4-6]).

Post-arbitration, on 2 June 2008, Mogaila attempted to have the arbitration award certified under Section 143(3) of the **Labour Relations Act, 1995 (LRA)**. However, she faced obstacles, including Coca Cola’s review application against the arbitration award, which resulted in further complications. Coca Cola contended that the arbitration award constituted a “debt” as defined under the **Prescription Act 68 of 1969** and, as Mogaila failed to take action to enforce the award within three years, it had prescribed ([paras 5-8]).

In April 2016, Mogaila sought direct access to the **Constitutional Court**, arguing that the Prescription Act was inconsistent with the Labour Relations Act and that her reinstatement order should not be categorized as a "debt” subject to the Prescription Act ([paras 1-3, 10]).

## I - Issues
The principal issues raised in this case were as follows:

1. **Does the Prescription Act apply to reinstatement orders issued under the Labour Relations Act?** 
2. **Is an arbitration award regarding reinstatement categorized as a "debt" under the Prescription Act?** 
3. **Should direct access to the Constitutional Court be granted in this matter?** ([paras 1, 9])

## R - Rules
The Court assessed the ruling under:
1. **Section 167(6)(a)** of the **Constitution** which allows for direct access in certain cases if it serves the interests of justice ([paras 10-12]).
2. **Labour Relations Act, 1995** (specifically **Section 143**) which stipulates the binding nature of arbitration awards and their certification for enforcement ([paras 4, 5]).
3. **Prescription Act 68 of 1969**; examining whether the arbitration award constituted a "debt" and the implications thereof ([paras 13-22]).

The precedents drawn upon included:
- **Myathaza v Johannesburg Metropolitan Bus Services (SOC) Limited** [2016] ZACC 49 ([para 5]).
- **Bruce v Fleecytex Johannesburg CC** [1998] ZACC 3, which elaborates on the nature of direct access and the criteria surrounding it ([paras 11-12]).

## A - Application
The court relied on its previous judgment in **Myathaza** to address the contentious issues. It reiterated that the Prescription Act was incompatible with the LRA based on fundamental differences that materialize between the two statutes, hence the arbitration award issued under the LRA would not prescribe. The varying interpretations of the terms "debt" in employment disputes were explored, with the conclusion that an order of reinstatement does not fit within the conventional understanding of "debt", as it does not impose an obligation of payment, goods delivery, or service rendering but instead restores the employment relationship with attendant contractual rights and obligations ([paras 27-29, 19-20]).

Given the facts surrounding Mogaila's case—she had actively sought enforcement of the award and pursued her rights—the court found compelling reasons for direct access, affirming the urgency and necessity of resolving her grievance without requiring her to navigate lower courts again ([paras 10-13, 28]).

## C - Conclusion
The Constitutional Court granted Mogaila's application for direct access, ruling that her reinstatement order had not prescribed according to the Prescription Act. Therefore, Coca Cola Fortune was mandated to reinstate her and to pay the costs of the application. This decision underscores the Court's commitment to ensuring that statutory rights are upheld, affirming that arbitration awards under the LRA are not overshadowed by prescriptive burdens typical of civil law obligations ([paras 2-3, 30-31]). 

This outcome not only clarifies the legal standing of reinstatement orders but also signifies the importance of access to justice, particularly for employees navigating potentially inequitable disputes with their employers.
