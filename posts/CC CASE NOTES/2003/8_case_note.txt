Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2003

## FIRAC Case Note: Norman Murray Ingledew vs. The Financial Services Board (CCT 6/02)

### F – Facts

This case arises from an application for leave to appeal against the decision of the Pretoria High Court, which dismissed Mr. Norman Murray Ingledew's interlocutory application for specific information regarding an impending trial for insider trading. The Financial Services Board (FSB) had initiated proceedings against Ingledew and another party on 26 May 2000, alleging violations under the Insider Trading Act, 1998 (the Act) due to their acquisition of insider information leading to profitable transactions concerning shares in Skills Accel (Pty) Ltd ([paras 1–2]).

Ingledew sought access to witness statements and documents obtained by the FSB during investigative interrogations, asserting that this access was crucial for a fair defense in light of his constitutional rights under sections 9(1), 34, and 35(3) of the Constitution. The FSB argued the need for such disclosure was unnecessary for pleading, as the underlying rules of court, particularly rule 35(14), only addressed documents relevant to a “reasonably anticipated issue” in litigation ([paras 3–7]).

The High Court found that the applicant was capable of pleading without the requested materials, leading to its dismissal of Ingledew’s application. Following this, the applicant sought condonation for the late filing of his appeal application, resulting in numerous procedural motions ([paras 8–12]).

### I – Issues

The core constitutional issues raised in the case revolve around the applicant's right to access information under both the procedural rules governing civil litigation and the broader constitutional framework. The specific legal questions are as follows:
1. Does rule 35(14) impose a limitation on the constitutional right to access information enshrined in section 32 of the Constitution?
2. Can an applicant invoke a constitutional entitlement to access information without challenging the constitutionality of the rules governing discovery?
3. Ultimately, is it in the interests of justice to grant leave to appeal despite the procedural shortcomings and the findings made by the High Court? ([paras 13–14, 18, 20]).

### R – Rules

The legal principles applicable to this case arise primarily from constitutional provisions and statutory frameworks governing access to information:
- **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996, section 32(1)**: Guarantees every individual access to information held by the state necessary for the exercise or protection of their rights.
- **Insider Trading Act, 1998 (Act 135 of 1998)**: Provides the context for the allegations of insider trading against Ingledew and his co-defendant.
- **Rule 35(14)** of the Uniform Rules of Court: Permits a party to request access to specified documents for the purpose of pleading in litigation, subject to limitations on the types of documents.

In addition, the court cites various precedents, including *S v Boesak* and *NEHAWU v University of Cape Town* to articulate the standards for granting leave to appeal, stating that it requires the presence of a constitutional question and consideration of the interests of justice ([paras 6, 13–14]).

### A – Application

The court analyzed Ingledew’s arguments concerning the limitation of access to information under rule 35(14) vis-à-vis the overarching right under section 32(1) of the Constitution. It assessed that while both provisions confer a right to information, the rule is more limited, confining requests to documents needed for pleading purposes. The High Court had found that Ingledew could adequately plead without the information sought under the provisions of rule 35, thereby validating the procedural constraints ([paras 15–19]).

Ingledew's attempt to invoke the general right under section 32 without challenging the constitutionality of rule 35(14) posed a substantial obstacle. Both his reliance on constitutional claims and the process followed were scrutinized, particularly given the legal doctrine of objective constitutional invalidity, which necessitates an explicit challenge to any applicable procedural rule that might be argued as unconstitutional ([paras 20–29]).

Ultimately, the court concluded that granting leave to appeal was not justified in this instance. It weighed the applicant's potential prejudice if the appeal were refused against the nature of grievances raised, determining that the refusal to grant access to the requested documents would not unduly impede Ingledew's ability to defend against the allegations. It further emphasized that any constitutional issues surfaced were unlikely to yield broad implications beyond the immediate circumstances of the case, given the readiness of the applicant to plead ([paras 30–34, 36]).

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court, affirming the High Court’s order, held that the application for leave to appeal must be refused—with costs awarded against the applicant. It emphasized that the procedural application added little to the substantive merits of the case. The ruling underlines the significance of adhering to procedural integrity in legal proceedings while balancing the constitutional rights of individuals within the judicial system ([paras 37–41]). 

### Implications

The judgment elucidates the interplay between constitutional rights and procedural laws in South African litigation. It affirms the need for litigants to navigate existing rules adequately when invoking constitutional provisions. By refusing the leave to appeal, the court reiterates the threshold for access to constitutional relief, especially in ongoing litigation contexts, while cautioning against dilatory tactics that do not substantiate genuine grievances ([para 42]).
