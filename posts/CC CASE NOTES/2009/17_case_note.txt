Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2009

# FIRAC Case Note on **Strategic Liquor Services v Mvumbi and Others**

## F – Facts:
The applicant, Strategic Liquor Services, sought leave to appeal against a Labour Court judgment dated 20 February 2007, which dismissed its application for the review of a ruling made by the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA) in favor of Mr. Wesley Redgard, a former employee. Redgard had worked as a merchandising supervisor and later as a regional manager from October 2003 until February 2004. Despite his resignation, the CCMA concluded that he was constructively dismissed and awarded him compensation equivalent to ten months' salary (R121,500) ([1]). 

The employer contested the CCMA's finding on grounds that Redgard had allegedly been given a choice to resign or face poor performance procedures, a representation they claimed undermined the finding of constructive dismissal. The Labour Court and subsequently the Labour Appeal Court dismissed the employer’s appeal, maintaining that constructive dismissal had been appropriately determined by the CCMA ([2]–[4]). 

The CCMA proceedings were initially held on 8 October 2004, resulting in an award on 19 January 2005. Despite prompt applications for review by the employer, the proceedings were beset by significant delays, compounded by the Labour Court's failure to provide written reasons for its judgments ([6]–[12]). 

## I – Issues:
The primary legal issues identified in this case include:
1. Whether the CCMA and lower courts erred in finding that Redgard had been constructively dismissed.
2. The adequacy of judicial reasoning in the Labour Court’s decisions, particularly concerning the right to written reasons for judgments ([3]–[6]).

## R – Rules:
The applicable legislation includes:
- Section 185(a) of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995, which provides the right not to be unfairly dismissed.
- Section 186(e) of the same Act, which defines "dismissal," including constructive dismissal scenarios where the employer renders continued employment intolerable ([3]–[4]).

Judicial precedents relevant to the ruling include *Murray v Minister of Defence* ([4] n.2), which clarified the nature of constructive dismissal, emphasizing that the test does not require that the employee have no choice but to resign.

## A – Application:
In applying the law to the facts, the court emphasized that the interpretation of "constructive dismissal" is that the employee’s resignation must result from a situation created by the employer that made continued employment intolerable, rather than a complete lack of alternative choices. The court noted that Redgard’s uncontested testimony indicated that the environment at Strategic Liquor Services became unbearable, thus rendering any "choice" presented to him as a false dilemma ([4]–[5]).

Moreover, the court highlighted systemic issues in the handling of the case, noting significant delays in proceedings and the failure of the Labour Court to provide written reasons for its decisions, despite repeated requests by the employer ([12]–[16]). This lack of transparency and accountability in the judicial process raised concerns regarding compliance with the principles of fair trial and access to justice, protected under section 34 of the South African Constitution ([15]–[20]).

## C – Conclusion:
The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the application for leave to appeal, affirming the CCMA's conclusion regarding constructive dismissal and the lack of judicial grounds to disturb the arbitrator's decision. The court found little merit in the employer's contention regarding the jurisdictional requisites for constructive dismissal and noted the troubling features in the case, especially regarding systemic delays and the failure to provide written reasons ([5]–[20]).

This decision reiterates the courts' obligations to provide timbered justifications for their judgments and highlights the systemic challenges within the South African labour adjudication framework. The case underscores the necessity of timely and reasoned judgments to uphold the principles of accountability and fairness enshrined in the judicial process.
