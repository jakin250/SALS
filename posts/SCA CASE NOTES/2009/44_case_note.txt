# Case Note: *King v The Attorneys Fidelity Fund Board of Control* (137/2008) [2009] ZASCA 44

## F – Facts
The case centers around an appeal wherein Mary Patricia King and others (appellants) sought reimbursement from the Attorneys Fidelity Fund Board of Control (respondent) after losing money to a fraudulent factoring scheme operated by Van Schalkwyk's Attorneys, which was allegedly misappropriated by the attorneys involved.

The procedural history began in the Grahamstown High Court, where a consolidated claim by 103 plaintiffs against the board for theft of money entrusted to the partnership was dismissed. The court found that the claims were excluded under Section 47(1)(g) of the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979, which concerns money that an attorney was instructed to invest ([paras 1-2]).

The plaintiffs had deposited money into the trust account of Van Schalkwyk's Attorneys, with the understanding that it was for a factoring scheme, which involved financing the discounting of estate agents' commissions. The scheme collapsed in May 2000, leading to claims of misappropriation by partners Van Schalkwyk and Du Mont ([paras 3-6]).

The court established that to succeed, the plaintiffs needed to show (a) pecuniary loss, (b) theft by practitioners at Van Schalkwyks, (c) money entrusted by them or on their behalf, and (d) that these events occurred in the course of the practitioners' practice. While the court recognized the first two elements, it concluded against the plaintiffs on the latter two ([paras 9-10]). The court emphasized that the plaintiffs instructed the firm to invest their money, fulfilling criteria set out under Section 47, which excludes claims where a practitioner has been instructed to invest ([para 30]).

## I – Issues
The central legal questions addressed in this case were:
1. Whether the funds in question had been "entrusted" to the attorneys by the plaintiffs ([para 10]).
2. Whether this entrustment occurred within the course of the attorneys' practice ([para 10]).
3. Whether the plaintiffs' claims were excluded by Section 47(1)(g) of the Attorneys Act, which provides that the Fund shall not be liable for theft of money which a practitioner has been instructed to invest since the subsection's effective date ([paras 8-10, 30-31]).

## R – Rules
The relevant legal authorities include:
1. **Attorneys Act 53 of 1979**, specifically:
   - Section 26(a) details the Fund's liability regarding theft of money entrusted to attorneys in the course of practice ([para 1]).
   - Section 47(1)(g) excludes liability for theft of money that an attorney was instructed to invest.
   - Section 47(4) clarifies interpretations related to the instructions given to practitioners for investment purposes ([para 8]).
   
2. **Case Law**:
   - *Industrial and Commercial Factors (Pty) Ltd v Attorneys Fidelity Fund Board of Control* (1997) delineated the elements that must be proven for claims against the Fund ([para 9]).
   
The case emphasizes that the interpretation of "investment" should align with its ordinary meaning, involving the expectation of profit ([para 33]).

## A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal applied the relevant legal provisions to the facts of the case. The court recognized that while the plaintiffs suffered pecuniary loss, they failed to demonstrate that the money was entrusted in the context envisioned by the law because they had explicitly instructed Van Schalkwyks to invest their funds in a factoring scheme ([para 9, 35]).

The plaintiffs contended that their investments were conditional on trust, yet the court pointed out they had knowingly participated in an investment scheme, thereby satisfying the criteria for exclusion set forth in Section 47(1)(g) of the Act. The acknowledgment of debt provided by Van Schalkwyk's Attorneys supported the idea that the funds were indeed to be invested, with the expectation of profit, thus falling under the non-liability clause of the Fund ([paras 33-35]).

The court did not find merit in the plaintiffs’ assertion that their money was stolen immediately upon deposit and thus could not constitute an investment ([para 37]). It noted that the pooling of funds in the trust account meant the money could have been used interchangeably, which denied the argument that identifiable funds were immediately misappropriated.

In considering whether the plaintiffs’ funds were entrusted, the court found that the act of depositing money into the firm’s account with instructions to invest constituted a clear instruction seeking investment on their behalf, concluding that all requisite conditions of Section 47 were met ([para 36]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the dismissal of the plaintiffs' claims against the Attorneys Fidelity Fund Board of Control. The court held that the money entrusted was indeed directed toward an investment scheme as stipulated under Section 47(1)(g) of the Attorneys Act. Consequently, the respondent was deemed not liable for reimbursement.

The implications of this case highlight the importance of understanding the legal definition of "entrustment" and "investment" within the scope of attorney-client relationships, particularly when financial transactions intended for investment purposes are involved. This case serves as a precedent on the limits of liability for the Attorneys Fidelity Fund, reinforcing the necessity for claimants to demonstrate that funds were not engaged in scenarios that trigger statutory exclusions. 

As a result, the appellants were left with the financial burden from their investments turned losses, affirming the legal doctrine that individuals engaging in investment schemes bear the risk associated with their financial decisions, particularly in legally regulated environments. Thus, the appeal was dismissed with costs, reflecting the court's endorsement of the existing statutory framework governing attorneys and their financial custodianship ([para 40]).
