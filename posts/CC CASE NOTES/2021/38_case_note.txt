Source Folder: 2021

# Case Note: Langa v Premier, Limpopo and Others [2021] ZACC 38

## F - Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([1]–[27])

The applicant, Kgabagare David Langa, was recognized as the senior traditional leader of the Mapela Traditional Community in 2009 by the Premier of Limpopo Province, who enforced this recognition under the Limpopo Traditional Leadership and Institutions Act 6 of 2005 (Limpopo Act). A dispute emerged over this leadership position involving the fifth respondent, Hans Masebe Langa, who was recognized by the Premier in 2017 following recommendations from the Ralushai and Kgatla Commissions. The applicant contested his removal from the position, arguing that the Premier’s decisions to withdraw his certificate of recognition and recognize the fifth respondent were unlawful and invalid as they failed to adhere to due process outlined in both the Limpopo Act and the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003.

These disputes were initially addressed in the High Court of South Africa, Limpopo Division, Polokwane, where the applicant's review application was dismissed ([28]–[32]). The High Court held that the required statutory processes were followed, rejecting the applicant’s argument that the Royal Family's involvement in identifying the new leader was inadequate. The applicant subsequently sought leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court, which was granted on the grounds of the leadership dispute’s constitutional implications for the Mapela Traditional Community ([36]).

## I - Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([37]–[37])

The primary issue before the Constitutional Court was whether the Premier of Limpopo had acted lawfully in recognizing the fifth respondent as senior traditional leader and in withdrawing the applicant’s recognition. The appeal raised several legal questions:

1. Did the Premier have the authority to act on the basis of recommendations made by the Kgatla and Ralushai Commissions under the relevant legislative framework?
2. Was the procedure followed in removing the applicant and appointing the fifth respondent compliant with the Limpopo Act and the Framework Act?
3. What was the proper interpretation of the powers and processes outlined in sections 12, 13, and 30 of the Limpopo Act concerning the recommendation and removal of traditional leaders?

## R - Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([4]–[43])

The primary legislative texts governing the appointment and removal of traditional leaders were the Limpopo Act and the Framework Act. Key provisions cited included:

- **Limpopo Act**: Sections 12 and 13 outline the processes for recognizing and relieving traditional leaders of their duties, emphasizing the role of the Royal Family in identifying successors and detailing the grounds for removal.
- **Framework Act**: Sections 25 and 26 provide commissions the authority to investigate traditional leadership disputes and the process for making recommendations to the Premier. The distinction between the terms "recommendation" and "decision" was a critical point in interpretation ([12], [14]).

The court also referenced the case of *Tshivhulana Royal Family v Netshivhulana* ([44]) to underscore the legal principles around the roles of various stakeholders in traditional leadership disputes and the procedural limitations on the powers of a Premier with respect to wrongful appointments.

## A - Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([38]–[56])

The court analyzed whether the Premier’s actions in issuing the withdrawal and recognition notices were legally valid. It determined that the removal of the applicant's certificate of recognition was based on a determination of “wrongful appointment or recognition” as outlined under sections 12(1)(c) and 13(1)(c). The court found that the Premier had improperly relied on section 13(3) of the Limpopo Act, which is applicable only when the removal is initiated by the Royal Family for grounds other than wrongful appointment. The court clarified that the Premier’s powers must align with the recommendations made by the Kgatla Commission regarding the removal of the applicant ([39]–[47]).

The court established that the procedural requirements for legitimate administrative action under the framework laid out in the PAJA were not met. It reinforced that the Premier's incorrect invocation of the statutory provisions and failure to follow proper procedures rendered the withdrawal notice invalid ([56]).

In addressing the recognition of the fifth respondent, the court reasoned that if the withdrawal notice was unlawful, so too must the recognition notice be invalid. The court emphasized that the two actions were inextricably linked, and a traditional leadership position cannot be held by two leaders simultaneously according to the Limpopo Act ([49], [50]).

## C - Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([57])

The Constitutional Court upheld the appeal, concluding that the notices issued by the Premier were unlawful and invalid, thereby reinstating the applicant's previous recognition as senior traditional leader. The court ruled that:

1. The Premier had acted beyond his legal authority in issuing the withdrawal notice, failing to adhere to the procedures outlined in both the Limpopo and Framework Acts.
2. The recognition of the fifth respondent was equally invalid due to its reliance on the flawed withdrawal process.

The order included a provision that the initial effects of these notices would be prospective to avoid disruption within the Mapela Traditional Community, considering the practical implications of the fifth respondent's actions since his inauguration in 2018. The court ordered that the Premier was liable to pay the applicant’s costs ([57]).

The judgment underscored the intricate balance required within traditional leadership structures and the obligation of public officials to comply strictly with statutory procedures, marking an important precedent concerning the governance of traditional leadership in South Africa.
