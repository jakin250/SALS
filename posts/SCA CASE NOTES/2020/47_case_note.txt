Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# Case Note: Pretorius v The State (705/2019) [2020] ZASCA 47

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–1])
On 13 September 2012, the appellant, Andre Pretorius, was convicted in the Regional Court of Pretoria on three counts of rape involving his then stepdaughter. Following his conviction, he was sentenced to 18 years’ imprisonment on 17 January 2013. Subsequently, Pretorius sought leave to appeal against this conviction from the regional magistrate, which was denied. Pursuing this matter further, he petitioned for leave to appeal from the Judge President of the Gauteng Division of the High Court, as stipulated in section 309C of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([para 1]). This petition was also dismissed on 2 February 2016 by Justices Louw and Baqwa. Pretorius then applied for special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) as per section 16(1)(b) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013. This application was favorably considered, allowing Pretorius’s appeal to proceed ([paras 1–2]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 3–3])
The primary issue before the SCA was whether Justices Louw and Baqwa of the High Court should have granted Pretorius leave to appeal against his conviction. The court examined whether there lay a reasonable prospect of success in the prospective appeal to the High Court ([paras 2–3]). In this context, the court determined that the appeal centered solely on the merits of the denial of leave to appeal rather than the merits of the original conviction itself ([para 3]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 2–2])
The SCA relied on established legal principles articulated in prior decisions, notably *S v Khoasasa* 2003 (1) SACR 123 (SCA), which posited that a refusal of leave to appeal by two High Court judges constitutes a 'judgment or order' that can be subjected to appeal ([para 2]). The court reaffirmed the standard for granting leave to appeal, which requires demonstrating a reasonable prospect of success, a test established in *S v Kriel* 2012 (1) SACR 1 (SCA) ([para 3]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 4–5])
In evaluating Pretorius's appeal, the SCA looked into critical concerns raised by the appellant regarding the admissibility of testimonies from two key witnesses, both minors. His argument hinged on their testimonies being inadmissible due to procedural failures in adequately placing them under oath or admonishing them appropriately in accordance with sections 162, 163, and 164 of the CPA ([para 4]). The court considered this contention alongside allegations of misdirection by the regional magistrate regarding the assessment of guilt beyond a reasonable doubt.

The state's response to these claims conceded to the existence of sound and rational bases that could warrant success on appeal, particularly regarding both the evidentiary points raised and the overall conviction outcome ([para 5]). The mutual recognition of these points by both parties influenced the court's decision, leading the SCA to hold that there were indeed reasonable prospects of success at the High Court level ([para 5]).

### C – Conclusion: Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 6–7])
Ultimately, the SCA's ruling was in favor of granting Pretorius leave to appeal. The court set aside the order refusing such leave, concluding that the merits of his case warranted a thorough reevaluation at the High Court ([para 7]). The implications of this judgment extend beyond merely allowing Pretorius another chance at appeal; it underscores the appellate court's role in ensuring fair judicial processes and the immersive nature of scrutinizing evidentiary admissibility, especially given the sensitivities surrounding testimony from minors in criminal proceedings.

In summary, the SCA's decision not only addressed the immediate procedural injustice faced by Pretorius but also reinforced the paramount importance of proper evidentiary handling in judicial proceedings, thereby supporting an environment of due process and fairness in the South African legal system.
