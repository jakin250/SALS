Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# FIRAC Case Note: Mabaso v The State (677/2020) [2021] ZASCA 98

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–7])

On 25 April 2018, Theresa Fortunate Mabaso (the appellant), was convicted of murder by the Mpumalanga Division of the High Court. She was sentenced to life imprisonment after the court determined that no substantial and compelling circumstances were present to justify a lesser sentence ([paras 1–2]). The conviction stemmed from the murder of Sifiso Michael Mabuza, a police officer, who was killed during a robbery at his home by three armed men, including Mabaso’s co-accused, Wiseman Mlamuli Ngomane ([paras 8–9]).

The state’s case against Mabaso largely relied on the testimony of Mr. Sindi Richman Mvubu, who identified her as having masterminded the murder. Mvubu had previously pleaded guilty to related offenses and implicated Mabaso, stating that she had offered him money to carry out the murder due to prior grievances between them. Subsequently, claims of a previous inconsistent statement made by Mvubu became a key aspect of the trial ([paras 11–18]). However, during the trial, Mvubu's account evolved, raising concerns regarding his credibility and reliability ([para 17]).

The appeal process was initiated due to the high court's verdict, where both parties engaged in procedural applications concerning the late filing of documents ([paras 2–7]). Ultimately, the appeal contested the conviction on multiple grounds, particularly focusing on evidentiary issues around Mvubu's statements and the lack of direct evidence implicating Mabaso in the murder.

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 20–31])

The primary issues emerging from the appeal included:

1. **Admissibility of Evidence**: Was the previous inconsistent statement made by Mr. Mvubu admissible against the appellant, and did it carry substantive value? Additionally, did the high court err in admitting Mvubu’s statements contravening longstanding legal principles regarding confessions made by co-accused?
   
2. **Credibility and Reliability of Testimony**: Were the contradictions in the testimony of Mr. Mvubu compelling enough to classify him as an unreliable witness, thus undermining the State’s case against Mabaso?

3. **Fair Trial Considerations**: Did the trial judge's conduct during the proceedings affect the fairness of the trial, thereby breaching the appellant’s right to a fair trial?

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 20–31])

The court relied on various provisions of the **Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA)**, notably:

- **Section 219**: Establishes that no confession made by one accused shall be admissible as evidence against another accused.
- **Section 220**: Pertains to the admissibility of admissions against co-accused.

Precedents that shaped the court’s reasoning included:

- **Makhubela v S; Matjeke v S**: Reinforced that confessions by one accused cannot be utilized against a co-accused, emphasizing a foundational principle of fairness in criminal proceedings ([para 25]).
  
- **S v Mathonsi**: Addressed the admissibility of prior inconsistent statements only as tools for discrediting the witness, requiring careful evaluation rather than substantive use as evidence ([para 24]).

These principles underscore the constitutional guarantees of fair trial, primarily enshrined in section 35(3) of the **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996**, which safeguards the rights of accused persons during criminal proceedings.

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 22–31])

In reviewing the high court's decision, the Supreme Court of Appeal identified critical flaws in the assessment of the evidentiary standards. It determined that the high court misapplied the principles established in **Mathonsi** by failing to contextualize Mvubu's inconsistent statements appropriately. The court emphasized that these statements were not merely admissible for their content but, as per statutory requirements, could not be used substantively against the appellant ([para 24]).

Moreover, the court found that the high court neglected to properly evaluate the credibility of Mvubu as a witness. The glaring contradictions across his testimonies and a lack of corroborative evidence further diminished his reliability as a sole source implicating Mabaso, leading the Supreme Court to conclude that his testimony was unsatisfactory ([paras 28–29]).

The appellate court articulated that evidence provided by Mvubu, particularly his admission in the plea agreement, was inadmissible against Mabaso due to the explicit legislative prohibitions against confessional evidence from co-accused ([para 25]). Inferences drawn from circumstantial evidence—especially given the presumption against reliance on the testimony of a single, contradictory eyewitness—further eroded the basis on which the high court rendered its judgment ([para 30]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 32])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal, finding that the evidence presented could not adequately support Mabaso's conviction for murder. It asserted that the high court had misdirected itself in accepting inadmissible evidence against the appellant, failing to safeguard the integrity of the trial process ([para 32]).

The ratio decidendi of the case underscores a vital aspect of criminal law—the admissibility of evidence, particularly confessional evidence involving co-accused, and the necessity for consistency and reliability in witness testimonies to ensure that an accused's fundamental right to a fair trial is maintained. The implications of this ruling resonate through the judicial system, reinforcing the importance of evidentiary standards and safeguarding against wrongful convictions in criminal proceedings. The court's decision serves as a reminder of the judicial principles that uphold the integrity of the legal process, ensuring that convictions are firmly rooted in reliable and admissible evidence. 

This case not only vindicated Mabaso but also highlighted the adjudicative responsibility of courts to critically engage with the evidentiary fabric woven through criminal trials, ensuring justice is served in accordance with the law.
