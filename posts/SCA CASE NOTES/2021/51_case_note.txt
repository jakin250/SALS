# FIRAC Case Note

### Case Title: Fidelity Security Services (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Police and Others [2021] ZASCA 51 (22 April 2021) 

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Coram:** Petse AP, Molemela and Schippers JJA, Eksteen and Poyo-Dlwati AJJA  
**Case No:** 1349/2019  
**Heard:** 11 March 2021  
**Delivered:** 22 April 2021  

---

## F – Facts

[1] The Firearms Control Act 60 of 2000 (the Act) aims to enhance public safety by regulating the possession of firearms while acknowledging the need for effective firearm management ([para 1]). 

[2] In **Minister of Safety and Security v South African Hunters and Game Conservation Association** [2018] ZACC 14, the Constitutional Court highlighted that gun ownership in South Africa is a regulated privilege, not a fundamental right ([para 2]). 

[3] The appellant, Fidelity Security Services (Pty) Ltd, initiated an application against the respondents in May 2016, seeking extensive relief related to the lawful possession of firearms following a directive from the National Commissioner ([paras 3–5]). 

[4] Fidelity, a prominent security service provider, was licensed to possess approximately 8,500 firearms. Following the departure of its responsible person, it discovered that 700 firearm licenses had not been renewed and thus terminated by law ([paras 5–6]). 

[5] Fidelity attempted to renew these licenses on 18 April 2016, but the Designated Firearm Officer refused to accept the late applications based on a circular issued by the National Commissioner ([para 10]).

[6] Unable to resolve the issue through correspondence, Fidelity filed its application for relief in November 2016. The grounds included challenges to the constitutionality of sections 24 and 28 of the Act, and requests for permission to submit late renewal applications and to apply for new firearms licenses ([paras 12–14]).

---

## I – Issues

[7] The principal issue in this appeal revolves around whether Fidelity, whose firearm licenses had terminated, could apply for new licenses under the Act. Specifically, the Court had to determine if a party whose license expired automatically is precluded from seeking a new license ([para 30]). 

[8] It was additionally pertinent to assess whether the provisions of sections 24 and 28 of the Act could be construed to allow for applications for new licenses by former license holders, contrasting with the respondents' claim that such applications were effectively renewals ([para 30]).

---

## R – Rules

[9] The relevant sections of the Firearms Control Act include: 

- **Section 3:** General prohibition on possession of firearms without a license ([para 6]).
- **Section 24:** Outlines the procedure for renewal of firearm licenses ([para 7]).
- **Section 28:** Details circumstances under which firearm licenses terminate ([para 8]).
- **Section 149:** Addresses the ownership and destruction of firearms ([para 34]).

[10] In interpreting the Act, the Court relied on established principles of statutory interpretation, emphasizing the need to consider the language, context, and purpose of the statute, as per **Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality** [2012] ZASCA 13 ([paras 23–25]).

[11] The Court also referenced **Endumeni**, illustrating the importance of sensible interpretations that prevent business unworkability ([para 34]).

---

## A – Application

[12] The Supreme Court of Appeal critically evaluated the arguments surrounding Fidelity’s right to apply for new licenses. The respondents’ assertion that Fidelity's applications were in essence renewals was rejected, as the relief sought specifically pertained to new applications, thereby differentiating it from the notion of renewals ([para 31]).

[13] The Court highlighted that there was no statutory prohibition against a former license holder applying for a new license. Interpreting the Act to prevent former license holders from applying for new licenses would undermine the basic structure of firearm regulation and ownership rights ([paras 32–34]).

[14] Fidelity's argument that the circular prohibiting the acceptance of late applications was not consistent with the statutory framework was compellingly articulated. The Court underscored that reliance on such directives could lead to unlawful possession scenarios contrary to the overall legislative aim ([para 30]).

[15] The interpretation of the Act as affording the right to apply afresh aligns with the fundamental purpose of firearm management and public safety objectives as outlined in the Act's preamble ([para 1]).

---

## C – Conclusion

[16] The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Fidelity’s appeal, allowing it to apply afresh for licenses to possess firearms despite prior licenses having expired. The Court determined that the refusal of the police to accept such applications constituted a misinterpretation of the Act ([para 6.1]). 

[17] The decision signifies a reaffirmation of the rights of juristic entities and the need for law enforcement to align with the statutory framework governing firearm possession ([para 6.1]). 

[18] This judgment elucidates the applicability of the Act, affirming that the process for licensure is inclusive, thus ensuring that entities like Fidelity can continue to operate within the legal framework without infringing on public safety measures.

[19] Regarding costs, Fidelity was ordered to cover costs associated with the reinstatement of the appeal and the hearing, while the respondents were directed to bear costs related to the appeal hearings ([paras 44-45]).
