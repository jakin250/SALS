Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note: Greater Tzaneen Municipality v Bravospan 252 CC [2024] ZACC 20

## F - Facts
The case presented before the Constitutional Court arose from a contractual dispute between the Greater Tzaneen Municipality ("the Municipality") and Bravospan 252 CC ("Bravospan"). The Municipality sought to extend an existing contract for security services without a lawful tender process, contrary to its own procurement policies and statutory obligations ([1]–[2]). 

The initial tender for security services was awarded to Bravospan in 2013, with a contract value of R2,757,666.60 for 12 months ([4]–[5]). As the initial contract approached its termination, Bravospan negotiated a 24-month extension, under which both parties agreed to provide additional services for a revised contract value of R9,624,000 ([8]). Despite the extension being declared invalid by the High Court on grounds of unlawful procurement, which was upheld through subsequent appeals up to the Supreme Court of Appeal, the Municipality failed to compensate Bravospan for services rendered during the contract period ([15], [22]).

Bravospan initiated separate proceedings to recover R9,624,000, basing its claim on the principles of unjustified enrichment, delict, fraudulent misrepresentation, and constitutional damages. The High Court ruled in favour of Bravospan, awarding compensation on unjustified enrichment grounds ([19]). However, the Supreme Court of Appeal shifted the legal foundation for compensation to a "just and equitable" remedy under section 172(1)(b) of the Constitution ([22]). The Municipality then sought leave to appeal this order before the Constitutional Court.

## I - Issues
The key legal issues in the case were:
1. Whether a court that later declares a contract invalid can grant compensation under section 172(1)(b) of the Constitution, despite the absence of a separate claim in those proceedings.
2. Whether the Municipality’s actions amounted to unconscionable conduct by seeking to exploit its own unlawful actions to avoid paying Bravospan for services rendered ([25], [55]).

## R - Rules
The relevant legal principles are primarily found in 
- Section 172(1) of the Constitution, which empowers courts to declare laws invalid and make any order that is just and equitable ([3]).
- The common law principles of unjustified enrichment, which suggest a party may not benefit at the expense of another without just cause ([20]–[22]).
- The requirements for lawful procurement under sections 217(1) and 111 and 112 of the Local Government: Municipal Finance Management Act ([11]).

## A - Application
The Constitutional Court, affirming the majority judgment of the Supreme Court of Appeal, rejected the Municipality's appeal, emphasizing that, while the extension agreement was invalid, it was unjust for Bravospan to receive no compensation for services provided during the period of the contract ([48]). The Court refrained from delving into an extensive analysis of unjustified enrichment, underscoring the ethical implications and long-standing conduct of municipalities seeking to benefit from services while avoiding payment through claims of unlawful procurement ([50]–[52]).

This case highlighted the significance of ethical governance, emphasizing the obligation of state actors to adhere to principles of honesty and good faith, particularly towards innocent contractors ([58]). The Court further supported the need to uphold public trust in governmental institutions by holding the Municipality accountable for its actions ([61]).

## C - Conclusion
The court ultimately refused the Municipality's application for leave to appeal, thereby affirming the Supreme Court of Appeal's ruling that Bravospan was entitled to just and equitable compensation for services rendered ([52]). This decision emphasized the Court's commitment to uphold justice and prevent the exploitation of contractors by state organs, sending a strong message about the ethical expectations of public bodies ([65]). Moreover, the case situates itself within a broader context of accountability, urging that public entities must duly pay for services rendered, irrespective of procedural missteps, thus enhancing community trust in governance ([48], [56]).

The ruling establishes a critical precedent reinforcing the necessity for ethical compliance in public procurement processes, supporting the ongoing conversation about the relationship between constitutional norms and ordinary principles of law ([47], [50]).
