Supreme Court of Appeal

City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality v Mathabathe & Another (502/12) [2013] ZASCA 60

Upon granting a special power of attorney to manage the sales process, Nedbank instructed attorney Y.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 60

F – Facts

The dispute arises from the interpretation of provisions in the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 (the Act), specifically section 118. The first respondent, Mr. Thomas Mathabathe, was the owner of Erf 1080 Kosmosdal Ext 17 and appointed the second respondent, Nedbank Limited, as the mortgagee to sell the property by auction ([paras 1–2]). The property was auctioned off on 11 December 2010 for R1.3 million and the sale was approved by Mr. Mathabathe on 13 December 2010.

Upon granting a special power of attorney to manage the sales process, Nedbank instructed attorney Y. Viviers to apply for the necessary clearance certificate from the appellant, City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality (the Municipality). The Municipality indicated debts amounting to R162,722.26 were owed, which included a "historical debt" of R151,324.22 relating to charges prior to the two-year period required for clearance certificates ([paras 3–4]).

Mr. Mathabathe and Nedbank sought relief in the North Gauteng High Court to compel the Municipality to issue a clearance certificate limited to debts incurred within two years preceding the application, as stipulated in section 118 of the Act ([para 4]). The Municipality opposed the application and filed a counter-application seeking to enforce payment of the historical debt before issuing the clearance certificate ([paras 5–6]). The High Court granted the respondents’ application and dismissed the Municipality’s counter-application, leading the Municipality to appeal on limited grounds regarding the latter ([para 6]).

I – Issues

The central legal issue revolves around whether the Municipality could require an undertaking for the payment of historical debts before issuing a clearance certificate mandated by section 118(1) of the Act. More specifically, it involves interpreting the rights and obligations under both subsection (1) and (3) of section 118 in the context of prior debts owed and the legal capacity of the Municipality to require additional security or undertakings ([paras 1, 7]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal framework centers on the provisions of the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000, particularly sections 118(1) and (3).

  • Section 118(1) stipulates that no transfer of property can occur without a clearance certificate indicating that all debts due in the preceding two years have been paid.
  • Section 118(3) asserts that amounts owed for municipal services and other charges create a charge upon the property that has priority over any mortgage bonds ([paras 1–2, 9]).

The case further references previous judgments, such as City of Cape Town v Real People Housing (Pty) Ltd and Kaplan NO, elucidating the nature of security provisions and their implications for municipal debts ([paras 9–11]).

A – Application

The court analyzed the distinct roles of sections 118(1) as an embargo provision and section 118(3) as a security provision ([para 12]). The Municipality’s argument conflated the two provisions, mistakenly asserting that their entitlement to historical debt payments was contingent upon the sale of the property, which misrepresented the nature of the charge created by section 118(3) ([para 12]).

The court reiterated that section 118(3) ensures municipalities have a lien on property for all municipal debts owed, regardless of the time of accrual, which grants the municipality preferred status over existing mortgage bonds upon sale ([paras 10–11]). The court concluded that having granted a clearance certificate under section 118(1), the Municipality could not attach conditions regarding historical debts that were not covered by that clause’s time limitation. Thus, the Municipality’s position violated the legislative intent of the Act ([para 12]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the Municipality's appeal, concluding that it had misconstrued the implications of section 118(3) of the Act in its interactions with the first respondent and Nedbank. The ratio decidendi emphasized that historical debts do not negate the right to a clearance certificate upon payment of up-to-date municipal debts as required by law. The judgment reinforces the principle that municipalities cannot unilaterally impose conditions outside the statutory framework, underpinning the protection of property rights and due processes in a real estate transaction ([paras 12–13]).

The implications of this case highlight the need for clarity and adherence to established legal frameworks concerning municipal properties and debts, ensuring that municipalities properly align their practices with statutory imperatives without overstepping their authority. The appeal's dismissal, with costs awarded to the respondents, reflects a clear judicial stance on legal interpretations which affect local governance and property law in South Africa ([para 13]).
