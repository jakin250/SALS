23 Case Note

Case Title: Permanent Secretary of the Department of Education, Eastern Cape and Member of the Executive Council for Education, Eastern Cape v. Ed-U-College (P.E.) (Section 21) Inc. Case No.: CCT 26/2000 Heard on: 14 September 2000 Decided on: 29 November 2000

Constitutional Court 2000 CCT 26/2000

Case Title:

Permanent Secretary of the Department of Education, Eastern Cape and Member of the Executive Council for Education, Eastern Cape v. Ed-U-College (P.E.) (Section 21) Inc.
Case No.: CCT 26/2000
Heard on: 14 September 2000
Decided on: 29 November 2000

F – Facts

The case revolves around the payment of subsidies made by the Eastern Cape Department of Education to independent schools, specifically Ed-U-College, a school established in 1995 that draws students primarily from impoverished backgrounds ([1]). Between 1995 and 1996, the Department paid subsidies calculated at R1,560 for each learner in grades 1-7 and R2,340 for those in grades 8-12. However, starting from 1 April 1997, these amounts were reduced to R700 and R1,000 respectively for the corresponding grades ([2]).

Ed-U-College initiated legal proceedings in the High Court in July 1998, claiming that outstanding subsidies for 1996 had not been paid in full, and that the subsidy reduction implemented in 1997 was unlawful ([3]). The total claim amounted to R1,252,706.50, which included interest and costs. The applicants contended that there was no shortfall in payments and asserted that the budgetary allocation by the Eastern Cape Legislature permitted the subsidy reductions ([4]).

The High Court, presided by Leach J, was asked to determine specific questions: firstly, whether the budget for independent schools was a legislative matter; and secondly, whether the court had jurisdiction to adjudicate on allocations to these schools during the financial year ([5]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues in this case are:

  1. Whether the reduction of subsidies to independent schools constituted a justiciable issue that fell within the remit of the court given the budgetary powers of the Eastern Cape Legislature ([6]).
  2. Whether the actions regarding subsidy allocations could be classified as administrative action as defined under section 33 of the Constitution, thus permitting judicial review ([6][10]).

R – Rules

The legal rules governing the case stem from several constitutional provisions and previous case law. Importantly, section 48(2) of the South African Schools Act confers discretionary powers upon the Member of the Executive Council (MEC) regarding subsidies to independent schools from funds appropriated by the provincial legislature ([6][20]).

The judgment heavily relied on Fedsure Life Assurance Ltd and Others v Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council and Others, which clarified the distinction between legislative and administrative actions under the Constitution ([11]). This precedent emphasized that while appropriation acts are legislative, the formulation of specific administrative actions regarding subsidy determinations is reviewable under the administrative justice provisions of the Constitution ([12]).

A – Application

Leach J ruled that the determination of the subsidy payments was not a legislative action, but instead constituted administrative action, hence justiciable in the courts ([6][16]). This determination was based on analyzing the nature of the power exercised, the budgetary constraints set by the legislature, and the lack of legislative debate over the subsidy formula by the MEC ([17][20]).

O’Regan J further expounded that the MEC's decision to establish the subsidy formula did not follow strict legislative procedures but adapted according to the financial landscape dictated by legislative appropriations ([21]). The case turned on whether the exercise of power related to administrative actions, necessitating adherence to procedural fairness as mandated by section 33 of the Constitution. The argument that the subsidy allocation represented a policy decision shielded against review was dismissed, reinforcing that even political decisions could fall under judicial scrutiny if they manifest as administrative actions ([23]).

C – Conclusion

The court concluded that the applicants’ objection to the legitimacy of the subsidy allocation process, framed as a policy decision, was erroneous. The court dismissed the application for leave to appeal, emphasizing that the applicants had no reasonable prospect of success ([25]). Consequently, the initial order of the High Court was upheld, with the applicants ordered to pay costs, including the fees for two counsel.

This case underscores the court's role in providing oversight on administrative actions in the face of legislative budgetary decisions, affirming the principle that the exercise of public power must remain accountable and within constitutional boundaries. Additionally, it affirms the necessity for decision-makers to act transparently and equitably, especially when public resources are at stake.
