Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Notes

**Case Title:** Bouttell v Road Accident Fund (324/2017) [2018] ZASCA 90 (31 May 2018)

**Coram:** Navsa, Majiedt, Mbha JJA, Plasket, and Hughes AJJA

---

## F – Facts

The appellant, Mr. Leon St Leger Bouttell, is a successful electrical engineer who, as of March 2006, operated two businesses: Exel Electrics & Instrumentation and Le Roux, Bouttell & Associates. He reported an annual income of approximately R4 million from which he voluntarily contributed 15% towards a retirement annuity fund ([1]).

On 25 July 2012, Mr. Bouttell was involved in a motor vehicle accident which resulted in bodily injuries. He subsequently initiated a claim against the Road Accident Fund (the RAF), a statutory body created by the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 to govern compensation claims. The RAF conceded liability for the accident, but only the quantum for future loss of earnings remained in dispute following a settlement on other heads of damages ([2]).

The central issue in this appeal arose from the High Court's ruling which distinguished between contributions made to an employer pension fund and those to a retirement annuity fund regarding claims for loss of earnings. The High Court upheld the RAF’s stance that contributions to a retirement annuity fund should not be factored into calculations for loss of earnings, leading to Mr. Bouttell’s contention of discrimination and violation of his constitutional rights ([2]-[6]).

## I – Issues

1. **Legal Distinction:** Whether the contributions to a retirement annuity fund can be claimed as loss of earnings in comparison to contributions made to an employer pension fund ([2]).
   
2. **Constitutional Rights:** Whether the conclusion of the High Court violates Mr. Bouttell's rights under Sections 1(a), 7(1), and 9 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa concerning equality and non-discrimination ([6],[12]).

## R – Rules

1. **Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996:** The RAF Act mandates compensation for damages incurred due to negligence resulting from motor vehicle accidents. This includes a statutory cap on claims for loss of earnings as per Section 17(4), which limits annual loss calculations ([3]-[4]).

2. **Judicial Precedents:** 
   - **Dippenaar v Shield Insurance Co Ltd:** This case outlined the treatment of salary and benefits in determining loss of earnings. The court reiterated that compulsory contributions by an employer should be included when assessing earnings but drew a clear line against voluntary contributions to a retirement annuity fund ([9]-[10]).
   - **Harksen v Lane NO & others:** Established that discrimination pertains to unequal treatment based on inherent characteristics or attributes, and the distinction created by voluntary contributions cannot be deemed discriminatory under this framework ([12]).

3. **Equality Framework:** Currie and De Waal's commentary on equality in the Bill of Rights emphasizes that similar situations should indeed result in similar treatment, though this case did not find Mr. Bouttell and a pension fund beneficiary to be similarly situated ([13]).

## A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the High Court correctly distinguished between a retirement annuity fund and a pension fund. The contributions made to the latter are part of an employee's contractual earnings, thus can be included as loss of earnings claims. Conversely, contributions to a retirement annuity fund are viewed as investments made voluntarily and do not constitute an employment benefit. The court ruled that the nature of these contributions dictates their treatment under the RAF Act and that the legal distinction drawn is sound and fair ([8]-[11]).

Moreover, the appellant's argument of discrimination, based on an alleged unfair treatment relative to employees with pension fund contributions, was dismissed. The court elucidated that the differentiation was not based on inherent characteristics but rather on the voluntary nature of the annuity contributions—a crucial distinction in determining the applicability of the equality clause. Therefore, no violation of constitutional rights occurred since the appellant's circumstances were not analogous to those of individuals who derive benefits from employer contributions ([12]-[13]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Mr. Bouttell's appeal with costs, agreeing with the High Court's reasoning that contributions to retirement annuity funds are not compensable as loss of earnings. This decision affirms the legal distinction between pension funds and voluntary retirement savings, reinforcing the principle that the law categorically treats these two financial instruments differently. The implications of this judgment serve to uphold statutory frameworks established by the RAF Act while simultaneously clarifying the constitutional boundaries concerning claims of discrimination and equality before the law ([14]-[15]). 

The dismissal signals a clear interpretation of "earnings" within the context of the RAF framework and hints at the importance of awareness among claimants regarding the nature of their financial undertakings in relation to workplace benefits.
