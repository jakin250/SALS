Supreme Court of Appeal

De Jager, A M v The State [2006] SCA 114 (RSA)

Upon conviction, the Regional Court sentenced the appellant to eight years' imprisonment for each count, which were ordered to run partially concurrently, resulting effectively in a ten-year sentence ([para 1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2006

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The appellant, Anthony Morné De Jager, was convicted in the Mpumalanga Regional Court of two counts of attempted rape against a minor complainant. At the time of the trial in November 2001, the complainant was nine years old; the alleged offenses reportedly occurred when she was aged seven and eight ([para 2]). The actions of the appellant included exposing the complainant and her friend to explicit materials, sexual instruction, and attempted sexual acts. Despite being married to the complainant's mother, the appellant's conduct raised serious concerns ([paras 2-4]).

Upon conviction, the Regional Court sentenced the appellant to eight years' imprisonment for each count, which were ordered to run partially concurrently, resulting effectively in a ten-year sentence ([para 1]). The appellant's initial appeal to the Transvaal Provincial Division was unsuccessful; however, he was granted leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 1]).

I – Issues

The primary legal question revolved around whether the evidence presented was sufficient to support the convictions for attempted rape. This included considerations of the credibility of the complainant and the admissibility of witness testimony, particularly the testimony of the complainant's mother and friend ([paras 10-12]). The case also raised issues pertaining to the reliability of child witness testimonies and the standards required to convict based on such evidence.

R – Rules

The court relied upon established legal principles regarding the assessment of witness credibility, particularly concerning child witnesses. Relevant case law establishes that evidence given by children should be evaluated with caution but does not preclude conviction on the basis of such testimony, provided it is deemed credible ([para 8]). The court also referenced the necessity for corroborating evidence in sexual offense cases, and while such evidence was desirable, it was not an absolute requirement for a conviction ([paras 9-10]).

A – Application

The SCA upheld the Regional Court's finding on the credibility of the complainant, stating that her testimony was reliable and consistent throughout the trial. The court noted that the complainant's developmental level suggested she was capable of providing truthful evidence and that discrepancies in witness statements were to be expected given their ages and circumstances ([para 10]).

The evidence from the complainant's friend lent additional support to her testimony, reinforcing the likelihood that the appellant had attempted sexual acts as described ([paras 8-12]). The presence of corroborating circumstances—such as the appellant's admission of prior inappropriate conduct towards the complainant—was considered a significant factor in affirming the convictions ([para 11]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appellant had been appropriately convicted of attempted rape beyond a reasonable doubt. The court found no substantial basis to interfere with the Regional Court’s assessments of credibility and the findings of fact. Consequently, the appeal was dismissed, affirming the ten-year sentence imposed by the lower court, which underscored the seriousness of the offenses and the need for strict scrutiny of sexual offenses against minors ([para 13]).

Implications

This case further establishes the principles governing the evaluation of child testimony and the strength of such evidence in sexual offense cases. It underscores that while child witness testimony requires careful consideration, it can support a conviction when the court finds it credible and corroborated by additional evidence.
