# Case Note: M and Another v MEC for Health, Western Cape (1258/2018) [2020] ZASCA 89  

## FIRAC Case Note  

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–3])  
On 23 August 2011, Mr. M brought his six-year-old son, J, to the Trauma Unit at Red Cross Memorial Hospital after J sustained a head injury. Upon examination, Dr. Horn deemed J’s injury a minor bump and discharged him. Later that night, J showed signs of distress and was taken back to the hospital, where a CT scan revealed an extradural haematoma leading to severe brain injuries, resulting in cerebral palsy and spastic quadriplegia. 

Subsequently, Mr. and Mrs. M initiated a negligence claim against the Member of the Executive Committee (MEC) for Health in the Western Cape, alleging that Dr. Horn had mismanaged J’s treatment by not conducting adequate assessments or providing sufficient discharge instructions. The trial court dismissed their claims, leading to the current appeal ([para 2]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 3–10])  
The primary issue in this appeal centered on whether Dr. Horn was negligent in her treatment of J. This encompasses two sub-issues: (1) whether Dr. Horn’s examination should have indicated the necessity for a CT scan, and (2) whether her decisions regarding discharge and post-treatment instructions were reflective of standard medical practice.

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 10–12])  
The court referenced established legal principles in determining negligence, notably the standard of care expected from medical practitioners. In medical negligence claims, a healthcare provider's conduct is deemed negligent if it falls below the standard of care that would be expected from a reasonably competent practitioner in similar circumstances ([para 10]). Also, precedents such as *Michael v Linksfield Park Clinic (Pty) Ltd* and *Medi-Clinic v Vermeulen* affirm that a practitioner is only liable if their actions deviate from generally accepted medical practices ([para 11]). The court, reiterating the trite principle from *Dhlumayo* ([para 8]), emphasized that appellate courts are typically reluctant to overturn a trial court’s factual findings unless they are demonstrably erroneous.

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 15–107])  
Regarding negligence, the court analyzed the conflicting evidence surrounding the nature of J's head swelling—was it firm or boggy? Dr. Horn’s testimony that she had palpated the bump and found it firm was given weight, while that of Dr. Edeling proposing it must have been boggy was treated as a reconstruction based on subsequent findings ([para 15–50]). The trial court had more credibility in assessing live witnesses and interactions, and therefore its findings were preferentially upheld.

The court also addressed the assertion that Dr. Horn should have kept J for further observation post-examination. The judge noted that, regardless of potential observations made post-discharge, the decision must align with sound clinical judgments pertaining to previous assessments. Given the absence of significant tumult to J’s condition as observed by Dr. Horn, the court concluded that her decisions aligned with accepted practices and guidelines ([para 60–73]).

Concerning discharge instructions, the court highlighted a critical dispute: did Dr. Horn provide adequate advice to J's father regarding monitoring symptoms? The instructions J's father received were deemed inadequate in that they failed to include explicit warnings regarding monitoring for drowsiness—an apparent risk factor in head injuries ([para 83–94]). Dr. Horn’s lack of specific recall when cross-examined did not necessarily undermine her conclusions about monitoring but raised questions regarding the clarity of her instructions during discharge ([para 84]).

The arguments presented by the appellants were ultimately assessed against the tragedy of the case. Despite the empathetic nature of the outcome, the judgment underscored that sympathy alone cannot substantiate legal liability when the medical protocols followed were deemed reasonable ([para 107]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([para 108])  
The court dismissed the appeal, stating that Dr. Horn’s examination and subsequent discharge procedures met the acceptable standards of care. The conclusions drawn by the trial court were not found to be in error given its assessment of the evidence, thus reaffirming the standard that medical professionals are not liable for adverse patient outcomes absent clear negligence that falls below accepted practice standards. The judgment emphasized the critical nature of procedural adherence in clinical settings without equating empathy with legal culpability ([para 108]).
