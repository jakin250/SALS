KET Civils CC v The Member of the Executive Committee: Police, Roads & Transport, Free State and Others

In November 2020, the Department notified KET that the Auditor General had discovered irregularities in the panel’s constitution, prompting the Department to seek KET's consent to terminate the contracts ([7]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 KET Civils CC v The Member of the Executive Committee: Police, Roads & Transport, Free State and Others (497/2022 & 820/2022) [2024] ZASCA 56

FIRAC Method Case Note

Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Neutral Citation: KET Civils CC v The Member of the Executive Committee: Police, Roads & Transport, Free State and Others (497/2022 & 820/2022) [2024] ZASCA 56
Judgment Delivered: 19 April 2024
Coram: Mocumie ADP, Zondi and Nicholls JJA
Heard: 15 February 2024

F – Facts

The case arose from an application by KET Civils CC (KET) under section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, seeking the reconsideration of a prior order that dismissed its petition for leave to appeal ([1]). KET, a close corporation involved in road construction, was appointed as part of a contractor panel constituted by the Free State Department of Police, Roads and Transport for various road maintenance projects ([2]).

In November 2020, the Department notified KET that the Auditor General had discovered irregularities in the panel’s constitution, prompting the Department to seek KET's consent to terminate the contracts ([7]). KET ultimately consented to terminate its contracts after alleging a breach by the Department ([8]).

KET filed a two-part application with the high court; however, its application was struck from the roll for lack of urgency, and a counter-application by the Department was settled with the contractors ([9]). Contrarily, KET claimed it was not aware that a settlement had been reached.

Two orders were issued by the high court on 29 April 2021: the first set aside the appointments in the panel, while the second amended the content of the first. KET contended that there was confusion regarding the orders made ([13], [16]). Ultimately, KET sought leave to appeal after the lower court dismissed its application for condonation due to delays ([14]).

I – Issues

The primary legal question concerned whether the high court's dismissal of KET's application for leave to appeal and the subsequent orders were made in violation of principles stipulating that consent of all affected parties is necessary for court-sanctioned settlement agreements, especially given that KET was not a party to the settlement between contractors and the Department ([19]).

Additionally, the question of whether exceptional circumstances existed under section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act that would warrant reconsideration of the court's prior dismissal ([5]) was crucial.

R – Rules

  1. Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, Section 17(2)(f): Allows for reconsideration of decisions by the Supreme Court of Appeal in exceptional circumstances ([5]).
  2. Constitution of South Africa, Section 172: Addresses the court's power to declare actions inconsistent with the Constitution as invalid, mandating a fair process ([16]).
  3. Principles from Case Law:
  • Airports Company South Africa v Big Five Duty Free (Pty) Ltd: Establishes the need for careful scrutiny of settlement agreements before being made judicial orders ([18]).
  • Buffalo City Metropolitan Municipality v Asla Construction (Pty) Ltd: Reinforces that a judicial order alters parties’ rights and must reflect their agreement ([20]).

A – Application

The court applied the principles from the Constitutional Court regarding the necessity of hearing all parties before making a settlement order. It determined that the high court had erred by not ensuring the consent of KET was obtained before granting the counter-application between the Department and the contractors, which directly affected KET’s rights ([19]).

The court evaluated whether exceptional circumstances existed warranting a reconsideration of the previous decision. It found that the confusion surrounding the multiple orders, coupled with the lack of proper hearing for KET's interests, constituted exceptional circumstances ([21]). The decision to grant leave to appeal was thus rooted in an understanding of the need for justice and the preservation of KET's rights.

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately set aside the high court's prior orders. It held that the contracts entered into by KET could not be invalidated without KET’s consent, thus declaring the conduct of the first respondent inconsistent with both the Constitution and the principles of fairness and justice outlined within it ([22]). The court’s decision solidifies the importance of ensuring all affected parties are heard in judicial processes, especially in cases involving settlement agreements.

Moreover, the ruling highlighted the constitutional principle of judicial oversight in the administrative processes within governmental departments ([22]). KET was awarded costs for the successful appeal, while also bearing some costs post an identified date due to the shared confusion among the parties involved ([23]).

This decision serves as a noteworthy precedent concerning administrative legality and judicial fairness, reinforcing the tenets that guide public procurement and contractual relationships in South Africa's constitutional framework.
