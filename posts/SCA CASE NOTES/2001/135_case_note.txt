# Case Note: The Road Accident Fund v Magdelene Caroline Sauls (2001) 

**Citation**: The Road Accident Fund v Magdelene Caroline Sauls [2001] 4 All SA 74 (SCA)

## F - Facts 

On March 18, 1994, Stephen Sauls ("Sauls") and his fiancée Magdelene Sauls (née Jackson) ("the plaintiff") were shopping in Cape Town. As they returned to their parked BMW, Sauls noticed a truck (the "insured vehicle") driven by Sadick approaching. Recognizing the risk, he refrained from entering the driver’s seat immediately and leaned against the car to wait for the truck to pass. Despite this, the truck struck Sauls, throwing him to the ground and rendering him concussed ([1]).

The plaintiff witnessed the incident, believing Sauls was severely injured or perhaps even killed, which led her into a state of shock. After Sauls was transported to the hospital, it was determined that he had suffered only minor injuries while the plaintiff began to experience significant psychological distress, reporting nightmares and the onset of chronic post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD) ([2]). 

She later initiated legal proceedings against the Road Accident Fund ("the appellant"), seeking damages for her medical expenses, loss of earnings, and general damages due to permanent disablement and loss of enjoyment in life ([3]).

The trial revolved around determining the merits of the case, after which the court established that the collision resulted from Sadick's negligence, causing injury to Sauls and secondary trauma to the plaintiff. As such, it was further agreed that the plaintiff suffered psychological trauma due to her witnessing the incident, thus allowing her claim to proceed ([4]–[5]).

## I - Issues 

The primary legal questions raised in this case included:

1. Whether the plaintiff could recover damages for psychiatric injury suffered as a result of witnessing the negligent act that caused physical injury to her fiancé.
2. Whether the relationship between the plaintiff and Sauls conferred standing for such a claim, considering the absence of a legal marriage at the time of the incident ([6]).
3. Whether the injuries claimed by the plaintiff constituted actionable damages, particularly given the distinct and independent nature of the secondary victim's claim ([10]-[11]).

## R - Rules 

The court primarily utilized the principles of negligence as established in *Bester v Commercial Union Versekeringsmaatskappy van SA Bpk* [1973] 1 SA 769 (A). A key consideration is that foreseeability of harm is crucial; that is, the reasonable person must have foreseen the possibility of harm to a third party witnessing an incident ([7]). 

Further, the unpredictability of psychiatric harm following a traumatic event has been recognized as a compensable injury under delict law, which emphasizes the necessity to establish both factual and legal causation ([12]). Here, it was essential for the plaintiff to demonstrate that Sadick’s negligence not only caused harm but that the type of harm sustained (psychiatric injury) was reasonably foreseeable from the negligent act ([12]-[13]).

The court also considered the prevailing legal policies concerning claims of emotional trauma arising from witnessing injury to another, affirming that relationships involving ties of affection, such as that of an engaged couple, may warrant recognition in such claims ([17]).

## A - Application 

The court applied the principles of foreseeability and reliance on the relationship between the parties in assessing whether the plaintiff's claim could succeed. The inquiry focused on whether a reasonable person in Sadick's position should have foreseen that his negligent driving would lead not only to Sauls' injury, but also would result in severe psychological trauma to someone closely related to the victim, namely the plaintiff ([9]-[10]).

In assessing the claim's legal causation, the court reviewed the specificity of the plaintiff’s alleged trauma against the backdrop of established case law. The court noted that while Sauls suffered only minor injuries, the profound psychological impact on the plaintiff was substantiated by medical evidence. The trauma she experienced was not only due to the impact itself but was compounded by her proximity and emotional connection to Sauls, which was significant ([6], [10]).

In addressing the appellant's concerns about potential floodgates of similar claims arising, the court took into consideration the necessity to evaluate the nature of the relationship between the primary and secondary victims carefully, ensuring that compensable claims were limited to those with sufficiently close ties of affection ([16]-[17]).

## C - Conclusion 

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately ruled in favor of the plaintiff, concluding that her claim for damages due to psychological trauma was actionable. The court affirmed that the relationship between the plaintiff and Sauls—despite their non-marital status—established sufficient grounds for foreseeability and causation in the context of the claim for psychiatric harm ([20]). 

The appeal was dismissed with costs, reinforcing the acceptance of claims for psychological harm in contextually appropriate relational circumstances. This decision underlines the legal recognition of emotional trauma and builds upon established precedents while maintaining a careful approach to the expansion of liability in negligent claims ([20]). This case thereby enhances the understanding of secondary victim claims within the realm of South African delictual law.
