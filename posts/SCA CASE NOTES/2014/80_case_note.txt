# FIRAC Case Note: The Body Corporate of “The Avenues” v Barney Hurwitz (2014) [2014] ZASCA 80 

## Facts
The Supreme Court of Appeal case The Body Corporate of “The Avenues” v Barney Hurwitz (2014) involves a legal dispute between two parties concerning rights of extension in a sectional title scheme ([1]). The appellant, the Body Corporate of the “Avenues” Scheme No SS120/1987, contended that they held the right to extend the scheme under Section 25 of the Sectional Titles Act No 95 of 1986 ([2]). The respondents—Barney Hurwitz, as trustee of the Hurwitz-Smilg Sea Point Trust, and Lawrence Miller, as trustee of the High Level Trust—claimed that the right was conferred to them through Rule 77 of the scheme ([1]).

The Body Corporate initiated proceedings in the Western Cape High Court, seeking a declaration that their right to extend the scheme was valid and that the developers' consent was not necessary ([3]). The court a quo dismissed this application, affirming that the developers possessed the sole rights of extension based on Rule 77 ([5]). The appeal was granted with costs ([6]).

## Issues
The central issues examined by the court were as follows:
1. Whether the Body Corporate held the right of extension of the sectional title scheme under Section 25 of the Sectional Titles Act 95 of 1986 after the repeal of the Sectional Titles Act 66 of 1971.
2. The implications of Rule 77’s validity and its conflict with statutory provisions, specifically regarding the powers conferred under the 1971 Act ([5],[30]).

## Rules
The relevant legal frameworks involved included:
- **Section 25(1) of the Sectional Titles Act 95 of 1986** indicates that a developer must reserve the right to extend the scheme at the time of registering the sectional plan ([22]).
- **Section 18(1) of the Sectional Titles Act 66 of 1971** provides similar conditions, wherein the developer could extend the scheme if part of the common property and with consent from all owners and mortgagees ([18]).
- **Rule 77 of the scheme**, adopted by the Body Corporate, was intended to confer rights of extension to the developers, but was contested based on whether it was ultra vires the statutory powers available to the Body Corporate ([11]).

## Application
The court conducted a thorough examination of the historical and legal context surrounding the sectional title scheme. The historical development indicated that while the developers claimed ownership of the right based on Rule 77, the court emphasized that the validity of Rule 77 was contingent upon adherence to the 1971 Act ([6],[26]).

The court found that Rule 77 was not enforceable as it conferred powers beyond those stipulated in the Act ([30]). Notably, it emphasized that the Body Corporate could not lawfully grant rights that conflicted with the statutory provision requiring unanimous consent from all section owners for any extension ([28]).

The analysis concluded that since the developers had not acquired any rights of extension, the earlier decision by the court a quo was erroneous. The developers' contravention of the local authority’s condition further solidified the Body Corporate's claim, nullifying the developers' authority to withhold consent ([35]).

## Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal subsequently overturned the earlier ruling from the High Court. It declared that no right of extension in respect of the sectional title scheme vested in the developers and affirmed that all rights remained with the Body Corporate under Section 25(6) of the 1986 Act ([37]). Furthermore, the court ruled that the developers had to divest ownership of certain units registered in their names, in accordance with the imposed conditions. Consequently, the Body Corporate was entitled to remedy for unlawful retention of those units ([37]).

The implications of this decision extend to the interpretation of the statutory framework governing sectional titles, highlighting the necessity for compliance with statutory requirements in the delegation of rights by the Body Corporate ([30],[35]). The case underscores the distinction between statutory rights and the contractual agreements that attempt to modify or extend those rights without adherence to legislative mandates.
