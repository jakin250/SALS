Constitutional Court

27 Case Note

M T (CCT 122/17): In 1999, Mr T raped a 10-year-old girl, was convicted, and sentenced to life imprisonment by the High Court after a referral from the Regional Magistrates’ Court ([4]–[6]).

Constitutional Court 2018 CCT 122/17

Case Note

Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa
Citation: M T v The State; A S B v The State; Johannes September v The State 2018 ZACC 27
Coram: Mogoeng CJ, Cachalia AJ, Dlodlo AJ, Froneman J, Goliath AJ, Jafta J, Khampepe J, Madlanga J, Petse AJ, Theron J
Judgment: Dlodlo AJ (unanimous)
Heard on: 10 May 2018
Decided on: 3 September 2018

F – Facts

The cases of M T, A S B, and Johannes September all involve applications for leave to appeal against life sentences imposed under the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 (Minimum Sentences Act) ([1]).

  1. M T (CCT 122/17): In 1999, Mr T raped a 10-year-old girl, was convicted, and sentenced to life imprisonment by the High Court after a referral from the Regional Magistrates’ Court ([4]–[6]). His conviction was later appealed on the grounds of procedural unfairness, specifically the failure to inform him about the Minimum Sentences Act during trial ([1] and [19]).
  2. A S B (CCT 220/17): Mr B was convicted in 2002 for multiple counts of rape involving his underage daughter and sentenced to life imprisonment. His appeals were successively dismissed due to the absence of substantial and compelling circumstances ([13]–[15]).
  3. Johannes September (CCT 298/17): Mr September was part of a violent robbery resulting in murder and was sentenced to life imprisonment. His earlier attempts to appeal were dismissed on procedural grounds ([17]).

The core argument from all applicants was that they were not adequately informed of the Minimum Sentences Act, thereby infringing their rights to a fair trial ([19]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues presented to the Court include:

  1. Whether the state failed to adequately inform the applicants of the applicability of the Minimum Sentences Act during their trials ([2]).
  2. Whether such lack of awareness compromised the fairness of the trials as per Section 35(3) of the Constitution, which ensures the right to a fair trial ([35]).
  3. Whether the High Court had the authority to impose life sentences without explicit mention of the Minimum Sentences Act in the charge sheets ([32]).

R – Rules

The judgment draws on several fundamental legal principles and statutory provisions, including:

  1. Section 51 of the Criminal Law Amendment Act: This section mandates life sentences for certain serious offenses, unless the court finds "substantial and compelling circumstances" to impose a lesser sentence ([3]).
  2. Section 173 of the Constitution: This grants courts the inherent power to protect and regulate their own process, taking into account the interests of justice ([36]).
  3. Section 35(3) of the Constitution: Guarantees the right to a fair trial, including the right to be informed of the charge with sufficient detail to respond ([35]).

The Court also referenced precedents such as S v Legoa and S v Ndlovu regarding the significance of adequately informing defendants of the legal implications of their charges ([38]).

A – Application

In applying these rules, the Court critically evaluated the facts of each case.

  1. M T's Case: The Court found that the necessary referral to the High Court had occurred according to the statute in effect at the time, and despite the applicants asserting lack of awareness of the Minimum Sentences Act, they failed to demonstrate that this ignorance materially affected their defense or would have led to different actions (e.g., pursuing a plea bargain) ([30], [32]).
  2. A S B's Case: The assessment of sentencing was based on individual circumstances and the gravity of the offenses. The Court affirmed the High Court's finding that there were no compelling reasons to deviate from the life sentence ([15]).
  3. Johannes September's Case: Mr September's application was also dismissed as the merits of his appeal did not suggest that he was prejudiced by a lack of information regarding sentencing procedures ([17],[19]).

In addressing the issue of the charge sheet omissions, the Court indicated that compliance with Section 84 of the Criminal Procedure Act was satisfied, and therefore, procedural irregularities were not established to infringe upon the right to a fair trial ([40]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court upheld the dismissals of all three appeals, finding that the courts below had acted within their jurisdiction in sentencing the applicants to life imprisonment. The court confirmed that the failure to notify defendants of the Minimum Sentences Act does not, in itself, incur an infringement of the right to a fair trial unless it can be demonstrated that it prejudicially affected the proceedings ([44]). The judgment reinforces the statutory framework surrounding minimum sentencing while underscoring the necessity for thorough consideration of each case's specific circumstances in relation to fairness claims.

Order:

  1. Condonation granted for all applicants.
  2. Applications for leave to appeal dismissed in all three cases.

This ruling illustrates a continuous commitment to maintaining judicial integrity and ensuring that procedural safeguards align with constitutional mandates.
