Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: Florence v The Government of the RSA [2013] ZASCA 104

### F – Facts

The facts of this case are largely agreed upon, derived from a statement of facts jointly signed by the parties prior to the trial in the Land Claims Court (LCC) ([1]). The appellant, Isabel Joyce Florence, is the widow of Mr. Lionel Florence, who had married, along with his brothers, about the purchase of the property named Sunny Croft in 1957. This acquisition was executed via a written installment sale agreement with the previous owner, Dr. Yeller. However, due to the Group Areas Amendment Act 77 of 1957—a product of racially discriminatory legislation—the property could not be formally transferred into the name of the Florence brothers ([2]).

Eventually, in 1970, the Florence brothers were coerced to cancel the sale agreement and vacate Sunny Croft, facing threats from inspectors acting under the Act. The refund received from Dr. Yeller was not market-related and did not equate to just and equitable compensation for their lost rights ([2]). Mr. Florence initiated a claim under the Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994 (the Restitution Act) in December 1995. Following his death, the appellant succeeded him in pursuing the claim, which was initially for the restoration of the property. However, since Sunny Croft is now privately owned, the claim was limited to compensation for erecting a memorial plaque on the property ([3]).

The LCC possesses broad remedial powers under Section 35 of the Restitution Act, which allows it discretion in deciding compensation-related matters ([4]). The key issues for the court to determine included the amount of compensation due to the Florence family for their dispossession, the adjustment method for the 1970 loss relative to current monetary value, and whether the state should cover the costs of erecting the memorial plaque ([6]).

### I – Issues

The principal legal questions in this case revolve around whether the LCC appropriately exercised its discretion in determining compensation for the Florence family. Specific issues included:
1. Whether the Consumer Price Index (CPI) was an adequate measure for evaluating changes over time in the value of money for compensation purposes ([7]).
2. Whether the state had a legal obligation to pay for the costs associated with erecting the memorial plaque commemorating the Florence family's dispossession ([11]).

### R – Rules

The relevant legal frameworks cited in this case include:
- **Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994**: This Act provides a framework for land restitution in South Africa and bestows extensive remedial powers on the LCC ([4]).
- **Section 33(eC)**: This provision requires consideration of "changes over time in the value of money" when determining compensation for dispossessed land rights ([5]).
- The precedent set in **Farjas (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Agriculture and Land Affairs** establishes that the LCC is entitled to apply the CPI for determining changes in monetary value ([8]). 
- **Hoffmann v South African Airways** highlights that fairness in restitution must consider the impact on all affected parties ([9]).

### A – Application

In determining the compensation due to the Florence family, the LCC chose to utilize the CPI to adjust the historical monetary value of the loss suffered due to dispossession ([9]). This decision was contested by the appellant, who argued for a method based on the potential investment returns had the compensation been made in 1970. However, the court dismissed this argument, noting that the LCC evaluated all the evidence and chose an equitable method for compensation based on the applicable statutory framework. The court confirmed that the application of the CPI was appropriate and aligned with previous rulings ([10]).

Regarding the costs associated with the memorial plaque, the LCC found it lacked jurisdiction to order the state to subsidize these expenses, reasoning that the arrangement was a private settlement between the land owner and the Florence family ([11]). However, the Supreme Court of Appeal disagreed with this conclusion, asserting that acceptance of a private agreement did not waive the Florence family’s rights to seek costs from the state. The court recognized the memorial plaque's symbolic significance in acknowledging historical injustices suffered by the Florence family and concluded that the costs should constitute part of the solatium owed to them ([12]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld part of the LCC's ruling while reversing others. It dismissed the appeal concerning the CPI's application for compensation, stating that the LCC exercised its discretion appropriately ([19]). Nonetheless, the appeal regarding the costs of erecting the memorial plaque was upheld. The court ordered the respondent to pay the costs incurred for the plaque, adjusting the award to a sum of R50,000, and also mandated the state to cover other applicable costs not previously paid by the Land Claims Commission ([19]).

This ruling reinforces the notion that equitable redress under South African law must account for both historical injustices and the specific circumstances of the claimants, reflecting a broader commitment to achieving justice for affected individuals while balancing public interests. The decision elucidates the enduring relevance of restitution frameworks in addressing past injustices and highlights the judiciary's role in fostering reparative measures in post-apartheid South Africa.
