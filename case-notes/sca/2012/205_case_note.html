Makhanya v Goede Wellington Boerdery (Pty) Ltd

Supreme Court of Appeal 2012 [2012] ZASCA 205 (30 November 2012) Coram: Heher, Bosielo

Neutral Citation: Makhanya v Goede Wellington Boerdery (Pty) Ltd (230/12) [2012] ZASCA 205 (30 November 2012)

Coram: Heher, Bosielo, Tshiqi, Theron JJA, Erasmus AJA

FIRAC Analysis

F – Facts

The case arises from an appeal against a ruling made by the North Gauteng High Court regarding a decision by the Water Tribunal that dismissed Goede Wellington Boerdery (Pty) Ltd's appeal against the Chief Director of Water Use's refusal to grant a water use license for agricultural development ([1]–[5]).

Goede Wellington sought a water license to utilize water from the Berg River for expanding its citrus orchard, relying on the water entitlements held by ECPA Boerdery (Pty) Ltd, its neighbor ([6]–[8]). The application was supported by various stakeholders, including the Berg River Irrigation Board ([7]). Initially, the Regional Director of the Department of Water Affairs and Forestry recommended approval after a detailed review of relevant factors under Section 27(1) of the National Water Act 36 of 1998. However, the Chief Director rejected the application, arguing it did not sufficiently address concerns of past racial discrimination ([9]–[11]).

Goede Wellington contested the Chief Director's decision through an appeal to the Water Tribunal. The Tribunal, however, upheld the Chief Director’s refusal, mainly focusing on the alleged inadequacy in addressing racial and gender discrimination ([12]–[13]). Consequently, Goede Wellington sought judicial review of the Tribunal's decision in the high court, which set aside the Tribunal's ruling and awarded the water license directly to Goede Wellington, also ordering costs against Mr. Makhanya in his official capacity ([14]–[20]).

I – Issues

  1. Does the refusal of the Water Tribunal’s decision constitute administrative action that is reviewable under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)? ([21])
  2. If PAJA applies, was the high court justified in substituting the Tribunal’s decision with its own instead of remitting the matter back to the Tribunal? ([21])
  3. Was it appropriate for the high court to impose a cost order against Mr. Makhanya in his official capacity? ([21]–[22])

R – Rules

The legal framework surrounding the case is primarily governed by the National Water Act 36 of 1998, specifically Sections 27 and 148, which outline the criteria for granting water licenses and judicial procedures related to appeals against administrative decisions ([5]–[8]). The PAJA provides grounds under which administrative actions can be considered reviewable, particularly focusing on lawfulness, reasonableness, and procedural fairness ([17], [31]).

The court referenced established precedents indicating that administrative bodies may not be classified as courts of law, despite performing some judicial functions ([28]). Importantly, the matter touches upon principles from South African constitutional law, notably regarding the right to reasonable administrative action as enshrined in Section 33 of the Constitution ([31]).

A – Application

The court noted that the Tribunal's dismissal of Goede Wellington's appeal constituted administrative action as per PAJA, emphasizing that it did not merely act as an appellate body but exercised substantial decision-making powers ([27]–[31]). The High Court had identified that the Tribunal had committed a material error of law by primarily focusing on one factor under Section 27(1)(b) at the expense of others, which constituted a misinterpretation of their duties ([18], [32]).

In determining whether to substitute its decision, the High Court highlighted the "exceptional circumstances" it relied upon, including the Tribunal’s errors and the extended delay that would result from remitting the matter back for reconsideration ([19], [41], [47]). The court concluded that the requisite balance of factors, including socio-economic impacts and past injustices, had been inadequately considered by the Tribunal, warranting direct decision-making by the High Court ([39], [40]).

As for the costs against Mr. Makhanya, the issue was contested on the grounds that no mala fides or grossly unreasonable conduct was displayed, thus challenging the appropriateness of the cost order in his official capacity ([50], [52]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the first appellant's appeal while dismissing the second appellant's claim. It found that the High Court was correct in its decision to classify the Tribunal’s refusal as administrative action and to directly substitute its ruling instead of remitting it back due to significant procedural and substantive deficiencies in the Tribunal's handling of the case ([53]).

The cost order against Mr. Makhanya was also overturned, underscoring that public officials should not be penalized in costs absent evidence of improper conduct ([53]). This case underscores the judiciary's commitment to appropriate oversight of administrative actions while affirming the need for fairness and efficiency in legal processes related to the allocation of water rights in South Africa.

This decision reaffirms the application of the tenets of administrative justice in the context of essential public resources, illustrating the interplay between legal interpretation and the substantive equity required to address historical injustices while regulating natural resource use.
