# Case Note: South Durban Community Environmental Alliance v MEC for Economic Development, Tourism and Environmental Affairs: KwaZulu-Natal Provincial Government (Case no 231/19)

## FIRAC Method Analysis

### F – Facts
The dispute in this case arises from the proposal to construct a logistics park on the site of the former Clairwood Park Racecourse, located in the South Durban Industrial Basin ([1]). The logistics park, planned by Capital Property Fund Limited, is intended to facilitate the transport of containers to and from the Port of Durban, responding to the increasing logistical demands in the region ([2]). However, the South Durban Community Environmental Alliance (the Alliance), an environmental justice NGO, opposed this development, arguing that it would lead to significant vehicular emissions detrimental to air quality, exacerbating existing pollution issues in a community already burdened by industrial pollution ([3]).

Following the Department of Economic Development, Tourism, and Environmental Affairs granting environmental authorisation for the logistics park under section 24 of the National Environmental Management Act 107 of 1998 (NEMA), the Alliance appealed to the MEC for Economic Development, who dismissed their appeal ([2] and [4]). Subsequently, an application was launched for judicial review of the MEC's decision in the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court on grounds stemming from the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([4]). The High Court dismissed the application, leading to the Alliance's appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([4]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal question presented by the appeal revolves around whether the MEC's decision to uphold the environmental authorisation was valid, specifically considering the grounds of review presented by the Alliance. The court needed to determine if the MEC had properly taken relevant facts into account or had ignored any uncontentious and objectively verifiable facts that could have resulted in a different outcome ([10], [18], [23]). Additionally, the implications of the alleged failure to challenge the Department's initial decision were significant, as it raised questions about the procedural adequacy of the Alliance's approach to their claims ([5], [6]).

### R – Rules
The court analyzed the relevant legal principles under NEMA and PAJA, particularly how they intersect regarding the review and appeal processes. The distinction between administrative review and appeals was discussed, emphasizing that an administrative decision is resistant to intervention unless clear and material errors of fact are shown ([12], [14], [23]). The legal framework provided that for an administrative decision to be set aside on review, the applicant must demonstrate that objectively verifiable facts were ignored, which would have materially affected the decision ([24]).

The court also considered precedential cases, such as Wings Park Port Elizabeth (Pty) Ltd v MEC, Environmental Affairs, Eastern Cape and Others, which elaborated on the necessity of challenging both the first-instance and appellate decision when an unfavourable decision is confirmed on appeal ([5], [6], [14]). 

### A – Application
In applying these legal principles to the facts of the case, the court assessed each ground of appeal raised by the Alliance. It found that the high court had correctly concluded that the Alliance failed to provide substantive evidence supporting their claims regarding air quality and community health impacts ([11], [16], [19]). The court reiterated that the MEC's decision was largely based on assessments conducted during the environmental authorization process, which had adequately addressed the issues raised by the Alliance ([28], [33], [35]).

The review of the claims presented by the Alliance indicated that their assertions, such as the non-consideration of health impact studies and failure to assess cumulative impacts, did not meet the threshold of demonstrating a material error or oversight by the MEC. Critically, the lack of specific and substantial evidence from the Alliance meant that the MEC’s reliance on the assessments and reports submitted by Capital was justified ([19], [31], [36]). 

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the Alliance's appeal, confirming that the grounds for judicial review were not sufficiently substantiated by evidence. Consequently, the court upheld the High Court’s ruling ([44], [54]), while ordering the Alliance to pay the costs of the second respondent, Capital Property Fund Limited ([54]). The ruling underscores the critical importance of providing adequate support for claims made in administrative reviews, particularly in environmental cases where the consequences of decisions can be significant for affected communities. 

Additionally, the judgment critically examines procedural aspects, emphasizing a need for plaintiffs to effectively challenge decisions at both levels of appeal to avoid procedural pitfalls that could negate their claims. The case reflects the complexity of balancing environmental concerns with developmental needs, a prevalent theme in constitutional and administrative law in South Africa.
