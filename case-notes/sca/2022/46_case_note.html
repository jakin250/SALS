FIRAC Case Note: Municipal Employees’ Pension Fund and Another v Pandelani Midas Mudau and Another (Case no 1159/2020) [2022] ZASCA 46

F – Facts

The case arises from the appeal made by the Municipal Employees’ Pension Fund (the first appellant) and Akani Retirement Fund Administrators (Pty) Ltd (the second appellant) against the ruling of the Pension Fund Adjudicator concerning Pandelani Midas Mudau (the first respondent) and the Vhembe District Municipality (the second respondent). Mr. Mudau, employed by Vhembe District Municipality, joined the Fund in 2003. Upon his resignation effective 31 May 2013, he claimed his withdrawal benefits, which were calculated based on the amended rules of the Fund that retroactively adjusted benefits from 1 April 2013 ([2]–[6]).

Initially, the pension fund established that members who joined after June 1998, like Mr. Mudau, were entitled to benefits calculated as their contributions plus interest times three (the "original rule"). However, due to warnings from the Fund's actuaries regarding financial sustainability, the rule was amended on 21 June 2013, changing the benefits calculation to contributions plus interest times 1.5 with retroactive effect. The amendment was approved by the Registrar on 1 April 2014 ([4]–[5]).

Mr. Mudau, unhappy with the reduced benefits, lodged a complaint with the Adjudicator, who ruled in his favor, asserting the amended rule's retroactive application could not limit benefits that had accrued before the rule was registered. The Fund subsequently challenged this decision in the Gauteng High Court, where it was dismissed. The appeal to the full court also upheld the Adjudicator's ruling, leading to the appeal before the Supreme Court of Appeal ([9]–[12]).

I – Issues

The following legal issues arise from the case:

  1. Jurisdictional Question: Whether the complaint fell within the Adjudicator’s jurisdiction defined in sections 30H and 30M of the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956 ([13]).
  2. Application of Amended Rule: Whether the amended rule could be applied retroactively to benefits accruing before the Registrar's approval and registration of the amended rules ([13]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal authorities and statutory provisions include:

  • Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956: Sections 12, 30H, and 30M, which govern the powers and roles of pension funds and their adjudicators.
  • Section 12 allows for the amendment of fund rules without infringing on any creditors' rights, contingent upon approval and registration by the Registrar.
  • Section 30P permits an aggrieved party to approach the High Court for a review of an Adjudicator's determination.
  • Judicial Precedents:
  • National Tertiary Retirement Fund v Registrar of Pension Funds: The court upheld that a pension fund may legally adopt rules affecting members' benefits if executed according to authority and statutory frameworks.
  • Joint Municipal Pension Fund and Another v Grobler and Others: Established parameters surrounding the Adjudicator’s role on the validity of rules.
  • Euromarine International of Mauren v The Ship Berg: Clarified legislative intent regarding retroactive laws must be respected when the statute’s language is clear and unambiguous.

A – Application

In examining the complainant's standing, the Court affirmed that it is within the Adjudicator’s authority to interpret and apply fund rules as per section 1’s definitional confines of a "complaint" regarding fund administration. The Adjudicator’s rulings related to the application of rules fell adequately within her powers, which provided no basis for negating this. The Court distinguished the present matter from previous cases where interpretations directly questioned rule validity ([14]–[15]).

On the question of the retroactive application of the amended rule, the Court found the amendments to have been made as per legal provisions, with both the language and circumstances provided clear intent for retroactive effect beginning from 1 April 2013. The respondents' argument that Mr. Mudau's benefits had vested before the rule registration lacked validity because the rule’s explicit language clarified its retroactive application ([16]–[22]).

The Court acknowledged the presumption against retroactivity, yet upheld that clear legislative intent, combined with established norms surrounding statutory language, allowed for the rule's retroactive effect without infringing on vested rights. Thus, the amended rule rightly applied to Mr. Mudau's benefits despite his contentions ([19]–[22]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, stating that the original ruling from the adjudicator was indeed incorrect in its interpretation of the amended rule’s application. The judgment concluded with a directive dismissing Mr. Mudau's complaint, indicating the amended rule was validly applied and that the appellants were entitled to their adjudicated costs.

The implications of this case reaffirm pension funds' authority to amend rules regarding members' benefits under established statutory frameworks, emphasizing the significance of clear drafting and intention in rule amendments, and providing guidance on the enforceability of pension fund rules retroactively, contingent upon compliance with regulatory prescribed norms ([22]). The appeal was thus decided in favor of the first appellant, solidifying the precedent concerning the interpretation of pension benefits associated with retroactive rule changes.
