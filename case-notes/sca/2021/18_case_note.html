Supreme Court of Appeal

Democratic Alliance and Others v Mkhwebane and Another (1370/2019)

During the litigation, the appellants requested the production of two specific documents: (1) Mkhwebane’s application for a position at the SSA and (2) her letter of acceptance for the same position, asserting that these documents were relevant to the case ([para 13]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021

F – Facts
The appeal arises from the dismissal of an interlocutory application by the Democratic Alliance (DA) and its members Glynnis Breytenbach and Werner Horn (the appellants), who sought to compel the production of documents from Busisiwe Mkhwebane, the Public Protector, and her office (the respondents) in relation to alleged defamatory statements made during a press conference on September 6, 2016 ([paras 1-2]). The statements made by the appellants questioned Ms. Mkhwebane's suitability for her nomination as the Public Protector, alleging connections to the State Security Agency (SSA) and casting doubt on her integrity and qualifications ([para 2]). Following a refusal from Mkhwebane to retract and apologize for these statements, filed in October 2017, the appellants initiated the application in question ([paras 6-10]).

During the litigation, the appellants requested the production of two specific documents: (1) Mkhwebane’s application for a position at the SSA and (2) her letter of acceptance for the same position, asserting that these documents were relevant to the case ([para 13]). The respondents resisted the request, claiming that the documents were irrelevant to the claims being made and that references to them in Mkhwebane's affidavit did not satisfy the requirements of Rule 35(12) of the Uniform Rules of Court, which governs the discovery of documents ([paras 16-17]).

The High Court, however, ruled against the appellants, leading to their appeal ([paras 1-2]).

I – Issues
The major legal issues at stake in this case concern the interpretation of Rule 35(12) in the context of discovery and the responsibilities surrounding the production of documents referred to in affidavits or pleadings. Specifically, the court was tasked with determining:

  1. Whether the two requested documents fell under the definition of “documents referred to” in Rule 35(12) and thus should be produced ([paras 12, 41]).
  2. The relevant standard to apply in deciding whether a document is “relevant” within the scope of Rule 35(12) ([para 20]).
  3. Whether the High Court erred in dismissing the interlocutory application and ordering the appellants to file their answering affidavits ([para 47]).

R – Rules
The relevant legal authorities that informed the court's decision included:

  • Rule 35(12) of the Uniform Rules of Court: This rule allows for the production of documents that are referred to in any pleadings or affidavits prior to the close of pleadings ([para 11]). It emphasizes the obligation to produce documents that are referenced, without a prescriptive requirement that they relate directly to issues that have been defined by the pleadings ([para 24]).
  • Case law precedents such as Universal City Studios v Movie Time 1983 (4) SA 736 (D) and discussions in Gorfinkel v Gross, Hendler & Frank 1987 (3) SA 766 (C), which elaborated on the relevance of documents referred to in annexures and defined the limits of discoverability and production obligations as related to relevance ([paras 18, 20]).
  • The principle established in these cases that a mere reference in an affidavit might not obligate a party to produce a document if it is irrelevant ([para 32]).

A – Application
In applying these legal principles, the Supreme Court of Appeal focused on the following aspects:

  • The court established that the application for the SSA position referred to in annexure 'PPSA5' was clearly material, thus necessitating its production under Rule 35(12) ([paras 42-45]). The appellants' argument that the timeline concerning Mkhwebane's employment was critical to their defence against the defamation claims was emphasized ([para 42]).
  • Conversely, regarding the letter of acceptance referenced in 'PPSA5,' the court noted the lack of evidence confirming its existence, treating the request for this document as speculative and unsupported by the appellants ([para 46]).
  • As for the lower court's decision, it was determined that the appellants were to be allowed to obtain documents that referenced Mkhwebane’s application effectively, whilst their dismissal of the associated order regarding the filing of answering affidavits was rectified as it didn’t account for the need for timely evidence from the appellants ([para 49]).

C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the High Court had erred in its dismissal of the interlocutory application. The court concluded that:

  1. The appellants were entitled to compel the production of Mkhwebane’s application for the Analyst position since it was referenced in her affidavit and was relevant to the defamation context of their statements ([para 49]).
  2. The court also ruled that the subsequent costs order favoring the appellants should apply, as they had been unjustly denied access to material documents necessary to frame their defense against serious allegations ([para 49]).

Thus, the judgment not only reinstated the appellants' rights to the document but also reiterated the significance of due process regarding the evidential requirements in defamation proceedings, setting a precedent regarding the interpretation of discovery rules which emphasizes transparency and accountability ([paras 49-50]).
