Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# Case Note: Barnes v Mangaung Metropolitan Municipality and Another [2022] ZASCA 77

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

This appeal stems from the decision by the Mangaung Metropolitan Municipality to appoint Mr. Ketsebae Israel Kgamanyane as the first executive head of its metropolitan police service ([1]). The appellant, Adrian Paul Barnes, who was an unsuccessful candidate for the position, sought the review and setting aside of Mr. Kgamanyane’s appointment ([1]). After being informed of his unsuccessful application on 23 November 2017, Mr. Barnes approached the Free State Division of the High Court, which dismissed his application with costs on grounds that he failed to demonstrate that the municipality had acted illegally ([2]), ([1]). Mr. Barnes was granted leave to appeal against this decision.

The position was advertised in July 2017, and both Mr. Barnes and Mr. Kgamanyane were shortlisted following an interview process. The interview panel rated Mr. Kgamanyane more favorably, resulting in the municipality's council resolving to appoint him as the metro police chief on 17 November 2017, effective from 1 January 2018 ([2]). The crux of Mr. Barnes’ contention was predicated on the argument that the municipality was legally bound to appoint a registered traffic officer as the metro police chief, claiming that Mr. Kgamanyane did not meet this requirement and thus the appointment was unlawful ([3]).

### I – Issues

The pivotal legal issue presented in this case revolves around the interpretation of the relevant provisions of the South African Police Service Act 68 of 1995, particularly sections 64D and 64C, to ascertain whether the municipality was obliged to appoint a registered traffic officer as the first chief of police, or if the mere requirement of being a "fit and proper person" sufficed ([6]), ([9]). Additionally, the question as to whether the decision constituted administrative action as defined in the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) was considered, although the court decided not to dwell on this point due to the clarity of the case's legal issue ([5]).

### R – Rules

The legal framework relevant to this case comprises the South African Police Service Act 68 of 1995 as well as the regulations promulgated under this Act ([6]). Key provisions include:

- **Section 64C(1)**: Mandates that a municipal council must appoint a member of the municipal police service as its executive head, expressly subject to section 64D.
  
- **Section 64D**: States that the municipal council must appoint a "fit and proper person" as the first executive head of the municipal police service, without specifying any additional criteria ([6]).

- **Regulation 11(1)(a)**: Specifies various qualifications for members of the municipal police service, which includes being a registered traffic officer. However, it is subject to the provisions of sections 64D and 64Q, and does not apply to the first appointment under section 64D ([7]), ([10]).

Judicial precedent reinforces the principle of legality which indicates that public entities must operate within the powers conferred upon them, or their actions may be rendered reviewable ([4]).

### A – Application

In assessing the legality of Mr. Kgamanyane's appointment, the court interpreted sections 64D and 64C in the context of the legislative framework ([6]). The court found that the requirement set forth in section 64D to appoint a "fit and proper person" stands alone without additional stipulations, thereby implying that no other criteria, including the need for the appointee to be a registered traffic officer, were legally required for the initial appointment of the metro police chief ([9]).

The interpretation aligned with the legislative intent which recognizes that at the inaugural appointment, there would be no existing members within the municipal police service from which to draw ([9]). Furthermore, Mr. Barnes' argument that the language of "fit and proper person" lacked meaning without inclusion of the additional criterion was found to be unsubstantiated, as the court emphasized that the phrase is a well-established standard across various legislative contexts ([11]).

Consequent to this analysis, the court articulated that the municipality did not overstep its jurisdiction nor acted unlawfully in appointing Mr. Kgamanyane, affirming that the review application by Mr. Barnes lacked legal merit on this basis, thereby adhering to the principle of legality ([15]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Mr. Barnes' appeal with costs, confirming that the Mangaung Metropolitan Municipality acted within its rights and authority when it appointed Mr. Kgamanyane as the first metro police chief, with the requirement of him being a "fit and proper person" being satisfied without necessitating traffic officer registration ([16]). The ruling fortifies the interpretation of statutory language as critical in the context of administrative law, reinforcing the principle that legislative intent and clarity are paramount in assessing lawful governance ([15]).

This case illustrates significant aspects of administrative law, particularly the balance of power and the legal interpretative frameworks guiding public authority within South Africa. The implications of this ruling extend to future appointments within municipal structures, clarifying the requisite qualifications under statutory frameworks and the reliance on established legal doctrines regarding the exercise of public power.
