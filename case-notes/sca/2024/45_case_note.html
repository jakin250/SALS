Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 45

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–4])

The case at hand, Centre for Child Law and Others v South African Council for Educators and Others, originates from a legal challenge to the disciplinary decisions made by the South African Council for Educators (SACE) regarding two educators, Mrs. Vangile Mirriam Mokoena and Mrs. Khutso Francinah Sathekge, who were involved in serious misconduct resulting in physical harm to students ([1]). The first appellant, the Centre for Child Law, together with the mothers of the harmed children, contested SACE’s application of its 2016 Mandatory Sanctions, arguing that these sanctions were rigidly enforced without consideration for rehabilitative or corrective measures, as they failed to involve meaningful input from the children and their parents in the sanctioning process.

In 2019, following a disciplinary hearing, both educators were penalized. Mokoena pleaded guilty to four charges, including assault and intimidation, and received a sanction of ten years' suspension, along with a fine ([6–7]). Despite numerous requests, SACE did not provide detailed reasons for these sanctions, leading to concerns about fairness and due process, culminating in the appellants launching a review application to set aside SACE's decisions and requiring them to adhere more closely to constitutional standards, particularly regarding children's rights ([2]).

The High Court initially dismissed the review based on claims of undue delay but upheld systemic relief compelling SACE to reconsider its sanctioning policies. The appellants appealed the dismissal of their primary review applications, while SACE cross-appealed on several grounds, which was later abandoned ([4]).

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 8–14])

The appeal raises critical constitutional and administrative law issues, particularly focusing on whether SACE's application of the 2016 Mandatory Sanctions amounted to a misuse of discretion and violated the rights of the children involved. The following key issues are addressed:

  1. Undue Delay: Did the High Court err in concluding that the appellants failed to act without unreasonable delay per the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)? The question pivots on when the ‘clock’ starts ticking for filing a review application when no reasons for the administrative actions were given ([9–10]).
  2. Mootness of the Appeal: Is the current appeal moot, considering that the educators have served their sanctions and the 2016 Mandatory Sanctions have been replaced by 2020 Mandatory Sanctions? This question examines whether the appeal presents an actual dispute requiring resolution ([12–14]).
  3. Lawfulness of SACE’s Disciplinary Decisions: Does SACE’s conduct fall afoul of specific constitutional requirements regarding children’s rights, particularly the principle of acting in the best interest of the child? This issue scrutinizes SACE's adherence to constitutional and legislative mandates when imposing sanctions on educators for misconduct ([15–20]).

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 16–27])

The decision rests on several key legal provisions and precedents that frame the constitutional obligations of SACE as an organ of state:

  1. Constitutional Provisions: Section 28(2) of the Constitution of South Africa, emphasizing that the best interests of the child must be a primary consideration in all matters concerning children, is fundamental to the court's findings. Moreover, sections of the Children’s Act reaffirm children’s rights to be heard in judicial and administrative processes ([16–18]).
  2. PAJA: The Promotion of Administrative Justice Act outlines the requirements for reasonable timelines in judicial reviews (s. 7(1)), emphasizing that the absence of reasons from SACE means the commencement of this period was unclear, contradicting the High Court's perspective ([9–10]).
  3. Past Precedents: The court referenced relevant cases, such as Commissioner, South African Revenue Service v Sasol Chevron Holdings Limited ([10]) and Police and Prisons Civil Rights Union v South African Correctional Service Workers' Union ([12]), underscoring the principle that mootness does not preclude a court from adjudicating legal disputes with potential practical effects.
  4. International Instruments: The South African commitment to the United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child and the African Charter on the Rights and Welfare of the Child provides additional context for the court's scrutiny of SACE’s decisions ([17]).

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 28–30])

The Supreme Court of Appeal applied these legal rules to determine the appropriateness of SACE's actions.

  1. Undue Delay: The analysis revealed that SACE's failure to provide reasons obstructed the commencement of the 180-day period stipulated by PAJA ([10]). The court criticized the High Court's decision, asserting that because the appellants had repeatedly sought reasons and were met with silence, it was unreasonable to hold them accountable for delays related to the time limits established in PAJA ([9–11]).
  2. Mootness: On the question of mootness, the court concluded that setting aside the administrative actions would have practical implications requiring SACE to reassess both the sanctions and the systemic deficiencies of their approaches to disciplinary processes. The imposition of penalties without considering rehabilitative frameworks remained an active concern, emphasizing that mere compliance with procedural orders does not necessarily resolve underlying issues affecting children ([12–14]).
  3. Lawfulness of Decisions: Critically, the court established that SACE unlawfully restricted its discretionary power by adhering rigidly to outdated sanctions, thereby failing to consider situational specifics that could warrant different approaches to discipline. Additionally, SACE’s lack of consultation with the affected children contravened their statutory and constitutional obligations to protect children's rights, leading the court to declare the decisions made invalid and unlawful ([27–28]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 31–31])

In a decisive judgment, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, declaring SACE's decisions regarding the sanctions imposed on the educators unlawful and invalid. The court remitted the matters back to SACE for reconsideration, emphasizing the necessity of implementing rehabilitative measures alongside exploring the interests and rights of the affected children ([31]).

This judgment reaffirms the importance of adhering to constitutional principles that prioritize children's rights within educational and disciplinary contexts. It reinforces the requirement for both procedural fairness and substantial compliance with children's rights legislation, calling into question the responsibilities of educational bodies to ensure discipline is exercised humanely and justly. The implications extend beyond this case, potentially prompting broader reforms within SACE and similar educational institutions, ensuring future practices reflect the paramount importance of protecting children's welfare ([30–31]).
