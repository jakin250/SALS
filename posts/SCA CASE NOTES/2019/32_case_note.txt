Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: Visagie & Associates CC & another v Small Enterprise Finance Agency Ltd (262/2018)

**Citation:** Visagie & Associates CC & another v Small Enterprise Finance Agency Ltd (262/2018) [2019] ZASCA 32 (28 March 2019)

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Bench:** Majiedt, Swain, Zondi, Mocumie JJA, Rogers AJA

**Heard:** 04 March 2019

**Delivered:** 28 March 2019

---

## F – Facts

The dispute arose out of a settlement agreement related to a lease between M Visagie & Associates CC (the first appellant) and Mullard Hamlet Visagie (the second appellant), and Small Enterprise Finance Agency Ltd (the respondent) ([1]). The first appellant had leased premises from Business Partners Ltd, which subsequently sold the property to Khula Enterprise Finance Ltd, renamed as the respondent ([2]). The original lease agreement, concluded on 16 July 2001, had a term that lasted until 30 June 2004 but continued on a month-to-month basis post-expiration due to the appellants remaining in occupation ([3]).

Following non-payment of rent, the respondent issued a notice of cancellation on 30 September 2013 and initiated legal proceedings for confirmation of this cancellation and to recover arrear rentals ([3],[4]). A settlement was reached on 18 July 2016, which involved mutual withdrawals of claims and an agreement on future rentals due for the remaining period of occupation ([5]). The settlement stipulated that rental payments were due as from June 2016 until the appellants vacated the premises, with specific terms indicating consequences for non-payment ([6],[7]).

Disputes arose regarding whether the rental payments had to be made in advance on the first working day of each month, consistent with the original lease agreement, and about the proportionality of costs to be paid by the appellants ([9],[11]). The magistrate's court ruled in favor of the respondent, concluding that the terms of the original lease had been tacitly relocated into the settlement agreement, leading to a judgment against the appellants for breach of the settlement ([10]).

---

## I – Issues

The principal legal issue before the court was whether the terms of the original lease agreement had been tacitly included in the settlement agreement, particularly regarding rental payment timelines ([12]). A related sub-issue involved the enforceability of the purported breach of the settlement agreement by the appellants, given the lack of express stipulations in the settlement regarding payment timing and costs ([11],[21]).

Additionally, the court considered the broader implications of whether a tacit term—specifically regarding a timeline for rental payments—could be inferred from the actions and intentions of the parties at the time of the settlement ([12],[13]).

---

## R – Rules

The court applied established jurisprudence regarding the interpretation of contracts and the notion of tacit terms, referencing the principles outlined in *Wilkins v Voges* ([13]) and further cases which delineate that a tacit term may be actual or imputed based on parties' intent ([13]). The criteria for importing tacit terms require a clear understanding of the parties' intentions as inferred from their expressed terms and surrounding circumstances ([12],[15]).

The applicable legal standard is that a court will be reluctant to impose a tacit term unless it is evident that both parties had a mutual understanding regarding the term in question, particularly in commercial contracts ([13]). Relevant case law support for the above is drawn from cases like *Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality* and *Novartis v Maphil* ([14]).

---

## A – Application

In evaluating the issue, the court closely scrutinized the express terms of the settlement agreement, the historical context of the lease, and the conduct of both parties during and after the drafting of the settlement ([15],[17]). Importantly, the court noted that while the settlement agreement did not specify due dates for the rental payments, the historical context of late payment acceptance by the respondent suggests that strict adherence to monthly payment timing (i.e., by the first working day) was not a fundamental aspect of their agreement.

The court also considered that both parties' conduct post-agreement (including the respondent's acceptance of late payments) implied that the specific timing for payments was mutually relaxed due to the practicalities involved ([16],[18]). The stipulation by the respondent that costs should apply on an attorney-and-client scale, contrary to standard practice of party-and-party costs unless otherwise stipulated, indicated further inconsistencies in enforcing the agreement extensively ([20]).

The analysis concluded that the respondent failed to meet the burden of proving that a tacit term regarding the month-to-month payment timeline existed, particularly given that they accepted late payments without protest ([21]). Therefore, the court found favor with the appellants, leading to the judgment being overturned.

---

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, concluding that the respondent failed to demonstrate that the terms of the original lease regarding payment timings had been tacitly incorporated into the settlement agreement; consequently, the alleged breach did not occur ([22]). The court's ruling indicates a marked hesitance to impose tacit terms absent clear mutual intent from the parties, reinforcing the importance of express contractual terms in settlement agreements.

The implications of this decision highlight the need for meticulous attention to detail in contractual agreements and the significance of mutual understanding in commercial relationships, particularly in the context of payment obligations. This case also reaffirms the necessity for parties in negotiation processes to clearly articulate their terms, thus reducing the potential for future disputes over tacit terms and unexpressed obligations. 

This decision stands as a significant precedent on the interpretation of tacit terms within settlement agreements and the broader implications for commercial law in South Africa.
