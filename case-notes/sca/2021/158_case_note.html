Supreme Court of Appeal

Rautini v Passenger Rail Agency of South Africa

The appellant, Masibulele Rautini, initiated a claim for damages against the Passenger Rail Agency of South Africa (PRASA) following an incident on 19 November 2011, whereby he sustained injuries while traveling as a passenger on a train operated by PRASA.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 158 (8 November 2021) --- ## F – Facts The app

Neutral Citation: Rautini v Passenger Rail Agency of South Africa (Case no. 853/2020) [2021] ZASCA 158 (8 November 2021)

F – Facts

The appellant, Masibulele Rautini, initiated a claim for damages against the Passenger Rail Agency of South Africa (PRASA) following an incident on 19 November 2011, whereby he sustained injuries while traveling as a passenger on a train operated by PRASA. The incident took place between the Du Toit and Lynedoch train stations in Cape Town ([2]).

Upon the commencement of legal proceedings, the trial court bifurcated the issues of merits and quantum under Uniform Court Rule 33(4), resulting in a judgment in favor of Rautini on the merits, establishing PRASA's liability for the appellant's damages ([3]). However, this decision was overturned by the full court of the Western Cape Division which dismissed Rautini’s claim ([3]).

Rautini testified as the sole witness, detailing that the train's doors were open throughout the journey. He recounted how he was assaulted by a gang of three men who demanded possessions from the passengers, leading to him being thrown from the moving train ([4]). Subsequently, he was found injured and taken to Stellenbosch Hospital ([5]).

The respondent called witnesses, including medical personnel who produced documents that became pivotal in the case. The full court rejected Rautini's account, asserting that the contents of the medical and ambulance reports contradicted his version. These documents suggested that the appellant either jumped from the train or was thrown out, leading to the conclusion that the version of events provided by Rautini could not be trusted ([7], [8]).

I – Issues

The core legal issues arising from the case were:

  1. Admissibility of Evidence: Whether the discovered documents (medical and ambulance reports) were correctly admitted without proof of their contents, and whether this amounted to a violation of the hearsay rule ([8]).
  2. Credibility of the Witness: Whether the full court appropriately evaluated Rautini’s credibility as a witness, particularly in rejecting his account of how the incident occurred ([7], [23]).
  3. Failure to Cross-Examine: Whether the respondent's failure to cross-examine Rautini on critical points undermined the validity of the inferences drawn by the full court from the evidence presented ([14], [16]).
  4. Legal Standard of Inferences: The extent to which the full court could draw inferences regarding the credibility of witnesses and the occurrence of events based on insufficient cross-examination and hearsay ([19], [24]).

R – Rules

The court relied on several rules and principles of evidentiary law relevant to the case:

  1. Hearsay Evidence Rules: According to Section 3(1) of the Law of Evidence Amendment Act 45 of 1988, hearsay evidence is generally inadmissible unless certain conditions are met; notably, if all parties agree to its admission, or if the person whose credibility the evidence depends on testifies ([9], [11]).
  2. Right to Cross-Examine: The principle established in President of the Republic of South Africa v South African Rugby Football Union (Commission) emphasizes the obligation to cross-examine witnesses to challenge their credibility effectively ([14]).
  3. Finding of Credibility: The court's ability to draw inferences is typically limited to those that are consistent with all proven facts. Courts must find that the inference drawn is the most probable given the circumstances ([24]).
  4. Negligence Standard: The precedent set in Mashongwa v PRASA establishes that failing to adhere to safety protocols, such as keeping train doors closed while in motion, can result in liability ([25]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) applied these rules primarily by reassessing the validity of the full court's reliance on the medical and ambulance records. The SCA determined that the contents of these records were indeed hearsay evidence and should have been evaluated under the strict criteria set forth in the Law of Evidence Amendment Act. The failure of the respondent to request the admission of this evidence according to lawful procedure was deemed a significant lapse, impacting the reliability of the findings ([11], [12]).

The court further noted that Rautini was never cross-examined on pivotal aspects of his narrative, such as interactions with medical personnel about his injuries. The absence of this critical examination essentially deprived the respondent of the opportunity to challenge Rautini’s version of events, which, according to legal principles, allowed the assumption that his testimony was credible ([15], [18]).

Moreover, the SCA highlighted that the full court's inference that Rautini's account was a recent fabrication was unsupported, as the inconsistencies cited could have been clarified through adequate cross-examination. Thus, the SCA concluded this misdirection invalidated the full court's decision ([23], [24], [26]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld Rautini's appeal, emphasizing that the full court had substantially misdirected itself in relying on inadmissible hearsay evidence and neglecting the importance of cross-examination. The court clarified that, while Rautini did not dispute the authenticity of the discovered documents, their admission without proper verification carried significant weight. The decision by the SCA reinstated Rautini's original claim for damages against PRASA, underscoring the imperative of adhering to fair trial principles, including the right to confront and cross-examine witnesses ([8], [26]). The determination of liability against PRASA was positively articulated through established legal precedents, reinforcing the standards of safety and duty of care owed to passengers by train operators ([25]).

This case also serves as a reminder to legal practitioners of the significance of rigorously considering evidentiary rules and the implications of failing to adequately challenge a witness's credibility during cross-examination.
