Case Title: National Union of Metalworkers of South Africa obo Khanyile Nganezi and Others v Dunlop Mixing and Technical Services (Pty) Ltd and Others [2019] ZACC 25

F - Facts

This case arises from the dismissal of employees of Dunlop Mixing and Technical Services (Pty) Ltd, following a protected strike that began on 22 August 2012 ([7]). The strike devolved into violence involving various acts such as setting fire to property and physical assaults, leading to an interdict being issued against further violence ([7]). The employees were dismissed on 26 September 2012 primarily for alleged misconduct during the violent episodes.

The dismissals were categorized into three groups:

  1. Employees positively identified as committing violence.
  2. Employees present during the violence but not actively participating.
  3. Employees not identified or present during the violence ([14]).

An arbitration award found the first two groups' dismissals substantively fair, but ruled the third group’s dismissal unfair, leading to their reinstatement ([9]). This decision was subsequently overturned by the Labour Court and later the Labour Appeal Court upon review, which deemed the arbitrator’s findings unreasonable due to a failure to consider circumstantial evidence regarding the presence of the applicants during the violent incidents ([10]).

The National Union of Metalworkers of South Africa (NUMSA) appealed to the Constitutional Court against the Labour Appeal Court's dismissal of their appeal ([11]). The appeal sought to address the fairness of the misconduct allegations against the applicants and the principle of derivative misconduct.

I - Issues

The court encountered several legal questions surrounding:

  1. The concept and applicability of “derivative misconduct” within South African labour law.
  2. Whether employees who failed to disclose information regarding their co-workers’ alleged misconduct, under the circumstances of violent strikes, can be considered guilty of derivative misconduct ([11]).
  3. The extent to which strikes, particularly protected strikes that turn violent, can affect an employer's rights in dismissing employees for misconduct ([11]).

R - Rules

The case primarily engages with the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995, which stipulates protections against unfair dismissal related to participation in protected strikes ([2]). Key legal principles include:

  • Section 67(5) of the LRA, which allows for dismissal if related broadly to misconduct or employers’ operational requirements ([7]).
  • The concept of derivative misconduct as articulated in previous cases, notably Chauke v Lee Services Centre and Food & Allied Workers Union v Amalgamated Beverage Industries Ltd, which discusses an employee’s obligation to disclose wrongdoing witnessed by them or known to them amid collective misconduct ([11], [8]).
  • The court's doctrine of "reasonableness" as established in Sidumo v Rustenburg Platinum Mines Ltd, which serves as a standard for evaluating arbitration awards under the LRA ([10]).

A - Application

The Constitutional Court, referred to various previous judgments to delineate derivative misconduct's understanding. The core analysis focused on whether the employees had actual knowledge of the wrongdoing and whether their silence raised a duty to disclose. The court also explored the implications of applying derivative misconduct in the context of a violent protest.

The Court noted that:

  • Mere presence at the scene of misconduct does not automatically entail culpability if one could not reasonably be expected to disclose information about wrongdoing ([27]).
  • A broader interpretation of derivative misconduct could lead to unintended consequences, especially in situations with heightened emotions like strikes, where the concept of worker solidarity is intrinsic ([29]).

Thus, the Court reasoned that the adjudicating bodies involved improperly applied the standard of proof and failed to adequately weigh the circumstantial evidence, leading to a broader finding of unfair dismissal regarding the third group of employees ([30]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal, ruling in favor of the applicants and reversing the orders of both the Labour Court and Labour Appeal Court. The Court clarified that the nature and extent of derivative misconduct must adhere to principles of fairness and reciprocally consider the safety and good faith obligations of both employers and employees amidst the complexities of a violent industrial action ([30]).

The Court concluded:

  1. The appeal is successful; the previous decisions are set aside.
  2. The original arbitrator’s determination regarding the third group was reinstated.
  3. There will be no order as to costs to promote resolution in the employment relationship ([32]).

This ruling underscores the need for careful consideration of collective labor dynamics, the role of violence in industrial action, and reinforces the safeguarding of employees' rights amidst complex labor disputes.
