Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: Executor of the Estate of the Late Josephine Terblanche Gouws (Charmaine Celliers N.O.) v Magnificent Mile Trading 30 (Pty) Ltd & others [2018] ZASCA 91

## FIRAC Method

### F - Facts ([paras 1-20])
The case revolves around the estate of the late Nicolaas Petrus Gouws, who held an unused old order mineral right to coal on Portion 9 of the farm Driefontein 338, situated in Mpumalanga. Following the enactment of the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA), Gouws filed an application for the conversion of his right into a new order prospecting right on 29 April 2005, just before the one-year window closed for such applications. Shortly after, on 3 May 2005, Magnificent Mile Trading 30 (Pty) Ltd submitted a competing application for the same mineral on the same land. The Department of Mineral Resources accepted both applications.

Tragically, Gouws passed away on 9 November 2005, prior to a decision being rendered on his application. Despite the pending status, the Department granted prospecting rights to both Gouws and Magnificent Mile for different categories of Driefontein. Following this, a series of review applications commenced, and the estate filed a counter-application asserting rights over the mineral.

The broader context involves the complex administrative errors and procedural missteps that ensued, as described by the presiding judges. The executor of Gouws' estate, Charmaine Celliers N.O., became involved after both Gouws and his widow passed away, leading to the present appeal [paras 1-20].

### I - Issues ([paras 4-5, 28-38])
The principal legal issue is whether a prospecting right could be granted to a deceased estate posthumously or if Gouws' death led to the invalidation of his pending application. Additionally, the implications of Gouws’ death on the estate’s entitlement to a decision on the application were also in question. Further, should the decisions made about prospecting rights by Magnificent Mile and the Department be set aside in light of procedural defects?

Furthermore, the appeal raised questions about the counter-application's legitimacy, specifically whether it constituted a collateral challenge or should be viewed through the lens of statutory review under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) [paras 4-5, 28-38].

### R - Rules ([paras 21-34])
The MPRDA fundamentally altered the way mineral rights are managed in South Africa, making the State the custodian of all mineral resources and allowing specific rights to be granted after the repeal of old order rights. Critical sections of the MPRDA and PAJA guide the decision-making processes involved in granting, reviewing, and administering mineral rights. 

Particularly relevant were provisions allowing a holder of an unused old order right an exclusive right to apply for a prospecting right, which remains valid while the application is pending ([MPRDA, item 8]). Judicial precedents, including *Minister of Mineral Resources & others v Mawetse (SA) Mining Corporation (Pty) Ltd* [2016] ZASCA 82 and *Ferreira v Levin NO* [1996] ZACC 13, clarify that administrative decisions, particularly those that create rights or obligations, must be legally sound and procedurally fair, reaffirming the principle of legality [paras 21-34].

### A - Application ([paras 30-40])
After thorough inspection, the court determined that Gouws’ death did not invalidate his application; therefore, the executor retained the right to a decision regarding that application. The judgment emphasized that the right attached was essentially a right to a lawful decision, which could not simply evaporate upon Gouws' passing. The executor of the estate was entitled to pursue the application as an entity standing in Gouws’ stead.

Judicial examination revealed that the Department's decisions after Gouws' death lacked the necessary legal grounding and coherence required for administrative actions, leading to the conclusion that these actions were void from the outset. Simply put, there had been a failure to recognize the pending application adequately, culminating in a series of administrative failures [paras 30-40].

The counter-application, which sought declaratory relief against Magnificent Mile, was mismanaged in the lower court, as the estate's claims were rooted in valid legal axioms surrounding the nature of administrative law rather than a collateral challenge. This mischaracterization and subsequent dismissal were identified as erroneous by the appellate court [paras 41-45].

### C - Conclusion ([paras 46-46])
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appeal by the executor of Gouws' estate was justified. The order by the court below was overturned, with specific declarations made regarding the legitimacy of Gouws' application for a prospecting right, now confirmed as still pending, and Magnificent Mile's application was declared unlawful.

This judgment reinstated a critical understanding of the procedural intricacies involved in the MPRDA's framework, reaffirming rights even after the death of the applicant, thus ensuring the executor's continued engagement in Gouws' mineral rights application. The ruling firmly establishes that administrative action must adhere to principles of legality and fairness, shining a spotlight on the importance of rigorous compliance with these standards in the management of mineral resources in South Africa [paras 46-46].
