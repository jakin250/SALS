Constitutional Court

City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality v Chairman of the National Building Regulations Review Board [2018] ZACC 15

This case concerns an application by the City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality (hereafter referred to as "the City") for confirmation of a declaration of invalidity issued by the High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Division, Pretoria.

Constitutional Court 2018 [2018] ZACC 15

FIRAC Method

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–17])

This case concerns an application by the City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality (hereafter referred to as "the City") for confirmation of a declaration of invalidity issued by the High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Division, Pretoria. The High Court found section 9 of the National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act 103 of 1977 (hereafter referred to as "the Act") unconstitutional, as it conferred appellate powers on the National Building Regulations Review Board (hereafter referred to as "the Board") over municipal decisions.

In June 2012, ATC South Africa Wireless Infrastructure (Pty) Ltd (hereafter referred to as "ATC") applied to the City for approval to erect a cellular mast on its property. The City approved the plans despite objections from neighboring property owners. Subsequently, the objectors appealed the City's decision to the Board. The City contested this appeal on the grounds of lack of standing for the appellants and jurisdiction of the Board.

After a ruling by the Board that supported the move to consider the merits of the appeal, the City contested the Board's jurisdiction in the High Court, declaring the positional invalidity of section 9 of the Act. The Board's decision was rendered void ab initio. The Minister responsible for the Act defended the validity of section 9, arguing that the application was premature and accentuating the intergovernmental dispute resolution mechanisms. However, these arguments were rejected by the High Court, which deemed that the section infringed upon the constitutional separation of powers.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 19–22])

The principal question arose whether section 9 of the Act is inconsistent with the Constitution by mandating the Board to assume appellate authority over decisions made by municipalities concerning matters that fall under their exclusive control. The issue encapsulates critical inquiries into the separation of powers, the autonomy of municipalities, and the limitations imposed on national legislative authority regarding local government functions.

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 20–37])

The constitutional framework mandates the division of power among the national, provincial, and local spheres of government, as detailed in Section 40(1) and Section 156 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa (1996). Precedents, including Gauteng Development Tribunal [2010] ZACC 11 and Habitat Council [2014] ZACC 9, highlighted that while national and provincial spheres might possess legislative authority over local matters, this does not extend to the usurpation of the executive functions of municipalities.

The Constitution acknowledges municipalities as autonomous entities with original powers. The relevant legal standard involved the interpretation of both constitutional provisions and the Act in light of their implication for local governance and the separation of powers doctrine.

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 20–38])

In its assessment, the Court reaffirmed the autonomy of municipalities under the Constitution. It established that the decisions taken by the City concerning building approvals were essential functions that fall exclusively within municipal authority and that section 9 of the Act, in empowering the national sphere to assert appellate control over such decisions, represented an unlawful encroachment.

The Court scrutinized the legislative text of section 9, declaring it unconstitutional as it effectively compelled municipalities to submit to external reviews that exceeded mere regulatory oversight, and thus, violated the separation of powers principle established in the Constitution. The judgment established that the legislative power intended to regulate must not convert into executive powers that could reverse municipal decisions.

The implications of this ruling indicated that municipalities could no longer be subjected to the conflicting oversight of national bodies in matters of local governance, fortifying the autonomy that municipalities possess in exercising their executive functions.

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 43])

The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court’s declaration of invalidity concerning section 9 of the Act, which effectively operated in a manner permitting the national sphere to interfere in municipal governance. The Court reasoned that this grants the Board powers unconstitutional by enabling it to reverse municipal decisions made in accordance with their legislative authority.

The declaration of invalidity was directed to operate prospectively, allowing for pending appeals to be finalised under prior established frameworks, devoid of retrospective effects that could disrupt ongoing municipal operations.

Thus, the judgment reflects profound implications for the independence of local governance in South Africa, emphasizing the need for legislative frameworks aligning with constitutional principles to support rather than undermine municipal authority.

This case note reflects an understanding of key constitutional principles regarding the distribution of powers across government spheres and the reaffirmation of municipal autonomy against national legislative encroachments.
