Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 62 (05 May 2023) Court: The Supreme Court of

Case Note: Magwala v Chief Sinthumule and Others

Neutral Citation: Magwala v Chief Sinthumule and Others (Case no 744/2021) [2023] ZASCA 62 (05 May 2023)

Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

Coram: Mocumie, Nicholls, Carelse, Matojane JJA, and Nhlangulela AJA

F - Facts ([1]–[6])

The matter revolves around a dispute concerning the rightful identification of a headwoman of Gogobole Village, following the passing of Ms Matamela Ndwammbi, the previously recognized headwoman. After her death in 2008, the Royal Family first identified Mr Maluta Simon Ramabulana as the headman, but following his subsequent passing—without official recognition from the Premier—a regency was conferred upon Ms Nyamukamadi Mukondi Mukhuba for the minor successor, Mr Percy Tshifhiwa Ramabulana.

Contrastingly, Ms Matodzi Annah Magwala, daughter of the late Ndwammbi, claimed to have been identified as headwoman by the Royal Family during her mother’s passing and asserted that she functioned as such since 2008. The essence of the conflict lies in her legitimacy, with the claimants—Chief Rudzani Harold Sinthumule, Ms Mukhuba, and the Gogobole Royal Family—disputing her identification based on lineage and adherence to customary law. The earlier High Court order mandated Ms Magwala to refrain from acting as headwoman without a certificate of recognition and demanded the return of community documents, leading to her appeal ([1]–[2]).

I - Issues ([14]–[18])

The legal questions before the Court pertain to:

  1. Whether Ms Magwala was properly identified as the headwoman of Gogobole in accordance with customary law.
  2. Who constitutes the legitimate membership of the Royal Family capable of making such identifications.
  3. The proper procedural mechanisms for resolving disputes surrounding traditional leadership as stipulated by the Limpopo Traditional Leadership and Institutions Act 6 of 2005 and the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003.

R - Rules ([18]–[24])

Central to the Court’s analysis are the governing statutes:

  • Limpopo Traditional Leadership and Institutions Act 6 of 2005:
  • Section 12 outlines the requisite procedure for filling positions of traditional leadership and mandates the engagement of the Premier for formal recognition following identification by the Royal Family.
  • Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003:
  • Section 21 prescribes the resolution process for disputes arising in traditional communities, emphasizing that resolution must occur internally before escalation to the Premier or judicial avenues.
  • Constitution of the Republic of South Africa:
  • Section 211 underscores the recognition of traditional leadership but subjects it to the Constitution and statutory frameworks, while Section 212 calls for the establishment of houses of traditional leaders and outlines their roles.

Judicial precedents including Netshimbupfe and Another v Carthcart and Others and Tshivhulana Royal Family v Netshivhulana further clarify the obligations of the Royal Family in these processes and the interaction with governmental recognition.

A - Application ([22]–[25])

The Supreme Court of Appeal underscored the inadequacy of the High Court and the full court's approach in overlooking the requisite procedures stipulated by pertinent legislation for resolving disputes on traditional leadership. The Court held that both lower courts misapplied the statutory requirements by ruling on the merits of identification when such matters should have been referred to the Premier for a formal decision.

The Court dismantled the claims made by Ms Magwala on the grounds of insufficient evidence pertaining to her identification as headwoman according to the customs recognized by the community. It highlighted that disputes related to identification must first be resolved through established channels before they can be adjudicated by courts, thereby reiterating the sanctity of customary law processes.

C - Conclusion ([26])

The Supreme Court of Appeal granted special leave to appeal and overturned the orders of the lower courts, affirming that the plaintiffs’ case against Ms Magwala is dismissed with no orders concerning costs. The Court emphasized that both parties had failed to heed the legislative directives intended to resolve such disputes amicably and within the customary structures, resulting in ongoing leadership vacancies in the Gogobole community since 2008. This decision reinforces the primacy of adhering to the statutory mandates embedded within South African law concerning traditional leadership and the importance of due process in the identification and recognition of such positions.

The implications of this ruling extend to all traditional communities in South Africa, reaffirming the necessity of legal frameworks in resolving disputes effectively and maintaining stability within customary governance.
