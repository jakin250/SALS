Case Title: Harrington NO v Transnet Ltd (32/09) [2009] ZASCA 146 (26 November 2009)

Case Title: Harrington NO v Transnet Ltd (32/09) [2009] ZASCA 146 (26 November 2009)

Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal, Republic of South Africa
Judges: Mpati P, Navsa, Heher, Mhlantla JJA, and Leach AJA
Date Heard: 9 November 2009
Date Delivered: 26 November 2009

F – Facts

[1] The appellants, Mark Harrington N.O. and Siyavuma Ngaleika, were employed as security guards by Kuffs Security Services CC (the third respondent) and were patrolling electric cables along the railway line in Cape Town on the night of 3 February 2002 ([para 2]).

[2] At approximately 22h45, while there were no scheduled trains operating after 22h00, an unscheduled train dispatched by Transnet Ltd, trading as Metrorail (the first respondent), struck the appellants from behind. The second respondent, J C Human, was the driver of the train, which was moving towards Salt River for repairs. The appellants suffered serious injuries, with the first appellant requiring a curator ad litem due to brain damage ([paras 4–6]).

[3] The appellants began legal proceedings against Metrorail and Human in the Cape High Court, asserting that their injuries resulted from the negligence of these parties regarding safety measures and warnings concerning unscheduled trains ([paras 7–8]).

[4] After detailed legal proceedings, Blignault J ruled that Metrorail and Human were jointly liable to compensate the appellants, though contributory negligence was considered ([para 9]). On appeal to the Full Bench in October 2008, the ruling was overturned, dismissing the appellants' claims ([paras 10–11]).

I – Issues

  1. Negligence: Did Metrorail and Human act negligently in failing to warn the appellants about the unscheduled train operation?
  2. Contributory Negligence: Were the appellants contributorily negligent by assuming there would be no train activity at night, thereby failing to keep an adequate lookout for trains?
  3. Legal Duty: Did Metrorail owe a legal duty to ensure the safety of the appellants during their patrol duties ([paras 12–14])?

R – Rules

  1. Duty of Care: The court considered the principles of delictual liability, noting that a legal duty arises from an omission subject to policy considerations ([para 21]).
  2. Negligence Standard: Negligence is assessed by determining whether the actions of a defendant fell below the standard of care that could reasonably be expected in the given circumstances ([para 48]).
  3. Contributory Negligence: The legal principle allowing for the reduction of damages if the injured party also failed to exercise reasonable care that could have prevented their injuries ([para 50]).
  4. Omissions: Metrorail's potential liability was linked directly to its negligence in failing to inform Kuffs and the appellants of train movements, which was a factor considered critical in determining Metrorail's legal duty ([paras 25–27]).

A – Application

Negligence

[1] The Supreme Court of Appeal held that Metrorail failed to take adequate precautions to inform the appellants of train movements, despite knowing the operational risks associated with unscheduled train dispatches at night ([paras 24, 27]). Evidence indicated that Metrorail had the means to alert Kuffs, given the existence of direct communication with their control room ([para 25]).

[2] The court rejected the Full Bench's reasoning that the appellants' subjective belief that no trains would run at night absolved Metrorail of any responsibility ([paras 22, 44]). The reasonable man standard was essential, considering the particular hazards of operating near rail lines ([paras 21, 30]).

Contributory Negligence

[1] The court examined the knowledge and experience of the appellants; they had operated under the belief that no trains would run during their shifts based on past experiences ([para 36]). Thus, their assumption of safety was found to be reasoned based on their limited information and past patterns, leading to the determination that their conduct was not unreasonably negligent ([para 45]).

[2] Furthermore, the evidence presented did not sufficiently prove that the appellants had acted with negligence that contributed to the outcome of the incident ([para 64]).

Legal Duty

[1] The ruling emphasized that Metrorail's position as the operator of a potentially dangerous service imposed significant expectations of reasonable care for employees working nearby, noting that the safety of workers was essential due to the inherent dangers associated with rail transport ([para 30]).

[2] The decision articulated that offering a warning about train movements was not an unreasonable demand of Metrorail, reinforcing their responsibility to maintain safety ([para 31]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal found in favor of the appellants regarding their claims against Metrorail and set aside the appeal of Metrorail against the initial judgment while affirming the findings regarding the driver, Human. The court concluded that:

  1. Metrorail was negligent in failing to warn the appellants about the unscheduled train, which was a breach of their duty of care and responsibility towards the security guards operating near the tracks ([para 66]).
  2. The appellants were not reasonably expected to foresee the train's movement given their previous experiences and the lack of communication or training regarding potential night train operations ([para 44]).
  3. There was a clear duty imposed on Metrorail to ensure adequate safety measures were in place, which they failed to satisfy ([para 30]).

Ultimately, the ruling underscored critical issues around railway safety and employer responsibilities, reinforcing the legal principles underpinning duty and negligence in tort law ([paras 1-66]).
