# FIRAC Case Note: Stewart v Botha (340/2007) [2008] ZASCA 84

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–2])

The case of *Stewart v Botha* revolves around Elizabeth Georgina Elzona Stewart and her son Brian Stewart, who was born on August 4, 1993, with severe congenital defects including spina bifida and hydrocephalus. The first appellant (mother) sought damages from the respondents (Dr. M Botha and Dr. S Smal), who were her medical practitioners during her pregnancy. The claim was predicated on their alleged failure to inform her of the foetal abnormalities, which she asserted would have led her to terminate the pregnancy. The second appellant (father) brought a delictual claim on behalf of his son for similar damages.

The Cape High Court dismissed the claim on the grounds of legal exceptions, stating that the particulars of the claim did not establish a legally recognized cause of action, particularly asserting that the medical practitioners had no duty to prevent the birth of the child. The court's decision was appealed.

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 2–4])

The primary legal issue in this case is whether medical practitioners can be held liable for failing to inform a parent of congenital defects in a foetus that would have led the parent to terminate the pregnancy, and whether this claim by the child constitutes a legally cognizable cause of action. Specific points of contention include:

1. **Wrongfulness**: Is the failure to diagnose and inform about congenital defects an act that establishes wrongfulness in delict?
2. **Public Policy**: What implications does recognizing such a claim have for public policy, particularly concerning the sanctity of life?
3. **Existential Question**: Is it justifiable for a court to assess whether a life lived with severe disabilities is preferable to non-existence?

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 5–10])

The court relied on various principles of delict, particularly emphasizing that wrongfulness is distinct from negligence. References include:

- *Telematrix (Pty) Ltd v Advertising Standards Authority SA* where it articulated that wrongfulness requires a broader policy consideration beyond mere negligence ([para 5]).
- The court's discourse on case law acknowledged claims regarding similar contexts, such as *Pinchin v Santam Insurance Co Ltd* and *Friedman v Glicksman*, wherein the courts recognized claims of economic loss due to negligence. Nevertheless, these cases did not directly address the existential questions posed by cases of "wrongful life" ([paras 9–10]).
- The rationale behind denying claims in cases such as *McKay v Essex Area Health Authority* highlighted the precedent set in other common law jurisdictions against recognizing "wrongful life" claims based on public health policy considerations ([para 12]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 5–29])

The court's application centered around the complex interplay between legal duties, wrongfulness, and public policy considerations. The following points were pivotal in the court's reasoning:

- The court emphasized that while negligent actions leading to physical damage are prima facie wrongful, claims for pure economic loss and negligent omissions require a clear legal duty that is informed by public policy ([paras 7–8]).
- Numerous international jurisdictions were canvassed to underline the complexity surrounding "wrongful life" claims, illustrating that the prevailing view dismisses the recognizability of such claims ([paras 12–14]).
- The court articulated the essential dilemma of the case, illustrating the metaphysical question of whether it is preferable for a child to be born with severe disabilities than not to be born at all. The court maintained that this question transcends legal reasoning, tapping into philosophical and ethical territories that the law is ill-equipped to address ([paras 15–28]).
- As for constitutional considerations, while references were made to sections 11, 12, 27, 28(1)(d) and 28(2) of the South African Constitution, the court concluded that advancing these arguments did not satisfactorily delineate a legal path toward liability ([paras 22–27]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 28–30])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, reaffirming that the claim did not disclose a legally valid cause of action. The court held that recognizing such a claim would necessitate assessing the comparative value of life experienced with disabilities versus non-existence, a question deemed inappropriate for adjudication within a legal framework. The ratio decidendi is grounded in the law's respect for life and the significant public policy implications that would emerge from acknowledging liability in such contexts.

This decision has profound implications for the areas of medical negligence, family law, and delict within South Africa. It underscores the ongoing debate regarding the limits of judicial intervention in matters deeply intertwined with ethical and existential questions, reiterating the law's reluctance to engage in inquiries regarding the value of life itself.

The appeal was dismissed with costs, reflecting the court’s acknowledgment of the complexities and sensitivities embedded in such claims. The ruling serves as a significant benchmark for future jurisprudence relating to medical liability and the broader philosophical discussions about the nature of existence and the legal system's role in such assessments.
