Supreme Court of Appeal

Price Waterhouse Meyernel v The Thoroughbred Breeders' Association of South Africa

This case presents a review of taxation in terms of Rule 17 of the Rules of the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2002

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([1–28])

This case presents a review of taxation in terms of Rule 17 of the Rules of the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA). The applicant (Price Waterhouse Meyernel) had previously been the respondent in a successful appeal brought by the respondent (The Thoroughbred Breeders' Association of South Africa) concerning alleged negligence in the performance of an audit. The plaintiff had claimed damages on the basis that the applicant failed to conduct an audit with reasonable care, which resulted in undetected thefts by an employee of the respondent ([1]).

The trial judge ruled that the audit was indeed carried out negligently, leading to a breach of the contractual obligation and awarding damages. It was determined that both parties held a degree of fault, with the plaintiff being found substantially more negligent than the defendant. Subsequently, the trial court considered the Apportionment of Damages Act 34 of 1956, which was contested on appeal by the plaintiff. Ultimately, the SCA upheld the lower court’s finding of negligence on both sides but ruled, by a majority, that the Apportionment of Damages Act was not applicable in this contractual context, thereby awarding the full claimed damages ([2]).

As part of the execution of this judgment, the parties submitted their bills of costs for taxation, including significant claims for counsel's fees and the inclusion of value-added tax (VAT) on these fees ([4–6]). The Taxing Master reduced the senior counsel's fee by 20% but rejected all other objections raised by the defendant ([7]).

The crux of the review revolves around two primary issues: the inclusion of VAT in the claimed costs and the determination of the quantum of fees charged by the plaintiff's counsel ([17]). The Taxing Master declined to consider the VAT objection, stating it was a matter between the plaintiff and the Receiver of Revenue ([9–10]), while also relying on the fees charged by the defendant's counsel as a benchmark for plaintiff's counsel's fees ([11–12]).

Upon examination, the SCA identified a misunderstanding in the Taxing Master's application and determination of fees, stating that it should not merely rely on the opposing party's charges but should account for the intrinsically relevant circumstances surrounding the case ([22–25]). Thus, the court’s task extended to ensuring fair evaluation according to the nature and complexity of the work involved in this substantial matter.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([2–3, 17, 24])

The primary legal questions raised in this case pertain to:

  1. The Applicability of VAT: Whether the VAT claims by the successful litigant can rightfully be included in the taxation of costs when the party has potential recourse to claim VAT as input tax from the revenue authority, and whether such inclusion can be deemed as a legitimate out-of-pocket expense incurred by the party.
  2. Quantum of Counsel's Fees: How to properly assess the quantum of counsel's fees, particularly in extraordinary cases, and whether reliance on opposing counsel's fees as a benchmark is appropriate.

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([18–24])

The court relied on several key legal principles and provisions, notably:

  1. Rule 17 of the Rules of the Supreme Court of Appeal: Specifically, Rule 17(2) permits the inclusion of VAT in costs where it is chargeable, interpreting “may” as an empowering provision for a party to claim but requiring the Taxing Master to determine the appropriateness of such inclusion based on established statutory provisions ([18]).
  2. Value-Added Tax Act 89 of 1991: This Act details the obligations of registered vendors regarding the levying and payment of VAT, identifying the distinctions between output tax (the VAT collected from clients) and input tax (the VAT paid to suppliers), and implications related to cost recovery ([20–21]).
  3. Case Law: Reference was made to the decision in President of the Republic of South Africa v South African Rugby Football Union ([23]), as well as the previous ruling in JD van Niekerk en Genote Ing v Administrateur, Transvaal 1994, which disapproved a purely time-related model for the assessment of legal fees for taxation ([26]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([17–26])

The SCA critically evaluated the Taxing Master’s approach, particularly regarding the VAT inclusion:

  1. VAT Analysis: The SCA determined that VAT payments cannot be characterized as legitimate out-of-pocket expenses if the plaintiff is entitled to recover such VAT as input tax. The Taxing Master’s dismissal of this issue was deemed inappropriate and remitted for reconsideration, clarifying that it is indeed a matter for the court’s determination ([19–22]).
  2. Counsel Fees Evaluation: On the aspect of counsel's fees, the SCA admonished the Taxing Master for relying too heavily on the fees charged by the defendant's leading counsel. It highlighted the necessity to assess counsel's fees based on the actual work and effort involved. The court opined that the circumstances of each party's counsel were significantly disparate due to different levels of familiarity with the case ([25]).

Furthermore, the appeal’s complexity called for a more nuanced approach to fee assessment rather than fixed benchmarks based solely on opposing counsel's charges.

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([27–28])

The Supreme Court of Appeal’s judgment resulted in the following critical findings:

  1. Reconsideration of VAT: The matter regarding the inclusion of VAT is referred back to the Taxing Master for a comprehensive reevaluation in line with the court’s guidance. This sets a precedent concerning the treatment of VAT in cost recovery, emphasizing the necessity for proof that such costs impose a genuine financial burden ([27]).
  2. Counsel's Fees Assessment: The ratio decidendi centers on the importance of evaluating counsel’s fees based on individual case factors rather than a comparative analysis with opposing counsel's charges. The SCA directed that fee assessments in similar extraordinary cases should consider the specific services rendered and the complexity involved ([28]).

The implications of this ruling extend beyond the parties involved, reinforcing the judicial standard for the taxation of costs, particularly in high-stakes litigation where the nature of legal work can be complex and multifaceted. The decision underscores the necessity for thoroughness and fairness in the assessment of legal fees and expenses incurred in pursuit of justice.
