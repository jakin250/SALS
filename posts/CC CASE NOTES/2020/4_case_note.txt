Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note: Mozamane Teapson Maswanganyi v Minister of Defence and Military Veterans and Others [2020] ZACC 4

### F – Facts:
The applicant, Mozamane Teapson Maswanganyi, was a permanent member of the South African National Defence Force (SANDF) since 1 April 2009. In 2014, he was convicted of rape and sentenced to life imprisonment. He appealed against his conviction, but was incarcerated without bail during the appeal. On 13 February 2015, Maswanganyi's conviction was overturned, and he was released from prison.

Following his release, he sought reinstatement by submitting the court order overturning his conviction, but his requests were met with silence from SANDF’s leadership. He subsequently filed an application with the High Court of South Africa for reinstatement to his prior position, along with retroactive payment of his salary and benefits.

The High Court ruled in favor of Maswanganyi, ordering his reinstatement, which was contested by the respondents—the Minister of Defence and Military Veterans, the Chief of the SANDF, and the Secretary for Defence—leading to appeals that reached the Supreme Court of Appeal and ultimately, the Constitutional Court ([paras 1–14]).

### I – Issues:
Two key issues arose in this case:
1. **Jurisdiction:** Did the Constitutional Court have jurisdiction to hear this matter, particularly a possible constitutional issue surrounding fair labor practices ([paras 21–22])?
2. **Interpretation of Section 59(1)(d):** Did the SANDF’s termination of Maswanganyi's employment operate ex lege (by operation of law) upon his conviction, despite his subsequent successful appeal against that conviction? Essentially, did this termination automatically reverse upon the appeal being granted ([paras 21–24])?

### R – Rules:
The relevant legal framework included:
- **Section 59(1)(d) of the Defence Act 42 of 2002**, which mandates termination of a SANDF member's service upon a conviction by a competent civilian court without the option of a fine ([para 2]).
- The **Military Discipline Supplementary Measures Act**, particularly section 42(1), which allows for the suspension of a member’s service pending trial or appeal, and also regulates the processes for employment in the SANDF ([paras 6–12]).
- The **Constitution of South Africa**, particularly section 23(1) (the right to fair labor practices) and section 35(3)(o) (the right to appeal), which were invoked by Maswanganyi ([paras 10–23]).

### A – Application:
The Constitutional Court engaged in a detailed analysis of the statutory interpretation of section 59(1)(d), determining that the law's intent was to ensure that SANDF did not retain members convicted of serious crimes. The Court found that once Maswanganyi's conviction was overturned, he was no longer liable under the statute as the foundational requirements for termination were absent.

The court emphasized that interpreting the section to require automatic reinstatement upon successful appeal adheres to the principles of justice and recognizes the hierarchy in judicial processes as articulated in section 35(3)(o). Thus, it ruled that the procedural safeguards surrounding his employment and rights were compromised due to the misconstrued application of the law by the SANDF's respondents ([paras 36–46]).

Furthermore, the interpretations from both the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal were scrutinized, ultimately determining that the latter had overlooked critical constitutional implications and misapplied legal principles regarding automatic dismissal based on an invalid conviction ([paras 14–19]).

### C – Conclusion:
The Constitutional Court granted Maswanganyi's appeal, ruling that the SANDF's invocation of section 59(1)(d) was unlawful. The Court unequivocally declared that his service with the SANDF had never been terminated and that he continued to be employed in the same capacity as before his conviction, dating back to 18 July 2014.

The implications of this judgment extend beyond the individual case, providing precedent regarding the interpretation of military employment law in the context of criminal convictions. The Court underscored the necessity for SANDF to act in compliance with judicial rulings and to respect fundamental human rights, thereby reinforcing the principles enshrined in the Constitution ([paras 46–51]).
