Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note: De Villiers v Elspiek Boerdery (Pty) Ltd [2017] ZASCA 4

## F – Facts
This matter revolves around a lease agreement (the lease) that was notarially executed on 25 January 2010 and registered against the title deed of a farm named Elspiek, located in the Hex River Valley of Western Cape Province. The appellant, Matthys Pieter Ruben De Villiers (De Villiers), was the registered owner of the property and entered into a 99-year lease agreement with the first respondent, Elspiek Boerdery (Pty) Ltd (Elspiek), represented by Mr G H de Kock (De Kock). This lease commenced on 15 April 2009 ([para 1]).

On 20 August 2012, De Villiers initiated proceedings in the Western Cape Division of the High Court, Cape Town, contesting the validity of the lease, claiming it to be void, and seeking deregistration of the lease and subsequent notarial cession ([para 2]). The trial proceeded before Binns-Ward J, where De Villiers’ attacks on the lease were primarily based on two grounds: firstly, that the agent who executed the lease lacked authority, and secondly, that the lease violated certain prohibitions in the Subdivision of Agricultural Land Act 70 of 1970 ([para 3]).

The trial court concluded that the lease reflected the common intention of both parties, dismissing De Villiers’ claims ([para 5]). De Villiers subsequently appealed the trial court's decision, focusing on the validity of the lease and the agent's authority to bind him ([para 3]).

## I – Issues
The primary issues in this case are:

1. Whether the agent, represented by Ms. Ronelle Miller, had the requisite authority to bind De Villiers in the execution of the lease ([para 3]).
2. Whether sections 3(d) and 3(e)(ii) of the Subdivision of Agricultural Land Act 70 of 1970 prohibited the conclusion of the lease, thereby rendering it invalid ([paras 18, 25]).

## R – Rules
The legal framework governing this dispute includes:

1. **Agency Law**: The authority of an agent to bind a principal is defined by the terms of the power of attorney, which must be clear and unambiguous. The principle of "actus reus" suggests that the actions taken under the aegis of an agent can be deemed binding if the agent's authority is established (see Kerr, *Law of Agency* 4 ed at 66) ([para 12]).
  
2. **Subdivision of Agricultural Land Act 70 of 1970**: 
   - Section 3(d) prohibits the letting of a portion of agricultural land for more than 10 years without the Minister’s consent.
   - Section 3(e)(ii) mandates that no right to a portion of agricultural land shall be granted for a period exceeding 10 years without the Minister’s written consent ([paras 18, 25]).

## A – Application
In determining the authority of the agent, the court emphasized the necessity of the power of attorney's wording ([para 12]). It found the language of the power clear and unambiguous, affirming that Miller had the authority to act on De Villiers’ behalf in executing the lease. The evidence provided during the trial indicated that the attached draft lease had initially been executed to facilitate the registration of pre-emption rights, which had since become obsolete due to a failed sale ([paras 9, 15]). 

Furthermore, the court noted that De Villiers' subsequent actions—such as executing a special power of attorney for cession of the lease—implicitly acknowledged the validity of the lease ([para 16]). 

Concerning the arguments surrounding the agricultural land act, De Villiers claimed that the lease unlawfully involved a portion of agricultural land due to the right retained for his residence ([para 19]). However, the court interpreted the lease as encompassing the entirety of the property in its registered form, thus rejecting the contention that the agreement was contrary to the provisions of the Act. The court's prior ruling in *Adlem & Another v Arlow* substantiated that the property, described as a single economic unit, did not fragment the land’s agricultural integrity simply due to De Villiers' limited right of residence ([para 20]).

Moreover, the court concluded that the conditions laid out in s 3(e)(ii) were inconsequential in this case, as granting De Villiers a right to reside did not constitute a subdivision that necessitated ministerial consent ([paras 25, 28]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, validating the lease agreement and maintaining that both the common intention of the parties and the actions undertaken by De Villiers supported the contract's legitimacy ([para 29]). The implications of this ruling reinforce the significance of direct actions and agreements in the context of agricultural land leases concerning the authority of agents and the regulatory framework that oversees agricultural land use.

The ruling emphasizes that while agency law and statutory regulations govern agreements, clarity in intention and actions can override potential technicalities, thus affirming the importance of practical outcomes in legal disputes ([para 29]).
