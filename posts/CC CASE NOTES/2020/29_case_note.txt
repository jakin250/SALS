Source Folder: 2020

# Case Note: Smit v Minister of Justice and Correctional Services and Others [2020] ZACC 29

## FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–20])
The case arises from two applications following an earlier High Court decision in the Western Cape concerning Jason Smit, the applicant, who is pursued for extradition to the United Kingdom on charges related to cannabis trafficking under the UK's Misuse of Drugs Act 1971 ([1]–[6]). The applicant challenged the constitutionality of two legislative provisions: Section 63 of the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 140 of 1992, which allegedly delegates plenary legislative power to the Minister of Justice, and Section 5(1)(a) of the Extradition Act 67 of 1962, which he claimed strips Magistrates of their discretion in issuing arrest warrants ([6]–[8]).

In August 2015, the applicant filed his application in the High Court aiming to declare both legislative provisions unconstitutional. The High Court found Section 63 unconstitutional for impermissibly delegating legislative power, thus invalidating certain ministerial amendments to drug schedules. However, it upheld Section 5(1)(a) of the Extradition Act, asserting it provided adequate safeguards to ensure judicial discretion was present ([19]–[20]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 21–24])
The court was tasked with confirming the High Court's declaration of invalidity regarding Section 63 of the Drugs Act and evaluating whether Section 5(1)(a) of the Extradition Act contravened constitutional protections against arbitrary deprivation of freedom ([21]–[24]). The specific constitutional questions included:
1. Can Section 63 of the Drugs Act be deemed unconstitutional for delegating plenary legislative power to the executive?
2. Does Section 5(1)(a) of the Extradition Act infringe upon judicial independence and the right not to be arbitrarily deprived of freedom?

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 30–44])
The court evoked the doctrine of separation of powers and the principle of legality from the Constitution, specifically Sections 43 and 44 which confer legislative authority primarily to Parliament ([30]–[32]). The judgment elaborated that plenary legislative powers could not be delegated to the executive. Key precedents in support of these positions included the *Executive Council* case and *Doctors for Life*, which affirm the legislative role as belonging to Parliament, thus underlining the significance of proper legislative procedure in law-making.

In relation to Section 5(1)(a), the court evaluated the need for judicial scrutiny in extradition processes against constitutional rights, emphasizing prior cases like *Harksen* and *Heath*, which stressed the necessity of a judicial examination of the reasons behind deprivations of freedom ([50]–[51]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 45–80])
Regarding Section 63, the court concluded it constituted a constitutionally impermissible delegation of plenary legislative authority to the Minister. It underscored that the process through which substances were added to or removed from drug schedules lacked the necessary legislative oversight and public consultation inherent in parliamentary processes. Thus, the majority opinion held that such delegation eroded the legislative authority of Parliament and violated the Constitution’s separation of powers principle ([39]).

On the issue of Section 5(1)(a), the court articulated a nuanced interpretation, arguing that while Section 5(1)(b) allows room for judicial discretion, 5(1)(a) essentially bound the Magistrate to issue a warrant upon receipt of a ministerial notification without exercising independent judgment. This was found to contravene the requirements of judicial independence and resulted in arbitrary deprivation of liberty, which the court deemed unconstitutional ([72]–[80]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([par 91–96])
The court confirmed the High Court's declarations, affirming that Section 63 of the Drugs Act was unconstitutional as it unconstitutionally delegated plenary legislative powers. It also ruled that Section 5(1)(a) of the Extradition Act was unconstitutional for its failure to afford Magistrates necessary discretion, thus undermining judicial independence. 

Therefore, the court mandated a suspension of the declaration of invalidity for 24 months to allow for legislative amendments, reflecting a recognition of the need for effective law enforcement interlinked with constitutional compliance ([91]–[96]). This judgment reinforces the critical balance between legislative authority and executive action in the South African legal framework and underscores the judiciary’s pivotal role in safeguarding constitutional rights against arbitrary state action.
