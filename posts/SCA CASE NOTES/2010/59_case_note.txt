# FIRAC Case Note: Molotlegi v Mokwalase (222/09) [2010] ZASCA 59

## F – Facts
The case revolves around a defamation action initiated by the respondent, Mosoko Mokwalase, against the appellants, Kgosi Leruo Molotlegi (the first appellant) and the Royal Bafokeng Administration (the second appellant). The first appellant, as the leader of the Royal Bafokeng Nation, allegedly uttered the words: "Mokwalase, you are fired. I don't want to see you again on my premises. You can excuse yourself," during a security meeting held on 13 October 2006 ([1]–[2]).

Following the utterances, the respondent claimed to have been defamed as a result of the context in which the statements were made. He contended that these words implied he was unable to perform his job professionally and was seen as an undesired person on the premises of the Royal Bafokeng Nation ([3], [11]). The appellants sought a separation of issues under Rule 33(4) of the Uniform Rules of Court, requesting the court to focus solely on whether the words were defamatory per se, without considering the context or any special circumstances surrounding the utterance ([4]).

The North West High Court determined that the words were defamatory without evidence regarding the context, which led to an appeal by the appellants on multiple grounds, primarily centering on procedural errors and mischaracterization by the trial court in findings about wrongful publication and intent ([6], [7], [8]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal were:

1. Whether the lower court erred in classifying the statements made by the first appellant as defamatory without evidence regarding the context and the surrounding circumstances of the utterance ([9], [15]).
2. Whether the finding of wrongful publication and animus iniuriandi (intent to defame) was appropriate given that the issue had not been properly submitted for determination as per the Rule 33(4) separation of issues ([8], [10]).
3. The appealability of the lower court's decision, as the parties had yet to lead evidence on remaining disputed issues ([9], [10], [24]).

## R – Rules
The court relied on several legal principles regarding defamation:

1. The need for evidence to establish context when an innuendo is pleaded, as articulated in Hassen v Post Newspapers [1965] 3 SA 562 ([15]).
2. The distinction between words that are defamatory per se and those that require contextual factors to establish defamation, citing authority in Marsay v Dilley [1992] 3 SA 944 (A) ([10]).
3. Rule 33(4) of the Uniform Rules of Court authorizing separation of issues, which mandates that the scope of the issues must be clear to avoid ambiguity and ensure fair proceedings ([20]).

The appealability test was guided by Zweni v Minister of Law and Order [1993] 1 SA 523 (A), which articulates that a non-appealable decision lacks finality or definitive character on the parties’ rights ([22]).

## A – Application
In its analysis, the court reaffirmed that no evidence had been led on the surrounding context during the trial, which was critical to determining whether the words uttered could be understood as defamatory in the sense that the respondent alleged ([13], [14]). The court criticized the lower court's decision for prematurely concluding that the statements were wrongful or defamatory without the requisite evidential basis ([16]).

Further, the attempt to classify the words as defamatory per se was mischaracterized; instead, the court should have evaluated the respondent's claim concerning the context in which the utterances occurred ([17]). The learned Judge President had transgressed the limited scope of the separation agreement, leading to findings on issues not within the original order ([17]).

Overall, the court concluded that the need for context necessitated further examination at the trial level, allowing the respondent the chance to establish evidence supporting his claim of defamation through innuendo as stipulated ([20]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that it was appropriate to set aside the order of the North West High Court. The court found that the judgment at the trial level was flawed due to a lack of evidence regarding the context essential to ascertain whether the statements were defamatory ([18], [21]). 

The appellants were ordered to bear the costs of both the trial and the appeal, as the court emphasized that fairness dictated this outcome considering the implications of the initial procedural missteps ([19], [21]). The matter was remitted to the lower court for further proceedings and a complete examination of the claims, ensuring that the respondent could present necessary evidence regarding the circumstances and context of the alleged defamation ([21]). 

This case reinforces the principles governing defamation claims, emphasizing the critical role of context and evidentiary support in legal determinations regarding defamatory meanings.
