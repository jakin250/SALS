Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2008

# FIRAC Case Note: Sandton Civic Precinct (Pty) Ltd v City of Johannesburg [2008] ZASCA 104

## F – Facts ([paras 1–5])

The case under consideration involves an appeal by Sandton Civic Precinct (Pty) Ltd ("the applicant") against the City of Johannesburg ("the first respondent") concerning the alleged rights to develop a ten-acre piece of publicly-owned land in Sandton. The City disputes the applicant's claim. 

The original resolution relevant to the case was passed on 14 November 2000 by the Eastern Metropolitan Local Council (now represented by the City), which recommended the alienation of the property to the "Sandton Civic Precinct Consortium" for R81.25 million, conditional upon statutory notice and the completion of a sale agreement. However, by November 2001, although a draft agreement was prepared, no formal agreement was concluded. The City later established a property-owning and development company, which led to the cessation of negotiations with the consortium and a recommendation to rescind the original resolution. 

Ultimately, the City resolved on 22 September 2005 not to proceed with the alienation to the consortium, prompting the applicant to challenge the resolution and seek a declaratory order that the first resolution was binding. The High Court dismissed the application with costs, leading to this appeal. 

## I – Issues ([paras 6–9, 20])

The central issues to be resolved by the Supreme Court of Appeal include:

1. **Legal Standing**: Does the applicant have the legal standing to assert rights purportedly conferred by the November 2000 resolution given that the consortium, which was to receive the rights, is not a party to the litigation?

2. **Nature of the Rights**: Did the resolution create enforceable rights capable of cession, and is it subject to review under administrative law?

3. **Procedural Fairness**: If the applicant lacks legal standing, what implications does this have for the authority of the court to determine the matter?

## R – Rules ([paras 10–19])

The legal principles at stake encompass several areas of contract and administrative law. 

1. **Legal Standing**: An entity must demonstrate sufficient and direct interest in the proceedings to warrant its title to prosecute a claim ([para 19]). The inability to show this interest or to establish a lawful claim limits the court’s jurisdiction to adjudicate ([para 20]).

2. **Administrative Action**: A distinction exists between policy formulation and the implementation of laws. The court considers whether the resolution constitutes administrative action, which would be subject to legal review, or merely a policy determination ([paras 6–8]).

3. **Cession of Rights**: Rights acquired within a consortium cannot be unilaterally transferred or asserted without appropriate legal mechanisms, such as a cession agreement or clear authority from all members of the consortium ([paras 11–16]).

## A – Application ([paras 10–19])

In assessing the applicant's legal standing, the court found that it is a private company incorporated after the events pertinent to this case, with only two shareholders. The resolution explicitly conferred rights to a consortium, not to individual shareholders or other entities. 

The applicant's claim relied heavily on the argument that its historical entrenchment in earlier resolutions conferred rights. However, the court noted the absence of the consortium within the litigation, further deepening the concern regarding standing. Dorrestein's assertion that he held shares in trust for absent members was found to be legally ineffectual, as individuals cannot unilaterally divest rights held by others through a trust mechanism. This interpretation aligned with established legal principles that require a clearer channel for the transfer of property rights ([para 18]).

The court underscored that the resolution’s language did not hint at the possibility of substituting members within the consortium or transferring their interests without a mutual agreement. The applicant's insistence on claiming consortium rights was rejected, affirming the principle that legal standing must be robustly substantiated ([para 19]).

## C – Conclusion ([paras 21–26])

The court ultimately concluded that the applicant lacked the standing to assert the rights arising from the resolution. Therefore, the substantive issues regarding the nature of the rights conferred by the resolution became moot, leading to the dismissal of the appeal save for the alteration of the costs order. 

Given the deficiencies in the applicant's claim and the deplorable conduct of the City in communication, the court decided to set aside the costs order from the lower court, ruling that there would be no order as to costs. This reflects the court's condemnation of the City’s conduct in dealing with the applicant, especially considering the public interest vested in the property and the historical context of the First Resolution ([paras 22–25]). 

### Order

1. The application for leave to appeal is granted.
2. The appeal succeeds only to the extent that the costs order of the court below is set aside, and that there is no further order as to costs.
3. The appeal is otherwise dismissed. 

This case emphasizes the importance of legal standing and the necessity for a litigant to possess the appropriate authority to assert any claim, particularly in matters involving public resources and governance.
