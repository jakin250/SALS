Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# Case Note: Lucky Thomas Khumalo v The State (723/20) [2022] ZASCA 39

### FIRAC Method

## Facts
[1] The applicant, Lucky Thomas Khumalo, faced multiple serious charges in the Regional Court of Nigel, including robbery with aggravating circumstances, attempted murder, and contravention of the Firearms Control Act. Khumalo was found guilty and sentenced to a cumulative term of 31 years' imprisonment—15 years for robbery, 10 years for attempted murder, 5 years for possession of an unlicensed firearm, and 1 year for possession of unlicensed ammunition. The sentences were imposed consecutively ([para 1]). 

[2] Following the trial court's dismissal of his application for leave to appeal against both the convictions and sentences, Khumalo escalated the matter to the North Gauteng High Court. This petition was also rejected ([para 2]). Eventually, Khumalo sought special leave to appeal from the Supreme Court of Appeal, which destined him to contest only the sentences, as clarified in the subsequent proceedings ([paras 2-3]).

## Issues
[3] The principal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the High Court erred in its refusal to grant leave to appeal regarding the sentences imposed on the applicant. Specifically, the court needed to determine the presence of reasonable prospects of success in relation to those sentences. While initially suggested that the court should consider the appropriateness of the sentences, the court clarified that its focus would be limited to whether the High Court should have permitted an appeal concerning the sentences imposed by the trial court ([paras 4-5]).

## Rules
[4] The case referred predominantly to established legal principles regarding appeals in criminal proceedings, particularly involving petitions for leave to appeal. Citing *S v Khoasasa* and *S v Matshona*, the court emphasized that the merits of the appeal are not considered by the Supreme Court unless the High Court had first granted such leave ([para 3]). Furthermore, the principles governing the concurrent versus consecutive sentencing were also highlighted—particularly the need for a sentencing court to consider the cumulative impact of multiple convictions arising from a singular episodic event ([para 20]).

## Application
[5] The court conducted an exhaustive analysis of the evidentiary basis underpinning the applicant's convictions to determine the reasonable prospects of success on the merits of the sentence appeal. While the applicant sought to contest the sentences on the assertion that an effective 31-year imprisonment was excessively severe given that all offences stemmed from a single behavioural episode, the State argued that the trial court appropriately considered the applicant’s prior convictions and the violent nature of the offences ([paras 20-21]).

[6] The court reviewed supporting witness testimonies that established a compelling case against the applicant, describing how witnesses consistently corroborated one another's accounts of the violent robbery. The applicant’s defence was perceived as unsubstantiated and inconsistent. Despite the overwhelming evidence against him regarding the convictions, the court did discern a potential for reassessment of the sentence given its cumulative effect, indicating that another court might find just grounds to consider some sentences as running concurrently ([paras 20-19]).

[7] Ultimately, the court expressed its belief that while there were no reasonable prospects of success regarding the convictions, the arguments presented concerning the sentences were compelling enough to potentially warrant a re-evaluation leading to a modification of the effective sentence ([para 22]).

## Conclusion
[8] The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal in part, recognizing that while the applicant possessed no grounds to contest the convictions successfully, he did have reasonable prospects regarding the sentences imposed by the trial court. The judgment rendered nullified the High Court’s previous order and granted leave to appeal solely concerning the sentences, remitting the matter back to the High Court for further deliberation on the cumulative effect of the sentencing ([paras 23]).

This case underscores the crucial distinction within South African criminal procedure between challenging the merits of a conviction versus the appropriateness of sentencing, thus providing clearer guidance on procedural posture for future appellate cases.
