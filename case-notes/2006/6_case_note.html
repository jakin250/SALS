Phumelela Gaming and Leisure Limited v Gründlingh & Others [2006] ZACC 11; CCT 31/05

The Applicant initially sought relief from the High Court, which granted an interdict against the bookmakers to cease taking bets based on Phumelela's published dividend results (para [9]).

Constitutional Court 2006 [2006] ZACC 11; CCT 31/05

F – Facts

The case revolves around a delictual dispute where Phumelela Gaming and Leisure Limited (the Applicant) contended that certain bookmakers, André Gründlingh and Ulrich Osmund Schüler (the First and Second Respondents), were unlawfully exploiting the results of its totalisator system for horse racing to offer competitive betting (para [2]). Phumelela operates as a licensed totalisator across several provinces in South Africa, utilizing a computerized system that aggregates bets and pays dividends based on the amount wagered (para [3]–[6]).

The Applicant initially sought relief from the High Court, which granted an interdict against the bookmakers to cease taking bets based on Phumelela's published dividend results (para [9]). However, on appeal, the Supreme Court of Appeal reversed the High Court's decision, expressing two key findings: (1) the "exotic bets" complained of were deemed "fixed odds bets," and (2) the bookmakers' conduct did not constitute unlawful competition (para [10]). Phumelela subsequently challenged this ruling in the Constitutional Court.

I – Issues

This case presents several legal and constitutional issues:

  1. Did the Supreme Court of Appeal appropriately determine the wrongfulness of the bookmakers' conduct in relation to the delict of unlawful competition?
  2. Should the common law concerning unlawful competition be developed in accordance with the provisions of the Constitution, particularly regarding the protection of intellectual property under section 25 (property rights) and section 39(2) (development of common law) of the Constitution?
  3. Is the constitutional validity of the definition of "open bets" in the National Gambling Act 7 of 2004 inherently flawed, and should Phumelela be permitted to challenge this directly in the Constitutional Court? (para [22]–[23]).

R – Rules

The Court relied on several key legal principles:

  1. Delict of Unlawful Competition: Under South African law, a successful claim requires showing that the competition was wrongful, determined through public policy and the legal convictions of the community (para [12]).
  2. Constitutional Principles: The interpretation and development of common law must align with the spirit and objectives of the Bill of Rights (section 39(2) of the Constitution), stressing the requirement for fairness and justice (para [30]).
  3. Property Rights Protection: Section 25 of the Constitution safeguards individuals from arbitrary deprivation of property, which includes recognition of goodwill as an intangible form of property subject to protection (para [19]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court evaluated the Supreme Court of Appeal's ruling on several grounds:

  1. Public Policy and Wrongfulness: The Court observed that the Supreme Court of Appeal’s application of the wrongfulness test appeared to neglect the Constitution’s normative framework, particularly concerning Phumelela's intellectual property rights. It pointed out that goodwill, as an economic asset, should be considered a form of property according to section 25, impacting assessments of wrongful competition (para [30]–[39]).
  2. Balancing Test: In weighing public interests and competing rights, the Court reiterated that the freedoms of competition and trade, while protected under the Constitution, should not eclipse the rights of a business to protect its intellectual property. The majority judgments of both the Supreme Court of Appeal and the Constitutional Court emphasized that competition, unless deemed unfair or wrongful, is a legitimate business practice, referencing principles surrounding fair play and community standards (para [34]–[39]).
  3. Constitutional Challenge to the Act: On the issue of the constitutionality of the definition of "open bets" in the National Gambling Act, the Court held that Phumelela needed to establish compelling reasons for direct access to challenge the Act's validity, ultimately denying this request. The reasoning was that this matter was not adequately pursued in previous courts (para [50]–[52]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court, while finding merit in Phumelela's application for leave to appeal, ultimately dismissed the appeal against the Supreme Court of Appeal's decision. It concluded that the latter had appropriately determined the legality of the bookmakers' conduct and that there was no arbitrary deprivation of Phumelela’s property rights in contravention of section 25 (para [54]). The Court underscored the expectation for both lower courts and litigants to invoke sections of the Constitution proactively, particularly section 39(2), for the development of common law regarding intellectual property.

This case signifies the necessity of reconciling commercial competition laws with constitutional doctrine, reiterating the importance of protecting intangible assets while also promoting fair market practices. Given that no cost order was made, it illustrates a recognition of the constitutional significance of the issues at stake (para [53]).
