Supreme Court of Appeal

Bogaards v The State (864/10) [2011] ZASCA 196

Initially, Bogaards and his wife were arrested, tried, and convicted at the North Gauteng High Court.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2011 [2011] ZASCA 196

F – Facts ([paras 1-39])

The case revolves around Jacobus Bogaards (the Appellant), who was charged with harbouring and concealing Herman Van Rooyen and Jan Rudolph Gouws, two escapees from a high-profile trial known as the ‘Boeremag trial’. Van Rooyen and Gouws were involved in serious charges including murder and terrorism under the now-repealed Internal Security Act 74 of 1982. They escaped from police custody during a lunch break at the high court trial on 3 May 2006, while in transit between the courtroom and the detention cells. The State alleged that Bogaards harboured these individuals on his farm and failed to report their presence to the authorities, violating sections 11 and 12 of the Protection of Constitutional Democracy Against Terrorist and Related Activities Act 33 of 2004 (the Terrorism Act) and section 115(e) of the Correctional Services Act 111 of 1998 (the Correctional Services Act).

Initially, Bogaards and his wife were arrested, tried, and convicted at the North Gauteng High Court. Bogaards was sentenced to five years' imprisonment while his wife received a suspended sentence. Both appealed their convictions, with the appeal concerning the validity of the underlying charges, specifically whether the escapees were "prisoners" under the law at the time of their escape.

I – Issues ([paras 1-4, 21-28, 38-39])

  1. Validity of Charges: Did the State provide sufficient evidence to support the convictions under the Terrorism Act, particularly regarding whether Gouws and Van Rooyen were "specified offenders" within the statutory definitions?
  2. Definition of Prisoner: Were Van Rooyen and Gouws considered "prisoners" under the Correctional Services Act at the time of their escape, given potential issues with the validity of their detention warrants?
  3. Prosecutorial Authority and Procedure: Was the prosecution in compliance with the requirements of section 16(1) of the Terrorism Act concerning the requisite authorisation for prosecution?
  4. Failure to Report: Did Bogaards fail to report the presence of the escapees on his property, thus contravening section 12 of the Terrorism Act?

R – Rules ([paras 1-9, 10-15, 72-82])

  • Correctional Services Act 111 of 1998: Defines a "prisoner" broadly to include individuals in lawful custody in various detention facilities, including police cells and lock-ups. Section 115(e) criminalizes the act of harbouring or concealing an escaped prisoner.
  • Protection of Constitutional Democracy Against Terrorist and Related Activities Act 33 of 2004:
  • Section 11: Criminalizes the act of harbouring persons known or suspected to have committed specified offences, which includes acts of terrorism.
  • Section 12(1)(b): Requires individuals to report the presence of persons suspected of committing acts of terrorism.
  • Legal Precedent: Statutes, particularly those creating criminal liability, are presumed not to have retrospective effect unless expressly stated. The repeal of the Internal Security Act by the Terrorism Act's section 27 and the transition provisions outlined therein play a crucial role in this case.

A – Application ([paras 10-23, 58-113])

The Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed the validity of Bogaards' conviction under the Terrorism Act. The key finding was that the State failed to prove the presence of Gouws and Van Rooyen as "specified offenders" because the charges they faced under the repealed Internal Security Act would not transfer to the Terrorism Act without clear legislative intention.

The interpretation of the definitions provided in both Acts was critical. The court emphasized that the offences committed before the enactment of the Terrorism Act do not fall under the latter's scope of prosecution, as per the transitional provisions of section 27, which were designed to protect against retrospective application.

Regarding the definition of "prisoner," the court noted that the escapees were not proven to have escaped from a legally defined custody situation at the time. The evidence presented only established their disappearance from court during a lunch adjournment, lacking definitive proof that they were secured in a facility categorized as a "prison" under the law at the time of their escape.

C – Conclusion ([para 19, 113, 115])

The Supreme Court of Appeal set aside Bogaards' convictions for harbouring the escapees under both the Terrorism Act and the Correctional Services Act, concluding that the State failed to demonstrate the essential elements of the offences charged. Specifically, the escapees had not been proven to be "prisoners" within the legal definition at the time of their escape, nor had the State substantiated its claims regarding their alleged prior wrongdoing under the Terrorism Act.

Thus, while there remained serious suspicions concerning Bogaards' actions, the deficiencies in the State's evidence concerning both the applicable law and the facts led to a complete acquittal on the charges, highlighting the critical importance of rigorous adherence to statutory definitions and the principles of criminal liability in judicial proceedings. The implications of this case stress the necessity for clear legislative expressions regarding the transition between laws that govern criminal behavior, especially in matters involving severe allegations such as terrorism.
