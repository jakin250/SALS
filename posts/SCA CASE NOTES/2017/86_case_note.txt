Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: Politis v MEC for Health, Limpopo [2017] ZASCA 86 (792/2016)

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts ([1]–[9])
On 7 August 2010, Ms Zodwa Mmatlaleng Mnisi was involved in a serious motor vehicle accident, resulting in multiple fractures. She was admitted to the Philadelphia Hospital in Limpopo, under the control of the Member of the Executive Council for Health, Limpopo (the MEC). On 19 August 2010, amidst a strike by hospital staff, Ms Mnisi was prematurely discharged. Tragically, she died on the same day, leaving behind two minor children, who became the focus of the subsequent legal action.

The appellant, Advocate Alexander Politis, was appointed as the curator-ad-litem for Ms Mnisi's children and initiated a claim for damages against the MEC for loss of support, amounting to R482,000. The claim hinged on alleged negligence by the hospital, which purportedly included failing to provide adequate medical care and improperly discharging Ms Mnisi ([2]).

The MEC contended that while the discharge may have been premature, it was a necessary action due to the strike conditions which forced the hospital to reduce its operations for safety reasons. The initial ruling by the Gauteng Division of the High Court upheld the MEC's position, finding no negligence attributable to the hospital staff during Ms Mnisi's treatment ([4]).

### I – Issues ([3]–[5], [44]–[46])
The central legal questions are whether the MEC can be held liable for the negligent discharge of Ms Mnisi, and whether the lack of established causation regarding her death prevents a viable claim for loss of support. In specific, the court needed to consider:

1. **Negligence**: Did the medical staff act negligently at the time of discharge given the emergency circumstances?
2. **Causation**: Can it be conclusively determined that the hospital's actions directly caused Ms Mnisi's subsequent death?
3. **Joint liability**: Should the organizers of the strike have been included in the litigation as joint wrongdoers?

### R – Rules ([44]–[50])
The court referenced established principles of delict and vicarious liability, notably capturing the requirements from *Evins v Shield Insurance Co Ltd* regarding a claimant's proof of reliance on the deceased for maintenance. Moreover, the requirements for establishing negligence were directed by *Kruger v Coetzee*, emphasizing foresight and reasonable actions to prevent harm.

Additionally, established legal precedents concerning employer liability during unlawful strikes were examined, particularly the possibility of vicarious responsibility for employees' negligence in their duties during industrial action, as discussed in *K v Minister of Safety and Security* and *F v Minister of Safety and Security*.

### A – Application ([8]–[49])
In determining negligence, the court acknowledged that the discharge occurred under extreme duress caused by a widespread strike, forcing the hospital to follow protocols to protect staff and remaining patients. Although the court accepted that the discharge was premature, it also held that the conflicting evidence on the quality of medical care prior to discharge was not sufficient to establish negligence, particularly since testimony confirmed that Ms Mnisi was stable prior to being sent home ([27], [39]).

Causation, however, emerged as the crux of the case. The post-mortem report’s findings of "polytrauma" were deemed inadequate in linking the hospital's actions to Ms Mnisi's sudden death. Expert testimony, particularly from Dr Isigler, ultimately undermined the claim; while he theorized potential causes for her death, his speculations lacked clinically observable evidence to substantiate them ([47]).

The court highlighted the failure of the appellant's legal team to consult with essential medical experts, such as orthopaedic surgeons and forensic pathologists, which significantly weakened the case’s foundation. This failure to gather persuasive medical evidence resulted in an inability to meet the burden of proving causation, leading to dismissal of the action ([46]).

### C – Conclusion ([52])
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, concluding that there was insufficient evidence to establish negligence on the part of the MEC’s hospital staff, especially in light of extenuating circumstances posed by the strike. Additionally, the lack of reliable evidence directly linking the hospital’s actions to Ms Mnisi's cause of death barred any successful claim for damages arising from loss of support.

The judgment underscores the importance of obtaining comprehensive expert testimony in negligence cases and the complexities surrounding liability when emergency conditions necessitate potentially life-altering decisions within healthcare settings. The implications extend to future claims against healthcare providers involving disputes over hospital discharge procedures amid industrial action.
