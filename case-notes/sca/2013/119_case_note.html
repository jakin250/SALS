Supreme Court of Appeal

Minister of Police v Du Plessis (666/2012) [2013] ZASCA 119

The case analyzes the circumstances surrounding the unlawful detention of Ashwell Du Plessis, following his arrest during an attempted robbery.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 119

F – Facts

The case analyzes the circumstances surrounding the unlawful detention of Ashwell Du Plessis, following his arrest during an attempted robbery. On 28 February 2004, a robbery occurred at Imperial Cargo and Logistics, where police had been alerted of the impending crime. They arrived at the scene post-incident but encountered vehicles fitting descriptions provided by an informant ([2], [3]).

David Bosch, who was previously unaware of the robbery plan, had driven his work colleagues to Wadeville that night. After his vehicle broke down, he contacted Du Plessis for assistance using a borrowed Toyota Corolla ([6], [7]). The police arrived while Du Plessis was attempting to help Bosch, leading to their arrest on suspicion of robbery.

Following the arrest, both Du Plessis and Bosch were subjected to prolonged detention under poor conditions and were charged with armed robbery. Ultimately, Du Plessis was released after the charges against him were withdrawn on 11 March 2004 ([9], [10]).

Subsequently, both individuals filed a lawsuit against the Minister of Police and the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) for unlawful arrest and detention, arguing that no evidence existed to justify their continued detention, especially since Du Plessis was merely an innocent bystander ([10]).

I – Issues

The main legal issues raised in the appeal relate to:

  1. Legal Justification of Continued Detention: Whether the police had sufficient grounds to continue detaining Du Plessis after initial reasonable suspicion was established during his lawful arrest ([11], [14]).
  2. Prosecutorial Duty: The extent of the prosecutors' obligations to review the evidence sufficiently before continuing with charges against the accused, and whether the NDPP erred in proceeding with the charges against Du Plessis despite a lack of evidence ([12], [32]).
  3. Assessment of Damages: If the awarded damages for unlawful arrest and detention were appropriate given the circumstances ([36]).

R – Rules

The court emphasized several legal principles surrounding the issues of unlawful arrest and the duties of law enforcement and prosecutorial authorities:

  • The onus to justify an arrest lies with the police. An arrest constitutes a significant infringement on an individual's liberty, underscoring the necessity for a lawful basis ([14], [15]).
  • The Constitution guarantees the right to freedom and security, stipulating that everyone has the right not to be deprived of freedom arbitrarily ([15], [16]).
  • Section 50 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 outlines that the police’s authority to detain an individual is exhausted once the individual appears in court ([28]).
  • The role of the prosecutor is not merely to represent the state but to uphold justice, requiring them to make informed decisions based on the evidence available ([29], [32]).
  • In the absence of reasonable and probable cause, prosecutions may be deemed improper, establishing grounds for claims of unlawful detention and damages ([30], [31]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal critically analyzed the situation leading to Du Plessis’ detention. It noted that although the initial arrest was reasonable based on the circumstances reported by informants ([11], [20]), the police failed to conduct a thorough investigation following the arrest. The court highlighted that key exculpatory evidence and potential corroboration from Du Plessis’ wife could have easily been pursued but were neglected, resulting in unlawfulness in the subsequent detention ([12], [19]).

Moreover, it was established that by later statements obtained from security personnel and further investigation, it should have become evident that Du Plessis was not involved in the robbery ([21], [22], [24]). The court reaffirmed its position that the knowledge of a detainee’s innocence necessitates release to uphold constitutional rights ([17], [19]).

The prosecutor’s responsibilities were scrutinized, revealing a lack of objectivity and diligence in assessing the evidence. The court found that the prosecution failed to adequately consider the available evidence, which would have precluded the initiation and continuation of charges against Du Plessis ([12], [30], [34]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court's finding that Du Plessis’s continued detention was unlawful and that the police and NDPP had derelicted their duties. The court upheld the damages awarded by Campbell AJ in the High Court, finding them justified given the significant infringement of Du Plessis's rights and the poor conditions of his detention ([35], [36]).

The case illustrates the critical balance between public safety and the protection of individual rights, emphasizing the legal obligation police and prosecutors have to conduct thorough investigations and rational assessments to safeguard personal freedoms. The ruling not only reaffirms obligations upon law enforcement but also underscores the importance of prosecutorial accountability in the legal system.
