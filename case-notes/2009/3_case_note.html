FIRAC Case Note on Willem Stephanus Richter v Minister for Home Affairs and Others [2009] ZACC 3

Constitutional Court 2009 [2009] ZACC 3 #### F – Facts In the lead case *Willem Stepha

FIRAC Case Note on Willem Stephanus Richter v Minister for Home Affairs and Others [2009] ZACC 3

F – Facts

In the lead case Willem Stephanus Richter v Minister for Home Affairs, heard by the Constitutional Court of South Africa, the applicant, Willem Richter, a South African citizen working as a teacher in the United Kingdom, sought to assert his right to vote in the forthcoming national elections scheduled for 22 April 2009. He challenged the constitutionality of section 33 of the Electoral Act 73 of 1998, which restricts the classes of South Africans registered as voters who can cast their votes while absent from the country on polling day. Specifically, the provisions discriminate against voters like Richter, whose absence does not fit within the narrowly defined exceptions allowed under the law [1–2]. Richter initially filed an application in the High Court in Pretoria on 26 January 2009, followed by a direct access application to the Constitutional Court the next day to transfer his case, which was granted on 30 January [2–3]. On 9 February, the High Court declared parts of section 33 unconstitutional, specifically sections 33(1)(b) and 33(1)(e), and certain regulations promulgated thereunder [3].

I – Issues

The core legal question before the Constitutional Court was whether the exclusion of registered voters who are absent from the country on polling day from being able to vote violates the Constitution of South Africa, specifically focusing on the nature of the limitation imposed by the Electoral Act and whether it amounts to unjustifiable discrimination or infringement of rights [1, 4, 44]. Additionally, the Court needed to consider related issues raised by the Democratic Alliance regarding the 15-day notification period and the restriction preventing these voters from participating in provincial elections [49].

R – Rules

The Constitutional framework for evaluating such disputes involves provisions of the Constitution that guarantee the right to vote (Section 19), which serves as a means through which citizens can express their political will. Section 36 of the Constitution permits limitations to rights, provided they are reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society [58–59]. The Court relied on prior rulings, including August v Electoral Commission where the right to vote was described as a “badge of dignity and personhood” [52–53].

A – Application

The Court found that the provisions in section 33(1)(e) limiting the categories of absentee voters represented an unreasonable limitation on the right to vote. The argument put forward by the Minister—offering logistical considerations and upholding electoral integrity—was deemed insufficient to justify the infringement of a fundamental right [60-69]. The Court highlighted the lack of justification for excluding citizens like Richter and others in similar situations from the voting process [72-74], concluding that the law lacked a legitimate purpose sanctioned by the Constitution. Conversely, the stringent requirement for voter notification within 15 days was upheld, as it was found necessary for the operational management of the electoral process [80-84].

Regarding the provision that permitted only national voting and excluded provincial elections for those voting while abroad, the Court found this differentiation unnecessary and administratively burdensome without a justified rationale, leading to a procedural inconsistency in treating voters differently based solely on their location [85-91].

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court issued several consequential orders, including the declaration of invalidity for portions of section 33 that discriminated against voters absent from the country and required that these voters have the right to vote regardless of their absence rationale [100-101]. The Court confirmed that the necessary amendment to the regulations would ensure that all South African citizens who are registered voters could participate in the national elections to be held. The implications of this judgment extend beyond the immediate electoral context, emphasizing the importance of enfranchisement in a truly democratic society. The Court’s decision highlights the ongoing commitment to uphold constitutional rights, particularly in the context of a fluid global landscape where citizenship and residence are not as thoroughly bound by geographical limitations [94-100].
