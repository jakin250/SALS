Constitutional Court

Dladla v City of Johannesburg [2017] ZACC 42

The case concerns a constitutional application initiated by Nomsa Ellen Dladla and ten residents of the Ekuthuleni Shelter against the City of Johannesburg and Metropolitan Evangelical Services (MES).

Constitutional Court 2017 [2017] ZACC 42

F – Facts

The case concerns a constitutional application initiated by Nomsa Ellen Dladla and ten residents of the Ekuthuleni Shelter against the City of Johannesburg and Metropolitan Evangelical Services (MES). The applicants were evicted from their long-term residences at 7 Saratoga Avenue, Berea, and sought temporary housing per the Constitutional Court's directives in Blue Moonlight Properties v City of Johannesburg. Upon moving to the Ekuthuleni Shelter, they were subjected to rules that imposed strict conditions: a "lockout" rule required residents to leave the shelter daily between 08:00 and 17:30, and a "family separation" rule mandated single-sex dormitories, preventing heterosexual partners from cohabiting ([1]–[10]).

The applicants argued these Shelter rules impinged upon their rights to dignity, security of the person, and privacy as per Sections 10, 12, and 14 of the Constitution. The High Court ruled in favor of the applicants, declaring the rules unconstitutional and unjustifiable under the limitations clause ([15]–[21]). Subsequently, the City of Johannesburg appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal, which upheld the City's argument that the rules were reasonable given the nature of temporary accommodation ([22]–[23]).

I – Issues

The court was tasked with determining several key issues ([31]):

  1. Whether leave to appeal should be granted.
  2. If granted, whether the rights enshrined in Sections 10, 12, 14, and 26 of the Constitution extend to temporary residents of shelters.
  3. Whether the "lockout" and "family separation" rules infringed the aforementioned constitutional rights.
  4. If so, whether such infringements constituted a justifiable limitation of those rights under Section 36 of the Constitution.

R – Rules

The relevant constitutional provisions included:

  • Section 10: Right to dignity.
  • Section 12: Right to freedom and security of the person, including protection from arbitrary deprivation of freedom.
  • Section 14: Right to privacy, prohibiting unreasonable search and seizure.
  • Section 26: Right of access to adequate housing, requiring that the state take reasonable measures within its resources to progressively realize this right.

The court also examined the limitations clause under Section 36(1), which allows for rights to be limited by a law of general application provided the limitation is reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society ([20]–[21]).

A – Application

In assessing the constitutionality of the Shelter's rules, the court highlighted that the interrelationship between the accommodation provided by the City and the imposed rules was central. The applicants' rights were deemed to be violated by the lockout rule, which limited their movements and left them vulnerable to violence during daytime hours. The Court emphasized that the forced separation of families contravened the principle of dignity foundational to both family units and individual liberty ([47]–[49]).

The court further identified that the rules had been imposed by MES as part of the City's managed-care policy but concluded these rules lacked a sufficient legal basis. They were not enacted by a law of general application, a necessary precursor to justifying limitations under Section 36. The Court stressed that even though temporary accommodation has specific conditions, such provisions must still respect the fundamental rights enshrined in the Constitution ([52]–[53]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court set aside the ruling of the Supreme Court of Appeal, restoring the High Court’s findings on the unconstitutional nature of the Shelter rules. It held that:

  1. The application of Shelter rules constituted an infringement of the applicants' rights to dignity, freedom, and security of the person, and privacy.
  2. The City of Johannesburg and MES were prohibited from enforcing these rules against the applicants during their stay at the Shelter.
  3. The applicants were entitled to reside with their partners of different sexes in communal rooms, thus reaffirming their rights as residents in temporary accommodation.

The implications of this judgment extend to future policy implementations regarding temporary shelters, indicating that all measures taken must simultaneously safeguard constitutional rights ([54]). The City was also ordered to cover the applicants’ costs, reinforcing accountability for state actions that violate the rights of vulnerable populations ([54]).
