Giesecke & Devrient Southern Africa (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Safety and Security

Supreme Court of Appeal 2011

F - Facts

The case revolves around an armed robbery that occurred on September 5, 2004, at the Monte Casino in Johannesburg, where approximately R24 million was stolen from a cash processing center operated by the appellant, Giesecke & Devrient Southern Africa (Pty) Ltd (hereinafter referred to as "G&D") [1]. Following the robbery, investigations revealed that a casino employee, Solomon Dube, was implicated in the crime and subsequently accused Police Inspector William Kgathi and other members of the South African Police Services (SAPS) of colluding with the robbers [2–3].

Subsequently, G&D filed a lawsuit against the Minister of Safety and Security, claiming vicarious liability for the conduct of the implicated police officers [2]. The main claim sought R23,914,610, and an alternative claim sought R4.2 million, alleging that police officers unlawfully appropriated the recovered amounts from the robbers instead of returning them to G&D [4]. Initially, the South Gauteng High Court dismissed both claims, leading to an appeal [4].

The factual background of the robbery indicated that after the incident, Dube initially claimed he was a victim but later confessed to his involvement and named individuals, including Kgathi, as participants in the crime [8–10]. Dube provided conflicting accounts, recanting his statements under various circumstances, including fears of police retribution [11–12]. In total, there were significant disputes regarding the integrity of the officers involved and the recovery of the stolen money [8-19].

I - Issues

The central issues in this appeal include:

  1. Admissibility of Hearsay Evidence: The court must determine whether hearsay statements made by Dube and Gumede, which were not presented in person due to their absences, can be admitted under Section 3(1)(c) of the Law of Evidence Amendment Act 45 of 1988 [23].
  2. Vicarious Liability: The case brings into question whether the Minister of Safety and Security can be held vicariously liable for the alleged wrongful conduct of the police officers during the investigation of the robbery [39].
  3. Determination of the Main and Alternative Claims: The appeal must ascertain whether G&D has proven its claims, given the ruling against it in the court a quo and the impact of the evidentiary issues raised [35].

R - Rules

The legal framework applicable to the issues at hand includes:

  1. Law of Evidence Amendment Act 45 of 1988: particularly Section 3(1)(c), which stipulates conditions under which hearsay evidence may be admitted, focusing on the interests of justice, the nature and purpose of the evidence, and its probative value [26].
  2. Civil Proceedings Evidence Act 25 of 1965: which includes provisions regarding the admissibility of evidence in civil proceedings [25].
  3. Vicarious Liability Precedents: South African case law, particularly "Minister van Veiligheid en Sekuriteit v Japmoco BK h/a Status Motors" 2002 (5) SA 649 (SCA) defines the contours of state vicarious liability when employees act within the scope of their employment [39].

A - Application

In addressing the admissibility of hearsay evidence, the court found that the lower court improperly excluded statements made by Dube and Gumede. The appellate court noted that the criterion for admissibility under Section 3(1)(c) focuses on whether exclusion would serve justice, taking into account factors such as the unavailability of witness testimony and the nature of the evidence presented [26-31]. The court critiqued the lower court's reasoning, indicating that factors such as the credibility of absconding witnesses should not unduly inhibit the admission of evidence that may reveal significant truths [29-34].

Regarding the main claim, the court ultimately determined that there was insufficient evidence to substantiate G&D's assertion that Kgathi was either directly involved in the robbery or had advance knowledge of it. The statistical evidence, though suggestive, was insufficient on its own, particularly since Kgathi was shown via video footage to have been in a separate area during the robbery [36]. Furthermore, conflicts in the testimonies of Dube and Gumede were deemed too speculative to rely upon for the main claim [36-37].

In contrast, the alternative claim found higher substantive support upon re-evaluation, particularly with the admission of previously excluded statements. The court recognized that if the allegations regarding police misconduct were proven, it would invoke vicarious liability, given that the officers were acting within their official capacities but without proper adherence to police conduct standards [39].

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that while the dismissal of the main claim was confirmed, the lower court's rejection of the alternative claim was overturned, as the hearsay statements of Dube and Gumede were deemed admissible and potentially critical for establishing a case of vicarious liability [40]. The matter was remitted to the trial court for further consideration regarding the alternative claim, allowing the respondent the opportunity to reopen the case and introduce rebuttal evidence [40]. This ruling significantly redefined the parameters for the admission of hearsay in civil litigation, emphasizing the judiciary's commitment to 'the interests of justice.' The case serves as a pivotal reference point for future litigations involving police misconduct and the admissibility of hearsay evidence in South Africa.
