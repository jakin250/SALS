Supreme Court of Appeal

Dhlamini v Schumann, Van den Heever & Slabbert Inc and Others

The case of Dhlamini v Schumann, Van den Heever & Slabbert Inc and Others arises from a motor vehicle accident involving the appellant, Thandi Caroline Dhlamini, in September 2014, in which she sustained severe injuries ([para 2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023

FIRAC Method

F – Facts [Paras 1–10]

The case of Dhlamini v Schumann, Van den Heever & Slabbert Inc and Others arises from a motor vehicle accident involving the appellant, Thandi Caroline Dhlamini, in September 2014, in which she sustained severe injuries ([para 2]). In January 2015, Dhlamini engaged the law firm Schumann, Van Den Heever & Slabbert Inc (the first respondent) to pursue a claim for damages against the Road Accident Fund (the seventh respondent). The claim was settled for R583,454.90, from which she received about R400,000 ([para 2]).

In 2019, after being tipped off about potential fraudulent conduct by her lawyers, Dhlamini sought an Anton Piller order to access files relevant to her and other claims against the Road Accident Fund ([para 4]). The respondents denied any wrongdoing but offered to release Dhlamini's files and those of a few supporting claimants while asserting that the files of other claimants belonged to the Legal Practice Council (LPC) ([para 5]).

An interim Anton Piller order was granted on March 24, 2020, allowing the identification and preservation of potentially fraudulent evidence ([para 6]). However, the high court eventually struck the application from the roll on July 7, 2020, declaring that the Anton Piller order lapsed because the required action, a class action lawsuit, was not instituted within the stipulated 30 days ([paras 9–10]).

I – Issues [Paras 11–14]

The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the appeal against the high court's order was moot due to a tender by the respondents to release the requested documents. Specifically, the court had to determine whether a live controversy still existed, or if the appeal had effectively been rendered moot by the respondents’ compliance ([para 11]).

Additionally, the court needed to assess the legality and fairness of the high court’s costs order, as both parties had agreed that costs were to be determined through further proceedings, not at the time the high court issued its ruling ([para 10]).

R – Rules [Paras 13–17]

The legal framework applicable to the case includes Section 16(2)(a) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which allows for the dismissal of an appeal on the grounds of mootness when the outcome would have no practical effect ([para 13]). The court elaborated that mootness arises when there is no existing or live controversy, as articulated in National Coalition for Gay and Lesbian Equality v Minister of Home Affairs ([para 12]).

Furthermore, the court recognized that in exceptional circumstances related to costs, the analysis of mootness might consider whether costs incurred in the lower court were unjustly awarded, which could allow the court to exercise its discretion ([para 15]).

A – Application [Paras 14–18]

The court analyzed the tender made by the respondents to deliver the documents. It concluded that, once the respondents expressed their intent to release the files, the fundamental objective of the Anton Piller application was satisfied, thus rendering the case moot ([para 14]). The court highlighted that Dhlamini could still pursue her intended litigation against the respondents, despite the high court's prior ruling.

However, the court found that exceptional circumstances warranted a review of the costs awarded by the high court. The court noted that no argument was presented on costs at the time of the high court ruling, and the costs issues were anticipated in the further proceedings related to the class action, thus invalidating the high court’s costs order as it lacked a sound basis ([paras 15–16]).

Ultimately, the Supreme Court set aside the high court's costs order, indicating the necessity for judicial discretion to be exercised appropriately, particularly when relevant arguments were not presented previously ([para 16]).

C – Conclusion [Para 18]

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, emphasizing that each party should bear its own costs. Their ruling effectively nullified the high court's order and specified that the matter was to be struck from the roll, highlighting the procedural propriety expected in handling costs ([para 18]). The case accentuates the significance of adherence to procedural norms in judicial processes and sets a precedent concerning the grounds for mootness in appeal considerations and the nature of costs orders in legal proceedings.

The implications of this ruling serve to clarify when appeals can be considered moot and the circumstances under which costs may still be contested despite a broader absence of material dispute in the underlying issue.
