Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: University of the Free State v Afriforum & Others [2017] ZASCA 32

## F – Facts
In **University of the Free State v Afriforum & Others** ([1]), the case revolves around the University of the Free State's (UFS) decision to adopt a new language policy in March 2016, which replaced the parallel-medium instruction in Afrikaans and English with English as the primary medium of instruction. The decision was made after the UFS encountered issues with the previous dual-language model, which had contributed to racial segregation among students ([3]-[10]).

The context of this case is significant in South Africa, where Afrikaans is recognized as one of the official languages and has a rich cultural history ([2]). The decision to focus predominantly on English was met with concerns from the respondents, Afriforum and Solidarity, who argued that such a move would undermine Afrikaans's status as a language of instruction and, by extension, its cultural significance. The respondents successfully challenged the language policy before the Free State Division of the High Court, which ruled that the decision constituted unlawful administrative action under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([1]-[14]).

UFS subsequently appealed this ruling. The court had to consider whether the decision to adopt the new policy constituted administrative action, if UFS had misconstrued its power, and whether the language policy was 'reasonably practicable' as stipulated in Section 29(2) of the South African Constitution ([15]-[16]).

## I – Issues
The primary issues that arose in this case can be summarized as follows:

1. **Whether UFS’s decision to adopt the new language policy was administrative action under PAJA** ([16]).
2. **Whether UFS misconstrued its powers in the formulation of the new language policy** ([21]-[22]).
3. **Whether the new policy was ‘reasonably practicable’ as per Section 29(2) of the Constitution** ([22]-[23]).
4. **Whether the University was obliged to adhere to the Higher Education Language Policy outlined in Section 27(2) of the Higher Education Act 101 of 1997** ([33]-[34]).

These questions aimed to investigate the balance between promoting linguistic diversity while also addressing racial inequalities in education.

## R – Rules
The case implicates several legal principles and statutory frameworks:

1. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**:
   - Defines 'administrative action' and sets out procedural fairness and reasonableness ([16]).
   - Section 1 of PAJA stipulates what constitutes administrative action ([16]).

2. **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996**:
   - **Section 29(2)**: Grants the right to receive education in the official language or languages of choice where this is 'reasonably practicable' ([22]).
   - Establishes the framework for assessing educational rights in the context of linguistic diversity ([29]).

3. **Higher Education Act 101 of 1997**:
   - **Section 27(2)**: Provides the Minister of Higher Education authority to establish the language policy for public higher education institutions and emphasizes the importance of multilingualism ([34]).

4. **Case Law**: Precedent was examined from various cases that dealt with administrative actions, such as *Hoërskool Ermelo* and *Pharmaceutical Manufacturers Association of South Africa* ([19]-[22]).

## A – Application
In addressing the issues, the court differentiated between administrative and executive decisions. The panel argued that the adoption of the policy was an executive rather than administrative decision because it involved the formulation of policy rather than the implementation of a pre-existing one ([18]-[19]). Consequently, the decision did not qualify under the PAJA’s definition of administrative action.

The court also emphasized the rationality requirement inherent in the exercise of public power, emphasizing that UFS's decision must be logically connected to the goals for which its powers were conferred. The court noted that UFS conducted extensive research and consultations before adopting the policy, evidencing a rational connection between the decision and its objectives of combating racial segregation within the university ([29]-[30]).

The ruling elaborated that the respondents' argument concerning 'reasonable practicability' hinged on a narrow interpretation of Section 29(2). UFS contended that the requirement was contingent not only upon practical considerations but also on the quality of educational access and the need to foster integration ([24]-[27]). The court accepted this interpretation, asserting that the practical application of the right to language instruction must resonate with prevailing social contexts, including racial dynamics.

The court also examined the implications of Section 27(2) of the Higher Education Act, determining that the stipulations of the Higher Education Language Policy did not impose rigid constraints on UFS’s discretion to formulate its language policy to suit the evolving demographic and educational landscape ([39][40]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, overturning the previous high court ruling. It determined that UFS's decision to adopt the new language policy was not constitutive of administrative action under PAJA and thus did not warrant the review sought by the respondents ([44]).

The court underscored the university's autonomy to evaluate and reform its language policies in ways that reflect the changing demographics and educational needs of its students. It concluded that the decision to transition to English as the primary medium of instruction was a rational response to the imperatives of social integration and equity in education.

This ruling has significant implications, not only for UFS but for other universities navigating similar tensions between linguistic rights and the imperatives of transformation in post-apartheid South Africa. The court clarified that institutions must be allowed freedom and discretion in formulating policies that balance cultural inclusivity against the need for integration ([45]).

### Order
1. The appeal in the review application was upheld with costs.
2. The appeal in the strike-out application was also upheld, with costs awarded on a scale as between attorney and client ([46]). 

The University of the Free State’s language policy was thus affirmed as a permissible exercise of its institutional governance powers, fostering an environment conducive to racial integration and linguistic diversity in higher education.
