Supreme Court of Appeal

Mbungela & Another v Mkabi & Others [2019] ZASCA 134

The first respondent initiated proceedings seeking a declaration of a valid customary marriage, compelling the Minister of Home Affairs to register this marriage ([para 2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 134

F - Facts

The matter involved an appeal from the Gauteng Division of the High Court concerning the validity of a customary marriage between Madala Philemon Mkabi (the first respondent) and the deceased Ms Ntombi Eunice Mbungela (the deceased). The crux of the appeal arose from the failure of the deceased's family to conduct a traditional “handing over” ceremony, which is commonly associated with customary marriages ([paras 1–2]).

The first respondent initiated proceedings seeking a declaration of a valid customary marriage, compelling the Minister of Home Affairs to register this marriage ([para 2]). The appellants, Piet Mbungela (the deceased’s brother) and Thobile Carol Mkhonza (the deceased’s daughter), contested the validity on grounds that the necessary elements of customary marriage as defined by section 3(1)(b) of the Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998 (the Act) were not fulfilled ([para 3]).

According to testimony, Mkabi and the deceased were in a long-term relationship, which included successful negotiations for lobola. Although R9,000 was paid, it was undisputed that the total lobola was R12,000 and the subsequent formal handing over of the bride did not occur — a pivotal customary rite ([paras 5–7]). Despite these omissions, Mkabi presented evidence of a culturally recognized marriage with support from both families that attended each other's significant funerals ([paras 8–10]).

The trial court found in favor of Mkabi, concluding that the absence of the ceremonial "handing over" did not invalidate their marriage due to the evolving nature of customary law and other fulfilled requirements, such as consent and lobola negotiation ([paras 15–16]). The items exchanged during their matrimonial negotiations and the support from both parties’ families were deemed sufficient by the trial court to denote a valid marriage ([paras 22–30]).

I - Issues

The central legal issue in this case revolved around whether the customary marriage between Mkabi and the deceased was valid, given the absence of the complete ceremonial and ritualistic markings traditionally required under customary law, specifically the "handing over" of the bride ([para 1]).

The constitutional questions raised involved the fluidity of customary law and whether strict adherence to traditional practices, like bridal transfer, could be relaxed in accordance with the evolving cultural norms in contemporary society ([paras 18–20]).

R - Rules

The applicable legal framework can be found in section 3(1)(b) of the Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998, which posits that for a customary marriage to be valid, it must be negotiated, entered into, or celebrated according to customary law, though it does not specify the necessity of each traditional ritual ([para 16]).

The court referred to various precedents affirming the idea that customary law is not static, but instead evolves and flexibly adapts to modern societal contexts ([para 17]). The dynamic nature of customary law was underscored, emphasizing that courts must consider both historical customs and current practices of the communities involved ([paras 18–19]).

Particular attention was drawn to authoritative statements from earlier cases such as Mabuza v Mbatha and Bhe v Magistrate, Khayelitsha, which highlighted the flexibility in customary practices and cautioned against rigid interpretations that could invalidate marriages based solely on unfulfilled ceremonial rites ([paras 21–28]).

A - Application

The court applied these principles by first assessing the factual background of the relationship, noting the mutual consent for marriage, informal cohabitation, and participation of both families in the customary practices leading up to the marriage ([paras 22–30]).

Mkabi's position was fortified by evidence detailing interactions between both families and the acknowledgment of the relationship as a marriage through various means, including communication records and family recognitions. The trial court's credibility findings favored Mkabi, dismissing the testimony of the appellants as evasive and inconsistent ([para 15]).

In particular, the court addressed the necessity of bridal transfer, concluding that the practice could be waived without invalidating the marriage. This conclusion was based on the understanding that both families were involved in and supportive of the union despite the absence of the complete traditional ceremonies that void claims to customary marriage validity ([paras 27–30]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the trial court’s decision, affirming that Mkabi and the deceased entered into a valid customary marriage, and the appeal was dismissed with costs ([paras 30–31]).

The ruling underscored the transformative nature of customary law, emphasizing the modern recognition of marriages that respect individual and family consent beyond rigid ritualistic expectations. The implications of this ruling affirm that customary weddings can be recognized through means and definitions that adapt to contemporary societal frameworks, promoting justice and reducing the vulnerability of parties in customary unions while adhering to constitutional mandates of equality and dignity. This case establishes a significant precedent in South African customary law, guiding how similar cases may be approached in the future.
