Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

### Case Note: Cloete and Another v S; Sekgala v Nedbank Limited [2018] ZACC 6

---

**FIRAC Format**

---

**F – Facts**:   
The case involves two separate applications for leave to appeal decided by the Constitutional Court of South Africa on 19 February 2019. The first application (CCT 324/17) involves Mr. Mathys Johannes Cloete and his son, Mr. Daniel Nicholas Cloete, who were convicted in the High Court for murder, kidnapping, and attempted murder. Following their convictions, their applications for leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) were unsuccessful. They subsequently sought reconsideration from the President of the SCA under section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013; this was initially dismissed but later granted a hearing, only to be dismissed again as res judicata due to prior decisions ([paras 3-6]).

In the second application (CCT 63/18), Mr. Rammutlana Boelie Sekgala faced a dispute with Nedbank Limited, stemming from a mortgage default. Nedbank obtained a default judgment against Sekgala for arrear payments, after which he sought rescission of that judgment, which was denied. His appeal to the SCA for leave to appeal was successive and declined. Sekgala also appealed to the President under section 17(2)(f), which was dismissed for lacking exceptional circumstances ([paras 8-10]).

Both applicants sought the Constitutional Court's ruling on the appealability of the President’s decisions under section 17(2)(f) of the Act, leading to a unified examination of jurisdictional queries regarding these applications ([paras 1-2]).

---

**I – Issues**:  
The primary legal issue focuses on whether a decision made under section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act is appealable to the Constitutional Court. Additionally, the parties raised questions about the nature of the President’s decisions under this subsection, whether they constitute judicial decisions, and the potential for a dual appeal process ([paras 1-3]).

---

**R – Rules**:  
The Constitutional Court's jurisdiction is framed within section 167 of the Constitution, which permits appeals based either on constitutional issues or arguable points of law, when in the interests of justice. Section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act allows the President of the SCA to refer a refusal of an application for leave to appeal to the SCA for reconsideration, themselves not forming part of the composite appeal process ([paras 22-24]). Relevant case law includes *Liesching I* and *Liesching II*, which asserted that the President exercises judicial discretion in these matters, thereby establishing parameters for reviewing decisions ([paras 15-19]).

---

**A – Application**:  
The Court, led by Justice Theron, examined the nature of the President's decisions under section 17(2)(f), ultimately concluding that they do not typically amount to final decisions suitable for appeal. It evaluated whether the jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court is engaged by asserting that appeals against factual determinations made by the President do not generally rise to constitutional questions necessary for this Court’s intervention ([paras 36-40]). Furthermore, the Court stressed that the section 17(2)(f) decisions lack finality, as they serve as a procedural mechanism to facilitate reconsideration of applications ([paras 41-46]). Consequently, the majority found that ordinarily, these decisions are not appealable to the Constitutional Court, which should require litigants to exhaust available remedies before approaching it on the merits ([paras 47-63]).

In evaluating both applications in line with the findings, the Court endorsed consistent principles regarding finality and the redundancy of a duplicative appeal pathway, effectively dismissing both applications for leave to appeal ([paras 64-66]).

---

**C – Conclusion**:  
The Constitutional Court unanimously dismissed both applications for leave to appeal, asserting that decisions under section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act are not appealable as they do not constitute final judgments. The Court affirmed that applicants have accessible recourse through the appeal process against decisions on merits in the relevant lower courts. This ruling reinforces the procedural integrity of appellate functions while ensuring efficient use of judicial resources, thus stabilizing litigation processes. The Court ultimately held that there would be no order as to costs, reflecting the consensus in both matters ([paras 66-67]).

---

### Final Observations

The implications of this ruling are significant for future litigants who may wish to consider the procedural landscape in which they navigate judiciary processes. The decision illustrates a structured approach towards jurisdictional elasticity within South African constitutional law, confirming the necessity for clarity in the nature of judicial discretion exercised by appellate bodies. The reaffirmation of standard procedural avenues ensures a balanced system whereby appeals are grounded on substantial legal tenets rather than procedural ambiguity.
