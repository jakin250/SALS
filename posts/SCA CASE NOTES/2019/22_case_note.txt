Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: Viljoen v S (663/18) [2019] ZASCA 22

### F – Facts
In the matter of **Viljoen v S**, the appellant, Nico Viljoen, was convicted in the Port Nolloth Regional Court on three counts of purchasing unpolished diamonds, in contravention of section 20 of the Diamonds Act 56 of 1956. The court imposed a sentence of three years’ direct imprisonment along with a fine of R160,000, which was suspended for four years, contingent upon no further contraventions of the Diamonds Act ([1]–[2]). 

The appellant was arrested as part of **Operation 2081**, a police initiative designed to combat the illicit trade in diamonds. During this operation, the police utilized traps and undercover operations sanctioned by the Director of Public Prosecutions under section 252A of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([3]). 

The transactions transpired in 2005 at Scotia Inn, a hotel owned by the appellant, where he bought diamonds from Inspector Leon Ferris, an undercover police officer. All three sales were recorded. The defence did not dispute the content of these recordings, acknowledging them as accurate representations of the events ([4]). 

The appellant's co-accused, Esmeralda Losper, was implicated as she introduced him to Ferris. Allegations were made by the appellant that Ferris's actions amounted to an entrapment beyond merely providing an opportunity to commit an offence, claiming that Ferris had exploited his relationship with Losper to lure him into committing the unlawful acts ([6]). Despite these claims, the trial court deemed Ferris's evidence admissible following a trial-within-a-trial to examine the allegations of entrapment ([7]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal questions before the court included:

1. Whether the interactions between Ferris and the appellant constituted entrapment that went beyond merely providing an opportunity to commit an offence ([6]–[8]).
2. Whether the evidence obtained during the undercover operation should be deemed admissible ([9]).
3. The adequacy of the State's proof regarding the sale of unpolished diamonds, in light of the appellant’s claims ([8]).

### R – Rules
Key legal provisions and precedents critical to this case included:

- **Section 20 of the Diamonds Act 56 of 1956**: This section prohibits the purchase of unpolished diamonds without the necessary licenses or permissions.
- **Section 252A(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977**: This provision outlines the framework for the admissibility of evidence obtained through undercover operations, stating that the evidence is admissible if police conduct does not exceed merely providing an opportunity to commit an offence. If it does exceed this threshold, the court must evaluate how the evidence was obtained ([9]).

In *S v Kotzé*, the Supreme Court of Appeal elaborated that two approaches exist regarding the admissibility of evidence obtained via traps: automatic admissibility if the trap was limited to opportunity provision, and a discretionary assessment if conduct was more intrusive ([10]). The court must consider numerous factors under section 252A(2) in evaluating whether an undercover operation exceeds its proper bounds ([11]).

### A – Application
The court engaged in a thorough evaluation of the evidence in light of the principles laid out in the aforementioned sections. In assessing the conduct of Ferris, the court determined that he did not induce the appellant into purchasing the diamonds through coercion or deception beyond the scope of merely creating an opportunity for criminal conduct. Notably, it was established that Ferris did not initiate the first sale; this was arranged by Losper, who had prior knowledge of illicit dealings involving the appellant ([12]).

The court scrutinized the timing and nature of the transactions, indicating that the appellant, after conducting his checks on the diamonds, displayed eagerness to engage in the sales. The appellant was deemed savvy regarding the characteristics of diamonds, thereby undermining claims that he was coerced by the rapid nature of the transactions ([13]). Moreover, the market values of the diamonds sold did not suggest that the sales were artificially restricted, indicating a nature of voluntary participation ([15]).

The court upheld that the appellant had ample opportunity to withdraw from the transactions yet chose to proceed, further affirming the propriety of Ferris's conduct under section 252A([14]).

### C – Conclusion
The ultimate conclusion by the court was that the appellant's appeal against his conviction was dismissed. The analysis of the undercover operation found that Ferris's conduct fell within acceptable limits, only providing opportunities rather than compelling actions. Moreover, the trial court's decision regarding sentence was upheld, emphasizing that a trial court has discretion in its sentencing that should not be overturned unless improperly exercised ([16]). 

Given the serious nature of the offences related to diamond smuggling, the court’s affirmance of the sentence reflects an insistence on rigorous enforcement in the Northern Cape, signifying a judicial commitment to deter similar future offences ([16]). 

In summary, the case emphasizes the delicate balance between law enforcement tactics and individual rights, particularly within the ambit of undercover operations, contributing significantly to discussions of legality and ethics in criminal procedure.
