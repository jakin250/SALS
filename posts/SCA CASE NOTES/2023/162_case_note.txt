Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: Member of the Executive Council for Health and Social Development of the Gauteng Provincial Government v Motubatse & Another (182/2021) [2023] ZASCA 162

## F - Facts
The case originated from a damages claim for R29,158,000 instituted by Elizabeth Mamanthe Motubatse and Andries Mokganyetsi Motubatse against the Member of the Executive Council (MEC) for Health and Social Development of the Gauteng Provincial Government. The respondents, acting as parents and guardians, contended that their minor child sustained cerebral palsy due to the negligence of the MEC's employees during birth ([para 3]).

The MEC defended the claim and denied liability. Following the closure of pleadings, the respondents sought discovery of hospital records, which the MEC failed to provide, resulting in a compelling order issued by the court. Despite this order, the MEC did not comply, leading to a striking order against her defense, allowing the respondents to seek default judgment ([para 4]).

On the eve of the respondents’ application for default judgment, the MEC sought an order to rescind the striking order, asserting that her employees were still locating the requested documents and maintaining she had a bona fide defense ([para 4]). The court of first instance, dissatisfied with the MEC's compliance issues, rescinded the striking order and allowed her defense ([para 5]). Subsequently, the full court dismissed the MEC's appeal, which prompted her application for special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 1–5]).

## I - Issues
The central issues in this case include:
1. Whether the full court's dismissal of the MEC's appeal constituted a reviewable error in light of the principle that a rescission order, being interlocutory, is not appealable ([para 2]).
2. The appropriate considerations for granting condonation for the late filing of documents in the context of procedural compliance failures by the MEC ([paras 6–11]).

## R - Rules
The Supreme Court of Appeal reaffirmed established legal principles regarding appealability, particularly that a rescission order is interlocutory in nature and, thus, not appealable. This is consistent with the ruling in *Zweni v Minister of Law and Order of the Republic of South Africa*, which requires a testing framework for appealability involving finality, definitive rights of the parties, and substantial relief claims ([paras 2, 10]).

Procedural rules pertaining to condonation are guided by the need for satisfactory explanations for delays, where ignorance of the rules typically does not suffice ([para 11]). Additionally, the court may consider whether a case presents special circumstances warranting leave to appeal, including the significance of the legal issue and the potential for a manifest denial of justice ([para 12]).

## A - Application
The Supreme Court reviewed the procedural history and grounds presented by the MEC for her late filing of the notice of appeal, record of appeal, and related documents. It noted that the rationale for the MEC's delays—including staff turnover and confusions with case management—were deemed unsatisfactory. The court stressed that endeavors to comply with procedural mandates should not only exist but should also demonstrate a genuine effort at compliance ([paras 7, 11]).

On the substantive appeal, the court highlighted that the full court had improperly exercised its discretion regarding the appealability of the rescission order. The importance of adhering to precedents underlined in *Camps Bay Ratepayers and Residents Association & Another v Harrison & Another* was pivotal, as this divergence threatened the stability of procedural law, thereby necessitating Supreme Court intervention ([para 13]). Accordingly, the Supreme Court articulated that while procedural compliance remains crucial, the case presented a discrete legal question of substantial public interest regarding the appealability of rescission orders ([para 13]).

## C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately ruled in favor of the MEC, granting the necessary condonation for her late filings, reinstating her application for special leave to appeal, and upholding the appeal on substantive grounds. This judgment reaffirmed the standing legal framework surrounding the appealability of rescission orders and addressed the procedural lapses by the MEC whilst maintaining the necessity for adherence to court rules ([paras 14–16]).

On costs, the court took an unusual stance, noting the MEC’s noncompliance history. Despite her successful appeal, the court decided to order that the MEC bear her own costs while the respondents would only cover the costs related to their delayed filing of heads of argument, reflecting discontent with the MEC’s procedural conduct ([para 16]).

The implications of this ruling extend beyond the parties involved, clarifying critical aspects of civil procedure regarding interlocutory orders and underscoring the necessity of adhering to procedural rules while still accommodating sufficient leeway for legitimate legal arguments in significant public interest cases.
