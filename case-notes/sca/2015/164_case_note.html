Supreme Court of Appeal

Masstores (Pty) Ltd v Pick n Pay Retailers (Pty) Ltd [2015] ZASCA 164

Lease Agreements: Masstores entered a lease agreement with the Capegate Regional Centre Joint Venture on 20 February 2006, which included a restraint clause prohibiting it from trading as a general food supermarket except under specific conditions ([2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 [2015] ZASCA 164

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The case originated from a dispute between Masstores (Pty) Ltd (the Appellant) and Pick n Pay Retailers (Pty) Ltd (the First Respondent). The Gauteng Division of the High Court of Pretoria, presided over by J W Louw J, had granted a final interdict restraining Masstores from unlawfully interfering in the contractual relationship between Pick n Pay and Hyprop Investments Limited (the Second Respondent) ([1]).

The factual matrix is characterized by the following salient points:

  1. Lease Agreements: Masstores entered a lease agreement with the Capegate Regional Centre Joint Venture on 20 February 2006, which included a restraint clause prohibiting it from trading as a general food supermarket except under specific conditions ([2]).
  2. Pick n Pay's Exclusivity: On 11 May 2006, Pick n Pay also entered into a lease with the same Joint Venture. This lease contained exclusivity provisions that restricted certain businesses from operating in the shopping center, specifically prohibiting the establishment of a supermarket, hypermarket, or certain food departments ([3]).
  3. Introduction of Foodco: By late 2010 to early 2011, Masstores began introducing food products, culminating in the introduction of the Foodco concept at Capegate on 19 September 2013. This development contravened Pick n Pay’s exclusivity ([4]–[5]).
  4. Interim Interdict: Following the introduction of Foodco, Checkers obtained an interim interdict against Masstores, which it later discharged. Upon this, Pick n Pay sought a final interdict against Masstores, leading to the case at hand ([6]).

The court concluded that Masstores was unlawfully trading as a general food supermarket, which breached the restraint clause in its lease ([7]).

I – Issues

The legal issues that arise in this case are:

  1. Unlawful Interference: Whether Masstores' actions constituted unlawful interference with Pick n Pay's contractual rights as established in its lease agreement with Hyprop ([8]).
  2. Breach of Lease Agreement: Whether Masstores was in breach of its lease obligations by operating as a general food supermarket and thus owed a duty of care to avoid interference with Pick n Pay's right of exclusivity ([17]).

R – Rules

The following legal principles underpinned the court's decision:

  1. Delictual Liability: An unlawful act, interference with the contractual relationship, and some form of intention (dolus) are necessary for a delictual claim based on unlawful interference ([19]).
  2. Breach of Contract: It has long been recognized that an outsider can claim for unlawful interference if they knowingly deprive a person of their rights under a contract with another party ([8]).
  3. Legal Convictions of Society: Wrongfulness in delictual claims revolves around whether an act breaches a legal duty and infringes upon subjective rights ([21]).

A – Application

The court rigorously applied the rules to the facts of the case:

  1. Finding of Breach: The court confirmed that Masstores was indeed operating a supermarket in violation of its lease agreement, which properly constituted a breach as it was aware of Pick n Pay’s exclusive rights. The operation of Foodco was a deliberate act that was against the contractual obligations ([20]).
  2. Unlawful Interference Analysis: The court concluded that Masstores’ conduct qualified as unlawful interference since they established a competing business that directly affected Pick n Pay's contractual exclusivity. The requirement for dolus was satisfied, as Masstores was aware of Pick n Pay's rights and continued operations in defiance of these ([22]).
  3. Recognition of Exclusivity: The appeal's argument that Pick n Pay's exclusivity was a 'collateral right' was dismissed. The court held that this right was integral to Pick n Pay’s occupancy and leasehold agreement, thus not subject to the rule 'huur gaat voor koop' ([24]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Masstores' appeal with costs, confirming the lower court's findings. The case reaffirms that contractual rights, particularly exclusivity clauses in lease agreements, are fundamental in commercial relationships. The judgment emphasizes the potential delictual liability incurred through unlawful interference, contributing to the broader legal understanding of competition and contract law within South Africa ([25]). The outcome signals to businesses the importance of adhering to defined contractual agreements and the legal consequences of knowingly contravening established rights of exclusivity in commercial leases.
