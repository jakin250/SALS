# FIRAC Case Note: S M Goldstein & Co (Pty) Ltd v Cathkin Park Hotel (Pty) Ltd & Another [2000] ZASCA 126

## F – Facts
The appellant, S M Goldstein & Co (Pty) Ltd, was a prominent building contractor responsible for the construction of the Drakensberg Sun Hotel owned by the first respondent, Cathkin Park Hotel (Pty) Ltd, with the second respondent, Drakensberg Sun Hotel (Pty) Ltd, operating the hotel. In the winter of 1988, a fire erupted in the lobby, initiated by an improperly constructed fireplace that the appellant had built. The respondents claimed delictual damages against the appellant, alleging negligence. After settling claims against other defendants, the court below found the appellant liable for damages sustained as a result of the fire. The present appeal is solely against the finding of liability, with quantum being separated out for future determination ([paras 1-1]).

The fireplace, designed to provide warmth and ambiance, was built using a Jetmaster firebox, but its installation deviated from the manufacturer's instructions and the National Building Regulations. Specifically, a railway sleeper, meant as a mantlepiece, was positioned directly atop the firebox, creating a fire hazard by ignoring the safety admonitions set forth by Jetmaster. This construction led to the ignition of the sleeper and a subsequent fire that spread to the hotel's roof ([paras 2-4]).

The respondents proffered several grounds of negligence against the appellant, which the court narrowed to a few core considerations: (i) the construction of the fireplace that placed the railway sleeper directly above the firebox; (ii) the failure to follow Jetmaster’s installation instructions; and (iii) the lack of insulation between the combustible materials and the firebox ([paras 5-6]).

## I – Issues
The central issues before the court pertain to:
1. Whether the appellant acted negligently in the construction of the fireplace and thus breached its duty of care owed to the respondents ([para 11]).
2. Whether the appellant’s reliance on the architect's design and the actions of third parties absolved it from liability ([para 9]).
3. Determining the existence of contributory negligence by the respondents, particularly the first respondent’s approval of the fireplace design ([para 12]).

## R – Rules
The court's analysis hinged on established principles of delictual liability, specifically the requirements of negligence, which necessitate showing (1) the existence of a legal duty, (2) a breach of that duty, (3) damages, and (4) causation. The landmark case Kruger v Coetzee 1966 (2) SA 428 (A) established the standard of the prudent person (‘diligens paterfamilias’) regarding foreseeability of harm ([paras 6-7]).

Moreover, the court underscored the principle that even if a builder operates under an architect's design or employs subcontractors, it remains liable for the construction's safety. The legal assessments included the consideration of the manufacturer’s instructions and adherence to building regulations as foundational expectations in construction contracts. Furthermore, the court invoked the decision in Government of the Republic of South Africa v Basdeo and Another 1996 (1) SA 355 (A) regarding the applicability of foreseeability and reasonableness in setting legal duties ([paras 7-9]).

## A – Application
The court examined the facts in light of the established rules of negligence. It found that the appellant constructed the fireplace without observing Jetmaster's safety instructions, resulting in a clear breach of the duty of care that any reasonable builder would uphold. The relationship between the appellant and the respondents established that the appellant did owe a duty of care to both the building owner and third parties. 

In evaluating the submissions related to contributory negligence and the roles of the architect and other professionals, the court found no merit in the argument that these parties’ involvement lessened the appellant's responsibility. The burden remained on the appellant to ensure that the construction complied with safety standards. The presence of independent architects and experts did not relieve the appellant from its obligations under the contract related to workmanship and safety ([paras 9-11]).

The court ruled that the claim for contributory negligence from the first respondent lacked substantive evidence, asserting that the first respondent had engaged professionals for the design and supervision processes and was entitled to rely on their expertise ([para 12]).

Ultimately, it was concluded that the appellant’s actions constituted negligence on a prima facie basis, rendering it liable for the damages incurred by the respondents [paras 11-13].

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court’s finding that the appellant was liable for negligence in the construction of the fireplace that led to extensive fire damage to the hotel. The court articulated that the fundamental duty of a builder encompasses the responsibility to adhere to safety protocols independent of architectural guidance. The implications of this judgment reinforce the accountability of builders to maintain safety standards, underscoring that contractual obligations do not absolve one from the duty to act reasonably and safely in construction practices ([paras 14]). 

This decision provides critical guidance on the boundaries of liability in construction law, particularly the insistence that safety must be paramount in construction irrespective of reliance on architectural designs or instructions. 

The judgment was delivered on 8 September 2000 and carries costs implications for the appellant, which reflects the court's stance on the matter ([para 14]).
