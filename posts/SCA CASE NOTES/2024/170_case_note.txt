Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# Case Note: Smith v The Legal Practitioners’ Fidelity Fund Board (541/23) [2024] ZASCA 170

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The case revolves around Ian Julian Smith (the appellant), who sought reimbursement from the Legal Practitioners’ Fidelity Fund Board (the respondent) for financial losses suffered due to the alleged theft of funds by an attorney's employee, Mr. Andruw Stephens. Smith lodged claims regarding four instances in which he purportedly entrusted money to Dadic Attorneys, represented by Stephens, to handle legally sanctioned transactions which turned out to be fraudulent ([1–3]).

1. **Claim 1**: In October 2015, Smith entrusted R1 million to the firm for a purported loan transaction involving Flake Ice.
2. **Claim 2**: In March 2015, R900,000 was transferred into the trust account from solicitors in England, instructed by Smith to be held until further notice.
3. **Claim 3**: In June 2016, Smith paid R4 million into the trust account as part of an alleged claim against Lubbe Construction.
4. **Claim 4**: In July 2017, Smith transferred R2.7 million into the trust account with the intention of acquiring a book debt from Trudon.

The Gauteng Division of the High Court dismissed all claims, determining Smith did not legally 'entrust' the funds in compliance with section 26(a) of the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979 ([4–5], [10]).

### I – Issues
The appeal presented two primary legal questions:

1. **Entrustment**: Did Smith 'entrust' the money in each claim to the firm or Stephens as outlined in section 26(a) of the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979?
2. **Exclusion of Liability**: Is the Legal Practitioners' Fidelity Fund excluded from liability under section 47(1)(g), which states that the fund is not liable if the money was instructed to be invested on someone's behalf after the commencement date of this provision? ([7])

### R – Rules
The court relied on several legal principles:

1. **Interpretation of Entrustment**: Entrustment is interpreted as the placement of property in the possession of another for the benefit of another party. This involves determining the subjective intention at the time of the payment ([12–15]).
2. **Legal Framework**: 
   - Section 26 of the Attorneys Act articulates circumstances under which the fund can reimburse individuals for losses caused by theft by practitioners or their employees.
   - Section 47(1)(g) of the Attorneys Act excludes the Fund's liability when funds are intended for investment on behalf of the claimant.

These rules were established through previous case law, such as *Provident Fund for the Clothing Industry v Attorneys* and interpretations from courts on the definition of 'entrust' ([8], [9]).

### A – Application
1. **Claim Analysis**:
   
   - **Claim 1**: The court found that Smith intended the funds to serve a specific function (to be eventually transferred to Flake Ice), thus the trust account was used solely as a conduit; no true entrustment to Stephens or the firm occurred ([21–22]).
   
   - **Claim 2**: Smith showed that he intended for the R900,000 to be held in trust. However, the high court concluded that his subsequent agreement for the loan distribution to Flake Ice implied that the original entrustment was no longer valid once he consented to the second use of the funds. The appeal court though differentiated this situation, affirming that the money was indeed stolen while it was still under trust, thus validating his claim for reimbursement ([30–33]).
   
   - **Claims 3 & 4**: Similar logic applied—Smith's intention when transferring the funds was clear: he aimed to utilize the funds for acquiring claims or debts rather than entrusting them to Stephens or the firm, thereby failing to establish legal entrustment ([26–28]).

2. **Exclusion of Liability**: The potential exclusion of liability as prescribed by section 47(1)(g) played a critical role in analyzing whether the Fund could be held accountable, which was ultimately found to be irrelevant for Claim 2 because the funds were stolen before any indication of a loan agreement was made ([34]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal partially allowed the appeal, concluding that:

1. Mr. Smith did not legally 'entrust' the funds in claims 1, 3, and 4 to the firm or Stephens, thus those claims were dismissed.
2. Conversely, the claim for R900,000 was upheld, with the Fund ordered to reimburse Smith due to the theft of funds that had indeed been entrusted ([35]).

The judgment clarifies the conceptual framework surrounding 'entrustment' in attorney-client relationships, reinforcing the necessity of discerning the intention behind the transfer of funds. This case highlights the delicate balance between legal interpretations of trust relationships and the preventable consequences stemming from fraudulent schemes within legal practices.
