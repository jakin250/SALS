Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# FIRAC Case Note on *Davidan v Polovin N O and Others* (167/2020) [2021] ZASCA 109

## F – Facts

The case arises from an eviction application brought by the trustees of the Botany Bay Trust against Petra Davidan, her mother Helene Schonees, and housekeeper Elizabeth Gunta. They occupied a property in Bantry Bay, Cape Town, which belonged to the Trust ([1]). On 13 September 2019, the Western Cape Division of the High Court granted the respondents an eviction order under section 4 of the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE) ([1]).

Following the death of Mr. Mercure Paizee, who was once a resident and cohabitant with Davidan at the property, tensions escalated, culminating in an eviction request. Davidan contended her right to occupy the property stemmed from both a one-year lease agreement entered into on 12 July 2004 and an oral agreement made post-expiry of that lease ([6]). The Trust disputed this, asserting that Davidan's occupancy was dependent on Mr. Paizee's consent, and that her right of residence was effectively terminated upon his death ([10]–[11]).

After several letters demanding rent payments and vacating the premises, the Trust was forced to apply for eviction due to Davidan's refusal to comply ([8],[9]). The High Court found that Davidan was an unlawful occupier, lacking independent consent to occupy ([10]). Following dismissal of her application for leave to appeal, Davidan subsequently appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal limited to two issues: the lawfulness of the termination of her right to occupy and the necessity of her mother and housekeeper as parties to the case ([2]).

## I – Issues

1. **Lawful Termination of Occupancy**: Was Davidan's right to occupy the property lawfully terminated by the Trust, considering the alleged oral agreement and any prior written lease?
   
2. **Necessity of Co-Respondents**: Did Ms. Gunta and Ms. Schonees have a direct and substantial interest in the eviction proceedings, rendering them necessary parties?

## R – Rules

The applicable laws and legal principles include:

1. **Prevention of Illegal Eviction from Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE)**: Section 1 provides the definition of "unlawful occupier," specifying that it includes those without express or tacit consent from the owner ([11]); section 4 governs the eviction procedure.
   
2. **Common Law Principles concerning Leases**: A lease can be either written or oral, and termination requires legal grounds, such as breach of terms, otherwise, an owner's consent must be established for occupancy ([24]).

3. **Factual Disputes in Motion Proceedings**: Following *Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd* [1984] ZASCA 51, disputes of fact arising in affidavits are resolved in favor of the respondent's version, unless it is implausible or lacking credibility ([20]).

## A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal examined whether Davidan had a lawful right of occupation. Evidence indicated a written lease had existed until its expiry in 2005, followed by an oral agreement supporting Davidan's claim that she and Mr. Paizee paid the bond instalments and municipal rates in lieu of rent ([7],[15]). Testimony from former trustee Mr. Gamsu corroborated Davidan's narrative ([15]).

However, the Trust's argument rested on the assertion that Davidan's right to occupy was contingent on Mr. Paizee's status, thus leaving her unlawful upon his death. The Court assessed the Trust's failure to show explicit termination of the oral agreement, which was never formally acknowledged or communicated in any letter ([22]). Moreover, section 1 of PIE was cited to highlight that if Davidan had a right to occupy, it needed legally sound termination, which the Trust did not establish ([11],[22]).

Consequently, the Court determined that Davidan enjoyed occupancy rights based on the oral agreement and was not an unlawful occupier under PIE conditions. The Trust's failure to legitimate the termination of that right led the Court to conclude Davidan's eviction should not proceed [(28)].

Regarding Ms. Gunta and Schonees, the judgment also concluded that they were necessary parties; however, this point was rendered moot since the main rationale for eviction failed on substantive grounds—the lack of lawful termination of Davidan's occupancy ([28]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Davidan's appeal, thereby ruling that she was not an unlawful occupier under PIE. The judgment specified that her occupancy was lawful, given the reasons outlined regarding the oral agreement and the absence of proper termination. Consequently, the Trust's quest for eviction was dismissed with costs ([30]).

The case underscores important principles on lease agreements, the status of oral agreements, and the procedural obligations of landlords in eviction applications, reaffirming that an eviction may not occur without proper legal grounds for terminating such occupancy rights. This ruling represents a significant interpretation of rights under the PIE Act, emphasizing due process in tenant eviction hearings as essential to landlord-tenant relationships in South Africa.
