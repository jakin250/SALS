Case Note: REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2006

# Case Note: Garrido v Director of Public Prosecutions [2006] SCA 122 (RSA)

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The appellant, **Nelson Yester Pablo Garrido**, challenged an order from a magistrate in Randburg, who had committed him to custody pending extradition to the United States (US) on charges of conspiracy to import and distribute narcotics ([1]–[2]). This order was based on a request from the US government, prompting an extradition inquiry under **Section 59 of the Extradition Act 67 of 1962** ([2]).

The extradition inquiry commenced on **22 August 2002**, during which Garrido filed a **Notice of Objection** citing sixteen grounds against the extradition and questioning the constitutionality of **Section 10(2)** of the Act ([8]). He specifically contested whether the authority who issued a certificate under **Section 10(2)** was the "appropriate authority" and claimed he was deprived of the opportunity to present evidence on this issue or the overall sufficiency of evidence against him ([17]).

The magistrate ruled against Garrido's preliminary objections, ultimately deciding that the evidence presented was adequate to warrant extradition ([11]). This ruling was challenged in the **High Court**, where **Willis J** dismissed the appeal for review but led to further appeal in the **Supreme Court of Appeal** ([21]).

### I – Issues
The legal issues revolve around:

1. Whether the magistrate conducted the extradition inquiry in a manner that complied with the principles of **audi alteram partem** (the right to a fair hearing), specifically regarding the appellant’s opportunities to present evidence and challenge the certificate issued by the US authorities ([18]–[24]).
  
2. The interpretation of the sufficiency and relevance of evidence pertaining to the extradition process under the **Extradition Act** ([22]).

### R – Rules
The following legal principles and statutory provisions were relevant to the court's determination:

1. **Extradition Act 67 of 1962**: 
   - **Section 9**: Governs the holding of inquiries for extradition, stating that they should be conducted akin to preparatory examinations, allowing the accused to present evidence ([4]).
   - **Section 10**: Outlines the magistrate's responsibilities and authority when determining if the individual should be committed for extradition, including reliance on appropriate certificates as conclusive proof ([5]).
   - **Section 11**: Grants the Minister discretion regarding surrender, outlining conditions under which extradition may be denied ([6]).

2. Previous case law, particularly **Geuking v President of the Republic of South Africa**, where the Constitutional Court addressed the constitutionality of **Section 10(2)**, affirming that a respondent in an extradition inquiry is entitled to present evidence relevant both for the magistrate's decision and the Minister's later discretion ([7], [22]).

### A – Application
The court critically assessed the magistrate’s procedures during the extradition inquiry. It found that the magistrate had limited the inquiry's scope by interpreting the appellant’s request to present evidence solely as an argument on the papers, rather than a full evidentiary hearing ([22]). This restricted interpretation led to a failure in observing the procedural requirements of **audi alteram partem**, as Garrido did not receive the opportunity to contest the validity of the evidence or the reliability of the US's authority in certifying the evidence ([24]).

Moreover, the court determined that the magistrate should have considered the potential impact of the evidence Garrido sought to introduce concerning the third-party state’s motives for extradition, which could influence the Minister's decision under **Section 11** ([23]). The court's ruling emphasized that assertions of non-credibility concerning the US's charges against Garrido were relevant inheritors' examination of extradition merits and should have been admitted in the inquiry ([25]).

### C – Conclusion
The **Supreme Court of Appeal** concluded that the magistrate's refusal to allow further evidence from Garrido constituted a procedural error, thereby rendering the original judgment flawed. The court overturned the High Court’s dismissal of the appellant’s review application, establishing that the extradition was not lawfully substantiated and the inquiry's procedural integrity was compromised ([28]).

The ultimate ratio decidendi affirmed that the appellant's rights to a fair hearing were violated and that the certificate under **Section 10(2)** must not only meet technical thresholds but also align with principles of substantive justice. This case has vital implications for future extradition inquiries, framing the procedural rights of individuals facing extradition and ensuring fairness in such proceedings. The decision is a landmark affirmation of the rights of accused persons within the extradition context, emphasizing the need for robust justification by foreign states claiming extradition.
