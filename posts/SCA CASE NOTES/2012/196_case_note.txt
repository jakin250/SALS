# Case Note: Colleen Mzingisi Dumani v Desmond Nair & Another (144/2012) [2012] ZASCA 196

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The case of **Colleen Mzingisi Dumani v Desmond Nair & Another** revolves around the appeal filed by **Collen Mzingisi Dumani** (hereafter "the appellant"), who was a magistrate suspended following findings of misconduct at a disciplinary inquiry. The inquiry was presided over by **Desmond Nair** (the first respondent), chief magistrate of Pretoria, on behalf of the **Magistrates Commission** (the second respondent). Dumani was charged with four counts of misconduct related to inappropriate touching of female employees (comprising staff members at the Graaff-Reinet Magistrate's Court) between December 2008 and January 2009([1]–[4]).

Dumani, appointed as acting head of the Graaff-Reinet Magistrate's Court on 1 November 2008, faced allegations that included:
- Stroking the cheek of administrative clerk Ms Salome Hartney.
- Touching the cheek of security officer Ms Marilyn Slavers.
- Touching the back of the neck of cleaning staff member Ms Regina Karolus.
- Putting his hand between the breasts of senior administrative clerk Ms Edwina Ele.

In March 2009, following the charges, the presiding officer conducted a disciplinary inquiry where evidence was presented, leading to Dumani's conviction on three counts, with an acquittal for one charge. Consequently, the presiding officer recommended Dumani’s removal from office, which was later accepted by the Magistrates Commission([4]–[5]).

Dumani applied for a review of the disciplinary proceedings in the Eastern Cape High Court but was unsuccessful; thereafter, he appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal([5]).

### I – Issues
The central issues in this appeal are:
1. Whether the presiding officer’s findings constituted a material error of fact sufficient to warrant a review of the disciplinary proceedings under the **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000** (PAJA).
2. Whether the presiding officer acted arbitrarily in his decision-making process.
3. Whether the presiding officer's conclusion was so unreasonable that it could not have been reached by a reasonable person.

The appellant contended that the findings were flawed due to misdirections regarding evidence and asserted that the outcome of the inquiry represented an arbitrary exercise of power ([5]–[6]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework governing this matter is found primarily within the **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000**, particularly sections 6(2)(e) and (f). These sections outline grounds for review of administrative action on the basis of the decision being unreasonable, arbitrary, or reliant on material errors of fact ([26]).

In **Pepcor Retirement Fund v Financial Services Board** (2003) (6) SA 38 (SCA), the court established the standard for material error of fact, which must pertain to true and objective facts essential to the administrative decision at hand. Further case law analysis includes consideration of prior decisions like **Bato Star Fishing (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Environmental Affairs** (2004) 4 SA 490 (CC), reinforcing the distinction between review and appeal, where misdirections in factual determinations should respect the discretion of the original decision-maker ([29]–[32]).

### A – Application
In assessing Dumani's claims, the court systematically examined the evidence presented at the disciplinary inquiry. The testimony of the complainants was found credible, with the presiding officer having carefully considered their demeanour and the consistency of their accounts. Despite attempts to challenge their reliability, the court concluded that minor discrepancies did not undermine the overall credibility of their testimonies ([10]–[18]).

Appellant claimed that the presiding officer placed undue emphasis on the evidence from a witness (Claassen), which allegedly led to a substantial misdirection. However, the court held that the presiding officer's references to Claassen's testimony were not determinative enough to derail the prevailing findings against Dumani. The presiding officer's conclusion was supported by a holistic appraisal of evidence, demonstrating that the decisions made fell within the range of what a reasonable person could conclude ([27]–[29], [31]).

The court notably pointed out that there was no substantial evidence to support Dumani's conspiracy theory regarding the complainants. Furthermore, evidence demonstrated that the allegations did not arise from racial or linguistic tensions but were based on Dumani's inappropriate conduct towards staff members. Hence, the claims of collusion were unfounded and dismissed as implausible ([20], [21]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Dumani's appeal, confirming that the presiding officer's decisions were not based on material errors of fact nor were they arbitrary or unreasonable. The findings of misconduct against Dumani were upheld, and his conduct was characterized as severe enough to warrant removal from office. The appeal was dismissed with costs, establishing the judiciary's commitment to maintaining integrity within the ranks of judicial officers ([25]).

This case underscores the judiciary's rigorous standards for administrative justice and reinforces the principle that findings of misconduct must be supported by credible evidence, illustrating the careful balance required in disciplinary matters involving public officials. The verdict serves as a significant reminder of the responsibilities vested in judicial officers and the imperative of accountability in public service.
