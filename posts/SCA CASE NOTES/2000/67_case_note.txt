Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2000

# FIRAC Case Note: ESS KAY ELECTRONICS PTE LTD v. FIRST NATIONAL BANK OF SOUTHERN AFRICA LTD

## F – Facts ([paras 1–6, 11–13, 20–23])

In **ESS KAY ELECTRONICS PTE LTD v FIRST NATIONAL BANK OF SOUTHERN AFRICA LTD**, the appellants, Singapore-based companies specializing in electronic equipment, claimed damages from the respondent, a South African commercial bank, seeking to hold it vicariously liable for the fraudulent actions of one of its employees, Wildig. The fraud occurred during a transaction in which the employees of the appellants were misled by fraudulent bank drafts purportedly issued by the respondent. 

The scheme began when a business associate contacted Mr. K Primalani, a director of the first appellant, indicating interest from someone named Mynhardt, who expressed the intention to purchase goods. Orders were finalized, with goods worth $130,000 and $120,000 being set to be dispatched. Payment was to be made via bank drafts, to be exchanged for the goods upon presentation of verified documents. 

Upon receiving what appeared to be valid drafts, Primalani authorized the release of the goods, which were then shipped. However, the drafts were subsequently dishonored, revealing that they were forgeries. Wildig, from the respondent bank, had been part of a fraudulent scheme orchestrated with an acquaintance, Jerome Clack, for which he was eventually revealed to have no authority to issue the drafts. The appellants subsequently pursued claims against the bank, arguing that Wildig’s actions during employment should render the bank vicariously liable.

## I – Issues ([paras 6, 16–18, 20–22])

The primary legal question that arose in this case was whether the respondent bank could be held vicariously liable for the fraudulent acts of employee Wildig, given that he acted outside the scope of his authority in the course of his employment. Specifically, key issues include:

1. **Did Wildig’s actions fall within the course of his employment?**
2. **Can the bank be held liable for the fraud under the doctrine of vicarious liability?**
3. **Was there any ostensible authority that might bind the bank to Wildig’s fraudulent conduct?**

## R – Rules ([paras 7–9, 16, 20–22])

The principle of vicarious liability in South African law dictates that an employer can be held liable for tortious acts of an employee if those acts occur within the course and scope of employment. The foundational principle can be traced to *Mkize v Martens* 1914 AD 382, which states that actions purely taken for the employee’s own interests and outside the authority of their employment do not invoke vicarious liability. More specifically, *Minister of Law and Order v Ngobo* 1992 (4) SA 822 (A) clarifies the application of the rule and its relationship to public policy considerations.

In this case, to determine if Wildig's actions attracted vicarious liability, one must assess the definition of “course of employment”. The standard for assessing whether unauthorized acts can be considered within the course of employment was evaluated through existing precedents. Furthermore, the court also referred to the concept of ostensible authority, which requires that the victim reasonably believe the employee was acting within their authority based on the employer's conduct ([paras 20–22]).

## A – Application ([paras 16–19, 21])

The court undertook a thorough examination of Wildig’s duties and actions. It was established that although Wildig was involved with the issuance of bank drafts, he acted outside the parameters of his authority when he forged documents and issued drafts without the requisite approval or documentation. 

While the appellants' counsel argued that Wildig’s use of his position afforded him an apparent authority, the court distinguished between actual and ostensible authority. Notably, Wildig was not authorized to act on behalf of the bank in transactions that lacked procedural safeguards like Form A authorization, nor had the bank in any manner represented that his actions were authorized. The court noted that relying on the appearance of authority, without more, would not suffice to hold the employer liable ([paras 16–19]).

As Wildig's actions were purely for his personal gain and not aligned with his employment responsibilities, the court ruled against vicarious liability. The court affirmed that fairness and public policy considerations should not automatically translate to employer liability in cases unconnected to authorized actions by employees.

## C – Conclusion ([paras 23])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the judgment of the lower court, dismissing the appeal with costs. The ruling reinforced that Wildig's actions fell outside the course of his employment and clarified the boundaries of vicarious liability in fraudulent contexts. The court emphasized that merely employing someone in a position of authority does not obligate an employer to bear the consequences of that individual's fraudulent acts when not conducted within the scope of their designated roles. This case serves as a critical affirmation of the need for precise boundaries in vicarious liability, particularly in commercial and fraudulent contexts. 

---

*Appeal dismissed with costs, judgment delivered on 28 November 2000.*
