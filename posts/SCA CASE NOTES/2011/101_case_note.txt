Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# Case Note: Klub Lekkerrus/Libertas v Troye Villa (Pty) Ltd [2011] ZASCA 101

## F - Facts
The case arises from a dispute concerning the ownership of shares in Troye Villa (Pty) Ltd and Lekkerrus Warmwaterbron (Pty) Ltd, stemming from two agreements of sale between the voluntary associations Klub Lekkerrus and Klub Libertas and Mr. P. J. H. van Tonder ([paras 1–4]). Following the amalgamation of the two clubs in 1991, the Club continued operating as a holiday club under a new constitution. The litigation initially began in the North Gauteng High Court, where the plaintiffs claimed ownership of the shares, asserting that the prior agreements were terminated due to the amalgamation and non-payment of the purchase price ([paras 3, 17]).

Mr. van Tonder sold all issued shares and loan accounts in the second and third plaintiffs to Klub Lekkerrus and Klub Libertas, respectively, with the full purchase prices required to be paid within sixty months from the agreements' signing ([para 10]). Despite the terms being fulfilled to some extent, by 9 August 1995, the full purchase price remained unpaid. Mr. van Tonder never demanded payment, even as financial difficulties arose, leading to his death in July 1997 ([para 12]). Subsequently, the Club’s governance shifted, culminating in a new board being elected in 2007 and a demand for the Club to vacate the properties owned by the plaintiffs ([paras 16–17]).

## I - Issues
The primary issues brought to the court’s attention were:  
1. Whether the ownership of shares in the second and third plaintiffs had lapsed due to the amalgamation of the constituent clubs ([para 25]).
2. Whether the non-variation clause in the agreements prevented the tacit renewal or continuation of said agreements ([para 28]).
3. Whether Mr. van Tonder's conduct post-agreement constituted a waiver of his right to declare the agreements void due to non-payment ([para 29]).
4. The legal authority of Mrs. van Tonder and Mr. Woite to enter into subsequent agreements on behalf of the Club after Mr. van Tonder’s death ([para 31]).

## R - Rules
The key legal principles relevant to this case include:  
1. **Tacit Agreements**: South African contract law allows for the formation of tacit agreements where the conduct of the parties implies an agreement, thus replacing previous written agreements ([para 26]).
2. **Non-Variation Clauses**: Non-variation clauses in contracts do not prevent parties from tacitly concluding new agreements under the same terms ([para 26]).
3. **Election to Maintain Agreements**: A party’s failure to exercise cancellation rights can indicate acceptance and continuation of a contract ([paras 27–30]).
4. **Authority to Contract**: Generally, the authority required to enter into a contract must align with the constitutive rules of the entity involved ([para 31]).

Key case law references include:
- *Telcordia Technologies Inc v Telkom SA Ltd* [2007] 3 SA 266 (SCA) regarding non-variation clauses.
- *Simon NO v Air Operations of Europe AB & Others* [1999] 1 SA 217 (SCA) with respect to assignment of rights and obligations.

## A - Application
The court applied the principles of tacit agreements to establish that continuous operation and acceptance of terms had implicitly formed new agreements between the involved parties after amalgamation. The conduct of all parties, including Mr. van Tonder's failure to demand payment and efforts to continue accepting payments, supported the conclusion that he elected to keep the agreements in effect ([para 28]).

It was determined that the non-variation clause did not impede these tacit agreements, as their practical execution suggested ongoing acceptance of the terms despite the formal change in ownership of the shares ([para 26]). The court also recognized that the authority of Mrs. van Tonder to sign agreements post Mr. van Tonder’s demise was flawed because such actions were not sanctioned by the Club’s constitution ([para 31]). The evidence demonstrated that Mr. van Tonder's estate acted in a manner indicating they acknowledged the agreements, further substantiating their legitimacy ([para 30]).

Moreover, the court asserted that the amalgamation did not lead to the dissolution of the original associations but rather the transfer of rights and obligations to the new entity, the Club, reflecting their unified operational status ([paras 28–29]).

## C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately ruled in favor of the Club, upholding its claim to ownership of the shares in the second and third plaintiffs and dismissing the plaintiffs' claims for rectification and demands for payment ([para 37]). The ruling emphasized the tacit nature of the agreements continuing to bind the parties despite the non-variation clauses, the valid assumption of continued operations post-amalgamation, and the electoral choice made by Mr. van Tonder to uphold the agreements ([paras 32–34]). The judgment highlights important principles of contract law and governance within voluntary associations, demonstrating that continuity of operational frameworks can transcend formal structural changes.

The implications of this case extend to the management of voluntary associations, particularly regarding the enforceability of contracts and the scope of authority held by members of governing bodies, underscoring the need for clarity in governance structures when amalgamating entities.
