ATB Chartered Accountants (SA) v Edna Bonfiglio [2010] ZASCA 124

This case originates from a dispute between the appellant, ATB Chartered Accountants (SA) (hereafter "ATB"), and the respondent, Edna Bonfiglio, concerning a professional services agreement.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2010 [2010] ZASCA 124

F – Facts

This case originates from a dispute between the appellant, ATB Chartered Accountants (SA) (hereafter "ATB"), and the respondent, Edna Bonfiglio, concerning a professional services agreement. The respondent, who was a member of the close corporation M Klisser CC (a stationery retailer), approached ATB on 5 April 2002 seeking assistance to sell her interests in the corporation due to financial difficulties ([paras 2–3]). An oral agreement was established where ATB agreed to find a buyer for Bonfiglio’s interests and provide professional advice regarding the sale. ATB was represented by Martin Venter ([paras 3–4]).

The respondent was introduced to a prospective buyer, Jacobus Wilhelm Raath, and signed a sale agreement that had been drafted by ATB ([para 4]). However, Raath failed to make the initial payment by 30 April 2002 and subsequently defaulted on the payment schedule ([para 5]).

The respondent's legal representatives communicated ATB’s potential liability for damages relating to this failed transaction on 7 August 2002 ([para 6]). In March 2003, following Raath’s inability to pay and subsequent liquidation of his company, Bonfiglio filed a lawsuit against Raath for the outstanding payment ([para 7]). She later instituted a claim against ATB for R856,400.10, asserting that ATB's negligence in their professional obligations caused her financial losses ([paras 8–9]).

ATB raised a special plea of prescription in response, arguing that the claim had prescribed under the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, contending that the claim became due on the date of the sale in 2002 or, at the latest, when Bonfiglio's attorneys notified them of the claim ([paras 9–10]). The Pretoria High Court rejected ATB's special plea, prompting the appeal ([para 1]).

I – Issues

The central issue was whether the High Court was correct in dismissing ATB's special plea of prescription. Additionally, the court examined when exactly the respondent's claim became due according to the Prescription Act, particularly looking into the relevant sections defining when a debt is considered "due" ([paras 1–2]).

R – Rules

The legal rules governing this case are primarily derived from the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 ([para 12]). According to this statute:

  • Section 12(1) states that prescription commences as soon as a debt is due.
  • Section 12(3) specifies that a debt is not deemed due until the creditor has knowledge of the identity of the debtor and the facts giving rise to the debt, unless the creditor could have acquired such knowledge through reasonable care ([para 13]).

The court referenced the decision in Truter & another v Deysel to elucidate that a debt is considered due when a creditor has a complete cause of action against the debtor ([para 14]). The critical timeframe is when the cause of action manifested; in contract cases, this typically occurs upon breach ([para 15]).

A – Application

The court's analysis began by deliberating whether the respondent sustained a complete cause of action when ATB allegedly failed to deliver adequate professional services. ATB contended that the claim was due following the breach of contract on the date of the sale ([para 15]). They further argued that damages incurred were immediately apparent.

Conversely, the respondent's counsel suggested that knowledge of the debt only materialized once she received a nulla bona return in July 2003, following Raath's inability to satisfy the financial obligations ([para 10]). The court ultimately determined that even though the losses were not quantifiable until the nulla bona return was issued, the essential facts creating the claim were apparent by 3 April 2003, when she was informed that Raath was liquidated and unable to pay ([para 18]).

Consequently, the court established that the respondent had sufficient knowledge of the necessary facts by 3 April 2003, at which point the three-year prescription period commenced ([paras 19–20]). The summons issued on 30 June 2006 thus fell outside the prescription period, leading to ATB's special plea being upheld ([paras 18–20]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the time period for prescription had indeed commenced from 3 April 2003, culminating in the prescription of the claim by 2 April 2006. As such, the court set aside the lower court's order and upheld ATB's special plea of prescription, dismissing Bonfiglio's action with costs ([paras 19–20]). The ruling emphasizes the importance of timely assertions of rights in contractual relations, reinforcing principles enshrined in the Prescription Act.

This judgment serves as a reminder for creditors to be diligent in asserting claims and understanding when such claims are deemed due, particularly in contexts where the knowledge of debt may not be immediately apparent. The case illustrates the necessity for legal professionals to navigate the complexities of legal remedies and ensure compliance with prescription timelines, thereby preserving the integrity of claims in commercial transactions.
