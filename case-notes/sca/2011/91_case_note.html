Supreme Court of Appeal 2011

Case Note: The South African Police Service Medical Scheme v Lamana (542/10)

FIRAC Method

F – Facts:
The case involves respondents Andile Robert Lamana, Nzwana VictorMzili, and Tandie Coleman Maloni, who are serving members of the South African Police Service and also members of the South African Police Service Medical Scheme (Polmed). The first appellant, Polmed, is a medical scheme registered under the Medical Schemes Act 131 of 1998, and it is administered by the second appellant, Qualsa Healthcare (Pty) Ltd ([1]). The respondents initiated motion proceedings against Polmed and Qualsa, compelling them to make payments for medical services rendered into a designated bank account instead of insisting on payment directly into the respondents' personal accounts ([1])–([3]).

Historically, medical services owed to Dr. Gualam Muhammed Peer for the respondents were paid directly by Qualsa on behalf of Polmed until a directive from Polmed in October 2008 mandated that payments must be made solely into members' personal accounts ([2]). This led the respondents to enter into agreements allowing payments to a trust account. However, Polmed and Qualsa refused to implement these payments into the trust account, asserting it was not an "acceptable bank account" under their rules, specifically citing concerns about potential fraud ([3])–([5]).

The North Gauteng High Court ruled that Polmed’s refusal to pay into the trust account was unjustified, leading to an attempted appeal by Polmed and Qualsa, which was contested ([6])–([7]).

I – Issues:
The primary legal issue in the case revolves around the interpretation of Polmed's rules concerning the definition of an "acceptable bank account" under rule 17.5. The outcome of this appeal hinges on whether the High Court correctly interpreted the rules when it held that "an acceptable bank account" should be understood as an account that is generally acceptable ([4]). A secondary issue concerning whether the appeal was rendered academic by amendments to the relevant rules registered on May 11, 2010, also arises, as these amendments directly address the issues under contention ([8]).

R – Rules:
The court relied heavily on statutory interpretation of the Medical Schemes Act and the rules of Polmed to assess whether the appellants acted within their rights. The specific rules in question included:

  • Rule 17.4, allowing Polmed to pay benefits directly to the member concerned, and
  • Rule 17.5, which governs the payment of amounts due to members through transfer to an acceptable bank account ([4]).

The legal framework provided by the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959, particularly section 21A, outlines the circumstances under which an appeal may be dismissed if the issues are moot or will not have practical effect ([9]).

A – Application:
In applying these legal standards, the court examined the changes to the rules that had occurred just prior to the hearing of Polmed's appeal, noting that the amended rules explicitly permitted payments to be made into members' personal accounts ([8]). Consequently, the court acknowledged that the amendment rendered the original issues moot, as even if Polmed had strong arguments concerning fraud prevention, the amendments fundamentally altered the relationship between the parties and the obligations of the scheme.

The court also addressed the procedural failure on the part of Polmed and Qualsa not to inform the court of these amendments sooner, which might have justified a dismissal of the application for leave to appeal under section 21A. Despite the arguments put forward by Polmed claiming potential benefits from a decision favorable to them, the court found that these claims were speculative and insufficient to override the already moot nature of the appeal ([10]–[13]).

C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, including the fees for two counsel, to be borne jointly and severally by the appellants ([14]). The ruling established that the interpretation of internal rules needs to be grounded in the current legal context, particularly following substantial amendments. The judgment emphasized the importance of timely and accurate communication with the court concerning rule changes and underlined the necessity for robust and clear procedural adherence in litigation to uphold judicial efficiency and order ([9]–[14]).

This case serves as a critical reference point for understanding the procedural and substantive aspects involved in medical scheme operations and the implications of legal amendments on ongoing litigation.
