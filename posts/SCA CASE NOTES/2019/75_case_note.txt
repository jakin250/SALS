Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: Chonco v The State (1247/2018) [2019] ZASCA 75

## F – Facts

The appellant, Thokozani Kwazi Chonco, was convicted in the Alexandra Regional Court for robbery with aggravating circumstances and sentenced to 18 years' imprisonment ([1]). His conviction stemmed from an incident on January 27, 2007, where a soft drink wholesaler was robbed at gunpoint, leading to the theft of a cellular phone, cigarettes, and R10,000 in cash ([3]). The appellant's palm print was found at the crime scene, linking him to the robbery, although he disputed his presence, claiming he had been invited to the premises due to a dispute over change ([3]).

Chonco, aged 30 at the time of sentencing, was a first offender and a sole provider for his three minor children, residing with his mother, a pensioner, after their mother's death ([4]). The regional magistrate, taking into account the seriousness of the offence and the prevalence of similar crimes, imposed a sentence of 18 years, emphasizing the lack of exceptional or compelling circumstances to justify a lesser sentence ([5]). The Gauteng Local Division of the High Court subsequently denied his initial appeal against both conviction and sentence; however, leave to appeal against the sentence only was granted ([1]).

## I – Issues

The central issue upon appeal was the appropriateness of the 18-year sentence imposed by the magistrate, particularly focusing on whether the court justified imposing a sentence exceeding the minimum of 15 years prescribed by section 51 of the Criminal Law Amendment Act, 1997 ([2], [6]). The appeal challenged whether the magistrate's failure to provide specific reasons for this choice warranted judicial intervention ([9]).

## R – Rules

The legal framework for sentencing in this case arises from section 51 of the Criminal Law Amendment Act, which sets minimum sentences for serious offenses, including robbery with aggravating circumstances ([7]). According to section 51(2)(a)(i), a first offender must receive a sentence of not less than 15 years' imprisonment. The Act provides the court discretion to impose a sentence beyond this minimum by up to five years if "substantial and compelling" circumstances are present ([8]).

Furthermore, case law establishes the requirement that judicial officers must articulate their reasons for sentencing, especially when it results in significant penal consequences for the accused. This principle ensures that sentences are not arbitrarily imposed ([10]). Particularly, in *S v Msimango* ([11]), the Supreme Court of Appeal emphasized that any addition to the minimum sentence must be justified with sound legal reasoning.

## A – Application

In applying these legal principles, the Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized the magistrate's failure to justify the 18-year sentence imposed. The court noted that while the magistrate identified the seriousness of the offense, there were no substantiated circumstances to warrant a sentence exceeding the prescribed minimum ([12]). The appeal court pointed out that it is a fundamental requirement for judicial officers to provide a reasoned basis when deviating from legislated minimum penalties, affirming that this omission entitled the appellate court to reassess the sentence afresh.

Additionally, the court acknowledged the appellant's status as a first offender and the time already spent in custody (five months awaiting trial), factors that traditionally merit consideration when determining an appropriate sentence. The ruling drew from precedent cases that demonstrated a consistent judicial expectation for reasoned sentencing, ultimately aligning the circumstances of Chonco's case with those proscribed by the minimum sentencing framework ([12]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the original sentence of 18 years was not justified due to the magistrate's failure to adequately explain the rationale for exceeding the minimum punishment. Consequently, the court set aside the earlier judicial decisions regarding sentencing and imposed a revised sentence of 15 years' imprisonment, antedated to August 30, 2011 ([13]). This ruling highlights the critical importance of judicial transparency and thorough justification in sentencing practices, reinforcing that adherence to prescribed minimum sentences requires clear articulation of the legal rationale when diverging from such standards. The implications of this decision further serve as a reminder of the judiciary's responsibility in safeguarding the rights of the accused through reasoned judgment.
