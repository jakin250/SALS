Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# Case Note: Dr JS Moroka Municipality v The Chairperson of the Tender Evaluation Committee of the Dr JS Moroka Municipality (937/2012) [2013] ZASCA 186

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The present appeal concerns the disqualification of a tender submitted by Betram (Pty) Ltd for the supply of 6,000 VIP toilets to the Dr J S Moroka Municipality, which arose due to a failure to provide an original SARS tax clearance certificate as mandated in the tender invitation ([1]–[2]). The municipality published an invitation to tender on 24 June 2012, stating that bids must include "a valid original Tax Clearance Certificate". The first respondent, Betram, submitted a tender that allegedly included only a copy of the certificate. The tender was thus disqualified based on this perceived non-compliance ([3]).

The first respondent subsequently initiated urgent review proceedings in the North Gauteng High Court after discovering that the contract for the project had been awarded to Eldocrete, which had submitted a higher tender ([4]). The municipality maintained its position that only a copy was presented, while the first respondent claimed it had submitted the original ([5]).

The High Court ruled in favor of the first respondent, asserting that the exclusion of Betram's tender was administratively unfair and directing the municipality to evaluate the first respondent’s tender alongside Eldocrete’s ([7]).

### I – Issues
The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the Dr J S Moroka Municipality was justified in disqualifying the first respondent’s tender solely for not submitting the original tax clearance certificate, given the implications of administrative fairness in public procurement ([1]–[2]). Additional questions included whether the municipality had the discretion to condone such non-compliance and if there were reasonable grounds for the advertisement's requirement of an original document ([12]).

### R – Rules
The court relied on various statutory frameworks governing municipal tenders in South Africa. The relevant rules included:

1. **Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act 5 of 2000** – mandates compliance with tender specifications for a tender to be considered acceptable ([8(a)]).
2. **Preferential Procurement Regulations** – Regulation 16 of the 2001 regulations, and Regulation 14 of the 2011 regulations, both specified that tenders cannot be awarded without an original tax clearance certificate ([8(b), (c)]).
3. **Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000** – requires supply chain management policies embodied in fairness, equity, and transparency ([8(d)]).
4. **Local Government: Municipal Finance Management Act 56 of 2003** – necessitates open and transparent procurement processes ([8(e)]).
5. Previous case law, notably *Millennium Waste Management* and *Pepper Bay*, provided guidance on condonation and the parameters of lawful tender processes ([10], [12]).

### A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized whether the original documentation requirement represented a peremptory condition versus one that could be waived. The High Court had characterized the municipality's actions as administratively unfair, leading them to suggest that an original tax clearance document was an immaterial requirement. However, the SCA determined that statutory provisions did not grant the municipality discretion to condone non-compliance ([9], [12]).

In assessing whether the requirement was critical, the SCA held that compliance with statutory procurement laws underscored the necessity of an original SARS tax clearance. Thus, disqualification based on failure to provide such documentation was deemed appropriate, reinforcing the legal principle of legality in procurement, which prohibits accepting bids that do not fulfill mandatory criteria ([18], [19]). The Court distinguished prior case law and clarified that general public policy could not serve as a basis to circumvent explicit legal and procedural requirements.

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the Dr J S Moroka Municipality acted within its rights to disqualify Betram's tender due to the absence of an original tax clearance certificate, a requirement clearly laid out in the tender invitation. The High Court's order to evaluate the first respondent’s tender was reversed. The appellate court's ruling emphasized the importance of strict adherence to procedural requirements in public procurement, thereby refining the interplay between administrative fairness and legal compliance in tender processes ([20]).

The decision reinforces the legal framework governing municipal procurement and clarifies that no implicit discretionary power to condone procedural lapses exists unless expressly provided within the regulatory framework. This case serves as a critical reference for ensuring compliance in bid submissions, reinforcing the principles of transparency and fairness in public administration.
