Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Rural Maintenance (Pty) Ltd and Another v Maluti-A-Phofung Local Municipality 

### F – Facts

In the matter before the Constitutional Court of South Africa, the dispute arose between Rural Maintenance (Pty) Limited and Maluti-A-Phofung Local Municipality regarding whether a transfer of business occurred under section 197 of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA). The Municipality, responsible for providing essential services including electricity, had contracted Rural Maintenance to manage its electricity distribution network due to its deteriorating infrastructure ([1-7]). 

In 2011, under an Electricity Management Contract (EMC) which lasted for 25 years, Rural was tasked with managing, operating, administering, and enhancing the municipality's electricity network. By September 2011, Rural had employed 127 workers, significantly investing approximately R96 million in infrastructure and materials to establish an operational system ([4-5]). 

However, by August 2013, the Municipality contended that the EMC was invalid due to the municipal manager's lack of authority, effectively repudiating the agreement ([6-8]). Rural argued that a transfer of business had occurred as it began servicing the Municipality’s electricity provision needs. Upon cancellation of the EMC, Rural sought recognition that all 127 employees should be viewed as employees of the Municipality, asserting that the business had been returned to it as a going concern ([8-10]). The Labour Court initially ruled in favour of Rural, but the Labour Appeal Court reversed this decision ([8-9]).

### I – Issues

The main legal issues presented in this case were:
1. Whether the Labour Appeal Court correctly evaluated the applicability of section 197 of the LRA concerning the transfer of a business as a going concern from Rural to the Municipality ([1-17]).
2. Whether the Labour Appeal Court erroneously formulated a new test concerning what constitutes a transfer in terms of section 197 ([18-19]).

### R – Rules

The Constitutional Court's analysis revolved around the interpretation of section 197 of the LRA, particularly the definitions of "business" and "transfer" as defined in the statute. 

- **Section 197(1)** specifies that “business” includes any part of a business and indicates that a transfer involves moving a business as a going concern from one employer to another.
- The decisions in earlier cases, particularly *NEHAWU v University of Cape Town* ([11]), clarified that the assessment of whether a business had been transferred should consider various factors including asset transfer, employee retention, customer transfer, and the overall operation of the business post-transfer ([28-30]).

The Court also referenced international jurisprudence concerning business transfers, drawing parallels between South African law and the European acquis on protection of employment rights during transfers, especially in instances of service provision changes ([26-30]).

### A – Application

The Constitutional Court, particularly through the first judgment delivered by Froneman J, emphasized that a holistic view must be maintained when assessing whether a transfer of business has occurred. While certain assets were not transferred, the Court held that the core operational elements—including the infrastructure necessary for electricity supply—had been effectively returned to the Municipality ([31-39]).

The Labour Appeal Court had focused on the absence of certain assets to determine whether a transfer had occurred, which Froneman J found to be overly rigid and inconsistent with the substance-over-form principle emphasized in *NEHAWU*. The Court found that the key purpose of section 197 is to ensure continuity of employment when a business is transferred, not necessarily requiring a complete inventory of assets ([40-41]). 

In the minority judgment by Jafta J, a differing view emerged, arguing for the need to reassess how various assets contribute to establishing a “going concern.” The minority contended that the lack of a seamless operational capability justified the Labour Appeal Court’s decision, thereby suggesting that operations must continue without substantial disruptions ([43-147]).

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the application for leave to appeal, affirming the Labour Appeal Court’s decision and maintaining that the assertions made by Rural regarding the transfer of business did not meet the required legal thresholds under section 197 of the LRA. The ruling underscored the judiciary's role in interpreting complex employment protections in evolving economic contexts, ultimately frequently balancing commercial realities against employee rights ([42-42]).

In holding this way, the Court did not reformulate or adopt any new test but rather clarified the existing legal framework regarding business transfers under South African law, maintaining that while assets and specifics of the transaction are relevant, they do not alone determine outcomes. The case reaffirms the complexity and nuance inherent in interpreting legislative provisions intended to protect employees during transitions of ownership in business contexts.
