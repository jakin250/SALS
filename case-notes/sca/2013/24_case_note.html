FIRAC Case Note: Picbel Groep Voorsorgfonds v Somerville (405/12) [2013] ZASCA 24

Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 24 F – Facts: Relevant background and proced

FIRAC Case Note: Picbel Groep Voorsorgfonds v Somerville (405/12) [2013] ZASCA 24

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–4])

In the case of Picbel Groep Voorsorgfonds v Somerville, the appellants, comprising several pension funds (namely, Picbel Groep Voorsorgfonds, Sable Industries Pension Fund, Mitchell Cotts Pension Fund, Lucas South Africa Pension Fund, Datakor Pension Fund, Datakor Retirement Fund, and Cortech Pension Fund), incurred substantial losses of approximately R946 million due to the wrongful removal of surplus assets under a scheme known as the Ghavalas Option. These actions led to the curatorship or liquidation of the funds. The Funds initiated delictual claims against Alexander Forbes Financial Services (Pty) Ltd (hereinafter “Alexander Forbes”), alleging that they were among several wrongdoers responsible for the losses incurred ([para 1]).

Alexander Forbes provided notice to other potential wrongdoers under section 2(2)(b) of the Apportionment of Damages Act 34 of 1956 (the "Act"), allowing those not sued to intervene in the action. None of the notified parties intervened. Subsequently, Alexander Forbes and the Funds reached a settlement agreement whereby Alexander Forbes compensated the Funds with R325 million and ceded its rights to contributions against other potential joint wrongdoers under the Act. The Funds subsequently filed actions against the respondents to recover contributions based on this ceded right ([paras 2–4]).

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 5–6])

The principal legal issue in this case was whether the Funds had a valid claim against the respondents based on the ceded rights following the settlement with Alexander Forbes, specifically under section 2(12) of the Act. The court was tasked with determining if the settlement constituted a full settlement of the claims as required by the relevant provisions of the Act, thereby activating the right of recovery for contributions from the other joint wrongdoers ([para 6]).

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 8–15])

The legal framework for this case is fundamentally grounded in the Apportionment of Damages Act 34 of 1956. Key provisions include:

  1. Section 2(12): This section stipulates that if a joint wrongdoer agrees to pay a sum in full settlement of the plaintiff's claim, the provisions of subsection (6) apply mutatis mutandis, allowing for recovery from other joint wrongdoers if certain conditions are met ([para 8]).
  2. Section 2(6): This section addresses recovery of contributions, requiring that a judgment must be in full settlement of the plaintiff’s claim for a right of recourse to arise against other joint wrongdoers ([para 9]).
  3. Previous case law, such as Lewis v Oneanate (Pty) Ltd and Maize Board v Tiger Oats Ltd, further clarifies the necessary conditions for establishing a cause of action against joint wrongdoers ([paras 7, 6]).

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 10–18])

The Supreme Court of Appeal critically analyzed the interpretation of the settlement agreement. The respondents contended that the Funds' claims were invalid because the payment from Alexander Forbes did not constitute a full settlement of the claims. The lower court had upheld an exception to the Funds' action, ruling that without a full settlement, the ceded rights to contributions could not arise under section 2(12) of the Act ([para 10]).

Ponnan JA, in his judgment, emphasized the necessity of interpreting the settlement agreement within the context of the Act. He determined that the settlement did bring finality to the action against Alexander Forbes, despite not covering the full extent of damages claimed. The court distinguished between the total loss and the share attributable to Alexander Forbes, reiterating that the right to recover contributions can still be valid even when not all claims are settled ([paras 11, 12]).

The court further noted that the justification for allowing settlements for less than the total claim is to encourage practical resolutions to disputes while preserving the victims' rights. Therefore, the appellate court concluded that the Funds did surpass the threshold for establishing a cause of action under the provisions of the Act due to the valid cession of rights stemming from the settlement ([paras 13–18]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 18–47])

The Supreme Court ultimately allowed the appeal, holding that the Funds had established a valid cause of action under section 2(12) of the Act after the settlement with Alexander Forbes, which although not covering the full amount of loss, did provide finality and constituted a settlement of claims against one wrongdoer. The court ruled that the interpretation of the agreement should favor maintaining the rights to pursue contributions from other wrongdoers under the statute ([paras 18, 46]).

The implications of this decision underscore the importance of legislative provisions that facilitate recovery from joint wrongdoers, even when settlements are not for the entire claimed amount. This ruling allows victims to negotiate settlements with responsible parties without forfeiting their rights to pursue other culpable parties for their respective shares of liability ([para 47]).

In summary, the case emphasizes the effective leveraging of the Apportionment of Damages Act for the recovery of damages and encourages parties to engage in settlements without fear of undermining their legal avenues against co-wrongdoers. This precedent will shape future dealings with joint wrongdoers in similar contexts, providing clearer guidance on the interpretation and application of the Act.
