Supreme Court of Appeal

Dube v The State (523/07) [2009] ZASCA 28

Implications This case reinforces the doctrine of impartiality in the judiciary and the necessity for judges to be vigilant regarding any relationships that may give rise to perceptions of bias.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009 [2009] ZASCA 28

FIRAC Case Note

F - Facts ([paras 1–2])

On 26 March 2002, the First National Bank in Koster was robbed of R119,000 by four armed men. The appellants—Bongani Dube, Lodrick Allen Mkhize, and Ntobeko Ndlovu—were arrested, charged with robbery with aggravating circumstances, convicted, and each sentenced to 16 years' imprisonment. They appealed to the Bophuthatswana High Court before Mogoeng JP and Gura J, raising several grounds, including alleged irregularities and identity issues. Their appeal was dismissed. Subsequently, a special entry concerning the Judge President's alleged bias due to his wife representing the State was argued before the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 2]).

I - Issues ([paras 3–4])

The core issue before the court was whether the Judge President should have recused himself from the appeal, given his wife's representation of the State, and if his failure to do so amounted to a procedural irregularity that vitiated the appeal process ([para 3]). The appellants contended that the failure to recuse compromised the integrity of the judicial process and led to a reasonable apprehension of bias, while the respondent argued otherwise ([para 16]).

R - Rules ([paras 6–10])

The applicable legal principles regarding judicial bias were articulated in President of the Republic of South Africa v South African Rugby Football Union (1999) 4 SA 147 (CC), which established that both actual bias and perceived bias disqualify a judicial officer from presiding over a case ([para 8]). The test for bias is whether a reasonable, objective person would think that the judge could not be impartial. The Bangalore Principles of Judicial Conduct also emphasize the necessity of impartiality for a judge’s ethical conduct ([para 9]). Furthermore, the notion that justice must not only be done but must be seen to be done highlights the importance of maintaining public confidence in the judicial system ([para 9]).

A - Application ([paras 17–19])

In applying these principles, the court concluded that the apprehension of bias was reasonable given the familial relationship between the Judge President and the state advocate ([para 19]). The appellant counsel's lack of initial objection did not negate the appellants' perception of bias, as they only learned of the relationship post-judgment ([para 16]). The court also cited R v Bow Street Stipendiary Magistrate, Ex parte Pinochet Ugarte (1999), reinforcing that perceived conflicts of interest undermine confidence in judicial impartiality ([para 18]).

C - Conclusion ([paras 20–21])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the special entry, confirming that the Judge President's failure to recuse himself constituted a procedural irregularity that tainted the appeal ([para 20]). As a result, the court set aside the decision of the lower court and remitted the appeal for a re-hearing before a differently constituted Full Bench ([para 21]). This judgment underscores the critical nature of perceived impartiality in judicial proceedings, emphasizing judges' obligation to prevent any reasonable perception of bias to maintain public trust in the judiciary.

Implications

This case reinforces the doctrine of impartiality in the judiciary and the necessity for judges to be vigilant regarding any relationships that may give rise to perceptions of bias. The decision signals a commitment to uphold the integrity of judicial proceedings, ensuring that all parties perceive a fair and unbiased trial. It highlights the importance of procedural fairness in maintaining the rule of law, underpinning the fundamental principles of justice in South Africa.
