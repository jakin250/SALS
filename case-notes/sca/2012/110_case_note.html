The Minister of Defence v South African National Defence Union [2012] ZASCA 110 (30 August 2012)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2012 [2012] ZASCA 110 (30 August 2012)

FIRAC Structure

F – Facts

In this case, a significant event took place on 26 August 2009 when approximately 1,200 members of the South African National Defence Force (SANDF) gathered at the Union Buildings in Pretoria to demonstrate their grievances. Some participants were armed with weapons and the demonstration escalated, necessitating police intervention, including the use of water cannons and rubber bullets ([para 1]). In response to the unrest, the military authorities provisionally terminated the services of these personnel through notices issued on 30 August 2009, instructing them to show cause why this termination should not be confirmed ([para 2]).

The South African National Defence Union (the Union) and its President initiated an urgent application to the North Gauteng High Court, claiming that the procedure adopted by the military parties (Minister of Defence, Secretary for Defence, and Chief of the SANDF) was unlawful and unconstitutional. The Union sought two main orders: a declaration regarding the unlawfulness of the procedure and an interdict preventing the military from terminating the services of its members pending resolution of the dispute through the Military Bargaining Council ([para 3]).

The High Court ruled in favor of the Union, granting both orders. However, the appellants applied for leave to appeal against the order of interdict ([para 4]).

I – Issues

The core issues presented to the Supreme Court of Appeal involved:

  1. Whether the interdict, which restrained the military authorities from acting against the members of the Union, was properly granted ([para 5])?
  2. Whether the appellants had forfeited their right to appeal against the interdict due to their subsequent actions and statements ([para 17])?
  3. The appropriate disposition of costs in light of the conduct of the parties throughout the proceedings ([para 27]).

R – Rules

Key legal principles and statutory provisions relevant to this case included:

  • The standard for granting an interdict: an applicant must establish a clear right, the potential infringement of this right, and the absence of similar protection by any other ordinary remedy ([para 11]).
  • The Military Bargaining Council and the Military Arbitration Board are established under the Defence Act and are responsible for labor disputes involving SANDF personnel ([para 6],[7]).
  • The principles of acquiescence and waiver in litigation, which dictate that a party's conduct may indicate a relinquishment of the right to appeal if it demonstrates unequivocal inconsistency with an intention to maintain that right ([para 22]).

A – Application

In examining the interdict, the court found that the Union's claim of a "prima facie" right was not substantiated. The interdict was improperly directed at the military authorities' ability to discharge personnel pending the resolution of disputes, rather than maintaining the status quo during judicial consideration of the rights involved ([para 12]). The court emphasized that simply being entitled to refer a dispute to the Bargaining Council does not preclude the military from proceeding with disciplinary action pending that referral's outcome ([para 14]).

Additionally, the court noted that once the initial declaratory order was granted, there was no longer a dispute to refer to the Bargaining Council, thus rendering the interdict unnecessary ([para 15]).

As for the appellants' conduct, the Supreme Court observed that while the Minister's spokesman had initially indicated a withdrawal of the appeal, the confusion that followed indicated instability rather than a clear acquiescence to the judgment ([para 17],[18]). The court considered two critical factors: the absence of any disadvantage to the Union arising from the supposed abandonment and the constitutional duty of the military to maintain discipline, which could be hindered by the continued existence of the interdict ([para 25],[26]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court ruled that the interdict granted by the High Court was improperly awarded and thus set it aside. The court emphasized the importance of ensuring that the SANDF can efficiently maintain discipline and administer justice without undue hindrance from the interdict. The appeal was granted with costs awarded against the Union, as they contributed to prolonged litigation through unnecessary resistance to the appeal ([paras 30-33]). This case underscores the necessity for government entities to conduct litigation with maturity and public interest in mind, particularly when the procedural outcome affects major public services such as the military.

References

The judgment cited various authorities consistent with South African case law and the Defence Act, including provisions concerning the conduct of military litigation and the rules for granting interdicts. It reiterated the principles established in cases such as Dabner v South African Railways and Harbours and Government of the Republic of South Africa v Von Abo, highlighting the importance of navigating procedural correctness alongside substantive rights within public institutions.
