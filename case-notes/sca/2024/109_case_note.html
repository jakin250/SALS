Supreme Court of Appeal

IRD Global Limited v The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria (504/2023)

The appellant, IRD Global Limited (IRD), is a Singapore-registered global health delivery and research organization founded in Pakistan.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 109 (04 July 2024) --- ### F – Facts The appel

Neutral Citation: IRD Global Limited v The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria (504/2023) [2024] ZASCA 109 (04 July 2024)

F – Facts

The appellant, IRD Global Limited (IRD), is a Singapore-registered global health delivery and research organization founded in Pakistan. It has operational affiliates in nine countries, including South Africa, where it collaborates with local governments and community organizations to bridge healthcare access gaps ([2]).

The respondent, The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria (The Global Fund), is an international entity established in Switzerland that manages funding for health initiatives. Both parties are peregrini to the South African High Court, lacking immovable property in the country ([2]).

On 1 April 2021, the respondent published a report on its website titled "Global Fund Grant in Pakistan - Prohibited Practices Compromised Procurement in Tuberculosis Program." This report alleged various misconducts by the appellant, notably concerning its role in administering a tuberculosis grant to Indus Hospital in Pakistan. Noteworthy allegations included conflict of interest and fraudulent practices ([3]). The publication was accompanied by a letter from the respondent’s executive director endorsing the allegations, which the appellant perceived as defamatory ([3]).

Prior to the publication, the respondent had notified the appellant about an investigation being carried out by its Office of the Inspector General (OIG), providing preliminary findings that indicated serious misconduct by IRD and Indus ([4]). Despite IRD's comprehensive rebuttal to these findings submitted in July 2020, the allegations were made public in the April 2021 report, leading to IRD’s application for interim relief to retract the statements ([5]).

On 10 May 2021, IRD sought urgent interim relief from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg, including an order to retract the published report and prohibit further defamatory statements. However, the respondent countered by asserting that the High Court lacked jurisdiction to entertain the matter, claiming it should instead be filed in either Pakistan or Switzerland ([7]).

The High Court, upon hearing the case, concluded that it indeed had no jurisdiction to consider the application and ruled for additional security for costs ([11]). The application was subsequently dismissed ([11]).

I – Issues

The issues central to the appeal are:

  1. Whether the Gauteng Division of the High Court correctly determined that it lacked jurisdiction to entertain IRD's application for interim relief.
  2. Whether the High Court correctly exercised its discretion in granting the respondent's application for additional security for costs ([1]).

R – Rules

The court's analysis concerning jurisdiction primarily rested on established principles regarding the effectiveness and appropriateness of a court's ability to enforce its judgments. It referenced the necessity of a sufficient connection between the cause of action and the jurisdiction. The core legal principles affirmed in Bid Industrial Holdings (Pty) Ltd v John Francis Roderick Strang and Others determined that jurisdiction requires not just service of summons within South Africa but adequate ties to the South African context ([14]).

Moreover, a court may exercise discretion in matters relating to interim relief through quantifiable legal standards, wherein a prospective claimant must demonstrate a prima facie case for relief, the prospect of irreparable harm, the balance of convenience, and the absence of an alternative remedy ([20]).

In cases such as NBC Holdings (Pty) Ltd v Akani Retirement Fund Administrators (Pty) Ltd and Tau v Mashaba and Others, the courts determined that requests for retractions or apologies traditionally necessitate final equitable relief following a substantive action rather than via an urgent motion ([22]; [24]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized the jurisdiction issue by considering both parties’ lack of substantive links to South Africa, emphasizing that an effective judgment necessitates some form of enforceability within the jurisdiction's boundaries. Despite the appellant's claims of accessing the report in Johannesburg and having an affiliate in South Africa, the court found no substantial local presence or connection to justify jurisdiction as established in Strang. Thus, the court maintained that the relevant cause of action arose in Pakistan, given that both the subject matter and involved entities were tied to operations there ([15]–[18]).

Further, regarding the interim relief sought by the appellant, the court highlighted that the allegations presented by the respondent were backed by an extensive investigative process, substantiating its claims of truth and public interest related to the report's publication. Notably, the temporal gap between the report's initial leak and the subsequent request for interim relief indicated that IRD did not establish a basis for immediate need of protection ([19]).

This undermined any argument for an urgent need to prevent ongoing harm since much of the damage had been established prior to any motion. The court also noted that IRD’s request for a retraction and an apology actually sought final relief rather than interim, thereby misaligning with procedural norms for defamation cases ([24]–[26]).

In analyzing the security for costs, the Court recognized that the trial court acted within its discretion. The court justified requiring additional security given the complexities of the case, emphasizing that the contextual nuances surrounding peregrini and potential enforcement costs warranted such a measure ([27]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal for both the jurisdictional and security issues, confirming the High Court's ruling that it lacked jurisdiction to entertain IRD's interim relief application. The court established that the absence of a substantive connection to South Africa rendered it an inappropriate forum for adjudicating the matter ([28]).

Moreover, the findings demonstrated a judicial adherence to the standards required for interim interdictory relief and jurisdiction, reaffirming established legal tenets regarding defamation claims and the necessity of clear connections between a cause of action and jurisdiction ([28]). Consequently, this judgment underscores the complexities of international litigation involving parties without substantial local ties while maintaining that procedural norms dictate that defamation claims must yield to thorough examination through full litigation, rather than rushed motions seeking interim relief. The implications for IRD and similar entities indicate the importance of understanding jurisdictional limitations and the procedural requisites necessary to safeguard their reputations in instances of claimed defamation ([28]).

This case note aims to articulate a nuanced understanding of the legal principles surrounding jurisdiction and interim measures in defamation actions, thereby contributing valuable insights to the field of constitutional and international law.
