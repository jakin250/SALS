Counter Point Furnishers CC v S M Fraser t/a Salica (896/10) [2012] ZASCA 7

The respondent issued a provisional sentence summons against the applicant, claiming interest amounting to R215,087 based on the aforementioned clause.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2012 [2012] ZASCA 7

F – Facts

The case arises from a contractual dispute between Counter Point Furnishers CC (the applicant) and S M Fraser, trading as Salica (the respondent). The dispute centers around a written agreement for the sale of immovable property. Key to this case is Clause 17.1 of the agreement, which stipulates that any party causing a delay in the registration of transfer must pay interest on the purchase price, calculated at the rate charged by financial institutions on first home loan mortgages, from the date of notification of default until the default ceases ([2]).

The respondent issued a provisional sentence summons against the applicant, claiming interest amounting to R215,087 based on the aforementioned clause. The claim was made on the grounds that the applicant failed to furnish a guarantee as required under Clause 4.1 of the agreement, leading to a delay in registration. The respondent alleged that this delay occurred from May 8, 2009, to September 3, 2009, and sought to substantiate this claim with reference to the bank interest rate of 11% charged by ABSA Bank ([3]).

The applicant resisted the summons through an answering affidavit. The resistance was focused on questioning the liquidity of the written agreement, arguing that various components—including the interest applied, the identity of the bank, and the dates of alleged default—were disputed and required evidential substantiation. The applicant posited that its liability was conditional on being found responsible for the delay in registration, which could not be established through affidavit evidence alone ([4]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue at hand is whether the order granting provisional sentence is appealable under exceptional circumstances, in light of the applicant's contention that the underlying document (the written agreement) lacks liquidity. The applicant invokes precedent set in Smit v Scania South Africa (Pty) Ltd to argue for the appealability of provisional sentence judgments based on unique factual scenarios that may indicate the document's non-liquid nature ([5]).

R – Rules

The legal principles governing provisional sentence, as established in precedential cases like Avtjoglou v First National Bank of Southern Africa Ltd, dictate that a provisional sentence can be granted based on a liquid document, which must be capable of determining the debt without resort to extensive evidence ([5]).

The case of Smit v Scania South Africa (Pty) Ltd establishes that there are exceptional situations in which a provisional sentence judgment may be shown to be appealable, albeit acknowledging that these instances are rare and must be sufficiently justified ([6]).

A – Application

In the judgment, the court examined the applicant's claim that the circumstances surrounding the provisional sentence granted were exceptional enough to render the order appealable. The court found that the applicant failed to demonstrate how the current case diverged from established precedents concerning provisional sentence, specifically in light of the arguments regarding liquidity.

While the applicant raised valid points regarding the need for evidentiary support concerning the contested aspects of the case, the court noted that the applicant did not sufficiently exemplify why these disputes warranted treating the provisional sentence as appealable. Instead, the applicant's arguments were deemed consistent with standard challenges associated with provisional sentence, which do not, by themselves, meet the threshold for exceptional circumstances ([5]-[6]).

The court ultimately dismissed the application for leave to appeal, concluding that the applicant's arguments lacked the necessary evidential weight to overturn the provisional sentence order, thus reaffirming the threshold for appealability remains high ([7]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the application for leave to appeal, underscoring the strictly defined parameters surrounding provisional sentence judgments. The decision reaffirms the principles of liquidity required for such sentences, illustrating that merely contesting various aspects of a claim does not suffice to elevate a provisional sentence to an appealable status under exceptional circumstances. Consequently, this judgment highlights the importance of demonstrating clear and specific grounds for appeal in provisional sentence cases, maintaining a rigorous standard that protects the integrity of such expedited processes in contractual disputes ([7]).
