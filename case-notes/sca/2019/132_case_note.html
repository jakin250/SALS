Ndimande v The State (248/2018) [2019] ZASCA 132

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 132

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

In the case Ndimande v The State, the appellant, Fanie Archibald Ndimande, was convicted by the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg, on multiple charges including murder, robbery with aggravating circumstances, and attempted murder, resulting in a life imprisonment sentence ([1]). The conviction arose from an armed robbery incident at Amabele Spar, Ivory Park, Johannesburg, on August 3, 2003. A gang of armed men attempted to rob the store while two staff members and a security guard were closing it for the night. During the incident, the security guard was fatally shot after a struggle ensued ([2]).

The key evidence against Ndimande was the identification made by Mr. Hamilton Mbatha, the security manager, who claimed to have identified Ndimande from video footage one day after the robbery, asserting that Ndimande had removed his balaclava at one point ([4]). However, the video evidence in question was never submitted at trial, and Mbatha's identification was made solely during trial proceedings from the witness box, a process referred to as "dock identification" ([5]).

Ndimande was arrested on December 24, 2003, and alleged that he was assaulted by the police during his arrest. He asserted that he had declared his intent to make a statement in court but was coerced into conducting a pointing out of the crime scene on December 26, 2003, due to these assaults ([6], [10]). The trial court admitted the pointing-out evidence, concluding that Ndimande had communicated a willingness to assist the police. This finding was central to both his conviction and the subsequent rejection of his appeal by the full court ([8]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues to be resolved in this appeal included:

  1. The reliability of Mr. Mbatha's identification of Ndimande ([3]).
  2. The admissibility of the evidence obtained through Ndimande's pointing out and whether its inclusion rendered the trial unfair ([10]).
  3. Whether the trial court properly evaluated the evidence regarding the infringements of Ndimande’s rights under section 35 of the Constitution of South Africa ([17]).

Additionally, issues around the admissibility of dock identification and the failure of the prosecution to produce relevant video evidence were considered significant factors ([9], [16]).

R – Rules

Several legal principles and statutory provisions were relevant to this case:

  1. Section 35 of the Constitution: Protects the right to a fair trial, including the right to remain silent and to not be compelled to testify against oneself ([17]).
  2. Evidence law on Identification: Courts have historically emphasized the need for a cautious approach to evidence of identification, especially when it relies solely on the testimony of a single witness ([11]). Case law such as S v Mdlongwa ([13]) illustrates the importance of corroborative evidence in identification proceedings.
  3. Exclusion of Evidence: Section 35(5) of the Constitution mandates the exclusion of evidence obtained in a manner that violates an accused's rights when its admission could render the trial unfair ([18]).

A – Application

In applying these principles, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that:

  • The trial court had erred in relying on Mr. Mbatha's dock identification, as it lacked corroborative evidence. Mr. Mbatha had not only failed to support his identification with the critical video footage, but the absence of this evidence made his testimony unreliable ([9], [12]).
  • There were serious discrepancies in the testimonies of the police, particularly that of Inspector Mogoboya, who alleged that Ndimande volunteered to assist in the pointing out. The contradictions regarding his role and the circumstances of the pointing out raised doubts regarding its voluntary nature ([15], [28]).
  • Importantly, the court recognized that Ndimande's constitutional rights were violated, notably his right to remain silent and the failure to inform him of the implications of his admissions. The trial court’s acceptance of the pointing out evidence was deemed a misdirection, as it followed mutual corroboration of conflicting testimonies in a manner that undermined Ndimande's rights ([18], [20], [34]).

Since the evidence relied upon by the prosecution was fundamentally flawed, the overall reliance on it rendered the trial procedurally and substantively unfair to Ndimande ([22]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, setting aside the conviction and sentence ([23]). The failure to produce the video footage combined with the unreliable nature of Mbatha's identification and the breach of Ndimande's constitutional rights made the trial prejudicial. Consequently, the State had not proven its case beyond a reasonable doubt, leading to the conclusion that the judicial process had indeed compromised the fundamentals of justice ([22], [38]).

Implications

This case highlights the critical importance of adhering to constitutional protections during criminal trials, especially regarding the acquisition of evidence. It reaffirms the cautious approach necessary for identification evidence and fortifies the necessity of ensuring that the rights of the accused are upheld at all stages of the criminal process. The rulings serve as a cautionary tale about the risks of relying on potentially coerced confessions and uncorroborated identification.
