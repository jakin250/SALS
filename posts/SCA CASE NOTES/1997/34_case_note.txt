# FIRAC Case Note: Saak nr 304/95

**Case Title**: Saak nr 304/95  
**Court**: Transvaal High Court  
**Judges**: Vivier AR, Olivier AR, Zulman AR  
**Date of Hearing**: 2 May 1997  
**Date of Judgment**: 2 May 1997  

## F - Facts

In this case, the appellant, Amos Malesele Masemeni, was charged along with two other co-accused with multiple counts related to a violent crime, specifically a robbery that led to the murder of Kenneth Engels and assault on his wife, Valerie Engels ([para 2]). The charges arose from an incident that occurred on 12 April 1992, where the Engels couple was attacked in their residence near Cullinan ([para 1]).

At the beginning of the trial, charges against the first accused were withdrawn as he had absconded. The trial resulted in Masemeni being convicted of various charges including housebreaking intending to rob, murder, assault of Mrs. Engels, attempted murder, and illegal possession of a firearm ([para 2]). On the murder charge, he received the death penalty, while on the other charges, he was sentenced to an effective 16 years' imprisonment.

Masemeni appealed against his conviction and sentence for murder under section 316A(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, claiming that the evidence against him was insufficient to justify his conviction ([para 3]). During the appeal, the attorney for Masemeni did not pursue the appeal on his conviction ([para 4]).

The evidence presented by Mrs. Engels indicated that she and her husband were awakened during the night. She witnessed her husband being shot as he attempted to protect them with his firearm ([para 5]). Following the incident, police found that the burglars had gained entry by breaking a window, and numerous stolen articles were recovered subsequently.

Masemeni's own account during the trial contradicted his initial statement to the magistrate, where he claimed coercion by one of the co-accused and described his fear when participating in the crime ([paras 9–12]). However, the trial court rejected his claim of duress, stating he acted willingly and knowingly from the outset of the robbery ([para 14]).

## I - Issues

The primary legal questions presented for the court's consideration were:

1. Whether Masemeni's conviction for murder was justified based on the evidence that indicated he had common purpose with the co-accused in the commission of the crime ([para 4]).
2. The legitimacy of applying the death penalty in this case against the backdrop of constitutional reforms and changes in the legal landscape, particularly following the decision in *S v Makwanyane* ([para 14]).

## R - Rules

The court primarily relied on principles of criminal law concerning common purpose and the mens rea associated with serious crimes such as murder. Under South African law, an individual can be held liable for the actions of co-perpetrators if there is a concerted effort toward the commission of a crime ([para 13]).

Additionally, the court referenced the implications of the enactment of the South African Constitution of 1996, particularly regarding the death penalty as a permissible form of punishment in light of previous judicial decrees ([para 15]). The ruling in *S v Makwanyane* clearly stipulated that the death penalty was deemed unconstitutional and therefore invalid as a punishment ([para 15]).

## A - Application

In its deliberation, the court analyzed Masemeni's actions during the robbery alongside those of his co-accused. It upheld that Masemeni’s behavior, characterized by his complicity and the deliberate actions he took during the commission of the crime, constituted a sufficient basis for a murder conviction under the principle of common purpose. The court noted that his claim of acting under duress was unsupported by credible evidence, emphasizing that there was no indication in his earlier statement that suggested he was coerced into participating in the crime ([paras 12–14]).

The trial court's assessment regarded Masemeni as having prior knowledge of the potential for violence and death, as he understood that a weapon was present ([para 16]). The court concluded that his level of awareness amounted to 'criminal negligence', making the murder conviction appropriate based on ‘possibility awareness’, a standard in South African jurisprudence concerning culpability ([para 14]).

As for the death sentence, the court acknowledged the significant shift in legal context following the inception of the new constitution. It cited the precedent set in *S v Makwanyane*, where the Constitutional Court invalidated the death penalty. It hence necessitated the overturning of the death sentence imposed on Masemeni, with instructions for the trial court to reconsider the sentencing under the new legal framework ([paras 14–15]).

## C - Conclusion

The court concluded as follows:

1. Masemeni's appeal against the conviction for murder was dismissed. The evidence sufficiently demonstrated his active participation and the foresight of violence integrated into the robbery, qualifying the conviction under common purpose ([para 15]).
   
2. The death penalty was lifted, making the sentencing invalid due to the pre-existing constitutional prohibitions against such punishment post-1995. The judgment mandated the lower court to resentence Masemeni for the murder charge ([para 15]).

Overall, the case illustrates the judiciary's evolving stance on capital punishment in South Africa and underscores the robust legal principles governing complicity and criminal liability in violent crimes.
