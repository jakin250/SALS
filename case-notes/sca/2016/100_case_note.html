Supreme Court of Appeal

Hewitt v The State (637/2015) [2016] ZASCA 100

Robert Anthony John Hewitt, a 75-year-old retired champion tennis player, faced conviction for two counts of rape and one count of indecent assault against three minors: two girls (ages 12 and 13) from the early 1980s and another girl (age 17) in 1994.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 100

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts ([1]–[6])

Robert Anthony John Hewitt, a 75-year-old retired champion tennis player, faced conviction for two counts of rape and one count of indecent assault against three minors: two girls (ages 12 and 13) from the early 1980s and another girl (age 17) in 1994. The incidents occurred during tennis coaching sessions, where Hewitt exploited his position of authority to abuse the complainants. Following a protracted period of inaction, largely due to various legal and societal constraints, Hewitt was ultimately convicted in March 2015.

The Gauteng Division of the High Court sentenced Hewitt to eight years' imprisonment for each count of rape and two years for the indecent assault, all to run concurrently, resulting in an effective sentence of six years after two years of each rape sentence were suspended, contingent on a monetary contribution to anti-abuse campaigns ([1]).

Hewitt’s alleged crimes had significant psychological and emotional repercussions on his victims, who struggled with relationships and personal trauma. Despite this, the court a quo acknowledged mitigating factors such as his age, health status, and lack of prior convictions ([5]–[6]).

I – Issues ([7]–[12])

The primary legal issue was whether the sentences imposed by the trial court were inappropriate given the mitigating circumstances presented, namely Hewitt’s advanced age and declining health. The appeal contended that the trial court failed to maintain an appropriate balance between these factors and the gravity of the offenses committed.

Additional questions included:

  1. Whether the trial court misdirected itself in its sentencing discretion.
  2. The importance of societal expectations regarding punishment for sexual offenses, especially in light of South Africa's historical context regarding such crimes, including the high incidence of rape in the country.
  3. How the courts must balance the rights and dignity of the victims against the candidacies for leniency based on mitigating factors.

R – Rules ([8]–[12])

The decision cited several key legal principles:

  • Under South African law, the imposition of sentence is primarily the prerogative of the trial court ([8]). An appellate court may only interfere if it finds a significant misdirection in how the trial court exercised its discretion.
  • The courts have consistently expressed the necessity of imposing stern sentences for sexual offenses, reflecting society's abhorrence for such crimes ([9]).
  • It is recognized that while mitigating factors, such as age and health, are essential considerations, they must be carefully weighed against the nature of the crime and its impact on the victims and broader society ([10]–[11]).

Key cases which informed the judgment include S v Pieters and S v Sadler, which detail the need for an appellate court to demonstrate a “startling” misalignment between its views and the trial court’s decision before interference is warranted ([8]).

A – Application ([13]–[17])

In its analysis, the Supreme Court of Appeal highlighted that while the appellant’s age and health condition were relevant, they did not outweigh the heinous nature of the sexual offences committed. The judgment detailed how Hewitt's actions inflicted lasting psychological harm on the victims, which persisted into their adult lives.

The court emphasized the need to reinforce societal norms against sexual crimes, denouncing the notion that reputational damage sufficed as sufficient punishment for a figure of high standing. It firmly asserted that the law treats all offenders equally, regardless of their social status.

The court found the appellant's insistence that he had learned his lesson insufficient, especially given his lack of remorse during the trial. The court also noted that the delays in prosecution should not serve as a basis for leniency, acknowledging that such delays are not uncommon in similar historical contexts ([17]).

C – Conclusion ([18])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal. The court reaffirmed that the sentences imposed by the trial court were appropriate, serving the dual functions of deterrence and retribution. It concluded that substantial weight was given to both the nature of the crimes and the long-term impact on the victims, validating the trial court's consideration of the factors in sentencing.

The decision illuminates the balance that South African courts must strike between compassion for the offender’s age and health and the imperative to uphold justice for victims of sexual violence. The court's ruling underscores the principle that heinous offences such as those committed by Hewitt cannot be mitigated strictly by personal circumstances, thereby serving as a clarion call for justice in the realm of sexual violence in South Africa.
