Supreme Court of Appeal

Charlton v Parliament of the Republic of South Africa (680/2010)

Charlton's allegation of retaliation stemmed from his initial report in December 2002, which uncovered improper claims by Members.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2011 1995 (LRA) and the Protected Disclosures Act 26

F – Facts

The case of Charlton v Parliament of the Republic of South Africa (680/2010) primarily concerns the appeal of Harry Mathew Charlton against the Labour Appeal Court's dismissal of exceptions raised by the Parliament regarding his claim for unfair dismissal ([1]). Charlton, who served as the Chief Financial Officer to Parliament from May 1, 2002, until his dismissal on January 13, 2006, alleged that he was terminated as retaliation for disclosing fraud by Members of Parliament concerning travel benefits, which is commonly referred to as the 'Travelgate scandal' ([1][2]).

Charlton's allegation of retaliation stemmed from his initial report in December 2002, which uncovered improper claims by Members. Despite his ongoing collaboration with various departments and authorities, support from Parliament declined after a change in the senior management ([3][4][5]). Charlton was subsequently suspended in November 2005 without a prior hearing, followed by a disciplinary inquiry that led to his dismissal due to charges of misconduct ([9]). He pursued a claim asserting that his dismissal fell within several categories of automatic unfairness per the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA) and the Protected Disclosures Act 26 of 2000 (PDA) ([10]).

In subsequent legal proceedings, the Labour Court (LC) dismissed some of Parliament's exceptions but granted leave to appeal to the Labour Appeal Court (LAC) ([11]). The LAC later upheld the exceptions, prompting Charlton’s appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([11][12]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues in this case involve the appealability of the Labour Court's dismissal of the exceptions raised by Parliament and the substantive merits of those exceptions. Specifically, two points of contention were paramount:

  1. Whether Members of Parliament can be classified as 'employers' or 'employees' within the context of the PDA, and consequently, whether Charlton's complaint regarding his dismissal under s 187(1)(h) of the LRA constituted an actionable cause of action ([12][13]).
  2. Whether the Labour Court lacked jurisdiction to entertain Charlton's claims regarding ordinary unfair dismissal, and if so, whether this lack of jurisdiction affected the appealability of this issue ([21][22]).

R – Rules

The court relied on several legal authorities and principles, among which were:

  1. Section 187(1)(h) of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995, which defines automatic unfair dismissals in cases of disclosures made by employees regarding their employer's conduct as outlined in the Protected Disclosures Act ([14]).
  2. Protected Disclosures Act 26 of 2000, specifically defining what constitutes a protected disclosure and the contexts in which such disclosures grant the employee protection against dismissal ([12]).
  3. **The precedent established in Zweni v Minister of Law and Order 1993 (1) SA 523 (A)**, clarifying that not all rulings are appealable, especially disposals that do not finally determine a party's rights ([15][16]).
  4. Rules of the Labour Court, particularly Rule 11(3), which allows procedural flexibility in handling exceptions and appeals ([16]).

The application of these rules served to guide the court’s determination of the appeal's legitimacy and the applicability of jurisdictional claims.

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal undertook an extensive analysis of the Labour Appeal Court's approach and the underlying principles governing exceptions within the litigation process.

  1. Regarding Exception A, the court found that the LAC's ruling—asserting that Members were both 'employers' and 'employees' under the PDA—was fundamentally flawed. The SCA reaffirmed that dismissals of exceptions do not traditionally carry appealability since they do not yield finality on points raised, reiterating established jurisprudence from Blaauwbosch Diamonds Ltd v Union Government ([18][19]). Consequently, they held that the issues raised could be revisited at trial, making the LAC's earlier ruling inappropriate for appeal ([20][21]).
  2. On the matter of Exception F, which questioned the Labour Court's jurisdiction to hear the ordinary unfair dismissal claims, the SCA clarified that the Labour Court had neither confirmed nor denied its jurisdiction. They emphasized that jurisdiction should have been conclusively established prior to further proceeding and that multiple claims presented by a party should not preclude a jurisdictional determination ([24][25]). The Court further articulated that a jurisdictional dispute remains a legal hurdle until competence is verified via evidence, rendering the apportionment to arbitration inapplicable at this stage ([22][24]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Charlton's appeal, determining that the Labour Appeal Court's decisions regarding both exceptions were fundamentally erroneous. The SCA ordered the LAC's ruling to be set aside, striking the appeal from the roll with costs ([1], [25]).

In both cases, the SCA underscored the legal principle that dismissals of exceptions do not conclude an action's merits, hence reaffirming the importance of allowing full consideration during trial. This case not only reiterates the procedural integrity of legal discourse in matters of employment law but also highlights the courts' critical role in safeguarding whistleblowers against retaliatory dismissal, especially pertinent in cases involving public interest and governmental accountability.
