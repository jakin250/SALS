Da Silva v Road Accident Fund and Another [2014] ZACC 21

Constitutional Court 2014 [2014] ZACC 21

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case arises from an application for confirmation of a constitutional invalidity order granted by the Free State High Court, Bloemfontein ([1]). The applicant, Vanessa Da Silva, sustained severe injuries as a passenger in a vehicle accident on 29 April 2006. The vehicle was operated by her husband, whose negligence caused the collision with a horse. Under the then-applicable section 19(b)(ii) of the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 (the "old Act"), Ms. Da Silva was barred from claiming damages from the Road Accident Fund (RAF), since she was a member of the driver’s household and the claim arose from her husband’s fault ([2],[3]). Although the provision was repealed by an amendment in 2005, it retained its application to claims arising before 1 August 2008, as demonstrated by Ms. Da Silva’s inability to obtain compensation ([4]).

Ms. Da Silva subsequently challenged the constitutionality of section 19(b)(ii) in the High Court on the grounds that it violated her right to equality under section 9 of the Constitution. The High Court ruled in her favor, declaring the section inconsistent with the Constitution and invalid ([5]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue before the Constitutional Court was whether section 19(b)(ii) of the Road Accident Fund Act, as it stood prior to its 2008 amendment, was unconstitutional on the basis of unfair discrimination ([1]). Specifically, the Court had to assess if the exclusion of claimants who were passengers and members of the driver's household was consistent with the right to equality and whether it constituted indirect discrimination based on marital status and age ([6]–[8]).

R – Rules

In determining the constitutional validity of statutory provisions, the Court referred to the Harksen test which establishes whether discrimination exists by examining whether there is a rational connection between the differentiation made by the law and a legitimate government purpose ([8]). Furthermore, section 9 of the Constitution prohibits unfair discrimination on various grounds, including marital status and age ([9]). The Court also acknowledged previous rulings, particularly Mvumvu and Others v Minister for Transport, which had addressed constitutional flaws in related provisions of the Road Accident Fund Act ([9]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court, guided by the principles outlined in the Harksen test, evaluated the rationale behind the exclusion prescribed in section 19(b)(ii). The relevant provision differentiated between passengers based on their relationship to the driver, excluding spouses and children from claiming compensation. Although the RAF, in its submissions, referenced an intent to prevent fraudulent claims through collusion among household members, the Court found this justification lacking in a rational connection to the purpose of the law and disallowed its current application ([7]).

The Court noted that even if a rational connection could be found, the law still constituted indirect discrimination due to its disproportionate impact on those in close familial relations with the driver. Both the RAF and the Minister of Transport failed to rebut the presumption of unfair discrimination regarding marital status and age, confirming that the provision was fundamentally flawed in its operation ([8]).

Resolving the remedy, the Court endorsed the High Court’s findings by confirming the invalidity of section 19(b)(ii). It imposed conditions limiting the relief to claims that were pending and not prescribed, thus striking a balance between victims' rights and the financial implications for the RAF. The order also specified that affected claims would be governed by the provisions of the Transitional Act, ensuring orderly resolution following the constitutional confirmation ([10],[11]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court’s declaration that section 19(b)(ii) of the Road Accident Fund Act, as it stood before 1 August 2008, was unconstitutional and invalid ([11]). Consequently, Ms. Da Silva was entitled to compensation, and the RAF was ordered to pay her costs, including those of two counsel. This ruling underscores the importance of upholding constitutional rights, particularly the right to equality and the prohibition of unfair discrimination, reinforcing the principle that legal provisions must align with constitutional guarantees to be deemed valid. The implications of this decision extend beyond the immediate parties, influencing the legislative framework governing the Road Accident Fund and common law principles regarding claims for compensation in similar contexts.
