The Occupiers, Shulana Court, 11 Hendon Road, Yeoville, Johannesburg v Steele [2010] ZASCA 28

Supreme Court of Appeal 2010 [2010] ZASCA 28

F – Facts

The appellants in this case are a group of individuals who occupy property located at 11 Hendon Road, Yeoville, Johannesburg ([1]). The matter presented two appeals: one against a default eviction order issued by the South Gauteng High Court and the other against the dismissal of their application for rescission of that order ([1]).

The respondent, Mr. Mark Steele, acquired the property in February 1993. The property consists of multiple flats which had been subdivided into smaller units, leading to overcrowding and dilapidation. The respondent decided to renovate the premises, resulting in a notice given to the appellants on 30 October 2007 to vacate by 31 January 2008, which they did not comply with ([2]–[3]).

Following the failure to vacate, the respondent initiated eviction proceedings in April 2008. The appellants, believing they had legal representation through the Inner City Resources Centre (ICRC), did not oppose the proceedings. As a result, the high court granted the eviction order on 18 June 2008 ([3]).

After their eviction application was granted, the appellants applied for rescission, arguing they had not wilfully defaulted and that they possessed a bona fide defense against the eviction. Their application was dismissed by Tsoka J, who ruled that no sufficient grounds had been shown ([4]).

The appellants asserted that the eviction was unjustifiable based on sections 4(6) and 4(7) of the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE), as well as section 26(1) and (3) of the Constitution, which guarantees the right to adequate housing ([9]). They argued that the high court had not considered the relevant circumstances that would render the eviction unfair, particularly the risk of homelessness for the occupants ([9]–[10]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues identified by the court include:

  1. Whether the appellants established sufficient grounds for rescission of the eviction order ([4], [17]).
  2. Whether the high court correctly applied the criteria for granting eviction orders under PIE, specifically regarding the consideration of the justice and equity of the eviction ([11], [14]).
  3. Whether the appellants’ belief that they would receive legal representation was reasonable and justified, thereby affecting their default in the original proceedings ([8], [17]).

R – Rules

The legal framework governing this case includes several critical statutes and case law principles:

  1. Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE), particularly sections 4(6) and 4(7), which dictate that courts can only grant eviction orders if it is just and equitable to do so, considering all relevant circumstances ([9], [10], [15]).
  2. Constitution of the Republic of South Africa (1996) – Section 26 provides for the right to adequate housing and protects individuals from eviction without due process ([10]).
  3. Case law precedents such as Grootboom v Government of the Republic of South Africa and Port Elizabeth Municipality v Various Occupiers, which highlight the necessity for courts to consider the circumstances of vulnerable occupants in eviction cases ([10], [12]).

A – Application

The court applied the legal principles by analyzing the circumstances surrounding the appellants' default and the eviction order:

  • The court was satisfied that the appellants established good cause for rescission, as they demonstrated a reasonable explanation for their default, believing they would be legally represented through ICRC ([8], [17]).
  • In determining whether the eviction order was just and equitable, the court scrutinized the high court's failure to consider critical factors such as the personal circumstances of the appellants and the absence of evidence related to their socioeconomic status. The high court did not obtain necessary information that could have informed its decision-making on the implications of eviction, thus failing its statutory obligations under PIE ([14]–[15]).
  • The evidence provided with the rescission application indicated that the appellant occupiers consisted largely of poor individuals, including vulnerable groups, and eviction could result in homelessness ([16]). This was significant for the court's determination that the appellants had a bona fide defense against the eviction ([16], [17]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal against the refusal for rescission, emphasizing that the high court had erred in not adequately addressing the factors relevant to the appellants' circumstances and the legal protections afforded to them under both PIE and the Constitution. The court's ruling implies a reaffirmation of the judiciary's duty to protect vulnerable individuals facing eviction and mandates that courts must be diligent in gathering and considering all pertinent factual information ([18]).

Order of the Court:

  1. The rescission appeal (case no 499/09) is upheld with costs.
  2. The default judgment against the appellants is rescinded, allowing them to oppose the eviction ([18]).
  3. The court made no costs order regarding the appeal against the original eviction order (case no 102/09) ([18]).

This ruling significantly reinforces the procedural and substantive rights of occupiers facing eviction, augmenting the judicial responsibility to ensure fairness and just principles in eviction matters.
