# Case Note: KwaZulu-Natal Joint Liaison Committee vs. Member of the Executive Council, Department of Education, KwaZulu-Natal

**FIRAC Method**

## Facts

[1] The applicant, the KwaZulu-Natal Joint Liaison Committee, is an association of independent schools seeking leave to appeal against a dismissal by the KwaZulu-Natal High Court, Pietermaritzburg, regarding an application to enforce payment of promised subsidies for the year 2009. Leave to appeal was also denied by the Supreme Court of Appeal.

[2] The case emerged from a subsidy that the first respondent, the Member of the Executive Council for Education in KwaZulu-Natal, granted to independent schools under section 48 of the South African Schools Act 84 of 1996. On 22 September 2008, the second respondent, the Department of Education in KwaZulu-Natal, circulated a notice delineating "approximate" funding levels for the upcoming financial year.

[3] The notice targeted independent school principals and advised them on the funding structure, notably promising specific financial support levels for both primary and secondary educational institutions based on previously established averages. However, the notice concluded by informing schools that "subsidy allocations will be reviewed annually."

[4] Subsequently, a letter dated 5 May 2009, indicated a budget cut of up to 30% in the promised subsidy allocation due to an overarching cash crisis affecting the provincial budget.

[5] The respondent’s budget allocated R55.861 million for independent schools, divided among the 97 schools included in the subsidy structure. Actual payments reflected a reduction of 30% compared to the amounts mentioned in the initial notice.

[6] In November 2010, the applicant initiated legal action to enforce the amounts indicated in the 2008 notice, claiming that it constituted an enforceable promise, which the Department subsequently breached.

## Issues

[9] The pivotal legal question is whether the 2008 notice, which outlined "approximate" funding levels and was treated by the applicant as a binding promise, constituted an enforceable obligation. Related questions involve the implications of the Department's intentions, the classification of the commitment (contractual vs. administrative), and whether the failure to fulfill the promise amounts to a breach of the right to a basic education as per the Constitution.

## Rules

[21] The relevant legal provisions underpinning this case include section 29 of the South African Constitution, which guarantees the right to basic education and acknowledges state subsidies for independent schools under subsection 4. Additionally, section 48 of the Schools Act empowers the MEC to grant subsidies, subject to the availability of funds appropriated by the provincial legislature.

[30] The principles regarding enforceability of government promises, established in legal precedents like *Bel Porto School Governing Body v Premier of the Western Cape* and *Dilokong Chrome Mines,* are crucial here. There exists an evolving dialogue about whether an administrative undertaking may create enforceable obligations or lead to legitimate expectations for recipients.

## Application

[30] The High Court initially accepted the applicant's assertion that the 2008 notice intended to create contractual obligations, focusing on the term "approximate" as essential to define the enforceability of the promise. The Court concluded that the qualification of "approximate" inherently limited the enforceability of specified amounts since the meaning conveys imprecision.

[34] In its analysis, the Supreme Court found that while the Department's letter constituted an administrative undertaking, the lack of clarity undermined enforceability. The court emphasized that the paramount consideration was whether a legitimate expectation was raised and if the government could legally manage budgeting constraints that interfered with pre-promised payments.

[60] In assessing the likeliness of the case, the court evaluated the historical context of budget management and fiscal responsibility within the provincial framework. The argument presented by the Department highlighted its inability to comply with the established promise due to unexpected budget cuts, suggesting the limitations of the law of contract in public administrative contexts.

[65] Ultimately, the Court indicated that the document in question, despite its implications of commitment, did not confer an absolute right to enforce the stated funding levels, indicating a complex relationship between budgetary constraints and the legal obligations that arise from governmental communication.

## Conclusion

[76] The Constitutional Court, led by Cameron J, framed its decision as pivotal in ensuring that promises made by governmental entities carry weight, but clarified that such promises must be examined within the practical realities of state obligations and budgetary restrictions.

[77] The Court ultimately held that while the 2008 notice expressed a serious intention to confer benefits, the word "approximate" undermined the exactness necessary for enforceability, thus denying the appeal but acknowledging the validity of the interest in basic education and financial support for independent schools.

[80] The ruling reflects a significant exposition of the tension between contractual intents in governmental communication and the underlying fiscal responsibilities restraints which dictate public law norms, thus, reaffirming fiscal accountability without losing sight of the constitutional right to education. 

This case serves as a critical precedent for understanding the intersection of administrative law and public funding, particularly regarding the enforceability of government communications to private entities.
