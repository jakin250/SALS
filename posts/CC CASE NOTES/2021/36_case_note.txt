Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

### FIRAC Case Note: **Booi v Amathole District Municipality and Others [2021] ZACC 36**

#### F – Facts
The case revolves around Mlungisi Wellington Booi (the applicant), who was employed by the Amathole District Municipality (the first respondent) as a Senior Manager of Municipal Health Services until his dismissal in December 2015 for alleged misconduct. The dismissal stemmed from four charges, including insubordination, dishonesty, and gross negligence ([4]–[5]).

Booi contested the fairness of his dismissal through the South African Local Government Bargaining Council (the second respondent). An arbitrator, Mr Mxolisi Alex Nozigqwaba N.O. (the third respondent), found that Booi was substantively unfairly dismissed and ordered his retrospective reinstatement ([6]). The municipality, however, sought to review this arbitration award in the Labour Court, claiming that the arbitrator had misconstrued the charges and, crucially, that the employment relationship had irretrievably broken down, rendering reinstatement intolerable ([7]).

The Labour Court determined that, although the arbitrator's findings on misconduct had a reasonable basis, the decision to reinstate Booi did not meet the threshold of reasonableness given the alleged breakdown of the trust relationship ([8]). The Labour Court subsequently set aside the arbitration award, replacing it with a compensation order equivalent to eight months' remuneration ([9]).

Booi attempted to appeal the Labour Court’s decision, initially seeking leave to approach the Labour Appeal Court. His application faced challenges regarding the doctrine of peremption, as he had accepted compensation based on the Labour Court’s ruling, which the Labour Court held indicated his intention to abide by the judgment ([10]–[12]). 

Having failed in the Labour Appeal Court, Booi then brought his case before the Constitutional Court, arguing that the Labour Court had incorrectly raised the issue of intolerability and that he was entitled to reinstatement due to his exoneration from the misconduct charges ([13]–[20]).

#### I – Issues
1. Whether the Labour Court misapplied the concept of intolerability in relation to section 193(2)(b) of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 when it reviewed the arbitrator’s decision to reinstate Booi.
2. Whether Booi's right to fair labour practices as enshrined in the Constitution was infringed by the Labour Court’s decision.
3. The validity of the Labour Court's invocation of the doctrine of peremption based on Booi's acceptance of compensation.
4. Whether the issue of intolerability was raised mero motu by the Labour Court without it being adequately pleaded by the municipality.

#### R – Rules
The case predominantly revolves around the interpretation and application of section 193(2) and section 145 of the Labour Relations Act, which govern reinstatement following a finding of unfair dismissal. Section 193(2)(b) mandates that reinstatement is the primary remedy unless it can be shown that the continued employment relationship would be intolerable ([9]).

Moreover, the rules governing the review process under the Labour Relations Act stipulate that for a review of an arbitrator's decision to be successful, it must establish that the decision was so unreasonable that no reasonable decision-maker could have made it ([44]). This aligns with the constitutional imperative arising from section 23 of the Constitution, which guarantees the right to fair labour practices.

#### A – Application
The Constitutional Court began by affirming the significance of reinstatement in unfair dismissal cases, emphasising that it should only be departed from upon clear evidence that the employment relationship is intolerable. The Court highlighted that the threshold for establishing intolerance is notably high and that concerns over workplace relationships must be substantiated with compelling evidence ([40]–[41]).

The Court reviewed the Labour Court's decision, finding that it had conducted the intolerability inquiry as if it were hearing an appeal rather than a review. This conflation of review and appeal is impermissible, as the Labour Court ought to respect the arbitrator’s decision unless there was a clear lack of reasonableness in the arbitrator's conclusions ([44]).

Further, the Court found that the Labour Court erroneously relied on evidence that had been rejected as insufficient to sustain a finding of misconduct as a basis for concluding that the employment relationship had irretrievably broken down. The former findings of the arbitrator regarding Booi's innocence from the charges were not considered adequately by the Labour Court ([47]).

Regarding the issue of peremption, the Court accepted Booi's explanation that his acceptance of remuneration was advised by his legal representation and should not preclude him from appealing the Labour Court's decision. It upheld the view that the interests of justice must prevail to ensure workers' rights are protected, particularly given the vulnerable position of employees in the labour market ([30]–[32]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court held that the Labour Court had erred in its review by imposing an incorrect standard when deciding the intolerability of Booi’s reinstatement, particularly since he had been exonerated of misconduct. The judgment reinstated Booi and mandated that he be compensated back to the date of his dismissal, less the payments already received through the Labour Court's order ([54]–[63]).

The ruling reinforced the primacy of reinstatement as a remedy for unfair dismissals and established clear guidelines on the threshold for claims of intolerability, essentially breathing life back into the principles of fair labour practices enshrined in South African constitutional law.
