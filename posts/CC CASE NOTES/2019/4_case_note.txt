# Case Note: Jacobs and Others v S [2018] ZACC 4 (CCT 73/17)

### FIRAC Method

**F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 5–11])**

The case involves Mr Shane Jacobs, Mr Frank Anthony, and Mr Sulaiman Ferris (the applicants), who were convicted of murder by the High Court in Mahikeng based on the doctrine of common purpose ([para 8]). The incident occurred on 19 December 2010, when the deceased, Mr Patrick Abakwe Modikanele, was brutally attacked by a mob, initiated by accusations from Maxine Anthony, the daughter of the second applicant, regarding her stolen cellphone. The assault unfolded in two stages; the first assault (stage one) took place in public at the Busy Corner, and the second assault (stage two) occurred at the home of the second applicant, where the applicants participated in the violence. The High Court sentenced Jacobs to 18 years in prison and the other two applicants to 15 years each ([paras 5–6]).

**I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 11–20])**

The principal issues raised revolve around whether the High Court and Full Court correctly applied the doctrine of common purpose in convicting the applicants. Specifically, the applicants contended that they did not participate in the first assault, and therefore could not be found guilty as charged. They argued that the misapplication of this doctrine by the lower courts constituted a breach of their constitutional rights to a fair trial and to freedom, thus raising a constitutional issue sufficient to invoke the jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court ([paras 20–21]).

**R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 25–33])**

The court referred to the doctrine of common purpose, which is well established in South African law and provides that individuals sharing a common intention to commit a crime can collectively be held liable for acts committed by any one of them in furtherance of that intention ([para 24]). The defining requirements for this doctrine were laid out in *S v Mgedezi* and *S v Thebus*, emphasizing the necessity for each accused to be present, aware of the unlawful acts, and to have the requisite mens rea ([paras 8–9]).

The case also relies on previous judgments, including *Boesak*, which established that a misapplication of the law may raise constitutional issues regarding fair trial rights ([para 23]).

**A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 36–56])**

The court's analysis began with the recognition that the applicants were not present during the first assault, raising questions about whether they could be charged under the doctrine of common purpose. The Full Court had found that the two stages of assault formed a continuous act. However, the key issue was whether it could be established beyond reasonable doubt that the applicants were actively involved in the fatal assault that ultimately led to death. The High Court indicated that since the fatal blow's timing was disputed, the prosecution had not met the burden of proof necessary for a murder conviction based on common purpose ([paras 39–48]).

Ultimately, the court differentiated between mere factual inaccuracies and constitutional implications, concluding that simply contesting the factual findings did not engage the jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court unless a specific constitutional issue was raised. The court emphasized that jurisdiction was only applicable when distinct constitutional violations—such as a wrongful application of the law—were evident ([paras 40–44]).

**C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 51–54])**

The Constitutional Court ultimately concluded that it lacked jurisdiction to entertain the appeal. The application for leave to appeal was dismissed, thus affirming the decision of the Full Court, which upheld the original convictions. The judgment underscored that disagreement with factual findings does not constitute a constitutional matter and that the doctrine of common purpose must be applied with caution to prevent unjust convictions ([paras 52–53]).

This ruling has significant implications for future cases involving the doctrine of common purpose, reaffirming the need for rigorous proof regarding each participant's role in violent crimes and the necessity of protecting constitutional rights during the application of criminal liability.
