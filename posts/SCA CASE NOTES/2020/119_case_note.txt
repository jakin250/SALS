Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# Case Note: Minister of Public Works v Roux Property Fund (Pty) Ltd (779/2019) [2020] ZASCA 119

## FIRAC Case Note

**F – Facts**

The case originated from a claim for damages brought by Roux Property Fund (Pty) Ltd ("the Respondent") against the Minister of Public Works ("the Appellant"). The Respondent claimed damages amounting to R340 million due to an alleged breach of a lease agreement by the National Department of Public Works ("DPW") ([paras 2–4]). The underlying lease was concluded on 20 July 2010 but had complications arising from non-occupation and lack of payment of rental by the DPW ([paras 6–10]).

Notably, the Respondent failed to serve a notice of intention to institute legal proceedings against the Appellant within the statutory six-month period outlined in section 3(2)(a) of the Institution of Legal Proceedings Against Certain Organs of State Act 40 of 2002 ("the Act"). The notice was only served on 28 August 2014, the same day the summons was filed, prompting a special plea from the Appellant that the notice had not been given timeously, and thus, the claim should be dismissed ([paras 4, 5, 16]). The Respondent’s application for condonation was initially granted by the High Court, leading to the present appeal ([paras 4, 37]).

**I – Issues**

The salient legal issue is whether the High Court should have condoned the Respondent’s non-compliance with the notice requirements stipulated in sections 3(1) and 3(2)(b) of the Act in relation to the claim against the Appellant ([paras 1, 4]). Specifically, the appeal questioned:

1. If the power of condonation under section 3(4) extends to failures of compliance with section 3(1).
2. Whether the Respondent established 'good cause' as required by section 3(4)(b).
3. Whether the Appellant suffered unreasonable prejudice due to the delay ([paras 5, 16–18]).

**R – Rules**

The legal framework is primarily governed by the Institution of Legal Proceedings Against Certain Organs of State Act 40 of 2002. The relevant provisions stipulate:

1. **Section 3(1)**: No legal proceedings for the recovery of a debt can be instituted against an organ of state unless the creditor provides written notice.
2. **Section 3(2)**: Notice must be served within six months from the date the debt became due, detailing the facts and particulars of the debt.
3. **Section 3(4)**: Allows a creditor to apply for condonation if the notice requirement is not met, provided:
   - (a) the debt has not prescribed,
   - (b) good cause exists for the failure,
   - (c) the organ of state was not unreasonably prejudiced ([paras 12–13]).

In interpreting these provisions, the court referenced **Minister of Safety and Security v De Witt** [2008] ZASCA 103, which emphasized the necessity of satisfying all three conditions for condonation to be granted and indicated that the requirements must be treated conjunctively ([para 14]).

**A – Application**

In its deliberation, the Supreme Court of Appeal explored the three criteria for condonation laid out in section 3(4)(b):

1. **Prescription**: The court found that the Respondent’s cause of action arose on 21 April 2011, when the DPW repudiated the lease by not occupying the premises and refusing to pay the associated rental fees. Thus, the Respondent could not demonstrate that the claim had not prescribed ([paras 21–26]).

2. **Good Cause**: The court scrutinized the Respondent's justification for the delay in providing notice. The Respondent claimed ignorance due to inadequate advice from previous attorneys but failed to explain the prolonged delay of over three years between October 2014, when it became aware of the need for a condonation application, and May 2018, when it actually filed the application ([paras 27–30]). The court deemed the explanation insufficient and indicated that the Respondent had not established a strong case or compelling reasons to excuse the delay.

3. **Unreasonable Prejudice**: The Appellant argued that significant delays jeopardized its defense, as key witnesses involved in the lease negotiations were no longer available. The court agreed that the delays were prejudicial and detrimental to the Appellant's case, further supporting the rejection of the Respondent's application for condonation ([paras 32–35]).

**C – Conclusion**

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the Respondent failed to meet the requisite conditions for the grant of condonation within the meaning of section 3(4)(b). The Respondent's non-compliance with the notice requirement was not adequately excused by the factors presented, and the Appellant demonstrated that it would suffer unreasonable prejudice should the Respondent be allowed to proceed with the claim. Consequently, the appeal was upheld, the High Court's order was set aside, and the original action was dismissed with costs, solidifying the importance of adherence to statutory procedural requirements when involving organs of state ([paras 36–37]).

The ratio decidendi emphasizes the court's pragmatic view on procedural compliance while upholding the principles of justice in contractual disputes involving state organs, reinforcing the stringent need for legal adherence to avoid prejudice within the court system.
