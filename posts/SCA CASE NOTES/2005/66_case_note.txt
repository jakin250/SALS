Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2005

# FIRAC Case Note: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa (Case No. 140/05)

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–11])

Mikro Primary School, located in Kuilsriver, is an Afrikaans-medium public school. The governing body of the school, as represented by its first respondent, refused a directive from the Western Cape Education Department, led by the Head of Education (second appellant), to amend its language policy and transition to a parallel medium school. This directive mandated the admission of English-language learners.

An urgent application was initiated by the governing body and parents of certain learners to the Cape High Court seeking the annulment of the directive and associated decisions by the Minister of Education (first appellant). The court granted the application, which resulted in several key orders, including the prohibition of the appellants from compelling the school or its principal to alter its language policy ([para 1]).

Following the High Court's ruling, the appellants appealed, despite various procedural intricacies that saw the parents of the learners join as third appellants, although they did not initially appeal the judgment themselves ([para 2]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 17–29])

1. **Interpretation of Section 29(2) of the Constitution**: Did the decision of the Head of Department to mandate English-learning instruction unlawfully contravene the rights established under Section 29(2), which guarantees the right to education in one's chosen official language when practicable? 

2. **Authority of Governance**: Did the governing body possess the sole authority to determine the language policy of the Mikro Primary School based on Section 6(2) of the South African Schools Act 1996? 

3. **Definition of 'Organ of State'**: Was the governing body considered an ‘organ of state’ under constitutional provisions, and how did this impact the ability of the appellants to compel changes to the language policy?

4. **Prematurity and Exhaustion of Remedies**: Did the court a quo err in dismissing the appellants' claims that the respondents had failed to exhaust internal remedies as required by the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 ([paras 17–19])?

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 3–6],[29–34])

1. **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996**:
   - Section 29(2): Right to receive education in official languages.
   - Section 41(3): Mandate for intergovernmental cooperation to avoid legal disputes among government entities.

2. **South African Schools Act 84 of 1996**:
   - Section 6(2): Governing body has the authority to determine the language policy of a public school.
   - Section 16(1): Governance responsibilities of the governing body.

3. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000**:
   - Section 7(2) and (c): Requirements for exhausting internal remedies in administrative actions.
   
4. **Previous Jurisprudence**:
   - **Directory Advertising Cost Cutters v Minister for Posts** (1996): Interpretative framework for defining 'organ of state'.
   - **Independent Electoral Commission v Langeberg Municipality** (2001): Clarification of the non-governmental status in certain statutory functions.

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 19–48])

1. **Interpretation of Section 29(2)**:
   The court determined that the right to receive education in one's chosen language does not equate to the right to receive that education at every public institution, thereby rejecting the appellants' expansive interpretation. The court reasoned that the constitutional guarantee allows for various educational alternatives that the state can evaluate, allowing the governing body’s decision substantial weight as it aligns with the Constitution’s philosophy ([paras 30–31]).

2. **Authority of the Governing Body**:
   Emphasizing Section 6(2), the court reaffirmed that the governing body retains autonomy to develop its language policy, free from mandatory directives from the provincial education department. The actions of the second appellant were deemed overreaching, as they intruded upon an area designated for independent governance ([paras 34–44]).

3. **Definition of 'Organ of State'**:
   The court assessed the first respondent's status as an organ of state, concluding that it does not function as an arm of the government for the determination of language policy, as it operates independently in this regard. This determination effectively nullified the basis for the appellants’ claims concerning the necessity of exhausting intergovernmental remedies ([paras 19–22]).

4. **Prematurity Concerning Internal Remedies**:
   The court found that the urgency of the matter warranted exceptions to the typical requirement for exhausting internal remedies, particularly due to the implications for children's educational futures. It was reasoned that taking the matter to arbitration would impose undue delay without suitable resolution ([paras 24–28]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 49–59])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the lower court's ruling, confirming that the directive issued by the second appellant was unlawful and imposed upon the second respondent’s language policy without legal authority. Consequently, the decision was settled that learners do not possess an absolute right to receive instruction in their language of choice at any public school, paving the way for clarity in the governance structure of public schools regarding language policy.

The court emphasized that while it is vital to provide educational settings that respect each learner's language rights, such provisions must occur within a framework of established governance processes and institutional autonomy, without arbitrary mandates from provincial authorities. 

The implications of this decision resonate across public education governance in South Africa, reinforcing the autonomy of school governing bodies in determining language policy, while underlining the constitutional responsibilities that must guide such determinations. The ruling provides a precedent for similar disputes regarding educational language policies, ensuring that the jurisdictional boundaries between governmental oversight and educational governance are both respected and maintained.
