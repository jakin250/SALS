# Case Note: International Trade Administration Commission v SCAW South Africa (Pty) Ltd
## Constitutional Court of South Africa
**Case CCT 59/09**  
**[2010] ZACC 6**  
**Heard on:** 12 November 2009  
**Decided on:** 9 March 2010  

### FIRAC Case Note

### F - Facts
[1] The case involves the **International Trade Administration Commission (ITAC)** as the applicant challenging an earlier order from the North Gauteng High Court that granted an interdict to **SCAW South Africa (Pty) Ltd** (SCAW), preventing ITAC from forwarding its recommendation to the Minister of Trade and Industry regarding the termination of anti-dumping duties on imports of stranded wire, ropes, and cables from **Bridon International Limited** (UK) ([4]-[9]).  

[2] The original anti-dumping duties, imposed in 2002, were due to lapse after five years but were subject to **sunset reviews**, which are investigations initiated to determine whether existing anti-dumping duties should be continued or terminated ([17]-[19]). SCAW, as a domestic manufacturer and competitor of Bridon, sought to have the duties extended ([6]-[8]).  

[3] On October 20, 2008, SCAW obtained a High Court order interdicting ITAC and the Minister of Trade from acting on ITAC's recommendation to terminate the existing duties. ITAC sought to appeal this order to the Constitutional Court, stating that the High Court had overstepped its bounds by restraining executive powers and impacting the process of statutory review ([5]-[8]).  

### I - Issues
[4] The case presents the following key legal issues:

- (a) Whether the **Constitutional Court** should entertain an appeal against a temporary restraining order from the High Court ([15]).
- (b) Whether the High Court’s granting of the restraining order was appropriate given the context of the law concerning anti-dumping duties ([15]).
- (c) What relief, if any, should be granted upon reviewing the High Court's decision ([15]).

### R - Rules
[5] Key statutory provisions include:

- **World Trade Organization ('WTO') Anti-Dumping Agreement**, particularly Article 2.1, which defines dumping and sets rules for the imposition of anti-dumping duties ([26]-[28]).
- **International Trade Administration Act 2002** and **Anti-Dumping Regulations**, which govern the imposition and review of anti-dumping duties in South Africa. Specifically, regulation 20 mandates that all investigations and reviews must be completed within 18 months after their initiation, but it provides exceptions wherein a sunset review may extend the duration of the duties ([31]-[36]).

- **Section 85 of the Constitution**, which establishes the executive powers of the Ministers involved in trade and the adherence of such powers to the law and the Constitution ([42]).

- **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act** (PAJA), underpinning fair administrative action in relation to decisions taken by state organs ([44]).

### A - Application
[6] The Constitutional Court determined that allowing the appeal was indeed in the interests of justice due to the constitutional implications involved, particularly regarding the principles of separation of powers and executive authority ([60], [68]). The court considered whether it was appropriate for the High Court to grant the interdict.

[7] The High Court had found flaws in ITAC's processes that warranted the interdict; however, the Constitutional Court criticized this reasoning, asserting that it was inappropriate for the court to extend the duration of an anti-dumping duty. The intended purpose of such duties is to be short-term and remedial unless substantiated by findings of further dumping ([80], [88]). 

[8] The Court noted that the legislative framework established a specific lifespan for anti-dumping duties and thus held that the High Court's action interfered with executive functions and extended powers that do not belong to the judiciary. Thus, the High Court should have allowed the sunset review process to run its course ([90], [96]-[102]). 

### C - Conclusion
[9] The court concluded that the High Court's interdict was inappropriate as it improperly extended the life of the anti-dumping duties beyond permissible limits, violating the separation of powers doctrine. It overturned the High Court's decision, stating that duties must only remain in force as long as necessary to counteract actual dumping ([110]-[114]). As a result, the interdict was set aside, and costs were ordered against SCAW due to its unsuccessful challenge ([115]). 

### Implications
This case underscores the importance of adhering to statutory timelines in administrative law and emphasizes the balance of power between the judiciary and the executive in matters relating to trade policies. The decision reinforces the principle that anti-dumping duties, meant to protect domestic industries, must be managed within defined legal parameters to adhere to international obligations and ensure fair competition.
