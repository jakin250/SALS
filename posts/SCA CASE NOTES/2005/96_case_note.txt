# Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL 

**Case No:** 561/04
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
**Judgment Delivered:** 29 September 2005
**Judges:** HARMS, CAMERON, MTHIYANE, NUGENT, JAFTA JJA

## FIRAC Method Case Note

### F – Facts
The appellants, Mary Patricia King and 92 others, challenged the validity of an amendment to the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979, which had been passed by Parliament in 1998. Their challenge was based on the argument that Parliament failed to fulfill its constitutional obligation under section 59 of the South African Constitution by not adequately facilitating public involvement in the legislative process ([paras 1–3]). The appellants claimed they suffered substantial losses after depositing money with a law firm, which subsequently misappropriated the funds instead of investing them as promised ([para 3]).

The amendment Act precluded recovery of funds deposited with attorneys for purposes other than those usual in their practice ([para 4]). Following a hearing, Chetty J of the Grahamstown High Court dismissed their application, asserting that the court should not prescribe to Parliament the procedures for enacting legislation, and finding sufficient public consultation had occurred ([paras 1, 2]). The appellants maintained that the statutory amendment was invalid as it did not meet the constitutional requirement of public engagement and thus lacked legal force.

### I – Issues
The central issue before the court was whether it had the jurisdiction to declare the amendment Act invalid, given that the appellants alleged a constitutional violation. Specifically, the court had to assess if a failure to facilitate public involvement in the legislative process constituted a breach of a constitutional obligation, and if so, whether such matters could be adjudicated by the Supreme Court of Appeal or were exclusively within the remit of the Constitutional Court ([paras 12, 19]).

### R – Rules
The case hinged on several key constitutional provisions:

1. **Section 59(1)** of the Constitution mandates the National Assembly to facilitate public involvement in its legislative processes ([para 1]).
2. **Section 167(4)(e)** stipulates that only the Constitutional Court can determine if Parliament has failed to fulfil a constitutional obligation ([para 12]).
3. **Section 172(2)(a)** allows the Supreme Court of Appeal and High Courts the authority to make an order concerning the constitutional validity of Acts of Parliament, although any order of invalidity must be confirmed by the Constitutional Court ([para 12]).
4. Precedents such as *President of the Republic of South Africa v South African Rugby Football Union* lent interpretative guidance on the roles of various courts in constitutional matters ([para 13]).

### A – Application
The court determined that despite the appellants’ claims regarding insufficient public involvement, their allegations fell within the specific framework established by section 167(4)(e). This section limits the court's ability to intervene when the allegations concern a failure to fulfill constitutional obligations. The judges opined that while constitutional provisions allow for a challenge to the validity of actions taken by Parliament, they must be framed correctly within the jurisdictional limits set by the Constitution itself ([paras 16–19]).

Furthermore, the court analyzed whether the alleged failure to facilitate public involvement amounted to a breach significant enough to declare the Act invalid. The judges noted that public involvement is an inherently flexible concept and that the Constitution allows Parliament leeway in determining how it achieves such engagement. They found that Parliament had fulfilled its obligation by inviting input from professional bodies and conducting public hearings ([para 22]). Thus, the appellants could not demonstrate that Parliament had entirely failed to perform its legislative duties in a constitutionally meaningful manner, falling short of the extreme measures necessary to invalidate the Act.

### C – Conclusion
The court concluded that it lacked jurisdiction to hear the appellants' claim, affirming the dismissal of their application by Chetty J. Consequently, the appeal was struck from the roll with costs—reflecting the absence of a substantive constitutional issue and characterizing the appellants’ claim primarily as a private compensation matter ([paras 24, 26]). The decision underscores the importance of adhering to constitutional protocol and provides clarity on the jurisdictional boundaries between the Supreme Court of Appeal and the Constitutional Court concerning alleged failures in legislative processes.
