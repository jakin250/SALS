FIRAC Case Note: Highway Junction (Pty) Ltd and Others v Di-Thabeng Truck and Taxi (Pty) Ltd and Others (Case no 946/2022) [2024] ZASCA 31

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 31 #### F – Facts In this case, the First Respo

FIRAC Case Note: Highway Junction (Pty) Ltd and Others v Di-Thabeng Truck and Taxi (Pty) Ltd and Others (Case no 946/2022) [2024] ZASCA 31

F – Facts

In this case, the First Respondent, Di-Thabeng Truck and Taxi (Pty) Ltd, was the owner of a property classified as agricultural, allowing for truck parking (para [1]). The property is located in Harrismith, Free State Province. It was under the control of Mr. PJ du Toit until his death in July 2021. The First Appellant, Highway Junction (Pty) Ltd, operated a truck stop that serviced over 1,500 trucks a day. The Second Appellant, Swinburne Village Homeowners Association NPC, and the Third Appellant, Swinburne Store CC, were entities associated with residential and commercial developments adjacent to the property ([para 2]).

The Appellants alleged that the Di-Thabeng entities engaged in several unlawful activities, including violation of the National Environmental Management Act 107 of 1998 (NEMA), local zoning laws, and the Petroleum Products Act 120 of 1977 (PPA) by conducting illegal retail sales of fuel without the proper licenses ([para 3]). Particularly salient was that Di-Thabeng was selling fuel in transactions of at least 1,500 litres without obtaining a retail license, which led to contention regarding whether this constituted wholesaling or retailing ([para 4]).

The Appellants sought a final interdict against several unlawful practices by the Di-Thabeng entities in the Free State Division of the High Court. They wished for a prohibition on activity until several regulatory approvals were obtained ([paras 6-7]). The High Court granted an interdict prohibiting the Di-Thabeng entities from using the property for purposes other than agricultural and truck parking, but it did not grant the interdict relevant to fuel retailing ([para 7]).

I – Issues

The principal legal issues in this case included:

  1. Whether the Di-Thabeng entities were unlawfully utilizing the property, specifically in relation to their activities concerning fuel sales.
  2. The precise interpretation of "transactions" as it applies under the PPA and how this distinction impacts the nature of the entities’ operations—wholesaling versus retailing ([paras 5-10]).
  3. Whether the Appellants maintained a clear right to seek a further interdict against fuel retailing following the High Court's ruling ([para 9]).

R – Rules

The legal framework for this case was constructed from various statutes and case law:

  1. National Environmental Management Act 107 of 1998 (NEMA) - Enforces environmental protections and requires compliance from parties engaged in activities with potential environmental impacts ([para 3]).
  2. Spatial Planning and Land Use Management Act 18 of 2013 (SPLUMA) - Provides guidance on land use changes and their necessity ([para 3]).
  3. Petroleum Products Act 120 of 1977 (PPA) - Specifically governs the marketing and sale of petroleum products in South Africa, differentiating between wholesaling and retailing ([para 4]).
  4. Case Law - The court relied on precedents such as National Coalition for Gay and Lesbian Equality and Others v Minister of Home Affairs and Others [1999] ZACC 17, which articulated principles surrounding mootness and justiciability ([para 12]).

A – Application

The court thoroughly evaluated the High Court's decision concerning the interdict for fuel retailing. It expressed that although the High Court did not grant the interdict commanding a prohibition against retailing, the existing interdict wholly obstructed any trading activities on the property, indicating that the Appellants were sufficiently protected ([para 12]). The court further interpreted that the Appellants’ concerns about potential future compliance by Di-Thabeng entities—thus raising mootness concerning the issue of res judicata or issue estoppel—were unfounded, as current conditions did not necessitate the appeal ([para 10]).

The court also emphasized the ambiguity present in the High Court's judgment regarding the interpretation of "transaction," noting that it failed to adopt a robust interpretative framework and did not provide a conclusive resolution which would inhibit future litigations ([para 11]). Therefore, the necessity of addressing the second interdict was rendered moot.

Furthermore, the potential broader implications of interpreting the PPA as it pertained to other businesses operating under similar systems meant that caution was warranted in making determinations without all relevant parties present ([para 14]).

C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the cross-appeal of the Appellants, affirming that the existing interdict sufficiently protected their rights against the operational activities of the Di-Thabeng entities. The dismissal accentuated that there was no live controversy regarding the interpretation of "transaction" as it pertained to judicial interpretation, which would ordinarily warrant judicial review given the uncertainty established by the lower court ([paras 12-17]).

The court’s decision upheld principles regarding the justiciability of cases that no longer indicate existing or immediate disputes, thus reinforcing the importance of concrete live issues in legal determinations. This decision also potentially limits opportunities for others to challenge fuel retailing practices under similar conditions, without adequately engaging in judicial discourse on existing statutory interpretations ([paras 14-16]).

This case sets a significant precedent for subsequent disputes involving zoning, environmental compliance, and the interpretation of trade activities under the PPA within South Africa’s complex legislative frameworks.
