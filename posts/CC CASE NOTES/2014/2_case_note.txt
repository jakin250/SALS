Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# Case Note: Ronald Bobroff & Partners Inc v De La Guerre; South African Association of Personal Injury Lawyers v Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development [2014] ZACC 2

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The cases involve applications for leave to appeal against judgments of the North Gauteng High Court, Pretoria, concerning the constitutionality of the Contingency Fees Act 66 of 1997 (the Act) ([1]). The South African Association of Personal Injury Lawyers (Personal Injury Lawyers) and Ronald Bobroff & Partners Inc (Bobroff), a law firm, challenged the Act, arguing it was unconstitutional either in its entirety or in specific sections ([1]). The Act allows legal practitioners to charge contingency fees based on the success of the client’s case, changing the prior common law prohibition on such fee arrangements. The core of the controversy revolves around whether the fees charged by Bobroff exceeded the allowable limits set forth by the Act, as Ms. De La Guerre was charged a 30% contingency fee instead of the mandated maximum of 25% ([2]-[3]).

The High Court dismissed the applications for unconstitutionality and ruled in favor of Ms. De La Guerre, which was subsequently upheld by the Supreme Court of Appeal ([4]). The Constitutional Court, in this context, was approached for leave to appeal on grounds that included the validity of the contingency fee framework ([4]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal questions presented in this matter can be summarized as follows:
1. Is the Contingency Fees Act unconstitutional as a whole, or do specific sections lack constitutionality?
2. Does the regulation of contingency fees for legal practitioners distinguish rationally from the prohibition of champertous agreements among laypersons?
3. Are the rights under section 34 of the Constitution (access to justice) impermissibly limited by the provisions of the Act?

The Court sought to determine these issues in connection to the constitutional framework and existing legal principles ([2]-[10]).

### R – Rules
The Constitution of the Republic of South Africa serves as the primary legal authority, particularly sections 34 and 36 ([12]-[13]). Section 34 guarantees the right to equal access to courts, while section 36 allows for reasonable limitations on rights in a manner justifiable in an open and democratic society. Additional applicable jurisprudence includes:

- **Rationality Standard**: The need for a rational connection between legislative means and ends ([6]), ensuring the government’s legislation passes a basic test of rationality without impeding policy formulation.
- **Reasonableness Review**: Applied where fundamental rights under the Bill of Rights are limited, allowing courts to more actively scrutinize legislative choices against constitutional freedoms ([8]).

The Contingency Fees Act itself defines contingency fee agreements and sets limits on attorneys’ fees ([16]-[17]).

### A – Application
The Court reviewed the challenge to the constitutionality of the Act under both rationality and reasonableness criteria. It upheld the High Court's reasoning concluding that the regulation of contingency fees is a legitimate legislative choice based on rational distinctions between legal practitioners and laypersons involved in similar financial agreements ([10]-[11]). The distinction is perceived as necessary given that legal practitioners possess specialized training and ethical obligations which laypersons do not.

In respect to the limitation of rights, although the applicants asserted that the Act impedes access to justice ([12]), the Court found no evidence of rights limitation on the part of legal practitioners. The Personal Injury Lawyers' challenge was determined to be made on their own behalf rather than as a representative retrieval of rights for clients ([12]). The Court found that the rights of clients remain intact under the current regulatory framework.

Consequently, the Court deemed that the applicants did not demonstrate reasonable prospects of success on appeal as they failed to substantiate claims that the Act infringes constitutional rights ([13]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court dismissed both applications for leave to appeal with costs ([14]). The decision underscored that the Contingency Fees Act is constitutional, affirming the separation and regulation of legal practitioners from laypersons regarding contingency fee agreements. The outcome emphasizes the permissible limitations of rights under the Act, illustrating the Court’s alignment with the rationality standard in legislative review, while also curtailing speculative claims regarding access to justice from legal practitioners without firmly established grounds. The ruling solidifies the role of legislative discretion in regulating contingency fees within the broader context of legal ethics and client protection in South Africa.

This case thus serves as a significant point of reference in the ongoing dialogue concerning legal fees and the constitutional framework that governs access to justice in the context of personal injury law ([1]-[14]).
