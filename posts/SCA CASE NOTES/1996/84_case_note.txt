Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1996

# Case Note: Industrial & Commercial Factors (Pty) Limited v The Attorneys Fidelity Fund Board of Control [1996] ZASCA 49; Case No: 690/94

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The appellant, Industrial & Commercial Factors (Pty) Limited (hereinafter "the appellant"), instituted an action against the respondent, The Attorneys Fidelity Fund Board of Control (hereinafter "the respondent"), claiming reimbursement for a pecuniary loss of R450,000 suffered due to theft by a practising attorney, Josephus Theodorus Johannes Mare (hereinafter "Mare"). The appellant contended that the money was entrusted to Mare in line with section 26(a) of the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979 ("the Act") ([par 2]).

In April 1992, Mare fraudulently procured a cheque for R450,000 from the appellant, purportedly on behalf of his client, Mrs Branken, for bridging finance pertaining to a property transaction. The cheque was drawn in favour of Branken and was marked "not transferable". Mare requested a change to this marking so that he could deposit the cheque into his trust account. Upon receiving the cheque, Mare misappropriated the funds, resulting in a loss for the appellant and leading to Mare's conviction for fraud ([paras 4–6]).

The case was tried in the Witwatersrand Local Division, where the court found that although the appellant had established a pecuniary loss and theft committed by an attorney, it failed to demonstrate that the money was “entrusted by or on behalf of” the appellant to Mare ([par 12]). The court a quo ordered absolution from the instance, which the appellant appealed ([par 3]).

### I – Issues
The central legal issue in this case was whether the appellant met the statutory requirements under section 26(a) of the Act, specifically whether it had "entrusted" money to Mare in his capacity as an attorney and whether such entrustment occurred "in the course of his practice" as required by the Act ([paras 18–19]). The intricacies of the issue revolved around the definitions of “entrustment” and the interpretation of the phrase "by or on behalf of" as stipulated in the statutory provision.

### R – Rules
The court relied on several precedents and statutory interpretations regarding entrustment and the liabilities associated with the Fidelity Fund. Section 26(a) of the Act states that the fund shall reimburse persons who suffer pecuniary loss due to theft committed by a legal practitioner of money or property entrusted to them in the course of their practice ([paras 2, 11]). The court noted critical definitions and distinctions regarding the terms "entrust" and the implications of money being held in trust accounts ([paras 14–15]).

In the preceding case of Provident Fund for the Clothing Industry v Attorneys, Notaries and Conveyancers Fidelity Guarantee Fund, the court determined that the obligation for reimbursement is contingent upon establishing that the money was entrusted with the understanding that it would be used for a specific purpose benefiting the entruster ([par 12]).

### A – Application
The court meticulously analyzed the appellant's claim through the lens of the statutory framework and facts at hand. It noted that the appellant's intention behind the cheque was to discharge its obligation to Branken by paying her directly. The crucial factor was whether Mare had the authority to accept this payment on behalf of Branken ([par 11]).

Initially, the appellant's legal director, Mr Colin Flax, intended to hand over the cheque to Mare solely to fulfill the appellant's obligation to Branken, rather than intending to entrust the money to Mare for further disbursement ([paras 7–10]). The alteration of the cheque marking from "not transferable" to "not negotiable" did not translate into an intention to create a trust relationship between the appellant and Mare, as Flax was primarily concerned with ensuring that the money would benefit Branken directly ([par 19]).

In distinguishing the facts from previous cases, the court emphasized that unlike the Provident Fund case, the appellant in this case actively insisted on the money being deposited into the attorney's trust account. This conduct demonstrated a level of intention to effectuate a lawful transfer that indicated entrustment ([paras 11–12]). The pivot of the ruling rested on the interpretation that the appellant's intention to discharge its obligation did not equate to the intention to create a trust on behalf of Branken ([para 11]).

### C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the court allowed the appeal, ruling that the appellant did indeed meet the requisite criteria for “entrustment” as stipulated under section 26(a) of the Act. The court found sufficient elements of entrustment, asserting that the appellant was entitled to reimbursement even though Branken herself did not suffer a loss ([paras 15–20]). The court's holding reinforced the legislative intention behind the Attorneys Act, emphasizing the statutory protections available to third parties who become victims of fraud perpetrated by attorneys. The judgment illuminated the complexities involved in interpreting relationships defined by agency and trust within legal frameworks, making the findings significant for future cases involving the fidelity fund and attorney-client interactions.

**Judgment:** The appeal was allowed with costs, and the court ordered reimbursement of R401,068.48 to the appellant, laying the groundwork for the implications on professional responsibility in the legal sector in South Africa ([par 20]).
