Joan Cynthia Griessel NO & Others v De Kock (334/18) [2019] ZASCA 95

This case arises from a dispute regarding the Arathusa Family Trust, established in 1999 by two sisters, Joan Cynthia Griessel and Shirley Ann Van Wyk.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 95

F - Facts

This case arises from a dispute regarding the Arathusa Family Trust, established in 1999 by two sisters, Joan Cynthia Griessel and Shirley Ann Van Wyk. Under the trust deed, the two sisters and a chartered accountant, Caryn Schutz, were appointed as trustees, and they were granted absolute discretion to select beneficiaries from a defined pool. The only asset of the trust was the shares in Manyeleti (Pty) Ltd, which owned a game reserve farm in Mpumalanga ([2–3]).

A family dispute ensued when Harold Lee De Kock (the first respondent), a beneficiary, was removed from his position as a beneficiary following disagreements about the development of the farm. An initial court ruling declared this removal invalid, reinstating his rights on 17 February 2016 ([3]). However, subsequent exchanges disclosed ongoing disputes regarding access to the farm ([4]).

Harold then filed a new application in the Gauteng Division of the High Court seeking the restoration of his beneficiary status and access to the farm, leading to further litigation. The High Court found that the trustees had unlawfully discriminated against him by denying access while allowing other beneficiaries to continue visiting ([6–7]). The court added the third applicant, Caryn Schutz, in her personal capacity as a tenth respondent due to a procedural non-joinder ([6]).

The High Court ordered that De Kock's rights as a beneficiary be reinstated and further directed the appointment of an independent co-trustee. The trustees appealed this decision ([8]).

I - Issues

The following legal issues were raised for the Supreme Court of Appeal to determine:

  1. Should leave to appeal be granted? If so,
  2. Did the first respondent, as a beneficiary of a discretionary trust, have rights that were capable of protection?
  3. Was the court a quo correct in reinstating the first respondent's rights and ordering the appointment of an additional trustee and a punitive costs order? ([8–9]).

R - Rules

The Court considered several fundamental legal principles concerning trusts, particularly discretionary trusts:

  • Non-Legal Personality of Trusts: Trusts do not have a legal personality; they consist of a fiduciary relationship governed by the trust deed and the Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988 ([11]).
  • Rights of Beneficiaries: Beneficiaries of discretionary trusts generally have contingent rights. They do not hold vested rights until the trustee has exercised discretion in their favor ([9, 16]).
  • Protection of Contingent Beneficiaries: The law affords contingent beneficiaries protection against arbitrary or discriminatory treatment by trustees ([17-19]).

The judgment cited precedents, including Potgieter v Potgieter NO ([16]) and Doyle v Board of Executors ([19]), establishing the fiduciary duties of trustees to treat all beneficiaries fairly and without discrimination.

A - Application

Leave to Appeal

The court granted leave to appeal, confirming that the substantive issues raised warranted a judicial review ([26]).

Rights of the Beneficiary

The principal focus was whether Harold De Kock held rights worthy of protection. While the trustees asserted he had only contingent rights, the court determined that he indeed had established rights of access due to prior usage, granting him standing to bring the case ([12–19]). The primary argument revolved around the interpretation of the trust deed's provisions and whether the prior access constituted a right worth protecting.

The judgment emphasizes that discrimination, such as that faced by De Kock, which unjustifiably favored other potential beneficiaries while excluding him, fundamentally breached the fiduciary duties of the trustees. The differential treatment was traced back to personal conflicts and not to legitimate interests of the trust ([18–19]).

Ancillary Orders

The court recognized that the decision directing the appointment of an independent trustee was not supported by sufficient evidence of mismanagement or impropriety within the current trustees' administration ([20–23]). The court found that the need for extra trustees was unfounded as the existing trustees were capable, professional, and aware of their fiduciary responsibilities.

Lastly, the punitive costs order was scrutinized. The court affirmatively ruled that the lower court failed to exercise its discretion judicially in awarding punitive costs, resulting in that part of the order being set aside ([24–25]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately overturned parts of the High Court judgment, reinstating Harold De Kock's status as a beneficiary of the Arathusa Trust and affirming his rights of access to the farm. The court's decision reaffirmed the protections available to contingent beneficiaries even without vested rights, emphasizing the necessity for trustees to administer trusts without discrimination.

The appeal succeeded only partially, reflecting the nuanced understanding of beneficiary rights in discretionary trusts and enabling a degree of equitable access that upheld the intentions of the trust's founders while scrutinizing the actions of the trustees ([26]).

This decision has implications for the treatment of discretionary beneficiaries, reinforcing their rights and the standards of conduct that trustees must adhere to in managing trusts. Future cases will likely look to this precedent when evaluating similar issues of trust administration and beneficiary rights.
