### FIRAC Case Note: *Burger v The State* (236/09) [2010] ZASCA 12

---

#### F – Facts

On 2 October 2007, the three appellants—Magiel Burger, Felokwane Goqo, and Joachim Prinsloo—were convicted by the Pretoria High Court of the kidnapping and murder of Sandy Botomane. Each was sentenced to 12 months' imprisonment for kidnapping and life imprisonment for murder ([1]–[2]). The case centered around the circumstances leading to the discovery of the deceased’s disfigured body on the N1 national highway on 24 May 2000 ([4]). Background events include a theft of R1.2 million from the Putco office where Botomane worked ([6]), which led to an investigation by private investigator Henry Beukes and a subsequent connection with the police ([8]).

On 22 May 2000, Beukes enlisted the help of the appellants to locate Botomane as part of their investigation into the theft. The appellants went to Botomane’s home, woke him, and told him he was being taken for questioning regarding the theft. Although they indicated they were taking him to the police station, they did not do so ([13]–[15]). After leaving his house, Botomane was not seen alive again, and his body was found approximately 26 hours later ([19]). The High Court rejected the appellants' account of events and, after evaluating the circumstantial evidence, found them guilty of both charges ([20]–[22]).

#### I – Issues

The appeal raised several legal issues:
1. Whether the High Court accurately assessed the circumstantial evidence concerning the murder charge, particularly given the absence of direct evidence linking the appellants to the act of murder ([3]).
2. Whether the kidnapping conviction was justified, considering the context in which Botomane was taken by the appellants ([32]).
3. The implications of police procedures and the blurring of lines between private investigations and police activities ([39]–[41]).

#### R – Rules

The court relied on established rules of evidence and legal principles regarding circumstantial evidence. Specifically, the requirements articulated in *R v Blom* highlighted that an inference drawn must be consistent with all proved facts, and that no other reasonable inference should be made ([28]). Furthermore, the definition of kidnapping was clarified, requiring that unlawful deprivation of liberty must be established ([32]).

South African constitutional provisions under Section 36, emphasizing the rights of arrested individuals, were also significant as they contextualized the actions of the police officers in the case ([39]).

#### A – Application

In analyzing the evidence, the Supreme Court of Appeal found significant discrepancies and unsubstantiated conclusions in the lower court's assessment of circumstantial evidence concerning the murder charge. The court noted that while multiple injuries on Botomane were confirmed, it was not definitively proved that the appellants caused the death, given that circumstantial evidence was inherently weak and inconsistent with other possible inferences, such as threats from other individuals ([23]–[28]).

Regarding the kidnapping charge, the court concluded that there was insufficient evidence to establish that Botomane was unlawfully deprived of his liberty, as he appeared to voluntarily accompany the appellants upon being informed they were looking for someone else. This finding negatively impacted the validity of the kidnapping conviction ([34]).

The court also condemned the police's methods during the investigation, noting the undesirable fusion of private and police activities, exemplified by Beukes’ dual role and the actions of the appellants while in their official capacity ([39]–[41]).

#### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, setting aside both the convictions and related sentences against the appellants. The court concluded that the murder conviction did not withstand scrutiny under the principles of circumstantial evidence due to the reasonable doubts raised. The kidnapping conviction was likewise found to be unjustified, leading to the conclusion that individuals should not be criminally liable based solely on uncorroborated assertions that they engaged in impropriety.

This decision underscores the balance of rights within the democratic framework of South Africa, reiterating the necessary scrutiny on police procedures and ensuring that individual rights are upheld in investigations ([39]–[42]). The court's decision not only affects the appellants but also raises critical questions about the conduct of police investigations in conjunction with private entities.
