Source Folder: 2022

# Case Note: Susara Magrietha Strohmenger v Schalk Willem Victor and Another [2022] ZASCA 45

### F – Facts
The case concerns an application for special leave to appeal from the judgment of the Northern Cape Division of the High Court, Kimberley. Susara Magrietha Strohmenger ("the applicant") sought to appeal against the full court's decision, which had set aside a trial court's ruling that had dismissed the first respondent Schalk Willem Victor's claim ([1]–[3]). The litigation stemmed from a claim for restitution regarding the property located at 5 Malan Street, Postmasburg, which Victor contended was transferred under an invalid agreement. The background facts included that on 1 October 2015, Victor, as plaintiff, issued a summons against Strohmenger for restitution arising from an alleged invalid agreement concerning the alienation of the property ([3]–[4]). 

Initially, Victor claimed restitution not only of the property at 5 Malan Street but also for various compensation concerning other properties. However, by the commencement of the trial on 5 August 2019, Victor abandoned these alternative claims, focusing solely on the property at 5 Malan Street ([6]). Strohmenger countered Victor’s claim by raising a special plea of prescription, affirming that the summons had been served more than three years post the alleged debt's due date ([5]).

The trial court dismissed Victor's claim and his special plea of prescription, determining that Victor had failed to substantiate his claim with evidence ([7]–[8]). Upon appeal, the full court concluded that Strohmenger had to comply with various pleading requirements substantiating her defenses ([9]–[10]). The full court eventually ruled in favor of Victor, prompting Strohmenger to seek special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([2]–[3]).

### I – Issues
The central issues arose from the following legal questions: 

1. **Whether special circumstances existed** for the Supreme Court to grant special leave to appeal regarding the full court's decision ([1]–[2]).
2. **The validity of Victor’s claim for restitution** given the upheld appeal from the trial court’s dismissal ([2], [8]).
3. **The prescription plea by Strohmenger**, specifically whether it was precluded from being relied upon due to the absence of a cross-appeal against the trial court’s decision on the plea ([8], [9]).

### R – Rules
The court's reasoning drew upon various legal principles and authorities, notably:

1. **Prescription**: The court referenced the general principles concerning prescription as per the Prescription Act, indicating strict adherence to time limits to raise claims ([5], [7]).
2. **Requirements for Validity of Agreement**: The Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981 stipulates that certain property agreements must be in writing to be legally enforceable ([14], [21]). The court referred to the abstract theory of ownership transfer, as detailed by Brand JA in *Legator McKenna Inc and Another v Shea and Others* ([21]).
3. **Pleading and Evidence**: The rules of pleadings require that all material facts and elements of a cause of action must be included in pleadings, ensuring fair notice to the opposite party ([10], [11]).

### A – Application
In analyzing the case, the Supreme Court considered the full court’s findings and assessed whether they held merit. It acknowledged that special circumstances existed warranting a review of the full court's ruling due to perceived misinterpretations of the facts and evidential burdens regarding the restitution claim ([28], [28]).

The judgment noted that Victor’s closure of his case without presenting any evidence was detrimental to his claim. The court emphasized that he had not substantiated essential elements that were directly contested by Strohmenger, such as the details concerning the alleged agreements and property transactions ([18], [26]). This failure directly impacted the validity of his restitution claim. 

The court further pointed out that without a valid underlying agreement, as required by statutory provisions, claims for restitution could not succeed, particularly given the lack of evidence in support of his assertions that he had purchased the properties for Strohmenger ([24], [26]). Thus, the reliance on a supposed oral agreement or donation was misapplied, since such claims did not meet the requirements of the law ([22], [23]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately granted special leave to appeal, ruled in favor of Strohmenger, and dismissed Victor's claims with costs ([29]). This judgment underscored the necessity for strict compliance with legal requirements in property transactions and reaffirmed the critical importance of adequate evidentiary support in civil claims. The court's emphasis on pleadings also served to illustrate the fundamental procedural safeguards in civil litigation, ensuring that parties are clearly informed of claims and defenses at each stage of proceedings.

The result serves as a pivotal reminder of the requirements under the Alienation of Land Act and the necessary legal rigor surrounding claims of restitution in the context of property law throughout South Africa.
