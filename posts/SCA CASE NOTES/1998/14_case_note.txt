Case Note: REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1998

# FIRAC Case Note: Republic of South Africa (Case 328/97)

### F – Facts
The case centers around the **Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council (JTMC)**, established by the **Local Government Transition Act** No 209 of 1993 (LOT). Following Proclamation No 24 of 1994 by the Premier of Gauteng, several city councils in Johannesburg, Soweto, and Sandton were dissolved, leading to the formation of the JTMC and subsequently four substructures, including the **Eastern Metropolitan Substructure (EMSS)** and the **Northern Metropolitan Substructure (NMS)** ([paras 1-3]).

The appellants, **ratepayers within the jurisdiction of the EMSS**, contested several resolutions adopted by the JTMC and the EMSS in June 1996. These resolutions imposed a general rate of **6.45 cents in the rand** on land and rights within the JTMC, intended to balance income and expenditure across the councils. However, the resolutions sought to collect excessive income from the EMSS and NMS to cover deficits in other areas, projecting surpluses of R438,330,000 from EMSS alone ([paras 4-7]).

The appellants argued that the resolutions were **ultra vires**, violating:
1. Section 23(c) of Annexure A to Proclamation 35 of 1995, which limited the JTMC's ability to levy contributions.
2. Section 178(2) of the **interim Constitution** (Act 200 of 1993), which mandated local government expenditure to be necessary for exercising its powers.
3. Section 58 of the **Local Government Ordinance 17 of 1939**, which required a detailed fiscal estimate be presented prior to expenditure ([paras 8-10]).

The respondents contested the appellants' **locus standi**, asserting that they lacked sufficient interest to challenge the resolutions ([paras 11-13]).

### I – Issues
The key legal issues presented in this case include:
1. Whether the appellants had **locus standi** to challenge the impugned resolutions of the JTMC and EMSS ([paras 11-13]).
2. Whether the JTMC's resolutions were consistent with relevant legislative frameworks, including:
   - Section 23(c) of Annexure A to Proclamation 35 of 1995.
   - Section 178(2) of the interim Constitution.
   - Section 58 of the Local Government Ordinance 17 of 1939.
3. The jurisdictional question of whether the Appellate Division had the authority to review administrative actions in light of the interim Constitution's provisions ([paras 14-15]).

### R – Rules
The court derived its findings from several statutory provisions:
- **Local Government Transition Act** No 209 of 1993 encompasses the formation and powers of local authorities ([para 1]).
- **Section 23(c) of Annexure A to Proclamation 35 of 1995** specifies permissible levies on transitional metropolitan substructures based on income ([paras 8-10]).
- **Section 178(2) of the interim Constitution** emphasizes the necessity of property rates for local governance and mandates a uniform structure for rates ([para 9]).
- **Section 58 of the Local Government Ordinance 17 of 1939** requires a detailed financial estimate to be presented and approved prior to incurring expenditure ([para 10]).
- **Section 101(5) of the interim Constitution** delineates the limits of jurisdiction of the Appellate Division concerning matters that fall under the Constitutional Court's purview ([paras 14-15]).

Furthermore, **case law** was cited to establish precedents regarding **locus standi**: *Jacobs en 'n Ander v Waks en Ander* 1992 (1) SA 521 (A), where it was determined that ratepayers have standing to challenge unlawful municipal expenditure ([para 11]).

### A – Application
In resolving the locus standi issue, the court analyzed the trust relationship between ratepayers and municipal authorities. Drawing from the precedent established in *Waks*, the court concluded that each appellant had a direct interest in contesting the actions concerning the unlawful expenditure of ratepayer funds, effectively granting them locus standi ([paras 11-13]).

On evaluating the **substantive merits** of the appellants’ claims, the court acknowledged that there was an administrative act contestation regarding resolutions by the JTMC and the EMSS. Nonetheless, it found that given the interim Constitution's provisions regarding jurisdiction, any disputes related to the legality of administrative actions required adjudication by the Constitutional Court instead of this appellate body ([paras 14-15]).

The court emphasized that even if the issues were significant, it ultimately did not possess the jurisdiction to hear the merits under the interim Constitution, which excluded the Appellate Division from adjudicating matters within the Constitutional Court's domain. This limitation also applied to common law grounds for review ([paras 16-17]).

### C – Conclusion
The court upheld that the appeal was to be directed to the **Constitutional Court** to address two core issues:
1. The legality of the administrative actions taken by the JTMC and the EMSS pursuant to the interim Constitution.
2. Whether the interim Constitution preserved a jurisdictional capacity for common law reviews concerning these administrative actions ([paras 18-19]).

Additionally, it ordered all parties to bear their own costs incurred in the abortive proceedings and indicated that the costs related to the proceedings in the court a quo would be reserved for determination by the Constitutional Court. The court affirmed the significant public interest inherent in the matters disputed, warranting the Constitutional Court's oversight for further adjudication ([paras 20-21]). 

This case underscores the complexities of local government authority in South Africa and the intersection of constitutional law with municipal governance, reflecting the transformative legal landscape post-apartheid.
