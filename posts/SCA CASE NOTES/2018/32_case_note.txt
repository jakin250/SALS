# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title:** Macinezela v The State (550/2017) [2018] ZASCA 32 (26 March 2018)

**Court:** The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Coram:** Navsa, Majiedt, Dambuza and Mocumie JJA, Hughes AJA  
**Heard:** 15 February 2018  
**Delivered:** 26 March 2018

---

## F - Facts 

The appellant, Sobahle Macinezela (aka Macimela), was convicted of rape and sentenced to life imprisonment by the Regional Court in Mount Frere, Eastern Cape, following an incident involving his relative, the complainant, who was 28 years old at the time ([paras 1-2]). The complainant had lived with the appellant and his wife, who is also her maternal aunt, and alleged that she was raped on multiple occasions in March 2015. At the heart of the trial was the question of the complainant's mental capacity, with the prosecution amending the charge sheet to indicate that the complainant was "not mentally stable." This amendment occurred without a prior inquiry into the implications of the amendment for admissibility of her testimony ([paras 3-4]).

During the trial, the complainant insisted that the sexual acts were non-consensual, while the appellant argued there was a consensual love relationship between them ([para 4]). The magistrate accepted the complainant's testimony and determined that, even if consent had been given, it was invalid due to her alleged mental illness. The conviction and sentence were subsequently upheld by the High Court ([para 6]). On appeal, the appellant contended that the process leading to the introduction of the complainant's mental condition was flawed, with no opportunity given to him to respond to the amendment or for the court to properly assess the complainant's capacity to testify ([paras 7-8]).

## I - Issues

The principal legal questions arising in this appeal include:

1. Whether the trial court erred in failing to conduct an inquiry into the complainant's mental state before allowing her to testify.
2. Whether the failure to ascertain the complainant's ability to distinguish between truth and falsehood rendered her testimony inadmissible ([paras 11-12]).

## R - Rules

The relevant provisions from the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA) outline the obligations of the trial court in relation to the testimony of witnesses, particularly those who may be deemed mentally impaired:

- **Section 162(1)**: Requires that all witnesses be examined under oath.
- **Section 163**: Provides for the administration of affirmation in lieu of an oath in certain circumstances.
- **Section 164**: Specifies that witnesses who do not understand the nature and import of the oath may testify only if admonished to speak the truth, contingent on their ability to differentiate between truth and falsehood ([paras 16-18]).

In S v Katoo, the court described the requisite conditions under which a witness may be disqualified from testifying due to mental incapacity ([para 14]). Furthermore, in S v Matshivha, it was clarified that an inquiry into a witness's understanding of the oath is essential for ensuring that testimony is properly admitted into evidence ([paras 17-18]).

## A - Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal critically analyzed the procedural flaws that permeated the trial. It noted that the magistrate failed to conduct a necessary inquiry regarding the complainant's mental condition. The prosecutor's assertion at the beginning of the trial about her mental instability necessitated a formal assessment to determine both her understanding of the oath and her ability to testify competently. The court further emphasized that the magistrate's lack of diligence in this regard invalidated the proceedings ([paras 19-20]).

The judgment referenced previous cases, specifically S v Nondzamba and Motsisi v S, to demonstrate the judiciary's responsibility to protect the rights of both complainants and accused individuals by ensuring that evidence is indeed admissible ([paras 21-22]). The failure to engage in a proper inquiry into whether the complainant could distinguish between truth and falsehood was determined to be a significant error, undermining the fairness of the trial and the integrity of the evidence presented.

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appeal must be upheld due to the trial court's failure to follow established legal protocols regarding the admission of witness testimony when mental incapacity is alleged. The court set aside the conviction and sentence, reinstating the fundamental principles of equitable legal process and underlining the importance of ensuring that all testimonial evidence meets the requisite standards of competence ([paras 22-23]).

The implications of this judgment extend beyond the present case, highlighting a systemic need for judicial diligence in similar future cases involving vulnerable witnesses. The ruling serves as a reminder of the responsibilities imposed on the courts to protect the rights of all participants in the judicial system, thereby reinforcing the integrity of legal proceedings in South Africa.
