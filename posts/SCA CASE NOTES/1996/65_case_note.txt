Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1996

# FIRAC Case Note: Greeff v Andrews (Case No 435/94)

## F – Facts

The case arises from a dispute between **Sophia Aletta Greeff** (Appellant) and **Donald James Andrews** (Respondent) regarding the distribution of commission from a property deal related to a significant afforestation project involving a timber company, **Mondi**. Prior to 1983, the Respondent was engaged as an estate agent and livestock auctioneer. The Appellant joined the Respondent's firm in July 1983, with an understanding to equally share the commissions earned, while also covering their own expenses, and had an amicable working relationship until it was terminated in October 1989 ([paras 1–2]).

The conflict ensued when a business associate named **Mills**, from a firm acting as land valuers, sought assistance from the Respondent to acquire options for about 40,000 hectares of land for potential sale to Mondi. Mills had conditionally agreed to share commissions from the project with the Respondent but objected to Greeff's involvement ([paras 2–4]).

During a pivotal meeting in January 1989, it was established that Mills wished to exclude Greeff from participating in the project due to concerns over her involvement. Testimonies differed: Greeff claimed the Respondent assured her she would share in the commission, while the Respondent asserted he communicated Mills's conditions to Greeff ([paras 4–5]). After the project was successful, yielding approximately R1 million in earnings for the Respondent, Greeff sought to claim half of this commission ([paras 6–11]).

Initial proceedings in the Eastern Cape Division led to a ruling in favor of Greeff by **Jennett J**, who found in her favour based largely on the burden of proof set on the Respondent. However, the Full Bench later reversed this decision, prompting the appeal to the Supreme Court ([paras 1, 7, 11, 14]).

## I – Issues

The central legal question in this case was whether the parties had a mutual agreement in January 1989 regarding Greeff’s exclusion from the Mondi project and the subsequent sharing of commissions. More specifically, the Court needed to determine if Greeff had a legitimate claim to a share in the commission earned by the Respondent ([paras 5, 7, 11]).

## R – Rules

The relevant legal principles include:

1. **Burden of Proof**: In civil matters, the party bearing the burden of proof must establish its case on a balance of probabilities ([paras 6, 10]).
   
2. **Mutual Exclusivity of Testimonies**: The court must analyze contradictory statements to determine which version of events is more plausible, relying heavily on credibility when probabilities do not provide a clear direction ([para 10]).

3. **Contractual Interpretation**: The Court may consider the intentions of parties within the context of the agreement and the conduct following its execution ([para 11]).

Relevant case law included the principles derived from **National Employers' General Insurance Co Ltd v Jagers** 1984 (4) SA 437, which establishes guidelines on the treatment of credibility and conflicting testimonies ([para 7]).

## A – Application

In applying the law, the Supreme Court assessed the credibility of the testimonies provided by Greeff and the Respondent. The Court noted that both Greeff and the Respondent appeared credible but ultimately found the probabilities surrounding the mutual agreement favoured the Respondent ([paras 9, 10, 14]). 

Crucially, the court concluded that Greeff’s claims lacked support in light of presented evidence, especially following acknowledged declarations by Mills to exclude her from the project. The Court argued that Greeff's understanding that she would receive half of the commission was implausible given prior acknowledgments of her exclusion, as well as the Respondent's conduct following the meeting with Mills ([paras 10–12]).

The Court expressed skepticism about Greeff's credibility, particularly concerning her reaction to communications from Mills that indicated exclusion from the project and her acceptance of a sequent payment of R25,000, which she suggested was incomplete but did not dispute during the termination of their business relationship. By not asserting her claim during the cessation of their association, Greeff's position was further undermined ([paras 12–14]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court ultimately dismissed Greeff's appeal, reaffirming the Full Bench’s conclusions that the probabilities supported the Respondent's version of events, particularly the exclusion of Greeff from the project and not sharing in the commission. The judgment underscored the importance of clarity and consistency in agreements and upheld the lower court's scrutiny over the critical aspects of the parties' interactions and the nature of their business arrangement ([paras 13–14]).

This case highlights the complexities involved in determining the recognition of informal agreements and the necessity of solid evidential support for claims of financial entitlement, especially in joint ventures marked by changes in relationship dynamics. The court’s ruling thus serves as a significant contribution to contract law and its principles of interpretation, particularly concerning verbal agreements and implied conditions therein ([paras 11–14]).
