# Case Note: Carter v Haworth [2009] ZASCA 19

## FIRAC Structure

### F – Facts 
This appeal arises from a judgment of the Cape High Court delivered by NC Erasmus J, concerning a claim for damages by the respondent, Kathleen Shirley Haworth, against the appellant, John William Carter, following bodily injuries sustained in a motor vehicle accident on 30 April 2001 while she was visiting South Africa. The appellant conceded liability concerning the merits of the claim, narrowing the dispute to the quantum of damages. Primarily, the claim encompassed several heads, including general damages, future medical expenses, and future loss of earnings ([paras 2–3]). 

The trial court was tasked with determining the quantum of damages but limited its findings to establishing the reasonability of estimated amounts—R100,500 for future medical expenses and R100,000 for general damages—without issuing an order for payment ([para 3]). Moreover, the judge made certain factual assumptions to be considered by an actuary for calculating past and future loss of earnings. The judgment allowed costs on a party-and-party scale but deferred the issue of costs related to a postponement to a later date ([paras 5–6]).

### I – Issues
Central to the appeal was the question of whether the trial court's judgment—permitting damages and making factual findings without issuing a definitive order—qualified as appealable under South African law. Counsel for both parties contended that the judgment was indeed appealable, despite the absence of traditional appealable attributes such as a final order ([paras 7–10]). The court needed to determine the finality and definitiveness of the judgment regarding the rights of the parties and the substantial nature of the relief sought. 

### R – Rules
The legal framework governing appealability is articulated in section 20(1) of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959. This section outlines that an appealable judgment must possess three characteristics: (1) finality in effect not subject to alteration by the court of first instance, (2) definitiveness of the rights of the parties in terms of relief granted, and (3) disposition of a substantial portion of the relief claimed. Relevant case law exemplifying these principles includes *Zweni v Minister of Law and Order* and *Ndlovu v Santam Ltd*, which emphasize the necessity of a definitive order from the trial court for a judgment to be deemed appealable ([paras 10–11]).

### A – Application
The court assessed the attributes of the judgment under scrutiny and found significant shortcomings regarding both finality and definitiveness. The trial judge's failure to issue a definitive order precluded any substantive resolution of the claim for damages ([para 12]). The absence of an instructive conclusion regarding the future course following the actuary’s involvement further complicated matters, leaving the court unable to ascertain whether final resolution lay ahead or persisted as a hypothetical discussion ([para 11]).

The court likened the case to previous rulings where a determination had been made without a conclusive order, thus rendering appeals premature ([para 8]). Regarding the issue of costs arising from postponements in proceedings, the court noted the lack of clarity surrounding the resolution and the necessity of further judicial intervention ([para 9]).

### C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the trial court's judgment failed to meet essential criteria for appealability. Consequently, the appeal was struck from the roll, leaving the substantive merits of the case unaddressed due to procedural deficiencies ([para 14]). The decision reiterated the imperative for trial courts to produce clear orders that substantiate judicial determinations. Each party was ordered to bear its own costs, reflecting a shared responsibility for the misunderstanding concerning the appealability of the judgment ([para 14]).
