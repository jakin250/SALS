Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 137

F – Facts

[1] The case involves the respondent, Mr. Mabhaso Nontsele, who instituted legal action against the appellant, the Minister of Police, and the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) for claims of wrongful arrest, detention, and malicious prosecution. The specifics note that Mr. Nontsele was arrested on December 8, 2013, and detained for 527 days until May 19, 2015. His arrest and subsequent detention arose from accusations of raping Ms. K[...] S[...] during a traditional ceremony on December 6, 2013.

[2] Upon trial on July 15, 2015, Mr. Nontsele was acquitted under section 174 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA), as the State conceded a lack of prima facie evidence against him. Following this acquittal, he pursued damages in the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, where he was partially successful. The court found that while he failed to prove unlawful arrest or malicious prosecution, his detention from the date bail was refused until his release was indeed unlawful, resulting in an award of R1.6 million in damages.

[3] Following the high court's ruling, the Minister applied for leave to appeal, which was initially denied but later granted upon petitioning the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA). Notably, Mr. Nontsele did not seek leave to cross-appeal from either court, although a Notice of Cross-Appeal was filed on August 27, 2022.

[4] The court was tasked with addressing the validity of entertaining the cross-appeal without leave sought, as well as whether the high court was justified in concluding that Mr. Nontsele's detention was unlawful for the period identified.

I – Issues

[5] The primary legal issues presented in this case include:

  1. Whether the Supreme Court of Appeal may consider a cross-appeal despite the lack of a formal application for leave to cross-appeal.
  2. Whether the high court correctly determined the unlawfulness of Mr. Nontsele's detention spanning from February 6, 2014, to May 19, 2015.

R – Rules

[6] The key legal authority in this case is encapsulated within the provisions of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, notably sections 16(1) and 19(d). Section 16(1) outlines that appeals against high court decisions require prior leave to be granted, reinforcing that without such leave, the court lacks jurisdiction to entertain an appeal. Section 19(d) offers the court the power to condone failures relating to procedural applications, although this is not an automatic remedy.

[7] The ruling also references previous case law, including Octagon Chartered Accountants v The Additional Magistrate and Newlands Surgical Clinic v Peninsula Eye Clinic, emphasizing the necessity for leave to appeal as a jurisdictional prerequisite in civil litigation. Additionally, foundational principles from Carmichele v Minister of Safety and Security articulate the obligations of prosecutors and police officers in conveying all relevant circumstances to the court.

A – Application

[8] The court first addressed the purported cross-appeal from Mr. Nontsele, concluding that as he had not formally sought leave to appeal, the appeal jurisdiction could not be exercised. The absence of an application for leave to cross-appeal rendered it impossible for the Supreme Court to entertain the cross-appeal despite arguments advocating for condonation based on cost considerations.

[9] In evaluating the substantive issue of Mr. Nontsele's detention, the court determined that the evidentiary deficiencies during the bail proceedings were significant. The court highlighted that the State's case rested heavily on circumstantial evidence and that neither the police nor the prosecutor had adequately apprise the magistrate of the substantial weaknesses in the prosecution’s case, including exculpatory evidence favoring Mr. Nontsele.

[10] The court applied established precedents regarding unlawful detention, explicating that mere procedural defects or community pressures do not justify the deprivation of freedom absent a robust prima facie case. Furthermore, it critically examined the failure to disclose negative DNA evidence, deemed particularly central to Mr. Nontsele's defense, reinforcing the court's expectation of complete candor from prosecutorial and law enforcement officials.

[11] The assessment of the detention period revealed that the continued opposition to bail by the police and prosecutor lacked proper foundation post-February 6, 2014. The inquiry thus underscored the importance of personal freedom and the implications of unjustified detention on Mr. Nontsele.

C – Conclusion

[12] The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately reached a dual conclusion. Firstly, it confirmed that the cross-appeal could not be entertained due to the absence of an application for leave to appeal, aligning with its previous rulings regarding jurisdictional fact requirements.

[13] Secondly, the Supreme Court upheld the high court’s determination regarding the unlawfulness of Mr. Nontsele’s detention, asserting that the entirety of his detention period was unjustified based on the evidentiary insufficiencies highlighted throughout the proceedings. The appeal by the Minister was successful, leading to the dismissal of Mr. Nontsele's claim with costs, affirming both the protection of individual rights under the South African Constitution and the responsibilities vested in state actors to uphold those rights.
