Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2006

# FIRAC Case Note: South African Liquor Traders Association v. Chairperson, Gauteng Liquor Board [CCT 57/05]

## F – Facts
The **case at hand** involves an application by the South African Liquor Traders Association (SALTA) and various individuals for the confirmation of an order of constitutional invalidity concerning the definition of “shebeen” under the Gauteng Liquor Act 2 of 2003 (“the Act”) ([1]-[2]). The judgment is rooted in a previous unopposed ruling by the Pretoria High Court, which declared a portion of the definition of “shebeen” unconstitutional due to vagueness and irrationality. The questioned definition describes a shebeen as: 

> "any unlicensed operation whose main business is liquor and is selling less than ten (10) cases consisting of 12 x 750ml of beer bottles" ([2]).

The applicants argue that the definition lacks a specified period for the sales threshold, effectively rendering it vague. They seek a resolution that eliminates the specified threshold while confirming their right to freely conduct their business ([6]-[12]).

Relevant parties included:
- **Applicants**: 
  - SALTA, representing approximately 200,000 liquor traders in South Africa, including shebeen owners([3])
  - Individual shebeen owners ([4])
  - Major liquor manufacturers and distributors ([4])
  
- **Respondents**: 
  - The Chairperson and the Gauteng Liquor Board, and the Member of the Executive Council ([5]).

The High Court previously issued a consent order, declaring the latter part of the shebeen definition invalid and severing it, leading to a redefinition that omits the sales quantity ([14]).

## I – Issues
The pivotal legal question arising from this case is whether the definition of “shebeen” in the Gauteng Liquor Act is constitutionally invalid due to its vagueness and irrationality. Specifically, the Court must determine if this lack of clarity violates constitutional principles, particularly the rule of law, and whether the remedy proposed by the applicants or the MEC (Member of the Executive Council) is appropriate ([25]-[26]).

## R – Rules
The relevant legal framework encompasses:
1. **Constitutional Principles** from Section 1, emphasizing the rule of law as a foundational value of the Republic ([27]).
2. **Precedents** set in prior decisions such as **Affordable Medicines Trust v Minister of Health**, which underscored that laws must be clear and accessible ([27]).
3. **Section 172 of the Constitution**, stipulating that courts must declare laws inconsistent with the Constitution invalid and may provide just and equitable remedies ([41]).

The MEC contended that the definition, while vague, should be amended by reading in the words "per week" to maintain regulatory aims ([22]-[24]). The Court assessed both the necessity for clarity and the broader legislative intent.

## A – Application
The Court, reflecting on whether the definition as amended fits within the legislative scheme, observed that vagueness leads to a violation of the rule of law. It highlighted that, as established in **Affordable Medicines Trust**, vagueness prevents individuals from regulating their conduct according to the law, which is crucial to a democratic society ([28]). 

Upon examining the proposals for remedy, the Court critically analyzed both the severance of the definition by the High Court and the MEC’s suggestion to introduce "per week." It noted that severance effectively broadens the definition beyond legislative intent, risking harm to regulatory objectives ([34]-[39]). The Court asserted that while severance could provide clarity, it must also align with the Act’s overall purpose of diligent regulation of liquor sales ([32]).

In weighing the alternatives, the Court sided with a compromise: it categorized "shebeen" as:

> "any unlicensed operation whose main business is liquor and is selling less than sixty (60) cases consisting of 12 x 750ml of beer bottles per week" ([46]-[47]).

This reading-in approach satisfied the need for specificity while respecting legislative intent.

## C – Conclusion
The Court concluded that the definition of "shebeen," as scoped in the Gauteng Liquor Act, was constitutionally invalid due to its inherent vagueness. The order from the High Court was deemed not suitable as it risked undermining regulatory frameworks vital for public safety. Hence, the Court’s modified prescription imposed a clearer limit on the operation of shebeens while obliging the Act to refine its provisions within six months ([55]).

Thus, the ruling not only reformed the legal landscape concerning shebeen operations but also reinforced the Constitutional Court's commitment to uphold the rule of law against vague legislation, requiring legislative adjustments that align with constitutional values. The MEC was held accountable for the costs incurred due to their inadequate response in the corporate litigation process ([49]-[53]). 

This landmark case underscores the delicate balance between regulation, business rights, and constitutional safeguards and establishes precedent for interpreting laws against the backdrop of constitutional clarity and purpose.
