Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note: *Scholtz & Another v De Kock NO & Others* (312/2023) [2024] ZASCA 132 

## F – Facts
The case revolves around the deceased estate of Mrs. Nicquelette Veronique de Kock and the obligations of her sister, Ms. Michelle Jacqueline Scholtz, who serves as the executrix of the estate. The deceased, who died on 26 September 2018, was awarded R7,067,736.80 by the Road Accident Fund (RAF) due to injuries sustained in a motor vehicle accident. Subsequently, on 28 November 2014, the deceased instructed her attorneys to transfer R5,600,000 into Ms. Scholtz’s attorney trust account. This amount was then subsequently moved to an investment account managed by Ms. Scholtz ([1]–[5]).

The first respondent, Mr. Leon De Kock, contended that Ms. Scholtz had a duty to account for these funds as the guardian of their two children, who are beneficiaries of the deceased's estate. Initially, the court of first instance dismissed his application, while the full court of the Western Cape Division overturned this, asserting that Ms. Scholtz had a legal obligation to account to the estate ([4]–[12]). 

## I – Issues
The primary legal questions in this case are:

1. Did Ms. Scholtz owe a fiduciary duty to account to her deceased sister's estate for the R5,600,000 disbursed during the sister's lifetime?
2. What constitutes the fiduciary relationship within the context of an attorney-client relationship, particularly concerning funds disbursed into a trust account and subsequently managed in an investment account?
3. Was there a legitimate basis on which De Kock could require Ms. Scholtz to provide an accounting of the funds ([1]–[12])?

## R – Rules
The court's examination of fiduciary duties draws from several legal principles:
1. The existence of a fiduciary relationship depends on the specific facts of each case. It requires a position of trust where one party is expected to act in the best interests of another ([16],[18]).
2. In particular, the guidance provided in *Robinson v Randfontein Estates Gold Mining Co Ltd* establishes that a fiduciary relationship necessitates a duty to protect another’s interests without placing oneself in conflicted positions. 
3. Additionally, the case of *Beningfield v Baxter* established an exception permitting certain parties, beyond executors, to assert claims against an estate ([4],[16]).

In the context of attorney-client dynamics, the court referenced *Joubert Scholtz Inc v Elandsfontein Beverage Marketing*, which clarifies that a mere deposit of funds into an attorney's trust account does not inherently create a fiduciary relationship. Such a duty arises through an agreement or mandate, which must be explicitly demonstrated ([18]).

## A – Application
The court critically evaluated the nature of the intentions behind the fund transfers and the roles of Ms. Scholtz and Mr. De Kock. The primary contention from Mr. De Kock was based on an alleged fiduciary obligation that purportedly arose simply due to the funds being deposited into Ms. Scholtz’s trust account as an attorney. The court strongly refuted this argument, asserting that the trust relationship was short-lived, terminating once the money was transferred to the investment account ([18],[19]).

In examining Ms. Scholtz’s assertion that she acted on her sister’s instructions in disbursing the funds, the court considered the circumstances under which these payments were made: specifically, that R3,000,000 was paid to the deceased before her passing and that Ms. Scholtz maintained an investment account that generated interest over a considerable period of time ([22],[23]). The absence of complaints or dissatisfaction regarding the management of the funds from the deceased indicated her acceptance of the transactions as satisfactory, further undermining the basis for Mr. De Kock’s claims ([24]).

Simultaneously, the court referenced the *Plascon-Evans* principle in addressing disputes of fact, emphasizing that the version of events presented by Ms. Scholtz was far less tenable, particularly given the strong sibling bond and the abandonment of her own claim against the RAF ([20],[21]).

## C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, asserting that Ms. Scholtz did not have a fiduciary obligation to account for the funds in question, either under statutory or common law principles of fiduciary duty, nor under the specific attorney-client relationship terms applicable to this case. The court asserted that the absence of any ongoing dissatisfaction voiced by the deceased indicated her acceptance of the fund disbursement and management ([26]). 

The judgment serves to elucidate the nature of fiduciary duties in relationships tangled with familial bonds and professional obligations, underscoring that clarity and documented instructions are paramount in such contexts. Furthermore, Ms. Scholtz was affirmed in her operational management of the funds, aptly supported by transactional records, and thereby relieved from any duty to account for the disputed amount.

The appeal was therefore upheld with costs, demonstrating the court's firm resolution in favor of the appellant against the respondent's claims ([26]).
