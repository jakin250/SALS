Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# Case Note: Thubakgale and Others v Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality and others [2021] ZACC 45

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The applicants, consisting of Thupetji Alexander Thubakgale, the Ekurhuleni Concerned Residents Association, and residents of the Winnie Mandela informal settlement, sought legal redress against the Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality and its officials for failing to provide adequate housing as mandated under section 26(1) of the South African Constitution. Having been allocated housing subsidies as far back as 1998, the applicants found themselves in deplorable living conditions, exacerbated by unlawful occupancy of the houses intended for them, a situation worsened by municipal mismanagement including a “dummy numbers” scheme that allocated multiple subsidies to single properties ([paras 1–10]).

The case had previously reached the High Court, which had ordered the municipality to provide housing to the applicants by December 2018, a deadline which was later shifted to June 2019 by the Supreme Court of Appeal. However, the municipality filed a late extension application shortly before the deadline, which the applicants contested alongside a counter-application for constitutional damages aimed at compensating them for the ongoing breach of their housing rights ([paras 11–14]).

### I – Issues
The core legal question revolved around whether the applicants were entitled to constitutional damages for the infringement of their right to access adequate housing, despite existing remedial orders from the courts. Specifically, the court needed to determine:
1. Whether constitutional damages constitute appropriate relief under section 38 of the Constitution.
2. Whether the applicants had sufficiently proven the need for such damages or alternative remedies like contempt proceedings would suffice ([paras 121–130]).

### R – Rules
The relevant legal principles arise from:
- **Section 26 of the Constitution**, which guarantees the right to access adequate housing, imposing an obligation on the state to take reasonable measures to realize this right progressively ([para 4]).
- **Section 38 of the Constitution**, which permits anyone to approach a competent court alleging that a right in the Bill of Rights has been infringed or threatened and allows the court to grant appropriate relief.
- The **jurisprudence established** in cases such as *Fose v Minister of Safety and Security* ([1997] ZACC 6), which outlined that appropriate relief includes constitutional damages, underlining the need for a fact-based assessment of each case ([paras 69, 102]).

### A – Application
The court scrutinized the Municipality's persistent failure to deliver housing, highlighting a lack of reasonable measures to correct their inaction and a pattern of bureaucratic evasion of obligations through various court orders. The majority judgment emphasized that the evidence supported the claim of systemic failure to provide housing, substantiating the need for effective relief ([paras 134–80]). 

However, the dissenting judgment led by Jafta J contended that the nature of socio-economic rights inherently limits claims for damages, focusing on the distinction between enjoying the right (current occupancy) and the state's obligations to progressively realize that right. Consequently, applicants were deemed to face no infringements that warranted constitutional damages given the judicial latitude afforded to the state under such rights ([paras 181–220]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the appeal for constitutional damages, aligning with the dissenting view that the applicants should have pursued contempt of court proceedings or other remedies to enforce the earlier High Court orders. Notably, the judgment reaffirmed that although socio-economic rights are justiciable, relief through constitutional damages for infringements is not suitable when existing remedies have not been fully exploited or when the right in question does not entail a direct claim against the state to fulfill its obligations within specified timelines. 

The court’s decision not to award damages underscores the balance the judicial system aims to maintain between enforcing constitutional rights and recognizing the state's obligations and constraints in realizing socio-economic goals in an equitable manner, reflecting ongoing debates surrounding public service delivery and accountability in a democratic South Africa ([paras 195–200]). 

This judgment has profound implications for future claims related to socio-economic rights, particularly in navigating the complexities of state obligations against resource limitations, potentially shaping public policy and future litigation in South Africa regarding housing and related rights.
