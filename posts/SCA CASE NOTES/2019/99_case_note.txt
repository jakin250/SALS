Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: Minister of Mineral Resources v Stern & others; Treasure the Karoo Action Group & another v Department of Mineral Resources & others

**FIRAC Case Note**

**F – Facts**  
The present consolidated appeal arises from two matters concerning the legality of the **Regulations for Petroleum Exploration and Production, 2015** (Petroleum Regulations) promulgated by the Minister of Mineral Resources on 3 June 2015. The first case, referred to as the Stern matter, was initiated by the trustees of the Stern Family Trust and several farmers from the Karoo regions, who sought to have the Petroleum Regulations set aside on the grounds that the Minister lacked the requisite power to issue the regulations and that the process was procedurally unfair [paras 1–2]. The initial application, citing the Minister alone as a respondent, did not prevail in the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, which held that the Minister was not empowered to promulgate the regulations and that their enactment was procedurally flawed [paras 3–4].

Concurrently, a similar application was launched by the Treasure the Karoo Action Group and AfriForum in the Gauteng Division, seeking the review and setting aside of the Petroleum Regulations based primarily on the principle of legality [paras 4–5]. The Gauteng High Court dismissed the application, which prompted an appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal.

**I – Issues**  
The key issues presented in this appeal include:

1. **Did the Minister of Mineral Resources possess the necessary legal authority under the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act, 2002 (MPRDA) to promulgate the Petroleum Regulations?**
2. **Were the processes employed to enact the Petroleum Regulations procedurally fair, and did they comply with relevant administrative justice principles?**
3. **What implications arise from the One Environmental System established under the National Environmental Management Act, 1998 (NEMA) that potentially limits the Minister’s regulatory powers?** [paras 2-4].

**R – Rules**  
The relevant legal framework includes:

1. **Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act, 2002 (MPRDA)**: Specifically, sections 107(1) and amendments under the One Environmental System [paras 20–21].
2. **National Environmental Management Act, 1998 (NEMA)**: Including provisions pertaining to environmental authorisation, regulations under NEMA, and the integration of environmental management [paras 21–23].
3. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: Governs the principles of procedural fairness in administrative action [para 2].
4. **Case law**: Various precedents provide insight into the limitations of administrative powers, including considerations of legality and the necessity of ensuring due process in regulatory contexts [para 48].

**A – Application**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal conducted a thorough analysis regarding the legal authority of the Minister to issue the Petroleum Regulations. It held that significant amendments had been made to the MPRDA as a result of the One Environmental System, transferring the responsibility for the regulation of environmental impacts from the Minister of Mineral Resources to the Minister of Environmental Affairs. This shift was a deliberate legislative choice aimed at establishing a unified environmental governance framework [paras 21, 32].

The Court concluded that the provisions concerning the management of environmental impacts were ultra vires for the Minister of Mineral Resources, as it had been expressly divested of such powers [paras 39-48]. In respect to procedural fairness, while the lower court found that the Minister failed to adequately engage in a fair process during the regulations' formulation, the Supreme Court ultimately deemed this finding unnecessary due to the primary determination regarding authority being sufficient to ground setting aside the regulations [paras 50-51].

The Petroleum Regulations were found to contain many provisions that regulated the process and requirements for environmental authorisation in a manner that exceeded the Minerals Minister’s scope of authority and interfered with the established environmental governance frameworks prescribed under NEMA [paras 48-49].

**C – Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal in the Stern matter, thereby upholding the High Court's judgment, while it upheld the appeal concerning the TKAG matter, setting aside the Petroleum Regulations entirely as they were ultra vires [paras 53]. The decision highlights the critical importance of adhering to designated legislative powers and the structural requirements of administrative law. The judgment not only clarifies the scope of authority afforded to different Ministers regarding environmental regulations but also underscores the necessity of comprehensive public participation and procedural fairness in administrative actions. The implications of this case extend to the governance of environmental practices within South Africa's mining sector, reinforcing the need for coherent and legally grounded regulatory frameworks [paras 53].
