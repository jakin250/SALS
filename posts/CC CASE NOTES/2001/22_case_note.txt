### FIRAC Case Note: Carmichele v Minister of Safety and Security and Another [2001] ZACC 22

#### F – Facts
The case revolves around an attack on Alix Jean Carmichele (the applicant) by Francois Coetzee, who had a documented history of violent and sexual crimes. On 6 August 1995, while at a friend's home, Carmichele was brutally assaulted by Coetzee, who had a history of serious offences, including an attempted rape just months prior. Following the attack, Carmichele sought damages from the Ministers of Safety and Security and Justice, claiming that the police and prosecutors had acted negligently in failing to prevent the assault despite knowledge of Coetzee's potential risk ([1]-[24]).

Carmichele's claim in the Cape of Good Hope High Court was initially met with an order of absolution from the instance, whereby the judge ruled there was insufficient evidence of a duty owed to her by the police and prosecutors. This decision was upheld by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ([3]-[4]). Carmichele pursued an appeal to the Constitutional Court, seeking a legal remedy based on claims of negligence and wrongful omission ([4]-[25]).

#### I – Issues
The legal issues primarily concern:
1. Whether the police and the prosecutors owed Carmichele a legal duty to act to prevent the assault.
2. The interpretation of the common law regarding omissions and negligence in the context of constitutional obligations ([30]-[31]).
3. The applicability of constitutional rights under the Bill of Rights to the actions and omissions of state officials ([29]-[33]).

#### R – Rules
The case emphasizes several key legal principles, including:
- **Constitutional Provision**: Section 39(2) of the Constitution requires courts to develop the common law so as to promote the rights contained within the Bill of Rights, thus embedding constitutional values into tort law ([33]-[35]).
- **Negligence and Duty of Care**: The common law surrounding delictual liability historically required a clear legal duty to avoid or prevent loss. This aligns with the need for a reasonable perception of duty, which may evolve via constitutional directives ([42]-[44]).
- **Public Policy Considerations**: Courts must weigh competing public policy interests, weighing the protection of individuals against the interests of the community, especially in cases involving public officials such as police and prosecutors ([46]-[49]).

#### A – Application
In examining Carmichele's appeal, the Constitutional Court highlighted critical failures on the part of the police and prosecutors:
1. **Duty to Act**: The Court ruled that the police and the prosecutors could indeed owe a duty to protect individuals from foreseeable harm, particularly where they have knowledge of a perpetrator's history of violence and where such individuals pose a credible threat to others ([62]-[64]).
2. **Legal Misinterpretation**: The High Court and the SCA had erred by applying pre-constitutional tests of wrongfulness without considering the implications of the constitutional framework that necessitates a broader and more vigilant approach to the duty to act in cases involving violence against women ([58]-[61]).
3. **Causation and Foreseeability**: The Court posited that had the police and prosecutors appropriately acted upon their knowledge of Coetzee’s criminal history, it is plausible that bail could have been denied, thus preventing the subsequent assault on Carmichele ([75]-[77]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court found significant merit in Carmichele's claims, asserting that the legal landscape concerning the duty of state officials to protect individuals in society must evolve to protect the rights enshrined in the Constitution. The Court overturned the orders of the lower courts, ruling that issues related to the duty to act must be further explored in full trial proceedings, thereby reaffirming the importance of constitutional safeguards in protecting citizens against violent crimes ([83]-[84]). This case highlights the evolving nature of the common law in South Africa, where constitutional imperatives necessitate a re-evaluation of legal obligations to act in the face of foreseeable harm, marking a significant development in the intersection of constitutional law and tort law.
