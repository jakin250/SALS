H v Fetal Assessment Centre [2014] ZACC 34

Constitutional Court 2014 [2014] ZACC 34

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–6])

The applicant, H, is a child born in 2008 with Down syndrome. His mother initiated a claim on his behalf against the Fetal Assessment Centre (the Centre) in the Western Cape High Court, alleging that the Centre had negligently failed to inform her of the probability of the child being born with a serious medical condition. Had she received proper medical advice, it is contended that she would have chosen to terminate the pregnancy. The claim included demands for special damages pertaining to past and future medical expenses and general damages for disability and loss of enjoyment of life. The Centre filed an exception to the claim, contending that it was legally faulty and did not establish a recognized cause of action under South African law.

The High Court upheld the exception, dismissing the claim on the basis of existing legal precedents, notably citing Stewart and Another v Botha. The Supreme Court of Appeal's stance was that the legal questions posed were inappropriate for adjudication, dismissing the petition based on the premise that a child’s claim for damages would require a court to evaluate the desirability of the child's existence, which it deemed too complex for legal inquiry ([paras 1–6]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 7–8])

The case raises several significant legal and constitutional questions:

  1. Should the applicant be granted leave to appeal against the High Court’s decision?
  2. Was the exception procedure the appropriate means to address the legal questions concerning the viability of the child's claim?
  3. Does the law recognize a delictual claim for damages by a child arising from negligent prenatal misdiagnosis, thus enabling the child to seek compensation for being born with a disability?

Each of these questions emphasizes the broader implications for the development of South African common law regarding “wrongful life” claims, and raises considerations around the constitutional rights of children ([paras 7–8]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 9–14])

The court principally relied upon several sections of the South African Constitution, particularly:

  • Section 28(2): Mandating that a child's best interests must be of paramount importance in all matters concerning the child.
  • Section 39(1): Indicating that foreign law may be consulted in interpreting the Bill of Rights.
  • Section 39(2): Directing the courts to develop common law in line with constitutional values.

Previous key cases referenced included:

  • Stewart and Another v Botha - the Supreme Court's view that evaluating the desirability of a child's existence posed insurmountable philosophical challenges for the law.
  • Mukheiber v Raath and Edouard - illustrated circumstances where claims arose from negligent acts that caused significant financial burdens on parents, thus establishing precedents on delictual claims relating to negligent prenatal actions ([paras 9–14]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 15–72])

The court’s analysis began with the acknowledgment that the existing common law in South Africa does not currently recognize a child’s delictual claim for damages due to negligent prenatal misdiagnosis. Nevertheless, there was a consensus that the common law should be developed to conform with constitutional principles, particularly respecting the best interests of children.

The Court highlighted the complexities surrounding the phrase "wrongful life," noting that framing the issue merely as a paradox between existence and non-existence obscured deeper normative choices inherent in the law. It emphasized that judges should openly articulate and defend these choices in light of constitutional imperatives. The argument contended by the Centre—that allowing such claims creates a burden on practitioners and promotes litigation—was counteracted by the potential virtue of recognizing a child’s right to seek redress in the event of being deprived of an informed choice due to negligence ([paras 15–29]).

The Court critically assessed the wrongfulness element of delictual liability, emphasizing that maintaining the best interests of the child may necessitate expanding liability to include prenatal negligence. The approach reframed the inquiry to establish that the medical practitioner, once found negligent, could be held liable not just to parents but also to the child, should those parents choose not to pursue their own claims ([paras 30–72]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 73–81])

In conclusion, the Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal, finding that the High Court had incorrectly upheld the Centre's exception. The appeal was successful, and the court ruled that the High Court's order should be set aside, allowing the applicant to amend the particulars of his claim. This decision did not ultimately resolve the fundamental question of whether a child’s claim could exist, instead instructing that the High Court must now evaluate the claim's merits against the newly articulated constitutional framework.

The judgment profoundly impacts the implications for South African common law in recognizing potential claims from children for being born with disabilities due to negligence. The case signifies a substantial step toward aligning the common law with constitutional values and advocates for an evolved understanding of the rights of children, particularly in the context of prenatal care, thereby recognizing their agency under South African law ([paras 73–81]).
