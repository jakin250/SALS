Steenhuisen and Another v Van Rooyen and Others (2023) [2023] ZASCA 78

In the case of Steenhuisen and Another v Van Rooyen and Others, the first applicant, Mr John Henry Steenhuisen, sought leave to appeal against a judgment from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 78

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–20])

In the case of Steenhuisen and Another v Van Rooyen and Others, the first applicant, Mr John Henry Steenhuisen, sought leave to appeal against a judgment from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria. This judgment had declared the Public Protector's report regarding Mr Van Rooyen unlawful and set it aside. The report arose from a complaint lodged by Mr Kevin Mileham, the second applicant, claiming that Mr Van Rooyen, then a Member of Parliament and former Minister of Finance, misled Parliament during a National Assembly sitting by not disclosing meetings with the Gupta family.

The complaint stemmed from a parliamentary question posed by Steenhuisen on 11 April 2016, asking whether Van Rooyen or his deputy ministers had ever met with members of the Gupta family since taking office. Had he met them, the question requested specifics regarding the meetings. Van Rooyen's response indicated he had never met them in an official capacity, leading to Mileham's complaint to the Public Protector, asserting that the Minister had intentionally misled Parliament.

The Public Protector acknowledged the complaint and launched an investigation, concluding that Van Rooyen’s response was fundamentally misleading and violated both constitutional provisions (ss 96(1) and (2)(b)) and the Executive Ethics Code. However, upon appeal to the High Court, it was found that the Public Protector's understanding of the parliamentary question was flawed. The High Court ruled that her conclusions were irrational due to misinterpretations of the question's time frame and content, thus setting aside her findings.

Following this, Steenhuisen and Mileham sought leave to appeal the High Court’s decision, as the Public Protector's findings were disregarded. The case not only questioned the veracity of Van Rooyen’s response but also addressed larger issues of accountability in parliamentary procedure.

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 1–20])

The primary legal issues in this appeal revolved around:

  1. Whether the Public Protector's interpretation of the parliamentary question was correct and whether her investigation into the alleged misleading of Parliament was rationally conducted.
  2. Whether the response given by Mr Van Rooyen constituted a deliberate effort to mislead Parliament, violating the provisions of the Executive Ethics Code and constitutional standards required for accountable governance.
  3. The implications of the High Court's ruling on the importance of clarity in parliamentary questions and the pervasive effects on the accountability mechanisms in place for members of the executive.

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 1–20])

In addressing these issues, the court considered several legal frameworks, including:

  • Section 96 of the Constitution: Stipulating that members of the executive may not mislead the legislature.
  • The Executive Ethics Code: Mandating ethical conduct among executive members, particularly regarding truthful responses to Parliament.
  • The overarching principles of administrative law concerning rationality and legality, relying on previous rulings, notably Minister of Home Affairs v Somali Association of South Africa [2015] ZASCA 35.
  • Principles relating to public accountability, reinforced by judicial emphasis on the necessity for clarity in parliamentary discourse to minimize misrepresentation.

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 1–20])

The court found that the Public Protector's interpretation of the parliamentary question was fundamentally flawed. The interpretation mistakenly implied that Van Rooyen’s response pertained solely to his official capacity, while the question allowed for a broader understanding, thus failing to accurately reflect the concerns raised. The judgment emphasized that because of the ambiguity inherent in Steenhuisen’s question, reliance on interpretative hints from both the question's wording and context was imperative.

Additionally, it was determined that the Public Protector ignored essential components of her own framing of the question and thus reached a conclusion that could not be upheld. The applicants aimed to establish that Van Rooyen’s answer would constitute misleading conduct, yet the court clarified that any misleading conduct should have been derived from a proper understanding of both the question and Van Rooyen's context, which had not occurred.

The court stressed the importance of accountability through Parliamentary processes, positing that the principles of governance require clarity in how questions are framed. The applicants’ insistence on the significance of excluding certain periods from the assessment of Van Rooyen's responses contributed markedly to the court’s ruling that the Public Protector’s investigation was ultimately devoid of rationality.

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 1–20])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the application for leave to appeal, concluding that there was no merit in the arguments posed by Steenhuisen and Mileham. The ruling reaffirmed the high court's original judgment, asserting that the Public Protector’s misunderstanding of the parliamentary question resulted in her irrational findings.

The decision underscored the critical nature of clear and unambiguous questioning within parliamentary proceedings to ensure that members of the executive are held to account effectively. Furthermore, the judgment implied that any future investigations into similar complaints would require adherence to strict interpretative methodologies that recognize the significance of both context and wording used in parliamentary inquiries.

This judgment holds considerable implications for future inquiries and the operations of the Public Protector, reiterating the necessity for clarity in parliamentary practices and establishing a standard for the rational basis necessary in any administrative investigation into legislative conduct. As a final note, the court refrained from imposing costs based on the principles outlined in Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources [2009] ZASCA 111, emphasizing the importance of public interest in matters of legislative accountability.

In summary, the Supreme Court of Appeal’s decision rectified the misinterpretations at earlier judicial levels while reinforcing the broader legal and democratic principles underpinning South African governance.
