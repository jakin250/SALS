Supreme Court of Appeal

Windrush Intercontinental SA v UACC Bergshav Tankers AS (556/2015) [2016] ZASCA 199

The case revolves around an appeal involving the MT Asphalt Venture, a vessel owned by Bitumen Invest AS and chartered by Windrush Intercontinental SA, in connection with a claim for unpaid wages by crew members who were held hostage by Somali pirates.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 199

F – Facts

The case revolves around an appeal involving the MT Asphalt Venture, a vessel owned by Bitumen Invest AS and chartered by Windrush Intercontinental SA, in connection with a claim for unpaid wages by crew members who were held hostage by Somali pirates. The procedural history begins in the KwaZulu-Natal Local Division of the High Court, Durban, where the court initially refused to set aside a deemed arrest of the vessel ([1]–[2]).

Windrush, incorporated in Panama, entered into a bareboat charter with Bitumen for the vessel, which was part of a sale-leaseback scheme involving Concord Worldwide Inc. Between April and August 2010, Concord, through a technical manager, employed 15 crew members from India for the Asphalt Venture ([2]). On 23 September 2010, Somali pirates hijacked the vessel and held the crew for ransom. After a ransom of USD 3.4 million was paid on 15 April 2011, only eight crew members were released, leaving seven still held captive ([3]).

Following this situation, Windrush terminated its charter with Concord and ceased any further financial obligations related to the crew due to Concord's failure to honor the employment contracts following the hostage crisis ([4]–[6]). Eventually, an action in rem was initiated by Bergshav, which claimed a maritime lien for the unpaid wages of the hostages, eventually leading to the arrest of the vessel in Durban ([9]).

I – Issues

The primary issue at stake was whether there existed a maritime lien for crew wages at the time of the arrest of the Asphalt Venture, allowing Bergshav to pursue an in rem action as per section 3(4)(a) of the Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983 ([14]). The appellants contended the lien was extinguished due to the supervening impossibility arising from the pirate hijacking, which rendered the crew contracts impossible to perform after 15 April 2011 ([15]).

Another significant aspect was the interpretation of relevant provisions of both the employment contracts and applicable Indian maritime law, particularly concerning frustration and the scope of contractual obligations for wages post-employment ([22]–[24]).

R – Rules

Relevant to this case are several statutory and legal frameworks:

  1. Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983: This act outlines the parameters within which maritime claims may be brought in rem ([16]).
  2. Maritime liens and claims definitions: Defined under section 1(1) and section 11 of the Admiralty Act, maritime claims include any claim arising from a maritime lien, which confers preference on claims based on service rendered to a vessel ([18]).
  3. Indian Merchant Shipping Act of 1958: Specifically section 141(1), which provides that a seaman is entitled to wages if their service has been terminated due to the ship's loss or abandonment ([24]) and notes the broader context of maritime employment law ([34]).

Additionally, case law, such as Horlock v Beal and other decisions discussing frustration of contracts, forms a precedent in determining the contractual obligations concerning the hostages ([29]–[30]).

A – Application

The court applied the relevant legal principles to assess the validity of the claim for the maritime lien. The primary inquiry was whether a valid contract and consequent obligation existed for the payment of wages post-termination of the crew’s employment due to the hostage situation ([42]).

The court critically assessed whether the hostages maintained an entitlement to wages following their detainment. It found that the contracts of employment were effectively terminated on 15 April 2011 when the pirates delivered the vessel but retained the crew. The inability to fulfill contractual obligations due to a supervening impossibility (the hijacking), together with the interpretation of the applicable laws, led to the conclusion that there was no maritime lien existing at the time of the vessel's arrest ([31]–[42]).

Moreover, it recognized that extended provisions of the Indian Maritime law did not support the claim unless the preconditions governing the continuous obligation of wages under normal contractual circumstances were met. The case, therefore, hinged on whether the crew’s contract still legally bound the employers to pay wages during the period of captivity ([41]–[42]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal held that Bergshav had not established a prima facie case for the existence of a maritime lien that would justify the in rem arrest of the Asphalt Venture. The appeal was upheld, and the court ordered that the deemed arrest of the vessel be set aside, with costs awarded to the appellants ([44]).

The decision reaffirms the stringent interpretative standards of maritime contracts and underscores the significance of contractual obligations, particularly in contexts affected by supervening impossibility. The ruling suggests that, despite humanitarian considerations, employment obligations cease when circumstances fundamentally change the nature of the service engagement. This case serves as an important precedent regarding the interplay of international maritime law, employment contracts, and the implications of unforeseen global events like piracy.
