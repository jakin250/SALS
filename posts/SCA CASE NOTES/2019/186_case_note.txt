Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: Witzenberg Municipality v Bridgman NO & Others (2018) 

**Neutral Citation:** Witzenberg Municipality v Bridgman NO & others (685/2018) [2019] ZASCA 186 (3 December 2019)  
**Coram:** Navsa, Mbha, Zondi and Van der Merwe JJA and Hughes AJA  
**Heard:** 15 November 2019  
**Delivered:** 3 December 2019  

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–20])

The case concerns a tragic incident where Miss L, an intellectually impaired individual, was raped while visiting the Pine Forest Resort, owned by Witzenberg Municipality ([para 1]). Miss L’s precarious background included a grim upbringing in Bulgaria, marred by a series of disabilities and neglect. After being adopted by a South African couple, Mr. and Mrs. L, she exhibited gradual but significant development facilitated by therapy ([paras 2–4]).

On January 20, 2009, during a family stay at the resort, Miss L was permitted to use the playground independently, consistent with advice aimed at promoting her autonomy ([para 5]). However, she was lured away and assaulted by three minors who had unlawfully accessed the resort ([para 6]). Subsequently, a curator ad litem initiated a lawsuit against Witzenberg Municipality, alleging negligence on the part of the municipality in failing to provide adequate security at the resort ([para 7]).

The municipality contested the action, asserting it had no duty to protect visitors from criminal acts, and sought indemnification from Miss L's adoptive parents, claiming their negligence contributed to the incident ([para 8]). The trial included testimonies from both families and security consultants ([para 9]), leading to a ruling by the Western Cape High Court that held the municipality liable and awarded damages to Miss L.

Despite the municipality's initial refusal to admit liability and subsequent appeals against both the merits and the damages awarded ([paras 20-21]), the issues escalated to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) for reconsideration and appeal concerning the quantum of damages ([para 1]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 21–28])

The case presents several critical legal questions:

1. **Duty of Care:** Did Witzenberg Municipality owe Miss L a legal duty to prevent harm resulting from foreseeable criminal acts on its premises ([para 22])?
   
2. **Negligence and Causation:** Was the municipality negligent in failing to implement adequate security measures, and did that negligence contribute to Miss L's harm ([paras 23–24])?

3. **Quantum of Damages:** Was the initial award of R780,780 excessive, particularly since it surpassed the amount sought by the plaintiff ([paras 30-34])?

4. **Indemnity Claims:** Could the municipality justifiably claim contribution from Miss L's adoptive parents given the circumstances ([paras 8–10])?

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 21–28])

The court applied the established test for negligence as formulated in **Kruger v Coetzee**, which requires that the defendant must foresee a reasonable possibility of harm and take steps to prevent it ([para 23]). The court emphasized that an organ of state, such as the municipality, is obligated to safeguard fundamental rights as expressed in the South African Constitution, including the rights to dignity and safety ([para 22]).

Moreover, the court referenced international obligations concerning the rights of individuals with disabilities, thereby bolstering its duty of care argument against the municipality ([para 22]). Prior case law, including **F v Minister of Safety and Security** and **M v Minister of Safety and Security**, provided guidelines for evaluating damages in sexual offenses ([paras 31–32]). 

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 23–34])

1. **Duty of Care:** The court found that the municipality bore a direct duty to provide adequate security at the resort, failing which it breached its legal obligations to protect guests ([para 22]). This breaches not only legal standards but also societal expectations of safety, particularly for vulnerable individuals like Miss L.

2. **Negligence and Causation:** The court examined the security measures at the resort, highlighting the inadequacy given that the resort had only two security guards present during the incident, which contradicted internal assessments suggesting at least four were necessary. The absence of proper access controls to the squash court, where the assault occurred, was further evidence of negligence ([paras 11–12]). The court concluded that the Municipality's failure to act on existing threats rendered the attack foreseeable, thus establishing a direct linkage between the municipality's negligence and the harms suffered by Miss L ([para 25]).

3. **Quantum of Damages:** The SCA analyzed the damages awarded in light of previous cases. The court recognized the agonizing trauma experienced by Miss L, evidencing the severity of the assault and its profound psychological effects. Ultimately, while acknowledging the horror of the situation, it found the initial damages excessive compared to similar awards in precedent cases, suggesting that the fair compensation for Miss L should be adjusted to R630,780 ([paras 30–34]).

4. **Indemnity Claims:** The court dismissed the municipality's claim for indemnification from Miss L's adoptive parents, underscoring the appropriateness of allowing a vulnerable individual to enjoy her independence without undue parental supervision in a supposedly secure environment ([para 28]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 35])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the Witzenberg Municipality was negligent in its duties and that its failures directly contributed to the traumatic assault on Miss L. The court dismissed the municipality's application for reconsideration regarding the merits, affirming the lower court's ruling on liability. However, it partially granted the appeal concerning the quantum of damages, reducing the amount awarded to R630,780 due to its excessive nature relative to the amount initially sought by the plaintiff ([para 35]).

This ruling emphasizes the ongoing legal responsibilities of municipalities in South Africa to uphold the safety and dignity of all individuals, particularly those with vulnerabilities. It reinforces the position that institutional negligence in safeguarding public premises can incur significant liability, shaping future conduct in public safety and the treatment of vulnerable populations, and highlights the sensitive nature of damage awards in cases of sexual violence—not merely reflecting a financial compensation but also bearing socio-ethical significance in affirming victims' worth and humanity.
