### F – Facts 
The appellant, T P Sithole, was convicted of rape in a lower court and sentenced to ten years' imprisonment, subsequently reduced to six years by the Transvaal Provincial Division, with two years suspended. The case began when the complainant, a girl estimated to be around 13 years old at the time of the alleged rape in December 1990, only reported the incident several months prior to the trial, approximately four years later ([paras 2-3]). 

The complainant disclosed the incident to a friend named Callie after seeing a television program on child molestation. Following this, she wrote a letter to her mother, which was introduced as evidence in court. The only witnesses were the complainant and the appellant ([paras 4-5]). The state did not call other potential witnesses, including the complainant’s mother or Callie, which raised questions about the completeness of the evidence relied upon for conviction. 

The complainant testified that the appellant forcibly engaged in sexual relations with her after threatening her life should she disclose the incident ([paras 7-8]). The appellant denied the allegations, suggesting motives for a false allegation by the complainant and her mother. The discrepancies between the complainant’s testimony in court and the content of her letter, written shortly before the trial, were significant and raised doubts about the reliability of her account ([paras 12-13]).

### I – Issues 
The primary legal questions pertain to:

1. **Credibility of the Complainant's Testimony:** Was the complainant's deferred reporting and the lack of corroborative evidence sufficient to undermine her credibility? ([paras 10-14])
2. **Evidence Evaluation:** How should the alleged inconsistencies between the complainant’s testimony and the letter she wrote be interpreted within the context of the evidence? Did the trial court err in admitting or utilizing this evidence? ([paras 4, 12-17])
3. **Standard of Proof:** Did the prosecution meet the requisite standard of proof "beyond a reasonable doubt" in its case against the appellant? ([para 18])

### R – Rules 
The applicable legal principles can be drawn from:

1. The necessity for corroboration in sexual assault cases and the guidelines surrounding evidence about the timing and context of complaints. Specifically, the absence of timely reporting and its impact on the credibility of the complainant is well established in case law, illustrated by precedents such as *R v Gannon* and *S v K* ([para 5]).
2. The standard of proof in criminal trials, which asserts that the accused is presumed innocent until proven guilty beyond a reasonable doubt. This is pivotal in evaluating the sufficiency of the evidence presented ([para 18]).
3. Principles regarding the admissibility of evidence, particularly concerning hearsay, self-incrimination, and prior consistent statements, must be adhered to in ensuring that the evidence presented is legally acceptable ([paras 5-6, 12-15]).

### A – Application 
In assessing the case, the court scrutinized the credibility of the complainant's testimony against the backdrop of her delayed report, which was argued to undermine the reliability of her allegations. The inconsistencies between her court testimony and the content of the letter she wrote to her mother were critically evaluated. The court noted that the letter did not explicitly mention rape, raising questions about her intent and the nature of the incident ([paras 12-15]).

Furthermore, the trial court’s acceptance of emotional display as a marker of truthfulness was challenged, emphasizing that an emotional response does not guarantee veracity. The court opined that while the complainant’s delayed response could be reasonably explained by her age and the alleged threats, it also raised substantial questions about the dependability of her narrative ([paras 14-16]).

Additionally, the court explored the possibility of a motive for false accusations suggested by the appellant, which though speculative, could not be entirely dismissed. The state bore the burden of proving the appellant's guilt beyond a reasonable doubt but failed to sufficiently establish credibility and reliability in the complainant's case as the evidence presented provided grounds for reasonable doubt ([paras 17-18]).

### C – Conclusion 
The court ultimately concluded that the state did not meet the burden of proof required to uphold the appellant's conviction. The discrepancies in the complainant’s accounts, her delayed reporting, and the lack of corroborative evidence led to the decision that the appeal against his conviction should succeed. Hence, the court set aside the appellant's conviction for rape, reinforcing the importance of credible evidence and the strict standards that govern criminal proceedings ([paras 18-18]). The outcome underscores the judicial caution necessary in cases hinging solely on the credibility of a single witness, particularly in serious accusations such as rape.
