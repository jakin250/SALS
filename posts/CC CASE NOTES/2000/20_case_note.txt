Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2000

# FIRAC Case Note: Levy v Glynos & Jammine [CCT 29/00]

## F - Facts
The applicant, Delene Johanna Levy, lodged a notice of motion before the Constitutional Court of South Africa seeking to set aside an order dated 15 August 2000. This order had denied her leave to appeal against a decision from the Supreme Court of Appeal. Levy's application included a request to address the Court regarding her application for leave to appeal. The Court, however, found that her application was insupportable in both procedural and substantive aspects ([1]).

The previous ruling by the full Court had evaluated her application without oral or written argument beyond the submissions she made. The Court came to a definitive conclusion that there was no reasonable prospect of success for an appeal, thus dismissing her application with finality. The essence of this ruling emphasized the significance of finality in litigation, underscoring that reopening such matters should occur only under exceptional circumstances and compelling reasons ([2]).

Levy’s subsequent grounds for revocation of the previous order, articulated through her founding affidavit and additional correspondence, were deemed neither exceptional nor sufficiently compelling by the Court. The ruling highlighted that despite her sincere belief in the merits of her case, there was no reasonable basis for considering the prior denial of her leave to appeal incorrect ([3]).

## I - Issues
The principal legal issues before the Court revolved around whether:
1. The previous order denying leave to appeal could be set aside on the basis that the applicant's grounds were exceptional or compelling enough to warrant reexamination ([2]);
2. The right to address the Court being a procedural matter could influence the outcome of her application ([1]).

## R - Rules
The Constitutional Court Rules, specifically Rule 18, govern the procedures for applications for leave to appeal. Rule 18(1)(b) states that such applications may be considered summarily without oral or written argument beyond what is presented in the application itself. Rule 18(10) permits the reopening of a final and definitive order only under exceptional circumstances ([2]).

The established legal precedent stresses the importance of finality in judicial determinations, underpinning the public interest in the resolution of disputes and the closure provided to the parties involved ([2]). The Court has previously ruled that the mere belief in the merit of a case does not suffice to meet the threshold of "exceptional circumstances".

## A - Application
In considering the application, the Court adhered strictly to its procedural rules. It emphasized that the request to reopen the matter did not meet the established threshold for compelling reasons. The Court indicated that the initial ruling had been thorough, with the previous judges having concluded that the applicant's arguments were insufficient to merit an appeal.

The Court's decision to refuse the application summarily reflected a commitment to procedural integrity and finality. The applicant's sincere yet unsubstantiated beliefs did not constitute the requisite compelling reason for overturning a judgment that had already been pronounced conclusively in regard to whether her appeal held merit ([3]).

Furthermore, the Court acknowledged the rights of the respondents to move forward with their lives, underscoring the importance of finality in litigation not only for the parties but for societal interests. Thus, in refusing to allow Levy to address the Court, the emphasis was placed on procedural efficiency and the avoidance of unnecessary litigation ([2], [3]).

## C - Conclusion
The Constitutional Court dismissed Levy's application to set aside the prior order denying her leave to appeal, highlighting that her claims did not reach the threshold of being exceptional or compelling ([4]). The Court reiterated its commitment to finality in litigation, emphasizing the delicate balance between individual claims and the broader public interest in legal certainty. The ruling clarifies the stringent criteria required to challenge final orders, establishing a precedent for future cases regarding the reopening of litigated matters. The Court’s decision also implied a caution for litigants regarding the risks associated with further applications that lack substantive merit, thereby reinforcing the judicial principle that finality should prevail in legal proceedings for the benefit of all parties involved ([3], [4]).
