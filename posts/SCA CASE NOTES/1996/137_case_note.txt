Case Note: In the Supreme Court of South Africa
Source Folder: 1996

# Case Note: BISMILLAH (VENDA) (PROPRIETARY) LTD v MAUNGEDZO ANDRIES NETSHITUNI [1996] ZASCA 90 

## FIRAC

### F – Facts
The appellant, BISMILLAH (VENDA) (PROPRIETARY) LTD, initiated negotiations in March 1991 with the respondent, Maungedzo Andries Netshituni, for the purchase of property identified as Stand 472, Thohoyandou. The parties reached an oral agreement where the agreed purchase price for the property was set at R42,000. However, the respondent preferred not to receive cash; instead, he sought to offset the payment against a passenger bus the appellant was to sell.

Due to the appellant not having a suitable bus, the respondent purchased a bus from a third party for R55,000. To formalize the transaction, a deed of sale was created, which falsely acknowledged that the purchase price of the stand had been paid in full, despite the actual payment including the arrangement regarding the bus.

Subsequently, the respondent borrowed R2,000 from the appellant to put the bus through a roadworthy test. Later, he relinquished possession of the bus and verbally agreed that the appellant would take the bus back, and the sale of the stand would proceed with the balance of R40,000 being payable by the end of August 1991. The appellant made the payment, but the respondent refused to transfer the stand. This led the appellant to seek a court order for occupation and transfer of the stand.

In his plea, the respondent claimed he had been fraudulently induced to sign the deed of sale under the belief that it pertained to a "half bus" and that the R40,000 payment was actually a loan to refund a former purchaser, Lukhalimana. These allegations were not further pursued during the trial, with the respondent later asserting that the sale of the stand was merely a security arrangement for the bus purchase.

At the close of the appellant's case, the respondent applied for absolution from the instance. The court granted this application, which prompted the appeal to the Supreme Court of South Africa ([paras 1–10]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues presented in this case are:
1. Whether the respondent’s application for absolution from the instance was justified given the evidence produced by the appellant.
2. The nature of the agreement between the parties—whether it constituted a sale of the stand or a mere security for the bus purchase.
3. Whether the court improperly assessed the evidence regarding the existence of a valid contract.

### R – Rules
Key legal principles and precedents relevant to this case include:
- The standard for granting absolution from the instance, which requires a court to evaluate whether there is any evidence before it that, if accepted, would support a finding in the plaintiff's favor ([para 8]).
- The principle of consensus as the cornerstone of contract formation in South African law. The parties' shared intentions at the time of contract are paramount, irrespective of how the agreement is labelled ([para 8]).
- Established precedents such as:
  - **Du Plessis v Van Deventer** 1960 (2) SA 544 (A),
  - **Neethling v Klopper en Andere** 1967 (4) SA 459 (A),
  - **Venter v Birchholtz** 1972 (1) SA 276 (A), which affirm the validity of oral agreements and the necessity of performing substantive obligations under a contract.

### A – Application
The Supreme Court, presided over by Olivier JA, assessed the evidence provided by the appellant, which asserted that the agreement detailed in the deed of sale reflected a valid exchange. The crucial element was whether the acknowledgment of receipt of the purchase price sufficed under the circumstances, despite subsequent claims of misrepresentation by the respondent. The court determined that the appellant had properly established its claim based on the contract, asserting that the delivery of the bus constituted performance consistent with the deed of sale.

The ruling examined whether the lower court, when granting absolution, overlooked essential facts that illustrated a prima facie case for the appellant. Olivier JA criticized the lower court’s reasoning, asserting that the evidence demonstrated an agreement and transfer of the stand as intended, fundamentally contradicting the respondent’s claims of misrepresentation. The court firmly held that the lack of evidence from the respondent substantiated the appellant’s assertions, thereby justifying the refusal of absolution from the instance.

Additionally, the argument concerning the alleged non-joinder of Lukhalimana was refuted as lacking substance, given the absence of a material deed or evidence substantiating his claim as a prior owner ([paras 7–10]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court ultimately ruled in favor of the appellant, overturning the lower court's decision and concluding that absolution from the instance was improperly granted. This reassertion of the appellant’s right to occupy and demand transfer of the property reflects the principle that parties are bound by their agreements as understood based on their intentions and performance, rather than how they later frame their dispute.

The court's judgment emphasized the importance of contractual clarity and upheld the notion that evidence must align with acknowledged agreements, thereby carrying significant implications for future contract law practice. The appeal was successful, with costs awarded in favor of the appellant ([para 10]).
