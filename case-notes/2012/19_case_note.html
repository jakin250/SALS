South African Transport and Allied Workers Union v. Lebogang Michael Moloto N.O. [2012] ZACC 19

The dismissed strikers were former employees of Equity Aviation Services (Pty) Ltd, which had 1157 employees, 725 of whom were SATAWU members.

Constitutional Court 2012 [2012] ZACC 19

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts:
This case arises from a dispute concerning the interpretation of section 64(1)(b) of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (the Act). The first applicant, South African Transport and Allied Workers Union (SATAWU), along with 63 co-applicants (the dismissed strikers), sought leave to appeal against the ruling of the Supreme Court of Appeal, which found that the dismissal of the strikers was lawful due to the absence of a personal strike notice issued by them ([1]–[2]).

The dismissed strikers were former employees of Equity Aviation Services (Pty) Ltd, which had 1157 employees, 725 of whom were SATAWU members. On December 15, 2003, SATAWU issued a strike notice to Equity, which explicitly mentioned only its members. None of the dismissed strikers, who were not SATAWU members, issued a separate notice. The strike commenced on December 18, 2003, and lasted until April 15, 2004. After repeated warnings to return to work, the strikers were dismissed for unauthorised absence ([3]–[4]).

Initially, the Labour Court ruled in favor of the dismissed strikers, stating their participation was protected under SATAWU's notice. However, the Labour Appeal Court, followed by the Supreme Court of Appeal, reversed this ruling, determining that the dismissed strikers were required to issue a personal strike notice for their actions to be legally protected ([5]–[8]).

I – Issues:
The primary legal issue presented in the case is whether the interpretation of section 64(1)(b) of the Act mandates every employee intending to participate in strike action to provide personal notice to their employer, and if the absence of such notice invalidates their participation in the strike ([9]–[10]). The case also raised constitutional questions regarding the right to strike as guaranteed in section 23 of the Constitution, particularly about whether requiring individual notice undermines this right ([11]).

R – Rules:
The relevant legal rules stem from several sources:

  1. Constitutional Provisions: Section 23(2)(c) of the South African Constitution grants every worker the right to strike without any express limitation ([10]).
  2. Labour Relations Act: Section 64(1)(b) outlines the stipulation that at least 48 hours’ written notice must be given to the employer for the strike to be protected. The Act does not explicitly state who must provide this notice, which has led to different interpretations ([15]–[16]).
  3. Precedents: Previous court rulings, including Ceramic Industries ([23]), elucidate that the purpose of strike notices is to preserve orderly collective bargaining and provide employers the necessary time to prepare for impending strikes.

A – Application:
The Constitutional Court approached the interpretation of section 64(1)(b) within the framework of the Act's primary objectives and the constitutional right to strike. It found merit in the argument that the notice requirement is not an undue limitation but rather a procedural necessity to allow employers to prepare adequately for strikes ([19]–[22]).

The court acknowledged various precedents that emphasized a purposive approach to interpreting provisions of the Act. The understanding emerged that although the legislation does provide for workers' rights, it simultaneously aims to maintain order during collective bargaining processes ([24]–[25]). The court assessed both the practical implications of requiring notice from every striking employee and the inherent disruptions that strikes cause, ultimately finding that requiring a separate notice undermined the objectives of both the Act and the Constitution ([29]–[33]).

C – Conclusion:
In its judgment, the Constitutional Court upheld the interpretation that section 64(1)(b) does require individual notice for striking employees, thereby affirming the Supreme Court of Appeal's ruling that the dismissed strikers' actions were unlawful. This decision underscored that the absence of personal notice invalidated their participation in the strike and consequently deemed their dismissal not automatically unfair ([38]) ([39]).

The court noted the importance of a clear and structured approach to interpreting the right to strike under the Act as essential for maintaining labor stability and protecting the interests of all parties involved. The ruling introduces significant implications for future industrial action by clarifying the necessity of compliance with notice provisions to ensure lawful participation in strikes ([40]).
