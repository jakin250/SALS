Supreme Court of Appeal

Rantlai v The State (1178/2016) [2017] ZASCA 106

The appellant, Karabo Rantlai, was convicted in the Regional Court at Orlando on three counts of robbery with aggravating circumstances, read with s 51(2)(a) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, as amended by the Criminal Law (Sentencing) Amendment Act 38 of 2007.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 106

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The appellant, Karabo Rantlai, was convicted in the Regional Court at Orlando on three counts of robbery with aggravating circumstances, read with s 51(2)(a) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, as amended by the Criminal Law (Sentencing) Amendment Act 38 of 2007. Additionally, he was convicted on one count of unlawful possession of a semi-automatic firearm and one count of unlawful possession of ammunition ([1]).

The trial court sentenced Rantlai to a composite sentence of 20 years’ imprisonment for the three robbery counts, labeling it a globular sentence, while separately imposing a sentence for his firearm and ammunition convictions ([1]). After his application for leave to appeal was initially denied, the Gauteng Local Division permitted an appeal regarding only the conviction. The appeal was partially successful: the conviction on count 1 was overturned, but the composite sentence of 20 years remained unchanged ([2]–[3]). Rantlai then petitioned the Supreme Court of Appeal for special leave to appeal against the original sentence ([3]).

I – Issues

The central issues in this case were two-fold:

  1. Whether the regional magistrate failed to consider substantial and compelling circumstances that would justify a departure from the minimum prescribed sentence of 15 years as required under s 51(3) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 ([4]).
  2. Whether the globular sentence imposed for the robbery counts was appropriate, especially after one count was set aside on appeal, effectively rendering the remaining sentence unworkable ([5]).

R – Rules

The primary legal authorities relevant to this case come from the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, particularly sections pertaining to minimum sentences for serious crimes, notably s 51(2)(a) and s 51(3), which allows for judicial discretion in instances of compelling circumstances ([1], [4]).

In guiding the approach to globular sentences, the court referred to established precedents, such as S v Young 1977 (1) SA 602 (A), emphasizing the discretionary nature of sentencing while expressing concerns regarding the complexity and potential injustice arising from such composite sentences ([9]–[10]). The judgments in S v Malgas 2001 (1) SACR 469 (SCA) and Director of Public Prosecutions, Transvaal v Phillips [2011] ZASCA 192 were also pivotal in framing the considerations surrounding both minimum sentencing and the dangers of globular sentencing ([8], [9]).

A – Application

In evaluating the facts and the arguments presented, the court was unpersuaded that the factors offered by the appellant's defense—youth, absence of violence during the robberies, the minor nature of the property taken, and time served awaiting trial—qualified as "substantial and compelling." Thus, the court upheld the prescribed sentences for the two robbery counts ([8]). However, the court recognized that the globular sentence of 20 years was impractical, particularly after the conviction on count 1 was overturned. It noted that the trial court had erred in failing to adjust the sentence following the successful appeal regarding one count ([12]–[13]).

The appeal court criticized the trial court's choice of a globular sentence, which can obscure the individual nature of offenses and complicate reassessment when a conviction is set aside. This approach potentially denies the appellant the benefits of acquittal on an individual count since the sentences were treated collectively ([14]–[15]).

Both parties concluded that a practical resolution involved setting aside the globular sentence and sentencing the appellant afresh for the remaining counts, ensuring the sentences imposed would run concurrently as delineated in s 280(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act ([17]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal against the sentence in respect of counts 2 and 3 while resetting the original composite sentence. The court replaced the prior sentence with separate terms of 15 years each for the remaining robbery counts, subsequently ordered to run concurrently to allow for an effective sentence of 20 years in total ([18]).

This ruling reaffirms the necessity for thorough judicial oversight in sentencing, especially in cases involving multiple counts. It underscores the importance of flexibility in interpreting sentencing laws to deliver just outcomes, reflecting an accused’s circumstances and the legal principles governing such prosecutions. The decision is significant in emphasizing the court's reluctance to uphold globular sentences as a standard practice, advocating instead for clarity and precision in applying the law to ensure fairness in sentencing outcomes.
