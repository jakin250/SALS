Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

## Case Note: Bamba v S (20089/14) [2014] ZASCA 219

### F – Facts

This case arises from the appeal of Thembani Bamba (the appellant), who was initially convicted of murder by the Wynberg Regional Court and sentenced to ten years’ imprisonment ([1]). The State alleged that on 20 January 2007, Bamba shot and killed Ludwe Golotile, a 23-year-old man, while police were conducting a crime prevention operation in Nyanga, an informal settlement. Bamba, represented by counsel, pleaded not guilty during the trial ([1]).

Two police officers, Constable Khunjulwa Koboni and Constable Lulamile Galela, were involved in the operation and encountered a group of people who scattered upon seeing the police ([2]). During this encounter, a gunshot was heard, and while Bamba claimed not to have fired the shot, he later found a man hiding in the shacks but did not search him. Following the incident, reports indicated that police had shot Golotile ([2]–[5]). 

The officer responsible for gathering evidence, Nkosiyedwa Booi, collected ballistic evidence, including a spent bullet and a cartridge case, allegedly linking them to Bamba's firearm. However, procedural inconsistencies in the handling of the evidence were noted, particularly a lack of documentation confirming the chain of custody of these exhibits ([7]–[9]). 

Ultimately, the trial court relied on the ballistic evidence to convict Bamba, asserting he was responsible for the fatal shot due to the matches found between the recovered items and Bamba’s firearm ([12]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issue was whether the State had established a clear and reliable connection between the ballistic evidence and the scene of the crime to prove beyond a reasonable doubt that Bamba had committed the murder ([10]–[12]). This included questions surrounding the adequacy of the investigative processes and the integrity of the evidence presented at trial. 

A secondary issue was the appropriateness of the trial court’s standards for drawing inferences from the circumstantial evidence available ([13]).

### R – Rules

The following principles underpin the legal framework applicable to this case:

1. **Standard of Proof in Criminal Trials**: The prosecution must prove its case beyond a reasonable doubt, as established in *S v Shackell* [2001] (4) All SA 279 (A) ([15]).
   
2. **Chain of Custody Requirement**: The prosecution has a duty to ensure that the recovery, storage, and conveyance of evidentiary items follow strict protocols, thereby guaranteeing their reliability and relevance ([17]).

3. **Inference Drawing**: According to *R v Blom* [1939] AD 188, any inference drawn must conform to the established facts, and no reasonable alternative inferences should exist ([13]).

### A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized the lower court’s reliance on the ballistic evidence, particularly focusing on the discrepancies in the management of the exhibits ([9]–[10]). It was noted that Booi, the investigative officer, could not confirm vital details regarding the handling of evidence; a critical failure considering the State's need to demonstrate strong links between the evidence and the accused ([10]).

The court emphasized that the lack of direct eyewitness testimony further compounded the difficulty in establishing guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. The only evidence against the appellant—ballistic findings—was significantly undermined by Booi's inability to affirm the chain of custody and the inconsistencies concerning the evidence bags and references used for the ballistic testing ([7],[10]). 

The trial court had erred in failing to recognize that the uncertainty surrounding the ballistic evidence meant that other reasonable inferences (i.e., that a different officer might have fired the shot) could exist, thereby imposing a doubt on Bamba's guilt ([14]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately found that the prosecution had failed to meet its burden of proof due to the inconsistencies in the evidence and the errors made by the trial court regarding the reliability of the ballistic evidence. The court determined that the only reasonable conclusion was that the evidence might not definitively link Bamba to the crime ([15]). 

Thus, Bamba's conviction was overturned, and he was found not guilty and discharged. This decision underscores the necessity for law enforcement to adhere strictly to evidentiary procedures and highlights the critical importance of the prosecution's burden in criminal cases ([17]–[19]). The implications of this case extend to the protocols followed in police investigations, reinforcing the requirement for meticulousness in the handling of forensic evidence to uphold judicial integrity.
