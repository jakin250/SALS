Source Folder: 2007

# FIRAC Case Note: Schwartz NO v Pike [2007] SCA 106 (RSA)

## F – Facts

The matter arose from the death of Michelle Ann Pennels on 7 December 2002. She was a member of a close corporation, Lore Marketing 46 CC, alongside the first respondent, S E Pike, and the second respondent, A Van der Merwe ([1]). Following her death, I Schwartz, the appointed executor of her estate, sought to enforce the provisions of a written Association Agreement dated 5 March 2001 that governed the disposal of a deceased member's interest in the corporation ([3]).

The Association Agreement stipulated that if a member dies, the remaining members and the executor must negotiate the value of the deceased member's interest within 30 days ([4]). If no agreement is reached, a chartered accountant would be appointed, either jointly or by the South African Institute of Chartered Accountants ([4]). Following a failed attempt by the executor to negotiate an agreement on the value of Pennels’ interest, Schwartz proceeded to appoint Lucro Auditing to conduct a valuation without obtaining the required consent from Pike and Van der Merwe, which led to further disputes ([8]–[10]).

The valuation determined by Lucro Auditing was R2 107 424,00, which Schwartz later demanded from Pike and Van der Merwe ([8]). Both respondents denied having agreed to appoint an accountant as per the contract's stipulations, arguing that Schwartz had acted prematurely in seeking a valuation without proper consultation ([9]–[14]).

Procedurally, Bredenkamp AJ of the High Court dismissed Schwartz's application, holding that he failed to follow the proper procedures outlined in the Association Agreement ([2]). Schwartz subsequently appealed the decision to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([2]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal questions revolved around:

1. Whether the appellant, as executor, complied with the procedural requirements outlined in Clause 16 of the Association Agreement regarding the appointment of an accountant ([14]–[16]).
2. The proper interpretation of the Association Agreement, particularly concerning the necessity for negotiation and consensus before moving to appoint an accountant or seeking external intervention ([15]–[18]).

## R – Rules

The legal principles at play include:

1. **Contract Law and Interpretation**: The intention of the parties, as expressed in their contractual language, governs interpretation. The court emphasizes a contextual approach while recognizing the four-stage process laid out in Clause 16 of the Agreement ([15]–[17]).
2. **Obligation to Negotiate**: Parties are bound to attempt negotiations in good faith before moving to other mechanisms for dispute resolution. The courts have previously reinforced that procedural compliance is vital for claims arising from contractual obligations ([17]).

Relevant precedents include:
- **Premier, Free State, and Others v Firechem Free State (Pty) Ltd** 2000 (4) SA 413 (SCA) at 431G-H, outlining the principle that agreements to agree may not be enforceable ([17]).
- **Southernport Developments (Pty) Ltd v Transnet Ltd** 2005 (2) SA 202 (SCA) at 208D, which highlights the need for the parties to engage in the dispute resolution process they have agreed upon ([17]).

## A – Application

The court's analysis revolved around whether Schwartz provided Pike and Van der Merwe with an opportunity to reach consensus on the accountant’s appointment. The interpretation of Clause 16 revealed that the parties intended to pursue a structured process consisting of negotiation, mutual agreement on appointee, and only then, external appointment if necessary. This indicated a cooperative spirit that Schwartz did not uphold ([16]).

The judgment delineated that while the absence of an agreement on valuation does not automatically imply an inability to agree, Schwartz failed to allow the respondents the opportunity to negotiate over the appointment of a chartered accountant as required under Clause 16. The court viewed that he did not adequately pursue the specified steps prior to resorting to appointing an accountant unilaterally ([16]–[18]).

Bredenkamp AJ's prior dismissal of Schwartz’s application was affirmed, as the court concurred that the appellant's premature action in seeking a valuation without proper adherence to the stipulated negotiation process resulted in the appeal being dismissed ([19]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that Schwartz failed to comply with the procedural stipulations outlined in the Association Agreement concerning the appointment of a chartered accountant for the valuation of the deceased member’s interest. The decision emphasized the importance of contractual compliance and cooperation among parties in resolving disputes. Consequently, the appeal was dismissed with costs, reinforcing that contractual obligations, particularly those requiring negotiation, must be observed to facilitate fair resolution ([19]). 

This case illustrates the critical importance of adhering to agreed contractual processes and the implications for executors and members of close corporations regarding the disposal of interests upon a member's death.
