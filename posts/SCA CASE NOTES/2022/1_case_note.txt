Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: Masinga and Others v Chief of the South African National Defence Force and Others

### Citation
Masinga and Others v Chief of the South African National Defence Force and Others (Case no 51/2021) [2022] ZASCA 1.

### Coram
Petse AP and Makgoka, Schippers, Nicholls, and Mabindla-Boqwana JJA.

### F – Facts ([paras 1–20])
The appellants—35 officers from the South African Military Health Service (SAMHS)—had been selected for military medical training in Cuba under a memorandum of understanding (MOU) between the South African Department of Defence (DoD) and the Cuban military. The group included 26 officers who commenced their studies in Cuba in 2017, and an additional 9 officers who started in 2018. 

On 26 March 2019, the appellants were officially informed of their dismissal from the South African National Defence Force (SANDF) for refusing to attend classes following an order from their commanding officer. Their dismissal was executed under section 59(3) of the Defence Act 42 of 2002, which stipulates that SANDF members absenting themselves from duty for over 30 days without permission should be considered dismissed.

The appellants refused to attend classes between 11 February and 20 March 2019, claiming the Inter Arms School was not accredited. They raised several complaints regarding their training conditions and the credibility of the academic program. The respondents contended that the appellants' dismissal was lawful, given their clear refusal to follow orders and their absence from classes.

The Gauteng Division of the High Court had initially ruled in favor of the appellants, declaring their dismissal unlawful on the grounds that procedural requirements had not been met, namely that a board of inquiry should have been convened prior to dismissal. 

### I – Issues ([paras 25–24])
Two primary issues arose:
1. **Was there a prior decision to dismiss the appellants regardless of their submissions?** The appellants claimed they were subjected to an ad hoc disciplinary process, which ultimately predetermined their dismissal.
2. **Were the jurisdictional requirements of section 59(3) of the Defence Act satisfied, thereby rendering the dismissal lawful?** This involved determining if the appellants had truly absented themselves from duty for the requisite duration and without permission. 

### R – Rules ([paras 31–49])
**Statutory Reference:** 
- **Defence Act 42 of 2002, Section 59(3)** mandates that SANDF members absenting themselves from official duty for longer than 30 days are to be deemed dismissed, based on misconduct.

**Judicial Precedents:**
- In **Louw**: A deemed discharge occurs by operation of law without necessitating a formal hearing.
- **Grootboom** established that the constitutional right to fair labour practices does not apply to circumstances governed by operational law.
- **Maswanganyi** upheld the principle that dismissal via section 59(3) occurs automatically without a hearing when the conditions of absence from duty are met.

### A – Application ([paras 30–49])
The court concluded that the appellants had indeed been absent from their official duties as defined in section 59(3), thereby satisfying all jurisdictional requirements for dismissal:
1. **Absence from Duty**: The appellants’ refusal to attend their required classes constituted voluntary absence, as they openly defied direct orders from their commanding officer.
2. **No Permission Granted**: Their actions reflected a clear absence without permission, which is corroborated by documented attempts made by their superiors to rectify their non-compliance. 

The notion that their prior presence at the institution negated their absence was rejected; being physically present does not equate to fulfilling military duties. Furthermore, the court found that the appellants’ dismissal was not contingent on any unfulfilled procedural requirements, as they were dismissed by operation of law as soon as the criteria in section 59(3) were met.

### C – Conclusion ([paras 50–52])
The Supreme Court of Appeal reversed the lower court's ruling and confirmed that the appellants’ dismissal was both lawful and effective as per section 59(3) of the Defence Act. The determination was based on the uncontested facts showing non-compliance with orders, satisfying the statutory conditions for dismissal. The appeal led to the costs order being reassessed, favoring the appellants regarding previous costs awarded against them.

**Holding**: The court affirmed that procedural niceties prior to the dismissal were unnecessary since the dismissal was enacted through the operation of law, thus safeguarding military discipline essential for the SANDF's function. The judgment emphasized the non-applicability of the right to a hearing under such legislative frameworks.

---

### Key Implications
This ruling underscores the principle that military discipline and statutory frameworks take precedence, particularly regarding dismissals enacted by operation of law in military contexts. It clarifies the delineation of rights and procedures, establishing that procedural protections may not apply in the face of categorical legislative provisions specifically designed to maintain order within military ranks.
