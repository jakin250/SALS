Supreme Court of Appeal

Fakude v The State (852/10) [2011] ZASCA 183

The appellants, Langa Fakude and Sthembiso Mthethwa, were arrested on 17 January 2000 and subsequently charged with robbery with aggravating circumstances.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2011 [2011] ZASCA 183

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The appellants, Langa Fakude and Sthembiso Mthethwa, were arrested on 17 January 2000 and subsequently charged with robbery with aggravating circumstances. Post-conviction on 1 November 2000, they were sentenced by the Pinetown Regional Court to 20 years and 25 years of imprisonment respectively. Both appellants appealed their convictions and sentences, but on 17 November 2005, the KwaZulu-Natal High Court dismissed their appeal, confirming both the convictions and the sentences ([paras 1–2]).

On 24 February 2010, the High Court granted the appellants leave to appeal, limited to the sentence imposed. The incident leading to their conviction occurred on 14 January 2000, when the complainant, Mr Daharaj Hurdial, was hijacked at gunpoint while entering his garage in Pinetown. Two armed assailants, later identified as the appellants, stole Hurdial's vehicle, wallet, cellular phone, and other personal items, amounting to a significant financial loss ([paras 1-3]).

In mitigation of their sentences, their respective personal circumstances were brought forward. The first appellant had a young child and was employed as a taxi driver earning R250 per week, while the second appellant, also with children, was self-employed with a monthly income of R850. Both had been in custody since their arrest ([paras 4–5]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues in this appeal were whether:

  1. The trial court misdirected itself by imposing sentences in excess of its jurisdiction, violating Section 92(1)(a) of the Magistrates’ Court Act 32 of 1944, which limits the ordinary penal jurisdiction of the Regional Court to a maximum of 15 years.
  2. The High Court erred in confirming these sentences ([paras 6–10]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal rules and principles revolved around appropriate sentencing guidelines, particularly the considerations of both aggravating and mitigating factors in sentencing. In S v Ingram 1995 (1) SACR 1 (A), Smalberger JA emphasized that sentencing must consider the triad of crime, the criminal, and the interests of society, necessitating a balance between harshness and leniency in punishment ([paras 7–9]). Furthermore, the decision in S v Matyityi 2011 (1) SACR 40 SCA highlighted that individuals above 18 years need to provide substantial evidence of immaturity to garner a successful mitigating argument ([para 10]).

A – Application

In its judgment, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that the trial court indeed misdirected itself by exceeding its jurisdictional limits in sentencing the appellants to 20 and 25 years. Counsel for the State conceded that 15 years’ imprisonment would be appropriate for both appellants, aligning with legislative provisions ([para 6]). The court took into account the nature of the crime, noting the serious implications of armed robbery, which instills fear within communities ([paras 8–12]).

The appellants’ personal background was discussed. The conditions of their lives and earlier criminal records were assessed, but the court ultimately found that the aggravating factors significantly outweighed the mitigating factors. Specifically, while the appellants had families and had been in custody for a substantial duration before sentencing, the absence of courage to testify under oath about their circumstances detracted from their cases ([paras 10–11]).

The judgment reiterated the gravity of the crime, particularly given the presence of firearms and the potential for injury. Additional aggravating factors considered were the psychological effects on the victims, the lack of remorse shown by the appellants, and indications of a systemic engagement in hijacking ([paras 11–12]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal allowed the appeal in part. The court set aside the previous sentences imposed and replaced them with a uniform sentence of 15 years imprisonment for each appellant. Their conviction remained intact due to the nature of the crime committed ([paras 13]). This decision reaffirmed the need for judicial adherence to sentencing limits defined by law and the necessity of balancing justice for the victims with equitable punishment for the offenders. The ruling underscores the importance of proper procedural adherence in sentencing, which enhances the overall integrity of the judicial process ([paras 6-13]).

In light of this case, the implications for sentencing practices in South Africa are significant, advocating for a careful assessment of jurisdiction limits coupled with the need for a balanced consideration of all mitigating and aggravating circumstances in criminal matters.
