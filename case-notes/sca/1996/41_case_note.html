Supreme Court of Appeal

The Commissioner for Inland Revenue v Rolf-Stephan Arthur Wilhelm Nussbaum

Case Number: 337/94 Court: Supreme Court of South Africa (Appellate Division) Date of Hearing: 1 March 1996 Date of Judgment: 29 March 1996 Coram: Corbett CJ, Nestadt JA, Nienaber JA, Howie JA, Zulman AJA

Supreme Court of Appeal 1996

Case Number: 337/94
Court: Supreme Court of South Africa (Appellate Division)
Date of Hearing: 1 March 1996
Date of Judgment: 29 March 1996
Coram: Corbett CJ, Nestadt JA, Nienaber JA, Howie JA, Zulman AJA

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The respondent, Rolf-Stephan Arthur Wilhelm Nussbaum, is a retired schoolteacher who inherited shares in 1946. Over the years, he expanded his portfolio through reinvestment of income, acquiring shares by rights issues and through additional inheritances ([paras 2-3]). During the tax years 1982, 1983, and 1984, the respondent sold numerous shares, resulting in significant profits, which led the appellant, the Commissioner for Inland Revenue, to issue additional assessments, categorizing the proceeds as taxable income. The respondent objected, claiming the proceeds were capital in nature and thus not subject to income tax.

His objections were initially dismissed by a Special Court, followed by a successful appeal to the Full Bench of the Cape of Good Hope Provincial Division, which reversed the Special Court's findings ([paras 4-5]). The appellant sought leave to appeal, which was granted by the Supreme Court, resulting in the present case ([paras 4-5]).

The respondent testified that throughout his investment career, his primary objective was generating dividend income. He maintained a meticulous approach, monitoring his portfolio vigilantly. However, during the mentioned tax years, his strategy evolved as he aimed to liquidate shares to raise cash for anticipated medical expenses and to secure a home ([paras 6-9]). This resulted in net disinvestment from the share market amounting to R307,000 during these years ([para 6]).

I – Issues

The primary legal question in this case centers on whether the sale of shares during the years in question constituted the realization of capital assets or the disposal of trading stock in the course of conducting a business ([para 16]). More specifically, the examination resolves around whether the respondent had a secondary profit-making purpose alongside his primary investment goal, which would constitute a taxable event ([para 15]).

R – Rules

The legal framework for resolving this case is deeply rooted in precedent concerning the distinction between capital gains and income. The relevant cases, including Elandsaeuwel Farming (Edms) Bpk v Sekretaris van Binaelandse Inkomste 1976 (1) SA 101 (A) and Commissioner of Taxes v Booysens Estates Ltd 1978 (A) 576, provide a basis for analyzing whether the respondent's actions fell within the spectrum of capital investment or business activity ([paras 16-18]).

The concept of dual-purpose investments was key to the appellant's argument, stipulating that if a taxpayer’s activities have both a primary and secondary purpose—with the latter aiming to generate profit—it can lead to taxation of gains ([paras 16-19]).

A – Application

The court evaluated the respondent's investment behavior and motivations. While he initially demonstrated a clear focus on dividend yield, the frequency and profitability of his share sales suggested a more complex investment strategy, especially during the concerned tax years ([paras 20-24]). This was compounded by substantial profits realized from sales, despite his claims that sales were motivated by poor dividend performance ([paras 22-23]).

The Court explored whether the respondent maintained a singular investment purpose or if, during the contested years, he shifted to a secondary objective of profit generation. The evidence highlighted that while he retained some shares, his overall portfolio management in pursuit of profit was deliberate and consistent ([paras 25-27]). The frequency of transactions and the profitable outcomes unsupported by claims of incidental income implied a systematic approach consistent with profit-making schemes and heightened the onus on the respondent to provide clear evidence to the contrary ([paras 21-27]).

Ultimately, the Court rejected the argument that profits from share sales were merely incidental or fortuitous, asserting that the respondent exercised considerable control over his portfolio indicative of a profit-oriented approach ([para 28]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court concluded that the profits from the respondent's share disposals were indeed not incidental but reflective of a secondary profit-making purpose alongside his primary investment aims. Consequently, the Court found that the respondent failed to discharge his burden of proving that these profits were capital in nature, thus affirming the tax assessments made by the appellant ([paras 28-29]).

The appeal was allowed, setting aside the Full Bench's order and dismissing the respondent's appeal, requiring him to bear the costs, inclusive of the costs of employing two counsel. This conclusion has significant implications for individuals engaged in investment activities, clarifying the contributory nature of investment strategies towards taxation obligations regarding capital gains in South Africa.
