Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# Case Note: EThekwini Municipality v Breakers Share Block Limited (42/2016) [2016] ZASCA 140 

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts 
The case arose from the interpretation of two lease agreements between the eThekwini Municipality (Appellant) and Breakers Share Block Limited (Respondent). The lease agreements originated with Sycol Properties (Pty) Ltd in 1975 and 1981, involving the leasing of property described as Lot 1066, Umhlanga Rocks Township. The agreements involved provisions for rental calculates based on the "floor area" of buildings on the leased property, with revisions every ten years ([1]–[2]).

In 2008, the Appellant and Respondent began discussions regarding the sale of the property, which also led to a disagreement over how to calculate the rental based on actual measurements of the floor area. The Respondent asserted that the rental should be calculated based on a significantly smaller floor area (12,079.10 m²) than what had been used previously (15,594.34 m²). The Respondent initiated legal proceedings, seeking a recalculation of rental and a refund for overpayments since September 2013 ([3]).

After a series of legal proceedings, the KwaZulu-Natal Local Division ruled in favor of the Respondent, prompting the Appellant to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal. The crux of the litigation revolved around the interpretation of clause 6(b) in the lease agreements that was amended in 1981, which stated that the rental should be based on the "floor area" calculated in accordance with the Umhlanga Town Planning Scheme No. 1 ([5]).

### I – Issues 
The primary legal issue was the interpretation of the term "floor area" as used in clause 6(b) of the lease agreements. Specifically, the court had to determine whether the amendments made to clause 6(b) changed the method of calculating "floor area" in such a way that it favored the Appellant's interpretation or the Respondent's ([4]–[10]). 

Key questions included:
1. Did the amendment indicate a change in how "floor area" should be defined and calculated?
2. Which definition of "floor area" from the Scheme should apply to the calculation of the rental?

### R – Rules 
The court relied on established principles of contract interpretation, which emphasize the intention of the parties, the context of the agreements, and the ordinary rules of grammar and syntax. The relevant legal precedent was drawn from **Port Elizabeth Municipal Council v Port Elizabeth Electric Tramway Co Ltd** 1947 (2) SA 1269 (A) and **Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality** [2012] ZASCA 13 ([6]–[10]). 

The original and amended definitions of "floor area" in the leases and the associated Umhlanga Town Planning Scheme provided a framework for interpreting the agreements ([12][13]).

### A – Application 
The court analyzed the amendment's wording and its implications. It found that the introduction of the word "calculated" in the 1981 amendment suggested a shift in interpretation rather than a mere rephrasing. The court rejected the lower court's ambiguity findings regarding the definition of 'floor area' and emphasized that the word "calculated" indicated a specific method of interpretation that aligned with the Town Planning Scheme's provisions ([6][12][20]). 

The SCA established that the particular words used in clause 6(b) directly linked to the provisions and definitions provided in clause 1.2 of the Scheme. The court underscored that rental calculations were to be conducted with commercial sensibility, focusing on the correct definitions and ensuring consistency with how rentals would realistically be computed, maintaining clarity without entering vague interpretations ([12][18]).

The dissenting opinion from the lower court regarding an alleged mutual mistake between the parties was rejected, concluding that the correspondence established a consistent understanding of "floor area" as distinct from 'bulk area' or 'floor area ratio' ([20]).

### C – Conclusion 
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the Appellant's interpretation of the term "floor area" was correct. The rental must be calculated based on the actual total floor area defined in the planning scheme, rejecting the Respondent's narrower interpretation. Consequently, the court set aside the order of the lower court and dismissed the Respondent's action with costs, affirming the clarity and legal prowess to uphold the Appellant's position ([22]).

This case underscores the importance of precise language in contractual agreements and illustrates the challenges that can arise when ambiguities in definitions are considered within commercial contexts. It reaffirms the contractual interpretation principles that continue to guide South African contract law, emphasizing parties' intentions and the application of clear definitions in legal agreements.
