Residents of Joe Slovo Community v Thubelisha Homes

Citation: Residents of Joe Slovo Community, Western Cape v Thubelisha Homes and Others (Centre on Housing Rights and Evictions and Another, Amici Curiae) [2011] ZACC 8; Case CCT 22/08.

Constitutional Court 2011 [2011] ZACC 8; Case CCT 22/08. ### F – Facts In this case, t

Citation:
Residents of Joe Slovo Community, Western Cape v Thubelisha Homes and Others (Centre on Housing Rights and Evictions and Another, Amici Curiae) [2011] ZACC 8; Case CCT 22/08.

F – Facts

In this case, the Constitutional Court of South Africa addressed the validity of a previous ejectment order concerning approximately 20,000 residents of the Joe Slovo informal settlement. The original order, issued under section 6 of the Prevention of Illegal Eviction From and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE Act), mandated the residents vacate their homes to facilitate urban development and the construction of temporary residential units (TRUs) ([1], [2]).

The order was contingent upon the provision of suitable alternative accommodation and required detailed engagement between the parties regarding the relocation process ([2], [4]). However, after significant delays and a failure to reach any agreement on the implementation of the order, the MEC for Human Settlements expressed concerns about the feasibility and appropriateness of the large-scale eviction ([5]–[8]).

A series of extensions to the original deadline for compliance were granted, yet reports repeatedly revealed a lack of substantial progress and meaningful engagement on the part of the government ([9]–[13]). Eventually, both parties acknowledged the plausibility of an in situ upgrading strategy instead of enforced eviction and relocation, leading to the current proceedings questioning the validity of the ejectment order ([12]–[14]).

I – Issues

The central legal question was whether the Constitutional Court had the jurisdiction to discharge the previous ejectment order based on the changes in circumstances surrounding the Joe Slovo residents since the order was initially issued. Specifically, issues included:

  1. Whether the Court had the authority to vary or discharge its prior order.
  2. Whether the original circumstances justifying the eviction had changed to a degree that would render the continued enforcement of the order unjust and inequitable ([20]–[37]).

R – Rules

Key legal principles involved in the decision included:

  1. Substantive Justification: Section 26(3) of the Constitution mandates that no person may be evicted without judicial consideration of relevant circumstances; effectively establishing an obligation on the court to assess whether eviction is just and equitable ([27]).
  2. Discharge of Orders: Drawing from prior rulings such as Zondi v MEC, Traditional and Local Government Affairs and principles articulated in Firestone South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Genticuro AG, the Court considered that while a final judgment typically becomes functus officio, exceptional circumstances may allow for the discharge of an order when justice and equity necessitate ([22]–[25], [39]).

A – Application

The Court applied these principles by evaluating the changes in circumstances since the original order was made. It noted the following significant shifts:

  1. Intention to Upgrade: The provincial government transitioned from a policy of relocation to one of in situ upgrading, indicating a fundamental change in the governmental plan regarding the settlement ([12]–[14], [30]).
  2. Lack of Engagement and Execution: The Court examined the government’s failure to comply with various aspects of the earlier order, such as engaging meaningfully with the residents, which had resulted in the order being left in abeyance ([30]–[37]).
  3. Relevance of Ejectment: The Court concluded that due to the prolonged suspension of the order and lack of alternative housing developments, the original grounds for eviction were no longer applicable, rendering the enforcement of the order unjust and inequitable ([31]–[38]).

Given these evaluations, the Court found that it retained the power to discharge the ejectment order based on a substantial change in circumstances which rendered the order itself no longer competent ([24]–[38]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately ruled that it was just and equitable to discharge the ejectment order issued in June 2009. It ordered the discharge of paragraphs related to the procedural aspects of eviction while maintaining the government’s obligation to cover related costs ([39]). This decision signifies an adherence to promoting equitable housing access while recognizing the evolving socio-political context surrounding the residents of Joe Slovo.
