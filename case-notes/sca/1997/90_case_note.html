Supreme Court of Appeal 1997 1995 (3) SA 391

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case of Maseala v The State (Case No. 239/94) involves the appellant, Marvyn Mokgatle Maseala, who was convicted in the Witwatersrand Local Division on counts of murder and other charges, specifically accused no. 3 ([para 2]). Notably, Maseala was sentenced to death on the murder count, which was a pivotal point of contention for the appeal ([para 2]). Following the enactment of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa Act 200 of 1993, which included provisions against the death penalty, Maseala's counsel appealed the death sentence but conceded that the conviction itself was "unassailable" ([para 2]). The context of the case is significant, as it came after the landmark decision in S v Makwanyane 1995 (3) SA 391 (CC), which declared the death penalty unconstitutional. Thus, the appellant sought remittal for the imposition of a new sentence in absence of the capital punishment ([para 2]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues at stake in this case pertain to the constitutionality of the death sentence imposed on Maseala, considering the significant legal shifts post-1993 constitutional enactment. Specifically, the key questions were:

  1. Was the death sentence imposed upon Maseala constitutionally valid given the provisions established by the new Constitution?
  2. Should the conviction for murder be upheld despite the appalling sentence?

These issues reflect broader questions regarding the application of the law in light of constitutional protections against cruel and unusual punishment ([paras 1–2]).

R – Rules

The court relied heavily on the principles laid out in the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa Act 200 of 1993, particularly regarding the inapplicability of capital punishment following the judgment in S v Makwanyane, which declared the death penalty unconstitutional ([para 2]). Additionally, relevant legal frameworks include Section 316 A(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which governs the process of appeal in criminal cases. The ruling in S v Makwanyane provides the foundational precedent that influenced the court’s decision to overturn the death sentence in the present appeal ([para 2]).

A – Application

In applying the law to the present facts, the Supreme Court of Appeal confirmed the appellant's conviction, which counsel conceded was sound and without merit for appeal ([para 2]). However, the court focused on the implications of the death sentence post-*Makwanyane*. It recognized that the sentence was imposed prior to the constitutional shift and therefore was inconsistent with contemporary interpretations of justice and constitutional safeguards against inhumane treatment ([para 2]). By aligning its decision with constitutional imperatives, the court acknowledged a misalignment between the imposed sentence and the evolving norms of justice in South Africa, emphasizing the necessity for a remittal to impose a new, fair sentence consistent with current legal standards ([para 2]).

C – Conclusion

The court upheld the conviction on the murder count, determining it not only valid but also beyond challenge ([para 2]). However, it allowed the appeal against the death sentence, which was set aside due to its inconsistency with constitutional provisions as highlighted in previous jurisprudence, notably S v Makwanyane. The matter was remitted to the lower court for the imposition of a new sentence, recognizing the importance of adhering to constitutional protections while ensuring that justice is served ([para 2]). This ruling reaffirms the evolving nature of legal interpretation in post-apartheid South Africa, focusing on human rights and the rule of law, with broader implications for legal practice in capital cases.

References

  1. Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977.
  2. Constitution of the Republic of South Africa Act 200 of 1993.
  3. S v Makwanyane 1995 (3) SA 391 (CC).
