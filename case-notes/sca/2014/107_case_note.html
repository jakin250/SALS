Minister of Police v Mboweni [2014] ZASCA 107

Supreme Court of Appeal 2014 [2014] ZASCA 107

F – Facts

On 15 March 2009, Mr. Wisani Mahlati was arrested and detained at the Ritavi police station. During his detention, he was assaulted by two other inmates, an event which went unnoticed by the police. The following morning, the police, finding no grounds for his detention, released him. Mr. Mahlati was visibly in distress and subsequently died five days later due to complications from the injuries sustained during the assault ([1]).

The first respondent, Vongani Sharon Mboweni, was married to Mr. Mahlati and is the mother of their daughter, born on 13 January 2009. The second respondent, Rudzani Lolla Makatu, is the mother of Mr. Mahlati’s other daughter, born on 27 November 2000. Both respondents claimed damages against the Minister of Police on behalf of their daughters, alleging that their constitutional right to parental care under Section 28(1)(b) of the Constitution had been violated due to the unlawful conduct of police officers ([2]).

The respondents’ claims were for “general damages” due to the "loss of parental support," which they argued constituted a breach of constitutional rights. At the trial in the North Gauteng High Court, an agreement was reached regarding the delictual damages owed for the loss of support, but the court also found in favor of the respondents concerning their rights to claim constitutional damages. This constituted the basis for the appeal ([2]–[3]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions inherent in this case included:

  1. Whether the respondents had the standing to sue for constitutional damages on behalf of their children due to the alleged infringement of their right to parental care ([4]).
  2. The procedural validity of the special case presented to the court, especially regarding the adequacy of the stated facts ([5]–[6]).
  3. The legal implications of whether the inaction of police constituted a breach of constitutional duties owed to the children ([18]).
  4. Whether a remedy for constitutional damages was appropriate in this context, considering existing delictual remedies ([22]).

R – Rules

The court examined several legal principles relevant to the issues:

  • Section 28(1)(b) of the Constitution: This provision stipulates that every child has the right to parental care and family support. It lays the foundation for the respondents' claim that their daughters suffered a breach of this right due to the circumstances surrounding Mr. Mahlati's death ([10]).
  • The findings in precedents such as Fose v Minister of Safety and Security dictate that damages may be awarded as a remedy for breaches of constitutional rights under certain conditions ([4]). Additionally, cases like Modderfontein Squatters and Kate established a framework for assessing damages resulting from such breaches, although these specific precedents differ in significant ways from the case at hand ([4]).
  • Uniform Rules of Court, Rule 33: The court highlighted the requirements for a properly framed special case, emphasizing the need for an agreed-upon statement of facts and legal questions ([6]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal noted significant procedural errors in the lower court’s handling of the special case. The submission failed to adequately set out facts crucial to the determination of whether the children experienced a deprivation of their constitutional right to parental care. The court criticized the approach taken by the lower court, which prematurely centered on the appropriateness of the remedy without first thoroughly establishing whether the right had been infringed ([5]–[6]).

The court underscored the necessity of elucidating the relationship between Mr. Mahlati and his daughters, as well as his role in their lives, prior to assessing entitlement to damages. It argued that the inquiry into quantum should not proceed unless there was clear evidence of harm or deprivation ([13]–[16]). Key considerations included whether the surviving mother provided adequate parental care or if the absence of Mr. Mahlati constituted a breach of their right under Section 28(1)(b) ([19]).

Furthermore, the court held that even if the evidence provided established a breach of the children’s rights, it did not necessarily confer a right to claim damages, as the actions of the police must also be deemed wrongful in relation to the children ([20]).

The court ultimately concluded that although the claim for constitutional damages had been posited, the foundational elements needed to substantiate such a claim were absent in the proceedings below. They emphasized a need for further trials to establish the factual matrix before determining liability or damages ([25]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, ruling that the lower court's judgment could not stand due to procedural flaws and a lack of substantive evidence underpinning the claim for constitutional damages. The matter was referred back to the high court for trial, directing that the actions must confirm whether the children had in fact been deprived of parental care, and whether the death of their father constituted a breach of their constitutional rights, without the premature consideration of damages in the air ([21]–[27]).

This ruling clarifies critical elements concerning the procedural and substantive requirements for claiming constitutional damages and reinforces the need for a grounded factual basis in similar future litigations. The implications of this case signify the importance of diligently framed special cases that thoroughly consider the intersection of constitutional rights and factual circumstances impacting children.
