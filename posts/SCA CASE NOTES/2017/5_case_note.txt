Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: M v M [2016] ZASCA 5

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The matter before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was a matrimonial property dispute following the tumultuous relationship between the appellant, REM, and the respondent, VM, who had been married and divorced three times ([para 1]). 

The respondent, as the plaintiff in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, sought proprietary claims based on an antenuptial contract (ANC) agreed upon by the parties, which established a marriage out of community of property with an accrual system under the Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984. The core issues revolved around certain trust assets and their treatment under the ANC ([paras 1–2]).

The respondent's claims included:
- **Claim A**: Fixed property valued at R300,000 following an extramarital affair by the appellant.
- **Claims B, E, and F**: Asserting that assets held in the Shajo Trust, RMF Trust, and Capmark Business Trust should be counted towards the appellant's estate for purposes of calculating accrual.
- **Claim C**: Challenging the transfer of a 50% membership interest in Milcar Development CC to the Shajo Trust as an attempt to defraud the accrual claim.
- **Claim D**: Asserting that "contributions" made to the RMF Trust should be considered part of the accrual ([paras 3–4]).

The appellant contended that the claims were either unfounded or based upon legal misinterpretations of the ANC ([para 4]). 

The SCA's examination revealed ambiguities in the ANC and its provisions concerning excluded assets and the categorization of the trusts ([paras 10–12]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues presented to the court included:
1. Whether the respondent had established that the appellant's extramarital affair was the cause of the divorce as stipulated in the ANC, thus triggering claim A.
2. Under what circumstances the assets of the Trusts would be included in the calculation of the appellant's accrual.
3. Whether the appellant had transferred assets with fraudulent intent to evade the respondent's claims, particularly regarding claims C and D.
4. The interpretation of the term "contributions" in the context of accrual benefits related to the RMF Trust ([paras 4, 20, 23]).

### R – Rules
In arriving at its decision, the SCA notably relied on:
1. The Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984, particularly its provisions relating to the accrual system and the treatment of excluded assets under antenuptial contracts ([paras 10–11]).
2. The Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988, which outlines principles regarding the administration and management of trust properties and the relations between trustees and beneficiaries ([para 19]).
3. Relevant case law, including *Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality* [2012] ZASCA 13 and *Van Zyl & Another NNO v Kaye NO & Others* [2014] 4 SA 452 (WCC), which guided the court's interpretation of the contracts and the standards for piercing trust veils due to perceived abuse of trust forms ([paras 10, 18]).

### A – Application
Applying the law to the facts, the SCA addressed each claim:

1. **Claim A**: The court found that the respondent proved the causative link between the appellant's extramarital affair and the divorce, with the clause in the ANC designed to discourage infidelity reaffirmed its validity ([paras 7–9]).
   
2. **Claims B, E, and F**: The assets in the Shajo and Capmark trusts were deemed non-excluded from the accrual, as the ANC’s exclusions were narrowly defined and did not encompass the assets in question. The SCA characterized the trusts as separate legal entities and upheld the lower court's finding that they were not an alter ego of the appellant ([paras 10–14]).

3. **Claim C**: In the absence of evidence proving fraudulent intent in the transfer of Milcar’s membership interest, the SCA ruled in favor of the appellant, affirming that the transfer was consistent with the business practices and the intent behind managing ownership through trusts ([para 22]).

4. **Claim D**: The interpretation of "contribution" was extended by the SCA to include all assets accumulated in the RMF Trust after marriage. This resulted in a recalculation of the accrual based on the contributions and growth within the trust ([para 25–26]).

In summary, the SCA rejected the majority of the appellant's defenses and upheld the lower court's orders relating to claims that could be substantiated.

### C – Conclusion
The SCA ultimately upheld the appeal concerning the financial contributions to the accrual calculation, correcting the net asset value of the appellant’s estate to R5,339,645.55, entitling the respondent to half, amounting to R2,669,822.78 ([paras 2, 26]). The court ordered that the respondent pay the appellant's costs of the appeal, indicating a partial victory for the appellant but acknowledging the complexity and significant implications of trust assets also in matrimonial property law ([paras 27–28]).

This decision has critical implications for the interpretation of antenuptial contracts, the treatment of trust assets in matrimonial disputes, and reinforces the significance of credible evidential bases in asserting claims of fraudulent intent within the management of trusts.
