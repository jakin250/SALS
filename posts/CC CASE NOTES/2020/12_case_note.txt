# Case Note: AB and Another v Pridwin Preparatory School and Others [2020] ZACC 12

## F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History

The case concerns parents AB and CB, who are the applicants challenging the cancellation of their sons' enrollment at Pridwin Preparatory School (Pridwin), an elite independent school in Johannesburg. The applicants entered into Parent Contracts with Pridwin for their two sons, then aged 6 and 10, which contained provisions allowing both parties to cancel the contract upon one term's notice ([6]–[9]). 

The relationship between the family and the school deteriorated due to AB's intrusive behavior and escalating conflicts with the school's staff regarding his children's schooling and sports performance ([10]–[14]). Following multiple incidents of misconduct by AB, including threats towards staff members and aggression at sporting events, the headmaster invoked clause 9.3 of the contract, canceling the contracts and requiring the children to leave the school ([26]–[30]).

The High Court initially granted an interim order allowing the children to remain at Pridwin while the validity of the termination was challenged. However, the main application was ultimately dismissed, upholding the school's decision based on the principle of pacta sunt servanda, reasoning that independent schools are not constitutionally required to ensure the right to education like public schools ([32]–[34]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal affirmed this view, holding that independent schools do not bear constitutional obligations to provide basic education in the same manner as public schools. Only the state is bound to provide basic education under section 29 of the Constitution ([39]–[44]). The applicants subsequently appealed to the Constitutional Court, focusing on the legality of the termination clause in the contracts ([45]).

## I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions

The key legal questions before the Constitutional Court include:

1. **Jurisdiction:** Whether the Court can rule on the matter given the mootness of the case, as the children had moved to another school, and no current dispute existed between the parties.
2. **Constitutional Validity of the Parent Contract:** Whether the clause allowing Pridwin to cancel the contracts without due process violates the applicants' children's rights to basic education and their best interests.
3. **Application of Constitutional Rights:** How the rights under sections 28 (best interests of the child) and 29 (right to basic education) apply within the context of private education and contractual obligations ([45]–[58]).

## R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon

The key legal authorities and constitutional provisions involved in this case include:

1. **Constitution of South Africa, 1996**:
   - **Section 28(2):** A child's best interests are paramount in all matters concerning the child.
   - **Section 29(1)(a):** Everyone has the right to basic education.
   - **Section 29(3):** Everyone has the right to establish and maintain independent educational institutions, which must maintain standards not inferior to public educational institutions.

2. **Precedents**:
   - **Juma Musjid Primary School v Essay NO** ([2011] ZACC 13): Addresses the obligations of private entities concerning children's rights.
   - **Barkhuizen v Napier** ([2007] ZACC 5): Discusses the enforceability of contractual terms in light of constitutional values.
   - **Centre for Child Law v Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development** ([2009] ZACC 18): Involves children's rights and participatory rights.

## A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law

The Constitutional Court acknowledged the unique context of children in independent schools and the implications of the specific contractual provisions at issue. The majority judgment focused on balancing the contractual obligations of a private entity against the children's constitutional rights to basic education and their best interests. 

Crucially, the Court concluded that while independent schools enjoy certain autonomy, they must still comply with constitutional mandates that protect children's rights. Specifically, the termination clause in the contract could not be enforced (or should be rendered unenforceable) if it infringed on the children's rights to a basic education, which includes allowing them participation in matters directly affecting them ([94]–[96]).

The Court found that Pridwin did not afford the applicants an opportunity to be heard before terminating the contract, which constituted a failure to adhere to the procedural fairness required under section 28(2). The decision to exclude the children was determined to be unconstitutional not just on procedural but also on substantive grounds—by failing to consider the children's best interests adequately, especially given the significant emotional and educational impacts of such a decision ([209]).

The dissenting opinion argued against the majority's view, stressing the importance of allowing independent schools latitude in managing their internal affairs without being overly burdened by procedural requirements, advocating for a clearer distinction between private and public obligations concerning education rights ([43]).

## C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications

The Constitutional Court concluded that:

1. Clause 9.3 of the Parent Contract was deemed unconstitutional and contrary to public policy, as it allowed the cancellation of education contracts without a fair procedure.
2. The right to a basic education entails a negative obligation on independent schools not to unjustly interfere with learners' rights and includes a requirement for procedural fairness when making significant decisions affecting children ([212]).

The judgment has broader implications for the South African education system, highlighting the constitutional obligations of independent schools regarding children's rights. It emphasizes that while private schools have the autonomy to establish and enforce contracts, they also bear responsibilities under the Constitution that protect the rights of the children they educate. This ruling reinforces the need for independent schools to recognize the importance of participation and fair process when making decisions that significantly impact students' rights and interests ([252]–[262]).
