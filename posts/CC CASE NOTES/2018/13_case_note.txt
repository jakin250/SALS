# Firac Case Note: Rustenburg Platinum Mine v SAEWA obo Meyer Bester and Others [2018] ZACC 13 

## F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–9])

The case revolves around the dismissal of Mr. Meyer Bester, a senior training officer at Rustenburg Platinum Mine, who was terminated for allegedly making racially derogatory remarks by referring to a colleague, Mr. Solly Tlhomelang, as a “swart man” (translated as "black man"). The dismissal occurred on May 28, 2013, after a disciplinary inquiry that found him guilty of insubordination and making racial remarks ([para 3]). 

The facts leading to Bester's dismissal include multiple failed attempts to resolve an issue regarding vehicle parking assigned by Mr. Ben Sedumedi, the chief safety officer. Tensions escalated when Bester, in a confrontational manner, accused Sedumedi of refusing to address his parking complaint and allegedly made the racially loaded remark during a meeting ([paras 4–6]). Bester denied using the term during the inquiry, instead asserting that he was being falsely accused and that the situation had been incorrectly depicted by witnesses ([para 7]).

Following a formal complaint, Bester was suspended and later dismissed following the inquiry chairperson's recommendation, which cited the violation of workplace rules against derogatory language ([paras 8–9]). Bester then challenged his dismissal as unfair through the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation, and Arbitration (CCMA), where a commissioner ruled in his favor, declaring the dismissal obstructive and unfair ([para 10]).

## I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 1, 10–11, 12–15, 18–22, 37–38])

The core legal issues in this case are:

1. **Was Bester’s reference to his colleague as a “swart man” derogatory and racist?** This involves determining the contextual implications of the words used and whether they contravened established workplace standards regarding language. The test for derogatory language is objective, focusing on the perception of a reasonable observer ([para 18]).

2. **Should the CCMA’s arbitral ruling regarding the fairness of Bester’s dismissal be upheld or overturned?** The Labour Court had reviewed and set aside the CCMA's decision, deeming it flawed ([paras 12, 13, 18, 20]). The appropriateness of sanctions for workplace infractions is another layer of this inquiry.

3. **What standard should be applied to upcoming language cases involving race in the realm of employment?** The broader social and historical context of post-apartheid South Africa necessitates scrutiny of language to understand contextual racism ([para 37]).

## R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 10–11, 18–22, 32, 38])

The court referenced several critical legal principles:

1. **Evaluation of Derogatory Language:** An objective test is required, shifting the burden of proof from the employer to the employee once derogatory status is established ([para 45]).

2. **Sidumo Standard:** This case utilizes the established principle from *Sidumo v Rustenburg Platinum Mines Ltd*, which indicates that the outcomes of decisions made by arbitrators may be reviewed based on whether a reasonable decision-maker could come to the same conclusion under the circumstances ([para 51]).

3. **Contextual Analysis of Language Usage:** Following precedents, the case emphasizes that the historical context, particularly the legacy of apartheid, influences how language can be perceived and interpreted in contemporary South African society ([para 37]).

4. **Zero Tolerance for Racism:** Cases such as *Lebowa Platinum Mines Ltd v Hill* illustrate the industry-wide zero-tolerance policy for racist behavior and language in workplace environments and underscore that racism cannot be tolerated in any form ([para 54]).

## A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 1, 10–11, 38–63])

The Constitutional Court conducted a thorough analysis of the language used and its implications:

1. **Contextual Interpretation:** The interpretation of the term "swart man" was subjected to a critical lens of context, where it was established that it could have a neutral connotation. However, within the racially charged atmosphere of post-apartheid South Africa, the historical implications transformed its interpretation to derogatory ([para 40–49]).

2. **Assessment of the CCMA’s Decision:** The court found that the CCMA’s conclusion—that Bester’s phrasing was racially innocuous—was contradictory to the evidence and contextual realities of the workplace, especially considering the prevailing zero-tolerance policy against derogatory language ([para 49]).

3. **Dismissal as a Sanction:** The court determined that despite Bester's past contributions to the mine and the calls for leniency due to a clean prior record, his substantive misconduct coupled with a lack of acknowledgement of wrongdoing eliminated the possibility of rehabilitation. Consequently, dismissal was deemed an appropriate response by the employer to uphold workplace integrity and adherence to disciplinary provisions ([paras 50–63]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 64–65])

The Constitutional Court ultimately upheld the appeal, substituting Sibanye Rustenburg Platinum Mines as the applicant and confirming Bester's dismissal. The ruling set aside the Labour Appeal Court's previous decision in favor of Bester and dismissed the appeal with no order as to costs ([para 65]).

The judgment underscores the importance of contextual understanding in cases involving language that may be interpreted as racially charged. Furthermore, it reinforces the notion that employers have a duty to maintain a workplace devoid of any form of racism, emphasizing the critical role that language plays in workplace dynamics post-apartheid. It serves as a guideline not only for future cases involving similar circumstances but also as part of the broader effort in South Africa to eradicate racism, upholding the principles of non-racialism and dignity as enshrined in the Constitution.
