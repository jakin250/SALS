Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Vilakazi v The State (636/2015) [2015] ZASCA 103

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–24])**

The appellant, Vusi Samuel Vilakazi, was convicted on May 10, 2004, by the Delmas Regional Court of raping the complainant, a 12-year-old girl. Following the conviction, the matter was referred to the North Gauteng High Court for the confirmation of the conviction and sentencing under s 52(1)(b) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997. The High Court confirmed the conviction and imposed a minimum life sentence, later reduced to 18 years' imprisonment upon an appeal to the full court.

The facts of the case indicated that the complaint of rape originated when the complainant's mother confronted her about unusual behavior and physical discomfort. When the complainant divulged that the appellant had sexually assaulted her, the mother sought discussions with the appellant, who denied the allegations. A medical examination confirmed that the complainant had been sexually penetrated.

During the trial, the complainant testified that the appellant had raped her multiple times, often in the presence of her younger sister, and recounted specific incidents of sexual acts. Her sister also supported this testimony, stating that she had witnessed the assault. The appellant's defense was a bare denial, claiming the allegations were false and stemming from a misunderstanding and revenge due to a scolding he had given the complainant.

The appellant’s criticisms of the case included alleged inconsistencies in the complainant's testimony, the manner in which the complaint had been elicited, and a lack of sufficient detail in the charge sheet regarding the timing of the incidents ([paras 1–24]).

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 12–21])**

The primary issue in this appeal was whether the State had adequately proven, beyond a reasonable doubt, that the appellant had committed the acts of rape as charged. Related sub-issues included:

1. The admissibility of the complainant's testimony and the manner in which it was obtained, particularly in light of her mother’s coercive behavior.
2. Whether the lack of specificity in the charge sheet regarding the dates of the alleged incidents constituted a procedural defect affecting the appellant's right to a fair trial.
3. The assessment of the credibility and reliability of child witnesses in sexual assault cases, particularly where their testimony includes contradictory elements or is given under duress ([paras 12–21]).

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 14–18])**

The court relied on established legal principles regarding the treatment of evidence in sexual assault cases involving children. In particular, the judgment discussed the need for trial courts to approach such cases with sensitivity and care, as outlined in **S v Vilakazi** [2009] (1) SACR 552 (SCA) and **Director of Public Prosecutions v Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development** [2009] (4) SA 222 (CC).

Additionally, the court referenced **S v T** [1963] (1) SA 484 (A), which provided guidance on the admissibility of complaints made under duress, emphasizing that a complaint resulting from intimidation is usually inadmissible unless the court can rule that its admission did not result in a failure of justice.

The court further evaluated the **Woji v Sanlam Insurance Co Ltd** [1981] (1) SA 1020 (A) guidance regarding the trustworthiness of child witnesses, stressing the importance of careful evaluation of a child’s capacity to observe, recall, and narrate ([paras 14–18]).

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 12–24])**

The majority of the court dismissed the appellant's appeal, concluding that the conviction was sound based on a holistic review of the evidence. The court acknowledged the imperfections in the complainant’s account but stated that such imperfections were not fatal to the credibility of the overall evidence. The medical report corroborated the physical evidence of sexual penetration, aligning with the complainant's testimony regarding the timeline of events ([paras 12–16]).

In addressing the issue of the admissibility of the complainant’s testimony, the court highlighted that while the circumstances of its elicitation were concerning—particularly the coercion by the mother—this did not ultimately diminish the evidential value of the other supporting testimonies, including that of the complainant's younger sister. The majority found that the evidence presented allowed for a reasonable inference of the appellant's guilt ([paras 16–24]).

The dissenting judgment raised serious concerns about the validity of the custodial confession obtained through duress, arguing that the influences exerted upon the complainant compromised the integrity of her testimony. The dissenting judge contended that without the complainant's testimony, the remaining evidence was insufficient to establish guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. Judge Mhlantla emphasized the need for meticulous evaluation of the complainant's evidence, which may have been improperly influenced by her mother's actions ([paras 25–38]).

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 24–38])**

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the conviction of the appellant. The ratio decidendi emphasized that imperfections and inconsistencies in a child’s testimony, particularly in sexual assault cases, do not preclude a conviction as long as the evidence, when considered holistically, demonstrates the appellant's guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. Moreover, the court reaffirmed that a lack of voluntary complaint, in this case, did not render the evidence insufficient.

The majority opinion underscored that the credibility of child witnesses must be rigorously assessed, and their testimony must be understood in the broader context of the circumstances surrounding the incident. The decision reinforced the principle that in sexual assault trials, especially involving children, courts must balance the rights and credibility of witnesses with the necessity of upholding the accused's right to a fair trial ([paras 24–38]). 

This case serves as a critical precedent in South African law regarding the treatment and evaluation of child witnesses in sexual assault cases, emphasizing the necessity for careful consideration of evidence while maintaining the rights of both the complainant and the accused.
