Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

# Case Note: Distell Limited v Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (416/09) [2010] ZASCA 103

## FIRAC Method Analysis

### F – Facts
[1] This case revolves around the proper classification for excise duty purposes of ten wine coolers manufactured by the appellants, Distell Limited (Distell) and Stellenbosch Farmers’ Winery Limited (SFW). The dispute originated when the South African Revenue Service (SARS) issued determinations regarding the duty applicable to these products under the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 (the Act) ([1]).

[2] Distell, a longstanding manufacturer of alcoholic beverages, acquired SFW on January 1, 2001, and continued to produce wine coolers, including notably Bernini Sparkling Grape Beverage and Crown Premium ([2]). The classification of these products was subject to various prior determinations by SARS, which were being contested by Distell and SFW ([4]).

[3] The appellants claimed that, as mixtures consisting primarily of wine and water, the wine coolers should be classified separately for the purposes of excise duty. Their legal challenge began with their appeal against SARS's determinations, which classified the wine coolers as "other fermented beverages" ([4]–[5]).

### I – Issues
[4] The central issue in this appeal is whether the wine coolers should be classified as “other fermented beverages” under the first part of Tariff Heading 22.06 or as “mixtures of fermented beverages and non-alcoholic beverages” under the second part of that heading, which would affect the applicable excise duties ([1], [40]).

[5] Specifically, the court had to determine whether water, as an ingredient in the wine coolers, qualified as a “non-alcoholic beverage” within the meaning defined in the Customs Tariff, and whether this classification would subsequently influence the duty payable on the coolers ([40], [54]).

### R – Rules
[6] The classification of goods for excise duty purposes falls under Schedule 1 of the Act, which sets out tariff headings and the applicable duties. The Interpretative Rules of the Harmonized System guide this classification process, notably Rules 1, 3, and 6, which establish how to determine which headings apply based on the goods' nature and characteristics ([22]).

[7] The relevant tariff headings cited were TH 22.06 (other fermented beverages) and the subsequent provisions of the Customs and Excise Act, particularly items 104.15.10 and 104.17 (which deal with various categories of alcoholic beverages) ([30], [32]).

[8] Reference was made to prior case law, notably **Secretary for Customs and Excise v Thomas Barlow and Sons Ltd** and **International Business Machines SA (Pty) Ltd v Commissioner for Customs and Excise**, which outline the standards for determining the classification of goods ([22]).

### A – Application
[9] The court undertook a detailed analysis of the characteristics and manufacturing processes of the wine coolers, finding that they essentially consist of a wine base mixed with water and flavorings. Thus, the composition of the coolers closely linked to both alcoholic and non-alcoholic components ([24]).

[10] The Commissioner’s argument, that the coolers were to be classified as fermented beverages, relied on the notion that fermentation imbued them with that character. However, the court pointed out that while wine is a fermented product, the process used to create the coolers did not involve fermentation; instead, it was a blending process ([20], [46]).

[11] The court placed significant emphasis on the legislative interpretation of the terms used within the tariff headings, concluding that the mixtures of wine and water, with substantial flavoring agents, qualify under the second part of TH 22.06 as “mixtures of fermented beverages and non-alcoholic beverages.” In defining whether water was a “non-alcoholic beverage,” the court drew upon dictionary definitions and legislative context to affirm its inclusion in this category ([51]).

### C – Conclusion
[12] The court found in favor of the appellants, setting aside the Commissioner’s classifications and confirming that the wine coolers should be categorized as mixtures under the applicable tariff heading. The implications of this ruling led to a correction in the amount of excise duty owed, allowing for rebates applicable to the wine used in the coolers ([73]).

[13] This judgment not only rectified the classification issue but also resolved the long-standing discrepancies regarding the excise duty paid, eliminating any potential for double taxation on the wine component. The court ordered that the previous determinations were incorrect, emphasizing that mixtures containing non-alcoholic ingredients must be classified distinctly to prevent unjust burdens on manufacturers ([73]).
