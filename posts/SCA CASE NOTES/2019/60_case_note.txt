Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: Motala v The Master of the North Gauteng High Court, Pretoria (92/2018) [2019] ZASCA 60

## F – Facts
The appellant, Enver Mohamed Motala, was previously listed on the Master’s panel of liquidators and trustees under the Administration of Estates Act 66 of 1965 ([1]). His name was removed amid allegations of dishonesty, including undisclosed prior criminal convictions for theft and fraud, along with failure to perform his duties as a liquidator satisfactorily ([17]–[21]). The case arose from the administration of several companies in the Pamodzi Group, which were under liquidation due to their insolvency ([7]).

Ms C. Rossouw, a Deputy Master, initiated an inquiry into the appellant's conduct after numerous reports concerning his mismanagement of the Pamodzi Group ([12]–[14]). Following an investigation, she terminated his appointments as liquidator on the ground that he was unsuitable ([30]–[31]). Subsequently, he sought to challenge both his removal from the panel of liquidators and his dismissal from the Pamodzi liquidations through review proceedings ([32]).

The appellant's failure to respond honestly about his criminal past was noted as a critical factor in this case ([40]–[84]). His application for reinstatement to the panel was refused by the Master, a decision that the appellant did not challenge, which ultimately rendered his claims moot as he remained disqualified([50]–[51]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issues presented in this case are as follows:
1. **Disqualification:** Whether the appellant was disqualified from being appointed as a liquidator at the time of his removal ([60]–[62]).
2. **Administrative Action:** Whether the Master’s decisions to remove the appellant from the panel constituted administrative action under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([66]–[75]).
3. **Unreasonable Delay:** Whether the appellant's failure to timely challenge the removal rendered his appeal untenable under PAJA ([76]–[80]).
4. **Constitutional Claims:** Whether the appellant’s claims regarding the enforcement of his constitutional rights were substantial enough to exempt him from costs after litigation ([97]–[102]).

## R – Rules
The legal authorities and rules relevant to this case include:
- **Companies Act 61 of 1973:** Specifically, section 372(f) which states that individuals convicted of fraud or theft may not serve as liquidators ([62]).
- **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA):** Sections 7 and 9 deal with the requirement for judicial review within 180 days after being informed of administrative action ([76]–[78]).
- **Constitutional Principles:** Relevant jurisprudence, including *Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources,* which examines the enforcement of constitutional rights in the context of costs orders ([97]–[102]).

## A – Application
The court applied these rules in various respects throughout the judgment. Initially, it confirmed that the appellant was disqualified from serving as a liquidator due to his previous convictions, which constituted a breach of the Companies Act ([60]–[64]). This disqualification provided sufficient grounds for the Master to remove him from the panel ([91]).

On the issue of administrative action, the court determined that the compilation of the Master’s panel, and the removal therefrom of the appellant, indeed constituted administrative action under PAJA. This decision opened the door for the appellant to seek judicial review under the appropriate legal framework ([66]–[75]).

However, the appellant’s significant delay—over three years after being removed from the panel—limited his recourse as he failed to comply with the PAJA timeline for such applications ([76]–[80]). The court ruled that this delay was unreasonable and ultimately precluded the appellant from seeking relief related to his removal or reinstatement ([91]).

In terms of costs, the court reiterated that while constitutional rights were involved, the context did not support a complete exemption from costs since the appeal did not raise significant constitutional issues for broader public interest and was viewed as an attempt to remedy his own private interests ([97]–[102]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the Master’s removal of the appellant from the liquidators’ panel as lawful and justified. The lack of timely challenge and his prior convictions established his disqualification ([92]–[96]). The ruling reaffirmed the importance of integrity and transparency in liquidations, particularly given the public interest involved in such fiduciary roles. Additionally, the decision highlighted the necessity for timely actions under PAJA, demonstrating the court's commitment to procedural fairness and accountability.

The implications of this landmark ruling underscore the standards expected of liquidators and the rigor of review processes established under South African law ([101]–[102]).
