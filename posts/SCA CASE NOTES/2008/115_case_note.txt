# Case Note: Chartaprops 16 v Silberman (300/07) [2008] ZASCA 115

## FIRAC Method Case Note

### F – Facts ([1]–[2])
The case involves Mrs. Michelle Silberman, the respondent, who suffered injuries after slipping on an unidentified slippery substance in a shopping mall owned by Chartaprops 16 (Pty) Ltd (the first appellant) and maintained by Advanced Cleaning Services CC (the second appellant). The incident occurred in a passageway of the mall, which had a cleaning service contract in place that required the floors to be maintained regularly. Mrs. Silberman sued both appellants for damages in the High Court of Johannesburg. The High Court found both parties jointly and severally liable. Both defendants were granted leave to appeal the decision, which led to this case being heard by the Supreme Court of Appeal ([1]).

The court noted that while the precise origin of the spillage remained unclear—whether it was caused by a cleaner or by a member of the public—the crux of the complaint centered on the negligence in failing to identify and remedy the existing hazard ([2]).

### I – Issues ([5]–[6])
Two primary legal issues arose during the appeal:

1. **Vicarious Liability**: Whether Chartaprops could be held vicariously liable for the alleged negligence of Advanced Cleaning, given the relationship of independent contracting and the nature of the duties involved ([6]).
   
2. **Liability of Independent Contractor**: Whether Advanced Cleaning owed a legal duty to members of the public, such as Mrs. Silberman, to prevent injury from its omissions in maintaining the mall floors ([24]–[25]).

### R – Rules ([6]–[7])
The principle governing vicarious liability stipulates that a principal is typically not liable for the negligent acts of an independent contractor. This is well-established in South African law and has been delineated in cases like *Colonial Mutual Life Assurance Society Ltd v MacDonald* ([6]).

The court referenced the definition of a non-delegable duty: it arises when a defendant is required to ensure that reasonable care is taken in performing hazardous tasks, and such obligation cannot be delegated to another ([8]–[9]). This principle applies subject to certain conditions outlined in *Langley Fox Building Partnership (Pty) Ltd v De Valence* and *Kruger v Coetzee*, which hold the standard of care to be situationally dependent, taking into account the relationship between the parties ([10]–[12]).

### A – Application ([19]–[25])
The court began by asserting that, based on the factual findings of the High Court, the spillage had persisted for over thirty minutes, constituting a hazard that required detection and prompt action by those in control of the shopping mall ([19]). 

The Supreme Court emphasized that Chartaprops had a duty to take reasonable precautions to ensure the safety of the premises, asserting that appointing a competent contractor alone does not absolve a principal from liability if it fails to ensure that adequate systems are adhered to ([16]). The evidence suggested that, although Chartaprops had systems to maintain safety, there was insufficient adherence to these systems, and hence could be held liable for not ensuring that reasonable precautions were taken ([19]).

Conversely, when addressing Advanced Cleaning, the court examined whether there existed any legal duty owed directly to Mrs. Silberman by the cleaning contractor or its employees. The court highlighted that mere negligence does not establish the wrongful nature of an omission without recognizing a duty of care towards third parties ([21]–[22]). The absence of a recognized legal duty for Advanced Cleaning to prevent harm to the public meant that any negligence on their part, or their employees' part, could not yield liability ([25]).

### C – Conclusion ([50])
The Supreme Court reversed the High Court's ruling regarding Chartaprops, finding it liable due to the failure to ensure a safe environment in the mall. It upheld the appeal by Chartaprops, dismissing claims against it for the alleged negligence of Advanced Cleaning due to the absence of vicarious liability. The appeal by Advanced Cleaning succeeded because no legal duty was established that would necessitate care towards the public, leading to the conclusion that Advanced Cleaning was not liable for the negligence claimed ([50]).

The case delineates the nuances in adjudicating vicarious liability in South African tort law, particularly concerning independent contractors, thus reinforcing the principles of tortious liability in the context of public safety in commercial premises.
