Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1999

# Case Note: The State v J M Twala CCT 27 /99

## FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts
The application for leave to appeal considered by the Constitutional Court of South Africa revolved around the constitutionality of sections 316 and 315(4) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977. These provisions stipulate that an accused person may only appeal a high court conviction or sentence with the leave of either the trial court or the Supreme Court of Appeal ([1]). The applicant, J M Twala, had been convicted of murder and sought to appeal this conviction. His application for leave to appeal was dismissed by the presiding judge, after which he petitioned the Supreme Court of Appeal for leave which was denied on 27 November 1998 ([3]). 

The essence of Twala's complaint, articulated in a letter received by the Constitutional Court, was that the process for appealing his conviction was arbitrary and unfair, constituting a frustration of his right to a fair trial as guaranteed by section 35(3)(o) of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa ([4]). The South African Human Rights Commission intervened in the proceedings to present arguments supporting Twala’s claims ([6]).

### I – Issues
The legal questions posed before the court were:
1. Do the leave provisions under sections 316 and 315(4) of the Criminal Procedure Act comply with the constitutional requirement for a fair trial, particularly the right to appeal as expressed in section 35(3)(o) of the Constitution? ([1], [5]).
2. In what manner do the amendments from the interim Constitution's section 25(3)(h) to the current provision of section 35(3)(o) affect the interpretation of the right to appeal? ([2], [12]).

### R – Rules
The case relied on the interpretation and application of section 35(3)(o) of the Constitution of South Africa, which provides that “every accused person has the right to a fair trial, which includes the right to appeal to, or review by, a higher court” ([2], [11]). The court further examined precedents such as *S v Rens* 1996 (1) SA 1218 (CC), where it was concluded that leave provisions could pass constitutional muster if they conformed to the tenets of fairness inherent in the right to a fair trial ([2], [18]).

The distinctions between “appeal” and “review,” and their appropriate legal interpretations were central to the court's analysis. Further, the court examined international standards like article 14(5) of the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights, which emphasizes the review of convictions by a higher tribunal ([11]).

### A – Application
In its analysis, the court determined that section 35(3)(o) requires a mechanism that offers a reassessment of proceedings by a higher court. The court decisively ruled that while the leave provisions might restrict immediate access to appellate review, they nonetheless upheld a fair process, ensuring an evaluation by higher court judges ([8], [22]). 

The court rejected the applicant's argument that the absence of the recourse phrase from the new Constitution meant an absolute right to appeal without leave. Instead, it reasoned that both sections 25(3)(h) and 35(3)(o) were to be interpreted as requiring a fair system of review that does not necessarily guarantee a complete rehearing or an unqualified right to appeal ([10], [19]).

The court also emphasized the necessity of maintaining a balance between offering justice and preventing the clogging of appellate processes with meritless appeals, thus supporting the fairness of the system as established by the leave provisions ([18], [21]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court concluded that the leave provisions under scrutiny did not violate the constitutional rights of accused persons under section 35(3)(o). It opined that the provisions facilitated a fair and efficient justice system, appropriately balancing the rights of defendants against the need for judicial efficiency ([22]). Consequently, the application for leave to appeal was dismissed, affirming previous judgments that these provisions complied with constitutional standards ([23]).

The implications of this ruling are significant for the South African justice system, reinforcing that procedural limitations, when governed by fairness, do not equate to a violation of the fundamental rights of accused persons. The ruling further clarifies the judicial interpretation of rights concerning appeals, encapsulating a nuanced understanding of justice within South Africa's constitutional framework.
