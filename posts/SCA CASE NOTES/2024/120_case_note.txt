# Case Note: Tholo Energy Services CC v Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service

**Neutral Citation:** Tholo Energy Services CC v Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (Case no 378/2023) [2024] ZASCA 120 (6 August 2024)

**Coram:** Schippers, Hughes, Weiner, Kgoele JJA, and Tolmay AJA

---

## FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts (Paras [1]–[65])

The appellant, Tholo Energy Services CC, is a licensed distributor of fuel in terms of the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 (“the Act”). In March 2017, Tholo Energy submitted four claims to the Commissioner of the South African Revenue Service (SARS) for a refund of fuel and Road Accident Fund levies amounting to R4.25 million. These claims applied to 25 consignments of diesel exported to the Kingdom of Lesotho ([1]–[5]).

The Commissioner disallowed the claims, arguing the appellant had failed to comply with the conditions set out in the Act and its accompanying Rules, specifically regarding the licensing of distributors and the sourcing of fuel ([2]–[5]). The appellant had claimed that it purchased fuel from Petroleum Oil and Gas Corporation of South Africa (PetroSA) for removal to Lesotho, but the fuel was not obtained from a licensed manufacturing warehouse as required ([9]–[10]). This determination was upheld by an internal appeal committee, prompting Tholo Energy to appeal to the High Court ([3]–[4]).

The High Court dismissed the appeal on the grounds that Tholo Energy failed to provide an export permit and had not complied with various statutory requirements ([5]).

### I – Issues (Paras [32]–[32])

Three principal legal issues arose during the appeal:

1. The interpretative scope of an appeal under section 47(9)(e) of the Act: Is the Commissioner limited to the grounds of disallowance stated in the initial determination, or can additional grounds be presented?
  
2. Was the High Court correct in dismissing the appeal based on non-compliance with section 64F(1)(b) of the Act and the lack of an ITAC export permit? 

3. Are the additional grounds for refusal of the refund valid if the Commissioner is permitted to raise such grounds?

### R – Rules (Paras [41]–[66])

The court referenced several key provisions of the Act and its rules, including:

- Section 64F, which governs the licensing of distributors and mandates that such distributors must obtain fuel from stocks of a licensed manufacturing warehouse ([41]–[43]).
- The requirements for obtaining refunds as laid out in section 75(1) and Schedule 6, which necessitate the production of an invoice from the licensee of a warehouse and compliance with specific conditions, including proof of fuel origin and export ([50]–[52]).
- Rule 19A4.04, which establishes the conditions under which fuel can be removed from a licensed warehouse for export ([44]–[45]).
- The ITAC Act, which requires export permits for specific goods, including fuel ([48]–[62]).

### A – Application (Paras [53]–[75])

The court held that the appeal under section 47(9)(e) was wide enough to allow for a complete rehearing of the evidence, permitting the Commissioner to defend its determination on substantive and procedural grounds ([38]–[40]). 

In evaluating compliance with regulatory requirements, the court highlighted that Tholo Energy failed to demonstrate that its fuel was sourced from a licensed warehouse, noting that certain depots from which the fuel was obtained did not satisfy the regulatory definitions of licensed warehouses. This was pivotal, as the Act's provisions demand strict adherence to such requirements to qualify for refunds ([58]–[59]).

Moreover, the appellant could not provide evidence that the diesel was manufactured in South Africa or transported in compliance with necessary export protocols ([51]–[60]). The appellant's argument that no export occurred, due to definitions separating removal and export, was rejected outright given the clear statutory requirement for documentation and permits in cross-border fuel transactions ([61]–[66]).

### C – Conclusion (Paras [76]–[76])

The Supreme Court of Appeal confirmed the rulings of the High Court, concluding that Tholo Energy had not proven compliance with the statutory provisions required for a refund of fuel levies. The appeal was thus dismissed with costs. The ruling reaffirmed the strict nature of compliance in customs and excise matters, underscoring the legal principle that privileges afforded under the Act are contingent upon rigorous observance of all statutory conditions ([76]).
