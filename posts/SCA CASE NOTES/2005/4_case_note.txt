# FIRAC Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA - Melvin Peter Paarwater v South Sahara Investments (Pty) Ltd

## F – Facts

The case centers around an appeal by Melvin Peter Paarwater (the appellant) against an order made by the court a quo, which discharged a provisional order to wind up South Sahara Investments (Pty) Ltd (the respondent) and ordered Paarwater to pay a significant portion of the costs ([1]). The appellant, a director and shareholder of the respondent, initially sought the winding-up based on claims of a breakdown in mutual trust and allegations of mismanagement by another director, Gideon Francois Bothma ([5.1]–[5.10]).

The evidence revealed that the respondent had only one significant asset—a 90% shareholding in South African Beef (Pty) Ltd ([5.3]). Both parties were involved in a joint agricultural venture governed by a shareholders' agreement, yet their relationship deteriorated. The appellant claimed that his partnership-like relationship with Bothma had irreparably broken down, justifying the winding-up of the company due to a failure in essential cooperative principles like good faith and trust ([6]). Notably, there were disputes concerning the nature of the agreement between the two individuals, the appellant's financial issues, and various incidents cited by the appellant as evidence of Bothma's mismanagement and betrayal of trust ([5.10], [8], [11]).

While the appellant argued for the justification of winding up the company based on these claims ([6]), Bothma refuted them extensively, asserting that there was no partnership and citing the clear terms of operation established in their agreements ([7]). Furthermore, Bothma denied any allegations of financial misconduct and misappropriation ([11], [12]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal question addressed by the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the circumstances surrounding the breakdown of relationships between the directors constituted sufficient grounds for a winding-up of the company to be deemed 'just and equitable' as per section 344 (h) of the Companies Act 61 of 1973 ([2]). Additionally, the ruling focused on the requisite burden of proof imposed on the applicant for such a final order of winding-up to be granted ([3]).

More specifically, the court inquired whether the appellant could successfully demonstrate, on a balance of probabilities, that the company should be wound up given the ongoing disputes and the way the appellant sought to establish his claims without solid evidence ([4], [8], [10], [15]).

## R – Rules

The relevant statutory authority guiding the court’s determination was section 344 (h) of the Companies Act, which allows for the winding-up of a company when it is deemed just and equitable. 

The court also reiterated the principle established in previous case law that the burden of proof in supporting a final winding-up order lies upon the applicant to satisfy the court of its necessity ([3]). The standard of proof required in such cases is higher than that for provisional orders, underscoring the need for more substantive evidence and the resolution of factual disputes. Precedents cited included *Kalil v Decotex (Pty) Ltd*, *Hilleke v Levy*, and *Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd* among others ([3], [4]).

## A – Application

In applying these principles to the facts of the case, the court systematically reviewed the appellant's claims against the evidence provided by Bothma, emphasizing the requirement for a clear factual basis in seeking a final wound-up order. 

The court pointed out that the key disputes raised—including allegations of a breakdown in trust and partnership—were unsupported by concrete evidence from the appellant and were explicitly denied by Bothma through affidavits ([6], [8], [16]). Specifically, the court found that the appellant failed to substantiate his claims regarding a partnership-like relationship and did not adequately address the implications of the contractual frameworks which outlined their business relationship ([7], [10], [14]).

Moreover, the appellant's allegations regarding mismanagement and financial improprieties were dismissed due to lack of credible evidence. Bothma provided clear rebuttals, detailing the circumstances of the alleged incidents, which further weakened the appellant's position ([11], [12], [15]).

The court also noted that factual disputes presented by Bothma were significant enough that, under the guidelines from *Plascon-Evans*, the claim for winding up was not justified solely on the papers without further evidence ([4], [14]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately affirmed the decision of the court a quo, holding that the appellant had not met the burden of proof necessary to warrant a final winding-up order under section 344 (h) of the Companies Act ([16]). The absence of a credible claim supported by substantial evidence, along with the acknowledgment of existing disputes that could not be resolved merely from affidavits, led the court to conclude that it was neither just nor equitable to liquidate the respondent at that time. The appeal was dismissed, and costs were ordered against the appellant ([16]).

The ruling underscores the importance of substantiating claims made in the context of company liquidation and clarifies the judicial expectation regarding the evidentiary burden placed upon an applicant in such proceedings. It highlights the balance between the rights of shareholders and the legal frameworks governing corporate governance and disputes.
