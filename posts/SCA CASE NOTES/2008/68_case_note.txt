Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2008

### FIRAC Case Note: Oliphant v The Road Accident Fund

**Neutral Citation:** Oliphant v The Road Accident Fund (395/2007) [2008] ZASCA 68  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal  
**Judgment Delivered:** 30 May 2008  
**Coram:** Harms ADP, Scott, Heher, Mlambo et Cachalia JJA  

---

#### F – Facts

[1] The appellant, Sanna Suzann Oliphant, initiated legal proceedings against the Road Accident Fund in the Free State High Court for damages following serious injuries inflicted by her husband's vehicle on 5 December 1999. While her husband, Mr. Isaac Ndeleni Oliphant, was ostensibly diagnosing an engine problem, a spanner inadvertently slipped from his hand, landing on two electrical contact points of the starter motor. This led to the vehicle unexpectedly starting and reversing into the appellant, resulting in her becoming paraplegic. 

[2] The trial court, presided over by Ebrahim J, determined the matter of negligence favorably for the appellant, concluding that her husband's negligence was the cause of the incident. However, this ruling was overturned by a full bench of the High Court (Van der Merwe J, Hattingh J, and van Zyl J concurring), finding that the evidence was insufficient to establish negligence. The appellant was granted special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal. 

[3] Only the appellant and Mr. Oliphant testified regarding the incident, over six years post-accident. Both parties engaged expert witnesses: Mr. B Grobbelaar for the appellant, and Mr. D L van Onselen for the respondent, contributing differing assessments on the credibility of the evidence presented by Mr. Oliphant.

[4] Mr. Oliphant provided a detailed explanation regarding the mechanics of the vehicle, stating that the vehicle was equipped with an automatic transmission and that the engine was started using a button installed in the cabin. He described the sequence of events leading to the accident, emphasizing that the vehicle could only reverse if several conditions were met simultaneously.

[5] The full court below scrutinized this evidence, ultimately deeming it improbable and unreliable. It highlighted critical flaws in Oliphant's narrative concerning the mechanical properties of the vehicle, the function of the ignition switch, and the role of the gear lever at the time of the incident.

---

#### I – Issues

[6] The primary legal issue revolves around whether Mr. Oliphant's conduct constituted negligence, which comprises two essential questions: 

1. **Foreseeability of Harm**: Could Mr. Oliphant reasonably foresee that his actions, specifically dropping the spanner, could result in harm to another person?
2. **Reasonable Precautions**: If harm was foreseeable, did Mr. Oliphant fail to take reasonable steps to prevent the accident?

[7] The legal consideration of negligence necessitates not only an examination of the foreseeability of harm but also whether a reasonable person in the same situation would have acted differently to avert the incident.

---

#### R – Rules

[8] The foundational principles of negligence are codified in cases such as *Kruger v Coetzee* 1966 (2) SA 428 (A) which outlines that for negligence to be established, the defendant must possess the capacity to foresee harm arising from their actions and must fail to take precautions to prevent that harm.

[9] The legal definition of negligence involves two components: 
1. **Foreseeability**: A negligent act can only be determined if the accused could have reasonably anticipated the risk of harm due to their actions.
2. **Duty of Care**: Establishing that a duty of care exists, necessitating a standard of reasonable conduct in preventing foreseeable harm.

[10] The burden of proof lies with the claimant (appellant) to establish the elements of negligence on a balance of probabilities.

---

#### A – Application

[11] The court assessed Mr. Oliphant's narrative meticulously. In stating that a spanner would have to fall in a precise manner onto specific electrical points, the court highlighted the improbability of such an extraordinary series of events leading to the vehicle starting and moving in reverse.

[12] The court reiterated that while Mr. Oliphant was not a qualified mechanic, his experience did not lend credibility to the assertion that the dropping of the spanner would realistically engage all requisite mechanisms for starting the vehicle unexpectedly. The court found the claim's reliance on a "freak accident" compelling, noting the rarity of such events.

[13] Moreover, the court evaluated whether Mr. Oliphant could have taken additional precautions beyond removing the key from the ignition. The judgment stated it was unreasonable to expect a layperson to anticipate the exact manner in which mechanical failure might cause injury.

[14] Importantly, the respondent's lack of substantial evidence following the incident further weakened the appellant's claims. The court noted contradictions and inconclusive testimony offered by the respondent, thus failing to shift the burden of proof back to Mr. Oliphant.

---

#### C – Conclusion

[15] The Supreme Court of Appeal, led by Cachalia JA, concluded that the appellant had failed to demonstrate negligence on the part of her husband sufficiently to warrant liability. The court ruled the accident an unpreventable "freak incident," underscoring that no reasonable individual in Mr. Oliphant’s position would foresee such a rare occurrence when working on their vehicle.

[16] The outcome emphasizes the legal standards of negligence and the necessity for plaintiffs to meet a high threshold of proof. The appeal was dismissed, and costs were awarded to the respondent, setting a precedent for future considerations of negligence in cases involving extraordinary or unforeseen mechanical incidents.

[17] This ruling reinforces the complexities surrounding the attribution of negligence, particularly in instances reliant on peculiar sequences of events, and underscores the importance of robust evidentiary support in claims for damages arising from accidents. The appeal’s dismissal encourages cautious precedent for subsequent similar cases where injuries arise from mechanical occurrences deemed freak accidents.
