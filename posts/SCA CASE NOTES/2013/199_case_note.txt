# FIRAC Case Note: Khorommbi & Another v The State (527/2013) [2013] ZASCA 199

### F – Facts:
The appellants, **Khorommbi Ntshengedzeni Alfred** and **Khorommbi Thifhelimbilu Kenneth**, were convicted by the Limpopo High Court for two counts of rape and sentenced to 23 and 21 years’ imprisonment, respectively ([1]). The convictions were based primarily on the testimony of two complainants, who alleged that while walking towards I Village, they were forcibly detained by the appellants and taken to a shebeen. It was reported that the complainants were drugged and raped by the appellants at the residence of a third party, identified as **Z**. The complainants claimed they were threatened and prevented from escaping ([2]).

After the alleged incident, the complainants were taken to the home of the second complainant's aunt and subsequently reported the matter to the police, but only after the second complainant’s mother allegedly coerced a confession through violence ([3]). Witness testimony indicated discrepancies regarding the events at the shebeen and the nature of the report made to the police. While medical reports were submitted that indicated no evidence of fresh injuries, they also stated that the absence of such injuries does not preclude the possibility of penetration ([5], [9]).

The appellants provided counter-testimony, denying any acts of sexual intercourse and asserting that the complainants were at the shebeen voluntarily ([6]). They were corroborated by **Z**, who contended that no sexual intercourse had occurred during the night ([10]).

### I – Issues:
The principal legal issues in this case involved:
1. The credibility and reliability of the testimonies of the complainants.
2. Whether the coercion alleged during the arraignment of the complainants affected the admissibility of their statements to the police ([8]).
3. The adequacy of the evidence presented to support the convictions for rape beyond reasonable doubt, particularly in the absence of objective medical evidence supporting claims of penetration ([5], [9]).

### R – Rules:
The ruling in this case adhered to standard evidentiary principles applicable in criminal proceedings, notably the onus of proof being placed on the State to establish the guilt of the accused beyond reasonable doubt. The court emphasized the importance of assessing the reliability of eyewitness accounts when the only evidence linking the accused to the crime is testimony from complainants ([7]). Key legal references included **S v T** 1963 (1) SA 484 (A) which elucidated issues related to coerced confessions and the necessity of admissible evidence to substantiate claims ([8]).

### A – Application:
The court scrutinized the credibility of the complainants' accounts, highlighting that their failure to seek assistance while allegedly being held against their will in a public setting created significant doubt regarding their version of events ([7]). The court noted that the conduct of the complainants—remaining in an environment where they could seek help—was not consistent with their claims of being forcibly detained. 

Moreover, the court underscored the problematic nature of the second complainant's disclosure of the alleged rapes only after physical coercion by a police officer, illustrating concerns about the reliability of that report ([8]). It concluded that the medical evidence did not sufficiently support the claims, particularly the absence of new injuries noted in the examination ([9]).

In light of the contradictions in the testimonies of the complainants and the details surrounding the alleged incidents, the court found the appellants' version corroborated by **Z** persuasive ([10]). 

### C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the convictions and sentences imposed by the trial court were unsustainable due to the inherent improbability of the complainants' accounts and the lack of reliable corroborative evidence. The appeal was upheld, and the convictions and sentences were set aside ([11]). 

The case underscores critical principles about the evidentiary burden in sexual offence cases and emphasizes the need for a comprehensive evaluation of the credibility of witness testimonies, particularly in circumstances where coercion is alleged. The ruling reaffirms the judiciary's commitment to safeguarding the rights of the accused while adhering to due process in criminal justice.
