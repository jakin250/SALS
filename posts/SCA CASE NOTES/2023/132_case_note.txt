Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: Jacob Gedleyihlekisa Zuma v William John Downer and Another [2023] ZASCA 132

## F – Facts
The case revolves around Jacob Gedleyihlekisa Zuma, the former President of South Africa, who has been charged with multiple counts of corruption, fraud, racketeering, and money laundering ([1]). Zuma's legal troubles began in June 2005, yet his trial was still pending as of the time of the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) decision in October 2023. William John Downer has served as the lead prosecutor, and he had previously prosecuted Schabir Shaik, a figure implicated alongside Zuma in corrupt practices ([1]).

In May 2021, Zuma raised a special plea asserting that Downer was unfit to prosecute him, which was dismissed by the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court, resulting in subsequent unsuccessful appeals to both the SCA and the Constitutional Court ([2]). On 5 September 2022, Zuma initiated a private prosecution against Downer and Karyn Maughan, a journalist, claiming they had violated the law by leaking confidential information. The respondents contended this was an abuse of process aimed at delaying his eventual trial ([3]).

The High Court found the private prosecution to be an abuse of process and interdicted it, prescribing a punitive costs order against Zuma ([3]). After his appeals were dismissed, Zuma appealed against the High Court's order to suspend the execution of its decision pending the outcome of the appeal. This appeal raised significant issues regarding the motivations and legality of Zuma’s private prosecution efforts ([6]).

## I – Issues

The legal questions posed in this case include:
1. Whether the High Court was justified in concluding that Zuma's private prosecution constituted an abuse of process ([14]).
2. Whether the execution of the High Court's order interdicting the private prosecution should remain in force pending the appeal ([10]).
3. Whether allowing Zuma's appeal against the interlocutory decision would contribute to further abuse of process and delay ([23]).
4. Whether there were sufficient grounds for Zuma to continue his private prosecution while the appeal was pending ([32]).

## R – Rules

The legal framework governing the issues includes:
1. **Section 18 of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013** governs the suspension of judgments pending appeal, particularly focusing on the balance of probabilities regarding potential harm to parties ([4] and [19]).
2. **Section 106(1)(h) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977**, which allows for challenges to the public prosecutor's legitimacy ([2]).
3. The doctrine against abuse of process established in **Solomon v Magistrate, Pretoria**, providing the judiciary’s duty to prevent the exploitation of legal procedures for ulterior motives ([26]).
4. Interpretation principles related to judgments, requiring courts to ascertain intentions from the entirety of the judgment ([20]).

## A – Application

The SCA analyzed whether continuing the private prosecution would entail undue harm, as it could extend Mr Zuma's "Stalingrad" strategy—a term indicating protracted legal battles aimed at delaying justice ([6]). The Court upheld the High Court's judgment that found Zuma's private prosecution was motivated by ulterior purposes, primarily to discredit the prosecution and obstruct his own legal proceedings. The SCA noted that allowing the continuation of the private prosecution could constitute a perpetual abuse of judicial processes without substantial grounds for prosecution ([11]).

Furthermore, the SCA closely examined the specific charges brought by Zuma against Downer and Maughan. The Court found that the disclosure charges lacked merit, being based on misattributed actions not taken by Downer but by another member of the prosecutorial team ([12]). The Court underscored that any public benefit derived from the private prosecution was overshadowed by the potential harm entailed in unfairly maligning the respondents and undermining the prosecutorial integrity and public trust in justice ([14]).

The SCA also highlighted the effects of the private prosecution on freedom of the press, indicating that Zuma’s actions could lead to chilling effects on journalistic freedoms, thus impacting the principle of open justice ([14]). The SCA referenced the ongoing public need for a competent and autonomous prosecutorial authority, noting that Zuma's conduct could erode public confidence in this vital institutional framework ([25]).

## C – Conclusion

The SCA dismissed Zuma's appeal, affirming the High Court’s decision to maintain the interdiction against his private prosecution of Downer and Maughan. The Court ordered Zuma to bear the costs, including those of two counsel on an attorney-client scale, encapsulating its stance against frivolous legal maneuvers aimed at delaying justice ([36]).

This ruling reaffirms the judiciary’s commitment to quelling litigatory tactics that obstruct justice and emphasizes the duty of courts to uphold procedural integrity. The impact of this decision extends beyond Zuma’s immediate legal situation, reinforcing a larger principle concerning the prevention of abuse of judicial processes by powerful figures in society. The judgment serves as a reiteration of the necessity for accountability within the prosecutorial system and broader implications for the rule of law in South Africa.
