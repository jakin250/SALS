Tjiroze v Appeal Board of the Financial Services Board [2020] ZACC 18

The second respondent sought leave to amend this notice to rectify the error, which Tjiroze opposed, arguing instead for a ruling that the notice be set aside ([4]–[5]).

Constitutional Court 2020 [2020] ZACC 18

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The case arises from a direct appeal to the Constitutional Court of South Africa initiated by Hitjevi Obafemi Tjiroze (the applicant), against the decisions of the Gauteng Division of the High Court ([1]–[2]). The underlying dispute began when the Financial Sector Conduct Authority (the second respondent, formerly known as the Registrar of Financial Services Providers) erroneously identified itself in a notice of intention to oppose a review application filed by Tjiroze, referencing itself incorrectly as the “Registrar of Financial Services Board” ([3]).

The second respondent sought leave to amend this notice to rectify the error, which Tjiroze opposed, arguing instead for a ruling that the notice be set aside ([4]–[5]). The High Court, presided over by Senyatsi AJ, permitted the amendment due to Tjiroze’s failure to substantiate any claimed prejudice ([6]). This was followed by Tjiroze's ill-fated attempts for leave to appeal the High Court's decision, which was denied due to a lack of reasonable prospects of success ([6]).

Subsequently, Tjiroze filed an urgent application requesting the recusal of Senyatsi AJ, claiming bias due to perceived conflicts of interest ([7]). This application was dismissed by Holland-Muller AJ due to a failure to demonstrate urgency and competence ([8]–[9]). Tjiroze then petitioned the Constitutional Court for direct appeal, asserting violations of his right to a fair hearing under section 34 of the Constitution, allegedly due to collusion between the second respondent and Judge Senyatsi ([10]).

I – Issues

The primary issues at bar include:

  1. Whether the applicant's right to a fair hearing, as delineated in section 34 of the Constitution, was infringed ([10]).
  2. The appropriateness of the applicant's subsequent appeals — including his request for the recusal of Senyatsi AJ and related nullifications — in light of the prior rulings ([10]–[11]).
  3. The mootness of the case concerning the substitution of the FSCA for the previous registry ([11]).
  4. The propriety of awarding costs on an attorney-and-client scale due to the applicant's litigation tactics ([12]).

R – Rules

The Constitutional Court's jurisdiction is defined under section 167 of the Constitution, permitting it to hear matters that raise constitutional issues ([12]). The Court referenced previous case law asserting that a case becomes moot if it no longer presents an existing or live controversy ([11]).

The decision to award costs, particularly on a punitive scale, can be guided by principles established in Public Protector v South African Reserve Bank ([12]), which advocates for punitive costs in cases demonstrating vexatious conduct or abuse of process.

A – Application

In addressing the issues, the Court found that Tjiroze's claims rested largely on factual disputes rather than genuine constitutional matters ([13]–[14]). The claim regarding Senyatsi AJ's alleged conflict of interest was dismissed as speculative and unsupported by credible evidence ([13]–[14]). Furthermore, the Court determined that since the second respondent had been substituted by the FSCA in the proceedings, any claimed prejudice by Tjiroze was moot ([14]).

The Court emphasized that amendment errors in legal documents, particularly those made inadvertently, do not merit judicial blockage without substantiated indication of real prejudice to the opposing party ([15]). Additionally, the burden of proof remains on the applicant to demonstrate valid grounds for the broader claims he pursued, which he significantly failed to do ([15]).

In assessing the applicant's conduct, the Court noted a pattern of vexatious litigation. Tjiroze had unnecessarily prolonged proceedings and unnecessarily escalated the matters by failing to engage with the substantive issues, instead focusing on procedural technicalities that were immaterial to the case ([15]). Under these circumstances, the Court deemed a punitive costs order warranted, citing Tjiroze's status as a legal professional who should have understood the implications of his allegations and the serious nature of his inappropriate claims ([16]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded that:

  1. Leave to appeal was refused, primarily due to the moot nature of the applicant's claims and lack of reasonable grounds to suggest a violation of constitutional rights ([17]).
  2. Tjiroze was ordered to pay the second respondent’s costs on an attorney-and-client scale, as a demonstrable necessity to deter similar vexatious litigation in future ([18]).

This case underscores the importance of substantive engagement with legal issues over procedural tactics, particularly within the context of constitutional rights. The ruling illustrates the balance the Court seeks to maintain between individual rights and the integrity of judicial processes, reflecting broader themes of litigation ethics and the responsibilities of legal practitioners within the judicial system.

In summary, the judgment serves to affirm existing legal principles on mootness, judicial recusal, and the imposition of costs in instances of abusive litigation. The outcome not only resolves this particular matter but sends a clear message regarding the expectations of conduct from litigants, particularly in the constitutional landscape.
