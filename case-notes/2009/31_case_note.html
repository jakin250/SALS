Constitutional Court

Abahlali baseMjondolo Movement SA v Premier of the Province of KwaZulu-Natal ([2009] ZACC 31)

The applicants contend that the Act facilitates the unlawful eviction of individuals living in informal settlements without alternative housing, undermining national legislation aimed at protecting insecure land rights.

Constitutional Court 2009 [2009] ZACC 31)

F – Facts

This application for leave to appeal before the Constitutional Court of South Africa involves the KwaZulu-Natal Elimination and Prevention of Re-emergence of Slums Act 6 of 2007 (the Act). The first applicant, the Abahlali baseMjondolo Movement SA, is a voluntary association representing many thousands of occupiers of informal dwellings in the province of KwaZulu-Natal. The second applicant, Mr Sibusiso Zikode, is the president of the movement. The four respondents are state officials in their official capacities: the Premier of KwaZulu-Natal, the MEC for Local Government, Housing and Traditional Affairs, and the national Ministers of Human Settlements and Rural Development and Land Reform ([1]–[2]).

The applicants contend that the Act facilitates the unlawful eviction of individuals living in informal settlements without alternative housing, undermining national legislation aimed at protecting insecure land rights. They specifically challenge the provincial legislature's authority to enact the law, arguing that it deals primarily with land tenure, a domain not reserved for provincial legislation. The applicants initially attacked the whole Act but later narrowed their challenge to focus on sections 9, 11, 12, 13, and 16, alleging inconsistency with the Constitution, specifically section 26(2), which guarantees the right to housing. The High Court dismissed their application, leading to the present appeal ([3]–[7]).

I – Issues

The key legal questions raised by the appeal are:

  1. Was the KwaZulu-Natal Provincial Legislature competent to enact the law in light of the division of legislative power established in the Constitution?
  2. Is section 16 of the Act consistent with the constitutional right to adequate housing as set out in section 26(2) of the Constitution?
  3. Does section 16 contravene the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act (PIE Act), the national Housing Act, and the National Housing Code? ([9]–[10]).

R – Rules

The court referred to several key legal principles:

  1. The Constitution of South Africa delineates the division of powers between national and provincial governments, particularly regarding concurrent legislative competencies outlined in Schedules 4 and 5 of the Constitution ([20]).
  2. Section 26(2) of the Constitution guarantees the right to have access to adequate housing and requires that no one be evicted from their home without just and equitable processes.
  3. The PIE Act establishes a framework for evictions that requires reasonable engagement and provides protections for unlawful occupiers ([5]–[6], [9]).

Additionally, the court reiterated the principle of purposeful and contextual interpretation, emphasizing that all laws must align with the spirit, purport, and objects of the Bill of Rights ([124]).

A – Application

In assessing the legislative competence, the court concluded that the Act primarily concerns housing rather than land tenure, thus falling within the concurrent powers of the provincial legislature. The court examined the Act's purpose, noting that it explicitly aims to eliminate slums and improve housing conditions, aligning with national housing policy ([40]–[41]).

Regarding section 16, the court found that while it mandates the commencement of eviction proceedings, it does not inherently conflict with constitutional protections. The majority opinion asserted that the provisions of the Act should be interpreted in a manner that upholds the stipulations of the PIE Act, ensuring that evictions can only occur when they are just and equitable ([56]).

The dissenting judgment opined that section 16 limits the discretion of municipalities and owners in deciding when to issue eviction proceedings and thus erodes the protections afforded under the PIE Act and constitutional law. It cited concerns about the potential for arbitrary evictions arising from the mandatory nature of section 16, effectively undermining the constitutional mandate ([112]–[124]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately granted leave to appeal and declared section 16 of the KwaZulu-Natal Elimination and Prevention of Re-emergence of Slums Act invalid. It held that the provision, while intending to facilitate slum elimination, does not adequately respect the rights enshrined in the Constitution and the protections outlined in the PIE Act. Furthermore, the court emphasized that any legislative framework must integrate mechanisms for dignified treatment of vulnerable populations ([128]–[129]).

The ruling highlights the delicate balance required between legislative power in managing housing issues and the constitutional obligation to protect the rights of marginalized groups, reaffirming the judiciary's role in upholding the rule of law and the constitutional mandate ([126]).
