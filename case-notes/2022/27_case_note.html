Social Justice Coalition and Others v Minister of Police and Others [2022] ZACC 27

The case of Social Justice Coalition and Others v Minister of Police and Others revolves around a significant failure in the allocation of policing resources in the Western Cape of South Africa, identified as unjustly discriminatory against poor and Black communities ([4], [6]).

Constitutional Court 2022 [2022] ZACC 27

FIRAC Analysis

F – Facts

The case of Social Justice Coalition and Others v Minister of Police and Others revolves around a significant failure in the allocation of policing resources in the Western Cape of South Africa, identified as unjustly discriminatory against poor and Black communities ([4], [6]). The applicants, comprising the Social Justice Coalition (SJC), Equal Education (EE), the Nyanga Community Policing Forum, along with the Women’s Legal Centre Trust as an amicus curiae, sought relief following a long-standing pattern of police resource allocation that undermined their safety and dignity ([4], [5], [19]).

The applicants challenged the system employed by the South African Police Service (SAPS) for resource allocation, arguing that it unfairly discriminated against communities based on race and economic status ([18]). This challenge arose after findings from the Khayelitsha Commission of Inquiry, which highlighted systemic biases in policing that were detrimental to these communities ([10], [12], [16]). The Equality Court had previously found that this systemic allocation of resources constituted discrimination, particularly on the unlisted ground of poverty, a finding of significant constitutional relevance in South Africa ([26], [29]).

Following the Equality Court’s determination on 14 December 2018, which recognized the unfair discrimination suffered by these communities, the state respondents failed to implement an adequate and effective remedy for the identified issues ([29], [31]). Subsequent attempts by the applicants to engage with the authorities regarding the implementation of remedies were met with delays, leading to claims of a constructive refusal of remedy as no action was taken for more than two years ([40], [106]).

I – Issues

The principal legal issues raised in the case include:

  1. Jurisdictional Power: Whether the Constitutional Court has the power to grant declaratory relief in a situation where another court has purportedly failed to deliver a remedy due to unreasonable delay ([40], [42]).
  2. Constructive Refusal: If so, did the circumstances constitute a constructive refusal of access to court for the applicants, thereby engaging the jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court? ([40], [67]).
  3. Adequacy of Remedy: Should the Constitutional Court intervene to determine the appropriate remedy or should it remit the matter back to the Equality Court? ([40], [114], [116]).

R – Rules

The legal framework relevant to this case includes:

  1. Constitution of the Republic of South Africa Act, 1996: Particularly, Section 9 (Equality) and Section 34 (Access to Courts) which provides that everyone has the right to have any dispute resolved by the application of law in a fair public hearing before a court ([1], [3]).
  2. Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000: This Act reinforces the prohibition of unfair discrimination and guides the judicial interpretation of fairness in the context of human rights ([25]).
  3. Precedent from New Clicks CC: The Court recognized the legitimacy of claiming a constructive refusal of remedy due to unreasonable delay in the context of rights guaranteed by the Constitution ([46], [82]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court, comprising a majority led by Unterhalter AJ and a minority judgment by Kollapen J, navigated complex issues regarding judicial powers and the need for effective remedies.

Majority Judgment:
Unterhalter AJ emphasized that while it is well-established that courts must avoid unnecessary interference in the processes of other courts, excessive judicial delay that hampers the access to judicial remedy must be scrutinized to sustain the principle of justice ([123], [132]). Nevertheless, the majority determined that the most appropriate avenue was for the matter to be remitted back to the Equality Court for effective resolution, underscoring the importance of allowing that court to retain jurisdiction over remedy ([114], [119]).

Minority Judgment:
Conversely, Kollapen J argued that the delay constituted a legitimate infringement of the applicants' Section 34 rights, asserting that the unreasonably protracted judicial consideration demanded intervention by the Constitutional Court. His perspective focused on the necessity of responsive judicial intervention to ensure the rule of law is upheld, highlighting systemic negligence in responding adequately to serious issues involving constitutional rights ([93], [122]).

C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Constitutional Court held that although the Equality Court had not explicitly refused relief, the undue delay in convening a remedy hearing constituted a constructive refusal of remedy. The majority ultimately granted leave to appeal but opted to remit the matter back to the Equality Court for the determination of a suitable remedy, thereby also emphasizing the need for a timeline to avoid further delays in implementing justice ([121], [122]). This case reinforces the necessity for responsive judicial intervention in instances of systemic discrimination and highlights ongoing challenges in the realization of equality and access to justice in South Africa.

The court’s decisions carry significant implications for future cases concerning discrimination and the timely administration of justice, serving to underline constitutional commitments to fundamental human rights and equality ([25], [60]).

References

  1. Constitution of the Republic of South Africa Act 1996.
  2. Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000.
  3. New Clicks CC [2006] ZACC 14; 2006 (2) SA 311 (CC).
  4. Social Justice Coalition v Minister of Police 2019 (4) SA 82 (WCC).
