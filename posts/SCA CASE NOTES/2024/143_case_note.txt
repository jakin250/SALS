Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# Case Note: African Centre for Biodiversity NPC v Minister of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries and Others (934/2023) [2024] ZASCA 143

## FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts
The case revolves around an appeal by the African Centre for Biodiversity NPC (ACB) concerning the approval of Monsanto South Africa (Pty) Ltd's application for the general release of a genetically modified maize variant known as MON87460. The regulatory framework governing the release of genetically modified organisms (GMOs) in South Africa is encapsulated in the Genetically Modified Organisms Act 15 of 1997 (Act) and the accompanying regulations ([1–2]).

Monsanto submitted its application on 14 July 2014, claiming that MON87460 was engineered to reduce yield loss under water-limited conditions. Despite objections from ACB highlighting safety and environmental concerns, the Executive Council approved the application on 12 June 2015, following a recommendation from the Advisory Committee. The ACB filed an appeal against this decision on 7 August 2015, which was subsequently dismissed by the Appeal Board on 1 September 2016. The Minister confirmed this dismissal on 2 December 2016 ([4–7]).

The ACB challenged the dismissal in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, seeking to review and set aside the decisions made by the Executive Council, the Appeal Board, and the Minister. The High Court dismissed the application on 27 June 2023 but granted leave to appeal ([9–10]).

### I – Issues
The pivotal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) include:

1. Whether the decision-makers adequately considered the risks associated with the general release of MON87460, particularly in light of the evidence and apprehensions raised by expert opinion ([10–22]).
2. Whether the precautionary principle, as enshrined in the National Environmental Management Act 107 of 1998 (NEMA), was appropriately applied by the Executive Council and other decision-makers.
3. Whether the Executive Council fulfilled its statutory obligation under Section 5(1)(a) of the Act to determine if an assessment, as required under NEMA, was necessary for Monsanto's application ([22–24]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework pertinent to the case consists of:

1. **Genetically Modified Organisms Act 15 of 1997**: This Act regulates the application and approval processes for GMOs, mandating a permit for GMOs’ release and requiring a thorough evaluation of potential risks ([1]).
   
2. **National Environmental Management Act 107 of 1998 (NEMA)**: NEMA enshrines the precautionary principle, compelling authorities to adopt protective measures when there is uncertainty regarding potential environmental harm ([11–14]).

3. **Precedent**: In **Fuel Retailers Association of Southern Africa v Director-General: Environmental Management**, the Constitutional Court emphasized the importance of a precautionary approach in environmental decision-making when scientific uncertainty exists ([14–19]).

4. **International Treaties**: The Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety reinforces the necessity for precautionary measures concerning GMOs ([13]).

### A – Application
The SCA conducted a detailed analysis of the decision-making process undertaken by the Executive Council, Appeal Board, and Minister, determining that they failed to properly engage with the safety concerns raised regarding MON87460 ([20]). The court highlighted that the Executive Council had not issued any express determination regarding whether an environmental impact assessment was required under Section 5(1)(a), which contravened the Act's mandatory obligations. This failure rendered the approval process fundamentally flawed and indicated a lack of independent scrutiny of Monsanto’s claimed safety ([22–24]).

Additionally, the court reaffirmed the necessity of applying the precautionary principle, which had not been adequately considered by the decision-makers. The absence of sufficient scientific certainty regarding the safety risks associated with MON87460 warranted a more cautious approach, necessitating thorough risk assessments and the imposition of protective measures before granting approval ([10–17]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, delivering a decisive critique of the original decisions made by the Executive Council, Appeal Board, and Minister. The court ruled that these decisions were unconstitutional due to their failure to observe the statutory obligations imposed by the Genetically Modified Organisms Act and NEMA. It emphasized the need for thorough evaluations to protect human health and the environment before the release of GMOs, particularly in cases where scientific evidence is inconclusive. The application was referred back for reconsideration under the proper statutory framework, reaffirming the importance of rigorous independent review in environmental decision-making ([25]).

This judgment not only reinforces the legal standards regarding GMO approvals in South Africa but also strengthens the applicability of the precautionary principle, making it clear that decision-makers cannot rely solely on applicant assertions without adequate supportive evidence. The court’s conclusions have significant implications for future GMO applications and the regulatory landscape surrounding environmental protection in South Africa.
