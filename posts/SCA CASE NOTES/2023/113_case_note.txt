Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: Rolston Pillay v The State (451/2022) [2023] ZASCA 113

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])

On 12 June 2020, Rolston Pillay (the appellant) was convicted by the Regional Court, Benoni for the murder of 17-year-old Veli Molala, in contravention of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 and the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (the CPA). The Regional Court sentenced the appellant to 15 years’ imprisonment, ruling that there were no substantial and compelling circumstances justifying a deviation from the minimum sentence. Following this conviction, the appellant sought to introduce further evidence and appealed the conviction and sentence. The full bench of the Gauteng Division of the High Court dismissed the appeal, prompting this appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which was heard on 2 May 2023 and decided on 27 July 2023.

The pivotal events occurred on 19 May 2017. The appellant, a member of the Ekurhuleni Metro Police Department (EMPD), responded to reports of a robbery at Wordsworth School, pursuing two alleged robbers. During the chase, he stated that gunfire was exchanged between him and the alleged robbers, resulting in the death of one of them. The appellant asserted that he acted in self-defence, fearing for his life when shots were fired at him. 

Key to the proceedings was the testimony of a single eyewitness, Mpilo Kubeka, who observed the events unfold. His contradictory statements and later revelations cast significant doubt on his credibility. The original trial relied heavily on his initial account that the deceased had been unarmed and that the appellant had fired first, whereas subsequent evidence revealed Kubeka himself admitted to witnessing a firearm in the possession of one of the alleged robbers, information he initially withstood under potential coercion. 

The circumstances surrounding the police's handling of the investigation and the presentation of witness statements further complicated the case, culminating in the central issue of whether the appellant had acted in self-defence.

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 11–15])

The principal issues before the SCA included:

1. **The credibility of a single eyewitness**: Was the evidence provided by Mr. Kubeka sufficient to sustain a conviction, especially given its contradictions and the emergence of further evidence admitting a firearm's presence?
   
2. **The application of self-defence**: Did the appellant successfully demonstrate that he acted in self-defence, and was the prosecution's burden met to show that his actions were unlawful?

3. **Evaluation of evidence**: Did the full bench err in its assessment and acceptance of the evidence from Mr. Kubeka without adequately addressing its flaws and contradictions?

4. **Assessment of onus of proof**: Did the full bench misapply the principles surrounding the onus of proof in relation to self-defence?

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 16–19])

The SCA's ruling relied upon various legal principles, particularly concerning the evaluation of evidence from single witnesses. Relevant case law included:

- **S v Webber** and **S v Sauls**: These judgments articulate the caution required when evaluating single eyewitness testimony, emphasizing that it must be "clear and satisfactory" to sustain a conviction.
  
- **Section 208 of the CPA**: This provision states that an accused may be convicted on the uncorroborated evidence of a single witness, provided that the evidence is found credible.
  
- **S v Ntuli** and **S v De Oliveira**: These cases establish that the onus remains on the prosecution to demonstrate guilt beyond a reasonable doubt, even when self-defence is pleaded. The credibility of the defence must be considered if it is reasonable to believe it could be true.

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 20–19])

In its analysis, the SCA highlighted several critical missteps by the full bench of the High Court in evaluating the evidence and the application of the legal principles surrounding self-defence. 

1. **Credibility of the Eyewitness**: The SCA underscored the fundamental errors in the full bench's acceptance of Mr. Kubeka's testimony. The contradictions in his accounts and the admissions of coercion significantly undermined his reliability, which the full bench failed to address thoroughly. His later acknowledgment of having witnessed a firearm in possession of one of the alleged robbers was deemed crucial, as it directly contradicted his earlier statements used to convict the appellant.

2. **Self-Defence and Onus of Proof**: The court noted that the evidence presented supported the appellant's claim of acting in self-defence. The prosecution failed to conclusively demonstrate that the appellant's actions were unlawful, particularly in light of emerging evidence that one of the alleged robbers was armed. The fundamental principle that the accused is presumed innocent until proven guilty was emphasized, and it was found that reasonable doubt existed regarding the appellant's guilt.

3. **Evaluation of Evidence**: The SCA evaluated the procedural flaws in the way the evidence was handled. The gap between the events and the police testimonies' timing raised further questions about reliability. The SCA concluded that the full bench's reasoning did not adequately consider the implications of the procedural time lapses and inconsistencies presented throughout the trial.

Ultimately, the SCA determined that the totality of the evidence, particularly the new evidence admitted under s 309B(5)(a) of the CPA, warranted a re-evaluation of the appellant's conviction and sentence.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 20–21])

The SCA upheld the appeal, concluding that the full bench had misdirected itself in handling critical aspects of the case. The court's holding established that the prosecution failed to discharge its onus of proof of demonstrating the appellant's guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. The socioeconomic implications of the reliance on a single eyewitness in a serious crime, combined with the necessity for a meticulous assessment of the evidence presented in self-defence claims, were underscored.

The judgment ultimately resulted in the setting aside of both the conviction and sentence, with the order that the appellant be found not guilty and discharged. This case exemplifies the importance of careful scrutiny of eyewitness testimony, the rigorous application of legal standards when assessing self-defence claims, and the inherent presumption of innocence afforded to the accused in South African law.

The implications of this ruling extend beyond the individual case, highlighting judicial responsibilities in ensuring that the rights of the accused are respected and that the principles of fair trial and burdens of proof are strictly adhered to in the South African legal system.
