Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2001

# FIRAC Case Note: **BANELE SINDANI v JP VAN DER MERWE & Others**

### F – Facts

The appeal arises from a defamation action filed by Banele Sindani (the appellant), who serves as the chief executive officer of Athletics South Africa ([para 2]). The appellant initiated proceedings against three respondents: JP Van der Merwe, the first respondent; the editor of *Rapport*, the second respondent; and journalist Johan Van Wyk, the third respondent ([para 3]). The controversy stemmed from an article published in *Rapport* on September 28, 1997, in which the third respondent reported on a conversation between the appellant and Van der Merwe, during which the appellant allegedly referred to Van der Merwe as "white trash" and suggested he should leave the country with his "white pals" ([paras 4–5]).

The article accused the appellant of making racially derogatory remarks after Van der Merwe criticized Athletics South Africa for not supporting athletes financially. The appellant claimed the article was defamatory, asserting that it portrayed him as a racist and that the imputations were understood as an attack on his character ([para 6]). The respondents denied the defamatory nature of the article and raised various legal defenses ([para 6]).

Before the trial commenced, the Court a quo, led by Judge Boruchowitz, bifurcated the defamation issue for preliminary determination ([para 7]). The trial court subsequently held that the article was not defamatory and dismissed the appellant's claim, leading to the present appeal ([paras 8–9]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal question before the court was whether the imputation that the appellant called the first respondent "white trash" in the article was defamatory ([para 1]). Specifically, two sub-issues were essential:

1. What is the ordinary or natural meaning of the article in question?
2. Is that meaning of a defamatory nature, particularly in the context of the South African legal and social landscape? ([paras 10–11]).

### R – Rules

Several legal precepts and precedents are relevant in this case:

1. **Definition of Defamation**: Defamation involves the publication of a statement that can harm the reputation of an individual ([para 15]).
  
2. **Objective Test for Meaning**: The ordinary meaning of words in a statement is determined based on what a reasonable reader of ordinary intelligence would interpret, considering the context of the entire article ([para 11]).
   
3. **Racial Context**: The appreciation of race in language is critical in assessing defamatory implications. Terms with derogatory connotations can be interpreted as racially charged, impacting the perceived intent of the speaker ([paras 12–13]).

4. **Precedents**: The case refers to *SA Associated Newspapers Ltd v Samuels* and *Argus Printing & Publishing Co v Esselen's Estate*, which elucidate standards for establishing defamation and how reasonable readers perceive articles ([para 10]).

### A – Application

The court analyzed both the natural meaning and the potential defamation of the article. It concluded that the words attributed to the appellant were indeed derogatory and held racial implications. The court rejected the respondents' assertion that ordinary readers would fail to see the racial connotation of "white trash," noting that words in media do not carry mere dictionary definitions but also depend on the reader’s context ([paras 12–13]).

However, the Court a quo held that the appellant's alleged use of racially derogatory language was portrayed contextually as a reaction to Van der Merwe's accusations of racism. The reasoning suggested that the appellant’s condemnation was based on his belief that the first respondent was racist rather than purely on his racial identity ([para 13]). This subtlety made the Court a quo conclude that the article wouldn't be read as portraying the appellant as a racist or prejudicial, aiming instead to emphasize a counterclaim against Van der Merwe.

In dissent, the appellate court maintained that the reasonable reader, upon an initial reading of the article, would interpret the appellant's conduct as malicious, triggering strong social disapproval and a negative societal impression. The imputation of using racially charged language without justification could not be considered defensible, thus the article was deemed defamatory ([paras 14–15]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that the article was defamatory of the appellant, overturning the lower court's decision ([para 16]). The court's ruling emphasized the grave societal implications of racism and the importance of holding individuals accountable for engaging in racially derogatory language. However, the court acknowledged the procedural intricacies remaining, including the respondents’ defenses that had yet to be fully adjudicated. It consequently ordered that while the appellant had succeeded in proving defamation, the question of costs would remain contingent upon the resolution of the remaining issues in the trial ([paras 17–18]).

The decision highlights the judiciary's role in upholding the principles of dignity and equality in South African society as stated in the Constitution, emphasizing zero tolerance for expressions that could incite racial prejudice. The judgment reinforces the idea that the impact and context of words, particularly those with racial implications, are paramount in defamation claims, setting a precedent for future cases involving similar allegations.
