Source Folder: 2003

# FIRAC Case Note: The Municipality of Plettenberg Bay v Van Dyk & Co Inc (CCT 42/03)

## F – Facts

The applicant, the Municipality of Plettenberg Bay, sought leave to appeal a judgment by the High Court in Cape Town ordering it to pay the respondent, Van Dyk & Co Inc, the sum of R69,684.74 for services rendered ([1]). The High Court had established that the respondent provided services that were accepted by the applicant, and that the charges for these services were reasonable ([2]). The key issue was whether the employee of the Municipality who instructed the respondent had the authority to do so. The High Court determined that the employee had ostensible authority, applying the rule in *Turquand*, and thus estopped the applicant from denying authority ([2]).

The applicant contended that the contract formed with the respondent was null and void due to a lack of actual authority from the employee, citing section 58(1) of the Municipal Ordinance 20 of 1974 (Cape), which mandates a special resolution from the Council for delegating powers ([3]). It was acknowledged that there was no such resolution, further supporting the municipality's argument that the contract was invalid ([3]).

In its application for leave to appeal, the applicant asserted that the doctrine of estoppel was incompatible with the constitutional framework, arguing that something unlawful could not be legitimized through estoppel or the *Turquand* rule ([4]). The municipality claimed that it would be a waste of resources to comply with the High Court’s rules for appeal given the negative likelihood of receiving a certificate for appeal ([7]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal question was whether the applicant’s failure to comply with the procedural requirements of Rule 18(2) of the Constitutional Court's Rules warranted condonation for the purpose of allowing the appeal to proceed ([5])_. Additionally, the court needed to consider the procedural validity of the employment decisions made by the municipality against the essential legal framework set out by the Municipal Ordinance ([3], [4]). 

## R – Rules

The pertinent legal frameworks included:

1. **Rule 18(2) of the Constitutional Court** - This rule stipulates that a litigant aggrieved by a lower court's decision must apply for a certificate from that court, which asserts that the appeal has substantive merit and that there is sufficient evidence to justify an appeal to the Constitutional Court ([1]).
  
2. **Estoppel Doctrine and the Rule in *Turquand*** - Under this doctrine, a party may be held to account for actions taken under the presumption of authority granted to an agent, even if that authority is not actual, provided there is no express notice against the authority ([2]).

3. **Section 58(1) of the Municipal Ordinance 20 of 1974** - This section requires a special resolution from the council for delegating powers to employees, placing the burden of authority on the municipality in this case ([3]).

## A – Application

The court determined that the applicant did not establish a sufficient case for condonation. The applicant's deliberate non-compliance with Rule 18(2) was noted as a significant factor, which resulted from its perception that compliance would serve no purpose ([8]). The court stressed that the procedural rules existed for a reason, specifically to ensure that the High Court could properly evaluate the substantive issues at hand ([10]). 

The court also addressed the municipality's argument that pursuing the required certificate would have been a futile exercise due to the High Court's prior refusal to grant leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal. However, the Constitutional Court emphasized the importance of adhering to procedural rules, indicating that any procedural failings could detrimentally affect the rights of parties involved, especially when there were disputes regarding the sufficiency of evidence ([9], [12]).

The applicant's reliance on the constitutional implications was deemed insufficient without appropriate litigation in the lower court. The procedural oversight consequently impacted the court’s ability to engage thoroughly with both parties’ arguments, especially concerning the nature and validity of the authority utilized by the applicant's employee ([9]).

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the application for leave to appeal, emphasizing the applicant's conscious failure to comply with established court rules can warrant denial of appeal rights. The court upheld that the deliberate disregard for these procedural requirements necessitated consequences. The applicant was ordered to bear the costs of the application as a result of its non-compliance with procedural norms ([13]).

This case underlined the necessity for litigants to adhere to procedural requirements, reflecting the balance between substantive justice and procedural integrity within the constitutional framework of South Africa. It reinforced the principle that procedural rules are not mere formalities but serve essential functions in ensuring fair adjudication in legal systems.
