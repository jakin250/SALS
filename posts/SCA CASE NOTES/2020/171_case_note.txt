Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note: Johannesburg Society of Advocates and Another v Seth Azwihangwisi Nthai and Others

**Case Citation:** Johannesburg Society of Advocates and Another v Seth Azwihangwisi Nthai and Others (879/2020 and 880/2019) [2020] ZASCA 171 (15 December 2020).

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa.

---

## F – Facts
The appeal stems from a complex disciplinary case involving Advocate Seth Azwihangwisi Nthai, who was originally admitted as an advocate on 19 January 1988 and later became a member of the Pretoria Society of Advocates (PSA) in 1996, achieving senior status in 2006 ([para 2]). In 2007, Nthai was appointed to represent the South African Government before an International Arbitration Tribunal but was later found to have solicited a bribe of R5 million from the claimants, in violation of his fiduciary duties ([para 3]). 

His misconduct came to light when the claimants recorded their conversations with him. The PSA initiated disciplinary proceedings after the State Attorney lodged a formal complaint ([para 4–5]). On 6 April 2010, the disciplinary committee found Nthai guilty of several serious ethical violations, leading to his removal from the role of advocate as per the provisions of the Admission of Advocates Act 74 of 1964 ([para 8]). 

In 2018, Nthai sought readmission to the Bar, serving his application only on the Polokwane Society of Advocates (POLSA), which supported his application. The PSA and the Johannesburg Society of Advocates (JSA) contested Nthai’s readmission, asserting that the ethical concerns raised in his case rendered him unfit for the profession ([paras 10–11]). Despite substantial opposition from these professional bodies, the Limpopo High Court initially granted Nthai’s application for readmission ([para 11]).

After subsequent appeals, multiple procedural disputes emerged over the standing of the PSA and JSA, as well as the application of the Legal Practice Act 28 of 2014, which had implications for how readmission applications would be processed moving forward ([para 20–21]). 

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the High Court’s readmission ruling, highlighting failings in recognizing the significant ethical breaches and asserting that Nthai did not meet the burden of proof necessary to demonstrate his rehabilitation ([para 99]).

---

## I – Issues
The core issues for determination were:
1. Whether the High Court erred in granting readmission to Mr. Nthai despite his previous serious misconduct.
2. The standing and locus standi of the JSA and PSA in opposing Nthai's readmission application, particularly concerning changes in professional body governance imposed by the Legal Practice Act ([para 20]).
3. The standards and evidentiary burden required for an advocate previously struck from the roll to prove their fitness for readmission ([paras 17–19]).

---

## R – Rules
The Supreme Court relied on various legal principles and precedents related to the readmission of advocates:

1. **Locus Standi**: The Court observed that professional bodies like the JSA and PSA, as guardian entities (custodes morum), retained a right to oppose the readmission of members previously struck off ([para 19]).

2. **Burden of Proof**: The burden lies on the applicant for readmission to show, on a balance of probabilities, that they have undergone a significant reformation and that the character defects that warranted their expulsion no longer exist ([para 17]).

3. **General Counsel Ethics**: The principles governing the ethical obligations of advocates and the requirements for maintaining the profession's integrity informed the Court’s reasoning ([paras 1–2]).

4. **Interpretation of Statutes**: The implications of repealing the Advocates Act in favor of the Legal Practice Act were assessed in determining procedural validity during the readmission application ([paras 22–23]).

5. **Precedents on Misconduct**: The decisions in *Swartzberg v Law Society of the Northern Provinces* and other relevant cases were cited concerning the seriousness of prior misconduct and its implications for trust within the profession ([para 37]).

---

## A – Application
The Court critically assessed Nthai's ethical violations, concluding that his solicitation of a bribe was not a mere lapse but a deliberate and systemic breach of professional conduct ([paras 44–46]). The findings highlighted that his actions compromised not only his client but also jeopardized the integrity of the legal profession. 

Regarding the burden of proof, the Court scrutinized the High Court's acceptance of Nthai's claims of reformation, ultimately determining that Nthai failed to demonstrate genuine contrition or an understanding of the gravity of his prior transgressions ([paras 99–100]). It emphasized the role of the applicant to present compelling evidence of their rehabilitation, which the High Court underestimated in its judgment ([para 86]).

The procedural aspects of the readmission process were also significant. The Court clarified that professional bodies retained their custodian roles even under the new legal framework, ensuring that the applications would consider the comprehensive body of evidence regarding Nthai’s character and history ([paras 15–19]).

---

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal resolved to uphold the appeal, thereby reinstating the view that Mr. Nthai did not adequately establish his fitness for readmission to practice as an advocate. The judgment underscored the legal profession's cardinal requirement for integrity and ethical conduct, determining that an advocate who has displayed significant misconduct must demonstrate exceptional and clear rehabilitation efforts to regain their professional standing ([paragraphs 118–119]). 

The implications of this decision reinforce the stringent scrutiny placed on the character and conduct of legal practitioners, emphasizing that the integrity of the profession necessitates unwavering adherence to ethical standards and full accountability for past transgressions. The Court's directive for the Registrar to forward their ruling to the National Director of Public Prosecutions signals the potential for criminal liability based on the severity of Nthai's alleged actions ([para 118]). 

This judgment serves as a critical marker in the evolving landscape of legal ethics within South Africa's juridical framework, particularly as it pertains to the regulation of advocates and the integrity of the profession.
