Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note: **AON South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Van den Heever NO**

**Neutral Citation**: AON South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Van den Heever NO (615/2016) 2017 ZASCA 66 (30 May 2017)

## F - Facts

This case pertains to the liquidation of New Protector Group Holdings (Pty) Ltd ("New Protector") stemming from its acquisition of the business of Protector Group Holdings (Pty) Ltd ("Protector"), financed by the Industrial Development Corporation (IDC) ([1]). New Protector liquidated following payments made to Glenrand MIB Financial Services (Pty) Ltd ("Financial Services"), which was a wholly owned subsidiary of Glenrand MIB Ltd ("Glenrand"). The payments, amounting to R63 million, included R50 million to Financial Services for its 65% stake in Protector ([2]). The liquidators of Financial Services subsequently pursued AON South Africa (Pty) Ltd ("AON"), which had acquired Glenrand's business and liabilities. AON raised a special plea of res judicata, arguing that the claims being pursued by the liquidators of Financial Services had already been resolved adversely to them in previous litigation ([3], [17]).

The previous litigation included claims against both Financial Services and Glenrand, with the liquidators of Protector seeking to recover funds they claimed were wrongfully disbursed ([9]). AON contended that the issues in the current proceedings were settled in the previous action, invoking the concept of issue estoppel as a basis for their plea ([21]). The High Court dismissed AON's special plea of res judicata, leading to this appeal ([24]).

## I - Issues

The primary legal questions raised in this appeal revolve around whether the liquidators of Financial Services are barred from pursuing their claims against AON based on the doctrine of res judicata or issue estoppel. Specifically, the court considered:

1. Whether the parties in the present claim are materially the same as those in the previous case.
2. Whether the issues raised in both actions are identical or sufficiently similar, engaging the principle of res judicata.
3. Whether the claims in the current litigation are barred by the findings made in the previous judgment ([21], [24]).

## R - Rules

The legal context within which this judgment is navigated is primarily grounded in the doctrine of res judicata and issue estoppel, which requires:

1. An identity of parties (idem actor): The parties involved must be the same in both actions.
2. An identity of issues (eadem quaestio): The substantive issues must be the same, effectively engaging the same cause of action and relief sought ([22], [23]).

The High Court's ruling, which held that the claims were different due to distinct plaintiffs, was a critical aspect of the scrutiny laid upon it by the Supreme Court of Appeal ([24]).

## A - Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal considered the relationship between the liquidators of Financial Services and the liquidators of Protector, determining that despite being separate legal entities, their similar interest and purpose meant there was an effective identity of interest. This was sequenced against the backdrop of financial dealings where Financial Services was essentially a vehicle for holding Glenrand's interest in Protector, with no independent creditors other than Protector itself ([26]).

On the issue of the identity of claims, the Supreme Court determined that the prior judgment had settled claims regarding the R50 million and the underlying transactions, focusing on the concept of collusion and dishonesty alleged against the parties. The findings from the initial trial, asserting that no fraud was committed by the directors of Glenrand or Financial Services, effectively precluded any subsequent allegations of a collusive scheme in the current matter ([30], [40]).

In rejecting the notion of compartmentalisation that suggested subsequent collusive actions could exist, the Court emphasized that the previous findings negated any claim to dishonesty and collusion concerning the payment of the R50 million and the subsequent dividend ([39], [40]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld AON's appeal, reversing the High Court's dismissal of its special plea and embraced the principles underlying res judicata and issue estoppel. The Court concluded that the liquidators of Financial Services were barred from pursuing claims concerning the R50 million and associated transactions, as these issues had already been litigated and resolved in AON's favor in the previous case ([41], [42]).

The importance of this ruling lies in its reaffirmation of the need for judicial economies and finality, particularly in complex insolvency proceedings where multiple layers of corporate structure and liquidation are involved. The Court's decision serves as a precedent reinforcing the application of issue estoppel in commercial disputes, ensuring that parties cannot repeatedly litigate the same issues under different guises, thereby preserving the integrity of previous judicial determinations.
