Constitutional Court 1999 [1999] ZACC 10

F – Facts

In this case, the third and fourth respondents, members of the South African National Defence Force (SANDF), were facing charges in an ordinary court martial convened under the Defence Act 44 of 1957 (the Defence Act) and the Military Discipline Code. The court martial proceedings were initially adjourned to allow the respondents to seek a review of certain orders made by the presiding officer, Lieutenant-Colonel Mardon (first appellant). The first order mandated that the proceedings be conducted in camera, classifying the entire record as secret ([paras 1–2]).

The second order, made on April 7, 1997, denied access to the military intelligence source reports sought by the Freedom of Expression Institute (first respondent) and M & G Media (PTY) Limited (second respondent), who argued that their constitutional right to freedom of expression, which encompasses the freedom of the press, had been infringed. The court martial ruled that these entities lacked locus standi ([paras 3–4]).

On December 18, 1998, the Cape of Good Hope High Court declared the challenged provisions of the Defence Act and the Code unconstitutional and invalid ([para 5]). The appellants appealed this decision under section 172(2)(d) of the Constitution, which is relevant for confirming declarations of constitutional invalidity ([paras 5–6]).

Before the hearing before the Constitutional Court, the Military Discipline Supplementary Measures Act 16 of 1999 (the new Act) was enacted, which introduced a new military court system and repealed certain provisions of the Defence Act. This prompted the Constitutional Court to consider whether the appeal had become moot due to the new legislation ([paras 7–9]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues in this case revolved around the constitutional validity of the framework governing the ordinary court martial, including questions regarding the court's independence, the qualifications of judicial officers, and the executive's interference in court proceedings. The key constitutional query was whether the ordinary court martial could be considered an "ordinary court" as understood under the South African Constitution ([paras 4–5]).

Additionally, the court had to determine whether the appeal had become moot following the introduction of the new Act, which significantly changed the legal landscape concerning military prosecutions and whether the Constitutional Court should exercise its discretion to hear the appeal in this context ([paras 9–12]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal principles included:

  1. Constitutional Provisions: South Africa's Constitution guarantees the right to a fair trial and safeguards for judicial independence within section 16 (freedom of expression) and section 34 (access to courts).
  2. Legal Precedents: The court referenced prior decisions emphasizing the necessity of judicial independence and the minimum qualifications required for judicial officers, grounded in the Constitution ([paras 4–5]).
  3. Section 172(2) of the Constitution: This section necessitates that a declaration of constitutional invalidity by a High Court must be confirmed by the Constitutional Court to have any force ([para 5]).
  4. Supreme Court Act: While section 21A empowers appellate courts to dismiss moot appeals, the Constitutional Court retains discretion regarding whether it should hear appeals under section 172(2) of the Constitution ([paras 12–16]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court analyzed the implications of the new Act and its provisions, which directly affected the proceedings against the respondents. The Court concluded that the new legislative framework rendered the previous challenges moot, as the new Act provided a fresh start for any pending cases, including those that might have been previously subject to constitutional challenge under the now-repealed provisions ([paras 9–12]).

Furthermore, the Court found that the constitutional invalidity of previous legislation would not bear practical consequences on the parties involved, effectively negating the need for a ruling on the appeals since the orders sought by the appellants would serve no purpose in light of the legislative changes ([para 17]).

In regard to Gihwala AJ's alleged conflict of interest, the Court determined this issue had similarly been overtaken by events due to the mootness of the primary appeal ([paras 20–23]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded that no practical effect would result from further proceedings regarding the constitutional invalidity of the prior legislation, thus dismissing the appeal without cost ([paras 24–25]). The decision highlighted the transitional provisions of the new Act and the necessity for any future challenges to be based on the new legislative framework rather than the defunct provisions previously in place. The ruling exemplified the Court's approach in balancing judicial authority with legislative changes in the interest of maintaining an effective and just legal framework in military proceedings.
