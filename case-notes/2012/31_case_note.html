Detailed FIRAC Case Note: Constitutional Court of South Africa Case CCT 109/12

The appellants sought relief on three primary grounds: (a) common law contractual rights; (b) infringement of constitutional rights under section 19(1)(b); and (c) judicial review under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([para 10]).

Constitutional Court 2012 CCT 109/12

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])

The case involves Mpho Ramakatsa and five other members of the African National Congress (ANC) in the Free State, who appealed the dismissal of their application by the Free State High Court. This application aimed to invalidate the decisions and outcomes of the ANC's Free State Provincial Conference held from June 21-24, 2012. The applicants contended that there were substantial irregularities in the branch meetings that had elected delegates to the conference. They affirmed their standing under section 38 of the Constitution, arguing that their right to participate in political party activities, enshrined in section 19, was infringed. The application was initially dismissed on procedural grounds without addressing the merits ([para 2]).

The appellants sought relief on three primary grounds: (a) common law contractual rights; (b) infringement of constitutional rights under section 19(1)(b); and (c) judicial review under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([para 10]).

The High Court did not consider the substantive merits due to various technical objections brought forth by the respondents ([paras 11-12]). These included grounds related to service validity, procedural defects regarding notices, and the assertion that the applicants did not exhaust internal remedies ([para 12]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 12–18])

The issues before theConstitutional Court primarily revolved around the following constitutional and legal questions:

  1. Constitutional Validity of Procedural Grounds: Did the Free State High Court correctly dismiss the application on procedural grounds without adjudicating the substantive merits of the applicants' claims concerning the alleged infringements of their constitutional rights to participate in political activities?
  2. Irregularities in Party Processes: Were the elections and subsequent decisions made at the Free State Provincial Conference compliant with the ANC's constitution and the relevant laws, particularly regarding the representation and election of delegates?
  3. Right to Participate in Political Processes: Did the actions of the ANC and its provincial executive infringe upon the applicants' rights under section 19 of the Constitution, which guarantees the right to participate in activities of a political party?

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 14–36])

The Court considered several key legal principles and precedents:

  • Section 38 of the Constitution: This provision allows individuals to approach a competent court claiming an infringement or threat to rights in the Bill of Rights ([para 9]). The appellants argued that their political rights under section 19 had been violated ([para 14]).
  • Section 19 of the Constitution: This section guarantees the rights to form and participate in political parties, emphasizing the significance of lawful and democratic processes within political organizations ([paras 63-72]).
  • Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA): While the appellants sought relief based on judicial review under PAJA, a significant portion of the judgment focused on whether their contractual rights as party members were violated ([para 10]).
  • Case Law: The judgment referred to Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd and Administrator, Transvaal v Theletsane, guiding principles related to motion proceedings and the treatment of bare denials in affidavits, which influence the determination of disputes of fact ([para 56]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 19–120])

The Constitutional Court, while analyzing the procedural dismissals, emphasized that:

  1. Substance vs. Form: The Court criticized the High Court’s prioritization of technical procedural defects over the substantive issues at hand, asserting that the merits of the case should have been evaluated as the allegations involved serious irregularities that could undermine democratic processes ([para 20]).
  2. Irregularity Findings: The Court found that several irregularities indeed occurred in the election processes leading to the Provincial Conference. Key allegations included the manipulation of delegate elections, exclusion of eligible branch members, and the failure to conduct valid audits as per ANC guidelines ([paras 90-120]).
  3. Constitutional Rights Enforcement: The Court reaffirmed that members have a right to ensure compliance with the ANC’s constitution and that such enforcement falls within the purview of constitutional guarantees, thereby framing the appellants’ claims as legitimate under section 19 ([paras 63-68]).

Through this lens, the Court determined that the applicants' rights to participate in their party had been severely compromised by the actions of the ANC and its leadership, culminating in a breach of the constitutional provisions designed to safeguard democratic participation ([para 118]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 121–133])

The Court ultimately upheld the appeal, setting aside the High Court's dismissal of the application. The findings were:

  1. Invalidation of the Provincial Conference: The Court declared the outcomes of the Free State Provincial Conference held from June 21-24, 2012, as unlawful and invalid, citing the various established irregularities affecting the legitimacy of the conference ([para 3]).
  2. No Costs Order: Despite the substantial success of the appellants, the Court refrained from issuing a costs order, citing the sensitive nature of internal party disputes and the importance of fostering reconciliation within the ANC ([para 126]).
  3. Significance of the Ruling: This ruling underscores the constitutional obligation of political parties to adhere to their own constitutions and ensures that members can hold their leadership accountable for maintaining democratic processes. It also emphasizes the role of the judiciary in protecting the rights of individuals in political contexts, setting a precedent for similar disputes in the future ([paras 126-133]).

The ruling reflects the importance of internal democracy within political parties as a cornerstone of South African constitutional democracy, thereby reinforcing the principles of accountability, representation, and lawful conduct within the political sphere.
