Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: Kekana v The State (37/2018) [2018] ZASCA 148

## F – Facts

The appellant, Lesiba Simon Kekana, was charged with the premeditated murder of his four children—Hlologelo (13), Keneilwe (10), Bokang (6), and Lekgoledi (4)—alongside one count of assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm against his wife, Mrs. Lorraine Kekana ([1]–[2]). These matters arose from events occurring in the early hours of 6 September 2015, in Moletlane, Limpopo Province. The appellant, after an argument with Mrs. Kekana regarding her alleged extramarital affair, murdered his children by slitting their throats. Subsequently, he attempted to take his own life, which failed, leading to his arrest by the police ([1]–[8]).

On 25 April 2016, the appellant pleaded guilty to all five counts, submitting a written plea statement that referenced the sentencing provisions under section 51(2) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 (the CLAA). The trial court sentenced him to 20 years imprisonment per murder count, with counts two, three, and four running concurrently with count one, resulting in an effective sentence of 52 years ([3]–[4])—a decision he later appealed but was unsuccessful at the full court ([5]).

## I – Issues

The appeal brought forth a few critical legal questions:
1. Was the sentence imposed by the trial court harsh, disproportionate, and shocking to the community? ([9])
2. Did the trial court misdirect itself by not considering the possibility of life imprisonment under section 51(1) of the CLAA, based on the appellant's guilty plea referencing section 51(2)? ([12], [13])
3. Did the appellant's actions demonstrate premeditation, excluding the possibility of a lesser sentence under section 51(2)? ([14]–[20])

## R – Rules

The Supreme Court of Appeal relied on several statutory provisions and precedents in addressing the appeal:

1. **Section 51 of the CLAA**: Sets forth the minimum sentences for specified serious crimes, including premeditated murder, which can warrant life imprisonment unless substantial and compelling circumstances are presented ([2]).

2. **Section 322(6) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA)**: Grants the court of appeal the authority to impose a sentence more severe than that imposed by the trial court ([10]).

3. **Precedents**:
   - **S v Mathebula**: Emphasizes that a trial court must identify aggravating factors when departing from minimum sentences ([11]).
   - **S v Ngubane**: Discusses the conceptual framework of the prosecutor’s acceptance of a guilty plea to lesser charges ([16]).
   - **S v Kekana (2014)**: Clarifies that premeditation need not involve a lengthy planning process, suggesting even a few minutes can suffice ([37]).

## A – Application

The court began by addressing the ambiguity surrounding the appellant's reference to section 51(2) in his plea. It was determined that such references do not inherently limit the trial court's sentencing discretion ([14]–[22]). The finding concluded that the trial court had misinterpreted its jurisdiction regarding the applicability of life sentences—believing it was constrained by the accepted plea when, in fact, it was not bound ([29]).

In evaluating the nature of the murders, the court pointed to the brutal and methodical nature of the acts—indicative of premeditation ([36]). The gruesome facts presented, including the psychological torment inflicted on the children and their mother, were emphasized as aggravating factors that merited the harshest sentence available under the law ([32]–[39]).

The court weighed the appellant’s personal circumstances against the gravity of the crimes. They determined that his status as a first offender and his prior mental state could not outweigh the heinous nature of the acts committed ([31]–[42]). The court also considered the broader societal implications, stressing that justice must serve as a deterrent against similar future offences.

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, recognizing the trial court's misdirection in not considering life imprisonment as a viable sentence option. It ruled that substantial compelling circumstances did not exist to reduce the sentence, thus re-sentencing the appellant to life imprisonment for each murder count, with imprisonment for the assault charge remaining intact, but running concurrently with count one ([43]). 

This case signifies the court’s commitment to addressing violence against vulnerable populations and establishing stringent sentencing to reflect public indignation, ultimately asserting that heinous and premeditative acts, especially against children, warrant the most severe penalties available under the law.
