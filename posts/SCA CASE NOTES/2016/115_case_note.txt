Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

### FIRAC Case Note: Malele v S; Ngobeni v S [2016] ZASCA 115

#### F – Facts

In this case, the **Supreme Court of Appeal** of South Africa dealt with two applications for leave to appeal, following convictions for murder stemming from a police incident. The first application was lodged by **Meshack Malele** (case no. 723/16), who was the first accused in a trial held in the **Gauteng Division of the High Court**, where he was convicted alongside three other individuals (the applicants in case no. 724/16). Collectively, they faced charges related to the murder of **Silvesta Jossefa Marcia**, a taxi driver. On **25 August 2015**, all accused were convicted of murder and subsequently sentenced to **15 years' imprisonment** on **11 November 2015** ([paras 1–2]).

The incident in question occurred on **26 February 2013**, when the deceased was stopped by police for allegedly obstructing traffic. Following an argument, the deceased was restrained and eventually dragged behind a police vehicle, leading to fatal injuries. The trial court found that the applicants acted with a common purpose, having foresaw the potential fatal outcome of their actions ([paras 3–7]).

The applicants sought leave to appeal their convictions, which was initially denied in December 2015. Subsequently, on **3 May 2016**, their application for special leave to appeal was rejected again by two judges of the SCA. In light of these developments, the applicants filed separate applications to the President of the SCA under **s 17(2)(f)** of the **Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013**, requesting the reconsideration of the dismissal of their appeals ([paras 2–3]).

#### I – Issues

The key legal questions before the court were:

1. **Whether the applicants had established exceptional circumstances that warranted the reconsideration of their special leave to appeal.** 
2. **The appropriateness of the doctrine of common purpose as applied in this case, particularly concerning the applicants' mens rea.** The court also considered whether a **grave injustice** might result from refusing to grant leave ([paras 8–12]).

#### R – Rules

The court's analysis relied on several legal principles and precedents, primarily concerning the doctrine of common purpose in criminal law:

- **Common Purpose**: The applicable standard is derived from **S v Mgedezi & others** (1989) 1 SACR 687 (A), which encompasses the notion that individuals acting together may share criminal liability even if only some actively engaged in the unlawful conduct.
- **Mens Rea**: The appropriate form of intent (mens rea) must be assessed in determining the liability for murder. The concepts of **dolus eventualis** and **negligence** were considered essential in evaluating the applicants' mental state ([paras 8–9]).
- **Exceptional Circumstances**: As per **s 17(2)(f)** of the **Superior Courts Act 2013**, the court has discretion to refer a matter for reconsideration where significant injustice might otherwise occur. The threshold for establishing these circumstances is comparatively high, often requiring evidence that the previous judgment was manifestly unjust ([paras 10–12]).

#### A – Application

In evaluating the applications, the court critically analyzed the trial court's findings regarding the doctrine of common purpose. The members of the court expressed **grave concerns** about whether the trial court appropriately applied the doctrine in concluding that all applicants possessed the requisite mens rea (dolus eventualis) concerning the deceased's death:

- **Common Purpose Doctrine Analysis**: A pivotal point raised was that the trial court did not sufficiently distinguish the actions of the applicants, particularly noting that the conduct of some indicated a degree of effort to assist the deceased, thus possibly negating common intent ([pars 6–9]).
  
- **Grave Injustice Considerations**: By referencing the previous decision where one of the applicants' co-accused was granted leave to appeal, the court underscored that inconsistencies in these judgments could be viewed as grounds for reconsideration ([para 10–11]).

In light of the foregoing, the court indicated that failing to allow the appeal would likely result in a miscarriage of justice, warranting a referral of the matter for further consideration ([para 12]).

#### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately granted Condonation for the late filing of the applications and referred the decision dismissing the applicants' application for special leave to appeal to the court for reconsideration. The court expressed the opinion that exceptional circumstances—including potential grave injustice—existed justifying the referral. This ruling highlighted the delicate tension between the legal requirements for conviction in serious criminal matters and the need for judicial discretion to ensure fairness and justice ([paras 13]).

This case signifies an important development in the application of the doctrine of common purpose in South African law and underscores the court's commitment to safeguarding against miscarriages of justice, particularly in light of evolving legal standards surrounding criminal liability and intent.
