Case Note: IN THE CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1996

# FIRAC Case Note: The State v. Wellington Mbatha and The State v. Nicolaas Marthinus Prinsloo

### CCT 19/95 and CCT 35/95
**Heard on:** 16 November 1995  
**Delivered on:** 9 February 1996   
**Judgment delivered by:** Langa J.

---

## F - Facts

The matters at hand involve two accused persons, Wellington Mbatha and Nicolaas Marthinus Prinsloo, referred from the Witwatersrand Local Division of the Supreme Court regarding the constitutionality of the presumption in section 40(1) of the Arms and Ammunition Act 75 of 1969 (the Act). 

In the first matter ([para 1]), applicant Mbatha was convicted of unlawful possession of two AK47 rifles and twelve rounds of ammunition under sections 32(1)(a) and 32(1)(e) of the Act, which resulted in sentences of eight and two years' imprisonment to run concurrently ([para 2]). This conviction was subsequently referred to the Constitutional Court by Judge Leveson, with Justice MacArthur agreeing, due to the constitutional concerns surrounding the presumption established by section 40(1).

The second matter ([para 3]) pertains to Prinsloo and twenty-five other accused, charged with various offences related to a series of bomb explosions before the 1994 national elections. Prinsloo faced four counts involving unlawful possession of firearms and ammunition, with the trial judge refusing a discharge application based on the presumption outlined in section 40(1) of the Act. The trial was postponed for a constitutional determination ([para 3]).

## I - Issues

The central issue in both cases is the validity of the presumption in section 40(1) of the Act, specifically whether it contravenes the accusers’ rights to a fair trial as protected under sections 25(3)(c) and (d) of the Constitution ([para 4]). The applicants argue that this presumption infringes upon their right to be presumed innocent until proven guilty and their right against self-incrimination, as guaranteed by the Constitution ([para 4]).

## R - Rules

Two primary legal rules apply here:

1. The **Presumption of Innocence**: As articulated in section 25(3)(c) and (d) of the Constitution, every accused is presumed innocent until proven guilty, has the right to remain silent, and is not compelled to testify against themselves ([para 6]).
   
2. The **Limitations Clause**: Section 33(1) allows for rights limitations provided such limitations are reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society ([para 13]).

In prior cases, such as **S v Zuma** and **S v Bhulwana**, the Court examined the implications of statutory presumptions that infringe on the rights entrenched within the Constitution, reiterating that such limitations must meet stringent criteria to be constitutionally permissible ([paras 7-8]).

## A - Application

The court's analysis began by recognizing that section 40(1) represents a reverse onus provision, requiring the accused to prove innocence after a presumed fact has been established, infringing upon the presumption of innocence ([para 9]). The implications of such a presumption were compared with the precedent in **S v Bhulwana**, where the court reaffirmed that imposing a burden on an accused to rebut a presumption effectively undermines fundamental rights to a fair trial ([para 9]).

The Court acknowledged the State's arguments regarding the need for efficient prosecution of crime, particularly in contexts of escalating violence and illegal firearm possession ([para 15]). While recognizing these concerns ([para 16]), the Court ultimately concluded that the blanket nature and broad application of the presumption failed to establish the necessary logical connection between the accused and the prohibited articles ([para 22]).

Further, the Court pointed out that the presumption's application has not been refined to separate the guilty from the innocent, leading to potential wrongful convictions ([para 21]). It emphasized the importance of evidence-based prosecutions over reliance on ill-defined statutory presumptions ([paras 24-25]).

Regarding the State's contention that the presumption facilitates law enforcement and prosecution, the judgment recognized that while the societal need to combat violent crime is commendable, the mechanism employed must align with constitutional mandates. The Court concluded that alternative measures could have been offered that impose less harm to constitutional rights, such as evidentiary burdens instead of irreversible presumptions ([para 26]).

## C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court declared section 40(1) of the Arms and Ammunition Act unconstitutional, stating it constituted an unreasonable and unjustifiable violation of the presumption of innocence ([para 27]). It determined that the provision could not be saved by the limitations clause as it failed to meet the required standards of reasonableness and justifiability.

The Court ordered the cases to revert to the Witwatersrand Local Division to be dealt with in accordance with the judgment, affirming that the legal clarity around the presumption is paramount for ongoing and future cases ([para 33]). This judgment underscores the significance of maintaining a balance between effective law enforcement and the protection of fundamental rights, enhancing the integrity of the criminal justice system in South Africa.
