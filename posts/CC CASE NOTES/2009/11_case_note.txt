Source Folder: 2009

# FIRAC Case Note: Bertie Van Zyl (Pty) Ltd and Montina Boerdery (Pty) Ltd v Minister for Safety and Security and Others [2009] ZACC 11

## F – Facts
The case involves an appeal from the North Gauteng High Court concerning the interpretation of the Private Security Industry Regulation Act 56 of 2001 (the Act). The first applicant, Bertie Van Zyl (Pty) Ltd, is a major farming entity in Limpopo Province, employing over 6000 workers, while the second applicant, Montina Boerdery (Pty) Ltd, employs about 2000 workers ([2]).

Due to criminal activity targeting their assets, the applicants employed members of their workforce as security personnel, who were tasked with protecting their premises and property. These individuals, designated as security guards, were unarmed and contacted the South African Police Service (SAPS) in emergencies ([5]–[6]).

Section 20(1)(a) of the Act specifies that only registered security service providers may render security services for remuneration. The SAPS, viewing the applicants' guards as unregistered security officers, initiated arrests, creating operational difficulties for the applicants. Following unsuccessful negotiations with the SAPS, the applicants sought declaratory relief in the High Court, arguing that the Act did not apply to them and that its relevant provisions were unconstitutional and invalid ([7]–[10]).

The High Court upheld the interpretation of section 20(1)(a), but declared sections 28(2) and 28(3)(b) unconstitutional as they extended the Code of Conduct to in-house security personnel without a rational government purpose. Both parties subsequently appealed ([10]–[11]).

## I – Issues
The case presents two primary issues for consideration:

1. **Constitutionality of Section 20(1)(a)**: Whether the interpretation of section 20(1)(a) regarding the registration of security service providers is constitutionally valid ([15]–[18]).
  
2. **Constitutionality of Sections 28(2) and 28(3)(b)**: Whether the extension of the Code of Conduct to in-house security personnel as per sections 28(2) and 28(3)(b) is rationally connected to a legitimate government purpose, and whether the High Court's ruling is correct ([15]).

## R – Rules
Key legal principles involved include:

- **Constitutional invalidity**: The court must declare any law inconsistent with the Constitution as invalid (section 172(1)(a) of the Constitution).
  
- **Purposive interpretation**: Courts must interpret legislation to promote the spirit and objects of the Bill of Rights (section 39(2)).
  
- **Overbreadth**: A law can be declared unconstitutional if it is excessively broad, thereby infringing rights protected under the Constitution ([20]–[23]).

- **Reasonable clarity**: Laws, especially those with criminal implications, must be formulated clearly to avoid vagueness, enabling citizens to understand what conduct is prohibited ([101]).

The Act defines "security service" broadly, encompassing various forms of protection and safeguarding ([26]).

## A – Application
### Constitutionality of Section 20(1)(a)
The court analyzed whether section 20(1)(a) could be reasonably construed to preserve constitutionality while promoting legislative intent. A textual interpretation suggested an excessively broad application that could include nearly all employment scenarios—potentially rendering sections of society vulnerable to criminal penalties for non-registration as security service providers.

The court adopted a purposive interpretation, suggesting that the definition should be limited to protection against unlawful harm—eliminating its applicability to incidental roles like childcare or general supervision. This interpretation aligned with both legislative objectives and constitutional mandates, thereby allowing for inclusion of those whose primary duties involve security protection within an organized context ([41]–[46]).

### Constitutionality of Sections 28(2) and 28(3)(b)
The High Court had declared sections 28(2) and 28(3)(b) unconstitutional, asserting they lacked a rational link to legitimate governmental interests. However, the Constitutional Court's analysis led to a conclusion that the purpose of these sections—to provide a code governing in-house security obligations—was indeed valid and necessary to ensure employee protection in the labour landscape, particularly for vulnerable workers within extensive hierarchies of power ([53]–[70]).

The court found that the extension of the Code's application to in-house security personnel was not only rational but essential given the high risks associated with private security provision, thus emphasizing the necessity for regulatory compliance and protection in a fraught industry ([60]–[70]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court dismissed the application against section 20(1)(a), reinforcing the High Court’s interpretation as constitutionally valid while affirming the necessity of regulation in the private security industry. In contrast, the declaration of sections 28(2) and 28(3)(b) as unconstitutional was overturned, indicating a recognition of fundamental protections for in-house security personnel under the Act ([82]).

The implications of this judgment extend to the regulation of security service provisions in South Africa, promoting clarity and accountability within a significant sector of the economy, thus enhancing the rule of law and corresponding employee protections against exploitation ([70]).
