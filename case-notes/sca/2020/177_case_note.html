Supreme Court of Appeal

President of the RSA and Others v Women’s Legal Centre Trust and Others; Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development v Faro and Others; and Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development v Esau and Others [2020] ZASCA 177

The case at hand revolves around the long-standing issue of the legal recognition of Muslim marriages in South Africa.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 177

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–12])

The case at hand revolves around the long-standing issue of the legal recognition of Muslim marriages in South Africa. Historically, such marriages have not been acknowledged under South African law, leading to significant legal and social inequities, particularly for women and children arising from these unions. The Women's Legal Centre Trust (WLC), serving as the first respondent, initiated litigation against the State in November 2015, claiming that the failure to recognize Muslim marriages constituted a breach of various sections of the Constitution, notably sections 7(2), 9(1), 28(2), and 34. The WLC asserted that the State, particularly the President and the Minister of Justice, had a constitutional obligation to enact legislation recognising these marriages.

Parallel to the WLC's application, two other cases involving Muslim marriages—Faro v Bingham NO and Esau v Esau—raised similar issues regarding the legal status of Muslim marriages and associated rights under the Divorce Act 70 of 1979 and the Intestate Succession Act 81 of 1987 ([paras 8–12]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 13–22])

The principal legal questions identified were:

  1. State Obligation: Does the Constitution impose a duty on the State to legislate for the recognition and regulation of Muslim marriages?
  2. Inconsistency with Constitutional Provisions: Are the existing provisions in the Marriage Act 25 of 1961 and Divorce Act 70 of 1979 inconsistent with the constitutional rights to equality, dignity, and access to justice?
  3. Retrospective Application: Should any legal recognition of Muslim marriages apply retrospectively to marriages concluded before the enactment of such legislation?

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 23–34])

The court referred extensively to the following legal principles and precedents:

  • Section 7(2) of the Constitution: Obligates the State to "respect, protect, promote and fulfil" rights within the Bill of Rights, creating a positive duty to enact laws that ensure equality and dignity for all citizens ([para 25]).
  • International Human Rights Instruments: Cited treaties such as the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women (CEDAW) and the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights, but the appellants argued that these do not impose a direct obligation to legislate for the recognition of Muslim marriages ([para 23]).
  • Case Law: Precedents such as Hassam v Jacobs NO and Daniels v Campbell NO, which highlight the historical discrimination faced by Muslim marriages, were critical to the court’s analysis of equality and dignity violations ([paras 4–5]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 35–50])

Upon examining the constitutional obligations of the State, the court acknowledged the systemic discrimination historically faced by parties to Muslim marriages, particularly women. The court pointed out that the lack of recognition infringes upon constitutional rights related to equality and dignity, thereby supporting the argument that existing matrimonial laws are outdated and unconstitutional ([paras 49–50]).

The court accepted the concessions made by the appellants—that the Marriage and Divorce Acts do infringe on the rights of Muslim women and children, thus calling for legislative reform. This acknowledgment was pivotal in shaping the ruling on legislative duties. The court also determined that the existing laws would be declared unconstitutional and that their invalidity would be suspended for 24 months to allow Parliament to draft the necessary legislation ([paras 45–51]).

Furthermore, the court ruled against the notion of retrospectivity concerning Muslim marriages, emphasizing that it is ultimately Parliament’s prerogative to decide on such matters of legislative scope and application. The rationale for this restraint hinged on the separation of powers doctrine, which delineates the roles between the judiciary and legislative branches of government ([paras 28–44]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 51])

The court concluded that:

  1. The Marriage Act and Divorce Act are unconstitutional as they fail to recognize Muslim marriages, infringing on the rights secured by the Constitution.
  2. The declaration of constitutional invalidity is referred to the Constitutional Court for confirmation, highlighting the necessity for legislative action in the period of suspension to remedy these legal shortcomings.
  3. The court crafted an interim order providing a framework for legal recognition of Muslim marriages until Parliament enacts the requisite legislation, thereby protecting the rights of affected parties to some extent during the transition ([paras 51–52]).

In summary, this landmark decision is not only a pivotal moment for the recognition of Muslim marriages under South African law but also underscores the imperative for legislative reform to eradicate systemic prejudices against vulnerable communities within the broader context of human rights and equality. The ruling also serves as a clarion call for the State to fulfill its constitutional duties in ensuring that all families, irrespective of their marital regimes, are afforded the dignity and respect inherent to the democratic values enshrined in the Constitution.
