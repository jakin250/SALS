# FIRAC Case Note on Tshavhungwa v NDPP (328/09 & 593/08) [2009] ZASCA 136

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–13])**

The case involves an appeal and a cross-appeal originating from an application brought by Tshivhumo Phanuel Cornwell Tshavhungwa against the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP), Mr. Leonard Frank McCarthy, and the Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development in the North Gauteng High Court. Tshavhungwa was employed as a Deputy Director in the Directorate of Special Operations, colloquially referred to as the 'Scorpions', led by McCarthy ([para 2]).

Tshavhungwa had faced serious allegations of misconduct, including dishonesty and abuse of authority. In March 2004, he was suspended on full pay while an investigation was conducted ([para 3]). By May, he was notified of various charges, and his disciplinary hearing was postponed until June 2004. However, he was arrested on corruption charges, and the disciplinary proceedings were suspended ([paras 5–6]).

Subsequent to his arrests and a year in custody, a decision was made by the Executive Committee of the National Prosecuting Authority to terminate Tshavhungwa's employment based on his inability to perform his duties due to incarceration. Tshavhungwa was informed of his termination in December 2004 ([paras 10–9]). He then filed for judicial review of several decisions, including his dismissal, claiming that the actions qualified as unlawful administrative conduct, infringing on his constitutional rights ([para 16]).

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 14–22])**

The central issue in this case was whether the actions taken against Tshavhungwa—specifically the decision by the NDPP and the subsequent dismissal—constituted “administrative action” as defined under section 33 of the South African Constitution and the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 ([para 16]). Tshavhungwa argued that the manner in which he was dismissed violated his right to lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair administrative action. 

Moreover, this raised a broader question regarding the interpretation and the jurisdictional limits of administrative law concerning employment relations in the public sector, in light of the decisions in *Chirwa v Transnet Ltd* and *Gcaba v Minister of Safety and Security* ([para 18]).

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 17–22])**

The foundational rule relates to the interpretation of “administrative action” within the meaning of section 33 of the Constitution. This constitutional provision guarantees individuals the right to lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair administrative action by the state.

The court also examined relevant precedents, most notably the *Gcaba* and *Chirwa* decisions. In *Gcaba*, the Constitutional Court affirmed that employment issues within the public sector typically do not amount to administrative action, emphasizing the need for a clear demarcation between administrative law and labor law ([paras 20–21]). The principle established in *Gcaba* essentially asserts that actions regulated under the Labour Relations Act do not constitute administrative action as envisioned in section 33 of the Constitution.

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 22–23])**

The court's analysis confirmed that Tshavhungwa's claim could not succeed because his dismissal was not categorized as administrative action. It was determined that the decision to terminate his employment was an outcome of an employment relationship, which falls under the purview of labor law, not administrative law. The court stated that employment and labor relationship issues do not generally pertain to the administrative action definition essential for triggering the protections outlined in section 33 of the Constitution ([paras 22–23]).

Additionally, the court considered procedural aspects, noting that Tshavhungwa had delayed bringing his application until 2006, which the court viewed as a factor that complicated the jurisdictional arguments ([para 12]).

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 24])**

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Tshavhungwa's appeal against the lower court's ruling, reiterating the lower court's conclusion that the NDPP's and McCarthy's actions did not constitute administrative action within the confines of section 33. Consequently, they found that there was no infringement of Tshavhungwa's constitutional rights ([paras 24]).

The cross-appeal regarding costs was upheld, specifying that while the NDPP and McCarthy were jointly liable for costs in the lower court, the facts did not warrant such a measure in the higher court. The ruling clarifies the limits of administrative law in relation to employment issues in the public sector, reinforcing the distinction between administrative law remedies and those available under labor law frameworks.
