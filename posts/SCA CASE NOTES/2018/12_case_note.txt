# Case Note: John Walker Pools v Consolidated Aone Trade & Invest 6 (Pty) Ltd (in liquidation) & another (245/2017)

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts

This case revolves around an application for leave to appeal by John Walker Pools (hereafter “JWP”), against an eviction order granted by the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court under Judge Steyn on 16 August 2016 ([para 1]). The eviction was sought by Consolidated Aone Trade & Invest 6 (Pty) Ltd (in liquidation) (hereafter “CAT”), asserting ownership of the shop premises at Ballito Bay Mall, alleging that JWP's occupation was unlawful. JWP contended that its right to occupy the premises stemmed from a lease agreement with Imperial Crown Trading (Pty) Ltd (in liquidation) (hereafter “ICT”) ([para 3]).

JWP's behalf was represented by its sole proprietor, Mr. Dharman Rajoo, who failed to file heads of argument for the initial hearing due to the withdrawal of his attorneys, resulting in the court refusing a postponement and granting the eviction order ([para 5]). Following the eviction order, JWP sought leave to appeal, which was subsequently dismissed by the court a quo ([para 1], [para 11]).

By the time JWP applied for leave to appeal in March 2017, the issue of mootness emerged, as JWP's claimed right of occupation had expired in September 2017, leading the appellate court to initially consider whether any appeal would hold practical significance given that the right to occupy had lapsed ([para 7]).

### I – Issues

The core legal issues to be determined include:

1. **Mootness of the Appeal**: Has the subject of the appeal become moot due to the expiration of JWP's right of occupation?
2. **Practical Effect of the Proposed Appeal**: Would the proposed appeal yield any practical result, particularly regarding costs?
3. **Merits of the Appeal**: Does JWP have reasonable prospects for success in challenging the eviction order under the grounds presented? ([paras 4, 8, 12]).

### R – Rules

The decision references several key legal principles:

1. **Occupational Rights**: The obligation on JWP to demonstrate a valid right of occupation against CAT, as the owner ([para 3]). Relevant case law is cited, such as *Chetty v Naidoo*, which establishes the onus on a party asserting rights against an owner ([para 3]).
2. **Mootness**: The test for whether an appeal is moot is discussed under s 16(2)(a)(ii) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, indicating that a decision may be dismissed without inquiry into its merits unless exceptional circumstances arise ([para 8]).
3. **Costs Orders**: The factors influencing the court's discretion in determining costs in instances where a proposed appeal becomes moot are analyzed ([para 10]).

### A – Application

The court analyzed the practical implications of JWP’s proposed appeal against the backdrop of mootness. It emphasized that, given JWP's occupancy claim had lapsed, any determination on the merits would be largely academic ([para 7]). JWP’s counsel conceded that any practical outcome would only pertain to costs, aligned with s 16(2)(a)(ii) for dismissal of appeals ([para 8]).

JWP’s failure to present a compelling defense during the eviction application was highlighted, particularly noting the absence of a lease attachment in initial court filings which weakened their position ([para 6],[para 12]). The court concluded that JWP did not satisfactorily establish that the lease entitled them to occupy the property against CAT, given the prior terminations of occupancy rights expressed in CAT’s founding affidavits ([para 13, 15]).

The court also considered the context of the litigation, marking JWP’s conduct as unmeritorious given their continued occupation without paying rent before the jurisdiction expired, resulting in a nominal finding against them regarding the costs order ([para 16]). In reconciliation, it was noted that, while there were no grounds for punitive costs, JWP was ordered to bear the costs of the application owing to the lack of reasonable prospects of success in their appeal ([paras 17, 16]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the dismissal of JWP's application for leave to appeal, primarily due to the mootness of the case arising from the expiration of their claimed right of occupancy in September 2017 ([para 17]). The court’s focus on the practical effect of appeals serves to reinforce the principle that issues devoid of practical significance should not burden judicial resources. JWP's conduct throughout the proceedings was critiqued, ultimately resulting in an order that held them liable for costs, cementing the imperative for parties to act judiciously in managing litigation and costs ([para 10, 16]).

This case reiterates principles surrounding eviction procedures, mootness in appeals, and judicial economy, providing a cornerstone case for practitioners and scholars in the domain of property law and appellate procedure.
