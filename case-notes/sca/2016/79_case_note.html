Supreme Court of Appeal

Roering and Another NNO v Mahlangu (581/2015)

After multiple court proceedings, on 13 October 2011, 3P Consulting instituted an application against the Department for the payment of approximately R99 million.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016

F – Facts
The case centers around the events following the service level agreement between the Department of Health, Gauteng (the Department) and 3P Consulting (Pty) Ltd (3P Consulting). Initially entered into in June 2007, the agreement was extended for three additional years after the 2009 elections. However, due to allegations of impropriety, the Department informed 3P Consulting on 1 July 2009 that it would not fulfill the agreement, leading to extensive litigation ([paras 1-2]).

After multiple court proceedings, on 13 October 2011, 3P Consulting instituted an application against the Department for the payment of approximately R99 million. The liquidators of 3P Consulting, appointed after its liquidation, sought to initiate an enquiry into the affairs of 3P Consulting pursuant to sections 417 and 418 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973 ([para 4]). They determined that Ms. Qedani Mahlangu, who had been the MEC for Health during the agreement, could provide crucial information related to the claims against the Department. Following a commissioner’s authorization, a summons was issued to Ms. Mahlangu for her appearance at the enquiry ([paras 5-6]).

Instead of complying, Ms. Mahlangu applied to the Gauteng Local Division, Johannesburg, to have the summons set aside, arguing it constituted an abuse of process ([para 5]). The High Court agreed, leading to the appellants' appeal against this decision ([para 5]).

I – Issues
The primary legal question was whether the summons issued to Ms. Mahlangu constituted an abuse of process, particularly in light of ongoing litigation involving the same matters ([paras 12-38]). Specific legal questions included:

  1. Does the existence of overlapping issues in litigation preclude an enquiry under ss 417 and 418 of the Companies Act?
  2. What constitutes an abuse of process in this context, particularly concerning the testimony of potential witnesses in ongoing litigation? ([paras 11, 46])

R – Rules
The relevant legal framework here derives from sections 417 and 418 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973, which allows for an enquiry into the affairs of a company in liquidation to ascertain the company’s financial position ([para 20]).

The concept of 'abuse of process' has been addressed in both statutory and case law. The courts have established that a summons cannot be evaluated solely based on its potential to advantage one party in ongoing litigation. Abuse must be demonstrated through actual malfeasance or improper procedural use ([paras 34-42]).

In cases such as Bernstein and Others v Bester and Others NNO and Ferreira v Levin NO ([para 21]), a broad interpretation was adopted around the inquiries, upholding the need for liquidators to ascertain relevant facts irrespective of any ongoing litigation.

A – Application
The court initially scrutinized Ms. Mahlangu's claims of abuse by assessing her prior cooperation with the enquiry and her subsequent change in position to oppose the summons ([paras 8-10]). Despite her claim that the enquiry was aimed at bolstering the liquidators’ case against the Department, the court found no supporting evidence indicating that the information she possessed was irrelevant or merely pre-trial in nature ([paras 12-16]).

The court noted that the liquidators had a legitimate interest in gathering information to assess the viability of the claims against the Department. They contended that Ms. Mahlangu’s testimony was integral for them to advise creditors appropriately about the ongoing litigation, meeting the statutory mandate outlined in the Companies Act. Furthermore, the inquiry was not merely a “fishing expedition,” and the Chairman acknowledged that Ms. Mahlangu, as the MEC during the relevant time, likely possessed pertinent insights ([paras 16-39]).

Ultimately, the court rejected claims that invoking the enquiry procedures constituted an abuse merely because Ms. Mahlangu was a potential trial witness. Instead, it emphasized that such summoning would not impede her rights beyond what was required to fulfill the inquiry's purpose, referencing precedents that reinforced the statute's intent ([paras 38-42]).

C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the High Court misapplied the standards for establishing an abuse of process. It identified no substantive abuse related to Ms. Mahlangu's summons as the grounds asserted were unfounded and did not prevent the liquidators from obtaining necessary evidence under statutory provisions. The appeal was upheld with costs, effectively reinstating the summons for Ms. Mahlangu to appear at the enquiry with a revised order from the High Court to dismiss her application for relief from the summons ([paras 45-55]).

In sum, this case underscores the robust protections granted to liquidators under the Companies Act, emphasizing the importance of inquiries into a company’s financial dealings, even amid ongoing litigation, to ensure the integrity and feasibility of claims brought in the interests of creditors and resultant wind-up proceedings.
