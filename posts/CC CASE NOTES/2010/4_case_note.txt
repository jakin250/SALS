# FIRAC Case Note: *Ryan Albutt v Centre for the Study of Violence and Reconciliation* [2010] ZACC 4

## F – Facts
The matter concerns the constitutional power of the President of South Africa to grant pardons under section 84(2)(j) of the Constitution. The case arose from the declaration of a "special dispensation" process for pardoning prisoners convicted of political offences by former President Thabo Mbeki on November 21, 2007. This initiative aimed to address “unfinished business” of the Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC), including the amnesty of individuals not previously considered by the TRC. The approved applicants were to be individuals convicted for politically motivated acts prior to June 16, 1999, who had not sought amnesty through the TRC ([1–4]).

The President formed a Pardon Reference Group (PRG) tasked with reviewing applications for pardon. Despite numerous attempts by a coalition of NGOs—representing the victims—to engage in the process and be heard regarding their perspectives, the PRG maintained that they were not obligated to involve the victims, leading to the filing of an urgent application for an interim interdict to halt the pardon process ([8–9]).

The High Court ruled in favour of the NGOs, affirming their standing to act on behalf of victims and recognizing that victims are entitled to representation in the pardon process. The High Court ordered an interdict, preventing the President from issuing any pardons based on the special dispensation until the matter could be fully resolved ([10–13]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal questions addressed by the Constitutional Court included:
1. Whether the victims of offences for which pardons are sought have the right to be heard in the Presidential pardon process under section 84(2)(j) of the Constitution ([1]).
2. Whether the process of granting pardons falls within the ambit of 'administrative action' as defined by the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act, 2000 (PAJA), requiring adherence to procedural fairness ([9, 11]).

## R – Rules
The ruling examined several key legal provisions:
- **Section 84(2)(j)** of the South African Constitution outlines the President's power to grant pardons.
- **Section 33** of the Constitution guarantees the right to administrative action that is lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair.
- **PAJA**, particularly section 1, defines administrative action, addressing the requirements for procedural fairness in public decision-making ((3, 7).

The High Court found sufficient rationalities to support the rights of the victims to a hearing before the pardon is granted, suggesting that:
1. The power to pardon under section 84(2)(j) constitutes administrative action, invoking PAJA requirements.
2. The rational purpose of pardons aimed at national reconciliation mandates that victims should have the opportunity to express their views concerning the alleged political motives behind the offences ([9, 11, 12]).

## A – Application
The Constitutional Court affirmed the High Court's decision, emphasizing several key factors in its reasoning:
1. **Rationality**: The Court determined that the exclusion of victims from the pardon process was irrational. The objectives of national unity and reconciliation could not be effectively achieved without victim input, reflecting the spirit of the TRC processes ([49–69]).
2. **Legitimacy of Victim Participation**: The importance of hearing from victims was articulated as essential to the holistic understanding of the offences' context. It was noted that both victims and perpetrators must be heard to ascertain the nature of the motives behind the acts constituting the crimes ([70–72]).
3. **Public Interest**: The Court recognized the intricate relationship between pardons and the national consciousness, stressing that the historical context of South Africa’s past necessitates a participative approach to justice. This aligns with broader democratic principles espoused in the Constitution ([28–37]).

Ultimately, the Court refrained from addressing the broader questions surrounding whether the exercise of the pardon power constitutes administrative action, determining that this was unnecessary for the resolution of the present case ([79–82]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court upheld the High Court's ruling that victims have the right to be heard prior to the granting of pardons under the special dispensation process. This landmark decision reaffirms the necessity of including victim representation in self-critical processes of national reconciliation, emphasizing that the exclusion of victims was not only unfair but irrational under the principles outlined in the Constitution. The decision highlighted the relevance of participatory democracy, particularly concerning matters that implicate historical injustices, which remains crucial in South Africa's journey toward healing and unity ([75–76], [87]).

The judgment had far-reaching implications, underscoring the importance of procedural fairness and accountability in the exercise of executive powers, particularly where the legacies of apartheid still resonate deeply in societal relations and governance (Ngcobo CJ) ([88-92]).
