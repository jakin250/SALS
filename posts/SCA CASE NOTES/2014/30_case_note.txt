Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# Case Note: Bapedi Marota Mamone v The Commission of Traditional Leadership Disputes and Claims & Others (260/13)

## F - Facts
The case arose from a dispute regarding the lineage of the Bapedi kingship and the commission's decision that it fell under the Sekhukhune lineage, rather than the Mampuru lineage as contended by the appellant, Bapedi Marota Mamone ([1]). The appellant, represented by the traditional leader Kgoshi Mampuru Mampuru III, challenged the findings of the Commission of Traditional Leadership Disputes and Claims (the Commission), which was tasked under the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003 (the Act) to determine the rightful kingship lineage ([2], [3]). 

The Commission found that the historical kingship established by Thulare I had incurred multiple succession struggles, particularly between Sekhukhune I and Mampuru II following the death of Malekutu I. The investigation revealed that Sekhukhune I had legitimated his claim through military conquest, while Mampuru II was considered never to have ascended according to Bapedi customary law ([6], [11]). The appellant's review application in the North Gauteng High Court was dismissed; therefore, the current appeal was pursued to challenge the correctness of this decision regarding lineage ([1], [13]).

## I - Issues
The critical issues for consideration by the Supreme Court of Appeal were: 
1. Whether the high court correctly concluded there had been a succession battle won by Sekhukhune I.
2. Whether the determination of succession should only be based on the capture of kingship by might, disregarding hereditary principle.
3. Whether the Commission ignored relevant evidence in determining lineage and whether its decision was rationally connected to the information available.
4. Whether the review of the Commission’s decision was approached correctly by the high court ([14]).

## R - Rules
The rules applicable in this case stem from:
1. The **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**, particularly Section 6(2), which sets out grounds for review including failure to consider relevant material and irrationality in decision-making ([17]).
2. The **Constitution of South Africa**, notably Section 33, which guarantees the right to just administrative action ([18]).
3. Precedent from previous cases, such as *Bel Porto School Governing Body & others v Premier, Western Cape* and *Pharmaceutical MNFRS of SA*, which reiterate the objective threshold for rationality in administrative actions ([19]).

## A - Application
In reviewing the Commission's findings, the Court established that the rationality test is fundamental in determining whether the Commission's conclusion was justified based on the evidence presented ([18], [21]). This included a thorough examination of pertinent historical records and the Commission’s methodology in evaluating customary law practices regarding succession.

The appellant's arguments largely focused on claimed omissions by the Commission regarding Mampuru II’s reign during Sekhukhune I’s incarceration, alleging that his actions effectively altered the lineage claim. Nonetheless, the Court concurred with the high court’s findings, affirming that the Commission’s conclusion – mainly that Mampuru II’s temporary installation by the British authorities did not align with Bapedi customs – was validated by substantial evidence ([19]). 

The Court further observed that the claims of judicial bias were unfounded and that the Commission had rigorously considered historical contexts and customary law dynamics, including the concept of bloodshed being an avenue to kingship ([21],[23]). Thus, the finding that Sekhukhune I's claim—legitimated by customary law—was forcibly obtained but nonetheless valid, was upheld ([20]).

## C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the Commission's decision regarding the lineage of the Bapedi kingship was rationally connected to the information and evidence available. The findings were consistent with customary law practices and comprehensively evaluated historical records. Consequently, the Court upheld the high court's dismissal of the appellant's review application, affirming that the appeal lacked merit and substantiated grounds for review, leading to a dismissal with costs ([23]).

### Implications:
This case reinforces the role of the Commission in adjudicating issues of traditional leadership and underscores the necessity for decisions to align with established customary law principles. The Court's emphasis on rationality and comprehensive evaluation of evidence serves as a precedent in administrative law regarding the adjudication of customary law disputes. This judgment elucidates the delicate balance between historical claims and traditional practices, contributing significantly to the discourse on administrative justice in South Africa.
