Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: S v Meje

**F** – **Facts:**
The matter at hand involves an appeal by the State against a decision by the North Gauteng High Court, which set aside the convictions and sentences of the respondents, Arthur Tshepo Meje and Edward Mqahayi ([1]). The respondents were convicted of multiple counts of fraud following their trial in a regional court located in Pretoria. Meje received a seven-year prison sentence, two years of which were conditionally suspended, while Mqahayi was sentenced to eight years, also with two years conditionally suspended ([1]). 

Their initial appearances in court were on 24 June 2004, after which the trial commenced ([3]). The pivotal question concerning jurisdiction arose when the High Court found that the trial court lacked territorial jurisdiction due to a restructuring of the regional courts in Gauteng, which occurred on 1 April 2004, prior to the commencement of the criminal proceedings ([4]). The High Court's ruling resulted in a significant shift, as it set aside the convictions without addressing the merits of the case ([1]). 

**I** – **Issues:**
The primary issue on appeal is whether the trial court had the jurisdiction to try the respondents for the alleged crimes ([2]). This legal question emerged as an outcome of the amalgamation of regional divisions in Gauteng and has implications for the interpretation of Section 110 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([2]). The ambiguity lies in whether jurisdiction should be assessed at the time of the commission of the offences or at the time proceedings commenced.

**R** – **Rules:**
The legal framework primarily involves the Criminal Procedure Act, particularly Sections 76 and 110. Section 76(1) outlines that criminal proceedings commence with the lodging of a charge sheet, while Section 110 stipulates that if a defendant does not contest jurisdiction, the court is deemed to have jurisdiction ([9]). The Supreme Court of Appeal also referenced *S v Mamase & another*, where it was established that the jurisdiction of a court should be determined when proceedings are initiated ([7]). Additionally, in *S v Pale & ‘n ander*, it was emphasized that Section 110 does not confer substantive jurisdiction but may convey territorial jurisdiction in the absence of a challenge by the accused ([10]).

**A** – **Application:**
In applying these principles, Plasket AJA found the lower court erred on two counts. First, the court below misapplied the temporal aspect of jurisdiction by determining it at the time of the offence rather than when proceedings began ([5], [7]). The crucial date for determining jurisdiction was 24 June 2004, when the respondents first appeared in court ([8]). At that time, the newly formed Regional Division of Gauteng had the authority to exercise jurisdiction, thus legitimizing the trial.

Secondly, the Supreme Court of Appeal articulated a misunderstanding of Section 110. The lower court reasoned that because the offences occurred in the Southern Transvaal's jurisdiction, a Pretoria court (associated with the Northern Transvaal) could not preside over the case. However, Plasket AJA clarified that territorial jurisdiction as defined by Section 110 could be established despite initial perceptions to the contrary—so long as the accused had not objected ([11]).

**C** – **Conclusion:**
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the High Court's determination regarding jurisdiction was erroneous ([12]). Therefore, the convictions and sentences of both respondents were reinstated, and the matter was remitted to the High Court to address the merits of the respondents’ appeals ([13]). This ruling not only affirms the interpretation of the territorial jurisdiction decision point as being at the start of proceedings but also clarifies the applicability of Section 110, strengthening the procedural integrity of criminal trials in South Africa. The implications of this case underscore the importance of statutory interpretations that align with legislative intent and judicial practices. 

In summary, the decision restored the convictions, demonstrating that procedural oversights at earlier judicial stages must be rectified to ensure justice is upheld while safeguarding the rights of the accused ([13]).
