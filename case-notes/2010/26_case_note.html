Bengwenyama Minerals (Pty) Ltd and Others v. Genorah Resources (Pty) Ltd and Others [2010] ZACC 26

Genorah's application for prospecting rights was accepted by the Department of Mineral Resources, despite the community's previous attempts to assert their rights through their own applications.

Constitutional Court 2010 [2010] ZACC 26

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case concerns the legality of granting prospecting rights to Genorah Resources (Pty) Ltd over land owned by the Bengwenyama community. The community had a long-standing claim over the land in question, with members having occupied it for over a century, and a successful land claim for one parcel. The community, represented by Bengwenyama Minerals (Pty) Ltd, lodged objections against prospecting on their land, asserting their right to be “meaningfully accommodated.” This was notably underscored by their historical dispossession due to racially discriminatory laws.

Genorah's application for prospecting rights was accepted by the Department of Mineral Resources, despite the community's previous attempts to assert their rights through their own applications. The Department did not notify the community about the grant of prospecting rights to Genorah and allowed Genorah to proceed without proper consultation. Following delays and inadequacies in the consultation process, the community challenged the decision through review proceedings ([1]–[26]).

I – Issues

The Constitutional Court had to determine several critical issues, which included:
a. Whether leave to appeal should be granted.
b. Whether the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act (the Act) provided for internal remedies.
c. Whether the review application was brought in time.
d. Whether there was adequate consultation and fairness in awarding the prospecting rights.
e. The nature of the right to a hearing for the community prior to the award of rights.
f. The environmental considerations in awarding prospecting rights to Genorah ([26]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal framework includes:

  1. Constitutional Principles: Sections dealing with equality and the rights to administrative justice ([1]–[3]).
  2. The Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act, Act 28 of 2002 (the Act): Key sections include:
  • Section 17(1) establishes requirements for granting prospecting rights.
  • Section 96 stipulates the procedural framework for internal appeals.
  • Section 104 provides for preferent rights for communities wishing to prospect on communal land ([28]–[41]).
  1. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA): Establishes requirements for lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair administration ([60]).

A – Application

In evaluating the facts against the legal standards:

  1. Leave to Appeal: The Court deemed the appeal necessary due to significant constitutional implications surrounding the rights of the community and environmental protections.
  2. Internal Remedies: The Court established that internal appeal processes under Section 96 of the Act were indeed available to the community. The failure to recognize these internal remedies was a pivotal error in prior judgments ([43]–[55]).
  3. Consultation and Fairness: The Court found that Genorah failed to meet its legal obligations to consult the community adequately before the award of rights, as outlined in sections 10 and 16 of the Act. The superficial consultation process violated principles of procedural fairness and did not allow for the community's rights and interests to be adequately represented ([62]–[74]).
  4. Environmental Criteria: Genorah’s application was scrutinized for environmental compliance, which the Court noted was insufficient as the required plans were approved after the rights had already been granted, contradicting the statutory requirements ([75]–[78]).

C – Conclusion

The Court ultimately held that the grant of prospecting rights to Genorah over the community's land was unlawful due to significant breaches of procedural fairness and statutory requirements as per the Act and the Constitution. The appeal was upheld, with the decisions of the lower courts overturned. The Court emphasized the need for fair administrative action and the importance of ensuring communities have reasonable access to mining rights on their land. Both the state respondents and Genorah were ordered to bear the costs of the proceedings, affirming the community's right to representation and equitable access to resources ([89]).

Implications

The judgment reinforces the constitutional and statutory mandates of fairness and transparency in mining rights administration, particularly for historically disadvantaged communities. It underscores the accountability of administrative bodies to engage meaningfully with affected parties and to adhere strictly to legal frameworks designed to promote equitable access to mineral resources, reflecting an ongoing commitment to rectifying past injustices ([80]).
