FIRAC Case Note: Illovo Opportunities Partnership #61 v Illovo Junction Properties (490/13) [2014] ZASCA 119

F – Facts

The case arises from an appeal against a decision by the Gauteng Local Division, Johannesburg ([para 1]). The appellant, Illovo Opportunities Partnership #61, sought a declaratory order requiring the first respondent, Illovo Junction Properties (Pty) Ltd, to pay R8.8 million to the second respondent, the City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality, following the rezoning of a property for further development. This amount was levied as a contribution towards the costs of engineering services, parks, and open spaces ([paras 2-3]).

The appellant purchased the property from the seller (the first respondent) in September 2009, prior to the contribution being levied but took transfer in January 2010 after the rezoning was formally approved ([paras 4-5]). Following the rezoning, the City directed the seller to pay the contribution due on January 5, 2010. The appellant contended that since the liability accrued before the transfer, the seller should be held accountable for the payment ([paras 7-10]).

The high court dismissed the application on the grounds that the appellant lacked standing, asserting that the issue primarily involved the rights and obligations between the City and the seller, not the appellant’s own rights ([para 3]).

I – Issues

The primary legal question addressed by the court concerned the standing of the appellant to seek declaratory relief, specifically whether the appellant had established a direct and substantial interest in the matter ([paras 3, 18]). Additionally, the interpretation of sections 48 and 63 of the Town Planning and Townships Ordinance 15 of 1986 was pivotal in determining the timing of when the obligation to pay the contribution arose ([para 20]).

R – Rules

This case draws upon key statutory provisions, notably section 19(1)(a)(iii) of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959, which empowers the court to grant declaratory relief to any interested person, but with the stipulation that the applicant must have a direct and substantial interest in the relief sought ([para 14]). The court looked at the definition of standing extensively, referencing the precedent in United Watch & Diamond Co (Pty) Ltd v Disa Hotels Ltd (1972), which establishes that an applicant must assert a direct interest rather than a derivative one ([para 14]).

Further, the court conducted an analysis of the Town Planning and Townships Ordinance, particularly sections 48 and 63. Section 63 mandates the local authority to demand payment from the property owner after a certain condition is met: where the local authority decides it is necessary to enhance services due to the rezoning that has been approved ([para 21]).

A – Application

The court undertook a detailed analysis of the appellant's interest in the relief sought. Initially, the appellant relied on the sale agreement to assert that the seller was liable for the contribution. However, this reliance was abandoned on appeal, leading the appellant to argue instead that its claim was based on principles of unjust enrichment or *negotiorum gestio*—asserting that should it be compelled to pay the contribution, it would be acting to protect its own interests and prevent the seller from being unjustly enriched by the appellant’s payment ([paras 12-13, 19]).

The court assessed the implications of the seller’s obligation under the Ordinance and concluded that it prescribes specific circumstances when payments become due, particularly linking them to the initiation of the implementation of the development rights ([paras 20, 30-31]). The crux of the judgment was whether the liability to pay arose immediately upon receiving the direction from the City or only when the new rights are exercised. The court clarified that the obligation to pay only arises once the land is utilized under the new zoning regulations, thus ruling against the appellant’s initial claim that the seller was liable for payment from the moment the directive was issued by the City ([para 31]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, holding that the appellant had not established that it had a direct and substantial interest concerning the liability of the seller to pay the contribution. As a result, the appellant was found to lack standing to claim the declaratory order it sought, given its interest was not direct but rather contingent upon the actions of third parties, namely the seller and the City ([paras 31, 32]). The implications of this case underline the importance of establishing direct interests when seeking declaratory relief and clarify the timing of liabilities concerning property development contributions under municipal ordinance. The court’s decision emphasizes the necessity for parties engaged in property transactions to be precise in their contractual obligations and the implications of local planning laws.
