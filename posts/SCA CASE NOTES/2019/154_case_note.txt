Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: Mbulelo v The State (148/2019) [2019] ZASCA 154 (26 November 2019)

## F – Facts

This case concerns the appellant, Malangabi Mbulelo, who was convicted of multiple serious offenses, including two counts of robbery with aggravating circumstances, kidnapping, attempted murder, and unlawful possession of a firearm and ammunition. The events leading to his conviction transpired in September 2005. On September 1, while working for Telkom in Soweto, the appellant, accompanied by an accomplice, confronted a technician at gunpoint, robbing him of his vehicle. They forcibly took the complainant's Telkom bakkie and later abandoned him a short distance from the crime scene ([1]–[3]).

The following day, armed with firearms, the appellant and his accomplice used the stolen vehicle to attempt to rob a Coin Security van in Jeppe, Johannesburg. During this incident, gunshots were exchanged, resulting in injury to a complainant from the van and the subsequent arrest of the appellant, who was found hiding with a firearm ([4]).

Initially sentenced to a cumulative 30 years in prison by the Regional Court, the appellant's application for leave to appeal his conviction and sentence was denied by both the Regional Court and, later, by the Gauteng Local Division of the High Court on March 16, 2009 ([2]). Nonetheless, the Supreme Court of Appeal granted special leave to appeal against the refusal of his petition, focusing strictly on the issue of sentencing ([2]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal question revolves around whether the appeal against the refusal of leave to appeal concerning the appellant’s sentence should have been granted. The appellant contended that there existed substantial and compelling circumstances—specifically his youth, first-offender status, and time served awaiting trial—that had not been sufficiently considered by the Regional Court ([5]).

In essence, the issues identified for determination included:
- Whether the Regional Court failed to recognize substantial and compelling circumstances justifying a deviation from the prescribed minimum sentences.
- The appropriateness of the cumulative sentences imposed for violent offenses and their reflection on broader societal needs for justice ([9]).

## R – Rules

The ruling by the Supreme Court of Appeal primarily rested on the principles established in the **Criminal Law Amendment Act 107 of 1997**, which prescribes minimum sentences for defined serious offenses, including armed robbery ([8]). The Court emphasized the foundational case of **S v Malgas**, which identifies that courts may only deviate from these minimum sentences if substantial and compelling circumstances are shown ([10]).

In addition to legislative guidelines, the court referenced **S v Kruger**, wherein the cumulative effect of multiple sentences was analyzed, asserting the necessity for sentencing courts to balance aggravating and mitigating factors effectively ([6]). 

The Court also acknowledged the serious societal implications of violent crime, affirming the need for robust sentencing as a deterrent ([9]).

## A – Application

In applying the law to the facts, the Supreme Court noted the Regional Court had indeed considered the mitigating factors, including the appellant's age, first-offender status, and custody period awaiting trial. However, these factors were weighed against the gravity and prevalence of the violent crimes committed ([8]).

The Court distinguished the appellant's circumstances from those in **Kruger** and reiterated that the violent nature of robberies perpetrated by the appellant directly negated the application of mitigating circumstances ([7]). The brazen nature of using firearms and the substantial risk posed to innocent victims were highlighted as aggravating factors.

Ultimately, the Court ruled that the sentences imposed were not shockingly inappropriate or unduly harsh. The seriousness of the crimes warranted significant penalties, and there were no compelling grounds to disturb the sentences ([11]). 

### The Court's reasoning encapsulated several dimensions:
1. **Severity of Offenses**: The violent methods employed in both robberies, involving firearms and the risk to human life, demanded strict sentences ([7]).
2. **Legislative Requirements**: The mandatory minimum sentences established by legislation aimed at addressing violent crime could not be casually disregarded, emphasizing the role of societal expectations in sentencing ([9]).
3. **Absence of Compelling Circumstances**: The Court reiterated that the circumstances presented did not meet the threshold for substantial and compelling circumstances necessary to deviate from the mandatory sentences outlined in **Malgas** ([10]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately refused the appeal against the refusal of leave to appeal regarding the sentences imposed on the appellant, holding that the punishments were appropriate given the heinous nature of the crimes. The ruling reaffirmed the necessity of adhering to legislative frameworks in sentencing, particularly in instances involving violent crime.

The implications of this decision resonate deeply within the South African context of addressing violent crime, underlining the judiciary's commitment to imposing sentences that reflect the severity of offenses and the community’s demand for justice. This case serves as a pivotal reference point for similar cases regarding the persistence of violent crime and the judiciary's role in deterrence through substantial sentencing ([12]). 

In summary, the order of the court was clear: "The appeal against the refusal of the application for leave to appeal in respect of the sentence imposed on the appellant is refused" ([12]). 

This case confirms the judiciary's strict adherence to legislative principles regarding minimum sentencing and the essential balance between mitigating and aggravating factors in serious criminal matters.
