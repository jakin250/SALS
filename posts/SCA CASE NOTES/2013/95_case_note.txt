Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: Lester v Ndlambe Municipality (514/12) [2013] ZASCA 95

**F - Facts**:  
The case centers on the demolition of a luxury home owned by Professor Matthew Robert Michael Lester in Kenton-on-Sea, Eastern Cape. The Ndlambe Municipality (the first respondent) sought a demolition order, which was granted by Alkema J in the Eastern Cape High Court. This occurred after a decade of legal disputes surrounding the legality of Lester's dwelling, which was constructed without approved building plans, contravening the National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act 103 of 1977 ([1–3]).

Lester's property had a complicated history, including multiple interdicts, review applications, and successive attempts to regularize the building plans. Initially, Lester's plans, created by architect Pollos Purden, were approved. However, due to personal changes, Lester hired another architect, Sam Pelissier, without notifying the local authority or the adjoining property owner, Mr. Haslam, resulting in additional legal conflicts. Despite various adjustments and resubmissions of plans over the years, the Ndlambe Municipality consistently refused approval based on statutory requirements ([4–12]).

Ultimately, the court determined that no approved plans existed, and thus the request for demolition was legitimate and legally binding under the Act ([13]).

**I - Issues**:  
The appeal raised significant legal questions regarding:  
1. Whether the existence of jurisdictional facts justified a demolition order under the relevant statute.
2. The extent of judicial discretion in ordering demolition when statutory non-compliance is evident.
3. Whether changing circumstances could justify alternative remedies such as allowing alterations to the unauthorized structure ([13–14]).

**R - Rules**:  
The legal framework for the decision rests primarily on several sections of the National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act 103 of 1977, particularly sections 4(1), 4(4), and 21. These sections outline requirements for building approvals and the consequences of non-compliance, including the imposition of criminal penalties for unauthorized construction ([19–20]).

The decision also engaged with the principle of legality, emphasizing that courts must uphold statutory mandates and public policy, particularly in the realm of local governance and urban planning. This aligns with the constitutional framework in South Africa, specifically section 26(3), which preserves the right to housing but mandates court consideration of relevant circumstances before eviction or demolition ([16–17]).

**A - Application**:  
In applying these legal principles, the court noted that the absence of approved plans transformed Lester’s property into an illegal structure, thus obligating the municipality to seek a demolition order. It concluded that no legitimate evidential basis existed for exercising discretion against ordering demolition, as the original infractions and continued non-compliance by Lester negated such considerations ([24–25]). The court emphasized that the principle of legality could not be compromised in favor of individual interests, regardless of the financial and personal implications for Lester ([31]).

Moreover, the court rejected the argument that neighbour law principles, which can sometimes afford discretion in demolition cases, were applicable in this scenario. The law firmly differentiated between public law remedies and private disputes regarding neighbour law ([21–22]).

**C - Conclusion**:  
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the lower court's ruling, confirming the demolition order. It concluded that the jurisdictional facts for such an order were satisfied under section 21 of the Act and that the legality doctrine requires public authorities to enforce compliance with statutory obligations. Consequently, the appeal was dismissed with costs, underscoring the vital role of the rule of law in maintaining order and compliance within municipal governance. This case notably reinforces the principle that individual hardships cannot override statutory compliance, particularly when the law itself categorically requires enforcement against illegal structures ([28–32]).
