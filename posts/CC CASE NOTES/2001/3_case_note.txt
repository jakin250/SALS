Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2001

# FIRAC Case Note: **Minister of Home Affairs v Dominique Liebenberg** 

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–7])
In this case, the Minister of Home Affairs (the Minister) approached the Constitutional Court of South Africa seeking confirmation of an order of constitutional invalidity issued by the Witwatersrand High Court. This order stemmed from an urgent application initiated by the respondent, Dominique Liebenberg, a South African citizen who had married a Senegalese national.

The respondent’s husband had entered South Africa with a temporary permit after seeking political asylum in 1998 but subsequently lost his Senegalese passport. In her application for a temporary residence permit on behalf of her husband, the respondent was informed that he could not remain in South Africa without a valid passport and that various fees were due for processing the necessary documentation. After the Department of Home Affairs did not oppose her High Court application, they agreed to issue a temporary residence permit subject to certain conditions, leading to the contested order being issued by the High Court ([paras 2–6]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1, 8–15])
The case raised critical issues regarding the jurisdictional authority of the Constitutional Court under section 172(2) of the Constitution. Specifically, the court considered whether the orders sought by the Minister constituted a valid order of constitutional invalidity within the context of the Constitution's requirements.

The core legal questions included:
1. Whether the parts of the High Court order that the Minister sought to confirm represent a declaration of invalidity of an Act of Parliament, a provincial Act or any conduct of the President, as required by section 172(2).
2. The implications of the High Court’s findings on the legality of the fees associated with temporary residence permits and whether the order correctly identified any constitutional inconsistency.

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 8–14])
The Constitutional Court grounded its analysis in section 172(2) of the Constitution, which outlines the procedures for confirming orders of constitutional invalidity. The provision specifies that an order requiring confirmation must concern an Act of Parliament or provincial legislation. The Court took guidance from precedents such as _Dawood v Minister of Home Affairs_ and _Booysen v Minister of Home Affairs_, which clarified the scope and definition of what constitutes an Act of Parliament as distinct from regulations ([paras 9–11]).

Additionally, the Court examined relevant sections of the Aliens Control Act and its regulations to determine whether the High Court had properly identified the relevant statutory framework that could be subject to invalidation ([paras 12–14]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 8–15])
The Court delineated the distinction between legislation and subordinate legislation. It noted that the fees challenged in the High Court order were part of the regulations, not statutes, and stated explicitly that the constitutional invalidity of regulations could not be confirmed under section 172(2). Consequently, the first part of the High Court order, which was declaratory in nature regarding the fees, was held not to fall within the Court’s purview for confirmation under section 172(2) ([paras 8–13]).

Turning to the second aspect, the Court affirmed that the order improperly mandated the parties to correct alleged constitutional inconsistencies without providing clarity on which statutory provisions were implicated or declared invalid. Therefore, the rationale for any potential mandamus was deemed insufficient to meet constitutional requirements ([paras 14–15]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 16])
Ultimately, the Constitutional Court dismissed the Minister’s application for the confirmation of the High Court order. The ruling established that the contested order failed to meet the constitutional threshold necessary for confirmation concerning facial invalidity requirements. The Court reaffirmed that the explicit identification of acts or statutes is crucial for determining constitutional invalidity, reflecting the significant constraints on the judiciary regarding the enforcement and interpretation of regulations as compared to primary legislation ([para 16]).

This judgment emphasizes the need for clarity and precision in judicial orders that purport to declare legislative provisions invalid, thereby ensuring adherence to procedural safeguards embedded in the Constitution. The implications of this ruling strengthen the distinction between the legislative frameworks while highlighting the responsibilities of state organs, including the judiciary, to clarify the boundaries of their powers within the constitutional context.
