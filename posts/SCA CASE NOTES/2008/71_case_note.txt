Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2008

# FIRAC Case Note: Saayman v Visser [2008] ZASCA 71

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History

[1] This case revolves around a tragic incident that occurred in the early hours of 13 February 1999, when 16-year-old Gideon Saayman was shot by security guard Sylvester Morebudi at the home of Christiaan Andreas Visser, the respondent. Gideon sustained severe injuries, leading his family to relocate for medical treatment ([1]).

[2] The legal proceedings commenced after the Kimberley High Court dismissed the appellant’s claim for damages against Mr. Visser, both personally and as Gideon's guardian, while the other defendants, Griekwa Security CC and Mr. Morebudi, were held jointly and severally liable but did not defend the action ([2]). The case came to the Supreme Court of Appeal following the lower court's ruling that found no grounds for liability on the part of Mr. Visser.

[4-13] The background to the incident indicates that Gideon and his friend had consumed alcohol and decided to play a prank involving a pot-plant at Mr. Visser's property. The prank triggered the guard's reaction, culminating in the shooting ([4-6]). Mr. Visser had engaged Griekwa Security to provide an armed guard, with the understanding that such a guard would be properly trained ([10-11]). However, unbeknownst to Mr. Visser, the security company and the guard lacked proper registration and training, which only came to light post-incident ([12]), creating a complicated legal backdrop for the appellant's claims ([13]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions

[14-15] The primary issue is whether Mr. Visser can be held liable for the actions of Mr. Morebudi, given that Griekwa Security operated as an independent contractor. The appellant’s argument hinges on the assertion that employing an armed guard in a residential area constitutes unreasonable risk, demanding a duty of care from Mr. Visser to prevent such dangerous situations ([15]).

[15-17] It was posited that a reasonable person in Mr. Visser's position would have foreseen the risk associated with trespassers and taken precautions, such as displaying warnings about the armed guard's presence and ensuring adequate illumination ([15]). Conversely, the respondent maintained that liability would not arise as he hired a recognized security service and expected them to act lawfully and judiciously based on their expertise ([16]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon

[18] The court underscored the legal principle that an employer typically isn't liable for the negligence of an independent contractor unless the employer has been negligent regarding the conduct of the contractor, as explored in **Colonial Mutual Life Assurance Society Ltd v Macdonald** 1931 AD 412 ([18]).

[19-23] Additionally, the court referenced **Langley Fox Building Partnership (Pty) Ltd v De Valence** 1991 (1) SA 1 (A), which stipulates that whether a duty exists depends on all circumstances, particularly the foreseeability of the risk, the required precautions, and whether they were duly taken. Importantly, these principles are not limited to cases of dangerous work but hinge on a general duty of care ([22-23]).

[19] The court identified that in English law, an employer can be liable for work performed by independent contractors when the work is inherently dangerous, per **Dukes v Marthinusen** 1937 AD 12 ([19]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law

[25-36] The court found that employing an armed guard on one’s property does pose a foreseeable risk of danger, leading to the first inquiry of foreseeability in **Langley Fox** ([25]). However, Mr. Visser had taken reasonable steps by hiring a security service, which presented itself as legitimate. His inquiry about the guard’s training reinforced his reasonable reliance on Griekwa Security's representations regarding proper firearm use ([26-27]).

[28] The court emphasized that Mr. Visser was not present during the incident and could not intervene; therefore, it would have been unreasonable to expect him to manage potential risk concerning the actions of the guard ([28]). Furthermore, the appellant failed to demonstrate how potential warnings about an armed guard would have materially altered the actions of the intoxicated intruders ([32]) or that the absence of such measures contributed to the injury ([31]).

[34] The court dismissed the idea that guards should use blank ammunition first or need specific positioning guidelines as impractical and potentially endangering to both the guard and the household ([34]). Thus, no additional liability could be ascribed to Mr. Visser based on failed assumptions about precautionary measures.

[37] Ultimately, the court articulated that holding Mr. Visser liable would unjustifiably extend the boundaries of legal accountability in this scenario and would not serve the interests of justice ([37]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications

[38] The Supreme Court of Appeal affirmed the lower court's decision and dismissed the appeal with costs ([38]). The key takeaway from the court's holding is the affirmation of the established principle that liability for the actions of independent contractors will not be imposed upon their employers unless clear negligence can be demonstrated.

This case illustrates the complexities involved when independent contractors are employed for potentially hazardous roles, emphasizing a homeowner's limited accountability when relying on professional expertise. The implications of this ruling reinforce property owners' rights to protect their assets while also outlining the responsibilities and expectations regarding security services.

Overall, the ruling serves as a significant precedent in South African tort law, delineating the boundaries of liability for independent contractors while also addressing the practicalities and dangers surrounding armed security in residential settings.
