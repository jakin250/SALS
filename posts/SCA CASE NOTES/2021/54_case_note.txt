# Case Note: Impact Financial Consultants CC and Another v Bam NO and Others (Case no 856/2019) [2021] ZASCA 54

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–26])

The case concerns a review application initiated by the applicants, Impact Financial Consultants CC and its member Michal Johannes Calitz, against the determinations made by the Ombud for Financial Services in relation to complaints lodged by multiple respondents regarding investment advice related to the Relative Value Arbitrage Fund Trust, managed by Abante Capital (Pty) Ltd. Following the collapse and liquidation of Abante Capital after the suicide of its manager, Herman Pretorius, respondents suffered significant financial losses, leading to complaints against Mr. Calitz for allegedly negligent financial advisory services ([paras 1–10]).

The Ombud found Calitz liable for negligent conduct, concluding that he failed to meet statutory obligations outlined in the Financial Advisory and Intermediary Services Act (FAIS Act) and the General Code of Conduct for Financial Services Providers ([paras 5–15]). After the Ombud's decisions were appealed unsuccessfully, the applicants sought a judicial review from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which ultimately dismissed their review application ([paras 20–26]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 1–26])

The substantive legal issue revolved around whether the Ombud committed a reviewable error in determining the complaints against Mr. Calitz and Impact Consultants, particularly regarding the jurisdiction to consider these complaints given that the respondents claimed they were advised to invest in a product not recognized as a financial product under the FAIS Act ([paras 2–4], [27]).

The review application also raised questions about whether there was a causal link between any alleged negligence and the financial losses suffered by the respondents, and whether the Ombud erred in concluding that negligence led directly to liability without considering common law principles of causation ([paras 22–25]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 1–26])

The key statutory framework governing the case includes the Financial Advisory and Intermediary Services Act, 37 of 2002 (FAIS Act), particularly sections relating to the Ombud's jurisdiction, the definition of complaints, and the statutory duties of financial advisers ([paras 28–39]). The Ombud's powers are established in section 20 of the FAIS Act, enabling her to investigate and adjudicate complaints in a procedurally fair manner. 

The relevant rules also encompass the General Code of Conduct for Financial Services Providers, which outlines the inherent duties and responsibilities of financial advisers to conduct proper due diligence and provide adequate disclosures regarding financial products ([paras 11–15]). The court also referenced the previous decision in *Atwealth (Pty) Ltd and Others v Kernick and Others*, which addressed similar issues related to hedge funds and the scope of financial advisory duties ([paras 40–44]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 1–26])

The Supreme Court of Appeal critically examined the Ombud's processes, focusing on her failure to definitively establish whether the financial product in question was indeed a "financial product" under the FAIS Act. The court highlighted that the product was labeled a hedge fund, yet it was unclear if it qualified under the legal definitions provided by the Act at the time of the complaints. The court expressed concern over the Ombud's assumption of jurisdiction without a thorough examination of the foundational facts related to the nature of the financial product ([paras 27–39]).

Moreover, the court questioned the Ombud's conclusions around the obligations of negligent conduct and liability. It underscored the need for a clear causal link between any alleged negligence and the respondents' losses, asserting that the Ombud's interpretation dismissed the necessity of this common law principle ([paras 20–26], [35–46]). Ultimately, the court found that the Ombud failed to fulfill her statutory duties regarding the ascertainment of facts that were essential for a proper adjudication of the complaints.

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 1–26])

In conclusion, the Supreme Court set aside the orders made by both the Ombud and the Gauteng High Court, ruling in favor of the applicants. The court determined that the Ombud had failed to establish her jurisdiction adequately and did not properly investigate the nature of the financial products in question. The appeals regarding the Ombud's determinations were referred back with directions for further investigation in accordance with the FAIS Act, ensuring that questions of jurisdiction and the facts surrounding each complaint were adequately addressed ([paras 49]). 

This case emphasizes the necessity for thorough examination and adherence to statutory duties by financial advisers, and it reinforces the importance of establishing jurisdictional foundations in complaints involving financial products. The decision provides clarity on the Ombud's powers and the boundaries within which she must operate, signaling to financial services providers the critical implications of their advisory roles in relation to defined legal standards and consumer protection.
