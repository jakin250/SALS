Body Corporate of Marine Sands v Extra Dimensions 121 (Pty) Ltd (1082/2018) [2019] ZASCA 161

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 161

F – Facts

The case arises from an appeal by the Body Corporate of Marine Sands (the appellant) against a decision by the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court, which invalidated a special resolution regarding the modification of levy contributions owed by sectional title owners ([paras 1-10]). The Body Corporate was constituted under the Sectional Titles Act 95 of 1986 (the STA) for a mixed-use sectional scheme known as Marine Sands, a 19-storey building located on Durban's Marine Parade ([para 2]).

The original developer determined, upon registration of the scheme in 1993, that non-residential owners would pay a different levy percentage than residential owners ([para 3]). This arrangement evolved following the addition of new non-residential units in 1997, adjusting participation quotas and modifying the levy responsibilities ([para 3]).

From 2002, Extra Dimensions 121 (Pty) Ltd (the respondent) owned six of the nine non-residential units, expecting levies to be consistent with their participation quota of 4,8409% ([para 4]). However, in January 2013, the Body Corporate implemented a new levy strategy based on a special resolution adopted at a subsequent meeting, which increased the respondent's monthly levy from R9 134,15 to R19 878,17 ([para 5]) without the respondent's consent ([para 12]).

The respondent challenged this resolution in court, arguing it was invalid under s 32(4) of the STA, which requires the written consent of any owner adversely affected by such modifications of levy contributions ([para 6-7]). The High Court dismissed the application ([para 8]). The Full Court found in favor of the respondent, leading the appellant to seek special leave to appeal.

I – Issues

The primary legal issue is the interpretation of the phrase "adversely affected" within the context of s 32(4) of the STA, which stipulates that modifications in the liability of owners for levy contributions must have the written consent of affected owners ([para 11]). Specifically, the court had to determine whether the respondent was "adversely affected" by the increase in the levy imposed by the special resolution, which directly impacted their financial obligations as unit owners ([paras 19-22]).

R – Rules

The relevant rule is derived from s 32(4) of the STA, which, conditioned by s 37(1)(b), allows the body corporate to amend rules modifying owners’ levy contributions but requires consent if owners are "adversely affected" ([para 11]). The court must interpret the wording of the statute using established principles, adhering to the ordinary meanings of the terms used ([para 19]).

The interpretation of "adversely affected" was at the core of the dispute, with the court referring to a variety of cases and interpretative rules, including [Bellevue Motors CC v Johannesburg City Council 1994 (4) SA 339 (W)], which mandates interpreting legislative language in its ordinary sense, and the principle that statutory words must be given their grammatical and ordinary meanings ([paras 13-14]).

A – Application

The court meticulously analyzed the phrase "adversely affected" and its implications under the STA. It emphasized that the respondent's increased financial liability for levies due to the new resolution meant they were indeed adversely affected, as their ownership position was less favorable than before ([paras 19-22]).

The argument presented by the appellant, based on past cases like Algar v Body Corporate of Thistledown, suggested that an increase in levies did not necessarily translate to "adverse" effects ([para 13]). However, the court distinguished their circumstance, clarifying that the interpretation of "adversely affected" should allow for the natural financial implications of increased levies, which diminish property rights and undermine investment value ([para 20]).

The judgment reiterated that legislative intent aims to protect owners from unilateral alterations that could deprive them of property rights without consent. This interpretation underscored the importance of the statutory safeguarding mechanism within the broader framework of the STA, which seeks to balance the interests of various owners within a sectional title scheme ([paras 21-22]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the Full Court's determination was correct. The resolution increasing the respondent's levy burden was ultra vires, as the required written consent of the adversely affected owner was not obtained ([paras 22-23]). Consequently, the court dismissed the appeal with costs, reaffirming the statutory requirement that protects owners from unexpected hikes in their financial liabilities within the collective decision-making structure of a body corporate ([para 23]).

This decision highlights the court's commitment to statutory interpretation grounded in the legislature's intent, ensuring compliance with owner protections embedded within the STA. It reinforces the significance of obtaining consent from affected owners as a necessary procedural safeguard before implementing substantive modifications regarding levy contributions.

Overall, the case stands as an important precedent for sectional title schemes in South Africa, particularly in clarifying the obligations of bodies corporate and the rights of individual owners within such structures.
