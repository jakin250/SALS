Supreme Court of Appeal

Republic of South Africa Case No. 38/97

The respondent, Anthony Michael Leveton, had a longstanding association with the medical aid industry and was once considered a leading expert in medical aids.

Supreme Court of Appeal 1998

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 2–11])

The respondent, Anthony Michael Leveton, had a longstanding association with the medical aid industry and was once considered a leading expert in medical aids. Having joined the Consolidated Employers Medical Aid Society (Cemas) in 1963, Leveton later became the executive chairman of two companies controlled by Southern Life Association Ltd (Southern) in 1990, including affiliated Medical Administrators (Pty) Ltd (Ama), which was itself a member of Cemas. Following deteriorating relations with Southern’s executives, Leveton entered into a settlement agreement on 12 August 1991, which allowed his immediate resignation from Ama, though he retained certain rights under his employment contract and specifically maintained his Cemas membership post-employment.

Leveton’s employment with Southern officially terminated on 30 June 1992, yet he was able to continue his membership with Cemas as a “retired member,” with Southern responsible for contributions until then. However, in March 1994, Ama informed Leveton of a unilateral decision to transfer all continuation members, himself included, to Southern Health medical aid, a scheme controlled by Southern. Leveton challenged this action, citing the terms of his employment letter and the rules of Cemas.

After failing to reconcile with the appellants, Leveton appealed to Cemas's disputes committee under the Medical Schemes Act 72 of 1967, which ultimately ruled in his favor, stating the transfer was not valid and recommended he be reinstated. The management committee of Cemas, however, ignored this recommendation, leading to Leveton’s subsequent court application seeking a declarative order that the disputes committee's decision was binding and that he should be reinstated as a member.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 12–16])

Several legal questions arose:

  1. Membership Validity: Did Leveton maintain his membership status in Cemas after the transfer to Southern Health, and was he entitled to continued coverage under the scheme based on the rules and statutory provisions?
  2. Dispute Settlement Authority: Was the decision of the disputes committee binding on Cemas and its management committee, considering Cemas's refusal to recognize the committee's authority?
  3. Proper Legal Procedures: Did the management committee possess the authority to disregard the decision of the disputes committee?

These issues reflect broader questions regarding adherence to statutory provisions and the mechanisms of internal dispute resolution within medical schemes.

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 17–22])

The pertinent legal framework encompasses the following:

  • Medical Schemes Act 72 of 1967: Specifically, sections 20(1)(g) and 22, which mandate that disputes must be resolved by a designated person or tribunal independent of management and stipulate that scheme rules are binding.
  • Cemas Rules: Subsequent amendments to Cemas's internal rules, particularly rule 6.3, established the conditions under which a member may retain membership after retirement.
  • Precedents regarding the interpretation of membership agreements, particularly regarding the binding nature of decisions made by dispute resolution bodies within organizations, were invoked, notably cases addressing similar governance questions.

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 23–21])

The court, led by Schutz JA, performed a detailed analysis of both the statutory provisions and the Cemas rules to determine Leveton's membership. It affirmed that, based on the definitions provided under the Act and prior arrangements, Leveton qualified as a member despite the applications of management's argument that his status was non-existent.

The court emphasized that the independent disputes committee had the authority to settle membership disputes, aligning its conclusions with the legislative intention to provide for competent third-party resolution in such matters. The management committee's refusal to recognize the recommendations was viewed as high-handed and contrary to the statutory framework specifying that the committee's determination was binding.

Furthermore, the court noted that the medical scheme’s rules were amended to reflect statutory changes and clarified that Leveton’s status must be determined as it stood in light of these new rules. Ultimately, it found that the management committee's interpretations were flawed and that the recommended reinstatement of Leveton was justified.

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 22–22])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal brought by the appellants, reaffirming the Full Court's decision that Leveton remained a member of Cemas after March 31, 1994, and that the disputes committee's ruling was binding. The court granted Leveton the relief sought, reinstating him as a member of Cemas.

The ratio decidendi underscores the importance of strict adherence to statutory mandates regarding dispute resolution and the autonomy of internal committees in governance structures, reinforcing the need for organizations to respect these protocols. This case highlights significant implications for medical schemes and similar organizations to ensure compliance with governance principles and respect for the rights of members in dispute scenarios, potentially impacting their operational procedures and conflict resolution protocols in the industry going forward.
