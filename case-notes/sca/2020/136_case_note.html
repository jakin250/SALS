Supreme Court of Appeal

The Premier for the Province of Gauteng and Others v Democratic Alliance and Others [2020] ZASCA 136

The Democratic Alliance (DA), aggrieved by this dissolution, sought a review in the Gauteng Division of the High Court.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 136

F – Facts

The case arises from the dissolution of the City Council of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality (the Tshwane Council) on 4 March 2020 by the Gauteng Executive Council (Gauteng EC), whereby an administrator was appointed to manage the municipality's affairs. This decision followed claims of dysfunction and inability to hold council meetings due to a deadlock caused primarily by the walkouts of ANC and EFF councillors ([1]–[5]).

The Democratic Alliance (DA), aggrieved by this dissolution, sought a review in the Gauteng Division of the High Court. On 29 April 2020, the full court granted the DA's application, declaring the dissolution decision invalid and ordering the ANC and EFF councillors to attend the council meetings unless legally absent ([2], [4]). The order was suspended until the lifting of a national lockdown due to COVID-19, allowing the administrator to manage the municipality during that period ([3]–[4]).

Following an application by the Gauteng EC and EFF for conditional leave to appeal, the DA sought enforcement of the High Court’s order pending appeal under s 18(3) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013. The High Court found exceptional circumstances were present, determining that the DA would suffer irreparable harm if the order was not enforced, while the Gauteng EC would face no such harm ([6]–[8], [20]–[22]).

I – Issues

The core legal issues pertained to the application of s 18 of the Superior Courts Act regarding interim enforcement of a judgment pending an appeal. Specifically, the central questions were:

  1. Did the DA meet the requirements laid out in s 18(3) of the Act for granting interim enforcement based on exceptional circumstances and irreparable harm?
  2. What is the proper interpretation of the 90-day election requirement stipulated under s 159 of the Constitution concerning the administrator's tenure?

R – Rules

Section 18 of the Superior Courts Act states:

  1. The operation of a judgment is suspended pending an appeal unless a court orders otherwise under exceptional circumstances (s 18(1)).
  2. For a court to grant an order to enforce a judgment pending appeal, the applicant must prove (i) exceptional circumstances; (ii) they will suffer irreparable harm without enforcement; and (iii) the respondent will not suffer irreparable harm if the order is granted (s 18(3)) ([9]–[11]).

The constitutional provisions relevant to the case include:

  • Section 139 of the Constitution, which governs provincial intervention in local government affairs.
  • Section 159(2) stipulates that elections must be held within 90 days following the dissolution of a municipal council or when its term expires ([9]–[10]).

The courts have established that the exceptionality and irreparable harm requirements must be proven on a balance of probabilities (Ntlemeza v Helen Suzman Foundation [2017] ZASCA 93) ([15]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) assessed whether the High Court correctly applied the criteria under s 18 in granting the DA’s application for interim enforcement. The DA constructed its case on two fundamental pillars:

  1. Exceptional Circumstances: The DA argued that the decision to dissolve the Tshwane Council effectively annulled the electoral votes of the municipality's residents, thus infringing upon the structural integrity of local government as intended by the Constitution. The SCA acknowledged that local governance is a fundamental constitutional principle and highlighted the significance of ensuring elected representatives conduct local government functions ([17]–[18]).
  2. Irreparable Harm: The SCA found persuasive the DA's claims that not enforcing the order would deprive residents of elected governance, emphasizing the injury to the principle of local autonomy, potential financial ramifications, and decisions made by an appointed administrator who lacked accountability to the voters. The SCA noted that the potential extension of the administrator's tenure beyond the constitutionally mandated period of 90 days constituted justified concerns regarding irreparable harm ([20]–[22]).

In its decision, the High Court found that the Gauteng EC would not suffer irreparable harm if the order was enforced, pointing to the dysfunctionality of the council which resulted from the failure of ANC and EFF councillors to attend meetings, a situation amenable to resolution through compliance with existing governance codes ([24]–[30]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the High Court had appropriately considered and applied the requirements under s 18(3) of the Superior Courts Act. As such, the appeal by the Gauteng EC, Premier, and MEC was dismissed, reaffirming the order of the High Court, thus allowing the elected councillors of Tshwane to resume their duties in accordance with constitutional provisions. The implications of this decision affirm the significance of local governance and the necessity for elections to remain within the allotted constitutional timeframe, reinforcing democratic principles at the municipal level ([31]–[43]).

The SCA’s ruling serves as a critical reminder that while provincial intervention is permitted under exceptional circumstances, such actions are bound by constitutional deadlines to safeguard democracy, ensuring that elected representatives are not unduly sidelined by provincial decisions ([41]–[42]).

This case highlights essential principles pertinent to governance structures in South Africa, focusing on accountability, the role of elected officials, and the importance of timeliness in electoral processes, particularly against the backdrop of unforeseen circumstances like a national disaster.
