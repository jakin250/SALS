Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2003

## FIRAC Case Note: Schabir Shaik v The Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development and Others

### F – Facts
In this case, Schabir Shaik (the applicant), a businessman and director of companies, sought leave to appeal against the High Court's judgment regarding the constitutionality of certain provisions in Section 28 of the National Prosecuting Authority Act 32 of 1998 (the "Act") that permit examination under oath of individuals connected to ongoing investigations ([1]–[3]). The respondents included various officials from the South African justice system, including the Minister of Justice, the National Director of Public Prosecutions, the Investigating Director: Director of Special Operations, the Deputy Director of Public Prosecutions, and an advocate employed by the Directorate of Special Operations.

The background of the case stems from an investigation initiated on August 24, 2001, concerning suspected fraud and corruption linked to armament acquisitions by the Department of Defence ([4]). Following a preparatory investigation, a search warrant was executed on October 9, 2001, leading to Shaik’s arrest on November 16, 2001. Charges of theft and related offences were set for trial on May 27, 2002.

On July 12, 2002, Shaik received a summons under Section 28(6) of the Act, requiring him to appear before the Directorate of Special Operations to answer questions and produce documents. Following complications regarding the investigative process, Shaik’s legal team sought to challenge the validity of this summons in the High Court ([5]–[7]). The High Court dismissed his application, finding that the questioning did not violate his constitutional rights, aside from determining that Section 28(6) infringed upon the right to remain silent under Section 35(1)(a) of the Constitution, although this limitation was justified ([11]–[12]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues raised in this case concern:
1. Whether Shaik’s right to a fair trial was infringed by the summons requiring him to testify and produce documents under Section 28(6) of the Act.
2. The constitutionality of Section 28(6) of the Act in light of various rights enshrined in the Constitution, particularly his rights under Section 35, which includes the right to remain silent and not to be compelled to give self-incriminating evidence ([9]–[10]).

An additional procedural issue arose from whether the High Court's finding that the procedure for questioning did not constitute administrative action was valid ([12]).

### R – Rules
The key legal authorities cited in this case include:
- **The Constitution of South Africa**: Particularly Section 35, which outlines the rights of arrested persons, including the right to remain silent and not to testify against oneself.
- **National Prosecuting Authority Act 32 of 1998**: The provisions of Section 28 were scrutinized, particularly subsections (6), (8), and (10), which detail the powers and obligations of the Investigating Director and affected individuals.
- **Relevant Case Law**: The court referenced Ferreira v Levin 1996 (1) BCLR 1 (CC) to illustrate constitutional principles and limitations regarding self-incrimination ([6]).

### A – Application
The court applied the constitutional provisions by scrutinizing the impact of Section 28(6) on the applicant's rights. It found that while this section infringed upon Shaik’s right to remain silent, it was a justified limitation as per Section 36(1) of the Constitution ([12][19]–[21]). The broader context in which "any person" was used in Section 28(6)(a) was also critical; the court held that it did not apply to accused persons in active criminal proceedings.

On addressing the mootness of the case, the court concluded that while the direct controversy had diminished (as the specific summons had not been enforced), the possibility remained that similar scenarios could arise for other affected parties ([16]–[20]).

The court also expressed concerns regarding the adequacy of provisions surrounding the questioning process under Section 28, pointing out that the Investigating Director's authority carried significant weight without the necessary oversight from an independent arbiter ([39]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed Shaik's application for leave to appeal, ruling that the constitutional attack was improperly directed at Section 28(6) rather than the pertinent subsections (8) and (10) which dealt with self-incrimination and the consequences of non-compliance. The court emphasized the need for precision in constitutional challenges, concluding that it was not in the public interest to determine the validity of the provisions in question without significant legal focus ([34][42]).

The implications of this decision underscore the balance between effective law enforcement and the protection of individual rights within the criminal justice framework. It highlights the necessity for practitioners to ensure that constitutional attacks are precisely articulated, given the potential wide-reaching consequences of any declarations of constitutional invalidity.
