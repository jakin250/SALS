Source Folder: 2021

## FIRAC Case Note: Competition Commission v Beefcor (Pty) Ltd and Another [2021] ZACC 9

### F – Facts
[1] This application for leave to appeal arose from a dispute regarding the reinstatement of a complaint by the Competition Commission (the Commission) against Beefcor (Proprietary) Limited (Beefcor) and Cape Fruit Processors (Proprietary) Limited (Cape Fruit). The complaint alleged that the respondents engaged in prohibited practices under section 4(1)(b) of the Competition Act 89 of 1998 (the Act), specifically colluding not to compete in the market for processing wet peels and citrus peel pulp used in livestock feed ([4]).

[2] The Tribunal was initially set to hear the case from 2 to 4 July 2018. However, shortly before the hearing, the Commission approached Cape Fruit seeking a postponement to explore a potential settlement, which Cape Fruit conditioned upon the Commission withdrawing the matter. The Commission ultimately withdrew the complaint, thinking it would allow for more productive negotiations ([5]–[6]).

[3] Upon the withdrawal, the Tribunal removed the matter from the roll, informing the Commission that it would need to file a substantive application to reinstate the complaint ([7]). When no settlement occurred, the Commission applied for reinstatement in October 2018, which the respondents opposed, citing section 67(2) of the Act as a barrier against referring the same complaint to the Tribunal once more ([8]).

### I – Issues
[4] The case primarily presents two legal questions: First, the interpretation of section 67(2) concerning whether the Commission could reinstate a withdrawn complaint, and second, whether the Competition Tribunal exercised its discretion properly in denying the reinstatement ([23]).

### R – Rules
[5] Section 67(2) of the Competition Act provides that "a complaint may not be referred to the Competition Tribunal against any firm that has been a respondent in completed proceedings before the Tribunal under the same or another section of this Act relating substantially to the same conduct" ([26]). 

[6] The underlying principles involve the right of firms not to face repeated complaints arising from the same conduct, thus preventing double jeopardy ([13]). The interpretation of the term "completed proceedings" is crucial and must align with constitutional protections under the Bill of Rights, particularly sections 34 and 39(2), which relate to access to a fair hearing and administrative justice ([18]).

### A – Application
[7] The Tribunal's initial decision identified that the withdrawal of the complaint was a unilateral act and did not equate to settlement. It concluded that such a withdrawal did not constitute "completed proceedings," allowing the Commission the potential opportunity for reinstatement, though it ultimately declined the application due to a lack of sufficient justification ([10]–[11]).

[8] The Competition Appeal Court (CAC), however, reversed this position, asserting that the withdrawal did equate to completed proceedings under section 67(2), thereby preventing reinstatement. The CAC argued that the interpretation of completed proceedings should reflect administrative fairness and firm protection from repeated disputes originating from the same conduct ([15]–[16]).

[9] The Constitutional Court examined whether the interpretations by the CAC properly advanced the objects of the Bill of Rights. It concluded that completed proceedings must mean cases distinctly resolved by adjudication rather than those merely withdrawn and that the CAC's interpretation diminished access to the Tribunal ([30]).

[10] Regarding the Tribunal's discretion in denying reinstatement, the Constitutional Court found that the Tribunal had appropriately considered the interests of justice, which included the need to promote fair procedures and avoid prejudice against the respondents. This finding underscored that the Tribunal had not misdirected itself in its assessment of the application ([51]).

### C – Conclusion
[11] The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal, upheld the appeal, and set aside the order of the Competition Appeal Court, thereby affirming the Tribunal's authority to reinstate a withdrawn complaint. The Court highlighted that the interpretation of section 67(2) should safeguard the right of access to the Tribunal while ensuring that the statute's intent to prevent double jeopardy is preserved ([52]). 

[12] The ruling emphasizes the importance of balancing the protection of responding firms from harassing repeated complaints with the need for the Commission to adequately enforce competition law. The orders established the scope for interpreting "completed proceedings" and reinforced the judiciary’s role in ensuring fairness and proportionality in competition compliance and enforcement. This case will have significant implications for how complaints are managed within the competition law framework in South Africa and the procedural guidelines followed by the Tribunal.
