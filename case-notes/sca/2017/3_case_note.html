Joubert v S (642/2016) [2017] ZASCA 3

During the appellate hearing, the State issued a notice of its intention to seek an increased sentence after leave to appeal was granted erroneously against both the conviction and the sentence ([2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 3

F – Facts

In the case of Joubert v S, Mr. Francois Johan Joubert, an accountant, was convicted by the Nelspruit Regional Court on 20 counts of fraud related to false VAT claims made to the South African Revenue Service ([1]). His initial sentence involved seven years’ imprisonment, wholly suspended under certain conditions for five years. He appealed this conviction, focusing solely on the conviction aspect, and was granted leave to appeal to the Gauteng Provincial Division of the High Court, Pretoria. However, the court a quo unexpectedly increased his sentence to a direct seven years' imprisonment, with four years conditionally suspended ([1]).

During the appellate hearing, the State issued a notice of its intention to seek an increased sentence after leave to appeal was granted erroneously against both the conviction and the sentence ([2]). The appellant's counsel raised procedural concerns regarding this notice, arguing that it violated the provisions of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA) ([2]). The case was reviewed against the backdrop of the pertinent Constitutional Court ruling in S v Bogaards, which affirmed an accused's right to receive prior notice of any intended increase in sentence ([4]).

I – Issues

The crux of the appeal revolved around the following legal questions:

  1. Did the court a quo infringe the appellant's right to a fair trial by increasing his sentence sans prior notice of this intention?
  2. Were the procedural irregularities, arising from the unexpected increase in sentence, sufficient to warrant the appeal’s success and the setting aside of the increased sentence ([1], [12])?

R – Rules

The relevant rules and precedents applicable in this case included:

  • Constitutional rights: The right to a fair trial, encapsulated within Section 35 of the Constitution of South Africa, obliges courts to adhere to principles of natural justice, including prior notice of any intended increases in sentence ([11]).
  • S v Bogaards: This Constitutional Court finding established the necessity for an accused to receive prior notification if there is an intention to augment a sentence upon appeal. Failure to provide such notice is considered a severe irregularity potentially leading to a miscarriage of justice ([4], [11]).
  • S v Nabolisa: This case articulated the requirement for the State to obtain leave for cross-appeal when seeking to appeal a sentence imposed by a lower court ([7], [12]).

A – Application

In applying these principles, the court critically examined the lack of procedural adherence during appellate proceedings. Notably, the court acknowledged that the appellant's counsel was not adequately prepared for a potential increase in sentence as no prior notice had been given ([6], [9]). The appellant had framed his defense and appeal strategy solely around the conviction and was thus caught by surprise when the sentence was unexpectedly escalated.

Furthermore, the court reviewed submissions made during the hearing, where it became apparent that the appellant’s counsel had been unaware of the court's intention to increase the sentence, which is critical for fair trial rights ([6], [10]). The court emphasized that the significant procedural irregularity—the absence of notice—was not cured by the State’s last-minute notification of intention to increase the sentence ([7], [8]).

In terms of prejudice, the court concluded that the appellant clearly suffered material detriment due to the irregular increase, noting that he was deprived of the option to withdraw his appeal given the unexpected nature of the proceedings ([10]). Also, the court reaffirmed that the right to be informed of possible increased standing before the court encapsulates the crucial element of natural justice ([11]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, citing a substantial miscarriage of justice due to the unfair procedural conduct by the court a quo. The increased sentence imposed on Joubert was set aside, with the court remitting the matter back to the Gauteng Provincial Division of the High Court for reconsideration of the appeal against the original sentence, ensuring that proper procedures as per the guidelines in S v Bogaards were followed ([12], [13]).

Consequently, the case underscores the vital importance of procedural fairness in criminal appeals, reinforcing that any increase in sentence requires clear communication and adherence to established legal frameworks to safeguard the rights of the accused.
