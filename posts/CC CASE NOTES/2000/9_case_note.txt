Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2000

# Case Note: First National Bank of South Africa Limited v Land and Agricultural Bank of South Africa CCT 15/00; Henry Niel Sheard v Land and Agricultural Bank of South Africa CCT 07/00

## Facts 

[1] These two applications for confirmation of orders of invalidity arise from the Orange Free State and Eastern Cape Divisions of the High Court, which struck down several provisions of the Land Bank Act 13 of 1944. Specifically, the High Court deemed sections 34(3)(b) to (7), (9) and (10) and sections 55(2)(b) to (d) invalid, as they authorized the Land and Agricultural Bank of South Africa (the Land Bank) to attach and sell a debtor’s property without recourse to a court of law. Section 34 pertains to the execution against movable property, whereas section 55 deals with immovable property. Moreover, section 34(5) grants the Land Bank a preferent right to the proceeds from such sales. The High Courts found that these provisions were constitutionally impermissible, as they infringe upon the right to access the courts, protected under section 34 of the Constitution of South Africa. This finding was consistent with the Court's prior ruling in *Chief Lesapo v North West Agricultural Bank and Another* 2000 (1) SA 409 (CC), which invalidated a similar self-help procedure utilized for debt recovery ([paras 1-3]).

[2] The applications arose from two High Court cases: *First National Bank v Land and Agricultural Bank of South Africa* (FS) 1995/98 and *HN Sheard v Land and Agricultural Bank of South Africa* (E) 1542/99, wherein the Land Bank conceded the constitutional invalidity of the provisions but argued for a suspension of invalidity to allow parliamentary rectification. The Orange Free State High Court issued an order prohibiting the Land Bank from engaging in any attachment or sale under the contested sections until confirmation by the Constitutional Court. Costs were ordered to be paid by the Land Bank ([paras 2-3]).

## Issues 

[3] The principal legal questions before the Constitutional Court concerned:

1. Whether sections 34(3)(b) to (7), (9) and (10) and sections 55(2)(b) to (d) of the Land Bank Act are unconstitutional due to their inconsistency with the right to access the courts under section 34 of the Constitution.
2. If found unconstitutional, should the invalidity order be suspended, and for how long? 
3. Should the Court grant retrospective effect to the order of invalidity regarding past sales executed under these sections? ([paras 4-6]).

## Rules 

[4] The Court relied primarily on constitutional provisions, particularly section 34, which enshrines the right to access the courts for dispute resolution. The ruling in *Lesapo* established that any law providing for self-help mechanisms in debt recovery violates this right, necessitating judicial oversight to uphold the rule of law ([paras 4-5]).

The Court also referenced section 172 of the Constitution, which grants the judiciary the authority to make orders that are just and equitable when declaring laws unconstitutional, including the suspension of invalidity and the limitation of retrospective effects ([paras 12-13]).

## Application 

[5] In its analysis, the Court noted that the impugned sections of the Land Bank Act allowed the bank to execute property seizures without judicial oversight, effectively permitting self-help measures that are inconsistent with the principles of the rule of law. The Land Bank’s process required only written notification to the debtor before attachment and sale, akin to the invalidated procedures in *Lesapo*. The Court emphasized that the absence of judicial involvement in such processes creates a significant risk of injustice, thereby violating the right to access to courts ([paras 5-6]).

[6] The Court examined the Land Bank's request for suspension of the invalidity order. It acknowledged the Land Bank's role in providing financial services to emerging farmers and its reliance on the impugned provisions for risk management. The inability to attach property could undermine the Bank's viability, affecting its ability to offer loans, thereby harming the rural economy. The Court balanced these concerns against the necessity of judicial oversight to ensure justiciable disputes are resolved within the legal framework. In the end, the Court ruled that while it recognized the potential adverse impact of immediate invalidity, allowing a suspension for two years was justifiable to enable parliamentary rectification ([paras 7-11 ][16]).

[7] The Court further reasoned that severing the offending provisions from the Act was not a viable solution because such action would fundamentally alter the legislative scheme, which was intended to allow for rapid debt recovery without judicial intervention. It reaffirmed that suspending the invalidity was a more appropriate remedy to allow for legislative amendments while still maintaining the Bank's operational integrity ([paras 12-16]).

## Conclusion 

[8] The Constitutional Court confirmed the orders of invalidity made by the High Courts regarding sections 34 and 55 of the Land Bank Act, finding them unconstitutional for violating section 34's guarantees of access to courts. It determined, however, that the order of invalidity for sections 34(3)(b) to (7), (9) and (10) would be suspended for a period of two years to allow parliamentary amendments, provided that future actions must include judicial intervention. The existing attachments not yet perfected by sales would be invalidated, thereby preserving the rights of affected parties ([paras 19-20]). 

This decision emphasizes the crucial balance between ensuring constitutional rights are upheld and allowing sufficient time for legislative bodies to correct deficiencies in the law, reflecting the Court's commitment to the principles of justice and equity within South Africa’s evolving legal landscape.
