Supreme Court of Appeal

Kitshoff v Fedsure Staff Pension Fund & Others (597/16) [2017] ZASCA 31

The appellant, Jacobus Johannes Kitshoff, was employed by the second respondent, The Building Industry Bargaining Council (BIBC), from 17 October 1977 until his retrenchment on 30 June 2003 ([1]-[2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 31

F - Facts

The appellant, Jacobus Johannes Kitshoff, was employed by the second respondent, The Building Industry Bargaining Council (BIBC), from 17 October 1977 until his retrenchment on 30 June 2003 ([1]-[2]). At the time of his employment, the Fedsure Staff Pension Fund (the Fund) was the pension fund associated with BIBC, which made contributions on behalf of its employees, including Kitshoff. The relevant legal framework was established by the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956 (the Act).

In March 2002, Investec Employee Benefits (Investec) acquired Fedsure Holdings Limited, which managed the Fund. Following this acquisition, on 1 July 2002, the Fund ceased accepting contributions from BIBC, leading to the termination of the latter’s membership in the Fund ([3]). BIBC subsequently joined the Wizard Universal Pension Fund (WUPF), and on 9 July 2004, approval was sought from the Registrar of Pension Funds for the transfer of benefits from the Fund to WUPF, effective retrospectively from 1 July 2002 ([3]-[4]).

After his retrenchment, Kitshoff received a payment from WUPF of R2,120,153, calculated based on the transfer of benefits, but claimed an additional R529,307 in enhanced retirement benefits he believed he was entitled to due to his termination of service ([3]-[4]). The appellant contended that his rights to enhanced pension benefits arose when he was retrenched, despite the Fund's application for transfer having a retrospective effect ([4]).

I - Issues

The principal legal issue was whether Kitshoff retained any vested rights to enhanced pension benefits under the rules of the Fund after his retrenchment on 30 June 2003, given that BIBC had ceased contributions to the Fund from 1 July 2002 and he was subsequently a member of WUPF ([4]-[6]). Specifically, the case concerned the interpretation of section 14(1) of the Pension Funds Act and the effect of the retrospective approval of the transfer of benefits on Kitshoff’s entitlement to benefits from the Fund ([4], [12]).

R - Rules

The court referenced several legal provisions and established principles regarding pension funds and employment relationships:

  1. Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956:
  • Section 14(1) governs the transfer of benefits between pension funds, requiring the approval of the Registrar of Pension Funds.
  1. Rules of the Fund:
  • Rule 8, outlining the conditions for enhanced retirement benefits upon retrenchment for employees with five years of service or more ([4]).
  1. Contract Law Principles:
  • The court also applied principles from contract law, particularly regarding offer and acceptance, repudiation, and the termination of contractual obligations ([9]).
  1. Precedent:
  • The case references Nash v Golden Dumps (Pty) Ltd 1985 (3) SA 1 (A), which discusses the repudiation of contracts and consequent termination ([9]).

A - Application

The court analyzed the chronological sequence of events, determining that when BIBC ceased contributions to the Fund on 1 July 2002, it effectively ended its relationship with the Fund ([5]-[12]). Consequently, Kitshoff's membership was also annulled as it was contingent upon the Employer's active participation in the Fund.

The court noted that Kitshoff's enhanced benefits under Rule 8 could not be claimed without an ongoing membership in the Fund, which was interrupted when BIBC transferred to WUPF ([6]-[12]). It was further reasoned that since Kitshoff accepted payment from WUPF, it was inconsistent for him to claim he was still a member of the Fund at the time of his retrenchment ([10]).

Moreover, the court emphasized that the retrospective nature of the approval for the transfer of assets between funds, as per section 14 of the Act, did not retroactively restore Kitshoff’s membership or rights under the Fund. The appellant's argument that the approval of the transfer was unlawful due to BIBC's perceived continuance in the Fund was also rejected ([8]-[12]).

C - Conclusion

The court concluded that Kitshoff did not retain any enforceable rights to the enhanced pension benefits following his retrenchment on 30 June 2003. The relationship between the employer and the Fund was legally terminated when BIBC ceased contributions, and without membership in the Fund, there was no basis for his claim to the additional benefits ([12]). Consequently, the appeal was dismissed, and costs were awarded against Kitshoff, reinforcing the notion that the legal status of pension fund memberships is intricately linked to active contributions and participatory status governed by fund rules and associated contractual obligations.

Order

The appeal was dismissed with costs ([13]).

References

  • Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956.
  • Nash v Golden Dumps (Pty) Ltd 1985 (3) SA 1 (A).
  • Oudekraal Estates (Pty) Ltd v City of Cape Town & others [2004] ZASCA 48.

This case highlights the critical nature of adhering to the stipulations of both employment and pension fund rules in maintaining rights to benefits, thus serving as a vital precedent for similar disputes within pension fund litigation.
