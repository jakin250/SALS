Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

### FIRAC Case Note: Petrus Johannes Bestbier and Others v Nedbank Limited (Case No. 150/2021) [2022] ZASCA 88

#### F - Facts
The proceedings arise from a financial dispute between the appellants, who are trustees of the Goede Hoop Trust, and the respondent, Nedbank Limited. The trust operates a wine farm in Stellenbosch, Western Cape ([1]). The appellants defaulted on their debt obligations to Nedbank, which had provided substantial financial assistance through an overdraft and mortgage loans totaling R9.2 million ([4]). Following their failure to comply with payment conditions set out in a settlement agreement reached in March 2019, Nedbank sought a judgment that included declaring the trust's property (Goede Hoop Wine Estate) executable ([5]). The high court ruled in favor of Nedbank, permitting execution against the property, which is the primary residence for the appellants, their family members, and trust employees ([3], [6]).

The appellants contended that the property served as a primary residence for the beneficiaries and employees of the trust. They argued that, consequently, the protections afforded by Rule 46A of the Uniform Rules of Court must apply ([7]). They suggested that the rule applies to the residential properties of individuals in a similar predicament, even if owned by a trust ([6], [7]). In the end, the high court's decision led to the appellants appealing both the debt judgment and the order declaring the property executable ([1]).

#### I - Issues
The primary issues before the court were:
1. Whether Rule 46A of the Uniform Rules of Court applies when the property sought to be declared executable is owned by a trust and serves as the primary residence of its beneficiaries, their families, and employees ([2], [6]).
2. Whether the trust beneficiaries and employees are entitled to the constitutional protections related to housing as etched in Section 26 of the South African Constitution ([2], [8]).

#### R - Rules
The legal framework relevant to this dispute centered on Rule 46A of the Uniform Rules of Court, which mandates judicial oversight before executing against the residential property of a judgment debtor. Rule 46A necessitates that:
- The court determines if the property is the primary residence of the judgment debtor.
- Alternatives to execution are considered ([10]).

The constitutional backdrop of this inquiry involves Section 26 of the South African Constitution, framing the right to adequate housing and the judicial precedents in **Jaftha v Schoeman** and **Gundwana v Steko Development**, which emphasized the necessity for judicial oversight in cases where execution might impair an individual's right to housing due to poverty ([11], [12]).

#### A - Application
The court examined the applicability of Rule 46A through the lens of past jurisprudence concerning residential properties in execution cases ([17]). The appellants argued the high court misinterpreted Rule 46A as applying only when the property in question is the direct residence of a natural person. They posited that a broader interpretation was necessary to encompass situations involving trusts that house vulnerable populations such as beneficiaries and farmworkers ([16], [32]).

The court, however, clarified that the purpose of Rule 46A is to protect individuals at risk of losing adequate housing, particularly among indigent populations ([18], [20]). Applying this reasoning to the current case, the court contended that the context—being a commercial loan secured against property not exclusively used as a residence—diminishes the avenue for claiming the protection against execution under Rule 46A. The court considered the assurances given in the consent order and the absence of evidence from the appellants demonstrating how the beneficiaries or workers would become homeless or lose access to adequate housing if the property was sold ([24], [31]).

While the court recognized the role of judicial oversight to protect rights under Section 26, it ruled that the unique circumstances of the case did not warrant execution being stayed because the trust beneficiaries had not established their status as indigent vulnerable individuals facing imminent loss of housing security. The property's valuation—significantly higher than the debt owed—also played a considerable role in the court's decision, concluding that there were alternative avenues for maintaining housing security ([31], [32]).

#### C - Conclusion
Ultimately, the court dismissed the appeal, upholding the high court's findings ([1], [33]). It articulated that Rule 46A applies in a case where those residing in the property at risk are in the Jaftha-kind category, which the appellants failed to demonstrate ([32]). The ruling affirmed the high court's interpretation of the application of Rule 46A while addressing the protections under Section 26 of the Constitution, clarifying it is primarily the vulnerable who are at risk of losing housing that are afforded this protection. The decision reinforces the position that remedies in execution procedures are contingent on concrete evidence of potential housing insecurity. The appellants were ordered to bear the costs of the appeal, including those for two counsel, reflecting a comprehensive judgment in favor of ensuring legal and procedural integrity within the execution process ([32], [33]). 

This case highlights the balance that courts must strike between enforcing financial agreements and safeguarding fundamental rights to housing, particularly within the context of trust-owned properties.
