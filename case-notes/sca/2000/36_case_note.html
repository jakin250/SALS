Supreme Court of Appeal

MARS INCORPORATED v CADBURY (SWAZILAND) (PTY) LIMITED

Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa Case No. 427/98 Heard: 25 August 2000 Delivered: 8 September 2000 Coram: Harms, Marais, Plewman JJA, Melunsky, and Mpati AJJA

Supreme Court of Appeal 2000

Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

Case No. 427/98
Heard: 25 August 2000
Delivered: 8 September 2000
Coram: Harms, Marais, Plewman JJA, Melunsky, and Mpati AJJA

F - Facts

The case revolves around the registrations held by Cadbury (Swaziland) (Pty) Limited ("Cadbury") for two trade marks: the name mark "Chappies" and a device mark depicting a dancing chipmunk with the words "Chappie Chipmunk" and "Chappie Tjokker" ([1]). The marks are notably well-known in Southern Africa, particularly within the context of bubble gum, where more than 8,826 tonnes were consumed in 1995 ([1]).

In an attempt to expand the scope of protection for its trade marks, Cadbury obtained defensive registrations that included Class 31, which encompasses animal foodstuffs ([1]). Historically, defensive registrations allowed for broader trade mark protection without the necessity of active usage in the registered class, serving as a remedy for statutory passing off ([2]). Under the repealed Trade Marks Act 62 of 1963, the criterion for such registration rested on the registrar’s opinion regarding potential market connection ([3]).

The appellant, Mars Incorporated ("Mars"), sought revocation of these defensive registrations, asserting that they impeded its applications for trade mark protection for its own mark "Chappie" and a corresponding device mark for animal food ([4]). Mars argued that Cadbury had registered defensive marks without sufficient evidence of any conceivable market connection between chewing gum and animal food ([5]). The application for removal of the marks was dismissed by the Transvaal Provincial Division, prompting Mars to appeal ([4]).

I - Issues

The core legal issues presented in this case include:

  1. Validity of Defensive Registrations: Did the registrar erroneously allow the registration of Cadbury's defensive trade marks by not adequately demonstrating potential market confusion between chewing gum and animal foods? ([5])
  2. Application of Statutory Provisions: Does Section 42 of the repealed Trade Marks Act continue to apply to defensive registrations after the enactment of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993? ([9])
  3. Right of Appeal: Did Mars retain any right of appeal against the registrar's opinion regarding the connection between the marks, especially after having withdrawn its opposition during the registration process? ([15])

R - Rules

Key legal provisions and precedents relevant to the case include:

  • Section 53(1) of the repealed Trade Marks Act, which provided for defensive registrations based on the registrar's belief in a likelihood of confusion ([2]).
  • Section 42 of the repealed Act, which established a presumption of validity for registered marks after a period of seven years unless specific disqualifying conditions were shown ([8]).
  • Section 70(1) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, which clarifies that the validity of trade marks registered under the previous act is determined by the law in force at the time of their registration ([10]).

Additionally, the court referenced the case Mars Incorporated v Candy World (Pty) Ltd, which established that the registrar's opinion serves as a jurisdictional fact in registration cases ([15]).

A - Application

In addressing the validity of the defensive registrations, the court reaffirmed the appropriate application of Section 42, outlining that the provision indeed upheld the validity of Cadbury's original registrations ([10]). The court interpreted that Mars’s attempt to revoke the registrations hinged on questioning the registrar's opinion. However, since Mars had previously withdrawn its opposition to the registrations, it lacked standing to contest that opinion in the current proceedings ([15]).

The court examined whether the transition from the 1963 Act to the 1993 Act affected the validity of the existing defensive registrations. It concluded that while the new act eliminated the possibility of defensive registrations, Cadbury's marks remained valid under the previous law’s provisions regarding their original registration ([11]).

Furthermore, the court dismissed Mars’s argument that Section 42 did not apply to defensive marks, considering that Cadbury's reliance on Section 42 was legitimate, given that the marks were registered more than seven years before the commencement of the new act ([8], [13]).

In terms of market connections, the court examined the evidence presented by Cadbury which demonstrated extensive use and recognition of the Chappies brand in relation to confectionery ([7]). It emphasized that the connection deemed relevant by the registrar at the time of defensive registration could not be appellatively attacked post-withdrawal of Mars's opposition ([15]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Mars's appeal, affirming that Cadbury's defensive registrations could not be revoked based on the claims articulated by Mars. The judgment reinforced the importance of the registrar's opinion in trade mark proceedings and clarified the transitional issues regarding the validity of marks under the new legislation ([14], [17]).

This decision highlights crucial implications for the treatment of defensive registrations and underscores the enduring role of the registrar in adjudicating trade mark disputes. Moreover, it illustrates the necessity for applicants to maintain their standing in opposition proceedings if they wish to preserve the right to appeal against registrar decisions. The ruling ultimately preserves Cadbury's rights in the competitive market of confectionery and animal food products.
