Constitutional Court

Ex parte Minister of Social Development and Others CCT 14/06

This case arose as an ex parte application for urgent relief submitted to the Constitutional Court of South Africa.

Constitutional Court 2006 CCT 14/06

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

This case arose as an ex parte application for urgent relief submitted to the Constitutional Court of South Africa. It involved the Minister of Social Development and various Members of Executive Councils (MECs) from different provinces ([1]–[7]). The applicants sought to extend the suspension period of an order declaring a presidential proclamation, Proclamation R7 of 1996, invalid. This original declaration followed a judgment from the Pretoria High Court on November 7, 2003, which invalidated the proclamation that assigned the administration of the Social Assistance Act 59 of 1992 (SAA) to the provinces ([2]).

On September 6, 2004, the Constitutional Court confirmed this order of invalidity but provided an 18-month suspension to allow for legislative and administrative compliance processes ([5]). The applicants claimed that substantial preparations had been made to comply with the court’s order, yet they could not complete the necessary preparations before the expiry of the 18-month period, which they argued would end on March 6, 2006. Consequently, they sought to extend this suspension to April 1, 2006, to avoid severe distress among social grant recipients ([6]–[8]).

I – Issues

The instant application raised several critical legal questions regarding:

  1. The calculation of the suspension period and whether it indeed expired on March 6, 2006, or on the previous day ([13]).
  2. The court's authority to issue further orders once an invalidity declaration took effect ([13]).
  3. The appropriateness of an ex parte application in circumstances where there were already interested parties ([13]).

R – Rules

The rules relevant to the case included:

  • Section 172(1)(b)(ii) of the Constitution: This provision allows the court to make any order that is just and equitable, including suspending the effect of a declaration of invalidity for any period ([28]).
  • Common Law Principles: The principle of functus officio prevents a court from altering its final orders unless there are recognized exceptions, such as clarifying ambiguities or correcting errors ([30]–[31]).
  • Previous Case Law: The court’s powers regarding suspending declarations of invalidity were shaped by Minister of Justice v Ntuli and Zondi v MEC for Traditional and Local Government Affairs, which clarified the court's authority regarding extensions of suspension timelines ([29]–[36]).

A – Application

In evaluating the application, the court distinguished between the lapse of a suspension order and its effect on the validity of prior legislation. Van der Westhuizen J emphasized that the original suspension period expired at midnight on March 5, 2006. Therefore, by the time the application was presented on March 6, the proclamation’s invalidity was already in effect, and the court no longer possessed the authority to grant the extension sought by the applicants ([24]–[28]).

The court also critically assessed the conduct of the applicants, describing their timing in bringing the application as a significant oversight, creating a situation of urgency that arose from their own remissness ([16]–[19]). The representation that urgent measures were not taken to secure necessary funds was met with skepticism, as the court noted that constitutional obligations concerning social welfare payments were paramount. The court was unconvinced that grant payments to recipients could not be made lawfully, as alternative administrative measures could exist ([20], [44]).

Furthermore, the ex parte nature of the application did not adhere to procedural fairness, as the original applicant, Mr. Mashavha, had an interest in the matter and should have been notified ([21])–[22]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the court dismissed the application, highlighting that the failure to comply with the original court order lies squarely with the applicants. By miscalculating the suspension’s expiry date and approaching the court too late, they forfeited the opportunity to seek an extension ([41]–[45]). The court emphasized the importance of finality in litigation and the constitutional imperative to uphold the law, even in cases where the societal impact of a decision, such as the discontinuation of social grants, might be dire ([43], [46]).

Order: The application was dismissed ([47]).

This case serves as a vivid reminder of the need for judicial bodies to act diligently within their constitutional mandates, underscoring the separation of powers and the importance of procedural fairness within urgent applications.
