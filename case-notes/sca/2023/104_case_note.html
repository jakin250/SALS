City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality v Vresthena (Pty) Ltd & Others [2023] ZASCA 104

In response, Vresthena filed an urgent application before the Gauteng Division of the High Court on 28 March 2022, seeking a court order to compel the Municipality to process its application for a separate electricity connection and to restore services ([para 3]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 104

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The respondents, Vresthena (Pty) Ltd, own six units within the Zambesi Retail Park, a sectional title scheme, and lease these properties for business use. The properties share a single electricity supply point from the City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality, which also supplied water services ([para 2]). The Body Corporate managing the scheme had been dysfunctional since its inception. Due to unpaid utility services by the tenants, the Municipality issued disconnection notices, resulting in the suspension of electricity and water services on 13 April 2022 ([para 3]).

In response, Vresthena filed an urgent application before the Gauteng Division of the High Court on 28 March 2022, seeking a court order to compel the Municipality to process its application for a separate electricity connection and to restore services ([para 3]). The High Court granted an interim order on 16 June 2022, instructing the Municipality to reconnect utilities within 14 days and authorized Vresthena to engage an electrician if the Municipality failed to comply ([para 4]).

Following the Municipality’s application for leave to appeal against this order, Vresthena sought a declaratory order under s 18(3) of the Superior Courts Act, asserting that the High Court's order should remain in effect while the appeal was considered ([paras 5-6]). The High Court ultimately granted the Municipality leave to appeal on 28 September 2022 but also ordered that the original order be executed in the meantime. Subsequently, the full court dismissed the Municipality's appeal on 10 November 2022 ([para 7]).

On 22 November 2022, the Municipality lodged a notice of appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal, claiming that the phrase “next highest court” in s 18(4) of the Superior Courts Act could allow for a second appeal, contrary to Vresthena’s assertion that it refers to a single appeal from the lower court ([para 7]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal were:

  1. Interpretation of 'next highest court' in s 18(4): Does s 18(4)(ii) provide for a second right of automatic appeal to the "next highest court" after a full court has already addressed an appeal under s 18(4)? ([para 1])
  2. Regularity of the notice of appeal: Was the notice of appeal filed by the Municipality irregular and thus void? ([para 7])

R – Rules

The key legal authorities and statutes relevant to this case included:

  • Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013: Specifically, sections 16 (general appeal provisions), 17 (conditions for leave to appeal), and 18 (automatic right of appeal) ([paras 8-10]).
  • Constitutional principles: The right of access to courts under s 34 of the Constitution, alongside statutory interpretations affirmed in cases such as Cool Ideas 1186 CC v Hubbard and Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality ([paras 20-22, 24]).
  • Judicial Precedents: The Supreme Court previously held that s 34 does not inherently include a right to appeal similar to other provisions in the Constitution ([para 25]).

A – Application

In its deliberation, the court thoroughly examined the specific wording of s 18(4). The court acknowledged the unique position of actions prescribed under s 18(3) and their interlocutory nature, which ordinarily limits the appeal scope. It concluded that the language in s 18(4)(ii) delineates a distinct, singular right to appeal, which does not permit a second appeal ([para 23]).

Moreover, the court emphasized that the interpretation of legislative texts should adhere to general principles that prioritize their grammatical meaning unless it leads to absurdity. The court reiterated that the legislative intent behind s 18(4) was to facilitate accessible and expedited relief for parties facing irreparable harm ([paras 19-21]).

The court also dismissed the Municipality’s assertions regarding the need for additional appeals to meet constitutional fairness standards, referencing the Supreme Court's prior remarks which clarified that s 34 does not imply an unlimited right of appeal ([paras 25-26]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that the notice of appeal lodged by the Municipality was irregular and void due to its failure to comply with the stipulations of s 18(4) by attempting to assert a second appeal opportunity that the statute does not permit ([para 27]). Therefore, the matter was struck from the roll with costs, including the costs of two counsels where applicable ([para 28]).

Implications

This ruling clarifies the application of s 18(4) within the Superior Courts Act and reinforces the principle that the language of the statute confines parties to a single automatic appeal under specific circumstances outlined by the legislature. It underscores the importance of preserving judicial efficiency while protecting litigants from procedural complexities that may impede timely resolutions of urgent matters.
