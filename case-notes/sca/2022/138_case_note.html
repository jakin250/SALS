Supreme Court of Appeal

Nesongozwi v Commissioner for SARS (838/2021) [2022] ZASCA 138

Tax Administration Act 28 of 2011 (TAA): Governs assessments and objections, providing the basis for the taxpayer's rights to object, appeal, and the Commissioner’s prerogatives in assessing tax liabilities ([12]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 138

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts ([1–6])

The appellant, James Matodzi Nesongozwi, is a mining engineer who was involved in the transfer of shares in the Nesongozwi Mining Corporation (Pty) Ltd (NMC) to the Nesongozwi Family Trust. The case arose from an additional tax assessment for the 2010 year by the South African Revenue Service (SARS), which deemed Nesongozwi liable for capital gains tax and donations tax due to the transfer of shares that was allegedly undervalued. Nesongozwi had sold the shares for R547,275, while SARS argued that the market value was significantly higher, amounting to R274,050,000. The taxpayer objected to this assessment, claiming that proper valuation methods had not been applied.

I – Issues ([8–10])

The primary legal issues addressed in this case included:

  1. Whether the tax court properly assessed the market value of the shares sold by Nesongozwi to the trust, particularly concerning the validity of the valuation methods used.
  2. Whether the taxpayer's grounds of objection and appeal were sufficient and properly articulated within the framework set by the Tax Administration Act 28 of 2011 (TAA).
  3. The applicability of provisions that prevent the inclusion of contingent liabilities or restrictions on share transferability in the valuation calculation.

R – Rules ([12–24])

The legal authorities and statutory provisions considered central to this case include:

  • Tax Administration Act 28 of 2011 (TAA): Governs assessments and objections, providing the basis for the taxpayer's rights to object, appeal, and the Commissioner’s prerogatives in assessing tax liabilities ([12]).
  • Section 106 of TAA: Outlines the rights of a taxpayer in relation to objections and the necessity for specificity in grounds of objection ([15]).
  • Rule 7 and Rule 10 of the TAA: Provide procedures for lodging objections and appeals, emphasizing the requirement for clarity in specifying grounds of objection ([14][17]).
  • Past case law, such as Lion Match Company (Pty) Ltd v Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service, which established that only the grounds specified in objections can be pursued on appeal ([10]).

A – Application ([27–47])

The court applied the statutory framework to evaluate whether the taxpayer had properly raised his objections. The tax court found that the valuation methods proposed by the taxpayer's experts were in line with those used by SARS, and thus the valuation methodology was acknowledged as correct. The taxpayer had argued for various discounts based on contingent liabilities and share transfer restrictions, but the court rejected these claims, citing that the existence of contingent liabilities such as the consultancy agreement with Sumo Coal did not lower the value of shares due to uncertainties surrounding their realization.

Furthermore, the tax court found that restrictions on share transferability should not factor into the market value assessment as per the Eighth Schedule of the Income Tax Act, reaffirming that assessments must consider shares as if they were freely transferable.

The full court upheld the tax court's findings, emphasizing that the characterizations of mineral resources and contingent liabilities were thoroughly assessed based on credible expert testimony presented by SARS. The late introduction of new grounds of appeal regarding valuation methods was deemed inappropriate, as they had not been part of the preliminary objections upon which the case had been based.

C – Conclusion ([58–65])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Nesongozwi's appeal, affirming the assessments made by SARS and the findings of both the tax court and full court. It held that there was no erroneous application of law regarding valuation methods or the characterization of mineral resources, affirming the Commissioner’s decisions based on valid expert valuations. The implications of this case underscore the importance of precision in articulating objections and grounds of appeal within the prescribed statutory framework, potentially creating precedents for future valuation disputes in tax law.

The court concluded that the taxpayer failed to establish that the lower courts had misdirected themselves or had erred in their application of statutory provisions or principles of law. Costs were awarded to the respondent, signifying the court’s alignment with the procedural regularities observed under the TAA ([64][65]).

This structured FIRAC provides a comprehensive analysis of Nesongozwi v Commissioner for SARS, showcasing the procedural nuances in tax assessment appeals, the importance of adhering to statutory requirements, and the critical evaluation of expert witness credibility in tax disputes.
