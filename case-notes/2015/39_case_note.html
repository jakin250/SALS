Case Title: Baloyi v Member of the Executive Committee for Health and Social Development, Limpopo and Others [2015] ZACC 39

F – Facts ([paras 1–14])

The applicant, Gezani Julius Baloyi, had a long employment history with the Department of Health and Social Development, Limpopo. He began as an artisan at Malamulele Hospital in 1987, eventually ascending to the role of senior artisan superintendent at Mankweng Hospital by 2003. In March 2004, he was charged with misconduct related to the initiation of repairs for incinerators that were allegedly unnecessary. The charges stemmed from his actions regarding services that purportedly had not been rendered by the supplier, Prominent Medical Supplies CC.

During the disciplinary hearing, Baloyi contended that he had acted upon a lawful instruction from Dr. Wasilota, the hospital’s superintendent. Despite his request to summon Dr. Wasilota as a witness—a request denied by the head of the department—he maintained that the testimonies of other witnesses at the hearing supported his defense. Ultimately, he was dismissed on 19 February 2005, following an appeal to the department which was also unsuccessful.

Baloyi then sought arbitration at the Public Health and Welfare Sectoral Bargaining Council, where the arbitrator ruled his dismissal as fair on both procedural and substantive grounds. After a series of failed review applications before the Labour Court, Labour Appeal Court, and Supreme Court of Appeal, Baloyi sought condonation and leave to appeal in the Constitutional Court, claiming that the necessary records for a fair review were missing.

I – Issues ([paras 1–2, 25–30])

The primary legal issue in this case revolved around the procedural integrity required when reviewing an arbitration decision in the absence of a complete record. Specifically:

  1. What should the Labour Court do when faced with a review application where the arbitration record is incomplete?
  2. Was Baloyi's dismissal substantively and procedurally fair given the limitations stemming from the missing record?

R – Rules ([paras 20–24, 58–62])

The rules applied in this case stem from South African labor law, specifically the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995, which governs the fairness of dismissal proceedings. Key aspects include:

  1. Onus of Proof: The employer must substantiate that the dismissal was fair both procedurally and substantively (section 191(1)(a)(i) of the Act).
  2. Procedural Fairness: Reflected in the Code of Good Practice found in the Act, which provides guidelines indicating that an employee must be given the opportunity to question witnesses and that all relevant parties should be present.
  3. Judicial Precedents: Several cases ([35] onwards) were referenced regarding situations where courts encountered incomplete records, indicating how courts might proceed in such circumstances, particularly under the principles established in Lifecare Special Health Services and related rulings.

A – Application ([paras 34–44])

The Constitutional Court found that the Labour Court's decision to adjudicate the review application without a complete record was improper. The Court highlighted several key factors:

  1. Ineffectiveness in Resolving the Matter: The Labour Court's handling of the case without a proper record undermined Baloyi’s rights to a fair hearing, particularly in light of omissions, such as the denial of witness testimonies vital to his defense.
  2. Borderline Unfairness: The lack of evidence from the employer regarding procedural adherence and the absence of contested evidence by senior employees further supported the premise that the dismissal was not adequately substantiated.
  3. Long Employment History Without Previous Issues: Baloyi's considerable tenure and unblemished record of service lent credence to the improbability of a justified dismissal based on the alleged misconduct.
  4. Judicial Discretion: The Court held that rather than remanding to a new arbitration—an action that could further delay justice and potentially prejudice Baloyi—the court could decide on the merits directly. Given the significant time lapse and the probable absence of key testimonies (notably, Dr. Wasilota’s death), remitting the case was seen as an unduly harsh remedy.

The Court thus determined that Baloyi’s reinstatement was justified given the circumstances surrounding his dismissal, thereby effectively overturning the arbitrator’s decision.

C – Conclusion ([paras 44–45])

The Constitutional Court ruled in favor of Baloyi, granting him condonation and leave to appeal. The Labour Court's decision was set aside, and Baloyi was reinstated to his former position effective from the date of his dismissal. The respondents were ordered to bear the costs of the application.

This case underscores the critical importance of procedural fairness in disciplinary matters, particularly when an employee's livelihood is at stake, and the potential consequences of a court proceeding without a full record. It highlights the necessity of ensuring that all parties have access to due process in labor disputes, aligning rulings with principles of natural justice and fairness in administrative actions.
