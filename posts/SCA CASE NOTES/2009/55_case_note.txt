# Case Note: Minister of Safety and Security v Tyulu [2009] ZASCA 55

## FIRAC Method

### Facts
[1] The respondent, Mr. Tyulu, a 48-year-old magistrate in Cape Town, initiated an action against the Minister of Safety and Security (the appellant) for unlawful arrest and detention, claiming damages amounting to R400,000. The High Court initially awarded him R280,000, which was appealed by the appellant to the Full Bench, resulting in a confirmation of the unlawful nature of the first arrest but a validation of the second arrest, alongside a reduced award of R50,000 for damages. 

[2] On 12 October 2003, while walking to a convenience store near his home in Milnerton, Tyulu was arrested by police officers, who accused him of being drunk in public. The officers, Captain Cordier and Inspector Dell, encountered him on Bosmansdam Road, where he was reportedly walking in the middle of the road and exhibiting signs of intoxication, just prior to a collision that had occurred nearby.

[3] After being detained and tested, a forensic analysis showed that the alcohol concentration in his blood was 0.23g per 100ml, indicative of being under the influence but, according to the examining doctor, Dr. Nel, not necessarily "drunk" as defined under the law. Tyulu affirmed that while he had consumed alcohol the night before, he did not believe himself to be intoxicated to a degree that would hinder his faculties or present a danger to himself or others. 

[4] During the legal proceedings, the trial court determined both arrests were unlawful and accepted Tyulu’s claim regarding humiliation and emotional distress, while the Full Bench confirmed the lawfulness of the second arrest based on corroborated visual identification and circumstantial evidence of intoxication.

### Issues
[18] The case primarily raised two legal issues: 
1. Whether the appellant's assertion that Tyulu’s first arrest complied with s 40(1)(a) of the Criminal Procedure Act (CPA) was justified, making the arrest lawful.
2. If the amended damages award of R50,000 was excessive, warranting adjustment.

### Rules
[12] The authority for the appellant's actions rested on s 40(1)(a) and (f) of the CPA, which allows a police officer to arrest without a warrant in specific circumstances:
- **(a)** Where the offence is committed in the officer's presence.
- **(f)** Where the officer reasonably believes the person was about to commit an offence during the night.

[15] Section 154(1)(c) of the Liquor Act 27 of 1989 states that it is unlawful to be drunk in or near public roads.

[21] The appellant bore the onus of proof to establish the legality of the arrest on a balance of probabilities (Minister of Law and Order v Dempsey 1988 (3) SA 19 (A); Zealand v Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development 2008 (2) SACR 1 (CC) at paras 24-25).

### Application
[19-22] The court examined the initial arrest, determining that the evidence presented by the appellant did not convincingly demonstrate that Tyulu was "drunk" as defined by law. The Full Bench acknowledged that drunkenness is distinct from mere intoxication, and no definitive evidence was submitted to justify the conclusion that Tyulu had lost control of his faculties. Therefore, it ruled that the first arrest could not be validated under s 40(1)(a) or (f).

[24-26] With respect to the quantum of damages, the court considered several key factors: the implications of Tyulu's status as a magistrate, his public humiliation during the arrest, the context in which the arrest occurred, and comparable case awards. The judgment emphasized that compensatory damages aim to address emotional injury rather than enrich the plaintiff. Conclusively, the majority agreed that R15,000 was a more reasonable figure reflective of the egregiousness of the unlawful arrest while accounting for the short duration of detention.

### Conclusion
[29] The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal concerning the damage quantum, reducing the amount awarded to the respondent from R50,000 to R15,000. The first arrest was deemed unlawful, having not met the statutory requirements under the CPA. This case underlines the importance of maintaining police accountability and highlights the significance of personal liberty rights in South African law. The court's decision also reinforces a broader understanding that claims of unlawful arrest must be substantiated with clear and convincing evidence regarding the alleged behaviour of the arrested individual. 

This judgment sets a precedent reflecting a careful balance between police powers and civil liberties, ensuring that the lawfulness of arrests is properly scrutinized in light of constitutional protections.
