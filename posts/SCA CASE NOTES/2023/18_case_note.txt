Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: South African Forestry Company SOC Ltd v Collins Sebola Financial Services (Pty) Ltd and Others [2023] ZASCA 18 

## F – Facts

The dispute originates from the tender process executed by the South African Forestry Company SOC Ltd (SAFCOL), a state-owned enterprise and the third largest forestry entity in South Africa. SAFCOL issued a tender (RFB 011/2019) for security services across its various forestry operations and business units ([para 1]). 

Three bids were deemed compliant: the first by Collins Sebola Financial Services (Collins Sebola), the first respondent; the second by Phepha MV Security Services (Phepha), the seventh respondent; and the third by Puthadichaba Trading Enterprise CC, the tenth respondent. Ultimately, the tender was awarded in such a manner that two of the service providers, Collins Sebola and Phepha, received contracts for different portions of the security services required. Collins Sebola's contract was valued at R18 285 386.27, while Phepha's contract was valued at R62 193 884.32. Both contracts were set for a three-year term, expiring on 31 March 2023 ([para 2]).

Dissatisfied with SAFCOL's decision not to award the entire tender to them, Collins Sebola sought judicial review from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, aiming to set aside the award given to Phepha and secure the services that Phepha was contracted to provide ([para 3]). The primary judge, Basson J, ruled in favour of Collins Sebola, thereby granting the relief sought and subsequently denying SAFCOL's application for leave to appeal ([para 4]).

The case subsequently reached the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa with the leave to appeal granted ([para 4]). During the proceedings, it was revealed that both parties acknowledged the impending expiration of the contracts on 31 March 2023 ([para 5]). They mutually concurred that section 16(1)(a)(i) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, indicating that an appeal may be dismissed if it holds no practical effect, was applicable ([para 5]).

## I – Issues

The pivotal issues before the court included:

1. Whether the appeal by SAFCOL against the High Court's decision was still valid given the impending expiration of the contracts awarded as part of the tender process ([para 5]).
2. Whether the appeal could be dismissed as it would have no practical effect, given that the contracts in question would expire before the appellate court could render a decision ([paras 5–6]).

## R – Rules

The relevant legal standards underpinning this case arose primarily from the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, particularly section 16(1)(a)(i), which states that an appeal may be dismissed if the decision sought will have no practical effect or result ([para 5]). This provision is intended to prevent the waste of judicial resources on appeals that cannot materially alter the existing situation ([para 5]).

Additionally, the principles surrounding the judicial review of administrative decisions under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 may also be relevant. However, the direct applicability of the rules concerning administrative justice was not central to the court's rationale for dismissing the appeal, as the immediate concern was the temporal limitation posed by the contracts ([para 6]).

## A – Application

The court engaged with the implications of section 16(1)(a)(i) in determining the fate of the appeal ([para 5]). Both counsels for the appellant and first respondent conceded that, irrespective of the outcome of the appeal, the status quo regarding the implementation of the contracts would remain unchanged. If SAFCOL's appeal were to succeed, the existing contracts would continue as is; conversely, if the appeal were dismissed, Collins Sebola recognized the infeasibility of taking over any services from Phepha within the contract duration ([para 5]). 

Thus, as both parties acknowledged that no practical result would follow from the appeal, the court recognized that proceeding with the appeal would essentially serve no purpose and therefore fell squarely under the ambit of section 16(1)(a)(i) ([para 6]).

In regards to costs, the court noted that costs must follow the result, a principle which was agreed upon by both parties, leading to the straightforward conclusion that costs should be awarded against SAFCOL given the dismissal of the appeal ([para 6]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, recognizing that the matter lacked practical implications as the contracts related to the tender would expire on 31 March 2023, thus rendering any decision moot ([para 6]). The court's rationale underscored a judicial economy principle, establishing that it is inefficient to engage in appellate review when no practical remedy can result from the court's decision.

This ruling has implications for future cases concerning tender awards and the administrative processes involved, particularly emphasizing the importance of timely resolutions in contractual matters where delays could render judicial decisions irrelevant. The decision reinforces the application of section 16 of the Superior Courts Act to prevent judicial resources from being expended on appeals that offer no effective remedy to the parties involved. 

Finally, as the appeal was dismissed with costs, this reiterates the standard legal principle that parties must bear the financial consequences of unsuccessful appeals, which serves to promote diligence in prosecuting appeals in a timely manner ([paras 6–7]).
