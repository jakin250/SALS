Constitutional Court

Du Plessis v. De Klerk [1996] CCT 8/95

Following the publication, De Klerk and Wonder Air issued a combined summons out of the Transvaal Provincial Division, claiming R750,000 and R5 million in damages for defamation, respectively.

Constitutional Court 1996 CCT 8/95

F - Facts

The case originates from a series of six articles published in February and March 1993 by the Pretoria News, focusing on South African citizens allegedly supplying arms to UNITA in Angola. The articles, authored by the journalist Dale Lautenbach, implicated specific individuals, including the plaintiff Gert de Klerk and his company, Wonder Air (Pty) Ltd., in illegal operations. The articles characterized these actions as "illegal" and suggestive of personal gain, causing significant reputational damage to the plaintiffs ([1]–[2]).

Following the publication, De Klerk and Wonder Air issued a combined summons out of the Transvaal Provincial Division, claiming R750,000 and R5 million in damages for defamation, respectively. The defendants, including the newspaper's editor, owner, and reporter, admitted the articles were published but denied their defamatory nature and claimed a defense of public interest and fair comment. Notably, on 7th October 1994, the defendants sought to amend their plea, invoking the protection afforded by section 15 of the Constitution, which guarantees freedom of expression ([3]–[4]).

However, the plaintiffs objected to the amendment on grounds asserting the inapplicability of the Constitution at the time of the publication and the unavailability of constitutional defenses in private disputes. The Transvaal Provincial Division, presided by Judge Van Dijkhorst, ruled to deny the amendment on these bases, emphasizing that the relevant proceedings must be viewed under the law in force before the Constitution, thus dismissing the constitutional argument ([6]–[9]).

I - Issues

The key legal issues presented before the Constitutional Court can be summarized as follows:

  1. Can the defendants invoke constitutional protections (specifically section 15), given that the harmful acts occurred before the Constitution's commencement? ([10])
  2. Is there a horizontal application of the rights enshrined in Chapter 3 of the Constitution, allowing individuals to rely on these rights in disputes against other private individuals? ([11])

R - Rules

The Constitutional Court primarily relied on the following legal rules and principles:

  1. Constitutional Provisions: The relevant constitutional provisions are found in the interim Constitution of South Africa, specifically section 15(1), which guarantees the right to freedom of expression. Additionally, section 251(1) establishes the commencement date of the Constitution (April 27, 1994), informing whether actions taken before this date could invoke constitutional protection ([12]–[13]).
  2. Legal Precedents: The court referenced prior cases, including S v Mhlungu, which clarified that rights conferred by Chapter 3 of the Constitution are available to persons in pending cases after the Constitution's commencement. The court further examined the debate concerning the constitutional protections' vertical vs. horizontal applicability, questioning whether such rights could extend to private disputes ([12]–[14]).

A - Application

The court applied several considerations regarding the scope of the Constitution and its interaction with past conduct. Kentridge AJ established that the provisions in the interim Constitution do not operate retroactively to change the legality of past actions. Therefore, the defendants could not claim protection for unlawful acts committed prior to the Constitution's enactment ([21]–[22]).

Further, the court determined that section 15 of the Constitution does not extend application horizontally to private disputes and asserted that Chapter 3's rights, while applicable to public law, do not serve as grounds for private entities to assert claims against each other. This reasoning was reinforced by the systematic examination of legal provisions which demonstrate the Constitution’s design to protect individuals primarily against state action ([61]–[63]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the appeal, concluding that the defendants could not invoke the Constitution in this instance, as the relevant articles had been published before its operation. The court clarified that the provisions of Chapter 3 are intended for vertical application, primarily concerning the relationship between the state and individuals, rather than horizontal application in private disputes. The ruling underscored that the Constitution does not retroactively validate past illegal conduct, maintaining the integrity of established law prior to its enactment ([67]–[68]).

The implications of this ruling affirm the Constitution's role in shaping public discourse and the enduring nature of defamation claims, while also solidifying the separation between constitutional rights and private law remedies in the South African context.

Citation: Du Plessis v. De Klerk [1996] CCT 8/95, Constitutional Court of South Africa.
