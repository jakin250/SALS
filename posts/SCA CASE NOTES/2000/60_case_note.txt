# FIRAC Case Note: South African Raisins (Pty) Ltd v S A D Holdings Ltd
## In the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
### Case Number: 176/2000
### Delivered: 29 September 2000
### Judges: Grosskopf, Howie, Plewman JJA, Melunsky and Farlam AJJA

---

## F – Facts
The case at hand arises from an appeal by South African Raisins (Pty) Ltd and Johann Petrus Slabber (the appellants) against a decision of Ngoepe JP in the Transvaal Provincial Division ([1]). The crux of the matter is whether the Competition Act 89 of 1998 applies to the raisin industry, particularly concerning the legality of certain orders issued by the Competition Tribunal. These orders, which were deemed null and void by the lower court, revolved around allegations of anti-competitive practices by S A D Holdings Ltd and its subsidiary, S A D Vine Fruit (Pty) Limited (the respondents) ([1], [5]).

Initially, the respondents held a dominant position in the industry, controlling the marketing of raisins in and outside South Africa prior to the enactment of the Marketing of Agricultural Products Act 47 of 1996, which allowed the first appellant to enter the market ([2]). Following this entry and a dispute over the use of storage containers, the first appellant sought redress from the Competition Commission in October 1999, alleging prohibited practices by the respondents ([4]). Subsequent actions by the Tribunal led to the current appeal, which challenges both the applicability of the Competition Act and the jurisdiction of the Tribunal to make the orders in question ([6]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal question central to the appeal is whether the Competition Act applies to the raisin industry, particularly in light of the definitions provided under the Marketing of Agricultural Products Act ([1]). Specifically, the appeal concerns:

1. Whether the marketing of raisins falls within the ambit of the Competition Act 89 of 1998 or is governed solely by the Marketing of Agricultural Products Act 47 of 1996 ([1], [8]);
2. The validity of the Tribunal's orders given the lower court's declaration that the Tribunal lacked jurisdiction ([6]).

## R – Rules
The relevant statutes in this case include:

1. **Competition Act 89 of 1998**: This act establishes the framework for governing anti-competitive practices and defines the scope of "economic activity" that falls under its jurisdiction. Notably, s 3(1)(d) provides exceptions for acts that are "subject to or authorised by public regulation" ([9], [10]).

2. **Marketing of Agricultural Products Act 47 of 1996**: This act serves as an enabling statute allowing the Minister of Agriculture to regulate the marketing of agricultural products, defined under s 1(1) to include "fruit and nuts" ([7], [12]). The lack of specific regulatory measures regarding the marketing of raisins under this act is crucial to determining whether it precludes the application of the Competition Act ([13]).

The court also referenced **Standard Bank Investment Corporation Ltd v Competition Commission and Others** 2000 (2) SA 797 (SCA), where it stipulated that "acts" referred under the Competition Act must relate to monopolistic or anti-competitive behaviors that are explicitly defined in the Act ([10]).

## A – Application
The court's analysis began with a review of s 3(1) of the Competition Act, which applies broadly to all economic activity unless exempted by specified public regulations ([8]). The key issue was whether the marketing of raisins was indeed subject to such regulation under the Marketing of Agricultural Products Act. The court noted that no directives or statutory measures had been established to regulate raisins under this Act ([14]). 

Given the broader policy aims of the Competition Act, which sought to combat monopolistic practices in the South African economy, the court concluded that a narrow interpretation of "subject to or authorised by public regulation" was necessary. The absence of any regulatory measures concerning the marketing of raisins indicated that the activities in question were not explicitly governed by the Marketing of Agricultural Products Act, thus falling squarely within the jurisdiction of the Competition Act ([16]).

Therefore, the Tribunal indeed had the requisite jurisdiction to issue the orders on 24 November and 24 December 1999. This decision was reached notwithstanding the lower court's findings that suggested a lack of jurisdiction ([14], [18]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the applicability of the Competition Act 89 of 1998 to the raisin industry was affirmed, thereby reversing the lower court's ruling that had deemed the Tribunal's orders null and void ([19]). Consequently, the appeal was allowed with costs, and the case underscored the necessity for clarity regarding the interaction between competition law and sector-specific agricultural regulations. This ruling not only reinforced the Tribunal's jurisdiction but also highlighted the broader implications for other agricultural sectors potentially affected by similar regulatory ambiguities ([20]).
