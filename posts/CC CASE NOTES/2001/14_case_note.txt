Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2001

# FIRAC Case Note: Lane N.O. v Dabelstein and Others

**Case Title**: Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Case Number**: CCT 60/00  
**Decided on**: 6 March 2001  
**Judges**: Goldstone and Kriegler JJ  

## F – Facts

The case at hand pertains to an application for special leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court against a judgment from the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) regarding the insolvency of one Jürgen Harksen ([1]). The SCA's decision had the effect of setting aside an attachment of South African assets owned by the respondents. The applicants, Michael Lane and Eileen Fey, were the trustees in Harksen's insolvent estate, seeking to set aside certain payments made to the respondents under purportedly fraudulent circumstances, driven by court orders that had not been examined for validity ([1]).

In the initial proceedings, the High Court found there to be a prima facie case of fraud, leading to the attachment order ([2]). However, the SCA overturned this, arguing that the requests made in the applicants' affidavits did not adequately establish a prima facie case of fraud, which prompted the current appeal ([2]). The Constitutional Court's intrusion was requested following claims of constitutional violations concerning access to justice, discrimination against foreign creditors (peregrini), and the right to a fair trial ([3]).

## I – Issues

The primary issues under consideration included:
- Whether the SCA's order infringed the applicants' constitutional right of access to courts as outlined in Section 34 of the South African Constitution.
- Whether the legal requirement for peregrini to attach assets to establish jurisdiction, while not required for incolae (local defendants), constitutes unfair discrimination under Section 9 of the Constitution.
- Whether the SCA’s failure to consider crucial evidence violated the applicants' right to a fair trial, again as articulated in Section 34 ([3]).

## R – Rules

In deciding this case, the court referenced key provisions of the South African Constitution, particularly Sections 9 and 34, which ensure equality before the law and access to courts, respectively ([3]). The court also considered principles stemming from the common law and earlier case law, such as *Sackstein en Venter NNO v Greyling*, which relates to the exclusion of certain dispositions made under court orders from being adjudicated as voidable if found fraudulent ([2]). 

The court’s stance is that constitutional matters should ideally be presented to lower courts to fully explore their merits before escalating to the Constitutional Court, ensuring that the High Court and SCA have the opportunity to address these legal and constitutional concerns thoroughly ([5]).

## A – Application

The court applied the law to the facts of the case by critically examining the grounds on which the applicants based their appeal. On the alleged infringement of the right of access to the courts, the court held that even if a factual error was made by the SCA, this did not inherently violate constitutional rights, focusing instead on whether the proceedings reflected fairness rather than absolute correctness ([4]). 

Regarding the claim of discrimination against peregrini, the court noted that this point had not been raised in earlier proceedings, thus complicating its consideration at this late stage. The court indicated a reluctance to alter longstanding common law principles unless necessary and supported by a clear record ([5], [6]). The potential implications of the case, while significant for trade and international business relations, did not convince the court to bypass standard procedural requirements ([6]).

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the application for special leave to appeal and ordered that the applicants pay the costs of the application ([8]). The ratio decidendi of the court emphasized the importance of due process and the establishment of substantive evidence of constitutional violations before appeal to the highest court is warranted. This case underscores the significance of adhering to procedural norms before invoking constitutional challenges in appellate litigation, reflecting a cautious judicial philosophy that prioritizes the integrity of the lower courts in constitutional adjudication ([7]).

---

This assessment reflects the court's careful balancing act between the need for judicial uniformity, respect for the constitutional framework, and the demands of justice in commercial contexts. The principles outlined in this judgment are pivotal for future cases involving both insolvency and constitutional law, maintaining the integrity and clarity of legal processes surrounding these issues in South Africa.
