Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 98

F – Facts

The case concerns appeals by Chicco Masango and Hendrique Muainga against their convictions and sentences for robbery with aggravated circumstances, as per the provisions of section 51(2) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997. Both appellants were convicted by the Regional Court for the District of Soweto on 24 January 2017. The first appellant received a sentence of twenty years, while the second was sentenced to fifteen years imprisonment. Their applications for leave to appeal were denied by the Regional Court and later by the High Court, prompting a petition to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ([1]–[2]).

The incident involved robbery at the residence of Mr. Jacques Porter, as testified by Ms. Wendy Ndlovu, who was employed as a housekeeper. On 1 December 2015, Ndlovu encountered two men purportedly there to measure the premises for air-conditioning installation. After gaining access under false pretenses, the men tied her up, assaulted her, and stole various items including a red BMW belonging to Mr. Porter. Shortly thereafter, law enforcement apprehended the appellants while they were found dismantling the stolen vehicle ([6]–[13]).

The appellants denied wrongdoing, claiming they were merely walking by when they were confronted by the police ([14]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions hinged on whether the High Court erred in refusing the appellants leave to appeal against their convictions and sentences, specifically whether they demonstrated reasonable prospects of success on appeal. Key aspects of this question included:

  1. The reliability of identification evidence provided by a single witness (Ms. Ndlovu) ([6]).
  2. The application of the doctrine of recent possession in assessing the appellants' guilt ([15]).
  3. The appraisal of the sentencing discretion exercised by the Regional Court in relation to the appellants' criminal histories ([16]).

R – Rules

The SCA emphasized that it traditionally lacks jurisdiction to assess the merits of a lower court's conviction or sentence without prior leave to appeal being granted. Legal precedent clarifies that the evaluation revolves around "reasonable prospects of success," as established in cases such as Nong and Masingi v The State and S v Smith. The latter articulates that for an appeal to succeed, there must be a dispassionate basis suggesting that a court of appeal could reasonably arrive at a different conclusion from that of the lower court ([4]–[5]).

Furthermore, matters of identification, particularly concerning dock identification, were addressed through historical rulings which caution against relying solely on such evidence due to its inherent unreliability ([15]).

A – Application

In assessing the appellants' arguments, the SCA scrutinized the single witness testimony provided by Ms. Ndlovu and the implications of her dock identification of both men. Although Ndlovu had initially expressed uncertainty, the circumstances under which the vehicle was discovered (found in the appellants' possession shortly after the robbery), invoked the doctrine of recent possession—implying a strong connection to the crime ([8]–[15]).

The Court concluded that, although there were discrepancies in witness accounts, the urgent recovery of the stolen vehicle sufficiently corroborated Ndlovu's testimony. The SCA ruled that the High Court did not err in denying leave to appeal on the basis that reasonable prospects of success were not demonstrated ([15]–[17]).

Regarding sentencing, the Court noted that it is within the Regional Court's discretion to impose sentences and highlighted the first appellant's prior criminal history. The SCA established that the previous convictions—particularly one for robbery and another for possession of stolen goods—suggested a pattern of criminal behavior and did not constitute a misdirection by the sentencing court ([16]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA ultimately dismissed the appellants' applications for leave to appeal against their convictions and the first appellant's appeal against his sentence. The ruling was predicated on the determination that the appellants had failed to establish reasonable prospects of success on appeal. Thus, the Court affirmed the lower court’s decisions, reinforcing the judicial discretion exercised in sentencing and the principles governing appeals against convictions on grounds of identification and recent possession ([18]).

This case underscores the nuanced applicability of the doctrine of recent possession in robbery cases and the careful consideration given to the reliability of witness identification in the context of overlapping criminal jurisprudence. The reaffirmation of the need for compelling evidence to warrant an appeal highlights the stringent standards required for overturning lower court rulings within the South African criminal justice system.
