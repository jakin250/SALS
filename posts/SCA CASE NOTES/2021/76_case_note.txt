Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# FIRAC Case Note: Kunene and Others v Minister of Police (260/2020)

**Citation**: Kunene and Others v Minister of Police (260/2020) [2021] ZASCA 76 (10 June 2021)

## F – Facts
The present case involves a dispute regarding the rescission of two court orders stemming from settlement agreements between the State Attorney and representatives of the first appellant, Ayanda Irvin Kunene. On August 7, 2013, Mr. Kunene was unlawfully shot by members of the South African Police Service (SAPS), resulting in serious injuries, including paraplegia. Following this incident, Mr. Kunene initiated a civil claim against the Minister of Police, seeking R39 million in damages ([1]).

In February 2017, the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg, convened for a trial concerning the merits of the case, during which the Minister, represented by the State Attorney's office, conceded liability ([6]–[8]). Subsequently, a consent order was issued where the Minister was obligated to compensate Mr. Kunene for damages related to the assault. By March 2, 2018, a further order was granted, compelling the Minister to pay R34,077,000 as damages, including additional claims for unlawful arrest and detention which were not initially part of the settlement ([15]).

In July 2019, the Minister sought to rescind both orders, arguing that the State Attorney lacked the authority to conclude the settlements due to internal procedural errors and misconduct by the attorneys involved ([2], [18]). The high court eventually rescinded the orders based on findings of fraud and irrationality on the part of the State Attorney ([23]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issues arising from this case include:

1. **Authority of the State Attorney**: Did Mr. Lekabe, the Head of the State Attorney’s office, have the authority to concede liability and enter into settlement agreements without proper instructions from the Minister or SAPS? ([18], [24]).
  
2. **Grounds for Rescission**: Were the grounds of fraud and justus error applicable in this case to support the rescission of the court orders based on the actions of the State Attorney? ([27], [28]).

3. **Principle of Legality**: Did the actions of the State Attorney, as they pertained to the compromise agreements and resulting orders, comport with principles of legality and the rule of law? ([34], [41]).

## R – Rules
Several legal principles governed the court's decision:

1. **Common Law Grounds for Rescission**: According to South African common law, a court order may be set aside on grounds such as fraud, justus error, or others relating to iusta causa ([27]).

2. **Ostensible Authority**: Precedent establishes the principle of ostensible authority, whereby the actions of an attorney can bind their client, provided that the third party had a reasonable basis to believe that the attorney was acting within their authority ([29]).

3. **Principle of Legality**: This constitutional principle stipulates that public authorities must act within the bounds of the law and cannot engage in conduct that undermines the rule of law ([36], [41]).

4. **Public Interest**: The public interest mandates that judicial orders should not endorse actions or agreements arising from fraudulent or illegal conduct ([36]).

## A – Application
In addressing the issues, the Supreme Court of Appeal highlighted several pertinent factors:

1. **Authority of the State Attorney**: The court found that Mr. Lekabe exceeded his authority, entered into compromises without appropriate instructions, and acted contrary to SAPS's directives. This was characterized by a lack of necessary consultation and irrational decision-making ([20], [34]).

2. **Fraud and Justus Error**: The actions of Mr. Lekabe and Mr. Kajee in conceding claims created a foundation for rescission based on fraud. The court noted that the underlying compromises led to the subversion of judicial processes, justifying the rescission of the orders ([27], [32]).

3. **Principle of Legality**: The court noted that the actions of the State Attorney were inconsistent with the rule of law. The Minister could not be bound by agreements made under fraudulent terms or undue influence. Consequently, the high court was right to rescind orders that failed to uphold public interest and legality ([36], [41]).

4. **Irregularities in Settlement Agreements**: The court emphasized that the subsequent addition of unlawful arrest damages, post-settlement, illustrated a clear infringement of procedural standards. This was viewed as further justification for rescission since it constituted an impermissible alteration of the claim without appropriate judicial authority ([31], [46]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the high court correctly rescinded the orders on the grounds of fraud and irrationality concerning the conduct of the State Attorney's representatives. The court affirmed that the principle of legality is paramount, ensuring that public authorities act within their legitimate powers. Thus, the appeal was dismissed, and costs were awarded against the second and third appellants on an attorney-and-client scale, reflecting the court's disapproval of their conduct ([51]). This judgment underscores the necessity for adherence to legal protocols within public institutions to maintain the integrity of judicial processes.
