Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: FIRAC Method

### Case Title
**AFGRI Operations Limited v Hamba Fleet (Pty) Limited**  
(542/16) [2017] ZASCA 24 (24 March 2017)

### F – Facts
The conflict arose from an application by AFGRI Operations Limited (the appellant) for a final winding-up order against Hamba Fleet (Pty) Limited (the respondent) ([1]). The appellant had previously secured a judgment for costs amounting to R156,796.64 against the respondent on 4 February 2014, following which the respondent failed to discharge its debt ([2]). The appellant based its application on the claim that the respondent was unable to pay its debts as stipulated in section 345(1)(a) of the Companies Act 61 of 1973 ([2]). 

The respondent did not dispute the existence of the debt but merely denied its insolvency, failing to provide adequate evidence supporting its financial stability ([2]–[4]). The appellant's demand for payment under section 345 of the Companies Act remained unchallenged by the respondent ([2]). The Gauteng Division, Pretoria, dismissed the initial liquidation application, citing the presence of a counterclaim by the respondent regarding alleged unlawful transfers by the appellant, purportedly totalling over R22 million ([3]). However, this counterclaim had not been rigorously pursued, rendering it illiquid and unsupported by a completed summation ([3]).

### I – Issues
The principal issues at hand were:
1. Whether the Supreme Court of Appeal could interfere with the lower court’s discretion to refuse a winding-up order due to the respondent's counterclaim ([5]).
2. Whether the respondent’s counterclaim provided legitimate grounds to dispute the established indebtedness to the appellant ([7]–[14]).

### R – Rules
The court relied extensively on key legal principles regarding the winding-up of corporations. It upheld that:
- Winding-up proceedings should not be utilized to enforce payment of a debt that is genuinely disputed on reasonable grounds ([6]).
- Once the creditor shows prima facie a debt is due, it is the onus of the respondent to rebut this by demonstrating bona fide disputes to the claim ([6], [17]).
- The existence of a counterclaim does not automatically negate the need for winding up, although it can influence the discretion of the court ([7], [11]).

The court also assessed the scope of judicial discretion under the case law following *National Coalition for Gay and Lesbian Equality v Minister of Home Affairs* ([11]). The discretion should be examined judiciously unless the lower court acted under misdirection or incorrect principles.

### A – Application
Upon application of these principles, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that:
- The court a quo erred in its discretion, primarily by overly emphasizing the counterclaim instead of recognizing that the respondent did not sufficiently demonstrate its solvency or the genuineness of its counterclaim ([13], [15]).
- The failure of the respondent to pursue its counterclaim effectively was a crucial factor that undermined its position against the winding-up order ([18]).
- Given the undisputed nature of the prima facie debt owed to the appellant, and the respondent's admission of not being solvent or engaging in any trade, the court concluded that the appellant had established a rightful claim for liquidation ([4], [18]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, fundamentally asserting that the lower court was guided by incorrect principles in denying the winding-up order. It determined that the counterclaim was insufficient to refute the clear indebtedness, and thus, a final winding-up order against the respondent was warranted ([21]). The case reinforces the principle that an unpaid creditor has a right to liquidation in instances of demonstrated indebtedness, highlighting the narrow scope for a court to exercise discretion against such orders in the absence of valid counterclaims ([12]–[14]).

The court’s decision not only emphasizes the stringent standards that must be met by any party opposing a liquidation order but also delineates the appropriate application of the discretion vested in judicial authorities concerning corporate insolvency matters in South Africa.
