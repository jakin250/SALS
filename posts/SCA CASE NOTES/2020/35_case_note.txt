Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title:** Khutala Property Consortium (Pty) Ltd v Mtubatuba Municipality and Others  
**Neutral Citation:** [2020] ZASCA 35  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Judgment Delivered:** 6 April 2020  
**Coram:** Navsa, Saldulker, Dambuza, Plasket JJA and Ledwaba AJA  

---

## F - Facts

The appeal involves the validity of a lease agreement allegedly concluded between the first respondent, Mtubatuba Municipality (the Municipality), and the appellant, Khutala Property Consortium (Pty) Ltd (Khutala). During November 2009, the Municipality invited bids for the development of a commercial site via a long-term lease related to Erven 197 and 312 Mtubatuba, known as the old taxi rank ([2]).

Initially, the Municipality accepted a bid from the Alliance Property Group (Pty) Ltd (Alliance), and a resolution was passed on 29 January 2010 authorizing the Municipal Manager to sign the lease agreement on behalf of the Council ([3]). However, it was noted at a subsequent meeting that the lease agreement presented was with Crowned Cormorant Investments (Pty) Ltd, raising concerns regarding its legitimacy ([4]).

Despite concerns from the MEC (Member of the Executive Council) and legal advisors regarding clauses in the lease and the authority of the entities involved, on 15 September 2011, a power of attorney was signed by Khutala, and a notarial lease was executed and registered ([16]). The Municipal Manager's actions were later called into question, and the Municipality ultimately re-advertised the tender in January 2013, awarding the rights to another entity, Green Mile Investments 340 CC ([17]).

Following a series of legal disputes, Khutala was compelled to apply for an order to force the Municipality to approve its building plans, which resulted in a favorable judgment for Khutala ([19]). Nonetheless, the Municipality sought to have the lease declared invalid, which led to this appeal after the High Court ruled against Khutala ([21]).

## I - Issues

The central issue before the court was whether the notarial lease between the Municipality and Khutala was valid. This raised several sub-issues, including:

1. **Authority to Conclude the Lease:** Whether the Municipal Manager acted within his authority in signing the lease, given that the original resolution specified a different entity as the lessee.
2. **Legality and Compliance with Procedural Norms:** Whether the lease complied with the requirements prescribed by the Municipal Structures Act 117 of 1998 and related regulations, particularly concerning the necessity of approval from the MEC.
3. **Principle of Legality:** Whether the actions of the Municipality adhered to the principle of legality, which requires public authorities to operate within the limits of their constitutional and statutory powers ([23]).

## R - Rules

The court drew upon several legal principles:

1. **Municipal Structures Act 117 of 1998:** This act establishes the framework for local government and the procedural requirements for municipal decisions, including the need for proper resolutions and authorizations for leases.
2. **Principle of Legality:** As articulated in *South African National Roads Agency Ltd v Cape Town City*, public delegates must act within the powers conferred by law and must not misconstrue the scope of their authority ([23]).
3. **Case Precedents:** The court referred to *City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality v RPM Bricks (Pty) Limited* to support the assertion that the doctrine of estoppel could not apply to this case, reinforcing the notion that legal compliance is non-negotiable despite any representations made ([28]).

## A - Application

The court examined the facts surrounding the Municipality's authority to enter into the lease. It noted that the original resolution of 29 January 2010 did not authorize the Municipal Manager to sign a lease with Khutala, as he could only act within the confines of that resolution. The lease executed was fundamentally flawed since it was with a different entity—Crowned Cormorant—without clear evidence proving any affiliation or authority to sign on behalf of Alliance ([22]).

Regarding compliance with the Municipal Structures Act, the court ruled that the lease agreement was subject to the MEC's approval and was concluded despite ongoing concerns flagged by the MEC and legal advisors, which indicated that the Municipality had acted unlawfully ([24]). The court highlighted that the inaccuracies in the documents presented, coupled with the lack of a valid resolution from Khutala and the illegal delegation of authority, compromised the lease's legitimacy ([25],[26]).

Additional complications arose from the Municipal Manager's actions surrounding the signing of the notarial lease, which was critiqued for lacking the necessary support and clarity regarding the authority claimed therein.

Finally, the court underscored the implications of the tender re-advertisement and Green Mile’s involvement, noting that the apparent chaos in municipal governance further clouded the lease’s validity. Nevertheless, it limited its judgment to the issue of the lease's validity without addressing the propriety of the second tender directly ([27]).

## C - Conclusion

The court affirmed the High Court's ruling, determining that the notarial lease between Khutala and the Municipality was invalid due to procedural non-compliance and lack of authority ([29]). The judgment further confirmed the legal principle that public officials must operate strictly within their granted authority, reinforcing the importance of adherence to statutory processes in municipal governance. Consequently, the appeal was dismissed with costs, thus upholding the legality and order of local governmental procedures within South Africa, presaging future governance implications given the highlights of administrative accountability ([30]). 

The implications of this ruling extend to practitioners and entities engaging in municipal contracts, emphasizing the importance of confirming the legitimacy of authority in contractual dealings with local government entities.
