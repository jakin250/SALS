Case Note: REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2005

# Case Note: Road Accident Fund v Makwetlane

## FIRAC Method

#### F – Facts
The case involves a motor vehicle accident that occurred on May 6, 1999, when the respondent, Bennet Lefu Makwetlane, a pedestrian, claimed he was struck by a vehicle driven negligently. The identity of the driver and owner remained unknown ([para 3]). The Road Accident Fund (RAF), appointed as the appellant in this matter, contended that Makwetlane failed to comply with a specific regulation promulgated under the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 (the Act) ([paras 4–5]). The regulation in question (regulation 2(1)(c)) stipulates that third parties must submit an affidavit to the police within 14 days following the accident ([para 2]). Makwetlane did report the incident to the police but did not file the requisite affidavit. Consequently, the RAF denied liability, arguing non-compliance with the regulation resulted in the dismissal of the claim ([paras 4–5]).

Initially, the trial court ruled against the RAF's special plea based on a previous case, Makwetlane v Road Accident Fund, concluding that the regulation was ultra vires ([paras 2, 5]). However, the RAF appealed the decision, leading to the Supreme Court of Appeal's review of the validity of regulation 2(1)(c) ([paras 1, 5]).

#### I – Issues
The central issue before the court was whether regulation 2(1)(c) was ultra vires and therefore invalid. Specifically, the court needed to determine if the Minister of Transport had the authority under section 26 of the Act to promulgate regulations imposing such a condition ([paras 1, 18]). Additionally, the court had to assess whether non-compliance by Makwetlane warranted a dismissal of his claim, despite the implications of poverty and limited access to legal remedies in South Africa ([paras 18, 21]).

#### R – Rules
Key legal principles and cases referenced include:
1. **Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996**: Establishes the RAF’s liability and the procedural obligations for claimants ([para 9]).
2. **Section 26(1)** of the Act: Entrusts the Minister with the authority to prescribe regulations necessary to achieve the Act's objectives ([para 11]).
3. **Constitutional Scrutiny**: The court emphasized that the exercise of public power must conform to constitutional norms, including rationality and legality ([paras 19, 29]).
4. **Case Precedents**:
   - **Bezuidenhout v Road Accident Fund** and **Thugwana v Road Accident Fund** evaluated analogous regulations and emphasized the need for clarity and the protection of claimants' rights ([paras 10, 15]).
   - **Fedsure Life Assurance Ltd v Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council** highlighted limits concerning powers conferred by law ([para 13]).

#### A – Application
The court undertook a detailed analysis of whether the regulation fell within the scope of the Minister's delegated powers as defined by section 26 of the Act. It looked at the legislative history, the overall object to compensate victims, and the nature of the requirements imposed by the regulation ([paras 6, 8, 12]). 

In asserting that the regulation was ultra vires, the majority opinion reasserted that the regulation's strict compliance requirements potentially contravened the overarching objective of the Act, which is to protect victims of unlawful incidents ([paras 21-22]). The court highlighted socio-economic realities, such as poverty and illiteracy, which made compliance with the regulation disproportionately burdensome for many claimants ([para 18]).

In contrast, the dissenting opinion held that regulation 2(1)(c) was a valid regulatory condition designed to mitigate fraud in cases of unidentified vehicles. It noted the regulation's rationality, asserting that the requirement for immediate reporting could deter dishonest claims and aid in establishing the facts ([paras 34–39]). The dissent further argued that the regulation did not materially undermine the primary objectives of the Act and was not inherently unfair ([para 43]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately reversed the lower court's ruling, declaring regulation 2(1)(c) to be valid. The court ordered Makwetlane to file a replication regarding compliance with section 24(5) of the Act within 15 days, or else his claim would be dismissed. The implications of this ruling may extend to increasing scrutiny of the regulatory requirements imposed on claimants in the context of socio-economic disparities, and it emphasizes the necessity of balance between fraud prevention and access to legal remedies for accident victims.

The ruling underscores critical themes in constitutional law and administrative justice in South Africa, particularly the need to ensure that regulations do not capriciously limit access to justice for vulnerable populations while maintaining the integrity of state resources against fraudulent claims.
