Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

### FIRAC Case Note: Grimbeek v Jakobo (922/2017) [2018] ZASCA 131

**F – Facts:**
This case arises from a professional negligence claim initiated by Mr. Mziwandile Alfred Jakobo (the respondent) against Mr. Johannes Petrus Louis Grimbeek (the appellant), an attorney. The claim involves allegations that the appellant negligently allowed the respondent's claim against the Road Accident Fund (RAF) to prescribe. The incident in question occurred on 25 January 2001, when the respondent, then aged 17, was injured in an accident involving an ambulance while riding a bicycle. After suffering injuries, the respondent consulted with the appellant in February 2001, alongside his father, who was advised by a mutual acquaintance to seek the appellant's assistance ([paras 1–5]).

The respondent's father paid the appellant R250 for the initiation of the claim. However, despite the appellant's assurances regarding the progression of the claim and a need for patience, the father chose not to pursue further inquiries due to trust in the appellant's competence ([paras 5–10]). The key issue arose when, in 2012, the respondent discovered that no claim had been lodged on his behalf after hearing about other neglected claims on the radio. By this time, the prescription period for his claim against the RAF had already elapsed ([paras 11–12]).

In the Free State Division of the High Court, the trial court upheld the appellant's special plea of prescription, citing a lack of action on the part of the respondent. However, a subsequent appeal to the full court, which improperly addressed the merits of the claim beyond the special plea, found the appellant liable for 100% of the damages. This latter finding was contested in the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 2–4]).

**I – Issues:**
The core legal question concerns whether the respondent had constructive knowledge of the claimed negligence of the appellant under section 12(3) of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, which states that a debt is not deemed due until the creditor has knowledge of the identity of the debtor and the facts from which the debt arises ([para 12]). Specifically, the court was tasked with assessing when the respondent could reasonably have learned that the claim against the RAF was not lodged in a timely fashion, thereby initiating the prescription period ([paras 18–22]).

**R – Rules:**
The court considered several precedents to interpret "reasonable care" in terms of the Prescription Act. The requirement necessitates diligence not merely in discovering facts underlying the debt but also in assessing their significance ([para 13]). The case of **Drennan Maud & Partners v Pennington Town Board** illustrates this principle by clarifying that a reasonable person would deduce the necessary facts regarding a debt ([para 13]). Other cases like **Macleod v Kweyiya** emphasized a subjective element in determining whether the plaintiff exercised reasonable care, indicating that each case should be viewed in light of the specific circumstances surrounding the plaintiff ([para 14]).

The court also noted the burden of proof lies with the defendant to demonstrate that the plaintiff failed to meet the requisite standard of care, shifting to the plaintiff only after a prima facie case has been established ([para 21]). 

**A – Application:**
In this case, the court applied the reasoning from relevant case law to the facts at hand. The evidence indicated that the respondent and his family had relied heavily on the appellant's assurances about the claim's status and had not made further inquiries due to their trust in him ([paras 19–20]). The trial court had attributed the start of the prescription period to the day the respondent turned 21, arguing that the respondent should have proactively checked on the claim ([para 18]). However, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that given the unusual circumstances—specifically, the appellant's misleading communication and the family's reliance on him—the respondent's inaction did not reflect negligence.

The court emphasized that "negligent and not an innocent inaction" is what section 12(3) aims to prevent ([para 22]). Since the respondent relied on the appellant's assurances, the court concluded that he had not acted unreasonably and thus had not obtained constructive knowledge of the claim’s status before the actual 2012 inquiry ([para 23]).

**C – Conclusion:**
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appellant's appeal against the special plea of prescription while also overturning the full court's finding regarding liability on the merits of the claim. The court determined that the appellant failed to inform the respondent and his father of any issues concerning the claim, thereby preventing the respondents from seeking alternative legal assistance before the claim prescribed. The court noted the importance of proper communication in a fiduciary relationship, emphasizing that a client's trust in their attorney should not equate to negligence on their part ([paras 24–25]). The court concluded that the appellant must bear the costs of the appeal, emphasizing the indefatigable significance of professional responsibility within legal practice.
