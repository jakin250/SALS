Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: Amalungelo Workers’ Union and Others v Philip Morris South Africa (Pty) Limited and Another [2019] ZACC 45

### F – Facts
The application for leave to appeal arose from a dispute involving the Amalungelo Workers’ Union and 75 of its members, who were employed by Philip Morris South Africa (Pty) Limited and Leonard Dingler (Pty) Limited. The core issue was an alleged breach of section 34 of the Basic Conditions of Employment Act 75 of 1997 (the Basic Conditions Act), whereby the respondents deducted income tax from the applicants' salaries in relation to company cars without consent. The applicants claimed these deductions were unlawful as they violated the stipulations of the Basic Conditions Act, particularly as they were not supported by prior written agreements or necessary authorizations per law, collective agreement, or court order ([paras 1–9]).

The applicants sought an order for refunds of the unauthorized deductions and an interdict against future unlawful deductions. The Labour Court ruled they lacked the jurisdiction to entertain the claim as it was not framed in contractual terms, which was necessary according to the provisions of section 77 of the Basic Conditions Act ([paras 5–6]). Although the respondents did not initially contest the Labour Court’s jurisdiction, the Court addressed it proactively. Consequently, the Labour Court dismissed the claim for lack of jurisdiction, asserting the necessity of deferring such claims to labour inspectors prior to approaching the Labour Court ([paras 4–7]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal question in this case is whether the Labour Court has jurisdiction to adjudicate claims pursuant to the Basic Conditions Act when such claims arise without an initial determination by a labour inspector. Specifically, it questions the interpretation of section 77 of the Basic Conditions Act, regarding its exclusivity and the implications surrounding the requirement (or lack thereof) to engage the labour inspector mechanism prior to accessing the Labour Court ([paras 10–12]).

### R – Rules
The relevant legal rules stem from the Basic Conditions Act, particularly section 77, which delineates the Labour Court's jurisdiction. Subsection (1) grants the Labour Court exclusive jurisdiction regarding all matters under the Act, save for specific exceptions stated within the Act itself. Concurrent jurisdiction exists with civil courts for matters regarding employment contracts ([paras 19–21]).

The nature of the claims presented requires scrutiny under constitutional principles, notably the right to just administrative action as encapsulated in section 34 of the Constitution and the principles established in previous cases such as *Fourie Stanford Driving School* and *Ephraim v Bull Brand Foods (Pty) Ltd*, which had addressed the jurisdictional aspects and procedural considerations under the Basic Conditions Act ([paras 32–35]).

### A – Application
The Court engaged in an extensive interpretation of section 77 to ascertain whether claims pursuant to the Basic Conditions Act necessitate preliminary resolution by a labour inspector. It emphasized that jurisdictional clauses must be interpreted in a manner that promotes access to justice, aligning with constitutional imperatives ([paras 16–22]).

The Court contended that the absence of a provision within the Basic Conditions Act mandating prior referral to a labour inspector meant that the jurisdiction of the Labour Court was not only exclusive but also immediate. It further posited that the imperative of access to courts underpins any procedural interpretation of jurisdiction ([paras 27–30]). Additionally, the Court evaluated the functions of the inspectors under the Basic Conditions Act, noting that their role was not dispute resolution but compliance and monitoring, which fundamentally differs from the adjudicative responsibilities of the Labour Court ([paras 29–33]).

Thus, the Court ultimately concluded that the Labour Court had erred in determining it lacked jurisdiction to resolve the applicants’ claim, and that dismissing it solely on those grounds contravened the objects of the Basic Conditions Act ([paras 27–35]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal, set aside the Labour Court's dismissal of the claim, and remitted the matter back to the Labour Court for consideration. This decision effectively reaffirmed the exclusivity of the Labour Court's jurisdiction regarding matters arising under the Basic Conditions Act without the precondition of a labour inspector's determination. It provided a significant clarification on the interpretation of section 77, ensuring that employees could access the Labour Court directly to enforce their rights against unlawful deductions, thereby reinforcing statutory protections in labor relations ([paras 36]). 

This ruling carries profound implications for the treatment of employment rights and reinforces the judiciary's role in guaranteeing access to justice in labor disputes, paramount in the advancement of fair administrative action and compliance with labor legislation ([paras 10–36]).
