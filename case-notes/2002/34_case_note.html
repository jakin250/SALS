Constitutional Court

The President of the Republic of South Africa v United Democratic Movement (CCT 23/02)

The respondent, the United Democratic Movement (UDM), argued that these Acts were unconstitutional and sought urgent relief in the Cape High Court on 20 June 2002.

Constitutional Court 2002 CCT 23/02

F – Facts

The case before the Constitutional Court of South Africa arose from the legal challenge against four Acts of Parliament signed into law by the President on 19 June 2002 that effectively suspended the constitutional anti-defection provisions for members of the National Assembly and provincial legislatures ([paras 1–3]). The interim Constitution had mandated that members vacate their seats upon ceasing to be members of their nominated party ([para 2]). However, despite the repeal of the interim Constitution, transitional provisions in the 1996 Constitution extended the effects of these competitive anti-defection mechanisms until the second elections under the new constitutional order ([para 3]).

The respondent, the United Democratic Movement (UDM), argued that these Acts were unconstitutional and sought urgent relief in the Cape High Court on 20 June 2002. The court granted an interim order suspending the operation of the Acts pending a substantive constitutional challenge ([paras 7–10]). The appellants, led by the President of the Republic, challenged the High Court’s authority to issue such a suspension ([para 14]).

I – Issues

The key legal issues presented to the Constitutional Court were:

  1. Whether the High Court could constitutionally issue an order suspending the commencement or operation of the impugned legislation, given the exclusive jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court over constitutional matters.
  2. If the High Court did possess such jurisdiction, whether it should have exercised it in this particular case ([paras 14–17]).

R – Rules

The rules governing this case are primarily derived from the South African Constitution, particularly:

  • Section 167(4), which confers exclusive jurisdiction on the Constitutional Court to decide constitutional matters ([para 18]).
  • Sections 80 and 122, which allow for applications regarding the constitutionality of an Act to be made by a certain number of members of the National Assembly and provincial legislatures, respectively, and provide the court the power to suspend the effect of the impugned legislation if required by the interests of justice ([para 18]).
  • Additionally, the court noted the common law principles surrounding interim interdicts, which depend on whether the court has jurisdiction to preserve or restore the status quo, irrespective of whether it can decide the main dispute ([para 15]).

A – Application

In reviewing the powers of the High Court to grant an interim order effectively suspending legislative enactments, the Court underscored the importance of preserving the will of the legislature. The majority opinion emphasized that while the Legislature holds a critical role in a democracy, courts must ensure no branch oversteps its authority or undermines constitutional supremacy. The Court noted that issuing such a suspension order constitutes a significant judicial intrusion into legislative action, which should be avoided unless absolutely necessary to prevent irreparable harm ([paras 25–31]).

The Constitutional Court determined that the High Court did not assess the necessity for the extreme remedy of a legislative suspension effectively. The respondent's application did not demonstrate that the impugned legislation would cause immediate and irreparable harm, thus failing the standard for the issuance of an interdict ([paras 33–34]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ruled in favor of the appellants, concluding that the High Court had erred in its decision and did not possess the jurisdiction to grant the order suspending the operation of the impugned legislation. It upheld the appeal and set aside the High Court orders, establishing that each party would bear its own costs ([paras 36–36]).

This judgment outlines the intricate relationship between legislative authority and judicial oversight in South Africa, reaffirming the commitment to uphold constitutional accountability while also respecting the roles of separate government branches. The ruling reinforces the limited scenarios in which courts can suspend the operation of legislation, thus preserving the integrity of the legislative process and the Constitution itself.
