Case Note: REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2002

### FIRAC Case Note: Jamieson v Sabingo

**F - Facts**  
The case concerns an appeal from the judgment of Willis J in the Witwatersrand Local Division of the High Court. The appellant, Neil Jamieson, is an incola of the court’s jurisdiction, while the respondent, Amindo Cesar Sabingo, is classified as a peregrinus. A judgment was obtained by the respondent against Madiba Air (Pty) Ltd for the equivalent of $142,000, plus associated costs. On 13 July 1999, Malan J issued a rule nisi that allowed Jamieson to initiate proceedings against Sabingo and attached the rights associated with Sabingo’s judgment against Madiba Air to confirm jurisdiction ([1]–[3]). 

The execution of the attachment order occurred on 7 September 1999, but prior to this execution on 19 August 1999, Sabingo's legal representatives voluntarily submitted to the jurisdiction of the Witwatersrand Local Division and appointed their office as his domicilium citandi et executandi ([4]). The appellant’s claim involved a contract related to the sale and installation of equipment in Luanda, Angola, and both parties accepted that the contract was effectively concluded in Johannesburg when Sabingo accepted Jamieson's quotation ([5]).

Willis J ultimately discharged the rule nisi on the basis of Sabingo's voluntary submission to jurisdiction and adherence to the principles outlined in *American Flag plc v Great African T-Shirt Corporation CC* ([7]).

**I - Issues**  
1. Whether a voluntary submission to the jurisdiction occurs after an attachment order is granted, affecting the validity of the attachment itself.
2. The implications of bilateral versus unilateral submission to jurisdiction on the necessity of attachment in cases involving peregrini ([8]–[12]).

**R - Rules**  
The judgment references several key legal principles:
- **Attachment to Confirm Jurisdiction**: Traditionally, an attachment is necessary for courts to exercise jurisdiction over a peregrinus to issue a competent judgment ([21]).
- **Voluntary Submission**: The legal principle asserts that when a peregrinus submits to the court's jurisdiction, it mitigates the need for attachment, making a judgment against them enforceable internationally ([24]–[26]).
- **Bilateral vs. Unilateral Submission**: The distinction between these two forms is critical; a bilateral submission can render the need for attachment unnecessary, while a unilateral submission may not ([13]) .

The court also considers previous cases' dicta, particularly *Veneta Mineraria Spa v Carolina Collieries (Pty) Ltd in Liquidation* and *American Flag plc case*, shedding light on juristic interpretations related to jurisdictional challenges ([9]–[10]).

**A - Application**  
The court determined that, given the voluntary submission by Sabingo before the execution of the attachment, the rule nisi's purpose had been rendered moot. Implementing its ruling, the court agreed that the principles articulated in *American Flag* accurately reflect the jurisdictional landscape in South African law. The court explicitly noted that a voluntary attachment at that juncture was unnecessary, as full jurisdiction was achieved through the respondent's consent ([22]–[26]). The distinction between bilateral and unilateral submissions was also critical. The court maintained that the essence of effective judgment is grounded in the nature of consent provided by the defendant ([27]).

Additionally, the argument that Sabingo's consent was given post-attachment was countered effectively, positing that he had voluntarily consented before the attachment action was executed and should retain all rights to contest the attachment ([30]). 

**C - Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appeal was dismissed with costs, solidifying the principle that a voluntary submission adequately confers jurisdiction and renders attachment applications unnecessary once such consent is given ([32]). The implications of this ruling assert a pivotal change in how voluntary submissions are treated in conjunction with jurisdictional attachments, emphasizing the importance of the timing of a defendant’s consent in relation to the procedural actions undertaken by the plaintiff. This case reinforces that jurisdiction may be established through consensual means, thus potentially changing the landscape of jurisdictional determinations involving peregrini.
