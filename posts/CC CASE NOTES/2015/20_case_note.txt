Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# Case Note: Molaudzi v S [2015] ZACC 20

## F – Facts
The case involves Mr. Thembekile Molaudzi, who was convicted alongside several co-accused for the murder of Warrant Officer Johannes Dingaan Makuna on 3 August 2002, and various related crimes. The trial occurred in the North West High Court, where the prosecution's case relied heavily on the extra-curial statements of Molaudzi's co-accused, which the trial court admitted despite challenges regarding their admissibility ([paras 2–5]).

Initially tried in 2003, the case extended into 2004, culminating in the conviction of Molaudzi and others on charges including murder, robbery, and unlawful possession of firearms and ammunition. Molaudzi received a life sentence for murder, along with additional concurrent sentences ([paras 3–5]). Following the trial, the Full Court of the North West High Court dismissed the appeals primarily based on the admissibility of the extra-curial statements and subsequently denied a petition to the Supreme Court of Appeal for further appeal ([paras 6–7]).

After a series of legal developments, in 2014, linked co-accused Mhlongo and Nkosi secured a constitutional appeal on similar grounds, prompting Molaudzi to seek leave to appeal again. He raised constitutional issues regarding the use of co-accused statements that had not been appropriately litigated in his first unrepresented application ([paras 10–11]).


## I – Issues
The primary legal issue revolves around the applicability of the doctrine of **res judicata**, which prevents the re-litigation of matters that have been conclusively settled. Molaudzi argued that his second application should not be barred under this doctrine, as it presented a distinct constitutional question about the admissibility of evidence that had not been decided previously ([paras 12–19]). 

Moreover, the Court had to consider whether the relaxation of this doctrine was warranted in exceptional circumstances, particularly given Molaudzi’s unrepresented status during his first application and the implications of his potentially wrongful conviction ([paras 14, 41]).


## R – Rules
The Court's reasoning was informed by constitutional provisions, notably:
- **Section 173 of the Constitution**, which grants inherent powers to the courts to protect and regulate their processes and develop common law in the interests of justice.
- **Section 39(2) of the Constitution**, which mandates that courts must develop common law to align with the rights enshrined in the Bill of Rights ([paras 31, 41]).

The legal framework surrounding **res judicata** indicates that this doctrine is intended to promote finality in litigation and prevent the re-litigation of settled matters ([paras 14–16]). Various legal precedents, notably **Mpofu v Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development**, emphasized that a rigid application of res judicata may result in injustices, especially concerning unrepresented individuals ([para 19, 38]).


## A – Application
The Court's application of law involved a nuanced evaluation of whether the interests of justice warranted the relaxation of res judicata. Though Molaudzi’s first application was dismissed for not engaging with constitutional issues, the second application introduced substantial constitutional arguments concerning the admissibility of co-accused statements. The Court highlighted that a refusal to consider these arguments would create an arbitrary and unjust distinction in treatment among similarly situated defendants ([paras 28, 40, 44]).

In the analysis of Molaudzi's and his co-accused's cases, the Court recognized that the evidence against him relied predominantly on the co-accused's extra-curial statements, similar to the cases of Mhlongo and Nkosi, whose convictions were overturned. Hence, the Court concluded there was merit in Molaudzi's claims regarding the inadmissibility of such statements ([paras 45, 46]).


## C – Conclusion
The Court ultimately granted Molaudzi leave to appeal, overturned his convictions, and ordered his immediate release from prison. It held that the appeals based on constitutional implications tied closely to the res judicata doctrine warranted exceptional treatment. The ruling underscored the necessity for judicial discretion when unrepresented individuals raise credible constitutional claims, thereby balancing the need for legal certainty with the imperative of justice ([paras 48, 49]).

The case holds significant implications for future proceedings that might involve the admissibility of confessions and statements in criminal trials, highlighting the importance of fair legal representation and the potential to revisit past convictions in the interest of justice where necessary.
