Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: MV Silver Star: Owners of MV Silver Star v Hilane Ltd (82/2014) [2014] ZASCA 194

## F – Facts

The dispute in this case arose from a maritime transactional relationship involving two vessels, the MV Silver Star (owned by Action Partner Limited) and the Sheng Mu (owned by Hilane Limited). Hilane and Phiniqia International Shipping LLC (Phiniqia) entered into a voyage charterparty on 6 July 2011 for the carriage of coking coal, which later led to claims against Phiniqia, prompting Hilane to arrest the Silver Star as an associated ship under the Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983 (the Act) on 12 August 2013 ([1]—[2]). 

The charterparty stipulated that it would be governed by English law and included provisions for issuing second sets of bills of lading against letters of indemnity (LOI). Issues arose when Phiniqia failed to surrender the original bills of lading as required, leading to disputes over liability for unpaid sums from Golden Waves FZC, the original shipper of the coal ([4]—[9]). 

Hilane ultimately sought to enforce an arbitration award obtained against Phiniqia in London concerning indemnities for the arrest and damages through an in rem action against the Silver Star, claiming about USD 3.8 million in total, alleging that the Silver Star was an associated ship to the Sheng Mu ([10]—[11]).

The owners of the Silver Star, Action Partner, contested the arrest, arguing that Hilane had failed to prove the necessary association within the meaning of the Act and contested the legal foundation for the arrest ([2]). The matter was brought before the Eastern Cape High Court, where action partner's application to release the vessel was dismissed, leading to the current appeal ([1]).

## I – Issues

The appeal raised critical legal and factual issues. The main legal questions included:

1. Whether an associated ship arrest is permissible based on claims advanced by Hilane, particularly given the arbitration award against Phiniqia and the claim's relation to the underlying maritime context ([2], [19]).
2. Did Hilane establish the necessary association between the Silver Star and the Sheng Mu in accordance with the statutory definition provided by the Act ([12], [18], [37])?

## R – Rules

The Governing legal framework is derived from the Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983. Of primary relevance are:

1. **Definition of Maritime Claim**: Section 1 includes that any claims arising from the carriage of goods in a ship or any agreement relating to such carriage qualifies as a maritime claim ([11]).
  
2. **Actions in Rem and Associated Ships**: Section 3 provides for an action in rem against an associated ship when a maritime claim arises. It states that an associated ship can be one owned by the same person who owned the original vessel or by another controlled by that owner ([12], [14], [19]).

3. **Proof of Association**: Section 3(7) details that an associated ship must be owned or controlled by the same individual(s) as those controlling the original vessel ([12], [18]).

The court was also guided by the principles set out in previous case law, including **Cargo Laden and Lately Laden on Board The MV Thalassini AVGI v MV Dimitris** ([39]) and **Bocimar NV v Kotor Overseas Shipping Ltd** ([39]).

## A – Application

### Legal Analysis of the First Issue

The first argument advanced by Action Partner that a claim arising from an arbitration award extinguishes the underlying maritime claims was addressed by examining whether such awards retain their links to the ships concerned. The court recognized that while arbitration awards may change status, they remain derivative of original claims and do not sever their basis in maritime law ([19]—[23]). The court emphasized that a maritime claim that arises in the context of the charterparty is pertinent to associated ship claims ([24]—[26]).

The implications of Action Partner’s assertions were addressed: should knowledge of the law's reach lead to a denial of seeking justice through the arrest, simply because an arbitration occurred, undermined the purpose of the Act ([24]—[26]).

### Legal Analysis of the Second Issue

The court examined whether Hilane proved the requisite association on the balance of probabilities. Evidence presented indicated close connections among the entities involved, notably between Action Partner, Stellar Shipping, and Tradeline, highlighting intertwined operations and ownership ([39]—[54]). 

The diminishing credibility of Action Partner's responses, particularly around the contractual structures between associated companies, indicated a lack of separate operation and solidified claims of common control demonstrated through documents ([45]—[54]). 

The judgment noted that circumstantial evidence could prevail, as according to the principle established in **Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd**—a significant amount of disreputable declarations negated the necessity for stringent proof ([46]—[54]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately concluded that the legal and factual contentions by Action Partner were insufficient to overturn the initial findings. The court dismissed the appeal with costs, highlighting that Hilane established its claims through proper interpretation of the Act, validating the arrest of the Silver Star as an associated ship ([2], [37]). 

This ruling reaffirms the necessity for creditors to have recourse under the maritime context while ensuring that provisions are effectively utilized to preserve the original claims through associated ship laws. The implications of this ruling extend to future maritime disputes, underscoring the robustness of the associated ship provisions designed to prevent evasion tactics by ship owners. The judgment therefore not only resolves the immediate claims but crystallizes the legal framework surrounding associated ship arrests under South African law.
