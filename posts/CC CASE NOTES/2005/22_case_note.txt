Source Folder: 2005

# FIRAC Case Note: Constitutional Court of South Africa - Veldman v Director of Public Prosecutions

## F - Facts
[1] Mr. Donald Veldman (applicant) faced conviction for several offenses, notably murder, and was sentenced by the Regional Magistrate’s Court to 15 years of imprisonment. At the time of the offenses, the law (sections of the Magistrates’ Courts Act 32 of 1944) stipulated that the maximum sentence for murder was 10 years. However, legislation amending this limit was enacted after his plea but prior to his sentencing, allowing for a higher maximum of 15 years ([1]).

[2] When Veldman was convicted alongside two others and sentenced, the amendment specifying a 15-year maximum was introduced after he entered his not-guilty plea. He appealed to the Johannesburg High Court, arguing that the 15-year sentence was disproportionate and not properly justified by his circumstances ([4]). The appeal was dismissed, leading him to seek leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), subsequently denied. Veldman based his application for direct access to the Constitutional Court on his perceived infringement of his constitutional right to a fair trial ([5]).

[6] In the proceedings, Veldman sought condonation for the late filing of his application, citing a lack of communication from his attorneys regarding the SCA’s ruling, which was granted due to the absence of prejudice against the respondent ([8]). 

## I - Issues
[12] The key legal questions in this case were twofold: 
1. Whether the Regional Magistrate's Court imposed the 15-year sentence under section 51 of the Minimum Sentences Act or the increased penal jurisdiction provided by the amended section 92(1)(a) of the Magistrates’ Courts Act.
2. Whether the retrospective application of the legislation violated Veldman’s right to a fair trial under section 35(3)(n) of the Constitution, particularly in relation to the maximum penalty that could be imposed based on the law at the time of his plea ([12–13]).

## R - Rules
[18] The relevant legal framework includes:
- **Section 35(3)(n) of the Constitution**, which asserts a defendant's right to the least severe prescribed punishment if a punishment changes after the crime but before sentencing.
- **Sections 92(1)(a)** and **51 of the Magistrates’ Courts Act** and the Minimum Sentences Act respectively, regulating sentencing authority and specifying minimum sentences for serious crimes.
- **Common law principles on the presumption against retrospective application of laws**, requiring statutes to apply only prospectively unless expressly indicated otherwise ([18–19]).

The Constitutional Court previously acknowledged that a fair trial encompasses broader rights beyond those explicitly enumerated in section 35(3), necessitating an interpretation of legal provisions aligned with justice and fairness ([22]).

## A - Application
[17] The court reviewed whether the Regional Magistrate had correctly applied either section 51 or the amended section 92(1)(a) when determining Veldman’s sentence. Lack of explicit mention of the Minimum Sentences Act led to speculation that the Magistrate operated within the confines of section 92(1)(a), raising the question of retrospective applicability. The court highlighted that a retrospective imposition of harsher sentences following legislative changes during trial proceedings constitutes a possible violation of the fair trial right established under the Constitution ([16–25]).

The applicant’s arguments revolved around the non-retroactivity of statutes affecting substantive rights. The retrospective implementation of section 92(1)(a), which effectively introduced a harsher penalty after his plea, was argued to undermine his legal certainty and the parameters set during the trial, hence violating his right to a fair trial ([26–39]). 

The court determined that the retrospective application of sentencing powers of regional courts, as it pertained to Veldman, infringed on the judicial principles of fairness and legality deeply embedded in the constitutional framework ([25–39]).

## C - Conclusion
[40] Ultimately, the Constitutional Court concluded that the retrospective application of the amended section 92(1)(a) violated Veldman’s rights under section 35(3)(n). The 15-year sentence was deemed unlawful as it was imposed using a legislative change that had not existed at the time of the offenses or the plea his counsel had prepared for ([40–41]). 

The Constitutional Court substituted the 15-year sentence with a 10-year maximum imprisonment, corresponding to the jurisdictional limits prior to the amendment, emphasizing that while the seriousness of the crime was not diminished, the integrity of the fair trial right must be preserved ([43]).

This case establishes critical precedent regarding the limits of legislative amendments in criminal sentencing and reinforces the commitment to upholding constitutional principles in criminal proceedings ([41–44]). 

Thus, the ruling underscores the necessity for clarity and fairness in legal proceedings, ensuring that accused persons are not subjected to punitive measures enacted after their offenses, aligning with the core values of the Constitution.
