Loni v Member of the Executive Council, Department of Health, Eastern Cape, Bhisho [2018] ZACC 2

Facts The case deals with Mzwandile Owen Loni's application for leave to appeal against a decision by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which denied him an appeal on grounds of prescription concerning a medical negligence claim ([1]–[3]).

Constitutional Court 2018 [2018] ZACC 2

Facts

The case deals with Mzwandile Owen Loni's application for leave to appeal against a decision by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which denied him an appeal on grounds of prescription concerning a medical negligence claim ([1]–[3]). Loni was admitted to Cecelia Makiwane Hospital on 6 August 1999, after suffering a gunshot wound that resulted in significant damage to his left femur ([4]). During his hospital stay, he underwent multiple treatments, including the insertion of a Denham pin and later a plate and screws; however, the bullet was never removed ([4]).

After his discharge, Loni experienced continual pain, an infected wound, and even removed the bullet himself in December 2000 ([6]). Despite subsequent visits to the hospital where he was informed that his leg was "fine," he discovered in November 2011—after becoming insured and consulting private practitioners—that his condition was due to medical negligence ([8], [9]). Initial proceedings were founded in contract, and the respondent, the MEC for Health, raised a special plea of prescription under the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 ([9]).

The High Court ruled against Loni, applying a "reasonable person" standard to determine when Loni had acquired knowledge of the facts giving rise to his claim ([12]). This conclusion was echoed by the Full Court, which acknowledged that Loni possessed sufficient facts about his treatment to sue before he sought professional advice in 2011 ([14], [15]).

Issues

  1. Did the High Court and Full Court appropriately apply the objective standard stipulated in section 12(3) of the Prescription Act, particularly in determining when Loni had the requisite knowledge to initiate a claim?
  2. Are the findings of the lower courts regarding Loni's actions after his hospital discharge justifiable based on the facts presented?

Rules

The relevant legal provision is section 12(3) of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, which states:
> “A debt shall not be deemed to be due until the creditor has knowledge of the identity of the debtor and of the facts from which the debt arises: Provided that a creditor shall be deemed to have such knowledge if he could have acquired it by exercising reasonable care” ([11]).

This principle necessitates an objective assessment of what a reasonable person would have understood regarding the facts giving rise to a claim for negligence, particularly in light of expert medical knowledge, as referenced in the court's earlier judgment in Links v MEC for Health, Northern Cape Province ([21]–[24]).

Application

Upon review, the Constitutional Court reiterated the lessons drawn from Links, emphasizing that understanding and interpreting the material facts of the case is vital ([21]–[28]). Despite Loni's assertions that he did not realize the negligence until he obtained formal medical advice in 2011, the Court maintained that a reasonable person, facing substantial and consistent pain, poor treatment outcomes, and the presence of an infection, would have sought further information far earlier than he did ([32]–[34]).

The Court noted that even after his discharge, Loni had sufficient indicators—his continuous pain, infection, and problematic treatment outcomes—that should have raised suspicion regarding the adequacy of the medical care he had received. Therefore, the assertion that he relied solely on the statements of hospital staff and was unaware of the severity of his condition was not compelling ([33]–[35]).

Additionally, the Court found that the introduction of further medical evidence from Loni did not address the central issue regarding his established knowledge of the facts nor did it undermine the conclusions reached by the lower courts ([36]).

Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed Loni's application for leave to appeal, affirming the lower courts' findings that he had the requisite knowledge to lodge his claim well before November 2011. The ruling underscores the importance of the objective standard under the Prescription Act and clarifies the threshold of knowledge necessary for initiating a claim based on medical negligence. The ruling serves as a poignant reminder of the obligations imposed on individuals in terms of seeking timely recourse in allegations of negligence while also protecting the rights enshrined in section 34 of the South African Constitution ([39]–[41]).

The case underscores the judicial commitment to interpreting legal provisions in ways that balance fair access to justice with necessary procedural safeguards, ultimately requiring claimants to act with diligence once they possess sufficient knowledge of the facts underpinning their claims.
