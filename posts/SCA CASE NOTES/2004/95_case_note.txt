# Case Note on SCA Decision: The Health Professions Council of South Africa v Dewald de Bruin

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History

[1] The respondent, Dr Dewald de Bruin, a medical practitioner, was found guilty of disgraceful conduct by a disciplinary committee of the appellant’s predecessor, the Interim National Medical and Dental Council of South Africa. This finding stemmed from his relationship with a complainant, Ms Lioni Kühn, during which De Bruin undertook to perform an abortion for her without any legal or medical justification, leading to severely unprofessional conduct.

[2] The disciplinary committee of the Council recommended removing De Bruin from the medical register as a penalty for his disgraceful conduct. Although De Bruin did not contest the finding of guilt, he sought review proceedings in the Pretoria High Court, alongside an appeal under § 20 of the Health Professions Act 56 of 1974. The High Court upheld his appeal, substituting the Council's penalty of removal with a three-month suspension.

[3] Following the High Court's decision, the Council sought to appeal on the grounds that the penalty was insufficient given the severity of De Bruin’s actions, which were characterized by a significant abuse of power and professional duty during a romantic relationship with a patient.

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions

[4] The primary issues for the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) were the appropriateness of the penalty imposed on De Bruin by the Council and whether the High Court correctly substituted the original penalty with a lighter one. Specifically, the court examined the extent to which the disciplinary committee's findings and the Council's application of penalties were justified in light of De Bruin's conduct.

[5] Additionally, the interpretation and application of § 20 of the Health Professions Act regarding appeals to the High Court and the standards for assessing whether a penalty is "appropriate" also presented themselves as critical constitutional questions.

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon

[6] The SCA relied heavily on the Health Professions Act 56 of 1974, particularly the provisions of § 20 concerning disciplinary actions and appeals. This Act establishes the professional standards expected of medical practitioners and the procedures for addressing misconduct.

[7] Key precedents referenced in the judgment included *Thuketana v Health Professions Council of South Africa*, which underscores the appellate nature of such reviews and the standards to which the Council is held, emphasizing its role as a statutory guardian of professional standards.

[8] The court also considered previous rulings regarding sentencing practices, drawn from cases like *S v Malgas*, which set the standard for when an appellate court may intervene in decisions regarding penalties imposed in disciplinary contexts.

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law

[9] The SCA found that the disciplinary committee had adequately detailed the grounds for its recommendation for severe penalties, reaffirming the gravity of De Bruin’s actions which included multiple illegal attempts at inducing an abortion under perilous conditions. The Committee's findings established a clear expectation of professional conduct, which De Bruin egregiously violated.

[10] The court criticized the High Court's application of leniency in imposing a mere three-month suspension, noting that this effectively minimized the serious nature of the misconduct. It was highlighted that the involvement of another party’s trust in De Bruin’s medical expertise only exacerbated the betrayal involved in his actions.

[11] Upon reviewing the penalties, the SCA determined that a two-year suspension was a more fitting penalty that properly reflected the seriousness of De Bruin’s actions, thus maintaining the integrity of the medical profession. This conclusion took into consideration various factors, including the need to uphold public confidence in the profession and the severity of the emotional and physical trauma inflicted on the complainant.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications

[12] The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the Council's appeal and substituted the penalty for a two-year suspension from practice. It concluded that the disciplinary committee's findings and the initial recommendations were reasonable and justified, particularly given the egregious nature of De Bruin's conduct.

[13] The SCA emphasized that while a period of suspension serves as a significant penalty, it must also reflect the nature of the offense to maintain the professional standards expected in the medical field. The court's decision highlights the importance of strict adherence to ethical and professional standards in healthcare, assuring that such misconduct does not jeopardize patient safety and trust.

[14] This ruling reaffirms the authority of professional councils to enforce disciplinary measures and emphasizes the gravity of misconduct, intended to serve as a deterrent to others in the field. The case sets a precedent concerning the standards applied to medical conduct judgments, indicating a clear delineation between leniency and necessary strict adherence to professional conduct codes.
