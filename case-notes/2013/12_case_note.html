Constitutional Court

Justice Alliance of South Africa v Minister for Safety and Security

Constitutional Court of South Africa Case CCT 02/13 [2013] ZACC 12 Decided on: 21 May 2013

Constitutional Court 2013 [2013] ZACC 12 Decided on: 21 May 2013 --- ### F –

Constitutional Court of South Africa
Case CCT 02/13
[2013] ZACC 12
Decided on: 21 May 2013

F – Facts

The Justice Alliance of South Africa (the Applicant), along with a second applicant (now liquidated), contested an adverse costs ruling issued by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA). The ruling concluded that the appeal did not raise a constitutional issue, hence the costs order was justified based on the representation of firearm owners with financial interests in the proceedings ([para 1]). The Supreme Court of Appeal's decision stemmed from litigation concerning the Firearms Control Act and issues of compensation for firearms surrendered under its guidelines, which were allegedly issued in an unlawful manner ([paras 6-7]).

The background of the case included a long-standing failure by the Minister of Safety and Security to draft guidelines for compensation under section 137 of the Act, leading to a complaint by the applicant organization regarding the lack of compensation for firearms surrendered and subsequently destroyed ([paras 5-6]). The SCA concluded that the claim for guidance on compensation was not significant enough to constitute a constitutional argument, dismissing any substantive constitutional issue ([para 10]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue before the Constitutional Court was whether the SCA properly evaluated the existence of genuine constitutional questions within the applicant's appeal, particularly in determining whether the appeal for costs raised a substantive constitutional issue ([para 4]). The court had to assess whether there were exceptional circumstances that warranted granting leave to appeal on costs alone, amid claims that the appeal did not democratically reflect Constitutional rights or interests ([para 14]).

R – Rules

The Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources, and Others case set critical precedents essential for evaluating the applicant's claims ([para 3]). The Constitutional Court articulated that:

  1. The award of costs in constitutional matters inherently raises constitutional issues.
  2. It is typically not in the interests of justice to grant leave solely on costs unless exceptional circumstances exist.
  3. The substantive nature of the issues must genuinely invoke relevant constitutional considerations.
  4. Each case must be assessed based on judicial discretion and whether the court's conclusions exhibited demonstrable flaws or injustifiable conclusions ([paras 3-4]).

Moreover, the principle of legality underpins the argument as it frames challenges against unlawful actions by state organs, thereby constituting constitutional issues of importance ([para 10]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court examined the nature of the legal issues presented. It first acknowledged the claim made by the applicant, highlighting its focus on compensation under the guidelines promulgated by the Minister ([para 10]). However, the court observed that the applicant limited its arguments too narrowly and abandoned any reliance on significant provisions under the Bill of Rights, specifically section 25, concerning property rights ([para 12]).

The court recognized that while legality challenges raise constitutional questions, the current appeal did not engage fundamental rights as articulated under the Bill of Rights. This limited the notion of "exceptional circumstances," distinguishing it from earlier rulings where fundamental rights could be asserted ([paras 15-16]). Moreover, there was no compelling constitutional issue that directly affected the fundamental rights of the applicant’s members as there was no direct claim that the guidelines violated such rights.

Ultimately, the court discerned that although the initial litigation involving the failure to promulgate guidelines might engage exceptional circumstances, the current appeal relating to compensation did not foster any authentic constitutional issues warranting review ([paras 15-16]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court refused the application for leave to appeal, determining that the SCA's assessment was appropriate and that no substantial constitutional issues merited further judicial scrutiny. The court reasserted that while challenges involving the interpretation of statutory provisions may invoke questions of legality, not all such challenges rise to the level of exceptional constitutional concerns necessitating appeal ([paras 17-20]).

In its final order, the court declined to grant costs, affirming a position that the applicant's representation, even if it involved financial interests, did not substantiate a constitutional crisis or violation ([para 17]).

The implications of this ruling underscore the necessity for litigants asserting constitutional issues to substantiate their claims robustly, particularly when navigating the complexities of legislation that intersects with constitutional rights. This case reiterates the care needed for courts when adjudicating on cost orders involving public interest litigation and establishes a clear demarcation between genuine constitutional claims and those laden with personal financial interests.
