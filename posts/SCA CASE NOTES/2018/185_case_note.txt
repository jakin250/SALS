Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title: The Law Society of the Northern Provinces v Pule Abram Morobadi (1151/2017) [2018] ZASCA 185**

**Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa**

**Judges: Navsa ADP, Mbha, Zondi, Molemela, and Makgoka JJA**

**Date Heard: 21 November 2018**

**Date Delivered: 11 December 2018**

---

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–21])

The appellant, the Law Society of the Northern Provinces (the Law Society), initiated proceedings in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, under section 22(1)(d) of the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979 (the Act), seeking the removal of the respondent, Pule Abram Morobadi, from the roll of attorneys. The high court, comprising Nochumsohn AJ and Molopa-Sethosa J, dismissed the application, ordering each party to bear its own costs, which prompted the present appeal ([para 1]).

The case emerged from multiple complaints against Morobadi, including allegations of unprofessional conduct linked to the administration of a deceased estate and unauthorized financial maneuvers involving estate funds ([paras 6-10]). A critical complaint was made by Dr. Kgarume, who alleged that Morobadi improperly charged excessive fees and without authorization used funds from the deceased estate ([paras 6-7]). A subsequent investigation by Mr. Reddy, of the Law Society, uncovered serious financial irregularities, prompting the Law Society to seek Morobadi's removal for a pattern of unprofessional behavior, including failing to comply with ethical obligations regarding trust accounts ([paras 10-13],[20]).

Morobadi contested the proceedings, raising procedural defenses related to the Law Society's alleged failure to follow its own internal disciplinary protocols ([paras 21-22]). The high court dismissed these procedural objections and found that sufficient misconduct had occurred, but determined that the gravity of the conduct did not warrant removal from the roll, leading to a dismissal of the Law Society’s application ([paras 29-31]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 2, 25, 36])

The central issues raised on appeal include:

1. Whether the high court erred in its interpretation of section 22(1)(d) of the Attorneys Act regarding the fitness of the respondent to remain on the roll of attorneys ([para 36]).
   
2. Whether the high court misconstrued its role as custodian of the public interest and misapplied the standard of proof required in disciplinary matters concerning attorneys ([paras 30, 36]).

3. In light of established misconduct, whether an interim suspension was warranted pending a full disciplinary inquiry ([para 38]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 3-4, 5, 26, 30])

The appeal was grounded in the provisions of section 22(1)(d) of the Attorneys Act, which allows for the striking off or suspension of an attorney if found unfit to practice ([para 3]). The case relied on established precedents including *Solomon v Law Society of the Cape of Good Hope* 1934 AD 401, which underscores that proceedings under section 22 are of a disciplinary nature rather than ordinary civil proceedings ([para 4]). The principles elucidated in *Malan & another v Law Society of the Northern Provinces* [2008] ZASCA 90 were also pertinent, indicating a structured three-stage inquiry concerning inquiries into attorneys' fitness ([para 5]).

Furthermore, the high court was bound by its duty to protect the public interest, necessitating an assessment of whether the misconduct established warranted a disciplinary hearing regardless of internal procedural adherence ([paras 30, 36]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 25–40])

The Supreme Court of Appeal articulated a departure from the high court's reasoning, asserting that the nature of the allegations against Morobadi warranted a more robust investigation and determination than was provided. The court found that the high court had misapplied its discretion by failing to consider the serious nature of the allegations, particularly the admission of taking unauthorized estate funds, which significantly undermined public confidence in the profession ([paras 34-35]).

The court expounded that, given the admitted misconduct (taking R48,000 from a deceased estate and charging fees for services improperly) and considering additional allegations of fraud raised by the Gauteng Department of Human Settlements, the high court was remiss in not prioritizing public protection ([paras 26-38]). The appeal court emphasized the importance of an interim suspension to shield the public while a thorough inquiry into the respondent's conduct was conducted, a measure the high court neglected to apply effectively ([paras 36, 39]).

Moreover, the Supreme Court noted that the Law Society's procedural irregularity claims had less bearing on public protection than the substantive issues of Morobadi's fit and proper status as a practicing attorney ([para 28]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 41-43])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal primarily on the grounds that the high court's dismissal of the application for removal from the roll was both a misinterpretation of the law and inept in safeguarding the public interest. The court set aside the high court’s order, imposing an interim suspension on Morobadi pending a full disciplinary inquiry to be completed within three months ([paras 42-43]).

The ratio decidendi of the judgement underscored the disjunction between technical procedural adherence and substantive justice, asserting that the fitness of an attorney must be critically assessed especially in cases implicating public trust. This case sets a compelling precedent for future disciplinary actions, reiterating that notwithstanding procedural irregularities, protective measures must be taken to address serious allegations against legal practitioners. It reinforces a judicial commitment to public interest and accountability within the legal profession in South Africa, ensuring that even when procedural hurdles are present, the overarching need to protect the public remains paramount.
