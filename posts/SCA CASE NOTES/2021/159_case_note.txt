Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# Case Note: Lötter N O and Others v Minister of Water and Sanitation and Others (725/2020) [2021] ZASCA 159

## FIRAC Structure

### F – Facts
The case arises from three consolidated appeals regarding the interpretation of section 25 of the National Water Act 36 of 1998 (NWA) and whether transfers of water use entitlements from one entity to another are permissible. 

In the **Lötter matter** ([4]–[9]), the Doornkraal Business Trust negotiated to purchase 30 hectares of water usage entitlement from Britzkraal Properties (Pty) Ltd for R1 950 000. As required, they obtained the necessary approval from the regulatory authority, which was contingent upon the Director-General's consent. Doornkraal prepared a detailed motivation for the license application, indicating that increased water use was necessary for expanding their dairy operations and would not harm existing authorized users. However, the Director-General rejected their application, asserting that section 25(2) does not permit transfers between separate legal entities ([7]).

The **Wiid matter** ([9]–[13]) followed a similar narrative, wherein Mr. Wiid and associated parties entered agreements with the GP Viljoen Trust to acquire substantial water use entitlements for a cumulative sum exceeding R20 million. Again, the Director-General denied their application on similar grounds as the Lötter case.

Lastly, the **SAAWUA matter** ([14]–[15]) was initiated by the South African Association for Water User Associations and various other entities seeking clarity on the meaning of sections 25(1) and 25(2) of the NWA concerning trading in water use entitlements. They sought a declaratory order after their requests for entitlement transfers were similarly denied.

All three cases resulted in an appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal after the Gauteng Division of the High Court dismissed their applications ([16]).

### I – Issues
The principal legal issues determined by this Court concern:
1. The interpretation of section 25 of the NWA to ascertain whether it allows for temporary or permanent transfers of water use entitlements from one person to another ([1], [17]).
2. Whether the Act permits trade in water use entitlements and, if so, under what conditions ([18]).

### R – Rules
The Court based its ruling primarily on the interpretation of section 25 of the NWA, which governs the transfer of water use authorizations ([2]). Key provisions include:

- **Section 25(1)** states that a water management institution may allow a holder of water use entitlements to temporarily use that water for a different purpose or on another property in the vicinity ([2]).
- **Section 25(2)** enables a holder to surrender their entitlement wholly or partially to facilitate a license application for another landholder ([2]). 

Additionally, the regulatory framework of the NWA, with its emphasis on equitable access to water, provides context for examining the permissibility of trading in water use entitlements ([26]–[29]).

The Court also refers to the principles of statutory interpretation, wherein sections must be read in cohesive terms and contextually in accordance with public interest ([44]).

### A – Application
In its analysis, the Supreme Court of Appeal rejected the interpretation of the full bench of the Gauteng Division that viewed section 25 as precluding any trading or transfer of water use entitlements between separate legal entities ([24]).

The majority opinion interpreted section 25(1) as explicitly allowing for temporary transfers to third parties. This assertion was based on the word usage in the section, which allows for a mechanism by which water holders can permit others to use their allocations ([42]–[46]). 

This interpretation extended to section 25(2)—the mechanism for a more permanent transfer of water rights—which the Court reasoned must also contemplate transfers to third parties, thereby facilitating the notion of trade ([47]–[49]). 

Moreover, the Court stated that allowing trading in water use entitlements aligns with the NWA's goals for economic development and efficient water use. It emphasized that the existing framework ensures that the state maintains authority over national water resources, thus safeguarding the public interest against monopolization ([50]–[61]).

The dissenting opinion, represented by Makgoka J, maintained that allowing such transfers would undermine the Act's objectives tied to equity and redress past discriminatory practices ([69]–[70]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the decisions of the Gauteng High Court, thereby affirming that:

1. Section 25(1) and 25(2) permit the temporary and permanent transfer of water use entitlements, respectively, thereby allowing trading with the necessary regulatory approvals.
2. The appeals of all parties were upheld, and the Director-General's decisions to refuse the license applications were reviewed and set aside ([66]–[68]).

The implications of the judgment not only clarify the use and transfer of water rights but also reinforce the NWA’s commitment to equity in water access, encouraging the potential for economic transactions while maintaining public oversight. This ruling also marks a significant turning point by shifting the interpretative landscape of water law in South Africa, thereby enhancing the regulatory framework established by the NWA.
