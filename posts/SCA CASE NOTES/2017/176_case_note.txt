Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: Mohamed’s Leisure Holdings (Pty) Ltd v Southern Sun Hotel Interests (Pty) Ltd (183/17) [2017] ZASCA 176

## F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–4])

The case involves a lease agreement concerning the property known as Remaining Extent of Erf 13164 Cape Town, originally concluded in 1982. The appellant, Mohamed’s Leisure Holdings (Pty) Ltd, obtained ownership of the property in 1996 and, in November 2001, entered into a written lease with the respondent, Southern Sun Hotel Interests (Pty) Ltd, which was to run until December 2021.

The appellant sought the respondent’s eviction due to breach of the rental payment terms articulated in clause 20 of the lease agreement, which stipulated that failure to pay rent on the due date allowed the lessor to cancel the lease and regain possession of the property. The respondent, a longstanding tenant with a significant operational history, failed to make timely rental payments on two occasions, once in June 2014 and again in October 2014, with both incidents attributed to processing errors by its bank, Nedbank.

While the high court recognized the breach, it declined to sanction the eviction, considering the cancellation to be manifestly unreasonable and contrary to public policy, thereby invoking principles of ubuntu and fairness in contract law ([paras 1–6]).

## I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 21–22])

The primary legal question addressed by the court was whether the enforcement of the cancellation clause (clause 20) was manifestly unreasonable or unfair, thereby amounting to a contravention of public policy. The high court’s decision to infuse the principles of ubuntu and good faith into the analysis raised concerns over the applicability of these concepts within the strict confines of contract law and the doctrine of pacta sunt servanda ([paras 21–22]).

Key issues included:
1. Whether the implementation of clause 20, resulting in eviction, constituted a breach of public policy or fairness.
2. The applicability of constitutional values, including good faith, ubuntu, and justice, in enforcing contractual obligations.

## R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 1, 21, 24, 29])

The court relied heavily on contract law principles, particularly the doctrine of pacta sunt servanda, which denotes that agreements must be honored. Important precedents cited include:
- **Venter v Venter** 1949 (1) SA 768 (A), concerning the enforceability of contractual terms ([para 2]).
- **Barkhuizen v Napier** [2007] ZACC 5, which discussed the public policy consequences of enforcing specific contractual terms ([paras 14, 27]).
- **Sasfin (Pty) Ltd v Beukes** 1989 (1) SA 1 (AD), emphasizing cautious application of the public policy doctrine in contract law ([para 22]).

Additionally, the court acknowledged previous judgments regarding good faith, specifically referring to **Everfresh Market Virginia (Pty) Ltd v Shoprite Checkers (Pty) Ltd** [2011] ZACC 30, which emphasized the need for considering good faith within the scope of contract law and public policy ([para 17]).

## A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 21–32])

The court meticulously analyzed the circumstances surrounding the breach and the subsequent invocation of clause 20. It noted that:
- The terms of the lease were not inconsistent with public policy and did not display any unequal bargaining power between the parties, as both operated on equal footing ([paras 28-29]).
- The lack of evidence demonstrating that the respondent's constitutional rights were infringed or any allegations of unconscionability in the contractual agreement rendered the high court's stance problematic. The court contended that the respondent's defense did not sufficiently undermine the sanctity of the contract or the principle of pacta sunt servanda ([paras 30-31]).
  
The court further considered that the respondent had been duly informed about the consequences of late payment and had adopted a supine approach after receiving repeated warnings. The mere existence of hardship for the respondent, resulting from the bank’s processing errors, could not be a viable excuse to disregard clear contractual obligations ([paras 32]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 34-34])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, overruling the high court’s decision. It reinforced the doctrine of pacta sunt servanda, stating that the enforcement of clause 20 was justified despite the respondent's arguments concerning fairness and public policy. The court determined that such considerations should not dilute the sanctity of contracts freely entered into. 

The final order mandated the respondent to vacate the premises by 31 March 2018, allowing for a notice period to mitigate the impact of the eviction. This ruling reaffirmed that while courts must consider fairness, they must also respect the clear contractual terms negotiated between parties ([paras 34]).

The implications of this judgment highlight the judiciary's cautious approach to modifying or invalidating established contractual terms based solely on perceived fairness or public interest, influencing future contract disputes and underscoring the importance of fulfilling contractual obligations as stipulated. The decision serves as a reminder that contractual agreements retain their enforceability unless proven otherwise under rigorous standards supporting public policy considerations.
