Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# Case Note: Olesitse NO v Minister of Police (470/2021) [2022] ZASCA 90

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])

This case involves an appeal by Ms. Mmabasotho Christinah Olesitse, representing her deceased husband’s estate, against a dismissal from the Gauteng Division of the High Court regarding her application for condonation for late filing of an appeal. The appeal concerns two actions that arose from the same incident: unlawful arrest and detention, followed by a claim for malicious prosecution. 

The deceased, Mr. Tebogo Patrick Olesitse, was arrested without a warrant on 19 May 2008, on charges related to theft and corruption within the South African Police Service (SAPS). He was detained for ten days and subsequently released on bail on 29 May 2008. The charges were formally withdrawn by the Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP) on 17 May 2011. Mr. Olesitse initiated a claim for damages due to unlawful arrest on 26 May 2011, seeking R400,000. He was awarded R90,000 following a subsequent hearing on the merits.

While the first claim was ongoing, a second claim for malicious prosecution was filed on 12 December 2012, alleging that the police acted without reasonable cause. The respondent raised a point of law against the second claim, asserting that it was a duplication of the first claim, violating the common law "once and for all" rule. The High Court agreed and dismissed the second claim. The appellant subsequently sought to condone the late filing of her appeal against this decision, leading to the current proceedings in the Supreme Court of Appeal.

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–5, 15–21])

Two main issues arose in this case: 

1. Should the Supreme Court of Appeal condone the late filing of the application for leave to appeal?
2. Was the High Court correct in ruling that the claim for malicious prosecution was a duplication of the claim for unlawful arrest?

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 13–21])

The Supreme Court referenced several legal principles in reaching its decision:

- The "once and for all" rule necessitates that all claims for damages arising from a single cause of action must be pursued in a single legal action. This principle is articulated in **Custom Credit Corporation (Pty) Ltd v Shembe** [1972] 3 All SA 489 (A) at 472A-D and **Evins v Shield Insurance** [1980] 2 All SA 40 (A); 1980 (2) SA 814 (AD), which emphasize the importance of preventing multiple litigations on the same cause of action.

- **MEC for Health and Social Development, Gauteng v DZ obo WZ** [2017] ZACC 37, 2018 (1) SA 335 (CC) also supports the application of this rule in the South African context.

These legal principles serve to promote judicial efficiency and prevent the defendant from repeated harassment in litigation.

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 7–21])

Upon examining the factual matrix of the case, the court found that both claims arose from the same event—the arrest and subsequent prosecution of the deceased. Despite the distinction that the claims for malicious prosecution and unlawful arrest are conceptually different, the court concluded that they were fundamentally connected. 

The Supreme Court held that the

appellant had sufficient information to pursue both claims in the same action. Since all relevant facts happened prior to the initiation of the first claim for unlawful arrest, the deceased should have included his claim for malicious prosecution within that action. Therefore, allowing a separate claim for malicious prosecution would contravened the once and for all rule, which mandates that all damages must be claimed concurrently.

The court acknowledged various considerations presented by the appellant but concluded that the public policy interests in preventing double jeopardy and potential prejudice to the respondent outweighed the appellant's right to seek redress for additional damages after a separate evaluation of the facts.

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 21–23])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court's decision that the claim for malicious prosecution was indeed a duplication of the earlier claim for unlawful arrest and detention. The court also granted condonation for the late filing of the application for leave to appeal, recognizing the importance of the case to the appellant and her family.

The implications of this decision reinforce the necessity for plaintiffs to consolidate claims arising from the same facts into one action, aiming to further judicial efficiency and finality in litigation. The ruling serves as a pivotal affirmation of the once and for all rule within South African law, ensuring that claimants are encouraged to construct comprehensive claims at the outset to avoid subsequent duplicative actions that burden the judiciary.

---

In summary, this case exemplifies the South African legal framework surrounding delictual claims and the emphasis on judicial economy in managing overlapping litigations. The court's rulings delineate the boundaries within which litigants must operate when pursuing multiple claims stemming from a singular wrongful act.
