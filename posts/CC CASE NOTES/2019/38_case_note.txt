Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: Gelyke Kanse and Others v Chairperson of the Senate of the University of Stellenbosch and Others [2019] ZACC 38

## F – Facts
The case before the Constitutional Court of South Africa concerns the 2016 Language Policy of the University of Stellenbosch (the "University"), which the applicants sought to challenge. The applicants—comprising a voluntary association advocating for equal treatment of Afrikaans and indigenous languages, along with several students who desired instruction in Afrikaans—sought to have the 2016 Language Policy set aside and the previous 2014 policy reinstated. The 2014 Language Policy provided significant access to Afrikaans as a medium of instruction ([1]–[2]).

The 2016 Language Policy marked a significant shift towards English as the primary medium of instruction, leading to the claim that Afrikaans's status was undermined and access to education in Afrikaans diminished. The applicants feared that the 2016 policy would lead to the marginalization and eventual disappearance of Afrikaans as a medium of instruction at the University ([5]–[7]). The High Court previously ruled against the applicants, confirming the constitutionality of the 2016 Language Policy, prompting the direct appeal to the Constitutional Court ([8]–[12]).

## I – Issues
The primary issue was whether the 2016 Language Policy of the University complied with the constitutional provisions relating to language rights, particularly sections 29(2) and 6 of the Constitution. Section 29(2) guarantees the right to receive education in the language of one's choice in public educational entities "where that education is reasonably practicable," while section 6(2) requires the state to take positive measures to elevate the status of indigenous languages ([1]–[3], [14]).

Furthermore, the case examined whether the University’s shift from a bilingual to a predominantly English offering constituted a violation of these provisions and whether the designation of Afrikaans within the policy was defensible and justified in light of its implications for student access and equity ([13], [20]–[21]).

## R – Rules
In adjudicating this matter, the Court referenced various constitutional provisions and precedents regarding language rights within the educational context. Of particular note was the determination from previous cases such as *AfriForum SCA* that outlined how the construct of "reasonably practicable" encompasses both factual and normative assessments ([11], [37]–[40]).

The framework for constitutional interpretation also drew upon the principles laid out in *Ermelo*, clarifying that courts must conduct a context-sensitive analysis balanced against elements of equity, practicability, and the need to remedy past discrimination in educational access ([24], [30]).

The Court also reiterated that any constitutional rights must be understood in the context of social justice and access to education. It emphasized the importance of maintaining a careful balance between supporting indigenous languages and ensuring equitable access for individuals not conversant in those languages ([43]–[46]).

## A – Application
Upon reviewing the evidence, the Court determined that the University had conducted a thorough and exhaustive review of its language policy following significant social changes at its campus. The decision to pivot towards offering more instruction in English was rooted in empirical data showing that a considerable number of prospective black students were not proficient in Afrikaans, which had previously created barriers to their access to the institution ([16]–[20], [27]).

The University presented a cogent argument regarding the cost implications of returning to a fully parallel language instruction model, asserting that the financial burden—totaling hundreds of millions in infrastructure and operational costs—rendered such a model "not reasonably practicable." This economic rationale was weighed against the right to mother's language education, ultimately leading the Court to find that the diminished Afrikaans offering was supported by appropriate justifications ([31]–[36], [45]).

The evidence also showed that, while students could opt for instruction in Afrikaans, the dominant trend reflected a preference for English among the larger student body, further underscoring the reasonableness of the University’s decision to adjust its language policy in response to changing demographics and educational demands ([22]–[40]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court upheld the 2016 Language Policy as constitutionally valid, concluding that the University had met its obligations under the Constitution to provide education in the language of choice in a manner that was "reasonably practicable." The judgment recognized the critical balance between upholding the rights of Afrikaans-speaking students and the University's imperative to foster an inclusive academic environment that reflects the demographics of its student population.

The decision also acknowledged the broader implications for language rights in South Africa, asserting the need for institutional structures to consider the historical inequalities of language use in education while navigating the realities of a multilingual society ([48]–[51]). No costs order was made against the applicants in this appeal, pursuant to the principles of constitutional litigation established in *Biowatch* ([49]–[51]).

The ruling carries significant implications for language policy in higher education and reinforces the constitutional mandate for equitable access to education, ensuring that the choices of language instruction remain dynamic and responsive to changing social needs in South Africa.
