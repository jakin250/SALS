Supreme Court of Appeal

Moipone Moroka v Premier of the Free State Province and Others (295/2020) [2022] ZASCA 34

The case centers on a traditional leadership dispute involving the Barolong Boo Seleka Royal Family, specifically the appellant, Moipone Moroka, and the first respondent, the Premier of the Free State Province.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 34

F – Facts

The case centers on a traditional leadership dispute involving the Barolong Boo Seleka Royal Family, specifically the appellant, Moipone Moroka, and the first respondent, the Premier of the Free State Province. The dispute began following the death of Kgosi Ramokgopa Moroka in 2013, leading to a conflicting succession claim between two factions within the royal family. The Premier referred the matter to the Commission on Traditional Leadership Disputes and Claims (the Commission) without allowing the Free State House of Traditional Leaders (the House) to address it first, contrary to section 21(2)(b) of the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003 (the Act) ([1]).

The High Court found no contravention by the Premier and dismissed Moroka’s application to review the Commission’s findings and the Premier’s decision. The appellant subsequently sought leave to appeal, contending that the Commission lacked the authority to investigate the dispute, relying on section 25(5) of the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Amendment Act 23 of 2009 (the Amendment Act) ([2]).

The controversy also revolved around two preliminary issues: whether the appeal was moot due to the death of the sixth respondent, and whether a new legal argument raised by the appellant was permissible ([3]–[4]).

The Commission had recommended that the rightful ruling house was that of Ramantshi Richard, a critical finding as it effectively challenged the long-standing leadership of Tshipinare's lineage, which had been in place since the 1880s ([17]).

I – Issues

The key issues at stake in this case were:

  1. Did the Premier contravene section 21(2)(b) of the Act by referring the dispute to the Commission before the House could deal with it? ([1])
  2. Did the Commission have jurisdiction to investigate the dispute given that it was referred after the six-month cut-off from the Amendment Act's effective date? ([2], [20], [25])
  3. Does the phrase ‘may not’ in section 25(5) of the Amendment Act indicate a strict prohibition against the Commission’s authority to investigate disputes submitted after the cut-off? ([22]–[24])

R – Rules

Statutory Framework

  1. Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003:
  • Section 21(2)(b) requires that disputes regarding senior traditional leadership first be referred to the relevant House.
  1. Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Amendment Act 23 of 2009:
  • Section 25(2)(viii) restricts the Commission’s jurisdiction to disputes arising from claims dating from September 1, 1927, onward.
  • Subsection (5) explicitly states that any dispute submitted after six months following the coming into operation of the Amendment Act may not be dealt with by the Commission.

Precedent

  • Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality ([22]), which lays out guidelines for statutory interpretation focused on context and purpose.

A – Application

The court applied principles of statutory interpretation to determine whether the referral to the Commission was valid.

  1. Non-compliance with the Act:
  • The Premier’s initial referral to the Commission without the House having an opportunity to resolve the issue was a critical factor. However, the High Court and subsequently the Supreme Court found that the House had ultimately consented to the referral after determining that it had been unable to resolve the dispute, which the House recognized in its minutes ([12]–[15]).
  1. Commission's Authority:
  • The Court emphasized that the Commission's authority was limited by the Amendment Act. It interpreted the phrase ‘may not’ in section 25(5) to mean a strict prohibition against the Commission from taking on disputes after the six-month period post-implementation of the Amendment Act. As the dispute was referred to the Commission in 2014—well beyond this timeline—the Commission had no authority to investigate the matter ([18]–[20]).
  1. Mootness of the Appeal:
  • The Court dismissed the argument that the appeal was moot due to the death of the sixth respondent, affirming that the core legal issues related to the rightful succession were still relevant and required resolution ([4]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately overturned the High Court's ruling. It held that the Premier did contravene the requirements of the Act by referring the dispute without allowing the House's involvement, thus failing to comply with procedural mandates ([1], [23]). The appeal was upheld, with the findings and recommendations of the Commission regarding the Barolong Boo Seleka senior traditional leadership position being reviewed and set aside ([2.1]–[2.3]).

The ruling underscores the importance of upholding statutory limits regarding the powers of traditional leadership bodies and reinforces the necessity for compliance with procedural statutes in traditional governance matters. This case also serves as critical guidance in interpreting the scope and authority of statutory commissions dealing with customary law disputes.

Implications:
This decision clarifies that commissions created by statute have a bounded scope of authority, reinforcing the rule of law and the legislative framework governing traditional leadership disputes in South Africa ([84]). The judgment highlights the need for adherence to statutory provisions when addressing leaders' succession in traditional governance, which remains vital for the legitimacy and stability of traditional institutions in the post-apartheid era.
