Case Note: Nandi Jacobs v The Minister of Justice and Correctional Services (431/2020) [2021] ZASCA 151

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 151 #### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Proc

Case Note: Nandi Jacobs v The Minister of Justice and Correctional Services (431/2020) [2021] ZASCA 151

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([1]–[3])

In the case of Nandi Jacobs v The Minister of Justice and Correctional Services, the appellant, Nandi Jacobs, initiated an action against the Minister claiming R 2,040,000 for pain and suffering incurred as a result of an attack by Ivan Botha, a convicted criminal who had previously been incarcerated for serious offences including rape and indecent assault. Botha was released on parole on 1 November 2010 after a Parole Board decision. On 1 April 2012, while on parole, Botha attempted to assault, rape, and rob Jacobs. The basis of Jacobs' claim was twofold: first, that the Minister failed to ensure that Botha should not have been released on parole given his criminal history; second, that Botha’s violations of his parole conditions were not adequately addressed, which resulted in a failure to protect Jacobs from his criminal acts.

During the trial presided over by Judge Mavundla in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Jacobs presented her testimony regarding the assault and provided evidence that Botha had a history of non-compliance with his parole conditions. Following Jacobs’ closing of her case, the Minister applied for absolution from the instance, which was granted by the High Court. Jacobs subsequently appealed this decision, asserting that the High Court erred in its application of the appropriate legal standards ([2]–[3]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([3], [9], [12])

The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether the High Court properly granted absolution from the instance against Jacobs’ claims. More specifically, the court needed to determine whether the High Court correctly applied the legal standard of absolution, which requires that a court must assess if it is reasonably possible for the evidence presented to support a finding in favor of the plaintiff.

Moreover, the SCA was tasked with addressing whether a cause of action exists against the Minister for alleged negligence by the Parole Board concerning its duty to protect the public, particularly in light of the Correctional Services Act 111 of 1998 and constitutional duties to protect citizens from violence and crime ([9], [12]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([6], [10], [12])

The SCA referenced Carmichelle v Minister of Safety and Security which clarified that a delictual action might arise from a failure by public officials to protect individuals from harm under certain circumstances ([1]). The court also reflected on the implications arising from statutory obligations under the Correctional Services Act, particularly concerning the legal duties of the Parole Board as it relates to the Minister’s liability ([10]).

In establishing standards for absolution from the instance, the SCA reviewed principles from past precedents that emphasize the necessity for a court to reasonably consider whether sufficient evidence exists to sustain the plaintiff’s claims. The necessity for a causal connection between actions of the Parole Board and the eventual harm suffered by Jacobs was also a focal point ([3], [6]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([6]–[30])

The SCA conducted a comprehensive analysis of the evidence presented to establish that the High Court had erred in granting absolution. While the High Court emphasized that Jacobs’ testimony alone did not constitute sufficient evidence to demonstrate negligence, the SCA found that the documentary evidence reviewed—and the Minister's own admissions—could reasonably suggest negligence on the part of the Parole Board.

The SCA noted the lack of important assessments, such as a psychologist's report concerning Botha’s sexual behavior—an omission that, when considered alongside the high risk he posed as a repeat offender, signified a potential negligence to assess adequately the risks associated with his release ([25], [28]).

Furthermore, the court highlighted that the Parole Board's decision lacked an adequate exploration of the inherent risks posed by releasing an offender with a history of serious sexual offenses, ultimately concluding that this warranted further examination at trial rather than a premature finding of absolution ([29], [30]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([31]–[34])

The SCA concluded that the High Court had erred in its decision to grant absolution from the instance based on the evidence presented. The Court held that Jacobs had sufficiently established that there were material questions of fact regarding the lawful exercise of discretion by the Parole Board and whether their actions could establish a cause of action against the Minister.

Thus, the appeal was upheld, the High Court’s order was set aside, and the application for absolution from the instance was dismissed with costs. This ruling implied that the issues surrounding the interpretation of the Correctional Services Act and the Minister's duties in relation to public safety were not conclusively settled, leaving room for further litigation on the merits of Jacobs’ claims ([33], [34]).

This case illustrates the intricate balance courts must strike between protecting public safety and permitting the exercise of discretion by parole boards, reflecting broader implications for constitutional responsibilities regarding the prevention of violent crime.

References

  1. Carmichelle v Minister of Safety and Security and Another [2001] ZACC 22; 2001 (4) SA 938 (CC); 2001 (10) BCLR 995 (CC).
  2. Steenkamp N O v Provincial Tender Board, Eastern Cape [2005] ZASCA 120; [2006] 1 All SA 478 (SCA); 2006 (3) SA 151 (SCA).
