Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Mfundo Nontshinga v S (770/2015) [2016] ZASCA 76

## F – Facts

The case of *Mfundo Nontshinga v S* involves the appellants, Mfundo Nontshinga, Mzwekaya Zagana, Mandla Mazwi, and Dumisani Sikakana, who were each charged with multiple criminal offenses, including robbery, kidnapping, and unlawful possession of a firearm and ammunition. Specifically, they were convicted on nine counts by the Regional Court in Port Elizabeth on 6 August 2010 and subsequently sentenced to an effective 17 years' imprisonment. Following their conviction, the appellants' application for leave to appeal was dismissed by the regional magistrate on 9 August 2010 ([para 1]).

Dissatisfied with the outcome, the appellants petitioned the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court for leave to appeal their convictions and sentences. This petition was refused on 12 March 2011. However, the co-accused, Mr. Vusumzi Bolo, later successfully petitioned the high court to appeal his convictions for unlawful possession of a firearm and ammunition, resulting in the court’s judgment to set aside those convictions ([para 2]).

Following Bolo's ruling, the appellants applied to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) for special leave to appeal against their convictions concerning counts eight and nine—unlawful possession of a firearm and ammunition. The SCA granted special leave, but the legality of this order hinged on whether they had followed the appropriate procedural rules after their petitions were refused ([para 3]).

## I – Issues

The central legal issue in this case is whether the Supreme Court of Appeal had the jurisdiction to grant special leave to appeal when the appellants had not first sought leave from the high court against the refusal of their petitions. In essence, the question was whether the appellants adhered to the procedural requirements under the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959 and its relevance to their appeal ([para 4]).

Another related issue is the effect of the earlier high court decision concerning the co-accused, which potentially opened a path for the appellants to seek the necessary leave to appeal from the high court ([para 5]).

## R – Rules

The applicable legal standards derive primarily from the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959 as well as the principles established in prior case law. In *S v Khoasasa* [2002] ZASCA 113, it was asserted that a refusal of leave to appeal by the high court constitutes an order under sections 20(1) and 20(4) of the Supreme Court Act, requiring an appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal only with the high court's leave, or alternatively, with the leave of the SCA if the high court denied the request ([para 4]).

This precedent was upheld in various subsequent cases, including *S v Matshona* [2008] ZASCA 58, *S v Tonkin* [2013] ZASCA 179, and *Johannes Windvogel v S* [2015] ZASCA 63, confirming the necessity of complying with procedural rules concerning the right to appeal ([para 4]).

## A – Application

Upon reviewing the matter, the SCA noted that the appellants’ trial and subsequent convictions predated the implementation of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, and thus the action had to be assessed under the older Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959 ([para 4]). 

The court emphasized that because the appellants did not seek leave from the high court concerning the refusal of their previous petitions, the special leave granted for their appeal was rendered a nullity. Effectively, this meant that the SCA lacked the jurisdiction to hear the matter since the necessary procedural steps had not been followed—a point both parties acknowledged ([para 4–5]).

While the SCA expressed that it might have been straightforward for the appellants to secure the requisite leave from the high court in light of Bolo's case, this did not mitigate their failure to follow the established legal process. The court reiterated that jurisdiction is foundational for any hearing, thereby reiterating the necessity of procedural compliance ([para 5]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that as the appellants had not sought the requisite leave from the high court to appeal the refusal of their earlier petitions, the order granting them special leave to appeal constituted a nullity. Consequently, the SCA ordered that the matter be struck from the roll on 4 May 2016, effectively concluding that it could not hear the appeal due to a lack of jurisdiction ([paras 4-5]).

This judgment underscores the importance of adhering to procedural rules in criminal appeals, reflecting on the rigorous requirements for jurisdiction and the ramifications of non-compliance. The case serves as a crucial reminder to appellants of the necessity of following established legal procedures to ensure that their right to appeal is actionable within the judicial system.
