Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# Case Note: Minister of Water and Environmental Affairs v Karan Beef Feedlot (20563/2014) [2015] ZASCA 157

## F – Facts
The respondent, I M Karan, trading as Karan Beef Feedlot, owns a tract of land adjacent to the Suikerbosrand River in Heidelberg, South Africa, which is primarily used for irrigation and feedlot activities requiring significant water resources. The respondent procured a permit on 28 September 1993 under the now-repealed Water Act 54 of 1956, allowing the extraction of up to 657,000 cubic meters of water per year for industrial purposes, specifically for the feedlot ([paras 1–2]).

The Water Act aimed to regulate the use of public water resources, vesting rights of usage in the Minister, thereby ensuring sustainable management of water ([para 6]). The appellant, the Minister of Water and Environmental Affairs, contended that the respondent was liable for water usage levies, while Karan disputed both the interpretation of the permit and his liability for such levies, claiming they applied only to irrigation water ([paras 4–5]).

Initially, the High Court ruled in favor of the respondent, declaring that the appellant could not charge for water used under the permit for industrial purposes, leading to this appeal ([para 5]).

## I – Issues
1. **Interpretation of the Permit**: What is the correct interpretation of the permit issued to Karan Beef Feedlot, specifically regarding the usage of water for industrial versus irrigation purposes?
2. **Liability for Water Levies**: Is the respondent liable for payment of levies associated with water supplied by the Trans-Caledon Tunnel Authority (TCTA)?

## R – Rules
The case hinges on several statutory provisions of the Water Act 54 of 1956, including:
- **Section 62**: Governs the control and use of public water, particularly in government water control areas. It delineates how rights to water are granted and under what conditions ([para 7]).
- **Section 62(2I)**: Provides an allowance for the Minister to grant permissions to abstract surplus water, emphasizing that these grants must be subject to conditions, including payment ([para 8]).
- **Section 138F**: Authorizes the Minister to levy charges on water supplied for any purpose related to government water works, stressing that beneficiaries of such water are liable for these charges ([para 20]).

Legal precedents cited include **Oudekraal Estates (Pty) Ltd v City of Cape Town & others** [2004] ZASCA 48, emphasizing that a permit remains in effect until legally varied, which underlines the requirement for Karan to seek a review to amend his permit ([para 17]).

## A – Application
The court took a nuanced approach to interpret the permit's language and its implications within the context of the Water Act. It clarified that the permit explicitly allows for the withdrawal of water for industrial purposes, and history showed that the respondent had previously paid for industrial water use and never contested this for over a decade. Therefore, the respondent's argument that the permit only relates to irrigation purposes was dismissed as inconsistent with the permit's explicit terms and past actions ([paras 15–16]).

On the matter of TCTA levies, the court assessed whether the respondent benefited from water supplied through government infrastructure as prescribed in Section 138F. The court concluded that Karan Beef Feedlot was not a beneficiary from such works, being situated upstream and thus not liable for the TCTA charges ([paras 21–23]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the appellant, finding that the terms of the permit were unambiguous and binding. Thus, the High Court's order was varied to reflect that Karan Beef Feedlot is liable for payment related to water abstraction under the terms stipulated in the permit. However, the appeal regarding the TCTA levies was dismissed, confirming that as the respondent did not benefit from the relevant government water works, he was not liable for such payments ([para 24]).

### Implications
This ruling underscores the importance of adhering to the expressed terms within water use permits and clarifies the responsibilities regarding payment for water usage under South African law. It also emphasizes the necessity for landowners to pursue formal review processes should they wish to contest the terms of such permits, hence reinforcing legal certainty in water management frameworks.
