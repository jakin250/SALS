Supreme Court of Appeal

Lewis v S [2007] SCA 3 (RSA)

The appellant, Keri Gwyn Lewis, was convicted of contempt of court by the Witwatersrand Local Division.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2007

F – Facts

The appellant, Keri Gwyn Lewis, was convicted of contempt of court by the Witwatersrand Local Division. The conviction arose from an incident where his cell phone rang during court proceedings, creating a disturbance ([1]). Despite being in a packed court and during the daily roll call, Lewis failed to silence his phone or immediately turn it off when it rang. Instead, he proceeded to answer the call while walking out of the court, thereby further disrupting the proceedings ([1] - [2]).

Following his conduct, the presiding judge found Lewis guilty of contempt and imposed a one-month prison sentence without granting him the right to appeal against the conviction ([1] - [4]). The judge justified the contempt finding by emphasizing that Lewis’s actions did not respect the court's dignity and disrupted the orderly progress of hearings ([4]). Lewis expressed regret post-conviction, but the court indicated that his demeanor conveyed a lack of understanding of the seriousness of his actions ([4] - [10]).

The case raised significant constitutional considerations regarding the right to a fair trial under Section 35(3) of the Constitution, particularly regarding the summary nature of the contempt proceedings ([8]). The circumstances of the case were such that Lewis had left the courtroom while the call was still ongoing, leading to arguments about whether immediate action was necessary and whether a summary procedure was constitutionally appropriate ([10]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues in this case included:

  1. Did the conduct of the appellant constitute contempt of court as defined under South African law?[5]
  2. Was the summary procedure employed by the court consistent with the right to a fair trial as guaranteed by Section 35(3) of the Constitution?[8]
  3. Did the appellant act with the requisite intent to disrupt court proceedings, thereby justifying the contempt conviction?[6]
  4. Were lesser actions adequate to address the disturbance caused by the appellant, thus making the summary procedure unnecessary?[10]
  5. Did the trial court fail to adequately ascertain the appellant's state of mind and intent when imposing the contempt conviction? ([12] - [14])

R – Rules

The legal framework governing contempt of court includes various precedents and statutory provisions:

  1. Contempt of Court Definition: The offence of contempt of court in facie curiae occurs when a person, by word or conduct, interferes with the administration of justice or violates the dignity or authority of the court ([5]).
  2. Intent Requirement: Intent is requisite for establishing contempt, meaning the accused must have intended to disrupt proceedings or show disrespect to the court ([6]).
  3. Constitutional Fair Trial Rights: Section 35(3) of the Constitution provides for the right to a fair trial, which includes various protections such as adequate time to prepare a defence ([8]).
  4. Summary Procedure for Contempt: The Constitutional Court has set forth that the administration of justice must generally employ traditional prosecutorial mechanisms, and summary procedures should be limited to urgent circumstances ([9]).

A – Application

In the present case, the Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized whether the actions of the appellant met the definitions and requirements for contempt. Notably, the Court highlighted that Lewis’s intention was pivotal to the legal determination of guilt. The appellant's conduct—allowing his cell phone to ring and subsequently answering it—was assessed through the lens of intent, emphasizing that his actions must indicate a willful disruption of court decorum ([10] - [12]).

The Court noted that the summary procedure utilized by the trial court was inappropriate given that immediate judicial intervention was not necessary; Lewis had exited the courtroom, thereby ceasing direct interference with proceedings ([10]). The appellant’s responses during the inquiry into his conduct were interpreted as showing a lack of understanding of the situation rather than outright contempt. The Court inferred that a more considerate rebuke or notification to the prosecuting authority might have sufficed to uphold the court's dignity without resorting to a punitive contempt conviction ([12] - [14]).

The Supreme Court also emphasized the commonality of cell phones ringing in various public venues, including courtrooms, and the possibility of inadvertent disturbance, thus arguing that a layperson might not grasp the severity of such actions ([12] - [14]). Therefore, the Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that Lewis had not demonstrated intent to be contemptuous, as required for a conviction in this context ([10]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal and set aside the conviction, finding that the summary contempt proceedings were unconstitutional due to a breach of the appellant's right to a fair trial ([15]). The Court stated that the trial court's approach failed to satisfy the requirement for intent and did not consider the possibility of the appellant’s inadvertence, undermining the legitimacy of the contempt finding. This case underscored the importance of safeguarding constitutional rights within contempt proceedings, particularly the necessity of intent and respect for due process.

This decision has significant implications for the application of contempt law in South Africa, illustrating the need for courts to cautiously navigate the balance between maintaining order in judicial proceedings and upholding fundamental rights guaranteed by the Constitution.
