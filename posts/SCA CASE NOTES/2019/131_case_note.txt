Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: Cash Paymaster Services (Pty) Ltd v Chief Executive Officer of the South African Social Security Agency and Others (1029/2018) [2019] ZASCA 131

## F – Facts

The South African Social Security Agency (SASSA) is an organ of state established under the South African Social Security Agency Act 9 of 2004 (the SASSA Act) with the responsibility to administer social assistance ([1]). SASSA awarded a tender to Cash Paymaster Services (Pty) Ltd (CPS) in January 2012 for the registration of beneficiaries and payment of social grants ([2]). Following a purported variation of the original contract during a meeting held on 15 June 2012, SASSA paid CPS R316,447,361.41 in what was described as an adjustment to the contract for additional services that were supposedly not covered in the original agreement ([3], [19]).

Corruption Watch, the third respondent, initiated legal proceedings to contest this payment, alleging that the payment lacked a lawful basis as SASSA had not requested nor formalized this additional service agreement as mandated by law ([12]). The Gauteng Division of the High Court ruled in favor of Corruption Watch, leading to this appeal by CPS ([4]), wherein the entire matter arose from allegations regarding the legality of public fund disbursement in the absence of proper authorization ([12], [25]).

## I – Issues

The key legal questions arising from this case are:

1. **Contractual Validity:** Did SASSA and CPS legitimately agree to vary their contract to compensate CPS for services rendered that were not stipulated in the initial agreement ([21], [25])?
2. **Lawfulness of Payments:** Was the payment made by SASSA to CPS for additional beneficiary registrations lawful under the provisions of the SASSA Act, Public Finance Management Act 1 of 1999 (PFMA), and associated procurement legislation ([12], [25])?

## R – Rules

The legal framework applicable to this case includes:

1. **Constitutional and Legislative Provisions:**
   - **SASSA Act**: Grants SASSA the authority to contract with service providers for efficient payment execution as stipulated in s 4(2)(a).
   - **PFMA**: Outlines the lawful management and expenditure of public funds, ensuring transparency and accountability in s 2.

2. **Contract Law Principles**: 
   - Contracts require mutual agreement and clarity in terms for variations, demanding formal written modifications pursuant to original agreement stipulations (particularly per clause 18.1 of their contract) ([21]).

3. **Principle of Legality**: Public entities must act within the bounds of their statutory authority, as reiterated in the case of *Corruption Watch NPC v President of the Republic of South Africa and Others* [2019] (2) SA 207 (WCC), defining the necessity for lawful decision-making by public authorities ([12]).

## A – Application

The court assessed the legitimacy of the alleged variation agreement and the resulting payment structure. It scrutinized the terms set out in the original request for proposals (RFP) and the service level agreement (SLA) binding SASSA and CPS. The court found that:

1. **No Valid Variation**: Evidence presented suggested that no formal agreement had been drafted or approved by the Bid Adjudication Committee (BAC), which contravened SASSA’s internal protocols ([21], [25]). The minutes of the 15 June 2012 meeting, though signed, did not constitute a valid alteration of the existing contract as they were not executed with the due process required ([20], [30]).

2. **Scope of Work and Payment Legitimacy**: The court established that the original SLA encompassed the enrollment of both recipients and beneficiaries, contrasting the assertions made by SASSA and CPS. Thus, the contention that additional payments were needed for registering beneficiaries contradicted the explicit obligations detailed in the SLA and the RFP ([24], [40], [51]). Such contractual obligations inherently included the enrolment of children, as evidenced by the definitions presented in both documents ([52]).

3. **Irregularities and Motivation for Payment**: The court determined that the process for approval of payment was fraught with irregularities, indicating ulterior motives possibly influenced by corruption or mismanagement, as revealed through inconsistencies in the claims made by SASSA and the internal reports presented by CPS ([25], [30]).

## C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal filed by CPS, affirming that there was no lawful basis for the payment made to it. The court's interpretation of the original contractual documents validated that CPS’s claims for remuneration for additional services were unfounded, thereby mandating restitution of the entire amount dispensed by SASSA ([60]). The implications of this ruling reinforced the importance of adherence to procurement regulations and accountability in the management of public funds, serving as a precedent for future cases involving public entity contracts and the management of social assistance programs in South Africa. The appellant was also ordered to bear the costs of the third respondent, highlighting the court's intent to discourage frivolous appeals ([61]).

This case serves as a critical reference for understanding the intersection of public finance law, administrative action, and the necessity for proper contractual adherence in public service delivery.
