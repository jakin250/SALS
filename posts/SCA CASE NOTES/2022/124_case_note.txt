# FIRAC Case Note: Solomons v The State (Case No 1292/21) [2022] ZASCA 124

## F – Facts
The appellant, Ms. Dawida Solomons, was convicted of the murder of her partner, Mr. Barnwell Sebenja, with whom she had a 15-year tumultuous relationship. The deceased was a father to two of Solomons’ children, and evidence presented at trial indicated a history of domestic violence. The Regional Court initially sentenced Solomons to 8 years' imprisonment, recognizing substantial and compelling circumstances that warranted a lesser sentence in defiance of the prescribed minimum of 15 years under the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997. 

The case proceeded to the Northern Cape Division of the High Court, where upon appeal, the conviction was upheld but the sentence was altered to 8 years’ imprisonment with a 3-year suspension, resulting in an effective 5-year term. The reasons for penalization included sparse medical evidence of abuse and significant emphasis on the events on the day of the incident, reducing the credibility of Solomons' claims of domestic violence ([1]–[11]).

## I – Issues
The principal legal questions arising from this case concerned:
1. Whether the high court misdirected itself in assessing the evidence regarding domestic violence in determining Solomons' sentence ([26]).
2. Whether appropriate considerations were taken into account concerning Solomons' status as a primary caregiver when deciding on a custodial sentence ([36]).

## R – Rules
Several legal principles and statutes informed the court’s decision:
1. **Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997** – establishes a mandatory minimum sentence for murder that does not provide flexibility unless substantial and compelling circumstances are proven ([3]).
2. **Domestic Violence Act 116 of 1998** – broadly defines domestic violence including physical and emotional abuse ([29]).
3. **Case Law** – The standard adopted from **S v Engelbrecht** [2005] indicated that in domestic violence cases where a victim seeks to ameliorate a sentence based on prior abuse, a rigorous evidentiary threshold must be met to demonstrate the existence and impact of that abuse ([28]).

## A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal assessed the facts against these rules. Solomons argued that the high court failed to give due regard to her experiences of domestic violence, including emotional manipulation and economic abuse that, according to her, played a substantial role in the relationship dynamics leading up to the incident. The appellate court found that although the high court recognized the history of abuse suggested by the interdict Solomons filed in 2015, her claims were undermined due to a lack of corroborating medical evidence and specific references to instances of physical violence ([5]–[8]).

The court also addressed the appellant's personal circumstances, including her role as the primary caregiver and implications of a custodial sentence on her children. However, it concluded that the high court had sufficiently weighed these factors against the seriousness of the offense committed and upheld its decision as neither disproportionate nor inappropriate ([33]–[34]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal against the sentence, affirming the high court’s ruling. The reasoning indicated that while issues of domestic violence are crucial, the evidence adduced did not sufficiently shift the balance required to overturn the sentence. The court highlighted the gravity of the offense and maintained that the high court appropriately exercised its discretion influenced primarily by the nature of the crime and the evidentiary burden set forth in **S v Engelbrecht** ([26]–[37]). This case underscores the complexities involved in sentencing within the context of domestic violence and the necessity for rigorous evidentiary standards when addressing claims of abuse in criminal proceedings.
