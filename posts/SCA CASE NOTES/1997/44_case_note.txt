# Case Note: Valerie Elsie Cornwell Purr v. ABSA Bank Ltd & Others [1997] 424/96

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts ([para 1–70])
This case arises from financial advice provided by Mr. Myles Stuart, a regional manager at the United Building Society (later absorbed by Absa Bank Ltd), to Mrs. Valerie Elsie Cornwell Purr and her family (collectively referred to as the Durrs). Mrs. Durr sought investment advice in 1985 as she found managing her investments burdensome. She was assured by Stuart that his recommendations for investments, particularly in the solid and secure company "Supreme," had merit, as Stuart claimed his own family money was invested there.

The Durrs, under Stuart's guidance, invested substantial amounts (over R590,000) in secured debentures and preference shares of two Supreme companies which were ultimately liquidated in 1992. The Durrs lost nearly all their investments, prompting Mrs. Durr to bring a delictual claim against both Stuart and Absa Bank, arguing negligence in the financial advice provided.

The court was thus tasked with determining whether Stuart acted with the requisite skill and care expected of a financial advisor, and if Absa Bank could be held vicariously liable for Stuart's actions.

### I – Issues ([para 1–70])
The primary legal questions addressed by the court include:
1. What standard of care is applicable to financial advisors in the context of giving investment advice?
2. Did Stuart meet the required standard of care, demonstrating reasonable skill and diligence, in advising the Durrs?
3. Can Absa Bank be held vicariously liable for Stuart's alleged negligence?

### R – Rules ([para 1–70])
The court relied on several legal precedents and statutes to guide its decision:
- **Negligence Standard**: Quoted from *van Wyk v Lewis* [1924] AD 438, establishing that a professional, including financial advisors, is expected to employ reasonable skill and care, contextualized within the specific branch of profession.
- **Expertise and Reasonable Skill**: The court evaluates whether the defendant’s actions align with that of a competent professional with expertise, rather than a typical broker, especially since Absa publicly positioned its financial advisors as experts.
- **Vicarious Liability**: The court applied principles of vicarious liability, noting that if Stuart was found negligent, Absa could be held liable for his conduct under employment law.

### A – Application ([para 1–70])
The court engaged in a comprehensive analysis of the evidence provided, particularly focusing on Stuart's actions and the expectations surrounding financial advisors:
- **Stuart's Competence**: While he was deemed competent in basic investment planning, the court decided he failed to conduct prudent inquiries regarding the companies, ignoring obvious warning signs, such as excessive commission and unsupported high returns. Stuart’s reliance on unverified information from Mannheim, without seeking independent verification through financial statements or a prospectus (which he acknowledged he was aware of), was deemed insufficient.
- **Comparison of Standards**: The court critically assessed the notion of an 'average broker' versus a bank’s investment advisor, settling on a higher standard of care appropriate for Stuart—especially given his position at Absa Bank and public representations of expertise.
- **Failure to Advise**: The lack of effort to ensure the safety of the investment for clients, neglecting to seek consultation from qualified professionals within the bank, formed a key basis for determining negligence.

### C – Conclusion ([para 1–70])
The court concluded that Stuart had indeed acted negligently in his advisory role, failing to exhibit the expected level of skill and diligence. As a result, Absa Bank was held vicariously liable for the negligence attributed to Stuart. The appeal was upheld, with the Durrs awarded R772,845.50, along with interest and costs. The case underscores the importance of maintaining high professional standards in financial advisory roles, especially for institutions that present themselves as expert entities in the field.
