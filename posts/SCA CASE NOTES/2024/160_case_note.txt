Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note: Kgoshi Ngoako Isaac Lebogo and Another v Headman Matome Kobe and Others (1204/2021)

## F – Facts

The matter concerns a dispute regarding traditional leadership within the **Bahananwa Traditional Community** in Limpopo Province, South Africa. The first appellant, **Kgoshi Ngoako Isaac Lebogo**, is a recognized senior traditional leader of the Bahananwa Community, while the second appellant, the **Bahananwa Traditional Council**, was established under the **Limpopo Traditional Leadership and Institutions Act 6 of 2005** (the Limpopo Act). 

The first to thirteenth respondents, referred to as the headmen and headwomen, were removed from their recognized positions by the Premier of Limpopo on **29 July 2013**, following a recommendation from the Traditional Council. This decision stemmed from internal factional disputes within the community, notably between supporters of Kgoshi Ngoako and those of the previous leader, **Mr. Tlabo Joseph Lebogo**. The Premier was empowered to issue and withdraw certificates of recognition under the Limpopo Act ([paras 1-5]).

The headmen/women respondents filed a rule 53 application in February 2019, nearly six years after their removal, to review and set aside the Premier's decision and sought reinstatement with full pay and benefits ([paras 9-10]). The High Court ruled against granting condonation for the late filing due to undue delay, which prompted an appeal ([paras 11-12]).

## I – Issues

The case presented two primary legal issues ([paras 15-16]):
1. Whether the provisions of **Section 21** of the **Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003** (the Framework Act) required the headmen/women to exhaust internal remedies before bringing their review application.
2. Whether the late filing of the review application necessitated condonation and, if so, whether it should have been granted by the court.

## R – Rules

### Legal Framework

- **Limpopo Traditional Leadership and Institutions Act 6 of 2005**: This Act governs traditional leadership structures in Limpopo, defining roles, recognition processes, and grounds for removal ([paras 6-8]).
- **Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003**: Section 21 obligates parties to attempt internal resolutions for disputes arising from customary law before resorting to the courts ([para 16]).
- **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: Section 7 specifies timelines for judicial review applications, stating that any such application must be instituted within **180 days**. Delays beyond this period are deemed unreasonable unless condoned ([paras 22-24]).

### Relevant Case Law
- The court cited **Tshivhulana Royal Family v Netshivhulana**, highlighting the distinction between disputes over administrative actions involving the Premier and traditional communities, asserting the Premier's concurrent roles as a party and a resolver are contradictory ([para 18]).
- **S v Mercer**: This case outlines the criteria for considering condonation applications, stressing the interests of justice ([para 20]) and the necessity for a full explanation regarding delays ([para 21]).

## A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal addressed the applicability of the Framework Act and found that the full court had erred in its application of **Section 21**. The dispute arose from the Premier’s decision, and thus the headmen/women were not required to pursue internal remedies since the Premier was directly involved as the decision-making authority ([para 19]). This established the basis that a condonation application was indeed necessary due to the significant delay in filing the review, as the action had not been executed within the statutory period set by PAJA.

The Court meticulously analyzed the headmen/women’s explanation for delay, which primarily claimed they were unaware of the full implications of their removal until **June 2015**, and later sought to resolve the issue through various internal discussions. However, the Court found that a lack of timely legal action following the notice of removal undermined their argument, and the explanation for the delay was not reasonable given the substantial delays over nearly six years ([paras 24-30]).

Additional scrutiny indicated that accepting such a prolonged delay would undermine judicial efficiency, legal certainty, and the finality of administrative actions ([paras 34-36]). The Court concluded that undue delay must be firmly addressed and could not simply be overlooked under sympathetic grounds ([para 37]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the appeal by the appellants was upheld, and the order of the full court was set aside. The Court asserted that the full court had incorrectly determined the necessity of a condonation application and had failed to account for significant legal precedents ([para 40]). 

The implications of this ruling clarify the procedural expectations for traditional leadership disputes under South African law, specifically concerning the necessity to exhaust internal remedies and the stringent requirements for condonation of delayed review applications. The decision emphasizes the court’s commitment to maintaining the integrity of administrative processes and ensuring rapid resolution of disputes to uphold the rule of law within traditional communities.

Namely, the Court concluded that:
1. The review application was indeed brought out of time, violating PAJA requirements.
2. There were no reasonable prospects of success warranting the grant of condonation.
3. The interests of justice did not favor granting condonation due to the long, unexplained delay ([paras 39-40]). 

The ruling reinforces the necessity for traditional leaders and their councils to adhere strictly to the procedural mandates outlined in South African statutory law to avoid disputes escalating into protracted legal battles.
