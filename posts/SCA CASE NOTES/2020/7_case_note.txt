# FIRAC Case Note for Aquarius Platinum (SA) (Pty) Ltd v Bonene & Others (1177/2018)

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History [paras 1–6]

The case revolves around an appeal from the Land Claims Court (LCC) regarding the eviction of 167 occupiers (respondents) from properties owned by Mr. Otto Johan Wenhold on the Kroondal farm in Rustenburg, North West Province. The respondents were employed by Murray & Roberts Cementation (Pty) Ltd (MRC) and resided in hostels on the farm. After their dismissals due to a 2009 unprotected strike, Aquarius Platinum (South Africa) (Pty) Ltd (the appellant) contracted MRC for mining operations at the site but later became the employer of the respondents.

The initial eviction order was granted by the North West Division of the High Court on September 8, 2009. However, this order was overturned by the LCC on September 20, 2009, restoring the respondents’ rights under the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA). Following protracted legal disputes over employment termination, notices for eviction were served on the occupiers in early 2015 and June 2016, leading to the eventual eviction application launched by the appellant in September 2016.

The LCC ruled against the appellants, asserting that merely terminating employment does not automatically nullify the right of residence under ESTA. The court highlighted the necessity of adherence to the two-step eviction process outlined in ESTA, which requires first the termination of the right of residence followed by notice of intended eviction.

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions [paras 6–8]

The primary legal issues in this case included:

1. Whether the appellant properly followed the procedures required by ESTA before evicting the occupiers, specifically the prerequisites for terminating an occupier's right of residence.
2. The interpretation of the statutory framework concerning the eviction application, notably the interplay between employment termination and the right of residence pursuant to sections 8 and 9 of ESTA.

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon [paras 9–14]

The court referenced key provisions of ESTA, particularly:

- **Section 8**: This section outlines the rules for terminating the right of residence, stating it must be on lawful grounds and just and equitable.
- **Section 9**: This section mandates that eviction orders can only be issued post-termination of the right to reside, requiring that written notice be provided at least two months before the eviction application.

The court also cited established precedents, including *Mkangeli and Others v Joubert and Others* [2002] ZASCA 13, which stressed the need for proper termination of rights of residence, and *Snyders and Others v De Jager and Others* [2016] ZACC 55, which reiterated the need for the termination to be just and equitable. Furthermore, the court highlighted the necessity of clear communication to occupiers regarding their rights, as articulated in previous rulings.

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law [paras 7–16]

In its decision, the LCC emphasized the necessity of a two-step eviction process under ESTA. The court articulated that:

- **Stage One** requires the owner to issue a specific notice terminating the right of residence under section 8. The court concluded that the appellant failed to properly terminate the respondents' rights, as it equated employment termination with residency rights, a fundamental misunderstanding of the law.
- **Stage Two** necessitates the provision of written notice of eviction under section 9(2). The court found that eviction notices were issued to some respondents while they were still technically employed or before their residency rights were formally terminated, rendering those notices ineffective.

The appellant's reliance on the termination of employment to assert the termination of occupancy was legally unfounded, as section 8 requires substantiated proof that the right of residence has been terminated correctly and justly. The court held that a mere dismissal from employment does not inherently invalidate a person’s right to reside, particularly when legislation expressly safeguards these rights.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications [paras 17–18]

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appellant’s appeal, affirming the LCC's ruling. The court's decision rested on the clear assertion that the appellant did not comply with the necessary legal requirements for eviction as established under ESTA. 

The court reinforced the necessity for strict adherence to both the procedural and substantive fairness required in establishing a termination of the right of residence, emphasizing protection for vulnerable occupiers. This ruling holds significant implications for future eviction cases under ESTA, underscoring the importance of understanding the interplay between employment related rights and residential occupancy rights.

The court's ruling serves as a firm reminder of the due process obligations imposed on property owners seeking eviction, ensuring that legal protections for occupiers are adequately respected and enforced in accordance with statutory provisions.
