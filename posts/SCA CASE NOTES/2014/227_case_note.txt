# Case Note: Van Deventer v Ivory Sun Trading 77 (Pty) Ltd (593/13) [2014] ZASCA 169

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–11])

In *Van Deventer v Ivory Sun Trading 77 (Pty) Ltd*, the appellant, Christoffel Wessel Jacobus van Deventer, contested the lower court's ruling regarding his right of pre-emption concerning a farm (Dartmouth) bequeathed to his brother, Johannes, in their parents' will. This right was established via a testamentary provision stating that should Johannes decide to sell the farm after their mother's death, Christoffel held the first option to purchase at the Land Bank’s valuation, exercisable within 60 days after an offer was made ([para 5]).

The sequence of events commenced in 2003 when Johannes granted a written option to Dr. Cronjé to buy the farm for R2,4 million. Christoffel, aware of this option, insisted on his pre-emption right, asserting it required a formal offer at the Land Bank valuation. Following their mother's death in 2004, Johannes communicated to Christoffel that he no longer wished to sell, leading to further compromises and disputes ([paras 6–9]). In March 2007, the respondent, as Cronjé's nominee, exercised the option to purchase without formally notifying Christoffel, which eventually led to Christoffel's appeal seeking acknowledgment of his binding right ([paras 10–11]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 12–12])

The appellant's primary issue was whether his right of pre-emption had prescribed prior to his exercise of that right. Specifically, the court was tasked with determining:

1. The interpretation of the pre-emption clause in the will and its requirements ([para 12]).
2. Whether the obligation for Johannes to offer the farm to Christoffel constituted a debt susceptible to prescription, and if so, whether it had indeed prescribed ([para 12]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 13–41])

The appellant's argument relied on the interpretation canon encapsulated in *Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality*, which emphasized the contextual reading of contractual provisions ([para 14]). The court considered the *Prescription Act 68 of 1969*, specifically sections 11(d) and 12(1), which detail the commencement and conditions under which prescription runs ([para 18]).

The appellant also referenced *Umgeni Water v Mshengu* to elucidate the concept of “debt due” emphasizing that for a debt—like the offer under contention—to be classified as due, all necessary conditions to enforce that right must exist ([para 21]).

Further, the court evaluated the effectiveness of possible remedies that could an interrupt prescription, such as interdicts or declaratory orders, referencing historical precedents like *Cape Town Municipality & another v Allianz Insurance Co Ltd*, which clarified the operative characteristics of legal processes that can obstruct the running of prescription ([paras 30–34]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 12–41])

In adjudicating the interpretation of the right of pre-emption, the court aligned its analysis with established principles of contractual interpretation. It refrained from accepting the respondent's claim that Christoffel was entitled to enforce his right merely upon Johannes’ indication of intention to sell, arguing that written notice was a prerequisite ([paras 13-14]). 

The court confirmed that because the right was registered against the title deed, it acquired a reflexive characteristic, impacting third parties by evidencing Christoffel's affirmative claim ([paras 15-16]). 

In addressing prescription, the court concluded that the obligation for Johannes to offer the farm was indeed a debt as defined under the Prescription Act, though it questioned the initiation of prescription. The judge postulated that prescription commenced only upon the grant of a formal option to Christoffel, which had not taken place. Consequently, there was no “complete cause of action” available for Christoffel to claim specific performance because Johannes failed to extend the required written offer ([paras 22–39]).

Moreover, the arguments surrounding waiver and estoppel were dismissed. Christoffel’s persistent insistence on a written offer negated any notion of having waived his right or having acted in a way that could be construed as an abandonment of his interests ([paras 41–47]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 47–48])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately ruled in favor of Christoffel, finding that his right of pre-emption had not prescribed due to the absence of a formal offer by Johannes. As such, the court upheld the appeal, setting aside the lower court's orders and emphasizing that any right derived from a testamentary disposition not only demands clariy but also protection from prescription until the obligations attendant thereupon are duly activated ([paras 47–48]).

This case reinforces the necessity for formal compliance in matters involving pre-emption rights, particularly the imperative for an offer to be legally valid for the subsequent enforcement of such rights. The ruling clarifies the procedural intricacies surrounding claims of prescription in the context of estate planning and property law, thereby contributing significantly to the jurisprudence on testamentary dispositions and personal rights attached thereto.
