Minister of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries v NSPCA [2015] ZACC 27

Constitutional Court 2015 [2015] ZACC 27

F – Facts

The Constitutional Court of South Africa heard the application by the Minister of Agriculture, Forestry and Fisheries (hereafter referred to as "the Minister") seeking an extension of the suspension of an order of invalidity that had been made on July 11, 2013, regarding sections 2 and 3 of the Performing Animals Protection Act 24 of 1935 (PAP Act) ([1]). This application was filed as urgent on July 6, 2015, only a week before the suspension was set to lapse on July 12, 2015. The Court had previously extended the suspension to August 28, 2015, to provide a holding measure while processing the application ([1]).

In November 2014, it became clear that the constitutional defect in the PAP Act identified by the Court would not be rectified by the initial deadline of January 10, 2015, leading to a request for a six-month extension ([3],[4]). The Court granted this request on November 27, 2014, extending the suspension until July 12, 2015 ([4]).

Despite the Cabinet's approval of a Bill intended to amend the PAP Act, there were delays inherent in the legislative process ([5],[6]). Between November 2014 to the filing of the latest application, various steps were undertaken, yet there was a realisation that legislative amendments required additional time, particularly due to the Portfolio Committee's decision to consider further amendments ([7],[8]).

I – Issues

The key legal question before the Court was whether it was just and equitable to extend the suspension of the order of constitutional invalidity concerning the PAP Act ([9],[12]). This consideration involved assessing the reasons for the delay in amending the Act and the potential consequences that could arise if the extension were denied ([11]).

R – Rules

The principles guiding the extension of the suspension of orders of invalidity are well-established in South African jurisprudence. The Court has previously ruled that it must consider factors such as:

  1. Rationale for Previous Deadlines: An applicant's explanation for why the timeframe originally established was insufficient ([9]).
  2. Diligence of the Applicant: Whether the applicant acted promptly and effectively to remedy the constitutional defect within the allocated time ([9]).
  3. Public Prejudice: The implications for the public and governmental operations if the extension were denied ([11]).

The approach the Court takes must uphold the overarching principles of justice and equity ([10]).

A – Application

In analyzing the current application, Zondo J noted the urgency with which the Minister sought the extension, though criticized the timing of its filing, coming only a week before the lapse of the existing suspension ([1],[4]). Despite this, the lack of opposition from the NSPCA indicated that the Minister's assertions regarding the need for more time were fundamentally unchallenged ([1]).

The Court examined the status of the Bill and the legislative processes ongoing at the time of the application, acknowledging the complexities involved in getting the Bill through Parliament ([5],[6],[8]). Zondo J emphasized that while the legislative journey involved challenges, the potential consequences of allowing the order of invalidity to take effect would be dire, leading to a "workable" PAP Act being rendered ineffective ([11],[12]).

The lack of mechanisms for issuing or renewing licenses under the PAP Act should it lapse was a crucial consideration; it would disrupt businesses relying on animal licensing and harm the public seeking to comply with the law ([11]).

The Court found that, as of the current situation, the applicant had acted sufficiently diligently since the last extension and that further time was indeed warranted to allow the legislative process to unfold ([10],[12]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Court concluded that it was just and equitable to extend the suspension of the order of constitutional invalidity by an additional 12 months from August 28, 2015, to August 27, 2016. The rationale was based on ensuring continuity and functionality in legislative governance concerning animal welfare protections and preventing public harm due to legal ambiguities within the PAP Act ([14]).

The holding underscores the Court's commitment to balancing legal mandates with practical governance considerations, reaffirming the importance of legislative processes while maintaining protections until such amendments can be duly processed. The ratio decidendi indicates a broader constitutional principle: the necessity for legislative bodies to respond to judicial findings of unconstitutionality within a reasonable timeframe while accounting for the complexities inherent in legislative procedures.
