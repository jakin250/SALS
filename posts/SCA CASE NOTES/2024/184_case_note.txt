Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# Case Note: MEC for Health, Gauteng v Dr Regan Solomons (1089/2023) [2024] ZASCA 184

### F – Facts
In this case, the MEC for Health, Gauteng Province (the MEC), issued a **subpoena duces tecum** on 21 May 2021 against Dr Regan Solomons, requiring him to produce certain documentation and tape recordings for a R29 million medical negligence claim (paras [1]–[2]). Dr Solomons, at the time, was a professor at Stellenbosch University and had co-authored a medical article referencing various medicolegal cases. The subpoena sought specific case-related documents and patient data (paras [2]–[3]).

Dr Solomons’ legal advisor countered the subpoena by asserting that the requested information was privileged due to confidentiality and legal obligations towards patient information, seeking the withdrawal of the subpoena from the MEC (paras [3]–[4]). Following this objection, the MEC initiated a high court application, claiming no lawful privilege could be applied by Solomons regarding the requested documents (paras [4]–[5]).

Key to the dispute was the claim that Solomons did not possess the documents, which was confirmed in his affidavit. This assertion rendered the MEC’s application moot, as the requested documentation could not be produced (paras [9]–[12]). The high court ultimately dismissed the MEC’s application, ordering her to pay the costs (paras [16]–[16]). The MEC later sought to appeal this decision, culminating in this matter being presented to the Supreme Court of Appeal.

### I – Issues
The primary legal questions before the Supreme Court of Appeal concerned whether a justiciable dispute remained between the parties when the application was heard, particularly given that Solomons had asserted he did not possess the requested documentation, rendering the MEC's claim moot (paras [21]–[22]). Secondary issues involved the applicability of privilege concerning patient information and whether the MEC's application violated established legal principles governing the confidentiality of patient data (paras [24]–[30]).

### R – Rules
The court relied on several key legal principles. It addressed the **Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013**, particularly s 36, which articulates the grounds for a "just excuse" for non-compliance in legal proceedings (para [15]). The court also referenced previous jurisprudence, notably *Minister of Justice and Correctional Services and Others v Estate Late James Stransham-Ford and Others* [2016] ZASCA 197, wherein it was established that courts do not have the jurisdiction to issue orders regarding causes of action that no longer exist at the time of judgment (paras [28]–[29]).

Additionally, the court considered the **National Health Care Act 61 of 2003**, especially sections concerned with patient confidentiality, which stipulate that patient information cannot be disclosed without consent or a court order (paras [6]–[10]).

### A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal critically evaluated whether the original high court had jurisdiction to entertain the MEC's application. It found that since Solomons did not possess the requested documents, the foundational basis for the MEC's application had evaporated, thereby extinguishing any cause of action (paras [30]–[33]). The importance of this finding cannot be overstated: the court underscored that dispensations addressing broader societal implications do not suffice if a case lacks jurisdictional grounding.

The court concluded that the determinations made by the lower courts regarding the merits of the application and the associated privilege were ultimately moot, given that they were unnecessary to address in light of the jurisdictional issues (paras [31]–[34]). Thus, the rulings regarding declaratory relief and costs were deemed inappropriate, as they were predicated on a fundamentally flawed premise: the existence of a live dispute.

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the MEC's appeal, underscoring that the high court had erred in proceeding with the matter given that there was no active dispute to adjudicate. The findings on the merits from both the court of first instance and the full court were deemed to hold no precedential value due to a lack of jurisdiction (paras [35]–[38]). Consequently, the court ordered that the MEC, as the unsuccessful party in the appeal, should bear the costs of the proceedings.

This judgment reinforces essential principles regarding both the necessity of a justiciable issue in legal proceedings and the paramountcy of patient confidentiality, reaffirming that legal procedures must align with established statutory requirements to ensure lawful governance in health-related legal matters.
