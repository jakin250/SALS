Constitutional Court

Mazibuko v Sisulu & Others

The motion followed Rule 98(1)(a) of the National Assembly Rules, which was tabled for consideration on November 13, 2012 ([7]–[8]).

Constitutional Court 2013 [2013] ZACC 28 ### F - Facts A dispute arose over the schedu

Constitutional Court of South Africa
Case No: CCT 115/12
[2013] ZACC 28

F - Facts

A dispute arose over the scheduling of a motion of no confidence in the President within the National Assembly, initiated by Ms. Lindiwe Mazibuko, the Leader of the Opposition, representing various minority parties ([1]–[2]). The motion was based on concerns regarding the President's leadership, specifically noting issues such as corruption and the politicization of the justice system ([7]–[8]).

The motion followed Rule 98(1)(a) of the National Assembly Rules, which was tabled for consideration on November 13, 2012 ([7]–[8]). However, the Chief Whip's Forum, responsible for scheduling motions, was unable to reach a consensus on the motion and subsequently referred it to the Programme Committee for further consideration ([9]–[10]). On November 15, 2012, the Programme Committee also failed to reach a consensus, leading to the motion not being scheduled for debate ([10]–[12]).

On November 16, 2012, Mazibuko initiated urgent proceedings in the Western Cape High Court seeking an order compelling the Speaker of the Assembly to schedule the motion for debate prior to November 22, 2012, the last sitting day of the Assembly for the year. The High Court dismissed her application, determining that the Speaker lacked the power to schedule the motion unilaterally ([14]–[18]).

I - Issues

The primary issues before the Constitutional Court were:

  1. Whether the Speaker had the authority to schedule a motion of no confidence in the President.
  2. The constitutionality of the National Assembly Rules regarding the scheduling of such motions, specifically their compatibility with Section 102(2) of the Constitution.
  3. Whether Parliament had fulfilled its constitutional obligation under Section 167(4)(e) by failing to provide a mechanism for scheduling the motion ([3]–[4]).

R - Rules

The pertinent legal rules include:

  1. Section 102(2) of the Constitution: Provides that if a motion of no confidence is passed by a majority of the National Assembly, the President and Cabinet must resign.
  2. Sections 57(1) and 167(4): Confer the Assembly the authority to regulate its internal arrangements and assign exclusive jurisdiction to the Constitutional Court for certain constitutional obligations ([4]–[6]).
  3. Rule 2(1) of the National Assembly Rules: Allows the Speaker to make rulings in cases where the Rules do not provide guidance ([26]).

A - Application

The Court highlighted that the High Court correctly dismissed the application for a mandamus against the Speaker. It recognized that while the right to introduce a motion exists, the internal arrangements and procedures of Parliament, including scheduling, rest with the Assembly ([24]–[29]).

The Court assessed whether the Speaker had residual authority under Rule 2(1) to break the deadlock in the absence of consensus, ultimately agreeing with the High Court’s assessment. The Supreme Court emphasized that the Rules are intended to facilitate orderly procedures and that an interpretation allowing unilateral Speaker actions during a deadlock would undermine the collective decision-making ethos of Parliament ([28]–[30]).

Regarding the question of urgency, the Court agreed that motions of no confidence are significant for accountability within the government; however, it concluded that the Assembly must be allowed to manage its schedule and procedures, and not every motion warrants immediate attention ([66]).

The Court further determined that a defect in Rule provisions was inappropriate for attributing a constitutional failure to the Assembly. The deadlock arose from a misapplication of procedures by the Programme Committee, not from the absence of the necessary Rules ([156]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately upheld the High Court's dismissal of the application, stating that there was no basis for the claim that the Rules were inconsistent with the Constitution. It declared that while the existing framework needed clarity, it did not deny the procedural rights of Assembly members and that the applicant had not sufficiently demonstrated the urgency or necessity for judicial intervention ([72]–[80]).

The Court additionally suspended the declaration of invalidity for six months, allowing for potential amendments to the Rules by the National Assembly, illustrating the need for parliamentary reform while maintaining institutional respect for legislative processes ([72]).

Implications

This ruling clarifies the legislative powers vested in the National Assembly, emphasizing the importance of the separation of powers principle while advocating for a functional parliamentary process to ensure accountability. It serves as a crucial precedent illustrating the judiciary's approach to conflicts between legislative autonomy and constitutional directives.
