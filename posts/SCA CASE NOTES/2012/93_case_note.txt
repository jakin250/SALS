## FIRAC Case Note: Minister of Minerals and Energy v Agri South Africa (CALS amicus curiae) (458/11) [2012] ZASCA 93

### F – Facts
The case arises from an appeal by the **Minister of Minerals and Energy** against a decision of the North Gauteng High Court, Pretoria, which awarded compensation to **Agri South Africa** (Agri SA) for the alleged expropriation of coal rights under the **Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002** (MPRDA) ([paras 1-2]). 

Prior to the MPRDA's enactment on 1 May 2004, **Sebenza Mining (Pty) Ltd**, which had taken a cession of coal rights, lodged a compensation claim contending that the MPRDA expropriated its rights without compensation. This claim was rejected by the State, leading to the cession of the claim to Agri SA for the purpose of litigation. Agri SA argued that the MPRDA expropriated all mineral rights existing prior to its enactment, failing which it required compensation under item 12(1) of Schedule II of the MPRDA ([paras 1-2]).

The trial court, led by Du Plessis J, found in favor of Agri SA, awarding it R750,000 in compensation ([paras 2]).

### I – Issues
The central issue is whether the MPRDA effected an expropriation of all mineral rights existing prior to its implementation and whether such an expropriation necessitates compensation under the constitution ([paras 6-11]). Specifically, the court needed to ascertain:
1. What constitutes an **expropriation** as per **section 25(2)** of the Constitution?
2. What were the rights enjoyed by holders of mineral rights prior to the MPRDA?
3. Were these rights expropriated under the MPRDA's provisions? ([paras 11-12])

### R – Rules
**1. Expropriation Definition:** The Constitution stipulates (Section 25(2)) that property can only be expropriated for public purposes in a manner that is not arbitrary and must be compensated. The distinction between deprivation and expropriation is crucial ([para 12]).

**2. Mineral Rights Structure:** Historically, South African mineral rights evolved through various legislative frameworks, and prior to the MPRDA, these rights were often described as common law rights and often vested in landowners ([paras 25-68]).

**3. MPRDA Analysis:** Sections 2 and 3 of the MPRDA articulate state custodianship over mineral resources, aiming to equitable access while shifting the ownership framework from private to state office ([paras 8-9]).

### A – Application
The court employed a comparative analysis of the rights holders before and after the MPRDA. It established that while the MPRDA altered the framework governing mineral rights, fundamentally shifting control to the Minister, it did not constitute an outright expropriation as claimed by Agri SA ([paras 81-82]). 

1. **Nature of Mineral Rights:** The court concluded that the rights detailed in the MPRDA can be considered statutory rather than purely common law. Hence, the notion of deprivation did not hold since what were formerly recognized as mineral rights were in fact privileges granted and regulated by legislation ([paras 69-84]).
  
2. **Transitional Provisions:** The MPRDA's transitional provisions allowed for the conversion of "old order rights" into new statutory rights under the MPRDA, which meant that existing rights were retained and not wholly abolished or expropriated ([paras 89-90]).

3. **Compensation Argument:** Agri SA's argument regarding compensation for a blanket expropriation was rejected because the MPRDA allowed for the continued exercise of mining rights through the conversion process, ultimately maintaining continuity rather than obliterating previous rights ([paras 91-92]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the MPRDA did not expropriate existing mineral rights in a manner requiring compensation under Section 25(2) of the Constitution. The court emphasized the State's longstanding control over mineral rights, affirming that while the new Act indeed modified the rights framework, it did not constitute an unlawful deprivation, thus ruling in favor of the Minister ([paras 100-101]).

The final order was that:
1. The appeal was upheld with costs, including those for employing two counsel.
2. The trial court’s judgment was set aside, dismissing Agri SA’s claim for compensation entirely, while ordering the Minister to pay Agri SA's wasted costs related to unnecessary amendments made during proceedings ([paras 2-101]).
