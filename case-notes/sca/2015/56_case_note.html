Supreme Court of Appeal

Greenwood v S (20075/14) [2015] ZASCA 56

After his application for leave to appeal against conviction was denied, he successfully obtained leave to appeal against the sentence ([para 2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 [2015] ZASCA 56

F – Facts

This case involves an appeal by Colin Allan Greenwood against a refusal to grant leave to appeal his conviction for serious sexual offenses. On 26 April 2006, Greenwood was convicted on two counts of indecent assault, two counts of rape, and three counts of sexual assault, following a trial in the regional magistrate’s court (East London) ([para 1]). He was sentenced to 10 years’ imprisonment, with 4 years suspended for 5 years under certain conditions.

After his application for leave to appeal against conviction was denied, he successfully obtained leave to appeal against the sentence ([para 2]). The matter was subsequently brought before the Eastern Cape High Court but ultimately escalated to the Supreme Court of Appeal after further petitions were rejected ([para 2]).

The core question before the Supreme Court was whether the High Court erred in dismissing the appeal for leave on the basis of the appellant’s alleged reasonable prospects of success on appeal ([para 3]).

I – Issues

The central legal issue addressed by the Supreme Court was whether the appellant had established reasonable prospects of success in appealing against his conviction ([paras 3–4]). This hinged on an evaluation of the trial court's decisions, specifically regarding:

  1. The application of cautionary rules regarding the testimony of minors.
  2. Potential contradictions within the testimonies of the complainants.
  3. The credibility of witnesses utilized by the trial court.
  4. The overall integrity of the evidence presented during the trial ([para 5]).

R – Rules

The Supreme Court employed legal tests established in precedent cases to evaluate the appellant's claims. The court referenced S v Khoasasa 2003 (1) SACR 123 (SCA) and S v Smith 2012 (1) SACR 567 (SCA) regarding the criteria for adjudicating applications for leave to appeal. The critical standard emphasized was whether there was a reasonable prospect that a court of appeal could reach a different conclusion from the trial court ([para 3]).

Plasket AJA in S v Smith defined the standard of "reasonable prospects of success" as requiring a "dispassionate decision, based on the facts and the law" ([para 4]). Furthermore, it necessitated the appellant demonstrating that the prospects of success were realistic and not merely a possibility ([para 4]).

A – Application

The court systematically assessed the appellant's arguments regarding misdirections made by the trial court, specifically:

  1. Cautionary Rules: The appellant asserted that the trial court failed to adequately apply the cautionary rules applicable to testimony given by minors. The court recognized that while caution is warranted in such cases, it must balance this with common sense, as indicated in the ruling from S v Artman ([para 8]).
  2. Contradictions in Testimony: The appellant highlighted contradictions between the complainants' testimonies and their police statements. The court acknowledged that some degree of contradiction was expected given the age of the complainants and the nature of the events’ timelines. Crucially, the court strove to view the evidence holistically rather than in isolation, which also appeared crucial in corroborating the complainants’ claims (@ para 9).
  3. Witness Credibility: Greenwood contended that the trial court erred in evaluating witness credibility, particularly that of clinical psychologist Karen Andrews and medical doctor Bomkazi Majeke. The court noted that the magistrate’s holistic approach did not lend credence to claims of collusion between the young complainants, as their separate reports corroborated each other ([paras 5-7]).

Ultimately, the Supreme Court was persuaded that the appellant failed to provide substantive grounds for his appeal. The alleged misdirections were not deemed sufficient to establish a probability of success on appeal, thereby failing to meet the established legal standard ([para 10]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal, with Shongwe JA writing the judgment, concluded that the appeal must be dismissed. The appellant did not satisfy the requisite burden of demonstrating reasonable prospects of success on appeal, and the thoroughness of the trial court's assessment left no alternative grounds warranting a further appeal ([paras 10–11]).

The implications of this judgment underscore the judiciary's commitment to upholding the integrity of convictions in sexual offense cases, particularly involving minors, and reiterate the necessity for appellants to present compelling reasons that go beyond mere speculation of success in their appeals.
