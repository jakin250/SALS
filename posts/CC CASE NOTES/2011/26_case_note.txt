Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

### FIRAC Case Note: Falk v National Director of Public Prosecutions

**Case Citation:**  
Falk v National Director of Public Prosecutions [2011] ZACC 26; CCT 95/10

---

**F – Facts**  
The applicants, Alexander Gerhard Falk and Falk Real Estate SA (Pty) Ltd, sought to challenge a restraint order issued against Mr. Falk, a German businessman, in relation to allegations of financial misconduct in Germany. The restraint order was issued by a German court and subsequently registered in South Africa under the International Co-operation in Criminal Matters Act 75 of 1996 (ICCMA) on 13 September 2004. Following this, the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) obtained an interdictory order in the High Court, preventing the applicants from dealing with their assets in South Africa. The applicants contested both the registration of the German restraint order and the validity of the interdictory order, arguing that the prerequisite conditions stipulated under the relevant legislation had not been met ([paras 2–30]), and asserting that the criminal proceedings against Mr. Falk had concluded in Germany, as a confiscation order had not been granted ([paras 21–30]).

Despite the applicants' arguments, the courts below found that the registration of the German order and the interdictory order were lawful. The High Court emphasized that the restraint order should remain in force pending the resolution of appeal processes in Germany ([para 28]). The Supreme Court of Appeal maintained this position, dismissing the appeal by the applicants ([paras 32–38]).

---

**I – Issues**  
The principal issues for determination were:
1. Should the registration of the German restraint order be set aside? ([para 6(a)]).
2. Should the interdictory order be rescinded? ([para 6(b)]).
3. What is the relationship between ICCMA and the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (POCA), particularly concerning the procedures for restraint orders? ([para 7]).
4. Whether any constitutional issues arose, justifying the court’s jurisdiction to entertain the appeal pursuant to section 167(3)(b) of the Constitution ([paras 47–48]).

---

**R – Rules**  
Two significant statutes govern the issues at hand:
1. **Prevention of Organised Crime Act (POCA)**: Established measures for the confiscation of proceeds from crime, outlining processes for the issuance and rescission of restraint orders ([para 10–14]). Specific sections (notably 26(1) and 26(10)) address the procedures for restraint orders and their rescission when undue hardship is demonstrable ([para 14]) and the requirements for court objections to such orders ([para 18]).
   
2. **International Co-operation in Criminal Matters Act (ICCMA)**: Provides for the registration and enforcement of foreign restraint orders, detailing under what conditions registration may be set aside ([para 16]). The applicable provisions (particularly section 24 and section 26) clarify the necessity for maintaining the interests of justice ([para 26]).

The interpretation of these statutes must align with constitutional principles as outlined in sections 25(1) (property rights) and 39(2) (interpretation to promote the Bill of Rights) of the Constitution ([para 20]).

---

**A – Application**  
The court evaluated the dual statutes, asserting that ICCMA governed the registration of the German restraint order, which did not convert it into a formal South African court order ([paras 54–57]). The argument presented by the applicants—that the enforcement of the order was contrary to the interests of justice—was rejected based on the risk of asset dissipation and ongoing proceedings in Germany, which indicated that the registration should remain intact during the appeal ([para 64]).

The court further held that the interdictory order, which was granted as an ancillary order under POCA, was indeed valid and did not contravene the procedural directives set forth in either statute. The court ruled that a contextual interpretation of section 26(8) establishes that courts can grant ancillary orders following the registration of foreign restraint orders without being confined to the sequence established for domestic orders ([paras 79–86]).

---

**C – Conclusion**  
The Constitutional Court ultimately upheld the decisions of the lower courts, affirming both the validity of the registration of the foreign restraint order and the legality of the interdictory order. The court found that the applicants had not successfully demonstrated that the enforcement of the registered German order was contrary to the interests of justice, nor did the conclusion of their criminal proceedings in Germany extinguish the necessity for the restraint ([paras 72–94]).  

Thus, the appeal was dismissed, but given that significant constitutional issues were raised throughout the proceedings, each party was directed to bear its own costs ([paras 96–97]). This ruling reinforces the framework of international cooperation in criminal matters while upholding the constitutional protections against arbitrary deprivation of property.
