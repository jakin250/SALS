Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: Minister of Defence and Military Veterans v Maswanganyi (739/18) [2019] ZASCA 86

## F – Facts

The case arises from an appeal regarding the interpretation of **s 59(1)(d)** of the **Defence Act 42 of 2002** (the Defence Act) concerning the automatic termination of the respondent, Mr. **Mozamane Teapson Maswanganyi's** service within the **South African National Defence Force** (SANDF). The respondent was a permanent member of the Regular Force until his membership was terminated following his conviction and life imprisonment for rape on **18 July 2014**. He was released after his conviction and sentence were set aside on **13 February 2015**. Following his release, the respondent sought reinstatement in the SANDF, claiming that the termination of his service should be reversible given the nullification of his conviction.

The appellants, the **Minister of Defence and Military Veterans**, the **Chief of the SANDF**, and the **Secretary for Defence**, argued that the termination of Maswanganyi's service was automatic upon sentencing and that reinstatement could not occur without a formal decision being made to that effect. The **Gauteng Division of the High Court** ruled in favor of Maswanganyi, leading to this appeal ([paras 1-3]).

## I – Issues

The case presented two primary legal questions:
1. Whether **s 59(1)(d)** of the Defence Act operates **ex lege** (automatically), or whether a decision by the appellants was necessary to give effect to the termination of service.
2. Whether the setting aside of Maswanganyi's conviction necessitated an automatic reinstatement of his service status within the SANDF ([paras 1-2]).

## R – Rules

The relevant statutory provisions include:
- **Section 59(1)(d)** of the Defence Act, which stipulates that a member's service is terminated upon conviction and sentencing by a competent court. It states: "The service of a member of the Regular Force is terminated... if he or she is sentenced to a term of imprisonment by a competent civilian court without the option of a fine..." ([para 7]).
- **Section 42(1)** of the **Military Discipline Supplementary Measures Act 16 of 1999 (MDSMA)**, which allows for suspension from duty if it is in the interest of good governance or justice, specifically during a trial or appeal ([para 7]).

These provisions raise questions about the nature of the termination of service and the requirements for a reinstatement when initial termination occurs automatically versus via discretionary means ([paras 6-11]).

## A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) reviewed the High Court’s interpretation of the statutory provisions and identified significant errors. The SCA concluded that **s 59(1)(d)** operates **ex lege**, meaning the termination of Maswanganyi’s service was mandated by law upon his conviction. This interpretation was supported by the plain meaning of the statutory language and the context within which these provisions exist ([paras 11-15]).

Regarding the issue of reinstatement following the setting aside of the conviction, the Court found that there was no similar provision for automatic reinstatement under **s 59(1)**, unlike the provisions stipulated in **s 59(3)**, which deals explicitly with circumstances allowing for reinstatement after a member has absented themselves without leave ([paras 14-15]). The SCA emphasized that requiring a formal decision for reinstatement runs counter to the automatic nature of termination dictated by **s 59(1)(d)**. Hence, there was no reviewable ‘decision’ made by the Chief of the SANDF to consider or set aside ([para 13]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal found in favor of the appellants, holding that the **Gauteng High Court** had erred in its interpretation of the law regarding automatic termination and reinstatement. The Court ruled that **s 59(1)(d)** operates automatically upon a conviction, affirming that no decision was required by the SANDF to effect this termination ([paras 13-16]). Consequently, since there was no provision for automatic reinstatement following the reversal of the conviction, the respondent's claim for reinstatement was dismissed. The appeal was upheld with costs, signalling a reaffirmation of the strict interpretation of statutory provisions governing military service and discipline ([para 16]). 

This decision underscores the importance of clarity in statutory language, particularly in administrative contexts involving military service, where serious implications for personnel status arise from criminal convictions.
