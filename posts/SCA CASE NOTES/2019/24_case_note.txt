Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: Minister of Police v Masina (1082/17) [2019] ZASCA 24

## FIRAC Method Case Note

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–8])
The case revolves around Mr. Mfanuka Jacob Masina, who was allegedly shot in the ankle by members of the South African Police Service (SAPS) during a community protest meeting on 16 May 2012. Following the incident, Masina initiated legal proceedings against the Minister of Police, seeking R660,000 in damages. 

The Minister submitted a defense that included two special pleas: (1) non-compliance with the procedural requirements set forth by the Institution of Legal Proceedings against Certain Organs of State Act 40 of 2002 (the Act), and (2) a claim of prescription under the Prescription Act 68 of 1969. On 19 February 2016, Mrs. Masina's attorneys applied for condonation regarding the non-compliance with the notice provisions, arguing that the Minister would not be prejudiced by this delay. The Gauteng Division of the High Court, presided over by Raulinga J, granted the application and ordered the Minister to bear the costs associated with this application. The Minister's subsequent application for leave to appeal was denied by the High Court, prompting this appeal ([paras 1–4]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 9–10])
The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the debt had prescribed before Masina initiated his claim. Central to this determination was the interpretation of sections 12(1) and 12(3) of the Prescription Act, specifically concerning when a debtor’s knowledge of the identity of the debtor and the facts giving rise to the debt must be considered ([paras 9–10]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 11–12])
The relevant law includes:
- **Prescription Act 68 of 1969**, particularly section 12, which dictates the circumstances under which prescription commences. Subsection (1) provides that prescription begins once a debt is due, while subsection (3) stipulates that a debt is not considered due until the creditor has knowledge of the identity of the debtor and the facts from which the debt arises.
- **Claasen v Bester** [2011] ZASCA 197, which affirmed that knowledge of the legal consequences related to a claim does not hinder the running of prescription.
- **MEC for Education, KZN v Shange** [2012] ZASCA 98, which discussed the implications of a minor's knowledge and understanding in a claim against an organ of state ([paras 11–15]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 13–17])
The court analyzed Masina’s claims regarding his lack of knowledge about the identity of the police officer who had shot him and his understanding of the options available to him for redress. The court found that while Masina knew he had been shot and could attribute that harm to police actions, his lack of awareness of the specific identity of the responsible officer did not delay the running of the prescriptive period. The court clarified that knowledge of who to cite in legal proceedings constitutes a procedural matter not necessary for initiating the running of prescription ([paras 11–13]).

Critically, the court contrasted Masina’s reliance on Shange, noting that his situation differed fundamentally because Masina had known the police were involved immediately following the incident. Therefore, the only delay was a result of his failure to pursue the claim, which remained unexplained up to the point of his formal instruction to attorneys in June 2014 ([paras 16–17]). His lack of legal knowledge did not serve as a valid excuse for the delay, as the court emphasized that ignorance of the law does not stop prescription from taking effect.

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 18–19])
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the High Court had erred in granting condonation for Masina’s late notice on the grounds that his debt had not been extinguished by prescription. The appeal was upheld, and the High Court’s decision was overturned, leading to a dismissal of Masina’s application for condonation with costs. 

This judgment reinforces the principle that knowledge of the identity of a debtor and the facts giving rise to a claim is sufficient to commence the prescriptive period, notwithstanding the claimant’s lack of understanding of legal procedures or potential remedies available. It underscores the importance of timely legal action and adherence to procedural requirements, emphasizing the courts' unwillingness to grant indulgences where a claimant fails to act within the statutory limits ([paras 18–19]). 

Finally, the implications of this ruling serve not only to clarify the legal standards concerning prescription and knowledge but also highlight the need for claimants to seek legal advice promptly after an incident to avoid jeopardizing their claims through undue delays.
