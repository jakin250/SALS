Case Note: Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs v British American Tobacco South Africa (Pty) Ltd and Others [2022] ZASCA 89 (14 June 2022)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 89 (14 June 2022) FIRAC Case Note --- F

Case Note: Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs v British American Tobacco South Africa (Pty) Ltd and Others [2022] ZASCA 89 (14 June 2022)

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1-5])

On 30 January 2020, the World Health Organisation declared COVID-19 a public health emergency. Subsequently, on 15 March 2020, the Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs declared a national state of disaster under the Disaster Management Act 57 of 2002. A series of regulations were promulgated, including one (Regulation 45) that prohibited the sale of tobacco and related products during the nationwide lockdown, effective from 23 March 2020.

This prohibition was unique to South Africa, diverging from practices in other countries where tobacco was classified as essential. The respondents, consisting of various stakeholders in the tobacco industry, initiated an urgent application in the Western Cape Division of the High Court in June 2020, challenging Regulation 45 on grounds of unconstitutionality for infringing on rights including dignity, bodily integrity, and freedom of trade ([paras 4-5]).

On 17 August 2020, while the application was pending, Regulation 45 was rescinded. However, the high court subsequently declared Regulation 45 inconsistent with the Constitution, prompting the present appeal by the Minister and President against this order of invalidity, while the respondents cross-appealed concerning costs ([paras 5]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 19-26])

The primary legal issues raised in this appeal include:

  1. Whether Regulation 45 imposed an unconstitutional limitation on fundamental rights enshrined in the Constitution, specifically:
  • The right to dignity as per section 10.
  • The right to privacy under section 14.
  • Bodily and psychological integrity encompassed in section 12.
  • Freedom to choose and practice a trade or profession detailed in section 22.
  • The right not to be arbitrarily deprived of property under section 25.
  1. If a limitation of those rights was established, could the appellants justify it under the limitation clause found in section 36 of the Constitution, which requires that any limitation be reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society?

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 20-31])

The court referenced several critical legal provisions and precedential cases in its analysis:

  1. Constitutional Provisions:
  • Section 10 (dignity), Section 12 (bodily integrity), Section 14 (privacy), Section 22 (freedom of trade), and Section 25 (property rights).
  • Section 36 (limitation of rights clause).
  1. Precedents:
  • Barkhuizen v Napier [2007] ZACC 5, on dignity ([para 21]).
  • Bernstein and Others v Bester N O [1996] ZACC 2, on privacy ([para 22]).
  • Soobramoney v Minister of Health, KwaZulu-Natal [1998] ZACC 17, and Dawood v Minister of Home Affairs [2000] ZACC 8, concerning the limitations placed on rights by the state ([paras 29-31]).

The court emphasized that the state must demonstrate that the limitations on rights are based on objectively ascertained facts, as reiterated in Minister of Home Affairs v NICRO [2004] ZACC 10 ([para 36]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 33-78])

The court performed a rigorous evaluation of the evidence pertaining to the justification for Regulation 45, focusing on several pivotal critiques of the Minister’s assertions:

  1. Determination of Limitation:
  • The court established that Regulation 45 did indeed limit the specified constitutional rights. The overarching objective expressed by the Minister—to reduce strain on the healthcare system by limiting tobacco sales to curb COVID-19 spread—was questioned, given the lack of compelling evidence to correlate existing smoking habits with increased COVID-19 susceptibility ([paras 41-44]).
  1. Failure of Justification:
  • The court articulated multiple failures in the appellants' justification under section 36. Notably, the Minister could not demonstrate that Regulation 45 was necessary to achieve its stated purpose. The reliance on studies asserting a direct correlation between smoking and severe COVID-19 was deemed inadequate and inconclusive ([paras 47-55]).
  1. Disproportionality:
  • The extensive limitations imposed on the respondents' rights were deemed disproportionate to the benefits claimed by the Minister ([paras 65-68]). The negative impact of the regulation on the tobacco industry, especially farmers and retailers, highlighted severe economic repercussions that were not mitigated by the anticipated public health benefits, thus undermining the maintenance of an open and democratic society ([paras 71-78]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 79-120])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal on the grounds that Regulation 45 constituted an unjustifiable limitation of numerous constitutional rights. The high court's conclusion that the Minister failed to justify the limitations under section 36 of the Constitution was upheld, reinforcing the principle that government actions must not only be lawful but also reasonable and justified in the context of constitutional rights ([para 68]).

In its final order, the court dismissed the appeal with costs and upheld the cross-appeal, ordering that costs be paid to the respondents, including expert witness expenses. The implications of this ruling extend beyond the specific case, emphasizing the necessity of substantive justification when the state imposes restrictions that infringe upon fundamental rights—even ostensibly for public health reasons during a crisis like the COVID-19 pandemic ([paras 120]).

The decision represents a significant affirmation of constitutional rights, ensuring that any governmental regulation implemented in the face of such emergencies must remain accountable to the rule of law, thereby protecting individual liberties against unnecessary infringement.
