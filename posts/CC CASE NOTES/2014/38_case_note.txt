Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

### Case Note: Stratford and Others v Investec Bank Limited and Others [2014] ZACC 38

#### FIRAC Case Note

**F - Facts**: 
The case revolves around a final sequestration order issued against the appellants, Ivor and Sheila Stratford, Clean Ngoma, Eric Dlokolo, and Andries Adonis (hereinafter referred to as "the Stratfords" and "domestic employees"), by the Western Cape Division of the High Court, Cape Town. These individuals were linked to the Pinnacle Point Group, which found itself in severe financial distress, leading Investec Bank Limited, a creditor, to initiate sequestration proceedings ([paras 1–6]).

Investec claimed that the Stratfords owed substantial debts, totaling over R240 million. A provisional sequestration order was granted amidst procedural controversies, particularly regarding whether the domestic employees had been properly notified of the sequestration application as stipulated by section 9(4A) of the Insolvency Act 24 of 1936 ([paras 8–12]). The domestic employees claimed their constitutional rights were infringed due to the lack of notification, arguing that this omission led to their inability to seek legal redress ([paras 9–10]).

The pivotal legal question arose as to whether "employees" under section 9(4A) included domestic workers, as the Supreme Court of Appeal’s interpretation in *Gungudoo* had excluded them ([para 12]). Following the High Court’s decision, which dismissed their constitutional challenge, the appellants sought leave to appeal at a higher level ([paras 15–16]).

**I - Issues**: 
The main legal questions in this case were:
1. Does the term "employees" in section 9(4A) of the Insolvency Act include domestic employees?
2. If not, does the differentiation render the provision inconsistent with the Constitution?
3. Is the compliance with section 9(4A) peremptory or directory?
4. Was the granting of a final sequestration order appropriate under the circumstances? ([para 17]).

**R - Rules**: 
The primary legal framework at play includes the Insolvency Act 24 of 1936, particularly sections 9(4A) and 12(1). Section 9(4A) mandates that when a sequestration application is made, a copy must be furnished to all employees of the debtor, either through direct notice or in ways that ensure accessibility ([paras 18–19]). 

The constitutional framework includes the Bill of Rights under the South African Constitution; namely:
- Section 9 (equality),
- Section 10 (human dignity),
- Section 23(1) (fair labor practices),
- Section 34 (access to courts) ([paras 16, 25]).

The court also referenced legislative interpretation principles derived from cases such as *Cool Ideas* on the necessity to promote the spirit of the Constitution in interpreting statutes, alongside the precedent set by the *EB Steam Company* decision regarding compliance with statutory requirements ([paras 20, 38–40]).

**A - Application**: 
The court’s analysis first tackled the interpretation of "employees" as used in section 9(4A). Conclusively, the court held that the term is broad enough to include domestic workers, directly departing from the restrictive interpretation favored in *Gungudoo* ([paras 21–37]). The court emphasized the importance of employee dignity and the constitutional obligation to interpret legislation in a manner that respects and promotes human rights.

In evaluating compliance with section 9(4A), the court noted that although the petition was left for the known domestic worker, Mr. Ngoma, the Strfords had a duty to ensure all domestic employees were informed. The court acknowledged the candidate attorney's efforts but underscored the need for proper notification to safeguard against the unfair exclusion of employees from the sequestration proceedings ([paras 39–41]). 

Additionally, on the issue of whether the final sequestration order met the criteria of "advantage to creditors," the court elaborated that the threshold should not be rigidified. The evidence presented indicated significant potential for creditor benefit, thus satisfying this legal requirement for a final sequestration order ([paras 43–46]).

**C - Conclusion**: 
The court ultimately dismissed the appeal but clarified that "employees" includes domestic employees under section 9(4A) of the Insolvency Act. Notably, the declaration was not made retrospective, except in specified circumstances to mitigate prejudice against domestic employees not previously informed ([paras 2, 3]). The constitutional challenge against the exclusion of domestic employees from sequestration notifications was not adjudicated as the court rendered the original interpretation of the statute sufficient to include them. The decision reinforces protections for vulnerable domestic employees and ensures their rights are recognized in insolvency proceedings ([paras 50, 49]).

In essence, while the appeal was dismissed on most fronts, the ruling reshaped the legal landscape regarding the inclusion of domestic employees in insolvency matters, highlighting the courts' ongoing commitment to expanding workers' rights in accordance with constitutional protections.
