Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

**FIRAC Case Note: Municipal Manager O.R. Tambo District Municipality and Another v Ndabeni [2022] ZACC 3**

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])**

The case revolves around a dispute between the Municipal Manager of the O.R. Tambo District Municipality and Nosipho Portia Ndabeni, involving Ms. Ndabeni's employment status. She was initially employed on a fixed-term contract as a Manager at the Aids Training Information and Counselling Centre (ATICC) on July 1, 2005. Despite extensions to her contract, her services were terminated in 2014. The Municipality passed Resolution 10/11 on January 30, 2011, converting all contract employees to permanent status. However, Ms. Ndabeni was excluded from this conversion, prompting her to approach the High Court on May 19, 2015, for a declaration of permanent employment.

The High Court, upon receiving no opposition from the Municipality, issued the ‘Mjali J order’ on December 13, 2016, declaring Ms. Ndabeni a permanent employee per Resolution 10/11. The Municipality failed to comply, leading Ms. Ndabeni to file for contempt in February 2019. Despite claiming ignorance of the order's validity, the Municipality's defenses were rejected by the High Court, reiterating that the order was binding. The Municipality's contention that the Mjali J order was a nullity due to concerns with section 66 of the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act led to appeals that escalated to the Supreme Court of Appeal and, subsequently, the Constitutional Court.

---

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 20–21])**

1. **Primary Issue:** Are the Municipal Parties obliged to comply with the Mjali J order, or is that order a nullity, thereby rendering it unenforceable?

2. **Secondary Issue:** Were the Municipal Parties in contempt of the Mjali J order, warranting the requirement to purge such contempt?

---

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 24–41])**

The case examines the binding nature of court orders under section 165(5) of the Constitution, which mandates that all court orders remain binding until set aside. The court referenced *State Capture* [2021] ZACC 18 for the principle that judicial orders are enforceable despite claims of invalidity. Additionally, the relevant provisions of the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act, especially sections 66(1), (3), and (5), are central to arguments concerning the legality of employment actions taken by the Municipality.

The court distinguished between *Tasima* and *Motala*, emphasizing the failure to establish the Mjali J order as a nullity. In *Tasima*, it was held that a court order, even if flawed, is not void unless explicitly overturned. The precedential reliance on *Kirland* stressed the heightened duty of public officials to comply with the law, further underscoring the Municipal Parties' obligations.

---

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 28–36])**

The court reasoned that the Mjali J order was lawful and issued by a court with jurisdiction. It rejected the Municipal Parties' nullity defense, noting that the High Court's reasoning was aligned with the legal material presented, particularly regarding the application of Resolution 10/11 to Ms. Ndabeni’s employment status. The absence of a compelling counter-argument during the initial proceedings rendered the Municipality's later claims inadequate.

Key to the court's analysis was the determination of whether the Mjali J order contravened the provisions of section 66. The court concluded that the Municipality failed to demonstrate that Ms. Ndabeni's employment post did not exist or was improperly categorized under the staff establishment. Consequently, the Mjali J order retained its binding force as no substantive legal or factual basis for nullity was established during proceedings.

Moreover, the court highlighted the implications of non-compliance with judicial orders on public trust and respect for the rule of law, emphasizing the necessity for enforcement to uphold constitutional norms.

---

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 43–44])**

The Constitutional Court concluded that:

1. The appeal was granted in part, specifically annulling the Supreme Court of Appeal’s finding of contempt against the Municipal Parties for failing to comply with the Mjali J order.
2. The court affirmed the binding nature of the Mjali J order, emphasizing that it was not a nullity and must be complied with within a set timeframe.
3. The second applicant was ordered to pay the respondent's costs on an attorney-and-client scale, reflecting the court's recognition of the Municipal Parties' failure to adhere to procedural norms and judicial duties.

The implications of this ruling reinforce the foundational principle that all court orders are binding unless legally overturned, thereby fostering adherence to the rule of law and safeguarding judicial authority in South Africa's constitutional landscape. This case also illustrates the court's view on the accountability of municipal officials in matters of compliance and governance.
