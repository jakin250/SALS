Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1996

# FIRAC Case Note: Lafras Luit ingh v. Minister of Defence

**Court**: Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Case Number**: CCT 29/95  
**Heard on**: 21 November 1995  
**Decided on**: 4 April 1996  
**Judgment by**: Didcott J  

### F – Facts 

In this case, the plaintiff, Lafras Luit ingh, a former member of a clandestine military unit, instituted a civil action against the Minister of Defence to recover a substantial sum of money claimed under an employment contract ([para 2]). The defendant contested the claim pursuant to section 113(1) of the Defence Act (No 44 of 1957), which provides that no civil action can be instituted against the State concerning actions "done or omitted to be done" under the Act after a period of six months, unless prescribed notice has been given ([para 1]). 

Issues arose regarding whether the requisite notice was given and whether the action was instituted within the six-month limitation period. The plaintiff denied these claims, asserting that the action was not covered by the statute ([para 2]). The plaintiff alleged compliance with the notice requirement but did not produce the notice or specify its details ([para 2]). The defendant's special plea included two objections; the non-compliance with notice, and the untimeliness of the instituted action ([para 2]).

The Transvaal Provincial Division agreed to refer the constitutional question of section 113(1) to the Constitutional Court, with the anticipation that an invalidation of the section may lead to the case being submitted to the Ombudsman instead of proceeding to trial ([para 3]).

### I – Issues

The principal constitutional issue was whether section 113(1) of the Defence Act was incompatible with rights enshrined in the Interim Constitution ([para 3]). The specific legal questions included:

1. Whether the action was initiated "in respect of" something done in pursuance of the Act, thereby activating the limitations of section 113(1) ([para 9]).
2. The constitutional validity of section 113(1) in light of various provisions of the Constitution, particularly regarding access to courts and equality before the law ([paras 6-8, 14]).

### R – Rules

The legal authority primarily invoked in this case is section 113(1) of the Defence Act, which necessitates compliance with certain procedural steps before initiating legal action against the State ([para 1]). Additionally, section 102(1) of the Constitution mandates that any potential constitutional issue that may be decisive of a case should be referred to the Constitutional Court ([para 4]). 

The Court also highlighted the need to assess the "interests of justice," which involve evaluating whether a referral is appropriate based on the prospect of the constitutional challenge's success ([para 6]). Relevant constitutional provisions mentioned included:

- **Section 8**: Equality before the law and equal protection.
- **Section 22**: Right of access to courts.
- **Sections 26 & 27**: Rights related to economic activity and fair labor practices, though the relevance of these sections was questioned ([paras 8, 14]).

### A – Application

The Court critically assessed whether the referral complied with the requirements set out in section 102(1) ([para 14]). It concluded that both the second (decisive impact) and third (interests of justice) criteria for a proper referral were not met. The unanswered questions surrounding the merits of the special plea indicated that the constitutional issue was premature for consideration at that moment ([para 14]).

The Court scrutinized the lack of clarity in the case's background regarding the timing of the cause of action and the potential for the defendant's special plea to stand unaffected by any constitutional ruling ([paras 10-12]). Given that the constitutional point might not have been decisive without resolving the factual issues first, the interests of justice did not warrant the referral as presented ([paras 11-15]).

### C – Conclusion 

Ultimately, Didcott J concluded that the referral was wrongfully ordered due to non-compliance with the requirements for a competent referral under section 102(1) ([para 14]). The Court struck off the referral and declined to grant direct access to resolve the constitutional issue, primarily because the circumstances surrounding the case were not exceptional as necessitated by Rule 17(1) of the Court's Rules ([paras 15-17]). As a result, the matter was remitted back to the Transvaal Provincial Division for further adjudication ([para 17]).

The ruling emphasizes the strict adherence to procedural requirements in constitutional referrals and underscores the necessity of resolving underlying factual questions before engaging in constitutional deliberation.
