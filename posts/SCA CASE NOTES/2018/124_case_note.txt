Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: Four Wheel Drive Accessory Distributors CC v Leshni Rattan NO (1048/17) [2018] ZASCA 124

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The appellant, Four Wheel Drive Accessory Distributors CC, brought a claim against the respondent, Leshni Rattan NO, as executrix of the estate of the late Mr. Ivin Rattan. The case originated from a dispute over a written agreement related to the use of a courtesy vehicle (a Land Rover Discovery) which Mr. Rattan had allegedly signed. On 26 November 2012, Mr. Rattan had delivered his own vehicle for repairs and received the courtesy vehicle in return, signing an agreement that purportedly obliged him to return the vehicle in the same condition as he received it. Tragically, Mr. Rattan was fatally shot while using the courtesy vehicle, which was subsequently returned in a damaged condition. The appellant claimed from the estate R559,817.45 to cover the repair costs for the vehicle.

The High Court dismissed the appellant's claim on multiple grounds: it found that the appellant lacked locus standi, had not proved its interest in the litigation, and that the alleged lease between appellant and Mr. Rattan was not substantiated, as the contract violated public policy and provisions of the Consumer Protection Act 68 of 2008 ([paras 1–2]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues before the court were as follows:
1. **Locus Standi**: Did the appellant have the legal standing to bring the claim for damages against the respondent?
2. **Existence of Contract**: Was there a valid legal agreement between the appellant and Mr. Rattan concerning the use of the vehicle?
3. **Application of Public Policy and the Consumer Protection Act**: Did the agreement violate public policy and the provisions of the Consumer Protection Act, rendering it invalid? ([paras 1–2]).

### R – Rules
The court relied on several legal principles regarding locus standi:
- A party must have a direct interest in the relief sought; this interest must be current and not abstract or hypothetical ([para 7]).
- The party asserting a claim bears the burden of proof to establish standing ([para 7]). 
- The court referenced **Dalrymple v Colonial Treasurer** for the principle that one cannot sue unless a duty is owed to them or damage is suffered as a result of a wrongful act ([para 8]).
- Regarding the Consumer Protection Act, the court examined provisions designed to protect consumers and ensure agreements are clear, fair, and in plain language ([paras 30-31]).

### A – Application
In determining locus standi, the appellants failed to sufficiently establish their interest in the vehicle or their legal standing. The appellant's witness, Mrs. Brown, provided contradictory testimony about the vehicle’s ownership and the nature of the agreement, often suggesting a complicated and unclear relationship among the parties involved ([paras 11–14]).

The court found that Mrs. Brown's claim that the vehicle was leased from Land Rover SA, who then leased it back to the appellant, lacked clarity and created doubts about the appellant's rights to sue. There was no clear agreement in writing to substantiate the claim of a formal lease agreement, and testimony indicated the possibility of a mere loan agreement ([paras 11–19]).

The court also ruled that the agreement did not comply with the necessary requirements of a lease, such as the existence of an identifiable rental payment ([para 16]). Moreover, the claim's foundation on alleged damages arising from actions not sanctioned by a valid contract was unsustainable ([para 19]).

Finally, while the lower court raised concerns regarding the agreement's validity under public policy and the Act, the appellate court held that these issues were outside the scope of the pleadings presented and emphasized that courts should confine their judgment to matters raised by the parties ([paras 21–24]).

### C – Conclusion
The appeal was dismissed with costs based on the lack of standing of the appellant to bring forth the case. The court upheld the lower court's decision that no enforceable agreement existed, which meant the appellant had no direct interest in the claim. Moreover, the raised issues of public policy regarding the agreement were deemed irrelevant to the outcome, with the ultimate finding that the elements necessary for a valid contractual relationship were not sufficiently proved ([paras 34–35]). This case underscores the importance of establishing clear contractual relationships and the evidential burden of establishing locus standi in civil litigation.
