Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note

### Case Title: Snyders and Others v de Jager and Others [2016] ZACC 55 

### Facts
The case arose from an eviction order against **Karel Snyders** (First Applicant), his wife **Sofia Snyders** (Second Applicant), and their children (Third Applicants) from a house on a farm owned by **FJN Stassen** and managed by **Louisa Frederika de Jager** (First Respondent) ([1]–[2]). Snyders had worked on the Stassen Farm since 1992, initially under the former owner and later under Stassen Jr. Following a contentious working relationship with de Jager, Snyders was dismissed in **April 2008**. De Jager claimed the dismissal automatically terminated Snyders' right of residence, leading to an eviction application in the **Magistrate's Court** ([6]–[11]).

The **Magistrate's Court** granted the eviction order on **14 November 2012**, allowing Snyders and his family until **20 December 2012** to vacate. This order was suspended pending confirmation by the **Land Claims Court** as mandated by **section 19(3)** of the **Extension of Security of Tenure Act, 1997 (ESTA)** ([14]–[20]). The **Land Claims Court** upheld the eviction order, which led the **Supreme Court of Appeal** to deny Snyders' appeal, asserting that an appeal lies only to the Land Claims Court and not to itself ([21]–[23]).

Following the eviction on **1 October 2015**, without prior notice, Snyders applied to the Constitutional Court seeking an appeal and interim restoration of possession ([24]–[26]).

### Issues
1. **Jurisdictional Question**: Whether an appeal from a Magistrate's Court eviction order under ESTA lies to the **Supreme Court of Appeal** or to the **Land Claims Court**, as construed under **section 19(3)**?
2. **Validity of Eviction**: Whether the eviction order against the applicants was justified and conformed to both substantive and procedural requirements under ESTA?
3. **Joinder of Parties**: Whether Mr. Willem Breda and his family should be joined in the proceedings, given their occupation of the property post-eviction of the Snyders ([1]–[2], [30]–[31]).

### Rules
- **Extension of Security of Tenure Act, 1997 (ESTA)**, particularly **sections 8** (concerning termination of right of residence and eviction), **10** (specifics related to eviction of individuals who were occupiers prior to 4 February 1997), and **19(3)** (protocol for reviewing eviction orders) ([58]–[60]).
- **Common Law Principles**: Rights to procedural fairness and the burden on a landowner to prove legitimate grounds for eviction ([56]–[76]).
- **Constitutional Framework**: Jurisdictional considerations leading to interpretation of statutory provisions in light of constitutional rights ([28]).

### Application
The Court, led by **Zondo J**, assessed the merits of the case, affirming that:
- The eviction order raised **jurisdictional issues** as the Supreme Court of Appeal had misinterpreted the scope of appeals under section 19(3) of ESTA, which allows appeals to the **Supreme Court of Appeal** when the **Land Claims Court** confirms a Magistrate's order ([40]–[49]).
- Snyders’ eviction was invalid as the procedural requirements under sections 8 and 10 of ESTA had not been satisfied. The dismissal did not effectively terminate Snyders' right of residence, as he had not been expressly notified about this termination ([72]–[75]).
- Furthermore, de Jager’s case was undermined by the irregular admission of evidence that exceeded the scope allowed by the Magistrate's Court, reinforcing the lack of procedural fairness ([66]).

The Court recognized that although there was an eviction order against Mr. Breda, a new order for eviction from the occupants living in Snyders’ former home was necessary to uphold his rights while ensuring that Breda's existing rights on the farm were not unjustly infringed ([75], [84]).

### Conclusion
The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal, set aside the eviction orders of the Land Claims Court and the Magistrate's Court, and dismissed de Jager's application for eviction against Snyders and his family. It also mandated the eviction of Breda and his family from the Snyders’ former home, establishing that any presumed right of residence was contingent on Snyders' previous occupancy ([84]). Thus, the judgment emphasized the necessity of compliance with both substantive and procedural fairness in eviction proceedings under ESTA, and clarified the appellate jurisdiction concerning the reviews of eviction orders confirmed under ESTA, underscoring the protection of occupiers’ rights against arbitrary eviction ([85]).
