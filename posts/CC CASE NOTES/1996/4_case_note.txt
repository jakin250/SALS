Source Folder: 1996

# Case Note: IN THE CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA (CCT 39/95) - FIRAC

## F – Facts
The case involved a constitutional dispute regarding the *School Education Bill of 1995*, enacted by the Gauteng Provincial Legislature. The Speaker was petitioned by members of the legislature, constituting at least one-third of its members, to request the Constitutional Court to resolve issues concerning the constitutionality of specific sections of the Bill ([1]). Although the Bill was passed and subsequently enacted as the School Education Act of 1995, various sections, particularly those regarding language and religious instruction, were not yet operational ([2]).

The object of the Bill was to regulate education in public schools and to reflect South Africa’s commitment to non-discrimination. One relevant provision was section 19(1), which prohibited language competence testing as an admission requirement for public schools ([3]). Additionally, section 21 outlined the religious policy of public schools, mandating respect for freedom of conscience, and section 22 articulated students' rights regarding religious practices ([4]). Critics, including various petitioners and the South African Foundation for Education and Training (amicus curiae), argued that these sections violated rights to attend schools based on specific cultural and linguistic identities ([5]).

Central to the dispute was the interpretation of section 32(c) of the Constitution, which purportedly conferred a right to establish educational institutions based on common culture, language, or religion, provided such establishment did not lead to racial discrimination ([6]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issues before the Court were:
1. Did section 19(1) of the *School Education Act of 1995*, which prohibited language competence testing for admission to public schools, infringe upon rights established under section 32 of the Constitution?
2. Did the provisions related to the religious policy of public schools (sections 21 and 22) violate the constitutional rights guaranteed to individuals in the context of education and language?
3. What was the nature of the obligation imposed on the state under section 32(c) regarding the establishment of educational institutions based on a common culture, language, or religion? ([5]–[6]).

## R – Rules
The relevant legal authorities and precedents included:
1. **Section 32 of the Constitution**: Imperatively details the rights to education, language of instruction, and the establishment of educational institutions grounded in culture, language, or religion ([6]).
2. **Constitutional principles of equality and non-discrimination**: Section 9 of the Constitution guarantees equality before the law and prohibits unfair discrimination on various grounds ([7]).
3. **Legal precedents concerning minority rights**: International human rights norms regarding minority protection and the obligations (or lack thereof) of the state to fund or establish minority educational institutions ([9]–[10]).

## A – Application
The Court, led by Mahomed DP, examined the arguments focusing primarily on the interpretation of section 32(c). It held that while the section provided a defensive right, it did not impose a positive obligation on the state to establish educational institutions for every culture, language, or religion. The court emphasized that the language of section 32(c) simply conferred the freedom to establish such institutions by private individuals without obligating the state to fund or oversee their creation ([8]–[9]). 

The Court further clarified that sections 19(1), 21(2), and 22(3) did not violate the rights of individuals or communities given that they allowed for the existence of educational institutions without requiring state endorsement or establishment of schools exclusively for minority groups ([20]). The argument that these sections infringed upon a constitutional obligation to provide education in a specific language or culture was dismissed by the Court as lacking constitutional backing and failing to align with the protective intent of international human rights law ([18]).

Additionally, the Court parsed through the legislative and historical context to conclude that the provisions were not discriminatory but aimed at promoting equality and inclusivity, thus ensuring that no racial discrimination was perpetuated by the State ([19]–[20]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court held that sections 19(1), 21(2), 21(3), and 22(3) of the School Education Act of 1995 were consistent with the Constitution. The Court explicitly stated that there were no grounds for claiming any violation of the constitutional rights asserted by the petitioners or the Foundation. It ruled that the interpretation of section 32(c) favored private initiative over state obligation in establishing educational institutions based on common cultural or linguistic ties ([36]).

**Implications**: The ruling sets a significant precedent regarding the limited scope of state obligations toward the establishment of educational institutions aligned with minority cultures and affirmatively solidifies the balance sought between the enforcement of constitutional rights and the necessity of preserving equitable access to education across diverse cultural identities. This decision reaffirms the principle that while cultural identity is to be respected and protected, it should not infringe upon the larger constitutional framework aimed at fostering equality and non-discrimination in the educational sphere.
