Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2000

# FIRAC Case Note: Brummer v Gorfil Brothers Investments (Pty) Ltd

**Constitutional Court of South Africa**  
**Case CCT 45/99**  
**Paul Phillipus Brummer** (Applicant)  
**v.**  
**Gorfil Brothers Investments (Pty) Ltd, Estate of the Late Solly Gorfil, David Gorfil, Nylstroom Hotel CC** (Respondents)  
**Decided on:** 30 March 2000

---

## F – Facts

The case presents a complex procedural history rooted in a longstanding litigation initiated by the applicant, Paul Phillipus Brummer, who claimed damages for the alleged repudiation of a contract with Nylstroom Hotel CC. This claim was initiated in 1987 and led to an adverse ruling in 1991, where the trial judge dismissed claims against the third respondent, David Gorfil, and his late father but did not absolve the fourth respondent, Nylstroom Hotel CC, regarding the damages owed. Costs were awarded against Brummer amounting to R52,436.48, which remained unpaid ([para 1]).

Subsequently, the third respondent and his father attached Brummer's rights from the pending case and sold them at execution to the first respondent, Gorfil Brothers Investments Pty Ltd, on 10 March 1993. Notably, the third respondent and his father, both directors and shareholders of the first respondent at the time of sale, were directly involved in this financial transaction ([para 2]). Following this event, Brummer sought to challenge the sale's validity through a combined summons in the Transvaal High Court on the premise that it constituted an abuse of the court process and transgressed public policy.

The High Court dismissed Brummer's claims on 28 March 1996, denying leave to appeal. This decision was later upheld by the Supreme Court of Appeal, which found that there was no constitutional basis for the sale to be set aside ([para 1]). Brummer's application for special leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court was notably filed late, over nine months after the Supreme Court's ruling ([para 1]).

## I – Issues

The central legal issue at hand involved the constitutionality of laws that permit a party to pending legal proceedings to sell the rights of another for the purpose of terminating litigation. Specifically, the court examined whether the sale in execution infringed section 34 of the Constitution, which guarantees the right to access courts. A significant aspect of the legal inquiry was whether the retrospective application of the interim Constitution or the current Constitution could apply to transactions that occurred prior to their enactment ([para 4]).

Another critical issue was the consideration of condonation for Brummer's delayed application for leave to appeal, encompassing requirements regarding the interests of justice, the reasons for delay, and the potential for prejudice ([para 4], [para 3]).

## R – Rules

The court alluded to established legal principles regarding the granting of condonation, specifically that such relief may be granted in the interests of justice, which encompass various factors like the nature of the relief, cause of delay, and potential prejudice ([para 3]). The assessment of these factors draws on precedential interpretation established in *Fraser v Naude* 1999 (1) SA 1 (CC), where the court underscored a holistic approach to determine what constitutes "the interests of justice" ([para 3]).

Further, the court referenced *Du Plessis and Others v De Klerk and Another* 1996 (3) SA 850 (CC) concerning the non-retrospective effect of the interim Constitution, suggesting that transactions executed prior to the Constitution do not usually come under its purview unless extraordinary circumstances render enforcement unjust ([para 4]).

## A – Application

In its analysis, the Constitutional Court articulated that the sale in execution took place before the commencement of constitutional mandates, thus aligning with the determination in *Du Plessis*, which limited retrospective application unless unusual circumstances were shown ([para 4]). The court concluded that no such extraordinary circumstances existed in Brummer's case, as the delay in invoking proceedings for the sale's exemption was prolonged and lacked valid justification ([para 6]).

With respect to the condonation request, the court evaluated the timeline of Brummer’s actions post-sale. His delay in responding to the sale—evident by the two-year gap before challenging the execution sale and the protracted time before appealing the Supreme Court's refusal—was significant ([para 6]). The court emphasized that allowing the appeal would not serve the interests of justice, especially given the absence of compelling reasons after so many years of inactivity ([para 6]).

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court dismissed the application for condonation and by extension the application for special leave to appeal. The ruling underscored the importance of timeliness and the need for parties to act with diligence in pursuing their claims. The court concluded that not only were the prospects of success in the intended appeal negligible, but also that condonation would not serve the interests of justice given the considerable delays attributed to the applicant ([para 8]).

Ultimately, this case reinforced the principle that litigation must progress efficiently and that the courts, whilst open to providing remedies, also must safeguard against undue delay that prejudices the rights of other parties involved. The absence of retrospective applicability of constitutional provisions in this context further emphasizes the need for parties to navigate their legal strategies actively and promptly within the judicial framework.

**Order:**  
1. The application for condonation is dismissed.   
2. There is no order as to costs. 

--- 

This case illuminates the intersection of constitutional rights, procedural integrity, and the evolving interpretations of access to justice in post-apartheid South Africa.
