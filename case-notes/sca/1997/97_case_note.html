Supreme Court of Appeal

Saak No 661/94

In Saak No 661/94, the accused, Mzukisi John Njobi (first appellant) and Wati M A Mama (second appellant), were convicted of murder and aggravated robbery ([para 1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 1997

F – Facts

In Saak No 661/94, the accused, Mzukisi John Njobi (first appellant) and Wati M A Mama (second appellant), were convicted of murder and aggravated robbery ([para 1]). Both were sentenced to death for murder, while the first appellant received a ten-year prison sentence for robbery, and the second appellant an eight-year sentence for the same offense ([para 1]).

The events unfolded as follows: On July 31, 1993, Carel Alberts was fatally attacked at his store, Jack's Cash Store in Port Elizabeth. The two appellants were implicated in the crime, and evidence against them included:

  1. A confession made by the first appellant before a magistrate.
  2. Witness testimony identifying two palm prints belonging to the first appellant found at the deceased's residence.
  3. The first appellant's admissions regarding the burial of the deceased's firearm and details about the robbery ([paras 3-5]).

After their arrests, multiple police witnesses provided corroborating evidence, although the first appellant challenged the admissibility of his confession, claiming it was extracted under duress and violence by the police ([paras 9, 10]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions before the court were:

  1. Was the confession of the first appellant admissible, considering the alleged coercion involved in its procurement?
  2. Did the evidence presented sufficiently establish the guilt of the appellants beyond a reasonable doubt?
  3. Was the application of the death penalty justified in light of recent constitutional developments concerning capital punishment? ([paras 1-2]).

R – Rules

The court relied on several legal precedents and rules in reaching its decision:

  • Admissibility of Confessions: Under South African law, a confession must be made voluntarily to be admissible in court. Relevant case law requires that evidence of violence and coercion be adequately addressed.
  • Standard of Proof: The prosecution must prove guilt beyond a reasonable doubt.
  • Constitutional Context: The 1995 Constitutional Court ruling in S v Makwanyane ([1995] 6 BCLR 665) effectively rendered the death penalty unconstitutional, meaning that existing death sentences could not be executed while such judgment was standing ([para 24]).

The court also referenced principles regarding the evaluation of witness credibility and the weight given to corroborating evidence in criminal adjudication ([para 12]).

A – Application

In their analysis, the court meticulously examined the circumstances surrounding the confession of the first appellant. Relying on witness accounts, the court stated that while allegations of police brutality were made, these were contradicted by police testimonies confirming no coercion took place. The trial court found the first appellant's testimony concerning his treatment by the police implausible, given the lack of visible injuries noted by the magistrate who recorded his confession ([paras 8-15]).

The court upheld the strength of the palm print evidence, corroborating it with expert testimony linking it to the first appellant. This, combined with the details of the confession that were consistent with the facts surrounding the crime, constituted a substantial basis for conviction ([paras 17-20]).

Regarding the penalty, the court recognized the implications of the ruling in S v Makwanyane, leading to a reevaluation of the death sentences. Consequently, while the murder conviction was unnecessary to overturn, it necessitated the removal of the death penalty attached, thus replacing it with an appropriate prison term following the re-evaluation of sentences for both appellants ([paras 24-25]).

C – Conclusion

The court concluded that:

  1. The first appellant’s confession was deemed admissible and given appropriate weight in the overall context of the evidence, which established his guilt beyond a reasonable doubt for both murder and robbery.
  2. The second appellant also faced sufficient evidence for his convictions, including his confession detailing his participation and knowledge of the robbery, which was unchallenged on appeal.
  3. The death sentences were set aside in light of unconstitutional status regarding capital punishment, reclassifying both appellants to serve time in prison, with the first appellant receiving a ten-year sentence and the second an eight-year sentence for robbery ([paras 24-25]).

This case underscores the importance of procedural safeguards in the admission of confessions and the impact of constitutional developments on sentencing practices in South Africa’s evolving legal landscape.
