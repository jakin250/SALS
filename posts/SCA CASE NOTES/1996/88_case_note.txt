Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1996

## Case Note Using FIRAC Method

### Case Title: Government of the Province of KwaZulu/Natal v Ngwane
**Case No:** 566/94  
**Court:** Supreme Court of South Africa (Appellate Division)  
**Coram:** Van Heerden, Grosskopf, Nienaber, Olivier, Zulman JJA  
**Heard:** 19 August 1996  
**Delivered:** 9 September 1996

---

### F – Facts
The case revolves around the succession of the chieftaincy of the Mnqobokazi tribe following the death of Chief Msongelwa, who passed away in May 1991. Chief Msongelwa had two sons, Zwelakhe and Bhekuyise, who both aspired to succeed him as chief. In 1989, Chief Msongelwa, who was elderly and declining in health, expressed his intention to relinquish his chieftaincy. Following this, he nominated Bhekuyise as his successor during various meetings in 1990, although these nominations were ambiguous and lacked a formal decision.

On 19 February 1991, the KwaZulu cabinet appointed Zwelakhe as the new chief, which was formally communicated only in June 1991. After the installation of Zwelakhe on 2 November 1991, Bhekuyise challenged this appointment in the Natal Provincial Division, seeking either a reversal of Zwelakhe’s appointment or the appointment of himself as chief. The court found in favor of Bhekuyise, leading to the present appeal by the KwaZulu government and Zwelakhe ([paras 2–4]).

### I – Issues
The principal legal questions in this case are threefold:

1. **Ultra Vires Authority**: Whether the KwaZulu cabinet had the authority to appoint Zwelakhe as chief, as Bhekuyise contended that such power resided with the chief minister acting on cabinet's advice, rather than the cabinet acting independently ([para 8]).
  
2. **Fettering of Discretion**: Whether the KwaZulu government's established practice of consulting the incumbent chief and the umndeni in these appointments constituted a fettering of its discretion, which was not followed in this instance ([para 10]).
  
3. **Legitimate Expectation**: The court a quo's finding that Bhekuyise had a legitimate expectation of being heard before the appointment of Zwelakhe, which was not fulfilled ([paras 11–12]).

### R – Rules
The legal authorities and statutory provisions pertinent to this case include:

1. **Black Administration Act, 1927**: This act empowers the Governor-General to recognize or appoint chiefs, a power interpreted as unfettered according to *Minister of Native Affairs and Another v Buthelezi* 1951 (1) SA 766 (D), and *Buthelezi v Minister of Bantu Administration and Development* 1961 (4) SA 835 (A) ([para 8]).

2. **Republic of South Africa Constitution Act, 1961**: In accordance with sections 3(b) and 16(1), it specifies that the executive government is vested in the state president acting on the advice of the executive council ([para 8]).

3. **National States Constitution Act, 1971**: This act also discusses the distribution of powers concerning appointments in self-governing territories like KwaZulu and specifically defines the Cabinet's role in appointments ([para 8]).

4. **KwaZulu Act on the Code of Zulu Law, Act 1 of 1985**: This act stipulated that the cabinet could conduct structured inquiries into chieftaincy matters under certain conditions but maintained that the cabinet's discretion remains unfettered ([para 12]).

### A – Application
In its application of the law, the court thoroughly evaluated each of the grounds for appeal presented by Bhekuyise:

1. **On Ultra Vires Authority**: The court found the KwaZulu cabinet acted within its authority as the power to appoint a chief rested with it, meaning the appointment of Zwelakhe was valid despite the claims from Bhekuyise ([para 8]).

2. **On Fettering of Discretion**: The court agreed with the lower court's dismissal of the claim concerning the fettering of discretion, noting that Bhekuyise's arguments did not establish a fixed procedure, and conceded this point during the proceedings ([para 10]).

3. **On Legitimate Expectation**: The court critiqued the lower court's assertion of a legitimate expectation. It noted that Bhekuyise had not articulated any expectation of consultation within the documentation presented before the court. More importantly, the court emphasized that there was no decision by the government or the magistrates to conduct a full inquiry as would have been required to fulfill such an expectation under the established legal framework ([paras 11–12]).

### C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Appellate Division overturned the order of the court a quo, declaring that Bhekuyise's application was dismissed with costs. The case reinforces the sovereignty of the cabinet in its appointment authority and delineates the boundaries of legitimate expectation concerning administrative procedures. By rejecting the lower court's logic, particularly regarding the belief that Bhekuyise should have been consulted, the ruling emphasizes that notification or consultation prior to appointment does not form an automatic legal obligation unless explicitly established ([para 13]).

The verdict signifies not only a reaffirmation of executive authority in customary law appointments but also delineates the need for procedural clarity when invoking rights based on legitimate expectations within the sphere of administrative law.
