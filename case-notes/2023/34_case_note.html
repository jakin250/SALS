Ex parte Minister of Home Affairs and Others [2023] ZACC 34

Constitutional Court 2023 [2023] ZACC 34

F – Facts

[1] On February 3, 2016, the High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Division, Pretoria, declared sections 34(1)(b) and (d) of the Immigration Act 13 of 2002 unconstitutional and invalid, specifically noting that these provisions limited constitutional rights enshrined in sections 12(1) and 35(2) of the Constitution. The Court ordered that any illegal foreigner detained under these provisions must be brought before a court in person within 48 hours of their detention ([para 1]).

[2] Following a unanimous decision by the Constitutional Court on June 29, 2017, these provisions were confirmed as invalid, with a 24-month suspension allowing Parliament to remedy the constitutional defects. However, this deadline expired on June 29, 2019, without corrective legislation being passed ([paras 4-5]).

[3] In 2023, having received no legislative amendments, the Minister of Home Affairs and Director-General sought a renewal of the prior order, proposing an ex parte application for its “revival.” The intervening party, Lawyers for Human Rights (LHR), was allowed to participate, citing concerns about the persistent invalidation of the aforementioned sections causing legal uncertainty for detainees ([paras 6-7]).

[4] The High Court, in a prior order from June 21, 2022, provided that the 2017 order would remain applicable pending resolution ([para 7]). The Constitution’s section 172(1)(b) grants the court the power to make just and equitable orders after declaring a law invalid ([paras 30-40]).

I – Issues

[5] The primary legal questions before the Constitutional Court were:

  1. Can the Constitutional Court extend a suspension order after its expiration to address the implications of the unconstitutional provisions on individuals’ rights?
  2. What new orders or modifications might the Court make to ensure compliance with constitutional standards regarding detention and deportation?
  3. What provisions should govern detention procedures in light of the invalidated sections, particularly regarding the automatic right of detainees to appear in court? ([paras 29-31])

R – Rules

[6] Pertinent legal principles shaping the Court’s decision included:

  • Sections 12(1) and 35(2) of the Constitution: These protect the rights to freedom and fair trial, respectively.
  • Section 172(1)(b): Authorizes courts to provide just and equitable remedies, allowing for the freezing of invalid provisions while legislative amendments are pursued ([para 39]).
  • Precedents: The Court relied on previous decisions such as Cross-Border Road Transport Agency and Minister of Social Development, which articulated the limits of a court's power to extend suspension periods after they have lapsed ([para 6]).

A – Application

[7] The Court indicated that it could not merely revive the lapsed sections of the Immigration Act following the suspension period but could, under section 172(1)(b), provide supplementary measures to protect the rights of individuals subjected to detention under invalid laws ([paras 40-41]).

[8] In evaluating the implications of the expired suspension and the need for clarity, the Court noted that various courts had offered conflicting interpretations regarding the applicability of the invalidated laws, creating substantial legal uncertainty for detainees ([para 11]).

[9] The Court proceeded to establish a new framework for detention procedures, specifying that immigration officers must consider whether the interests of justice would permit the release of a detainee under reasonable conditions. It mandated that detainees must be brought before a court within 48 hours, maintaining the procedural safeguards necessary to respect their rights ([paras 1-4]).

C – Conclusion

[10] The Constitutional Court held that while it could not extend the prior suspension order, it had the authority to reassess the necessary protective measures in light of the constitutional defects of the Immigration Act. The court issued an order establishing new provisions regarding the detention of illegal foreigners and their rights, ensuring that detainees would be afforded fair and just treatment as outlined in the Constitution ([paras 117-118]).

[11] The applicants (the Minister and Director-General) were ordered to pay costs, reflecting the court's dissatisfaction with their failure to enact remedial legislation, thereby implicating their accountability under constitutional mandates. Personal costs were partly assigned to the Minister and the Director-General due to their roles in the delayed legislative response, demonstrating the broader implications for public accountability and adherence to rule of law in governance ([paras 90-112]).

This decision underscores the Court's commitment to upholding constitutional rights, emphasizing that the failure of legislative bodies must not translate into a disregard for the rights of individuals potentially impacted by such failures. The judgment serves as a crucial reminder of the ongoing obligation of government actors to enact and uphold lawful processes consistent with constitutional protections.
