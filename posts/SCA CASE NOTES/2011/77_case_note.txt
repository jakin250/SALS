Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# Case Note: Ramokolo v The State (251/10) [2011] ZASCA 77

**FIRAC Method**

---

## F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–31])

The appellant, Bruce Sabelo Mpumelelo Ramokolo, was indicted in the Mthatha Regional Court on two counts of extortion under Section 156 of the Transkei Penal Code, Act 9 of 1983. Despite a not guilty plea, he was convicted as charged. The case arose from his role as a consulting engineer overseeing a state road construction project executed by Mageba Construction (Pty) Ltd. Evidence indicated that the appellant coerced the contractor into making illicit payments.

In the Mthatha Regional Court, Ramokolo received a sentence comprising a R50,000 fine or two years of imprisonment, along with a three-year imprisonment suspended for similar offenses. Upon appeal, the Eastern Cape High Court upheld the conviction on the second count while dismissing the first. The sentence was also set aside, replaced with a custodial sentence consisting of four years, of which two years were suspended. This case was appealed further to the Supreme Court of Appeal, examining both conviction and sentence aspects ([paras 1–13]).

The primary evidence was provided by Vatiswa Poswa, a complainant from Mageba, and corroborated by bank officials and a government engineer. Poswa testified that Ramokolo threatened to withhold payments unless he received a payment of R180,000, which, after significant pressure, she complied with. Following this incident, Poswa and her brother sought intervention from law enforcement ([paras 2–12]).

## I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 16–23])

The legal questions revolved around the validity of the extortion conviction and the appropriateness of the sentence. Specific issues included:
1. Whether the evidence presented was sufficient to uphold the conviction under the extortion provisions of the Penal Code.
2. Whether the High Court's intervention concerning the sentence was justified, particularly in light of the court’s obligation to balance personal circumstances against the seriousness of the crime, given societal interests ([paras 16, 24]).

## R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 17–28])

The court primarily referenced Section 156 of the Transkei Penal Code, which defines extortion as obtaining an advantage through unlawful pressure, indicating that such threats must be considered when establishing the charges. The principles established in **S v Boesak** 2001 (1) SA 912 (CC) emphasized the necessity for caution when evaluating single witness testimonies, applicable here as Poswa was the sole eyewitness for the contested charges ([paras 17, 19]).

The judgment also drew on case law addressing the seriousness of extortion and its profound societal implications, stressing the need for deterrent sentences in cases of corruption. Citing **S v Shaik**, 2007 (1) SA 240 (SCA), the judgment reiterated the judiciary's role in combating corruption as a threat to democracy and the rule of law ([paras 26–29]).

## A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 20–22])

The appeal court affirmed that the prosecution established a prima facie case of extortion based on Poswa’s credible testimony and the circumstantial evidence presented, which included the bank transactions involving the cash payments made to Ramokolo. Notably, the court highlighted that Ramokolo's decision not to testify raised adverse inferences in evaluating the evidence against him. 

The court analyzed the extortion charge within the context of the appellant's actions, focusing on his role as a trusted professional who exploited his position. The judgment emphasized that the fulfillment of a duty to ensure proper disbursement of funds was marred by the appellant’s illicit conduct, which was inconsistent with the duties expected of a consultant in a government project. 

The issues surrounding the victim's credibility and the sufficiency of evidence were thoroughly examined; while Poswa's reliability as a single witness was acknowledged, the accumulation of documentary evidence corroborated her testimony. The court ultimately concluded that the conviction was appropriate ([paras 20–23]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 24–34])

The Supreme Court upheld the conviction, confirming the appellant's culpability for extortion. In relation to sentencing, the original sentence was deemed excessively lenient given the severity of the offense and societal implications of corruption. The court emphasized that custodial sentences are necessary for serious crimes like extortion, which threaten societal and governmental integrity.

Consequently, the court sentenced Ramokolo to four years of imprisonment, with two years suspended under specific conditions. It advanced the notion that crimes of this nature undermine democracy and informed public governance, necessitating a firm judicial response to deter similar conduct in the future ([paras 24–34]).

This case ultimately serves as a critical reference point reinforcing the judicial system's commitment to addressing corruption and extortion within public procurement and resource management frameworks, signaling to future offenders the serious ramifications their actions entail.
