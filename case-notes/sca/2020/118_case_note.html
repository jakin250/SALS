Case Title Former Way Trade & Invest (Pty) Ltd v Bright Idea Projects 66 (Pty) Ltd (Case no 1341/2018) [2020] ZASCA 118

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 118 ## F – Facts [paras 1–41] The case centers

Case Title

Former Way Trade & Invest (Pty) Ltd v Bright Idea Projects 66 (Pty) Ltd (Case no 1341/2018) [2020] ZASCA 118

F – Facts [paras 1–41]

The case centers around an eviction order issued by Bright Idea Projects 66 (Pty) Ltd ("All Fuels"), the owner of property at 238 Albert Luthuli Street, Pietermaritzburg, against Former Way Trade & Invest (Pty) Ltd ("Former Way"), which conducted business as a Caltex service station at the site. The franchise agreement allowing Former Way to operate the service station expired on 31 December 2017. Following expiration, Former Way refused to vacate the premises, leading All Fuels to initiate eviction proceedings on 15 January 2018 ([para 1]).

Former Way countered by arguing that an alternative franchise agreement extending its tenure had been agreed upon and sought a stay of the eviction proceedings based on an alleged agreement to arbitrate disputes concerning the franchise under section 12B of the Petroleum Products Act, 120 of 1977 ("the Act"). The High Court, presided over by D Pillay J, ruled against Former Way, dismissing both the counter-application and the request for leave to appeal ([paras 2–12]).

After further refusals of leave to appeal by this court, Former Way applied for reconsideration under section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act, 10 of 2013 ([paras 3–41]).

I – Issues [paras 3, 24, 40]

The core legal issues in this case are:

  1. Existence of a New Franchise Agreement: Whether an enforceable franchise agreement—allegedly extending the tenure of Former Way—had been concluded prior to the expiration of the previous agreement.
  2. Stay of High Court Proceedings Pending Arbitration: Whether the referral to arbitration under section 12B of the Act ousted the jurisdiction of the High Court.
  3. Possession Rights Post-Franchise Termination: The legality of Former Way's continued occupation of the premises after the expiration of the franchise agreement, contingent on whether rights of occupation were established.

R – Rules [paras 8, 25, 30]

In adjudicating the case, the court relied on several legal principles and statutory provisions:

  1. Contract Law: Fundamental aspects of contract law dictate that an agreement must be clearly established and evidenced ([paras 21–22]).
  2. Franchise Agreements and Cession: Section 12B of the Petroleum Products Act, which outlines the framework for arbitration of disputes stemming from franchising relationships, was pivotal. This section mandates arbitration for alleged unfair or unreasonable contractual practices ([paras 24, 30]).
  3. Property Rights and Eviction: The constitutional protection against unlawful deprivation of property (as per the South African Constitution) necessitates establishing a legal right to occupy the property ([para 41]).

A – Application [paras 24–41]

The court first assessed the argument surrounding the alleged new franchise agreement proposed by Former Way. It found that no substantial evidence existed to demonstrate that the agreement had effectively been concluded or that Former Way had taken necessary steps to solidify its rights of occupancy ([paras 22–23]).

Regarding the claim for arbitration through section 12B, the court emphasized that the referral did not automatically oust the jurisdiction of the High Court. The court cited relevant jurisprudence indicating that arbitration processes serve as an alternative dispute resolution mechanism rather than an exclusive one ([paras 30–34]). The court evaluated whether the referral had occurred prior to the initiation of litigation, ultimately finding that litigation had commenced and that the referral to arbitration did not negate the High Court's authority to adjudicate the eviction matter ([paras 28–39]).

The High Court had determined—correctly, according to the SCA—that Former Way failed to establish any legitimate rights to remain in possession of the premises following the expiration of the franchise agreement and that its occupation was not grounded in law ([para 41]).

C – Conclusion [paras 42-43]

The Supreme Court of Appeal confirmed the dismissal of Former Way's application for leave to appeal, finding that no reasonable prospects of success existed should the matter proceed. It determined that the petition did not present compelling circumstances warranting a reversal of previous decisions. Consequently, the eviction order stood, and Former Way was ordered to vacate the premises while incurring costs related to the appeal process, including counsel fees ([paras 42–43]).

In sum, the case underscores the critical importance of formalizing agreements through written contracts in franchise transactions as well as clarifying the parameters of arbitration as related to court jurisdiction. The court's ruling reinstates the principle that, absent a legitimate legal basis, continued occupancy in the realm of commercial property operations cannot be sustained.
