Herbal Zone v Infitech Technologies (204/2016) [2017] ZASCA 8

Upon realizing that Herbs Oils was marketing a competing product under the same name, Herbal Zone undertook several strategies to communicate to the public that their product was the authentic source.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 8

F – Facts

In Herbal Zone v Infitech Technologies, Mr. Hakim Herzallah, associated with Herbal Zone (Pty) Ltd (the first appellant), introduced a product named Phyto Andro, believed to enhance male sexual performance, into South Africa in 2006 ([1]). Mr. Herzallah claimed to be the majority shareholder of Herbal Zone International Sdn Bhd (a Malaysian company manufacturing the product). Infitech Technologies (the first respondent) was the sole distributor of Phyto Andro until the termination of their distribution agreement in 2014, after which competition arose from another distributor, Herbs Oils and Co (Pty) Ltd ([2]).

Upon realizing that Herbs Oils was marketing a competing product under the same name, Herbal Zone undertook several strategies to communicate to the public that their product was the authentic source. This included advertisements and direct communications emphasizing Herbs Oils' products as counterfeit ([3]-[5]). In retaliation, Infitech and Herbs Oils initiated legal proceedings against Herbal Zone, seeking an interdict against defamation, while Herbal Zone countered by claiming passing off ([7]).

The High Court, under Bozalek J, granted the interdicts sought by Infitech but dismissed the counter-application on grounds that Herbal Zone did not prove the reputation of Phyto Andro vested in them ([8]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues centered on:

  1. Passing Off: Did Herbal Zone establish sufficient reputation and goodwill in Phyto Andro to warrant protection against claims by Herbs Oils?
  2. Defamation: Were the statements made by Herbal Zone regarding Herbs Oils’ product being counterfeit defamatory, and could Herbs Oils rely on an interdict against future publications of such statements?

These issues arise from the need to balance rights to reputation in trade against freedom of expression in commercial speech ([31]-[39]).

R – Rules

The rules of law applicable to this case stem from the principles governing:

  1. Passing Off: Established in the case Capital Estate and General Agencies (Pty) Ltd v Holiday Inns Inc. which outlines the requirement of demonstrating a misrepresentation concerning the source of goods, reputation, and resultant damage ([9]). Here, misrepresentation and the existence of a reasonable likelihood of confusion or deception were critical to the passing off claim.
  2. Defamation: The court reiterated that an interdict against defamation is a serious remedy that restrains the exercise of freedom of speech, with Hix Networking Technologies v System Publishers (Pty) Ltd serving as precedent on how such claims should be approached—requiring a clear right and evidence on a balance of probabilities to succeed ([36]).

The court outlined the criteria for passing off, requiring proof of reputation, misrepresentation, and damage ([10],[29]), while also identifying the standards needed to establish defamation claims, particularly the need for the plaintiff to show that there was no valid defense available to the defendant ([33]-[38]).

A – Application

The application of law in the present case demonstrated a detailed evaluation of the evidence concerning the reputation associated with Phyto Andro. The court found that Herbal Zone failed to demonstrate that the reputation of Phyto Andro in South Africa rested solely with them rather than with Herbal Zone International, which led to the conclusion that the misrepresentation essential for a passing off claim was not established ([10]-[28]).

The court scrutinized the marketing strategies, distribution agreements, and affiliations between the various companies involved. It concluded that since Infitech’s role was limited to distribution and not ownership, it was improbable for them to claim rights in the mark ([12]-[15]). Consequently, Herbal Zone could not prove the required reputation necessary to support their passing off claim ([30]).

In relation to the defamation claim, the court carefully evaluated the statements made by Herbal Zone, determining that the validity of the statements—labeling the competitor's product as counterfeit—had not yet been legally settled. As Infitech contended that they held legitimate rights to market Phyto Andro, this created a genuine dispute of fact regarding the truth of Herbal Zone’s statements ([32]-[36]). The court reference to Hix Networking underscored the constitutional protection of free speech and elucidated the necessity for Herbal Zone to present a factual basis for any defenses available to them in a potential trial setting ([39]), ultimately denying the interdict sought by Infitech.

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that Herbal Zone's appeal against the High Court's dismissal of their counter-application for passing off must fail due to their inability to establish requisite reputation in Phyto Andro ([30]). Meanwhile, the defamation interdict granted by the High Court was set aside as the statements made were likely to have a factual defense based on the competitive nature of the products marketed and the ambiguities surrounding ownership rights in the trademark context ([41]).

The court ordered that the appeal succeed in part, dismissing both the application and counter-application with costs, reinforcing that each party should bear its own legal expenses associated with the appeal ([42]). This decision resonates with the critical balance between commercial interests and constitutional rights, emphasizing the importance of establishing clear rights to avoid undue restrictions on freedom of speech in trade.
