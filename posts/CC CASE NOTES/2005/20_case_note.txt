Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2005

# Case Note: Minister of Home Affairs v Fourie and Another; Lesbian and Gay Equality Project v Minister of Home Affairs

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–9], [33–41])

In the Constitutional Court of South Africa, the cases of *Minister of Home Affairs v Fourie and Another* (CCT 60/04) and *Lesbian and Gay Equality Project v Minister of Home Affairs* (CCT 10/05) collectively scrutinized the exclusion of same-sex couples from the institution of marriage. The primary applicants, Marié Adriaana Fourie and Cecelia Johanna Bonthuys, sought legal recognition of their relationship and the right to marry, challenging the common law definition of marriage as “the union of one man and one woman” ([para 1]).

The *Fourie* case reached the Pretoria High Court, where Roux J concluded that the marriage formula within section 30(1) of the Marriage Act, which framed marriage exclusively as a union between a man and a woman, was peremptory and thus denied the applicants the ability to marry legally ([para 6]). The applicants argued against this legal exclusion, claiming it constituted unfair discrimination under Section 9 of the Constitution, which prohibits discrimination on the basis of sexual orientation ([paras 4–5]).

Simultaneously, the *Lesbian and Gay Equality Project* initiated a direct challenge in the Johannesburg High Court against both the common law and section 30(1) of the Marriage Act, arguing that these provisions were unconstitutional because they infringed on the rights to equality and dignity ([para 34]). The Constitutional Court agreed to hear both cases to ensure comprehensive resolutions.

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 44–56])

The central issues examined by the Court encapsulated whether the exclusion of same-sex couples from marrying constituted unfair discrimination in violation of their constitutional rights to equality and dignity. Specifically, the court sought to determine:

1. Does the law deny equal protection to and discriminate unfairly against same-sex couples by not including them in the provisions of the Marriage Act?
2. If unfair discrimination is established, what would be the appropriate remedy?

The contested definitions of marriage straddled both societal values embedded in the historical fabric of South African law and the progressive aims of the 1996 Constitution ([para 45]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 9–10], [60–110])

The Court relied heavily on the Constitution, particularly sections 9(1) and 9(3), which mandate equality before the law and prohibit unfair discrimination based on sexual orientation. Previous judgments, including *National Coalition for Gay and Lesbian Equality v Minister of Justice* (1999), set essential jurisprudential foundations for arguing against discrimination based on sexual orientation, underpinning the claim for marriage equality ([paras 60–61]).

The Court also evaluated relevant international standards, including the Universal Declaration of Human Rights and the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights, which implicitly recognized the right of individuals to enter into partnerships akin to marriage, albeit not explicitly framed as such in international instruments ([para 100]). Additionally, comprehensive public engagement led by the South African Law Reform Commission illustrated a clear societal shift towards recognizing and normalizing same-sex unions ([para 115]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 49–107], [114–160])

Sachs J's analysis illuminated how the existing definitions of marriage discriminated against same-sex couples by excluding them from the rights and responsibilities conferred on heterosexual couples. The Court underscored the harm of maintaining an archaic and exclusionary view of marriage, which did not reflect modern understandings of family diversity. The ruling articulated that marriage's legal status is necessary for securing dignity, recognition, and social value attached to familial relationships ([paras 6–7]).

In scrutinizing possible remedies, the Court faced the delicate balance between judicial intervention and legislative responsibility. It acknowledged the historical context of the discrimination experienced by same-sex couples and proposed that immediate legal recognition should be afforded to these relationships. However, the declaration of invalidity concerning marriage laws was suspended for twelve months to allow Parliament the opportunity to enact appropriate reforms tailored to meet constitutional obligations ([para 112]). The ruling ultimately affirmed that same-sex couples are entitled to the same legal status and benefits through marriage without discriminatory barriers ([para 164]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 161–171])

The Constitutional Court ruled that the common law definition of marriage and the Marriage Act were unconstitutional to the extent that they barred same-sex couples from marrying. The Court emphasized that the failure to provide legal recognition to same-sex marriages constituted a violation of the right to equality, dignity, and implicit protection of private life ([para 161]). 

Moreover, the ratio decidendi stressed the necessity of societal inclusion and the promotion of human dignity without undermining the historical institution of marriage. The Court required that legislative changes be made within a year, yet allowed for the temporary application of the current law until Parliament acted ([para 138]). 

This landmark decision not only set a precedent in South African law but also echoed across the international stage, presenting a compelling argument for equal rights and recognition regardless of sexual orientation. The implications for South African society were profound, fostering an environment promoting social justice, equality, and a dismantling of antiquated legal barriers that transported historical prohibitions into a modern democratic framework. 

In summary, *Fourie* and *Lesbian and Gay Equality Project* represent seminal cases in the struggle for equality, reflecting the Constitution’s spirit and its role in fostering a pluralistic and inclusive society. The developments following this judgment necessitate continued engagement and legislative sensitivity to ensure rights are comprehensively recognized in practice.
