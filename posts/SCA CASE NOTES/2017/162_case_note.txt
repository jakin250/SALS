Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: Mathibela v The State [2017] ZASCA 162

## FIRAC Summary

### F - Facts

The appellant, **Jerry Bafana Peter Mathibela**, was convicted alongside two co-accused in the North West Division of the High Court at Garankuwa on **14 November 2005**. He faced charges of murder, as defined under section 51(1) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 (the CLA), and attempted robbery, resulting in a **life sentence for murder** and **seven years for attempted robbery** ([1]).

Following the trial, Mathibela's application for condonation for the late filing of his leave to appeal was dismissed. Subsequently, he petitioned the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which granted him leave to appeal against his convictions and sentences on **31 August 2016**. According to Rule 7 of the SCA Rules, he was required to file a notice of appeal within **one month**, but failed to do so, ultimately submitting it **ten months late** on **26 June 2017**, and the appeal record **eleven months late** on **11 July 2017**, leading to the appeal lapsing per Rule 8(3) ([1], [2]).

Mathibela sought to reinstate his appeal alongside an application for condonation for the late filing of the notice of appeal, appeal record, and heads of argument. In his founding affidavit, he alleged delays due to reliance on legal representation that had ceased to assist him after the trial, and he had been in communication with the Mahikeng Justice Centre regarding new legal representation. However, his explanation was vague and lacked specific details ([3]).

The State opposed the application primarily on the grounds of unsatisfactory explanations for the delays and the assertion that there were no prospects of success regarding the appeal. They highlighted that Mathibela had already filed multiple condonation applications, all of which were characterized by significant delays ([4]).

### I - Issues

The case centers around several legal issues:

1. Whether the appellant provided a satisfactory explanation for the extensive delays in filing the notice of appeal and appeal record.
2. Whether the appellant demonstrated reasonable prospects of success on the merits of his appeal.
3. What factors should be considered by the court in deciding an application for condonation in a criminal context ([4], [8]).

### R - Rules

The court evaluated several legal principles relevant to applications for condonation and reinstatement of appeals:

1. **Good Cause**: An applicant seeking condonation must show satisfactory and acceptable explanations for the delay (Mogorosi v State [2010] ZASCA 147) and demonstrate reasonable prospects of success in their appeal ([6]).

2. **Cautionary Approach**: In handling applications involving circumstantial evidence or single witness testimonies, courts are required to approach such evidence cautiously and assess credibility thoroughly (S v Sauls & others [1981] 3 SA 172 (A)) ([12]).

3. **Comprehensive Explanation**: Courts expect a full, detailed, and accurate account of the causes of delay alongside an evaluation of its effects to assess responsibility. The analysis involves factors such as the extent of non-compliance, prospects of success, the importance of the matter, and the convenience of the court ([8], [9]).

### A - Application

The SCA found that Mathibela failed to satisfactorily explain the excessive delays involved in his application process. His affidavit lacked necessary specifics about communications with the legal aid body, application timelines, and attempts to seek representation. The court noted that, even accounting for periods when the appellant was unrepresented, this did not excuse the lengthy duration of over **eight years** taken to address the appeal ([7], [9]).

The applicant's argument for incorrect procedural handling or exclusion of critical evidence was scrutinized and ultimately did not yield enough merit to signify valid prospects of succeeding in his appeal. Testimonies indicated that the state's case relied heavily on circumstantial evidence, particularly from a single witness, whose credibility was deemed reliable upon multiple evaluations throughout the process. The explanations presented by the appellant regarding alleged discrepancies in evidence collection procedures were also insufficient to undermine the conviction ([10], [13]).

Moreover, the court reiterated the settled legal maxim that solely presenting a weak explanation for delays in filing does not justify the reinstatement of an appeal. The absence of a proper prospect of success further solidified this position ([16]).

### C - Conclusion

Ultimately, the SCA ruled to **dismiss the application for condonation and the request to reinstate the appeal**. The lack of satisfactory reasoning for procedural delays, compounded by insufficient prospects of success, were determinative factors in the court’s decision ([16], [17]). The case underscores the necessity for appellants to adhere rigorously to procedural rules and the substantive expectation that they articulate cogent explanations for delays—factors that collectively ensure the integrity of the judicial process and the efficient administration of justice. 

This ruling serves as a cautionary tale concerning the importance of timely appeals in criminal law and illustrates the careful judicial scrutiny applied to condonation applications, reaffirming the court's role in maintaining procedural fairness and integrity.
