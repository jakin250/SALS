Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2008

# FIRAC Case Note: Merafong Demarcation Forum v President of the Republic of South Africa [2008] ZACC 10

## F – Facts

The case revolves around a challenge to the constitutionality of the Twelfth Amendment Act of 2005, which altered provincial boundaries in South Africa, specifically relocating a portion of the Merafong City Local Municipality from Gauteng to North West ([1]–[5]). The applicants included members from the Merafong community, represented by the Merafong Demarcation Forum, who argued that the Gauteng Provincial Legislature failed to facilitate public involvement as required by section 118(1)(a) of the Constitution during the legislative process for the Twelfth Amendment ([1]–[4]). They contended that the Gauteng Provincial Legislature acted irrationally by supporting the amendment without adequately considering the community's objections, which could threaten their service delivery and sense of identity as part of Gauteng ([29]–[35]).

The primary group involved, the applicants, consisted of various community members and organizations campaigning against the amendment. Their assertions claimed that a vast majority of Merafong’s population, primarily situated in Gauteng, opposed the municipality's incorporation into North West ([2]–[30]). A series of public consultations were conducted, but the committee's final decision to support the amendment appeared to disregard the community's objections ([11]–[44]).

## I – Issues

1. Whether the Gauteng Provincial Legislature failed to comply with its constitutional obligation to facilitate public involvement as required by section 118(1)(a) when considering and approving the Twelfth Amendment ([41]).
2. Whether the Gauteng Provincial Legislature acted irrationally by supporting the Bill, failing to exercise its legislative powers lawfully and rationally under the Constitution ([41]–[42]).

## R – Rules

The key constitutional provisions relevant to this case include:

1. **Section 74 of the Constitution**: Outlines the procedure for constitutional amendments, stating a two-thirds majority is required in the National Assembly and support from at least six provinces in the National Council of Provinces (NCOP) for amendments affecting provincial boundaries ([16]–[19]).
2. **Section 74(8)** states that if a Bill concerns only a specific province, the NCOP may not pass that Bill unless it is approved by the relevant provincial legislature ([18]–[19]).
3. **Section 118(1)(a)** requires provincial legislatures to facilitate public involvement in their legislative processes, reinforcing the importance of participatory democracy as explored in *Doctors for Life International v Speaker of the National Assembly* and *Matatiele* judgments. This emphasizes the necessity for a meaningful consultation process to maintain civic dignity and legitimacy of law-making ([19]–[24]).

## A – Application

The court undertook a detailed examination of the legislative conduct of the Gauteng Provincial Legislature, juxtaposing it with the standards of rationality and public involvement mandated by the Constitution.

### Public Involvement

Despite the assertion of inadequate public involvement, the court noted that:
- Public hearings were conducted, providing a platform for community input. However, the applicants argued that the process was superficial as key positions became evident prior to the hearings, concluding their voices did not meaningfully influence the outcome ([42]–[56]).
- The court assessed whether the Legislature acted rationally in its decision-making, considering community feedback, and concluded that mere failure to amend the legislation post-hearing did not constitute a failure to comply with constitutional requirements since the Legislature created avenues for public engagement ([56]).


### Rationality

The Gauteng Provincial Legislature argued that it acted on valid governmental interests in supporting the amendment. However, the court scrutinized:

1. **Change in Position**: The decision to support the Bill after initially recommending to include Merafong in Gauteng indicated a rational process; however, there was significant concern that this reversal was prompted by misconceptions of their legislative powers, highlighting the need for proper appreciation of section 74(8) ([62]–[105]).
2. **Implications of Veto**: The Legislature's concerns about potential repercussions for failing to support the Bill—such as complications regarding the local government elections—were found to misinterpret their constitutional power, which could lead to misinformed legislative decisions ([107]–[190]).

Ultimately, it was established that while the Legislature aimed at a legitimate purpose in abolishing cross-boundary municipalities, the decision to incorporate Merafong into North West failed the rationality test due to fundamental misunderstandings about its powers ([116]).

## C – Conclusion

The court dismissed the application for the validity of the Twelfth Amendment as the Gauteng Provincial Legislature failed to facilitate genuine public involvement and acted irrationally. Moseneke DCJ's judgment emphasized that the failure to adequately engage with public sentiment and the misapprehension of constitutional obligations rendered the legislative process constitutionally indefensible. The declaration of invalidity was deemed appropriate, with a suspension period of 18 months to afford the Legislature time to remedy the situation ([116]–[198]). The court highlighted that the continuing resentment and discontent in the Merafong community necessitated a re-evaluation of its status and boundaries, marking this case as significant for future considerations of provincial delineation and democratic engagement in South Africa. 

This case reaffirms the necessity for legislative bodies to uphold participatory democracy, promoting public engagement as a fundamental constitutional duty rather than a procedural formality.
