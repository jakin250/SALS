Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: Centre for Child Law and Others v Media 24 Limited and Others [2019] ZACC 46

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])**

The case involves an urgent application brought before the Constitutional Court of South Africa by the Centre for Child Law, a minor (referred to as KL), and several child protection organizations against major media entities (Media 24 Limited, Independent Newspapers (Pty) Limited, Times Media Group Limited) and governmental officials (Minister of Justice and Correctional Services; National Director of Public Prosecutions) regarding Section 154(3) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA). 

KL, a minor kidnapped at birth, was found and became embroiled in media coverage when her identity was at risk of being published as she turned 18, potentially exposing her as both a victim and a witness in the criminal proceedings against her abductor ([paras 5-7]). The applicants sought an interim order prohibiting the publication of her identity amidst fears that the CPA's anonymity protections would expire when she reached adulthood, as the current law did not extend protections to child victims beyond the age of 18 ([para 8]).

The High Court had granted an interim interdict against the publication of KL's identity but dismissed the application in Part B that asked for a permanent extension of protections under the CPA ([para 8]). This was subsequently appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal, which confirmed the High Court's ruling but declared Section 154(3) constitutionally invalid, highlighting irrationality in its exclusion of protections for child victims ([para 14]).

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 11–18])**

The central legal questions arising from the case were twofold:

1. **Scope of Protection:** Does Section 154(3) of the CPA provide absolute protection against the media disclosing the identities of child victims who have not yet testified or been formally called as witnesses in criminal proceedings? If not, is this limitation constitutionally valid?

2. **Ongoing Protection:** Should the anonymity protections for child victims of crime, as delineated in Section 154(3), extend into adulthood, or do these protections cease upon the individual turning 18? If so, is this constitutionally justifiable?

These questions necessitate a balancing act between the rights enshrined in the Constitution, including the right to equality, dignity, privacy, and the best interests of the child (Section 28(2)), against the principles of open justice and freedom of expression (Sections 16 and 20).

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 19–32])**

The court relied on various constitutional provisions and interpretations, including:

- **Constitution of South Africa, Section 9(1):** Guarantees equal protection under the law, which was deemed violated due to the exclusion of child victims from anonymity protections that are accorded to child witnesses and accused.
- **Constitution, Section 28(2):** Asserts that a child's best interests are paramount in all matters concerning them, positioning the court to analyze the failure to protect child victims adequately.
- **Constitution, Sections 10 and 14:** Addresses the rights to dignity and privacy specially implicating the welfare of child victims, necessitating protective measures against public exposure not afforded under existing legislation.

Furthermore, the court considered relevant case law, including past judgments regarding the interpretation of the CPA and the principles guiding the best interests of the child, particularly in the context of openness within the judicial process.

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 33–60])**

In its ruling, the Constitutional Court confirmed the Supreme Court of Appeal's declaration of invalidity regarding Section 154(3) of the CPA for failing to extend protections to child victims. The court found that the current section effectively discriminated against child victims by not affording them the same protections as child witnesses and accused ([paras 14, 33]).

The judgement extended to the need for ongoing protections, highlighting that the psychological and emotional vulnerabilities of child victims remain significant past their 18th birthday. Testimonies from expert witnesses illustrated that identification could perpetuate trauma, discourage reporting crimes, and hinder the rehabilitation of young offenders ([paras 39, 68]).

Moreover, the court reiterated that dignity and privacy concerns are amplified in the case of minors transitioning to adulthood, asserting that the absence of anonymity protections after turning 18 unjustly places the onus on vulnerable individuals who are often not in a position to advocate for themselves against powerful media entities ([paras 72, 83]).

The court implemented a reading-in approach permitting a temporary provision: Section 154(3) now mandates that ongoing protections be maintained unless a former child participant consents to their identity being disclosed or a court authorizes it, thereby mitigating potential harms to dignity and privacy ([paras 114-125]).

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 126–128])**

The court ultimately upheld the appeal, confirming that Section 154(3) of the CPA is constitutionally invalid for failing to provide ongoing anonymity protections for child victims. The declaration of constitutional invalidity was suspended for 24 months to allow Parliament to amend the law adequately. 

The implications of this ruling emphasize the necessity of protecting the identities of child victims not only during criminal proceedings but extending their protections into adulthood. This case reinforces the overarching constitutional principles prioritizing the rights of vulnerable individuals, particularly children, in the justice system, thereby ensuring their dignity and privacy are respected and preserved in public discourse. 

The ruling sets a significant precedent for future cases involving the intersection of media rights, child protections, and constitutional law in South Africa, mandating reforms that affect not only legislative frameworks but also societal attitudes towards child victims and the stigmatization they face.
