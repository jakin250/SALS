# Case Note: Hitler Adolf Klokow v Michael Boyton Sullivan  
**Case Number**: 410/04  
**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Judges**: Mpati DP, Cameron, Brand JJA, Nkabinde, Cachalia AJJA  
**Heard**: 6 September 2005  
**Delivered**: 29 September 2005  

## FIRAC Case Notes

### F – Facts  
The appellant, *Hitler Adolf Klokow*, filed a claim for the repayment of R250,000, which represented half of the purchase price for a liquor-licensed business he had entered into an agreement to buy from the respondent, *Michael Boyton Sullivan*. The agreement was deemed illegal and void due to non-compliance with the requirements set forth by the Liquor Act 27 of 1989 (hereafter referred to as "the Act") ([paras 1–2]). 

On 4 September 2000, Klokow paid the sum to Sullivan as part of a total purchase price of R500,000 for an “adult entertainment” business that provided liquor to patrons. On 5 September 2000, Klokow took control of the business but returned it to Sullivan on 18 October 2000, ultimately initiating legal proceedings to recover the R250,000 ([para 2]). 

The matter was first adjudicated by Hartzenberg J in the Transvaal Provincial Division, who ruled in favor of Klokow but was later appealed by Sullivan. The full court subsequently upheld Sullivan’s appeal, leading Klokow to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 3–4]). 

Both parties acknowledged that neither had complied with the application requirements under the Act. Specifically, Sullivan failed to obtain the necessary permission from the Chairperson of the Liquor Board before permitting Klokow to procure a controlling interest in the business, thus violating Section 38(1) of the Act ([paras 5–6]).

### I – Issues  
The legal questions presented in this case include: 
1. Does Section 38(1) of the Act apply to the agreement between the parties?
2. If applicable, does non-compliance with Section 38(1) render the agreement void?
3. If the agreement is void, can any part of it be severable from the whole?
4. Does Klokow have a right to recover the R250,000 paid, despite both parties being in pari delicto? ([paras 8–9]).

### R – Rules  
The legal principles governing this case are rooted in contract law and statutory interpretation, specifically:
- **Section 38(1)** of the Liquor Act, which prohibits a liquor license holder from allowing another person to procure a controlling interest without permission from the Chairperson ([para 4]).
- **Section 148** of the Act indicates that any contract containing a provision whereby a person purports to relinquish a right in terms of the Act shall be void ([para 10]).
- The rule of **in pari delicto potior est conditio defendentis** holds that where two parties are equally at fault for a legal wrong, the law does not assist either party ([para 12]).

Additionally, the doctrine developed in **Jajbhay v Cassim** ([1939] AD 537) acknowledges that while the par delictum rule generally denies relief to a plaintiff found to be equally guilty, courts may relax this rule where preventing injustice or enforcing public policy necessitates equitable relief ([paras 18–19]).

### A – Application  
Hartzenberg J initially ruled that since Sullivan bore the responsibility of obtaining the Chairperson’s consent, the par delictum rule did not apply, and thus Klokow had valid grounds for recovery. However, the full court subsequently interpreted the situation differently, asserting that both parties were in pari delicto and that since the agreement was tainted by illegality, Klokow could not recover the funds ([paras 9–19]).

The full court concluded that Klokow failed to plead "further facts" that would warrant the relaxation of the par delictum rule. It held that while Klokow and Sullivan shared moral blame for the contract's illegality, Klokow had not presented the necessary arguments for why public policy considerations should override the general bar against recovery in cases of equal fault ([paras 16–20]).

Cachalia AJA critiqued this reasoning, noting that the equities favored Klokow's argument as Sullivan retained both the money and the business while he unjustly enriched himself at Klokow’s expense. The court emphasized that public policy considerations should allow for the relaxation of the par delictum rule to prevent undeserved enrichment ([paras 26–28]).

### C – Conclusion  
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the full court erred in its determination regarding the par delictum rule. It reinstated Klokow's claim, stating that public policy would support the recovery of the R250,000 paid, as the mechanics of justice necessitated a return to the status quo. The appeal was granted with costs, overturning the full court's ruling and restoring Klokow's entitlement to the funds ([paras 29]).

The ratio decidendi of this case emphasizes the principle that while courts generally refrain from aiding parties to illegal contracts, they possess discretion to ensure justice prevails where one party is unjustly enriched at the expense of another, thereby reinforcing the need for a careful balance of legal technicalities, moral culpability, and public policy ([para 19]).
