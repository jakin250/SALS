Case Note: Mhlontlo Local Municipality & 2 Others v Ngcangula and Another (Case No 1154/2022) [2024] ZASCA 5

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 5 ### FIRAC Method --- ### F – Facts The

Case Note: Mhlontlo Local Municipality & 2 Others v Ngcangula and Another (Case No 1154/2022) [2024] ZASCA 5

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case arises from proceedings initiated by two employees of the Mhlontlo Local Municipality, Gcinikhaya Ngcangula and Malibongwe Nqeketho, against their employer in the High Court of the Eastern Cape. They contended that deductions made from their December 2020 salaries were unlawful under section 34(1) of the Basic Conditions of Employment Act 75 of 1997 (the Employment Act) ([paras 1-2]).

The municipality had initially granted a 2.5% notch increase in salaries to its employees, including the respondents, through a resolution passed on 25 March 2019 (Resolution No. 01-18/19)[[para 3]. Later, the municipality issued a letter demanding repayment of the salary increment, stating that it was paid in error following an audit identifying it as an irregular expense. The municipality argued that the respondents were not entitled to the increase due to issues with their salary grading ([paras 4-5]).

The High Court ruled in favor of the respondents, determining that the municipality unlawfully reduced their salaries and ordered the reinstatement of the deducted amounts ([para 6]). Following the municipality's subsequent application for leave to appeal and various procedural applications, the matter was brought before the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 8-10]).

I – Issues

The central legal issues in this case were:

  1. The jurisdiction of the High Court to adjudicate claims concerning the employment contracts of the respondents under section 77(3) of the Employment Act ([paras 20-21]).
  2. Whether the High Court correctly determined that the municipality's withdrawal of the 2.5% notch increment constituted a breach of the employment contract ([paras 19, 26]).
  3. The applicability of the doctrine of peremption regarding the municipality's appeal after making payments to the respondents ([paras 12-13]).
  4. Whether the appeal was moot due to subsequent events post the High Court's order ([paras 17-18]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal principles included:

  • Jurisdiction: The Employment Act, particularly section 77(3), provides concurrent jurisdiction to the Labour Court and civil courts regarding matters concerning employment contracts, irrespective of the presence of basic conditions of employment ([paras 20-21]).
  • Employment Rights: Section 34(1) of the Employment Act prohibits employers from making deductions from an employee’s remuneration unless expressly agreed upon in writing ([para 2]).
  • Peremption: It denotes the abandonment of an appeal where a party's conduct demonstrates a clear intention not to appeal. The onus lies with the party claiming peremption to provide evidence of unequivocal conduct indicating acquiescence to a judgment ([paras 12-13]).
  • Mootness: A case is moot if no live controversy exists; courts avoid adjudicating matters that offer merely academic guidance ([para 17]).

A – Application

  1. Jurisdiction of the High Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal affirmed that the High Court had jurisdiction to hear the claims of the respondents based on their pleadings, which indicated a breach of their employment contracts. The resolution of employment-related disputes does not exclusively fall under the Labour Court’s purview, allowing civil courts to adjudicate if the relevant contractual issues are appropriately framed ([paras 22-24]).
  2. Breach of Contract: The appellants contended that the respondents were not entitled to the notch increment, arguing that it was a payment made erroneously. The respondents’ failure to provide sufficient evidence that the increment formed a term of their employment contracts was critical. The Supreme Court determined that the payments made were in error and noted that simply receiving an erroneous payment does not create an entitlement to that payment going forward ([paras 26-30]).
  3. Doctrine of Peremption: The respondents failed to establish that the municipality’s payment of amounts post-judgment constituted an abandonment of its appeal. The Court found that the municipality did not manifest an intention to waive its right to contest the High Court's judgment. Thus, the appeal was not perempted ([paras 12-15]).
  4. Mootness Argument: The Supreme Court rejected the mootness argument, asserting that a "live controversy" persisted due to the ongoing liabilities under the High Court’s order and the implications of the legality of the payments made by the municipality ([paras 17-18]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court concluded by reinstating the appeal, holding that the High Court had overstepped its jurisdiction by determining the entitlement to the 2.5% salary increase without adequate foundation in the employment contracts of the respondents. The Court set aside the High Court’s order and substituted it with an order dismissing the applicants' applications ([para 35]).

The Court’s findings bear significant implications for the interpretation of the Employment Act concerning the rights of employers to rectify perceived overpayments and the concurrent jurisdictional authority of civil and labor courts in employment disputes. Furthermore, this judgment reinforces the necessity for employees to substantiate claims regarding entitlements to benefits purportedly derived from employment contracts while clarifying the rigorous standards needed to claim peremption or mootness in employment disputes.
