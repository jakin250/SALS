Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: Leysath v Legal Practitioners’ Fidelity Fund Board of Control (770/2021)

**Citation:** Leysath v Legal Practitioners’ Fidelity Fund Board of Control (770/2021) [2022] ZASCA 115 (28 July 2022)

## F – Facts
The case arises from an appeal against the dismissal of Mr. Lindon Clifford Leysath’s application for reimbursement from the Legal Practitioners’ Fidelity Fund Board of Control, which he claimed under section 26(a) of the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979 ([para 1]). Mr. Leysath, a practising advocate, alleged funds he rendered as fees were entrusted to an attorney’s firm (M F Martins Costa Attorneys) but were misappropriated by its managing partner, Mr. Costa. The claim involved R472,666, which he asserted had been held in trust ([paras 2-6]).

The Legal Practice Council identified a substantial deficit in Costa Attorneys' trust account, leading to Mr. Costa's abscondment ([para 7]). Leysath claimed that clients had deposited money for his fees. However, he acknowledged that in some instances, he lacked proof of client payments ([paras 10-12]). A high court judge permitted an appeal based on potential legal errors relating to the interpretation of legal precedents concerning attorney-client relationships and fiduciary duties ([paras 2, 9]).

## I – Issues
The central legal issues presented in this case were:

1. Whether the appellant sufficiently established that funds were entrusted to Costa Attorneys on his behalf as required by section 26(a) of the Attorneys Act ([para 24]).
2. Whether the high court correctly determined that there existed no direct claim for reimbursement by the appellant against the Fidelity Fund ([para 16]).
3. Whether the appellant could rely on hearsay evidence to substantiate his claims ([para 41]).

## R – Rules
The applicable laws and legal doctrines include:

- **Section 26(a) of the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979**: Outlines conditions under which a claimant may be reimbursed for losses incurred due to theft by an attorney from whom the claimant entrusted funds ([para 23]).
- **Industrial and Commercial Factors (Pty) Ltd v The Attorneys’ Fidelity Fund Board of Control**: Established precedent on what constitutes an "entrustment," clarifying that not all funds received in trust automatically qualify ([para 25]).
- **British Kaffrarian Savings Bank Society v Attorneys, Notaries and Conveyancers Fidelity Guarantee Fund Board of Control**: Discussed the two elements of entrustment, stating it involves both possession and an obligation to act for the benefit of another ([para 26]).

## A – Application
In its judgment, the Supreme Court of Appeal highlighted that the onus rested on Leysath to prove that funds were entrusted to Costa Attorneys on his behalf as per section 26 ([para 24]). The court determined that simply alleging clients deposited money for Leysath's fees did not fulfill this requirement. The high court had assumed, incorrectly, that funds were deposited as cover for counsel’s fees, despite respondent’s clear denial of such assertions ([paras 28-29]). 

Additionally, the court examined the sufficiency of the evidence provided by Leysath, revealing it was largely hearsay. Leysath failed to obtain confirming affidavits from clients regarding their payments, relying instead on self-serving assertions and mere indications of what clients purportedly told him ([paras 30-39]). This was insufficient to establish a basis for reimbursement under section 26(a).

Regarding the hearsay evidence, the court concluded that it did not possess probative value and that admitting such evidence would cause serious prejudice to the respondent, fundamentally undermining the purpose of the fidelity funds ([paras 40-41]). Leysath's inability to produce direct evidence of his claims ultimately led to the dismissal of his appeal.

## C – Conclusion
The appeal was dismissed with costs ([para 43]). The court affirmed that Leysath did not satisfy the legal requirements to substantiate his claims under section 26(a) of the Attorneys Act. The ruling emphasized the necessity for concrete evidence in claims for pecuniary loss due to theft by attorneys, reinforcing the fidelity fund’s purpose of providing redress only to those who can prove direct entrustment of funds. This case delineates the boundaries of a claimant's obligation to demonstrate loss and the evidentiary hurdles that must be overcome to succeed in claims against legal fidelity funds, further affirming the legal principles dictating the fiduciary relationships in attorney-client interactions. 

This outcome not only upholds the fidelity fund's integrity but also clarifies the legal standing regarding the relationship between attorneys, advocates, and clients in financial matters.
