Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

## FIRAC Case Note

### Case Title:
National Commissioner of the South African Police Service v Southern African Human Rights Litigation Centre (485/2012) [2013] ZASCA 168

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–15])
The case concerns a review of decisions made by the Acting National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP), the Head of the Priority Crimes Litigation Unit (HPCLU), and the Acting National Commissioner of the South African Police Service (SAPS), regarding the refusal to investigate alleged crimes against humanity committed by Zimbabwean officials against Zimbabwean citizens. The Southern African Human Rights Litigation Centre (SALC) and the Zimbabwe Exiles Forum (ZEF) filed an application with the North Gauteng High Court in December 2009, after the NDPP communicated to them on 19 June 2009 that the SAPS would not initiate an investigation into claims of widespread torture.

The SALC's initial memorandum, submitted on 16 March 2008, detailed numerous incidents of torture and suggested the involvement of senior Zimbabwean officials. The High Court found the refusal to investigate to be unlawful, inconsistently aligned with the Constitution, and declared it invalid ([para 1]–[7]). The NDPP and the Commissioner sought leave to appeal against the High Court’s decision, leading to this case in the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 3]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 5, 32])
The core issue revolves around the investigative powers of the SAPS and the NPA, particularly whether they hold competence to investigate crimes against humanity allegedly committed outside South Africa. The legal questions posed include:

1. Do the SAPS and the NPA possess the authority to investigate crimes against humanity occurring extraterritorially, specifically when the alleged perpetrators are not present in South Africa?
2. To what extent does the ICC Act allow for the investigation of such crimes, and what jurisdictional implications arise from the interpretation of its provisions?

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 32–38, 49–50])
The court primarily analyzed the Implementation of the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court Act 27 of 2002 (the ICC Act), alongside relevant constitutional and statutory provisions, including Sections 205 and 13 of the South African Police Service Act 68 of 1995 and the National Prosecuting Authority Act 32 of 1998.

Key principles governing jurisdiction under international law were highlighted, particularly its core tenet of state sovereignty, which asserts that countries may legislate and enforce laws primarily within their territories ([paras 32]-[34]). The Rome Statute established a duty for states to exercise jurisdiction over crimes against humanity, including provisions permitting domestic prosecution irrespective of where the crimes occurred ([para 42]).

Section 4 of the ICC Act specifically allows South African courts to exercise jurisdiction over crimes committed outside its territory under certain conditions, notably on the basis of the nationality of the perpetrator or victim, or their presence in South Africa ([para 49]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 54–70])
The Supreme Court of Appeal emphasized that the investigative authority of the SAPS is not limited by the location of the crime or the presence of the accused. While the appellants maintained that an investigation cannot commence without the alleged perpetrator being present in South Africa, the court rejected this narrow interpretation of Section 4 of the ICC Act. It affirmed that the Act criminalizes acts committed abroad and establishes conditions under which South African courts may exercise jurisdiction over such acts ([paras 50–56]).

Furthermore, the court found the arguments concerning diplomatic and practical constraints on initiating investigations to be unsubstantiated. It recognized the substantial evidence presented by the SALC and ZEF, which warrant further investigation, regardless of any potential impact on relations with Zimbabwe. This reasoning supported the conclusion that a proactive investigation into alleged crimes against humanity is a responsibility[2] incumbent upon the SAPS and NPA, reaffirming their duty to uphold human rights standards ([paras 56–67]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 68–70])
The court’s final ruling dismissed the appeal by the SAPS and the NDPP, reinstating the High Court's order that the SAPS is empowered and obliged to commence investigations into the alleged crimes against humanity irrespective of the alleged perpetrators' presence within South Africa. The ratio decidendi underscores the responsibilities of the South African police to act on human rights violations, aligning domestic law with international obligations stemming from the ICC.

This case sets a significant precedent on the extraterritorial application of South African criminal law and reinforces the importance of investigating crimes against humanity, thereby contributing to global efforts against impunity. The decision mandates domestic coordination with international standards and reaffirms South Africa's commitment to uphold its obligations under the Rome Statute, marking a pivotal moment in the intersection of domestic law and international human rights enforcement.
