Minister of Tourism and Others v Afriforum NPC and Another [2023] ZACC 7

The necessity for the Tourism Relief Fund was spurred by the economic fallout from the national lockdowns instituted in March 2020 due to Covid-19, which had substantial adverse effects on businesses within the tourism sector ([6]–[8]).

Constitutional Court 2023 [2023] ZACC 7

F - Facts

The case revolves around an application brought by the Minister of Tourism, the Department of Tourism, and the Director-General (collectively referred to as the "Applicants") seeking leave to appeal a judgment from the Supreme Court of Appeal. This judgment was primarily focused on the Minister's obligation or entitlement to include Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment (B-BBEE) status as a selection criterion for beneficiaries of the Tourism Relief Fund intended for small, micro, and medium-sized enterprises (SMMEs) affected by the Covid-19 pandemic ([1]–[2]).

The necessity for the Tourism Relief Fund was spurred by the economic fallout from the national lockdowns instituted in March 2020 due to Covid-19, which had substantial adverse effects on businesses within the tourism sector ([6]–[8]). The Minister established a fund with R200 million allocated for grants of R50,000 to qualifying SMMEs to help alleviate the economic consequences ([8]). The selection criteria were guided by the Tourism B-BBEE Code of Good Practice, which had been formulated under the umbrella of the B-BBEE Act ([9]).

Afriforum and Solidarity Trade Union (the "Respondents") contested the inclusion of race-based criteria, asserting that the Minister had overstepped her authority; they sought an order to set aside the selection criteria ([11]). The High Court initially dismissed their application, leading the Respondents to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal, which ruled in their favor, declaring the Minister’s inclusion of B-BBEE criteria unlawful ([13]–[15]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the Minister was acting administratively and subjected her actions to the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA). It held that she was not legally bound to include the B-BBEE criteria when directing eligibility for funds ([14]).

The Applicants sought to appeal this decision at the Constitutional Court, arguing that the matter raised important constitutional questions and was not moot, although the Respondents contended that the state of disaster had terminated, rendering the issue irrelevant ([16]–[17]).

I - Issues

The core issues in this case are:

  1. Whether the Minister of Tourism was legally obliged or entitled to include B-BBEE status as a criterion for the Tourism Relief Fund ([2], [16]).
  2. The question of mootness: whether the appeal could proceed given the termination of the state of disaster and the exhaustion of the Fund ([22]).

R - Rules

  1. Disaster Management Act 57 of 2002: This legislation provides for the management of disasters in South Africa, outlining powers for the Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs to declare states of disaster and make regulations ([3]–[5]).
  2. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000: This Act governs the legality of administrative actions taken by public officials, allowing for review when decisions are based on errors of law ([14]).
  3. Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996:
  • Section 9 (Equality): Asserts that everyone is equal before the law and allows for measures to protect or advance those disadvantaged by unfair discrimination ([17]).

A - Application

In applying the rules to the facts, the Constitutional Court considered:

  • The Minister's reliance on the B-BBEE criteria as an integral part of transforming the tourism industry versus the arguments presented by the Respondents regarding the legality and fairness of such criteria in the context of the Covid-19 relief ([12]).
  • The Supreme Court of Appeal had established that the Minister was not legally required to apply the B-BBEE criteria, which created confusion in both lower courts ([14][15]).

Despite these issues, the Court found that the matter was moot because the state of disaster had ended, and the funds had already been disbursed ([22]). The potential for future applications of the B-BBEE criteria under similar circumstances was acknowledged; however, the Court expressed reluctance to entertain moot issues that offer no practical guidance or resolution ([26]–[27]).

C - Conclusion

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court refused the application for leave to appeal and ordered the Applicants to pay the costs of the Respondents ([29]). The decision emphasized the principle of mootness and the necessity for live controversies in constitutional litigation. Although the judgment fostered preliminary discussion on the intersection of B-BBEE policy within emergency funding contexts, the Court indicated a future preference for addressing matters with tangible relevance. This ruling underlines the increasing caution of the Court concerning engagements with moot disputes, particularly as its own backlog of cases grows ([27]).
