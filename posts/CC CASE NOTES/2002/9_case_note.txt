Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2002

# FIRAC Case Note on *The National Director of Public Prosecutions & Others v Yasien Mac Mohamed NO & Others* [CCT 13/02]

## F - Facts

The Constitutional Court of South Africa, in *The National Director of Public Prosecutions v Yasien Mac Mohamed NO & Others* [2002] ZACC 11, addressed the constitutional validity of section 38 of the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 ("the Act"). The National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) and the Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development appealed an order of the Witwatersrand High Court, which declared section 38 unconstitutional to the extent that it required ex parte applications for preservation orders without providing for a rule nisi to call upon interested parties ([1]–[3]).

The High Court had found that the provision unjustifiably limited the right to a fair hearing guaranteed by section 34 of the Constitution, as it allowed the NDPP to seek preservation orders without notifying affected parties ([4]–[5]). The original preservation of property order was granted on an ex parte basis, which ultimately led to the respondents counter-applying for a declaration of unconstitutionality of several sections of Chapter 6 of the Act ([6]–[9]).

The High Court's full order also invalidated any preservation of property orders made under section 38 that did not allow interested parties to show cause why such orders should not be made ([3]). The appellants sought confirmation of the High Court's ruling under section 172(2) of the Constitution, leading to this appeal ([3]–[4]).

## I - Issues

The principal issue before the Constitutional Court was whether section 38 of the Prevention of Organised Crime Act unjustifiably limited the fair hearing right as per section 34 of the Constitution, specifically in relation to ex parte applications for preservation of property orders ([23]). The Court also considered a broader question regarding the High Court’s authority to declare a section unconstitutional based solely on a procedural objection, as well as the appropriateness of its remedial actions ([25]–[30]).

## R - Rules

The relevant legal frameworks included:

1. **Section 34 of the Constitution**: Guarantees the right to a fair public hearing in court ([4]).
2. **Section 38 of the Prevention of Organised Crime Act**: Details the process and requirements for the NDPP to obtain preservation orders ([1]).
3. **Section 172 of the Constitution**: Establishes the Constitutional Court's power to confirm any order of constitutional invalidity made by other courts, which includes the need for any declared invalidity to be confirmed by the Constitutional Court ([10]).

In addition, the principles regarding constitutional interpretation and the appropriate remedial action for unconstitutional provisions were pivotal. The Court noted that a "reading in" of missing provisions is a viable remedy when an omission renders a provision unconstitutional ([27]–[28]).

## A - Application

The Court applied the rules to the facts of the case by examining whether section 38's procedural limitations indeed limited the fair hearing rights as claimed. It scrutinized the High Court’s judgment, which broadly interpreted the constitutional challenge to only a facet of section 38’s procedure without addressing the entirety of Chapter 6 of the Act, or the serious implications for affected parties ([26]–[31]).

The Court found that the High Court had erred in its approach by deciding on a narrow procedural issue while neglecting the broader constitutional challenge raised by the respondents. It emphasized that comprehensive resolutions must be provided to ensure constitutional rights are upheld and that partial decisions did not adequately resolve the overarching disputes ([32]–[34]).

Additionally, the Court noted that the High Court should have considered the "reading in" option, which would allow for a more constitutionally compliant mechanism to be included in section 38, enabling a rule nisi to be issued and providing affected parties the opportunity to contest preservation orders ([29]–[30]).

Thus, any contention regarding the constitutionality of the entire Chapter 6 of the Act also needed to be duly considered, which was unaddressed and rendered the High Court’s resolution incomplete ([31]–[35]).

## C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded that the High Court's order declaring section 38 unconstitutional was flawed due to its narrow focus and failure to address the full breadth of the constitutional challenges to Chapter 6 of the Act. The Court set aside the High Court's order and referred the matter back for comprehensive consideration regarding the constitutionality of the entire Chapter 6, thus preserving the rights of the affected parties while also ensuring that the legislative scheme's integrity could be evaluated in its entirety ([39]).

The decision reinforced the principle that when a constitutional challenge is broad, courts must address all facets to ensure justice is served and rights are not merely upheld in partiality. Costs were reserved in the cause, emphasizing the ongoing need for an equitable resolution ([39]). 

This judgment elucidates key procedural safeguards necessary in the context of property preservation actions and the broader implications for the enforcement of constitutional rights in the enforcement of public law.
