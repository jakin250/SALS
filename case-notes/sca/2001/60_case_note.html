Supreme Court of Appeal

Levi Strauss & Co v Coconut Trouser Manufacturers (Pty) Ltd

The appeal arises from competing applications for the registration of the trademark "Dockers" under the Trade Marks Act 62 of 1963 ([1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2001 1963 ([1]). 2

Case Title: Levi Strauss & Co v Coconut Trouser Manufacturers (Pty) Ltd

Case No: 274/99

Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

Judgment Delivered: 17 May 2001

F - Facts

  1. The appeal arises from competing applications for the registration of the trademark "Dockers" under the Trade Marks Act 62 of 1963 ([1]).
  2. The appellant, Levi Strauss & Co ("Levi Strauss"), is a prominent American clothing manufacturer that commenced marketing its "Dockers" brand in 1986 and initiated international registrations in 1987 ([1]).
  3. Prior to Levi Strauss entering the South African market, local businessman Mr. Chairman Nathoo filed a trademark application in September 1988 for "Dockers," which was registered under number 88/8163 ([1]).
  4. Levi Strauss submitted its trademark applications for "Dockers" on 9 August 1989 ([2]).
  5. The Trade Marks Registrar was called to determine the rights of both parties under section 17(3) of the Trade Marks Act, due to the resemblance of the trademarks [2].
  6. The Registrar initially ruled in favor of Levi Strauss; however, the ruling was appealed by Coconut (the successor to Nathoo) and subsequently upheld by a Full Court of the Transvaal Provincial Division, which accepted Coconut’s application ([4]).
  7. The issue arose during Coconut's application, where Nathoo sought to substitute Coconut for himself as the applicant, a decision made without notifying Levi Strauss ([5]).

I - Issues

  1. The core issue was whether the trade mark applications by Levi Strauss were entitled to precedence under the 1963 Act, given the substitution of Coconut for Nathoo ([6]).
  2. The appeal also raised questions surrounding the validity of the substitution of applicants and the implications of initial rights to the trademark based on intention to use ([8], [12]).

R - Rules

  1. The determination of rights concerning trademark applications is expressly governed by section 17(3) of the Trade Marks Act 62 of 1963, which outlines the procedure when there are competing applications that may cause confusion ([2]).
  2. Section 20(1) stipulates that the trademark application requires evidence of the applicant’s intention to use the mark ([7]).
  3. Substitution of applicants may be permitted under sections 49(7) and 56(2) of the Trade Marks Act, yet any substitution must follow the stipulations set by the Act ([7]).
  4. The Full Court's reliance on section 20(9) was critiqued for creating a retrospective application effect that was inconsistent with existing legal principles ([9-10]).

A - Application

  1. The judgment critically examined the evidence presented relating to Nathoo’s intent to use the "Dockers" mark, concluding that he lacked genuine intention to do so when filing the application ([5],[6]).
  2. Levi Strauss's application for the trademark was deemed superior because it included an explicit assertion of intention to use the mark, which was uncontested by Nathoo ([12]).
  3. The Registrar's decision to allow the substitution of Coconut for Nathoo was cited as fundamentally flawed. The arguments presented by Coconut's counsel regarding the substitution's retrospective effect were dismissed due to statutory misinterpretation ([9-11]).
  4. The analysis further clarified that amendments in trademark applications do not create jurisdictional facts, such as the intention to use, which did not exist under Nathoo’s initial application ([11]).

C - Conclusion

  1. The Supreme Court of Appeal affirmed Levi Strauss’s appeal, ruling that it had precedence given Nathoo's lack of intent to use the "Dockers" mark at the time of applying for registration ([14]).
  2. The judgment highlighted the necessity of proving not only the filing of an application but also genuine intent to use the trademark, solidifying the precedent that misleading claims undermine an application’s legitimacy ([12],[14]).
  3. The court concluded that the Full Court’s decision to accept Coconut's application was flawed and set aside its ruling, awarding costs to Levi Strauss, encompassing the costs for two counsel ([14]).
  4. The implications of the judgment encompassed a reinforcement of the importance of factual truth in trademark applications and the protection of established brand rights against improper conduct in the registration process.

This case note provides a comprehensive view of the legal intricacies surrounding trademark rights and the doctrinal principles applicable to trademark registration disputes in South Africa, particularly under the Trade Marks Act 62 of 1963.
