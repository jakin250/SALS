Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

### Case Note: Lewarne v Fochem International (Pty) Ltd (1073/18) [2019] ZASCA 114

#### FIRAC Case Note

#### F – Facts
The case arises from an employment dispute between **Cheryll Lewarne** (the appellant) and **Fochem International (Pty) Ltd** (the respondent). Lewarne was employed by Fochem as a Financial Manager for approximately eight years and subsequently became a director, earning a gross monthly salary of R75,000 and a 13th cheque payable annually by December 20 ([2]). In December 2016, she received only R50,000 of the expected 13th cheque, leading to the appellant filing an application on May 5, 2017, to claim the balance and outstanding salaries for January to April 2017 ([4]).

The matter escalated when the respondent accused Lewarne of various improprieties and suspended her on January 12, 2017. In response to demands for her owed salary, the respondent claimed these had been offset against Lewarne's alleged debt to the company ([2], [3]). The appellant's application was dismissed on the grounds of jurisdiction by the High Court, which held that the Labour Court alone had the jurisdiction over employment contract matters under section 77(1) of the Basic Conditions of Employment Act (BCEA) 75 of 1997 ([5], [6]).

#### I – Issues
The key legal question in this appeal was whether the Gauteng Division of the High Court had jurisdiction to hear the appellant's application for non-payment of salary and other remuneration when the respondent contended that such matters were exclusive to the Labour Court in accordance with section 77 of the BCEA ([1], [5]). 

#### R – Rules
The case primarily involves the construction of sections 77(1) and 77(3) of the BCEA. Section 77(1) states that the Labour Court has exclusive jurisdiction regarding all matters under the Act, while section 77(3) confers concurrent jurisdiction on civil courts over matters concerning employment contracts, irrespective of any basic condition of employment constituting terms of that contract ([6]). The legal framework established by the Labour Court and Labour Appeal Court notably refines the notion of “exclusive jurisdiction,” emphasizing that it pertains to matters explicitly requiring resolution under BCEA provisions ([7], [8]).

Moreover, applicable precedent includes *Fedlife Assurance Ltd v Wolfaardt*, wherein it was clarified that an identifiable common law claim can exist outside the exclusive jurisdiction of the Labour Court if it does not squarely fall under a Labour Court's purview ([9]).

#### A – Application
In analyzing the case, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) emphasized the need to examine the pleadings to determine jurisdiction ([7]). The nature of Lewarne's claim was fundamentally about remuneration owed under her employment contract, thus falling within the concurrent jurisdiction established by section 77(3) of the BCEA ([8]). The SCA indicated that the appellant’s action was not based solely on accusations of misconduct or the legality of deductions as asserted by the respondent but instead on the respondent's failure to comply with contractual obligations regarding salary payments.

The High Court’s reliance on the minority judgment in *Fedlife Assurance* was critiqued, where the majority contextually framed the claim as a common law claim for damages, creating grounds for non-exclusivity of the Labour Court ([9]). The SCA found that the lower court had misapprehended the essential nature of the action by neglecting to appreciate that the matter was fundamentally about unpaid salaries and contractual claims, irrespective of the respondent’s stated reasons for withholding payments ([10]).

In conclusion, the SCA found that the High Court erred in its jurisdictional ruling and accordingly overturned its decision, thereby affirming the concurrent jurisdiction of the civil courts over Lewarne's claim for the unpaid remuneration and compelling the respondent to make the requisite payments alongside interest and costs ([10]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, confirming that the Gauteng Division of the High Court had jurisdiction to hear Lewarne's claim. The ruling underscored a pivotal interpretation of the BCEA, reaffirming that employment disputes involving remuneration can indeed be adjudicated within the civil courts alongside the Labour Court ([10]). The decision affirms that jurisdictional questions must be resolved through an accurate understanding of the nature of the claim, as established in precedent and the specific provisions of the BCEA.

Thus, this case significantly clarifies jurisdictional boundaries, reinforcing the position that the Labour Court does not possess exclusive jurisdiction over claims rooted in common law and contractual obligations regarding payment, thereby broadening the avenues available for employees to seek redress for unpaid salaries and employment-related claims.
