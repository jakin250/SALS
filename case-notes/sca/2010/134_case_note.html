Supreme Court of Appeal

Sello v Grobler (623/09) [2010] ZASCA 134

Under the guise of a controlled purchase, a female inspector was tasked with buying restricted medications—Stilpain and Stilnox—from Sello’s pharmacy.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2010 [2010] ZASCA 134

F – Facts

The case of Sello v Grobler arose from events occurring on 8 July 2008 when the appellant, Abram Sello, a pharmacist and owner of Lake Field Pharmacy in Benoni, was subject to a search and seizure operation led by the South African Police Services (SAPS) and inspectors from the Medicines Regulatory Affairs Inspectorate (MRAI) ([paras 1-2]). The operation originated from an allegation that Sello was illegally selling scheduled medicines without prescriptions ([para 2]).

Under the guise of a controlled purchase, a female inspector was tasked with buying restricted medications—Stilpain and Stilnox—from Sello’s pharmacy. The purchase was successfully made without presenting a prescription and involved proper monetary compensation ([paras 3-4]). Upon Sello’s arrival at the pharmacy, he learned of the operation, which led to the police searching the premises and seizing various items, including medicines and cash, some of which were suspected to be stolen ([paras 4-5]).

Following these events, Sello submitted an application to the South Gauteng High Court, seeking to have the searches declared unlawful and demanding the return of the seized items. The High Court dismissed his application, citing that while the searches lacked a warrant, it did not automatically render them unlawful. This ruling prompted Sello to appeal ([paras 6-7]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues in this case encompassed:

  1. The lawfulness of the search and seizure operation conducted on 8 July 2008, specifically whether the lack of a warrant invalidated the actions taken by the police and MRAI inspectors ([paras 8-9]).
  2. Whether Sello was entitled to the return of all items seized during the search, particularly those which may be deemed unlawful to possess ([para 10]).

R – Rules

The case relied upon several critical legal provisions and principles:

  • Section 22 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 allows for warrantless searches when there is a belief that obtaining a warrant would defeat the purpose of the search. However, this belief must be substantiated ([para 8]).
  • Medicines and Related Substances Control Act 101 of 1965 which delineates the authority of MRAI inspectors to conduct searches and seizures ([para 8]).
  • The principle of spoliation, in which a party in peaceful possession of property has a right to recover it regardless of the lawfulness of the possession depends on whether the party can demonstrate lawful entitlement to the goods in question ([paras 10-11]).

A – Application

The court acknowledged admitted unlawful conduct by the police, conceding that the searches conducted without a warrant were indeed unlawful. The officers failed to justify the necessity of proceeding without one, and the MRAI inspectors could not demonstrate legal authority to carry out the searches under the Medicines and Related Substances Control Act ([para 8]).

The court then proceeded to analyze the implications of the unlawful searches on Sello’s request for the return of the seized items. It emphasized that returning items that may be unlawful to possess—even if seized unlawfully—was not warranted ([para 10]). The court clarified that merely being in peaceful possession does not automatically entitle a person to the return of property if it contravenes applicable health or safety regulations, or if some of the items were potentially stolen ([para 9]).

To facilitate resolution, the court proposed a compromise where the legal representatives from both sides would provide a schedule of items agreed to be returned, yet noted that no consensus was reached following further discussions ([para 8]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Sello’s appeal, declaring the searches conducted on his pharmacy and home unlawful and ordering the immediate return of items he may lawfully possess. The appellate court recognized the necessity of confirming lawful claims before returning seized items and emphasized the need for police and regulatory compliance with statutory mandates. The final order not only reaffirmed rights under privacy and property laws but also instilled a sense of procedural correctness within law enforcement actions ([paras 11-12]).

This ruling underscores the imperative that searches and seizures must adhere strictly to established legal frameworks, otherwise risking unwarranted infringing on individual rights under South African law. The implications of this case extend beyond Sello's claim, offering precedent on the balance between regulatory enforcement and constitutional protections.
