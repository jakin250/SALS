Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2007

# FIRAC Case Note: University of Witwatersrand Law Clinic v Minister of Home Affairs

## F – Facts
The case at hand concerns an application by the University of Witwatersrand Law Clinic (the Clinic) for direct appeal to the Constitutional Court of South Africa, presented in terms of Rule 19(2) of the Constitutional Court Rules ([para 1]). The Clinic had previously acted as amicus curiae in proceedings held at the Pretoria High Court, where Mr. Ismail Ebrahim Jeebhai sought declaratory relief related to the circumstances of the arrest and subsequent disappearance of Khalid Mahmood Rashid ([para 1]). 

Following the dismissal of Jeebhai's application by the Pretoria High Court on 16 February 2007, he filed a plea for leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal on 2 March 2007 ([paras 2–3]). Subsequently, on 22 March 2007, Jeebhai lodged an application with the Constitutional Court, requesting to join the Clinic's direct appeal and also seeking further direct appeal to the Court ([para 3]). 

At the time of the hearing, the application for leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal remained pending, leading to the Court's scrutiny on whether it was in the interests of justice to consider the applications from the Clinic and Jeebhai concurrently while the High Court proceedings were active ([paras 4–5]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issue before the Court was whether it was appropriate to grant direct leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court while an application for leave to appeal was still pending in the High Court ([paras 4–5]). This raises the broader question of the procedural propriety in appellate proceedings involving an existing pending appeal, and whether the Clinic, as amicus curiae, could supplant Jeebhai’s choice of appellate forum ([para 5]).

## R – Rules
The Court drew from established procedural rules, notably Constitutional Court Rule 19(3)(d), which necessitates that any application must clearly indicate whether there is a pending appeal in another court and its status ([para 7]). Furthermore, the Court referenced its previous judgment in *Campus Law Clinic, University of KwaZulu-Natal v Standard Bank of South Africa Ltd* ([para 6]), which stipulates that an amicus curiae may appeal only where the actual parties are not pursuing an appeal, ensuring that the rule of party autonomy (dominus litis) is respected. 

The Court also relied on the principles articulated in the case of *Minister of Health and Another v New Clicks South Africa (Pty) Ltd* regarding judicial efficiency and the avoidance of duplicative proceedings ([footnote 6]). 

## A – Application
In application of the law to the facts, the Court held that Jeebhai’s decision to first appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal merited respect, underscoring the principle that he, as dominus litis, must independently determine the appropriate appellate forum without interference from an amicus curiae ([para 5–6]). 

The Court emphasised that Jeebhai’s application for leave to appeal remained undisposed of in the High Court, thereby rendering the concurrent consideration of two appeals in different courts imprudent and potentially confusing to the judicial process ([paras 7–9]). While the matter raised significant public interest due to the implications surrounding enforced disappearances and human rights, it did not warrant a deviation from procedural rules that protect the dignity and order of the legal process ([paras 8–9]). 

Moreover, the failure of Jeebhai's application to disclose the pending appeal was noted as a significant procedural misstep, reinforcing the need for compliance with Constitutional Court rules ([para 7]). 

## C – Conclusion
The applications for direct appeal by both the University of Witwatersrand Law Clinic and Jeebhai were ultimately dismissed by the Constitutional Court. The decision underscored the importance of respecting established procedural norms, particularly when an appeal is pending in another court ([para 10]). Such a ruling not only reinforced the significance of judicial economy but also the autonomy of parties in litigation, ensuring that justice is administered in a structured and coherent manner without the complications that would arise from overlapping applications ([para 9]). 

The ruling set a clear precedent concerning the role of amici curiae within the judicial process, emphasizing their limitations in supplanting the agency of principal parties in ongoing litigation ([para 6]). The Court’s insistence on adherence to procedural regulations thus has implications for future cases where similar conflicts arise, potentially influencing the conduct of both parties and amici curiae in appellate proceedings.
