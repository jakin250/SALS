Constitutional Court

South African Police Service v Police and Prisons Civil Rights Union

This case arises from an application for leave to appeal against a decision of the Labour Appeal Court which upheld a Labour Court ruling.

Constitutional Court 2011 CCT 89/10

Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa
Case Number: CCT 89/10
Decided On: 9 June 2011
Judgment by: Nkabinde J

F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–5])

This case arises from an application for leave to appeal against a decision of the Labour Appeal Court which upheld a Labour Court ruling. The Labour Court had determined that only members of the South African Police Service (SAPS) appointed under the South African Police Service Act 68 of 1995 (SAPS Act) are engaged in essential service as defined under the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA). This interpretation excluded non-member employees of the SAPS who are appointed under the Public Service Act 103 of 1994 (PSA) from the classification as "engaged in an essential service" under the LRA.

The context of the dispute is rooted in the national general public service strike of 2007 during which POPCRU, a trade union representing approximately 109,500 members within the SAPS, sought to strike over wage disputes. The SAPS, employing about 163,000 individuals (approximately 129,000 functional officers and 33,000 support staff), filed for an urgent interdict against the strike, arguing that participation was prohibited for all SAPS personnel on the basis that they constitute an essential service ([paras 2–4]).

I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 8–12])

The central issue for determination is the legal interpretation of what constitutes an "essential service" as defined under section 213 of the LRA, in relation to the right to strike under section 23(2)(c) of the Constitution. Specifically, it must be resolved whether employees of the SAPS appointed under the PSA should also be considered as engaging in an essential service and thus be prohibited from striking.

Key Questions:

  1. Does the designation of the SAPS as an essential service under section 71(10) of the LRA prohibit all SAPS personnel from striking, or only those deemed members under the SAPS Act?
  2. What is the proper meaning of "engaged in" within the context of engaging in an essential service?

R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 18–24])

  1. Constitution of the Republic of South Africa: Section 23(2)(c) guarantees every worker the right to strike, but limitations exist for employees engaged in essential services ([para 18]).
  2. Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA): Section 65(1)(d)(i) restricts participation in strikes for those engaged in essential service; section 213 defines essential service to include the "South African Police Services."
  3. SAPS Act 68 of 1995: This Act delineates between members and non-members of the SAPS. Sections 1, 5, and 13 detail the composition, powers, and functionalities of members, while section 41 explicitly prohibits strikes by members of the SAPS but does not apply to non-members.
  4. Precedents: References include NEHAWU v University of Cape Town ([para 15]) and Bader Bop ([para 19]), both underscoring that the right to strike is fundamental and that its limitation must be interpreted narrowly.

A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 29–39])

The Constitutional Court echoed the Labour Appeal Court's interpretation that not all SAPS employees are classified as "engaged in an essential service." The Court affirmed that the SAPS must be distinguished per the definitions provided in the SAPS Act. It reasoned that "essential service" refers to specific policing roles assigned to members of the SAPS and does not extend to the support roles filled by non-member personnel under the PSA.

The judgment emphasized that a restrictive interpretation of "essential service" is crucial to preserve the right to strike under the Constitution. It concluded that while the SAPS serves a pivotal role in public safety, not all individuals employed within the SAPS deliver core policing functions that justify the restriction of their right to strike.

The Court dismissed the applicant's arguments that all employees should be viewed as part of a singular, integrated essential service and highlighted key distinctions within the legislative framework designed to demarcate between the roles of members and non-members.

C - Conclusion: Court's Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 40–42])

In conclusion, the Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal but dismissed the appeal against the Labour Appeal Court's decision. The ruling affirmed that only those employed under the SAPS Act and designated as members are engaged in an essential service, thus only they are barred from participating in strikes. The judgment reaffirmed the importance of maintaining the balance between operational effectiveness in policing and the constitutional rights of employees.

The implications of this case clarify the legal boundaries of essential services within the context of the right to strike, emphasizing the necessity of a nuanced interpretation that protects workers’ rights while addressing public safety and service delivery.

Overall, this case underscores vital intersections among constitutional rights, statutory interpretation, and the operational framework of public service entities in South Africa and has set a significant precedent for future labor relations within the security domain.
