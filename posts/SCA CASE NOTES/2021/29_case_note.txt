Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# FIRAC Case Note: Malatji v Ledwaba NO and Others (Case no 1136/2019) [2021] ZASCA 29

## F – Facts
The case arises from a dispute concerning the election of trustees within the Mamphoku Makgoba Community Trust. The trust was created to manage land claims lodged by the Makgoba community under the Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994. The initial trustees were appointed in 2010 for a three-year term but failed to vacate their positions despite the expiration of that term ([1]–[4]). 

In 2018, an order was issued by the Superior Court requiring the existing trustees to convene a general meeting to elect new trustees. Following this order, a meeting was held on January 12, 2019, during which allegedly irregular processes allowed attendees, some of whom were proxies for deceased beneficiaries, to vote ([3]–[5]). Mr. Thetele Joseph Malatji, a beneficiary and former trustee, contested the validity of the election, arguing that the voting process contradicted both the trust deed and the 2018 court order. He challenged several aspects of the election process, including the acceptance of proxy votes and the limitations on voting per candidate ([7]–[10]).

## I – Issues
The primary issues that arose from this case included:
1. Whether the general meeting on January 12, 2019, was convened in compliance with the 2018 order and the trust deed.
2. Whether the method of voting, including the acceptance of proxies and limitations on the number of votes per beneficiary, adhered to the requirements of the trust deed ([13], [20]).
3. Whether the arguments of waiver and estoppel precluded Malatji from bringing his claims, given that he participated in the election meeting without initial objection ([28]–[32]).

## R – Rules
The relevant legal rules and authorities in this case were:
1. **Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988** - This statute outlines the powers and duties of trustees in South Africa, particularly in terms of governance and election procedures.
2. **Trust Deed Provisions** - The trust deed outlined specific requirements regarding the composition and voting rights of beneficiaries entitled to vote, stating that only beneficiaries "present at such meeting" could vote, explicitly prohibiting proxy voting for deceased beneficiaries ([19]).
3. **Court Order Interpretation** - The court noted that court orders are to be interpreted according to the intentions conveyed in the language and context of the order itself, referencing prior case law establishing clear principles regarding such interpretations ([13], [24]).

## A – Application
The court conducted a thorough analysis of the election process, as well as the provisions of both the 2018 court order and the trust deed. It concluded that:
- The inclusion of proxies for deceased beneficiaries was not supported by the trust deed, which required physical presence of voters ([20], [21]). This was significant in that it reflected the court's intention to uphold the trust deed against unauthorized modifications.
- The voting method employed—where each attendee could only cast one vote—contravened the expressed provisions of the trust deed, which mandated that beneficiaries be able to vote in respect of each candidate for trustee positions ([24], [26]).
- The defense of waiver or estoppel was found to lack substance, given the absence of any explicit agreement by Malatji to the voting procedure as it deviated significantly from the agreed terms of the 2018 order ([28]–[33]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Malatji's appeal, declaring the election process unlawful and irregular based on the improper inclusion of proxy voting and the restrictive voting method employed. The court ordered that the previously elected trustees could no longer act as trustees, setting aside their election results and mandating a new election process compliant with the original trust deed and the 2018 order ([1], [34]). This decision underscores the necessity of adhering to established legal frameworks in managing trusts, particularly when it involves the rights of beneficiaries and their respective voting powers. The judgment serves as a pivotal reminder of the importance of clarity in legal documents and the role of courts in enforcing compliance with established governance procedures.
