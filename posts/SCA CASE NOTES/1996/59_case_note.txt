### FIRAC Case Note: ABSA Bank Ltd v Natasha Investment Co (Pty) Ltd Case No: 614/94

**F – Facts**  
The case concerns an appeal by ABSA Bank Limited (the Appellant) against a judgment of the Witwatersrand Local Division that ordered it to pay damages of R600,000.00 to Natasha Investment Company (Pty) Ltd (the Respondent) for breach of duty of care ([paras 2-3]). The trial court found that the Appellant, acting as a collecting banker, had improperly allowed Marilor Properties and Investments CC (Marilor) to withdraw the proceeds of a cheque drawn by the Respondent ([paras 2, 4]). 

The critical facts are as follows: on 22 March 1993, the Appellant’s Troyeville/Bertrams agency received a cheque for R600,000.00, dated 20 March 1993, drawn in favor of Chemsteel Mineral Developments (Pty) Limited (Chemsteel), and presented for collection on behalf of Marilor. The cheque was not endorsed and was crossed out in a manner typically indicating that it should not be negotiated ([paras 1-2, 4]).

Following the cheque's deposit, the Appellant allowed Marilor to withdraw R120,000.00 and subsequently the entire cheque's amount ([paras 3-4]). The Appellant was reimbursed by Standard Bank, which acted in good faith ([para 5]). 

The Respondent maintained that it was the legitimate owner of the cheque and had not authorized Marilor to withdraw the funds ([paras 11-12]). The Respondent’s witness, Levisohn, emphasized Chemsteel's ownership and the necessity for proper authorization regarding the cheque, raising questions about the intentions of the parties involved ([paras 6-10]).

**I – Issues**  
The primary legal issues before the court were:  
1. Whether the Respondent could establish itself as the true owner of the cheque ([paras 5, 19]).
2. Whether the Respondent could demonstrate that it had incurred a loss of R600,000.00 due to the Appellant's actions ([pars 5, 19]).

**R – Rules**  
The underlying legal principles primarily involved the duty of care pertaining to banking practices and the relationship between a drawer and the payee of a cheque. The Appellant's duty to act in good faith and exercise due diligence while collecting funds, as well as the principles governing the transfer of ownership of cheques, were central to the case. 

The court relied on precedent that established that a payment made to a bank, intended for a party that is not the named payee of a cheque, is generally unauthorized unless the drawer has expressly permitted such payment ([para 12]). Additionally, applicable principles from case law, such as *First National Bank of SA Limited v Quality Tyres (Pty) Limited* 1995 (3) SA 556 (A), which addressed the nuances of cheque ownership and intention, provided essential context ([paras 12-14]).

**A – Application**  
The court critically assessed the evidence presented by both parties, noting the Appellant did not dispute the finding that it breached its duty of care to the true owner of the cheque. It focused its argument on challenging the Respondent’s claims regarding ownership and loss ([para 5]). 

The court found that the Respondent failed to provide sufficient evidence to demonstrate its status as the cheque's owner or clarify its dealings with Du Toit, who held the cheque ([paras 14-19]). The judgment of Myburgh J, which concluded that the intention to transfer ownership to Chemsteel could be presumed from the cheque's language, was deemed flawed. The court highlighted that intentions should not be presumed without corroborating evidence from the parties involved, particularly from those who dealt directly with the cheque ([[paras 12-15]).

Levisohn's testimony, which revealed uncertainties regarding the cheque’s handling and the lack of consultation with the Respondent by Du Toit, ultimately indicated that the evidence did not unequivocally establish ownership ([paras 8-10, 16-18]). Thus, the court determined that the Respondent had failed to prove that it was the owner of the cheque at the relevant time, subsuming all arguments raised by the Appellant regarding ownership and loss ([paras 14-19]).

**C – Conclusion**  
The Appellate Division ruled in favor of the Appellant, allowing the appeal and setting aside the original order of the trial court. The court concluded that the Respondent failed to discharge its burden of proof regarding both ownership of the cheque and any sustenance of a loss ([para 19]).

The implications of this decision underscored that in disputes involving the ownership of negotiable instruments, mere presumptions from a cheque's language are insufficient without supporting evidence that clearly indicates the intention behind the cheque's issuance ([paras 18-19]). This ruling serves to reinforce the standards of diligence required by banks in handling cheques and emphasizes the necessity for clear transactions and communication between all parties involved to avoid similar legal disputes in the future.
