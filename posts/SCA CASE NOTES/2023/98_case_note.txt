Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: Minister of International Relations and Co-operation and Others v Simeka Group (Pty) Ltd and Others

**Facts**
The case concerns the procurement process followed by the Department of International Relations and Cooperation (DIRCO) regarding a tender awarded to a joint venture formed between Simeka Group (Pty) Ltd and Regiments Capital (Pty) Ltd on May 17, 2016 ([paras 2-3]). The tender was aimed at appointing a development partner for the design, construction, operation, maintenance, and financing of suitable office and residential accommodation for South African diplomatic missions in Manhattan, New York City ([para 8]).

Following the award, significant irregularities emerged, prompting DIRCO and associated ministers to institute a legality review in the High Court, alleging egregious failures in compliance with the procurement regulations set out in section 217 of the South African Constitution and relevant statutory frameworks, particularly the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA) and the Treasury Regulations ([paras 27-28]). However, the application was dismissed by the High Court on the grounds of unreasonable delay, without delving into the substantive merits of the case ([para 7]).

**Issues**
1. Whether the delay in initiating the legality review was unreasonable.
2. If the delay was unreasonable, should it nonetheless be condoned, allowing the review to proceed on its merits.
3. Did the award of the tender to the Joint Venture satisfy constitutional and statutory procurement requirements as outlined in section 217 of the Constitution? ([paras 63-84]).

**Rules**
The relevant legal framework arises primarily from:
- **Section 217 of the Constitution**: Mandates that procurement by state organs must be fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective ([para 36]).
- **Public Finance Management Act 1 of 1999 (PFMA)**: Sets out procedures for financial management and procurement for government entities, reinforcing compliance with constitutional principles ([para 37-39]).
- **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: Provides grounds for reviewing administrative actions; while the appellants maintained this review fell outside PAJA, the principles of legality governed the approach to the case ([para 28]).

**Application**
The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) found that the tender process significantly deviated from the requirements of the RFPs ([para 62]). The Joint Venture failed to provide necessary documentation—specifically audited financial statements from each member of the consortium, contrary to RFP mandates ([paras 50-52]). 

In assessing the delay, the SCA highlighted that while the High Court deemed it unreasonable, the context required a broader examination of whether these delays should be condoned in light of the egregious nature of the irregularities. The court laid emphasis on the importance of ensuring adherence to constitutional standards in public procurement, suggesting that the substance of the matter outweighed the procedural defects that had initially barred the review ([paras 60-100]). 

The SCA applied the standard set in previous decisions regarding the requirement for promptness in review applications, while accepting that in matters involving legality reviews, delays might be overlooked if justified by strong substantive merits (citing **Asla** and **Tasima**) ([paras 79-98]). 

The judgment concluded that the Department's actions in awarding the tender failed to comply with the constitutional and statutory framework, thus rendering the award invalid ([paras 88-89]). The Court exercised its discretion to overlook the delay, emphasizing the compelling public interest involved in the matter (the improper expenditure of public funds), and determined that the High Court had erred in its exercise of discretion by concluding the delay should not be condoned ([paras 112-113]).

**Conclusion**
The SCA upheld the appeal, recognizing the unlawful nature of the tender award based on multiple procedural and substantive irregularities. The Court declared the tender constitutionally invalid and set aside the associated Project Preparation Agreement ([paras 114-115]). The implications of this ruling underscore the significance of adherence to legal standards in public procurement and the necessity for timely reviews of administrative actions, balancing procedural fairness against the need for accountability in government transactions. Thus, the ruling reestablished clear lines of compliance with constitutional mandates, reinforcing the integrity of procurement processes in South Africa.

**Order**
The Court’s final order included the following key points ([para 123]):
1. The late institution of the legality review application was condoned.
2. The tender award to the Joint Venture was declared constitutionally invalid and set aside.
3. The Project Preparation Agreement with Lemascene (Pty) Ltd was ruled to be of no legal effect.
4. The respondents were ordered to pay the costs of the application.

This case serves as a crucial reference point regarding procurement law and judicial approaches to reviewing administrative actions within a constitutional framework, reiterating the foundational principles that govern public governance in South Africa.
