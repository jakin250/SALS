Occupiers of Skurweplaas 353 JR v PPC Aggregate Quarries (Pty) Ltd & Others

Case Citation: Constitutional Court of South Africa, Case CCT 26/11, [2011] ZACC 36

Constitutional Court 2011 [2011] ZACC 36 --- ## F – Facts [1] The applicants in this

Case Citation: Constitutional Court of South Africa, Case CCT 26/11, [2011] ZACC 36

F – Facts

[1] The applicants in this case are approximately fifty families who unlawfully occupy land in the Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality, specifically the Remaining Extent of the Farm Skurweplaas 353, J.R. The owner of this land, PPC Aggregate Quarries (Pty) Ltd, successfully obtained an eviction order against the applicants from the North Gauteng High Court. The High Court, while granting the eviction, also imposed certain obligations on the City of Tshwane, requiring it to provide alternative land for the applicants ([1]).

[2] The High Court's order allowed for the eviction to occur even if the City failed to provide alternative accommodation, thereby rendering the occupiers at risk of homelessness. This order was challenged by the applicants on the grounds that the eviction was not just and equitable according to Section 4(6) of the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act (PIE Act) ([1], [4]).

[3] The legality of the High Court's ruling was further complicated by the municipality’s stance that it lacked both the obligation and funding capacity to provide emergency housing. The City’s unwillingness to formally challenge the eviction order compounded the legal questions surrounding the duty to provide housing to unlawful occupiers ([6]).

I – Issues

[4] The primary legal issue at stake was whether the order for eviction was just and equitable under the provisions of the PIE Act, particularly given the potential for the occupiers to be rendered homeless. This question necessitated an examination of the constitutional requirements surrounding eviction processes and the obligations of the state in ensuring housing for vulnerable populations ([4], [7]).

[5] A secondary concern involved the obligation of the City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality to provide alternative housing, as well as the implications for the property rights of PPC Aggregate Quarries. The Court had to critically assess whether the property owner's rights were absolute or could be limited in favor of upholding human rights and dignity ([11], [12]).

R – Rules

[6] The relevant law is primarily encapsulated in the PIE Act, which aims to prevent the unlawful eviction of individuals from homes without due consideration for their circumstances. Section 26 of the Constitution of South Africa also provides that no one may be evicted without an order of court made after considering all relevant circumstances ([10]).

[7] The Court in City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality v Blue Moonlight Properties 39 (Pty) Ltd emphasized that property owners' rights need not be viewed as absolute and must be balanced against the rights of unlawful occupiers, who may face homelessness ([11]).

[8] The principle of linking the eviction date with the provision of alternative accommodation was also reiterated. As observed in Blue Moonlight, the court recognized the necessity of a linkage to avoid unjust circumstances for occupants who may otherwise be immediately rendered homeless following an eviction order ([13]).

A – Application

[9] The Constitutional Court undertook a detailed analysis of the implications of the eviction order made by the High Court. It found that the city’s failure to adequately provide for the housing needs of the occupiers prior to their eviction contravened the principles laid out in the PIE Act and the Constitution. The Court emphasized that the eviction order lacked compassion and failed to consider the dire consequences for the applicants who faced homelessness ([11], [14]).

[10] In reversing the High Court's order, the Constitutional Court established that it was necessary to require the City to provide alternative accommodations at least one month before the eviction took place. This decision aimed to uphold the dignity and rights of the occupiers, while still allowing PPC Aggregate Quarries to exercise its ownership rights, albeit with an understanding of social justice and human rights ([12], [16]).

[11] The Court also dismissed the City’s argument that it did not have the funding or obligation to provide emergency housing, indicating that the state has a duty to ensure adequate housing for all citizens, regardless of their legal status regarding the land in question ([6]).

C – Conclusion

[12] The Court ultimately granted the applicants' appeal, set aside the High Court's eviction order, and imposed specific directives on the City of Tshwane. It obligated the City to gather information about the occupiers and ensure that those at risk of homelessness were provided with alternative accommodation before the eviction could legally proceed. The eviction order was amended to ensure it would take effect only once suitable alternative housing was made available ([15], [16]).

[13] This case highlights critical intersections between property rights, housing rights, and the state’s obligations under the Constitution. The decision underscores the importance of compassion in the judicial process and sets a precedent for future cases involving unlawful occupation and evictions, reflecting an evolving understanding of social justice in South African law.

The implications of this ruling extend beyond the immediate parties involved, framing a legal landscape where the rights of vulnerable populations must be adequately safeguarded in the face of property rights, thereby reinforcing the transformative nature of South Africa's constitutional democracy ([11]-[16]).
