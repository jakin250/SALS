# FIRAC Case Note: Saak No: 21/99

**Court:** IN DIE HOOGSTE HOF VAN APPÉL, SUID-AFRIKA  
**Date of Hearing:** 9 Maart 2001  
**Date of Judgment:** 27 Maart 2001  
**Appellant:** Hendrik Gerber  
**Respondent:** Die Voorsitter: Komitee oor Amnestie van die Kommissie vir Waarheid en Versoening  
**Coram:** Hefer Wnd HR, Olivier, Cameron ARR, Brand en Mthiyane Wnd ARR  
**Reference:** Gerber v Voorsitter: Komitee oor Amnestie van die Kommissie vir Waarheid en Versoening 1998 (2) SA 559 (T)

---

## F – Facts

The appellant, Hendrik Gerber, was convicted in a 1993 murder case for the murder of Samuel Kganakga ("the deceased") on May 21, 1991. Gerber, along with two accomplices, tortured and eventually shot the deceased, leading to a sentence of 20 years in prison. Post the enactment of the Promotion of National Unity and Reconciliation Act 34 of 1995 ("the Act"), Gerber applied for amnesty, claiming his actions were politically motivated and thus protected under the Act. However, the Amnesty Committee denied his application, leading to an unsuccessful review application in the Transvaal Provincial Division. Gerber appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal, stating two grounds: first, that he was unfairly discriminated against contrary to section 9 of the 1996 Constitution, and second, that the Committee's failure to grant amnesty was erroneous as the murder was politically motivated as required by the Act ([1]–[3]).

The crux of Gerber's argument centered around alleged similarities between his case and the amnesty granted to applicants Christopher Makgale and Boy Diale ("the Makgale case"). In that case, the deceased was viewed as a political figure connected to the then-government, and his murder was motivated by political factors ([4]–[11]).

Gerber contended that the circumstances of his actions shared similarities with those of the Makgale applicants, arguing that the deceased had connections to the PAC (Pan Africanist Congress), based on information from an informant. Still, this claim was subject to rigorous examination during the hearing, leading the Committee to reject the political motivation argument ([8]–[10]).

The respondent, in denying amnesty, emphasized that Gerber had not raised any political motives during his initial criminal proceedings or in appeals following his conviction. Moreover, they noted that no evidence suggested that the deceased had any political affiliation, as Gerber had asserted ([10]–[11]).

## I – Issues

The case raised notable legal issues primarily focusing on:

1. **Discrimination (Article 9 of the Constitution):** Did the Amnesty Committee discriminate against Gerber by denying him amnesty while granting it to others with similar circumstances?
   
2. **Political Motivation (Article 20 of the Act):** Was the murder committed by Gerber politically motivated, thereby qualifying for amnesty under the provisions of the Act?

## R – Rules

Several legal rules and precedents were highlighted by the court:

1. **Constitutional Principles**: Article 9 of the South African Constitution prohibits discrimination on various grounds, including race, gender, and political affiliation ([3]).
   
2. **Promotion of National Unity and Reconciliation Act 34 of 1995**: Article 20 establishes criteria for amnesty, specifically requiring that the act be committed in furtherance of a political objective ([8], [10]).

3. **Judicial Precedents**: The judgment illustrated the distinction in political motivations between differing cases, emphasizing that judicial discretion regarding amnesty rights was dictated by the specific facts surrounding each individual case ([10]–[16]).

## A – Application

In determining Gerber's case against the backdrop of the Makgale case, the court undertook a fact-based analysis. It concluded that there existed no unfair discrimination because the circumstances of Gerber's actions differed materially from those of the Makgale applicants. Particularly, the court noted that Gerber's actions were driven by personal motives (potential self-preservation) rather than any form of political agenda ([16]).

The court further articulated that Gerber's failure to mention any political motives during his trial and prior appeals cast doubt on his current claims. The credibility of his present assertions was weakened as he provided no satisfactory explanation for omitting these critical details earlier, suggesting that his claims were disingenuous. Therefore, the respondent's evaluation of Gerber's credibility was upheld ([20]–[22]).

Given that the Act explicitly requires that an applicant’s crime stem from a political motive, and in light of the unchallenged factual determinations made by the respondent, the court found that the denial of Gerber's application for amnesty was justified and legally sound ([14]–[23]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Gerber's appeal, confirming the respondent’s decision to deny him amnesty. This dismissal was anchored upon an affirmed belief that Gerber's actions did not meet the statutory requisites of political motivation necessary for amnesty under the Act. The ruling reaffirmed that substantive differences in factual contexts can lead to divergent outcomes in similar cases, underscoring the principle of equality before the law as it relates to the political exceptions encapsulated in the Act. 

The court also ruled in favor of the respondent regarding costs, determining that the request for the costs of two advocates was both fair and warranted in light of the complex legal considerations involved in the case ([24]–[25]). 

This judgment serves as a significant precedent in discerning the boundaries of political motivations in violent crimes and the implications for amnesty applications. It illustrates the judiciary's role in navigating the nuances of the historical context of political conflict in South Africa.
