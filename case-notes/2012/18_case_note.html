National Treasury & Others v Opposition to Urban Tolling Alliance & Others ([2012] ZACC 18)

Constitutional Court 2012 [2012] ZACC 18)

F – Facts

The case centers on the Gauteng Freeway Improvement Project (GFIP), approved by the Cabinet in 2007, which involved significant road upgrades in Gauteng Province carried out by the South African National Roads Agency Limited (SANRAL) ([1]–[3]). SANRAL, an organ of state, holds the responsibility for national road management and financing under the South African National Roads Agency Limited and National Roads Act 7 of 1998 ([1]–[2]). The project, costing approximately R21 billion, was financed through tolling, invoking a “user pay” principle endorsed by the Government, a decision contested by the respondents—comprising various civil and consumer organizations ([5]–[6]).

On 23 March 2012, the respondents sought an interim interdict from the North Gauteng High Court to prevent SANRAL from implementing tolling until a final determination was made regarding the legality of declaring certain roads as toll roads and granting associated environmental approvals ([7]). The High Court granted the interdict on 28 April 2012, prompting the applicants, including National Treasury and SANRAL, to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court on an urgent basis ([8]).

I – Issues

The central issues in this appeal are:

  1. Whether it is in the interests of justice to hear the appeal directly on an urgent basis.
  2. If leave is granted, did the High Court err in granting the interim interdict against SANRAL? ([9]).

Additionally, preliminary matters were raised concerning the Democratic Alliance's application to intervene and the Road Freight Association’s application, both of which were ultimately dismissed ([9], [10], [17]).

R – Rules

The legal framework governing this case includes several statutes:

  1. South African National Roads Agency Limited and National Roads Act 7 of 1998 (SANRAL Act): Establishes the powers and responsibilities of SANRAL in declaring roads as toll roads ([6]).
  2. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA): Governs judicial reviews of administrative actions ([22]).
  3. Constitution of the Republic of South Africa: Contains provisions relating to just administrative action and separation of powers ([9]).

The High Court originally relied on the established legal principles for granting interim interdicts as outlined in Setlogelo v Setlogelo and Webster v Mitchell, which require:

  • A prima facie right.
  • Reasonable apprehension of irreparable harm.
  • The balance of convenience must favor the granting of the interdict.
  • No other remedy is available ([41]).

A – Application

In considering whether to hear the appeal on an urgent basis, the Constitutional Court determined that the issues presented significant implications for the separation of powers, particularly regarding the Executive's authority to implement policy decisions on financing public infrastructure through tolling ([22], [25]).

Regarding the merits of the interim interdict, the Court assessed whether the High Court properly applied the Setlogelo test. It concluded that the High Court made errors in determining that the respondents had established a prima facie right and a reasonable apprehension of harm that warranted the interdict. The Court noted that the harm claimed by respondents stemmed from a financial burden linked to the pending implementation of tolls, which had not yet been legally imposed. Therefore, the link between the past declarations and the alleged harms was tenuous at best ([30], [51]).

The Court emphasized the importance of distinguishing the executive’s policy decisions from claims of administrative justice, asserting that the respondents' challenge rested more on policy preferences rather than unlawful administrative action. Consequently, the application of the interdict imposed undue constraints on SANRAL's statutory function and had broader implications for public finances ([27], [63]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court made a decisive ruling by granting leave to appeal and subsequently overturning the High Court's interim interdict. It found that the interdict not only lacked proper justification under the legal frameworks applied but also intruded upon the separation of powers by restraining the Executive's established role in policy implementation regarding the funding of public works through tolling ([73]).

The holding ultimately reinforces the principle that challenges to governance and administrative action must not compromise the foundational separations of power, thus underpinning the necessity for courts to respect the democratic decision-making processes of elected officials. The implications of this ruling underscore the need for stakeholders to engage within political forums rather than seek judicial remedies for policy disputes ([96]).

This case note synthesizes the FIRAC method, pivoting on principles of governance, administrative justice, and the nuances of legal interpretation affecting public policy. The comprehensive analysis highlights the intricate balance between judicial oversight and executive authority vital in constitutional governance contexts.
