Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2002

### FIRAC Case Note: Zulu v Majola

**F - Facts**  
This case involves the estate of Aaron Ngqongqoza Mchunu, who died intestate on 11 April 1997, leaving behind a considerable estate valued at over R1.3 million, including minibus taxis and immovable property ([paras 1-2]). The deceased was in a customary union with the respondent, Duduzile Majola, who claimed sole ownership of the estate under customary law, asserting that only her eldest son was entitled to inherit. The first appellant, representing the second and third appellants (who also claimed customary marriages with the deceased), was appointed as the estate representative following the initial appointment of attorney Frans Mashele by a magistrate ([para 2]). Mashele prepared a liquidation and distribution account allocating estate assets among the three parties, leading to disputes regarding the transfers of assets, particularly the minibus taxis ([para 3-4]). After conflicts arose, including the failure to transfer the assets to the second and third appellants, the first appellant sought an order to compel the respondent to fulfill the distribution, leading to the dismissal of the application by the Witwatersrand Local Division ([para 8-9]).

**I - Issues**  
The central issues before the Court were whether the appointment of the first appellant as the representative of the estate was competent and if the magistrate had the authority to substitute the estate representative after an account had been approved ([para 12]). Additionally, the case raised questions about the interpretation and application of the Black Administration Act 38 of 1927 and the related regulations ([para 12])—specifically whether the estate was finalized despite some assets remaining undistributed, and whether the magistrate's actions were within scope to allow for substitution of the estate representative ([para 11]).

**R - Rules**  
The case hinged upon the interpretation of Regulation 4(1) of the Regulations promulgated under section 23(10) of the Black Administration Act 38 of 1927, which governs the appointment of estate representatives for deceased Black persons. The regulation permits a magistrate to appoint a suitable person to represent the estate without needing a formal executor ([para 2]). Furthermore, section 35(12) of the Administration of Estates Act 66 of 1965 was referenced to discuss the finalization of estates, leading to a misunderstanding of processes applicable under both statutes ([para 12]) and the potential for judicial review ([para 14]).

**A - Application**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the lower court's determination that the estate had been finalized was erroneous given that not all assets had been distributed ([para 15]). The reasoning of the judge in the lower court was criticized for implying finality based on the approval of the liquidation and distribution account while acknowledging ongoing disputes regarding asset transfers. The court confirmed that while indeed some assets had been collected and distributed, the regulatory framework permitted the magistrate to appoint a new representative when certain tasks remained unfinished—specifically, the responsibility for undistributed assets ([paras 15-16]). Moreover, the Court highlighted that the functus officio doctrine did not apply since the estate was still open for administration and distribution given the ongoing uncertainties ([para 16]).

**C - Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal determined that the first appellant's appointment to substitute the estate representative was competent because the estate had not been fully executed, and there were remaining assets to be addressed ([para 17]). The appeal was thus granted, restoring the appointment of the first appellant and directing the respondent to cooperate with him in collecting the remaining assets of the estate ([para 21]). This ruling underscores the importance of the regulatory framework governing the administration of deceased estates, particularly in the context of customary marriages and disputes surrounding inheritance in South Africa. The Court's final orders also addressed procedural issues, including costs and conflicts of interest concerning the dual role of the first appellant as both estate representative and attorney for the second and third appellants ([paras 18-20]).
