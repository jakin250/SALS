Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: Molusi v Voges NO (1008/13) [2015] ZASCA 64

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1-12])

The case of *Molusi v Voges NO* revolves around the evictions of peri-urban occupiers from the farm Boschfontein 330-JQ, Rustenburg. The appellants (occupiers) disputed the validity of their eviction after the respondents (landowners) terminated the lease agreements due to alleged non-payment of rent. The lease agreements were both written and oral, concluded around October 2001. The appellants claimed they had not defaulted on rental payments, asserting that their attempts to pay in May 2008 were denied by the respondents, who allegedly intended to demolish their dwellings ([paras 5-6]).

The Land Claims Court, in January 2013, ordered the appellants to vacate the property, leading to the present appeal ([paras 2, 24]). The appellants argued that they had not received proper notice of termination and that the court's decision relied on a different legal foundation than that presented in the founding affidavit. The respondents contended that they were entitled to cancel the lease agreements based on non-payment or under common law principles relating to periodic leases ([paras 12, 32]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 13-16])

The principal legal issues arising from this case include:

1. **Validity of Lease Termination**: Whether the lease agreements were effectively terminated in accordance with the *Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA)*, particularly regarding the requirements for lawful eviction ([paras 12, 22]).
2. **Right of Residence**: Whether the appellants' right of residence was terminated in a just and equitable manner under ESTA, taking into account the prescribed procedural safeguards ([para 8]).
3. **Change of Cause of Action**: Whether the court below erred by allowing the respondents to argue a different ground for termination than that pleaded in their founding affidavit ([paras 17-20]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 14-15, 22-39])

The case engages with several critical statutory provisions and precedents, including:

1. **Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA)**: Specifically, sections 8 and 9, which articulate the grounds for the termination of rights of residence and the conditions under which eviction may occur. Section 8 mandates that termination must be just and equitable, whereas section 9 stipulates procedural requirements for eviction ([paras 16-22]).
   
2. **Precedents on Lease Agreements**: 
   - *Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd* 1984 (3) SA 623 (A), affirming that a dispute of fact that cannot be resolved on papers may necessitate oral evidence ([paras 17, 32]).
   - *Minister of Safety and Security v Slabbert* [2010] 2 All SA 474 (SCA), addressing the need for clarity in pleadings and avoidance of trial by ambush ([para 20]).

3. **Constitutional Principles**: The judgment also reflects upon sections 26 and 25 of the Constitution of South Africa, relating to the right to adequate housing and property rights, thus framing the eviction within a broader constitutional context ([paras 8, 24]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 18-39])

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) evaluated the actions of the landowners against the statutory framework established by ESTA. It ruled that for a termination to be lawful, it must meet two essential criteria: it must be based on lawful grounds, and the process must be just and equitable ([paras 16-22]). 

The court noted:
1. **Grounds for Termination**: The respondents had relied on non-payment as a basis for termination, which was contested by the appellants. The failure to attach the necessary demands for unpaid rental was a critical factor affecting the validity of termination. The SCA found that the absence of proper service of notices further complicated the matter, aligning with *Plascon-Evans*, where disputed facts must be favorably viewed for the non-moving party ([paras 17-18]).
  
2. **Common Law Principles**: The court also examined whether the respondents could invoke common law principles of lease termination, initially not detailed in their founding affidavit. The SCA concluded that parties must be clear about their grounds for legal action to avoid ambushing the opposing party during litigation ([paras 19-20]).

3. **Just and Equitable Consideration**: The court emphasized the legislative intention behind ESTA, highlighting the need for evictions to be carried out with empathy and consideration of the socio-economic realities of the occupants. The SCA took issue with the Land Claims Court's failure to ensure that requisite governmental bodies were engaged in providing adequate alternative accommodation for the evictees before granting the eviction order ([paras 22, 36]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 23, 40-46])

The SCA held that the appeal was upheld primarily on the grounds that the eviction order was not sustainable under ESTA due to procedural deficiencies and that the respondents had not sufficiently established the grounds for termination as initially claimed. The court concluded:

1. **Termination Invalidity**: The appellants were not given adequate notice as required by law, thus rendering the eviction order void ([paras 24, 40]).
  
2. **Final Orders**: The court dismissed the appeal but noted the procedural failings of the original court, remanding the necessity for a more just approach regarding future occupiers’ rights under ESTA, especially in alignment with the requirements of the Constitution. The implications extend to ensuring that in future cases, landowners comply not just with statutory requirements but also engage with the constitutional duties to safeguard individuals’ rights to housing and dignity ([paras 40-46]).

This case reinforces the critical balance between property rights and the protection of vulnerable occupiers, underscoring the necessity of adhering to legislative and constitutional mandates.
