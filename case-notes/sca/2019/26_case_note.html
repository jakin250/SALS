The Minister of Safety and Security v Kholeka Nancy Msi (273/2018) [2019] ZASCA 26

Initially, Msi was successful in her claim at both the Regional Court and High Court levels, but the Minister appealed the High Court judgment ([1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 26

FIRAC

F – Facts

The case concerns the aftermath of an assault perpetrated by Warrant Officer Thambo (Thambo) of the South African Police Service (SAPS) on the respondent, Kholeka Nancy Msi (Msi), during a private meeting prompted by personal grievances ([1], [2]). Msi had previously engaged in a romantic relationship with Thambo, which ended in 1996, but they maintained a friendship until tensions arose concerning Thambo's alleged intimate involvement with a relative of Msi’s friend in February 2013. This led to a tumultuous meeting on 20 February 2013 which Thambo attended while on official duty, dressed in uniform and armed, despite being prohibited from attending private meetings ([2], [3], [5]). The meeting, convened to discuss Msi's confrontations regarding Thambo’s conduct, resulted in Thambo assaulting Msi, prompting her to seek damages through a court claim against the Minister of Safety and Security ([1], [2], [6]).

Initially, Msi was successful in her claim at both the Regional Court and High Court levels, but the Minister appealed the High Court judgment ([1]).

I – Issues

The key legal issue in this case revolves around the concept of vicarious liability of the Minister for Thambo's actions. Specifically, the court needed to determine whether Thambo was acting within the scope of his employment when he assaulted Msi, and whether the minister should be held liable for the wrongful acts of an employee acting outside the scope of his employment, classifying the situation as a "deviation case" ([8], [10]).

R – Rules

The standard for establishing vicarious liability requires that the employee must have acted within the course and scope of his employment. Two tests apply: the standard test for actions within the scope of employment and the deviation test for actions outside of it ([9], [10]). The South African case law has developed these principles through significant precedents such as Feldman (Pty) Ltd v Mall and Minister of Police v Rabie which examine the relationship between the act committed and the employee's official duties, while K v Minister of Safety and Security and F v Minister of Safety and Security extended these principles to incorporate constitutional norms and public trust in police conduct ([10], [11], [15]).

A – Application

In this case, the court emphasized that Thambo's attendance at the meeting was purely personal and had no connection to his duties as a police officer. The assault emerged from a personal confrontation, with Thambo acting on his own initiative rather than under police orders ([22]). The court noted that Msi had summoned Thambo to discuss personal grievances, devoid of any criminal complaints, and consequently, the subjective element of the inquiry pointed against liability ([22]).

From an objective standpoint, the test required assessing the degree of connection between Thambo’s conduct and his employment. The court observed that while Thambo’s uniform, firearm, and police vehicle contributed to Msi potentially placing her trust in him as a police officer, the context of the assault was entirely outside his official function. Msi’s understanding of the situation was informed by her prior relationship with Thambo rather than his role as a police official ([26], [28]). Critically, the trust placed in Thambo was deemed ineffective as it stemmed from their personal acquaintance rather than his capacity as a police officer. Furthermore, the court highlighted that Thambo’s personal motivations and emotional response, not his employment as a policeman, spurred the assault. Therefore, the employment connection was insufficient to impose liability ([31], [36]).

The case of Bezuidenhout v Eskom was referenced, reaffirming the notion that a breach of instructions could negate vicarious liability, especially when the injured party willingly engaged in actions that diverged from a lawful expectation, heavily influencing the court's reasoning in this matter ([32], [35]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that there was not a sufficiently close connection between Thambo's conduct and the functions of SAPS to justify the application of vicarious liability. The appeal was granted in favor of the Minister, overturning the rulings of the lower courts and dismissing Msi's claims with costs ([40]). This decision underscores the necessity of a clear and substantive link between the actions of an employee and their official responsibilities, particularly in cases involving claims against government entities related to the conduct of their employees.

This ruling is significant as it delineates the boundaries of vicarious liability, particularly in deviation cases involving police conduct, and the relationship between personal and professional spheres in the assessment of liability. Although public trust in law enforcement is acknowledged, the court stressed that it must be rooted in an application of constitutional principles and connected to the performance of police duties.
