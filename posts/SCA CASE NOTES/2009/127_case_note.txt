Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2009

# FIRAC Case Note: Mr Video (Pty) Ltd v Nu Metro Filmed Entertainment (Pty) Ltd [2009] ZASCA 127

## F – Facts

The case arose from an appeal against orders issued by Fourie J in the Cape High Court regarding issues of copyright infringement ([para 1]). The appellants, collectively referred to as the "Video Group," included various franchisees operating under the brand "Mr Video," which specializes in the rental of recorded films ([para 2]). The respondents were Nu Metro Filmed Entertainment (Pty) Ltd and several prominent film corporations, termed "the Owners," who held copyright in the films ([para 1], [para 2]).

Nu Metro discovered that the Video Group was offering DVD recordings of their films for hire, which had been imported from the USA ([para 3]). This led to the initiation of legal proceedings by Nu Metro, seeking interdicts and delivery of the infringing DVDs, claiming that such activities infringed on the Owners' copyright and exclusive license rights ([para 3]). Initially, the Video Group contested the claims but later conceded ownership of the copyright to the Owners and indicated a willingness to comply with the interdict ([paras 3–4]).

The case presented several contentious issues, notably the locus standi of Nu Metro to participate in the proceedings, the Owners' entitlement to delivery of the infringing items, and various cost-related matters ([para 4]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues that arose in this case included:

1. **Locus Standi**: Whether Nu Metro, as a claimed exclusive licensee, had the standing to bring forth the application against the Video Group ([para 4]).

2. **Delivery of Infringing Copies**: The legality of the court ordering the Video Group to deliver up DVDs imported from the USA, which were claimed to be infringing copies under section 24(1) of the Copyright Act No 98 of 1978 ([paras 4, 8]).

3. **Cost Orders**: Challenges to various cost awards made by Fourie J, including whether the Video Group's conduct warranted a special costs award against them ([para 10]).

## R – Rules

The court's considerations were framed within the parameters of the South African Copyright Act No 98 of 1978, particularly focusing on section 24(1), which provides for relief in cases of copyright infringement ([para 8]). The definition of "infringing copies" was central, indicating that such copies must be made in a manner that infringes the copyright's protection ([para 8]).

Precedent and legal principles established in prior case law regarding the interpretation of copyright licenses and infringement also shaped the court’s analysis, particularly regarding the necessity of demonstrating exclusivity of license rights held by Nu Metro ([para 5]).

## A – Application

### Locus Standi

The court evaluated the arguments surrounding Nu Metro's locus standi. The Video Group contended that Nu Metro failed to prove it was an exclusive licensee, asserting that its rights did not extend to preventing the Video Group's conduct. However, the court recognized that Nu Metro had established its role as the sole local distributor of the films. Moreover, the Video Group’s acknowledgment that their conduct infringed the copyright of the Owners weakened their argument regarding Nu Metro’s standing ([para 5]).

### Delivery of Infringing Copies

The court further analyzed the facts surrounding the imported DVDs. The Video Group claimed that the DVDs were not infringing copies since they were lawfully obtained from the USA. The court dismissed this argument, emphasizing that the Owners had not authorized the distribution of these DVDs in South Africa. The proprietary considerations and the restrictions inherent in the encoding of DVDs by region were pivotal to the court's conclusion that the DVDs were indeed infringing copies as defined by the Act ([paras 6–9]).

The court concluded that the infringing items were acquired for unlawful purposes, and therefore an order for delivery to Nu Metro and the Owners was justified, enabling the monitoring and enforcement of their rights ([para 9]).

### Costs

The court's analysis of the cost orders revealed that the Video Group’s arguments against the costs awarded were unfounded. The flagrant disregard for copyright laws and the multiple franchises implicating their conduct warranted punitive costs, particularly given earlier warnings issued to the Video Group regarding the legality of their practices ([para 12]). 

The decision by Fourie J to impose attorney-and-client costs further highlighted the serious nature of the infringement and disapproval of the Video Group's conduct ([para 12]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the decisions made by the High Court ([para 1]). The ratio decidendi highlighted that:

1. Nu Metro was entitled to join the proceeding as an exclusive licensee, satisfying the requirement for locus standi.
  
2. The court correctly applied section 24(1) of the Copyright Act in ordering the delivery of infringing copies, recognizing the unlawful nature of the Video Group’s actions.

3. The cost orders, particularly the punitive attorney-and-client scale, were justified due to the Video Group's conduct throughout the proceedings.

This case underscores the stringent application of copyright law in South Africa and the court's willingness to protect the rights of copyright holders against infringements, coupled with a clear message that improper conduct in such disputes will incur substantial costs ([paras 10–13]). The appellants will need to reassess their compliance with copyright laws to avoid similar repercussions in the future.
