Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# Case Note: Jantjies v S (871/13) [2014] ZASCA 153

## F – Facts

In Jantjies v S, the appellant, Siyabonga Jantjies, appealed against a conviction of murder (dolus eventualis) handed down by the Regional Court in Middelburg, Eastern Cape. Jantjies had been sentenced to ten years’ imprisonment, a sentence which he sought to overturn on appeal, arguing that there was insufficient evidence to support a conviction ([1]).

The events leading to the conviction unfolded on 17 September 2008 when the appellant and the deceased, who was Jantjies's girlfriend, were together at her residence. After an upsetting phone call from an ex-girlfriend, a heated argument ensued between the couple. In the heat of the moment, the deceased attempted to stab Jantjies with a large knife; however, he managed to subdue her and throw the knife aside. After leaving the room to collect himself, the appellant claimed that the deceased announced her intention to commit suicide ([2]). 

Upon returning to the deceased’s room half an hour later, he found her lying under a blanket and, initially unaware of her injuries, he lay down next to her. It was only the next morning that Jantjies attempted to wake her for school, discovering she was unresponsive and bleeding ([3]). Despite noting this, he went to his cousin's home to report the incident while neglecting to seek medical assistance. His cousin, along with Jantjies's father, subsequently informed the police, who discovered the deceased on the bed with multiple stab wounds ([4]–[5]).

Evidence was presented by witnesses including Jantjies’s cousin and father, both of whom contradicted Jantjies's account of their conversations regarding the events leading to the discovery of the deceased. The State's case relied heavily on circumstantial evidence, bolstered by a post-mortem report indicating six stab wounds, three of which had penetrated the heart, leading to her death ([7]–[8]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issue in this case revolved around whether the circumstantial evidence presented was sufficient to convict Jantjies of murder ([10]). Specifically, the court had to determine if it could conclude beyond a reasonable doubt that the appellant, rather than an intruder or the deceased herself, had caused the fatal injuries. The appellant argued that the trial court erred in refusing to discharge him at the close of the State’s case, asserting that this refusal breached his constitutional right to a fair trial ([11]).

Additionally, the case raised questions of evidentiary reliability, particularly concerning the credibility of witnesses and the inferences that could be drawn from the facts established during the trial ([13]).

## R – Rules

The legal principles governing circumstantial evidence were critically examined. The judgment referenced **R v Blom** (1939 AD 188), which established that for an inference to be drawn from circumstantial evidence, it must be consistent with all the proved facts and exclude every other reasonable inference. Additionally, the discretion afforded to trial judges under Section 174 of the **Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977** regarding the discharge of an accused was also a focal point, with **S v Luxaba** (2001 (2) SACR 703 (SCA)) indicating that this is not subject to appeal ([11]).

The court underscored that circumstantial evidence can be compelling and should not be treated as less worthy than direct evidence. The distinctions between probable and improbable inferences were also pivotal; **S v Van der Meyden** (1999 (2) SA 79 (W)) and other sources highlighted how probabilities influence the burden of proof ([17]).

## A – Application

The court evaluated the circumstantial evidence against the standard set by the aforementioned cases. It noted that the appellant’s claims of the deceased attempting suicide were not credible in light of the medical findings that ruled such a scenario improbable ([12]–[19]). The correct inference, according to the trial court and reaffirmed by the appellate court, was that the appellant inflicted the wounds.

Key contradictions in testimony, particularly between Jantjies and his father and cousin, were noted as indicators of dishonesty aimed at shielding the appellant from culpability. The courts found their testimonies lacking credibility, with the inference being overwhelmingly against the appellant. The failure to seek immediate medical assistance further indicated a lack of reasonable actions expected of someone who had just learned of a significant injury ([15]).

The appellant's submission regarding needing to take the stand to avoid conviction was deemed irrelevant. The court maintained that the prosecution had presented sufficient circumstantial evidence while refraining from prejudging the appellant's right not to testify as determinant of guilt ([11]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Jantjies's appeal, upholding the murder conviction. The findings that an intruder was implausible due to a lack of forced entry and that the deceased could not have inflicted her injuries upon herself provided a solid basis for concluding Jantjies’s guilt beyond reasonable doubt. The inference drawn was not merely a possibility but the only viable explanation, thus reinforcing the trial court's original ruling ([20]). 

The judgment signals strong adherence to evidentiary standards and the correct application of principles concerning circumstantial evidence, reporting that the credibility of witness testimony and the rationality of inferential reasoning were central to the court's analysis. Jantjies remains sentenced to ten years’ imprisonment, a decision reflecting the serious nature of his actions and the implications of murder within the South African legal context ([21]).
