Supreme Court of Appeal

CSARS v Saleem (21/2007) [2008] ZASCA 19

[2] The factual allegations in SARS's answering affidavit—including that the goods had no import documentation and that the respondent had failed to demonstrate lawful ownership—stood unchallenged due to the absence of a replying affidavit.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2008 [2008] ZASCA 19

F – Facts

[1] The respondent, A Saleem, is the owner and manager of Payless Fashions, a small clothing retail business in Brakpan, Gauteng. In September and October 2006, officers from the South African Revenue Service (SARS) seized goods worth approximately R1.2 million from Saleem's shop, claiming they were illegally imported without paying the necessary duties. The respondent subsequently filed an application in the High Court of Pretoria to declare the seizure unlawful and to demand the return of the goods. The High Court ruled in favor of Saleem, leading SARS to appeal the decision.

[2] The factual allegations in SARS's answering affidavit—including that the goods had no import documentation and that the respondent had failed to demonstrate lawful ownership—stood unchallenged due to the absence of a replying affidavit. SARS's agents, Van der Merwe and Jansen, were responding to complaints regarding illegal imports in the Brakpan area. During their visit, they requested import documentation, which Saleem could not adequately provide. He mentioned that goods were obtained from Chinese traders in Johannesburg and produced a few inadequate invoices. After several interactions, the officers concluded they had reasonable grounds to seize the goods based on the lack of documentation.

[3] On October 3, 2006, SARS served a notice of detention citing Section 88(1)(c) of the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964, claiming the goods were liable to forfeiture. The noted inadequacies in the invoices provided intensified the officers' rationale for the seizure. Ultimately, on October 5, the goods were seized, leading to Saleem's application for their return on October 9.

[4] The High Court found the seizure unlawful, asserting that Van der Merwe's suspicion was unreasonable, given that he did not otherwise substantiate his inference of illegality beyond the mere presence of "Made in China" labels.

I – Issues

[5] The central legal issue is the reasonableness of Officer Van der Merwe's suspicion when seizing the goods without confirming the legitimacy of their importation, as mandated by Section 88(1)(c) of the Customs Act. The court had to assess whether the officer adequately applied his mind to the jurisdictional facts required for lawful seizure under the Customs and Excise Act, particularly in light of the fundamental rights to dignity and privacy enshrined in the South African Constitution.

R – Rules

[6] The relevant legal rules are contained within the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964, particularly Section 88, which allows officers to seize goods believed to be liable for forfeiture. According to Section 87(1), goods are subject to forfeiture if they have been imported unlawfully or have not duly declared. The constitutional provisions governing administrative justice, particularly the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000, also applied, highlighting the need for power exercised by public functions to be rational and reasonable.

[7] The case references the principle from Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd 1984 (3) SA 623 (AD), which asserts that in disputes of fact, the court must accept the version presented by the respondent unless it is inherently improbable.

A – Application

[8] In analyzing the law, the court emphasized that the mere presence of Chinese labels on the goods did not constitute sufficient ground for Van der Merwe’s suspicion. The High Court noted that SARS officers failed to investigate further after Saleem's offer to point them to his suppliers, which constituted a lapse in their duty to ascertain the legality of the goods comprehensively.

[9] The appeal court, however, considered various factors establishing reasonable grounds for suspicion: the goods’ origin (indicia of being made in China), the respondents' inability to produce supportive documentation, their inconsistent statements regarding ownership, and Chen's submission of false tax numbers. Hence, the court disagreed with the earlier ruling that solely focused on the inadequacy of documentation, deeming this an incomplete assessment of the overall circumstances.

[10] Additionally, the appellate court clarified that while the respondent bore a duty to keep proper records of suppliers, the seizure did not require comprehensive documentation if alternative proofs of legality could be furnished, which should have informed the officer’s perception of a reasonable suspicion.

C – Conclusion

[11] The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the High Court's decision, ruling that Van der Merwe possessed reasonable grounds for his suspicion based on the cumulative circumstances and the conduct of the respondent and his associates. The court dismissed Saleem’s application, concluding that the officers acted within their jurisdiction under the Act. This decision reinforces the standards of evidence required for lawful seizure under customs regulations, balancing the need for enforcement with individual rights protected by the Constitution.

[12] The implications of this ruling underscore the complexities surrounding the enforcement of customs regulations, the responsibilities of officers in ascertaining legality prior to seizure, and the ongoing dialogue regarding rights to trade and privacy versus regulatory compliance in South Africa. The court's decision also illustrates the judiciary's role in scrutinizing application of administrative powers to ensure actions taken fall within the identified boundaries of legality and reasonableness.
