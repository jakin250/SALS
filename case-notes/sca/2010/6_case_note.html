CSARS v Fascination Wigs (204/09) [2010] ZASCA 06

The dispute particularly focused on two types of products: “wefts,” which can be integrated into natural hair or glued onto the scalp, and “braiding fibres.” These terms denote synthetic products that can be used to create the appearance of wigs or enhance natural hairstyles.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2010 [2010] ZASCA 06

F – Facts

This appeal arises from an order of the North Gauteng High Court, wherein the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (hereinafter “the Commissioner”) determined the tariff classification of synthetic hair products imported by the respondent, Fascination Wigs (Pty) Ltd (hereinafter “Fascination Wigs”). On 6 December 2005, the Commissioner classified certain synthetic hair wefts under tariff heading 67.04 of the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964, asserting these products were complete items of false hair. In contrast, Fascination Wigs contended that these synthetic products were prepared for making wigs and should therefore be classified under tariff heading 67.03, which refers to materials meant for use in the production of wigs ([1]–[4]).

The dispute particularly focused on two types of products: “wefts,” which can be integrated into natural hair or glued onto the scalp, and “braiding fibres.” These terms denote synthetic products that can be used to create the appearance of wigs or enhance natural hairstyles. The high court initially upheld the application by Fascination Wigs, leading the Commissioner to appeal the ruling ([2]–[4]).

I – Issues

The central legal issue in this case concerns whether the imported synthetic hair wefts should be classified as finished products subject to customs duty under tariff heading 67.04 or as materials prepared for making wigs, thereby qualifying for tax exemption under tariff heading 67.03. The court needed to resolve whether the imported goods were end-products that required no further processing or, alternatively, materials intended for further alteration and inclusion in wig-making ([1]–[4]).

R – Rules

In determining tariff classifications, the court considered the principles and hierarchy established under the Customs and Excise Act with respect to the Harmonized System. The definitions and distinctions between tariff headings 67.03 and 67.04 were crucial:

  • Tariff Heading 67.03: Covers "human hair, dressed, thinned, bleached or otherwise worked; wool or other animal hair... prepared for use in making wigs or the like."
  • Tariff Heading 67.04: Covers "wigs, false beards, eyebrows and eyelashes... made of human or animal hair or textile materials" ([5]).

The explanatory notes clarify the nature of materials included under these headings, emphasizing whether items were completed products or materials designed for further processing. The court referenced International Business Machines SA (Pty) Ltd v Commissioner for Customs and Excise, which sets out a three-stage classification process involving interpretation of headings, examination of goods, and selection of the appropriate classification ([7]).

The importance of the General Rules for the Interpretation of the Harmonized System was also acknowledged, with Rule 1 emphasizing the determination of classification based on headings and relevant notes ([8]–[9]).

A – Application

The court analyzed the nature of the imported wefts, distinguishing them from finished products as classified by the Commissioner. It underscored the complexity and skill involved in attaching these wefts to a person’s natural hair, and acknowledged that while Fascination Wigs contended these items were prepared for wig-making, the final attributes of the products could not transform their essential character as they were imported ([12]–[14]).

The evidence presented by the parties, including testimony from Mr. Chan Kwok Keung, a manufacturer from Evergreen, indicated that the wefts were not suitable for direct use in wig-making but were intended as additions to hairstyles. The court found that the nature of the attachment process did not alter the fundamental classification of the wefts as non-dutiable unless they were processed into a final product ([14]–[19]).

The judgment highlighted that the accepted complexity and appearance of wefts did not negate their classification as raw materials under 67.03. The court reaffirmed that for the wefts to qualify for exemption under 67.03, they needed to be expressly prepared for inclusion in wig-making, which was not demonstrated on the evidence presented ([20]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the high court's ruling, reinstating the Commissioner’s classification of the imported synthetic hair products under tariff heading 67.04, thus confirming their status as finished products subject to customs duty. This finding emphasized the importance of correct tariff classification and adherence to legislative provisions regarding imports ([21]).

The case serves as a significant precedent for clarifying the nuances surrounding tariff classifications within the Customs and Excise framework, particularly in contexts involving cosmetically enhanced products. It also underscores the necessity for clear distinctions between raw materials and finished goods in customs law, facilitating greater compliance and understanding of duties associated with imports in South Africa.
