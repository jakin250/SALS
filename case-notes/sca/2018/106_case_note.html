Scholtz & Others v The State (428/17, 491/17, 635/17, 636/17) [2018] ZASCA 106 (21 August 2018)

FIRAC Case Note: Scholtz & Others v The State

Case Title: Scholtz & Others v The State (428/17, 491/17, 635/17, 636/17) [2018] ZASCA 106 (21 August 2018)
Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Coram: Leach, Mathopo, Van der Merwe, Mocumie JJA and Mothle AJA
Heard: 3 May 2018
Delivered: 21 August 2018

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–4])

The case arises from a series of criminal charges brought against 12 individuals, including nine appellants, linked to leasing agreements made with the Northern Cape's Department of Social Services. The primary charges included corruption, money laundering, and fraud stemming from the dealings of the Trifecta Group of Companies in securing leases for state buildings from May 2006 to August 2008. The trial took place in the Northern Cape Division of the High Court, Kimberley, commencing on 3 February 2014 and concluding with a judgment on October 13, 2015. At the end of the State's case, the accused's application for discharge was denied.

The court convicted several individuals, including Mr. Scholtz (the first accused) and his associated companies on various counts of corruption and money laundering. Sentences included substantial fines and terms of imprisonment, with Mr. Scholtz and Mr. Block, another accused, receiving 15-year sentences. The court a quo granted some appellants leave to appeal their convictions but denied leave regarding their sentences.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 4, 119–120])

Several legal issues were raised on appeal:

  1. Corruption Definition: What constitutes the offence of corruption under sections 3 and 4 of the Prevention and Combating of Corrupt Activities Act [PCCA]?
  2. Gratification Timing: Can payments accepted after the commission of a corrupt act still be considered as gratification for the purposes of the offense?
  3. Procedural Compliance: Were the actions of the accused compliant with the constitutional requirement for transparent and equitable procurement processes as mandated by section 217 of the Constitution and supported by the Public Finance Management Act [PFMA]?
  4. Recusal of Judge: Did the judge in the court a quo create an appearance of bias by refusing a discharge?
  5. Sentencing: Were the sentences imposed excessive in relation to the circumstances, considering the mitigating factors and the confiscation orders served?

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 15–20, 119–128])

The court relied upon several legal rules and previous case law to reach its decisions:

  • Prevention and Combating of Corrupt Activities Act: Sections 3 and 4 define corruption and stipulate the parameters for evaluating bribery and corruption.
  • Common Law: Reference was made to past cases, including R v Chorle (1945) and S v Shaik (2007), to illustrate judicial interpretations of corruption encompassing both past and future deeds regarding gratification.
  • Constitutional Mandates: Section 217 of the Constitution requires state contracting to be conducted with fairness, equity, transparency, competitiveness, and cost-effectiveness, underpinned by the PFMA and relevant Treasury Regulations concerning procurement.
  • Case Analysis: Precedents such as S v Selebi (2012) were cited regarding the nature of corrupt relationships and the interpretation of quid pro quo, especially about understanding the timing and nature of gratification in corrupt practices.

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 113–201])

The court undertook a detailed factual analysis to affirm the convictions based on the statutory definitions and precedents:

  1. Corruption Definitions: The court emphasized that corruption is established when a person receives gratification in exchange for influencing government actions. It rejected the notion that gratification must precede the corrupt act definitively, arguing that post-facto payments could still establish a corrupt intent.
  2. Procedural Irregularities: The court laid out systematic failures in the procurement processes, noting that the necessary competitive bidding was not executed, and proper authority was bypassed. The judges highlighted testimony from various state officials that established a culture of circumventing procurement protocols.
  3. Judge’s Recusal: The request for recusal was dismissed as the court found no objective basis for reasonable apprehension of bias. The grounds were rooted in procedures rather than personal bias, and refusals of discharge based solely on legal discretion were deemed appropriate.
  4. Sentencing Considerations: The court found the 15-year minimum sentences for corruption in line with legislative intent, dismissing personal circumstances invoked as mitigating factors. It reiterated the gravity of corruption in public service and emphasized that upholding integrity in public office warranted stringent repercussions.
  5. Corruption Findings on Appeal: The appeals on counts of corruption, particularly Counts 15 and 16, were meticulously unpacked, confirming convictions based on the establishment of quid pro quo relationships. However, in some instances, the court did limit convictions to specific payments, validating the necessity for precision in prosecutorial arguments.

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 211–212])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld several convictions while also dismissing others. Key points of conclusion:

  • The first appellant was acquitted of certain corruption charges where insufficient evidence linked gratifications directly to his actions.
  • The appeals of money laundering counts from other appellants were upheld due to procedural failures and lack of evidence.
  • Importantly, the court confirmed that corruption undermines democratic governance and that stringent sentences were justified to deter such maladministration.
  • A referral to the Law Society regarding the conduct of Mr. Naude, the attorney involved, flagged potential professional misconduct, signaling the court's commitment to upholding ethical standards in legal practice.

In sum, this case highlights significant jurisprudential principles regarding corruption, accountability in public service procurement, and the ongoing judicial commitment to enforcing integrity standards in governmental processes.

This case note demonstrates the complex interplay of law, public ethics, and judicial oversight, shedding light on the challenges faced in combating corruption within state mechanisms.
