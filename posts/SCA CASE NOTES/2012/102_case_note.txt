# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of *Minnies v State* (881/2011) [2012] ZASCA 102 

## F – Facts:
The case of *Minnies v State* arose from events on 19 July 2006, when the appellants—Mark Minnies (First Appellant), Iekeraam Hini (Second Appellant), Mark Adams (Third Appellant), and Linford Pilot (Fourth Appellant)—were tried in the Specialised Commercial Crime Court at Bellville on charges of unlawfully tendering counterfeit money, in contravention of section 34(1)(b) of the South African Reserve Bank Act 90 of 1989 (the “Act”). They were convicted and sentenced to various terms of imprisonment. Following the dismissal of their appeal by the Western Cape High Court, the appellants sought leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal against their convictions ([1]–[2]).

The State's case was built around the testimony of Alfred Robert Laidlaw, a state witness and acquaintance of the First Appellant. Laidlaw was approached by the First Appellant with an offer to sell counterfeit money at 50% of its face value ([2]). Subsequently, a police operation was arranged where Laidlaw, accompanied by an undercover police agent, met with the appellants to inspect a large quantity of counterfeit R100 banknotes. The appellants were arrested following a high-speed chase when they attempted to evade arrest upon noticing police presence ([2]).

The charge framed against the appellants was that they “unlawfully tendered” the counterfeit banknotes, as defined under section 34(1)(b) of the Act. The appellants argued that merely offering the counterfeit notes, with both parties aware of their counterfeit nature, did not amount to unlawful tendering ([3]–[5]).

## I – Issues:
The legal issues before the court involved:
1. Whether “tender” should be construed synonymously with “utter” in the context of section 34(1)(b) of the Act ([6]).
2. Whether the court erred in affirming a conviction of “tendering” based on an offer to sell counterfeit notes, despite the absence of evidence demonstrating an intent to pass them off as genuine ([7]).
3. The reliability and credibility of Laidlaw's evidence as the sole incriminating witness and whether the trial court erred in refusing to draw an adverse inference for the State's failure to produce corroborative witnesses ([6]).

The critical question was whether offering to sell counterfeit notes, with mutual acknowledgment of their counterfeit status, constituted unlawful “tendering” under the statute ([8]).

## R – Rules:
The applicable statutory provision is section 34(1)(b) of the South African Reserve Bank Act, which states: “any person who utters, tenders or accepts any note which has been forged, altered, or unlawfully issued, knowing it to be forged, altered or unlawfully issued, commits an offence” ([4]). This section broadly addresses the unauthorized handling of counterfeit currency.

The appellants and State cited various precedents to support their arguments, notably, *S v Modisakeng* 1998 (1) SACR 278 (T), where it was held that to utter counterfeit currency, the individual needs to denote an intention to pass off the counterfeit as genuine ([7]). The interpretations of terms like “utter” and “tender” were pivotal in assessing the legal definitions and implications pertaining to the offence in question ([9], [10]).

## A – Application:
The Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized the interpretation of the terms “tender” and “utter” within the context of section 34(1)(b). It noted that while the Act refers to both terms, the nature of tendering is closely related to the intention behind the act of offering money. In this case, all parties were aware that the money being discussed was counterfeit, negating the essential element of passing off counterfeit notes as genuine ([11], [12]).

The court rejected the previous interpretation from *Modisakeng* that separated the concepts of tendering and uttering, thereby affirming that the intention inherent in tendering need not be distinct from that required in uttering. The failure to show an attempt to offer the counterfeit notes as genuine money meant the appellants could not be convicted under section 34(1)(b) as there was no unlawful tendering taking place ([12])—the act was simply an offer for sale of counterfeit currency.

The court also deemed the State's reliance on Laidlaw's testimony flawed due to inconsistencies and contradictions that compromised its credibility and the integrity of the judicial process ([6], [8]).

## C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal thus upheld the appeal, setting aside the convictions and sentences of the appellants ([14]). The court concluded that the appellants did not commit the offence of unlawful tendering under section 34(1)(b) of the Act, as their actions, coupled with the mutual understanding of counterfeiting, fell outside the legislative intent of the provision as intended for offences involving genuine misrepresentation of currency. This case underscores the importance of intent and the nuances in statutory interpretation within criminal law, particularly concerning counterfeit currency provisions. The judgment calls into question the adequacy of charges related to counterfeiting and highlights the need for precise drafting of legal charges to align with the facts of the case ([13]).
