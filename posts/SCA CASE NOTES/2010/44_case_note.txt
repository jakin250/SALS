Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2010

# FIRAC Case Note: Maree v Booysen t/a NVM Beleggings & Versekeringsadviseurs (307/09)

## F – Facts
The present case involves a legal dispute between Mr. P P Maree (the Appellant) and Mr. Chris Booysen, trading as NVM Beleggings & Versekeringsadviseurs (the Respondent), over commission due to the cancellation of an insurance policy. Mr. Booysen, an insurance broker, sought payment of R47,638.27 as commission from Mr. Maree, asserting that he was entitled to this amount under their written agreement, which provided for the payment of commission even in the event of a policy cancellation within the statutory cooling-off period ([paras 1–2]).

The background of this conflict can be traced to advice given by Mr. Booysen in July 2006, suggesting that Mr. Maree cancel his existing Sanlam annuity policy in favor of a new Momentum Life policy. However, after receiving further advice from others, Mr. Maree decided to cancel the newer policy and informed Mr. Booysen and the Momentum Life Company of his decision ([paras 5–10]). Mr. Booysen's claim was initially dismissed by the Kroonstad Magistrate’s Court, which found the agreement contravened Section 49 of the Long-term Insurance Act 52 of 1998 ([para 2]). On appeal, the Free State High Court reversed this dismissal, leading to the present appeal by Mr. Maree ([paras 3–4]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issue in dispute relates to the enforceability of the agreement between Mr. Maree and Mr. Booysen given the statutory provisions of the Long-term Insurance Act, particularly regarding whether Mr. Booysen is entitled to claim commission for a policy that was canceled within the cooling-off period as prescribed by legislation ([paras 2, 15, 16]).

## R – Rules
The case primarily revolves around the interpretation of several sections of the Long-term Insurance Act 52 of 1998, particularly:
- **Section 49**: States that no consideration shall be accepted by an intermediary (such as Mr. Booysen) other than commission as specified in regulations.
- **Section 56**: Voids any agreement waiving rights conferred by the Act to policyholders.
- **Section 72(1)(d)**: Empowers the Minister to regulate the remuneration of intermediaries.
  
Additionally, relevant regulations, such as Regulation 3.2 and Regulation 3.8, echo the sentiments of Section 49 and provide that agreements for consideration beyond commissions are void ([paras 17–22]).

## A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized the Free State High Court's interpretation of the statutory provisions. The court noted that the key to understanding John Booysen's entitlement to the commission was the acknowledgment that Mr. Maree canceled the policy within the stipulated cooling-off period. Thus, no commission was due to Mr. Booysen ([para 30]). 

The court concluded that enforcing the agreement would conflict with the legislative protections afforded to consumers, which intend to prevent financial penalties under such circumstances. The court rebuffed the argument that Section 49 was ambiguous and affirmed that it strictly limits the consideration to be given to intermediaries like Mr. Booysen ([para 28]). The findings also emphasized the rational legislative purpose of the cooling-off period, which serves to protect policyholders by allowing them to reconsider their decisions without financial repercussions ([para 27]).

Furthermore, the court addressed the notion of absurdity raised by Mr. Booysen's counsel, stating that the potential loss of commission due to the cancellation within the cooling-off period was a risk anticipated in the compliance framework established for the insurance industry ([para 26])​.

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, effectively reversing the previous decision of the Free State High Court and affirming the Magistrate's court ruling that the agreement was unenforceable. The court's judgment solidified the principle that no commission is due for policies canceled within the cooling-off period established under the Act, thereby reinforcing consumer protections in insurance agreements. The outcome not only clarified the legal obligations between brokers and policyholders but underscored the need for intermediaries to operate within the boundaries set by legislation to maintain consumer trust and protection in the insurance sector. The final order was that Mr. Maree's appeal was upheld, and Mr. Booysen’s claim was dismissed with costs ([paras 35]). 

The implications of this judgment are significant, as they reaffirm the regulatory framework designed to protect consumers in the long-term insurance market, explicitly delineating the limitations on intermediaries concerning commission claims in light of cancellations within legally designated periods.
