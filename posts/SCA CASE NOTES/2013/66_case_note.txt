### Case Note: FIRAC Method on *Plaatjies v DPP, Transvaal* [2013] ZASCA 66

**F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–4])**  
The case centers around Boisile Amos Plaatjies, the appellant, who was initially tried in the Potchefstroom Regional Court for multiple charges, including murder and unlawful possession of a firearm, among others. Following a conviction on all counts, Plaatjies was sentenced to a total of 11 years’ imprisonment. He subsequently appealed to the then Transvaal Provincial Division of the High Court, which upheld the appeal on the basis that the trial magistrate had failed to sit with assessors as mandated by *s 93 ter (1) of Act 32 of 1944*. This failure rendered the court improperly constituted, leading to the conviction being set aside. 

Following this, the Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP) reinitiated criminal proceedings against Plaatjies on the same charges before a different magistrate. Plaatjies subsequently entered a plea of *autrefois acquit*, claiming he could not be tried again on the same charges. The DPP appealed the acquittal, contending it was based on a technicality and did not consider the merits of the original case ([paras 1–5]).

**I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 5–6])**  
The key issue presented before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) involved the interpretation of *s 35(3)(m) of the South African Constitution*, which guarantees the right not to be tried for an offense for which a person has previously been acquitted or convicted. The court had to determine whether this provision had altered the application of the *autrefois convict* or *autrefois acquit* principles and whether it necessitated the development of common law in this context. Additionally, the court considered whether the DPP's grounds on appeal regarding the merits of the initial trial were valid ([paras 6–8]).

**R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 9–13])**  
The court referred to several legislative provisions and case law to determine the applicable principles:

1. *s 106(1)(c) and (d) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977* allows a defendant to plead *autrefois acquit* or *autrefois convict* if previously convicted or acquitted of the same offense.
2. *s 324 of the Criminal Procedure Act* delineates the conditions under which a retrial can be reinstituted after a conviction has been set aside due to technical irregularities, such as the lack of a properly constituted court ([paras 9–10]).
3. The decision in *S v Moodie* and *S v Naidoo* elucidated boundaries around retrials stemming from technical irregularities, particularly that the legal merits must be considered separately from procedural defects ([paras 11–12]).
4. The SCA also referenced prior judgments in the *Basson* cases, which affirmed that prior acquittals must be based on the merits; otherwise, they cannot preclude a retrial ([paras 13]).

**A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 14–16])**  
The SCA dismissed each of the appellant's grounds for appeal by systematically addressing the implications of *s 35(3)(m)*. The court reasoned that the Constitutional Court had already made clear in the *Basson* decisions that an acquittal resulting from procedural flaws, such as the failure to follow legal requirements regarding assessors, lacked the necessary substance to preclude further prosecution ([paras 14–13]). 

The court identified that the DPP's appeal was legitimate since the earlier acquittal did not evaluate the factual merits of the case. The appellant's argument that this rendered the DPP's recharging of the case impermissible under the new constitutional framework was deemed unfounded: procedural integrity must be observed, but substantive justice could require different outcomes when former proceedings did not question the merits ([paras 14–16]).

Moreover, the court held that a substantive application for a permanent stay of the prosecution could only succeed if clear violations of *s 35(3)(d)* had been proven, emphasizing that the burden rested upon Plaatjies to demonstrate unjust delays or infringements on his rights—alluding that the failure to do so mirrored his responsibility for the protraction of the legal proceedings ([paras 15]).

**C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([para 17])**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, affirming that the legal principles surrounding double jeopardy retain their original interpretations, despite constitutional developments. The court underscored that acquittals must be based on substantive merits for the plea of *autrefois acquit* to be valid and recognized that procedural shortcomings do not bar subsequent litigation on the same charges. 

This decision reinforces the prerogative of the legal system to pursue justice in light of procedural deficiencies while ensuring the rights enshrined in the Constitution are respected. It underscores the necessity for clarity in procedural conduct and the substantiation of claims surrounding past acquittals, paving the way for further legal clarifications regarding the intersection of constitutional rights and procedural integrity in criminal law. 

In conclusion, the implications of this case extend to future cases where procedural irregularities may arise, affirming that the principles laid out in the Criminal Procedure Act function to preserve the integrity of the justice process while respecting the constitutional rights of accused individuals.
