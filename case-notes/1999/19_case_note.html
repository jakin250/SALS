Constitutional Court

The State v Godfrey Baloyi (CCT 29/99)

In this case, the Transvaal High Court declared section 3(5) of the Prevention of Family Violence Act 133 of 1993 ("the Act") invalid, prompting a referral to the Constitutional Court for confirmation of this ruling.

Constitutional Court 1999 CCT 29/99

F – Facts ([1]–[3])

In this case, the Transvaal High Court declared section 3(5) of the Prevention of Family Violence Act 133 of 1993 ("the Act") invalid, prompting a referral to the Constitutional Court for confirmation of this ruling. The appellant, Godfrey Baloyi, an army officer, was involved in a domestic dispute with his wife (the complainant), who had obtained an interdict against him in response to alleged assaults. This interdict mandated that Baloyi refrain from assaulting his wife and their child.

Following multiple breaches of this interdict, Baloyi was arrested after an enquiry was initiated, where evidence was presented by both the complainant and her brother. Despite his denial of wrongdoing, Baloyi was convicted of violating the interdict and sentenced to imprisonment. He subsequently appealed, contending that the imposition of a reverse onus of proof onto him within the proceedings violated his constitutional right to be presumed innocent under section 35(3)(h) of the Constitution of South Africa.

The High Court agreed with Baloyi, asserting that the reverse onus contravened the constitutional presumption of innocence and could not be justified under section 36 of the Constitution, leading to its declaration of invalidity for the relevant section of the Act.

I – Issues ([8]–[10], [14]–[15])

  1. Does the definition of "accused person" in section 35(3)(h) include individuals facing proceedings under the Prevention of Family Violence Act?
  2. Did section 3(5) of the Act impose an unconstitutional reverse onus on the appellant?
  3. If it did impose a reverse onus, can such a limitation on the presumption of innocence be justified within the parameters set by section 36 of the Constitution?

R – Rules ([1], [11]–[12], [14])

  • Constitution of South Africa: Article 35(3)(h) guarantees every accused person the right to a fair trial, specifically the presumption of innocence until proven guilty.
  • Prevention of Family Violence Act 133 of 1993: Section 3(5) incorporates certain procedures from the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which concerns how violations of interdicts are to be handled.
  • Section 36 of the Constitution: Permits limitation of rights provided it is reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society based on human dignity, equality, and freedom.

A – Application ([13], [21]–[32])

The Court conducted a dual analysis addressing both the balance between the state's duty to address domestic violence and the rights of the accused. First, it affirmed that Baloyi, as a defendant facing the possibility of conviction and imprisonment under the Act, qualified as an accused person entitled to the presumption of innocence.

The Court then evaluated whether the procedures specified under section 3(5) constituted a reverse onus. This was pivotal in determining if the High Court's declaration of invalidity was warranted. The reasoning also examined three interpretations of the law—ranging from one that excludes a reverse onus to one that necessarily incorporates it. The constitutional obligation to deal with domestic violence without infringing on civil liberties was paramount.

Ultimately, the Court favored Interpretation A, concluding that section 3(5) merely dictated procedural elements without imposing a reverse onus. The Court stressed that any deviation from the established presumption of innocence must not only be justified but also aligned with constitutional requirements and the overarching goal of curtailing domestic violence.

C – Conclusion ([33])

The Constitutional Court declined to confirm the Transvaal High Court's declaration of invalidity concerning section 3(5) of the Prevention of Family Violence Act. It held that the Revised interpretation did not impose a reverse onus on the accused, emphasizing that Baloyi's rights were intact. This judgment illustrates a significant stance on balancing the imperative of protecting victims of domestic violence while safeguarding the constitutional rights of the accused, thereby maintaining the integrity of the legal system.

Implications: This case reinforces the judiciary's role in ensuring that legislative actions align with constitutional principles, particularly the right to a fair trial, while also acknowledging the complexities inherent in domestic violence legislation. The ruling is a critical reference point for future cases involving domestic violence and the delineation of rights and responsibilities under such legislation.
