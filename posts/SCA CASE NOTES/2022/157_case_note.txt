Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# Case Note: Rustenburg Platinum Mines Limited and Another v The Regional Manager, Limpopo Region, Department of Mineral Resources and Others [2022] ZASCA 157

### FIRAC Case Note

#### F – Facts
The case concerns an appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa, where the appellants, Rustenburg Platinum Mines Limited (RPM) and ARM Mining Consortium Limited (ARM), sought to review and set aside seven administrative decisions made by the Department of Mineral Resources and Energy (DMRE) related to the granting and refusal of prospecting and mining rights over certain properties known collectively as the Modikwa Deeps Properties. These properties are adjacent to the Modikwa Platinum Mine operated by RPM and ARM.

The procedural history began when RPM applied for a prospecting right for platinum and associated minerals on 16 March 2004, just before the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA) came into effect. When the MPRDA became effective, RPM's application was treated as pending. RPM argued that it was a holder of an unused old order right, entitling it to a one-year exclusivity period during which no competing applications could be considered by the DMRE ([paras 1–8]). 

Despite this exclusivity, King Sekhukhune III applied for a prospecting right on 29 April 2005, just as RPM's exclusivity period expired. The DMRE's Regional Manager subsequently granted prospecting rights to King Sekhukhune III and Genorah Resources (Pty) Ltd while refusing RPM's application based on provisions in s 17(2) of the MPRDA, which prohibits the granting of rights if it would lead to the concentration of mineral resources under the control of one entity ([paras 9–16]).

After a protracted review application process initiated by RPM, the Gauteng Division of the High Court dismissed RPM's review of the DMRE's decisions, primarily on the grounds of unreasonable delay in prosecuting the matter ([paras 25–32]). The current appeal arose from this dismissal, with ARM continuing the proceedings.

#### I – Issues
The central legal issues before the court include:
1. Whether the DMRE acted unlawfully in granting prospecting rights to Genorah and King Sekhukhune while refusing RPM's application for a prospecting right.
2. The interpretation of the provisions of the MPRDA, particularly s 17(2), concerning the avoidance of the concentration of mineral rights.
3. The implications of unreasonable delay in the prosecution of the review application ([paras 25–32]).

#### R – Rules
The legal authorities relevant to this case include:
- **Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA)**, particularly:
  - Section 16(2), addressing the acceptance criteria for prospecting right applications. 
  - Section 17(2), detailing the grounds for refusal to grant a prospecting right, particularly regarding the concentration of mineral resources.
- **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**, which provides grounds for reviewing administrative decisions based on procedural fairness.
- **Minister of Mineral Resources and Others v Sishen Iron Ore Company (Pty) Ltd**, which elucidates the objectives of the MPRDA and the importance of equitable access to mineral resources for historically disadvantaged persons ([paras 35–60]).

#### A – Application
The court's analysis focused on several dimensions, including the interpretation of statutory provisions and the administrative actions taken by the DMRE. The court first addressed the background and legislative context surrounding the MPRDA, noting its objectives of transforming the mining sector in South Africa by promoting equitable access and preventing monopolies on mineral resources ([paras 35–60]). 

In applying the law, the court emphasized that while RPM claimed the right to the properties based on an alleged old order right, the DMRE made its decisions in line with its statutory mandate to prevent the concentration of mineral rights that could stifle competition and engagement from historically disadvantaged stakeholders ([paras 61–64]).

On the point of delay, the court noted that the review application was significantly delayed, resulting in the lapse of prospecting rights and the operational reality that Genorah was already engaged in mining activities under a granted right. This rendered much of the appeal's context moot, as the granting of a mining right was not contingent upon holding a prospecting right, according to the provisions of the MPRDA ([paras 49–50]). Thus, the lawfulness of the initial granting of prospecting rights was not necessarily relevant to the legitimacy of the mining rights already granted ([para 54]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, finding the high court's ruling adequately supported by reasoning regarding both procedural missteps and the merits of the refusal of RPM's application for a prospecting right. The court upheld the DMRE's interpretation of the MPRDA as aligned with its transformative objectives, emphasizing the importance of preventing monopolistic control of mineral resources by a single entity ([paras 18, 62–66]). 

### Implications
This judgment underscores the court's commitment to ensuring that the mining industry in South Africa progresses in a way that is equitable and aligns with constitutional principles. It highlights the significance of preventative measures against the concentration of mineral rights, asserting that the MPRDA serves a public interest beyond mere procedural compliance. The ruling affirms the DMRE's discretion in balancing historical injustices with current applications and the necessity for diligence in administrative proceedings. 

This case serves as an important precedent in mining law and administrative justice in South Africa, reinforcing the need for transparent processes that uphold both legal frameworks and socio-economic transformation objectives.
