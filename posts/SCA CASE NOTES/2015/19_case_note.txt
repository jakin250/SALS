# Case Note: Van den Heever v Minister of Minerals and Energy (150/14) [2015] ZASCA 19

## FIRAC Method

### F - Facts

The appeal concerns a dispute over mining rights on two contiguous pieces of land situated on the farm Richtersveld No 11 in the Namaqualand district, Northern Cape. The appellant, Gert van den Heever, sought mining permits from the Department of Minerals and Energy in 2008, which were denied due to existing rights held by Trans Hex Operations (Pty) Ltd (the fourth respondent) over the same property. The mining right was initially granted to Trans Hex Mynbou Limited (the third respondent) and subsequently ceded to Trans Hex Operations in May 2001 ([1–2]).

The core of the appellant’s argument hinges on a letter sent by Trans Hex Mynbou to the Department in January 2001, which the appellant interpreted as an abandonment of its mining right over the land. This claim was countered by the respondents, who contended that the letter merely requested the Department to amend the mining lease to facilitate the land's use for agricultural purposes by the Richtersveld community, indicating no intention to abandon the mining rights ([2–3]).

The appellant sought a review of the decisions made by the Northern Cape High Court, including a declaration of abandonment of mining rights, a review of the conversion of the old order mining right, and the rejection of his mining permit applications ([4–5]).

### I - Issues

The main legal issue raised is whether Trans Hex Mynbou's letter to the Department on 26 January 2001 constituted an abandonment of its mining right over the specified portions of the farm, which would invalidate its cession of rights to Trans Hex Operations ([6]). 

An ancillary issue concerns the interpretation of the letter, which includes whether the context and background facts indicate a relinquishment of the mining rights or merely an intent to amend the existing lease ([6–7]).

### R - Rules

The principles governing the abandonment of rights in this case are clear: abandonment or relinquishment is not presumed and must be proven with clear evidence of the individual’s intention, regarded objectively through their actions ([7]). Importantly, it is established that the intention to relinquish a right must be demonstrated clearly, and any interpretation must take into account the surrounding context and facts ([20]). Relevant statutes involved include the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA), which governs mining rights and leases in South Africa ([4]).

### A - Application

The court scrutinized the letter to discern whether it objectively reflected an intention to abandon the mining rights. The letter included requests to amend the mining lease and referenced excising portions of land for agricultural use; however, it retained the structure of the existing mining lease and confined the amendment to surface rights for agriculture ([8–19]). 

The court highlighted that Mynbou was bound by its mining lease agreement, which explicitly restricted its obligations to mining only, and required Ministerial approval for any alterations to land use ([12–20]). The court also noted that an agreement made subsequent to the January letter further confirmed Mynbou’s commitment to retaining its mining rights ([21]).

The conclusion drawn was that the appellant’s interpretation failed to encapsulate the objective meaning of the letter. The appellant’s reliance on the highlighted phrase did not demonstrate an intention to abandon mining rights; instead, Mynbou intended to retain those rights while facilitating agricultural use of specific land portions. Thus, the intent to abandon was not substantiated ([23]).

### C - Conclusion

The appeal was dismissed, confirming that Mynbou did not relinquish its rights to mine for diamonds on the property. The court underscored the importance of objectively interpreting the correspondence and intentions of the parties involved. The ruling clarifies that clear evidence of intent is required for a relinquishment of legal rights regarding mining permits under the MPRDA ([24]). 

This decision reinforces the legal principle concerning the necessity of explicit intention and clarity when addressing rights to property, particularly in the mining sector, ensuring mining companies are aware of their ongoing obligations and rights even in negotiations concerning land use ([8–22]).
