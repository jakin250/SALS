Case Note: REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2003

### FIRAC Case Note: Republic of South Africa

#### F – Facts
The case involves an appeal by Robert Stanley Ross Armstrong (the Appellant) against Sehadew Oree, trading as Oree's Cartage & Plant Hire (the Respondent) concerning the unauthorized removal and payment of sand from a property. The trial court initially ordered the Respondent to pay the Appellant R342,000, representing 76,000 cubic meters of soil removed without due payment ([paras 1-2]).

The contested property, Portion 43, belonged to the Umhlali Beach Town Board, but the Appellant was allowed to use it, anticipating a property exchange agreement. A notarial agreement was later signed between the parties, granting the Respondent rights to extract sand from Sub 28, with the agreement stipulating payments per cubic meter and the necessity of written amendments to the contract ([paras 3-4]). 

Disputes arose regarding sand extraction from Portion 43, leading to claims for "extra sand" based on multiple causes of action, including unjust enrichment and oral agreements ([paras 10-11]). The Respondent contended that the extraction was unauthorized and invalid due to the failure to comply with formalities established by Section 3(1) of the General Law Amendment Act 50 of 1956, which governs the leasing of mineral rights ([paras 10-12]).

#### I – Issues
The legal questions at the center of the dispute include:
1. Did the Respondent remove the additional sand without payment?
2. Was there a valid contract regarding the extraction of sand?
3. Did the oral agreement concerning sand extraction comply with Section 3(1) of Act 50 of 1956, which requires notarial attestation for the transfer of mineral rights? ([paras 17].

The court also considered whether ordinary sand qualifies as a "mineral" under the statute ([paras 24-25]).

#### R – Rules
The primary legal authority relevant to this case is Section 3(1) of the General Law Amendment Act 50 of 1956, which states that any lease of rights to minerals must be notarized to be valid. The precedent set in **Wiseman v De Pinna** was also cited, clarifying the term "lease" and its application to contracts concerning mineral rights ([paras 19-20]).

The court further examined the definition of "minerals" as per South African legal standards, referencing decisions such as **Minister of Land Affairs v Rand Mines Ltd** and other related cases that established that ordinary sand is typically not classified as a mineral ([paras 24-25]).

#### A – Application
The trial court found that the Respondent had indeed removed the extra sand, yet deemed the oral agreement invalid due to non-compliance with the statutory formalities of Section 3(1) ([para 14]). However, it was suggested that the Respondent was unjustly enriched since he benefitted from the sand without compensating the Appellant. 

The Supreme Court of Appeal lent its agreement with the fact that the Respondent was responsible for the removal, aligning with the lower court's findings regarding the evidence presented. The focus then shifted to determining the applicability of Section 3(1). The Court scrutinized whether the sand extracted could be classified as a mineral, ultimately concluding that it was "ordinary sand" and not necessarily bound by the stipulations of the statute. Specifically, it argued that if ordinary sand was classified as a mineral, it would impose unreasonable conditions on casual transactions — a situation not envisioned by the legislature ([paras 24-26]).

The Appellant's entitlement to the amount of R342,000 was reaffirmed, stating that he established a valid cause of action based on the contract despite the earlier findings against the oral agreement's validity ([para 27]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal determined that the oral agreement regarding Portion 43 was not invalidated by the formal requirements of Section 3(1) of Act 50 of 1956. Consequently, it upheld the Appellant's claim against the Respondent, thereby reversing the prior ruling of the Natal Provincial Division and reinstating the original judgment in favor of the Appellant ([paras 28]). 

Thus, the implications of this ruling emphasize the necessity for clarity regarding the definitions of minerals in law, particularly for common materials such as sand, outlining that not all forms of earth materials require notarized agreements, ultimately ensuring that commercial practices relevant to such materials remain viable and accessible. 

The appeal was successful, with costs awarded in favor of the Appellant, thereby reinforcing his right to compensation for the unauthorized extraction of sand from his associated property ([para 28]).
