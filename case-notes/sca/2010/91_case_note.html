Supreme Court of Appeal 2010 [2010] ZASCA 91 ### F - Facts The appellants, Andile William

FIRAC Case Note: Libazi v The State (424/2009) [2010] ZASCA 91

F - Facts

The appellants, Andile William Libazi (the first appellant) and Mabuthi Mbayimbayi (the second appellant), along with several others, stood trial in the Sterkspruit Circuit Court of the Eastern Cape High Court. They faced multiple charges, including murder, conspiracy to commit murder, attempted murder, and unlawful possession of firearms ([1]). Ultimately, only appellant Shasha was convicted of murder and theft, while Libazi and Mbayimbayi were convicted of conspiracy and attempted murder related to specific individuals ([1]).

The incidents in question arose from violent encounters between rival taxi associations, namely the Herschel United Taxi Association (HUTA), to which the appellants belonged, and the Herschel Long Distance Taxi Association (HLDTA), to which the victims belonged. The specific shootings occurred on 27 May 1995, where members of HUTA reportedly pursued and attacked HLDTA members ([3]).

Crucial evidence included eyewitness accounts and a statement made by Shasha, which the State sought to introduce as hearsay evidence against the appellants. The High Court admitted this statement, leading to the appellants' convictions. They appealed against their convictions and sentences ([2]–[6]).

I - Issues

The key legal issues in the appeal were:

  1. Whether the High Court erred in admitting Shasha’s extrajudicial statement as hearsay evidence against the appellants ([6]).
  2. Whether the state’s witnesses could be deemed reliable, given the potential influence of rivalries and the circumstances of the shooting ([6]).

R - Rules

The legal principles at play involve:

  • Hearsay Evidence: Governed by section 3(1)(c) of the Law of Evidence Amendment Act 45 of 1988. This section allows hearsay evidence to be admitted under certain conditions, typically when it serves the interests of justice. However, it also embodies the right to challenge evidence, which is critical to ensuring a fair trial ([5], [11]).
  • Conspiracy to Commit Murder: Defined under section 18(2)(a) of the Riotous Assemblies Act 17 of 1956, which specifies that a person can be convicted if there is a definitive agreement between at least two individuals to commit a crime, even if that crime was not completed ([18]).
  • Admissibility of Confessions and Admissions: Sections 217 and 219A of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 dictate how confessions and admissions are treated, particularly their admissibility as evidence against co-accused ([7]).

A - Application

In evaluating the admissibility of Shasha’s statement, the Court noted that the statement did not constitute a confession; rather, it contained admissions of facts regarding the planning of criminal conduct. In contrast to a confession, an admission does not equate to guilt and is considered differently under the law ([8]).

The Court critically assessed the reliance on Mbulawa’s testimony to establish a conspiracy. Mbulawa testified that the appellants sought assistance to fight back against their rivals, but this did not sufficiently demonstrate that they conspired to commit murder specifically. The Court found that mere intentions to engage in violent conflict did not amount to a criminal conspiracy ([23]).

With respect to the attempted murder convictions, the Court scrutinized the testimony of eyewitnesses who identified the appellants during the shootings. The Court concluded that despite the rivalry that might influence their testimonies, the evidence of certain eyewitnesses was reliable, especially given their prior familiarity with the appellants ([24], [29]). The Court also upheld the principle of common purpose, noting that Mbayimbayi's presence at the scene during the shootings was sufficient for his accountability under this legal doctrine ([28]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal regarding the conviction and sentence for conspiracy, ruling it unsustainable due to insufficient evidence of a specific agreement to commit murder, thereby expunging the role of Shasha’s statement against the appellants ([32]). However, the convictions for attempted murder were sustained based on reliable eyewitness accounts, leading to revised sentences of 10 years of imprisonment for each assault count, to run concurrently ([32]).

The implications of this ruling reaffirm the necessity of a sound evidential basis for conspiracy charges, while simultaneously illustrating that eyewitness testimony, if credible, may underpin convictions for serious offenses like attempted murder. The case also highlights the tensions between hearsay evidence and fair trial rights, emphasizing the importance of allowing the defense to challenge the reliability of such evidence ([10], [12]).
