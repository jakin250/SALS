Supreme Court of Appeal

Bisschoff & Others v Welbeplan Boerdery (Pty) Ltd [2021] ZASCA 81

Upon discovering breaches in these lease agreements by the respondent, the appellants, via letters dated 1 February 2016, communicated the cancellation of all agreements and instructed the respondent not to access the land.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 81

FIRAC Case Notes

F – Facts

The appellants in this case are Abraham Paulus Bisschoff, the trustees of the Paul Bisschoff Trust, Rietvlug Landgoed (Pty) Ltd, David Eduard Bisschoff, and the trustees of the David Bisschoff Trust. They collectively own several farms in the North West Province of South Africa, utilized for agricultural production. In 2015, the appellants entered into a series of agreements with Welbeplan Boerdery (Pty) Ltd, represented by Mr. Gerhard Olivier, for the sale of 14 portions of the farms, conditional upon the company securing financing for the purchase price. When the financing was not obtained, the agreements were converted into lease agreements for a one-year term.

Upon discovering breaches in these lease agreements by the respondent, the appellants, via letters dated 1 February 2016, communicated the cancellation of all agreements and instructed the respondent not to access the land. Subsequently, the respondent obtained a spoliation order from the North West High Court, which the appellants contested, resulting in the appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 1–4]).

I – Issues

The central legal issues in this case focus on whether the letters sent by the appellants constituted an unlawful deprivation of possession, thereby justifying a claim under the mandament van spolie. The appeal also raised questions regarding the court's authority to avoid an appeal on the grounds of mootness under Section 16(2)(a) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 ([paras 3–4], [12–13]).

R – Rules

The rules governing the mandament van spolie require the claimant to demonstrate (1) peaceful and undisturbed possession of property and (2) unlawful deprivation of that possession. The purpose of this remedy is to maintain public order by preventing self-help. Notably, the standard for “unlawful deprivation” necessitates a degree of physical interference that effectively strips the party of control over the property ([paras 5–6], [10], [15]).

The relevant principles were supported by South African precedents, including Yeko v Qana (1973), which establishes the necessity of actual loss of possession, and Tswelopele Non-Profit Organisation v City of Tshwane (2007), which emphasizes the integrity of the rule of law in property disputes. Additionally, case law from Namibia had significant persuasive value, particularly The Three Musketeers Properties ([para 15], [20]).

A – Application

Upon reviewing the appellants' actions, the court aimed to delineate whether the letters acted as lawful notifications of contract cancellation or if they transcended into unlawful deprivation of the respondent's possession. The court criticized the lower court for interpreting the appellants' letters as acts of spoliation, asserting that instructing a party not to enter one's property—absent any actual removal or interference—does not meet the threshold of unlawful deprivation necessary for a spoliation claim ([paras 12, 15]).

The Supreme Court clarified that the letters served as a demand for compliance with the contractual terms rather than a direct act of dispossession, which the high court had misconstrued. It emphasized that practical consequences arising from the letters, such as not allowing access, did not constitute unlawful deprivation, as the appellants maintained the option of seeking further legal recourse, underscoring the absence of the self-help characteristic of unlawful possession ([paras 10–12], [20–21]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court upheld the appeal, conclusively stating that the high court erred in its determination of spoliation by misapplying the legal principles surrounding unlawful deprivation of possession. The court ruled that the letters merely represented contractual rights exercised by the appellants to cancel the leases and did not amount to spoliation. Thus, the appeal was granted, and the order of the court a quo was set aside, leading to significant implications for the interpretation of possession and the application of the mandament van spolie in property law ([paras 21]).

It was articulated that a strong-reworded letter instructing a party not to enter property, coupled with a threat of legal action, was insufficient to constitute an unlawful deprivation of possession, validating the appellants' legal position. Consequently, this case reinforces the boundaries of spoliation claims in South Africa, highlighting the necessity for physical dispossession as a prerequisite for such actions.
