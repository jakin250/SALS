Rustenburg Local Municipality v Vincent Mdango (937/13) ZASCA 83

This case arose from an application for eviction initiated by the Rustenburg Local Municipality (the appellant) against Vincent Mdango and others (the respondents), comprising various unlawful occupiers of Reconstruction and Development Program (RDP) houses at Seraleng Township,...

Supreme Court of Appeal 2014

F – Facts

This case arose from an application for eviction initiated by the Rustenburg Local Municipality (the appellant) against Vincent Mdango and others (the respondents), comprising various unlawful occupiers of Reconstruction and Development Program (RDP) houses at Seraleng Township, Rustenburg ([paras 1-3]). The backdrop to this litigation can be traced back to November 2007, when the respondents unlawfully occupied a number of RDP houses intended for allocation to approved applicants ([para 4]). The municipality initiated eviction proceedings in 2011 under the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE) without involving the Member of the Executive Council (MEC) or the Minister, who were also cited as respondents ([para 5]).

The municipality claimed that the unlawful occupation hindered its housing program, and the founding affidavit detailed the municipality’s claims regarding the unavailability of alternative accommodations ([para 6]). Conversely, the respondents presented a detailed history illustrating their plight, emphasizing a continuous struggle for adequate housing dating back to 1996, highlighting their forced relocations and failed expectations regarding RDP housing allocations ([paras 7-9]).

The High Court (Gura J) concluded that the respondents were in unlawful occupation of the houses. However, it expressed awareness of the broader socio-economic factors affecting the eviction decision and ultimately granted an eviction order, suspending it pending the availability of suitable alternative accommodation for the respondents ([paras 10-11]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues in this case stemmed from two questions:

  1. Was it just and equitable to evict the respondents from the properties?
  2. Did the High Court err in suspending the eviction order, thereby obliging the municipality to address the question of alternative accommodation? ([para 12]).

R – Rules

The legal framework guiding this case includes:

  • Section 26 of the Constitution of South Africa, which guarantees the right to adequate housing and stipulates that no one may be evicted from their home without a court order considering all relevant circumstances ([para 13]).
  • Section 28 of the Constitution, which ensures children's rights to basic housing and shelter ([para 14]).
  • Section 6 of PIE, which mandates that an organ of state can only evict if it is just and equitable to do so, considering factors like the nature of the unlawful occupation, the time spent on the land, and the availability of suitable alternative accommodation ([para 15]).

Additionally, the decision in Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality & another v Various Occupiers, Eden Park Extension 5 serves as critical precedent, underscoring the necessity for comprehensive evaluations of all circumstances related to housing and evictions ([para 17]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) highlighted inadequacies in the information presented to the High Court, especially regarding the MEC's decision to abstain from providing input on alternative accommodation ([para 16]). The SCA reiterated the importance of a full consideration of each party's circumstances in eviction applications, particularly when vulnerable groups—such as elderly, children, and disabled persons—are involved ([paras 17-18]). The SCA expressed concern that the High Court had insufficient information to make an informed decision about whether eviction was just and equitable.

Furthermore, the court found that neither party had adequately addressed the availability of alternative housing options for the respondents, thereby necessitating the remittance of the matter to the High Court for further proceedings ([paras 19-20]). The orders issued by the SCA included requirements for both the MEC and the respondents to submit affidavits detailing their housing situations and the steps taken to provide alternative accommodations ([paras 4-6]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA upheld both the appeal and the cross-appeal to the extent of remitting the matter for reconsideration in the High Court. The court ordered a stay on the evictions pending the finalization of the matter, emphasizing the necessity of assessing the full context—including personal circumstances—before making decisions affecting vulnerable populations. The court's holding affirms the judicial obligation to scrutinize the interplay between the right to housing and the public interest, ensuring that decisions are made based on comprehensive and equitable considerations ([paras 1-2], [20]). The case illustrates the judiciary's role in balancing individual rights against broader municipal duties, highlighting the complex nature of housing law in South Africa’s constitutional framework.
