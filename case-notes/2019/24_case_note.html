Constitutional Court

Nandutu and Others v Minister of Home Affairs and Others

The applicants in this matter are foreign nationals who are married to or in life partnerships with South African citizens or permanent residents.

Constitutional Court 2019

FIRAC Method

F - Facts

The applicants in this matter are foreign nationals who are married to or in life partnerships with South African citizens or permanent residents. Robinah Sarah Nandutu (the first applicant), a Ugandan citizen, married James Ferrior Tomlinson (the second applicant), a British citizen and permanent resident of South Africa, while visiting South Africa on a visitor’s visa that she obtained in Uganda. Her visa was set with restrictions, including a duration not exceeding 30 days ([paras 7–8]).

Shortly after entering South Africa, Nandutu applied for a temporary visitor’s visa, which she believed to be a spousal visa, but the application was ultimately rejected. The rejection was based on the ground that no change of status could be granted while they were in the Republic ([paras 9–10]).

Ilias Demerlis (the third applicant), a Greek citizen, and Christakis Fokas Ttofalli (the fourth applicant), a South African citizen, were in a similar plight as they faced rejection of Demerlis’s application for a spousal visa ([paras 12–13]). The applicants raised similar concerns about their denied applications for visas and the inability to alter their visa statuses without exiting the Republic.

After their applications were rejected by the Department of Home Affairs, the applicants approached the High Court of South Africa, Western Cape Division, challenging the constitutionality of regulation 9(9)(a) of the Immigration Regulations, which required foreign spouses to leave the country to apply for a change of visa status. The High Court ruled against them, which led the applicants to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court ([paras 15–22]).

I - Issues

The central legal issues raised in the appeal are:

  1. Constitutional Validity: Is regulation 9(9)(a) of the Immigration Regulations constitutionally valid, considering it does not extend the “exceptional circumstances” required for a change in visa status to include foreign spouses or children of South African citizens or permanent residents? ([para 23])
  2. Infringement of Rights: Does the requirement for foreign spouses to exit South Africa infringe upon their constitutional rights, specifically the right to dignity and the best interests of the child? ([para 23])
  3. Jurisdiction and Relief: Should leave to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court be granted and what is the appropriate judicial remedy in this case? ([paras 26, 29])

R - Rules

The Constitutional Court primarily relied on the constitutional provisions found in Chapters 2 and 28 of the Constitution of South Africa, which enshrine the rights to dignity ([section 10]), the rights of children ([section 28]), and legislative undertakings regarding family unity and familial rights.

  • Dawood v Minister of Home Affairs is a pivotal case cited by the applicants, where the Court held that legislation requiring foreign spouses to leave the country infringed upon the right to dignity ([paras 35–39]).
  • The Immigration Act 13 of 2002, particularly sections 10(6)(b) and 31(2)(c), provides the framework for visa applications and the potential for waiving requirements by the Minister under certain exceptional circumstances.

The Court's examination entails applying the limitation analysis under section 36 of the Constitution, determining if the limitation on rights is permissible regarding its justification and relation to the purpose of regulation 9(9)(a) ([paras 70–71]).

A - Application

The Constitutional Court elucidated that regulation 9(9)(a) imposes a limitation on the dignity of foreign spouses by necessitating that they exit the country to apply for a change in visa status. The impact of this regulation creates a situation where families are unreasonably separated, directly counteracting the rights to family life, cohabitation, and fulfilling responsibilities toward each other ([paras 57–60]).

The Court analyzed whether the limitation imposed by regulation 9(9)(a) can be justifiably defended against the constitutional rights it violates. The argument rested on the government’s assertion of a need to manage borders effectively and prevent fraudulent marriages. However, the Court found insufficient justification presented by the respondents to support coercing foreign spouses to leave and remained skeptical about whether less restrictive means existed to fulfill the objectives purportedly accomplished by the regulation ([paras 75–78]).

The applicants further argued that the reading-in of additional sub-regulations into regulation 9(9)(a) to include foreign spouses would remedy the unconstitutionality without undermining legislative power ([paras 83–90]).

This brought into the light the necessity of balancing the separation of powers versus a judicial remedy to immediately protect the interests of the affected parties ([paras 91–92]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court held in favor of the applicants, declaring that regulation 9(9)(a) is unconstitutional for failing to account for the rights of foreign spouses and children, as it imposes a limitation that is neither reasonable nor justifiable ([paras 80–81]).

In terms of remedy, the Court imposed a 24-month suspension of the declaration of invalidity, allowing Parliament time to correct the legislative deficiency while simultaneously introducing an interim reading-in process to include foreign spouses within the definition of “exceptional circumstances.” The Court underscored the importance of familial rights and the necessity of coherence in legislative frameworks that respect human dignity ([paras 92–95]).

The implications of this case emphasize the balance needed between immigration control and human rights, fundamentally asserting that familial ties must be honored within the immigration regime, thereby expanding protective measures for foreign spouses and children while maintaining the integrity of legislative power.
