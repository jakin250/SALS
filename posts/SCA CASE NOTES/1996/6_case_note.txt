Case Note: IN THIS SUPREME COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1996

# FIRAC Case Note: Hlongwana Phako v The State (Case No 423/95)

## F – Facts

In the case of **Hlongwana Phako v The State** ([paras 2–3]), the appellant was convicted in the Komga Magistrate's Court for dealing in 292 kilograms of dagga, a violation of section 5(b) of Act 140 of 1992. Following his conviction, he was sentenced to eight years' imprisonment, with three years suspended for five years under specific conditions. The appellant had pleaded guilty, admitting possession and dealing with the dagga, though no further facts about the offense were disclosed, except that he was traveling to Cape Town when apprehended.

The appellant's personal circumstances were noted; he was 25 years old, married, with two minor children, and held steady employment. He was a first offender and had expressed remorse for his actions. While these mitigating factors were acknowledged by the sentencing magistrate, the gravity of the offense and its prevalence in the Komga district were also considered. Notably, the magistrate observed a significant increase in such crimes in 1994 compared to 1993, and emphasized the substantial street value of the drugs involved, estimated at R292,000, hence further justifying a severe sentence.

Despite the appeal to the Eastern Cape Division being unsuccessful, the appellant sought to challenge his sentence in the Supreme Court of South Africa, claiming the magistrate misdirected himself by referencing the offense's prevalence and the dagga's street value, which were not supported by evidence presented during the trial ([paras 3–4]).

## I – Issues

The principal legal questions arising from this case included:

1. **Did the magistrate err in considering the prevalence of the offense and the street value of the dagga without specific evidence to support these assertions?** ([para 4])
2. **Was there a misdirection by the magistrate regarding the balancing of societal interests against the appellant's personal circumstances?** ([para 5])
3. **Did the magistrate fail to consider alternative sentencing options, such as correctional supervision, in light of the recent legislative changes?** ([para 6])

## R – Rules

The court relied on several legal principles related to the sentencing process, including:

- The consideration of personal circumstances and mitigating factors in sentencing as outlined in **S v M** 1990 (2) SA CR 509 (E) and **S v Nkosi** 1992 (1) SA CR 607 (T), establishing that a magistrate's knowledge of local crime prevalence is credible and can inform sentencing ([para 4]).
- Judicial discretion in determining the severity of sentences based on the specifics of each case, emphasizing that comparative analysis across cases may not be constructive ([para 5]).
- Recognition of shifts in legislative policy regarding sentences, as articulated in Act 140 of 1992, and the expectation that sentencing must reflect current legal standards ([para 6]).

## A – Application

In applying the law to the facts, the Supreme Court found that the magistrate had exercised appropriate discretion by considering the crime's prevalence and the street value of the dagga. The court acknowledged that local magistrates possess firsthand knowledge of crime trends, which justified the magistrate's consideration of these factors, despite the appellant's argument of a lack of direct evidence ([para 4]).

Regarding the claim that the magistrate focused too heavily on societal interests, the court held that protecting society from increasing drug trade was a legitimate concern, indicating a balance was struck in weighing the appellant's personal circumstances against the crime's severity ([para 5]). The Supreme Court reaffirmed that the appellant's purported role as merely a transporter of drugs did not lower his moral culpability, as no evidence substantiated this claim ([para 5]).

Finally, the claim concerning overlooked options for correctional supervision was dismissed. The court noted that the matter of correctional sentencing was not presented as a ground for appeal in the lower court, implying that the magistrate likely considered it but deemed it inappropriate under the circumstances ([para 6]).

## C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of South Africa upheld the decision of the lower court, ruling that the magistrate did not misdirect himself in any material way, nor was the sentence unduly harsh. The court emphasized that the seriousness of drug offenses warrants significant sentences, particularly given societal impacts ([paras 5–7]). The implications of this ruling reinforce the necessity for courts to balance individual circumstances against the need for public protection in drug-related cases. The appeal was, therefore, dismissed ([para 7]).

This case serves as a critical reference point regarding the principles governing sentencing in South African law, particularly in the context of drug offenses, where judicial discretion remains paramount while adhering to legislative frameworks.
