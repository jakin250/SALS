# Case Note: Glynn Rudolph v Commissioner for Inland Revenue

**Court**: Supreme Court of South Africa (Appellate Division)  
**Case Number**: 503/94  
**Judges**: Corbett CJ, Smalberger JA, Vivier JA, Nienaber JA, Plewman AJA  
**Heard On**: 12 March 1996  
**Judgment Delivered On**: 25 March 1996

## FIRAC Method Case Note

### F – Facts ([paras 2–8])

The first applicant, Glynn Rudolph, a businessman, and the second applicant, Glynn Rudolph & Co Pty Ltd, both sought legal relief against the first respondent, the Commissioner for Inland Revenue, and seven of his officials. The legal conflict originated from the failure of the applicants to submit accurate income tax returns from the years 1988 to 1992, despite repeated assurances. 

On 20 October 1993, Mr. C T Prinsloo issued fourteen authorizations under section 74(3) of the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962 (the "Act"), enabling specific officials from the Revenue Department to exercise certain search and seizure powers related to the applicants' businesses. Following a search that took place on 22 April 1994 at the company’s Johannesburg premises, crucial documentation was seized. The applicants contended that the search warranted violated common law principles of validity, primarily arguing that the original authorizations had flaws in their delegation of authority and vagueness in wording.  

After initial proceedings in lower court which led to the dismissal of the application for interdicts against the respondents, the applicants sought leave to appeal, successfully petitioning the Chief Justice after an initial refusal of appeal. 

### I – Issues ([paras 1, 2])

The primary issues presented to the court involved:

1. **Constitutionality of Section 74(3) of the Income Tax Act**: Whether this provision was contrary to the protections afforded under Chapter 3 of the Constitution, particularly regarding administrative justice ([para 2(a)]).

2. **Jurisdiction**: Whether the Appellate Division had the jurisdiction to decide the common law grounds of invalidity or whether such matters exclusively fell within the domain of the Constitutional Court ([para 2(b)]).

3. **Procedural Directions**: If the Appellate Division could adjudicate on common law grounds, what further directives were needed for the resolution of the appeal ([para 2(c)]).

4. **Validity of Common Law Grounds**: If the issues were indeed within the Constitutional Court’s exclusive jurisdiction, whether the grounds of invalidity raised had merit ([para 2(d)]).

### R – Rules ([paras 7, 8])

The court referenced several legal principles and constitutional provisions relevant to the case:

- **Income Tax Act 58 of 1962**, specifically section 74(3), which authorizes search and seizure without a warrant under certain conditions ([para 3]).
- **Section 24 of the Constitution**, detailing the right to lawful and procedurally fair administrative actions ([para 7]).
- **Section 98(2) and Section 101(5) of the Constitution**, which delineate the jurisdictional limits of the Appellate Division and grant the Constitutional Court exclusive jurisdiction over constitutional matters involving violations of fundamental rights ([paras 7, 8]).

### A – Application ([paras 9–10])

The court engaged in an analysis that underscored the distinction between common law and constitutional grounds for invalidity. The Appellate Division recognized the appellants' assertion that the common law issues raised could amount to breaches of constitutional rights as identified under section 24. However, it ruled that the common law grounds directly overlapped with constitutional interpretations, which the Appellate Division was not authorized to make according to section 101(5).

The argument for parallel jurisdiction by the appellants was dismissed on the basis that determining the validity of the authorizations would inherently require constitutional interpretation, something outside the court's remit. Consequently, it was asserted that any constitutional issue must be referred to the Constitutional Court to ensure proper adjudication as mandated by section 102(6).

### C – Conclusion ([paras 10, 11])

The court concluded that the common law grounds raised by the appellants were indeed entwined with constitutional matters to the extent that a definitive ruling could not be made without involving the Constitutional Court. Thus, the Appellate Division's order to refer the case for constitutional interpretation was validated, outlining a clear delineation of jurisdictional boundaries between the two courts.

The implications of this decision underscored the broader constitutional mandate regarding administrative justice, as articulated in the Constitution, marking a significant point for future income tax disputes and illustrating the interaction between common law and constitutional law in South Africa.
