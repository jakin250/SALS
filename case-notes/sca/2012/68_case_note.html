The Gap Inc v Salt of the Earth Creations (Pty) Ltd and Others [2012] ZASCA 68

Supreme Court of Appeal 2012 [2012] ZASCA 68

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The ongoing legal battle between The Gap, Inc. (the Appellant) and three South African companies—Salt of the Earth Creations (Pty) Ltd, Kingsgate Clothing (Pty) Ltd, and Paul Vivaldi Fashions (Pty) Ltd (the Respondents)—centers on the Appellant's attempt to maintain its registered trademark "Gap" in South Africa (para [1]). The case originates from an application made by the Respondents to expunge the Appellant's trademark registration for lack of bona fide use, as catered for by Section 27(1) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 (the Act) (para [1]).

The initial application for expungement was filed on 5 March 2008, targeting the relevant period from 4 December 2002 to 4 December 2007 (para [8]). The North Gauteng High Court under Southwood J ruled in favor of the Respondents, leading to the Appellant's appeal (paras [1], [8]). The Registrar of Trade Marks did not participate in the proceedings (para [1]).

During the hearings, it was disclosed that a settlement had been reached between the parties. However, the Appellant proceeded with the appeal to address issues concerning the public registry as the court's judgment had implications beyond individual parties (para [2]).

The Appellant claimed bona fide use through two distinct periods: the initial phase from 2002 to 2003, which involved a contract with Clicks Group Limited leading to minimal sales, and a subsequent period from 2006 onwards involving Stuttafords Stores (Pty) Ltd, wherein substantial interest was observed in Gap products (paras [9], [10], [11]).

The High Court found insufficient evidence to establish bona fide use, primarily noting the inconsequential sale figures and viewing the efforts as mere attempts to maintain trademark validity, hence supporting the expungement request (paras [10], [12]).

I – Issues

The legal questions before the Supreme Court of Appeal were:

  1. Whether the Appellant had proved bona fide use of the "Gap" trademark during the relevant period as required under Section 27 of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 (para [1]).
  2. Whether the negotiations between the Appellant and South African retailers constituted sufficient "use" of the trademark within the regulatory framework (paras [12], [14]).

R – Rules

The relevant statutory provision is the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, especially Section 27(1) which permits the removal of a trademark based on factors such as:

  • Lack of bona fide intention to use the trademark by its applicant for registration (s 27(1)(a)).
  • A lapse of five years without bona fide use (s 27(1)(b)).

The Court also referred to established legal precedents for interpreting "bona fide use." For example, in AM Moolla Group Ltd v The Gap Inc ([2005] 6 SA 568 (SCA)), the definition was clarified, emphasizing the necessity for use that goes beyond mere token efforts aimed at securing trademark rights (para [4]).

The Court's analysis also highlighted that trademark ownership is centered around actual use in trade for the goods or services registered, referencing the European Court of Justice stating that genuine use signifies a commercial activity consistent with the purpose of the trademark (para [4] and [9]).

A – Application

Upon evaluating both periods of claimed use presented by the Appellant, the Court was particularly critical.

  1. First Period (2002 - 2003): Sales figures of 21 units during an illicit tenure with Clicks were deemed insubstantial and suggested that these transactions could be construed as strategic moves merely intended to preserve trademark rights rather than genuine market engagement (para [10], [12]).
  2. Second Period (2007): While the Appellant demonstrated attempts to engage with Stuttafords for greater market reach, the High Court was not persuaded. It noted that the negotiations termed as "use" lacked evidentiary backing regarding actual sale or usage within South African territory (para [11], [12]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal, however, took a contrary view. It reasoned that while the Appellant’s operational scale was modest compared to global benchmarks, the evidence presented surpassed the minimum threshold of bona fide use required to counter expungement (paras [15], [16]). Furthermore, it contended that the geographic location of negotiations should not dilute the notion of use if the intent was clearly aimed at establishing business in South Africa (para [14]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the Appellant’s appeal, thereby overturning the lower court's ruling. The judgment emphasized that the standard for proving bona fide use does not necessitate extensive commercial performance but rather a genuine intent and effort to engage in local commerce (para [17]).

The judgment holds critical implications for trademark holders in South Africa, reaffirming that minor sales or preliminary negotiations can constitute genuine use provided they are aligned with legitimate business objectives. Additionally, the case underscores the importance of protecting trademarks within the context of their intended markets, potentially influencing future trademark litigation and delineating the boundaries within which bona fide use must be assessed.

As a result, the appeal was upheld, the order of the High Court was vacated, and the Respondents' application for expungement was ultimately dismissed (para [17]).
