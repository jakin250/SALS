Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

### FIRAC Case Note: Stu Davidson v Eastern Cape Motors (260/2017) [2018] ZASCA 26

#### F – Facts
The litigation commenced in the Regional Court, Port Elizabeth, when Eastern Cape Motors (Pty) Ltd (Motors) sued Stu Davidson and Sons (Pty) Ltd (Davidson) for damages stemming from an alleged breach of warranty related to a vehicle sale ([1]). Davidson had traded in a Volkswagen Transporter motor vehicle, which was sold to Motors for R245,000 as part of an agreement where Davidson purchased a Ford Ranger from Motors. Motors also sought to recover costs incurred from attempting to resell the vehicle ([1]).

Initially, the regional court dismissed Motors’ claims and did not adjudicate Davidson's counterclaim for rectification of the trade-in declaration. On appeal to the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, the court found that Davidson breached the warranty stating that the vehicle had not been involved in any accident ([2]). It ruled that Davidson was liable to pay R95,000 as damages, derived from the difference between the purchase price and the resale price of the vehicle. Davidson’s claims for costs and for rectification were dismissed ([2]).

Davidson appealed the full bench’s decision with special leave from the Supreme Court of Appeal ([3]). The facts reveal that Mr. Patrick Davidson, acting on behalf of Davidson, had disclosed to Motors’ representative that the vehicle had been involved in an accident. Notably, the warranty included a declaration asserting that the vehicle had not been involved in a “substantial/major accident,” which became the pivotal issue in the case ([5]).


#### I – Issues
The central legal questions were twofold: first, whether Davidson breached the warranty regarding the vehicle’s accident history ([22]); and second, whether sufficient special circumstances existed to justify granting Davidson special leave to appeal ([22]). The latter issue depended on the outcome of the first query.


#### R – Rules
The court relied on established principles of contract law concerning warranties and their enforceability. Specifically, a warranty is deemed a binding term of a contract, and warranties must be interpreted within their plain context, emphasizing that the statement regarding the vehicle’s condition was clear and not vague ([15]). The judgment cited *Westinghouse Brake & Equipment (Pty) Ltd v Bilger Engineering (Pty) Ltd* to illustrate that the court only intervenes in lower court decisions on grounds that they are manifestly incorrect or where special circumstances warrant such intervention ([3], [18]).

Additional case law clarified that reasonable prospects of success on appeal do not occur merely from being granted special leave; such entitlement necessitates an examination of special circumstances ([18]).


#### A – Application
Upon examining the evidence, the court affirmed that the vehicle had indeed been involved in a major accident, thus breaching the warranty ([9],[10]). Expert evidence demonstrated that the vehicle had extensive damage amounting to R261,946, further confirming that the resale value was substantially impacted ([10]). This was supported by testimony from multiple witnesses, including Mr. Hechter, who asserted that he would not have purchased the vehicle had he been aware of its previous accident ([29]).

Davidson contended that the warranty clause was vague and thus unenforceable. However, the court dismissed this argument, affirming that warranties are binding, and the warranty in question specifically referred to substantial damage affecting resale value, which was well substantiated by the available evidence ([15], [32]).

Regarding the special leave appeal, the court determined that Davidson had failed to demonstrate any reasonable prospects of success or special circumstances. The absence of a compelling issue of law or fact, coupled with the minimal amount in dispute, suggested that appellate consideration was unwarranted ([19],[20]).


#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that the appeal by Davidson was devoid of merit and struck it from the roll with costs ([20],[21]). The court reaffirmed the validity and clarity of the warranty in the trade-in declaration, indicating unequivocally that Davidson had breached said warranty. The ruling has implications for the interpretation of warranty clauses in vehicle sales, emphasizing that such warranties will be enforced when their language is clear and unambiguous. Furthermore, the decision reinforces the principle that special leave to appeal requires demonstrable special circumstances beyond mere discontent with a lower court's decision, highlighting the court's reluctance to entertain appeals lacking substantial legal or factual disputes.
