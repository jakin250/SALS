Source Folder: 2009

### FIRAC Case Note: Erasmus Ferreira & Ackermann v Francis (311/08)

#### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–4])

This case stemmed from a claim for damages arising from the professional negligence of attorneys, Erasmus Ferreira & Ackermann, following the death of Bruce Francis in a motor vehicle collision on 9 October 1998. Kerry-Lynn Francis, the plaintiff, was the widow of the deceased and, after his death, sought damages for loss of support from the Road Accident Fund (RAF) under the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1956. She instructed the appellants, who are attorneys, to pursue this claim. However, the appellants failed to issue summons within the statutory time limit, resulting in the loss of her claim against the RAF due to prescription. Consequently, Francis initiated a lawsuit against the defendants for professional negligence, claiming R850,000, the amount she would have recovered had the appellants acted diligently.

The deceased was employed by Douglas Colliery Services and was covered by a Commuting Journey Policy with Rand Mutual Assurance, entitling the plaintiff to a pension of R695,525. While this amount was non-recoverable from the RAF due to the Assessment of Damages Act 9 of 1969, which prohibits the deduction of insurance money or benefits from loss of support claims, it also introduced complexities in the litigation regarding the defendants' liability to the plaintiff ([paras 2–4]).

#### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 5–6])

The appeal featured two main issues for consideration:

1. Whether the defendants, being the attorneys, constituted “third parties” as defined under Condition 8 of the Commuting Journey Policy (CJP), which would determine the applicability of obligations to indemnify Rand Mutual from damages recovered from third parties.

2. Whether Section 1 of the Assessment Damages Act applied to the plaintiff’s action against the defendants, potentially influencing the defendants' liability—a critical distinction as this would determine if the defendants could deduct the insurance benefits when calculating damages owed to the plaintiff ([paras 5–6]).

#### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 9–12, 17–19])

The legal framework primarily revolves around two statutes: the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1956 and the Assessment of Damages Act 9 of 1969. Section 1(1) of the Assessment of Damages Act expressly states that in an action for damages for loss of support due to death, no compensation from insurance, pensions, or benefits may be deducted in assessing damages ([para 9]).

The court also examined the definition and application of “third parties” as it relates to the CJP. The rule being addressed is whether a legal obligation can arise from negligence in failing to pursue a claim, thereby causing economic loss. The court referenced several precedents, including standard delictual principles, which dictate that plaintiffs cannot receive "double compensation" unless expressly justified through legal statutes or conditions that provide coverage ([paras 10–12, 17–19]).

#### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 7–20])

In assessing the first question, the court upheld the high court's judgment, concluding that the defendants were not "third parties" as defined under the CJP because their liability arose from their negligence rather than the accident itself. This interpretation reinforced the necessity of clarity in policy definitions and the need for exact causality between actions and obligations ([para 8]).

Regarding the second issue, the court rejected the defendants' claim that the Assessment Act did not apply to the present case. It pointed out that while the action against the RAF differed conceptually from claims for professional negligence, both were intrinsically linked to the loss of support. The focus remained on the damages stemming from the negligence that led to the loss of recovering support claims, thereby qualifying under the ambit of the Assessment Act ([paras 11–12]).

The court also deliberated on whether the pension benefit constituted a collateral benefit: should it be disregarded while computing damages due based upon fairness principles. Ultimately, the court ruled it would not be equitable to allow the defendants to reduce their responsibility due to the pension benefit, as it stemmed from the negligence that barred the plaintiff from claiming against the RAF. Judging alongside the moral obligations of the plaintiff vis-à-vis Rand Mutual, the balance of fairness leaned towards full compensation for the plaintiff's loss of support claim ([paras 18–20]).

#### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 21–24])

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) dismissed the appeal, concluding that the defendants were liable to pay the full amount of R850,000 to the plaintiff, inclusive of interest and costs as previously agreed. The rationale was rooted in the principles of fairness: it would be unjust for the defendants to benefit from the fact that the plaintiff continued to receive a pension while having caused her to lose her claim for loss of support.

The implications of the decision emphasize the broader understanding of negligence and liability in professional settings. By establishing that the Assessment Act language protected the plaintiff's rights, the court reinforced the principle that a claimant should not suffer for the professional failings of their legal representatives. This ruling lays down significant precedents for future negligence claims, particularly in cases involving complex insurance and benefit structures within South Africa's legal context ([paras 19–20]).
