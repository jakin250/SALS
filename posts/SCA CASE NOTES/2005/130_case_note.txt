Source Folder: 2005

# FIRAC Case Note: Republic of South Africa v M G Phenithi

### F – Facts

The appellant, M G Phenithi, had been employed as an educator for 15 years at Nkgodise Primary School in Dewetsdorp. On 31 July 2000, she received a letter from the Head of the Department of Education in the Free State (the third respondent), informing her that she was "deemed to have been discharged from service" under section 14(1)(a) of the Employment of Educators Act 76 of 1998 due to her absence from work for more than 14 consecutive days without the employer’s permission ([1]).

Phenithi contended that she had submitted medical certificates justifying her absences due to illness from 16 February 2000 to April 2000 and after her son’s death on 6 June 2000. However, her principal, Mr. Ramakau, denied receiving these certificates for the critical periods of her absence ([5]-[6]). Following the failure of conciliation efforts at the Education Labour Relations Council, Phenithi sought relief through the High Court, claiming her dismissal was both unfair and unconstitutional. The court a quo dismissed her application, leading her to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([3]).

### I – Issues

The appeal raised two primary legal issues: 

1. **Whether the appellant’s discharge from duty constituted administrative action**, and if so, whether it was carried out fairly ([4]).
2. **The constitutionality of section 14(1)(a) of the Employment of Educators Act**, which deems an educator discharged after 14 consecutive days of absence without permission ([15]).

### R – Rules

The legal framework surrounding this case includes:

- **Section 14(1)(a) of the Employment of Educators Act 76 of 1998**: This section provides that an educator who is absent for more than 14 consecutive days without employer permission is deemed to be discharged for misconduct, applying automatically by operation of law without requiring a discretionary decision from the employer ([2]).
- **Audi alteram partem**: The legal principle requiring that a party be given an opportunity to present their case before a decision affecting their rights is made.
- **Peremptory provisions**: The court referred to *Minister van Onderwys en Kultuur v Louw* to determine that the deeming provision in question is not contingent on a decision by the employer, and thus does not constitute administrative action subject to review ([9],[10]).
- **Section 33(1) of the Constitution**: Mandates lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair administrative action.
- **Section 188 of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995**: Addresses unfair dismissals and procedural fairness.

### A – Application

In concluding that the discharge did not constitute administrative action, the court applied the principles established in *Louw*. It determined that the operation of section 14(1)(a) merely notified the appellant of a legal consequence resulting from her absence, not from a discretionary decision made by the employer ([10]). 

The court examined the circumstantial facts surrounding Phenithi's absence. It found that despite her claims of ill health and bereavement, she had failed to effectively communicate these circumstances to the employer in a timely manner or to prove that her absences were justified in a manner that would interrupt the 14-day threshold ([14]). As such, the statutory requirements for discharge under this section were objectively determined to be met.

Regarding the constitutionality of section 14(1)(a), the court noted that while the provision does not explicitly require a hearing before the operation of the deeming provision, it does not exclude the possibility for an educator to submit reasons to the employer in an effort to prevent the application of section 14(1)(a) ([21]). The court ruled that the law serves a critical purpose in ensuring that absences without leave do not disrupt educational operations, hence balancing educator rights with students' needs ([25]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the decision of the court a quo, dismissing Phenithi’s appeal. The court concluded that her discharge was neither administrative action nor unconstitutional under the provisions of sections 23(1) and 33(1) of the Constitution. The ruling affirmed the validity of section 14(1)(a) as a necessary legislative measure for the education sector designed to maintain operational stability while allowing for possible post-discharge reinstatement under section 14(2) of the Act ([26]). 

While the dismissal did not prejudice Phenithi’s right to a hearing under all circumstances, it highlighted deficiencies in how such procedures are communicated by the employer ([28]). Therefore, no costs were awarded against Phenithi, reflecting the nuances of procedural fairness within public service employment ([30]).

In summary, the court’s decision serves as a precedent on the interpretation of employment law provisions concerning automatic discharges, the limits of administrative action requirements, and the balance between educator rights and institutional responsibilities.
