Case Note: REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2006

### Case Note: Mano et Mano v Nationwide Airlines (Pty) Ltd and Others [2006] SCA 156 (RSA)

#### F - Facts 
The legal dispute arose from a commission claim filed by Mano, an aircraft broker, against Nationwide Airlines for the sale of two Boeing 737-200 aircraft. The procedural history began with a trial at the Johannesburg High Court, where the court ruled in favour of Mano, ordering Nationwide to pay a commission. Nationwide appealed this decision, and the full court subsequently reversed it, prompting the current appeal ([1]).

In late 1998 or early 1999, Mano's director, David Stark, and Nationwide's chief executive, Vernon Bricknell, agreed that Mano would seek aircraft to replace Nationwide's fleet of BAC 111s, with a commission of one percent of purchase price ([2]). Mano received specifications from El Al Israel Airlines for two Boeing 737-200s and began negotiations, which were complicated when the aircraft were sold to a rival purchaser, Aero Al ([3]–[6]). Although Bricknell had shown interest in the aircraft, eventual negotiations ceased when Nationwide procured two aircraft from Croatia Airlines instead ([6]–[8]).

In early 2000, due to new aviation regulations that jeopardised the operation of the BAC 111s, Bricknell resumed his search for aircraft ([9]). Bricknell then discovered the El Al aircraft advertised in a trade journal, recalling his earlier interaction with the airline, and he initiated negotiations leading to a purchase by Aerotrans, part of the Nationwide group ([10]).

The appeal revolves around whether Mano was the effective cause of the sale, which would establish their entitlement to the commission ([11]).

#### I - Issues
The primary issue is whether Mano was the effective cause of the sale of the aircraft, thus entitled to the commission. The court analyzed this against the backdrop of whether there was a break in the chain of causation linking Mano’s efforts to the ultimate sale of the aircraft to Aerotrans by Nationwide ([12]).

#### R - Rules
In determining the effective cause of a sale, South African law adheres to the principle articulated in established case law that emphasizes substance over form concerning agents' entitlement to commission. Key principles include:

1. **Efficient Cause**: An agent entitled to commission must demonstrate they are the efficient cause of the transaction, which can occur even if the sale is concluded after the agent’s involvement ([13)–[14]).
   
2. **Intervening Cause**: The existence of an intervening cause that is sufficiently weighty can break the chain of causation, making the agent ineligible for commission ([21]).

3. **Factors Considered**: The court assesses not only the lapse of time but also the nature of the sales negotiations ([20]).

4. **Case Comparisons**: The court referenced various estate agency cases to establish benchmarks for determining effective causation, including *Doyle v Gibbon* and *Aida Real Estate v Lipschitz*, which indicate that unless resumed actions significantly veer from the agent's introduction, the agent's initial efforts remain operative ([15]–[17]).

#### A - Application
Applying the above principles, the court concluded that by the time of the aircraft's purchase by Aerotrans, Mano's input had ceased and there were no further actions taken to negotiate the acquisition following Nationwide's purchase of Croatian aircraft. The lapse in engagement meant that Mano could not be deemed to have been the effective cause of the subsequent sale ([22]–[24]).

Furthermore, the emergence of new aviation regulations created an urgent need for Nationwide to replace its aircraft, making any prior negotiations with Mano irrelevant in the context of the immediate purchase decision. The court highlighted that the intervening cause—not Mano's previous actions—was the impetus for the eventual sale ([23]).

Lastly, the court dismissed claims based on the alleged ‘Mutual Non-circumvention, Non-disclosure Agreement,’ noting that it did not change the common law situation as Mano was not the effective cause of the sale ([25]).

#### C - Conclusion 
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that Mano was not entitled to the commission as it was not the effective cause of the sale of the aircraft. The appeal was dismissed with costs ([26]). This judgment reinforces the importance of establishing a direct causal link between the agent's efforts and the completion of a sale, clarifying the circumstances under which commission claims can succeed in commission-based agreements. The decision has implications for the standards of effective cause in agency relationships within both the aviation and broader commercial sectors.
