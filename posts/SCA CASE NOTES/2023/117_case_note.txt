### FIRAC Case Note: De Graaf NO v C[…] (565/2022) [2023] ZASCA 117

#### F – Facts
The case concerns an appeal by **Anthony Robert De Graaf NO** (the Appellant), executor of the estate of his late husband **R[…] C[…]**, against the judgment of the **Western Cape Division of the High Court** that ordered him to pay **C[…] S[…] C[…]** (the Respondent), the ex-wife of the deceased, a substantial amount calculated as 50% of the deceased’s pension benefits. The Respondent had previously been awarded a total of **R3 225 302.66** which included payments from the deceased's pension fund, specifically from the **Munich Reinsurance Company Pension Fund (MR pension fund)** and a **Sanlam Retirement Annuity Fund** ([paras 1-2]).

The couple had been married in 1983, with a divorce finalized on August 2, 1999, through a consent paper incorporated into their divorce order. The consent paper covered the deceased's pension interests and specifically stated that 50% of his pension interest accrued at the date of divorce was to be paid to the Respondent ([paras 2-4]). Disputes arose concerning the interpretation of clauses 9.4 and 9.7 within this consent paper, which specified additional payments to ensure the Respondent received half of the deceased's net entitlement upon his withdrawal from the pension funds.

The Appellant argued that the clauses in question were vague, unenforceable, or should only permit the Respondent to receive 50% of the growth in pension interest, amounting to far less than the high court ruling. The High Court had accepted the Respondent's claim, and the Appellant subsequently appealed ([paras 5-6]).

#### I – Issues
The primary legal question in this case involved the interpretation of clauses 9.4 and 9.7 of the consent paper to determine if they clearly stipulated that the Appellant was obligated to pay the Respondent half of the deceased's entire retirement benefits as defined under the relevant legislative framework, particularly focusing on whether:

1. The clauses were vague and unenforceable.
2. The Respondent was entitled to 50% of the entire benefit accrued beyond the date of divorce until the time of withdrawal from the respective pension funds ([paras 4-6, 16]).

#### R – Rules
This case was governed by significant legal principles contained in the **Divorce Act No. 70 of 1979**, especially Section 1 defining "pension interest," and Sections 7(7) and 7(8) aligning with the provisions of the **Pension Funds Act** (PFA) as amended by the **Pension Funds Amendment Act 11 of 2007**, which introduced the "clean break principle." These statutory provisions assert a non-member spouse’s entitlement to a share of a member spouse’s pension interest that should be adjudicated upon divorce ([paras 9-14]).

Relevant case law included:

- **Old Mutual Life Assurance Co (SA) Ltd v Swemmer** highlighting the interpretation of pension interests in divorce ([para 13]).
- **Wiese v Government Employees Pension Fund**, where the constitutional context around non-member spouse rights to pensions was discussed ([para 14]).
- **Sentinel Retirement Fund v Masoanganye**, which confirmed that once pension interest is paid, it loses its protected status ([para 15]).

#### A – Application
The court assessed the language in clauses 9.4 and 9.7, considering whether they contained sufficient clarity to impose obligations on the deceased consistent with the intentions of both parties as expressed at the time of their agreement. Following careful analysis, the **Supreme Court of Appeal** determined that the wording was unequivocal in demanding that the deceased would pay the Respondent half of his net entitlement at the withdrawal date from both pension fund schemes.

Moreover, the court rejected the Appellant's assertions regarding vagueness by arguing that the clauses intentionally provided additional amounts payable upon withdrawal, which necessitated a straightforward interpretation. The decision emphasized contractual intention, whereby the clear expression of the parties' intentions in the consent paper guided the judgment ([para 18]). 

Distinct calculations were provided by both parties illustrating scenarios based on different interpretations of the impugned clauses. Ultimately, the **Supreme Court of Appeal** endorsed the Respondent's interpretation that amounted to **R805,125.00** from the MR pension fund as the suitable entitlement, illustrating the Court's adherence to objective interpretations regarding contractual obligations ([paras 24-25]).

#### C – Conclusion
The **Supreme Court of Appeal** upheld the appeal in part, redefining the actual amount recoverable by the Respondent to be **R805 125.00** (not the higher figure cited by the High Court) pertaining to the pension fund and **R22 320.94** from the Sanlam RA, which was based on calculations clarifying the actual economic entitlement under the agreed terms ([paras 26-28]). 

Notably, the court underscored the importance of clear drafting in consent papers and reaffirmed the fairness of allowing each party to bear their own legal costs, recognizing the complexities of the case ([paras 27-28]).

This case affirms the significance of precise language in legal agreements, particularly in the context of divorce and indigent spouse rights to pension benefits. The **De Graaf** ruling sets a critical precedential backdrop for future interpretations surrounding pension entitlements following divorce, highlighting the judiciary's role in safeguarding equitable distribution while balancing party intentions and statutory mandates.
