# FIRAC Case Note: Rhino Oil and Gas Exploration South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Normandien Farms (Pty) Ltd & Another (100/2018) [2019] ZASCA 88

## F - Facts ([paras 1-34])
The appeal in question arises from an application made by **Rhino Oil and Gas Exploration South Africa (Pty) Ltd** (Rhino) to the **South African Agency for Promotion of Petroleum Exploration and Exploitation SOC** (PASA) for a petroleum exploration right under the **Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002** (MPRDA) concerning nearly 5,500 farms in KwaZulu-Natal, some of which belonged to the first respondent, **Normandien Farms (Pty) Ltd** (Normandien) ([para 1]). 

Normandien challenged the acceptance of Rhino's application and other procedural steps taken by PASA in the Western Cape High Court, seeking to set aside PASA’s acceptance of the application and related actions, arguing that these had not complied with the statutory requirements ([para 2]). The High Court, presided over by **Dlodlo J**, ruled in favor of Normandien, leading Rhino to appeal against the orders granted ([para 3]).

Rhino had initiated its process by notifying landowners of its intentions to apply for exploration rights through its environmental consultant ([paras 20-21]). An application was lodged on 12 April 2016, accepted by PASA shortly thereafter, and notices required by the MPRDA for public participation were published, albeit not universally,—with some procedural deficiencies noted ([paras 22-24]). Normandien alleged that procedural misdirections occurred, contending that due process was not followed, violating principles established in the MPRDA ([para 26]).

## I - Issues ([paras 26-35])
The central legal issue raised is whether the actions of PASA, regarding the acceptance of Rhino’s application and related public notices, constituted administrative action subject to judicial review under the **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000** (PAJA) ([para 28]). Specifically, the court needed to assess the implications of the alleged defects in the procedural steps taken by PASA, especially regarding whether these actions had indeed resulted in actual prejudice to Normandien’s rights and interests.

Another pivotal issue is the ripeness of Normandien's application for review, given that it arose before any final decision had been made regarding the exploration rights ([para 34]). The merits of Normandien’s challenges were thus considered alongside procedural concerns about timing and the resultant lack of prejudice ([para 35]).

## R - Rules ([paras 5-18])
The rules applicable to this case are grounded in the MPRDA, particularly sections relating to the application process for exploration rights ([paras 5-15]). Section 79 stipulates the requirements for lodging applications with PASA, while section 10 mandates that a public notice be issued once an application is accepted to invite comments from interested parties ([paras 11-12]). 

The principle of judicial review is framed by the requirements of administrative action as defined under PAJA, which necessitates that decisions must impact rights and interests adversely for such actions to be subject to review ([para 28]). The precedent set in **Jockey Club of South Africa v Feldman** serves as a critical reference point, asserting the non-intervention by courts where no demonstrable prejudice has been shown to arise from alleged procedural shortcomings ([para 30]).

Additionally, the notion of ripeness in judicial review is significant, as articulated by legal scholars, which asserts that a matter should be ripe for adjudication only when the complainant has incurred prejudice as a result of the alleged irregularities ([para 34]).

## A - Application ([paras 29-35])
The court undertook an analysis to determine whether Normandien had demonstrated sufficient prejudice resulting from the PASA’s procedural actions. It affirmed that Normandien’s admissions indicated no actual adverse effects as the company conceded that PASA had no intentions of immediately encroaching upon its land ([para 31]). The court noted that no binding decision had yet been made by PASA, meaning that Normandien had initiated its challenge prematurely. 

It was determined that the actions of PASA regarding the acceptance of the application, and the issuance of notifications did not culminate in administrative decisions meriting review under PAJA; rather, these actions were characterized as clerical failings within the procedural framework ([para 28]). Consequently, the matter's procedural posture was pronounced not ripe for adjudication as any future decisions from PASA could alter the landscape of the dispute ([paras 33-34]).

## C - Conclusion ([paras 36-36])
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Rhino's appeal, ruling that Normandien's application to challenge the actions taken by PASA was premature and that it had shown no actual prejudice arising from the disputed procedural acts ([para 36]). The court's decision highlighted the importance of ripeness as a criterion for adjudication in matters seeking judicial review. 

This ruling not only overturned the previous orders granted by the High Court but also underscored the significance of due process and the necessity for a complainant to exhibit concrete adverse impacts before seeking judicial remedies in cases concerning administrative actions ([para 36]). The implications of this ruling reinforce the principle that challenges against administrative decisions must stem from concrete and demonstrable prejudice, establishing a clear framework for future claims within similar contexts under the MPRDA and related statutes.
