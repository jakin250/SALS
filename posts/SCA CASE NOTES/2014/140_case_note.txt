Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: *Bengwenyama-ya-Maswazi Community v Genorah Resources (Pty) Ltd* [2014] ZASCA 140

## F – Facts
The case revolves around the contested claim for preferent prospecting rights over the farm Eerstegeluk in Sekhukhuneland, Limpopo Province. The appellants are the Bengwenyama-Ya-Maswazi Community (BYMC), the Bengwenyama-Ya-Maswazi Tribal Council (the Tribal Council), and Miracle Upon Miracle Investments (Pty) Ltd (MUM) ([1–2]). The respondents are Genorah Resources (Pty) Ltd, the Roka Phasha Phokwane Traditional Council, and the Roka Phasha Community ([2]). The Minister for Mineral Resources did not participate in the litigation at either level.

The background indicates that previous litigation culminated in a decision by the Constitutional Court which emphasized that the BYMC had been historically dispossessed and had a right to participate in the application for prospecting rights ([6]). Subsequently, around November 2010, the Tribal Council submitted a prospecting rights application utilizing MUM as the corporate vehicle ([8]). The application was met with competing applications from the Roka Phasha Community and Genorah which culminated in the Minister's denial of the BYMC's application due to a lack of registered title ([13], [17]).

Before the High Court, the Tribal Council and MUM sought to review and set aside the Minister's decision ([16]). The High Court concluded that the BYMC had locus standi and that MUM’s application was compatible with the stipulations of section 104 of the MPRDA ([26]). However, it refrained from granting the prospecting rights directly to MUM, ultimately leading to the appeal ([32]).

## I – Issues
The following legal issues were identified for determination by the court:

1. Whether MUM, acting as a corporate vehicle, could apply for and be granted a preferent community prospecting right as recognized under section 104 of the MPRDA ([3]).
2. Whether the Tribal Council has the necessary legal existence to represent the BYMC and apply for the prospecting rights ([3]).
3. The implications of the absence of registered title over the land on the ability of the BYMC to obtain such rights ([5]).
4. The propriety of the Minister’s decision to grant rights to the Roka Phasha Community to the exclusion of the BYMC and MUM ([4]).

## R – Rules
The judgment primarily relied on several statutes and legal principles, notably:

- **Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA)**: Specifically sections 104, which governs the entitlement of communities to prospecting rights, and sections 17 and 23, which outline the conditions for granting such rights ([44], [46]).
- **Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003 (TLGFA)**: This statute provides a foundation for recognizing traditional communities and councils ([35, 12]).
- **PAJA (Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000)**: Used in the judicial review process to assess the legitimacy of administrative actions ([28]).

The court also referenced prior judicial precedents including *Bengwenyama Minerals (Pty) Ltd v Genorah Resources (Pty) Ltd* [2011] (4) SA 113 (CC) for its assessment of the requirements of community consultation and rights to natural resources ([6]).

## A – Application
In considering the legal status of the appellants, the court found that the BYMC, while described as an amorphous entity, had historically represented the community, hence giving it some locus standi ([34]). It acknowledged that the Tribal Council had operated in a capacity consistent with the legal framework established under the TLGFA ([35]). 

The court also examined MUM's role as a corporate vehicle through which the BYMC could apply for prospecting rights. It expressed that the statutory framework’s intent is to empower historically disadvantaged communities and recognized MUM's use as a vehicle, as previously sanctioned by the Constitutional Court ([48–49], [57]). 

Importantly, the court dispensed with the argument that the lack of registered title negated the BYMC's claims, noting the overwhelming historical connection to the land and a likely successful land claim in progress ([60]). There were substantial evidential supports including historical affidavits that sustained the claim of the BYMC to the land ([19]).

The court found the arguments presented by Genorah on corporate stakeholder dilution insufficient due to proposed amendments to MUM’s shareholding agreements that would enshrine community control over essential decisions ([54–59]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal by the BYMC and set aside the Minister's decision to grant prospecting rights to the Roka Phasha Community and Genorah Resources. It determined that the Tribal Council possesses legal standing and that MUM was competent to apply for community prospecting rights under the MPRDA ([68]). The court ordered that MUM be awarded the prospecting rights on the condition that requisite amendments to the shareholder agreement be executed, signifying the court’s effort to balance the legislative objectives of community benefit with practical business operations ([68]).

This ruling not only reinforces the rights of traditional communities over mining resources but also emphasizes compliance with constitutional prescripts aimed at redressing historical injustices ([62]). Moreover, it highlights the judiciary's proactive stance in promoting equitable access to natural resources in South Africa while setting a precedent for future cases involving community claims within the MPRDA structure.
