Shoprite Checkers (Pty) Limited v Member of the Executive Council for Economic Development, Environmental Affairs and Tourism: Eastern Cape and Others [2015] ZACC 23

Shoprite contended that the regulatory change amounted to an arbitrary deprivation of property under section 25 of the South African Constitution ([3]).

Constitutional Court 2015 [2015] ZACC 23

F – Facts

The case arises from an application by Shoprite Checkers (Pty) Limited (“Shoprite”) for confirmation of a High Court order which declared certain provisions of the Eastern Cape Liquor Act 10 of 2003 (“Eastern Cape Act”) unconstitutional ([1]-[2]). Under the repealed Liquor Act 27 of 1989, Shoprite was granted licences to sell wine alongside groceries. The Eastern Cape Act created a new framework which allowed existing grocer’s wine licences to operate for a transitional period of ten years, at which point it would lapse unless converted into different registrations. Shoprite’s failure to convert its licences resulted in their automatic lapse on 14 May 2014 ([2]-[8]).

Shoprite contended that the regulatory change amounted to an arbitrary deprivation of property under section 25 of the South African Constitution ([3]). The original High Court found in favour of Shoprite, holding that the termination of its licence constituted property under the Constitution, was indeed a deprivation of property, and that such deprivation was arbitrary ([12]-[14]).

I – Issues

The main legal issues presented before the Constitutional Court were:

  1. Whether the entitlement to commercial trade via a licence constitutes property under section 25 of the Constitution ([6]).
  2. If it does, whether the provisions of the Eastern Cape Act deprived Shoprite of its property rights ([6]).
  3. If deprivation occurred, whether that deprivation could be classified as arbitrary ([6]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal provisions cited include:

  • The Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996, particularly section 25(1), which states that no one may be deprived of property except in terms of law of general application, and provides that no law may permit arbitrary deprivation of property ([1]).
  • Previous case law from the Constitutional Court guiding interpretations of property rights, including First National Bank of SA Ltd t/a Wesbank v Commissioner, South African Revenue Service and Another ([2002] ZACC 5), which established the framework to assess deprivation and arbitrariness ([6], [14]).

A – Application

Property Qualification

The Constitutional Court noted that the definition of property under section 25 should not be unduly narrow, as property rights can evolve within the context of constitutional principles ([6]-[7]). It ultimately supported the High Court's finding that Shoprite's licences were indeed a form of property, despite administrative characteristics inherent to regulatory regimes ([66]-[70]).

Deprivation

The Court analyzed the nature and extent of the deprivation. Shoprite lost the ability to sell wine simultaneously with groceries, a significant aspect that constituted the essence of its business model. It contested that the mandatory transition to selling liquor on separate premises did not counterbalance this loss effectively ([72]-[76]).

Arbitrariness

On the element of arbitrariness, the Court found that while the legislative change was empirical rather than capricious, the reasons provided for the deprivation lacked sufficient depth or evidence to justify the termination comprehensively. The Court established that the legislative measures aimed at simplification must not only exist in rhetoric but also be substantiated by empirical data linking the measures to the ends they pursued ([84]-[106]).

C – Conclusion

The majority judgment held that the deprivation of Shoprite's right to sell wine alongside groceries constituted a significant impact on its property rights under section 25. Consequently, the Court could not sustain the argument built upon the state’s rationale for administrative simplification and control ([88], [169]). Therefore, the Court declined to confirm the High Court’s order of constitutional invalidity. The concluding order was that the application for leave to appeal was dismissed, with no order as to costs ([90]).

The implications of this ruling holler for a vibrant debate about the nature of property rights in South Africa, particularly as they intersect with legislative authority to regulate business amid pressing socio-economic realities.
