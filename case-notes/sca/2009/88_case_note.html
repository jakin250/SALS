Supreme Court of Appeal

Petersen v Minister of Safety & Security (514/2008) [2009] ZASCA 88

Petersen initiated legal proceedings in the Hermanus Magistrates' Court seeking damages for her son's injuries.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009 [2009] ZASCA 88

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The case under review is the appeal of Dolora Petersen in her capacity as the mother and natural guardian of J L P (the minor son) against the Minister of Safety and Security. The incident involved the minor son, J, sustaining gunshot wounds to his leg on 19 July 2002, at Gansbaai, near Hermanus in the Western Cape, allegedly caused by a policeman acting in the course and scope of his employment with the respondent ([1]).

Petersen initiated legal proceedings in the Hermanus Magistrates' Court seeking damages for her son's injuries. The respondent raised the defense of justification, citing self-defense and necessity as justifications for the police's actions. An agreement was reached to try the issue of liability first, reserving the question of quantum for later resolution. The trial court accepted the defense of necessity and subsequently dismissed the claim with costs. Petersen's appeal to the Cape High Court was also unsuccessful, leading to the current appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([1]–[2]).

I – Issues

The primary issue on appeal concerns whether the actions of the police, which resulted in the injuries to J, were justified under the doctrine of necessity. A secondary issue is whether the appellant can establish that the police's use of force was excessive or inappropriate given the situation they faced ([2]–[8]).

R – Rules

The legal principles relevant to this case include:

  1. Delictual Liability: The actions of the police must not be wrongful if they are justified by necessity.
  2. Necessity Doctrine: According to legal scholars, an act of necessity can be defined as lawful conduct aimed at protecting legal interests, which may involve harm to an innocent person under imminent danger ([11]).
  3. Justification in Self-Defense vs. Necessity: Unlike self-defense, which requires an attack, necessity can justify actions directed at averting an imminent threat without the need to establish that the innocent party was directly involved in an attack ([11]).

The courts referenced in establishing the necessary legal standards include Crown Chickens (Pty) Ltd t/a Rocklands Poultry v Rieck ([11]) and legal treatises on delict ([11]).

A – Application

In analyzing the facts, the court evaluated witness credibility, particularly the testimony from both police and defense witnesses. Although there were contradictions in the testimonies of police officers regarding specifics of the incident, the court concluded that the material facts corroborated each other and were supported by the defense witnesses ([6]–[9]).

The court emphasized that the operational situation faced by the police was chaotic and threatening. The crowd, incited by Crause, began to stone police officers, leading to a scenario where the police had to respond with rubber bullets and subsequently real ammunition to protect themselves ([3], [4]).

Petersen argued that the necessity defense was exaggerated by the police. However, both the trial court and the Cape High Court found credible evidence that the police were indeed in imminent danger, confirming the police's need to use lethal force as a last resort. The courts reasoned that since other means of de-escalation had been employed without success, the police's actions were justified under the circumstances ([12]–[13]).

The court's critical assessment of J's testimony also played a significant role; the court found him to be an unreliable witness who attempted to deny observable facts regarding the violent confrontation. Consequently, it was determined that J's injuries were sustained as a result of police actions directed towards the rioting crowd ([10]–[13]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal subsequently upheld the lower courts' decisions, concluding that the police's actions were justified under the doctrine of necessity, thus dismissing Petersen's appeal with costs. The court reaffirmed that the police had met their burden of proving that their conduct was not wrongful, given the dangerous situation they confronted ([14]).

The ruling underscores the balance that must be struck between public safety and individual rights, particularly when law enforcement agencies are required to act under perilous and immediate threat scenarios. The outcome may have significant implications for future cases involving police use of force, particularly concerning the necessity and proportionality doctrine within South African law.
