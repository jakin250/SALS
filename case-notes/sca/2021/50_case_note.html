Supreme Court of Appeal

Merifon (Pty) Ltd v Greater Letaba Municipality and Another [2021] ZASCA 50 (22 April 2021)

The present appeal stems from a dispute over the validity of an agreement for the sale of immovable property and the municipal financing obligations therein.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 50 (22 April 2021)

F – Facts

The present appeal stems from a dispute over the validity of an agreement for the sale of immovable property and the municipal financing obligations therein. The Greater Letaba Municipality, faced with the urgent need for land to facilitate human settlement, resolved to acquire certain properties for housing development but ran into legal complications due to alleged non-compliance with the Local Government: Municipal Finance Management Act 56 of 2003 (MFMA).

On April 4, 2011, the Executive Mayor of the Greater Letaba Municipality communicated with the Provincial Member of the Executive Council (MEC) proposing that the latter purchase three farms on behalf of the municipality due to its financial inadequacies. This plan eventually led to the Housing Development Agency (HDA) getting involved for assistance. By March 6, 2013, the MEC's letter confirmed a budget of R52 million for the acquisition of the property necessary for human settlement.

The municipal council convened a special meeting on March 22, 2013, where it approved the resolution regarding the acquisition of the properties. Subsequent to this, Merifon entered into a written agreement of sale with the municipality on March 7, 2013. However, before this transaction was fully realized, the Provincial Treasury denied the disbursement request due to the perceived excessive purchase price.

Merifon subsequently demanded payment from the municipality, which went unheeded, leading to the initiation of legal proceedings in 2014. Merifon sought specific performance for the purchase price of R52 million plus transfer costs amounting to R209,892.90. The municipality countered this claim by asserting that the sale agreement was null and void, citing lack of compliance with MFMA provisions, specifically Section 19, which governs municipal spending on capital projects.

The High Court dismissed Merifon's claim, leading to an appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 1-30]).

I – Issues

The core legal issue revolves around whether the municipality could be compelled to fulfil its obligations under the sale agreement when such an agreement arguably contravened the provisions of Section 19 of the MFMA, which seeks to safeguard transparency, accountability, and fiscal discipline in municipal undertakings ([paras 4, 20-21]). Additionally, considerations were given to:

  1. The authority of the municipal representative to execute the sale agreement.
  2. The implications of the doctrine of estoppel in the context of public law and illegality.

R – Rules

The rules applicable to this case arise from several legal principles and statutory provisions:

  1. Doctrine of Legality: The exercise of public power must have a lawful basis, meaning that no public official or department may act beyond the powers conferred on them by law ([paras 1-3]).
  2. Section 19 of the MFMA: This section mandates that a municipality may only spend on capital projects if specific preconditions are met, including:
  • Appropriation of funds in the capital budget ([para 4]).
  • Approval by the municipal council of the project ([para 4]).
  • Consideration of funding sources and related costs ([para 4, 18]).
  1. Legal Authority: The principle that a municipality can only act within the bounds of legally granted authority ([para 3]).
  2. Estoppel: Generally, estoppel cannot circumvent illegal transactions, as upholding such claims would undermine statutory compliance and the rule of law ([paras 26-27]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal, through its judgment, outlined that it was clear from the language and intent of Section 19 that compliance with its provisions was essential for any capital project undertaken by a municipality ([paras 20-25]). The court confirmed that the agreement was indeed a capital project worth R52 million that required adherence to these peremptory conditions.

The court analyzed whether the municipality had complied with Section 19 when it entered the agreement. It determined that the resolution adopted by the municipality did not constitute an actual approval of the purchase, but rather a mere acknowledgment of a funding commitment, which failed to satisfy the requirements of the MFMA. Consequently, it found that both the legal authority of the municipal representative and the validity of the agreement were fundamentally flawed due to non-compliance with statutory mandates.

In relation to Merifon's reliance on the doctrine of estoppel, the court firmly stated that such a claim could not rectify an unlawfully concluded agreement. Allowing estoppel to validate the contract would defeat the purpose of the MFMA and compromise good governance principles ([paras 27-29]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the High Court's ruling, dismissing the appeal with costs. The judgment reinforces the principle that municipalities must adhere to statute when exercising public authority and finances. The court stated that it is impermissible for a municipality to engage in agreements that do not conform to legislative mandates, emphasizing the core tenet of legality that underpins South African constitutional law ([paras 30]).

This decision has significant implications for municipal governance, reiterating that adherence to legal protocols is non-negotiable and serves the public interest in promoting accountability and transparency in the management of public funds.

The appeal was dismissed with costs, ensuring that Merifon bore the legal expenses incurred, which also included the costs of two counsels ([Order]).
