FIRAC Case Note: Gumede v President of the Republic of South Africa [2008] ZACC 23

Constitutional Court 2008 [2008] ZACC 23 #### F – Facts [1] The case revolves around

FIRAC Case Note: Gumede v President of the Republic of South Africa [2008] ZACC 23

F – Facts

[1] The case revolves around Mrs. Elizabeth Gumede, a woman married under customary law in KwaZulu-Natal, who filed an application for the confirmation of a High Court order declaring certain provisions of the Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998 and the KwaZulu and Natal Codes of Zulu Law unconstitutional. Mrs. Gumede contended that these provisions led to unfair discrimination based on gender and race, particularly concerning the proprietary rights of women in customary marriages ([2]–[4]).

[2] Mrs. Gumede married Amos Gumede in May 1968 under customary law. The couple had four children, and throughout the marriage, Mrs. Gumede was primarily responsible for the household and child-rearing while her husband worked. Upon seeking a divorce in 2003, Mr. Gumede offered to sell their shared home, leaving Mrs. Gumede with considerably less than her husband ([6]–[9]).

[3] The High Court found that the relevant laws unjustly discriminated against women like Mrs. Gumede—the Recognition Act provided that marriages contracted before its commencement (15 November 2000) would be governed by customary law, which typically favored male property control. Consequently, the High Court declared sections 7(1), 7(2), and certain provisions of the KwaZulu Act and the Natal Code unconstitutional due to gender bias ([3]–[4]).

I – Issues

[4] The primary issue for determination was whether the provisions of the Recognition of Customary Marriages Act and the relevant codes denied Mrs. Gumede and other similarly situated women the equality guaranteed under the Constitution ([15]). This encompassed the questions of unfair discrimination based on both gender and race ([15]). If discrimination was found, the next issue was whether any justifications existed that could sustain the constitutionality of the these provisions ([15]).

R – Rules

[5] The court's analysis grounded in various constitutional provisions, notably:

  • Section 9 (Equality): Prohibits unfair discrimination on various grounds including gender and race ([3]);
  • Section 167(5): Provides that the Constitutional Court has the authority to confirm orders of constitutional invalidity made by lower courts ([2]);
  • Section 211(3): Mandates that customary law must be applied by the courts when applicable, provided it aligns with constitutional principles ([12]);
  • Section 36: Discusses the limitations of rights, stating these rights may only be limited by law in a way that is reasonable and justifiable ([37]).

The court also referenced prior case law that highlighted gender discrimination, particularly concerning property rights and the evolution of customary law ([32]).

A – Application

[6] The Constitutional Court examined the impugned provisions in light of the principles outlined in the Constitution. The gender bias in the codified customary law, which designated men as the family heads with exclusive control over property, was assessed to be discriminatory against women, undermining their dignity and economic independence ([34]–[36]).

[7] The government argued that the differentiation between old and new marriages was justified as customary law had a historical basis and that the courts could address property disputes upon divorce. However, the court found this reasoning insufficient as it failed to justify the ongoing discrimination during the marriage, which was contrary to the constitutional guarantee of equality. The court also noted that the provisions not only impacted Mrs. Gumede's current rights but also perpetuated a legacy of gender inequality in customary marriages ([46]).

[8] The court reinforced the view that customary marriages should not be strictly analyzed through the lens of common law. Instead, the realities of customary law should be adapted to align with the Constitution, therefore making outdated and discriminatory practices untenable ([42]–[46]).

C – Conclusion

[9] The Constitutional Court affirmed the High Court’s ruling, confirming the unconstitutional nature of sections 7(1) and 7(2) of the Recognition Act, as well as the relevant provisions of the KwaZulu Act and the Natal Code ([49]). The court highlighted the urgent need for legislative reform to eliminate gender bias in customary law and ordered that all customary marriages, even those entered into before the Recognition Act, should be treated as marriages in community of property.

[10] The implications of this ruling were profound, setting a precedent for the protection of women's rights under customary law in South Africa. It not only sought to rectify historical injustices but also aimed to ensure that customary law could evolve to reflect contemporary understandings of equality and gender ([57]–[59]).

In summary, the court's decision recognized the necessity of constitutional compliance and reform in customary law to uphold the values of dignity, equality, and non-discrimination, as enshrined within South Africa's transformative constitutional framework ([58]).
