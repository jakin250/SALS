Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# AllPay Consolidated Investment Holdings (Pty) Ltd and Others v Chief Executive Officer of the South African Social Security Agency and Others [2013] ZACC 42 Case Note

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts

This case concerns the constitutional validity of a tender awarded by the South African Social Security Agency (SASSA) to Cash Paymaster Services (Pty) Ltd for the payment of social grants across South Africa. Approximately 15 million individuals depend on these grants for essential financial support ([1]).

AllPay Consolidated Investment Holdings (Pty) Ltd, rejected in its tender application, sought to have the award set aside on the basis of various alleged irregularities concerning the tender process. In the North Gauteng High Court, the tender process was declared invalid, yet the court refrained from setting aside the award to avoid practical disruption for grant beneficiaries ([2]). AllPay subsequently appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which found no unlawful irregularities but which also faced criticism for its handling of alleged procedural issues ([3]).

AllPay's appeal to the Constitutional Court centers on the interpretation of the procurement process as set out in section 217 of the Constitution, which underscores the importance of fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective public procurement ([4]).

### I – Issues

1. **Alleged Irregularities**: AllPay alleges several irregularities in the tender process, which it contends should invalidate the award ([5]).

2. **Legal Approach**: The Supreme Court of Appeal's interpretation of “inconsequential irregularities” and the impact on judicial review standards under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA) ([6]).

3. **Impact of Irregularities on Outcome**: The question of whether proven irregularities materially affected the outcome of the tender process and the subsequent awarding of the tender to Cash Paymaster ([7]).

4. **Adherence to Section 217**: Whether the processes set out by SASSA and the conditions outlined in the tender complied with South Africa's constitutional procurement framework ([22-33]).

### R – Rules

The court relied upon several constitutional and statutory provisions, including:

1. **Section 217 of the Constitution**: Establishes the framework for procurement in the public sector, mandating that such processes be fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective ([31]).

2. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act** (PAJA): Sets standards for administrative action, including the grounds for review in terms of fairness and procedural compliance ([42]).

3. **Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act** and **Public Finance Management Act**: These Acts elaborate on the principles of fair procurement and the obligations of state institutions regarding procurement ([34-38]).

4. **Case Law Precedents**: Important decisions like *Steenkamp NO v Provincial Tender Board, Eastern Cape* and *Bengwenyama Minerals (Pty) Ltd v Genorah Resources (Pty) Ltd* were cited to highlight existing legal principles concerning procurement disputes and the review process ([20-21], [26]).

### A – Application

The court scrutinized the procurement process employed by SASSA, identifying several significant irregularities. These included:

- **Bid Submission Conditions**: SASSA's requirement of separate bids for each province was explicitly stated, yet Cash Paymaster had submitted a single tender for all nine provinces ([61]).
  
- **Composition of Evaluation Committees**: The Bid Evaluation Committee did not meet the minimum membership requirements ([63]).

- **Failure to Adequately Assess Empowerment Credentials**: AllPay contended that SASSA failed to investigate the competencies of Cash Paymaster's empowerment partners, which is critical under the constitutional procurement framework ([66-70]).

The court found the Supreme Court of Appeal's approach flawed, particularly its stance on "inconsequential irregularities", stating that it conflated procedural fairness with the ultimate outcome of the procurement process. The court held that such a perspective undermines the purpose of ensuring fair treatment for all bidders and could allow for corruption ([22-24]).

Additionally, the court emphasized that objectively determining compliance with tender requirements is essential for maintaining the integrity of public procurement ([88-92]). The lack of clarity regarding the requirements of biometric verification in the tender process led to further confusion, ultimately contributing to the exclusion of AllPay from the bidding process ([73-83]).

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ruled that the tender awarded to Cash Paymaster was constitutionally invalid and that the procedural irregularities identified by AllPay were indeed material enough to warrant review under PAJA. The decision was based on extensive analysis of the tender process and highlighted the significant implications for the public interest concerning social grant beneficiaries ([93]).

While declaring the award invalid, the court suspended the declaration to allow for the continuation of the social grants system until a new, compliant tender process could be established. The case underscores the importance of adhering to constitutional and statutory requirements in public procurement and the role of judicial review in safeguarding these principles against potential lapses and irregularities in public administration ([98]).

This case note articulates the vital intersection of constitutional values, public procurement standards, and the principles of administrative justice, thereby contributing to a deeper understanding of the regulatory framework governing public tenders in South Africa.
