Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Psychological Society of South Africa v Qwelane and Others [2016] ZACC 48

### F – Facts 
The case concerns an appeal by the Psychological Society of South Africa (PsySSA) regarding the High Court's order granting a postponement in proceedings addressing allegations of hate speech against the LGBTI community by the first respondent, Mr. Dubula Jonathan Qwelane. The controversy was initiated by an article published by Qwelane in 2008, which derogatorily likened gay and lesbian individuals to animals, ultimately sparking a complaint filed by the South African Human Rights Commission (SAHRC). 

This complaint, based on sections 10(1) and 11 of the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act, alleged that Qwelane's article constituted hate speech contrary to sections 9(4) and 16 of the South African Constitution. Following a default judgment against him, Qwelane managed to rescind this judgment in 2011. Subsequently, he filed a constitutional challenge against the validity of sections of the Equality Act, causing a delay in the main proceedings, which were ultimately consolidated.

On 28 August 2016, mere days before the hearing, Qwelane’s legal representatives requested a postponement due to his medical conditions. PsySSA and SAHRC opposed this request but the High Court granted the postponement sine die after only oral arguments were presented. PsySSA subsequently sought leave to appeal this decision, claiming its rights and those of the LGBTI community were jeopardized by the High Court’s actions ([paras 1–16]).

### I – Issues 
The central issues in this case are twofold: 
1. **Legal Standing and Appealability**: Whether PsySSA, a non-party to the original proceedings and an amicus curiae, holds standing to appeal the High Court's decision to grant a postponement sine die, and whether such an interim order is appealable.
2. **Procedural Fairness**: Whether the High Court properly exercised its discretion in granting the postponement without allowing opposing parties to file affidavits, thus potentially infringing upon the audi alteram partem principle and the rights of the parties involved ([paras 17–21], [26–29]).

### R – Rules 
The relevant legal principles are derived from both statutory provisions and case law. The South African Constitution, specifically section 16, guarantees freedom of expression while also prohibiting advocacy of hatred that incites harm. The Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act underpins the case's foundation concerning hate speech.

In terms of procedural law, the appellate principles established in **Campus Law Clinic** articulate that amicus curiae may have standing to appeal in the public interest. The principles of **audi alteram partem** dictate that parties must be given a fair opportunity to present their case, ensuring that any judicial decision is made after considering all pertinent information ([paras 17–29], [30–36]).

### A – Application 
In its deliberations, the Constitutional Court acknowledged PsySSA's claims regarding its standing and the timeliness of its appeal. The Court underscored that PsySSA was not merely acting in its own interest but sought to protect the rights of the vulnerable LGBTI community affected by Qwelane's statements ([paras 26–29]).

The Court scrutinized the High Court’s decision to grant a postponement without permitting PsySSA and SAHRC to present opposing evidence, framing this as a significant procedural oversight. The High Court's adoption of the "no difference" approach — insisting that additional affidavits would not alter the outcome — was critiqued. The Constitutional Court asserted that this perspective contradicted the tenets of natural justice and procedural fairness, emphasizing the need for all parties involved to be heard ([paras 30–39]).

Despite acknowledging the procedural failings, the Constitutional Court concluded that it would not intervene, as the delay was not indefinite — the matter was set to be heard in March 2017. Thus, intervention at this stage was deemed unnecessary, reflecting a balance between public interest and procedural propriety ([paras 43–47]).

### C – Conclusion 
In conclusion, the Constitutional Court dismissed PsySSA’s application for leave to appeal against the High Court’s decision. While the High Court’s failure to uphold the principles of fairness was noted, the practical implications of postponing a hearing in an ongoing legal proceeding led to the determination that intervention was not suitable at that juncture. The court’s ruling reinforced the principles of procedural justice while stressing the importance of timely resolutions in judicial processes ([paras 46–47]).

This case underscores the ongoing tension between the right to engage in free expression and the legal frameworks that protect against hate speech, particularly within South Africa's constitutional context. PsySSA's legal standing and the procedural safeguards essential for ensuring justice in matters of significant public interest were affirmed, albeit within the confines of the specific facts and procedural posture at the time ([paras 26–37], [40–43]).
