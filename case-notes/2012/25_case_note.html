Case Note: Children's Institute v Presiding Officer of the Children's Court, District of Krugersdorp and Others

Constitutional Court 2012 CCT 69/12

Case Note: Children's Institute v Presiding Officer of the Children's Court, District of Krugersdorp and Others

CCT 69/12; ZACC 25

FIRAC Method

F – Facts
The appeal arises from the South Gauteng High Court's (High Court) refusal to allow the Children’s Institute (the applicant) to adduce evidence as an amicus curiae in a case involving SS, a minor orphan living with relatives. SS, together with his great-aunt and great-uncle, sought a declaration that he was a “child in need of care and protection” under section 150(1)(a) of the Children’s Act 38 of 2005 to qualify for a foster child grant, which is greater than the child support grant available to many other impoverished children ([3]–[4]). The Children’s Court denied the application, arguing there was no need for regulation since SS resided with family ([4]).

In subsequent proceedings at the High Court, the Children’s Institute expressed concerns that a ruling against them might adversely affect approximately 350,000 orphaned children living with family ([5]). The Institute sought permission to present statistical evidence that would elucidate why these children should be eligible for foster child grants. However, the High Court maintained that, under Rule 16A of the Uniform Rules of Court, an amicus could not adduce evidence ([9]).

The High Court's finding rested on its interpretation of Rule 16A, stating that although an amicus could participate in proceedings, leading evidence was not permitted and would constitute the creation of a new right, which might infringe upon the court's established procedures ([10], [12]). This led to the Children’s Institute appealing to the Constitutional Court, which granted leave to appeal ([12]).

I – Issues
The central issue before the Constitutional Court was whether Rule 16A permits an amicus curiae to adduce evidence in support of their submissions. A secondary issue was whether the High Court's inherent power, as provided for in section 173 of the Constitution, allows for the introduction of evidence tendered by an amicus ([1], [17]).

R – Rules
The determination centers with Rule 16A of the Uniform Rules of Court, concerning the admission of amici curiae. The rule specifies under what terms an interested party may intervene in proceedings concerning constitutional issues and establishes procedural requirements for the application and participation of an amicus curiae ([7]).

Section 173 of the Constitution states that the Constitutional Court, Supreme Court of Appeal, and High Courts possess the inherent power to regulate their processes and develop common law considering the interests of justice ([2], [12]).

Precedent from previous cases suggests that the interpretation of statutes regarding evidence can influence judicial discretion and procedural legitimacy ([17]).

A – Application
The Court undertook a textual and purposive interpretation of Rule 16A, contending it does not categorically prohibit the admission of evidence by an amicus. The language of "submissions" was interpreted broadly including potential evidence that could aid the court ([17]–[20]).

The judgment emphasized that although the High Court had concluded that evidence could not be admitted, it overlooked the significance of procedural discretion endowed to the courts. The Court argued that a wide interpretation was essential, particularly in the context of public interest law, where the outcomes of cases often have deeper societal ramifications ([25]–[34]).

The precedential reliance on Oosthuizen v Road Accident Fund was determined to be inapposite since the adduction of evidence from an amicus relates to procedural rights rather than substantive rights ([36]). By this reasoning, even if Rule 16A did not explicitly allow for evidence, the inherent power under section 173 could justify its admission when pertinent to the interests of justice ([18]–[34]).

C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Constitutional Court found that Rule 16A does not impede an amicus from adducin evidence and instead supports the interpretation that such evidence can be valuable in assisting the court’s understanding of complex issues, especially those affecting vulnerable populations ([39]).

The Court ordered that:

  1. Leave to appeal is granted.
  2. The appeal is upheld, and the High Court's previous determination is overturned.
  3. Rule 16A is confirmed to permit an amicus curiae to adduce evidence in appropriate circumstances aimed at the interests of justice ([40]).

The implications of this ruling extend beyond this case, as it provides clearer guidance on the role of amici curiae in South African constitutional litigation and affirms the courts’ capacity to include diverse sources of relevant evidence. This ruling is likely to embolden future amici curiae, enhancing their contributions to the justice system by facilitating a more informed adjudication process.
