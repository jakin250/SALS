# Case Note: MOMABOLO v RUSTENBURG REGIONAL LOCAL COUNCIL (Case No. 229/98)

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The appellant, J W Mamabolo, was appointed as an RDP Director by the Rustenburg Regional Local Council ("the Council") on 16 May 1995, with an initial probationary period of six months, ending on 15 November 1995 ([1]). Following the expiration of this probation, the Council extended Mamabolo's probation for an additional six months on 28 November 1995, purportedly on the grounds that he had not adequately performed his duties. Mamabolo requested reasons for this extension on 11 March 1996, which the Council provided on 19 March 1996, asserting that the reasons had been discussed with him earlier ([1]).

On 14 May 1996, the Council decided not to confirm Mamabolo's appointment and to terminate his employment, providing one month's notice from 16 May 1996. Subsequently, on 23 August 1996, Mamabolo initiated review proceedings seeking to annul the Council's resolutions regarding his probation and termination ([2], [3]).

At the trial in the Transvaal Provincial Division, Judge Kirk-Cohen dismissed Mamabolo's claims regarding his initial probationary appointment but accepted that the resolutions extending his probation and terminating his employment were overly delayed and invalid. The Council appealed to the Full Bench, which ruled in the Council's favor primarily due to the appellant's unreasonable delay in seeking a review ([4], [5]). The Supreme Court of Appeal subsequently granted the appellant leave to appeal ([5]).

### I – Issues
The key legal issues arise from the actions leading to Mamabolo's dismissal, including:

1. Whether there was undue delay in bringing the review application regarding the Council's decisions ([6]).
2. Whether the Council had the authority to extend Mamabolo's probationary period ([6]).
3. The validity of Mamabolo's employment termination and compliance with principles of procedural fairness ([6]).

Additionally, a procedural question was raised concerning the failure to file a complete record of the proceedings, which the Council argued warranted dismissal of the appeal ([6]).

### R – Rules
The court examined several legal principles in determining this case:

- **Probationary Employment Regulations**: The employment was governed by clause 6.2.7 of Regulation No R1828, which outlines the procedures for probation extensions and terminations ([2]).
  
- **Judicial Review on the Grounds of Delay**: An unreasonable delay in bringing review applications may preclude relief, a principle derived from earlier cases such as *Wolgroeiers Afslaers* and *Setsokosane Busdiens* ([11]).
  
- **Procedural Fairness**: The principles of natural justice require that an employee be given the opportunity to defend themselves before a decision that adversely affects their employment is made ([20], [21]). 

### A – Application
#### Preliminary Point on Record
The court first addressed the procedural issue regarding the completeness of the record, noting that while the rules stipulate that an appeal may lapse if a record is not properly submitted, the absence of additional documents did not hinder the court's ability to address the essential issues ([7]). Consequently, the appeal would not be dismissed solely on that ground.

#### Undue Delay
In examining the delay, the court emphasized that the extension of the probationary period was moot due to Mamabolo's termination rendering any later review of it insignificant ([8]). Thus, the court refrained from addressing claims about the delay in challenging the extension, as the termination had already taken effect ([9], [10]).

#### Termination of Employment
The court assessed the substantive and procedural fairness surrounding Mamabolo’s termination. It posited that assuming Mamabolo was a permanent employee on probation, he was afforded an opportunity to reply to the Council's decision ([19]). The Mayor informed him that the Council had not yet finalized its decision and invited him to present his case, which he did not adequately pursue. The court concluded that he was given a reasonable chance to challenge the termination before it occurred ([22], [24]).

Moreover, the court stated that even if the initial termination discussions happened before he was heard, the Council proceeded correctly by allowing him to respond subsequently ([21], [23]). Given the procedural context, the council’s actions were deemed compliant with the principles of natural justice.

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the Rustenburg Regional Local Council, dismissing Mamabolo's appeal and confirming the validity of his termination. The findings established that any claim about the Council's actions being ultra vires was unfounded, as the Council had acted within its regulations regarding his employment status ([25]). The ruling affirmed that a delay in filing the appeal was not inherently prejudicial to Mamabolo and that procedural fairness had been adequately maintained through the manner in which the Council handled his case. The implications of this ruling emphasize the significance of strict adherence to procedural guidelines in employment law and the importance of timely actions in judicial review applications. 

---

This case illustrates critical issues surrounding procedural fairness and the reasonable expectations of both employees and employers during employment termination processes. The court's decision serves as a precedent for similar cases involving probation and termination of public sector employees.
