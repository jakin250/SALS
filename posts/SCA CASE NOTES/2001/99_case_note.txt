### Case Note: FIRAC Method

**Case Title:**  
DIE LID VAN DIE UITVOERENDE RAAD VIR PLAASLIKE BESTUUR: MPUMALANGA, CNM PADAYACHEE NO v INDEPENDENT MUNICIPAL AND ALLIED TRADE UNION (“IMATU”) and Others [2001]

**Coram:**  
HEFER ACJ, HARMS, SCOTT & MTHIYANE JJA, CONRADIE AJA

**Heard:**  
13 September 2001

**Delivered:**  
25 September 2001

**Subject:**  
Constitutional duties of provincial governments towards local authorities.

---

### F – Facts
The case stemmed from significant financial difficulties faced by the Transitional Local Council of Leandra, a local authority in the Mpumalanga province ([para 1]). By June 30, 1999, the Council had outstanding debts exceeding R16 million and was struggling to meet current expenses. Due to these financial crises, the Council withheld deductions from employees’ salaries intended for taxation and other statutory contributions, leading to mounting grievances from the affected employees ([paras 2-4]).

The employees filed for relief in the Transvaal Provincial Division, firstly seeking an order for the Council to pay outstanding amounts. Following the court’s decision against the Council, employees sought further relief against the Mpumalanga Provincial Government, asking for a joint payment by the Province or a mandamus to compel the Province to fulfill its obligations under sections 139 and 154 of the Constitution ([para 5]).

The trial court ruled that while the Province could not be compelled to pay the debts of the Council, it was still required to support and strengthen the Council, hence granted an order against the Province ([paras 5-6]).

### I – Issues
The legal questions presented in this appeal revolved around whether the provincial government has a constitutional obligation to provide financial assistance to a local authority to fulfill its financial obligations to its employees. Specifically, the court needed to clarify the extent and implications of sections 139 and 154 of the Constitution, particularly concerning executive responsibilities and the nature of the duty owed by the provincial government toward municipalities ([paras 8-10]).

### R – Rules
1. **Constitutional Provisions:**  
   - **Section 139(1):** Outlines the power of the provincial government to intervene when a municipality fails to fulfill its executive obligations under legislation, without imposing a mandatory duty to allocate funds ([para 8]).
   - **Section 154(1):** Mandates that national and provincial governments support municipalities to manage their affairs and perform their functions but not in a manner that creates a direct debtor-creditor relationship ([para 10]).

2. **Legislation Context:**
   - **Local Government Transition Act:** Details the transitional phases of local government and financial obligations ([para 6]).
   - **Income Tax Act, Pension Funds Act, Medical Schemes Act, Unemployment Insurance Act:** Relevant to the statutory duties of local authorities towards deductions and contributions from employees ([para 3]).

### A – Application
The court evaluated whether the Province had a legal obligation to financially support the Council and its employees. It noted that while section 139(1) of the Constitution allowed for provincial intervention, it did not create a duty to provide funds specifically. Furthermore, the court emphasized that the nature of the obligations being sought (i.e., payment for unpaid salaries) did not meet the definition of "executive obligations" that could invoke section 139 intervention ([paras 9-10]).

The court also analyzed the implications of section 154 and whether it could be interpreted to require the provincial government to act in favor of municipal creditors. The court pointed out that obligations under section 154 are primarily horizontal, concerned with intergovernmental relations rather than vertical relationships between the state and citizens ([para 12]). Given the context, it found that the Province had limited financial resources and had proactively assisted the Council within these constraints ([para 11]).

### C – Conclusion
The appeal was upheld, and the court found that the original order compelling the Province to pay the Council's debts was unfounded. The Court clarified that while the provincial government has a role in supporting municipalities, it cannot be coerced into financial obligations beyond its statutory duties. Moreover, the court ruled that the employees, while creditors, could not compel the provincial government to finance their salaries directly ([paras 15-16]).

The judgment underscored the distinction between supporting municipalities as a constitutional responsibility and the specific financial obligations that arise from employment and contractual relationships. This case illustrates the complexities of intergovernmental relations and the limitations of judicial remedies when financial obligations are involved, marking an important moment in understanding the local government's operational jurisdiction within South Africa's constitutional framework ([paras 16]).

---

This case ultimately establishes important legal precedents for local authorities in financial distress, clarifying the constitutional limits of provincial support and the procedural protections available for employees and creditors.
