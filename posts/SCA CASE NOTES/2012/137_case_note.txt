# FIRAC Case Note: Law Society of the Northern Provinces v Siphiwe Freeman Dube (874/2011)

**F** – **Facts**:  
The Law Society of the Northern Provinces (the appellant) appealed against the decision of the North Gauteng High Court, which had suspended the respondent, Siphiwe Freeman Dube, for one year instead of striking him off the roll of attorneys, as per the provisions of¹ the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979 (the Act). Dube was found guilty of multiple counts of dishonesty and misconduct, including failing to obtain consent from his employer before registering his own practice, the theft of client files, conflicting representation in a third-party claim, and late submissions of auditor’s reports ([1]–[12]).

The appellant had filed an application as per section 22(1)(d) of the Act, seeking the removal of Dube's name from the roll of attorneys based on findings from a disciplinary inquiry that Dube was not a fit and proper person to practice law. The inquiry conducted by the appellant detailed several grave transgressions, including misappropriation of client funds and submission of false accounts, leading to the firm of Maluleke Msimang & Associates losing trust in Dube ([2]–[13]).

The High Court, however, decided on a one-year suspension, emphasizing Dube’s naivety, immaturity, and inexperience, which affected his decision-making and led to his misconduct ([14]).

**I** – **Issues**:  
Analysis of the case raised two key issues: 
1. **Whether the High Court misdirected itself in deciding that a one-year suspension was an appropriate sanction given Dube’s unprofessional conduct and dishonesty** ([5], [15]).
2. **Whether the High Court should have issued a punitive costs order against Dube** instead of a party and party scale ([5]).

**R** – **Rules**:  
The governing rules are established within the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979 and referenced precedents, including:

1. **Section 22(1)(d)** of the Attorneys Act, which permits striking an attorney from the roll if deemed unfit.
2. In *Law Society, Northern Provinces v Mogami*, a three-stage inquiry is outlined to determine if an attorney should be suspended or removed, focusing on: (i) whether the conduct is proven; (ii) whether the attorney is unfit to practice; (iii) the appropriateness of the sanction to be imposed ([3]).

The case of *Summerley v Law Society, Northern Provinces* further elucidates the discretion exercised by the court at the third inquiry stage ([4]). Moreover, the discretion is constrained, allowing appeal courts to intervene if the lower court did not act judicially or misdirected itself ([15]).

**A** – **Application**:  
The Supreme Court of Appeal examined the appropriateness of the sanctions applied by the High Court. In weighing Dube's misconduct, multiple acts of dishonesty were catalogued, yet they were assessed cumulatively rather than in isolation, allowing the court to consider mitigating factors such as Dube's youth and inexperience ([19], [20]). 

The claim of theft of client files and misappropriation of funds was weighed against Dube's expressions of remorse and absence of prior instances of professional misconduct. The court likened the circumstances surrounding previous cases involving dishonesty, notably comparing Dube’s situation with those in *Kekana v Society of Advocates of South Africa*, where the acts of dishonesty were far more severe, leading to striking off decisions ([27], [28]).

Regarding costs, the court held that the appellant, as a regulatory body, was entitled to a punitive costs order due to the public interest it serves in maintaining the integrity of the legal profession ([33]).

**C** – **Conclusion**:  
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the decision that the High Court did not misdirect itself regarding the sanction. However, it altered the costs order, ruling that Dube should bear the costs on an attorney and client scale due to the nature of the actions and the duty the Law Society undertook in pursuing the matter ([34]). 

Thus, while affirming the validity of the Junior Judge's discretion concerning suspension, the appeal court emphasized that dishonesty – though gravely serious – did not uniformly require a striking off, particularly considering the nuances of Dube's case ([32]). The appeal was dismissed, save for the allowable alteration in costs, reinforcing the significance of rehabilitative prospects in professional misconduct assessments.
