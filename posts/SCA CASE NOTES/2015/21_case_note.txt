Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

### FIRAC Case Note: Euro Blitz 21 (Pty) Ltd v Secena Aircraft Investments CC (102/14) [2015] ZASCA 21

#### F – Facts
The case at hand arises from a contractual dispute over a lease agreement, where the South Gauteng High Court, Johannesburg, had to interpret an order issued by the Magistrate's Court, Germiston, concerning the interest on arrear rental payments. The appellants, Euro Blitz 21 (Pty) Ltd and Ivo Branco, were ordered to pay the respondent, Secena Aircraft Investments CC, interest on a principal sum of R353,126.40 at a rate of prime plus 5% calculated from 24 March 2006 until the date of payment ([1]).

In the Magistrate's Court, the claim for payment included words indicating that interest would be "calculated daily," but it did not specify whether this interest was simple or compound. The Magistrate found in favor of Secena, granting the claim as set out in the particulars of claim without explicitly addressing the nature of the interest ([2]–[3]). Following the ruling, Secena sought a declaration from the High Court that this meant interest was to be compounded daily, a claim which the High Court accepted ([4]).

#### I – Issues
The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether the interest indicated in the order from the Magistrate's Court was to be interpreted as simple or compound interest. This necessitated an analysis of the plain meaning of the language used in the original order and the financial implications of such a designation ([1], [5]).

Additionally, the applicability of the "in duplum" rule was mentioned, where the respondent sought to ensure that the accrual of interest was managed correctly within the confines of the law ([13]).

#### R – Rules
The legal principles relevant to this case include the established rules for interpreting court orders, which dictate that the court's intention must primarily be derived from the language used in the judgment, a principle derived from cases such as *Firestone South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Gentiruco A G* [1977] 4 SA 298 (A) ([6]). 

Moreover, it is a well-recognised legal standard that compound interest can only be claimed under defined circumstances, as established in *Davehill (Pty) Ltd v Community Development Board* [1988] 1 SA 290 (A), and that unless explicitly stated, parties are generally entitled to simple interest ([12]). 

Stakeholders must demonstrate mutually agreed terms or customary practices if they claim compound interest, as highlighted in *Standard Bank of South Africa Ltd v Oneanate Investments (Pty) Ltd* [1998] 1 SA 811 (SCA) ([12]).

#### A – Application
The SCA applied these rules by evaluating the ordinary meaning of the term "calculated," ultimately determining that it refers purely to the method of calculating interest, not necessarily implying that such interest is compounded. The SCA asserted that "to calculate" does not equate to "to compound," maintaining that these are distinct financial concepts ([7]–[10]).

The judgment highlighted a key observation: if the purpose of the order had been to compound interest on a daily basis, such wording would need to be explicitly included in the order. The appellants contended, and the court agreed, that without an explicit statement allowing for compounding, the presumption should default to simple interest pursuant to the legal standards in South Africa, as reflected in previous rulings ([11], [12]).

Most importantly, the SCA indicated that the respondent’s initial interpretation, which sought to conflate the two distinct concepts of calculation and compounding, was legally untenable. The absence of a substantive argument for compounding in the original proceedings and the phrasing used in the magistrate's order led the court to conclude that it was dealing with simple interest calculated daily ([10]–[12]).

#### C – Conclusion
In conclusion, the SCA upheld the appeal, ruling that the interpretation previously adopted by the South Gauteng High Court was incorrect. The SCA clarified that the phrase "calculated daily" in the Magistrate's Court order did not equate to daily compounding of interest. The court set aside the order of the High Court and confirmed that the interest should be understood as simple interest only. The implications of this ruling are significant for similar cases where contractual interest terms require precise language to avoid ambiguities concerning the nature of interest ([14]).

This judgment reinforces the necessity for clear drafting in legal documents and sets a precedent for the interpretation of interest clauses in contracts, emphasizing the differentiation between calculating and compounding interest.
