FIRAC Case Note: Chevron Engineering (Pty) Ltd v Nkambule and Others

Case Title: CHEVRON ENGINEERING (PTY) LTD v NKAMBULE AND OTHERS
Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Case Number: 68/2002
Judgment Date: 2 June 2003
Coram: Vivier ADP, Zulman, Farlam, Lewis JJA et Mlambo AJA

F – Facts:

The applicant, Chevron Engineering (Pty) Ltd, sought leave to appeal against a decision by the Labour Appeal Court (LAC) delivered on 28 November 2000 ([1]). This judgment confirmed a previous order from the industrial court on 15 October 1999, which mandated the reinstatement of the respondents—Nkambule and 23 others—who had been dismissed for participating in an illegal strike on 23 March 1995 ([1]–[2]).

The industrial court retained jurisdiction over disputes pending when the Labour Relations Act 28 of 1956 was repealed by the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 ([2]). The applicant's appeal to the LAC was dismissed, leading to the application for leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([1], [3]). However, the LAC, in a subsequent judgment by Nugent AJA, refused the application for leave to appeal, citing item 22(6) of Schedule 7 of the 1995 Act, which expressly stated that no appeal lies against a judgment from the LAC in cases such as this ([4]–[7]).

I – Issues:

The central legal issue revolves around the interpretation of item 22(6) of Schedule 7 of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995, specifically whether it allows for an appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal from a decision of the LAC regarding an industrial court's judgment ([10], [12]). The secondary issue consists of determining the compatibility of item 22(6) with section 168(3) of the Constitution, which affords the Supreme Court of Appeal jurisdiction to decide appeals in any matter, thus potentially overriding the provisions of the 1995 Act ([11], [15]).

R – Rules:

Key legal provisions involved in this case include:

  1. Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995:
  • Item 22(6), which states that no appeal lies from a decision of the LAC in cases arising from the industrial court ([10]).
  1. Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996:
  • Section 168(3), which permits the Supreme Court of Appeal to decide appeals in any matter and underscores its role as the highest court of appeal, barring constitutional matters ([11]).

Precedents relevant to this case include Khoza v Gypsum Industries Ltd, where a similar issue arose concerning the appealability of LAC decisions ([13]).

A – Application:

The court undertook an interpretation of item 22(6) and concluded that while the provision seemed to preclude appeals to the Supreme Court of Appeal, the language "subject to the Constitution" suggests that the Constitution takes precedence ([15]–[18]). The inclusion of this phrase means that if the Constitution provides for a right of appeal in conflict with the provisions of the 1995 Act, the constitutional provisions would prevail.

The court noted that such an interpretation does not render item 22(6) unconstitutional; rather, it clarifies that the Constitution allows for the possibility of further appeals, reflecting the drafter's intention to ensure constitutional compliance ([16]).

Farlam JA criticized the reliance on the Khoza decision, arguing it was reached per incuriam, as the attorneys involved failed to consider updated statutory provisions related to item 22 ([18]). This pointed out a lack of proper legal argumentation regarding the interpretation of relevant statutes, which the court found essential to correctly resolving the appealability of the LAC's decision.

C – Conclusion:

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately concluded that Chevron Engineering (Pty) Ltd is entitled to appeal against the LAC’s decision and that no leave for such an appeal was required, as neither the Constitution nor the 1995 Act stipulated the need for leave ([19]). The court ordered compliance with procedural rules regarding the prosecution and conduct of the appeal and decreed that the costs of the application should be treated as costs in the cause of the appeal ([23]).

This decision reinforced the supremacy of the Constitution in labour relations matters and clarified the scope of appellate jurisdiction concerning the Labour Appeal Court's decisions, shedding light on the judicial scrutiny of statutory provisions vis-à-vis constitutional rights.
