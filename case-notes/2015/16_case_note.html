Acting Speaker of the National Assembly v Teddy Bear Clinic for Abused Children and Another [2015] ZACC 16

In Acting Speaker of the National Assembly v Teddy Bear Clinic for Abused Children and Another, the Constitutional Court of South Africa examined the request by the Acting Speaker of the National Assembly to extend the period of suspension of a declaration of invalidity...

Constitutional Court 2015 [2015] ZACC 16

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–4])

In Acting Speaker of the National Assembly v Teddy Bear Clinic for Abused Children and Another, the Constitutional Court of South Africa examined the request by the Acting Speaker of the National Assembly to extend the period of suspension of a declaration of invalidity concerning certain provisions of the Criminal Law (Sexual Offences and Related Matters) Amendment Act 32 of 2007 (the Act). The origins of this case lie in the judgment of Teddy Bear Clinic for Abused Children and Another v Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development and Another [2013] ZACC 35, where sections 15 and 16 of the Act were declared unconstitutional for infringing upon the rights to dignity and privacy of adolescents aged 12 to 16, as protected by sections 10 and 14 of the Constitution, and for violating the best interests of the child principle under section 28(2) of the Constitution. The declaration of invalidity was initially suspended for 18 months to allow Parliament to remedy these defects ([paras 1–4]).

Prior to the current application, the Acting Speaker pursued a limited extension of the suspension, granted on 31 March 2015, which lasted until 15 May 2015. The Acting Speaker's application for a further extension to 5 August 2015 was heard urgently on 7 May 2015, as the initial suspension period was set to expire shortly after ([paras 4–6]).

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 5–10])

The central issue before the Court was whether there was sufficient justification to extend the suspension of the declaration of invalidity of sections 15 and 16 of the Act until 5 August 2015. Additional considerations included the adequacy of the Acting Speaker's explanation for the delays in legislative action, the potential prejudice to affected parties if the extension was denied, and Parliament's ability to rectify the unconstitutional provisions within the proposed time frame.

Furthermore, the Court had to address the appropriateness of continuing the urgency of the application under a heightened scrutiny of state compliance with constitutional obligations ([paras 5–10]).

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 11–12])

The Court’s analysis was framed within the constitutional framework provided by section 172(1) of the South African Constitution, which empowers the Court to declare laws that are inconsistent with the Constitution as invalid, while also allowing for remedial orders that are "just and equitable."

In Ex Parte Minister of Social Development [2006] ZACC 3 and Cross-Border Road Transport Agency v Central African Road Services (Pty) Ltd and Another [2015] ZACC 12, it was established that the power to suspend declarations of invalidity should be exercised sparingly and with consideration of various factors, including the constitutional norms, the need for finality, and the balance between conflicting interests ([paras 11–12]).

These principles were applied to ascertain whether maintaining the suspension would facilitate functional governance while simultaneously upholding constitutional imperatives.

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 13–19])

The Court critically assessed the application for extension based on the submissions made by both the Acting Speaker and the respondents, which included non-opposition to the extension up to 5 August 2015 but clear opposition to any subsequent applications for extension.

In evaluating the Acting Speaker's explanation for why the constitutional defects were not resolved within the original timeframe, the Court acknowledged that public consultation processes had been extensive and justifiably required adequate handling. Nevertheless, it noted the urgency with which the application was submitted, critiquing the Applicant's delayed timing and inadequate elucidation regarding the urgency invoked.

The Court weighed the potential prejudice against children under 16 who might be adversely affected due to the invalidity of the statutes, yet determined that the existing moratorium would continue to safeguard their interests during the extension period ([paras 13–19]). Moreover, it noted the significance of public engagement and legislative thoroughness, reaffirming the principle of separation of powers and the traditional reluctance of the judiciary to dictate parliamentary processes ([paras 16–19]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 20–22])

The Constitutional Court ultimately granted the extension of the suspension of the declaration of invalidity until 5 August 2015, citing the requirement for legislative processes to be completed thoroughly and transparently. The Court awarded costs in favor of the respondents, acknowledging their participation in ensuring parliamentary compliance with the Court’s directives.

This decision underscored not only the protection of children's rights in sensitive legal contexts but also affirmed the judiciary's deference to Parliament in legislative matters that carry substantial public interest. The ratio decidendi emphasized the critical importance of legislative diligence in responding to judicial findings of unconstitutionality while balancing the procedural interests of all parties involved ([paras 20–22]).

The Court's ruling further reinforces the need for legislative bodies to adhere to constitutional mandates expeditiously while maintaining inclusive processes, thus fostering public trust and engagement in governance.

This case note captures the essence of the court's ruling and its implications for constitutional law and child rights in South Africa.
