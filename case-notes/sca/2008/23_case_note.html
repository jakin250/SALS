Supreme Court of Appeal

Bisnath v Absa Bank Ltd (117/07 and 674/07) [2008] ZASCA 23

The protracted litigation between Absa Bank Ltd (hereinafter "the Bank") and Mr and Mrs Bisnath, along with the Gita Family Trust (the "Trust"), has its roots in mortgage and law regarding property executed under a bond.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2008 [2008] ZASCA 23

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The protracted litigation between Absa Bank Ltd (hereinafter "the Bank") and Mr and Mrs Bisnath, along with the Gita Family Trust (the "Trust"), has its roots in mortgage and law regarding property executed under a bond. The Trust owned a singular property, registered in 1994, and the Bank had two mortgage bonds registered in their favour ([1-3]).

The Trust failed to make timely payments under the bonds, prompting the Bank to file a summons (case 8912/98), which resulted in a judgment and an order declaring the Trust's property executable on 8 December 1998 ([4]). The Trust's property was subsequently sold on 4 October 2000, with the Bank acquiring it and selling it to a third party in 2002, crediting the Trust's account with the proceeds ([5]).

Simultaneously, the Bisnaths were embroiled in litigation regarding their own properties, for which the Bank also held mortgage bonds. After extensive litigation concerning installments, arrears, and properties attached in execution, the first action culminated in a settlement agreement ([6-12]).

In April 2006, the Bank provided notice of its intention to obtain a judgment by consent based on a prior settlement agreement with the Bisnaths ([11]). The outcome was a consent judgment rendered against the Bisnaths for their remaining properties, later leading to the current appeals ([30]).

I – Issues

The current appeals raise several legal questions:

  1. In the first appeal (case 117/07), whether:
  • The Trust was entitled to a credit of R66,000 arising from the sale of a property.
  • The Trust's property had been declared specially executable.
  • The Trust is due a credit for unpaid rentals.
  1. In the second appeal (case 674/07), whether:
  • Appropriate notice of the Bank's intention to apply for judgment was given to the Bisnaths.
  • The confirmation of the rule nisi by Radebe AJ should be overturned ([14]).

R – Rules

The legal landscape governing the relationship between mortgagor and mortgagee relies on principles from Roman-Dutch law regarding obligations related to mortgages and pledges. Key rules include:

  1. The obligations of a pledgee, such as accounting for fruits derived from the pledged property, are not automatically applicable to a mortgagee unless in possession of the property ([23-25]).
  2. The evidence provided must satisfy the party alleging any dispute; hence, the onus lies on the mortgagor (in this case, the Bisnaths) to prove that the mortgagee was in possession ([25]).

In regard to procedural compliance and settlement agreements, notice requirements defined in the agreement itself dictate the method by which parties must be informed about proceedings ([32-34]).

A – Application

First Appeal:

  1. Credit of R66,000: The court found Mr. Bisnath's testimony claiming an agreement with the Bank unpersuasive. Key facts included discrepancies in Bisnath's account of the events leading to the credit. The court affirmed the lower court’s finding that he lacked credibility ([15-18]).
  2. Trust Property Executability: The appellants contended that the property involved was misidentified in court orders regarding executability. The Bank's affidavits clarified the property designation, asserting only one relevant property (Erf 2643). The court determined that the description was sufficient to declare it executable, given that the identification of property was adequate despite the extension number issue ([19-21]).
  3. Rental Collection: The appellants alleged that the Bank was in possession of the property and failed to collect potentially extinguishing rental income. The court maintained that, under law, a mortgagee’s obligation to account for rentals applies only when in possession, a condition not proven by the appellants ([22-28]).

Second Appeal:
The appellate court scrutinized the procedures leading up to the judgment by consent. The court held the Bank fulfilled its notice requirements as per the agreement when it communicated via registered post to the Bisnaths. Notably, the other points raised by the Bisnaths were found lacking in merit. The court recognized that the right to execute based on consent judgment was valid due to proper notice having been provided ([30-38]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that:

  1. The first appeal was dismissed, with costs granted against the appellants due to the frivolous nature of the arguments presented ([39]).
  2. The second appeal was upheld; the previous order was overturned, discharging the rule nisi and requiring the appellants to cover the Bank's costs ([39]).

The implications of this decision underscore the necessity for precise legal compliance in mortgage agreements and the burdens placed on the parties to provide clear and competent evidence substantiating their claims, as well as adherence to procedure concerning notifications. This ruling further delineates the responsibilities of mortgagees in relation to property occupancy and income, establishing clearer guidelines on the obligations toward mortgagors.
