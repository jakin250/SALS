Supreme Court of Appeal 2010 [2010] ZASCA 5 ### F – Facts This case involves a defamation

FIRAC Case Note: The Citizen v McBride (277/08) [2010] ZASCA 5

F – Facts

This case involves a defamation suit brought by Robert John McBride against The Citizen newspaper and several of its contributors, including the publisher, editor, and journalists. McBride sought damages alleging that editorial comments and articles published by The Citizen between 11 September 2003 and 30 October 2003 defamed him and impacted his reputation ([1], [5]). The articles discussed McBride's candidacy for the position of Chief of Police for the Ekurhuleni Metro and referenced his history, particularly the bombing of Magoo’s Bar in 1986 that resulted in civilian deaths. This incident led to his conviction for murder, although he was later granted amnesty by the Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) under the Promotion of National Unity and Reconciliation Act 34 of 1995 ([2], [3]). McBride claimed that the statements made by The Citizen implied that, notwithstanding the amnesty, he remained a criminal and was morally corrupt ([13]).

In the trial court, McBride was awarded R200,000 in damages, R100,000 of which was against the first four appellants jointly, and R100,000 against the first three appellants. The appellants appealed the judgment, contesting both the findings of defamation and the award of damages ([1], [48]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue at stake is whether the statements made by The Citizen regarding McBride were defamatory and, if so, whether the appellants could successfully claim a defense of fair comment. Key questions include:

  1. Did the editorial comments and articles imply McBride's criminality despite his granted amnesty under the TRC Act?
  2. Were the statements about McBride being a "murderer" or "criminal" considered true or false within the context of the law, especially given the implications of the TRC Act ([4], [20], [23])?

R – Rules

The legal framework governing defamation in South Africa revolves around the balance between the right to freedom of expression and the protection of an individual's dignity and reputation. The primary rules applicable in this case include:

  1. Defamation Criteria: A statement is defamatory if it is false and tends to lower a person in the estimation of right-thinking members of society ([20], [23]).
  2. Defense of Fair Comment: For a successful defense of fair comment, the following elements must be established:
  • The statement must constitute comment or opinion rather than a statement of fact ([20]).
  • The comment must be fair.
  • The facts upon which the comment is based must be true ([21]).
  • The comment must concern a matter of public interest ([20], [21]).
  1. Promotion of National Unity and Reconciliation Act 34 of 1995: Particularly sections 20(7) and (10), which stipulate that individuals granted amnesty for political offenses shall no longer be held criminally liable for those actions, and their conviction is considered as having never occurred for all intents and purposes ([4], [20]).

A – Application

The court's analysis hinged on the interpretation of the statements made in the editorials and whether, given the context and the TRC amnesty, they were defamatory. It focused on the legal implications of the granted amnesty:

  • Truth of Statements: The court examined if referring to McBride as a "murderer" was factually true or if the TRC's granting of amnesty nullified this characterization. The court concluded that despite the historical facts of McBride's actions, amnesty transformed his legal status, suggesting he could no longer be labeled a murderer in a legal context ([31], [32], [93]).
  • Public Interest and Fair Comment: The court found that the statements were made in the context of McBride's public candidacy, thus falling under public interest, which granted the appellant some leeway. However, it emphasized that any comment insinuating that someone is a murderer without acknowledging their amnesty status lacked a factual basis essential for the fair comment defense ([41], [70], [84]).

Ultimately, the ruling in favor of McBride was based on the premise that the defamatory statements about his character had been made in ignorance of the legal implications of his amnesty under the TRC Act ([34], [94]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal partially upheld McBride's appeal, indicating that the statements made by The Citizen were indeed defamatory. The Court concluded that McBride could no longer be labeled a murderer due to the amnesty granted to him, which substantively altered his legal persona in terms of public discourse. The appellants were ordered to pay McBride R150,000 in damages and 75% of the respondent's costs. This case underscores the complex interplay between historical actions, legal amnesty, and contemporary defamation law in South Africa, revealing how the outcomes of past conflicts are navigated within a modern constitutional democracy ([36], [46]).
