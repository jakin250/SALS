Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note: Langa v The State (640/16) [2017] ZASCA 2

## F – Facts
On 17 June 1999, Sydwell Langa (the appellant) was convicted in the Regional Court, Soweto, of one count of murder and one count of attempted murder. He received a sentence of 10 years’ imprisonment for murder and 4 years’ imprisonment for attempted murder, with both sentences ordered to run consecutively ([1]). Following his conviction, Langa appealed to the Witwatersrand Local Division; however, this appeal was dismissed, and the record of the judgment could not be located ([1]). Subsequently, he sought leave to appeal from the South Gauteng High Court, which was granted ([1]).

The appeal was heard by the Supreme Court of Appeal on 3 November 2016 and judgment was delivered on 23 February 2017 ([1]). The State raised an initial procedural point, arguing that the record did not show that Langa had properly applied for leave to appeal, necessitating compliance with section 309B of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA) ([2]). However, the State conceded that it must be assumed the trial court had denied leave to appeal, thereby affirming the appeal's propriety ([2]).

Langa’s conviction stemmed from events on 20 April 1997, at Joe’s tavern in Meadowlands, where Mr. Wilson Mofokane was shot and killed, and Mr. Smangilso Simelane was injured. Testimonies from various witnesses, primarily those who were patrons at the tavern, were pivotal in establishing Langa's involvement ([4]-[10]). Various accounts emerged, with key witnesses asserting they had seen Langa as the shooter despite the appellant contending he was innocent, claiming instead that his friend had fired warning shots to protect him ([9]).

## I – Issues
The case raised several legal questions, primarily concerning the integrity of the identification evidence presented at trial. Key issues included:
1. **Was there sufficient evidential basis for Langa's conviction given the alleged contradictions in the State witnesses’ accounts?** ([4]-[11]).
2. **Did the refusal by the magistrate to entertain a s 174 application constitute a fatal irregularity?** ([3]).
3. **Was there compliance with the procedural requirements of section 309B of the Criminal Procedure Act concerning leave to appeal?** ([2]).
4. **In the context of sentencing, should the sentences for the two convictions have been ordered to run concurrently?** ([13]-[14]).

## R – Rules
The Supreme Court of Appeal relied on several key legal principles and statutory provisions:
1. **Section 309B of the CPA** mandates that a convicted individual must seek leave to appeal from the trial court, establishing a procedural requirement for valid appeals ([2]).
2. The court evaluated the contradictions in witness testimony through the lens of materiality; minor inconsistencies do not undermine overall credibility if the truth is conveyed ([11]).
3. **The principle of judicial discretion** in sentencing, as provided under section 280(2) of the CPA, permits a court to decide whether sentences should run concurrently; appellate courts can interfere only if it is shown that discretion was exercised unreasonably ([13]-[14]).

## A – Application
In addressing the appeals, the court first acknowledged the procedural posture regarding Langa's application for leave to appeal. The admission by the State that the trial court had effectively denied leave lent weight to the appeal's acceptability ([2]). 

Turning to the merits of the case, the court scrutinized the identification evidence presented by the State witnesses. Testimonies of Mr. Khutsoane and Mr. Letsolo, who identified Langa as the shooter, were pivotal despite some inconsistencies regarding the events leading to the shooting. The court found that the crucial aspects of the witnesses' accounts — the identification of Langa as the shooter and the circumstances of the shooting — were sufficiently corroborated and credible despite minor discrepancies ([11]). Both witnesses were present during the incident, and their observations were deemed detailed and reliable, negating Langa's arguments concerning the unreliability of the identification process ([4], [11]).

Regarding the alleged irregularity in denying the s 174 application, the court concluded that the State had presented a prima facie case, thereby rendering Langa’s request for discharge moot ([3]). 

On the sentencing issues, while the appellate court was aware that the sentences were not ordered to run concurrently, it noted that the trial court did not offer reasons for this decision. Nonetheless, the court justified maintaining consecutive sentences based on the nature of the offenses and their context — specifically, the random shooting of patrons evidenced an intention to cause wide-ranging harm, justifying the differentiation in sentencing ([14]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Langa's appeals against both his convictions and sentences. The court held that the identity of the appellant as the perpetrator of the offenses was substantiated beyond a reasonable doubt. Furthermore, the procedural issues regarding the appeal's legitimacy were effectively resolved in favor of upholding the trial court's judgment, and the sentencing was deemed appropriate given the serious nature of the offenses ([15]).

The court's judgment highlights the significant role of witness credibility and evidential consistency in securing convictions in criminal matters, alongside a rigorous adherence to procedural requirements in appellate processes. The outcome reiterates the judicial system's willingness to uphold sentences that reflect the gravity of violent crimes, particularly those involving public safety concerns in social contexts such as taverns.
