Constitutional Court

Mazibuko and Others v City of Johannesburg and Others [2009] ZACC 28

The applicants asserted that the Free Basic Water policy discriminated against them, being merely based on household rather than individual needs and failing to account for larger families.

Constitutional Court 2009 [2009] ZACC 28

F – Facts

The case involves five impoverished residents from Phiri, Soweto, led by Lindiwe Mazibuko, who challenge the City of Johannesburg’s Free Basic Water policy and the introduction of prepaid water meters ([1]-[4]). The City allocated 6 kilolitres of free water per month to every household, based on legislation and local bylaws, to combat the issue of water scarcity and promote equitable water access. Though the policy was rooted in attempts to manage water resources efficiently, the applicants contended that it failed to comply with the constitutional right to sufficient water as stipulated by section 27 of the South African Constitution ([6], [19]).

The applicants asserted that the Free Basic Water policy discriminated against them, being merely based on household rather than individual needs and failing to account for larger families. They argued that the prepaid meters, which require residents to pay for water once the free allocation is exhausted, led to unlawful discontinuation of water supply ([6]-[12], [128]). The case escalated through the courts, resulting in the High Court ruling in favor of the applicants, declaring both the prepaid meters and the policy unconstitutional ([25]-[27]). The City and Johannesburg Water appealed, leading to scrutiny of the constitutional and legislative frameworks governing water rights ([28]-[29]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues arise around two key queries:

  1. Does the City’s Free Basic Water policy, which limits the provision of 6 kilolitres of free water per household, violate section 27 of the Constitution and section 11 of the Water Services Act, particularly in terms of reasonableness and compliance with the constitutional guarantee of access to sufficient water? ([6], [19])
  2. Was the installation of prepaid water meters lawful and compliant with existing statutory provisions, or did it result in unlawful cut-offs of water supply contrary to the principles embedded in constitutional rights? ([6]-[7])

R – Rules

Central to the Court’s ruling are several legal statutes and constitutional provisions:

  • Section 27 of the Constitution: Ensures everyone has the right to sufficient water and obliges the state to take reasonable legislative and other measures to progressively realize this right within its available resources ([19]).
  • Water Services Act (Act 108 of 1997): The Act delineates the duties of water services authorities and providers, requiring them to ensure citizens have access to basic water supply and sanitation ([20]-[21]).
  • National Water Standards Regulations issued under the Water Services Act establish a minimum standard which the Free Basic Water policy sought to follow, including the provision of 25 litres per person per day as basic supply. The City must ensure compliance with this across all demographics ([23]).
  • Promotion of Administrative Justice Act: Provides guidelines around the procedural fairness required for administrative actions, relevant for assessing the lawfulness of how policy changes were implemented ([125]).

A – Application

Analysis of the Free Basic Water Policy:

The Court concluded that the City’s Free Basic Water policy did not conflict with the Constitution, emphasizing that while the applicants sought a higher allocation of water (50 litres per person), the legal framework allows the state discretion to allocate resources based on available evidence and circumstances ([44]-[64]). The City argued convincingly that establishing a per person allocation was administratively burdensome given fluctuating household sizes and economic factors, aligning with established norms of governmental discretion in social rights ([67]).

Justification for Prepaid Meters:

On the matter of prepaid meters, the Court found that their installation was lawful under municipal regulations, allowing for the recovery of previously unaccounted water. The structure of the policy aimed to prevent wastage and ensure payment efficiency, with the installation process backed by a comprehensive community consultation precedent that effectively informed residents about their options ([106]-[126]).

The evidence indicated that many residents accepted the shift as a means of improving the unsustainable former system, which had failed to account for water losses and payment neglect ([160]-[168]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately ruled in favor of the City of Johannesburg and Johannesburg Water, declaring the Free Basic Water policy reasonable and lawful, thus setting aside the earlier High Court and Supreme Court of Appeal decisions ([170]-[171]). The ruling emphasized the state’s duty to progressively realize social and economic rights while maintaining a balance between legislative powers and the legislative need for flexibility in meeting those rights. The case illustrates a key moment in South Africa’s transformative constitutionalism, demonstrating how legal frameworks can adapt to meet social challenges and foster government accountability in resource management.

Implications:

This ruling reinforces the accountability of government entities in managing basic utility provisions, while also ensuring that the policy-making process remains responsive to the socio-economic disparities affecting vulnerable populations. The balance between enforcing constitutional rights and allowing discretion is a crucial consideration for social rights litigation moving forward.
