The National Director of Public Prosecutions (Ex Parte Application) [2021] ZASCA 142

Following this, the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) applied for a preservation order under section 38 of the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (POCA) in the Mpumalanga Division of the High Court.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 142

F – Facts

This case arises from the actions of three individuals who were arrested on 16 August 2019 in a timber plantation in Mpumalanga, discovered in possession of several poached animals. The South African Police Service (SAPS) seized the carcasses and a firearm from a Toyota Hilux belonging to one of the accused ([paras 2–3]). The case against them was unexpectedly struck from the roll on 30 January 2020 by the magistrate, without notice to the prosecution or a proper legal basis for the order, leading to the return of the seized property to one of the accused ([paras 4–5]).

Following this, the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) applied for a preservation order under section 38 of the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (POCA) in the Mpumalanga Division of the High Court. This application was initially struck from the roll due to the court's finding that the NDPP had failed to demonstrate urgency as per a specific practice directive, despite agreeing that a substantive case for the preservation order was made ([paras 5–6]).

I – Issues

The principal legal question presented before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether the NDPP is required to demonstrate urgency in the founding affidavit of an application under section 38 of the POCA before the substantive merits of the case could be adjudicated ([para 1]). This necessitated an evaluation of the interplay between statutory provisions, practice directives of the High Court, and established case law regarding ex parte applications.

R – Rules

The relevant legal frameworks include:

  1. Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998: Specifically, section 38 sets out the procedures for applying for a preservation order. It allows the NDPP to make an ex parte application to prohibit any person from dealing with property that is believed to be involved in criminal activities ([para 7]).
  2. Uniform Rules of Court: Rule 6(4)(a) governs the procedure for ex parte applications. It allows the court to dispense with notice to the respondent under specific circumstances ([para 8]).
  3. Case Law: The SCA's reasoning referenced precedents such as NDPP v Mohamed NO and NDPP v Ramadhani, which established that applications under section 38 are inherently urgent, suggesting that the legislative intent was to allow for expedited consideration without the need for a formal demonstration of urgency in every case ([paras 9–10]).

A – Application

In its analysis, the SCA highlighted that the nature of applications under section 38 of POCA is fundamentally urgent, and reiterated that the relevant practice directives from the Mpumalanga Division of the High Court contradict both the intent of the statute and the Uniform Rules ([paras 14–18]). It examined the High Court's overreliance on the practice directive, clarifying that such directives should not undermine statutory provisions or constitutional rights. The SCA concluded that implying a necessity to prove urgency for ex parte applications effectively negated the urgency characterised by POCA applications ([para 20]).

Consequently, the SCA determined that the High Court erred in striking the NDPP's application from the roll on the grounds of insufficient urgency and treated the ex parte nature of the application incorrectly, as no need existed for establishing urgency when the law clearly provided for it ([paras 20–21]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA upheld the NDPP's appeal, concluding that the High Court had misapplied the law concerning urgency and the procedural rights granted to the NDPP under section 38 of POCA. The SCA set aside the original order and permitted the NDPP to re-enroll its application in its original ex parte form. The judges mandated that the application be heard without the need for service upon the respondent, emphasizing the need for substantive justice concerning property that may be linked to criminal activity ([paras 21]). This ruling affirmed that applications for preservation orders under POCA are inherently urgent and reinforced the primacy of statutory provisions over practice directives, ensuring that the enforcement of the law is not unduly hindered by procedural requirements.

This case note synthesizes the facts, issues, legal rules, applications, and conclusions drawn by the court, serving as a concise yet comprehensive legal summary suitable for academic and professional use.
