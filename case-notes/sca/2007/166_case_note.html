Supreme Court of Appeal

Aartappel Koöperasie Bpk v Pricewaterhousecoopers [2007] SCA 166 RSA

The appellant, Nasionale Aartappelkoöperasie Beperk (NAK), engaged in extensive litigation against the respondents, Pricewaterhousecoopers Inc.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2007

FIRAC Method

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–4])

The appellant, Nasionale Aartappelkoöperasie Beperk (NAK), engaged in extensive litigation against the respondents, Pricewaterhousecoopers Inc. (PWC) and its affiliated firms. NAK alleged that PWC had conducted negligent audits from 1984 to 1998 leading to substantial financial damages, which NAK claimed amounted to around R500 million. A special plea asserting that NAK's claim was champertous was dismissed, and this dismissal was upheld on appeal ([para 1]).

The trial commenced in the Transvaal Provincial Division but faced delays, including a change in judges due to recusal. After approximately 105 court days, PWC applied for security for costs on 20 November 2006, which NAK opposed. On 5 December 2006, Judge Botha ruled in favor of PWC, requiring NAK to provide security for costs, a decision which was subsequently appealed ([para 1]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 11–30])

The primary legal issue on appeal involved whether the trial court, specifically Judge Botha, had exercised its discretion properly in granting PWC's application for security for costs. This included questions such as:

  1. Whether PWC's delay in applying for security constituted an unreasonable and oppressive exercise of discretion.
  2. Whether NAK's financial difficulties were attributable to PWC's alleged negligence.
  3. Whether an order for security would infringe upon NAK's right of access to court under section 34 of the Constitution.
  4. The relevance of PWC's costs being funded by an insurer in the context of the application for security ([paras 15–30]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 15–30])

The application for security for costs was governed by section 13 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973. This statute grants the court the authority to require a company to provide security for the costs of litigation if there is cause to believe that it would be unable to satisfy an adverse costs order ([para 15]).

The case law established that courts exercise discretion in a "strict sense," requiring that any misdirection of fact or law must be demonstrated to warrant interference on appeal ([para 24]). Relevant precedents included the rulings in cases such as Giddey NO v J C Barnard and Partners and Bookworks (Pty) Ltd v Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council which clarified the balancing of interests between a plaintiff's right to access the courts and a defendant's right to recover costs if the plaintiff's claim fails ([paras 15–29]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 1–30])

In assessing the ruling by Judge Botha, the SCA found that he had thoroughly examined the evidence, particularly with regard to the delay of PWC in bringing the application for security. The court agreed that any delay was not unreasonable, as PWC could not have anticipated NAK's shifting financial state during the trial ([paras 17–26]).

The Court acknowledged that while NAK faced significant financial distress, there was no evidence indicating that this was directly caused by PWC's audits. Thus, NAK's argument that they could not meet the costs of litigation was weakened by their past financial status and the ongoing support from its shareholders ([para 19—22]).

Regarding NAK's claim of potential infringement of access to courts, the SCA cited case law indicating that such rights must be weighed against the defendant's right to recover costs, particularly in light of third-party financial support ([paras 15, 19]). The court concluded that the balance of justice favored granting security, considering that NTK (a majority shareholder) had ample financial interests and obligations to assist NAK ([para 22—30]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 29–30])

The SCA dismissed NAK's appeal, affirming Judge Botha's decision to grant security for costs. The court ruled that Botha had appropriately conducted the necessary balancing exercise, weighing the respective rights and positions of both parties sensibly. This case underscores the principle that financial support from shareholders or affiliates can influence decisions regarding security for costs, while clarifying that insurance coverage by a defendant does not negate the need for security in litigation.

The implications of this ruling highlight the importance of maintaining access to justice while recognizing the rights of defendants, especially in complex commercial litigations involving claims of negligence. The judgment reiterates the judiciary's responsibility to ensure fairness and equity in litigation orders, particularly regarding security for costs ([paras 29–30]).
