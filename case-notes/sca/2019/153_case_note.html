Supreme Court of Appeal

Tellytrack v Marshalls World of Sport (Pty) Ltd & Others (971/2018)

The Supreme Court of Appeal heard an appeal from Tellytrack, a partnership composed of Phumelela Gaming and Leisure Limited, Gold Circle (Pty) Ltd, and Kenilworth Racing (Pty) Ltd, against an order from the KwaZulu-Natal Division of High Court, Durban ([1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 153 (25 November 2019)

Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Neutral Citation: [2019] ZASCA 153 (25 November 2019)
Judges: Navsa, Saldulker, Van der Merwe, Dlodlo JJA and Weiner AJA

F – Facts

The Supreme Court of Appeal heard an appeal from Tellytrack, a partnership composed of Phumelela Gaming and Leisure Limited, Gold Circle (Pty) Ltd, and Kenilworth Racing (Pty) Ltd, against an order from the KwaZulu-Natal Division of High Court, Durban ([1]). Tellytrack sought an interdict against several respondents, including Marshalls World of Sport and its affiliates, to prevent them from allowing public viewing of horse racing events broadcast on Tellytrack's channel ([1]).

The factual backdrop includes a meeting in 2000 where a verbal agreement was formed between Tellytrack and licensed bookmakers regarding broadcasting horseracing events ([11]). However, as of September 2013, Tellytrack amended the subscription arrangements, leading to a dispute over the payment obligations of the bookmakers ([14]). The respondents ceased subscriptions and continued to display the races without payment, which led Tellytrack to claim copyright infringement under the Copyright Act 98 of 1978 ([18]).

Tellytrack’s content comprises domestic and international race feeds, which are enhanced and broadcast via DSTV channel 239. Over 75% of the broadcasts are international races ([4], [5]). Tellytrack claimed that by allowing public access to the broadcasts, the respondents had infringed their copyright, as they were responsible for producing and enhancing the raw feeds ([15], [19]).

I – Issues

The central legal issue was whether the respondents' activities constituted an infringement of Tellytrack’s copyright under sections 6, 8, 9, 11B, and 23 of the Copyright Act ([3], [18]). This raises the following specific questions:

  1. Do the actions of the respondents in publicly displaying Tellytrack's broadcasts constitute an infringement of copyright, specifically concerning cinematographic films?
  2. Is Tellytrack the rightful owner of the copyright to the broadcasts as claimed?
  3. Should Tellytrack be entitled to the interdict it seeks against the respondents?

R – Rules

The relevant legislative framework includes the Copyright Act 98 of 1978, particularly sections that pertain to copyright eligibility and infringement:

  • Section 2 outlines the eligibility for copyright, stating that a work must be fixed or represented in a material form to qualify ([31]).
  • Section 8 defines the nature of copyright in cinematograph films, granting exclusive rights to reproduce and publicly display the works ([22]).
  • Section 23 clarifies that copyright infringement occurs when someone without a license engages in acts reserved for the copyright owners ([18]).

Precedent cases, notably Golden China TV Game Centre & Others v Nintendo Co. Ltd and Nintendo regarding the requirement of fixation for copyright protection, significantly inform the court’s interpretation of these sections ([24], [31]).

A – Application

In its judgment, the Supreme Court of Appeal evaluated whether the displays of the broadcasts at the respondents' premises constituted “seen in public” as required under Section 8 ([32]). The court highlighted that while the respondents argued they were broadcasting live events, Tellytrack maintained that these events were indeed recorded and thus eligible for copyright protection.

The court noted the necessity of work being fixed or stored to qualify as a cinematographic film ([30]). Tests for what constitutes a copy require that it be fixed in some material form. Given that the material shown on channel 239 was recorded simultaneously, the contention was that the broadcasts could not be classified as infringing a copyrighted work since they were not fixed at the moment of transmission ([28], [29]).

Despite arguments from Tellytrack about the enhancements made to the feeds, the court emphasized that the claim primarily rested on whether a “cinematograph film” existed at all, as defined in the act ([35]). The conclusion reached was that Tellytrack could prove that the work had indeed been fixed before being displayed, thus affirming its rights ([36]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court held that Tellytrack was entitled to copyright protection for its cinematographic films, asserting that the broadcasts were fixed and thus infringing under Section 8(1)(b) of the Act ([38]). The previous judgment by the KwaZulu-Natal Division was set aside, and the court granted Tellytrack the interdict it sought against the respondents with costs, including allowances for two counsel ([38]).

The implications of this ruling clarify the definition of cinematographic films in the context of live broadcasts and emphasize the necessity of copyright compliance in the broadcasting industry. The outcome underscores the significance of material fixation for copyright claims and may serve as precedent for future disputes involving digital content and broadcasting rights.
