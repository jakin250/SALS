Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# Case Note: Minister for Transport and Public Works: Western Cape & others v Adonisi and Others (522/2021 & 523/2021) [2024] ZASCA 47

## F: Facts

The central issue in this case pertains to the Provincial Government of the Western Cape's decision to sell the Tafelberg property, located in Sea Point, Cape Town, to the Phyllis Jowell Jewish Day School ([1], [2]). This property, historically a school, was declared surplus to provincial needs following the closure of the remedial school located there in 2010 ([3]). The Department of Transport initiated a sale process in 2016, with the sale price set at R135 million, amid opposition from various parties, including residents and advocacy groups arguing that the property should be used for social housing in compliance with constitutional obligations to reverse the apartheid spatial legacy ([6]-[12]).

The respondents launched two applications against this sale in the Western Cape Division of the High Court, claiming that the Province and City had failed to fulfil their constitutional and statutory duties regarding land and housing access ([18]). The High Court ruled in favor of the respondents, declaring the sale unlawful and setting it aside, which prompted an appeal from the Province and related parties ([28]).

## I: Issues

1. Whether the respondents could directly invoke sections 25 and 26 of the South African Constitution concerning their rights to land and housing, given the existence of statutory frameworks like the Housing Act and Social Housing Act ([31]).
   
2. Whether the Provincial Government was obliged to provide social housing at the Tafelberg site,[35] and whether Sea Point was classified as a designated restructuring zone under the Social Housing Act, impacting the legality of the sale ([66]-[71]).
   
3. The constitutionality of regulations under the Western Cape Land Administration Act that governed the procedural aspects of the property sale ([94]-[102]).

## R: Rules

The Court stipulated the following legal principles and statutory rules:

1. **Constitutional Subsidiarity**: This principle asserts that when specific legislative measures exist to realize constitutional rights, litigants must rely on this legislation rather than directly on the Constitution ([31]-[35]).

2. **Social Housing Obligations**: The Court examined the obligations imposed by the Housing Act and the Social Housing Act, particularly related to equitable access to housing and integration of development within urban areas. The legislation emphasizes that municipalities must facilitate social housing where demand exists ([42], [44]).

3. **Regulatory Framework of GIAMA and WCLAA**: The Government Immovable Asset Management Act (GIAMA) provides a framework for managing state immovable assets, while the Western Cape Land Administration Act (WCLAA) governs the disposal procedures for provincial land ([52], [59]).

4. **Public Participation Requirements**: The regulations promulgated under the WCLAA necessitate a specific process for public involvement when proposing to dispose of land. The High Court determined that meaningful public participation is a prerequisite for compliance with procedural fairness as mandated in the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act ([97], [102]).

## A: Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) applied the aforementioned rules as follows:

1. **Invocation of Constitutional Rights**: The Court stressed the importance of constitutional subsidiarity, indicating that the respondents should have relied on the legislation pertinent to the housing rights they sought to enforce, rather than on the constitutional provisions directly ([31]-[35]). 

2. **Obligation to Provide Social Housing**: The Court found that while the existence of the Housing Act and Social Housing Act acknowledges the need for social housing, these laws do not obligate the Province to provide social housing at specific sites, such as the Tafelberg property ([36]-[44]). 

3. **Interpretation of Restructuring Zones**: In determining whether Sea Point qualifies as a restructuring zone, the Court engaged in a thorough examination of the statutory definitions and purposeful limitations outlined in the regulations, ultimately concluding that the property did not fall under the definition of a restructuring area, reaffirming the executive’s discretion in spatial planning ([66]-[80]).

4. **Legality and Procedure of the Sale**: The Court addressed the challenges against the procedural legitimacy of the sale based on GIAMA and WCLAA regulations, emphasizing that the procedures prescribed for public notice and comment were indeed followed. It underscored that the absence of a user or custodian asset management plan at the time of sale did not invalidate its legality under the WCLAA ([52], [59]-[102]).

## C: Conclusion

The SCA upheld the appeals from the Provincial Government and others, effectively overruling the High Court’s earlier rulings ([28]). The court concluded that:

1. **No Direct Constitutional Claims**: Respondents lacked the standing to invoke direct constitutional rights without aligning their claims with the requisite legislative framework established to give effect to such rights ([31]-[35]).

2. **Discretion on Housing**: The court determined that there is no strict obligation for the government to provide social housing in specific locations, maintaining that policy decisions concerning housing development remain within the executive's discretion ([36]-[44]).

3. **Compliance with Regulations**: The findings that the Province failed to comply with certain procedural aspects were deemed unfounded, as the necessary legal and regulatory frameworks for the property disposal had been adequately observed ([52], [59]-[102]).

The court’s judgments thus offer significant implications regarding the interpretation of socio-economic rights, the role of legislation in realizing these rights, and the authority of governmental bodies in property disposals and urban planning ([30], [34]).
