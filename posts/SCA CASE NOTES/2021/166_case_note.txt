# Case Note: Nongogo v The State [2021] ZASCA 166

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1-8])
The case of **Nongogo v The State** involved the appellant, Noxolo Victoria Nongogo, who was convicted of two counts: murder and conspiracy to commit murder of her husband in 2013, with sentencing taking account of both counts under the minimum sentencing provisions of section 51(1) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997. The trial was conducted by Judge Mjali in the High Court of the Eastern Cape Division, Mthatha. Upon conviction, the appellant was sentenced to life imprisonment. 

The appellant sought leave to appeal against her conviction and sentence, which was granted by the High Court on two grounds: (1) the acceptance of a special entry regarding irregularities in the trial process, and (2) the potential duplication of convictions for murder and conspiracy to murder. 

Key facts leading to the appeal included a prior trial where Mr Temba Tsekemane, co-accused, initially pleaded not guilty but later changed his plea to guilty after 12 state witnesses testified. His plea led to a separation of trials as ordered by Judge Griffiths, with the appellant's trial commencing anew before Judge Mjali ([paras 1-7]). 

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 2-8])
The central legal issues for determination were: 
1. Whether the appellant was wrongly convicted of both murder and conspiracy to commit murder, leading to a duplication of convictions ([para 2]);
2. Whether the court a quo erred in ordering the trial to start de novo before another judge as this raised questions of procedural irregularities ([para 8]);
3. Whether the appellant’s fair trial rights were violated due to an excessive delay in finalizing her matter ([para 13]);
4. Whether the appellant had been tried twice for the same offence ([para 14]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 3-12])
The court referenced several legal authorities and provisions, primarily the **Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977**, particularly section 317, which outlines the procedures for challenging the regularity of trial proceedings ([para 4]). 

In **S v Fraser** [2005] (1) SACR 455 (SCA), it was established that typically, when a conspirator's crime is committed, they should be charged with the completed crime rather than conspiracy ([para 3]). This principle supported the argument that the appellant's conviction on both counts was inappropriate and amounted to duplication ([para 3]).

The court also examined **R v Zonele and Others** [1959] (3) SA 319 (A) to clarify the necessity of separating trials to avoid prejudice when a co-accused pleads guilty ([para 10]). The overarching principle that determined the need for a separation of trials was whether prejudice would ensue for the accused—a consideration that informed numerous precedents concerning trial procedure and fair trial rights ([para 11]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 8-15])
In assessing the appellant's appeal, the court analyzed whether the conviction for both murder and conspiracy was justified. Noting the State's concession regarding the improper dual convictions, the court identified that the appellant should be convicted of either count, but not both, thus deeming the appeal on this point successful ([paras 3-5]).

In relation to the alleged irregularities during the trial, the court held that the order for a fresh trial was justified due to potential conflicts of interest between co-accused and the separation of trials was consistent with the principles established in previous cases. The court concluded that Judge Griffiths' decision to order a separation—and consequently a trial de novo—was neither erroneous nor resulted in prejudice to the appellant ([paras 9-11]).

Additionally, the court addressed the argument concerning the delay in finalizing the appellant's matter. The court pointed out that the delays were largely attributable to the appellant’s actions, and as such, could not be used to claim a violation of her fair trial rights ([para 13]). 

Lastly, regarding the claim of double jeopardy, the court clarified that section 106 of the Criminal Procedure Act allows for specific defenses against being tried for the same offence, but the appellant had not invoked these rights appropriately. As a result, this claim was dismissed as unfounded ([paras 14-15]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 16])
The court ultimately concluded that the appeal against the conviction for conspiracy to commit murder succeeded. However, it dismissed the appeal regarding the conviction for murder, confirming the life sentence imposed by the trial court. The court emphasized the appropriate legal principles concerning trial procedures, fair trial rights, and the implications of dual convictions within South African criminal law. 

The **ratio decidendi** established here accentuates the importance of ensuring that prosecutions do not lead to unfair duplications of charges and highlights judicial discretion in matters of trial separations due to conflicts of interest among co-accused. The ruling serves as a precedent for future cases involving similar procedural concerns, thus reinforcing adherence to fair trial rights within the criminal justice system. 

The decision is significant in providing clarity on the legal interpretation of conspiracy versus the completed criminal act, guiding both prosecution and defense strategies in future criminal proceedings ([paras 3-16]).
