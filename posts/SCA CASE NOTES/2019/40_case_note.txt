Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: Johannesburg Society of Advocates v Edeling (326/2018) [2019] ZASCA 40

## F – Facts

Mr. Christiaan Serfontein Edeling (the respondent) commenced his legal career as an advocate in South Africa in 1979, practicing initially at the Johannesburg Bar before moving to the Free State Bar in 1984. By the mid-1990s, serious allegations concerning dishonesty arose due to his involvement in fraudulent property transactions designed to exploit VAT loopholes, culminating in a High Court ruling on December 11, 1997, that struck him off the roll of advocates ([1]–[6]).

After his removal, Edeling unsuccessfully sought leave to appeal the order, which remained firm even when escalated to the Supreme Court of Appeal and the Constitutional Court ([2]). Edeling made two ex parte applications for re-admission in 2009 and 2015, both opposed by the Johannesburg Society of Advocates (the Society) on grounds related to his lack of honesty and full disclosure regarding his prior conduct ([3], [9]). 

The court a quo initially granted Edeling's application for re-admission without costs, a ruling contested by the Society and subsequently appealed ([3]–[4]). 

During his tenure, Edeling's deceitful conduct was detailed, including testimonies identifying his participation in two fraudulent schemes involving inflated property valuations and untruthful statements to both tax authorities and courts ([4]–[8]). The court concluded that he was not a fit and proper person to practice as an advocate, highlighting systematic dishonesty and a failure to demonstrate genuine reform ([8]).

## I – Issues

The central legal questions in this case revolved around the definition of a “fit and proper person” for re-admission as an advocate and whether Edeling had adequately demonstrated “genuine, complete and permanent reformation” since his striking off ([11]). Specifically, the court needed to assess:

1. Whether Edeling's previous conduct, which led to his removal, still indicated a lack of fitness to practice law, even in light of the time elapsed since the judgment.
2. If Edeling discharged the burden of proof to demonstrate reformation, including a responsible acknowledgment of his past dishonesty ([11], [36]).

## R – Rules

The primary legal standard applied for re-admission of a previously struck-off advocate involves examining whether genuine reform has occurred. Key precedents underpinning this decision include:

- **Swartzberg v Law Society of the Northern Provinces** ([2008] ZASCA 36) emphasizes the necessity of the applicant to establish a reformed character adequately.
- **Ex parte Knox** further delineates that the court must prioritize public protection over individual applications and serve the interest of the integrity of the legal profession ([11], [36]).
- **Ndleve v Pretoria Society of Advocates**, serves as a reminder that practicing law after being struck off constitutes contempt of court, stressing that removal addresses public protection, not mere punishment ([20]).

## A – Application

The court meticulously scrutinized Edeling's affidavits, revealing significant discrepancies between his claims of reformation and his actual conduct. For instance:

- Edeling's insistence that he was not dishonest was starkly evidenced by his failures to acknowledge his wrongdoing in substantive terms, merely suggesting that past judgments were misunderstood ([12], [36]).
- The judge noted Edeling’s reliance on character references from individuals unaware of his past fraud, which did not assist in affirming his reformed character ([14]–[15]).
- Psychological assessments and character testimonies indicated a lack of responsibility and acknowledgment of his past failings, undermining his assertions of reform ([16]–[17]).

Moreover, Edeling's conduct following his initial removal raised concerns regarding compliance with legal standards, including practicing in Lesotho while remaining unlicensed in South Africa, demonstrating grievous disregard for his duty as a legal professional ([20]–[21]).

The conclusion drawn involved a meticulous balance between Edeling's assertions of being a "fit and proper person" against the backdrop of his past misdeeds, which he appeared to minimize rather than accept ([30], [32]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately found that Mr. Edeling failed to demonstrate he was a fit and proper person for re-admission as an advocate. The court emphasized that credible, conclusive evidence of reformation should be robust and transparent, asserting that Edeling’s consistent denials of dishonesty and misrepresentation undermined his claim of having changed ([33]).

Thus, the appeal was upheld, and the order of the court a quo was set aside, concluding that Edeling’s re-admission would pose risks to the legal profession and the public, reflecting a firm stance on integrity within the legal profession ([37]). The implications of this ruling reaffirmed the vital requirement for advocates to exhibit not only ethical compliance but also a clear acknowledgment of past transgressions and genuine reform before being considered for reinstatement.
