Supreme Court of Appeal

The President of RSA v M & G Media Ltd [2010] ZASCA 177

Facts The case revolves around the refusal of the President of South Africa to disclose a report compiled by two judges regarding a visit to Zimbabwe before its 2002 elections, requested by M&G Media Limited (M&G), the publisher of the Mail & Guardian newspaper.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2010 [2010] ZASCA 177

Facts

The case revolves around the refusal of the President of South Africa to disclose a report compiled by two judges regarding a visit to Zimbabwe before its 2002 elections, requested by M&G Media Limited (M&G), the publisher of the Mail & Guardian newspaper. The report was commissioned by former President Thabo Mbeki at the request of the Presidency and has never been made public. M&G, seeking access to this report under the Promotion of Access to Information Act (PAIA) 2 of 2000, applied to the North Gauteng High Court after their request was denied ([paras 1-4]).

Mr. Trevor Fowler, the Deputy Information Officer and Acting Director-General in the Presidency, initially refused the request. This refusal was later affirmed by the then Minister in the Presidency, Ms Mantombazana Tshabalala-Msimang, and was based on claims that the report contained confidential information provided by another state or an international organization ([paras 6-8],[24-30]).

The appellants argued that the refusal was justified under sections 41(1)(b) and 44 of the PAIA, which allow for refusal of access if it would reveal confidential information from other states or if it contains opinions or recommendations made for policy formulation ([paras 21-22],[23]). M&G contested this refusal, resulting in the High Court ruling in favor of M&G. The President, Fowler, and other governmental figures subsequently appealed ([paras 4-5]).

Issues

The primary legal issue was whether the refusal to disclose the judges' report was justified under the PAIA, focusing on the interpretation of the sections cited for the refusal and the level of evidence required to substantiate claims of confidentiality ([paras 1-3],[21-22]).

Secondary Issues:

  1. Whether the appellants properly fulfilled their burden of proof in justifying the nondisclosure of the report as required by the PAIA.
  2. Whether the reasons given for refusal constituted adequate legal justification under the Act ([paras 12-13],[24-30]).

Rules

The relevant legal rules stem from the PAIA and its interpretation in the context of constitutional rights to access information held by state bodies. Specifically:

  1. Section 32(1)(a) of the Constitution states that everyone has the right of access to any information held by the state.
  2. Sections 41(1)(b) and 44(1)(a) of the PAIA, which outline the grounds for refusing access on the basis of confidentiality from other states or for the purposes of formulating policy.
  3. The burden of proof lies with the public body to justify its refusal to grant access to information ([paras 2-3],[14-15],[23-25]).

Additionally, previous cases establish the principles surrounding the need for transparency and accountability in a democratic society, emphasizing that limitations on rights must be clearly justified ([paras 9-10],[32]).

Application

The court analyzed the appellants' claims and the corresponding evidence (or lack thereof). The appellants primarily failed to demonstrate adequate factual support for their assertions about the nature of the confidentiality of the report. The judgment emphasized that mere assertions or conclusions, devoid of supporting evidence, do not satisfy the requirements of the PAIA ([paras 18-19],[34-36]).

  1. Confidentiality Claims: The court found that the assertion of confidentiality under section 41(1)(b) was weak, as there was no indication that the judges had received or were expected to receive confidential information as mandated. The lack of direct evidence from individuals who were actually present during the Zimbabwe visit further weakened the case for refusing access ([paras 40-44]).
  2. Policy Formulation Justification: The court also scrutinized the usage of section 44 in justifying the refusal. It was determined that the report’s potential utility in the policy-making process was insufficient to meet the stringent requirements set by the PAIA, which required evidence that the document was specifically prepared for such purposes ([paras 33-35],[53-54]).
  3. Judicial Review Standards: The court asserted that it is not a simple review of the information officer’s decision but rather an original proceeding, which required the public body to produce sufficient evidence that no lawful grounds for access existed. In this case, the repeated reliance on perfunctory conclusions without substantive evidence did not satisfy the burden placed on the appellants ([paras 12-13],[31]).

Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the High Court’s ruling that the appellants had not met their burden of proof in justifying the refusal to disclose the report. The appellants’ failure to provide credible evidence to substantiate their claims of confidentiality established that the refusal was not compliant with the requirements of the PAIA and the Constitution ([paras 54-55]). This ruling reinforces the principles of transparency and accountability in a democratic State, affirming the necessity for governmental bodies to provide justifiable reasons based on credible evidence when denying public access to information. The case is significant as it illustrates the interpretation and application of the PAIA in ensuring a balance between state secrecy and the public's right to information.
