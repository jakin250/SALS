Supreme Court of Appeal

Park 2000 Development 11 (Pty) Ltd v Mouton and Others; SCA [2021] ZASCA 140

[2] Park 2000 Development 11 (Pty) Ltd (the appellant), a property development company, was the registered owner of two properties, namely Erf 541 in Riversdale and Erf 4573 in Stilbaai West.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 140

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

[2] Park 2000 Development 11 (Pty) Ltd (the appellant), a property development company, was the registered owner of two properties, namely Erf 541 in Riversdale and Erf 4573 in Stilbaai West. This appeal arose from a judgment by the Western Cape Division, Cape Town, rendered by Sher J on 23 July 2019. The court had declared invalid a resolution adopted by the appellant’s directors to place the company under business rescue, and it subsequently discharged the appointment of the fourth respondent as business rescue practitioner.

[4] The appellant was financially distressed and, in order to raise funds for its developments, it had issued debentures that were redeemable under certain conditions. Following a series of failed payments to the first respondent, Johan Mouton, who held two of these debentures, Mouton obtained judgment against the appellant for an amount of R400,000. This culminated in a writ of execution allowing the attachment of the appellant's immovable properties.

[10] Prior to the scheduled auction of the properties, a liquidation application was made against the appellant by Meiprops Twee en Twintig (Pty) Ltd (Meiprops), prompting the appellant to submit a business rescue application. Subsequently, the liquidation application was withdrawn, and Mr. Stewart was appointed as the business rescue practitioner.

[12] The court a quo found that the resolution to place the appellant in business rescue was not passed in good faith and was intended to delay the sale of the properties. The properties were ultimately sold to the second respondent and ownership was transferred.

I – Issues

[14] The main issues raised in the appeal included:

  1. Whether the alleged failure of Mr. Mouton to serve and join the appellant's creditors in the main application was fatal;
  2. Whether the resolution to place the appellant under business rescue complied with the requirements of section 128 of the Companies Act 71 of 2008;
  3. Whether the setting aside of the resolution was just and equitable as contemplated under section 130(5) of the Companies Act;
  4. Whether the court a quo erred in its treatment of the timing of the business rescue resolution in relation to the liquidation proceedings.

[15] Additionally, the issue arose regarding the mootness of the appeal, as the properties had already been sold and the appellant was no longer under business rescue, which negated any practical effect of the appeal.

R – Rules

[22] The governing provision in this case is section 16(2)(a)(i) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which allows for the dismissal of appeals where the outcome would have no practical effect. Furthermore, the court relied on established legal principles regarding business rescue as stipulated in the Companies Act 71 of 2008, especially sections 128 and 130. The judgment also made reference to the principle from Legal Aid South Africa v Magidiwana and Others which emphasized that courts should refrain from deciding issues of academic interest.

A – Application

[21] The court applied the principle from section 16(2)(a)(i) by determining the practical implications of restoring the appellant to its former business rescue status. It concluded that restoring the appellant to business rescue would have no practical effect as the two properties, which were its only significant assets, had already been sold and transferred to an independent purchaser. The deliberation surrounding the validity of the business rescue resolution and the liquidation proceedings was rendered moot since there was no longer any live controversy.

[19] The appellant argued its case based on the premise that if the appeal were successful and its business rescue status restored, the property transfers could be annulled. However, the court found this argument flawed and distinguished the case from Knox N.O. v Mofokeng, noting the differing legal contexts involving rescission versus business rescue.

C – Conclusion

[24] The appeal was dismissed with costs, including those incurred through the instruction of two counsel, as stated in paragraph [1]. In essence, the court's rationale was that to entertain the appeal would serve no practical purpose given that the significant issues had become moot. This ruling underscores the necessity for parties to consider the practical outcomes of appeals, particularly where the core subject matter may have already been resolved by other proceedings.

This case serves as a compelling reminder of the court's intent to avoid academic disputes, emphasizing the importance of tangible outcomes in legal proceedings. It clarifies the boundaries of business rescue laws within the context of property sales and the restoration of companies to previous statuses.
