Supreme Court of Appeal

Advertising Regulatory Board NPC and Others v Bliss Brands (Pty) Ltd [2022] ZASCA 51

The Advertising Regulatory Board NPC (ARB), first appellant, is a non-profit organization functioning as a self-regulatory body in the South African advertising industry.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 51

FIRAC Analysis

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–3])

The Advertising Regulatory Board NPC (ARB), first appellant, is a non-profit organization functioning as a self-regulatory body in the South African advertising industry. Its members must comply with the Code of Advertising Practice, which aims to protect consumers and ensure professionalism in advertising ([para 1]). The second and third appellants, Colgate-Palmolive (Pty) Ltd and Colgate-Palmolive Company, along with the respondent, Bliss Brands (Pty) Ltd, are competitors in the toiletries sector.

In December 2019, Colgate lodged a complaint against Bliss Brands, alleging that its Securex soap packaging breached the Code by imitating Colgate’s Protex soap packaging architecturally. Although Bliss Brands was not a member of the ARB, it did not object to the ARB's jurisdiction and participated fully in the hearings, even reaching the ARB’s Final Appeal Committee (FAC) ([paras 2–3]). The FAC ultimately sided with Colgate, compelling Bliss Brands to cease distributing the offending packaging. Following the FAC's decision, Bliss Brands sought judicial review from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which, upon questioning the constitutionality of the ARB’s powers, issued declarations that undermined the ARB’s authority ([para 4]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 3–9])

Central to the appeal was whether the high court was correct in declaring parts of the ARB's Memorandum of Incorporation (MOI) unconstitutional, thereby stripping it of its powers to adjudicate complaints involving non-members. The high court had also questioned the legality of the ARB's authority over non-member complaints and whether this authority represented a usurpation of judicial function ([para 3]).

Furthermore, the court raised concerns about the fairness of procedures employed by the ARB, particularly regarding its ability to influence advertising through mechanisms such as ad alerts, which the high court found could restrict commercial activity, invoking constitutional principles surrounding access to courts and the right to trade ([paras 50–58]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 15–24], [25–34])

The court primarily referenced PAJA (Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000), which allows entities other than state organs to perform public functions without statutory authority, provided these are based upon reasonable empowering provisions ([para 16]). The MOI and Code constitute these provisions, giving the ARB the authority to consider advertisements from non-members under various conditions, even where such advertisements concern its own members ([paras 18–19]).

The court highlighted Herbex (Pty) Ltd v Advertising Standards Authority as a crucial precedent, establishing that private bodies could exercise public powers and supervise advertising irrespective of membership status, reflecting self-regulatory practices in various sectors ([para 25-29]). The high court's ruling in this case had incorrectly interpreted these principles, thus prompting a review in the current appeal.

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 10–54])

In adjudicating the case, the Supreme Court of Appeal criticized the high court for overstepping by raising constitutional issues mero motu, which was deemed inappropriate given the scope of the pleadings ([paras 10–11]). The appellate court maintained that Bliss Brands’ voluntary participation in the ARB process indicated its acceptance of the ARB's jurisdiction, thus undermining any jurisdictional challenge raised subsequently ([para 12]).

The SCA rejected the notion that the ARB's exercise of authority over non-member advertisements was unconstitutional. Instead, it argued that the ARB’s powers stem from legitimate authorizations in its MOI, allowing it to adjudicate complaints benefiting its members ([paras 17–20]). The argument asserting that the ARB’s role infringed on judicial function was deemed unfounded, distinguishing the ARB's adjudications from those of courts and reaffirming its function as a self-regulatory body ([paras 58–59]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 59])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal, overturning the high court's orders and reinstating the ARB's capacity to adjudicate complaints, including those involving non-members. The court ordered the high court's relief sought by Bliss Brands dismissed with costs and remitted some issues for further determination ([para 59]).

This ruling clarifies the legal capacity of the ARB and similar private regulatory bodies to exercise public functions in the advertising sector, reinforcing the importance of self-regulation within the industry. By confirming that voluntary submission to a regulatory body's jurisdiction precludes subsequent challenges to that jurisdiction, the court has addressed significant aspects of administrative justice and authority within commercial contexts.

In doing so, the decision not only bolsters the operational framework for advertising practices in South Africa but also enhances the understanding of how constitutional principles apply to non-state actors engaged in public regulatory functions.
