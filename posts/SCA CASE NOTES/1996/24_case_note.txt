# FIRAC Case Note for National Media Limited v Jooste

**Case Reference:**  
National Media Limited v Jooste (1996) 335/94 (Supreme Court of South Africa, Appellate Division)

## F – Facts

The facts of this case revolve around the publication of private details concerning the respondent, Anna Jooste, by the appellants, National Media Limited and Neil Hamman. Jooste, an unmarried postgraduate student, became the center of media attention in September 1988 after it was reported that she had given birth to a child, purportedly fathered by the well-known rugby player Naas Botha. Following the initial press coverage of her relationship with Botha, which included some personal details and a photograph, Jooste reluctantly began to cooperate with the media due to escalating press pressure ([paras 5-6]).

By June 1991, aware of Botha’s impending marriage and dissatisfied with a maintenance agreement, Jooste contacted a journalist from Huisgenoot, Miss Gomes, offering to sell her "story" regarding her relationship with Botha, contingent on specific terms that would allow her to maintain some control over the narrative ([paras 7-8]). They reached an agreement whereby Jooste would receive R5,000 for an exclusive interview, with publication dependent on her approval of the article's content and publication date ([para 8]).

Despite Jooste editing and approving portions of the article, she later withdrew her consent to publish due to threats of litigation from Botha and concerns about sub judice implications ([paras 10-11]). Nevertheless, the article was published on June 27, 1991, without her final approval or consent, leading Jooste to sue the appellants for damages claiming invasion of privacy ([paras 10-12]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issue presented to the court was whether the actions of the appellants in publishing Jooste's story constituted a breach of her right to privacy. Specifically, the court examined:

1. **Whether Jooste had a legitimate expectation of privacy concerning the details published.**
2. **If so, whether her prior consent to publish, based on their agreement, was valid or had been effectively rescinded.**
3. **Whether the publication infringed her privacy rights even considering her initial willingness to disclose information ([para 12-15]).**

## R – Rules

The court relied on several legal principles in assessing the case:

1. **Right to Privacy:** Invasion of privacy claims are evaluated based on the ‘reasonable expectation of privacy’ standard, derived from principles articulated in earlier cases such as *Mr and Mrs "X" v Rhodesia Printing and Publishing Co Ltd* [para 12].

2. **Consent as a Defence:** The concept of consent regarding the publication of personal facts is crucial, as established in Neethling, Potgieter, and Visser’s texts on personal rights ([paras 14-15]). Consent must be informed and in accordance with the terms of any agreement made.

3. **Objective Assessment of Privacy:** The determination of whether an invasion of privacy occurred is considered through an objective standard, assessing public interest against the individual's privacy rights ([para 12]).

4. **Breach of Contract Relevance:** The breach of the terms of the consent agreement holds significance in evaluating the wrongfulness of the appellants’ actions ([paras 15-16]).

## A – Application

In its judgment, the court meticulously examined whether any private facts were improperly disclosed that warranted protection. It noted that Jooste, as the child's guardian, shared a joint interest in maintaining privacy regarding details about her child. The article was deemed to disclose intimate details of her life that had not already been made public, thereby undermining her right to privacy ([para 12]).

The appellants’ argument that Jooste's earlier cooperation with the media amounted to an implicit waiver of her privacy rights was dismissed. The court highlighted that the transaction's specific conditions — consent regarding publication content and timing — were not adhered to by the appellants. They published the article without her necessary approval, despite her withdrawal of consent ([paras 14-15]).

Additionally, the appellants argued that Jooste’s acceptance of payment post-publication implied continued consent; however, the court found that this did not absolve them of breaching the terms of their agreement. The essence of the ruling emphasized the need for compliance with the contractual terms governing the publication ([paras 17-18]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of South Africa upheld the ruling of the lower court, confirming that the appellants had violated Jooste's right to privacy by publishing her story without her consent and in breach of their contract. As a result, the appeal was dismissed with costs, reinforcing the importance of maintaining personal privacy in the face of media engagement.

This case serves as a significant precedent in South African law regarding the boundaries of media publication and individual privacy rights, highlighting the necessity of clear consent in media agreements and the protection of intimate personal details against unwarranted disclosure ([paras 18-19]).
