Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

### FIRAC Case Note: Dart Industries Incorporated and Another v Botle Buhle Brands (Pty) Ltd and Another (636/2021) [2022] ZASCA 170

---

#### F – Facts

This case arises from a dispute concerning the validity of a registered trade mark for a water bottle and a claim for passing off. The appellants, Dart Industries Incorporated and Tupperware Southern Africa (Pty) Ltd (hereafter collectively referred to as "Tupperware"), sought to interdict the first respondent, Botle Buhle Brands (Pty) Ltd ("Buhle Brands"), from infringing upon their registered mark, South African trade mark no 2015/25572 for the "ECO BOTTLE" ([1]). The registered mark, effective from 10 September 2015, is classified under class 21 and is endorsed as consisting of “a container for goods” including household containers and water bottles ([3]).

Tupperware has marketed the Eco bottle since 2011, while Buhle Brands began marketing a competing product in 2019, which Tupperware alleged infringed its trade mark ([4–5]). In defense, Buhle Brands countered by applying for the cancellation of Tupperware’s mark on various grounds under the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, particularly that the mark was incapable of distinguishing Tupperware’s goods due to its non-distinctiveness as outlined in section 10(2)(a) ([5]).

The High Court found for Buhle Brands, ordering the cancellation of Tupperware's trade mark and dismissing the infringement claim ([7]). The case was appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal, which reconsidered both the trade mark’s distinctiveness and the passing-off claim.

---

#### I – Issues

The primary legal issues addressed in this case include:

1. **Trade Mark Validity**: Whether the shape of the Tupperware Eco bottle is inherently distinctive or, if not, whether it has acquired distinctiveness such that it can serve as a valid trade mark under section 9(2) of the Trade Marks Act ([8–10]).
2. **Passing-Off**: The likelihood of confusion arising from Buhle Brands’ marketing of its water bottle as could be perceived to be associated with Tupperware’s Eco bottle, potentially constituting passing off ([36–51]).

---

#### R – Rules

The relevant legal rules and statutory provisions include:

1. **Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993**: 
   - **Section 9**: A mark must be capable of distinguishing the goods of the applicant from those of others. This entails an evaluation of inherent distinctiveness (9(1)) and acquired distinctiveness through substantial prior use (9(2)) ([6]).
   - **Section 10**: Lists marks that are unregistrable, emphasizing that non-distinctive marks may be cancelled from the register ([6]).

2. **Criteria for Passing-Off**: The law requires that three criteria be satisfied to establish passing off:
   - **Goodwill**: The claimant must show a sufficient reputation in the market ([39]).
   - **Misrepresentation**: The defendant’s representation must be such as to confuse the public into believing the goods or services are those of the claimant ([36]).
   - **Damage**: The claimant must demonstrate that they are likely to suffer harm from the misrepresentation ([39]).

---

#### A – Application

In addressing the trade mark validity, the Supreme Court of Appeal emphasized a two-pronged test for distinctiveness. It first examined whether the Eco bottle is inherently distinctive. The court found that the Eco bottle did not significantly depart from the norms of similar products within the water bottle sector. Evidence indicated the shape was not unique to Tupperware as other bottles on the market had similar hourglass shapes with indentations ([19]). The court aligned its reasoning with previous jurisprudence, including judgments from *Bergkelder Bpk v Vredendal Koöp Wynmakery* and *Beecham v Triomed*, which outlined the challenges faced by claims on the distinctiveness of shape marks ([12–18]).

Furthermore, the court examined whether the mark had acquired distinctiveness due to prior use. While Tupperware presented evidence of substantial sales and advertising of the Eco bottle, the court concluded that the promotional strategy predominantly emphasized the Tupperware brand rather than the shape of the bottle ([31]). Consequently, the court found there was no evidence that consumers associated the shape itself with Tupperware ([31–35]).

Regarding passing off, the court analyzed the likelihood of confusion between the two competing water bottles. Although they were deemed nearly identical in appearance, the court adopted a more expansive view of potential consumer encounters, recognizing that the crossover in the two companies’ direct selling models and online marketing platforms would likely contribute to confusion ([49–51]). 

The court overturned the High Court's ruling regarding the likelihood of confusion, concluding that consumers might associate Buhle Brands’ water bottle with Tupperware due to their similar presentation and marketing strategies ([52]).

---

#### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Buhle Brands' counter-application for the cancellation of Tupperware's trade mark due to its lack of distinctiveness, agreeing with the findings of the High Court ([54]). However, it reversed the dismissal of Tupperware's passing-off claim, asserting that there was a reasonable likelihood of confusion regarding the source of the two bottles due to their substantial similarities and the nature of their sales strategies ([54]).

The overall implications of this judgment highlight the difficulties associated with asserting rights over non-traditional trade mark shapes unless they establish significant market recognition. Furthermore, the court's emphasis on consumer perception underscores the necessity for traders to not only use distinctive marks but also effectively signify their geographical and source-specific identities through packaging and marketing efforts.

--- 

This case demonstrates the dynamic interaction between trade mark registration and enforcement, particularly in the context of shape marks in the consumer goods sector, as well as the intricate balance maintained within the legal framework to prevent consumer confusion.
