### Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of Richards Bay Bulk Storage (Pty) Ltd v The Minister for Public Enterprises

**F - Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 2–8]):**

In 1982, Richards Bay Bulk Storage (Pty) Ltd (the appellant) entered into a lease agreement with the South African Transport Services, later succeeded by Transnet Ltd, for a site in Richards Bay harbour for bulk liquid storage and distribution ([para 2]). Since then, the appellant utilized the facilities provided under this lease. Concurrently, Island View Storage (Pty) Limited (third respondent) sought a lease for a similar site. However, despite various attempts, their requests were declined, prompting the Competition Board to investigate whether Transnet's refusal constituted a "restrictive practice" under the Maintenance and Promotion of Competition Act 96 of 1979 ([para 3]).

The investigation culminated in Government Notice 1101 of 1991, outlining the Board's investigation parameters. By November 1992, the Minister for Public Enterprises published a notice declaring certain practices of Transnet unlawful, mandating them to allocate land for bulk storage facilities and ensure competitive parity ([para 4–7]). Subsequently, the appellant appealed to a special court against this notice and also initiated review proceedings in the Durban and Coast Local Division, asserting that the Minister acted ultra vires by failing to comply with procedural laws of the Act and issued vague directions ([para 8]).

**I - Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 9–12]):**

The primary legal issue was whether the court a quo had jurisdiction to hear the appellant's review application against the Minister's notice under section 14(1)(c) of the Competition Act. A consequent issue was whether the provisions of the Act impliedly excluded the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court to review decisions made by the special court, particularly concerning potential procedural irregularities that might have arisen during the enactment of the Minister's notice ([para 12]).

**R - Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 13–20]):**

The court relied on the statutory framework laid out in the Maintenance and Promotion of Competition Act, specifically sections 10, 12, 14, and 15, outlining the processes and powers conferred to the Competition Board and the Minister. Section 15(10) through (13) noted that while appeals could be lodged with the Minister, such decisions from the special court would not be subject to review or further appeal by any other court ([paras 10, 11, 16]).

The court cited multiple precedents comparing the powers of the special court to the Supreme Court's review jurisdiction, emphasizing that the legislature must clearly indicate any intention to exclude such powers of review. Notably, it referred to Welkom Village Management Board v Leteno [1958], which establishes that the exclusion of jurisdiction must be explicit or flow from necessary implication ([para 12]).

**A - Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 21–27]):**

The court evaluated whether the powers bestowed upon the special court were indeed exclusive, reasoning that since the Act did not explicitly confer review powers upon the special court, there was no basis for inferring an intent to oust the review jurisdiction of the Supreme Court. The judgment of Broome DJP in the court a quo was examined thoroughly, noting that while it contended the special court acted similarly to specialized courts with appeals, the fundamental distinctions between an appeal and a review were overlooked.

The court asserted that appeals involve a re-examination of a matter's merits, while reviews focus on procedural legality. It emphasized the absence of any explicit prohibitions regarding judicial review in the Act concerning the measures taken under section 14(1)(c), suggesting that the right to seek review through the Supreme Court remains unaffected despite the special court's jurisdiction over appeals ([paras 21–27]).

**C - Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 28–28]):**

Ultimately, the Supreme Court held that the appeal was valid on the grounds that the court a quo incorrectly concluded that it lacked jurisdiction to hear the review application. The court determined that the Minister's notice was subject to possible review, affirming the Supreme Court's jurisdiction in such matters ([para 28]). The judgment underscores the importance of maintaining avenues for review in complex regulatory matters, ensuring that procedural rights are preserved even when specialized tribunals are established to adjudicate specific areas of law. The case reinforces the necessity for legislation to clearly articulate the bounds of jurisdiction to avoid ambiguities concerning the review powers of established courts ([para 28]).

The Supreme Court's ruling effectively reinstates the appellant's right to pursue a review application in the Supreme Court, contributing to a coherent understanding of statutory interpretation and the balance between specialized and general judicial powers in South Africa.
