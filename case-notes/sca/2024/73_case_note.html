Case Note: Molosi and Others v King Phahlo Royal Family and Others (1005/2022) [2024] ZASCA 73

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 73 #### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Proce

Case Note: Molosi and Others v King Phahlo Royal Family and Others (1005/2022) [2024] ZASCA 73

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([1]–[4], [6]–[27])

The appeal in Molosi and Others v King Phahlo Royal Family and Others addresses the lineage and succession of kingship within the traditional community of AmaMpondomise. The appellants include Simpiwe Sydwell Molosi, Ntombenkonzo Maseti, and the Dosini Royal Family, while the respondents consist of the King Phahlo Royal Family, Luzuko Matiwane, the President of South Africa, the Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs, and the Premier of the Eastern Cape Province.

The dispute traces back to the colonial era, specifically to the 1300s, when King Ngcwina disinherited his son Dosini in favor of Cira, fuelling claims from both the Dosini and Phahlo families regarding royal legitimacy ([6]–[7]). In modern times, the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003 (the Act) governs the nomination of kings and queens in South Africa, demanding that the royal family identify a legitimate heir. After historical recognition was granted by the Brooks judgment, which reinstated the kingship of AmaMpondomise, both families nominated candidates—Dosini family nominated Maseti, whereas the Phahlo family nominated Matiwane ([1]–[5]).

Initially, the President refused to recognize either nomination due to internal disputes and referred the matter back to the royal families for resolution, as outlined in the Act ([10]–[11]). Legal action ensued, leading the High Court to grant declaratory and interdictory relief in favor of the Phahlo family, asserting their right to nominate the king. Following this, the High Court ruled to recognize Mr. Matiwane as king ([16]–[18]).

Subsequent events revealed that Mr. Matiwane had already been recognized by the President prior to the appeal, a crucial fact not disclosed to the courts ([18]–[19]). Consequently, the primary issue of the legitimacy of the royal succession became moot, as the appeal was rendered unnecessary.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([1]–[22], [24]–[27])

The main legal issue was the legitimacy of the claims to kingship within the AmaMpondomise community, specifically the determination of which family constituted the authoritative royal family to identify an heir under section 9 of the Act ([1]–[4], [10]). Additionally, the appeal raised jurisdictional questions regarding the adherence to proper dispute resolution mechanisms outlined in section 21 of the Act, as well as procedural missteps concerning the non-disclosure of critical facts leading to mootness ([15]–[27]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([5]–[27])

The court primarily referred to the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003, particularly its provisions for the nomination and recognition of kings and queens ([5]–[6]). Significant reliance was placed on the Brooks judgment, which restored the kingship of AmaMpondomise, further establishing the historical basis for the royal family's claims ([4]–[12]). The Supreme Court of Appeal also invoked the rules governing mootness as per section 16(2)(a)(i) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, asserting that an appeal may be dismissed if it has no practical effect ([21]–[22]).

Additionally, precedents concerning the hierarchy and legitimacy of royal families under customary law were instrumental in shaping the statutory interpretation of the Act, significantly the need for community consensus and internal resolution of disputes ([22]–[24]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([18]–[27])

In analyzing the facts, the court determined that the pending recognition of Mr. Matiwane effectively rendered the dispute moot since any further review of the High Court's declarations would have no practical implications. The court considered the failure of both families to disclose the President's recognition during prior proceedings as significant misconduct that undermined judicial efficiency ([19]–[20]).

The court underscored the procedural expectations of mutual transparency and good faith in disclosing critical developments. This oversight not only complicated the appellate process but also hindered the court’s ability to adjudicate effectively ([20]–[21]).

Despite the appellants’ arguments that the appeal should proceed due to ongoing review processes regarding the President’s recognition, the court held that unless the recognition itself was set aside, it remained in effect and valid ([26]–[27]). The court emphasized that the underlying conflict revolved more around factual historical claims than statutory interpretation, suggesting that the primary legal issues had already been resolved through the President's recognition of Mr. Matiwane.

Ultimately, the court concluded that, since the appeal did not possess practical significance, no further legal action was necessary ([22]–[27]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([28])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming that it was rendered moot due to the President’s prior recognition of Mr. Matiwane as king. The court criticized the legal representatives for their failure to disclose relevant developments during the appeal process, marking such conduct as deeply unsatisfactory and potentially harmful to the integrity of judicial proceedings ([27]).

This ruling emphasizes the importance of transparency and good faith in legal representations, particularly in cases involving significant traditional and customary issues. Furthermore, it asserts judicial efficiency by applying the mootness doctrine, reinforcing that resolved matters should not occupy valuable judicial resources once practical resolutions have been achieved.

The case sets a precedent regarding the importance of timely and truthful disclosure in matrimonial matters and contributes to the ongoing discourse regarding traditional governance frameworks in South Africa. The decision underscores the primacy of legitimate historical claims over protracted disputes among rival factions within traditional communities, reflecting an effort to stabilize traditional leadership amid evolving statutory frameworks.
