Source Folder: 2022

## Case Note: Govan Mbeki Local Municipality and Another v Glencore Operations South Africa (Pty) Ltd and Others

**Neutral Citation:** Govan Mbeki Local Municipality and Another v Glencore Operations South Africa (Pty) Ltd and Others (334/2021 and 338/2021) [2022] ZASCA 93 (17 June 2022)  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Judgment Delivered By:** Salie-Hlophe AJA (with concurrence from Maya P, Dambuza, Plasket, and Musi AJJA)  
**Date of Hearing:** 6 May 2022  
**Date of Judgment:** 17 June 2022  

### FIRAC Method Analysis

### F – Facts
The case comprises two appeals from the High Court of Mpumalanga, concerning the validity of by-laws enacted by the Govan Mbeki Local Municipality and the Emalahleni Local Municipality. The High Court declared sections 76 of the Govan Mbeki Spatial Planning and Land Use Management By-law ([para 1]) and section 86 of the Emalahleni Municipal By-law on Spatial Planning and Land Use Management ([para 1]) unconstitutional. These provisions imposed significant restraints on the registration of property transfers without prior certification from the respective municipalities that all spatial planning and land-use management conditions had been met ([para 3–5]). 

The respondents, comprising several companies, sought to invalidate these provisions on grounds that they infringe upon property owners' constitutional rights under section 25 of the South African Constitution, by imposing unreasonable barriers to property registration. The respondents further contended that the by-laws fell outside the municipalities' legislative competencies as defined by the Constitution ([para 4]).

### I – Issues
The appeals raised several core issues surrounding the constitutionality and statutory validity of the by-laws enacted by the municipalities. Key issues included:

1. Whether the impugned by-laws fell within the legislative competence of the municipalities as per section 156 of the Constitution ([para 6–7]).
2. Whether they exceeded the functional area of 'municipal planning' by regulating property transfer ([para 6–7]).
3. Whether the provisions imposed arbitrary deprivation of property contrary to section 25(1) of the Constitution ([para 7]).

### R – Rules
The decision was ultimately anchored in constitutional provisions relevant to local government operations, specifically:

1. **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa 1996**: Sections 25(1) (right to property), 156 (municipal competencies), and 118 (debt obligations related to property) ([para 6, 18]).
   
2. **Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000**: This Act delineates the jurisdiction and powers of local governments, emphasizing that by-laws must adhere to the framework established by national legislation ([para 6]).

3. **Spatial Planning and Land Use Management Act 16 of 2013 (SPLUMA)**: This provides a framework for spatial planning that local municipalities must adhere to, emphasizing compliance with national legislation ([para 23, 26]).

4. **Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937**: Governs property registration and outlines the powers of the registrar, reinforcing that registration authority is not within municipal jurisdiction ([para 39]).

### A – Application
The court meticulously analyzed whether the by-laws were constitutionally valid. It found that:

1. The provisions in question, particularly those contingent upon municipal certification before property registration, constituted an unlawful restriction on property transfers ([para 34]).
2. The court noted that such regulations stray beyond the designated municipal planning authority, as they impose certification requirements resembling those governed by the Deeds Registries Act, a domain reserved for national legislation ([para 39–40]).
3. The majority concluded that the imposition of a certificate requirement derived from the by-laws created an embargo that did not align with municipal powers as intended under the SPLUMA, nor did it serve a legitimate municipal planning objective ([para 36–40]).
4. The provisions were also found to be in conflict with section 118 of the Systems Act, leading to arbitrary deprivations of property rights as articulated in section 25 of the Constitution ([para 41]).

### C – Conclusion
The appeals by the municipalities were dismissed, affirming the High Court's ruling that the impugned by-laws were unconstitutional and invalid. The cross-appeal against the High Court's suspension of the declaration of invalidity for six months was upheld, reinforcing the necessity for adherence to the clear statutory framework governing municipal powers and property rights. The court's order mandated the immediate cessation of enforcement concerning the invalid by-laws, thereby underscoring the importance of cooperative governance and adherence to constitutional principles ([paras 43–44]).

**Implications**: This decision clarifies the boundaries of legislative power within the municipal context, particularly concerning property rights. It serves as a significant reminder that local government operations must respect and operate strictly within the confines of national legislative authority, particularly on matters integral to property registration and transfer. The ruling emphasizes the vital importance of cooperative governance in the South African legal landscape.
