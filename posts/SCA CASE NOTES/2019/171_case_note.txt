Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

### Case Note: Samuel Democracy Zwane N.O. v Andre Pretorius (399/2018) [2019] ZASCA 171

#### F – Facts
The matter concerns an application for condonation regarding the late filing of an appeal by the first applicant, Samuel Democracy Zwane N.O., acting as the trustee of the Democracy Zwane Family Trust, and the second applicant, Samuel Democracy Zwane ([1]). The trust purchased a farm in Ermelo from Mr. Pretorius, the executor of the estate of Ms. Lesley Megan Pretorius, in a series of agreements beginning on 23 October 2014 (the first sale agreement) and culminating in the third sale agreement on 6 February 2015, which was successfully completed on 4 May 2015 after the trust secured financing ([2]–[7]).

Following the first sale agreement's lapse, the respondent, Mr. Andre Pretorius, entered an oral agreement with Mr. Pretorius, wherein the respondent would maintain the farm's condition while awaiting the transfer ([4]). After the trust acquired ownership, disputes arose concerning the respondent's claim to ownership of 692 bales of grass produced under this arrangement, leading to a trial in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, which favored the respondent ([8]–[9]).

The applicants filed for leave to appeal the High Court's judgment, which was subsequently referred for oral argument after an initial filing failure ([9]). The crux of the case lies in the applicants' failed attempt to condone their late appeal, attributed to alleged negligence by their attorneys ([11]).

#### I – Issues
The principal legal question arose whether the court should grant the applicants’ request for condonation for the delayed filing of their appeal ([10]). Secondary issues included evaluating the prospects of success of the appeal and whether the applicants demonstrated sufficient justification for the delays incurred ([11], [24]).

#### R – Rules
The court applied relevant legal principles regarding condonation as outlined in prior cases, specifically noting that a party seeking condonation must provide sufficient explanation for any delays and show reasonable prospects of success ([14], [15]). The principles established in *Saloojee v Minister of Community Development* and *Darries v Sheriff, Magistrate's Court* were particularly relevant, emphasizing the necessity of adequate diligence on the part of litigants and their counsel ([13], [14]). Additionally, the court cited that a flagrant breach of procedural rules without acceptable explanation could result in the denial of condonation irrespective of prospects ([16]).

#### A – Application
In assessing the applicants’ explanation for their delay, the court identified a lack of detail and accountability in the second applicant's account. Delays stretching from the refusal of leave to appeal by the High Court until their representation in the Supreme Court were inadequately justified ([13], [15]). 

While the second applicant attributed delays to negligence by his attorneys, the court stressed that an appellant cannot escape the consequences of their attorney's actions, referencing the need for proactive measures once a failure to comply with court rules became apparent ([13], [14]). The court further noted that the statement of prospects of success was indispensable; however, it found that the applicants failed to demonstrate a credible basis for their appeal’s merits ([23], [24]).

The court analyzed the merits of the underlying claim regarding the ownership of the grass bales, concluding that the respondent had adequately established ownership based on the oral agreement, irrespective of the applicants' arguments regarding procedural claims of bona fide possession ([17]–[22]). The court determined that the applicants had failed to substantiate their assertions or demonstrate how the alleged procedural errors would have influenced the trial's outcome ([23]).

#### C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court dismissed the application for condonation with costs and struck the appeal from the roll, indicating that both the failure to file properly and the absence of compelling prospects for success weighed significantly against the applicants' case ([25]). The ruling underscores the importance of procedural adherence in legal proceedings and reflects judicial reluctance to grant indulgences for failure to act diligently, thereby reinforcing the integrity and efficiency of judicial processes.

This judgment serves as a potent reminder of the need for careful and prompt legal action by litigants to adhere to procedural timelines, as failures in this regard can lead to substantial disadvantages in the pursuit of justice.
