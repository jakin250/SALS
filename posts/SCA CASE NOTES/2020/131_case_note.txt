Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note: Thipe & Another v City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality & Others (Case no 254/2019) [2020] ZASCA 131

## F – Facts

The appellants, Anna Mmakodi Thipe and 286 Denneboom Traders, are informal traders who operated from the Denneboom Train Station in Mamelodi, Pretoria, under lease agreements with the City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality ([1]). The second respondent, Isibonelo Property Services (Pty) Ltd, a property development company, was involved in constructing a shopping mall on the property ([1]). 

On February 3, 2017, the Gauteng Division of the High Court issued a rule nisi, prohibiting the demolition of the appellants' structures pending relocation ([4]). Subsequent to the rule nisi, a court order was established on February 10, 2017, which mandated that the City of Tshwane and Isibonelo refrain from demolishing the traders' structures or interfering with their trade ([5]). 

On September 22, 2017, the appellants’ structures were demolished, allegedly authorized by the City of Tshwane and the contractors from Isibonelo ([6]). Following this demolition, the appellants claimed damages due to the destruction of their property and subsequent looting of their trading stock ([7]). The matter escalated when the appellants sought to hold the respondents in contempt of the February 10 order, filing contempt application and requesting joinder of the Municipal Manager, the Mayor, and Isibonelo's CEO ([8]).

The respondents disputed the allegations of contempt, claiming no knowledge or involvement in the demolition ([10], [14]). The high court eventually upheld the respondents' exception, leading to the dismissal of the appellants' application ([22]) due to lack of clear averments supporting the contempt claim against the third to eighth respondents ([22]).

## I – Issues

The principal issue addressed in this case is whether the high court properly upheld the exception regarding the appellants' contempt application against the Municipal Manager, the Mayor, and the CEO of Isibonelo. Specifically:

1. Did the appellants adequately allege the necessary elements of contempt against the third to eighth respondents?
2. Should the high court have granted the appellants leave to amend their pleadings rather than outright dismissing their application?
3. What are the implications of the factual disputes regarding the destruction of the appellants' trading facilities?

## R – Rules

In addressing the appeal, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) considered several rules and principles, notably:

1. **Contempt of Court:** As established in legal precedent, for a party to be held in contempt of court, there must be proof of willful and mala fide non-compliance with a court order ([22]). This includes affirming that the individual cited in contempt acted with knowledge of the order and deliberately defied its terms.
   
2. **Right to Amend Pleadings:** The general principle laid out in *Ocean Echo Properties 327 CC v Old Mutual Life Assurance Company (South Africa) Limited* asserts that when an exception is upheld, the appropriate remedy typically includes allowing the unsuccessful party the opportunity to amend their pleadings, unless good reason is provided against such amendment ([23]).

3. **Dispute Resolution:** The SCA emphasized the necessity of addressing core factual disputes before delving into broader legal questions ([25]).

## A – Application

The SCA applied these rules methodically, concluding that the high court's judgment was flawed in two significant aspects. First, it held that the high court's reasoning in dismissing the appellants' application neglected to afford them the right to amend their pleadings when the exception was upheld ([23]). The court pointed out that there was no justifiable reason to preclude the appellants from amending their contingent claims for contempt. 

Secondly, the SCA found that the factual disputes surrounding whether the respondents were responsible for the demolition and whether the appellants were unlawfully deprived of their trading facilities remained central to the overarching claims of contempt. These facts had not been resolved, and thus, the respondents' objections were premature and inappropriate at that procedural stage ([25]). Instead of addressing whether contempt could theoretically lie in any of their capacities, the SCA determined that a factual determination was the first step necessary.

## C – Conclusion

The SCA ultimately ruled in favor of the appellants, setting aside the high court's order that upheld the exception and dismissed the claims against the third to eighth respondents ([28]). It ordered the case back to the high court for further proceedings while allowing the appellants the opportunity to amend their application. The implications of this decision reaffirm the importance of a party's right to amend its pleadings when confronted with exceptions, and the necessity of resolving factual disputes in legal proceedings prior to legal determinations being made.

In conclusion, this ruling serves not only as a procedural victory for the appellants but also as a significant reiteration of the standards governing contempt proceedings, emphasizing the need for clear allegations and substantiated claims against public officials in their personal capacities when seeking to enforce court orders. The case illustrates procedural diligence in litigation involving public interests, especially in the context of informal trading and municipal governance.
