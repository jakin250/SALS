Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1997

### FIRAC Case Note: **Certification of the Amended Text of the Constitution of the Western Cape, 1997** (CCT 29/97)

**F – Facts**  
The case concerns the certification of the Constitution of the Western Cape, passed on 21 February 1997 by the province’s legislature. On 2 September 1997, the Constitutional Court of South Africa issued an order refusing to certify the original text of this Constitution in its entirety based on non-compliance with section 143 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996 ([1]). Following this refusal, an amended version of the constitutional text was passed by a two-thirds majority in the Western Cape legislature on 11 September 1997 ([2]). This amended text was then submitted to the Constitutional Court for certification in line with section 144 of the national Constitution ([2]).

The Court provided an opportunity for the government and relevant political parties to present objections regarding the amended text, yet no objections were raised ([3]). The counsel for the Speaker of the legislature contended that the new text satisfied the requirements of section 143, aligning with the provisions highlighted in the earlier judgment ([4]). The amendments primarily addressed the specific issues identified in the initial certification refusal, and included an alteration to the composition of the provincial cabinet, which must comply with constitutional values laid out in section 1 and Chapter 3, albeit not with the constitutional framework as a whole except as specified ([5]).

After thorough scrutiny, the court unanimously concluded that the amended text complied fully with the necessary constitutional provisions ([6]).

---

**I – Issues**  
The central legal question revolved around whether the amended constitutional text of the Western Cape complied with the stipulations of section 143 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa ([4], [6]). This section mandates that a provincial constitution or amendment must not conflict with the national Constitution and must adhere to the democratic values therein. Specifically, the court examined whether all provisions within this amended text, including those addressing the provincial cabinet's composition, conformed to the constitutional framework, ensuring that none fell outside the competencies allocated to provincial authorities ([5]).

---

**R – Rules**  
The Court relied on several key legal provisions:

1. **Section 143** of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, which outlines the requirements for provincial constitutions and amendments, focusing on compliance with the national Constitution and adherence to fundamental values ([5]).
   
2. **Section 142** requires that any amendments to a provincial constitution must be approved by at least two-thirds of the provincial legislature ([2]).

3. Additionally, the Court took into account interpretations of these sections as provided in previous case law and constitutional principles, such as the notion that any provincial legislative framework must not confer powers beyond those established by the national Constitution ([5]).

---

**A – Application**  
The Court engaged in a rigorous examination of the amended provisions against the requirements subsumed under section 143. The unanimous decision stemmed from the conclusion that the changes effectively rectified the issues raised in the initial judgment concerning non-compliance ([6]). 

The examination focused on the newly formulated provisions, ensuring they adhered not only to the technical requirements of legislative approval but also conformed to the broader constitutional values, such as respect for democracy, accountability, and public participation that are enshrined in section 1 and Chapter 3 ([5]). The Court highlighted that while the composition of the provincial cabinet had to align with these values, flexibility in structure indicated that complete consistency with all provisions of the national Constitution was not strictly required for this specific aspect ([5]).

The absence of any objections from the political parties and the government further reinforced the assumption of compliance, signaling a consensus regarding the text's alignment with the necessary constitutional parameters ([3]). This facilitated a smoother path to certification, underscoring the collaborative rather than adversarial spirit anticipated within the provincial governance framework.

In concluding its analysis, the Court affirmed that thorough scrutiny had established full compliance, enabling the certification process to progress, essentially validating the legislature's actions and the amended text ([6]).

---

**C – Conclusion**  
The Constitutional Court of South Africa certified the amended constitutional text of the Western Cape, concluding that all provisions complied with section 143 of the national Constitution ([6]). This judgement not only affirmed the legality of the new text but also reinforced the importance of provincial autonomy within the bounds of national constitutional mandates. The ruling stressed the balance of powers within the South African constitutional framework, emphasising that while provincial legislatures have the capacity to formulate their own constitutions, such documents must invariably reflect adherence to foundational national values.

This case underscores the critical intersection between provincial legislative powers and national constitutional integrity, enabling ongoing discussions regarding the nature of federalism within South Africa. The Court's ruling has implications for future legislative efforts in other provinces, establishing a precedent for provincial legislative autonomy constrained by the overarching principles of the national Constitution, thus marking a significant step in the evolution of South African constitutional law.
