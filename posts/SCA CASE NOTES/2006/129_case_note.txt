# Case Note: REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA – LEGAL AID BOARD v JORDAAN [2006] SCA 157 (RSA)

### FIRAC Method

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–3])

This case involves the Legal Aid Board, the appellant (hereinafter referred to as "the applicant"), and Gertruida Cecilia Jordaan, the respondent (hereinafter referred to as "the employee"). The employee, who was found guilty of two charges of misconduct by a disciplinary tribunal appointed by the Legal Aid Board, initiated review proceedings in the Pretoria High Court. She sought to have the tribunal's decision overturned ([1]). 

During the review proceedings, the employee filed for an interim interdict to prevent the applicant and the chairperson of the disciplinary tribunal from continuing with the hearing until her review application was completed. The High Court granted this order, leading to the applicant's appeal, primarily questioning the High Court's jurisdiction to entertain the interim relief sought ([2]). The applicant subsequently filed an application for condonation for failing to lodge the appeal record by the set deadline ([3]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 2–4])

The central issue raised in this case is whether the High Court possesses the jurisdiction to grant interim relief pending a review of an administrative decision made by a disciplinary tribunal. The appeal was confined specifically to this jurisdictional question, as posited by the learned judge in the court a quo ([2]). The counter-argument presented by the employee focused on the merits of the appeal, asserting that the applicant had no credible basis for success and thus the application for condonation should be dismissed ([4]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 5–9])

The court referenced established legal principles governing jurisdiction, particularly the High Court's power to grant interim relief, which is contingent upon its ability to maintain or restore the status quo pending a determination of the primary rights of the litigants. This principle was reiterated in *National Gambling Board v Premier, KwaZulu-Natal* 2002 (2) SA 715 (CC) ([5]). 

Furthermore, the applicant contended that its jurisdiction was exclusively governed by sections 157(1) and 158(1)(a)(ii) and (iii) of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995. Section 157(1) delineates the Labour Court's exclusive jurisdiction in matters outlined within the Act, whereas section 158(1)(a) empowers the Labour Court to grant various forms of interim relief ([7]). 

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 6–10])

The court critically analyzed whether the High Court's jurisdiction to grant interim relief was ousted by the Labour Relations Act. It underscored that while the powers conferred by section 158(1)(a) allow the Labour Court to grant interim orders, these provisions do not usurp the High Court's jurisdiction to provide interim relief in cases involving administrative actions under relevant statutes ([8]). 

The court asserted that if the High Court is empowered to determine whether the disciplinary inquiry constituted 'administrative action' as per the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000, it follows that Parliament did not intend for the Labour Court to have exclusive jurisdiction over interim relief related to such determinations ([9]). The court concluded that the applicant's arguments failed to demonstrate a legitimate claim of exclusive jurisdiction, thus leading to the rejection of the applicant's propositions and the dismissal of the condonation application for lack of merit ([10]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 10–11])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the applicant had no prospects of success regarding the appeal's merits, thereby dismissing the application for condonation and reinstatement of the appeal with costs. The case underscores the jurisdictional boundaries between the High Court and Labour Court, affirming the High Court's authority to grant interim relief in instances where a litigation party contests an administrative action, emphasizing the importance of preserving judicial oversight until final determinations can be made ([10]).

This case not only elucidates the jurisdictional interplay between courts but also highlights the imperative of interim relief mechanisms in protecting litigants' rights during potentially protracted administrative review processes. The decision can be a cornerstone for future cases where similar jurisdictional questions arise, reinforcing the role of High Courts in safeguarding legal rights amidst administrative proceedings.
