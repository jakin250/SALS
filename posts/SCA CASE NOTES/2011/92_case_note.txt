Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

### FIRAC Case Note: Eedenprop (Pty) Ltd v Kouga Municipality (541/10) [2011] ZASCA 92

#### F – Facts
The case arose from an appeal concerning a contractual agreement made on 24 October 2000 between Eedenprop (Pty) Ltd (the appellant) and the Jeffreys Bay Transitional Local Council, which was the predecessor of the Kouga Municipality (the respondent) ([1]). The appellant owned a portion of land measuring approximately 34.92 hectares in the Eastern Cape, intending to develop a retirement village on 16.48 hectares of this property. Their application for re-zoning and subdivision, under the aegis of the Land Use Planning Ordinance 15 of 1985 (LUPO), was approved on 14 September 2000 with certain conditions ([2]). The agreement stipulated that the appellant was to develop internal services for the retirement village, which would then be transferred to a homeowners association, and the appellant agreed to bear the costs associated with these services. The council agreed to reimburse the appellant 60% of the municipal rates generated from the development, capped at 12.5% of the total cost of the infrastructure ([4]).

Following the completion of the construction, the appellant requested payment based on this agreement. While the respondent initially complied with payments, they ceased in January 2009, questioning the agreement’s enforceability ([5], [6]). The respondent argued non-compliance with the conditions of subdivision and rezoning, the legality of the reimbursement clause, and violations of sections 172 and 173 of the Municipal Ordinance 20 of 1974 ([7]). The appellant's application was dismissed by the Eastern Cape High Court ([6]).

#### I – Issues
The central legal questions presented in this case were:
1. Whether the conditions of subdivision and rezoning imposed by the former Western District Council were waived and if the appellant had complied with them ([7]).
2. Whether the charge of sharing municipal taxes implied by the reimbursement agreement was unlawful ([7]).
3. Whether there was non-compliance with sections 172 and 173 of the Municipal Ordinance 20 of 1974, rendering the agreement void ([7]).

#### R – Rules
The legal principles at play included the terms of the LUPO, specifically section 42, which allows local councils to impose conditions on subdivision and rezoning applications ([10]). The governing statutes regarding municipal financing and obligations included the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996, and various Municipal Acts, which emphasize accountability and efficient management of municipal resources ([14]–[15]). 

The relevant sections from the Municipal Ordinance stipulate the requirements for inviting tenders for municipal contracts (s 172) and the conditions under which contracts can come into force (s 173) ([16]–[19]). Case law, including *Oudekraal Estates (Pty) Ltd v City of Cape Town* and others cited, helped establish relevant precedents regarding municipal decision-making ([8]).

#### A – Application
In analyzing the respondent's contentions, the court began by addressing the claim of non-compliance with conditions set by the Western District Council. The court found that the respondent had effectively waived these conditions when signing the agreement, thus rejecting the argument that the appellant should have appealed the conditions as per the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 ([8]).

The court further elaborated on the rationale of the agreement's reimbursement clause. It clarified that the respondent’s interpretation of the agreement as sharing taxes contradicted the established premise that the rates would be utilized to benefit the municipality and facilitate the appellant's development ([14]). The ruling emphasized that the respondent retained the authority over rates and that the obligation to reimburse from these rates was contractual and in alignment with the interests of both parties ([15]).

Lastly, the court evaluated the applicability of sections 172 and 173 of the Municipal Ordinance. It concluded that the terms of the reimbursement agreement fell outside the purview of these sections as they were not contracts concerning the execution of work or supply of goods to the council. Thus, the ruling of the court a quo was found to be erroneous ([18]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appellant's appeal, deciding that the agreement with the Jeffreys Bay Transitional Local Council was indeed valid and enforceable. It ruled that the agreement's terms should compel the Kouga Municipality to pay the amounts due to the appellant, affirming that the municipality had bound itself contractually to reimburse for the infrastructure costs ([21]).

The implications of this decision are significant in affirming the sanctity of contractual agreements between private developers and municipal authorities while elucidating the parameters within which municipalities can operate concerning development agreements and the stipulations of prevailing statutes. The judgment reinforces the necessity for clarity in municipal financial engagements and underscores the need for municipalities to adhere to agreements they have entered into, thereby fostering a predictable environment for both public entities and private developers.
