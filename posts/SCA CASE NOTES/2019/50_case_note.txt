# FIRAC Case Note: N C v The State (285/2018) [2019] ZASCA 50

## F – Facts
[1] The appellant, N C, was charged in the Magistrate's Court of Cape Town with two counts of contempt of court, as outlined by sections 305(1)(q) and 395(6) of the Children’s Act 38 of 2005 (the Children’s Act). The first count concerned a contravention of a Children’s Court order between 21 November 2014 and 15 April 2015, while the second count addressed non-compliance from 16 April 2015 onwards. Following his conviction on 21 November 2014, the appellant was sentenced to one year of periodical imprisonment.

[2] Subsequent to the sentence, the magistrate referred the case to the High Court for review, indicating a procedural error in not imposing a proper periodical imprisonment sentence. The High Court modified the sentencing, ordering the appellant to serve 2,000 hours of periodical imprisonment. Dissatisfied, the appellant sought leave to appeal against both the conviction and the sentence, but this was denied at both the magistrate and High Court levels, prompting a petition for special leave to appeal.

[3] The factual backdrop reveals a custody dispute between the appellant and the complainant, arising after the birth of their child. Following a facilitator's directive on 3 November 2014, both parties agreed the child should attend M Private School. On 14 December 2012, a parenting plan was ratified by the court, formalizing both parents' roles and responsibilities.

[4] The Children's Court issued an order on 21 November 2014, mandating the appellant to cover additional school fees, clearly outlining conditions under which this order could be modified or reviewed. However, the appellant neither complied proactively nor sought to modify the order until significant delays had transpired, leading to the contempt charges.

## I – Issues
[5] The principal legal questions in this case include whether the appellant demonstrated a reasonable prospect of success in his appeal against the convictions and sentence. Specifically, this involves evaluating whether the requirements for contempt of court were met—namely, (a) existence and clarity of the order, (b) service or notification to the accused, and (c) non-compliance demonstrating wilfulness and mala fide intentions ([paras 4–6]).

## R – Rules
[6] The legal standards governing contempt of court are well-established, with essential case law set forth in *Fakie NO v CCII Systems* ([2006] ZASCA 52), which outlined the burden of proof in such instances. The key requisites for establishing contempt—proof of an unequivocal court order, proper notification, and wilful non-compliance—must be satisfied.

[7] The test for granting leave to appeal requires a determination of a reasonable prospect of success on the merits of the appeal ([para 4]). Relevant statutory provisions include section 304(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which provides guidelines for magistrates concerning the review of sentences.

## A – Application
[8] The court assessed the appellant's conduct and his interactions with the court's orders. In evaluating Count 1, the context of the appellant's legal representation was critical. Although his appeal processes had been poorly advised, the court found lack of malicious intent or wilfulness in the non-compliance due to the appellant's reliance on his attorney's guidance. Therefore, the court ruled that the requisite malice was not sufficiently proven, granting leave to appeal on this count ([paras 5–6]).

[9] In contrast, the analysis of Count 2 revealed clear evidence of wilful non-compliance. The appellant's assertions—claiming the impossibility of compliance—were undermined by his communications and actions post-judgment, indicating an unwillingness to comply with the court’s directive regarding the child's schooling. The court recognized that the appellant's preferences for his child's education did not justify his actions and determined a lack of reasonable prospects for success in appealing this conviction ([para 6]).

[10] With regards to the sentence, the court noted that since leave was granted only concerning Count 1, it was necessary to also allow an appeal on the sentence to enable the High Court to assess an appropriate penalty, considering fairness and proportionality ([para 7]).

## C – Conclusion
[11] The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal to the extent that it granted leave to appeal against the conviction on Count 1 and the sentence imposed by the Magistrate's Court. Conversely, the appeal concerning Count 2 was dismissed due to a lack of reasonable prospect of success. The ruling effectively articulates significant implications for future contempt cases, emphasizing the necessity of clear intent and adherence to court orders, as well as the importance of proper legal representation and advice ([paras 1–8]).
