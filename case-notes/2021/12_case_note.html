Constitutional Court 2021 [2021] ZACC 12 F – Facts: In the case at hand, Mr. Gao

FIRAC Case Note: Senwedi v The State [2021] ZACC 12

F – Facts:

In the case at hand, Mr. Gaolatlhe Senwedi sought leave to appeal a ruling from the High Court of South Africa, Northern Cape Division, Kimberley, concerning his life sentence and a non-parole period of 25 years imposed on him for his conviction on charges, including murder ([paras 1–3]). The background of the case reveals that on 2 April 2001, Mr. Senwedi participated in the armed robbery that resulted in the murder of Mr. Coetzee. He was sentenced on 14 May 2002, receiving a life sentence for murder, along with sentences for robbery and illegal possession of a firearm, all of which ran concurrently ([paras 2–3]).

The central contention arose from the imposition of the 25-year non-parole period, given that legislation effective at the time of sentencing required individuals sentenced to life imprisonment to be eligible for parole after serving a minimum of 20 years ([paras 7–8]). This non-parole period, fixed by the court at sentencing, would not align with the prevailing legal standards, causing the applicant to serve five additional years beyond what was applicable at the time of sentencing. This discrepancy prompted Senwedi to seek reconsideration via the Superior Courts Act, ultimately culminating in motions to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) and then to the Constitutional Court ([paras 4–5]).

I – Issues:

The primary legal questions presented for determination were:

  1. Whether the High Court's imposition of a non-parole period of 25 years was permissible given that the fixed non-parole periods described in section 276B of the Criminal Procedure Act (CPA) were enacted only after the offence’s commission and sentencing ([paras 6–7]).
  2. Whether the refusal of the SCA to entertain Senwedi's application for reconsideration under section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act infringed his constitutional rights to equal protection under the law, access to the courts, and the right to appeal ([paras 5, 15]).

R – Rules:

The provision at the heart of this case, section 276B of the CPA, outlines the authority and parameters within which courts can impose fixed non-parole periods ([para 20]). This section, enacted on 1 October 2004, delineates that a non-parole period may not exceed two-thirds of the imprisonment term or 25 years, whichever is shorter, under specific conditions ([paras 20–21]).

Additionally, constitutional provisions relevant to the case include section 12(1)(a), ensuring the right not to be deprived of freedom arbitrarily or without just cause ([para 16]), along with section 35(3)(n), which guarantees the right to receive the least severe sentence possible if punishments are modified post-offence ([para 20]).

The case of S v Zono ([2014] ZASCA 188) serves as a precedent, illustrating that courts lacked the legislative authority to impose non-parole periods exceeding the applicable legal framework at the time of sentencing, thus making such orders impermissible in the absence of explicit legislative backing ([paras 19–20]).

A – Application:

The Constitutional Court concluded that the High Court had misdirected itself by imposing a non-parole period that exceeded statutory limits in place at the time of sentencing ([para 25]). The temporal disconnect between the commission of the crime, sentencing, and the introduction of the non-parole legislation invoked the principle that sentencing frameworks must align with legislative provisions at the time of the offence. The court underscored that any non-parole order constituted an increased sentence, which cannot have retrospective effect unless expressly sanctioned by legislation.

Moreover, the Court noted that the applicant was not provided an opportunity to contest the non-parole period at sentencing, raising grave concerns about compliance with procedural fairness, which could constitute a violation of fair trial rights ([para 28]).

The Court acknowledged the applicant's claims regarding the constitutional implications of the non-parole order, affirming the importance of protecting individual freedoms in a constitutional democracy, particularly following the lessons of apartheid ([paras 27–28]).

C – Conclusion:

The Constitutional Court, in a unanimous decision led by Majiedt J, ruled in favor of the applicant, emphasizing that the imposition of a 25-year non-parole period was both ultra vires and a significant infringement upon constitutional rights ([para 30]). The High Court's order was set aside, reinstating the legal position that had mandated a minimum of 20 years' service for life sentences prior to the enactment of section 276B. This ruling not only rectifies the specific injustices in Senwedi's case but also reinforces the overarching constitutional principles of legality, procedural fairness, and protection of individual freedoms ([para 30]). The implications of this judgment may extend to similar cases where non-parole periods have been improperly applied, ensuring that those sentenced under prior legislative frameworks are duly compensated by the law's protections.
