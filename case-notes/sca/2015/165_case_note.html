Supreme Court of Appeal

A Hendricks v M Hendricks & Others (20519/14) [2015] ZASCA 165

The appellant’s eviction application in the Somerset West Magistrates' Court was premised on the assertion that the respondents were "unlawful occupiers" as defined under the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE Act).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 [2015] ZASCA 165

F – Facts

The case involves a long-standing familial conflict between Annie Hendricks (the appellant) and her daughter-in-law, Margaret Hendricks (the first respondent), concerning the rights of habitation over a residential property in Macassar. The appellant, then 72 years old, sold the property on 5 November 1990 to her son Graham Hendricks (the second respondent), while retaining a lifelong right of habitation registered on the title deed. Over time, family relations deteriorated, prompting the appellant to leave the property, first seeking refuge with her daughter and then her other son. In 2010, a divorce between the first and second respondents resulted in further complications.

The appellant’s eviction application in the Somerset West Magistrates' Court was premised on the assertion that the respondents were "unlawful occupiers" as defined under the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE Act). The magistrates' court ruled against the appellant, stating the respondents were not unlawful occupiers. The Western Cape Division of the High Court upheld this decision on appeal, leading to the appellant seeking further leave from the Supreme Court of Appeal under s 16(1)(b) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 ([paras 1–4]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues presented to the Supreme Court of Appeal included:

  1. Whether the first respondent and her occupants qualified as "unlawful occupiers" under the PIE Act, given the appellant's retained right of habitation.
  2. Whether the appellant, holding a right of habitation, constituted a "person in charge" of the property, consequently entitling her to seek eviction.
  3. The standard of "just and equitable" under s 4(7) of the PIE Act for granting eviction orders ([paras 5–12]).

R – Rules

Key legal principles and governing statutes identified in this case include:

  • Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE Act): Defines "unlawful occupier" as a person occupying land without consent, and states conditions under which eviction requests may be considered, particularly in relation to the circumstances surrounding occupants such as elderly or children as per s 1 and s 4(7).
  • Common law: Definitions regarding rights of habitation, asserting that such rights are recognized as limited real rights derived from ownership, traceable to Roman-Dutch law.
  • Precedents from Kidson & another v Jimspeed Enterprises CC and Galant v Mahonga, which illustrate that rights of habitation can influence property rights and that eviction orders can be issued to protect the rights of holders against unlawful occupants ([paras 6–9]).

A – Application

The Court critically examined whether the first respondent, as the property's owner, could be considered an unlawful occupier. It found that the appellant's right of habitation limited the first respondent's rights as the property owner, suggesting that a right of habitation effectively modifies the nature of ownership vis-à-vis eviction laws.

The Court distinguished the case from the lower courts, which had erroneously prioritized ownership over habitation rights and failed to interpret the PIE Act's terms correctly. It highlighted that the appellant, as the holder of the right of habitation, was indeed a "person in charge" of the property and thus had the entitlement to initiate eviction proceedings against unlawful occupants ([paras 7–11]).

The final determination was that the eviction appeal must be remitted to the Magistrates' Court to properly consider whether it would be "just and equitable" to grant the eviction order, directing that specific factors be evaluated, including the living conditions of the respondents and the implications for their family ([paras 12–14]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the decisions of the lower courts, recognizing that the respondents were indeed unlawful occupiers given the appellant's lawful habitation rights. The judgment held that the appellant could seek eviction due to the non-consensual occupation by the respondents. The court ultimately set aside the previous rulings, ordering a remittance to the lower court for a detailed review under s 4(7) of the PIE Act to determine if an eviction order would be just and equitable based on all relevant contextual factors.

This ruling emphasizes the importance of rights of habitation within property law, demonstrating how such rights can influence eviction proceedings significantly. The outcome reflects the need for courts to engage deeply with both property law concepts and associated equities, particularly in family disputes ([paras 15]).
