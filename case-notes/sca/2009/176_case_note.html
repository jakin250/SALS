Transman (Pty) Ltd v Graham Dick (147/2008) [2008] ZASCA 38

The inquiry, which began on 13 February 2006, concluded with Dick being found guilty on some charges, leading the presiding officer to recommend his dismissal.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009 [2008] ZASCA 38

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The appellant, Transman (Pty) Ltd, faced a review application initiated by the first respondent, Graham Dick, who had been both a director and an employee of the company, alongside his wife, who was the managing director. The couple had established the company in 1983; however, their marital issues began to affect their professional relationship around 2001. On 10 October 2005, Graham Dick was removed from the board due to allegations of misconduct. Following his suspension on 15 August 2005, he was charged with 13 counts of misconduct and subjected to a disciplinary inquiry, presided over by the second respondent.

The inquiry, which began on 13 February 2006, concluded with Dick being found guilty on some charges, leading the presiding officer to recommend his dismissal. However, the employer's board resolved to retire him instead of summarily dismissing him on 27 March 2006. Dick subsequently filed a review application challenging both the disciplinary verdict and his termination of employment, which the High Court initially entertained ([paras 1-7]).

I – Issues

The case presented the following legal issues:

  1. Jurisdiction: Did the High Court possess the jurisdiction to hear the application for review regarding disciplinary decisions made by a non-State employer?
  2. Validity of Administrative Review: Is it competent to challenge the employer’s decisions through administrative review processes, considering the context of contractual employment?
  3. Entitlement to a Pre-Dismissal Hearing: Based on the terms of employment, has the employee established a case for the right to a hearing before his employment was terminated? ([paras 15-19]).

R – Rules

Several legal principles and precedents were pivotal in determining these issues:

  1. Jurisdiction of High Courts: The High Court has jurisdiction to adjudicate administrative reviews, but it has been established that employment dismissals typically cannot be contested via administrative law grounds as per the verdict in Chirwa v Transnet Ltd [2008] (4) SA 367 (CC).
  2. Separation of Administrative Law and Employment Law: Employment relationships operated under the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA) are distinct from administrative actions governed by statutes and can only be assessed through contractual obligations or unfair dismissal claims.
  3. Right to a Pre-Dismissal Hearing: The authority enshrined in Old Mutual Life Assurance Co of SA Ltd v Gumbi [2007] (5) SA 552 (SCA) confirmed employees' right to a fair hearing before dismissal, but this right derives from common law as amended by constitutional principles ([paras 10-12, 15-24]).

A – Application

In its application of the law, the Supreme Court of Appeal emphasized that the High Court erred in its judgment by treating the disciplinary actions through an administrative lens rather than strictly interpreting them in light of contractual obligations. The court underscored that while the common law acknowledges the right to fair dismissal procedures, the foundation for its review must rest on a contractual basis, not on administrative grounds.

The Supreme Court criticized the High Court for assuming Dick's employment contract contained an implied term for a pre-dismissal hearing without substantial proof of such terms being articulated within the actual employment agreement. This failure to demonstrate the specific terms of employment led to the conclusion that Dick had not made out his case adequately for a breach of contract ([paras 29-32]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal, concluding that the High Court had incorrectly applied the law regarding the nature and scope of the review proceedings initiated by the employee. The court ordered the dismissal of the review application on the basis that the relief sought was not cognizable within the framework of contractual claims as outlined under common law and reaffirmed the disjunction between administrative and employment law. This decision clarifies the limitations of claiming an administrative review in employment termination scenarios and emphasizes the necessity for clear contractual obligations to substantiate claims related to dismissal. The appeal was upheld with costs, including compensation for the employment of two counsel ([paras 30-31]).

This case serves as a significant precedent regarding employment termination and the judicial limits of administrative review, reinforcing the requirement for distinct categories of law relating to administrative action and contractual employment relations under South African law.
