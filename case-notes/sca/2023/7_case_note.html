Supreme Court of Appeal

City of Cape Town v Commando and Others (1303/2021)

The occupiers were residents of a property at erf 10626, Bromwell Street, Salt River, Cape Town, who were facing eviction from their homes due to actions taken by Woodstock Hub (Pty) Ltd, the owner of the property.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023

F - Facts

The case deals with significant issues surrounding emergency housing in South Africa, specifically in Cape Town, highlighting the deeply entrenched urban housing crisis exacerbated by historical inequalities. The City of Cape Town (the City), the appellant in this regard, faced an appeal against an order from the Western Cape Division of the High Court compelling it to provide emergency housing for the occupiers, identified in the proceedings as the first to twenty-sixth respondents ([para 2]).

The occupiers were residents of a property at erf 10626, Bromwell Street, Salt River, Cape Town, who were facing eviction from their homes due to actions taken by Woodstock Hub (Pty) Ltd, the owner of the property. Following their eviction, the occupiers had sought both suspension of the eviction orders and subsequent emergency housing solutions from the City ([paras 12–16]). The High Court initially ordered the City to provide emergency housing within specific areas, namely Woodstock, Salt River, or the Inner-City Precinct. This order was grounded in the recognition of the City’s constitutional obligation to ensure access to adequate housing under section 26 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa ([paras 7–8]).

The City contested the High Court's order, contending that it infringed on the doctrine of separation of powers by interfering with executive discretion in housing delivery policies. They asserted that the extensive socioeconomic factors determined where and how emergency housing could be provided, claiming it was unreasonable to bind the City to specific locational provisions ([para 8]).

I - Issues

The pivotal issues at the core of this appeal arose from the necessity of determining:

  1. Whether the City's emergency housing programme, as implemented, constituted a breach of constitutional obligations regarding the right to adequate housing ([para 6]).
  2. If the High Court’s mandatory order for the City to provide emergency housing at specific locations intruded upon the separation of powers doctrine, further questioning if such an order could be viewed as reasonable given the inherent complexities of municipal resource allocation and policy-making ([paras 8–9]).
  3. The extent of the City’s obligation to provide temporary emergency accommodation and whether this obligation extends to a specific location as demanded by the occupiers ([paras 7–8]).

R - Rules

The applicable legal framework includes but is not limited to:

  1. Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996: Section 26 guarantees the right to adequate housing and obliges the state to take reasonable legislative and other measures to progressively realise this right ([para 30]).
  2. Housing Act 107 of 1997: Mandates municipalities to ensure access to adequate housing and to take necessary steps to achieve integrated housing development in their jurisdiction ([para 36]).
  3. National Housing Code, 2009: Articulates the establishment and framework of the Emergency Housing Programme designed to assist households facing immediate housing crises ([para 37]).
  4. Case Law: The judgement refers extensively to the precedents established in cases like Grootboom and Blue Moonlight Properties, which articulate the constitutional duty of municipalities to provide emergency housing and the parameters of the reasonableness of housing policies ([paras 6, 32–34]).

A - Application

The Court’s analysis centred on the reasonableness and implementation of the City's housing programme. It held that in the context of the emergency housing programme, the City had a duty to respond adequately to situations rendering individuals homeless, such as eviction or natural disasters ([para 56]). However, it noted that the specific obligation to provide housing at a determined location was not a constitutional mandate but rather complexly tied to resource allocation and broader policy considerations.

The High Court's findings of unconstitutionality were not substantiated by detailed evidence, particularly regarding the failure of the City to fulfil its constitutional obligations, and lacked necessary prerequisites outlined in its own precedents and statutes ([paras 47–49]). The determination of a specific location for housing was seen as overstepping judicial bounds into executive powers, rendering the earlier decisions ineffective and unlawful due to lack of grounding in statutory obligation or established case law regarding acute locational demands ([paras 53–56]).

The appeal favored the City’s argument that its emergency housing policies were sufficiently comprehensive and adhered to the legal standards set forth by the Constitution. As such, the prior order mandating specific reservations of emergency housing in certain areas contradicted the need for flexible, adaptable, and viable municipal policies ([paras 60–68]).

C - Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, thereby overturning the High Court's order, but directed that the City still had an overarching duty to provide suitable temporary emergency housing for the occupiers ([para 76]). New timelines were stipulated for compliance, with a firm completion date set for before 30 May 2023, ensuring consideration for access to essential local amenities ([para 76]).

The decision not only addressed the immediate housing needs of the occupiers but also reaffirmed the separation of powers in resource allocation related to housing policy. Significantly, it underscored the need for municipalities to navigate complex socioeconomic realities and the limits of judicial intervention, ensuring that the principles of dignity, equity, and practicality remain foundational to the right to housing provisions under the South African Constitution ([paras 73–74]). This judgement serves as a reference point for future considerations of municipal housing obligations in light of constitutional imperatives amidst evolving urban crises ([para 76]).
