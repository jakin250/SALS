# Case Note: Eamonn Courtney v Izak Johannes Boshoff NO & Others (483/2023) [2024] ZASCA 104

## FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts

This appeal arises from an order granted on May 4, 2020, by Moultrie AJ in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg, which placed the estate of the appellant, Eamonn Courtney, under final sequestration without a preceding provisional order as mandated by the Insolvency Act 24 of 1936 (the Act) ([1]). The first and second respondents, Izak Boshoff NO and Winnie Gumede NO, were appointed as trustees of Mr. Courtney's insolvent estate, while ABSA Bank Ltd acted as the sequestrating creditor ([1]).

Courtney's financial difficulties commenced in 2018 with the liquidation of his primary income-producing company, Allied Mobile Communications Pty Ltd (AMC), leading to considerable debts of approximately R1 billion ([8]). Following multiple defaults under guarantees provided for his companies, ABSA initiated sequestration proceedings against Mr. Courtney on November 26, 2019. Notably, Mr. Courtney did not oppose the sequestration application and did not appear at the hearing on May 4, 2020, resulting in the unopposed final sequestration order ([9] - [11]).

On April 29, 2022, almost two years post-sequestration, Mr. Courtney sought to declare the final order of sequestration a nullity, arguing that the absence of a provisional order rendered it void ab initio. His application was met with opposition from the trustees and ABSA, who filed a conditional counter-application seeking a variation of the order ([2], [3]). The High Court dismissed Mr. Courtney's application on December 20, 2022, leading to this appeal ([4]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) concerns whether the final sequestration order issued on May 4, 2020, is valid despite not being preceded by a provisional order as required by the Act. Additionally, the case raises questions regarding the proper process for seeking rescission of orders within the context of insolvency law ([17]).

### R – Rules

The applicable legal framework includes the Insolvency Act 24 of 1936, which prescribes a process for sequestration that must typically begin with a provisional order, followed by a final order ([1] - [2]). The legal precedents relevant to this case include:

1. *The Master of the High Court Northern Gauteng High Court, Pretoria v Motala NO and Others* [2012] ZASCA 238, which addresses the nullity of orders made outside statutory authority.
2. *Knoop NO and Another v Gupta and Another* [2020] ZASCA 163, which similarly dealt with compliance with statutory requirements in issuing court orders.

These cases establish the principle that orders not conforming to legislative mandates may be rendered null and void, necessitating strict adherence to procedural requirements in issuing sequestration orders ([19] - [20]).

### A – Application

The SCA, delivering judgment through Nicholls JA, distinguished the present case from *Motala* and *Knoop*. The court recognized that while the order's issuance might have constituted an error in timing (not being preceded by a provisional order), it did not amount to a lack of jurisdiction or authority to issue a final order. The SCA emphasized that incorrect judicial orders retain legal force and effect until specifically set aside by a court ([20], [24]).

In rejecting Mr. Courtney's claims, the SCA noted that his failure to participate in the original sequestration proceedings precluded him from later asserting that the order was a nullity. The court highlighted that the final order of sequestration continued to operate as law and that the trustees had been appointed to administer Courtney's estate based on that valid order ([25]). Furthermore, it was emphasized that Mr. Courtney had not provided any valid grounds for rescission under Rule 42 or common law standards, as he had not shown that he was not in wilful default, nor did he present a suitable legal defense ([26] - [29]).

### C – Conclusion

The SCA concluded that the appeal by Mr. Courtney must fail, affirming the High Court's decision to dismiss his application against the sequestration order. The court held that the initial final order was not a nullity and continued to be effective until legally rescinded. The SCA thus dismissed the appeal with costs and set aside certain paragraphs of the High Court's order that could no longer stand ([31]).

This decision underscores the critical importance of adhering to procedural mandates under the Insolvency Act while also clarifying the legal standing of orders within the context of unopposed legal proceedings. The ruling has significant implications for the administration of insolvency proceedings, particularly in relation to the responsibilities of creditors and the rights of insolvents who may subsequently challenge previously unopposed orders.
