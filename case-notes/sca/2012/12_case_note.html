Supreme Court of Appeal

Mathewson & Another v Van Niekerk & Others (260/11)

Subsequently, on 6 May 2009, the appellants' attorney notified the respondents that, due to the lack of these services, they believed that either a breach had occurred or that they were entitled to repudiate the contract.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2012 1994 (3) SA 130

F – Facts:
The case centers around a deed of sale dated 26 March 2007, wherein the first and second respondents sold a residential erf to the first and second appellants. According to Clause 17 of this deed, there was a warranty that basic services—specifically electricity, water, and sewage—would be provided by the developers (the respondents) ([1], [2]).

Subsequently, on 6 May 2009, the appellants' attorney notified the respondents that, due to the lack of these services, they believed that either a breach had occurred or that they were entitled to repudiate the contract. The letter demanded rectification of the service provisions within seven days, failing which they would consider the contract cancelled ([3]). The following application was initiated on 2 June 2009, and the North Gauteng High Court (Ebersohn AJ) granted the appellants’ application for cancellation of the agreement and refused leave to appeal ([1]).

The court a quo asserted that the respondents had indeed breached the contract by failing to provide the stipulated services and therefore ruled in favor of the appellants ([4], [18]). However, the respondents contended that there existed a tacit term in the contract indicating that the appellants needed to specify where these services should be installed, a claim the court a quo neglected to address properly ([5]).

I – Issues:
The case presented several legal questions to the Supreme Court of Appeal, including:

  1. Whether the respondents had a binding obligation to install the services without the appellants providing specific instructions.
  2. Whether the tacit term asserted by the respondents could be legitimately incorporated into the contract despite the ‘sole contract’ clause (Clause 11) designed to preclude the introduction of other contractual terms or representations ([6], [11]).

R – Rules:
The court relied on established principles of contract law, particularly regarding the recognition of tacit terms in contracts. Reference was made to Wilkens NO v Voges 1994 (3) SA 130 (A), where it was stated that a tacit term, once identified, effectively forms part of the contract and does not infringe the validity of the written terms under the Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981 ([6], [7]). The comments in Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints Pty Ltd 1984 (3) SA 623 (A), concerning the rejection of a version of events as far-fetched or untenable, were also pertinent ([8]).

A – Application:
In reviewing the case, the Supreme Court of Appeal was compelled to determine whether the respondents' assertion of the tacit term was so untenable that it could be dismissed on the papers alone. It highlighted that:

  • The existence of the tacit term that required the appellants to indicate where the services would be installed was a reasonable interpretation given the context of the sale.
  • The appellants had not provided conclusive proof that refuted the claim of the tacit term; therefore, the version of the respondents could not be categorized as far-fetched ([10]).

The court also evaluated the appellants' arguments that the respondents' defense had been raised too late and derived from a contradiction in the respondent's statements. The court found that these contradictions were not sufficient to invalidate the respondents' position ([9], [10]).

As a result, the court concluded that the respondents supplied a plausible explanation as to why the tacit term should be considered, specifically in light of the large size of the erf and the reasonable expectation that detailed specifications were required before implementation of services ([10]).

C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court ultimately held that the appeal was successful, overturning the decision of the court a quo and ruling that the respondents’ defenses based on the tacit term were valid. The order was rescinded to dismiss the applicants’ claim ([11]).

This case illustrates the nuanced relationship between express terms in contracts and the potential for tacit terms to be recognized, showing how additional oral assertions or circumstantial evidence can affect legal agreements. The implications extend to how parties must heed clear communication in contracts involving implied obligations, especially in real estate dealings.
