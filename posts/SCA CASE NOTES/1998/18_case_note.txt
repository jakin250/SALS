Source Folder: 1998

# FIRAC Case Note: Omar Barries v The Sheriff of the Magistrates' Court, Wynberg

**Case Citation**: Omar Barries v The Sheriff of the Magistrates' Court, Wynberg (Case No 25/96) (SCA)

**Court**: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Judges**: Hefer JA, Eksteen JA, Olivier JA, Plewman JA, Melunsky AJA

**Date of Hearing**: 17 March 1998

**Date of Reasons**: 25 March 1998

---

**F - Facts**: 
Omar Barries was a cabinet maker who operated his business from leased premises. Due to financial difficulties, he fell behind on his rent in 1991, leading to judgments against him for both his debt and eviction from the factory he occupied ([paras 2–3]). Pursuant to these judgments, the Sheriff issued a warrant for the seizure of Barries's property to satisfy a judgment debt amounting to R16,449.20 and a warrant of ejectment for the premises ([para 3]).

On 15 and 16 August 1991, the second respondent, the Sheriff, executed these warrants by breaking into the locked factory to seize equipment for storage, pending sale ([para 4]). However, Barries claimed that the Sheriff acted unlawfully by allowing unknown persons to remove items from the factory ([para 4]). The trial court, presided over by Motala AJ, dismissed Barries's claims and ordered absolution from the instance, awarding costs to the respondents ([para 5]).

After being granted leave to appeal, Barries's counsel failed to comply with several procedural requirements, resulting in the filing of three petitions for condonation of late filings, including the notice of appeal, power of attorney, and the record of proceedings ([paras 5–6]). The court dismissed the applications for condonation, ordering Barries's attorneys to pay costs de bonis propriis ([para 2]).

---

**I - Issues**: 
The primary issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal were:
1. Whether the delay in filing the notice of appeal and the accompanying documents warranted condonation.
2. Whether the Sheriff acted lawfully in the execution of the warrants, particularly concerning the removal of Barries's goods by unidentified persons ([paras 2–3]).

---

**R - Rules**: 
The legal framework governing this case includes:
1. **Rule 5(1)** of the Supreme Court of Appeal Rules requires filing a notice of appeal within 20 days after leave to appeal is granted ([para 5]).
2. **Rule 5(3)(b)** requires lodging a power of attorney within the same timeframe ([para 5]).
3. **Rule 6(2)** necessitates that before lodging the record of proceedings, an appellant must provide security for the costs of the appeal, to be done within a specific period ([para 7]).

Case law established relevant precedents for determining condonation applications, emphasizing the necessity for a satisfactory explanation for non-compliance and the prospect of success ([para 9]). Key cases referenced include *Federated Employers Fire and General Insurance Co Ltd v McKenzie* 1969 (3) SA 360 (A), *United Plant Hire (Pty) Ltd v Hills* 1976 (1) SA 717 (A), and *Ferreira v Ntshingila* 1990 (4) SA 271 (A) ([paras 9–10]).

---

**A - Application**: 
The Court analyzed Barries's applications for condonation by considering his attorney's explanations for the delays. Critically, the attorney's assertion of misunderstanding regarding responsibilities and miscommunication with counsel did not suffice to rationalize the length of the delays, particularly given that he was already aware of the procedural requirements ([para 10]). The Court noted that ignorance or negligence is not a valid excuse for failing to comply with the rules ([para 10]).

The Court also emphasized the importance of providing a reasonable explanation for each instance of non-compliance and noted that the overall conduct of Barries's legal representatives contributed to the delays. The failure to file affidavits from Barries himself or to provide detailed explanations regarding each application was viewed as a significant shortcoming ([paras 7–10]).

---

**C - Conclusion**: 
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Barries's petitions for condonation, reinforcing the notion that compliance with procedural rules is paramount in appellate proceedings. The ruling underlined that the cumulative effect of various lapses warranted the refusal of condonation for late filing ([paras 6–10]). Consequently, Barries was ordered to bear the costs associated with his unsuccessful applications, highlighting the judiciary’s increasing intolerance of procedural non-compliance in the interests of justice and efficiency ([para 2]). The case thus serves as a stern reminder of the necessity for diligent adherence to prescribed legal protocols when pursuing appeals.
