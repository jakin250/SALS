Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: Van Vuuren v eThekwini Municipality (1308/2016) [2017] ZASCA 124

## FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts

On May 21, 2011, John Ray Jacques Van Vuuren, an eight-year-old boy, was injured while using a water slide at a municipal pool in Durban, KwaZulu-Natal, South Africa. The slide was one of the facilities provided by the eThekwini Municipality (the Municipality) for children. During his descent, where he lacked supervision, Jacques lost his balance, reportedly due to being pushed by another child, leading to serious injuries, including a fractured jaw and tooth loss requiring surgical intervention ([1]–[3]).

Subsequent to the incident, Jacques's mother, Karlien Van Vuuren, initiated legal action against the Municipality in the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court, seeking damages for the injuries sustained by her son. Among the claims was the assertion that the Municipality was negligent in multiple aspects, mainly its failure to provide adequate supervision and control over the use of the pool and slide ([3]).

In its defense, the Municipality denied negligence, arguing that the appellant was aware of the risks and had effectively assumed the risk of injury, thereby asserting a defense of voluntary assumption of risk. Additionally, they contended that the appellant had failed in her parental duty to supervise her child during the incident ([4]).

The trial court found in favor of the Municipality, dismissing Van Vuuren's claim, prompting the appeal ([7]).

### I – Issues

The core legal issue in this case revolves around whether the Municipality had a legal duty to supervise children and control access to the water slide, potentially establishing negligence due to a failure to meet this duty. There was also the issue of whether the appellant's actions or omissions contributed to the circumstances leading to Jacques's injuries ([5], [15]).

### R – Rules

The court relied on established principles of negligence within delictual law, particularly the necessity of demonstrating both a legal duty and a breach of that duty. The analysis focused on whether the requirements for imposing a duty of care were met, which necessitates a determination of public and legal policy considerations ([16], [28]).

Key cases referenced include:
- **Hawekwa Youth Camp & another v Byrne** 2010 (6) SA 83 (SCA), which emphasizes that negligent omissions are not prima facie wrongful unless a legal duty exists.
- **Kruger v Coetzee** 1966 (2) SA 428 (A), providing the test for determining negligence involving foreseeability and reasonable conduct.
- The constitutional norm outlined in Section 28(2) which stipulates that a child's best interests are of paramount importance ([22]).

### A – Application

The court's analysis began with the consideration of whether a legal duty existed for the Municipality regarding the provision of supervision at the slide. The judgment evaluated the nature of the facility being specifically designed for children under twelve years of age and the inherent risks connected with allowing such children to use the slide without supervision. The court noted that chaos ensued with children using the slide without supervision, which contributed significantly to the risk of injury ([22], [23]).

The judgment also highlighted that the Municipality had created a potential risk for users by providing the water slide. Therefore, even though it was argued that parental supervision should suffice, the absence of such supervision, coupled with the Municipality's control over the facility, created grounds for imposing a duty of care ([24], [29]).

The notion that the presence of parents would automatically assume liability was rejected; rather, the court found that parents may not always be able to supervise due to the nature of the facility, particularly since it restricted access to children under twelve ([25]).

The court also addressed the Municipality's assertion of assumed risk, concluding that this defense was not applicable because a parent typically cannot consent to risk of injury to their minor child. Furthermore, the Municipality's argument regarding the appellant's alleged contributory negligence was addressed, with the judgment recognizing that wrongful conduct by the Municipality triggered liability regardless of parental involvement ([32], [33]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal, finding that the Municipality was negligent due to its failure to provide adequate supervision and access control at the water slide. This negligence was identified as the proximate cause of Jacques's injuries. The court replaced the lower court's order, holding that the Municipality would be liable for damages sustained by Jacques, both in his personal capacity and through his mother, Karlien Van Vuuren, as his guardian ([36]).

The court's decision emphasizes the importance of imposing appropriate duties of care on public entities responsible for facilities intended for children, thereby balancing parental responsibility with municipal obligations. This ruling aligns with public policy aimed at safeguarding children's interests within such recreational environments.
