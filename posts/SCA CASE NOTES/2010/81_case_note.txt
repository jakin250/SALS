Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

# Case Note: SA Metal & Machinery Co (Pty) Ltd v S (398/09) [2010] ZASCA 81

## FIRAC Method

### F - Facts
The appellant, SA Metal & Machinery (Pty) Ltd, was charged alongside Mr. Allan de Klerk, an employee, with theft and contravention of Section 37(1) of the General Law Amendment Act 62 of 1955 ([1]–[2]). The charges stemmed from allegations that they unlawfully acquired approximately 2,900 kg of copper cathodes, among other items, which were reported stolen following a hijacking five weeks prior to the incident. The Regional Court acquitted both on the counts of theft but convicted them under Section 37(1) concerning stolen goods. The appellant was fined R100,000, with half suspended, while De Klerk received a R10,000 fine or two years' imprisonment. Upon appeal to the South Gauteng High Court, De Klerk's conviction was set aside, but the appellate court dismissed SA Metal & Machinery's appeal against conviction, altering only the sentence to R80,000, also with half suspended ([2]–[3]).

In their business operations as prominent scrap metal merchants, the appellant relied on sourcing from registered dealers, such as Meadon Scrap, and not from the open market. The transaction in question involved loading the cathodes based on a report from Visser, a yard foreman, without De Klerk personally inspecting the goods. The police later intercepted the container bearing the hijacked cathodes ([4]–[10]).

### I - Issues
The primary issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal revolved around:
1. **Corporate Liability**: Whether the conviction of a corporate entity could stem from the conduct of employees not explicitly mentioned in the charge ([3], [14]).
2. **Reasonable Cause Under Section 37(1)**: Whether the State successfully proved beyond a reasonable doubt that the appellant lacked reasonable cause to believe that the goods were legally acquired, as required under Section 37(1)(b) of the Act ([3], [17]).

### R - Rules
The legal framework involved several crucial statutes:
- **Section 37(1)** of the General Law Amendment Act 62 of 1955: It outlines the responsibility regarding the possession of stolen goods. The provision indicates that possession serves as evidence of the absence of reasonable cause unless contradicted ([2]).
- **Section 332** of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977: This section addresses corporate liability, giving an employer liability for acts committed by employees in the course of their duties ([13]).
- **Amendments to Charges**: According to **Section 86** of the Criminal Procedure Act, amendments may be made to rectify charges, provided they do not prejudice the accused ([15]).

### A - Application
The Court analyzed the validity of the convictions focusing on whether the appellant had a reasonable cause to believe they could lawfully purchase the cathodes. The appeal court pointed out that the trial court fundamentally rested the appellant’s conviction on De Klerk's actions, whose individual conviction had been overturned ([2], [18]). The considerations included:

1. **Evidence Review**: The High Court had accepted De Klerk's belief in the legitimacy of the copper cathodes due to his past dealings. The Court found it crucial to ascertain the appellant’s belief, as corporate liability hinges upon the actions of its employees ([11], [17]).
2. **Prejudicial Amendments**: The State’s attempt to amend the charge sheet to include other employees after the trial had closed raised concerns of unfairness, undermining the appellant’s preparation and ability to defend themselves accordingly ([15], [16]).
3. **Conviction Standards**: The Court held that since De Klerk had a reasonable basis for believing in the lawfulness of the transaction, and as this belief disallowed a finding of guilt against the appellant, the lower courts erred in their conclusions ([17]).

### C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appellant's appeal against the conviction, concluding that the appellant could not be found guilty when the only basis for culpability was linked to the actions of De Klerk, which had already been invalidated. The amended charge sheet's implications for a fair trial were significant, emphasizing the principles of due process and the rights of the accused ([18]).

The judgment affirms the necessity of maintaining strict adherence to procedural fairness in criminal prosecutions, particularly in corporate liability cases, where employees' actions critically influence the corporate entity's liability. The case also underscores the need for clarity in charges brought against corporate entities to align with the principles of fair trial as enshrined in the Constitution ([16]–[17]). 

This case represents a pivotal reassessment of corporate accountability within the framework of South African jurisprudence, and the implications of employee conduct on corporate liability echo throughout various aspects of corporate law and criminal procedure.
