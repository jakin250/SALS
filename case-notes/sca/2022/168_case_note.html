Mapholisa N O v Phetoe N O and Others (163/2021) [2022] ZASCA 168

The procedural backdrop is rooted in the Health Professions Act 56 of 1974 (the Act), which stipulates the mechanisms for lodging complaints and conducting inquiries into alleged unprofessional conduct of health professionals ([4], [6]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 168

F – Facts

The case at hand stems from an application for leave to appeal lodged by Mr. Meshack Mapholisa, acting in his official capacity as a pro forma complainant appointed by the Registrar of the Health Professions Council of South Africa (HPCSA) ([1], [2]). The appeal challenges the decision of the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which had previously dismissed a review application concerning a ruling made by the Professional Conduct Committee (PCC) of the HPCSA regarding Dr. Percy Miller, a registered medical doctor ([1], [11], [26]).

The procedural backdrop is rooted in the Health Professions Act 56 of 1974 (the Act), which stipulates the mechanisms for lodging complaints and conducting inquiries into alleged unprofessional conduct of health professionals ([4], [6]). The complaint in question originated from Ms. Malinda Miller on 8 July 2013 regarding Dr. Miller's treatment of Ms. Violet Senna, who was deceased at the time of the proceedings ([8], [10]). The PCC, by its ruling on 3 July 2017, upheld a point in limine raised by Dr. Miller regarding the locus standi of Ms. Miller in lodging the complaint, resulting in the dismissal of the complaint ([10]).

Mr. Mapholisa subsequently sought judicial review of the PCC's decision, but the High Court dismissed the application on grounds that he had not exhausted the internal remedy of appeal as mandated by section 7(2) of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([11], [12]). The High Court's ruling also aligned with its conclusion that Ms. Miller lacked standing to bring the complaint due to her not being Dr. Miller’s patient ([12]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue posed for resolution was whether the high court correctly asserted that the internal remedy of appeal must first be exhausted under section 7(2) of the PAJA before proceeding with a review application brought by Mr. Mapholisa, an organ of state, against another organ, namely the PCC ([13]). Linked to this was the secondary question of whether the PCC’s determination regarding Ms. Miller's standing to file a complaint was lawful and justified under the prevailing legislative framework ([13], [23]).

R – Rules

The legal framework at the centre of this case involves both the PAJA and the Health Professions Act, particularly its provisions regarding professional conduct complaints. The PAJA outlines administrative justice and specifies that any administrative action is subject to review ([14], [15]). Section 7(2) of the PAJA establishes the requirement that no court shall entertain a review of administrative action unless internal remedies have been exhausted, although exceptions exist for cases of exceptional circumstances ([15]).

The court also referred to the critical distinction in the approach to internal remedies as per the common law, which does not impose an obligation to exhaust remedies unless explicitly required by statute ([21]). Further, the Supreme Court of Appeal emphasized its reliance on the Constitutional Court decision in Gijima regarding the application of PAJA in organ-to-organ state reviews, concluding that the rights set out in section 33 of the Constitution are predominantly for private persons, allowing for legality review when state organs act against their own decisions ([17], [19]).

A – Application

In its analysis, the Supreme Court of Appeal observed that the high court had erred in concluding that Mr. Mapholisa was required to exhaust the internal remedy of appeal under the PAJA provisions, particularly given that both parties were governmental entities ([22]). This misinterpretation guided the decision to dismiss the review application prematurely. The Court further clarified that the principle of legality, which governs review in this context, does not impose a requirement for exhaustion of internal remedies, countering the specific procedural stipulation of section 7(2) of the PAJA ([20], [22]).

The Court also addressed the merits of the PCC’s ruling regarding Ms. Miller's locus standi, distinguishing between standing to complain and evidentiary capacity in disciplinary proceedings. The PCC had conflated these two distinct questions, incorrectly ruling that because Ms. Miller was not Dr. Miller's patient, she could neither lodge a complaint nor substantiate allegations of unprofessional conduct ([23], [24]). The Court found that the definition of ‘complainant’ was sufficiently broad to ensure that public interest is protected from potential misconduct by practitioners ([24]).

C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal issued a decisive ruling that not only granted Mr. Mapholisa leave to appeal but also fundamentally overturned the High Court’s dismissal of the review application. The Court held that Mr. Mapholisa had reasonable prospects of success, having correctly invoked the legality principle rather than being bound by the PAJA’s exhaustion requirement, which did not apply in this context ([26]). The Court further ruled that the PCC's decision was invalid due to severe legal misinterpretation relating to locus standi, thereby reinstating the necessary procedural inquiry into Dr. Miller’s conduct.

The implications of this ruling affirm the boundaries between procedural and substantive interpretations of locus standi in administrative law and underscore the protection of public interest in professional conduct reviews. Furthermore, the decision illuminates the distinct legal pathways available to state organs in exercising their review powers under South African law.
