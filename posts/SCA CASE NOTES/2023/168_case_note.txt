Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: Kouga Local Municipality v St Francis Bay (Ward 12) Concerned Residents’ Association and Others (Case No. 1056/2022) [2023] ZASCA 168

## F - Facts
The appeal concerns the Kouga Local Municipality's legality in establishing a special rating area (SRA) in St Francis Bay, regulated under section 22 of the Local Government: Municipal Property Rates Act 6 of 2004 (PRA) ([paras 1–3]). The appellant, Kouga Local Municipality, adopted an amendment to its rates policy and a corresponding by-law on 19 December 2017 ([para 5]). This amendment aimed to provide a framework for establishing SRAs following consultation with local communities and securing their consent ([para 6]).

The second respondent, St Francis Property Owners Association, submitted an application for establishing an SRA on 23 February 2018, which included plans for improved maintenance, security enhancements, and budget estimations ([paras 7–8]). This application was supported by a majority of the ratepayers and culminated in the municipal council approving the SRA on 23 May 2018, effective from 1 July 2018 ([paras 10–11]).

However, the newly formed St Francis Bay (Ward 12) Concerned Residents’ Association, the first respondent, challenged the municipality's decision in the Eastern Cape High Court ([paras 11–12]). They sought declarations that the rates policy was unconstitutional and that the establishment of the SRA was unlawful, arguing that the municipality had unlawfully delegated its powers to the second and third respondents ([para 13]).

## I - Issues
The primary legal issue was whether the Kouga Local Municipality’s rates policy and the establishment of the SRA were consistent with the mandates of section 22 of the PRA. Specifically, the court had to decide whether the municipality had unlawfully delegated its powers, contravening constitutional and statutory provisions governing local government ([paras 13–14]). Moreover, it had to determine whether the procedural compliance of the municipality in establishing the SRA was sufficient given the arguments from the first respondent regarding public participation and compliance with the rates policy ([paras 16–17]).

## R - Rules
The applicable legal principles primarily stem from section 22 of the PRA, which outlines how municipalities must initiate and consult with local communities concerning the establishment of SRAs ([para 3]). The Constitution of South Africa, particularly sections 152 and 153, sets out the objects of local government and emphasizes community involvement ([para 4]). The PRA and related municipal legislation establish the parameters under which municipalities may levy property rates and manage SRAs ([paras 4–5]).

Notably, the court recalled its previous decision in *South African Property Owners Association v Council of the City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality* which stressed that local government legislation should be read together, forming a coherent body of law ([para 28]).

## A - Application
The court rigorously examined whether the municipality’s rates policy permitted the delegation of the establishment of the SRA to non-government entities. It interpreted section 22 as allowing municipalities flexibility in how they initiate SRA establishment, implying that community involvement through private entities is permissible, as long as a majority of affected ratepayers consent to the proposal ([paras 24–32]).

The court found that the first respondent's arguments, which indicated that the entire framework of the rates policy was unconstitutional, lacked merit. They did not convincingly establish that Part A of the rates policy abrogated the municipality's functions ([paras 32–34]). The municipality's provision for creating a management body to oversee the SRA was deemed an administrative function rather than a legislative one, thus not violating the provisions of the PRA ([paras 37–39]).

Furthermore, the court clarified that even if procedural missteps were identified, they were not sufficient to invalidate the establishment of the SRA, particularly since the core objective of raising funds for local improvements was fulfilled ([para 36]).

## C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, ruling that the Kouga Local Municipality acted within its legal framework when establishing the SRA and that the rates policy did not unlawfully delegate municipal powers ([para 40]). The judgment underscored the importance of community involvement while recognizing the municipality's discretion in managing local governance issues ([para 32]). The court thus replaced the High Court's ruling with an order dismissing the first respondent's application without costs, reinforcing the legitimacy of collaborative initiatives in local governance ([para 41]).

This case highlights significant interpretations of statutory provisions governing local inputs in municipal decision-making and reinforces the principle that municipalities must engage with communities while retaining the ability to delegate certain administrative functions to enhance service delivery.
