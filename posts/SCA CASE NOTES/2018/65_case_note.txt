Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: Minister of Defence v Xulu (337/2017) [2018] ZASCA 65

**FIRAC Method**

## F – Facts

The case revolves around Mr. Bongani Postolie Xulu, who was a soldier in the South African National Defence Force (SANDF). He initially joined the SANDF on 29 July 1996, with his employment contract renewed multiple times until its expiration on 30 June 2011 ([1]). In November 2010, Xulu was informed by the SANDF that his contract would not be renewed, a decision communicated formally at the end of November or early December 2010. The SANDF cited certain disciplinary offences from his past as considerations for this decision ([1]-[2]).

Xulu challenged this decision in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, where he was initially unsuccessful but later succeeded on appeal. The High Court ordered that Xulu be reappointed on a contract for six more years, expiring on 30 June 2017 ([2]). The Minister of Defence and the Chief of the SANDF appealed this ruling to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), seeking clarity on their legal obligations regarding the renewal of fixed-term contracts for members of the SANDF ([2]-[3]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal questions raised in this appeal included:

1. Whether the SANDF's refusal to renew Xulu's fixed-term contract constituted administrative action under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([28]).
2. Whether the full court's decision, based on the principle of legality, could stand without addressing the question of administrative action ([28]-[29]).
3. The implications of the SANDF's failure to comply with its stated policies regarding the non-renewal of Xulu's contract ([26]-[27]).

## R – Rules

1. **Administrative Action**: Defined under PAJA as any decision of an administrative nature made under an empowering provision and taken by an organ of State which adversely affects rights and has a direct external legal effect ([33]-[34]).
2. **Principle of Legality**: This principle governs all exercises of public power, ensuring that actions taken by state organs conform with both statutory and common law standards of legality and rationality ([47]).
3. **Legislative Framework**: The governance of members of the SANDF is primarily dictated by the Defence Act 42 of 2002 and associated policies, including the interpretation of fair labour practices ([18]-[19]).

## A – Application

The court's analysis commenced with the consideration of whether the decision not to renew Xulu's contract amounted to administrative action. The court noted that the SANDF's decision did qualify as administrative due to its nature, bearing direct consequences on Xulu's rights and failing to align with the internal policy governing non-renewal ([35]-[43]). 

The SANDF argued that Xulu's failure to secure renewal was a contractual issue rather than administrative, which the SCA refuted. The court highlighted that despite the SANDF's assertion, the decision not to renew Xulu's contract relied heavily on past disciplinary actions without adequate consideration of relevant mitigating factors, including a lack of similar disqualifications for past contract renewals ([51]-[52]). 

The SCA rejected the position taken by the SANDF and held that the actions undertaken were not merely contractual but were indeed exercises of public power, as they fell under statutory obligations. Moreover, they failed to observe their own procedural requirements for non-renewal dictated by their internal policies ([26]).

In addressing the relief granted, the SCA concurred with the full court that the initial refusal to renew Xulu's contract was unlawful but nuanced the order to address only financial implications rather than reinstatement or extension beyond the originally imposed limits ([53]-[54]).

## C – Conclusion

The SCA ultimately granted special leave to appeal, amending the terms of the prior order made by the full court. The court held that the SANDF’s failure to renew Xulu's contract constituted administrative action, successfully challenging the legal principles invoked in defense of its determination. Consequently, the SCA stressed the necessity for compliance with both legislative requirements and their internal policies, allowing for the recognition of rights in the associated military context ([55]-[57]).

This judgment substantially reinforces the importance of due process in administrative actions affecting the livelihoods of its members, elucidating on the constitutional protections afforded to military personnel even in the scope of employment-related decisions.
