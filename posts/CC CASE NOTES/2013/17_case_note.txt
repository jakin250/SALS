Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# Case Note: FIRAC Method

**Case Title:** Frank Nabolisa v The State

**Court:** Constitutional Court of South Africa

**Case Number:** CCT 105/12

**Judgment Date:** 12 June 2013

## F – Facts

The applicant, Frank Nabolisa, was embroiled in a criminal enterprise to import cocaine into South Africa during 2008 ([para 2]). He, along with Sheryl Cwele, was charged with dealing in drugs under section 5(b) of the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 1992. Nabolisa pleaded not guilty ([paras 3-4]). The KwaZulu-Natal High Court ultimately convicted both Nabolisa and Cwele on 5 May 2011, sentencing them to 12 years’ imprisonment ([para 5]). When Nabolisa applied for leave to appeal, the State indicated its intention to seek a longer sentence, ultimately seeking an increase to 20 years ([para 6-7]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Nabolisa's appeal against his conviction but increased his sentence to 20 years, highlighting that the minimum sentencing legislation applied and that the High Court's original sentence was "disturbingly inappropriate" ([para 9]). Nabolisa's appeal to the Constitutional Court centered on whether the State was required to cross-appeal in order to seek an increase in sentence ([paras 10-11]).

## I – Issues

Three critical issues arose in the appeal:

1. **Cross-Appeal Requirement**: Does section 316B of the Criminal Procedure Act necessitate a formal cross-appeal by the State when the accused has initiated an appeal?
2. **Fairness of the Appeal**: If the State's failure to cross-appeal constitutes an irregularity, does this make the appeal process unfair?
3. **Manner of Proceedings**: Was the appeal conducted in a manner consistent with the rights to a fair appeal as guaranteed by the Constitution ([para 18])?

## R – Rules

The legal examination centers on the interpretation of section 316B of the Criminal Procedure Act, which grants the State the right to appeal against a sentence imposed in a superior court ([para 11]). This rule reflects the broader principles enshrined in section 35 of the South African Constitution, which guarantees the right to a fair trial, encompassing the right to appeal ([para 19]).

In Bogaards v S, this Court identified the need for a formal notice to ensure the appellant understands the risk of an increased sentence ([para 56]). Furthermore, it explored the implications of formal requirements and whether parties were adequately notified of potential sentence increases ([paras 55, 33]).

## A – Application

The court examined the implications of the State's failure to formally cross-appeal. It reasoned that section 316B does not impose a strict requirement for cross-appeal; rather, it creates a pathway for the State to present its arguments on sentence within the framework of an existing appeal initiated by the accused ([para 26]). The judgment emphasized that the appeal court retains extensive powers regarding sentencing, meaning it can amend or set aside judgments even without a cross-appeal if the issue of sentence was raised ([para 27]).

Nabolisa's defense contended that he was prejudiced by the State's failure to formally cross-appeal; however, the court highlighted that he was adequately notified through the State's Heads of Argument and had ample opportunity to defend against the proposed increase in sentence ([para 34]). The court determined that there was no "irregularity" that could undermine the fairness of the appeal process ([para 36]).

Further, the court concluded that, even if there had been an irregularity, it did not result in a "failure of justice" which would warrant interference with the increased sentence imposed ([para 41]).


## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court held that the appeal would be dismissed, as there was no irregularity in the proceedings of the Supreme Court of Appeal. Section 316B does not require a formal cross-appeal in instances where the accused has already initiated an appeal, and the State had adequately notified Nabolisa of its intentions ([para 42]). 

The majority judgment underlines the importance of ensuring that criminal appeal systems allow for substantive fairness while navigating procedural requirements. The consequent implication is that the judgment clarifies the procedural expectations of the State in appeal matters, thereby simplifying the legal landscape around appeals against sentencing ([paras 40, 75]). 

The court ultimately clarified that the existing framework could accommodate the State's arguments without formal cross-appeal mechanisms, ensuring that both parties’ interests and rights to a fair trial are upheld ([paras 28, 60) while also enhancing the predictability of appeal processes against sentences in criminal matters.
