Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note: **Sonke Gender Justice NPC v President of the Republic of South Africa and Others** [2020] ZACC 26

## F – Facts
This case involves the **Sonke Gender Justice NPC** (the **Applicant**), a non-profit entity advocating for proper oversight in correctional services, who challenged several provisions of the **Correctional Services Act 111 of 1998** (the **Act**) concerning the **Judicial Inspectorate for Correctional Services** (the **Inspectorate**). The parties include the **President of the Republic of South Africa**, the **Minister of Justice and Correctional Services**, the **National Commissioner of Correctional Services**, the **Inspecting Judge** for Correctional Services, and two other ministers (the **Respondents**).

Initially, the Applicant challenged Chapters IX and X of the Act but subsequently narrowed its claims, focusing specifically on sections **88A(1)(b)**, **88A(4)**, and **91**, asserting that they failed to provide the Inspectorate with the independence necessary for effective oversight and reporting on inmate treatment and conditions. The High Court found these provisions unconstitutional, holding that the limited financial controls by the Department of Correctional Services constituted a threat to the Inspectorate’s independence and, thus, to the constitutional rights of inmates ([paras 37–40]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issues raised in this case include:

1. **Does the Constitution impose an obligation on the State to ensure the Judicial Inspectorate has a sufficient level of independence to protect inmates' rights?**
2. **Do the provisions of sections 88A(1)(b), 88A(4), and 91 of the Act ensure such independence?**

These issues gauge the relationship between the constitutional mandate for the protection of inmate rights under the Bill of Rights, specifically sections **10**, **11**, **12**, and **35**, and the nature of the institutional independence required of oversight entities in the South African correctional system ([paras 32–37]).

## R – Rules
Several legal principles and precedents underpin the Court's reasoning:

1. **Constitutional Obligations**: Section **7(2)** of the Constitution requires the State to "respect, protect, promote, and fulfill the rights in the Bill of Rights," imposing both positive and negative duties on the State ([para 51]).
2. **Independence of Oversight Bodies**: Previous case law, notably **Glenister II**, recognizes that the State may be required to establish independent bodies where their absence undermines constitutional rights, highlighting that lack of independence may fail the reasonableness test ([paras 189–195]).
3. **International Law**: The principle of interpreting the Bill of Rights in light of international law under section **39(1)(b)**, as well as the application of international instruments such as the **Optional Protocol to the Convention Against Torture**, works to inform the standards for independence and oversight mechanisms in correctional facilities ([paras 60–66]).

## A – Application
The Constitutional Court analyzed the requirements for independence as follows:

1. **Adequate Independence**: The Court emphasized that independence encompasses both structural and operational aspects. The financial constraints imposed by sections **88A(1)(b)** and **91** were found to unduly tether the Inspectorate to the Department of Correctional Services, compromising its autonomy and effectiveness. For instance, the Inspectorate is compelled to account for financial expenditures directly to the Department it oversees, raising conflicts of interest and inhibiting effective oversight ([paras 104–131]).
  
2. **Referral Mechanism for CEO Issues**: The review of section **88A(4)** revealed concerns regarding perceived independence when misconduct by the CEO must be referred to the National Commissioner for action. This relationship was deemed non-threatening and thus constitutionally adequate in respect to operational independence because the Inspecting Judge retains ultimate control over the CEO's functioning. However, the High Court's conclusion that this process undermines independence did not align with the broader interpretation of structures already established to safeguard against undue influence ([paras 56–102]).

3. **Lack of Special Circumstances**: The dissenting judgment stressed that Sonke did not demonstrate the special circumstances required to substantiate the heightened obligations concerning inmate oversight, noting the existing mechanisms already in place to protect inmates' rights ([paras 184–195]).

## C – Conclusion
The majority of the Court confirmed the High Court's declarations of invalidity for sections **88A(1)(b)** and **91**, holding that they did not afford the necessary independence to the Judicial Inspectorate, consequently impeding its constitutional obligations regarding inmate oversight. This declaration was suspended for **24 months** to allow Parliament to remedy the identified deficiencies. 

Conversely, section **88A(4)** was found to be adequately interpreted as allowing the National Commissioner only an administrative role in misconduct matters, thus not undermining the Inspectorate’s independence and not found validly unconstitutional by the majority ([paras 132–136]).

The implications of this ruling underscore the constitutional requirement for adequate independence in bodies tasked with oversight in correctional services, affirming the necessity of both legal and financial autonomy to ensure the protection of inmates' rights in accordance with the Constitution and international standards ([paras 141–142]).
