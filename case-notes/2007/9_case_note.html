Fanuel Sitakeni Masiya v Director of Public Prosecutions (Pretoria)

Constitutional Court 2007 [2007] ZACC 9 ## F - Facts This case examines the constituti

Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa
Case Number: CCT 54/06
Judgment Date: 10 May 2007
Citation: [2007] ZACC 9

F - Facts

This case examines the constitutional validity of the common law definition of rape, particularly its exclusion of anal penetration and its gender-specific nature ([1]). The applicant, Mr. Fanuel Sitakeni Masiya, was convicted of rape in the Regional Court after being charged with the unlawful anal rape of a nine-year-old girl on 16 March 2004 ([6]). The Regional Court acknowledged the constitutional obligations to develop common law to align with constitutional values. Masiya was convicted based on the non-consensual anal penetration of the complainant ([10]).

The High Court upheld the conviction, finding that the current legal definition of rape, which only included non-consensual vaginal penetration, failed to reflect the serious nature of all forms of sexual penetration and violated the rights to dignity and equality as embedded in the South African Constitution ([3], [15]). Charges against Masiya were thus confirmed, alongside a declaration of constitutional invalidity regarding the gender-specific provisions in the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 and the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 ([2], [60]).

I - Issues

The primary legal questions to be addressed include:
(a) Whether the common law definition of rape is inconsistent with the Constitution and should be developed to include anal penetration;
(b) Whether Masiya can be convicted under a developed definition of rape;
(c) Whether the declarations of invalidity of the statutory provisions should be confirmed;
(d) How the court should approach the merits of Masiya's criminal conviction;
(e) The appropriate relief for Masiya and the constitutional implications of such relief ([19]).

R - Rules

In this case, several constitutional principles were cited, including:

  • Section 10 of the Constitution ensuring the right to dignity;
  • Section 9(1) affirming equality before the law;
  • Section 12, focusing on bodily integrity and the right to be free from violence;
  • Section 39(2) requiring every court to promote the spirit and objects of the Bill of Rights in interpreting law;
  • Sections 172 and 173 outlining the courts' powers to invalidate legislation inconsistent with the Constitution ([14], [18]).

The court also referenced historical perspectives from Roman law, English law, and the legal evolution of the definition of rape in response to societal changes and values ([20]-[29]).

A - Application

The court recognized that the existing definition of rape, which excludes non-consensual anal penetration, fails to protect and reflect the inherent rights and dignity of victims of such acts ([32]). The court emphasized the importance of evolving legal definitions, stating that the focus should be on the trauma and violation experienced by the victim rather than on outdated patriarchal constructs ([21]).

The court deliberated on whether extending the definition of rape to include anal penetration would infringe upon Masiya’s rights under section 35(3)(l) [principle of legality], which states that no one should be convicted for an action that was not a recognized crime at the time it was committed ([47]). The court ultimately concluded that the development of the common law to include anal penetration should be prospectively applied only, avoiding any violation of Masiya’s fair trial rights ([52]).

Furthermore, the court did not find it appropriate to expand the definition to include male victims in this case, highlighting the necessity of respecting the existing social context where vulnerability significantly affects women, thereby suggesting that male rape would require separate consideration in future cases ([30]-[31]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ruled that the common law definition of rape is constitutionally invalid insofar as it does not include acts of non-consensual anal penetration. The definition was extended to encompass such actions while retaining its gendered framework for the time being. Masiya’s conviction was adjusted from rape to indecent assault, which aligns better with the legal framework at the time of his offense. This decision reinforces the commitment of the judiciary to adapt the common law to uphold the principles of dignity, equality, and justice rooted in the Constitution ([74]).

In conclusion, the court upheld the notion that the definition of rape must evolve to ensure comprehensive legal protections are afforded to all victims of sexual violence without diminishing existing protections for female victims. This case ultimately sets a precedent for further discussion on the expanding definitions of sexual offenses as it relates to both male and female victims, emphasizing the role of the legislature in ongoing legal reform.
