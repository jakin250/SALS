Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

### FIRAC Case Note: HBR Foundation v President of the Republic of South Africa

**F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–11])**

The Constitutional Court of South Africa heard a case involving the HBR (Hola Bon Renaissance) Foundation as the applicant and multiple government officials and entities as respondents. The applicant sought direct access to the court to interdict the President from announcing the local government election date and to establish a tribunal for investigating the removal of the Municipal Demarcation Board members. The Foundation argued that the Board had violated both the Constitution and relevant legislation related to municipal governance.

On 15 October 2010, the applicant had already applied to the Board for establishing a local municipality for Soweto, which currently falls under the City of Johannesburg's jurisdiction. The Board, however, indicated that discussions regarding municipal boundary changes had been suspended and that any requests for redetermination would resume following the upcoming local government elections in 2011. The applicant was advised that to proceed with the application properly, it needed to submit its request through the provincial government, not directly to the Board.

Despite this, the applicant expressed dissatisfaction with the Board's response, leading to demands for the Board's resignation and notices to various government officials, indicating the applicant’s intention to litigate if no response was received. The application to the Constitutional Court was formally lodged on 9 February 2011, just prior to the State of the Nation Address planned for that evening.

**I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 12–16])**

The key legal issues in this case revolve around:

1. **Direct Access**: Whether the applicant met the legal criteria for direct access to the Constitutional Court as per section 167(6)(a) of the Constitution. This involves determining if it is in the interests of justice to grant direct access and whether the applicant demonstrated the necessity for such a bypass of lower courts ([para 12]).

2. **Authority and Jurisdiction**: The issue of whether the Municipal Demarcation Board had the authority to act on the applicant's requests for redetermining municipal boundaries without the proper legal request from the provincial government ([para 16]).

3. **Grounds for Tribunal**: Whether the applicant established sufficient grounds for the establishment of an independent tribunal to investigate the alleged misconduct of the Board members and the Board's failure to act on the applicant's requests ([para 17]).

**R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 12–18])**

The court applied several legal principles and statutory provisions:

1. **Direct Access**: Based on the criteria set forth in section 167(6)(a) of the Constitution, which allows direct access only when it is shown to be in the interests of justice to do so ([para 12]).

2. **Municipal Demarcation Act**: Section 22 of the Local Government: Municipal Demarcation Act (Act No 27 of 1998) was pivotal here, explaining the procedures available for requesting boundary changes ([paras 6–8, 16]).

3. **Removal of Board Members**: Section 13 of the Municipal Demarcation Act outlines that Board members can only be removed for misconduct, incapacity, or incompetence, based on findings from an appointed tribunal. The applicant had alleged these grounds but failed to demonstrate that the Board's actions met these criteria ([para 17]).

4. **Cost Order Principles**: The general rule in constitutional litigation, particularly regarding costs against the state, was cited from the precedent in *Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources, and Others*, noting that unsuccessful private parties in constitutional matters typically do not pay costs ([para 19]).

**A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 12–19])**

The Court first assessed the merits of granting direct access, noting that the applicant had not adequately demonstrated the necessity of avoiding lower court processes and failed to substantiate why such access was in the interests of justice ([para 14]). Furthermore, the Court pointed out that direct access is permitted only under exceptional circumstances if the prospects of success are evident. Given the specifics of the case, the applicant couldn’t show that its request for direct access had a substantial basis for success ([para 15]).

Addressing the core issue of authority, the Court clarified that the Municipal Demarcation Board could not autonomously establish a municipality, as that power resides with the provincial government. The applicant's failure to route the request correctly through the Gauteng provincial government hindered its application, as it bypassed established legal procedures ([para 16]).

The Court also considered the allegations of misconduct against the Board members, concluding the applicant's claims did not fulfil the legal thresholds for establishing an inquiry into their conduct. The Board's response, which merely indicated procedural requirements, did not constitute misconduct as envisioned under the law for initiating tribunal investigations ([paras 17–18]).

**C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 19–20])**

In its decision, the Constitutional Court dismissed the application, concluding that the applicant had not established sufficient grounds for direct access or for the relief sought concerning the Board’s alleged misconduct. The ruling reaffirmed that the boundaries of municipalities must be addressed through proper legal channels, thereby not allowing arbitrary bypassing of established legislative processes ([para 20]).

While the decision reflected the importance of compliance with administrative protocols and legislative frameworks in municipal governance, it emphasized the Court's reluctance to engage in matters best suited for lower courts unless compelling reasons were presented. Consequently, the decision carried significant implications for how municipalities and local governance matters are litigated, reinforcing the principles of lawful process and administrative accountability in South Africa.
