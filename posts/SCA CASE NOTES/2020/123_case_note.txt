Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# Case Note: Hattingh v Furman and Others NNO (Case no 388/2019) [2020] ZASCA 123 

## FIRAC Analysis

### F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–3])

This appeal arises primarily out of a dispute regarding the validity of a Buy and Sell Agreement (BSA) entered into between Carl Frank Hattingh (the appellant) and the late Milton Lawrence Weinbren, concerning the purchase of a 25% interest in Air and Allied Technologies Close Corporation (the CC). The underlying issue also involves an Addendum to their Memorandum of Agreement (MOA) signed on 29 August 2013, which purported to cancel the MOA and effectively remove Hattingh’s interest in the CC ([para 1]).

Weinbren passed away on 31 October 2016. Shortly after, Hattingh claimed and received R15,829,833 from a life insurance policy taken out on Weinbren’s life, pursuing such claim under clause 4 of the BSA. Following Hattingh’s claim, the executors of Weinbren's estate (the first to fourth respondents) initiated proceedings against him, arguing he was required to surrender the insurance proceeds based on the contentions surrounding the BSA ([para 2]). The High Court ruled in favour of the plaintiffs, leading to Hattingh seeking to appeal the decision ([para 3]).

### I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 4–5])

The key legal question revolves around whether the BSA was validly terminated by virtue of the AMA when Hattingh allegedly withdrew from the business according to clause 13.1.3 of the BSA. The court must also address whether the AMA constitutes a simulated transaction and whether it has legal effect on the contractual obligations derived from the BSA, which involves life insurance policies ([paras 4–5]).

### R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 6–12])

The governing laws in this context include the Close Corporations Act 69 of 1984, particularly concerning the validity of member interests and the importance of proper registration of such interests as per section 29 ([para 21]). The court will rely on principles of contract law, particularly regarding the interpretation of contractual terms, and simulation, where the actual intent of the parties is examined against the formal scope of the agreements.

Previous case law relevant to simulated transactions includes *Zandberg v Van Zyl* 1910 AD 302, which discusses the definition and handling of simulated or disguised transactions in contractual arrangements ([paras 83–85]). The principles established in *Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service v NWK Ltd* [2010] ZASCA 168 further elucidate the standard for courts to ascertain the true intentions behind agreements purportedly structured to evade legal obligations ([para 52]).

### A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 13–56])

The court's analysis focused on the interpretation of what constitutes 'withdrawing from the business.' Hattingh’s defense claimed that the signing of the AMA effectively terminated the BSA due to his alleged withdrawal from the business. However, evidence pointed to the fact that Hattingh continued to perform his duties in the business in his capacity as a general manager, which contradicted the assertion of having withdrawn from the CC ([paras 66–70]).

The proposed cancellation via the AMA hinged on financial motives, namely to protect Hattingh’s member interest from claims by his late wife’s heirs. Evidence from witnesses disclosed that despite signing the AMA, there existed agreements and discussions indicating that Hattingh could still reclaim his interest, signifying that the intent behind the AMA was not to genuinely divest Hattingh’s interest ([paras 88–90]). This led to the conclusion that the AMA was indeed a simulated transaction aimed at concealing real interests from outside scrutiny, particularly from Blackburn’s estate ([paras 95–96]).

The court deemed that a correct understanding of withdrawal encompassed cessation of membership and that Hattingh's continued engagement with the business nullified the claim of termination of the BSA due to withdrawal. Thus, the BSA remained enforceable ([paras 78–79]).

### C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 57–80])

The court upheld the contrary findings from the High Court regarding the non-termination of the BSA, concluding that Hattingh had not effectively withdrawn from the business despite the signing of the AMA. Consequently, the appeal was granted, overriding the previous ruling and dismissing the plaintiffs’ claims against Hattingh. The ruling reiterated that fraudulent intent inherent in simulated agreements impedes any party's attempt to enforce rights stemming from such transactions ([paras 56–80]).

The implications of this ruling reaffirm the importance of genuine intent behind contractual agreements and underscores that the law will not permit parties to mischaracterize agreements to evade obligations—especially concerning family claims in estate matters.
