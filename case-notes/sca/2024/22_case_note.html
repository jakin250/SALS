FIRAC Case Note: Queen Sibongile Winnifred Zulu v Queen Buhle Mathe and Others [2024] ZASCA 22

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 22 ### F – Facts The case arises from disputes

FIRAC Case Note: Queen Sibongile Winnifred Zulu v Queen Buhle Mathe and Others [2024] ZASCA 22

F – Facts

The case arises from disputes following the death of King Goodwill Zwelithini kaBhekuzulu Zulu (the "late Isilo") and involves multiple members of the Zulu Royal Family. Queen Sibongile Winnifred Zulu (the "appellant Queen") appeals a decision from the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court that dismissed her application for declaratory relief. She sought to establish her marriage to the late Isilo as a civil marriage under the Marriage Act 25 of 1961, in community of property, and to argue that this marriage precluded the late Isilo from contracting customary marriages while it was in force ([2]–[4]).

The appellant Queen and the late Isilo were married in community of property on 27 December 1969. Despite this civil marriage, the late Isilo entered into customary marriages with the second respondent, Queen Shiyiwe Mantfombi Dlamini (deceased), and the first, third, fourth, and fifth respondents ([3]–[4]). The respondent Queens admitted the validity of the civil marriage but contended that it did not preclude the late Isilo from entering into further customary marriages ([8]).

The high court found no practical effect in granting the appellant’s declaratory application, noting that disputing the validity of the customary marriages was not part of her pleaded case. Consequently, it dismissed her application with costs, leading to the appeal ([17]–[29]).

I – Issues

  1. Did the High Court exercise its discretion properly in refusing to grant the declaratory relief sought by the appellant Queen?
  2. Was the appellant Queen's assertion that the validity of customary marriages was moot justified?
  3. Did the absence of a 'live dispute' necessitate the dismissal of the appellant's application?
  4. What are the legal implications of the Marriage Act 25 of 1961 concerning the late Isilo's ability to contract further marriages?

([9]–[28]).

R – Rules

  1. Declaratory Relief: The granting of declaratory orders is discretionary and governed by §21(1)(c) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which requires that an applicant must show an interest in an existing, future, or contingent right or obligation applicable to the case ([11]–[12]).
  2. Common Law and Statutory Framework: The Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998 (the "Recognition Act") recognizes valid customary marriages entered into under customary law and confirms their legal standing regardless of parallel civil marriages ([10]).
  3. Mootness Doctrine: Courts do not grant declaratory relief based on moot or academic issues; a tangible advantage or a compelling reason must exist for the request ([14]–[16]).
  4. Discretionary Considerations: Courts consider various factors before exercising discretion in declaratory relief applications, including whether the applicant has established a clear legal right and whether the matter is ripe for adjudication ([11], [17]).

A – Application

The court analyzed whether the high court's dismissal of the appellant Queen's application constituted an erroneous exercise of discretion. It evaluated the existing facts, including the admission of the civil marriage's validity and the fact that no case had been made against the validity of the customary marriages.

The court determined that the admissions from the respondent Queens rendered the initial dispute moot; hence, the appellant Queen could not establish a clear right for the court to decide ([10]–[25]). The appellant Queen's argument that the absence of an existing or concrete dispute was not a jurisdictional requirement for the granting of declaratory relief was acknowledged. However, the court reaffirmed that without a dispute, there were no live issues upon which the court could effectively rule ([19]).

Given the circumstances, including the late Isilo’s valid customary marriages as recognized under the Recognition Act, the court found no reason to interfere with the high court’s decision ([23]–[28]). Thus, the necessity of a declaratory order was questioned, as it was ultimately deemed abstract and did not yield tangible benefits for the appellant Queen ([27]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal, agreeing with the high court's reasoning, dismissed the appeal and upheld the lower court's order. The court ruled that the high court had properly exercised its discretion by denying the declaratory relief sought by the appellant Queen, culminating in the conclusion that the matter had become moot due to the absence of disputable facts relating to the validity of the marriage or the customary unions of the late Isilo. The court ordered the appellant Queen to bear the costs of the appeal, which included the costs of two counsels due to the complexity of the case ([29]).

In essence, the implications of this ruling reinforce the standing of the Recognition Act concerning customary marriages and the discretionary principles surrounding declaratory relief. This case will serve as a pivotal reference in future cases concerning similar disputes within family law and customary law frameworks in South Africa.
