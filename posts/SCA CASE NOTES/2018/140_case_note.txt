Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: Centre for Child Law & Others v Media 24 Limited & Others

## F - Facts
This case originates from a prior decision of the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, regarding the anonymity of child victims in criminal proceedings ([1]). 

The primary appellant, under pseudonym KL, was abducted shortly after birth and only found at the age of 17. Her story attracted significant media interest, raising concerns over her privacy and identity ([3]). The appellants—various child law organizations—sought to have the High Court declare that the protections of *section 154(3) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977* (CPA) should extend to child victims of crime and continue to protect their identities into adulthood ([1][4]). 

Historically, the CPA offered anonymity to accused persons and witnesses under 18, but did not extend this protection to child victims ([4]). The High Court ruled that while the section applied to child victims, it ceased to protect them once they turned 18. The appellants appealed this decision and the media respondents cross-appealed the High Court’s ruling that the section protected the identities of child victims ([2][4]).

## I - Issues
The central legal issues include:
1. Whether *section 154(3)* of the CPA should provide anonymity protection to child victims of crime under the age of 18.
2. Whether this protection should extend indefinitely beyond the age of 18 ([6][39]).
3. If the current wording of the CPA violates the South African Constitution, particularly regarding the rights of children as guaranteed under *section 28(2)* ([12][30]).

## R - Rules
The court evaluated multiple legal principles concerning the constitutionality of the CPA:
- **Section 28(2) of the South African Constitution** states that a child's best interests are paramount in all matters concerning the child ([50]).
- **Open justice principle**, which supports public access to court proceedings, found in sections of the CPA and relevant case law ([56][89]).
- **Proportionality analysis** under *section 36* of the Constitution, which allows for rights to be limited provided such limitations are reasonable and justifiable in a democratic society ([15][30][85]).
- Case precedent from *Johncom Media Investments Ltd v M & Others* ([14][77]) and *Teddy Bear Clinic for Abused Children* ([65][69]) affirmed the necessity of robust protections for children in legal matters.

## A - Application
The court found that the limitations of *section 154(3)* do not provide adequate protection to child victims of crime. The existing law's failure to include child victims in its protections was deemed irrational and unconstitutional as it violates the principle of equality established by *section 9(1)* of the Constitution. Importantly, it highlighted the disproportionate impact on child victims compared to accused persons or witnesses ([29][94]).

In considering the plausible extension of anonymity into adulthood, the court emphasized that experiences in childhood could have lifelong implications, thereby justifying the need for ongoing protection ([10][12][96]). The appellants had posited that the understanding of victimization and the psychological effects could persist into adulthood, advocating for a reading into the CPA to enforce this protective layer ([30][84]).

Ultimately, the court reasoned that while the media's right to freedom of expression must be balanced against the need for protection of child victims, this does not warrant an absolute ban but rather effective safeguards that can be adaptable to specific circumstances as they arise ([27][56]).

## C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal by the appellants but upheld the cross-appeal to the extent of declaring the constitutional invalidity of *section 154(3)* of the CPA in its failure to protect child victims of crime ([2][95]). The new provisions mandated that Parliament must rectify this failing within 24 months. While protection for anonymity will persist, the court limited this to the necessity of further legislative action rather than an immediate restoration of comprehensive protections ([35][104]).

Consequently, the ruling outlines the imperfections within current legislation and calls for legislative reform that acknowledges and safeguards child victims' identity, balancing it against the rights of media in a transparent democratic society.
