Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

### Case Note on *Ekurhuleni West College v Segal and Another* (1287/2018) [2020] ZASCA 32

#### FIRAC Method

---

#### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])

The appellant, Ekurhuleni West College (the College), is a public educational institution established under the Continuation of Education and Training Act 16 of 2006. The College engaged Trencon Construction (Pty) Ltd (the second respondent) to construct a conference center through a written building contract. Following the practical completion of construction, disputes arose between the College and Trencon, leading Trencon to seek adjudication from Mr. Stanley Harold Segal (the first respondent).

In response to the adjudicator’s decision, which favored Trencon, the College sought to review and set aside the adjudication ruling in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria. The High Court dismissed the application, supported Trencon’s counter-application to enforce the adjudicator’s determination, and granted costs against the College. The College was granted leave to appeal, leading to this judgment in the Supreme Court of Appeal.

---

#### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 10–12])

The primary legal issues before the court included:

1. Whether the College was entitled to bring a review application despite having referred the dispute to arbitration.
2. Whether natural justice principles applied to the adjudicator's decision-making process.
3. The validity of the High Court's findings concerning procedural fairness and the substantive merits of the adjudicator's determinations.

---

#### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 13–16])

The court considered various rules and legal principles:

- The JBCC Adjudication Rules, particularly those concerning procedural requirements and adjudicator responsibilities, established that adjudication serves as an expedited resolution process, with decisions binding until overturned by arbitration ([para 3]).
- The principles governing judicial review, as articulated in *Wahlhaus and Others v Additional Magistrate, Johannesburg*, 1959 (3) SA 113 (A), limit the high court's review powers to rare instances where grave injustice might result or where justice cannot be achieved by other means. Such powers should not be routinely exercised in the context of uncompleted proceedings.
- The concept of natural justice was understood in the context of contractual obligations and the specific rules governing adjudication, where parties must adhere to the agreed process unless there are clear grounds showing unfairness ([paras 14–15]).

---

#### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 11–23])

The court first examined ground (a) for the dismissal of the review: the College's election to refer the disputes to arbitration, which was viewed as a mutually exclusive remedy. The court noted that the adjudication process was not complete; thus, allowing a review would disrupt the intended dispute resolution process established in the agreement and the JBCC Rules.

In addressing ground (b), the court recognized that the principles of natural justice, albeit generally applicable, were not inherent in the contractual framework unless specifically incorporated. The adjudicator had operated within the bounds of the contractual terms and procedures, and any claims of procedural unfairness by the College had no merit. 

Ground (c) was less central to the judgment but addressed the inherent scope of judicial review, emphasizing that review focuses on the procedural integrity rather than substantive correctness of the adjudicator’s decisions. The court acknowledged that no gross procedural unfairness was demonstrated that would warrant the review.

The court also examined whether the conditions for a rare exercise of review powers had been met, concluding they had not been. The initial determination by the adjudicator was reaffirmed as it was subject to further scrutiny in arbitration without causing irreparable harm.

The Court ultimately held that since the adjudicator's decision was always subject to reassessment in arbitration, the County’s recourse to judicial review prior to the arbitration's completion lacked sufficient justification.

---

#### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 24–24])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court’s ruling and dismissed the appeal with costs, including those for two counsel. The court concluded that the College’s review application was misplaced, primarily because the adjudication process had not been completed. The court reaffirmed the principles of contractual adherence, highlighting the necessity for parties to respect the processes they have collectively agreed upon.

The judgment establishes significant precedent regarding the relationship between adjudication and arbitration in construction contracts, reinforcing the notion that challenges to interim decisions are generally inappropriate during the pendency of agreed dispute resolution processes. The implications extend to the contractual obligations of parties in managing their disputes efficiently and the limited scope of judicial intervention in interim adjudicative findings.
