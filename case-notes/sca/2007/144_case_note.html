Bid Industrial Holdings (Pty) Ltd v Strang [2007] SCA 144 (RSA) - FIRAC Method

The High Court dismissed the application, ruling that the appellant failed to present a prima facie case for jurisdictional arrest and under the ruling of S v Mhlungu, where it was advised to avoid constitutional questions when possible ([paras 6–8]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2007

F – Facts

The appellant, Bid Industrial Holdings (Pty) Ltd, a South African company based in Johannesburg, sought to initiate legal action against two Australian citizens, John Francis Roderick Strang and Andrew John Donald Strang, for delictual damages regarding alleged wrongful acts involving a joint venture partnership ([paras 1–3]). The respondents, being directors of companies with interests in Southern Africa, opposed the appellant's application for their arrest to establish jurisdiction in the Johannesburg High Court. They contended that the appellant had not shown a prima facie case for the claim and that their arrest would contravene their constitutional rights as foreign nationals ([paras 4–5]).

The High Court dismissed the application, ruling that the appellant failed to present a prima facie case for jurisdictional arrest and under the ruling of S v Mhlungu, where it was advised to avoid constitutional questions when possible ([paras 6–8]). The case was appealed, and the Supreme Court of Appeal examined the constitutionality of the requirement for arrest to found or confirm jurisdiction concerning foreign defendants in light of the Bill of Rights ([para 59]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue before the Court was whether the common law principle requiring the arrest of a foreign defendant to establish jurisdiction amounted to a breach of the respondents' rights under the South African Constitution ([paras 23–24]), specifically concerning rights to freedom, dignity, and fair trial. Additionally, the issue of whether the relevant statutory provision, section 19(1)(c) of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959, infringed constitutional rights and was consequently unconstitutional was also raised ([paras 4, 19]).

R – Rules

The constitutional framework relevant to this case comprises:

  • The right to freedom and security of the person as enshrined in section 12(1) of the Constitution, which prohibits arbitrary arrest and detention ([para 32]).
  • The right to a fair trial provided under section 34 of the Constitution ([para 44]).
  • The limitation of rights in terms of section 36, which must satisfy requirements of reasonableness and justifiability in a democratic society ([para 45]).
  • The common law doctrine, which until this case mandated arrest to establish jurisdiction over foreign defendants, drawing from historical practices ([paras 24, 25, 30]).

A – Application

The Court analyzed whether the historical requirement of arrest was constitutionally justifiable. It determined that simply securing jurisdiction through arrest did not ensure an effective remedy or judgment for the plaintiff ([para 39]). The Court underscored that arrest, especially if deemed symbolic or without the possibility of ensuring a judgment’s enforceability, could not serve as a valid justification for limiting fundamental rights ([paras 40, 41]).

Furthermore, the Supreme Court of Appeal acknowledged the significant implications of this case for other situations involving resident plaintiffs against foreign defendants, thereby necessitating a definitive judicial stance ([para 16]). The Court highlighted that alternative means to establish jurisdiction, such as service of summons while the defendant is physically present in South Africa, could satisfy constitutional requirements without infringing rights to freedom ([paras 56, 59]).

The judgment overetailed that the common law requirement of arrest for jurisdiction must be abolished as it could not meet the limitations test under section 36 and failed to align with the Bill of Rights ([para 59]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled that the common law requirement of arrest to establish jurisdiction was unconstitutional as it violated multiple entrenched rights guaranteed by the Constitution, including the right to freedom and security of the person ([para 59]). The Court determined that jurisdiction could be established through alternative means, without infringing constitutional rights. This judgment has significant implications for future cases involving foreign defendants in South Africa as it necessitates a reformulation of jurisdictional principles and practices within the legal system ([para 46, 61]). The appeal was dismissed, with costs ordered against the appellant, reflecting the Court's position that the application did not advance a constitutional right or public interest ([para 65]).

This legal case note comprehensively addresses the FIRAC method and offers insights into the doctrine of jurisdiction in relation to constitutional law, emphasizing its implications on future litigation involving foreign nationals in South Africa.
