Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# Case Note: Chapeikin v Mini (103/2015) [2016] ZASCA 105 
## FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts
Ms Loretta Charmaine Mini (“Ms Mini”) suffered a stroke on April 17, 2007, while at work. She consulted Dr. S Chapeikin, who misdiagnosed her condition as hypertension and anxiety, failing to refer her for hospital treatment. The following day, she consulted Dr. J Sher, who diagnosed her with a mild stroke but similarly did not refer her to a hospital. Ms Mini's condition deteriorated, resulting in hospitalization on April 20, 2007, where she was diagnosed with complete hemiplegia and required extensive rehabilitation. Consequently, she initiated a claim for damages against both doctors in the Western Cape Division of the High Court, alleging negligence for failing to appropriately diagnose, treat, and refer her for necessary hospital care ([paras 1–5]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues revolved around the alleged negligence of both doctors regarding their failure to diagnose Ms Mini's stroke correctly and to refer her adequately for specialized treatment. The questions at hand were whether the appellants deviated from the standard of care expected of general practitioners, and whether any negligence established by the court causally linked to Ms Mini's subsequent sequelae ([paras 23–24]).

### R – Rules
The established legal principles guiding this case were rooted in the South African doctrine of delict, particularly concerning negligence. The test for negligence follows the precedent set in **Kruger v Coetzee**, which delineates that a reasonable person in the defendant's position would foresee the possibility of injuring another and would take reasonable steps to avert such injury. Additionally, the court clarified that in medical malpractice cases, a practitioner must adhere to the standard of care established by their profession ([paras 25–27]). Furthermore, for causation, the court applied the *but-for test*, determining whether the harm would not have ensued but for the negligent conduct ([paras 48–49]).

### A – Application
The court analyzed the conduct of both Dr. Chapeikin and Dr. Sher against the prescribed standards of care within the medical field. While the High Court found the doctors negligent, the Supreme Court of Appeal highlighted discrepancies in the evidential foundation regarding the classification of Ms Mini's stroke as a "stroke in evolution." Testimony from medical experts, particularly Dr. Kesler, argued that even if the doctors had referred her, the treatment she would have received would not have significantly altered her outcome, given the nature of her condition ([paras 29–30], [34–55]).

Consequently, the Supreme Court emphasized the absence of a direct causal connection between the alleged negligence of the doctors and the sequelae suffered by Ms Mini. Even accepting that they may have failed in their duties, the actual causation as per the *but-for* test did not hold since the stroke's progression could not be directly linked to their misdiagnosis or lack of referral ([paras 50–62]).

### C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that both Dr. Chapeikin and Dr. Sher acted within reasonable bounds of a general practitioner’s duty of care. With no firm causative link established between their actions and Ms Mini's injuries, the appeal was granted, with the High Court’s ruling set aside. The court ordered that Ms Mini’s claim be dismissed with costs. This outcome underscores the complexities involved in medical malpractice cases, particularly when distinguishing between misdiagnosis and causative factors in medical treatment outcomes ([paras 63–66]).

---

This case reinforces the significance of medical standards in negligence claims and the complexities of establishing causation in delictual liability, especially in circumstances involving evolving medical conditions.
