# Case Note: Macleod v Kweyiya (365/12) [2013] ZASCA 28

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The case of **Macleod v Kweyiya** revolves around a claim for damages stemming from a motor vehicle accident that occurred on **30 January 1988**, leaving the respondent, **Babalwa Kweyiya**, paralyzed at the age of four ([1]). The appellant, **Charles Robert Macleod**, a practicing attorney, represented Kweyiya's mother in a damages claim against the insurers of the involved vehicles, specifically the statutory insurer of a Valiant and a Toyota. The claim commenced with summons issued on **26 March 1993**, with an initial claim amount of **R25,000** related to the Valiant and **R870,220** for damages from the Toyota ([1]).

The claim settled partially on **18 March 1996**, with the Valiant insurer paying R25,000 and agreeing that the insurer for the Toyota would cover 35% of any proven damages after that. The ultimate settled amount, including a later increase, totaled **R124,500**, which was accepted by Kweyiya's mother, the guardian at the time ([2]).

On **5 March 1998**, the appellant provided a detailed accounting of funds related to the settlement and the establishment of a house in Khayelitsha registered in Kweyiya's name. The claim's intricacy deepened when Kweyiya, at approximately **25 years old**, issued summons against Macleod on **8 April 2009**, alleging negligence linked to the settlement amount and claiming damages totaling around **R4.7 million** ([5]).

Central to this case was the issue of prescription under the **Prescription Act 68 of 1969**, particularly questions of when the respondent had acquired knowledge of the debtor's identity and the facts related to her claim ([7], [9]).

### I – Issues
Two primary issues emerged for determination:

1. **Whether the respondent knew or could reasonably have known the identity of the debtor and the essential facts from which her debt arose before April 2006**. This consideration interrogates the limitations of prescription and whether Kweyiya could have exercised reasonable diligence ([7]).

2. **Whether any adverse inference should be drawn from Kweyiya's failure to testify regarding her state of mind or her actions leading up to the issuance of the summons in April 2009**. This question addresses the evidentiary burdens and the implications of her choice not to provide evidence ([7]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework governing this appeal is primarily set out in the **Prescription Act 68 of 1969**, notably sections **11(d)** and **12(3)**, which stipulate the conditions under which civil debts prescribe and the role of knowledge in this process. Section 12(3) provides that a debt is not deemed due until the creditor has knowledge of the debtor's identity and the underlying facts, establishing a standard of both actual and constructive knowledge ([9]).

Constructive knowledge, a pivotal aspect in this case, requires that a creditor be regarded as having knowledge if it could have been acquired through the exercise of reasonable care, emphasizing a standard of diligence expected from the creditor under specific circumstances ([9]). The court in **Gericke v Sack** clarified that while a defendant bears the burden of proof for a plea of prescription, such burden can shift to the claimant once a prima facie case has been established ([10]).

Further, section **12(3)** underscores that the innocent inaction rather than negligent inaction is to be protected, thus framing the assessment of the respondent's behavior concerning her claim ([13]).

### A – Application
The court applied these principles to establish that the appellant, Macleod, bore the onus to demonstrate that Kweyiya had at least constructive knowledge of her claim against him prior to **April 2006**. In examining whether Kweyiya could have reasonably learned the relevant facts earlier, the court considered her age, the context of her disability, and her reliance on the judgment of her mother and the appellant, the attorney ([15]).

The appellant's argument suggested that since Kweyiya’s mother was privy to the settlement terms and that she lived with her, it followed that Kweyiya should have acquired such knowledge as well ([8]). However, the court recognized that Kweyiya, particularly at a young age, had no reason to suspect wrongdoing and had relied on the perceived integrity of the adults involved ([14]).

In responding to the evidentiary burden, the court found that the absence of testimony from Kweyiya did not warrant an adverse inference against her because the underlying facts were largely undisputed and the onus remained with the appellant ([11]). Moreover, the respondent's assertion that she first became aware of the settlement terms in **April 2006**, and insight into possible negligence only when consulting her attorneys in February **2009**, were taken as reasonable ([12]).

Ultimately, the court framed the delay in bringing forth the claim as innocent, noting that Kweyiya's belief in the sufficiency of the settlement was reasonable based on her circumstances and the information available to her ([15]). It established that the prescription period did not begin until she had adequate knowledge of her claim, ruling that prescription only started on **19 April 2006**, when she first became aware of the necessary information regarding the alleged negligence ([15]).

### C – Conclusion
The **Supreme Court of Appeal** dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming the lower court’s decision that the respondent's claim had not prescribed. This ruling illustrates the court's commitment to ensuring that statute limitations do not unjustly penalize individuals whose circumstances hinder their capability to assert claims promptly. By emphasizing the need for constructive knowledge to initiate the prescription period, the court reinforced the protections afforded to individuals who rely on the integrity and professional conduct of their representatives.

The implications of this case extend to the broader understanding of the protective mechanisms within the **Prescription Act**, particularly in cases involving minors or individuals with diminished capacity. It highlights the necessity for courts to assess knowledge and diligence requirements contextually, ensuring fairness in the pursuit of justice in civil claims arising out of personal injuries.
