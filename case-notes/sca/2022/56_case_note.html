Supreme Court of Appeal

Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service v Sasol Chevron Holdings Limited (Case No 1044/2020) [2022] ZASCA 56

[2] The facts indicate that Sasol Chevron, a joint venture company lawfully registered in Bermuda, had purchased certain movable goods from Sasol Catalyst, a division of Sasol Chemical Industries (Pty) Ltd, intended for export to Nigeria.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 56

F – Facts

[1] This case arises from an appeal by the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (SARS) against a decision by the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which had ruled in favor of Sasol Chevron Holdings Limited (Sasol Chevron) on December 20, 2019. The high court had reviewed and set aside a SARS decision made on December 6, 2017, regarding Sasol Chevron's entitlement to a refund of Value Added Tax (VAT) associated with goods purchased for export.

[2] The facts indicate that Sasol Chevron, a joint venture company lawfully registered in Bermuda, had purchased certain movable goods from Sasol Catalyst, a division of Sasol Chemical Industries (Pty) Ltd, intended for export to Nigeria. These goods, specifically catalysts, were supplied on an "ex-works" basis and sold to Sasol Chevron for onward export to the Escravos Gas-to-Liquids Project (EGTL). As per Regulation 15(1) of the Export Regulations, such goods were required to be exported within 90 days of sale. Sasol Chevron, however, exported the goods only on April 24, 2015, subsequent to which SARS required VAT to be levied at the standard rate rather than the zero rate initially elected.

[3] Following the delay in export, Sasol Catalyst sought a ruling from SARS on January 30, 2015, seeking an extension of the 90-day export requirement, attributing the delay to various factors including bureaucratic hurdles in Nigeria. Despite some extensions being granted, SARS denied the request for others, leading to dissatisfaction from Sasol Chevron. After further correspondence, SARS reaffirmed on December 6, 2017, that Sasol Chevron was not entitled to the VAT refund. Sasol Chevron eventually initiated a review application under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA) on September 21, 2018, stating it acted within the prescribed 180-day period for judicial review.

I – Issues

[11] The central legal issue before the court was whether Sasol Chevron's review application was instituted within the 180-day limit set by section 7(1) of PAJA, which requires any judicial review to be initiated without unreasonable delay and not later than 180 days after being informed of administrative action.

[12] An ancillary issue was the interpretation of when the "clock" started to run based on SARS' communication about the administrative action. It involved determining whether the date of the letter from SARS providing reasons for their decision constituted the triggering event for the delay period.

R – Rules

[15] The governing legal standards are found in the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000, particularly s 7(1) and s 9(2). Section 7(1) states that proceedings for judicial review must commence not later than 180 days after the relevant administrative action or the date when the party concerned became aware of it.

[16] Section 9(2) allows for the 180-day period to be extended by agreement between the parties or upon a court application if the interests of justice necessitate it. The courts have established criteria for assessing whether these interests merit an extension, as discussed in Camps Bay Ratepayers and Residents Association v Harrison and other cases, emphasizing the need for a full and reasonable explanation of the delay.

A – Application

[14] The high court found that since SARS provided its reasons for the decision on March 26, 2018, Sasol Chevron's review application issued on September 21, 2018, was within the 180-day limit. This interpretation relied on the notion that the delay effectively began with the provision of reasons rather than the decision itself.

[24] In contrast, SARS argued that the high court misinterpreted the triggering date for the 180-day limit. The Commissioner contended that the crucial decision was made on December 6, 2017, and that all indications of administrative action had been clear from that date onward. Thus, the review application was filed significantly after the expiration of the 180-day period, rendering it void for lack of compliance with PAJA stipulations.

[42] The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that Sasol Chevron's application was indeed instituted after the time prescribed in section 7(1) of PAJA and that it had not sought an extension as required by s 9(2). Consequently, the high court's ruling that Sasol Chevron's action was timely was erroneous.

C – Conclusion

[46] The appeal was upheld with costs, including the costs of two counsel, and the court replaced the high court's order with a dismissal of Sasol Chevron's application for review. The overriding principle was that, in the absence of the required application for an extension of the 180-day period, the courts could not consider the substantive merits of the review application, as any delay over the statutory limit is pre-determined to be unreasonable per se.

The implications of this decision underscore the importance of adhering strictly to procedural requirements within PAJA, with significant ramifications for parties involved in administrative disputes. The judgment reinforces the expectation that parties must act diligently within set timeframes to preserve their rights under South African administrative law.

This case serves as a pertinent reminder of the necessity for clarity and timeliness in administrative dealings, delineating procedural thresholds that, when crossed, bar judicial recourse regardless of the merits of the case.
