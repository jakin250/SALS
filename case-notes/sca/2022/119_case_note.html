Supreme Court of Appeal

Minister of Social Development v SA Childcare (Pty) Ltd & Others (Case no. 71/2021)

The case centers on the issue of Early Childhood Development (ECD) facilities and the payment of subsidies by the South African national and provincial Departments of Social Development during the COVID-19 lockdown.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 119 (29 August 2022). Coram: Ponnan, Plasket

Neutral Citation:
Minister of Social Development v SA Childcare (Pty) Ltd & Others; MEC, Social Development, Eastern Cape & Others v SA Childcare (Pty) Ltd & Others [2022] ZASCA 119 (29 August 2022).

Coram: Ponnan, Plasket and Hughes JJA and Tsoka and Savage AJJA

F – Facts

The case centers on the issue of Early Childhood Development (ECD) facilities and the payment of subsidies by the South African national and provincial Departments of Social Development during the COVID-19 lockdown. SA Childcare (Pty) Ltd and seven other organizations initiated an urgent application seeking various declarations against the Minister of Social Development and the MECs for Social Development of eight provinces ([1]–[2]). The applicants contended that the governmental bodies had unlawfully withheld subsidies from ECDs, thus infringing upon the constitutional rights of children to life, nutrition, and social services ([22]).

The Gauteng Local Division of the High Court found that the Minister and the MECs had violated constitutional provisions concerning the payment of subsidies during the lockdown. The court ordered the Minister and MECs to rectify this situation but declined to grant a structural interdict as sought by the applicants ([3]). The appellants filed appeals, and it was agreed that the matter was moot given that the state of disaster had been lifted by the time the appeals were heard ([4]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues arose from whether the Minister and the MECs had breached constitutional obligations regarding the payment of subsidies to ECD facilities during a period of national disaster. Specifically, the court needed to ascertain:

  • Did the actions of the Minister and the MECs amount to a breach of the constitutional right to social assistance, basic nutrition, and the best interests of the child? ([1])
  • Were the facts presented by the applicants sufficient to justify the relief sought, given the respondents’ claims of compliance with statutory duties? ([21])

R – Rules

The court's analysis followed the principles established in previous case law regarding the constitutional obligations of the state to provide social assistance and protect the rights of children ([1]). Key statutory frameworks included:

  1. Constitution of the Republic of South Africa (Sections 27 and 28, particularly concerning children's rights to nutrition and assistance).
  2. Disaster Management Act 57 of 2002 – providing the legal basis for emergency measures and responses during national disasters.
  3. Division of Revenue Act 4 of 2020 – under which subsidies for ECDs are allocated ([7]–[8]).

The principles for resolving disputes of fact in application proceedings were primarily guided by the decisions in Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd ([18]) and Wightman t/a JW Construction v Headfour (Pty) Ltd and Another ([20]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) meticulously analyzed the evidence and arguments presented. It assessed the claims that existing subsidies were unlawfully withheld by evaluating both the factual context and the actions of the Minister and MECs during the national lockdown.

While the applicants argued the significant harm caused to children due to the alleged non-payment of subsidies, the respondents countered that ECDs were closed and that the payment of full subsidies was impractical under the circumstances ([38]–[40]). The Minister avowed that instead of the full subsidies, the provincial departments were authorized to pay a portion (60%) for administration and salaries while addressing children's nutritional needs through alternative measures ([41]).

The SCA found that the lower court had misdirected itself by focusing mostly on the applicant's narrative while neglecting the state's responses and evidence ([52]). The court concluded that the Minister's directives to pay subsidies had been conveyed and acted upon and that the claims of systemic malfeasance were not substantiated ([49]–[58]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the SCA upheld the appeal, ruling that the Minister and MECs did not breach their constitutional obligations regarding the subsidies for ECDs. The rescue efforts and reallocation of resources during the pandemic, including a focus on certain subsidies while recognizing the lockdown constraints, were deemed sufficient ([59]). The court emphasized that the relief sought by the respondents was not appropriate to rectify individual grievances but rather reflected a broader systemic claim without sufficient legal grounding.

The order of the lower court was set aside, and the application was dismissed ([60]). This result underscores the need for careful and contextual analysis of state obligations in times of crisis, affirming that while the state must act to protect children's rights, claims of dereliction must be substantiated with cogent evidence.

Implications

The decision has significant implications for the management of social assistance programs during states of national emergency. It reinforces the legal framework within which public officials operate and highlights the principle that decisions made during extraordinary circumstances must consider both the implications of immediate actions and the broader constitutional commitments to vulnerable populations.
