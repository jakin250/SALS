Supreme Court of Appeal

Kekana v The State (581/11) [2012] ZASCA 75

The appellant, David Malisela Kekana, was initially convicted by the Regional Court in Germiston for conspiracy to commit murder under section 18(2)(a) of the Riotous Assemblies Act 17 of 1956.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2012 [2012] ZASCA 75

F – Facts ([paras 1–11])

The appellant, David Malisela Kekana, was initially convicted by the Regional Court in Germiston for conspiracy to commit murder under section 18(2)(a) of the Riotous Assemblies Act 17 of 1956. Following this conviction, he was sentenced to ten years in prison. On appeal to the South Gauteng High Court, his conviction was amended to an attempt to commit murder, and his sentence reduced to five years ([para 1]).

The facts central to the case unfolded on January 23, 2006, when Kekana allegedly conspired with Sipho Gift Ndlovu to murder Frederick Ngoma, a co-worker who was appointed as a supervisor in the same department as Kekana within the Ekurhuleni Municipality, which led to animosity between them. Following his unsuccessful application for the supervisor position, Kekana expressed anger over Ngoma's appointment, threatening him and other colleagues ([para 2–3]).

The State's case primarily rested on the testimony of Ndlovu, who claimed he was approached by Kekana to assist in planning the murder. Ndlovu was allegedly offered R3000 to carry out the killing but purportedly feigned interest while intending to notify the authorities. As orchestrated, he contacted Ngoma, who subsequently informed the police, leading to Kekana's arrest during a follow-up meeting. The appellant denied these allegations, suggesting that Ndlovu and Ngoma had conspired against him ([para 4–5]).

The High Court ruled that the evidence supported a conviction for attempting to commit murder rather than conspiracy due to insufficient demonstrable agreement between the parties involved to achieve the objective of murder ([para 6]). Despite these findings, the High Court found that the trial magistrate had correctly determined the credibility of the witnesses, particularly relating to the evidence provided by Ndlovu, and the appeal court noted that despite contradictions in the witnesses' testimonies, they corroborated each other substantially ([para 7–10]).

I – Issues ([paras 7–11])

The principal issues addressed in this appeal were whether the evidence supported a conviction for conspiracy to commit murder, and if the High Court had appropriately assessed the reliability of the witnesses involved. Specific legal queries included:

  1. Whether the trial court misdirected itself in its findings on the credibility of the witnesses and the implications of witness evidence.
  2. Whether the sentence imposed was appropriate given the appellant's personal circumstances and the severity of the offence.

The core question revolved around the validity of the findings of fact by both the Regional Court and the High Court, particularly in relation to witness credibility and the merits of the alleged conspiracy ([para 8]).

R – Rules ([paras 8–11])

The Supreme Court of Appeal noted the established legal principle regarding the court’s limited powers to interfere with findings of fact made by a trial court, as articulated in S v Monyane & others 2008 (1) SACR 543, which requires identification of material misdirection to justify an alteration of factual findings. The absence of demonstrable misdirection precludes overturning the trial court's factual conclusions ([para 8]).

Additionally, the appeal court referenced S v Hadebe and Others 1997 (2) SACR 641, emphasizing the conferring of deference to trial courts in assessing witness credibility due to their direct interaction with the witnesses. The legal threshold for appeal is high, requiring not just interpretation but substantiated errors in the trial process ([para 8]).

Regarding sentencing, the court reiterated that it would not interfere unless the sentence was vitiated by a material misdirection or was disturbingly inappropriate, reinforcing the necessity of balancing the severity of the offence against mitigating personal circumstances ([para 11]).

A – Application ([paras 8–11])

Upon reviewing the trial court's decisions, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that there was no misdirection evident in the trial court's evaluation of Ndlovu's testimony. The record indicated that the magistrate had diligently assessed the credibility of witnesses and corroborated testimonies before concluding, thus supporting the High Court's determination of reliability and credibility within the trials ([para 9, 10]).

The court was unpersuaded by the appellant's version of events, particularly regarding the supposed conspiracy against him. Their scrutiny suggested that Ndlovu's evidence was internally consistent and corroborated by other witnesses, while Kekana's versions thereof lacked sufficient plausibility ([para 10]). The appellant's difficulty in providing a credible rationale for his actions diminished his claims of innocence, undermining his narrative ([para 9, 10]).

In terms of sentencing, while the mitigating factors regarding the appellant's character were noted, the court highlighted the severe nature of the crime and the societal implications of attempted murder. Emphasizing the community's need for a deterrent against such conduct, the court found that the sentence remained appropriate and that the appellant had not demonstrated remorse for his actions ([para 11]).

C – Conclusion ([paras 11–12])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal against both the conviction and the sentence. The court reaffirmed the trial court's factual findings and credibility evaluations, all while firmly establishing the seriousness of the appellant's conduct in attempting to arrange a murder. The absence of material misdirection at the trial level warranted deference to the magistrate's conclusions regarding witness credibility and overall case assessment.

The ruling underscores the high burden required for appeals concerning factual findings and the critical balance between individual circumstances and the gravity of criminal actions. As a result, the decision establishes a clear precedent that reinforces the judiciary's deference to lower courts in their appraisal of evidence and witness reliability within the context of serious criminal conduct.

This case note provides a comprehensive analysis that encapsulates the essential elements and legal principles of Kekana v The State, suitable for academic citation and judicial references.
