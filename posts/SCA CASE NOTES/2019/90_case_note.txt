# Case Note: DPP, Gauteng v Ramolefi (705/2018) [2019] ZASCA 90

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The case revolves around the appellant, The Director of Public Prosecutions of the Gauteng Local Division, Johannesburg, challenging a sentence imposed on the respondent, Pule Andrew Ramolefi, following his conviction for murder on 27 April 2013 ([para 1]). Initially sentenced to 15 years' imprisonment by the regional court—where no substantial and compelling circumstances warranted a lesser sentence as per the **Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997**—Ramolefi sought leave to appeal against both his conviction and sentence. The High Court granted him leave against the sentence only and later reduced it to five years' imprisonment, wholly suspended for five years, conditional upon no further offenses that would result in imprisonment without an option of a fine ([para 1–2]).

The procedural history revealed that while the respondent had initially not filed heads of argument, the issue of the appealability of the High Court’s order became central to the proceedings ([para 2]). It was established that jurisdiction concerns must be addressed before considering any other matters ([para 2]). 

### I – Issues
The core legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether the court has jurisdiction to determine an appeal against a sentence imposed by a High Court sitting as a court of appeal. This matter was particularly contentious given that prior jurisprudence, notably **Director of Public Prosecutions v Olivier**, had held that the State has no jurisdiction to appeal a sentence imposed by a High Court in such a capacity ([paras 3, 15]).

Furthermore, the appeal raised questions about the framework governing State appeals against sentence as articulated in the **Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977** and the implications of the **Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013** on existing legal doctrines regarding the appeal process ([para 3–4]).

### R – Rules
The principles governing the appeal rights of the State are rooted in the **Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977**. In particular, Section 316B(1) limits the State to appeal against sentences imposed by a Superior Court in its original jurisdiction only ([para 3]). This restriction was reaffirmed in **DPP Western Cape v Kock**, which addressed similar jurisdictional limits under the **Superior Courts Act**, emphasizing constitutional and policy considerations that restrict the right of the State to appeal ([para 4]).

Furthermore, as per **Director of Public Prosecutions v Olivier**, the SCA recognized that allowing the State an unlimited right to appeal against sentences raised potential constitutional issues ([para 3]). Additionally, the applicability of Section 311 of the Criminal Procedure Act was considered, which allows for appeals following decisions in favor of a convicted person where legal questions are involved ([para 16]).

### A – Application
In examining whether the SCA possessed jurisdiction to entertain the appeal, the Court reaffirmed the bindings of prior decisions, particularly **Olivier** and **Kock**: both emphasize that the State cannot appeal sentences where the High Court acted as an appellate body ([paras 3, 20]). Although the circumstances of Ramolefi differed from those in Kock—where a further appeal was at stake—the Court held that the fundamental issues of jurisdiction remained unaltered.

The Court recognized that the attempt by the State to argue for jurisdiction based on a mischaracterization of the High Court’s findings (especially around notions of diminished capacity) did not transform factual errors into legal questions warranting appellate review as per Section 311 ([paras 17–20]). The SCA concluded that the State's assertion of Fatigue over "diminished responsibility" versus "criminal incapacity" was critical, underscoring that the latter involves factual determinations rather than legal principles ([para 18]).

Overall, the Court emphasized that the approach taken by the High Court regarding Ramolefi’s level of responsibility when committing the crime was fraught with misdirection, undermining any grounds upon which a successful appeal could be maintained under the existing legal framework ([para 20]).

### C – Conclusion
The SCA ultimately concluded that it lacked jurisdiction to hear the appeal against the sentence imposed by the High Court, resulting in the appeal being struck from the roll ([para 21]). The Court articulated a firm stance on the need to respect the statutory limitations placed upon the State’s right to appeal against sentences, acknowledging potential gaps in the law that might require legislative attention but clarifying that remedial action would need to originate from the legislature, not the judiciary ([para 15]). The decision reflects a commitment to upholding established legal principles around jurisdiction and the constraints of prosecutorial appeals, thereby leaving the current restorative justice framework intact while also drawing attention to possible legislative reform in response to identified deficiencies.
