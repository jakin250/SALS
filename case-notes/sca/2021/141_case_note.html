Santam Limited v Ma-Afrika Hotels (Pty) Ltd & Another (2021) ZASCA 141

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021

F – Facts

This appeal arises from the judgments of the Western Cape Division of the High Court, where Santam Limited (the Appellant) was involved in a dispute with Ma-Afrika Hotels (Pty) Ltd and The Stellenbosch Kitchen (PTY) Ltd (collectively referred to as the Respondents) concerning business interruption insurance following the outbreak of COVID-19 ([1]). The Respondents sought a declaratory order regarding the indemnity periods applicable under their insurance policies with Santam, specifically, whether this indemnity period was to be interpreted as 18 months or three months ([2]).

The facts of the case outline that the Respondents held various insurance policies from Santam, which included coverage for losses due to business interruption linked to notifiable diseases, a category into which COVID-19 fell ([6]). These policies offered various indemnity sums for losses during the stipulated indemnity periods. After claiming losses due to COVID-19, the Respondents’ claims regarding certain policies were rejected by Santam, leading them to seek legal recourse ([8]).

The High Court ruled in favor of the Respondents, affirming that the indemnity period should indeed be set at 18 months, a decision that Santam subsequently appealed ([2]). The grounds for the appeal were narrowed down to the issue of the applicable indemnity period, particularly in light of an earlier court judgment (Guardrisk Insurance Company v Café Chameleon) clarifying that COVID-19 was a notifiable disease covered under similar insurance contracts ([2]-[3]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the indemnity period stipulated in the business interruption insurance policies extended to 18 months as claimed by the Respondents or was limited to three months, as contended by Santam ([3]). This raised several specific legal questions:

  1. How should the indemnity period be interpreted based on the language of the insurance policy?
  2. In what manner did the outbreak of COVID-19 and subsequent government measures interact with the insurance coverage?
  3. What constitutes the proximate cause of the business interruption losses—was it the local outbreak of COVID-19, the government’s responses, or both?

R – Rules

In determining the issues at hand, the court's analysis relied on established legal principles regarding the interpretation of insurance contracts, specifically favoring an interpretation that aligns with the parties' intentions and the purposes of the insurance ([46]). Key legal authorities and principles referenced include:

  1. Contra Proferentem Principle: This principle provides that ambiguities in insurance contracts should be resolved against the insurer, who has the duty to clearly outline exclusions and limitations ([25]).
  2. Factual and Legal Causation: The court evaluated both factual and legal causation concerning the business interruption claims, referencing the precedent set in Guardrisk, where the court held that government responses to COVID-19 were included within the insured risk ([31]).
  3. Interpretation of Coverage: Interpretation of insurance policy provisions should consider both the context and the plain language used to arrive at a commercially sensible understanding ([46]).
  4. Policy Terms: The specific wording within the insurance policy regarding indemnity periods and the definitions of covered events greatly influenced the court's ruling ([24]-[44]).

A – Application

The court engaged with the language of the insurance contract, particularly differentiating between the main business interruption cover and the infectious diseases extension ([19]). The main judgment emphasized that the initial schedule clearly stipulated an indemnity period of 18 months for business interruptions related to notifiable diseases, contrasting this with a memorandum that suggested a three-month limit for extensions ([49]-[58]).

The court rejected Santam’s argument that the indemnity period for the infectious diseases cover was merely an extension limited to three months, asserting that the specific inclusion of 18 months in the policy's core provisions took precedence and did not fall under the memorandum’s limitations ([25]).

Moreover, in addressing the causation debate, the court underscored that both the local outbreak of COVID-19 and the government’s lockdown measures were inseparable elements that contributed to the business interruption losses. Hence, it recognized that the insured peril encompassed both aspects ([27]-[32]).

Thus, the court concluded that the Respondents were indeed entitled to an indemnity period of 18 months given the direct connection between the insured peril (COVID-19) and the resultant business interruptions ([62]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Santam's appeal, affirming the High Court's ruling that the indemnity period applicable to the business interruption insurance was 18 months ([64]). The court's reasoning underscored the importance of clear contractual language in insurance policies and the necessity for insurers to explicitly define any limits on coverage. The judgment illustrates the judiciary's willingness to favor insured parties in cases of ambiguity while reasserting the significance of both local events and governmental responses in determining claims related to notifiable diseases.

The implications of this case extend beyond the immediate parties involved, reinforcing a precedent that acknowledges the complexities of insurance coverage in the context of unprecedented events such as the COVID-19 pandemic and promoting fairness in contractual interpretations affecting business continuity during crises.
