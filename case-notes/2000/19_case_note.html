Constitutional Court

Grootboom v Government of the Republic of South Africa and Others (CCT 11/00)

The case centers on the dire housing crisis in South Africa post-apartheid and the constitutional obligations of the government concerning access to adequate housing.

Constitutional Court 2000 CCT 11/00

F – Facts

The case centers on the dire housing crisis in South Africa post-apartheid and the constitutional obligations of the government concerning access to adequate housing. The respondents, led by Irene Grootboom, comprised 510 children and 390 adults living in extreme conditions in Wallacedene, a squatter settlement. Their living conditions included overcrowding, lack of sanitation, and inadequate shelter. In pursuit of better living conditions, they moved to a privately owned plot of land earmarked for low-cost housing, called "New Rust," without consent, leading to their eviction in May 1999 ([paras 3–9]).

Following the eviction, which was marked by brutality reminiscent of apartheid-era practices, the respondents sought relief from the Cape of Good Hope High Court, arguing that the state failed to meet its constitutional obligations under sections 26 and 28 of the Constitution of South Africa. These sections guarantee the right to adequate housing and shelter for children, respectively. The High Court ordered the state to provide temporary accommodation, including essentials such as tents and water supplies ([paras 4–5], [11–14]).

The appellants—the national, provincial, and local government representatives—appealed the High Court's decision, claiming they had made reasonable legislative and other measures to address the housing shortage. They emphasized that their housing programs prioritized long-term solutions given the constraints of resources ([paras 3, 14]).

I – Issues

The central legal disputes in the case are:

  1. Whether the state has fulfilled its constitutional obligation under section 26 to provide access to adequate housing for the respondents.
  2. The interpretation of section 28 concerning the state's duty to provide shelter to children and their parents.
  3. The justiciability of socio-economic rights outlined in the Constitution and how they pertain to the current housing crisis.
  4. The implications of the appellants' alleged inaction or insufficiency in addressing the urgent needs of vulnerable populations.

These issues underscore the key questions about the interpretation and implementation of pertinent constitutional rights in relation to socio-economic conditions ([paras 19, 93]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal provisions and precedents include:

  1. Section 26 of the Constitution of South Africa: Grants everyone the right to access adequate housing and mandates the state to take reasonable legislative and other measures, within its available resources, to achieve the progressive realization of this right ([para 19]).
  2. Section 28(1)(c) of the Constitution: Ensures that every child has the right to basic nutrition, shelter, basic health care services, and social services ([para 19]).
  3. Judicial Precedents: The interpretation of these rights was influenced by previous rulings stressing the justiciability of socio-economic rights, as established in Certification of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa ([para 20]).
  4. International Law: The court also considered international instruments, namely the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights, which informs the understanding of these rights and their minimum core obligations ([paras 26, 27]).

A – Application

The court's reasoning revolved around the interpretation of the obligations imposed on the state by sections 26 and 28. It emphasized that the Constitution requires active measures to address urgent housing needs, acknowledging that many citizens are living in crisis conditions. The court noted that while the national housing program was commendable in its overall framework, it had failed to accommodate individuals in immediate distress, such as the respondents who were denied a place to live after their eviction ([paras 45–66]).

Regarding section 28, the court rejected the notion that children have an unfettered right to demand shelter from the state, asserting that the primary obligation to provide shelter lies with parents. The obligations under sections 26 and 28 are interlinked; hence the court sought to harmonize the interpretation of these provisions. It determined that the state's failure to provide specific relief for those in critical conditions undermined its constitutional mandate ([paras 70–78]).

The evidence presented solidified the respondents' argument, as severe housing shortages existed in the Cape Metro, exacerbated by unemployment and poverty. The court highlighted that an established housing program must have the flexibility to cater to both immediate and long-term needs of the population ([paras 57–66]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court held that the state had not met its obligations under section 26 of the Constitution by failing to provide reasonable measures for people in desperate need. The existing housing programs were deemed inadequate because they did not address the urgent situation faced by the respondents. The court concluded that a program compliant with section 26(2) must provide immediate relief to those without shelter or living in intolerable conditions ([para 96]).

The Court allowed the appeal in part, emphasizing the state's responsibility to develop a comprehensive, coordinated housing program addressing immediate needs while also planning for future housing requirements. The order included that such measures must be monitored by the Human Rights Commission, ensuring compliance with constitutional obligations ([paras 97-99]).

This case represents a significant affirmation of socio-economic rights in South Africa, underlining an essential commitment to human dignity, equality, and the government's duty to provide for its citizens.
