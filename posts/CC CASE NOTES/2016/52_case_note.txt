Source Folder: 2016

# Case Note: Snyders and Others v de Jager [2016] ZACC 52

**FIRAC Method**

### F – Facts

The case revolves around a protracted legal dispute regarding the eviction of Karel Snyders (first applicant), Sofia Snyders (second applicant), and their minor children (third applicant) from a house on a farm managed by Louisa Frederika de Jager (respondent). The Snyders family had resided in the house continuously since 1992 ([2]).

Following the termination of Karel Snyders' employment in 2008, the respondent sought legal processes to evict the family, culminating in a 2012 Magistrate’s Court eviction order ([3]–[4]). This order was affirmed during a review by the Land Claims Court ([5]). However, the Snyders family attempted to appeal this ruling. On September 30, 2015, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) struck their appeal off the roll, compelling them to redirect their efforts back to the Land Claims Court ([5]).

On October 1, 2015, without providing any prior notice, the respondent executed the eviction order, resulting in the Snyders family being forcibly removed from their home and their belongings dumped in the street ([6]–[10]). Subsequent to this, the respondent permitted another family (the Bredas) to occupy the newly vacated house ([10]–[12]).

Mr. Snyders sought urgent interim relief from the Constitutional Court, requesting restoration of peaceful possession of the home ([16]), arguing that alternative accommodations were either unfit or insufficient for the needs of his family ([18]–[19]). 

### I – Issues

The primary issues before the Constitutional Court included:
1. **Whether the Snyders family had a prima facie right to interim relief** due to the manner of their eviction and the unavailability of suitable alternative accommodation ([22]).
2. **Whether the respondent's actions constituted an unlawful eviction without due process, violating section 26(3) of the Constitution** which protects against arbitrary eviction ([43]).
3. **The implications of procedural fairness**, particularly regarding the rights of the Bredas who subsequently occupied the house without being given an opportunity to be heard ([46]).

### R – Rules

The case is principally governed by the **Constitution of South Africa**, specifically section 26(3), which prohibits evictions without a court order after considering all relevant circumstances ([43]). 

Additionally, the **Extension of Security of Tenure Act, 1997 (ESTA)** is relevant as it provides specific guidance on the conditions and processes surrounding evictions of occupants on farms ([4]). The requirements for interim relief, established by previous case law, include:
(a) a prima facie right, even if open to some doubt;
(b) a reasonable apprehension of irreparable harm if the interim relief is not granted;
(c) a balance of convenience favoring the granting of interim relief; and
(d) the applicant having no other satisfactory remedy ([22]).

### A – Application

Zondo J, delivering the majority judgment, analyzed the facts in light of these rules. He concluded that the Snyders family demonstrated a prima facie right due to their long-standing occupation of the home and the abrupt nature of their eviction. The fact that they were living in inadequate conditions further illustrated the irreparable harm they faced if interim relief were not granted ([22]).

Despite the respondent’s argument that alternative accommodations were unsuitable for the Bredas, the Court noted that other vacant properties were available, which could have been offered to allow for a smooth transition back to the Snyders family ([20]). The lack of procedural fairness in the eviction process was highlighted, especially regarding the absence of notice and the refusal to discuss alternatives with the Snyders’ legal representation ([8]–[10]).

Cameron J dissenting, argued that the Court failed to fully consider the rights of the Bredas, who were not part of the proceedings and thus did not have an opportunity to present their circumstances ([46]). He expressed concern regarding the potential implications of enforcing the order without first hearing all affected parties.

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ruled in favor of granting urgent interim relief to the Snyders family, restoring their peaceful possession of the dwelling they had occupied for 17 years. The judgment emphasized the importance of following legal due process in eviction matters, reinforcing protections against arbitrary eviction ([34]).

The Court condemned the respondent’s actions as reminiscent of unjust evictions during apartheid, showing a clear disapproval of the manner in which the eviction was carried out ([31]). Additionally, costs were awarded against the respondent on a punitive scale, marking the Court's disapproval of the conduct involved in the eviction ([32]–[34]).

This case serves as a vital precedent highlighting the necessity for procedural fairness in eviction processes, reinforcing the significance of providing all affected parties an opportunity to be heard in accordance with constitutional provisions.
