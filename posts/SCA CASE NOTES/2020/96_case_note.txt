Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# Case Note: *Samancor Chrome Ltd v VDH Holdings (Pty) Ltd and Others* [2020] ZASCA 96

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The case involves an appeal by *Samancor Chrome Limited* (hereafter referred to as "Samancor") against a decision of the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, which had set aside the Minister of Mineral Resources' grant of a mining right to Samancor. The appeal is against the decision in which the High Court found that Samancor failed to properly notify and consult with interested and affected parties as required by section 22(4)(b) of the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA) ([1]-[4]).

The facts presented reveal that Samancor is contending for mining rights to substantial chrome resources across several farms in Limpopo Province. The Minister granted the mining right to Samancor on 31 October 2016, following a lengthy process during which Samancor had obtained prospecting rights in accordance with the conditions set out in the MPRDA ([12]-[22]).

The essence of the dispute arises from a series of procedural interactions involving Samancor, the Minister, and various communities purportedly affected by the mining operations as advocated by the first and second respondents—*VDH Holdings (Pty) Ltd* and *Absolute Group Management (Pty) Ltd*—who sought to have the decision of the Minister overturned, claiming insufficient consultation ([3]-[4]).

### I – Issues
The principal issues considered by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) in this case include:
1. Whether Samancor met its obligations of notification and consultation with interested and affected parties under section 22(4)(b) of the MPRDA ([1]-[4]).
2. The adequacy of evidence presented regarding the consultation process undertaken by Samancor with the affected communities.
3. Whether the High Court made an error by setting aside the Minister's decision in light of the information available to it ([18]-[24]).

### R – Rules
The SCA's reasoning is rooted in the interpretation of section 22(4)(b) of the MPRDA, which stipulates that upon acceptance of a mining application, the Regional Manager must notify the applicant to consult with relevant landowners, lawful occupiers, and any interested and affected parties. The object of the MPRDA is to promote the orderly and sustainable enhancement of mineral resources, which underscores the importance of involving affected communities in the decision-making process ([2], [25]).

Furthermore, reliance was placed on the definitions provided in the act concerning 'community' and 'interested and affected person,' which clarify the broad obligation on mining companies to consult with all parties who may be affected by mining operations ([25]).

### A – Application
The SCA conducted an extensive analysis of the environmental management programs (EMPR) and public participation processes executed by Samancor. The court found that despite criticisms from the respondents about the consultation process (including claims of inadequate attendance at meetings and failure to consult thoroughly with the Kgoshi Kgolo), Samancor had undertaken genuine attempts to notify and consult appropriately ([26]-[34]).

It was noted that the consultation efforts included meetings with local communities, engagement with traditional authorities, and documentation demonstrating that responsive measures were taken. Despite the respondents' claims, the SCA found that no affected individuals came forward to contest the legitimacy of the consultation process. This was a critical finding, reinforcing Samancor's position that it satisfied its obligations under the MPRDA ([34]-[39]).

The government's silence on the matter—particularly the Minister's non-participation in either court proceedings—was noted as an extraordinary factor that shifted the burden to the Minister to clearly articulate the rationale behind decisions regarding consultation and public interest ([10], [40]).

### C – Conclusion
The SCA upheld Samancor's appeal, reversing the High Court's order and reinforcing that the Minister's decision to grant the mining right was well-founded given the efforts made by Samancor to comply with the legislative obligations. The SCA emphasized that the right to consultation requires individuals to engage actively with the process, not merely to be notified. In conclusion, the court found that since no affected persons had demonstrated a failure of consultation, Samancor's right to mine remained intact. The decision also clarified future expectations regarding compliance and community engagement under the MPRDA ([40]).

This case underscores the weight of procedural integrity in administrative decisions within the context of environmental management and community rights associated with mining resources in South Africa. The implications for the mining sector are significant, illuminating the necessity for clear standards in community consultation practices to secure mining rights efficiently and legally.
