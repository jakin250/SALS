Masilo v Betterbridge (Pty) Ltd (37/2015)

The central issue is whether the withdrawal of the claim impacts the ability of the appellants to invoke the delay in the completion of prescription as provided under section 13(1)(g) of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 ([paras 1–4]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 73 (25 May 2016) Court: The Supreme Court of

Neutral Citation: Masilo v Betterbridge (37/2015) [2016] ZASCA 73 (25 May 2016)

Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

Coram: Cachalia, Seriti, Willis, and Mbha JJA, Victor AJA

F – Facts

The case arises from a dispute regarding a claim by the appellant creditors (Michael Mmathomo Masilo No, Philippus Giovanni Torre No, and Monica Cowin No) against Betterbridge (Pty) Ltd, a company that was undergoing liquidation. The claim was previously admitted to proof under section 44 of the Insolvency Act 24 of 1936, but subsequently withdrawn by the appellants before the presiding officer at the meeting of creditors determined whether to formally admit or reject the claim.

The central issue is whether the withdrawal of the claim impacts the ability of the appellants to invoke the delay in the completion of prescription as provided under section 13(1)(g) of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 ([paras 1–4]). The court a quo (the lower court) had dismissed a special plea by the defendants, asserting that the claim had prescribed ([para 1]).

I – Issues

The primary legal question before the court was whether the appellants' withdrawal of their claim before the determination of its admissibility negated any benefits from the protection against prescription provided by section 13(1)(g) of the Prescription Act. Specifically, the issue to be resolved was whether a creditor must fully participate in the claims process under the Insolvency Act to benefit from the delay in prescription ([paras 3–4]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal framework consists of:

  • Insolvency Act 24 of 1936: This Act outlines the procedure for claims against a company undergoing liquidation, particularly section 44, which governs the admittance of claims.
  • Prescription Act 68 of 1969: Section 13(1)(g) establishes that the completion of prescription is delayed for debts the object of claims filed against a company in liquidation when those claims have been admitted to proof ([para 1]).

Judicial precedents interpreting these sections note the significance of being ‘admitted to proof’ in establishing the legal status of a claim ([para 4]).

A – Application

The court assessed the appellants' argument concerning the necessity of ongoing participation in the claims process to benefit from the delay in prescription. The appellant’s contention—that merely withdrawing the claim negated its status as an ‘object of a claim filed’—was deemed erroneous.

The court held that the impediment to the completion of prescription under section 13(1)(g) takes effect as soon as the claim has been 'admitted to proof', which, in the present case, had indeed occurred before the claim was withdrawn. Thus, the withdrawal after the claim was acknowledged did not nullify its admitted status, and prescription remained delayed ([paras 4–5]).

Additionally, it was noted that the new argument raised by the appellants was not presented in the court a quo, thereby diminishing its weight, emphasizing the judicial principle that parties must plead their defenses coherently from the outset ([para 4]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court's judgment that the claim had not prescribed. The ruling underscored the principle that once a claim is admitted to proof, the creditor retains the benefit of protection against prescription, notwithstanding later withdrawal from the process. The court clarified that a withdrawn claim still retains its status for the purpose of prescription, as long as it was admitted prior to withdrawal ([paras 5–6]).

The implications of this decision reaffirm the importance of procedural safeguards within insolvency law, protecting creditors' rights in the context of prescribed debts. Hence, creditors need to understand that their claims, once admitted, remain protected from prescription even in cases of subsequent withdrawal before formal adjudication.
