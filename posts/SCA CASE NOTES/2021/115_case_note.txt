Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# FIRAC Case Note: Padayachee v The State (679/2020) [2021] ZASCA 115

## F – Facts
This case concerns an appeal by Mr. Michael Raju Padayachee against decisions made by the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court, Pietermaritzburg, which refused him leave to appeal against his convictions and sentences imposed by the regional court in Verulam. Mr. Padayachee was convicted on multiple counts: rape (count 2), assaults with intent to do grievous bodily harm (counts 3 and 6), and attempted murder (count 7), resulting in an effective sentence of 24 years’ imprisonment, as none of his sentences were ordered to run concurrently ([1]).

Initially, Mr. Padayachee sought leave to appeal against these convictions and his resultant sentence. His application for condonation for the late filing of this appeal was rejected by the regional court on 9 February 2018. Following this, he brought applications to the Full Court for leave to appeal against both the refusal of condonation and directly against his convictions and sentence. These were also denied on 25 June 2019 ([2]).

Subsequently, on 31 July 2020, Mr. Padayachee petitioned the Supreme Court of Appeal for special leave to appeal the Full Court's decision, which the court granted. The state did not oppose his application for condonation regarding the late filing of this request ([2]).

## I – Issues
The central legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether there were reasonable prospects of success on appeal against Mr. Padayachee's convictions and the resultant sentences. This entailed a determination of both procedural aspects relating to the granting of leave to appeal as well as the substantive merits of the convictions ([4]).

The specific questions included:
1. Did the Full Court err in refusing leave to appeal based on the merits of the case?
2. Are there contradictions and inconsistencies in the evidence that could undermine the convictions ([5])?
3. Was there a potential misdirection in the imposition of the cumulative sentences resulting in excessive punishment ([6])?

## R – Rules
The relevant statutory provisions include Section 309C of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which governs the processes for appeals against conviction and sentence, and Section 16 of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which deals with special leave to appeal ([2]). 

The court referenced its earlier decision in *S v Khoasasa*, which held that the refusal of leave to appeal is considered a ‘judgment or order’ under the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959. Furthermore, the applicable test for the granting of leave to appeal is whether there exists a reasonable prospect of success, rather than whether the appeal is likely to succeed ([3]).

Precedent cases, including *S v Van Wyk* and *S v Tonkin*, were also considered to provide guidance on what constitutes reasonable prospects of success ([4]). The focus of these precedents lies on evaluating evidence and assessing possible contradictions or shortcomings in the trial proceedings.

## A – Application
The court closely analyzed the evidence presented during the trial, particularly since the appellant's counsel asserted that inconsistencies existed in the accounts given by the State witnesses. Furthermore, it was argued that crucial witnesses were not called by the State, which could have clarified significant aspects of the case. The trial court's evaluation of the testimony offered by the sole witness regarding the rape and second assault was also critiqued as inadequate ([5]).

The court noted that, upon reviewing the record, there were sufficient grounds to believe that the appellant could demonstrate a reasonable prospect of success in an appeal against both his convictions and sentence. The State conceded that reasonable prospects existed, indicating a recognition of potential errors in the original proceedings ([5]). 

In terms of the individual sentences, the court examined whether the aggregate sentence imposed could be viewed as overly harsh given the circumstances—specifically, determining if there was a misdirection in the trial court’s sentencing discretion. The lack of concurrent sentences raised concerns that another court might well interpret the sentence as unduly punitive ([6]).

## C – Conclusion
In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Mr. Padayachee's appeal against the refusal of leave to appeal, ruling that he was granted permission to appeal against his convictions and resultant sentences. The court's judgment emphasized the existence of reasonable prospects of success in the envisioned appeal, thus setting aside the previous court's order. The implications of this decision allow for a thorough examination of the original case by the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court, potentially leading to a reconsideration of both the factual basis for the convictions and the fairness of the imposed sentences ([7]).

The court’s decision highlights the importance of fair trial rights and the accessibility of appellate review in instances where serious criminal convictions are involved, thereby reaffirming the judicial system's commitment to justice and due process.
