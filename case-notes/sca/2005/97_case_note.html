Supreme Court of Appeal

The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa (Case No: 467/04)

This matter arises from a protracted dispute initiated by Archibald Barry Nichol (the first appellant), a pensioner formerly contributing to the Sage Schachat Pension Fund (the second appellant), regarding a merger of pension funds.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2005 1956 ([8]). The Pretoria High Court dismissed his application to exempt him from the requirement to exhaust internal remedies as stipulated in section 7

FIRAC Method Analysis

F – Facts

This matter arises from a protracted dispute initiated by Archibald Barry Nichol (the first appellant), a pensioner formerly contributing to the Sage Schachat Pension Fund (the second appellant), regarding a merger of pension funds. His opposition centers on the alleged improper amalgamation of pension funds, which he contended diluted the surplus of the Sage Schachat Fund—more favorable than the other funds involved ([1–2]).

The dispute began following management restructuring within the Sage Group in 1997, leading to a decision in August 1998 to amalgamate the Sage Schachat Fund and other funds. This decision was formally enacted on 1 December 1998, but retrospective approval from the Registrar did not occur until 18 December 2001 ([3–6]). Nichol, feeling aggrieved by the lack of consultation in this process, lodged a complaint with the Pension Funds Adjudicator (sixth respondent) in April 1999. This adjudicator declared that the merger lacked legal validity pending Registrar approval, which was provided retrospectively only after Nichol’s complaint ([4–5]).

Nichol subsequently launched judicial review proceedings in February 2002, seeking to set aside the Registrar's certification under section 14(1)(e) of the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956 ([8]). The Pretoria High Court dismissed his application to exempt him from the requirement to exhaust internal remedies as stipulated in section 7(2) of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA). This appeal, alongside a cross-appeal from the respondents regarding procedural issues, was subsequently heard in the Supreme Court of Appeal ([12–13]).

I – Issues

The key issue in this appeal pertains to the interpretation and application of section 7(2) of PAJA, specifically regarding whether Nichol could be exempted from the requirement to exhaust internal remedies before pursuing judicial review. The court needed to consider:

  1. What constitutes “exceptional circumstances” under section 7(2)(c) of PAJA?
  2. Whether Nichol had demonstrated any such circumstances justifying an exemption from internal remedy exhaustion ([1]).

R – Rules

Section 7(2) of PAJA provides:

  • (a) No court shall review administrative action unless internal remedies are exhausted.
  • (b) Courts must direct parties to exhaust remedies if not satisfied they have been exhausted.
  • (c) A court may exempt a person from this requirement in exceptional circumstances if it is in the interest of justice ([14]).

Judicial precedent has noted that the failure to exhaust internal remedies is a significant barrier to accessing judicial review and that exceptions to this rule are strictly interpreted ([15]). The court elaborated on “exceptional circumstances” as necessitating factors that would make it inappropriate to require exhaustion of internal remedies. General dissatisfaction with administrative action or allegations of bad faith do not typically suffice to meet this high threshold ([16–18]).

A – Application

In the application of these principles, the court examined the merits of Nichol’s arguments for demonstrating exceptional circumstances. It ultimately concluded that:

  • The FSB Appeal Board, which was a remedial option available to Nichol, possessed adequate jurisdiction and capabilities to address his grievances adequately ([20–22]). Therefore, his assertion that the internal remedy would not suffice was unconvincing as it ignored the board’s powers to thoroughly review the Registrar's decisions.
  • Nichol's various claims of procedural irregularities and bad faith were found not to constitute exceptional circumstances ([23–26]). Allegations of mala fides or procedural misconduct do not inherently warrant bypassing established legal frameworks unless sufficiently substantiated.
  • His contention regarding the expiry of appeal periods was found to be poorly substantiated and appeared to be a late addition to his arguments, lacking foundational support from his initial application ([30–34]). The court noted that even if overlooked initially, the respondents had offered assurances that a late appeal would be accepted, thereby negating potential arguments of exceptional circumstances rooted in timing [[32]].

Consequently, the court found no grounds to grant Nichol’s appeal based on the existing legal framework, reinforcing the importance of adhering to procedural requirements under PAJA.

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Nichol’s appeal with costs, holding that he was unable to demonstrate exceptional circumstances justifying an exemption from the obligation to exhaust internal remedies as per section 7(2) of PAJA. The court further struck the cross-appeal from the roll, acknowledging it lacked the requisite elements of appealability ([34–37]).

This ruling underscores the judiciary’s commitment to procedural rigor and the necessity for parties to engage with available administrative remedies before seeking judicial intervention, thereby reinforcing the principles laid out in PAJA. The decision elucidates the nature of exceptional circumstances, emphasizing a clear delineation between dissatisfaction with administrative decisions and the higher threshold required for exemption from procedural requirements.
