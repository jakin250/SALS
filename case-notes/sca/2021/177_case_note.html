Graham Robert Herbert N.O. and Others v Senqu Municipality and Others (Case no 742/2020) [2021] ZASCA 177

The Municipality's defense included arguments that the Upgrading Act, as amended, did not apply to the Trust owing to its historical involvement with apartheid-era land laws and its prior benefits from such racially discriminatory legislation ([5]–[6]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 177

F – Facts

The matter under consideration involves the TEBA Property Trust (hereinafter “the Trust”), which held a Permission to Occupy regarding Erf 88 in Sterkspruit, a property historically granted to its predecessors by the Governor-General under the Native Trust and Land Act 18 of 1936 ([1]–[3]). The Trust applied for conversion of this permission into full ownership under section 3 of the Upgrading of the Land Tenure Rights Act 112 of 1991 (the “Upgrading Act”). However, the first respondent, Senqu Municipality, opposed the application, asserting that the Upgrading Act had no legislative effect in the Transkei region at the time the permission was granted, thus excluding the Trust from benefits under the Act ([3]–[4]).

The Municipality's defense included arguments that the Upgrading Act, as amended, did not apply to the Trust owing to its historical involvement with apartheid-era land laws and its prior benefits from such racially discriminatory legislation ([5]–[6]). In prior proceedings, the High Court ruled that the exclusion of section 3 from the application of the Upgrading Act was unconstitutional ([7]–[8]). Ultimately, Roberson J of the Eastern Cape Division dismissed the Trust's application, ruling that the Trust did not fall within the intended class of beneficiaries for the Upgrading Act, thus disallowing its conversion rights ([9])—this decision led to the current appeal.

I – Issues

The core issues presented for determination by the Supreme Court of Appeal include:

  1. Whether section 3 of the Upgrading Act, as interpreted, excludes the Trust from the class of persons eligible to convert their Permission to Occupy into ownership.
  2. If it does exclude the Trust, does this exclusion render section 3 unconstitutional ([2]–[4], [9])?

R – Rules

The legal framework pertinent to this case includes the following statutory provisions and judicial interpretations:

  1. Upgrading of Land Tenure Rights Act 112 of 1991 (the “Upgrading Act”): Section 3(1) allows holders of certain land tenure rights to apply for conversion to ownership. Item 2 of Schedule 2 defines “land tenure right” as encompassing permissions to occupy land previously granted under the Native Trust and Land Act ([15]–[19]).
  2. Constitution of South Africa: Section 25(5) mandates the state to promote equitable access to land, while section 25(6) affirms the rights of individuals or communities whose land tenure is insecure due to past racially discriminatory laws ([12]–[14]).
  3. Case Law: The interpretation principles established in cases such as Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality [2012] ZASCA 13, DVB Behuising (Pty) Ltd v North West Provincial Government [2000] ZACC 2, and others recognized by the Constitutional Court form a crucial part of the interpretative lens ([10]–[11], [39]).

A – Application

The Court employed a purposive interpretation of the Upgrading Act, focusing on the historical context and objective of the legislation, which was aimed at addressing the injustices of apartheid land dispossession ([32]–[34]). The Court noted that the Upgrading Act was designed specifically to assist populations, primarily Black families, who suffered from inadequacies in land tenure resulting from past racially discriminatory practices.

The Trust argued that the use of the term “any” in section 3(1) implied eligibility devoid of categorization. However, the Court rejected this argument, emphasizing that such a reading would overlook the legislative intent to prioritize the restoration of land rights to those who were disadvantaged by apartheid. The Court asserted that the Trust was not in a position of disadvantage under the Native Trust and Land Act, thus falling outside the Act's intended beneficiaries ([29]–[42]). The argument for interpretation based merely on the statutory language was deemed insufficient when contextualized within the broader legislative aims concerning past injustices ([29]).

The Court further considered the implications of allowing the Trust's application, indicating that it would undermine the foundational goals of the Upgrading Act. The historical context demonstrated that the Trust benefited from the very system that the Upgrading Act sought to rectify ([41])—the Trust and its predecessors were implicated in the perpetuation of apartheid-era labor practices, casting doubt on their victim status ([42]).

C – Conclusion

The appeal was ultimately dismissed. The Supreme Court of Appeal affirmed the High Court's decision that the Trust did not belong to the defined category of beneficiaries intended by the Upgrading Act for the conversion of land tenure rights. The interpretive analysis confirmed that the Trust's prior affiliation with problematic legislative frameworks disqualified it from seeking benefits under the Upgrading Act, thus not rendering section 3 unconstitutional ([43]).

The ruling emphasizes the importance of context in statutory interpretation and the need for legislative frameworks to align with historical justice and transformation objectives. The implications of this decision underline the ongoing effort to redress past inequalities in South African land ownership, carefully delineating who is considered deserving of such rectification in light of historical complicity in discriminatory laws. This case reaffirms judicial commitment to equitable land redistribution and transformation in South Africa's post-apartheid landscape.
