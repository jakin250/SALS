Stopforth Swanepoel & Brewis Incorporated v Royal Anthem Investments 129 (Pty) Ltd and Others [2014] ZACC 26

Facts The case arises from a failed sale agreement concerning immovable property between Royal Anthem Investments 129 (Pty) Ltd (first respondent) and Yeun Fan Lau and Shun Cheng Liang (second and third respondents) ([1]).

Constitutional Court 2014 [2014] ZACC 26

1. Facts

The case arises from a failed sale agreement concerning immovable property between Royal Anthem Investments 129 (Pty) Ltd (first respondent) and Yeun Fan Lau and Shun Cheng Liang (second and third respondents) ([1]). The applicant, Stopforth Swanepoel & Brewis Incorporated, acted as the conveyancers in the transaction ([1]). The respondents paid a deposit of R720,000 and an additional R264,723 in transfer duty into the attorneys' trust account, as mandated by the terms of the sale agreement ([3]–[4]).

However, due to certain conditions of the sale agreement being unfulfilled, notably delayed registration of the property, the sale ultimately fell through ([5]). Subsequently, the respondents withdrew their action against the attorneys and pursued a claim solely against Royal, which the High Court granted ([6]). Royal appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal, notwithstanding the fact that the attorneys were not parties in that appeal. The Supreme Court of Appeal amended the restitution order, compelling the attorneys to repay the sums plus interest ([6]–[8]). The attorneys, aggrieved by this ruling, approached the Constitutional Court seeking leave to appeal ([9]–[10]).

2. Issues

The core legal questions addressed by the Court are:

  1. Whether the attorneys' right to a fair public hearing was violated by the Supreme Court of Appeal’s decision ([17]).
  2. Whether the Supreme Court of Appeal acted beyond its jurisdiction (functus officio) and had the power to amend its prior order against parties not involved in the appeal ([17]).
  3. Whether the communications addressed to the Supreme Court of Appeal by the attorneys were an appropriate means of seeking relief ([17]).

3. Rules

The Court relied on relevant provisions of the South African Constitution, particularly Section 34, which guarantees the right to access courts for fair public hearings ([19]). Furthermore, the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979 was cited, particularly Section 78(2A), detailing the obligations of attorneys regarding client funds ([4]). Importantly, principles of procedural and substantive fairness were invoked, including the audi alteram partem principle, asserting that all parties must be afforded a fair chance to be heard ([19]–[20]).

4. Application

The Constitutional Court noted that the Supreme Court of Appeal had effectively imposed liability on the attorneys who were absent from the proceedings. By making this order without hearing the attorneys, the Supreme Court of Appeal denied the applicants their right to be heard, thereby infringing their rights to a fair public hearing ([20]–[26]). However, while the actions of the attorneys were noted to be on the instructions of Royal, the determination wrongly assigned liability to the attorneys despite their non-party status ([21]–[22]”).

Furthermore, the Constitutional Court highlighted the potential consequences for attorneys acting in accordance with clients' instructions. The implications of requiring attorneys to bear liabilities above those accrued in trust accounts was deemed unjust and exceeded the powers of the appellate court ([30]).

The attorneys argued that the relief sought against them constituted an infringement of their procedural rights, and the Court concurred, stating that they had not only been excluded from the hearings but were also denied the ability to present their case ([26])—an essential requirement under the rule of law ([19]).

5. Conclusion

The Constitutional Court, therefore, granted leave to appeal and upheld the appeal, setting aside the decision of the Supreme Court of Appeal. The Court re-established that Royal Anthem Investments 129 (Pty) Ltd was wholly responsible for refunding the amount paid in relation to the sale agreement. The ruling underscored the critical importance of ensuring all parties receive a fair opportunity to be heard, affirming the procedural rights protected under Section 34 of the Constitution ([31]–[32]).

This case emphasizes the necessity for judicial processes to adhere strictly to principles of fairness and the need for courts to exercise caution when imposing liabilities on parties who are not present to defend themselves. The implications for legal practitioners acting in fiduciary capacities are significant, reinforcing the necessity for clarity in advisory roles concerning client funds and liabilities owed to third parties.
