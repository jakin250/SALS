Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2009

# FIRAC Case Note: Yellow Star Properties 1020 (Pty) Limited v MEC: Department of Development Planning and Local Government (Gauteng) 

**Case Citation:**  
Yellow Star Properties v Department of Development Planning and Local Government (549/07) [2009] ZASCA 25 (27 March 2009)

**Coram:**  
BRAND, MAYA, CACHALIA, MHLANTLA JJA and LEACH AJA

## F - Facts

This case arises from a series of legal disputes concerning the transfer of a property known as "the remaining extent of Erf 137 Dunkeld West" (the property) between Yellow Star Properties (the appellant) and the MEC for the Gauteng Department of Development Planning and Local Government (the respondent) ([paras 1–3]). 

In June 2000, the respondent mistakenly believed that the property belonged to the Gauteng Provincial Government and auctioned it, during which the appellant purchased it for R452,900. A formal deed of sale was executed, but no transfer of ownership occurred because it was later discovered that the property vested in the National Government, complicating the transfer process ([para 4]). 

Attempts by the respondent to facilitate the transfer were met with refusal from the National Government due to concerns regarding the low sale price relative to the property’s true value ([paras 5–10]). Subsequent legal actions initiated by the appellant were aimed at compelling the necessary transfer but ultimately yielded no successful results ([paras 11–15]). 

Finally, in August 2004, following the refusal of the respondent to transfer the property, the appellant claimed damages, initiating litigation for breach of contract and alternative delictual claims, alleging negligence by the respondent’s servants in facilitating the sale ([paras 16–18]).

The respondent raised special pleas of res judicata and prescription, claiming that the appellant’s action was time-barred and that the issues had already been conclusively determined ([paras 19–20]).

## I - Issues

The primary legal questions presented to the court concerned:

1. Whether the appellant's claims for breach of contract were barred by the principle of res judicata or issue estoppel given that the contract had been previously determined to be void ab initio ([paras 20–23]).
2. Whether the appellant's delictual claims had prescribed under the provisions of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 ([para 33]).
3. The validity of the earlier judgments implying the inability to enforce a contract deemed invalid ([paras 24–32]).

## R - Rules

The legal principles relied upon in this case included:

1. **Res Judicata** - This doctrine bars re-litigation of issues that have been conclusively determined in a previous judgment involving the same parties ([para 21]).
2. **Issue Estoppel** - A variant of res judicata, where a party is estopped from raising an issue that has already been decided ([para 22]).
3. **Prescription** - Governed by the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, which stipulates the time limits for bringing actions based on claims ([para 33]).
4. **Validity of Contracts** - A contract that is void ab initio cannot provide for any actionable remedies ([para 15]).

## A - Application

In evaluating the applicability of res judicata, the court noted that Smit J's ruling in a prior case had established that the sale to the appellant was legally invalid. The court engaged in a thorough examination of whether the earlier judgments could serve as binding precedents in the current case. It found that since the Minister of Public Works, who was not a party in the earlier litigation, became involved in the second case affecting the substantive issues at hand, the requirements of identity of parties and issues had not been fulfilled, thus undermining the appellant's argument ([paras 20–26]).

Regarding the prescription of the delictual claims, the court reiterated that the claims had arisen in mid-2000 when the breach occurred but were not brought before the courts until 2004. The court held that the appellant had sufficient knowledge of the facts giving rise to the claims from the respondent's communication in December 2000, marking the commencement of prescription ([paras 33–38]).

The appellant’s attempt to argue that the period of prescription should only commence after Smit J's judgment was rejected, as the court found that reasonable care could have led the appellant to discover the pertinent facts before that judgment ([para 37]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the special pleas presented by the respondent, affirming that both the contractual and delictual claims were barred due to res judicata and the prescription of claims. The court concluded that the proceedings initiated by the appellant had no merit, resulting in the dismissal of the application for leave to appeal with costs ([paras 39–42]). 

The ruling underscores the importance of proper procedural adherence when parties are engaging in litigation involving statutory limitations and previous judicial determinations, particularly emphasizing the consequences of engaging in litigation based on invalid contractual foundations. The implications of this case extend to the understanding of how legal principles interact within the context of public property and administrative law, particularly in relation to government entities and their authority over immovable property.
