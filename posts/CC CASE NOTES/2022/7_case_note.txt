Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note on *Commercial Stevedoring Agricultural and Allied Workers’ Union and Others v Oak Valley Estates (Pty) Ltd and Another* [2022] ZACC 7

## F – Facts
The case involves an appeal from the Labour Appeal Court concerning a strike initiated by the Commercial Stevedoring Agricultural and Allied Workers' Union (CSAAWU) at Oak Valley Estates (Pty) Ltd, a company operating in Grabouw, Western Cape. The strike, which began on May 6, 2019, was aimed at addressing grievances related to alleged racial discrimination in employee housing allocation and the classification of seasonal workers as temporary, which denied them fair pay ([paras 2–3]).

The initial strike involved 364 workers, but by the time of the court proceedings, the union represented only those who remained on strike, namely the second to 174th applicants. The Commission for Conciliation, Mediation, and Arbitration (CCMA) had established picketing rules limiting protests, which were violated, leading to intimidation, property damage, and unlawful conduct during the strike ([paras 2–6]).

Oak Valley, asserting that the strike had instigated unlawful conduct, sought a final interdict against not only the striking workers but also unnamed individuals associated with them, claiming that the conduct interfered with its business operations ([paras 8–9]). The Labour Court initially granted interim relief but, upon seeking a final interdict, faced objections that the broad relief sought was without sufficient cause ([paras 9–11]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issue was whether an employer can secure an interdict against employees striking in a protected strike without demonstrating a specific link between the employees and the alleged unlawful conduct. The Labour Appeal Court had ruled that such a link was not necessary, which the appellants challenged, asserting that a rational basis was required to connect specific individuals to specific accusations of unlawful conduct ([paras 1, 14]).

## R – Rules
The fundamental legal principles surrounding interdicts necessitate the establishment of three key requirements: 
1. The existence of a clear right.
2. Reasonable apprehension of irreparable harm.
3. Absence of similar protection through any other remedy ([paras 18–19]).

The judgment relies on established case law, particularly emphasizing that for an interdict to issue against individuals, there must be a causal connection between the respondents and the unlawful conduct. The requirement is grounded in principles that safeguard due process and protect against the potential chilling effects on lawful assembly and protest ([paras 17, 39, 42]).

## A – Application
The court analyzed whether Oak Valley had adequately demonstrated a sufficient link between the unlawful conduct associated with the strike and the specific respondents, the second to 174th applicants. The court scrutinized the allegations and evidence presented, highlighting several instances where Oak Valley failed to substantiate claims against individual workers.

1. **Failure to Establish Connection**: The evidence, including testimony and video recordings referenced by Oak Valley, was insufficient to directly associate the respondents with acts of violence, intimidation, or other unlawful conduct. The court identified that while collective acts may have occurred, individual culpability was not established ([paras 49–58]).

2. **Causation and Innocence**: The court emphasized the absence of a clear relationship between participation in a strike and the unlawful actions therein, echoing the established view in earlier cases that mere participation does not entail liability for violent or unlawful acts taken by others ([paras 40–42], [26]).

3. **Specific Cases**: However, the court noted that certain individuals, such as Mr. Nikelo Magaba, had made specific threatening remarks that created a reasonable apprehension of harm, justifying an interdict against him ([paras 62–64]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court concluded that the Labour Appeal Court erred in its interpretation by not requiring a logical connection between the respondents and the unlawful conduct alleged by Oak Valley. While the appeal was partially upheld, interdicts against CSAAWU and Mr. Magaba were sanctioned based on their active participation or provocation of unlawful acts. 

Thus, the judgment emphasizes the necessity of a factual basis when imposing legal consequences on individuals, reinforcing the rule of law in the context of labor rights and the inviolability of striking workers from broad punitive measures without adequate justification ([paras 68–69]). The court ultimately set aside certain reliefs granted by the Labour Appeal Court, except with respect to specific individuals, ensuring protection for workers engaged in lawful protests while protecting the rights of employers against unlawful conduct.
