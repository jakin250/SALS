Using the FIRAC Method: The Chairman of the Board on Tariffs and Trade v. Volkswagen of South Africa (Pty) Ltd

This note allowed manufacturers to cede specific amounts of foreign currency earnings to other customs and excise manufacturing warehouses, thereby enhancing their rebate claims.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2000

F – Facts:
This case revolves around the understanding and application of the provisions in the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 ("the Act") concerning rebates on excise duty for motor vehicles. The first respondent, Volkswagen of South Africa (Pty) Ltd ("VW"), is engaged in manufacturing and exporting motor vehicles. Under the previous legislation, VW could claim a rebate on excise duty, reliant on its foreign currency earnings from exports and fulfilling certain requirements, notably the provision of note 5(vi)(a)(ii) of rebate item 609.17 ([paras 1–2]).

This note allowed manufacturers to cede specific amounts of foreign currency earnings to other customs and excise manufacturing warehouses, thereby enhancing their rebate claims. However, this note was repealed on 1 September 1995 when the Motor Industry Development Program replaced the Phase VI Scheme. Before its repeal, VW faced a conflict over the interpretation of royalty payments that affected its foreign currency usage calculations and thus the rebate entitlement. Despite being aware of an amendment that limited what could be included as foreign currency usage, the Commissioner insisted on including all royalty payments. This resulted in VW not claiming the full extent of the rebate to which it believed it was entitled ([paras 3–6]).

After the note was repealed, VW sought permission from the Board on Tariffs and Trade ("the Board") to cede previously reported foreign currency earnings to another manufacturer. The Board refused to consider VW's application, reasoning that it lacked the legal authority to act under the repealed provision. This led to VW challenging the Board's refusal in the Transvaal Provincial Division of the High Court, which ruled in VW’s favor, prompting the Board’s appeal ([paras 8–11]).

I – Issues:
The primary legal questions in this case concern whether the repeal of note 5(vi)(a)(ii) effectively disempowered the Board from considering VW's application for a recommendation required for cession, and whether VW had an accrued or acquired right to approach the Board for such a recommendation post-repeal. Specific issues include interpreting the implications of section 12(2)(c) of the Interpretation Act 33 of 1957, which addresses the effect of repeals on rights accrued or acquired under the repealed law ([paras 12–18]).

R – Rules:
The court relied heavily on the provisions of the Interpretation Act, particularly section 12(2)(c), which states that the repeal of a law shall not affect any rights, privileges, or obligations acquired or incurred under that law unless a contrary intention appears. This informed the analysis of whether VW's right to cede its foreign currency earnings, along with the associated right to request a recommendation from the Board, survived the repeal of the note ([para 12]).

Legal precedents reaffirming the principle that repeal legislation primarily addresses future matters were also referenced, suggesting legislative intent does not usually extend to affecting rights or privileges that had already accrued ([paras 12–19]).

A – Application:
The court analyzed the nuances surrounding VW's dealings with the Commissioner regarding its foreign currency usage and the resultant impact on its rebate claims. VW's predicament arose from the Commissioner’s insistence on a broader interpretation of royalty payments in calculating foreign currency usage. This stance, which persisted until the repeal, created a situation wherein VW believed it lacked the surplus necessary to approach the Board for a recommendation under the now-repealed provision ([paras 5–8], [paras 13–19]).

Nienaber JA, in the majority opinion, argued that VW had satisfied all necessary preconditions before the repeal—essentially, it had accrued a right to approach the Board for a recommendation concerning its surplus foreign earnings based on its foreign currency earnings before the repealed note's deletion. The court posited that the Commissioner’s subsequent acknowledgment of VW's rightful eligibility to cede its earnings was a de facto recognition of VW's entitlement that had existed independently of the note ([paras 14–20]).

Harms JA, in his dissenting opinion, contended that VW could not claim an accrued right due to the conditional nature of its requests and the lack of a formal application or approval process prior to the repeal. This dissent focused on procedural rigidity, suggesting VW should have pursued a declaratory judgment or acted independently before the note's repeal ([paras 6–12], [paras 22–27]).

C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the Board had the obligation to consider VW’s application for a recommendation concerning the excess foreign currency earnings despite the repealing of note 5(vi)(a)(ii). The ruling underscored that VW's right to approach the Board for its recommendation was indeed accrued prior to the repeal and that the Board's refusal to act was erroneous ([paras 21–22]).

This case significantly impacts the interpretation of statutory rights in relation to administrative boards and the legislative process, illustrating that repeals primarily affect future applications rather than past accrued rights. It emphasizes the necessity for administrative bodies to act within the context of established legal entitlements, which remain enforceable even when superseded by new statutory provisions.
