Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# Case Note: City of Cape Town v Carelse and Others [2020] ZASCA 117

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The incident leading to this case involved the first respondent, Ms. Fatiema Carelse, who was attacked by a dog while visiting the Harmony Park Resort, a facility under the control of the City of Cape Town (the City) on 7 December 2013 ([1]). Fatiema sustained serious injuries and developed post-traumatic stress disorder from the attack. The dog was owned by the second respondent, Mr. Quinton Eksteen, and was brought to the Day Camp by the third respondent, Mr. Dylan Eksteen. 

Fatiema initiated a damages claim against the City in the Western Cape Division of the High Court, arguing that the City had negligently breached its duty to ensure visitor safety ([1]–[2]). The City acknowledged a duty of care but denied liability, asserting that the dog was unlawfully brought onto the premises, violating the City’s By-Laws, which prohibit dogs at such facilities, and that adequate warning was provided through prominently displayed signage ([2]–[3]). 

The High Court, presided over by Vos AJ, ruled that the City was liable for damages, holding that it failed to take reasonable precautions to prevent the incident ([4]). The City’s application for leave to appeal against this decision was subsequently denied ([5]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues revolved around whether the City was negligent and whether it had a legal duty to prevent the dog from entering the resort. The secondary issue concerned the liability of third parties involved in the incident ([5]).

### R – Rules
The court relied on established principles concerning negligence, particularly the standard of care owed by public entities. The courts referred to the test for negligence from *Kruger v Coetzee*, which requires that a reasonable person in the defendant’s position would foresee the possibility of harm and take reasonable steps to prevent it ([40]). Additionally, the court referenced prior cases emphasizing that a legal duty arises only if public policy considerations warrant it ([37]) as seen in *Hawekwa Youth Camp v Byrne*.

The court analyzed wrongfulness, determining that an omission may not be prima facie wrongful unless it is established that a legal duty exists ([37]). Judicial precedents included *Van Vuuren v Ethekwini Municipality* and *Country Cloud Trading CC v MEC Department of Infrastructure Development*, which provide frameworks for determining legal duties and wrongfulness involving public entities ([38], [45]).

### A – Application
In ruling on the issues, the court below found that while the City enacted By-Laws prohibiting dogs from entering the facility, it failed to enforce these provisions adequately at all access points. Signage warning against dogs was placed prominently at the primary entrance, but there were no such measures at the secondary entrances, where the dog entered ([38], [52]).

The High Court judged that the City’s decision to focus security measures primarily at one entrance while neglecting others was irrational and ineffective. The City had some knowledge of prior incidents involving dogs and, thus, should have taken greater precautionary measures, including the placement of security personnel at unmanned entrances and additional signage ([39]). 

In addressing the element of foreseeability, the court determined that given the historical instances of dogs entering the Day Camp, the City ought to have anticipated that a dog attack was likely. The court concluded that the City’s inaction constituted a breach of its duty of care ([41], [56]).

The court considered the actions of Quinton and Dylan, relating to the principles of third-party liability. Quinton was found partially liable under *actio de pauperie* due to direct ownership of the dog, while Dylan did not incur liability despite his role in bringing the dog to the Day Camp ([44], [58]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the decision of the Western Cape High Court, dismissing the City’s application for leave to appeal with costs ([44], [57]). The judgment reiterated the necessity for public entities to implement effective safety measures and enforce By-Laws diligently to protect the public from foreseeable dangers. The court’s holding communicates a strong message regarding the accountability of municipalities in ensuring visitor safety at public facilities, emphasizing that inadequate preventive measures could lead to liability for negligence.

The ruling clarifies existing legal standards concerning the duty of care and the expectations from municipalities, potentially setting a precedent for future cases involving public safety and liability.
