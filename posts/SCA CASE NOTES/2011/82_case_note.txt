Source Folder: 2011

# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of *Bidoli v Bidoli & another* (436/10) [2011] ZASCA 82

## F – Facts
The case *Bidoli v Bidoli & another* concerns a family feud among the three Bidoli brothers—Guido (the appellant), Romolo (the second respondent), and the late Fabrizio, whose estate is represented by Barbara Lieselotte Bidoli (the first respondent). The Bidoli family engaged in various business ventures, predominantly as building contractors in South Africa, Namibia, and Italy since 1960. The businesses were conducted under a partnership known as "Bidoli and Sons," which dissolved in 1995 ([1]).

In 2007, a settlement agreement was reached amidst ongoing disputes regarding financial dealings and property management, notably a block of flats in Rome, which had been registered in the names of the three brothers and later sold, with proceeds deposited in a joint bank account in Rome ([2]–[3]). Disputes arose regarding the distribution of these funds and other property rights, leading to a formal arbitration agreement under the Arbitration Act 42 of 1965, as the family sought to resolve their issues through arbitration rather than litigation ([3]).

On 7 December 2007, the brothers reached a settlement agreement. However, Romolo expressed dissatisfaction shortly thereafter, leading to a reopening of the arbitration process on 10 December 2007. The arbitrator ruled that he would adopt the settlement agreement as part of the official award, despite Romolo's objections ([4]–[5]). The subsequent application to enforce this arbitral award was made to the Western Cape High Court by Guido in February 2008, but Romolo countered this by claiming the award was void due to the arbitrator lacking jurisdiction post-settlement ([6]).

The High Court, presided over by Fourie J, held that the arbitrator’s mandate had terminated upon the conclusion of the settlement, rendering the award invalid ([7]). This decision prompted Guido to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([6]–[8]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issue involved in this case was whether the arbitrator had the authority to issue an award recording the terms of the settlement agreement after the parties had purportedly resolved their disputes. Specifically, the court had to consider if an arbitrator can still act after a settlement has been reached, or if the expiry of the arbitrator's mandate renders any subsequent award void ab initio ([7]–[8]).

A secondary issue arose regarding the interpretation and scope of the Arbitration Act 42 of 1965 compared to its English counterparts, and what common law principles surrounding arbitration apply to the facts at hand ([9]–[10]).

## R – Rules
The case's determination hinged significantly on statutory interpretation of the Arbitration Act 42 of 1965, particularly Section 31, which allows an arbitral award to be made an order of court ([6]). The court also considered common law principles regarding arbitration, particularly principles established in cases such as *Parekh v Shah Jehan Cinemas (Pty) Ltd* and discussions regarding whether an arbitrator can issue an award post-settlement ([11]–[12]).

The distinction between the South African Arbitration Act and the English Arbitration Act 1996 was pivotal, as the latter provides a statutory framework enabling arbitrators to record settlement agreements in a formal "agreed award," a provision absent in South African law ([8]–[9]).

## A – Application
In adjudicating the appeal, the Supreme Court of Appeal examined whether Fourie J had misinterpreted the arbitrator's powers. The court emphasized that the essence of arbitration is derived from the parties' consent, highlighting that both parties had requested the arbitrator to issue an award reflecting their settlement ([13]).

The appellant’s contention was that the arbitrator’s authority did not simply terminate upon the agreement of the settlement, particularly when the arbitration had formally commenced and a valid dispute existed prior to the agreement. The court highlighted that authority derived from the initial arbitration agreement remained until a final award was issued ([14]–[16]).

Furthermore, the court underscored that an arbitrator can make varied types of awards, including declaratory awards concerning the rights of parties. The argument that the award could not be enforced due to potential extraterritorial implications was rejected, as both parties were within the court's jurisdiction when the matter was adjudicated ([17]).

Ultimately, the court found that the arbitrator maintained his mandate and jurisdiction to issue an award even after the settlement was reached, thereby validating the arbitral award ([18]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the original High Court had erred in declaring the arbitral award void. The appeal was upheld with costs, and the court reaffirmed that the arbitrator's award from 10 December 2007 should be made an order of court, thus recognizing the binding nature of the arbitration agreement as per Section 31 of the Arbitration Act ([18]).

This case is significant as it reaffirms the principles of consent in arbitration proceedings, clarifying the extent of an arbitrator’s jurisdiction following a proposed settlement. It highlights a need for the development of statutory provisions within South African law to facilitate the recording of settlements as awards, akin to provisions present in international arbitration law ([9]–[10]).

In essence, *Bidoli v Bidoli & another* underscores the importance of clear legislative frameworks in arbitration and reaffirms the validity of awards made by arbitrators, ensuring the enforceability of arbitration outcomes within the South African legal landscape.
