Supreme Court of Appeal

FIRAC Analysis of Mashamaite & others v Mogalakwena Local Municipality & others

Kekana’s conflicts with the municipal council and its members spanned several years, culminating in a power struggle where allegations of corruption and misconduct were leveled between parties ([para 7]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017

F – Facts

The case arises from a series of events involving the Mogalakwena Local Municipality (the “municipality”) and its municipal manager, Shella William Kekana (the “second respondent”). Kekana initiated an urgent application against the appellants—37 councillors of the municipality—after being suspended on 4 December 2014, pending a disciplinary enquiry ([para 2]). The application sought various interdicts to prevent actions taken by the council, asserting that his suspension and the subsequent actions of the council were unlawful ([para 3]).

Kekana’s conflicts with the municipal council and its members spanned several years, culminating in a power struggle where allegations of corruption and misconduct were leveled between parties ([para 7]). Following his review application, Kekana was ultimately dismissed for gross misconduct on 31 March 2015 ([para 5]).

The court a quo, presided over by Hiemstra AJ, ruled in favor of Kekana by declaring that various meetings convened by the council were unlawful and reinstated him as the municipal manager on 1 April 2016 ([para 6]). This order became a focal point for the appellants, who appealed against the ruling, contending it contravened the doctrine of res judicata, as it revisited issues already decided upon ([paras 14-15]).

I – Issues

The following core issues emerged for determination by the court:

  1. Was the court a quo barred from granting reinstatement due to the doctrine of res judicata?
  2. Did Kekana possess authority to bring proceedings on behalf of the municipality?
  3. Were any of the appealed orders moot due to subsequent events?
  4. Did the appellants establish that the MEC’s non-compliance with a prior court order was wilful and mala fide?
  5. Was the court a quo justified in imposing costs upon the appellants?

R – Rules

The legal doctrines and principles pertinent to this case included:

  • Res Judicata: This principle prevents a party from pursuing the same cause of action in successive lawsuits after it has already been judged ([para 15]).
  • Authority in Judicial Proceedings: An employee (such as Kekana) who is on suspension does not possess authority to act on behalf of their employer unless explicitly permitted ([para 24]).
  • Mootness Doctrine: An appeal may be dismissed if the decision sought will have no practical effect due to changes in circumstances ([para 29]).
  • Contempt of Court: For liability to exist in contempt proceedings, there must be proof of an existing court order, knowledge of that order, non-compliance, and that such non-compliance was wilful and mala fide ([para 12]).
  • Judicial Discretion on Costs: Courts have discretion on whether to award costs, which will not be easily interfered with unless there is a clear misdirection ([para 52]).

A – Application

The court’s reasoning hinged substantially on the principles outlined above:

  1. Res Judicata: The court found that the issues raised in Kekana's application had already been addressed in an earlier ruling by Hughes J, which had determined that the Labour Court had jurisdiction over the matter. This had barred the court a quo from revisiting the matter, thus creating risk for conflicting judgments ([para 19]).
  2. Authority to Sue: The appellants argued that Kekana lacked the authority to act on behalf of the municipality since he was suspended during the application’s initiation. The court acknowledged this point by specifying that a suspended employee does not have the authority to act in their official capacity ([para 24-25]).
  3. Mootness: Several findings by the court a quo, specifically regarding the council’s actions, were rendered moot due to subsequent local government elections, which installed new leadership and effectively voided the earlier issues ([para 30]).
  4. Wilful and Mala Fide Non-compliance: The court concluded that the evidence failed to establish that the MEC had acted mala fide or in direct violation of the previous orders. It upheld the principle that breaches of an order could arise from misunderstandings of authoritative powers rather than intentional wrongdoing ([para 45]).
  5. Costs Award: The court determined that ordering costs against the appellants was unjustified, particularly since Kekana had not obtained the relief he initially sought ([para 53]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appellants’ appeal, set aside the judgment of the court a quo, and dismissed Kekana's application ([para 54]). The ruling reclarified the applicability of the doctrine of res judicata in municipal governance disputes, emphasizing the necessity of clarity in authority among suspended municipal officials. The case underscores the constitutional and legal complexities surrounding local governance in South Africa, including the challenges posed by overlapping jurisdictions and the function of the judiciary in managing municipal disputes.

This case, therefore, acts as a touchstone for future adjudication in the lines of local government and administrative law, especially regarding the enforcement and respect of court orders alongside the governance responsibilities of municipal officials.
