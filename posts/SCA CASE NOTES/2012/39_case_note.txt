Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# Case Note: **Adcock Ingram Intellectual Property (Pty) Ltd v Cipla Medpro (Pty) Ltd** [2012] ZASCA 39

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The case revolves around a trade mark dispute concerning the marks "ZEMAX" and "ZETOMAX," both of which are associated with pharmaceutical products ([1-4]). The appellants, Adcock Ingram Intellectual Property (Pty) Ltd and Adcock Healthcare (Pty) Ltd, sought the removal of the ZEMAX trade mark, registered under number 2004/05322, from the South African Trade Marks Register. The mark was held by the first respondent, Cipla Medpro (Pty) Ltd, for pharmaceutical products including generic medicines ([1-2]).

Adcock's interest in the case stemmed from its ownership of the ZETOMAX mark, which is also a generic medicine containing Lisinopril, an active ingredient used for treating moderate hypertension. The registration similarities led Adcock to argue that ZEMAX was likely to cause confusion among consumers, violating sections 10(12) and 10(14) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 ([3-4]).

Cipla initially gained registration for their product under the Medicines and Related Substances Act and, upon the approval for the name change from "Prilosin" to "ZEMAX," disputes later arose ([6]). The North Gauteng High Court denied Adcock’s application to expunge ZEMAX, which led to the current appeal ([8]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues include:
1. Whether the marks ZEMAX and ZETOMAX are so similar that their registration leads to confusion among consumers, violating section 10(14) of the Trade Marks Act.
2. Whether the considerations regarding confusion include patients as end-users in the context of the pharmaceutical market.
3. The relevance of the classification of pharmaceutical products and their marketing conditions under the Medicines and Related Substances Act in this context ([9-12]).

### R – Rules
The relevant legal statutes and principles include:
- **Section 10(14) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993**: This section prohibits the registration of a mark that is identical or confusingly similar to an existing registered mark belonging to a different proprietor.
- **Section 24 of the Trade Marks Act**: Permits an interested party to apply for removing a trade mark that was incorrectly registered ([10-12]).
- Case law precedents including:
  - *Adcock-Ingram Laboratories Ltd v Lennon Ltd* (1983), which highlights the significance of understanding consumer behavior and market specificity in determining confusion among marks.
  - *Bristol Laboratories Inc v Ciba Ltd* (1960), discussing the implications of broader product specifications on trademark applications ([15-29]).

### A – Application
The court analyzed the likelihood of confusion by comparing the two trade marks contextually rather than strictly by their pharmaceutical use, thus considering both the medical professionals and patients as potential consumers ([30-33]). In essence, both ZEMAX and ZETOMAX were evaluated not just on their pharmaceutical aspects but also on the potential for confusion amongst a broader range of goods categorically listed under their respective registrations.

The court agreed with the appellants, noting that both marks possess phonetic and visual similarities that could lead to confusion, particularly among patients who are increasingly engaged in health-related discussions, contradicting the previous court's stance that limited consumer confusion consideration to medical practitioners and pharmacists ([24-28]). The ruling emphasized the evolving role of patients in the healthcare decision-making process and the importance of their recognition of a drug’s branding in prescriptions ([24]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Adcock's appeal, ruling that the ZEMAX trade mark was indeed likely to cause confusion with ZETOMAX, and consequently ordered its removal from the register. The decision underlined a more expansive consideration of consumer confusion, including the potential for patient confusion—a vital issue in contemporary healthcare consumption dynamics. The implications of this ruling have significant repercussions for the pharmaceutical industry in South Africa, specifically regarding how trade marks are registered and managed in relation to patient needs and perceptions ([33]). 

This case serves as a pivotal reference for future trade mark disputes, especially within the pharmaceutical context, redefining the understanding of "consumer" in trade mark law to include end-users and their interactions with prescribed medicines.
