Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

### FIRAC Case Note: Hanekom N O and Others v Nuwekloof Private Game Reserve Farm Owners’ Association (502/2023) [2024] ZASCA 154

---

#### F – Facts [paras 1–8]

This appeal arises from an order issued by the Western Cape Division of the High Court, which set aside an order made by the adjudicator from the Community Schemes Ombud Service (CSOS). The first and second appellants, Willem Tobias Hanekom N O and Lourens Hermanus Taljaard N O, are trustees of the WTH Trust, which holds property within the Nuwekloof Private Game Reserve, a community scheme as defined by the Community Schemes Ombud Service Act 9 of 2011 (the Act) [para 1]. The Trust failed to pay levies to the Nuwekloof Private Game Reserve Farm Owners’ Association (the Association), leading to the invocation of clause 5.13 of the Association’s Constitution, which restricts membership privileges for defaulting members [paras 5–6].

On 24 February 2022, the Trust applied to the CSOS to declare clause 5.13 invalid. The adjudicator ruled in favor of the Trust on 11 August 2022, declaring the clause invalid. The Association, dissatisfied with this ruling, appealed to the High Court, which determined that the appeal must be limited to a question of law. The High Court subsequently upheld the Association’s appeal, setting aside the adjudication order and dismissing the Trust's application [paras 6–8]. After obtaining special leave to appeal from the Supreme Court of Appeal on 5 May 2023, the Trust subsequently contested that the Supreme Court had jurisdiction over their case [paras 8].

---

#### I – Issues [paras 1, 9–10, 12]

The primary legal question pertains to whether the High Court was functioning as a court of first instance or as an appellate court concerning the adjudicator’s decision. If it was deemed to be sitting as a court of first instance, then the appellants should have sought leave to appeal to the High Court under section 16(1)(a) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 (the SC Act) instead of seeking special leave to appeal from the Supreme Court under sections 16(1)(b) and 17(3) of the SC Act [paras 1, 9]. 

The key legal considerations in this case are:
1. The status of the adjudicator’s decision: Is it deemed an administrative decision or judicial in nature?
2. The appropriate channels for appeals following the ruling of the High Court on the adjudicator’s decision.

---

#### R – Rules [paras 9–10, 12–21]

The applicable legal framework is established in the following statutes:
- Community Schemes Ombud Service Act 9 of 2011 (the Act): specifically, sections 38(1) which allows aggrieved parties to seek recourse through the CSOS, and section 57(1), which governs appeals to the High Court limited to questions of law.
- Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013: particularly sections 16(1)(a) and 16(1)(b), which delineate the appeal procedures based on whether the High Court acts as a court of first instance or on appeal.

The Supreme Court of Appeal previously ruled in *National Credit Regulator v Lewis Stores (Pty) Ltd* that appeals from bodies outside the judicial system constitute appeals from a court of first instance, thus subject to the leave of that court, as opposed to requiring special leave from the Supreme Court [para 9].

Notably, this ruling emphasizes that administrative decisions, like those from the CSOS, do not equate to judicial appeals, reinforcing the necessity for judicial confines and structure to maintain procedural integrity [para 12]. The court possesses inherent powers under section 173 of the Constitution to regulate its own procedures, but this does not extend to assuming jurisdiction that is not conferred by statute [paras 14, 18].

---

#### A – Application [paras 22–36]

During the proceedings, the appellants argued the High Court was incorrectly classified as a court of first instance and claimed that the Supreme Court had jurisdiction due to the special leave granted [para 8]. However, the Supreme Court clarified that the nature of the appeal was statutory and the appellants failed to follow the required legal channels, lacking the necessary leave from the High Court [paras 19–22]. 

The Supreme Court reiterated the ruling in *Lewis*, emphasizing that statutory appeals to the High Court for a decision of a statutory body are categorized as appeals from a court of first instance, necessitating leave to appeal from the High Court before pursuing matters before the Supreme Court [para 9]. Furthermore, as per section 173 of the Constitution, the Supreme Court's exercise of inherent powers does not extend to matters lacking jurisdiction, further supporting the argument that the special leave during this case is void [paras 18–22].

Given this premise, the appeal was consequently stricken from the roll due to the absence of jurisdiction, reinforcing the foundational principle that all courts must adhere to statutory protocol to maintain judicial integrity [paras 30–36].

---

#### C – Conclusion [paras 23–24, 36]

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that it lacked jurisdiction to hear the appeal because the appellants failed to seek the requisite leave from the High Court after it sat as a court of first instance. Thus, the order granting special leave was a nullity, compelling the appeal to be struck from the roll with costs [paras 23–24]. This case underscores the importance of adhering strictly to procedural requirements outlined within the statutory framework to ensure the legitimacy of appeal proceedings, thereby averting unnecessary jurisdictional disputes in future cases.
