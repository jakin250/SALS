Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: Ezulwini Mining Company (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Mineral Resources and Energy and Others (Case No: 289/2021) [2023] ZASCA 80

## F – Facts
In the case of *Ezulwini Mining Company (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Mineral Resources and Energy and Others* ([paras 1-4]), the appellant, Ezulwini Mining Company (Pty) Ltd (Ezulwini), operates a mining permit on the West Rand of Gauteng. The mine has been operational since 1961, and Ezulwini took over the mining operations from its predecessor in 2014. The mining operations necessitate managing extraneous groundwater that seeps into underground mining areas, which is essential for the safety and efficacy of mining activities. The first respondent, Minister of Mineral Resources and Energy, along with other governmental departments, oversees regulatory compliance concerning mining and environmental impacts.

Ezulwini had been conducting pumping operations to remove extraneous water but ceased underground mining in September 2016 due to economic constraints while continuing surface-level processing. In 2017, Ezulwini sought regulatory approval to discontinue pumping operations but was initially denied. Subsequently, Ezulwini approached the Gauteng Division of the High Court to seek declaratory relief regarding its obligations under the relevant mining and environmental legislation, specifically asserting that it was not legally required to continue pumping extraneous water due to the cessation of underground mining ([paras 6-8]).

The High Court ruled that Ezulwini remained responsible for the pumping and treatment of this water until a closure certificate was issued under section 43 of the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA). This prompted Ezulwini's appeal ([paras 12-14]) against the decision of the High Court.

## I – Issues
The foremost issue on appeal was whether Ezulwini had a continuing legal obligation to pump extraneous water from its underground workings following the cessation of its underground mining operations ([paras 12-13]). Additionally, the appeal raised a subsidiary question regarding the timing and conditions under which this obligation would cease, particularly in light of the closure certificate issued under section 43 of the MPRDA ([paras 42-44]).

## R – Rules
The court’s interpretation relied heavily on several legislative provisions that govern mining and environmental obligations in South Africa. Key statutes included:

1. **Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA)**:
   - **Section 43(1)** stipulates that the holder of a mining permit remains "responsible for... the pumping and treatment of extraneous water" until a closure certificate is issued ([paras 24-28]).
   - **Section 43(5)** indicates that no closure certificate may be issued unless environmental impacts have been adequately addressed, including water pumping and treatment obligations ([para 27]).

2. **National Environmental Management Act 107 of 1998 (NEMA)**:
   - **Section 24N(7)** outlines the responsibilities regarding environmental damage and the treatment of extraneous water ([para 32]).

Additionally, the principles enshrined in the **Constitution of South Africa**, specifically Section 24 regarding the right to an environment that is not harmful to health or well-being, serve as a backdrop for the interpretation of obligations under both MPRDA and NEMA ([paras 13-17]).

## A – Application
The court analyzed the obligations imposed on the holder of a mining permit, determining that Ezulwini’s past and ongoing activities constituted an integral part of its mining operations, thereby necessitating compliance with the relevant legal framework. The court found critical that the pumping of extraneous water was a direct consequence of the mining activities and that ceasing this action would pose significant environmental risks ([paras 34-39]).

Notably, the court rejected Ezulwini's argument that its obligations ceased with the termination of mining operations. The rationale was that stopping the pumping without a closure certificate would not only breach statutory requirements but could also adversely impact surrounding areas by filling the mine with water, thereby undermining public safety and environmental health ([paras 28-39]).

Moreover, the court addressed the implications of Section 24R of NEMA regarding post-closure obligations, concluding that such responsibilities extend beyond the certified closure and focus more on environmental liabilities and financial provisions rather than continuous operational duties like water pumping. Thus, the court reaffirmed the necessity to interpret these obligations in a manner that harmonizes statutory environmental protections with mining operations ([paras 45-46]) .

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the High Court’s order, affirming that Ezulwini remains responsible for the pumping and treatment of extraneous water from its mine until a closure certificate is issued. The court underscored the imperative nature of regulatory compliance, noting that legislative provisions are designed to ensure environmental health and safety throughout the lifecycle of mining operations, thereby embedding ongoing obligations that transcend operational cessation ([paras 48]). This ruling signifies a critical affirmation of environmental stewardship in South African mining law, reinforcing operator accountability even post-activity. 

## Implications
The outcome holds significant implications for mining operations in South Africa, emphasizing the essential nature of enduring liabilities and regulatory responsibilities as part of the mining lifecycle. The court's interpretation reinforces the interrelationship between mining legislation and environmental law, ensuring that environmental impacts are adequately managed throughout the closure process, thus safeguarding public health and ecological integrity.
