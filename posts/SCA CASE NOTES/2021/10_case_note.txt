### Case Note: Fusion Properties 233 CC v Stellenbosch Municipality (932/2019) [2021] ZASCA 10

#### FIRAC Method

---

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–17])**

The case revolves around an application for leave to appeal from a judgment of the High Court of the Western Cape, where the applicant, Fusion Properties 233 CC ("Fusion"), sought relief against the Stellenbosch Municipality ("the municipality")—an organ of state. The legal dispute arose from Fusion's response to an invitation for proposals by the municipality in 2005 for the purchase and development of several erven. After initial negotiations fell through due to disagreements regarding the existence of a valid agreement, the municipality sought a declarator asserting there was no contractual relationship. The High Court ordered the parties to negotiate in good faith, which ultimately failed. 

Fusion then initiated legal proceedings against the municipality in 2015, claiming damages for breach of contract amounting to approximately R32 million. As the litigation progressed, it became apparent that Fusion was financially vulnerable, leading the municipality to invoke section 8 of the Close Corporations Act 69 of 1984 to request security for costs in the amount of R2,626,431.06 due to concerns about Fusion’s ability to cover any potential costs awarded to the municipality should Fusion lose the case.

After several procedural exchanges, the High Court granted the municipality's application for security, prompting Fusion to seek leave to appeal against this determination, arguing that it had been made improperly and infringed upon its constitutional right of access to the courts.

---

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 18–30])**

The primary legal questions raised in this case include:

1. Whether the High Court exercised its discretion judicially when granting the municipality’s application for security for costs.
2. Whether the procedural delay in requesting security for costs by the municipality was reasonable under the Uniform Rules.
3. Whether the constitutional provisions protecting access to courts negate the requirement for Fusion, as a close corporation, to furnish security for costs.
4. Whether the prospects of success for Fusion's underlying action were sufficiently favorable to justify a denial of the application for security for costs.

These issues necessitate a careful examination of the interplay between statutory requirements, the Constitution, and principles of judicial discretion.

---

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 20–29])**

The court relied on several statutory provisions and legal precedents:

1. **Section 8 of the Close Corporations Act 69 of 1984**, which allows the court to require a close corporation to furnish security for costs if it appears that the corporation may be unable to pay the opposing party's costs.
2. **Section 17 of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013**, particularly subsections (1) and (2)(b) concerning the criteria for granting leave to appeal.
3. The **Uniform Rules of Court**, specifically **Rule 47(1) and (4)**, which outline the procedures for demanding security for costs and the consequences of failing to provide such security.
4. Case law such as **Giddey NO v J C Barnard and Others** (2007) and **Shepstone & Wylie and Others v Geyser NO** (1998), which elaborated on the discretionary powers of courts regarding security for costs and underscored that appellate courts should defer to the judgment of the original court unless there has been a clear misapplication of discretion.

---

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 18–38])**

In its judgment, the Supreme Court of Appeal critically assessed the High Court's exercise of discretion regarding security for costs, recognizing that such discretion must be grounded in reasoned evaluations of all relevant factors. The Court noted that Fusion's financial state was uncontroverted, thus validating the municipality's concerns about the potential impossibility of recovering costs.

The Court addressed Fusion's argument concerning the timing of the municipality's application for security, indicating that the municipality acted within the framework permitted by section 8, which allows for security to be requested "at any time during the proceedings." The Court clarified that while immediacy is preferable, the absence of urgency does not nullify the request's validity.

Moreover, the Court underscored that the requirement for security should not infringe upon a plaintiff’s constitutional right of access to the courts, as articulated in **Section 34 of the Constitution**, but also affirmed that such access may rightfully include considerations regarding the financial viability of pursuing litigation—especially for a party that may invoke procedural safeguards to prevent vexatious claims from being lodged against financially insecure defendants.

Ultimately, the Court engaged with the merits of Fusion's underlying case and determined that the High Court's finding regarding Fusion's lacking prospects of success on the pleadings was not unreasonable. Given this assessment, the court upheld the municipality's application noting that Fusion had not demonstrated any evidence of an imminent financial recovery plan or assistance that could negate the requirement for security.

---

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 39])**

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the application for leave to appeal, concluding that the High Court had exercised its discretion in a judicial manner. The Court established that there was no compelling reason to interfere with the High Court's decision, as all relevant legal principles were duly applied, and the financial facts of Fusion's situation warranted a security order.

The ratio decidendi emphasizes the judicial respect for discretion exercised by lower courts in applications for security for costs, affirming that courts must consider both access to justice and the need to safeguard against vexatious litigation. This ruling reinforces the principle that financial responsibility—especially in entities like close corporations—can become a significant factor in litigation dynamics, shaping access to courts in ways that balance equity and procedural integrity.

The implications of this case resonate within the broader framework of corporate law and civil procedure, particularly in assessing the relationship between financial solvency and the ability to engage in legal redress, marking a critical precedent for similar future litigation contexts.
