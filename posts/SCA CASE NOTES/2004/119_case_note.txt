Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2004

# FIRAC Case Note: B J V DURANDT v FEDSURE GENERAL INSURANCE LTD [2004] ZASCA 135

## F – Facts
This case arises from insolvency proceedings concerning the joint trusteeship of B J V Durandt (the Appellant) and Johannes Janse van Rensburg (the Co-Trustee) for the estate of Mr D J and Mrs M A C Spies, appointed on 20 May 1994 ([2]). On the same day, the Appellant and Van Rensburg executed separate ‘Undertaking and Bond of Security’ documents, each binding themselves in the amount of R220,000 to compensate the Master of the Supreme Court for losses resulting from maladministration ([3]). Additionally, Fedsure General Insurance Ltd (the Respondent) provided a suretyship securing both trustees equally for the same amount ([4]).

An order of sequestration was granted on 15 June 1994, and the Appellant and Van Rensburg were officially appointed as the estate's joint trustees ([5]). The two trustees mutually agreed, without a formal division of duties, that Van Rensburg would handle daily estate administration, necessitating the Appellant’s consent for more significant decisions ([6]). Problems arose when Van Rensburg misappropriated funds from the property sale because he forged the Appellant’s signature to effect the sale ([7]).

After Fedsure made a payment of R243,045.52 to the Master in response to the misappropriation, it sought recovery from both trustees but was only able to serve the Appellant, initiating proceedings against him alone ([9]-[10]). The Appellant contended that as a co-trustee, he should not be liable for the wrongful acts of his co-trustee, as he had no knowledge of these actions and thus was termed an 'innocent trustee' ([11]). The High Court ruled in favor of the Respondent, leading to the Appellant's appeal ([12]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal question revolves around whether a joint trustee can be held liable for losses attributed solely to the wrongful acts of a co-trustee, particularly in light of the provisions of the Insolvency Act 24 of 1936. Further, the interpretation of the bond of security signed by the Appellant is crucial to determine the extent of his liability in this context ([1], [12]).

## R – Rules
The court relied on section 56(4) of the Insolvency Act, which states that when two or more trustees are appointed, they must act jointly and shall be jointly and severally liable for acts performed by them jointly ([12]). This section raises a significant question regarding the liability of a trustee who did not participate in a wrongful act.

The court also drew upon principles from common law, which historically held innocent co-trustees liable for defaults made by their co-trustees ([12]). In Millman NO v Twiggs and Another 1995 (3) SA 674 (A), it was noted that common law concerning co-trustees applies unless it is inconsistent with statutory provisions. 

The interpretation of the 'Undertaking and Bond of Security' signed by the Appellant was also central; the document required that each trustee accept liability for losses due to their own actions or maladministration ([14]).

## A – Application
The court examined the applicability of section 56(4) in determining the joint and several liabilities of the trustees, ultimately finding ambiguity in the undertaking signed by the Appellant that allowed for an interpretation limiting his liability ([12], [16]). Judge Heher found that the Act did not explicitly impose liability on a trustee acting independently of their co-trustee. His interpretation of the 'Undertaking and Bond of Security' noted that since the theft committed by Van Rensburg was irrelevant to the Appellant's performance or administration, the Appellant had no duty to answer for the co-trustee's malfeasance ([16]).

Conversely, Judge Conradie argued for a broader interpretation, advocating that the roles of both trustees must be understood in conjunction, emphasizing that the Master required joint and several liability as a standard in such undertakings. He suggested that viewing each trustee’s actions together resulted in a clear understanding of their responsibilities, which should encompass collective liability for the misappropriation notwithstanding the Appellant’s claims of being unaware of the wrongdoing ([18]-[19]).

The court did not find any evidence that the Appellant took proper measures to delineate his liability at the onset of his co-trusteeship or that he sought distinct advantages from his undertaking ([25]-[28]). The fact that the suretyship issued by the Respondent recognized the Appellant as jointly liable for the estate's obligations further aggravated the argument against his claim of innocence ([33]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately sided with Judge Heher, ruling that the Appellant was not liable for the misappropriation by his co-trustee, as he had acted properly within the framework established by his undertaking ([16]). The court found no substantive basis for the Respondent's claim against the Appellant derived from the nature of his engagement and effectively dismissed the claim ([17]).

Consequently, the appeal against the High Court's decision was successful, reversing the order that had favored the Respondent and emphasizing the complexities involved in the joint trusteeship framework under the Insolvency Act. This decision underscores the need for clear definitions of liability among co-trustees in insolvency contexts and serves as a cautionary tale regarding the interpretation of fiduciary duties and contractual obligations within joint management frameworks in insolvency law. The implications of this ruling highlight the crucial reliance on statutory frameworks to guide fiduciary relationships and safeguard against potential abuses by co-trustees ([17]-[19]).
