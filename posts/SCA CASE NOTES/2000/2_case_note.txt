# Case Note: Cadbury (Pty) Limited v Beacon Sweets & Chocolates (Pty) Limited [2000] ZASCA 49

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
This appeal stems from an application by Cadbury (Pty) Limited (“Cadbury”) to amend the trade marks register under section 15 of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, seeking a disclaimer regarding the trade mark registered in favour of Beacon Sweets & Chocolates (Pty) Limited (“Beacon”). The trade mark in question is 86/3570—registered in class 30 for confectionery products, prominently featuring the term "Liquorice Allsorts" ([1]–[3]). 

The registration included a disclaimer affirming that the mark does not convey exclusive rights to the "sweet device" depicted within it and that only descriptive matter could occupy a blank space within its design ([2]). Cadbury contends that "Liquorice Allsorts" is merely descriptive and therefore lacks the distinctiveness required for trade mark protection ([3]).

In its application for rectification, Cadbury sought to include a further disclaimer asserting that registration does not confer exclusive rights to "Liquorice Allsorts" as a standalone term. Beacon opposed this application, arguing that since its original registration was not being contested, the validity of "Liquorice Allsorts" could not be assessed post-registration, focusing instead on its usage since 1986 ([5]–[7]). 

### I – Issues
The primary legal issue before the court was whether Cadbury's proposed disclaimer was warranted under section 15 of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, specifically whether "Liquorice Allsorts" could be categorized as "matter" incapable of distinguishing goods per section 9 of the Act ([4]). Furthermore, the court was to decide if it had jurisdiction to rectify the registration without challenging the validity of the entire trade mark ([7]–[9]).

### R – Rules
The relevant statutory frameworks include:

1. **Section 15 of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993**: This provision allows for the disclaimer to be entered in cases where elements of a trade mark are incapable of distinguishing goods as per section 9, which defines the criteria for registrability of trade marks. Section 9(1) asserts that in order to be registrable, a trade mark must distinguish goods from those of others ([5]).
   
2. **Section 9 of the Trade Marks Act**: It stipulates that a mark is registrable if, at the time of application, it is inherently distinctive or has acquired distinctiveness through use ([4]).

3. **Precedent Case Law**: The court refers to the decision in *Luster Products Inc v Magic Style Sales CC* which hints at the importance of maintaining a register free from marks that do not adequately perform a distinguishing function ([8]).

### A – Application
The court scrutinized the argument that "Liquorice Allsorts" fails to distinguish Beacon's products from others in the market. The appellant presented dictionary definitions and evidence from confectionery manufacturing literature indicating that "Liquorice Allsorts" describes a type of confectionery rather than being a distinctive mark solely associated with Beacon ([8]–[10]). 

In contrast, Beacon argued it was the sole producer of "Liquorice Allsorts" since 1952, thereby asserting a level of secondary meaning associated with the use of the term ([9]). The court found such arguments insufficient to validate Beacon's exclusive rights, as merely being the sole producer does not equate to the term being distinctive in a legal sense. 

Moreover, the court addressed the procedural argument made by Beacon that Cadbury had to challenge the entire mark if seeking a disclaimer. The court rejected this view and maintained that the law supported the need for a disclaimer when parts of a mark are non-distinctive ([11]).

Ultimately, the analysis concluded that "Liquorice Allsorts" is indeed descriptive and is used generically across the confectionery market, thus supporting Cadbury's position for rectification of the register ([10]–[13]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Cadbury's appeal, issuing an order for the rectification of the trade marks register. It included an additional disclaimer stating that the registration does not confer exclusive rights to the name "Liquorice Allsorts" apart from the registered mark. The court ruled that the jurisdictional requirements for entering a disclaimer were met, finding that the term had been generically used in the industry hindering its capacity to function as an exclusive trade mark ([15]). 

This ruling has important implications for trade mark law, reinforcing the principle that marks must possess distinctiveness and should not function merely as descriptive terms within the relevant market.
