Constitutional Court

Bhe and Others v The Magistrate, Khayelitsha and Others; Shibi v Mantabeni Freddy Sithole and Others

In a parallel case, Charlotte Shibi challenged the determination that the estate of her brother, Daniel Solomon Sithole, would devolve according to male primogeniture, as per the mandates of the Black Administration Act ([paras 21–35]).

Constitutional Court 2004

F - Facts:
The case involved three interconnected applications concerning intestate succession under the Black Administration Act 38 of 1927 and the Intestate Succession Act 81 of 1987. The main facts arise from the situation of Nontupheko Maretha Bhe (third applicant), who, on behalf of her two minor daughters, Nonkululeko and Anelisa, challenged the appointment of the deceased's father as the heir of Vuyo Elius Mgolombane’s estate. The Bhe family’s relationship to Mgolombane was tested following his intestate death in 2002, with contentions regarding whether Ms. Bhe's daughters were considered extra-marital children, a classification that would deny them inheritance under customary law ([para 10–17]).

In a parallel case, Charlotte Shibi challenged the determination that the estate of her brother, Daniel Solomon Sithole, would devolve according to male primogeniture, as per the mandates of the Black Administration Act ([paras 21–35]). Both cases raised serious constitutional questions about the discriminatory nature of section 23 of the Black Administration Act concerning gender and illegitimacy in succession rights, as this legacy perpetuated injustices and failed to recognize the rights of heirs.

I - Issues:
The principal issues before the Constitutional Court included:

  1. The constitutional validity of section 23 of the Black Administration Act and its regulations that perpetuate male primogeniture as the governing principle for intestate succession among Black South Africans ([para 3]).
  2. The applicability and fairness of the customary law principle of male primogeniture, particularly in relation to the rights of women and extra-marital children under sections 9 (equality) and 28 (rights of children) of the South African Constitution ([para 6]).

R - Rules:
The court relied upon several statutes and constitutional provisions:

  1. Section 23 of the Black Administration Act 38 of 1927: Governs the intestate succession of estates controlled by Black law, specifically enforcing male primogeniture ([para 35]).
  2. Intestate Succession Act 81 of 1987: Intended to provide a uniform framework for intestate successions, yet explicitly excludes estates governed by section 23 of the Black Administration Act ([para 37]).
  3. Constitution of South Africa: Articles protecting equality (s 9), human dignity (s 10), and children's rights (s 28) were interpreted to assess whether the legacy of male primogeniture and race-based laws are consistent with the constitutional framework ([paras 48–56]).

A - Application:
The Court analyzed whether section 23 and the corresponding provisions indeed denied women and extra-marital children their rights under the Constitution. It concluded that:

  1. The origins and application of section 23 were rooted in discriminatory practices that fundamentally violate the constitutional principles of equality and dignity, particularly in its failure to recognize the contributions of female heirs or extra-marital children ([para 63]).
  2. The principle of male primogeniture was criticized for perpetuating gender-based discrimination and was deemed inappropriate for the transformed socio-economic dynamics of contemporary South African society ([para 189]).
  3. The court highlighted how the rule, which may have served community stability in the past, no longer applied given the current socio-economic conditions in which women often play significant roles as breadwinners ([para 190]).

In articulating these points, the court found the need for the development of customary law to align with constitutional tenets, emphasizing the evolution of societal norms that relegate such discriminatory practices to the past ([para 192]).

C - Conclusion:
The Constitutional Court declared that:

  1. Section 23 of the Black Administration Act is unconstitutional and invalid.
  2. The discriminatory practice of male primogeniture, as it applies to the inheritance of property within the customary law context, is unconstitutional.
  3. Until a legislative framework is enacted to address these inconsistencies, intestate estates previously governed by section 23 of the Black Administration Act should be administered under the Intestate Succession Act.
  4. The ruling emphasized a transitional regime to accommodate polygynous unions and ensure equitable treatment of younger siblings and extra-marital children in the succession process, thus nurturing a just society that upholds constitutional values ([paras 136–241]).

Overall, the judgment serves as a significant step toward dismantling remnants of apartheid-era laws in South Africa, advocating for transformation to achieve equity, fairness, and respect for human dignity in the administration of justice. The implications of this decision extend far beyond the individual cases, establishing a precedent for the constitutional treatment of customary law in modern South Africa.
