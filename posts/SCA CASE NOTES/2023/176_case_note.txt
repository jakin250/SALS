Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: Mashisane v Mhlauli (903/2022) [2023] ZASCA 176

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
In **Mashisane v Mhlauli** ([1]–[2]), the appellant, Moses Muxe Mashisane, and the respondent, Nosiphiwe Linda Mhlauli, were involved in a legal dispute concerning the existence of a customary marriage. Mhlauli sought declaratory relief in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg, asserting that she and Mashisane had entered into a valid customary marriage according to customary law, invoking the *Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998* (RCMA). Mhlauli further claimed that their ante-nuptial contract (ANC) was null and void due to alleged non-compliance with registration requirements stipulated by the *Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937* (DRA), thereby asserting that they were married in community of property (COP). 

Mashisane disputed the existence of a customary marriage, claiming that he had never consented to be married under customary law and emphasized that their discussions indicated a mutual intent to marry civilly, out of community of property with an ANC, to protect their financial interests. This accentuated a significant divergence in the parties’ narratives regarding their intention and the effect of traditional customs observed during their relationship ([3]–[4]).

The High Court initially granted Mhlauli's application, but denied Mashisane leave to appeal, precipitating Mashisane's appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which heard the matter on 8 November 2023 and delivered its judgment on 14 December 2023 ([2]).

### I – Issues
The primary issues before the SCA were: 
1. Whether genuine disputes of fact existed regarding the existence of a customary marriage and Mashisane's consent to such a marriage, given the conflicting testimonies and assertions ([5]–[6]).
2. The appropriateness of declaratory relief in circumstances where material factual disputes were present, and whether the High Court exercised its discretion correctly in granting such relief without a trial ([17]–[20]).

### R – Rules
The court relied on several statutory provisions and precedents to frame its analysis:
- **Section 3 of the RCMA** outlines the requirements for a valid customary marriage, specifically emphasizing mutual consent to marry under customary law and adherence to customary law practices ([6]–[7]).
- **Sections 86, 87, and 89 of the DRA** govern the registration process of ante-nuptial contracts, making it clear that failure to comply nullifies the contract's enforceability under certain conditions ([1]).
- The **Plascon-Evans rule** establishes that in motion proceedings, the onus is on the applicant to prove a clear right in cases where material disputes of fact exist ([15]).
- The SCA referenced the principles from **Gounder v Top Spec Investments (Pty) Ltd**, reinforcing that an applicant is at risk of their motion being dismissed if there are existing factual disputes known to them ([26]).

### A – Application
The SCA undertook a comprehensive review of the factual imbalances in the parties' submissions. It determined that substantial disputes existed regarding consent and the nature of their marriage intentions. The court highlighted that Mashisane and Mhlauli had conflicting statements; Mashisane maintained he had not consented to a customary marriage, while Mhlauli asserted that customary traditions had been followed to signify such permission ([8]–[10]).

The SCA echoed the sentiments expressed by the Constitutional Court in **Mayelane v Ngwenyama**, emphasizing that courts must carefully evaluate the evidence concerning customary law and urged for expert evidence on such matters in trials. The absence of such expert evidence hindered the court's ability to determine the nature of the parties’ marriage. The SCA ultimately concluded that the High Court's reliance on affidavit evidence to determine the validity of the marriage was inappropriate because of the disputes’ complexity, warranting a factual inquiry via a trial rather than through motion proceedings ([13]–[14]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the High Court had improperly exercised its discretion by granting declaratory relief without resolving the factual disputes adequately. Consequently, the SCA upheld the appeal and dismissed the application initially granted by the High Court, thereby concluding that the matter should be resolved through trial proceedings, as the issues raised were substantial and warranted thorough examination ([27]). 

The implications of this decision underscore the necessity for clarity in matters concerning customary marriages and the potential enforceability of ante-nuptial agreements, reflecting the realities of relationships that blend diverse cultural norms. The ruling emphasizes the importance of ensuring factual disputes are addressed adequately through appropriate legal channels, thereby preserving the integrity of legal proceedings in family law matters.
