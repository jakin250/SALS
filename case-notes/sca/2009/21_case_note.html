Supreme Court of Appeal

Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality v Dada N.O. (280/2008) [2009] ZASCA 21

This case arises from the Johannesburg High Court's order directing the Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality (the Municipality) to purchase a property on which an informal settlement had been established.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009 [2009] ZASCA 21

F – Facts

This case arises from the Johannesburg High Court's order directing the Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality (the Municipality) to purchase a property on which an informal settlement had been established. The first six respondents, trustees of the Islamic Dawah Movement Trust (the Trust), own the property described as "Portion 41 of the Farm Rooikop 140" ([para 2]).

In November and December 2004, approximately 76 families (the seventh respondents) moved onto this property after their previous informal settlement became uninhabitable due to flooding and marshy conditions ([para 2]). In July 2006, the Trust sought the eviction of these occupiers, resulting in the application being governed by the Prevention of Illegal Eviction From and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998. The Municipality was joined as a second respondent ([para 3]).

The occupiers opposed the eviction application, acknowledging their unlawful occupation but asserting they did so under a belief that they had the Municipality's authority. They filed a counter-application invoking Section 26(2) of the Constitution, arguing that the Municipality was duty-bound to implement a progressive plan to facilitate access to adequate housing ([para 4]).

In response, the Municipality cited compliance with statutory frameworks and claimed there were ongoing efforts to mitigate the occupiers' plight ([para 5]). The matter was heard by Cassim AJ, who solicited oral evidence from the Municipality regarding its actions to assist the occupiers ([para 7]). Ultimately, Cassim AJ issued an order requiring the Municipality to purchase the property, a decree not sought by either party ([para 10]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue involves whether the High Court judge overstepped judicial boundaries by ordering the Municipality to purchase the property, which effectively usurped the Municipality's executive function and failed to consider the principle of "judicial deference" ([paras 11–13]). Additionally, the appeal raised questions concerning the appropriate scope of relief under Section 38 of the Constitution, particularly in relation to the Constitutional Court's emphasis on the progressive realization of housing rights ([para 14]).

R – Rules

The court's decision relied on established principles of judicial review and separation of powers, particularly the guidelines from the following cases:

  1. National Director of Public Prosecutions v Zuma: Judges must remain constrained by law when exercising judicial functions ([para 1]).
  2. Grootboom: This case emphasizes the need for municipalities to progressively realize housing rights ([para 10]).
  3. Bato Star Fishing (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Environmental Affairs: This case highlights the need for judicial deference in administrative matters ([para 10]).
  4. South African Constitution, Section 26(2): Specifies the obligation of the state to take reasonable legislative and other measures to achieve the progressive realization of the right to adequate housing.

These principles underline the judiciary's role in reviewing administrative actions without encroaching on legislative or executive prerogatives.

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) found that the order of Cassim AJ was influenced by a pre-conceived notion that the Municipality was failing to act effectively, thus compelling him to take drastic measures without being asked by the parties ([para 13]). The judge was expected to operate within the confines of the law and the evidence presented rather than impose a solution outside those constraints.

The evidence provided by the Municipality aimed to demonstrate that it had been working on long-term plans to alleviate the housing crisis but that these plans required time and adherence to specific statutory procedures ([para 5]). The judge's directive to purchase the property did not reflect a rational connection to the evidence provided ([para 13]). Rather, it reflected the judge’s frustration with perceived inaction, leading him to exceed his judicial role and infringe upon the Municipality's administrative functions ([para 13]).

In concluding his remarks, Hurt AJA noted that while the judge’s concerns regarding municipal inaction were valid, the judicial solution he offered was not appropriate, thereby highlighting the need for the judiciary to respect administrative processes and recognize their technical nature ([para 14]).

C – Conclusion

The court held that the order compelling the Municipality to purchase the property lacked a legal basis and was thus set aside. The ruling reaffirmed the principles of judicial restraint and respect for administrative decision-making, emphasizing the need for courts to exercise caution in imposing solutions that bypass established legislative frameworks.

The critical takeaway from this case lies in its affirmation of the separation of powers, particularly regarding the judiciary's role in social issues related to housing rights. Despite the serious plight of the occupiers and the need for swift action, the SCA underscored the principle that solutions must arise through lawful avenues rather than judicial mandates that intrude on governmental functions ([paras 14–15]).

Thus, the appeal succeeded with the provision of services order by the Municipality remaining intact, highlighting the ongoing challenge in addressing informal settlements in a manner that respects both individual rights and systemic governance ([para 15]).

This detailed analysis stands as a significant precedent regarding the interplay between judicial decisions and municipal responsibilities in enforcing housing rights under South African law.
