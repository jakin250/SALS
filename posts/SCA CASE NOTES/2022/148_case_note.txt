# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title:** Nyamukamadi Mukumela Denga (Mabirimisa) & Others v Mabirimisa Tshililo Arnold N N O & Others (1296/2021) [2022] ZASCA 148

**Coram:** Van der Merwe, Molemela, Hughes JJA, Daffue and Chetty AJJA

**Heard:** 15 August 2022  
**Delivered:** 31 October 2022  
**Neutral Citation:** [2022] ZASCA 148

## F - Facts ([paras 1–21])

This case concerns the finalisation of the estate of the late Mudzielwana Josiah Denga Mabirimisa, who died intestate on 19 April 1998. He had three wives and children from each marriage, leading to claims from the appellants (daughters from his second marriage) regarding their entitlement as heirs. 

The deceased's estate was registered at the Magistrate's Court of Dzanani under estate number 44/98 shortly after his death. The first respondent, Mabirimisa Tshililo, the deceased's first-born son, was appointed by the Magistrate as the representative of the estate on 30 April 1998, adhering to the principle of male primogeniture under the now-repealed Black Administration Act 38 of 1927.

The estate included a range of assets, including livestock and a 50% share in Mabirimisa Bus Service (Pty) Ltd, which the first respondent claimed to have successfully managed and revived after the deceased's death. On 7 March 2006, a settlement agreement amounting to R1.4 million was purportedly concluded, purportedly representing the final resolution regarding claims on the estate. The appellants claimed they received no benefits and sought legal redress by appealing the High Court's dismissal of their application to declare the estate unfinalised and to administer it under the Administration of Estates Act 66 of 1965 instead. 

The High Court determined the estate had been finalised and upheld the appointment of the first respondent, leading to this appeal.

## I - Issues ([paras 1–21])

The pivotal legal question in this appeal is whether the estate of the deceased, reported at the Magistrate Court under file number 44/98, was duly finalised. If it was, then the appellants' claims for relief under the Administration of Estates Act are impermissible. Conversely, if the estate was unfinalised, there arise secondary questions regarding the proper authority to administer it—whether the Master of the High Court or the Magistrate could assume jurisdiction. 

Additionally, the issue arises concerning the validity and legal implications of the settlement agreement from 2006 and whether it should preclude re-administration under the Administration of Estates Act, especially in the context of the historical repeal of the Black Administration Act.

## R - Rules ([paras 1–21])

The legal principles and rules invoked in this case include:

1. **Black Administration Act 38 of 1927**: This Act governed the administration of deceased estates among black South Africans and established the framework for intestate succession under the conditions of male primogeniture, subsequently declared unconstitutional in Bhe and Others v Khayelitsha Magistrate and Others 2005 (1) SA 580 (CC).

2. **Administration of Estates Act 66 of 1965**: Provides a framework for the administration of deceased estates and outlines the obligations of the Master of the High Court in overseeing deceased estates.

3. **Plascon-Evans Principle**: As established in Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd [1984] 2 All SA 366 (A), the factual dispute is resolved in favor of the respondent when conflicting versions emerge, unless the applicant’s version raises no real dispute of fact.

4. **Settlement Agreements**: Reflected in the Bhe judgment, the court upheld the provision allowing parties to agree on the distribution of an estate, as long as such agreements are not exploitative.

## A - Application ([paras 1–21])

The Supreme Court of Appeal applied the aforementioned legal rules to ascertain whether the estate had indeed been finalised. It recognized that the requisite process commenced with the appointment of the first respondent and included the submission of an inventory of the estate, albeit executed under the now-repealed Black Administration Act.

Evidence presented by the first respondent indicated compliance with his duties as the appointed representative, including an alleged settlement agreement made an order of court and the allocation of estate assets among the households. Furthermore, the court noted the appellants had previously accepted funds under the settlement agreement, which rendered their claims for re-administration weaker.

The court rejected the argument that the agreement should be dismissed simply due to the repeal of the Black Administration Act, emphasizing that finalisation could occur under the provisions available as of the critical dates, per the guidance provided in Bhe. The court also noted that records demonstrated the estate had been concluded as of the court order that recognized the settlement agreement, reaffirming the stance that no new re-administration was warranted.

## C - Conclusion ([paras 1–22])

The appeal was dismissed with no order as to costs, affirming the High Court's judgment that found the estate had been validly finalised under the Black Administration Act. The appellate court's emphasis on the binding effect of the settlement agreement illustrates judicial respect for agreements entered into by the parties, even under legislative frameworks later found to be inconsistent with constitutional norms.

The decision reinforces the principle that once an estate has been finalised and agreements made, beneficiaries cannot retroactively challenge the administration unless on the grounds of misconduct or failure to respect the proper distribution of estate assets. The implications of this ruling advocate for the finality of estate proceedings while acknowledging the risks associated with extant inequalities in customary law arising from discriminatory legislative practices.
