Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: Golden Core Trade and Invest (Pty) Ltd v Merafong City Local Municipality and Another (338/2022) [2023] ZASCA 126

## F – Facts
The appellant, Golden Core Trade and Invest (Pty) Ltd, was involved in a protracted legal dispute with the first respondent, Merafong City Local Municipality, regarding water tariffs over multiple years ([1–9]). Golden Core, previously AngloGold Ashanti Ltd, operates mining activities across various locations and is reliant on Rand Water for its water supply, purchasing approximately 502,600 kiloliters per month from this entity. The Municipality assumed the role of water services authority in 2004, leading to a series of disputes regarding the tariffs imposed for both domestic and industrial water use ([3–6]).

In 2005, the Minister of Water and Sanitation (the second respondent) made a ruling that the Municipality's imposed tariffs were unreasonable, particularly for industrial water usage, and determined that the Municipality had exceeded its authority by imposing surcharges on industrial water ([9]). Following this ruling, attempts by the Municipality to engage with the Minister led to legal opinions indicating the Minister's decision could not be overturned without review, yet the Municipality did not act upon these legal clarifications at the time ([10–11]). Eventually, disputes escalated into civil litigation, leading to numerous proceedings across different judicial forums over a decade ([12–18]).

In 2021, the Gauteng Division of the High Court dismissed Golden Core's request to enforce the Minister's 2005 ruling and upheld the Municipality's counter-application seeking review of the Minister’s decision, citing significant delays and complications that arose over the years ([19–22]). 

## I – Issues
The appeal presented several legal questions, notably:
1. Did the high court err in overlooking the substantial delay by the Municipality in bringing its review application regarding the Minister’s decision ([1–2], [24–26])?
2. Was the counter-application for a review of the Minister’s decision legally sound following the extensive history of delays and legal complexities ([22–23])?
3. Should the Municipality be permitted to raise a reactive challenge against the enforcement of the Minister's decision, especially considering its previous conduct in ignoring the decision ([24–26])?

## R – Rules
Key legal principles derived from the Water Services Act 108 of 1997, specifically sections 7 and 8 regarding the powers granted to water services authorities and the role of the Minister in appeal processes ([4–5], [6–8]). The legislative framework elaborates on the roles and authority delineated to ensure a balance between municipal powers and state oversight.

Judicial precedents, particularly *Oudekraal Estates (Pty) Ltd v City of Cape Town & Others* [2004] ZASCA 48, play a pivotal role in determining the binding nature of administrative decisions until they are overturned by competent authority ([16]). Further case law, including *Khumalo v Member of the Executive Council for Education: KwaZulu Natal* [2013] ZACC 49 and *Buffalo City Metropolitan Municipality v Asla Construction (Pty) Ltd* [2019] ZACC 15, provide guidelines for assessing the implications of delays in bringing reviews to court ([30–31]).

## A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal reviewed whether the high court was correct in allowing the Municipality’s counter-application, despite the long delay in its filing. The court pointed out that the lengthy period (13 years) underlined by the Municipality was both unreasonable and lacked sufficient justification, as the reasons given for inaction were considered unsatisfactory ([26–49]). Consequently, the court determined that prior compliance with the Minister's decision was mandatory for the Municipality and any defiance of it necessitated judicial review—not self-help measures.

The court further evaluated the implications of entertaining such a delayed challenge. It concluded that allowing the Municipality to effectively disregard the Minister's decision underpinned a troubling precedent that would undermine administrative legality ([60–63]). The court highlighted that the legal relationship between the parties had evolved and that the Minister's decision had neither an expiring nor enduring effect past the point of the Municipality's subsequent tariff adjustments ([56–60]).

Concerning the reactive challenge raised by the Municipality, the court rejected it, affirming that a reactive challenge should not allow an entity to ignore prior administrative decisions when a valid review process was available but unused ([66–75]).

## C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal held that the high court erred in its judgment. It overturned the high court's decision, dismissed the Municipality's review application, and reaffirmed that both the industrial and domestic tariffs imposed by the Municipality before new tariffs were enacted were unlawful ([1–2], [80]). The ratio decidendi underscored the necessity for entities to follow due process when contesting administrative decisions, as well as the importance of timely judicial review to maintain the rule of law within governmental operations.

The appeal was upheld with costs, affirming that the Municipality not only had to comply with the Minister’s earlier decision but also bear the financial repercussions due to this protracted dispute ([1], [80]). This case underscores significant implications for administrative law regarding the duty of compliance and the lawful authority of municipal tariffs versus overarching state regulations under the Water Services Act.
