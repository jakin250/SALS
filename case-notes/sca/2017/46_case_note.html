Marais & Others v Varicor Nineteen (Pty) Ltd (843/16) [2017] ZASCA 46

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 46

FIRAC Structure

F – Facts ([paras 1–6])

This case concerns the contractual relationship between the appellants—Hendrik Christoffel Marais, Christina Susanna Müller in her capacity as trustee of the Albertinia Dekriet Trust, and Christina Susanna Müller in her personal capacity—and the respondent, Varicor Nineteen (Pty) Ltd, trading as BP Atlantic (BPA). The core issue arises from a dispute over whether an agreement for the establishment of a new diesel depot constituted an extension of an existing supply agreement between BPA and the Trust or a new agreement binding Ms. Müller personally.

In the initial court proceedings, BPA sought to recover an outstanding debt related to bulk diesel supplied to the Trust, which had become financially delinquent, owing BPA R7 million over a period from July to October 2008 ([paras 1–2]). The agreement in question was originally established in 2001, wherein BPA supplied the Trust with fuel under a structured credit agreement. By 2003, in the context of BPA’s business expansion, discussions were held to open a diesel depot at the premises operated by the Trust ([paras 3–5]).

Central to the appeal was the interpretation of the depot agreement; BPA claimed it was merely an extension of the existing contract with the Trust, while the appellants contended it constituted a new agreement with Ms. Müller acting as a personal guarantor ([paras 6–8]). The lower court found that the agreement with BPA was consistent with the understanding that the Trust remained liable ([paras 11–12]).

I – Issues ([paras 2, 3, 11, 30])

The primary legal issues presented in this case revolve around:

  1. Whether the 2003 depot agreement was an extension of the prior contract between BPA and the Trust, thus binding the Trust as the debtor, or a new agreement with personal liability arising for Ms. Müller.
  2. The application of the doctrine of quasi mutual assent as applied by the lower court in determining the nature of the contract ([paras 11, 30]).

Additionally, there was a critical concern regarding the burden of proof—was it correctly placed on Ms. Müller to demonstrate that a new agreement existed, or did BPA retain the onus to prove the contractual terms?[ [paras 33]].

R – Rules ([paras 10, 33])

The legal principles examined in this case are grounded in established contract law, particularly regarding the formation of contracts and the intentions of parties involved. The doctrine of quasi mutual assent—a principle emphasizing that mutual agreement is established even in the face of imperfectly understood terms—was central to the trial court's reasoning ([paras 12, 30]).

Regarding onus, South African case law states that the party who asserts the existence of the contract has the burden to prove it. It has previously been established in Stocks & Stocks (Pty) Ltd v T J Daly & Sons (Pty) Ltd 1979 (3) SA 754 (A) that the plaintiff bears the overall burden of demonstrating the existence and terms of the contract, even if it involves disproving a defendant's assertions ([para 33]).

A – Application ([paras 21–32])

The Supreme Court of Appeal extensively analyzed witness testimony and documentary evidence to elucidate the parties' intentions regarding the depot agreement. Mr. Otto, BPA's sales representative, testified that the 2003 agreement was intended as an extension of the prior contractual arrangement with the Trust, emphasizing that no new clients could be brought on without formal documentation approved by BPA ([paras 19–20]).

Conversely, Ms. Müller claimed the 2003 agreement was a new collective that saw her stepping in as a separate entity from the Trust, asserting significant differences in credit terms ([paras 14–15]). Yet, her credibility was undermined during cross-examination, as she failed to provide satisfactory explanations regarding the confusion over invoicing, the VAT registration, and the management of the depot's financial affairs ([paras 16–26]).

The court scrutinized the evidence, concluding that the operational context and the manner in which the depot was run did not indicate a severance from the Trust's obligations. Furthermore, the invoices consistently referenced the Trust, reinforcing BPA's claim that the Trust continued as the debtor ([paras 31–32]).

In regards to the burden of proof, the appellate court found that the trial court incorrectly shifted the onus onto Ms. Müller concerning the existence of a new agreement, reaffirming that BPA initially bore the burden to substantiate its claims against the Trust ([para 33]).

C – Conclusion ([paras 32–34])

The Apex Court ultimately dismissed the appeal, holding that the appellants failed to prove the existence of a new binding contract with Ms. Müller personally. Consequently, the established relationship and obligations between the Trust and BPA remained intact ([para 32]). Furthermore, the court underscored that the notion of a new agreement was not substantiated by sufficient evidence or credible documentation, aligning with established principles regarding contract formation and liability ([para 30]).

The ruling sets a significant precedent, clarifying the contracts law in South Africa regarding liability in long-standing commercial relationships and the implications of the shift in burden of proof in contract disputes ([para 33]). The case emphasizes the importance of clarity in contractual agreements and the necessity for substantial proof in asserting claims outside of established contracts.
