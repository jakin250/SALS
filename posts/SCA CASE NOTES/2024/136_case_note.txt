# Case Note: Maria Luisa Palma Codevilla v Paula Jane Kennedy-Smith NO and Others (494/2023) [2024] ZASCA 136

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts:

[1] This case concerns an appeal from the Western Cape Division of the High Court regarding the revival of an agreement for the purchase and sale of immovable property following the lapsing of a suspensive condition. The suspension condition was related to the buyer’s obligation to secure a mortgage bond.

[2] On **February 4, 2020**, Maria Luisa Palma Codevilla (the appellant) participated in a sale transaction involving residential property in Kenilworth, Cape Town. The first respondent, Paula Jane Kennedy-Smith, was the seller, and the third and fourth respondents were the appellant's children, who made the offer to purchase.

[3] Under the terms of the Offer to Purchase (OTP), a mortgage bond approval was to be obtained by February 14, 2020; however, this deadline was extended twice: first to February 19, 2020, and later through a second addendum signed on February 20, 2020.

[4] Despite efforts made by the appellant and her children, they failed to secure the required financing by the deadline, leading to the conclusion that the OTP lapsed on **February 19, 2020**. Following this, the appellant requested an extension, leading to a second addendum, the validity of which became a central issue in this case.

[5] The appellant sought a declaration that the OTP was invalid and that the subsequent addendum did not revive the contract after the suspensive condition had failed. The High Court ruled in favor of the respondents, confirming the contract's validity, which prompted the appellant to appeal.

[6] The appeal brought to light whether a binding agreement could exist after the lapse of a contract due to the failure of a condition. 

### I – Issues:

[12] The primary legal question revolves around **whether the Offer to Purchase could be revived post-lapse of the suspensive condition** contained therein due to the appellant’s failure to secure financing by the set date.

[13] Additional considerations include:
- The interpretation of intentions behind the second addendum.
- Compliance with the **Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981** regarding the terms of the contract.
- The impact of previous case law on the enforceability and revival of contracts after a suspensive condition has failed.

### R – Rules:

[16] A critical aspect of the rules governing this case lies in the application of common law principles regarding suspensive conditions. The courts have consistently held that **contractual agreements lapse automatically** upon the failure of a suspensive condition, rendering them unenforceable.

[17] The **Alienation of Land Act (1981)** mandates that any agreement for the sale of land must be in writing and signed by the parties; any alterations must also comply with this requirement.

[18] In related precedents, most notably **Pangbourne Properties Ltd v Basinview Properties (Pty) Ltd** and **Abrinah 7804 (Pty) Ltd v Kapa Koni Investments CC**, the courts have stated that a lapsed contract cannot be revived simply through subsequent agreements that attempt to extend or reset conditions that have already expired.

### A – Application:

[24] In applying these principles, the court discerned whether the **intention** to revive the OTP was manifest in the actions and agreements of the parties following the lapse of the suspensive condition.

[29] The court noted that there was a marked discrepancy between the intentions expressed through the second addendum and the actual legal inability to extend the time for fulfilment given the previous lapse.

[30] The appellant’s refusal to acknowledge the revival of the OTP was substantiated by citing precedents wherein contracts similar in nature to the one at hand had failed upon the condition's lapse.

[35] Furthermore, the court examined the terms of the second addendum, noting that while it explicitly referenced the original OTP, the conditions contained therein, specifically those under clause 7.2, could not simply be resurrected once they had expired.

[38] As case law established a pattern where the intentions of parties could not resurrect a lapsed contract through informal agreements, it became pivotal in determining the outcome.

### C – Conclusion:

[39] The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately ruled that the agreement of sale embodied in the OTP could not be revived or reinstated as it had lapsed due to the failure of a contractual condition. The court emphasized the enforceability of the law surrounding suspensive conditions, affirming that timescales set therein are crucial.

[40] The order of the High Court was set aside, resulting in the appellant being awarded the sum of R1,950,000, paid on behalf of the purchasers, along with interest. The decision underscored the courts’ consistent adherence to established contractual principles and their resistance in allowing informal agreements to substitute for formal legal obligations as outlined in the *Alienation of Land Act*.

In summary, the court maintained that failure of a suspensive condition leads to automatic lapse, and any attempt to create binding obligations thereafter must stem from a new agreement, replete with all necessary formalities to be valid. This outcome illustrates the importance of strict adherence to statutory requirements surrounding land transactions.
