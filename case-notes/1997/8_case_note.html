Constitutional Court 1997

F – Facts

The Constitutional Court of South Africa was tasked with certifying the Constitution of the Western Cape (WCC), adopted by the provincial legislature on 21 February 1997, in accordance with the requirements of the national Constitution (NC) ([1]–[4]). The Court's role under NC section 144(2)(b) was to confirm that the WCC complies with NC section 143, which prescribes the permissible contents for provincial constitutions. The Speaker of the Western Cape legislature submitted the WCC for certification ([2]). The ANC and the national government challenged several provisions of the WCC, whereas the Speaker and supporting political parties defended its compliance ([4]).

The certification process involved two critical obligations: (i) assessing whether the WCC had been adopted per the procedures dictated by NC section 142, which requires a two-thirds majority vote of the provincial legislature, and (ii) determining that the provisions contained within the WCC do not conflict with the national constitutional framework, particularly with NC section 143 ([8]–[9]). The government focused objections primarily on certain features of the WCC, leading to a thorough evaluation of both contested and unchallenged provisions ([3]–[4]).

I – Issues

The central legal issue for determination by the Constitutional Court was whether the entirety of the WCC, as submitted for certification, complied with NC section 143 ([5]–[8]). Specific inquiries included:

  1. Legal Competence: Did the WCC provisions exceed the constitutional powers assigned to provincial legislatures under NC chapter 6?
  2. Permissibility of Content: Did the content of the WCC violate the incompatibility criteria set forth in NC sections 143(1) and (2), relating to the structure and procedure of provincial governance?
  3. Repetition of Provisions: Did the inclusion of provisions identical to those found in the NC convert into a legislative inconsistency wherein the province had no authority to replicate them? ([21]–[22]).

The Court sought to ascertain if any of the contested provisions indeed fell foul of the established constitutional framework, including limits on provincial competence as articulated in NC schedules 4 and 5.

R – Rules

The pertinent rules were derived from the following constitutional provisions:

  • NC Section 104(1)(a): Bestows on provincial legislatures the power to draft their own constitutions.
  • NC Section 142: Mandates that a provincial constitution must be passed by a two-thirds majority of the legislature.
  • NC Section 143: Details the permissible content for provincial constitutions, prohibiting inconsistencies with the NC. Specifically, section 143(1) stipulates that the provisions must not contravene the essence of the national Constitution, and section 143(2) places additional restrictions that require compliance with the values enshrined in NC section 1, amongst other criteria.

In prior case law, including Ex Parte Chairperson of the Constitutional Assembly: In Re Certification of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996 and Ex Parte Speaker of the KwaZulu-Natal Provincial Legislature: In Re Certification of the Constitution of the Province of KwaZulu-Natal, the Court emphasized the integral relationship between general constitutional compliance and specific legislative provisions concerning provincial governance ([5]–[6]).

A – Application

The Court conducted a meticulous examination of the WCC against the constitutional parameters described. This included evaluating the legislative and executive structures permitted under NC section 143 ([10]–[19]).

  1. Constitution-Making Powers: The Court reiterated that the constitution-making powers conferred upon provinces must align with overarching national constitutional standards. Any attempt to grant broader powers or functions outside those allotted by the NC would render the provincial constitution non-compliant ([8]–[18]).
  2. Electoral System and Legislative Structure: The Court identified specific provisions regarding the electoral system that diverged from national statutes and determined that such deviations could not be certified due to inconsistencies with NC sections 105 and 106, which prescribe a list proportional representation electoral system ([42]–[46]). Thus, the Court found that provisions such as requiring a system based predominantly on geographic multi-member constituencies, and delegating the power to establish electoral systems to provincial legislation, were inconsistent with national standards ([47]–[50]).
  3. Provisions Relating to Oaths of Office: The repeat provisions concerning procedural requirements for administering oaths also revealed inconsistencies, notably regarding the responsibilities assigned to the President of the Constitutional Court versus the Justice of the High Court of the Western Cape, leading to a lack of certification ([53]–[58]).
  4. Final Complications: The Court considered additional challenges to the provisions relating to the appointment of cabinet members, provincial symbols, and political provisions, determining that most of these did not infringe on the defined constitutional powers as they were canons of local governance exempt from direct conflicts with national legislation ([61]–[68]).

While certain arguments raised by the ANC regarding clarity and constitutional manner were summarily dismissed, the Court placed emphasis on strict adherence to the constitutional framework, ultimately deciding not to certify the WCC in its current form due to these noted inconsistencies ([86]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded that the Western Cape Constitution could not be certified as it stood due to specific instances of inconsistency with the national Constitution. The highlighted areas of non-compliance included provisions relating to the electoral structure, the administration of oaths, and regulatory authority assignments that strayed beyond the ambit of provincial legislative power granted under the NC ([87]).

The case struck a significant chord in affirming constitutional supremacy and delineating the powers bestowed upon provincial legislatures, thereby reshaping considerations for future provincial constitutions within South Africa's constitutional democracy. The ruling underscored the importance of balancing provincial rights with the integrity of national governance frameworks, promoting robust procedural compliance as central to the constitution-making process.

Overall, the judgment reinforced the critical nature of provincial legislative acts being in harmony with the overarching national Constitution, thereby preventing any potential undermining of the unitary framework within which South Africa operates.
