Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2004

### FIRAC Case Note: Road Accident Fund v B Shabangu & Another

**F – Facts:**   
The case involves an appeal by the Road Accident Fund (the Fund) against the partners of a law firm who submitted a claim for compensation on behalf of an impostor pretending to be the widow of a deceased individual. The impostor claimed compensation following a purported accident resulting in the death of the deceased, asserting that she was both the widow and mother of his children. The Fund settled the claim for R258,593.00, which was then disbursed by the attorneys to the impostor. It later emerged that the impostor used a forged identity document and that the deceased had died due to a collision with a tree, not another vehicle. The Fund sought to recover the settlement amount on various grounds, all of which were dismissed by Van der Walt J in the court below, leading to the appeal ([paras 1–3]).

**I – Issues:**  
The key legal issues addressed in this case include:
1. Whether the attorneys owed a legal duty to the Fund regarding the authenticity of their client’s claim and identity ([para 10]).
2. Whether the attorneys breached any express or implied warranties regarding the identity of the claimant ([para 4]).
3. Whether the attorneys were negligent in failing to ascertain the true identity of their client or in failing to conduct further inquiries that could have prevented the fraud ([para 10])?

**R – Rules:**  
The court relied upon a number of legal principles and precedents:
- The concept of an attorney's duty of care to their client, notably that such duty does not extend to opposite parties inherently ([para 11]).
- The test for negligence established in *Kruger v Coetzee* 1966 (2) SA 428 (A), which sets out the criteria for determining culpa (fault) in a legal context ([para 19]).
- Relevant statutory provisions from the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund Act, 93 of 1989, and the Road Accident Fund Act, 56 of 1996, as they outline the Fund's investigatory duties ([para 16]).

**A – Application:**  
The court applied these rules to conclude that the attorneys did not owe a legal duty to the Fund to verify their client's identity. They interpreted "relevant claimant" in the discharge form purely as an authority to settle, not as a warranty of the claimant's identity. The documents submitted did not impose any obligation on the attorneys to ensure the claimant's legitimacy ([paras 5–6]). 

On the issue of potential negligence, the court noted that the facts surrounding the claim—such as the impostor’s temporary identity document and identification by the deceased's brother—were sufficient to obfuscate suspicions of fraud. Essentially, the court held that there were no grounds to assert that the attorneys should have foreseen the fraudulent scheme, and that requiring them to verify identity would undermine the attorney-client relationship ([paras 10, 19]).

Furthermore, while the court acknowledged situations where further inquiries by attorneys may be warranted, it determined that no such indicators existed in this case. Thus, even if a duty had existed, there was no negligence as the attorneys acted reasonably based on the information at their disposal ([paras 17–18]).

**C – Conclusion:**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the attorneys owed no legal duty to the Fund to investigate the genuineness of their client's claims and were not negligent. Consequently, the appeal was dismissed with costs, affirming the lower court's ruling that the Fund could not claim damages from the attorneys for the fraudulent claim submitted by the impostor. The judgment emphasizes the balance between an attorney's responsibilities towards their client and the potential implications of imposing duties towards third parties, particularly in matters involving fraud ([paras 19–20]).
