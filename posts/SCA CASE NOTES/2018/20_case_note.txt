# FIRAC Case Note: Phetoe v State (1361/2016) [2018] ZASCA 20

## F – Facts

The appellant, Tebogo Patrick Ledwaba Phetoe, was initially tried in the Gauteng High Court, Johannesburg, alongside six co-accused on multiple counts, including housebreaking with intent to rob, robbery with aggravating circumstances, and several counts of common law rape [1]. In January 2000, he was convicted on all counts, receiving a life sentence for the rape convictions, along with additional sentences ranging from two to twenty years for the other convictions [1]. 

Following the dismissal of his application for leave to appeal in May 2000, a significant development occurred in November 2012 when the Supreme Court of Appeal granted Phetoe leave to appeal. In June 2016, a majority of the full court changed his convictions from direct involvement to complicity, convicting him as an accomplice to the rapes and maintaining the life sentences while dismissing his appeals on the other counts [2]. 

The events leading to his conviction took place in September 1998, when a group of young men invaded the Umthambeka Section in Tembisa. Among their victims were Ms. D.M. and her two younger sisters, who were assaulted, robbed, and raped during the incident [4]. Ms. D. M. identified Phetoe as a participant in the crime, stating that he laughed when she asked him about the assaults [5]. 

During the trial, although Phetoe was identified by Ms. D.M. as being present during the incidents, she could not definitively link him to the act of rape itself. The trial court, however, inferred his complicity and convicted him based on perceived joint criminal enterprise among the assailants [7]. 

The full court upheld the convictions, emphasizing Ms. D.M.'s credibility while downplaying the lack of direct evidence linking Phetoe to the rapes [8]. The dissenting opinion by Dama AJ argued for the need for direct proof of participation in the crime beyond mere presence [9].

## I – Issues

The central legal issues in this case revolve around the following questions:

1. **Whether the appellant's conviction for rape as an accomplice was justified given the evidence presented** ([12]).
2. **The legal standard for accomplice liability, specifically regarding the necessity for some form of conduct that facilitates or encourages the commission of a crime** ([15]).
3. **The sufficiency of evidence required to establish common purpose when criminal acts occur in multiple locations involving different perpetrators** ([18]).

## R – Rules

The judgment refers to several pivotal legal authorities regarding accomplice liability and principles of common purpose in criminal law:

1. **Accomplice Liability**: As defined in *Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development & another v Masingili* [2013] ZACC 41; 2014 (1) SACR 437 (CC), an accomplice is someone whose actions, although not satisfying all elements for criminal liability in the definition of the crime, nonetheless assist in furthering a crime committed by another [12]. 

2. **Conduct Requirement**: C.R. Snyman articulates that a person is guilty as an accomplice if they unlawfully engage in acts that facilitate the commission of a crime by another [13]. 

3. **Common Purpose Doctrine**: The requirements for common purpose were elucidated in *S v Mgedezi* [1988] ZASCA 135; 1988 (1) SA 687 (A). The prosecution must establish that the accused was present at the scene, aware of the offenses being committed, and shared a common purpose with the perpetrators [19].

4. **Standard of Proof**: The principle requiring the state to prove the guilt of the accused beyond reasonable doubt is enshrined in the Constitution as a fundamental right regarding fair trial standards [20].

## A – Application

In applying the law to the facts, the court found that the State failed to establish that Phetoe participated actively in the commission of the rapes. Despite Ms. D.M.'s identification of Phetoe at the scene, the court recognized that mere presence is insufficient to satisfy the requirements for accomplice liability. The court noted that Phetoe did not actively facilitate or encourage the rapes; his conduct of laughing did not amount to complicity [15]. 

Furthermore, the inference of common purpose based on the appellant's presence at the scene of some crimes did not extend to the other violent acts committed by different co-accused in separate locations. The evidence failed to meet the necessary legal standards to establish that Phetoe had both the requisite mens rea and conduct that would justify his convictions for the additional rapes and related offenses [19][20]. 

Additionally, the dissenting opinion highlighted that the absence of a shared agreement among the perpetrators and the lack of evidence linking Phetoe to multiple crime scenes weakened the State’s case, reinforcing that the prosecution had not met the burden of proof required for a conviction beyond reasonable doubt [20].

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately concluded that the convictions of the appellant except for count 9 were not justifiable based on the evidence provided. The court set aside the majority of the convictions, retaining only the conviction for robbery with aggravating circumstances from count 9, recognizing the necessity for rigorous application of the principles surrounding accomplice liability and common purpose [20]. The ruling underscores the imperative that convictions in criminal law must be rooted in clear evidence demonstrating active participation and not merely inferred from presence or associations during the commission of an offense.

### Implications
This case sets a critical precedent in affirming the standards of proof for accomplice liability and the importance of evidentiary support in establishing guilt beyond reasonable doubt. It emphasizes for future cases that convictions predicated on mere presence or participation without direct involvement in the crime are insufficient for establishing liability in criminal law. The legal clarity provided by this decision reinforces constitutional protections for accused individuals, upholding the foundational tenet of innocent until proven guilty in South African law.
