# Case Note: DE v RH [2015] ZACC 18

## FIRAC Method

### F - Facts
The case at hand arises from the Constitutional Court of South Africa, concerning a delictual claim for damages based on adultery. Mr. DE (the applicant) successfully sued Mr. RH (the respondent) in the North Gauteng High Court for damages resulting from an adulterous relationship between RH and DE’s former wife, Ms. H. DE based his claim on the actio iniuriarum, specifically for **loss of consortium** and **contumelia** (\[3\]). This claim had historically been part of South African law, having been recognized by the Appellate Division in **Viviers v Kilian** (1927). However, a subsequent ruling by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) raised questions about the relevance of this claim in contemporary society, arguing it should be abolished due to changing social norms and legal dynamics (\[4\],[12\]).

The factual backdrop reveals that DE and H began divorce proceedings in June 2010, after which H entered into a relationship with RH (\[6\]). While DE attributed the breakdown of his marriage to the adulterous conduct, H indicated that marital issues had existed long before the affair began (\[7\]).

### I - Issues
The core issue in this case revolves around whether the claim for damages based on adultery should continue to exist within South African law. Specifically, the Constitutional Court examined whether the concept of wrongfulness should attach to the act of adultery, considering contemporary societal values and public policy implications (\[10\],[11\]). Additionally, it explored the constitutional implications regarding the rights of dignity and privacy for the parties involved, questioning if the law could or should intervene in private marital matters (\[39\]-\[62\]).

### R - Rules
In evaluating the legal landscape surrounding the claim, the court primarily relied on foundational principles from the South African Constitution, notably sections 10 (dignity), 14 (privacy), and 18 (freedom of association). The court highlighted the need to develop common law in line with contemporary public policy, as articulated in **Barkhuizen v Napier** \[2007\] ZACC 5. According to **Du Plessis** (1996), judges must adapt common law to reflect societal changes while cautioning that incremental changes should be grounded in solid legal foundations \[16\]. The historical context of the delict was considered, particularly its patriarchal origins where only men could sue for damages against an adulterous third party (\[14\]).

### A - Application
The court upheld that the changing societal norms regarding marriage and adultery have significantly altered the claims to the extent that it ought to be reassessed. The historical perspective illuminated that while the law had once sanctioned claims against adulterous third parties for loss of consortium, the current societal outlook on adultery is far softer. **Public policy**, now infused with constitutional values, suggests that assessing marital fidelity in monetary terms is both misguided and anachronistic (\[63\]).

The court elucidated that the act of adultery itself lacks the requisite element of wrongfulness needed for delictual liability. It emphasized that both the adulterous spouse and the third party have rights to freedom and privacy that must be weighed against the non-adulterous spouse’s right to dignity. Furthermore, it posited that a blend of constitutional rights and evolving societal attitudes must inform adjudications on matters concerning personal relationships (\[62\],[63\]).

### C - Conclusion
In conclusion, the Constitutional Court dismissed DE’s appeal, ruling that the delictual claim for damages based on adultery is no longer valid. The court emphasized that contemporary legal perspectives support the notion that personal relationships should not be subject to punitive damages based on conduct that many in society now view as acceptable or at least understandable in the context of marital dissolution. The court reiterated its commitment to developing the common law in alignment with constitutional values (\[64\],[66\]).

The implications of this judgment notably signal a substantial shift in the understanding of personal relationships within legal frameworks, starkly reflecting the changing mores of society. Thus, while the court recognized the intrinsic sanctity of marriage, it ultimately determined that the law should not force the reparation of damages for personal grievances born of a marital contract that had already disintegrated.
