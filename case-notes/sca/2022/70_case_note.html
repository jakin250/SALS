Case Note: Luke M Tembani and Others v President of the Republic of South Africa and Another (Case no 167/2021) [2022] ZASCA 70

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 70 ### F – Facts This case arose from a comple

Case Note: Luke M Tembani and Others v President of the Republic of South Africa and Another (Case no 167/2021) [2022] ZASCA 70

F – Facts

This case arose from a complex legal battle involving claims for damages by several appellants, including individual farmers and companies, following the dispossession of agricultural land in Zimbabwe due to a government-initiated agrarian reform program that involved land expropriation without compensation ([1]–[5]). Many of these individuals were South African citizens who had filed complaints with the Southern African Development Community (SADC) Tribunal prior to its effective dissolution by a subsequent protocol ([1]–[4]).

After the annulment of the Tribunal’s jurisdiction, the appellants sought recourse against the South African government, accusing it of facilitating Zimbabwe's unlawful actions through its participation in signing the 2014 Protocol, which effectively stripped the Tribunal of its powers and barred individuals from accessing it for claims ([4]–[5], [11]). The high court upheld certain exceptions raised by the government regarding factual and legal causation essential to the delictual claims brought by the appellants. In its decision, it established that no causal link was sufficiently demonstrated between the actions of the South African government and the appellants' claims, substantiating its ruling with an upholding of exceptions related to causation ([11], [24]).

The high court’s order allowed the appellants to amend their particulars of claim but, crucially, upheld the state’s exceptions regarding the claims' viability on causation grounds ([11], [12]). This led to an appeal by the appellants against the high court's finding regarding causation and a cross-appeal by the government to challenge aspects of the high court's ruling that were unfavourable to it ([12], [27]).

I – Issues

The central legal questions in this case revolved around whether:

  1. Causation: The appellants established sufficient factual and legal causation between the alleged wrongful conduct of the South African government and the dispossession experienced by the appellants due to Zimbabwe's actions.
  2. Appropriateness of Exception Proceedings: Whether the high court correctly employed exception proceedings to address complex legal and factual issues, especially given that their nature was marked by intricate questions of policy and law ([11], [14]).
  3. Appealability: The last legal issue pertained to the matters that the appellants and the government could properly appeal, particularly concerning the exceptions that were upheld ([27], [28]).

R – Rules

The court referenced several significant legal standards in reaching its decision:

  • Delictual Responsibility: Causation in delict entails both factual causation (the "but for" test) and legal causation (proximity and policy considerations). Legal authority establishes that the defendant's actions must be closely linked to the harm suffered to establish liability ([23]).
  • Exception Proceedings: An exception is a legal mechanism to dismiss claims without a trial when pleadings do not disclose a cause of action. This involves strict scrutiny of whether, under any interpretation of the pleadings, a cause of action may be established ([14], [20]). The threshold for sustaining an exception is high, requiring that cases with potentially valid claims should proceed to trial unless they are entirely without merit ([14]).
  • Appealability of High Court Decisions: The judgment clarified that an exception's dismissal is not appealable unless the exception pertains directly to jurisdiction or proper legal grounds as determined by previous substantive rules of law ([27]).

A – Application

In its application of these principles, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) found the high court had erroneously conflated factual and legal causation, thus failing to properly analyse the appellants' claims. The SCA asserted that the high court's determination regarding the factual causation exception did not warrant a finding on legal causation, as this was not adequately or appropriately raised in the exceptions ([21], [24]).

The SCA maintained that, for factual causation, the allegations of the appellants should have been considered in a light favourable to them, focusing solely on whether the criteria for factual causation could reasonably lead to a sustainable claim ([25]). Since the appellants' claims rested on unprecedented legal issues, the court concluded that these were better suited for resolution at trial, where full evidence could be examined ([19]–[20]).

C – Conclusion

In summary, the SCA upheld the appeal, finding that the high court had erred in upholding the factual causation exception and rendered no decision on legal causation ([34]). The dismissal of the high court's ruling led to the conclusion that the appellants' claims were to proceed without the termination of their causes due to alleged shortcomings in their pleadings. The court also struck the government’s cross-appeal from the roll, affirming that no legally appealable order had been issued by the high court concerning the uncodified exceptions ([34], [28]).

This ruling reinforces the legal principle that cases involving potentially valid delictual claims should not be dismissed prematurely through exception proceedings, particularly where the factual background remains complex. Furthermore, it underscores the importance of courts assessing the interconnectedness of state actions with the harms suffered by individuals in the context of international treaties and obligations, thereby promoting access to justice and judicial recourse ([11], [12], [34]).
