Zulu and Others v eThekwini Municipality and Others [2014] ZACC 17

The appellants, led by Jabulani Zulu, are residents living on a property they refer to as Madlala Village, situated in Durban (Erf 1112, Mobeni) ([2]).

Constitutional Court 2014 [2014] ZACC 17

F - Facts

The appellants, led by Jabulani Zulu, are residents living on a property they refer to as Madlala Village, situated in Durban (Erf 1112, Mobeni) ([2]). They moved onto this land starting from September 2012 after a series of unmet promises regarding RDP housing from the eThekwini Municipality ([4]–[5]). Following their occupation, the Municipality's Land Invasion Control Unit repeatedly demolished their informal homes without any court order, allegedly on the grounds that they were illegally occupying government land ([6]–[7]).

In response to this ongoing situation, the MEC for Human Settlements and Public Works, KwaZulu-Natal (the third respondent), filed an application in the High Court seeking an interim order to prevent further "invasion" of the Lamontville property ([10–12]). The High Court granted a rule nisi on March 28, 2013, which authorized the Municipality to demolish any structures deemed illegal, leading to concerns from the appellants that they were not cited in these proceedings despite being directly affected ([11]).

The appellants subsequently sought leave to intervene in the High Court application but were denied by Judge Kruger, whose ruling did not provide explicit reasons ([14]–[15]). They appealed both the refusal to intervene and subsequent rulings.

I - Issues

The primary issues before the Constitutional Court revolved around:

  1. Locus Standi: Did the appellants have the legal standing to intervene in the High Court proceedings regarding the interim eviction order?
  2. Constitutionality of the Interim Order: Was the interim eviction order granted by the High Court unconstitutional, particularly in the light of the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE)?
  3. Impact of the Eviction Order: Did the interim order essentially function as an eviction order affecting those already residing on the property?

R - Rules

Locus Standi: The courts must assume the truth of the appellants’ allegations when assessing standing ([21]). The test for locus standi generally requires a direct and substantial interest in the matter. Relevant jurisprudence includes Giant Concerts CC v Rinaldo Investments (Pty) Ltd and Others [2012] ZACC 28, which underscores the need for the court to accept allegations as true when evaluating standing ([21]).

Constitutional Provisions: The Constitution of South Africa guarantees protection against arbitrary evictions in Section 26(3) and condemns the deprivation of property without just cause (Section 25(1)). The PIE outlines procedures for lawful evictions that must be followed before any individual can be removed from their home.

Judicial Precedents: The court referenced Port Elizabeth Municipality v Various Occupiers [2004] ZACC 7, which addressed issues of unlawful eviction and procedural fairness in eviction proceedings, emphasizing the necessity for judicial oversight before eviction.

A - Application

The Constitutional Court found that the appellants had a legitimate interest in the proceedings, as the interim order under scrutiny could adversely affect their rights to occupiers' protections. Zondo J articulated that the order arguably authorized actions that could evict the appellants from homes they claimed to occupy ([25]–[29]).

The Court further reasoned that the interim order did not limit its scope to prevent only new occupants from invading but included those who already occupied the property, thereby invoking strong eviction implications without the necessary due process ([24]–[27]). This indicative reading of the order laid the groundwork for concluding that the High Court had erred in denying the appellants' right to intervene.

Moreover, the Court took note of the Municipality's inconsistent arguments both during the appeal and regarding its actions that contradicted its claim that the interim order did not permit eviction ([36]). It highlighted the Municipality's reliance on the interim order for demolition actions carried out post-appeal, signaling a failure to uphold the protections afforded to vulnerable occupants by law ([30]–[34]).

Judicial scrutiny drew upon both the provision of PIE and constitutional imperatives which sought to enforce fair and just processes before any eviction could occur ([41]–[46]). The Court firmly held that the interim order failed to conform to these established protections and was thus inherently unlawful ([44]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately upheld the appeal, providing a crucial affirmation of the appellants' locus standi to intervene in the eviction proceedings. It found the interim order to be effectively unconstitutional as it allowed evictions without the protections guaranteed by PIE. The Court ordered the Municipality and MEC to pay the appellants’ costs and set aside Judge Kruger's ruling, thereby replacing it with a new order granting the appellants leave to intervene ([38]).

The implications of this decision extend beyond the immediate case, reinforcing the legal principle that evictions must follow lawful procedures with adequate protections, particularly for the most vulnerable populations. It underscores the judiciary's role in safeguarding constitutional rights against hasty or arbitrary governmental actions.
