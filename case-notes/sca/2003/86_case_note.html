Du Plessis v Road Accident Fund (Supreme Court of Appeal)

After being medically boarded in September 1994 due to health issues, du Plessis relied primarily on support from Clack, who continued to work and share their income.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2003

F – Facts

The appellant, Antonie Michael du Plessis, and the deceased, Albert Ernest Clack, were partners in a stable and permanent same-sex union, having lived together since March 1988. They participated in a ceremony resembling a marriage in the presence of witnesses in August 1988, establishing their commitment despite the lack of legal marriage options for same-sex couples at that time ([1]–[3]).

After being medically boarded in September 1994 due to health issues, du Plessis relied primarily on support from Clack, who continued to work and share their income. Clack promised ongoing financial support to du Plessis, which he provided until his death in a motor vehicle accident on September 1, 1999 ([4]–[5]). The Road Accident Fund (RAF) was found liable under the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 (the Act) to compensate for certain damages arising from claims linked to the deceased's death. However, the court a quo dismissed du Plessis's claims for loss of support and for funeral expenses, leading to this appeal for judicial review ([2]–[6]).

I – Issues

The primary issues were:

  1. Whether du Plessis is entitled to claim damages for loss of support from the RAF, considering the common law's limitations regarding the definition of a dependent in the context of same-sex relationships ([6]).
  2. Whether du Plessis can claim funeral expenses incurred after the death of Clack, as his heir ([6]).

R – Rules

The legal framework for this case includes:

  • Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996, particularly sections 17 and 19(a), which establish the RAF's liability concerning loss or damage resulting from negligent driving ([6]).
  • The common law principle that only recognized dependents (e.g., a legally married widow) could claim economic loss due to the death of a partner ([8]).
  • The precedent set in Amod v Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund, where the court acknowledged a contractual right to support arising from a marriage in terms of Islamic law, highlighting the evolving lens through which judges must view partnership dynamics ([10], [22]).

A – Application

The court assessed whether the common law definition of a dependent should be developed to include parties in a same-sex permanent life relationship akin to marriage. The analysis began with the recognition of the reciprocal duty of support established between du Plessis and Clack, bolstered by evidence of their commitment, including wills naming each other as sole heirs and financial interdependence ([12]–[15]).

Guided by evolving constitutional imperatives emphasizing equality and dignity (sections 9 and 10 of the Constitution), the court invoked prior decisions that expanded the notion of familial duty beyond traditional marriage definitions. The judgment reflected acknowledgment of societal changes and demonstrated a willingness to protect the rights of same-sex couples ([19]–[22]).

The court concluded that Clack had indeed undertaken a contractual obligation to support du Plessis, worthy of legal protection. Thus, the death being a wrongful act against du Plessis became apparent, as withholding recognition of such duty would endorse unjust discriminatory practices and fail to align with constitutional values ([26]).

Furthermore, for funeral expenses, the court noted previous case law supporting the entitlement of heirs to recover such expenses incurred due to another's unlawful death, reinforcing du Plessis's standing in this regard ([44]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that:

  1. Du Plessis, as a same-sex partner in a permanent life relationship, is entitled to claim damages from the RAF for loss of support, establishing legal recognition for partners in similar arrangements ([42]).
  2. Du Plessis can also recover funeral expenses as the deceased's sole heir ([44]).

This landmark decision marks a significant evolution in South African law, affirming rights of same-sex partners in dependent actions, and underscores a commitment to equality, dignity, and the recognition of diverse familial structures as aligned with constitutional principles. The judgment not only addresses the issues directly brought forth but also opens avenues for deeper reflections on same-sex relationships in South Africa's evolving legal landscape, illustrating the judiciary’s role in reconciling historical precedents with contemporary societal values ([42]–[43]).
