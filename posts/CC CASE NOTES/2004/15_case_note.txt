Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2004

# Bato Star Fishing (Pty) Ltd v The Minister of Environmental Affairs and Tourism [2004] ZACC 32

### FIRAC Case Note

**F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–7])**

The case at hand involves an application for special leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court of South Africa from a judgment of the Supreme Court of Appeal. The applicant, Bato Star Fishing (Pty) Ltd (hereinafter referred to as "Bato Star"), is a company that has held fishing rights in the deep-sea trawl sector of the hake fishing industry since 1999. The hake industry contributes significantly to the South African economy, generating sales exceeding R1.45 billion annually and employing about 8,000 workers. 

In the allocation process for fishing quotas for the 2002-2005 seasons, Bato Star sought an allocation of 12,000 tonnes of hake but was ultimately awarded only 856 tonnes. This allocation decision, made by the Chief Director of Marine and Coastal Management under the Marine Living Resources Act 18 of 1998, prompted Bato Star to initiate legal proceedings to review this allocation.

Bato Star's dissatisfaction stemmed from both the amount allocated and the procedures followed during the allocation process. The High Court initially sided with Bato Star, citing the Chief Director’s decision as arbitrary and unsupported by clear evidence. However, this decision was overturned by the SCA, which held that the Chief Director had adequately considered the relevant factors, ultimately leading to the appeal to the Constitutional Court.

**I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 19–20])**

The key legal issues presented in this case revolve around:

1. Whether the SCA misconstrued the objectives and principles in section 2 of the Marine Living Resources Act, particularly regarding the necessity for restructuring the fishing industry to address historical imbalances (section 2(j)).
2. Whether the Chief Director’s decision not to fully account for the quantum of hake requested by Bato Star constituted a failure to apply his mind as required by law.
3. Whether an alleged "undisclosed policy change" in the allocation process violated the applicant's right to procedural fairness.

**R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 22–25])**

The court relied on several key legal principles and statutory provisions:

- **Constitution of South Africa, Section 33**: Establishing the right to lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair administrative action.
- **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: This legislation codifies the rights enshrined in section 33 of the Constitution and outlines the grounds for judicial review of administrative decisions (e.g., procedural fairness, misapplication of legal standards).
- **Marine Living Resources Act 18 of 1998**: It establishes the objectives guiding the management of marine resources, particularly the principles laid out in section 2 aimed at promoting equity and historical redress in the fishing industry.

The court also considered case law, particularly *Pharmaceutical Manufacturers Association of SA v President of the Republic of South Africa* [2000] ZACC 1, which emphasizes the constitutional nature of public power review.

**A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 26–55])**

The court extensively analyzed the Chief Director’s decision-making process. It assessed whether he had taken account of the objectives outlined in section 2 of the Act while allocating fishing quotas. The Chief Director, it was argued, was required to ensure the transformation of the fishing industry and to consider the historical context of the fishing rights distribution.

Critical to the court's reasoning was the interpretation of section 2(j) of the Act, which obliges decision-makers to consider the historical imbalances within the industry. The Chief Director's decision to maintain quotas based on a "redistribution pool" raised questions of whether he had applied his mind adequately to the implications of these objectives.

In addressing the argument of procedural fairness, the court indicated that even if a failure to follow internal remedies could arise, it was permissible under exceptional circumstances, as deemed by the High Court.

The Chief Director's allocation process, juxtaposed against the policy guidelines published by the Department, indicated that while transformation was acknowledged as a critical factor, it was not singularly determinative.

**C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 68–114])**

The Constitutional Court dismissed the appeal, affirming the SCA's view that the Chief Director's decision was neither arbitrary nor devoid of rationality. The court concluded that the Chief Director met his legal obligations to consider transformation and historical imbalances, even if the resulting decisions did not yield the outcomes sought by Bato Star.

The court underscored that while Bato Star’s application for a significantly larger quota was not entirely entertained, the context of quota allocation in a heavily regulated industry — shaped by both economic and ecological concerns — required a balance that the Chief Director was tasked with managing.

This ruling has profound implications for the future of regulatory frameworks affecting historically marginalized communities, affirming the necessity of a nuanced approach to transformation while maintaining stability within the vital fishing industry. The court's decision reinforces the importance of both constitutional and statutory mandates to promote equitable economic participation while recognizing the complexities inherent in transforming entrenched industries dominated by legacy and capital. 

This judgment serves as a fundamental touchstone in administrative law, particularly in contexts where historical injustices intersect with contemporary regulatory practices, further solidifying the role of transformation in public decision-making processes in South Africa.
