Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

# FIRAC Case Note: Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development v Mqabukeni Chonco and Others [2010] ZACC 9

## F – Facts
The case concerned an interpretation of a costs order previously issued by the Constitutional Court in **Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development v Chonco and Others (Chonco 1)** ([1]). In that earlier litigation, the Minister's appeal was successful, overturning the decisions of the North Gauteng High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal. The court held that the applicants for pardon should not bear the costs of their legal actions due to delays and misunderstandings during the proceedings ([2]).

The order of the court awarded costs against the Minister despite the initial appeal being upheld, stating that the applicants “should not be out of pocket” due to their recourse to legal proceedings ([3]). The current dispute arose when the State Attorney asserted that the costs awarded only pertained to the Constitutional Court and did not cover the expenses incurred in the High Court or the Supreme Court of Appeal. Upon receiving this information and after being denied full compensation, the applicants sought clarification from the Constitutional Court about the extent of the cost order ([3]).

The composition of the court had changed since Chonco 1, with four judges replaced by new appointments ([4]). This raised a question of whether the current bench had the authority to interpret the earlier order. The interpretation of the original order was critical, particularly regarding what legal costs it encompassed ([5]).

## I – Issues
The primary issues were:
1. **Does the current Constitutional Court, constituted of different members, possess the authority to interpret a previous order made by an earlier composition of the Court?** ([5]).
2. **What is the scope of the costs order from Chonco 1? Specifically, does it extend to the costs incurred by the applicants in the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal?** ([3]).

## R – Rules
The court derived its authority from several constitutional and procedural provisions. Section 167(1) of the Constitution states that the Constitutional Court is composed of the Chief Justice, Deputy Chief Justice, and other judges, with a requirement that at least eight judges must hear a matter ([5]). This provision affords the court continuous jurisdiction over budding legal matters even when its composition changes.

Furthermore, Rule 29 of the court’s rules allows the application of High Court Rules, including Rule 42, which permits a court to vary its judgments if there is ambiguity, error, or omission ([11]). Relevant case law such as **Firestone South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Genticuro AG** provides a framework for interpreting judgments collectively with the court’s reasoning ([9]). 

## A – Application
The Court affirmed its jurisdiction to decide the dispute concerning the interpretation of the costs order from Chonco 1 ([6]). It ruled that, regardless of changes in the bench's composition, the court must ascertain the meaning and effect of its own order. The justices recognized that the prior judgment's intention was clear—that the applicants "should not be out of pocket" due to their legal challenge ([8]).

Upon reviewing the specific terms of the costs order, it was evident that although the order commanded the Minister to pay costs in the Constitutional Court, it did not explicitly address costs from the High Court, leading to ambiguity about this aspect ([9]). However, the court interpreted that the context and underlying rationale supported the applicants' assertion that costs from all jurisdictions, including the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal, were indeed covered by Chonco 1 ([8]).

The interpretation that the order should encompass High Court costs falls under the ambiguity provision in Rule 42, allowing the court to rectify this lack of explicitness. The ruling noted that the absence of specification likely stemmed from an oversight rather than a deliberate exclusion ([12]), thus necessitating a corrective variation of the previous order.

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court held that the cost order from Chonco 1 must be clarified to include the costs incurred by the pardon applicants in the High Court as well as the Supreme Court of Appeal. Consequently, the court varied its prior order to explicitly state that the Minister is responsible for all the respondents' costs across all stages of litigation ([14]). This decision not only addresses the immediate confusion surrounding costs but also reinforces principles of justice, ensuring that litigants are not disadvantaged due to procedural uncertainties. The ruling signifies that courts maintain the flexibility to remedy previous decisions through its inherent powers and applicable rules ([14]). 

Overall, this case underscores the iterative nature of judicial interpretation and the necessity for clarity in court orders, ensuring equitable treatment for litigants in the judicial system.
