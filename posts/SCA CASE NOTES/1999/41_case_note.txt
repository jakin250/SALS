Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 1999

# Case Note: Dorbyl Marine (Pty) Limited v Department of Trade and Industry (Supreme Court of Appeal)

## F - Facts

This case revolves around a dispute concerning the General Export Incentive Scheme (GEIS) and the binding nature of an arbitrator's award regarding local content requirements in the context of shipbuilding ([para 1]). The appellant, Dorbyl Marine (Pty) Limited ("Dorbyl"), was primarily engaged in shipbuilding and repair and sought to appeal an earlier decision ([para 2]). 

In October 1989, the Government announced the introduction of the GEIS effective from April 1990, aimed at incentivizing the export of processed products, with subsidies determined by a formula based on local content ([para 2]). The appellant constructed and exported three vessels between February 1992 and March 1993, attempting to achieve substantial local content to qualify for the maximum subsidy ([para 4]). 

To qualify for the GEIS, Dorbyl needed to receive approval from the Department of Trade and Industry ("the Department") for certain 'transformations' of imported inputs into locally manufactured products ([para 4]). After the Department initially refused these approvals, it indicated that omissions of certain imported items from the subsidy calculations might still allow Dorbyl to qualify for a higher subsidy ([para 5]). 

Following the Department's rejection of the transformation claims, an arbitration agreement was formed, wherein the dispute over the subsidy was submitted to an arbitrator who issued directions regarding submissions and documents required ([paras 6-8]). Throughout the arbitration, it became apparent that the parties had defined the issues around four specific points, excluding transformation claims ([para 10]).

Despite this, the arbitrator referred to transformations in his award, even stating that the permission for omissions was invalid but suggesting that some transformation claims should have been allowed ([paras 12-13]). The Department contended that these remarks were not binding, as transformation issues had not been a part of the arbitration ([para 14]). 

The High Court agreed with the Department, resulting in Dorbyl's appeal before the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 15]).

## I - Issues

The central issue in this appeal was whether the statements made by the arbitrator regarding the transformation claims were binding on the Department, given that those claims had not been considered during the arbitration process ([para 1]). This raised several sub-issues, notably:

1. Whether the arbitration agreement encompassed the transformation aspect that was not part of the original disputes defined by the written submissions.
2. The implications of the arbitrator's authority and the definition of his mandate regarding the binding nature of his award about transformations ([paras 15-19]).

## R - Rules

The legal framework governing the arbitration in this matter was primarily rooted in the principles surrounding the arbitration agreement as outlined in McKenzie N.O. v Basha 1951 (3) SA 783 (N) ([para 16]). The parties had explicitly defined the issues to be arbitrated, which did not include transformations, thus setting the parameters within which the arbitrator operated.

Furthermore, the nature of an arbitrator's authority is determined by the scope of the arbitration agreement; any award or statements made outside of this scope would typically not bind the parties involved ([para 16]).

## A - Application 

The Supreme Court of Appeal focused on the intended scope and understanding of the arbitration agreement. It was clear that the four issues defined in Dorbyl's written submissions did not include any transformation claims. Thus, when the arbitrator did reference transformations in his award, this was outside the mandate agreed upon by the parties ([paras 18-19]).

The court concluded that by accepting the arbitrator's written directions, the parties did not modify the arbitration agreement to encompass issues not originally in dispute. The arguments presented by Dorbyl, claiming that the arbitrator was empowered to decide on unaddressed issues, were rejected by the court, which emphasized a need for clarity in arbitration agreements concerning what disputes are to be resolved ([para 19]).

Moreover, the court highlighted that the essence of arbitration is the mutual consent of the parties regarding what issues should be subjected to arbitration, and to expand these issues without express agreement could result in significant unfairness ([paras 18-19]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Dorbyl's appeal, affirming that the remarks made by the arbitrator regarding the transformation claims were not binding due to the absence of these claims within the originally defined arbitration issues ([paras 19-21]). 

This decision reinforces the importance of clear, explicit definitions in arbitration agreements and the limitation of an arbitrator's authority to the scope laid out by the parties. The implications of the judgment are significant for parties entering into arbitration agreements, as it reaffirms that any claims or aspects not explicitly included in the submissions may not be considered by the arbitrator, thus underscoring the principle of party autonomy in arbitration. 

In summary, the court's ruling illustrates the boundaries of arbitratorial mandates and serves as a cautionary tale for parties in structuring their arbitration agreements comprehensively to avoid potential disputes regarding the scope of arbitration.
