Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

### FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title:** EB Steam Company (Pty) Ltd v Eskom Holdings Soc Ltd  
**Neutral Citation:** EB Steam Company (Pty) Ltd v Eskom Holdings Soc Ltd (979/2012) [2013] ZASCA 167 (27 November 2013)  
**Court:** The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Coram:** MTHIYANE AP, CACHALIA, WALLIS, PILLAY and WILLIS JJA  
**Heard:** 6 November 2013  
**Delivered:** 27 November 2013  
**Summary:** This case concerns a winding-up application and the peremptory requirement under s 346(4A) of the Companies Act 61 of 1973 regarding the service of application papers to employees.

---

#### F – Facts

[1] The appeals arise from final winding-up orders made by Tsoka J for twenty companies, all subsidiaries of EB Steam Holdings (Pty) Ltd, due to their failure to satisfy arbitration awards issued against them on 1 November 2011. Despite a notice under s 345(1)(a) of the Companies Act 61 of 1973 being delivered to each company, no payments were made, leading to the winding-up applications under s 346(1), read with s 344(f) of the same Act. Although the 1973 Act was repealed, its provisions remained applicable through the Companies Act 71 of 2008 as per s 9 of Schedule 5.

[2] The applications were not contested on the merits. The sole contention was that there was improper service to the employees of the companies, contrary to s 346(4A) of the 1973 Act which imposes strict obligations on applicants regarding the notification of employees and their representatives.

[3] The appellant companies argued that the method of service employed by the sheriff—affixing the application to the front door of the registered office—was inadequate. There were claims that this did not comply with the directives of s 346(4A)(a)(ii) as employees had not been served properly.

[4] The appellants further contended that the nature of the companies’ operations, offering steam at various locations, indicated that employees likely worked elsewhere, and therefore the service executed failed to meet the statutory requirements. 

[5] The background also detailed that the sheriff served the applications at the registered office, which was central to the companies' operations, yet there was no clear indication of employee presence or numbers at that location. The supervisory affidavit did not clarify whether any employees belonged to a trade union, thus complicating compliance with service requirements.

---

#### I – Issues

[1-2] The primary legal issues considered by the court concerned:
- The interpretation and application of s 346(4A) of the Companies Act, particularly whether the service performed by the sheriff constituted compliance with the statutory mandate.
- Whether the court could grant a winding-up order without the applicant having fulfilled the peremptory service obligations stipulated by the Act to ensure that employees are adequately notified of the proceedings.

---

#### R – Rules

[5-7] The court referenced specific provisions within the Companies Act 61 of 1973, emphasizing the mandatory nature of s 346(4A)’s requirements. This section mandates that:
- A copy of the application must be provided to registered trade unions representing employees and directly to employees themselves.
- The service methods prescribed are exclusive yet allow for reasonable alternative measures if prescribed methods are ineffective.

[15-17] The court cited previous case law to elucidate on the nature of these obligations. The requirement for furnishing application papers was deemed peremptory, contrasting the methods of service, which were considered directive and adaptable to circumstances as long as the statutory purpose of notification was met.

---

#### A – Application

[18-21] The judge elaborated on s 346(4A), clarifying that while it is necessary to furnish application papers to specified individuals, the chosen method of doing so is less rigid. In this instance, the court found that merely proving theoretical compliance—such as affixing applications to the premises—did not fulfill the obligation if it did not reasonably ensure that the employees were informed.

[22-24] The judgment highlighted that the court retains discretion in determining the adequacy of service, capable of adapting the method used based on the realities of the case before it. It also acknowledged potential barriers to effective communication, such as location changes or the closure of business operations.

---

#### C – Conclusion

[25-28] The court concluded that the failure to adequately inform employees, in light of the specifics of the case, precluded the validity of final winding-up orders. Therefore, the court upheld the appeal, setting aside the final orders and substituting them with provisional orders to allow for proper compliance with the notification requirements.

The court mandated that the appellant companies must furnish employees with the necessary application papers within a specified timeframe. Costs were directed to follow the future findings on these provisional orders, safeguarding the respondent’s position in the ongoing proceedings.

The decision reflects the judiciary's commitment to ensuring that statutory provisions regarding employee notification are respected in liquidation proceedings, emphasizing protective measures for employees in corporate insolvency scenarios.

**Order Summary:**
1. The appeals were upheld, final winding-up orders were set aside, and provisional orders were implemented.
2. Appellants were ordered to furnish the application papers to employees.
3. The respondent will recover costs unless the provisional orders are discharged.
