Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# Case Note: Speaker of the National Assembly and Another v Women's Legal Centre Trust and Others [2024] ZACC 18

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts

This case arises from an urgent application for an extension of the period of suspension regarding a prior declaration of invalidity made by the Constitutional Court concerning the Marriage Act and the Divorce Act ([1]-[2]). In **Women’s Legal Centre Trust v President of the Republic of South Africa** ([2022] ZACC 23), the Court declared these Acts inconsistent with sections 9, 10, 28, and 34 of the Constitution for failing to recognize marriages solemnised under Sharia law ([1]).

Originally, the declaration of constitutional invalidity was suspended for 24 months, expiring on 27 June 2024, to allow Parliament to rectify the identified constitutional defects ([2]). On 23 April 2024, approximately two months before the suspension's expiry, the applicants—comprising the Speaker of the National Assembly and the Chairperson of the National Council of Provinces—submitted an urgent application for a further extension of the suspension until 27 June 2026 ([5]-[6]).

The First Respondent (Women’s Legal Centre Trust) and the Second Respondent (President) filed notices to abide, while others did not participate in the proceedings ([7]). Parliament argued that the extension is necessary to facilitate meaningful public consultation regarding the proposed Marriage Bill, reflecting a commitment to constitutional democracy and participatory governance as enshrined in sections 59(1)(a) and 72(1)(a) of the Constitution ([8], [9]).

Due to the complexities of the proposed legislation, which included multiple religious contexts, Parliament demonstrated that the timeframe initially allotted was insufficient for adequate public involvement and deliberation ([10]-[12]). They asserted that not granting the extension would contravene the initial court order, leading to legislative gaps affecting affected communities' rights ([12], [13]).

### I – Issues

The principal legal issues addressed by the Court include:

1. **Urgency of the Application:** Whether the circumstances justified an urgent application for an extension of the suspension period ([15]).
2. **Power to Grant Extension:** Whether the Court has the authority to grant the requested extension and the implications of doing so within the framework of the Constitution, particularly considering section 172(b) ([17]).
3. **Justification for Public Participation:** Whether the need for public involvement in the legislative process qualifies as a just and equitable reason for extending the period of suspension ([19]) and whether the constitutional commitment to participatory democracy contextually supports this request.

### R – Rules

The key rules of law relevant to this case include:

1. **Section 172 of the Constitution:** This section provides courts with the authority to rule on the constitutionality of legislation and to provide remedies that are just and equitable ([17]).
2. **Precedent in **New Nation Movement** Cases:** Prior judgments established the Court's discretion in extending the suspension of an invalid law as a means to afford Parliament the opportunity to remedy defects ([17]).
3. **Principles of Participatory Democracy:** Courts have emphasized the importance of public participation in legislative processes, which is a cornerstone of South Africa’s constitutional democracy as noted in cases like **Doctors for Life** ([19]).

### A – Application

The Court analyzed the application for urgency by assessing the factors surrounding it. It noted that Parliament initiated proceedings only five weeks prior to the expiry of the initial suspension, which justified urgency due to the impending legislative vacuum and the duty to comply with the initial order ([16], [19]).

In evaluating parliamentary actions, the Court took judicial notice of the complexities involved in drafting a Marriage Bill that incorporates multiple religious law frameworks ([10], [20]). It found that delaying the legislative process would not only contravene constitutional obligations but also undermine the principles of participatory democracy essential to crafting effective laws ([19]).

The Court highlighted Parliament’s proactive steps taken already, such as the approval of the Divorce Amendment Act, and recognized that the First Respondent had not opposed the application, suggesting no significant prejudice would be incurred by granting the extension ([18], [19]).

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court, affirming the principles of participatory democracy and respecting the need for adequate public consultation prior to enacting laws that significantly affect societal relations, concluded that the extension of the declaration of invalidity was necessary and just. The Court ordered that the suspension be extended until 27 June 2026, reasoning that this would facilitate a meaningful legislative process while fulfilling constitutional obligations ([3], [21]).

The implications of this decision emphasize the Court's commitment to ensuring that legislative processes remain inclusive and responsive to all affected communities, particularly in matters as sensitive and culturally significant as marriage laws. Thus, the Court reaffirmed its role as a guardian of constitutional rights while supporting the legislative process designed to rectify past legislative deficiencies.
