FIRAC Case Note: City Power (Pty) Ltd v Grinpal Energy Management Services (Pty) Ltd and Others [2015] ZACC 9

F – Facts

The case involves an application for leave to appeal against the decision of the Labour Appeal Court, following the dismissal of City Power’s appeal concerning the automatic transfer of employees under section 197 of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA) ([1–6]). City Power, a municipal entity responsible for electricity distribution in Johannesburg, had awarded a tender to Grinpal Energy Management Services to manage prepaid metering systems for electricity. Following allegations of fraudulent certification, City Power terminated its service agreements with Grinpal, but later agreed to a full handover of the project infrastructure, services, and employees to City Power until a new contractor was appointed ([2–6]).

Grinpal contested that this transfer amounted to a “transfer of business as a going concern” as envisioned in section 197 of the LRA, which stipulates the automatic transfer of employees' contracts upon the transfer of a business. The Labour Court ruled in favor of Grinpal, asserting that a transfer had occurred. City Power's subsequent appeal to the Labour Appeal Court also failed ([7–10]).

I – Issues

The central legal questions before the Constitutional Court were:

  1. Does section 197 of the LRA apply to municipal entities like City Power, especially in the context of the relationship between the LRA and the Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 and the Municipal Finance Management Act 56 of 2003? ([14–19])
  2. Was there a legitimate transfer of business as a going concern, triggering the provisions of section 197? ([35–40])

R – Rules

The relevant legal provisions and precedents include:

  • Section 197 of the LRA: This section stipulates that if a business is transferred, the new employer automatically replaces the old employer regarding employment contracts, rights, and obligations. This provision is pertinent in determining the fate of Grinpal’s employees ([25–28]).
  • Municipal Systems Act: This Act outlines the regulatory framework for municipal entities. Section 1 defines a municipal entity, while sections 66 and 93A specify the operational parameters for municipal employment practices ([19–21]).
  • Constitutional Provisions: Sections 152 and 160 of the Constitution emphasize the duty of municipalities to provide services sustainably and efficiently ([31–33]).

A notable precedent cited was NEHAWU v University of Cape Town [2002] ZACC 27, which discussed the criteria for establishing whether a transfer has taken place ([36]).

A – Application

The Court analyzed the interplay between the LRA and the Municipal Systems Act. It established that section 197 prevails over conflicting provisions in other laws related to employment during transfers ([28–30]). The Court acknowledged that while municipal entities like City Power must operate within the constraints of these Acts, they also cannot evade the provisions of the LRA which seek to protect employee rights.

The findings emphasized that the facts clearly indicated a transfer of business as a going concern ([39]). The evidence showed that City Power assumed all necessary business elements, including infrastructure, customer base, and personnel expertise from Grinpal. This established continuity in service delivery while maintaining the essence of a “going concern” ([39–40]).

On the applicability of section 197, the Court reiterated that if the defined criteria are met—specifically the transfer of business by one employer to another—the automatic transfer of employees applies unless otherwise agreed in writing per section 197(6) ([31–33]). In this case, no such agreement existed.

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court upheld the Labour Appeal Court's findings and confirmed the applicability of section 197 of the LRA to municipal entities like City Power. It concluded that a transfer of business had indeed occurred, mandating the automatic transfer of Grinpal's employees to City Power. The judgment emphasized the need for all employers, including public entities, to recognize labor rights within the framework of their operational agreements.

The order thus granted condonation and leave to appeal, upheld the Labour Court's ruling but amended it to explicitly require City Power to comply with section 197 regarding employee transfers. City Power was further ordered to bear the costs of the application ([41]).
