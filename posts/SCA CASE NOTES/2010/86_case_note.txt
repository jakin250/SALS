# FIRAC Case Note: Lombaard v Droprop CC (377/09)

**Neutral Citation:** Lombaard v Droprop CC (377/09) [2010] ZASCA 86 (31 May 2010)  
**Coram:** NAVSA, HEHER, MHLANTLA, MALAN and SHONGWE JJA  
**Heard:** 17 May 2010  
**Delivered:** 31 May 2010  
**Corrected:** 4 August 2010  

---

### F – Facts

This case involved an appeal by Mr. Jan Antonie Lombaard (the appellant) against a judgment of the KwaZulu-Natal High Court (Ndlovu J), which dismissed his application to compel Droprop CC (the first respondent) and its members to transfer immovable property to him ([1]–[2]). Lombaard claimed he had a right to enforce the transfer based on an agreement of sale following his exercise of an option to purchase included in a lease agreement with Droprop. The property in question was described as “Portion 526 (of 432) of the Farm Melkhoute Kraal No 789” and comprised approximately 2.0797 hectares ([2]).

The High Court dismissed Lombaard's application on two primary grounds: (1) the exercise of the option was invalid as the third respondent, Dharumdaw Preethepaul, did not possess written authorization from Droprop to sign the lease agreement on its behalf, and (2) the description of the property did not comply with the requirements of section 2(1) of the Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981 (the ALA), as the property was not identified with reasonable certainty ([3]).

During the appeal, the respondents retracted the first ground regarding authority, focusing instead on the adequacy of the property description and raising a defense regarding the true intent of the original contract, which had not been sufficiently addressed in the lower court ([4])–[6]. 

### I – Issues

The central issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:
1. Whether the description of the immovable property in the lease complied with the requirements of section 2(1) of the ALA, particularly regarding identifying the property with reasonable certainty ([3]).
2. Whether the principle from the case of Northview Shopping Centre (Pty) Ltd v Revelas Properties Johannesburg CC & another [2010] ZASCA 16 could be applied, allowing consideration of the respondents' defense concerning the intention behind the agreement, despite that issue not being initially raised in the lower court ([4]).
3. The court's discretion to refer the matter for further evidence when a factual dispute arises, and the implications of the appellant's failure to request such a referral at the initial stage ([19]–[20]).

### R – Rules

The legal framework guiding this case primarily involved the ALA, specifically section 2(1), which stipulates the requirements for the validity of property descriptions in agreements of sale ([3]). Previous case law also significantly influenced the court's considerations, including:

- **Cromhout v Afrikaanse Handelaars en Agente (Edms) Bpk** 1943 TPD 302 and **Lugtenborg v Nichols** 1936 TPD 76, which discuss property descriptions and interpretations of intent ([9]).
- **Blundell v Blom** 1950 (2) SA 627 (W) and **Van Niekerk v Smit** 1952 (3) SA 17 (T), which reaffirm the principle that a description may not be vitiated merely by the inclusion of the word "certain" ([10]).
- The **Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd** 1984 (3) SA 623 (A) case, establishing that when factual disputes arise in motion applications, the respondent's version must be accepted unless clearly untenable ([24]).

### A – Application

The court conducted a detailed analysis of the property's description and the surrounding circumstances of the lease. It ultimately found that the description of the property, although differing from that in the title deed, was legally sufficient under the ALA and did not render the agreement void ([11]). The phrase "certain" was interpreted in a way consistent with the principle that it described a specific, identifiable portion of property as intended by the parties ([10]).

Further, regarding the absence of a request for rectification or a referral to oral evidence, the court acknowledged the appellant's prior legal strategy, which effectively limited his engagement with the factual allegations raised by the respondents. The respondents had alleged that the agreement incorrectly reflected their intentions, a point that the appellant had initially dismissed as irrelevant ([23]). The court emphasized that a genuine dispute of fact had not been adequately addressed and that such a dispute warranted an opportunity for oral evidence to resolve ambiguities regarding the terms of the agreement ([19]–[26]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the lower court's dismissal of the appeal, finding that the description of the property complied with the statutory requirements of the ALA. However, the judges concurred on the need for further inquiry regarding the parties' intentions and the legitimacy of the claims raised by the respondents. The judgment underscored the principle that in situations where the factual basis of the contract is contested, there is a judicial obligation to ensure that both parties have the opportunity to present their case fully.

The court dismissed the appeal with costs, indicating that despite the sufficient property description, factual disputes related to intent and authority required resolution through potential rectification or further examination ([27]). The implications establish a precedent for how factual disputes surrounding real estate contracts are considered, emphasizing the necessity of clear and accurate descriptives in contracts to avert litigation disadvantages, particularly regarding intentions and expectations between the parties involved.
