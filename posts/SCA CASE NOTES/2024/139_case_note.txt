Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

### Case Note: Naraidu v The State (894/2023) [2024] ZASCA 139

---

#### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–2])

The appellant, Mr. Seshin Naraidu, a licensed tax practitioner, was charged, alongside two co-accused, with multiple counts of fraud and alternative charges under the Value-Added Tax Act 89 of 1991 (VAT Act), related to the fraudulent claim of VAT refunds. The specific allegations included that Mr. Naraidu and his co-accused misrepresented facts to the South African Revenue Service (SARS), claiming that Serghony’s Shoes Fashion CC (SSF) had incurred expenses that entitled it to a refund, knowing that the documentation submitted was false and that SSF was not entitled to any such refunds ([para 1]).

Mr. Naraidu was convicted on three counts of fraud in the Regional Court, Gauteng, and sentenced to six years of imprisonment. His co-accused did not appear for sentencing and remained at large. Following the Regional Court’s decision, an appeal was lodged with the Gauteng Division of the High Court, seeking to contest the conviction. This appeal was dismissed, prompting Mr. Naraidu to seek special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 2]).

---

#### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 2–3])

The key legal issue in this case revolved around whether the state met its burden of proof to establish that Mr. Naraidu knowingly participated in fraudulent activities, specifically whether he possessed the requisite intent to defraud SARS through the submission of fictitious documents ([para 5]). The court was tasked with evaluating both the direct evidence of intent to defraud and the implications of Mr. Naraidu’s actions in relation to the fraudulent claims made by SSF. 

Additionally, questions arose regarding the validity of the statutory charges under the repealed VAT Act, particularly whether the prosecution could validly rely on provisions that were no longer in effect due to the enactment of the Tax Administration Act 28 of 2011 ([para 12]).

---

#### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 5–6, 12–13])

The court relied on the principles governing criminal fraud, which entail proving both the actus reus (the guilty act) and the mens rea (the guilty mind) beyond a reasonable doubt. The evidentiary burden lies with the state to establish that the accused acted with fraudulent intent, understanding that their actions would mislead SARS ([para 11]).

The court also noted the relevant statutory provisions, specifically referring to the VAT Act and the Tax Administration Act, emphasizing that while the charges of fraud were directly under common law, the statutory charges must connect to the provisions available at the time of the alleged offenses ([para 12]). The Supreme Court underscored the critical elements of statutory interpretation to ascertain the applicability of previously repealed provisions.

---

#### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 7–11])

In its analysis, the court examined the emails and communications sent by Mr. Naraidu to SARS. These communications indicated his involvement and purported to communicate issues related to the VAT refunds. However, the court emphasized that merely seeking updates on the claims did not equate to knowledge of the documents’ fraudulent nature. 

The court expressed concerns about the quality and credibility of Mr. Naraidu’s evidence during the trial, noting that while it suggested a reckless disregard for his duties as a tax practitioner, it did not establish direct intent to defraud. The mere act of resubmitting documents did not culminate in a definitive conclusion of guilt; the court required explicit proof of Mr. Naraidu’s awareness of the fraudulent nature of the claims ([para 10]).

Ultimately, the court concluded that the state had not discharged the burden of demonstrating beyond a reasonable doubt that Mr. Naraidu had the requisite intent to defraud. Given the absence of direct evidence linking him to the knowledge of fraud, his conviction was rendered unsafe, leading to the overturning of the lower court's decision ([para 11]).

The discussion surrounding the alternative statutory charges highlighted further complexity, particularly the relevance of repealed provisions. Since the charges were based on an interpretation of the VAT Act prior to the enactment of the TAA, the court demonstrated skepticism about the validity of such charges when considering the chronology of events and applicable legislation ([para 12]).

---

#### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 13–14])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the appellant, Mr. Naraidu. The court upheld the appeal, set aside the conviction and sentence imposed by the Regional Court, and acquitted Mr. Naraidu of all charges. The judgment underscored the importance of proving intent in fraud cases, clarifying that a conviction cannot rest solely on actions that suggest recklessness without corroborating evidence of knowledge or intention to deceive.

The implications of this ruling resonate within the context of tax law and practices of tax practitioners, emphasizing the necessity of rigorous standards for establishing intent to defraud in criminal proceedings. The court’s observations on the statutory charges further highlight the challenges posed by legislative repeals and their implications for prosecuting historical offenses, prompting a reevaluation of charges based on prior law post-enactment of new legislation ([para 14]).

In conclusion, the ruling serves as a significant precedent reinforcing the standard of proof required in fraud-related cases and clarifies the interpretation of statutory provisions in light of legislative changes.
