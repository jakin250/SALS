Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# Case Note: AK v Minister of Police [2022] ZACC 14

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The case of **AK v Minister of Police** [2022] ZACC 14 arises from a deeply distressing incident where the applicant, AK, was attacked, held captive, and raped from the evening of December 9 to the morning of December 10, 2010, at Kings Beach in Gqeberha (formerly Port Elizabeth) ([1]–[3]). The incident illustrates the ongoing and pervasive issue of gender-based violence within South Africa, a country grappling with severe rates of such violence even 25 years post-apartheid ([2]).

The applicant sought to hold the Minister of Police, as the officer responsible for the South African Police Service (SAPS), liable in delict for the negligence exhibited during the police's search and subsequent investigation after her assault ([4]). Upon her capture, the police were alerted and arrived at the scene, yet their search efforts were claimed to have been inadequate and non-compliant with the constitutional and statutory obligations to protect the citizenry ([8]–[10]).

Following her escape, the applicant endeavored to promote a thorough investigation by SAPS but found herself repeatedly frustrated by their inadequate response and actions. Eventually, after extensive efforts to compel the police to conduct a thorough investigation, she initiated legal action against the Minister of Police in November 2013 ([14]).


### I – Issues
The foremost legal questions before the Constitutional Court revolved around whether the SAPS breached its duty of care towards the applicant by failing to conduct a competent search and investigation, which resulted in psychological harm to the applicant. Specific issues included:

- Did the police’s act or omission in the search operation constitute a breach of their constitutional duty to the applicant?
- Was there wrongful conduct on the part of the police, and is there a need to affirmatively establish negligence merely on the grounds of outcomes or judgment?
- Should the actions or omissions of the SAPS from both the search and investigative phases be deemed negligent according to delictual standards? ([46]–[60]).

### R – Rules
In its judgment, the Constitutional Court referred to several crucial rules and precedents, primarily grounded in the principles of delict. Legal authorities forming the backbone of the case included:

- The constitutional obligations under **Section 205(3)** of the Constitution, which mandates the SAPS to prevent, combat, and investigate crime ([162]).
- The principles of negligence delineated in **Kruger v Coetzee** ([9]), which establishes that negligence arises when a reasonably competent individual would foresee potential harm and fails to take reasonable steps to prevent it.
- This legal framework must be aligned with the values espoused in the **Bill of Rights**, particularly concerning gender-based violence, signifying the state's duty to protect vulnerable groups ([117]).
  
Furthermore, various precedents, including **Carmichele v Minister of Safety and Security**, asserted the necessity of imposing a duty of care on the police as part of their constitutional mandate ([124]).

### A – Application
In applying these rules, the court analyzed the actions undertaken by the SAPS during both the search and investigation phases. The High Court, in its earlier ruling, found that the police officers failed to conduct a basic search in the immediate vicinity where the applicant was believed to be held. It noted that even when the police had arrived on the scene, they did not conduct the necessary foot searches ([18]–[20]).

The SAPS’s negligence was further compounded by inadequate communication and coordination between the police units, which ultimately demonstrated an “extreme indifference” to the applicant’s situation. This was seen in the failure to adequately follow up with potential suspects, including nearby “bush dwellers,” and the delay in reviewing critical CCTV footage that could have supplied vital evidence for the investigation ([224]–[249]).

The Constitutional Court ultimately found that the SAPS had not taken reasonable care in executing their mandated duties, which directly contributed to the applicant's prolonged trauma. The panel ultimately held that the police’s actions exhibited substantial negligence, where their failures could reasonably be linked to the psychological harm suffered by the applicant ([88]–[106]).


### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court overturned the decision of the Supreme Court of Appeal and held the Minister of Police liable for 40% of the damages suffered by the applicant due to the negligent omissions of the SAPS ([129]). The ruling underscored the critical statutory and constitutional duty of the SAPS to protect citizens from gender-based violence and suggested that failing to meet these obligations may invoke institutional liability.

This case reaffirms the vital need for police accountability and adequate response mechanisms when dealing with gender-based violence, sending a clear message about the imperative for the SAPS to uphold its duties with due diligence. This ruling is significant in that it establishes legal grounds for holding police accountable for negligent omissions, thereby reinforcing the rights of individuals in circumstances involving extreme vulnerability ([126], [275]). 

Furthermore, the court's decision regarding the costs suggests a protective stance for victims, thereby mitigating the potential chilling effect that costs claims might have on future victims seeking redress [287]. 

The implications of this ruling resonate across public and legal policy landscapes and highlight the continuous struggle against gender-based violence, aligning judicial accountability with societal norms.
