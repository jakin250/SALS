Case Title: Shipalana v S [2019] ZACC 20 Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa Coram: Mogoeng CJ, Cameron J, Froneman J, Jafta J, Khampepe J, Ledwaba AJ, Madlanga J, Mhlantla J, Nicholls AJ, and Theron J Judgment: Froneman J (Unanimous) Decided On: 17 May 2019

Constitutional Court 2019 [2019] ZACC 20 Court: Constitutional Court of South Afr

Case Title: Shipalana v S [2019] ZACC 20
Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa
Coram: Mogoeng CJ, Cameron J, Froneman J, Jafta J, Khampepe J, Ledwaba AJ, Madlanga J, Mhlantla J, Nicholls AJ, and Theron J
Judgment: Froneman J (Unanimous)
Decided On: 17 May 2019

F – Facts ([paras 1–4])

The case involves an application for leave to appeal by Hyden Godfrey Shipalana against his convictions for murder and kidnapping, as well as the sentences imposed by the High Court of South Africa, Circuit Local Division for the Northern Circuit District, Tzaneen. The applicant contended that the trial court had misapplied the doctrine of common purpose, potentially rendering the conviction unconstitutional. He presented several arguments, including (1) misapplication of the common purpose doctrine, (2) the unconstitutionality of the doctrine, (3) insufficient evidence to prove his guilt beyond a reasonable doubt, and (4) incorrect interpretation of facts that violated his right to be presumed innocent.

Despite the complexities involved, the applicant’s request for leave to appeal was ultimately late, requiring him to seek condonation to address the procedural deficiency.

I – Issues ([paras 1–3])

The central legal issues presented to the Constitutional Court included:

  1. Whether the trial court improperly applied the doctrine of common purpose, impacting the validity of the conviction.
  2. Whether the potential unconstitutionality of the doctrine warranted the court's intervention.
  3. Whether the lack of credible evidence undermined the finding of guilt beyond a reasonable doubt and infringed the applicant’s right to be presumed innocent.

Additionally, the court needed to determine the appropriateness of granting condonation for the late application for leave to appeal, considering the interests of justice in the case of an incarcerated applicant.

R – Rules ([paras 1–3, 5])

The court referred to established principles surrounding the common purpose doctrine in criminal law. The principle permits the attribution of liability for criminal acts by one member of a group to other participants, under specific circumstances. The court also highlighted the necessity of factual accuracy in findings made by trial courts, indicating that appellate courts generally refrain from reassessing evidence unless substantial constitutional issues arise.

Moreover, the court acknowledged previous cases where the application of the common purpose doctrine had been scrutinized, particularly citing Jacobs v S [2019] ZACC 4, discussing whether its misapplication raised constitutional concerns.

Under the framework of the Constitution of South Africa, specifically regarding the right to a fair trial, including the presumption of innocence, the court was guided by the importance of ensuring that no undue procedural technicalities obstructed the attainment of justice.

A – Application ([paras 5–6])

In applying the law to the facts of the case, the court reaffirmed that the applicant's arguments concerning the misapplication of the common purpose doctrine lacked merit. The facts established that the applicant was present during the commission of the crimes, participated in the assaults, and endeavored to conceal the body of the deceased after the fact. Thus, the trial court's findings were deemed credible and supported by sufficient evidence.

The court noted that the trial judge had the prerogative to assess the credibility of witnesses and the weight of the evidence submitted, and it found no substantive justification to overturn these factual determinations. Consequently, the court concluded that no compelling constitutional issue arose from the trial court's application of the law.

Regarding the late application for leave to appeal, the court displayed leniency towards incarcerated applicants acting pro se. However, it ultimately decided that condonation would be granted solely to assess the substantive merits of the case and not to substantiate the appeal itself.

C – Conclusion ([paras 7–8])

The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the application for leave to appeal on the basis that it was not in the interests of justice to exercise its jurisdiction under the present circumstances. The court recognized that while the applicant's arguments suggested potential substantial implications regarding the common purpose doctrine, they did not sufficiently establish a constitutional issue warranting appellate review.

The order rendered was clear: condonation for the late application was granted, but the application for leave to appeal was dismissed. This outcome underscores the Judicial Authority's adherence to both procedural rigor and substantive justice, affirming the role of trial courts in making determinations based on the credibility of evidence presented, reaffirming the importance of the presumption of innocence, particularly in serious criminal matters.

This case reinforces the court's stance that, while procedural leeway may be afforded to incarcerated individuals, substantial grounds must exist to alter findings of fact established by lower courts, particularly in relation to established doctrines such as common purpose. The implications of this ruling may extend to future applications concerning the doctrine and the rights of accused individuals under the Constitution.
