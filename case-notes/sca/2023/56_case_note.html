Supreme Court of Appeal

Cohen N O & Others v D[…] [2023] ZASCA 56

The Trust made substantial capital gains from property sales between 2013 and 2014, resulting in significant allocations to beneficiaries, including a total of R6,329,939 to Ms D[…] ([2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 56

F – Facts

The case Cohen N O & Others v D[…] concerns the appellant-trustees of the Century City Property Investment Trust (the Trust) and the respondent, Ms M[…] L[…] D[…] (hereafter “Ms D[…]”). The appellants, who are the trustees, include Mr G[…] W[…] D[…] (a former spouse of Ms D[…] among others who are related as beneficiaries). Notably, Ms D[…] was a beneficiary of the Trust during her marriage to Mr D[…], which ended in divorce on February 8, 2019 ([1]–[2]).

The Trust made substantial capital gains from property sales between 2013 and 2014, resulting in significant allocations to beneficiaries, including a total of R6,329,939 to Ms D[…] ([2]). In July 2021, she issued summons against the Trust for this amount, which the trustees contested, leading to her application for summary judgment ([3]).

The Western Cape Division of the High Court initially granted summary judgment in favor of Ms D[…] for an alternative amount not claimed in her particulars of claim, prompting the trustees to appeal. Importantly, the appeal centered on whether summary judgment could be granted when the cause of action was based on an incorrect trust deed ([3]–[4]).

The appellants asserted several grounds of appeal, primarily focusing on the compliance with procedural rules under the amended Rule 32, which mandates verification of the cause of action and an explanation of why the defence does not raise a triable issue ([4]–[5]).

As it turned out, both parties discovered during the hearings that the proper governing document was not the amended trust deed dated 2015 but the original trust deed of 2006, raising issues of pleader integrity and accuracy ([14]–[15]).

I – Issues

The key legal issues identified in this case include:

  1. Compliance with Rule 32(2)(b): Whether Ms D[…] complied with the procedural rules concerning the verification of her cause of action and the nature of her claims ([20]).
  2. Cause of Action Variance: Whether the summary judgment application was based on a cause of action materially different from that in her particulars of claim ([21]–[22]).
  3. Bona Fide Defence: Whether the trustees disclosed a bona fide defence to Ms D[…]’s claim, especially considering the trustees’ acknowledgment of the allocation amounts but contesting the basis of their payment ([29]–[30]).
  4. Applicability of Trust Deeds: The legal implications stemming from the reliance on an incorrect trust deed in the particulars of claim ([14]–[22]).

R – Rules

The court applied several legal principles governed by trust law and the procedural rules of civil procedure:

  1. Summary Judgment Standards: Under Rule 32(2)(b) of the Uniform Rules of Court, summary judgment can only be granted where the plaintiff verifies the cause of action and identifies the legal basis for the claims, alongside an explanation for why the defence proposed does not raise any issue for trial ([20]).
  2. Cause of Action: A cause of action is defined as the factual basis upon which the court can impose liability ([22]). The court, therefore, evaluated whether Ms D[…]’s claim was grounded in the flawed trust deed relied upon during proceedings.
  3. Bona Fide Defence Requirement: The trustees were required to demonstrate that they had a legitimate defence that warranted being heard in court. The standard does not necessitate that they prove the defence at the summary judgment stage ([29]).
  4. Trustees' Discretion: Clause interpretations from both the original and amended trust deeds, focusing on the discretion allotted to trustees regarding payments and vesting, were pivotal in determining the case ([15]–[16]).

A – Application

In evaluating the facts against the established rules, the court scrutinized Ms D[…]’s particulars of claim, which were based on the alleged powers conferred under the amended trust deed. However, the inherent flaw lay in that the allocations in question occurred under the original trust deed—the relevance of which was evident only at the summary judgment hearing ([14]).

The court found that Ms D[…]’s reliance on an incorrect trust deed rendered her particulars of claim fundamentally defective and that she could not verify the cause of action as required ([22])—despite her justification that the trustees were aware of this reliance. The trustees, though admitting to the allocations for taxation purposes, contended that the payment was contingent upon a vesting event, which had not yet occurred, thus arguing for their discretionary power in withholding payments until such an occasion ([30]).

The high court's remarks were scrutinized, especially in regard to whether the trustees behaved in bad faith by allegedly allowing Ms D[…] to rely on an incorrect basis for her claims ([18]–[19]). The court also delineated factors under which a discovery of common cause facts interferes with claims that are not preserved in pleadings, stressing that merits cannot cure procedural defects.

Ultimately, the court concluded that the issue of discretion exercised by the trustees fell within an acceptable legal framework, recognizing that their defence was genuine and not a mere sham ([30]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the high court had erred in granting summary judgment in Ms D[…]’s favor as the trustees presented a bona fide defence that entitled them to have their case heard. The appeal was successful, and the summary judgment was set aside, allowing them to defend the main action ([34]).

The implications of this ruling extend to the interpretation of trust deeds and the rigorous verification requirement under procedural law. The case highlights the delicate balance between statutory compliance and equitable relief, reaffirming the necessity for both procedural correctness and the legitimate exercise of discretion by professional trustees in trust-related disputes. The decision encourages parties to ensure precise procedural adherence or risk undermining their claims at the summary judgment stage.
