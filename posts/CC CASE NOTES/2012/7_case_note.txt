Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note: Maccsand (Pty) Ltd v City of Cape Town and Others

### CCT 103/11  
[2012] ZACC 7  

**F – Facts**:  
This case involves a dispute regarding the overlapping jurisdiction of national mining laws and provincial land use regulations. Maccsand (Pty) Ltd (Maccsand) was granted mining permits by the Minister for Mineral Resources to mine sand from the Rocklands and Westridge dunes in Mitchell's Plain, which were zoned as public open space under the Land Use Planning Ordinance (LUPO) ([paras 1-20]). Upon beginning mining operations at Rocklands in February 2009, the City of Cape Town sought an interdict on the grounds that Maccsand was in violation of LUPO by engaging in mining without rezoning the land ([para 22-23]). The High Court sided with the City, issuing interdicts against Maccsand until it obtained the necessary authorizations under both LUPO and the National Environmental Management Act (NEMA) ([para 26]). Maccsand and the Minister appealed this decision, arguing that LUPO should not apply to land for which mining rights have already been granted under the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act (MPRDA) ([paras 24-28]).

**I – Issues**:  
1. Does the MPRDA permit a holder of mining rights to exercise those rights without complying with LUPO, especially in situations where the land is not zoned for mining?
2. Should the court rule on the conditional cross-appeal by the MEC regarding the constitutionality of the MPRDA and its impact on LUPO?
3. Does Maccsand's situation highlight a conflict between LUPO and the MPRDA that requires judicial intervention ([paras 34-35])?

**R – Rules**:  
The legal framework consists primarily of:
- **MPRDA**: Governs mining rights, emphasizing the state's custodianship of mineral resources and ensuring that mining is conducted sustainably and in a manner that promotes social and economic justice ([para 3-5]).
- **LUPO**: Regulates land-use in the Western Cape, requiring municipalities to ensure that activities comply with land use zoning ([paras 15-18]).
- **NEMA**: Advocates for environmental protection and outlines the processes for obtaining necessary authorizations for activities that may affect the environment ([para 9-10]).
- **Constitution**: Division of powers among different levels of government, asserting that the mining function is allocated to the national government, while land use planning falls under provincial jurisdiction ([para 41]).

**A – Application**:  
The court analyzed whether LUPO applied concurrently with the MPRDA in regulating land use for mining. It acknowledged that despite the MPRDA’s exclusive focus on mining, LUPO regulates land use and zoning, which is a separate but critical concern. The constitutional requirement for cooperative governance implies that both laws can coexist without one infringing upon the other’s jurisdiction ([paras 42-48]). It further held that neither statute invalidates the other; rather, they serve different but complementary roles in governance ([para 44]).

In addressing the conditional cross-appeal by the MEC, the court noted that the failure to establish a clear conflict justifies denying this relief, maintaining that the MPRDA’s provisions on mining do not automatically negate the relevance of LUPO ([paras 52-53]).

**C – Conclusion**:  
The Constitutional Court concluded that the lower courts correctly interpreted the applicable laws, affirming that a holder of mining rights, such as Maccsand, must still comply with LUPO's zoning requirements. The appeal was dismissed as it was not in the public interest to exempt Maccsand from compliance with provincial regulations simply due to the existence of a mining permit under the MPRDA ([paras 59-60]). Additionally, the Court found no merit in the MEC’s cross-appeal, thereby reinforcing the constitutional framework that mandates cooperation among government spheres rather than creating an adversarial relationship ([paras 30, 58]).

This case underscores the importance of both environmental sustainability and compliance with land use regulations during mining operations, reflecting broader constitutional mandates that emphasize cooperative governance and environmental protection within South Africa's legal landscape.
