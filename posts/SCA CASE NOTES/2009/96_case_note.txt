Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2009

# Case Note: Manyatshe v M & G Media Limited [2009] ZASCA 96

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts ([1]–[25])

The appellant, Maanda Manyatshe, was the former CEO of the South African Post Office and, at the time of application, the CEO of MTN South Africa (Pty) Ltd. He filed an urgent application in the Johannesburg High Court seeking an interim interdict against the publication of an article in *Mail & Guardian* that he alleged was defamatory ([1]). The article was to discuss serious allegations made against him concerning his actions while at the SA Post Office, particularly regarding a tender process for refurbishing retail outlets ([2]).

Prior to the article's publication, Manyatshe received a questionnaire from journalists at *Mail & Guardian* detailing allegations of fraud related to his involvement with a company called Vision Design House ([3]). Following the dismissal of his application for an interdict by Snyders J, Manyatshe asked for and obtained leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([1]). Despite the publication of the article, Manyatshe argued that the case involved significant legal principles concerning press freedom and justice ([1]).

The details of the allegations included serious misconduct in the tender process, misrepresentation to the Post Office Board, and involvement in a fraudulent scheme ([3]–[4]). The respondents countered with supporting evidence for these allegations, specifically an affidavit from Manyatshe's successor, which was submitted in a separate civil claim concerning the same matter ([5]).

After his application was dismissed, Manyatshe's appeal was argued on various legal grounds, primarily focusing on the implications of his high public profile and the effect of the article on the administration of justice ([21]).

### I – Issues ([10]–[26])

1. **Mootness of the Appeal:** The central issue was whether the appeal was moot, given that the article had already been published, and the appeal would have no practical effect ([10]–[11]).

2. **Defamation and Press Freedom:** Key considerations included whether the publication should have been banned based on defamation and the balance between Manyatshe's right to reputation and the media’s right to freedom of expression ([9], [22]).

3. **Potential Interference with Administration of Justice:** The appellant argued there was a risk of prejudice to his rights as a future accused in a potential criminal trial due to the publication of the allegations ([19]).

4. **Weight of Public Profile and Dignity:** The appeal also raised questions about how the court weighed Manyatshe's standing as a public figure and the concept of ubuntu, emphasizing respect and dignity ([21]).

### R – Rules ([8]–[24])

1. **Legal Principles for Interim Interdicts:** The court evaluated the application under established principles for interim interdicts from *Hix Network Technologies v System Publishers (Pty) Ltd*, requiring a prima facie right, a well-grounded fear of irreparable harm, the balance of convenience, and a lack of alternative remedies ([8]). 

2. **Section 21A of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959:** The provision that allows dismissal of an appeal if it has no practical effect was invoked. It indicates that courts should not engage with moot cases unless they involve significant issues of law or public interest ([11], [12]).

3. **Defamation Case Precedents:** The appeal involved interpretations of previous cases, such as *Independent Newspapers Holdings Ltd v Suliman*, which related to the timing and manner of disclosing a suspect's identity in connection with criminal allegations ([14]).

4. **Balancing Rights:** The constitutional rights to freedom of expression (s 16) and to human dignity (s 10) are heavily weighted in cases of defamation. The court previously recognized that both sides must be balanced carefully, taking into account the public interest ([22]).

### A – Application ([9]–[23])

1. **Mootness Determination:** The court concluded the appeal was moot because the article had already been published. Even if the court found that an interim interdict should have been granted, the appellant could not be restored to his former state as the publication had already occurred ([12], [13]). 

2. **Evaluation of the Application's Merits:** The court affirmed Snyders J's finding that Manyatshe failed to refute the substantive evidence backing the allegations, which undermined his claim of defamation and the urgency for an interdict ([9]). The balance of convenience was found to favor the respondents as the press had a right to disseminate information on matters of public importance ([8], [12]).

3. **Interference with Justice Claim:** The appellant's argument about potential interference with the administration of justice was dismissed as speculative. The court posited that it is inadequate to suggest any publication harms justice simply because it involves allegations that might be part of future legal proceedings ([19], [20]).

4. **Public Profile and Ubuntu Consideration:** While Manyatshe's public profile and dignity were acknowledged, the court found that such factors alone did not elevate the case to one of significant legal interest. The balancing act prescribed by defamation law had already been adequately articulated in previous decisions and left no ground to reinterpret it in this appeal ([22], [23]).

### C – Conclusion ([24]–[26])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Manyatshe's appeal, finding it moot under section 21A of the Supreme Court Act. The decision emphasized the absence of practical effect since the article had already been published and reinforced established legal principles regarding the balance between press freedom and individual reputation. The dismissal included costs awarded to the respondents, given Manyatshe’s unsuccessful bid for an interim interdict and the nature of the battle regarding constitutional rights. The court noted that the core issues did not present novel legal questions worthy of reconsideration ([26]). 

This ruling underscores the judiciary's aversion to adjudicating cases that do not present ongoing relevance, especially in the realm of defamation and press freedom, reflecting on the delicate balance between these competing interests in a democratic society.
