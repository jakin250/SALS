City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality v Gauteng Development Tribunal and Others [2010] ZACC 11

Constitutional Court 2010 [2010] ZACC 11

Facts:
The case arises from a constitutional challenge by the City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality (the City) against Chapters V and VI of the Development Facilitation Act 67 of 1995 (the Act) that empowered provincial development tribunals to adjudicate applications for land rezoning and township establishment. The constitutional challenge resurfaced due to significant disputes about the jurisdictional powers of various governmental spheres concerning land development in Johannesburg. Following unsuccessful attempts to resolve these disputes, the City instituted proceedings in the South Gauteng High Court, contesting the validity of the Act and seeking a review of specific Tribunal decisions approving land developments contradictory to its integrated development plan ([1-4]).

Initial hearings in the High Court supported the Tribunal's powers to operate under the Act, asserting that "municipal planning" did not encompass the challenged powers, which were determined to form part of "urban and rural development" ([14-17]). Consequently, the City’s appeal led to a ruling from the Supreme Court of Appeal, declaring Chapters V and VI unconstitutional but suspending the invalidity for 18 months ([2]), prompting the matter's escalation to the Constitutional Court for confirmation.

Issues:
The primary issue for determination was whether Chapters V and VI of the Act were unconstitutional due to inconsistencies with the constitutional framework governing the distribution of powers between national, provincial, and local governments ([19]). The Court also had to address whether the suspension of the declaration of invalidity served the interests of justice ([19]).

Rules:
The Court’s analysis drew heavily from Section 156 of the 1996 Constitution, which delineates local government powers concerning municipal areas (where "municipal planning" is specific and exclusive to local authorities) and relevant functional areas listed in Schedule 4 of the Constitution, which includes “municipal planning” ([45]). The Constitution’s principles outlined in Sections 40 and 41 emphasize the autonomy of each government sphere and reiterate that one sphere cannot unilaterally assume roles reserved for another ([44]).

The Court also referenced interpretations from previous cases, establishing that legislative authority does not confer the right for overlapping functional powers between spheres without constitutional expressiveness ([43-45]).

Application:
The Court evaluated whether the authority granted to provincial development tribunals by the Act encroached on the City’s exclusive powers surrounding land usage through its integrated development plan ([70]). The decision hinged on dissecting the ordinary meaning of "municipal planning," concluding it encompassed the authority to regulate land development applications, including rezoning and township establishments ([57-58]). Thus, the Court upheld the Supreme Court of Appeal's earlier ruling, confirming that the powers attributed to the provincial tribunals undermined the constitutional scheme and local governmental integrity by infringing on the City’s exclusive rights to plan and permit land use within its jurisdiction ([70-71]).

In analyzing remedies, the Court acknowledged significant concerns regarding the immediate cessation of the Act’s powers, which may lead to governance disruptions ([74-83]). Ultimately, the Constitutional Court opined that a suspension of invalidity for 24 months, coupled with strict conditions to safeguard municipal autonomy during this time, would achieve a just and equitable balance ([83]).

Conclusion:
The Constitutional Court confirmed the Supreme Court of Appeal's ruling declaring Chapters V and VI of the Development Facilitation Act unconstitutional, thereby upholding the supremacy of municipal planning authority as enshrined in the Constitution ([86]). The order for suspension of invalidity was extended to 24 months under carefully crafted conditions intent on preserving municipal powers and facilitating effective governance while legislative amendments were pursued. The Court further reinforced the importance of adherence to constitutional frameworks governing intergovernmental relations and planning authority within the multi-layered governance structure of South Africa ([95]).

This ruling has profound implications for governance, urban development, and land use management, highlighting the necessity for clear functional demarcations between municipal and provincial powers within the constitutionally prescribed intergovernmental relations.
