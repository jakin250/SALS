Constitutional Court 2019 [2019] ZACC 37

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

[1] This case concerns Mr. Deverajh Moodley, whose son, Remano, attended Kenmont School, a special needs institution. During Remano's enrollment, he faced a disciplinary inquiry due to an alleged incident involving another student. As part of the inquiry, Remano was subjected to an isolation measure, which prompted Mr. Moodley to seek urgent legal intervention. A consent order from the High Court dictated that Remano should not be isolated and that the school would conclude its disciplinary process within a specified timeframe.

[3] Subsequently, Kenmont School’s Governing Body amended its admission policy, instituting a rule that disqualified re-admission for students with "behavioural problems." Mr. Moodley was informed that Remano would not be re-admitted based on this new policy. He opted not to make representations to the school regarding the decision and instead challenged the new policy in the High Court.

[5] The High Court ruled that the decision to deny Remano's re-admission was unlawful, but the Governing Body appealed this decision. By the time the matter reached the Supreme Court of Appeal, Remano had completed his matriculation, raising questions about the practical effect of the case. The Supreme Court, consequently, dismissed the appeal, ordering the school to pay Moodley's legal costs.

[7] Following the judgment, the school attempted to have a warrant of execution set aside based on section 58A(4) of the South African Schools Act, which prevented the attachment of public school assets for legal actions. In response, Mr. Moodley launched a counter-application seeking payment of assessed costs and declared the mentioned section of the Schools Act unconstitutional on grounds of violating the rights to equality and dignity under sections 9(1) and 10 of the Constitution.

[11] The High Court affirmed this view, declaring section 58A(4) constitutionally invalid and imposing costs on the school respondents. The school then sought confirmation of the High Court's declaration from the Constitutional Court.

I – Issues

[12] The Constitutional Court was tasked with addressing the following key issues:

  1. Whether section 58A(4) of the South African Schools Act, which prohibits the attachment of public school assets in civil litigation, is constitutionally valid.
  2. Whether this prohibition infringes upon Mr. Moodley’s rights to equality and dignity, given the differential treatment in enforcing court orders against public schools compared to private parties in similar circumstances.

R – Rules

[17] The case relied heavily on constitutional provisions, particularly those enshrined in the Bill of Rights, namely:

  • Section 9(1): Ensures equality before the law and equal protection and benefit of the law.
  • Section 10: Guarantees the right to dignity.
  • Section 28(2): States that a child's best interests are of paramount importance.
  • Section 29(1)(a): Affirms the right to basic education.

The judgment also referenced relevant case law, including Nyathi v Member of the Executive Council for the Department of Health Gauteng [2008] ZACC 8, which identified similar limitations concerning state liability and had declared unconstitutional provisions that imposed unequal burdens on private litigants.

A – Application

[23] The Court evaluated whether the limitation posed by section 58A(4) was reasonable and justifiable under section 36(1) of the Constitution. The judgment acknowledged that public schools operate under significant constraints and that allowing creditors to attach school assets could gravely disrupt educational access, particularly for disadvantaged communities. The judiciary underscored that these schools serve an essential social function and that depleting their resources through litigation could harm the very children the law aims to protect.

[28] It was maintained that while the rights to equality and dignity are fundamental, the state has a compelling interest in ensuring that schools remain functional and provide education, especially in historically underserved areas. The inevitable harm that would come from allowing asset attachments, including the loss of facilities and educational materials crucial for student success, justified the limitation.

[32] The Court ultimately concluded that while the limitation is absolute, it serves the crucial purpose of safeguarding access to education. The necessity to protect vulnerable children’s rights to basic education outweighed potential disadvantages to litigants seeking legal remedies.

[34] Regarding the costs awarded initially to Mr. Moodley, the Court delineated that the prohibition on attachments does not extend to barring cost orders, reinforcing the principle laid out in earlier judgments that orders of court must be obeyed, regardless of contentions related to their lawfulness.

C – Conclusion

[57] The Constitutional Court ultimately ordered that the High Court’s declaration of unconstitutionality regarding section 58A(4) of the Schools Act be not confirmed. Furthermore, the Court directed Kenmont School to pay Mr. Moodley’s taxed costs within three months, emphasizing compliance with court orders to uphold the rule of law and the dignity of court processes.

The ruling illustrates the balance courts must maintain in protecting constitutional rights while recognizing the operational realities of educational institutions in South Africa. The implications of this decision affirm the importance of maintaining functional public schools, whilst also clarifying the nature of liabilities and costs associated with legal proceedings involving public educational bodies.

References

Moodley v Kenmont School and Others [2019] ZACC 37; Nyathi v Member of the Executive Council for the Department of Health Gauteng [2008] ZACC 8.
