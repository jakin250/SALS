Case Note: SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

## Case Note: CTP Limited and Others v The Director-General, Department of Basic Education and Others ([2018] ZASCA 156)

### F – Facts
The present case arises from a tender process instigated by the First Respondent, the Director-General of the Department of Basic Education (DBE), for the printing, packaging, and distribution of workbooks to learners in South Africa ([1]). The tender was originally awarded to the Lebone Consortium, which had been the incumbent since 2011. Bids were invited on 13 November 2015, with a closing date of 14 December 2015, for a contract that would span from 1 April 2017 to 31 March 2020, with an option to extend for two additional years ([2]–[3]). 

The evaluation criteria were set to assess compliance, functionality, price, and empowerment credentials, with a stringent requirement that bidders must achieve a minimum of 80% in the functionality phase to proceed ([4]). After evaluation, only CTP Limited Joint Venture (CTP JV) and the Lebone Consortium met mandatory requirements, yet CTP JV scored below the functionality threshold and was excluded from further evaluation ([8]).

This evaluation process faced several hurdles, including a subsequent cancellation of the tender in April 2016, and a complex litigatory backdrop before a court order was issued, allowing CTP JV's bid to be re-evaluated ([9]–[12]). The re-evaluation ultimately reaffirmed the Lebone Consortium's superiority on functionality metrics, leading to the continued awards of the tender ([13]).

CTP JV brought an action against this decision, challenging it on four main grounds: material irregularities in scoring, a failure to consider legal advice, and improper involvement of officials associated with the bidding process ([14]–[15]).

### I – Issues
The critical issues to resolve included: 
1. Whether the evaluation process employed by the Bid Evaluation Committee (BEC) was constitutionally valid under Section 217 of the South African Constitution, which mandates fairness and transparency in procurement processes ([16]).
2. Whether the deviation from established evaluation protocols constituted ground to declare the tender award unconstitutional ([16]–[18]).
3. The impact of alleged procedural irregularities in the scoring process regarding CTP JV's exclusion and differential treatment of the bidders ([28]–[30]).

### R – Rules
Relevant laws and precedents include:
- **Section 217 of the Constitution**: It outlines the requirement for procurement processes to be fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective ([16]).
- **Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act No. 5 of 2000**: This Act provides a framework that must be followed by state entities in procurement ([17]).
- **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA)**: This act allows for judicial review of administrative actions that are procedurally unfair ([46]).
- **Allpay Consolidated Investment Holdings (Pty) Ltd & others v Chief Executive Officer of the South African Social Security Agency & others**: The judgment establishes that any irregularity in the procurement process must be assessed legally to ascertain its impact on the fairness of the decision ([18]).

### A – Application
The court examined the applicability of these rules against the facts presented. It noted that the BEC adopted a consensus-seeking scoring method rather than the standard independent scoring required by the established evaluation protocol ([21]–[23]). CTP JV contended this method led to a deviation that affected its scoring outcome negatively, asserting that the raw average score would have met the functionality threshold ([27]).

Further review indicated inappropriate and differential treatment, as only CTP JV’s scores were moderated downwards without similar scrutiny applied to Lebone Consortium's higher scores ([24]–[25]). The court highlighted the importance of not conflating procedural norms with ultimate outcomes in deciding on fairness of the execution process ([38]). The failure to have a consistent, transparent scoring method led to the conclusion that the tender award was constitutionally indefensible and did not meet the requirements of Section 217 ([46]).

Moreover, it was also brought to light that the opinion of independent legal advice was not adequately considered in the decision-making process, suggesting an absence of objectivity and accountability ([42]). The involvement of officials previously engaged in the tender process demonstrated further weaknesses in maintaining the integrity of the procurement procedure ([44]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the tender award to Lebone Consortium was constitutionally invalid, stating that the process did not uphold the principles of fair and equitable treatment of bidders as mandated by Section 217 ([50]). The court ordered that the invalidity would be suspended until 31 March 2020 to allow for the uninterrupted provision of workbooks to schools, mitigating potential disruptions in education delivery ([50]). 

The implications of this ruling extend beyond the case at hand; it underlines the necessity for transparency and equal treatment in public procurement, ensuring that all processes adhered strictly to procedural fair play to engender public trust in state decisions. The judgment sets a significant precedent in the realm of public procurement law and administrative justice in South Africa, reaffirming that procedural integrity remains a cornerstone of constitutional governance.
