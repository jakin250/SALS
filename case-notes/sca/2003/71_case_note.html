The Road Accident Fund v Rhesia Scholtz

Supreme Court of Appeal 2003

F – Facts

The appellant in this matter is the Road Accident Fund (the "Fund"), established under the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996, and it succeeded the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund, which was created under the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund Act 93 of 1989 (the "Act") ([1]). The respondent, Rhesia Scholtz, allegedly sustained injuries in a motor vehicle collision on June 3, 1994 ([4.1]). At the time of the collision, Rhesia was a minor, having been born on March 24, 1976 ([4.2]).

Upon becoming aware of her injuries, the respondent lodged a claim with the Fund on February 29, 2000, and served a summons on July 20, 2000 ([4.4], [4.5]). The Fund raised a special plea of prescription, arguing that the claim had lapsed under the Act's provisions. The parties agreed to separate the issue of prescription from the merits of the case, which subsequently led to a hearing before Swart J in the Transvaal Provincial Division of the High Court ([2]).

Swart J ruled against the Fund, dismissing the special plea with costs. The Fund appealed this decision, and the essential issue was whether the summons had been served within the prescribed time frame ([3]). Complicating matters was the respondent's status as a minor at the time of the accident, which implicated the relevant prescription periods outlined in articles 55, 56, and 57 of the Act ([5]).

I – Issues

The central issue in this appeal was whether the five-year prescription period contained in article 57 of the Act began to run at the time of the collision or from the time the respondent attained the age of majority ([10]). The Fund argued that the five-year period was not suspended for minors, while the respondent contended that it should be treated similarly to the three-year period under article 55, which, by virtue of article 56, does not run against a minor ([11]). Thus, the appeal delineated the interplay between the provisions regarding the suspension of prescription for claims made by minors.

R – Rules

The relevant legislative framework includes:

  1. Article 55 of the Act states that claims for compensation shall become prescribed three years from the date the claim arose, notwithstanding any other law regarding prescription, subject to articles 56 and 57 ([6]).
  2. Article 56 provides that the prescription of a claim does not run against:
  • (a) a minor;
  • (b) any person detained under mental health legislation;
  • (c) a person under curatorship ([7]).
  1. Article 57 asserts that no claim lodged under article 62 shall prescribe before the expiry of a period of five years from the date the claim arose ([8]).

Therefore, the rules consist of the foundational procedural timeline outlined by the articles in the Act and their interrelation with respect to minors and their legal rights.

A – Application

The court meticulously applied the relevant articles to the facts surrounding the case. It acknowledged that, as per article 56(a), the three-year prescription period under article 55 does not begin to run until the claimant turns 18. The court accepted that the claim was timely lodged by the respondent on February 29, 2000, as she filed it before the expiration of three years post-age of majority ([9]).

The pivotal point arose regarding article 57. The Fund argued that since article 57 was not expressly qualified by article 56, the five-year prescription period began from the date of the collision ([12]). However, the court refuted this assertion by underscoring that the three-year and five-year prescription periods were implicitly interlinked. Notably, it emphasized that the legislative intent behind the articles reflects an understanding that both periods should be suspended during the minority of a claimant ([19]).

The court clarified that the provisions of articles 55, 56, and 57 must be read harmoniously. It noted that while the language of article 56 speaks specifically to claims under article 55, it implicitly encompasses all claims referred to within the Act that arise from the very nature of being unprepared to pursue legal remedies due to minority status. Thus, aligning the periods of prescription accordingly avoids unjust outcomes and aligns with the protective intent of the law regarding minors ([14], [19]).

The Fund's reliance on the unreported judgment in Toerien v Padongelukkefonds was discredited by the court for lacking substantive reasoning and being contrary to the established interpretation of the articles ([20]).

C – Conclusion

The appeal was ultimately dismissed with costs, reaffirming the court’s interpretation that the five-year prescription period contained in article 57 does not commence against a minor until they reach the age of majority. Consequently, the summons issued by the respondent on July 20, 2000, was deemed to be timely. The court's ruling highlighted the necessity of protecting minors in legal contexts and the importance of interpreting legislative provisions in a manner that aligns with the principles of fairness and justice [21].

This decision has broader implications for future claims by minors under the Road Accident Fund Act, reaffirming the judiciary's role in interpreting statutory provisions to ensure equitable access to justice for vulnerable populations. Legislators may also take this ruling into consideration when drafting or amending legislation that affects rights and claims related to personal injury and compensation.
