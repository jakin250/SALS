Case Note: IN THE SUPREME C O U R T OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1996

### Case Note for **CASE NO. 653/94** - FIRAC Method

#### F – Facts
The Appellants, The Minister of Public Works and Land Affairs and the Director-General of the Department of Public Works and Land Affairs (collectively referred to as "the Appellants"), were involved in a building contract with Group Five Building Limited ("the Contractor"). This contract, concluded in October 1983, pertained to construction works at the Komani Hospital in Queenstown, which involved both new constructions and modifications to existing structures ([para 2]).

In April 1988, the Contractor initiated legal action in the Transvaal Provincial Division, claiming that it was owed additional sums due to unjustified denial of extensions of time amounting to 85 days, leading to penalties specified in the contract. The initial claim (Claim A) sought R65,450, alleging wrongful deductions due to latency attributed to the contractor. Subsequently, Claim B was added in 1992, which claimed R3,467,217.12 based on alleged tacit terms of the contract obligating the timely provision of drawings and site access ([paras 2–4]).

Further complexity arose with Claim D, introduced in 1992, based on an alleged tacit agreement for compensation due to delays and administrative breaches. During trial proceedings, issues regarding the timeliness of actions and resultant damages were contested, alongside a special plea by the Appellants regarding prescription (the expiration of the available time to raise a legal claim) on Claims B and D, leading to various procedural developments ([paras 6–12]).

#### I – Issues
The main legal issues before the court centered on:
1. Whether Claim D constituted a valid cause of action based on implied or tacit terms regarding provision of timely instruction and access ([paras 10–14]).
2. Whether Claim D had prescribed, arising from the alleged delays and administrative lapses suffered by the Contractor ([paras 16–19]).
3. The implications of the contractual relationship's duration concerning when a debt was considered "due" under South African law ([para 24–30]).

#### R – Rules
The court's analysis relied on relevant provisions of the Prescription Act No. 68 of 1969, particularly sections concerning when a debt is due and the commencement of prescription ([para 19]). The court engaged with precedents such as *Electricity Supply Commission v Stewarts and Lloyds of S A (Pty) Ltd* 1981 (3) SA 340 (A), and considered the implications of tacit agreements and implied terms in contracts as defined under common law ([paras 18, 24]).

The court's decision also reflected jurisprudential parameters surrounding breach of contract, the implicit understanding of obligations within construction contracts, and precedents outlining how prescription periods commence only when a debt is "due"—hardly before knowledge of loss or the identity of the debtor is ascertainable ([paras 17, 18]).

#### A – Application
In applying the law to the facts, the court critiqued the lower court's view that final certification of works constituted a moment when the Contractor's claim was actionable. It concluded that Claim D was grounded in breaches which had occurred before the final certification, hence the prescription period had started from a much earlier date ([paras 19–30]).

The court also dismissed the argument suggesting that complexities in construction might delay the Contractor's ability to ascertain losses, emphasizing that such rationale could not delay the start of the prescription period. It held that the Contractor realized the financial implications of any delays upon their occurrence, countering Claims D's validity ([paras 30–34]).

Ultimately, the court found no basis for the lower court's decisions regarding claims of unconscionable delays, as Claim D had undeniably become prescribed by the time litigation was pursued ([paras 33–35]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of South Africa (Appellate Division) overturned the decisions of the court a quo concerning Claims D and its associated orders. It ruled that Claim D had prescribed and thus was dismissed, ordering the Contractor to bear the costs of the appeal, including the fees for two counsels ([para 35]). The decision elucidates the strict adherence to the defined commencement of prescription periods, reinforcing the necessity for contractual clarity and the obligations therein, thereby emphasizing the legal consequences of administrative lapses in the context of contractual obligations in the public works sphere. 

This landmark ruling clarifies the boundaries of liability in contract law, particularly concerning construction contracts, establishing a critical precedent that implications from administrative delays must be recognized promptly, or risk falling beyond the remedy of the law.
