Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note: *Breetzke and Others NNO v Alexander NO and Others* (232/2019) [2020] ZASCA 97

## F – Facts

The case involves a dispute arising from alleged breaches of fiduciary duty by Robert Edward Alexander, a trustee of the Sleepy Hollow Trust (the Trust), concerning its property portfolio in Pietermaritzburg, particularly the SARS property. In September 2012, the Trust resolved to sell its property portfolio. By early 2013, Mr. Alexander offered to buy these properties. On 17 May 2013, the Trust entered into a sale agreement with Ziningi Properties (Pty) Ltd (Ziningi), a company controlled by Mr. Alexander, for slightly more than R179.5 million, which significantly overestimated the value of the SARS property ([1]–[2]).

After acquiring the properties, Ziningi sold the SARS property to Delta Property Fund Limited for R110 million, yielding a profit of over R19 million within six months ([2]). The appellants—trustees for the St Francis Trust (SF Trust), a beneficiary of the Sleepy Hollow Trust—alleged that Mr. Alexander breached his fiduciary duty by failing to disclose the opportunity to sell the property to Delta at a profit to his co-trustees ([6]).

Following an exception raised by Mr. Alexander and Ziningi, the High Court upheld the exception, leading to an appeal ([3]–[4]). The key allegations revolved around Ziningi's knowing participation in the alleged breach of trust by Mr. Alexander ([8]).

## I – Issues

The principal issue in this case is whether the appellants could successfully bring a claim against Ziningi, which was alleged to have knowingly participated in Mr. Alexander's breach of trust. Specifically, the court had to determine:

1. Whether Ziningi's actions—acting as a purchaser through a nominee and profiting from a transaction conducted in breach of trust—constituted wrongful conduct under South African delict law.
2. Whether the allegation of knowing participation in Alexande’s breach of fiduciary duty provided a valid cause of action against Ziningi ([15]).

## R – Rules

The court's analysis began with established legal principles surrounding fiduciary duties and the liability resulting from breaches thereof. This included the seminal ruling in *Robinson v Randfontein Estates Gold Mining Company Limited* which outlines that individuals in fiduciary positions must prioritize the interests of those they serve and cannot profit secretly from their positions ([10]).

Additionally, the court emphasized the joint liability of parties involved in a trustee's breach of duty as recognized in *Yorkshire Insurance v Barclays Bank* and *Standard Bank v Van Rhyn*, indicating that aiding a breach of trust entails liability under the Aquilian action, provided the party had knowledge of the breach ([24], [18]).

## A – Application

The court evaluated how these legal principles applied to the facts of the case. It affirmed that the allegation that Ziningi participated knowingly in Mr. Alexander's breach indicated a sufficient cause of action. The argument that a corporation is a separate legal entity from its directors did not absolve Ziningi from potential liability. The judgment clarified that an entity aiding a breach of fiduciary duty, aware that it was engaged in breaching such duties, bears responsibility for the losses resulting from the breach ([27], [35]).

Furthermore, the argument that Ziningi’s conduct could be likened to that of an innocent third party (akin to a bystander) was rejected. Instead, the court found that Ziningi, being Mr. Alexander's corporate vehicle, could not escape liability simply by virtue of its corporate status—especially given the knowledge that Alexander acted contrary to his fiduciary obligations ([41]).

The court reiterated that or the appellants merely needed to allege the requisite knowledge of the breach to sustain their action against Ziningi, further illustrating that the prohibition against aiding, enabling, or facilitating breaches of fiduciary duty exists to protect vulnerable parties in fiduciary relationships ([37], [39]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal determined that the exception raised by the High Court was improperly upheld. The findings emphasized that Ziningi’s alleged knowing participation in Mr. Alexander's fiduciary breach constituted a valid cause of action under South African law. Consequently, the court upheld the appeal with costs, including those for the employment of two counsel, and ordered the dismissal of the exception ([42]).

This ruling underscores the duty that third parties have not only to refrain from aiding breaches of fiduciary duties but also to act with awareness of the implications of their involvement when a trustee is in breach of their obligations. Failure to adhere to these fiduciary standards invites liability, thereby reinforcing principles of good faith and the protection of beneficiaries within trust contexts. The decision is pivotal for establishing clarity in how fiduciary duties intersect with corporate involvement in transactions managed by trustees.
