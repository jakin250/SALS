Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# Case Note: Wright v Wright (494/13) [2014] ZASCA 126

## F – Facts
The case revolves around a dispute between two brothers, Alec Peter Wright (the appellant) and William Robert Wright (the first respondent), concerning the profits and accounting of their partnership business, Wright Metals CC, the second respondent ([1]–[2]). The brothers consented to appoint a referee, Mr Desmond Snyman, as provided for under section 19bis of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959, to investigate and report on the partnership's accounting ([2]).

Following their consent to refer the matter to the referee, disputes emerged regarding the profitable allocation from 1989 up to the partnership’s dissolution, ordered by Satchwell J in the South Gauteng High Court ([3]). The referee determined that a sum of R1,085,000 was owed to the first respondent from the appellant based on a detailed account review ([3]).

The appellant contested the referee's report on multiple grounds, notably questioning the inclusion of certain expenses, specific calculations of profit, and various interpretations of financial documentation. He argued that genuine disputes of fact necessitated a trial or a hearing for oral evidence, relying on principles from *Room Hire Co (Pty) Ltd v Jeppe Street Mansions (Pty) Ltd* ([4], [5]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal were twofold: 
1. Whether the appellant's challenges to the referee's factual findings constituted genuine disputes of fact that warranted a formal trial or an oral hearing ([4], [5]).
2. How the court should assess the factual findings of a referee appointed under section 19bis of the Supreme Court Act, particularly in the context of permissible grounds for introducing challenges to such findings ([1], [11]).

## R – Rules
The legal authorities governing the appointment of referees and challenges to their findings were predominantly based on section 19bis of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959, which allows a court to refer matters involving extensive documentation or disputes related to accounts to a referee for factual investigation. The court may adopt the referee’s findings as its own unless found unreasonable or incorrect ([7], [8]).

The court recognized the precedent set forth in *Young's Estate v Estate Young*, which dictates that the factual findings of a referee must generally be accepted unless shown to be unreasonable or incorrect ([9], [10]). Furthermore, the principles guiding the evaluations included the expectation that any disputes of fact must be substantiated through admissible evidence, a criterion emphasized in cases such as *Doyle & another v Fleet Motors PE (Pty) Limited* ([15], [16]).

## A – Application
The court highlighted that the appellant's contestation of the referee's findings lacked the necessary primary supporting evidence, particularly pointing out the absence of a corroborative affidavit from the spouse who maintained the business accounts ([12], [14]). 

Furthermore, it was noted that while the appellant's accountant contests the referee’s findings, the methodology of the referee’s comprehensive evaluation of the documents submitted was adequately established. The absence of direct evidence rebutting the referee’s findings meant that the challenge could not stand, given the admissibility standards required in such proceedings ([13], [16]).

The court also elaborated that the approach taken by the appellant inversely treated the referee's report as an assertion needing to be defended rather than being the default finding that must be impugned effectively through admissible evidence ([13]).

## C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court's decision by dismissing the appeal, emphasizing that the appellant had not met the burden necessary to challenge the factual determinations in the referee's report. The court reaffirmed that without substantive counter-evidence from the appellant, the referee's findings remained binding and effective ([18]). The implications of this decision underscore the essentiality of proper evidential foundations in legal challenges against referees, as well as the role of consent in the referral process under section 19bis.

In summary, the court dismissed the appeal with costs, asserting that the factual findings of the referee were sound, and the procedural expectations to dispute such findings were unmet by the appellant ([18]).
