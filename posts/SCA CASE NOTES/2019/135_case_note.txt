Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: Mncwengi & others v The State (395/2018) [2019] ZASCA 135

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history
The appellants, Mziwabantu Madiba Mncwengi, Mzimasi Madiba Mncwengi, Buyelwa Nokwandisa Mncwengi, Lumko Bambalaza, Xolani Makapela, and Mawande Siboma, were tried in the Western Cape Division of the High Court, Cape Town, on multiple counts including three charges of murder and four charges of kidnapping ([1]). They were initially tried before Judge Boqwana and two assessors, Mr. H Swart and Ms. S Solomons. However, as the trial progressed, Ms. Solomons did not return after a prolonged absence due to reported financial distress and mental strain ([2]–[8]).

Despite attempts to address the issue, the trial continued in Ms. Solomons' absence after a seven-month period during which 22 witnesses were already called ([1]). The High Court, reasoning that justice would be better served by continuing the trial rather than restarting it, upheld this course of action, leading to the appellants' conviction on 19 November 2014 and subsequent sentencing on 24 March 2015 to an effective term of 18 years’ imprisonment each ([1], [8], [31]).

The appellants submitted an application for leave to appeal on 26 June 2015, citing various grounds for their appeal, most notably regarding the alleged procedural irregularity caused by the trial court's decision to continue without one of the assessors ([2]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions
The central issue in this appeal pertains to whether the trial court's decision to continue in the absence of Ms. Solomons constituted a fatal irregularity under section 147 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977. Specifically, it questioned whether her absence amounted to an inability to act as an assessor and whether this justified the trial proceeding without her ([3]–[4]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon
The key statutory provisions are found in section 147 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which stipulates conditions under which a trial may continue in the absence of an assessor ([10]–[12]). Relevant judicial precedents include:

1. **S v Gqeba** ([14]–[16]): Differentiated between an assessor’s inability due to genuine incapacity versus mere unwillingness.
2. **S v Malindi** ([17]): Provided clarity on the conditions surrounding assessors' inability to act, emphasizing the need for objective factors indicating actual incapacity.
3. **S v Jaipal** ([19]): Stressed the essential role of assessors in ensuring fair trials, applicable to the issue of Ms. Solomons' situation.

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law
The court examined whether Ms. Solomons' absence fell within the definition of "unable to act" as stipulated in section 147. It established that Ms. Solomons' reasons for not attending—stemming from financial difficulties and distress—did not constitute an actual incapacity under the statute. The judgment highlighted:

- The lack of medical evidence supporting a claim of mental or physical incapacity ([21]).
- The interpretation of “unable to act” requiring tangible proof of incapacity rather than subjective distress due to financial circumstances ([22]).
- The High Court had erred in relying on the interests of justice to continue the proceedings without a proper legal foundation, as the law mandates a trial to be conducted as constituted at the commencement ([29]).

The court concluded that the call to continue the trial undermined the procedural integrity necessary for a fair trial, thus vitiating the proceedings altogether ([31]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, setting aside all convictions and sentences against the appellants. It ruled that proceeding without all assessors constituted a significant procedural irregularity that vitiated the entire trial ([31]). The court's decision underscores the critical importance of having a fully constituted court in legal proceedings to uphold the principle of a fair trial, as guaranteed under section 35(3) of the South African Constitution. 

Furthermore, the court ordered that a copy of the judgment be sent to the Magistrate’s Commission and the Legal Practice Council to investigate Ms. Solomons' conduct, stressing the need for accountability among judicial officers ([30]–[31]). 

This judgement reinforces the precedent that assessors play a vital role in maintaining the integrity of criminal trials and that any deviation from procedural norms without adequate justification can have severe consequences for the validity of judicial proceedings.
