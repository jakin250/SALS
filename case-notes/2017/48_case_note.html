Constitutional Court 2017 [2017] ZACC 48

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–21])

In the case of AfriForum and Another v University of the Free State, the applicants, AfriForum (a civil rights NGO) and Solidarity (a trade union), challenged the language policy implemented by the University of the Free State (UFS), which effectively phased out Afrikaans as a medium of instruction. The historical context of this decision is significant; Afrikaans had been a dominant language of instruction at the University, closely tied to its legacy of exclusivity and privilege during apartheid. The applicants argued that the decision to transition to English undermined the rights of Afrikaans-speaking students, potentially violating their constitutional right to education in the language of their choice as stipulated in section 29(2) of the Constitution.

The university, however, argued that the phasing out of Afrikaans was necessary to promote equity, non-racialism, and inclusivity, given the historical role of Afrikaans as a medium of oppression and the observed racial segregation arising from its usage in dual-language instruction ([paras 1–21]).

Initial litigation in the Free State High Court resulted in a ruling favoring the applicants; however, the Supreme Court of Appeal reversed this decision, leading to the current appeal to the Constitutional Court.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 22–29])

The case primarily engaged the following legal issues:

  1. Standing: Whether the applicants possessed the necessary standing to challenge the university's policy.
  2. Administrative Action/Legality: The nature of the university’s decision regarding language policy – whether it constituted administrative action under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA) or fell under the doctrine of legality.
  3. Section 29(2) Compliance: Whether UFS acted in compliance with the Constitution's provision guaranteeing the right to education in a language of choice.
  4. Ministerial Policy Framework Compliance: Whether the university's language policy was developed in accordance with the ministerial framework outlined in the Higher Education Act ([paras 22–29]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 30–39])

The Constitutional Court considered several key rules, statutes, and precedents:

  • Constitution of the Republic of South Africa: Section 29(2) guarantees the right to receive education in the official language of one’s choice, provided it is "reasonably practicable".
  • Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA): Addresses requirements for administrative decisions, asserting that administrative action must be lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair.
  • Legislation: Higher Education Act 101 of 1997, particularly sections 3 and 27(2), which outline the duties of the Minister and universities regarding language policies.

Legal precedents, particularly the decisions in Head of Department, Mpumalanga Department of Education v Hoërskool Ermelo [2009] ZACC 32 and Affordable Medicines Trust v Minister of Health [2005] ZACC 3, provided interpretive guidance on reaching a balanced and just determination of competing rights ([paras 30–39]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 40–79])

The court explored the concept of "reasonably practicable" under section 29(2) through a multi-dimensional lens encompassing practicality, equity, and the need to redress past discrimination. It acknowledged the need for a nuanced interpretation of how these principles interacted to inform the university's language policy ([paras 40–49]).

The ruling emphasized that educational institutions must pursue policies that do not perpetuate racial segregation or inequity. Therefore, while Afrikaans holds a legitimate historical significance, its continuation as a language of instruction must be balanced against prevailing socio-political realities and the overarching constitutional mandate for transformation ([paras 50–79]).

The court held that the dismissal of the applicants' claims regarding emotional harm or undue prejudice had to be assessed within the broader context of historical injustice associated with the language, framing the debate as one not merely about language but about equity and inclusion ([paras 50–79]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 80–81])

The court ultimately refused leave to appeal, affirming that the university's language policy was consistent with constitutional principles and the ministerial framework. The order reflected the court's recognition of the critical need for societal transformation in educational contexts.

The decision emphasized that the quest for a unified and inclusive educational landscape mandated the reduction of dual-language provisions, especially given the historical implications of language as a tool of exclusion. This ruling is significant as it navigates the complex intersection of language rights, historical injustices, and the dynamics of contemporary educational policy in South Africa, reinforcing the ongoing dialogue about inclusivity and transformation within the nation's universities ([paras 80–81]).

The implications of the decision extend beyond UFS, signaling a potentially precedent-setting moment regarding how language rights will be balanced against the constitutional imperatives for equity and transformation in South Africa's evolving higher education landscape.
