Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: Workforce Group (Pty) Ltd v Motor Industry Bargaining Council (20076/2014) [2015] ZASCA 66

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–3])**

The appeal in question arose from a decision made by the Gauteng Division of the High Court in Pretoria concerning an amendment to an agreement concluded on 17 September 2010 by the Motor Industry Bargaining Council (hereafter "the Bargaining Council"). This amendment placed restrictions on employers' abilities to engage "temporary employment services" (previously termed "labour brokers"). Section 32 of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (hereafter "the LRA") permits the Minister of Labour to extend a collective agreement concluded by the Bargaining Council to non-parties within its registered scope.

In response to a request from the Bargaining Council, the Minister extended the agreement to non-parties via a proclamation published in the Government Gazette on 28 January 2011. Subsequently, in August 2011, The Workforce Group (Pty) Ltd (the appellant) sought relief from the High Court, arguing that specific clauses regulating temporary employment were unlawful and invalid. The appellant's challenge was based on allegations that the restrictions constituted a trade boycott, were void for vagueness, exceeded the Bargaining Council's constitutional scope, and infringed upon various constitutional rights, including the right to freedom of association and fair labour practices ([paras 1–2]).

The appellant was ultimately unsuccessful in the High Court, which ruled against it with full costs, including the costs of two counsel ([para 3]). The High Court's decision subsequently led to the granting of leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal.

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 4–6])**

The core issues on appeal were whether:

1. The restrictions outlined in the Bargaining Council's agreement were validly extended to non-parties, such as The Workforce Group (Pty) Ltd, and whether they constituted lawful limitations on their ability to operate as an employment agency.
  
2. The parties could still proceed with the appeal in light of subsequent events that had rendered the matter academic. Specifically, whether the extension of the agreement beyond its initial expiry, coupled with the promulgation of the Labour Relations Amendment Act 6 of 2014, diminished the relevance of the case.

These issues required assessment of both statutory interpretations under the LRA and constitutional implications concerning the limitations imposed on the appellant ([paras 4–5]).

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 6–10])**

The precedent for the consideration of academic disputes was taken from prior judgments indicating that when the legal issues have been resolved due to subsequent legislative changes, it is within the court’s discretion to strike matters from the roll and address cost implications ([paras 8–9]). 

The court referenced Deutsche Altersheim Zu Pretoria v Roland Heinrich Dohmen [2015] ZASCA 3, which affirmed that an appellant is dominus litis and retains responsibility for prosecuting their appeal. Furthermore, it cited the Constitutional Court's decision in JT Publishing (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Safety and Security 1997 (3) SA 514 (CC), emphasizing that if a statute or agreement has been rendered obsolete by subsequent legislation, the court may find no merit in proceeding with the appeal ([para 10]).

Given these legal foundations, the court was tasked with evaluating the acceptability of claims concerning the constitutional validity of the extended agreement and whether it remained pertinent due to the changes introduced by the amended law ([paras 6–10]).

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 10–11])**

The court concluded that the issues raised by the appellant had become academic. The Labour Relations Amendment Act, which came into effect on 1 January 2015, altered the legal landscape regarding temporary employment services, effectively nullifying the very provisions that the appellant sought to contest. Consequently, they no longer applied, and this subsequent development was deemed significant enough to shift the nature of the case to one without an active dispute worth adjudication.

The court noted with concern the appellant's apparent failure to reassess its position in light of the new legislation, which was suggested to have rendered its appeal moot even prior to the original agreement's final expiry. Thus, while the appellant continued to engage in litigation fields after the amendment, the court deemed this inaction to be problematic, as it continued to incur costs despite the lack of viable issues remaining ([paras 10–11]).

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 11])**

The Supreme Court of Appeal ordered that the appeal be struck from the roll with costs, including provision for the costs of two counsel. The court's ratio decidendi underscored that the legal framework under which the appellant had sought to contest the agreement was overtaken by subsequent legislative amendments, leading to a situation where further proceedings served no meaningful purpose ([para 11]).

The implications of this ruling extend to the responsibilities of appellants to remain alert to legislative changes that could devalue their legal arguments proactively. It also reaffirms the principle that courts have the discretion to dismiss academic appeals while imposing costs on those who improperly prolong litigation despite the resolution of the central legal questions involved.
