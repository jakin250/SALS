Case Note: Independent Candidate Association South Africa NPC v President of the Republic of South Africa and Others [2023] ZACC 41

Constitutional Court 2023 [2023] ZACC 41 #### F – Facts The applicant, the Independent

Case Note: Independent Candidate Association South Africa NPC v President of the Republic of South Africa and Others [2023] ZACC 41

F – Facts

The applicant, the Independent Candidate Association South Africa NPC, initiated proceedings before the Constitutional Court of South Africa seeking direct access to challenge provisions in the Electoral Amendment Act 1 of 2023. Specifically, the applicant contested the constitutionality of item 1 of Schedule 1A, which divides seats in the National Assembly equally (200 regional and 200 compensatory) and contended that this constituted unjust discrimination against independent candidates ([1]–[2]).

The applicant argued that such a division would render independent candidates disproportionately disadvantaged in attaining seats compared to political parties, a violation of constitutional rights related to voting and standing for public office ([34]–[41]). The court was tasked with reviewing the legal and constitutional validity of this provision in light of previous judgments, notably the New Nation II judgment which mandated provisions for independent candidates in national elections ([11]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions presented for determination included:

  1. Whether direct access to the Constitutional Court should be granted ([42]).
  2. Whether the 200/200 seat allocation split rationally achieves a legitimate governmental purpose ([50]).
  3. Whether this split imposes an unjustifiable limitation on fundamental rights, including the right to vote and the right to stand for public office, as articulated in Chapter 2 of the Constitution ([42]).
  4. If limitations are found, are they justifiable under section 36 of the Constitution? ([42]).

R – Rules

The court referenced various legal precedents and constitutional provisions, particularly:

  • Section 1(c) of the Constitution establishing the supremacy of the Constitution and the rule of law ([24]).
  • Section 46(1)(d) concerning the requirement for an electoral system that ensures general proportional representation ([96]).
  • Past judgments such as New National Party v Government of the Republic of South Africa and the principles established therein regarding Parliament's discretion in electoral systems and the balance of political representation ([54]–[60]).

The rationality test required any governmental measure to demonstrate a connection between the means applied and the legitimate objectives pursued ([52]–[53]).

A – Application

The court granted direct access, noting the urgency due to the imminent 2024 elections and the absence of opposing views from the respondents on this procedural step ([42]–[46]).

Upon examining the substantive claims, the court recognized that both the applicant and respondents accepted the 200/200 allocation would achieve proportionality. The difference highlighted by the applicant focused on the burdens of winning votes under the new scheme. However, the court found that the Electoral Amendment Act provided sufficient arguments for the distinction between independent candidates and political parties, particularly the necessity to avoid "overhang" situations which could disrupt proportionality in representation ([62]–[70]).

The applicant's arguments surrounding sections 3(2)(a) and 9(1) regarding equality were scrutinized, and the court observed that such differential treatment must be justified based on rational legislative purposes. The court upheld that the seat allocation method was rationally connected to the objectives of inclusion and proportionality, thus not constituting an arbitrary discrimination as alleged ([100]–[120]).

Furthermore, the applicant's invocation of international jurisprudence concerning electoral fairness was deemed unpersuasive against the backdrop of the South African constitutional framework and its inherent demands for proportional representation. The court underscored that legislative design might affect groups differently but does not equate to a constitutional violation ([156]–[157]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court held that the applicant had not sufficiently demonstrated any infringement on the rights alleged nor any substantive irrationality in the seat allocation scheme of the Electoral Amendment Act. The application was dismissed without costs, thereby reinforcing the validity of the existing electoral provisions and allowing the Electoral Commission to proceed with preparations for the upcoming elections ([160]–[161]).

This decision illustrates the court's commitment to uphold constitutional mandates of proportional representation while balancing the rights of independent candidates within an established electoral framework.
