Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2009

# FIRAC Case Note: Laerskool Generaal Hendrik Schoeman v Bastian Financial Services (Pty) Ltd

**Case Title:** Laerskool Generaal Hendrik Schoeman v Bastian Financial Services (Pty) Ltd  
**Court:** Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Case Number:** CCT 22/09  
**Judgment Date:** 7 May 2009  
**Citation:** [2009] ZACC 12  

## F - Facts
Laerskool Generaal Hendrik Schoeman (the applicant) is a public school located in Schoemansville, Hartebeespoort, within the North West Province of South Africa. The school sought leave to appeal a judgment from the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which held the school liable to Bastian Financial Services (the respondent) for outstanding payments under a lease agreement for a photocopier ([1]).

The SCA's decision specifically stated that Section 60(1) of the South African Schools Act 84 of 1996 (prior to its amendment in 2007) did not transfer contractual liabilities from public schools to the State ([1]). This ruling overturned earlier decisions from both a magistrate’s court in Brits and the North Gauteng High Court, which had absolved the school from its contractual obligations ([1]). The applicant argued that the liability of governing bodies must be seen within the context of its role in ensuring the right to education enshrined in the Bill of Rights ([3]).

Before granting leave to appeal, the Court needed to establish if the school had appropriately and timely submitted its application. The applicant failed to provide satisfactory evidence for delays in bringing the application ([4]-[5]). The SCA's judgment was delivered on 30 May 2008, with the application due by 23 June 2008. However, due to inaction and delays—including a failure to consult its governing body in a timely manner— the school only filed its application on 23 March 2009, nine months post-deadline ([5]-[10]).

## I - Issues
The central legal issue raised by the applicant was whether the constitutional and statutory interpretation of Section 60(1) would afford them grounds for appeal regarding the imposition of contractual liability on public schools. Additionally, the Court also had to address whether to grant condonation for the significant delay in filing the application for leave to appeal, specifically evaluating the reasons provided by the school for their inaction ([3], [11]).

## R - Rules
1. **South African Schools Act 84 of 1996 (as amended):** Section 60(1) specifies that the State is liable for damages or loss due to actions connected to school activities, pertinent to understanding the liability framework within which public schools operate ([1]).
2. **Constitution of South Africa:** Section 29(1) guarantees everyone the right to basic education, which implicates the State's duty in educational matters ([4]).
3. **Court Procedure Rules (Rule 19):** Govern the timelines and requirements for filing applications for leave to appeal, stipulating an application must be made within 15 days of the SCA's decision ([5], [22]).
4. **Legal Precedents:** The court referred to previous judgments such as *Van Wyk v Unitas Hospital*, emphasizing that applications for condonation must provide a comprehensive explanation for delays ([15]-[18]).

## A - Application
The Court assessed the applicant's explanation for delays against the criteria established in legal precedents. It noted the applicant's failure to act decisively for a significant period after learning about the SCA's judgment, leaving a gap of over three months without adequate justification ([13], [20]).

The only explanations offered—covering school vacations and the uncertainty within the governing body—were deemed insufficient. The Court concluded that the lack of action indicated indecision and potential ambivalence towards pursuing the appeal, undermining the credibility of their claims ([14], [21]). Failing to act also prompted the respondent to seek a default judgment, which the school had to later rescind, further adding to the complexity of the delays ([10]).

The Court stressed that the interests of the respondent had to be considered; allowing the appeal would not only erode the finality of the SCA's judgment but also constitute a manifest injustice to Bastian Financial Services ([19], [24]).

## C - Conclusion
The Constitutional Court of South Africa ultimately refused to grant condonation for the applicant's delayed appeal, dismissing the application based on an inadequately explained timeline that lacked decisive action ([24]). The Court reinforced the importance of adhering to procedural rules and timelines established for applications to protect the integrity of the judicial process, particularly in financial matters. The outcome underscores the necessity for public institutions, like schools, to maintain accountability for their contractual obligations while navigating the complexities of statutory interpretations of liability within the educational framework ([23]). 

This case sets a significant precedent regarding the need for prompt action in legal matters and further delineates the boundaries of liability for public schools under South African law.
