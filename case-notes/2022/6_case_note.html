Constitutional Court

National Education Health and Allied Workers Union v Minister of Public Service and Administration and Others; South African Democratic Teachers Union and Others v Department of Public Service and Administration and Others; Public Servants Association and Others v Minister of Public Service and Administration and Others; National Union of Public Service and Allied Workers Union v Minister of Public Service and Administration and Others [2022] ZACC 6

The Labour Appeal Court found that clause 3.3 exceeded the fiscal budgetary provisions, which required a realistic calculation of costs, compliance with Treasury regulations, and sufficient budgetary provisions to cover the costs.

Constitutional Court 2022 [2022] ZACC 6

F – Facts

The case involves a challenge to the validity and enforceability of clause 3.3 of a collective agreement that governs public sector wage increases over the financial years 2018/2019, 2019/2020, and 2020/2021. The applicants, trade unions representing various public sector employees, sought leave to appeal against a judgment of the Labour Appeal Court that deemed the clause invalid due to non-compliance with the Public Service Regulations and the Constitution ([1-5]).

The Labour Appeal Court found that clause 3.3 exceeded the fiscal budgetary provisions, which required a realistic calculation of costs, compliance with Treasury regulations, and sufficient budgetary provisions to cover the costs. The State contended that it could not afford the wage increases stipulated in the clause, estimating that enforcing it would cost R29 billion. Conversely, the unions argued that Cabinet's prior approval of the agreement demonstrated substantial compliance with the regulatory framework, asserting that the collective agreement was valid and enforceable. They also claimed that a delay by the State in challenging the agreement should not prevent enforcement ([6-10]).

I – Issues

The court had to resolve several interrelated legal questions, primarily concerning:

  1. Whether the matter engaged the constitutional jurisdiction of the court.
  2. Whether the collective agreement, and specifically clause 3.3, was enforceable under the legal and constitutional framework.
  3. Whether the State's delay in challenging the collective agreement's validity could bar its claims.
  4. The implications of enforcing or not enforcing the collective agreement for both the State's fiscal responsibilities and the rights of public servants ([11-12]).

R – Rules

The key legal provisions at issue include:

  • Sections 213, 215, and 216 of the South African Constitution, which govern public finance and the appropriations process, intending to ensure transparency and accountability regarding state funds.
  • Public Service Regulations 78 and 79, which impose requirements for concluding collective agreements, including ensuring costs are covered within budgetary constraints.
  • The principles of estoppel and delays in the context of legality reviews governed by precedents such as Khumalo v MEC for Education, KwaZulu-Natal and Gijima Holdings (Pty) Ltd v State Information Technology Agency SOC Ltd, which examine the balance of equity in judicial remedies ([13-19]).

A – Application

In its judgment, the Constitutional Court, led by Madondo AJ, unanimously upheld the Labour Appeal Court’s findings, ruling that the collective agreement was invalid as it did not comply with mandatory fiscal requirements. The court underscored that the absence of a written commitment from Treasury or other governmental departments meant that the requisite conditions for the agreement were not satisfied. The ruling effectively situated the financial management obligations of the State within constitutional parameters, emphasizing that public financial commitments must rest on solid legal and budgetary foundations ([20-37]).

Moreover, the court examined the justifications for not enforcing the clause given the public interest. It acknowledged that enforcing the financial commitments under the collective agreement would impose a significant burden on public resources, particularly in the face of declining economic conditions exacerbated by the COVID-19 pandemic. The notion of the "public purse" and the need to prioritize essential services and protections for vulnerable citizens were pivotal to the ruling ([38-44]).

The court also addressed the delay issue, contextualizing it against the broader public interest. It affirmed that while there was a delay in the State’s legal challenge, it was justified given the significant implications of the financial obligations the State would otherwise incur. The necessity of fiscal restraint during economically challenging times was deemed paramount, overtaking potential claims to estoppels based on the prior conduct of the State and the unions ([45-50]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded by dismissing the appeal, solidifying that clause 3.3 of the collective agreement was invalid and reaffirming the non-negotiable requirement for compliance with relevant legal and regulatory frameworks in public finance. The court held that compelling enforcement of the agreement would not serve justice but rather detract from the State's capacity to meet its constitutional obligations to the citizenry.

The implications of this ruling underscore the importance of rigorous adherence to fiscal laws in public sector agreements, asserting that the rule of law remains fundamental in public finance, especially during fiscal emergencies. The court's framing of the public interests aligns with prior constitutional views that prioritize the welfare of the broader society over individual contractual entitlements, thus reinforcing the State's role in safeguarding public resources ([51-64]).

This FIRAC case note synthesizes the legal reasoning and findings of the Constitutional Court, demonstrating the complex interplay between public finance law and collective bargaining rights within the South African constitutional framework.
