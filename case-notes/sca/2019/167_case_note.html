Qwelane v SAHRC & Others (686/2018) [2019] ZASCA 167

The case centers on Jonathan Dubula Qwelane, the appellant, whose 2008 article published in the Sunday Sun sparked controversy for its offensive content towards the gay community, including statements that were deemed to advocate hatred based on sexual orientation.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 167

F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–34])

The case centers on Jonathan Dubula Qwelane, the appellant, whose 2008 article published in the Sunday Sun sparked controversy for its offensive content towards the gay community, including statements that were deemed to advocate hatred based on sexual orientation. The South African Human Rights Commission (SAHRC), the first respondent, received over 350 complaints alleging that the article constituted hate speech as defined under Section 10 of the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000 (PEPUDA).

Subsequently, the SAHRC initiated proceedings against Qwelane in the Equality Court, asserting that he violated PEPUDA by inciting harm and propagating hatred. During these proceedings, the Equality Court heard extensive evidence regarding the impact of hate speech on the LGBTI community, including testimonies from members detailing personal experiences of discrimination and violence.

The Equality Court ultimately upheld the complaint, declaring Qwelane's statements as hate speech and ordered Qwelane to apologize unconditionally to the LGBTI community. Qwelane then appealed to the Gauteng Local Division of the High Court, and after an unfavorable outcome, he appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), challenging the constitutionality of certain provisions in PEPUDA as they pertained to freedom of expression under Section 16 of the Constitution ([paras 1–34]).

I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 35–37])

The principal legal issue presented in this case revolves around the constitutionality of Section 10 of PEPUDA, specifically whether the provisions unjustifiably limit the right to freedom of expression as guaranteed in Section 16 of the South African Constitution. The Court evaluated two key sub-issues:

  1. Whether the provisions of Section 10(1) extend beyond the limits set out in Section 16(2)(c) of the Constitution, which prohibits hate speech based only on race, ethnicity, gender, or religion that constitutes "incitement to cause harm."
  2. Whether the legislative framework established by PEPUDA, including definitions and prohibitions concerning hate speech, is vague or overbroad to the extent that it fails to pass constitutional muster ([paras 35–37]).

R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 35–54])

The SCA extensively referenced Section 16 of the Constitution, which outlines the right to freedom of expression while providing certain exclusions specifically for hate speech. The court closely analyzed the following statutory and judicial precedents:

  1. PEPUDA: Section 10(1), which prohibits the publication of words based on prohibited grounds that could be construed as hurtful or hateful.
  2. Constitution of South Africa: In Section 16(2)(c), hate speech is defined narrowly, allowing for the restriction of speech that constitutes "advocacy of hatred" that incites harm based only on the specified grounds.
  3. Judicial Precedents: The court examined relevant case law such as Islamic Unity Convention v Independent Broadcasting Authority and Laugh It Off Promotions CC v SAB International, both of which addressed the thresholds for determining hate speech and freedom of expression ([paras 35–54]).

A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 55–88])

In arriving at its decision, the SCA determined that the provisions in Section 10(1) of PEPUDA create a significantly lower threshold for hate speech than that provided in Section 16(2) of the Constitution. The court reasoned that while Section 16(2) provides a clear framework for what constitutes impermissible speech, the broader language of PEPUDA renders it vague and overbroad, potentially criminalizing speech that only expresses unpopular or offensive opinions without actual intent to incite harm.

The court also noted that:

  • The vagueness in terms such as "hurtful" leads to interpretative difficulties and could stifle legitimate expression in an open democracy.
  • Addressing the distinction between advocacy of hatred (which must incite harm) and general expressive content, the court concluded that PEPUDA's broad approach effectively expands the limit on free expression beyond what is constitutionally permissible.
  • The provision fails to align with international norms regarding hate speech regulation, which demand specificity and clarity to avoid infringing on fundamental freedoms ([paras 55–88]).

C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 89–96])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Mr. Qwelane’s appeal, declaring Section 10 of PEPUDA unconstitutional and invalid due to its inconsistency with the freedom of expression protections of the Constitution. It emphasized that while protecting marginalized communities from hate speech is essential, the law must do so without infringing upon the fundamental right to free expression.

The court provided a remedial framework, granting Parliament a period of 18 months to amend the offending legislation. In the interim, it established that Section 10(1) would temporarily read that no person may advocate hatred based solely on specified prohibited grounds. The decision also sparked a critical look at hate speech regulations in the context of constitutional freedoms, calling for a careful balance that protects free expression while addressing societal harms ([paras 89–96]).

This ruling underscores the importance of precise statutory language in legislation intended to regulate speech and highlights the ongoing tension between maintaining societal values and protecting individual rights within South Africa's constitutional democracy.
