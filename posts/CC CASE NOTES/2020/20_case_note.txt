# FIRAC Case Note: Chisuse and Others v Director-General, Department of Home Affairs and Another [2020] ZACC 20

## F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([para 1-11])

The case of *Chisuse and Others v Director-General, Department of Home Affairs and Another* arose from a challenge to the constitutionality of certain provisions of the South African Citizenship Act 88 of 1995, as amended by the South African Citizenship Amendment Act 17 of 2010. The applicants, five individuals born outside South Africa to South African citizen parents, sought to be recognized as South African citizens despite their births occurring before the amended Act's commencement date of 1 January 2013. They claimed the amended provisions deprived them of citizenship that was guaranteed under previous legislation.

The High Court found in favor of the applicants, declaring sections 2(1)(a) and (b) of the amended Citizenship Act constitutionally invalid. The Court proposed a remedy of reading-in certain phrases to the provisions to restore the citizenship rights of those born before the amendment. The respondents, which included the Director-General of the Department of Home Affairs and the Minister of Home Affairs, did not file a defense during the High Court proceedings, leading to a default judgment. The respondents later sought to introduce new factual information during the proceedings at the Constitutional Court, prompting a debate on compliance with procedural requirements and the implications of the respondents' conduct in the lower court ([para 1-9]).

## I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([para 2, 16-18, 29-30])

The primary legal questions presented before the Constitutional Court involved the constitutionality of sections 2(1)(a) and (b) of the amended Citizenship Act and whether these provisions stripped the applicants of their citizenship rights in a manner that was irrational and arbitrary, contravening various constitutional protections. Specifically, the Court examined if the provisions allowed for a retrospective application concerning citizenship acquisition by birth and descent, as well as the legitimate expectations of those affected by the Citizenship Amendment ([para 2, 18, 29-30]).

Furthermore, the Court had to decide if it could accept new evidence from the respondents and whether their non-participation in the earlier High Court proceedings would impede the integrity of the current proceedings ([para 16-18]).

## R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([para 3, 10, 18])

The Constitutional Court referenced several key legal principles from the Constitution of South Africa, including:

1. **Section 3** and **Section 20** of the Constitution, which outline the right to citizenship and protection against deprivation of citizenship. These provisions affirm that all citizens are entitled to their citizenship and that national legislation must be consistent with constitutional guarantees.

2. **Interpretative Principles**: The Court relied heavily on the principle of a purposive interpretation of statutes, notably as governed by **Section 39(2)** of the Constitution, which mandates that legislation should be interpreted in a manner that promotes the spirit, purport, and objects of the Bill of Rights.

3. **Judicial Precedent**: The Court cited decisions in *Mphahlele* and *Strategic Liquor Services*, reinforcing the necessity for lower courts to provide comprehensive reasons for declaring legislation invalid and highlighting the judiciary's role in ensuring legislative coherence with constitutional norms ([para 3, 10, 18]).

## A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([para 19-40])

The Constitutional Court critically analyzed the High Court's reasoning and the statutory language at issue. The applicants contended that the amendments effectively stripped them and similarly situated individuals of citizenship acquired under previous laws. The Court assessed whether the language of the amended Citizenship Act could be interpreted in a manner consistent with constitutional protections without falling foul of retrospective application constraints.

Upon thorough examination, the Court determined:

1. The phrase “who is born in or outside the Republic” should be interpreted to apply retrospectively, thus including individuals born prior to the amendment's enactment.
   
2. The interpretation that would restrict citizenship to those born post-amendment would unjustly create distinctions that violate the rights to equality and dignity, as articulated in sections 9 and 10 of the Constitution.

3. Since sections 2(1)(a) and (b) could reasonably accommodate interpretations that were constitutionally compliant, the Court declined to confirm the High Court's order of invalidity ([para 19-40]).

The Court also addressed the issue of respondents' attempts to introduce new evidence. It found that the respondents' inaction during the High Court process precluded them from challenging the factual assertions made by the applicants. The Constitutional Court emphasized the importance of procedural integrity and the need for government agencies to act diligently in legal proceedings ([para 33-36]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([para 41-44])

The Constitutional Court concluded that while the amendments posed significant constitutional concerns, they did not inherently invalidate the citizenship rights of the applicants when appropriately interpreted. The Court declined to confirm the High Court's declaration of unconstitutionality concerning sections 2(1)(a) and (b) and instead upheld the claim of citizenship for the first and third to fifth applicants. 

The Court’s ruling underscored the necessity for legislative clarity and reinforced the principle that individuals born to South African citizens are entitled to citizenship regardless of the timing of their birth in relation to legislative amendments. This judgment reinforces the judiciary's proactive role in interpreting legislation in alignment with constitutional values and serves as a precedent for future citizenship claims, affirmatively shaping the understanding of citizenship rights in South Africa ([para 41-44]). 

The Court's directive for the Department of Home Affairs to immediately register the births and provide identity documentation exemplifies an essential judicial initiative to rectify historical injustices associated with citizenship recognition ([para 41-42]).
