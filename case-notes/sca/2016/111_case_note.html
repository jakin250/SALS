Monye v S (107/16) ZASCA 111

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016

FIRAC Method

Facts

[1] The case concerns the appellants, Ambrose Monye and Andre Gouws, who were charged with the murder of Ms. Chanelle Henning, along with offences related to the possession of unlicensed firearms. The murder occurred on 8 November 2011. Both appellants pleaded not guilty but were convicted of murder while being acquitted of the firearm charges.

[2] During the sentencing proceedings, the appellants admitted their respective roles in the murder. They were subsequently sentenced to life imprisonment, and this appeal focuses solely on the sentence.

[3] The murder was executed as a contract killing. On the morning of the crime, two men, Pieterse and du Plessis, followed the deceased's vehicle, at which point du Plessis shot her twice at close range after she dropped off her son at a crèche. Both attackers were arrested alongside Monye and Gouws, who arranged for the hit.

[4] Pieterse and du Plessis entered plea agreements and received reduced sentences. Their testimonies against the appellants established that Monye and Gouws not only facilitated but orchestrated the murder.

[5] After a year-long legal battle, the appellants’ change of heart during the sentencing phase indicated a level of guilt but was marked by insincerity and opportunism rather than true remorse.

Issues

[6] The legal issues presented revolved around whether there were "substantial and compelling circumstances" that warranted a departure from the mandatory life sentences established by the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997. Specifically, the court needed to determine if the remorse claimed by the appellants, post-conviction, sufficiently qualified as a factor for leniency.

[7] The critical questions were:

  • Did the appellants exhibit genuine remorse?
  • Were the circumstances surrounding their confession substantial and compelling enough to mitigate their life sentences?

Rules

[8] The legal framework governing sentencing in murder cases is primarily outlined in the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997. Section 51 prescribes life sentences for planned murders, allowing for lesser sentences only in the presence of "substantial and compelling circumstances" as defined in S v Malgas [2001] ZASCA 30.

[9] The court's interpretation of "substantial and compelling" indicates that reasons for leniency must be significant; not merely regret or a change of heart after conviction. Rather, they must provide "weighty justification" to merit a deviation from mandatory sentencing ([paras 7–8]).

Application

[10] The court rigorously examined the personal circumstances and actions of the appellants. Monye had a background as a successful athlete and owned a business but had prior unconsidered convictions. Gouws was a first-time offender who had previously served with the police and owned a guesthouse ([paras 9–12]).

[11] The appellants argued they had shown remorse by admitting their complicity. However, the court highlighted that genuine remorse is a factual determination that cannot simply derive from regret or opportunistic admissions made only under the duress of impending sentencing. The court reasoned that their assertions of remorse lacked sincerity, given their prior denials and attempts to distance themselves from the crime, particularly Monye’s lack of disclosure until after the trial ([paras 15–16]).

[12] The gravity of the crime—a contract killing motivated by financial gain—was underscored, with the court reaffirming the strong societal interest in deterring such acts. The court cited precedents emphasizing the community's expectations for harsh penalties for hired killings, noting that failing to impose life sentences in this instance would undermine public confidence in the justice system ([paras 18–20], [22]).

[13] Arguments that the appellants’ willingness to testify against the alleged instigator constituted substantial and compelling circumstances were dismissed. This change was viewed as self-serving rather than altruistic, lacking the requisite sincerity to influence sentencing ([para 23]).

Conclusion

[14] The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appellants’ appeals against the life sentences, confirming the original sentencing by the trial court ([para 24]). The court's holding solidified the notion that mere acknowledgment of guilt, particularly when not accompanied by genuine remorse, does not suffice to mitigate sentences for serious crimes such as murder.

[15] The case serves as a stern reminder of the standards expected when examining claims of remorse and the seriousness with which society views crimes involving contract killings, thereby reinforcing the principle of deterrence in criminal justice.
