South African Health Products Regulatory Authority and Another v African Christian Democratic Party (869/2021) [2022] ZASCA 158

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 158

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–12])

In this case, the African Christian Democratic Party (ACDP) initiated proceedings in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, seeking a court order that directed the South African Health Products Regulatory Authority (SAHPRA) to lift restrictions on the use of Ivermectin, an animal treatment, for Covid-19 in humans. Specifically, the ACDP requested that these provisions remain effective until clinical evidence indicated otherwise ([para 1]).

During proceedings, the parties involved in this matter—including the ACDP and other applicants—settled with SAHPRA and the Minister of Health, forming a draft order that did not include certain additional provisions imposed by the High Court judge, Sardiwalla J ([para 2]). Despite SAHPRA and the Minister registering objections, the judge included supervision orders mandating quarterly reports from SAHPRA on Ivermectin's status ([paras 2–3]). Following the order, SAHPRA and the Minister applied for rescission of the supervisory orders, or alternatively, sought leave to appeal, which Sardiwalla J granted ([para 3]).

The ACDP initially opposed the appeal but later withdrew its opposition, allowing for a determination under Section 19(a) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, permitting court disposal without oral argument ([para 4]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 13–24])

The critical legal issues addressed in the appeal centered primarily around three points:

  1. Procedural Justice: Whether the SAHPRA and the Minister were denied the opportunity for a fair hearing prior to the issuance of the supervisory order, thereby infringing upon the principles underpinning procedural fairness as protected under Section 34 of the Constitution ([paras 15–16]).
  2. Scope of Judicial Authority: Whether the supervisory order was permissible given that it had not been applied for by any party and was outside the scope of the pleadings presented ([para 19]).
  3. Evidentiary Support: The extent to which evidence was required to justify the issuance of a supervisory order, particularly against the backdrop of the separation of powers doctrine ([para 21]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 13–24])

The court's analysis relied on several key legal authorities and constitutional provisions:

  1. Constitutional Framework: Section 34 of the Constitution entitles every individual the right to a fair public hearing ([para 16]), establishing that judicial power must respect existing legal frameworks and authorities, as emphasized in S v Mabena and Another [2006] ZASCA 178, which affirmed that any exercise of state authority, including judicial power, must align with the law ([para 13]).
  2. Judicial Limitations: The principles articulated in National Director of Public Prosecutions v Zuma [2009] ZASCA 1 drew attention to the necessity for a court to remain within its jurisdiction when adjudicating, underscoring that judicial functions should strictly abide by legal stipulations ([para 14]).
  3. Case Precedents: The judgment also referenced contemporary interpretations of procedural fairness and due process, including Knoop NO and Another v Gupta and Another [2021] ZASCA 149, which clarified that courts must limit their decisions to issues raised within the pleadings ([para 18]). Significant reference was made to Minister of Health and Others v Treatment Action Campaign and Others (No. 2) [2002] ZACC 15 regarding the conditions under which supervisory orders are justified ([para 21]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 15–21])

In analyzing the case, the Supreme Court of Appeal addressed the procedural failings of the High Court's issuing of the supervisory order. Importantly, the court noted that SAHPRA and the Minister were denied a hearing despite their objections and the context of opposition to the supervisory order put forth ([para 15]). This violation of their right to be heard constituted a fundamental breach of procedural justice and rendered the High Court's decision invalid.

The court further emphasized the importance of the adversarial system of litigation in which it is the prerogative of the parties to define the nature of their disputes. The issuance of a supervisory order that was not requested or argued by any party violated the established norms of judicial proceedings and undermined the parties' rights to contest the basis for any judicial determination ([paras 19–20]).

Moreover, the court highlighted the absence of necessary evidentiary support justifying the supervisory order, thus reinforcing concerns regarding the separation of powers. The lack of factual basis to warrant such intrusive judicial oversight further supported the argument for the order's invalidation ([para 21]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 23–24])

The Supreme Court ultimately upheld the appeal, decisively concluding that the High Court erred in issuing the supervisory order: first, by failing to provide a fair hearing to SAHPRA and the Minister; second, by granting an order outside the scope of any party's application or the issues outlined in pleadings; and third, by issuing the order without an evidentiary foundation ([para 23]).

Consequently, the court set aside paragraphs 1 and 2 of the High Court’s order, affirming that judicial authority must be exercised within the constraints of law, reinforcing the principle of fair hearing as a vital aspect of the rule of law ([paras 24]). This decision underscores the judiciary's fundamental obligation to maintain procedural integrity, thereby influencing subsequent judicial conduct regarding matters involving overlapping state authority and public health considerations.
