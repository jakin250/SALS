Source Folder: 1997

# FIRAC Case Note 

**Case Title**: *Leon Swartzberg v Leon Swartzberg & Company (Pty) Ltd* [1997] ZASCA 10, Case No 373/95

**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa 

**Date Heard**: 20 February 1997  
**Date Delivered**: 14 March 1997  

**Coram**: Mahomed CJ, Grosskopf JA, Nienaber JA, Howie JA, Streich AJA 

---

## F – Facts

The appellant, Leon Swartzberg, initiated litigation in a magistrate's court, successfully claiming rental and ancillary amounts owed under a lease. The respondent, Leon Swartzberg & Company (Pty) Ltd, appealed to the Witwatersrand Local Division, where the appeal was upheld. With the granting of leave from the local division, Swartzberg now appeals to the Supreme Court of Appeal.

The crux of the matter revolves around whether a lease agreement was active during the period for which Swartzberg was claiming payment. Historically, Swartzberg founded the respondent company, which engaged in plumbing and tiling sales. A significant transaction occurred in 1983, where Swartzberg and his partner sold a controlling stake to Boumat Limited, although Swartzberg continued as managing director.

In connection to the premises utilized for business, a lease, dubbed the "Bradmarc lease," commenced on 1 March 1983 and was set to terminate on 28 February 1988, with an option for renewal for another five years. In October 1983, during a board meeting, it was noted that Swartzberg was constructing a warehouse, and an offer to lease this space—referred to as the "Swartzberg lease"—was accepted.

The Swartzberg lease began on 15 April 1984, expiring on 28 February 1989, which was a year later than the Bradmarc lease. As stipulated, the option for renewal for the Swartzberg lease was due on 31 August 1988, corresponding to the original lease terms.

In 1986, a new managing director, Mr. Klitzner, inadvertently exercised the renewal of the Bradmarc lease without being cognizant of the discrepancies in lease terms. As discussions regarding the Swartzberg lease renewal became contentious in 1988, Klitzner exercised the renewal option and continued disputing rental payments until 1991.

On 18 February 1993, as discussions continued regarding the Swartzberg lease, the respondent contended that a mutual error occurred concerning the intended duration of both leases, arguing that they should have run concurrently. Following this assertion, the respondent vacated the premises on the Bradmarc lease termination date, 28 February 1993, leading to Swartzberg's current claim for rental due in March 1993 under the Swartzberg lease.

## I – Issues

The primary legal question before the court concerns whether the Swartzberg lease remained enforceable during the period for which rental was sought by the appellant. Specifically, the matter necessitated examination into the legitimacy of the respondent's claim for rectification of the lease agreement on the grounds of mutual mistake of fact regarding the concurrent running of both leases ([paras 1–10]).

## R – Rules

The court relied upon principles of contract law specifically concerning rectification. The key legal authorities referenced included:

1. **Rectification of Contracts**: Punishment for mutual error under settled principles was pivotal. Meyer v Merchants' Trust Ltd 1942 AD 244 elucidated that the objective of rectification is to align written agreements to with the true intentions of the contracting parties when a mutual mistake exists ([paras 21–23]).
   
2. **Common Intention**: Essential to establishing rectification is the affirmation of what both parties originally intended when executing the lease agreements. Both the terms of the resultant contract and the conduct of the parties following its execution serve as evidence of their intentions ([paras 10–19]).

3. **Onus of Proof**: The party seeking rectification bore the burden of demonstrating historically shared intent, upheld by evidence from various parties to the original agreement ([paras 10–12]).

## A – Application

The court meticulously analyzed the evidence surrounding the mutual intentions of the contracting parties at the time the leases were established. Significant focus was placed on a board meeting conducted on 11 October 1983, which illustrated a collective intention described as the leases being intended to run concurrently.

Testimonies from board members affirmed that the intention to have the leases coincide was reflected in the record of the meeting. Although Swartzberg offered a contrary interpretation of “concurrently,” the court found this rationale tenuous and unsupported by the evidential record ([paras 12–16]).

The assertion by the respondent that the dates in the Swartzberg lease did not align with those of the Bradmarc lease due to mutual error illustrated a credible case for rectification. The court ruled that the erroneous insertion of differing termination dates was plausible given the parties’ partnership and mutual interests ([paras 16–20]). It was deduced that the discrepancies in dates were likely overlooked due to the inherently cooperative nature of their relationship when negotiating the terms ([para 21]).

Ultimately, the court agreed that evidence demonstrated the two leases were meant to be coterminous, thus legitimizing the respondent's claim for rectification, despite Swartzberg's contentions regarding the timing of the option exercise ([paras 22–23]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the respondent successfully established its defense of rectification arising from a mutual error. The court dismissed Swartzberg's appeal, concluding that the leases were intended to run concurrently but were misrepresented in the written agreements. Thus, the lower court's ruling was upheld, and the appeal was denied with costs, including the fees for two counsel ([Paras 21–23]).

The implication of this judgment reinforces the importance of clarity in contractual drafting and the legal significance of mutual intent in rectifying contractual agreements when discrepancies arise. This case stands as a pivotal example reinforcing legal principles concerning lease agreements and contractual intention in South African law.
