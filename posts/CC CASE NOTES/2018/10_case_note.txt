# Case Note: Pretorius and Another v Transport Pension Fund and Others 2018 ZACC 10

## F - Facts

The case of *Pretorius and Another v Transport Pension Fund and Others* centers on the livelihood of pensioners who previously worked within the South African state-operated transport enterprise. This dispute traces back to a promise made in 1989 stipulating that employees transitioning to a new commercial entity, Transnet, would retain their pension benefits previously secured under the statutory South African Transport Services (SATS) and its old pension funds. Initially, these pension benefits were honored, but alterations after 2003 led to a discontinuance of these commitments ([1]–[2]).

The applicants, Johan Pieter Pretorius and Montana David Kwapa, initiated a class action in the High Court to assert several claims against Transnet and its current pension funds regarding this discontinuation. Their claims included: (1) breach of the 1989 promise as an enforceable contract, state promise, or unfair labor practice, (2) obligations regarding legacy debts from prior pension funds, and (3) the unlawful donation made by one of the new pension funds to Transnet. The respondents, comprising the Transport Pension Fund, the Transnet Second Defined Benefit Fund, and Transnet Limited, raised exceptions to all three claims ([2]–[3]).

The High Court granted three exceptions concerning the 1989 promise claim, labeled as vague and lacking detail, while dismissing the other exceptions. Consequently, the applicants sought leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court while respondents sought conditional cross-appeal on the other claims ([3]–[4]).

## I - Issues

The primary legal questions before the Constitutional Court included:

1. Whether the exception related to the 1989 promise claim was appropriately upheld based on grounds of vagueness and lack of cause of action ([10]–[11]).
2. The constitutional nature of the claims, specifically whether the contractual claim implicated constitutional issues, and whether the unlawful state action and unfair labor practice claims were valid ([13]–[12]).
3. If Transnet and its successor pension funds bore liability under the allegations stemming from the 1989 promise, and the implications of the previous institutional obligations regarding pensions ([24]–[25]).

## R - Rules

The court ruled based on several established legal principles, including:

- **General Principles of Contractual Enforceability**: A promise is enforceable if clear and unequivocal rights and obligations are outlined. The court referred to precedents which assert the necessity of upholding claims that can be reasonably interpreted from available facts ([15]–[20]).
- **Unlawful State Action**: Relying on *KwaZulu-Natal Joint Liaison Committee v MEC for Education, KwaZulu-Natal*, the court established that a state promise may be enforceable if retracting the promise would be unconscionable under public law standards of reliance, accountability, and rationality ([24]–[26]).
- **Unfair Labor Practices**: Under Section 23(1) of the South African Constitution, every individual is entitled to fair labor practices, which can extend beyond formal employment relationships ([46]–[52]).

## A - Application

The court conducted a meticulous examination of how these principles applied to the facts of the case:

### Breach of Contract:
The applicants' claim highlighting the oral and written promise regarding pension benefits, made by officials of SATS and endorsed at employee meetings, was deemed sufficiently clear. The Court concluded that the promise was not vague, as sufficient details regarding the pledge were included, thus warranting legal standing ([16]–[19]).

### Unlawful State Action:
The court distinguished the applicants' interpretation of the unlawful state action from that of the High Court, arguing that the significant public reliance on the promise warranted judicial recognition. The court emphasized that constitutional principles could yield grounds for claims outside the structures of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA), permitting the appeal to progress. The features of public reliance on the promise indicated an element of resilience against arbitrary state action ([24]–[30]).

### Unfair Labor Practice:
The Court rejected the High Court's ruling that restricted the definition of an employer-employee relationship. The court highlighted that the fundamental rights enshrined in the Constitution allow for broader interpretations of unfair labor practices. They posited that labor relations are evolving in the contemporary context, and the protection under Section 23 extends beyond traditional employment contracts ([46]–[55]).

Overall, the court's application of these principles underscored a commitment to safeguarding pensioners' rights and reaffirming state accountability to its citizens.

## C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court held that all exceptions raised by the respondents should be dismissed, reinforcing the argument for the continued enforceability of the promise made under the 1989 agreement. Significant consequences flowed from this decision, as it emphasized the judiciary's role in protecting individual rights against state entity actions, particularly concerning pensioners' benefits ([57]–[58]).

In summary, the Court granted leave to appeal, upheld the appeal on all claims, and reaffirmed that the respondents failed to adequately justify their exceptions. The implications of this ruling extend beyond this case, potentially setting a pivotal precedent for how promises made by state entities may be perceived and enforced in future legal contexts, especially involving the rights of pensioners in South Africa.
