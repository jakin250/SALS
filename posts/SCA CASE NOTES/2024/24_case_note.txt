Source Folder: 2024

# Case Note: FIRAC for *Murray and Others NNO v Ntombela and Others* (729/2022) [2024] ZASCA 24

### F – Facts ([paras 2–6])

The appeal involves three joint liquidators of Phehla Umsebenzi Trading 48 CC (in liquidation) — Cloete Murray, Gert Louwrens Steyn De Wet, and Magda Wilma Kets — as appellants (collectively referred to as "liquidators") against Madala Louis David Ntombela and Sefora Hixsonia Ntombela, the respondents. The respondents purchased immovable property from Phehla Umsebenzi for R2,500,000 in August 2015. Despite payment being made prior to the contract's signing, the property was never transferred to the respondents' names, remaining under the registered name of Phehla Umsebenzi. Over four years, the respondents attempted to resolve the delay but were misinformed about the status of their case, later discovering that Phehla Umsebenzi had been placed in liquidation in June 2018.

The liquidators chose not to complete the sale transaction, instead deciding to sell the property via auction. This prompted the respondents to seek an order to review and set aside the liquidators' decision, as well as to compel the liquidators to transfer the property to them. Following the liquidators' contestation of the review application, asserting it lacked legal competence, the respondents filed a rule 30/30A interlocutory application, seeking to compel the liquidators to file the record of their decision under rule 53(1)(b).

### I – Issues ([paras 1, 6, 9])

The core legal issue presented by this appeal is whether the High Court correctly compelled the delivery of a rule 53 record in the face of an assertion by the liquidators that the review proceedings were incompetent. This raises questions regarding the junction of procedural requirements and judicial competence in review applications, especially concerning the obligation to provide records prior to determining the primary issues of law regarding the competency of the review.

### R – Rules ([paras 8, 19, 22])

The relevant legal principles arise from Rule 53 of the Uniform Rules, which governs the procedure for judicial review in South Africa. Rule 53(1) sets forth the requirements for an applicant seeking a review and underscores that access to the record should follow the submission of a review application. The case of *Standard Bank* ([para 12]) from the Constitutional Court establishes that a court must first determine its jurisdiction to adjudicate review proceedings before compelling the production of a record. This principle is underscored by additional case law, indicating that if the court lacks jurisdiction, any orders it issues would amount to a nullity ([paras 12–13]).

### A – Application ([paras 8–42])

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) addressed the implications of compelling delivery of the rule 53 record before confirming whether the review risks being treated as competent judicial action. The SCA held that compelling a record delivery at this stage would be premature without resolving the liquidators' objections about the legal competence of the respondents’ review application. It concluded that the interlocutory application, which sought the records, could not circumvent the examination of whether the actions of the liquidators constituted administrative action warranting reviewability.

The SCA further elaborated that the question of whether the liquidators acted within their legal authority as public powers or merely as private agents is central to the review's competency. If the liquidators' decision to resile from the agreement did not constitute administrative action, then the procedural rights under Rule 53 would become moot, as the respondents could not claim entitlements that do not exist due to the legal context ([paras 18, 45]).

Ultimately, the court articulated the importance of complying with procedural rules before adjudicating substantive legal issues, emphasizing that the review's merits must be settled only after procedural rights had been fully acknowledged ([para 41]).

### C – Conclusion ([paras 46–47])

The SCA dismissed the appeal, asserting that the High Court had correctly ordered the liquidators to provide the record, highlighting that procedural requirements under Rule 53 could not be sidestepped. The court's ruling reaffirms the significance of these procedural safeguards in reviews and establishes a firm ground for the judiciary to exercise its function to ensure fair and just access to the courts as mandated under Section 34 of the Constitution. Thus, the judgment elucidates the interplay between administrative law and liquidations, enhancing clarity around the obligations of liquidators amid statutory demands and reinforcing the judiciary's role in reviewing such actions.
