Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: Minister of Safety and Security & Another v Marius Schuster & Another (114/2018) [2018] ZASCA 112

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

The case revolves around the alleged unlawful arrest and detention of the respondents, Marius Schuster and Darryl Campher. The events leading to this appeal commenced on January 10, 2013, when the respondents were arrested by members of the South African Police Service on robbery charges without a warrant ([1]). The primary claims arose from their detention at St Alban's prison until January 30, 2013, after which they were acquitted of the charges ([2]).

The respondents claimed that their arrest was unlawful as they were taken into custody without a warrant and were subjected to arbitrary detention at the Gelvandale police station ([3]). The appellants, namely the Minister of Safety and Security and the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP), maintained that the arrests were lawful, as warrants had been issued prior, on November 21, 2012 ([4]).

Following their arrest, the respondents were brought to court the same day and requested a bail application. They were remanded in custody until January 21, 2013, but subsequently abandoned their bail application to expedite their trial ([4]–[5]). Constable January, who conducted the arrest, testified that he did show the respondents the warrant for their arrest, although copies were not provided ([6]). However, no evidence was produced in court regarding the original arrest warrant's existence ([6]).

The civil action for damages initiated by the respondents resulted in the magistrate awarding R50,000 each for unlawful arrest and R250,000 for continued detention, leading to an appeal by the appellants to the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, which was dismissed ([10]).

### I – Issues

The central legal questions presented in this appeal included:
1. Whether the respondents were unlawfully arrested and detained in the absence of a warrant ([2]).
2. Whether the arrest was warranted under the provisions of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([12]).
3. Whether the continued detention of the respondents post their court appearance constituted an unlawful detention ([12]).

### R – Rules

The legal framework informing this case included:
- **Section 39(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977**, which mandates that a person arrested must be informed of the cause of arrest and, if without a warrant, one must be provided upon request ([13]).
- Jurisprudence establishing that the onus is on the Minister (or the police) to justify any loss of liberty through arrest ([11]).
- **Zealand v Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development & another** [2008] ZACC 3, which underscores this burden ([11]).
- Relevant case law distinguishing lawful arrests from unlawful detentions, including **Isaacs v Minister van Wet en Orde** and **De Klerk v Minister of Police**, offering principles on justification for continued detention post-arrest ([14], [19]).

### A – Application

The court's analysis began with the acknowledgment that both respondents were arrested without a warrant being produced, but this alone was not sufficient to deem the arrest unlawful. The court highlighted the procedural difficulties and deficiencies in the respondents' claims. It noted that the appellants had established that warrants had been validly issued by cross-referencing police procedure records and testimony by Constable January ([6]–[10]).

The evidence indicated that despite not presenting written documentation of the warrant at the time of the arrest, the inherent credibility of Constable January’s testimony—unrefuted during trial—and procedural documentation supported their lawful arrest claims. The court rejected the argument that the absence of a physical copy of the warrant during arrest constituted unlawful action, emphasizing the existence of a warrant and the respondents' prior failure to comply with police instructions to report to the station ([11], [12]).

Regarding the continued detention, the court evaluated whether legal authority justified holding the respondents in custody due to the seriousness of the alleged crime (armed robbery), which fell under schedule 6 of the CPA. Under **Section 60(11)(a)**, detention was permissible unless the accused could show exceptional circumstances warranting their release ([15]). Notably, the respondents chose to expedite their trial over pursuing bail, leaving no grounds for arguing that their detention was unjust ([15]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) concluded that the arrest and subsequent detention of the respondents were lawful. The court found that sufficient evidence supported the issuance of warrants for both respondents’ arrests ([26]). Therefore, it reversed the judgment of the Eastern Cape Division and asserted that the claims for damages should be dismissed, upholding the general rule that costs follow the result ([27]).

This ruling reaffirmed the principles governing arrest procedures while clarifying the legal standards for the lawful exercise of police powers in South Africa, specifically in relation to the necessity of warrants and the justifications for continuing detention following arrest. It also underscored the notion that claims for unlawful arrest and detention are delictual in nature and do not automatically invoke constitutional protections necessitating deviations from the standard costs rule in litigation involving state organs ([18]–[25]).
