Supreme Court of Appeal

Johannes Petrus Basson v The State (442/12) [2012] ZASCA 204

The appellants appealed to the North Gauteng High Court, which amended the conviction for attempted murder to assault and reduced the sentence significantly.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2012 [2012] ZASCA 204

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The appellants, Johannes Petrus Basson and Pieter Johannes Hendrik Du Plessis, were initially convicted by the Pretoria Regional Court on 8 August 2003 for multiple serious criminal offenses—including robbery with aggravating circumstances, theft of a motor vehicle, and attempted murder ([1]). They received lengthy sentences: for counts 1, 2, and 5, they were sentenced to 15 years each; for count 4, to 20 years; and for count 6, to 5 years. The first appellant received an additional 10 years for attempted murder (count 3) ([1]). The trial court ordered that many of these sentences run concurrently, resulting in a total of 50 years for the first appellant and 40 years for the second ([1]).

The appellants appealed to the North Gauteng High Court, which amended the conviction for attempted murder to assault and reduced the sentence significantly. Ultimately, it ordered that the sentences run concurrently with counts 1, 2, 3, 5, and 6, adjusting the effective imprisonment to 20 years for Basson and 19 years for Du Plessis ([2]). However, subsequent legal complexities led them to seek further clarification on whether their sentences for separate prior convictions should run concurrently with their new sentences. On 27 July 2011, the appeal court granted leave to appeal again, recognizing that the sentence’s cumulative effect might be excessively harsh ([5]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal concerned the following:

  1. Whether the sentences imposed by the appeal court should run concurrently with previous sentences given by the Brits Regional Court, thereby adjusting the effective terms of imprisonment.
  2. The appropriateness of the cumulative sentences and if the trial court had been informed of the appellants’ prior sentences during sentencing ([9], [10]).
  3. Whether imposing a lengthy cumulative term of imprisonment violated principles of proportionality and fairness ([11]).

R – Rules

In determining the case, the court evaluated statutes including the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, which stipulates minimum sentences for aggravated robbery. The ruling referenced the importance of considering the cumulative effect of all sentences imposed, emphasizing that courts must strive for sentences that balance society's interests with the circumstances of the offenders ([7], [14]).

Precedent regarding the application of concurrent sentences established that while severe sentences may be warranted for serious crimes, the cumulative effect should not result in disproportionately lengthy imprisonment ([14]).

A – Application

In their deliberation, the court acknowledged the violent nature of the crimes committed by both appellants and the severe applicable sentences under the minimum sentencing legislation. However, the court also recognized the initial error in calculating the length of cumulative imprisonment and the incorrect assumption that the trial court was unaware of the appellants' prior sentences ([6], [14]).

The court noted that while violence was a factor, there was also consideration for the appellants' personal circumstances, including their previous convictions and the context of their actions ([13], [14]). The desire for a fair and proportionate sentencing process was evident in their discussion on balancing punitive and rehabilitative objectives.

The court decided to adjust the sentences to reflect that portions would run concurrently with prior sentences, leading to an effective sentence of 25 years for the first appellant and 24 years for the second appellant, while still retaining some of the punitive measures ([15]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately altered the previous order by allowing some sentences to run concurrent with those from the Brits Regional Court. This adjustment resulted in effective terms of imprisonment of 25 years for Basson and 24 years for Du Plessis. The court ruled that while the applicants had committed serious and violent crimes, the cumulative effect with prior sentences was disproportionate, necessitating this adjustment to ensure fairness and justice in sentencing ([15]).

This case underscores the importance of considering the totality of an individual's criminal history and the principle of proportionality in sentencing, reflecting an adherence to the values underpinning South African criminal law.
