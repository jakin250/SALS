Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

# Case Note: The Minister of Trade and Industry v E L Enterprises (193/10) [2010] ZASCA 149

## F – Facts 

The case revolves around the application of the Counterfeit Goods Act 37 of 1997, particularly focusing on the procedural requirements outlined in section 5(4)(a). The second appellant, an inspector employed by the first appellant, seized suspected counterfeit goods without a warrant from the premises controlled by the respondents ([1–2]). The seizure followed a reasonable suspicion that the respondents were in violation of the Counterfeit Goods Act and were based on complaints received ([4]).

On April 22, 2005, the second appellant procured a search warrant, authorizing him to search the respondents' premises for specific counterfeit goods, including screen printing plates and items bearing trademarks registered by Unilever. Upon executing the warrant, the inspector discovered numerous items that were identified as counterfeit ([5]–[6]).

However, following the search and seizure, the appellants made an application to confirm the seizure as per section 5(4)(a) of the Act. This application was lodged within ten days but was not served on the respondents until after the expiration of that period. The court below upheld the respondents' argument that the application failed to comply with the statutory requirements, resulting in the dismissal of the appellants' initial application, and they were denied leave to appeal ([9–10]).

The appellants subsequently pursued this matter in the Supreme Court of Appeal, arguing that two conflicting interpretations of section 5(4)(a) necessitated clarity on proper procedural adherence, regardless of the settlement reached with the respondents ([2], [17]).

## I – Issues 

The primary legal issue presented before the Supreme Court of Appeal concerned the correct interpretation of section 5(4)(a) of the Counterfeit Goods Act regarding procedural requirements for the confirmation of a warrantless seizure of goods. Specifically, the court was tasked to determine:

1. Whether the application for confirmation required notice to be served on the respondents prior to the expiration of the ten-day period.
2. Did the appellants' compliance with the procedural requirements of section 5(4)(a) suffice when the application was filed within ten days but served outside that period? ([8], [9]).

## R – Rules 

The relevant rules of law in this case stem from the Counterfeit Goods Act 37 of 1997, particularly sections 5 and 6, which delineate the powers and procedures for inspectors acting under the Act:

- **Section 5(4)(a)** specifies that any actions taken by an inspector without a warrant must be confirmed by a magistrate or a judge within ten court days after the actions are executed.
- **Section 6(1)** outlines the process for obtaining a warrant, emphasizing the need for reasonable grounds to believe that dealing in counterfeit goods is occurring.

Additionally, the court looked at the interpretation of what constitutes a formal 'application' versus an 'application on notice,' as well as the implications of serving such notice within temporal constraints established by the Act ([12], [17]).

## A – Application 

In interpreting the statutes, the court noted significant distinctions between sections 5(4)(a) and 6. It emphasized that whilst section 6 necessitates a formal warrant application procedural structure that requires notice, section 5(4)(a) does not explicitly mandate that the application be made on notice nor requires that notice be served before the expiry of the ten-day window. 

The court assessed that the wording and context of the two sections indicated legislative intent not to require notice to respondents in relation to confirmatory applications ([13]–[15]). The judgment underscored that the nature and exigency surrounding counterfeit goods operations often necessitate immediate actions without prior notice to potential respondents ([12]).

Thus, the court concluded that the appellants had followed the correct procedure by submitting their application within the statutory timeframe, and that the requirement for notice did not apply in the circumstances. Consequently, the court found that the questioning of the procedures used in the lower court was without merit ([17]). 

## C – Conclusion 

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that the requirements of section 5(4)(a) of the Counterfeit Goods Act did not necessitate that the application for confirmation of a seizure be treated as a motion on notice, nor that it had to be served within the ten-day limitation period. The appeal was upheld, the previous court's order was set aside, and it was declared that the legislative provisions were adhered to effectively by the appellants ([18]).

This ruling clarifies the procedural landscape under the Counterfeit Goods Act, establishing that the procedural requirements can be satisfied through timely filing of the application, regardless of the timing of service to respondents. This decision not only addresses the immediate factual matrix but also serves to guide future applications concerning counterfeiting operations, encouraging prompt and decisive action by enforcement bodies within legal frameworks.
