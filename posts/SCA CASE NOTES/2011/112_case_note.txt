# Case Note: Pienaar v Master of the Free State High Court (579/10) [2011] ZASCA112

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])

In this case, the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa considered the testamentary dispositions of Frederik Jacobus du Toit, who executed two wills prior to his death on June 30, 2007. The first will was executed on November 27, 2006, and the second on May 28, 2007. The testator was divorced from his first wife and subsequently remarried to Cynthia du Toit, with whom he had a son, Derick. Pienaar and Steven, the appellants, are the daughters of the testator from his first marriage ([paras 1–2]).

The 2006 will explicitly revoked any previous wills and bequeathed a Sanlam Personal Portfolio to Du Toit if payable to the estate, an immovable property, a motor vehicle to Derick, and the residue of the estate to the appellants ([para 3]). Conversely, the 2007 will made different provisions, including bequests of an immovable property to all children and a lifelong right of use of the property bequeathed to Derick for Du Toit. Notably, the 2007 will omitted the specific bequest of the Sanlam Personal Portfolio, leading to confusion regarding its disposition ([para 4]).

After the testator's death, a distribution account prepared by the third respondent treated the Sanlam Personal Portfolio as part of the estate’s residue. Du Toit and Derick objected, claiming that the policy should be paid to Du Toit as per the earlier will. The appellants sought a court order confirming the revival of the 2007 will, alleging it impliedly revoked the 2006 will regarding the Sanlam policy ([paras 6–8]).

The Free State High Court dismissed the application, determining that the 2007 will did not revoke the 2006 will and that the bequest to Du Toit remained valid ([para 10]). The appellants appealed this decision, bringing the matter before the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 11]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 10–11])

The primary issue for determination was whether the 2007 will impliedly revoked the earlier 2006 will concerning the bequest of the Sanlam Personal Portfolio. Specifically, the court had to ascertain whether the two wills could be reconciled and whether the later will sufficiently indicated an intention to revoke the terms of the earlier instrument ([para 11]).

Important sub-issues included:
1. The scope of revocation through later testamentary instruments.
2. Whether the absence of a revocation clause in the 2007 will meant that it could not revoke the dispositions of the 2006 will.
3. The interpretation of the testator’s intentions in light of conflicting provisions between the two wills.

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 11–14])

The court referenced established principles regarding the interpretation of testamentary documents. It noted that where a testator executes more than one will, their provisions must be harmonised and construed collectively ([para 11]). Any earlier provisions are deemed revoked to the extent of inconsistencies between the later will and earlier testamentary instruments (Ex parte Estate Adams 1946 CPD 267; Vimpany v Attridge 1927 CPD 113). The prevailing understanding is that clear and unequivocal language is necessary to establish the revocation of an earlier bequest ([para 14]).

The court emphasized that the testator’s intent must be discerned from the language used in the wills, and once established, must be honored ([para 14]). In Price v The Master 1982 (3) SA 301, the court articulated the importance of understanding wills as a “new and different scheme” when the provisions change significantly. 

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 12–15])

In its analysis, the court concluded that both wills intended to dispose of the testator’s entire estate, evidenced by similar introductory language affirming the disposition of the estate ([para 12]). Despite the lack of an explicit revocation clause in the 2007 will, the court found a clear inconsistency between the dispositions of the two wills concerning the treatment of the Sanlam policy and other assets, implying that the later will was intended to supersede and implicitly revoke inconsistent bequests ([para 13]).

The court also recognized that the alterations made in the later will represented a substantial departure from the prior will’s provisions, thereby indicating the testator's intention to revise rather than preserve the earlier will ([para 12]). Thus, it ruled that the inclusion of the Sanlam policy as part of the residue in the later will reflected a definitive shift in the testator's wishes, contrary to the earlier will’s provisions.

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 16])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, emphasizing that the testament of May 28, 2007, impliedly revoked the earlier testament of November 27, 2006, to the extent of any inconsistencies ([para 16]). The ruling clarified that where a later will deals comprehensively with a testator's estate and introduces significant changes, such changes inherently signal an intention to revoke earlier provisions ([para 16]).

The judgment has critical implications for probate law in South Africa, particularly regarding the interpretation of multiple wills, testamentary intentions, and the clarity required in legal language to ensure a testator's wishes are respected. This case reaffirms the judiciary's role in actively discerning and effecting the true intent of the testator while upholding the formal requirements of testamentary gifts. Additionally, it reinforces the necessity for clear drafting in testamentary documents to prevent costly disputes among beneficiaries.

This case sets a significant precedent by illustrating how courts may interpret seemingly contradictory or inconsistent wills, providing a clearer framework for future testamentary disputes and the administration of estates.
