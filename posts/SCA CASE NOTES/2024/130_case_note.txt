Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note on *Jooste NO and Another v Pretorius and Others* (Case No: 695/2023) [2024] ZASCA 130

## F – Facts ([paras 1–24])

The case arises from an administrative dispute within the Rhino Pride Foundation (the Trust), where the appellants, Tersia Jooste NO and Jens Lievens NO, sought to force the resignation of the first respondent, Dr. Jana Annelise Pretorius, a founding trustee, by invoking a clause in the trust deed. The Trust was established with the main objectives of protecting rhinos and preventing poaching. The appellants expressed concerns regarding the management of the Trust by Pretorius, particularly her failure to adhere to budget protocols and her unilateral decision-making that jeopardised funding from a significant donor. The Trust’s critical reliance on this funding, which accounted for approximately 90% of its income, intensified the situation.

During a trustees' meeting on 3 March 2022, the appellants succeeded in passing a resolution requiring Pretorius to resign under clause 11 of the trust deed, which stipulates the conditions for a trustee's removal. Pretorius later sought an interdict from the Gauteng Division of the High Court to prevent enforcement of this resolution, claiming it was precipitously and unfairly enacted. 

The High Court initially ruled in favour of Pretorius, finding insufficient evidence to support the resolution's legitimacy and asserting it violated procedural fairness as encapsulated in her constitutional rights. The subsequent appeal by the appellants contested this decision, seeking validation of the removal.

## I – Issues ([paras 28–30])

The core issues for determination by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) included:

1. **Interpretation and Enforcement of Trust Deed**: Whether the resolution to remove Pretorius as trustee was valid under the terms set forth in the trust deed, specifically clause 11.1.5, alongside compliance with the provisions of the Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988 (the Act).
  
2. **Procedural Fairness**: Whether the High Court erred in its application of procedural justice and the statutory provisions aimed at protecting the interests and rights of trustees, particularly regarding the rights guaranteed under section 34 of the South African Constitution, which outlines the right to a fair hearing.

## R – Rules ([paras 40–61])

The court relied on various legal principles and statutory provisions in its analysis:

1. **Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988**: Section 20(1) permits the removal of a trustee by a court if such action serves the best interests of the trust and its beneficiaries. Section 9(1) articulates the standard of care required of trustees.

2. **Trust Deed Interpretations**: The deed's provisions, particularly clause 11.1.5, empowered trustees to remove a fellow trustee unanimously. The interpretation of this clause necessitated a balance between the immediate administrative needs of the Trust and the legal safeguards for individual trustees.

3. **Procedural Fairness**: The principles of audi alteram partem (hearing the other side) and nemo iudex in propria causa (no one should judge their own case) were examined to assess whether the processes followed in Pretorius’s removal were procedurally fair.

4. **Judicial Precedent**: The SCA referenced the *Plascon-Evans rule*, which asserts that in motion proceedings, disputed facts will typically require a hearing unless the respondent's version is implausible or lacking in credibility ([para 33]).

## A – Application ([paras 32–64])

In applying the law to the facts, the SCA examined the substantive details surrounding the trustees' meeting on 3 March 2022 and the contexts leading to the impugned resolution. The Court concluded that:

1. **Valid Resolution**: The resolution for Pretorius’s resignation was deemed valid based on clear evidence indicating that her actions had compromised the Trust. The circumstances surrounding her unilateral decisions and subsequent behaviour demonstrated a breakdown in the collaborative governance of the Trust, justifying her removal.

2. **Not Unlawful or Unjust**: The decision to enforce the resolution did not contravene statutory requirements or procedural justice as claimed by Pretorius. The court concluded that the immediate financial and operational risks to the Trust warranted swift action that would otherwise be hindered by lengthy court proceedings.

3. **Constitutional Rights Considered**: The Court found that Pretorius's rights under section 34 were not unjustly infringed, noting that finding ways to resolve disputes amongst trustees is normative in trust law, aligning with the collective governance of the Trust as intended by its founder.

4. **Cost Implications**: The Court imposed costs on Pretorius in her personal capacity due to her actions being deemed reckless and made in bad faith, especially regarding the false claims that led to the ex parte order previously granted in her favour.

## C – Conclusion ([paras 64])

The SCA ultimately ruled in favour of the appellants, overturning the High Court’s decision, validating the resolution that required Pretorius to resign as trustee, and appointing Ms. Marielle Borgström in her stead. This decision underscores the lawful autonomy of trust operations while emphasising the necessity for trustee accountability, aligning with both the Trust's objectives and public interest. The ruling also clarifies the procedural obligations for trustees when instigating removal processes, reinforcing the delicate balance between collegial governance and individual trustee rights within the context of trust law. This case serves as a significant development in South African trust law, particularly concerning the interpretation of trust deeds and fiduciary duties.
