FIRAC Analysis of Narius Moloto v The Pan Africanist Congress of Azania [2023] ZASCA 140

This case arises from a dispute within the Pan Africanist Congress of Azania (PAC) regarding the invocation of clause 14.2 of its disciplinary code by the appellant, Narius Moloto, who was then the President of the PAC.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 140

F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1-9])

This case arises from a dispute within the Pan Africanist Congress of Azania (PAC) regarding the invocation of clause 14.2 of its disciplinary code by the appellant, Narius Moloto, who was then the President of the PAC. The background context dates back to a consent order issued by Mavundla J in earlier proceedings aimed at uniting factions of the PAC, which resulted in the formation of a joint National Executive Committee (NEC) composed of members from the conflicting factions ([para 3]).

The joint NEC, under the leadership of the appellant, resolved to convene a national congress from August 29 to 31, 2019. Following preparations, dissent arose when the acting National Chairperson requested clarity regarding the congress's logistics. Subsequently, Moloto invoked clause 14.2 on June 9, 2019, claiming emergency powers to suspend the constitution of the PAC to unilaterally determine the logistics of the congress and subsequently altered the date to August 24, 2019 ([paras 5-6]).

This unilateral action led to an urgent application by the Secretary-General, Mr. Apa Shadrack Ntsiki Pooe, resulting in the High Court's order on July 12, 2019, which set aside Moloto's decree and validated the arrangements for the congress set for August 29 ([para 8]). The appeal was launched following leave granted by the High Court ([para 1]).

I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 10-14])

The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the appeal had become moot, given that the national congress was successfully convened without the appellant's decree, and thus sought to address whether any practical effect could arise from the appeal ([para 11]). Additionally, the court had to consider whether Moloto's reliance on emergency powers as provided in clause 14.2 was lawful and if any interpretation of this clause, should be subjective or objective in nature, considering the internal conflict within the PAC ([para 12]).

R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 14-42])

In addressing the issues, the court relied on provisions within the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, particularly section 16(2)(a)(i), relating to situations where an appeal may be dismissed if the issues are deemed moot—the relief sought having no practical effect ([para 12]).

The court noted precedents from earlier rulings regarding conflicts within the PAC, specifically contrasting the previous interpretation of clause 14.2 from the Free State High Court, which supported a subjective interpretation, with the High Court's finding that required an objective standard ([paras 27-30]). The judgments cited included Western Cape Education Department v George and Mohamed v President of the RSA, highlighting the principle regarding mootness while noting exceptions where legal issues of public interest remain ([para 13]).

The court additionally referred to the principle that a court must avoid issuing advisory opinions on abstract legal questions, thereby supporting its discretion to determine the appropriateness of hearing moot cases when public interest is at stake ([paras 30-32]).

A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 10-42])

The court found that the agreement to hold the national congress had been duly adopted by the NEC prior to the appellant’s invocation of his emergency powers. The reasoning presented by the High Court suggested that the actions taken by Mr. Pooe in preparation for the congress did not present any exceptional emergency situation, countering Moloto’s claims ([paras 11-12]).

Significantly, the High Court had concluded that the invocation of clause 14.2 was not justified and thus set aside the decree Moloto issued. The Supreme Court acknowledged that the contextual reading of clause 14.2 requires a genuine emergency before it could be invoked, thereby agreeing with the view that no such emergency existed at the time of the decree ([paras 38-40]).

Moreover, the dissenting opinion emphasized the legal confusion stemming from the conflicting interpretations of clause 14.2, urging a resolution to provide clarity regarding procedural authority within PAC. This included asserting that an effective governance structure, including adherence to democratically established resolutions, should take precedence over individual presidential authority ([paras 32-42]).

C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 18-42])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming the High Court’s decision. The ruling established that the appellant's unilateral invocation of clause 14.2 was improper, reinforcing the importance of adhering to constitutional provisions regarding emergency powers within political organizations ([paras 18-19]).

Moreover, the court’s decision signified that due process and compliance with organizational constitutions must be upheld, as deviating from established protocols not only undermines internal governance but also jeopardizes the party's democratic integrity. The court expressly stated that it would not provide advisory guidance on political disputes unless such decisions bore significant legal implications or clarify uncertainties regarding constitutional interpretation within organizational law ([paras 17-43]).

This ruling serves as an essential reference point in South African constitutional law regarding emergency powers in political party governance and solidifies the precedent for future conflicts within political organizations, ensuring an accountability framework aligned with democratic principles.
