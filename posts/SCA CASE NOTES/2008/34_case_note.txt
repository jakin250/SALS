Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2008

# FIRAC Case Note: Madinda v Minister of Safety and Security (153/2007) [2008] ZASCA 34

## F – Facts ([1–4])

The appellant, Thembela Madinda, is a 32-year-old unemployed woman residing in Grahamstown, South Africa. In July 2006, she applied to the High Court for condonation of her failure to serve a notice of intention to bring legal proceedings against the Minister of Safety and Security, the respondent, as required under section 3(2)(a) of the Institution of Legal Proceedings against Certain Organs of State Act 40 of 2002. The application was opposed by the Minister ([1]).

Madinda's legal claim stemmed from alleged unlawful arrest, detention, and assault by members of the South African Police Service (SAPS) on September 11, 2004. The notice of intention to sue was served on the National Commissioner of Police on August 19, 2005, approximately five and a half months past the statutory deadline of March 11, 2005 ([5]).

The High Court, presided over by Van der Byl AJ, dismissed the application for condonation, ruling that Madinda failed to demonstrate 'good cause' for her late notice and that the police were unreasonably prejudiced by her failure ([3–4]). The High Court denied her leave to appeal, but the Supreme Court of Appeal subsequently granted her such leave ([4]).

## I – Issues ([5–6])

Central to this case were several legal questions:

1. Did Madinda demonstrate 'good cause' for her failure to serve the notice within the prescribed period?
2. Was the SAPS unreasonably prejudiced by the late notice?
3. Should the court exercise its discretion to grant condonation despite the delays in serving the notice and filing the application?

These questions revolve around the interpretation and application of the provisions of section 3 of the Institution of Legal Proceedings against Certain Organs of State Act 40 of 2002.

## R – Rules ([2], [6–8], [18–20])

The following legal principles and statutory provisions were relevant:

1. **Institution of Legal Proceedings against Certain Organs of State Act 40 of 2002**:
   - **Section 3(2)(a)** mandates that a notice must be served within six months from when the debt becomes due.
   - **Section 3(4)** outlines the conditions under which a court may grant condonation for the failure to serve the notice, which includes: 
     - The debt must not be extinguished by prescription;
     - The applicant must show good cause for the failure to provide timely notice;
     - The organ of state must not suffer unreasonable prejudice due to the applicant's delay ([2]).

2. **Legal Precedents**:
   - **Die Afrikaanse Pers Beperk v Neser** (1948) established that 'if the court is satisfied' requires an overall impression rather than strict proof ([8]).
   - **Silber v Ozen Wholesalers (Pty) Ltd** (1954) highlights that the notion of 'good cause' necessitates a comprehensive understanding of the factors influencing the delay, including the prospects of success ([11]).
   - The court must balance the right to access justice against the interests of the organ of state, which emphasizes both 'good cause' and the absence of unreasonable prejudice ([15–16]).

## A – Application ([18–30])

### Good Cause
The court examined whether Madinda provided sufficient reasons for her failure to serve the notice timely. It recognized that despite her initial delay, she established a prima facie case of assault and unlawful arrest. The court accepted her explanation of ignorance regarding the legal requirements, as she believed that the police would investigate her complaint, allowing her to delay legal actions based on her physical condition and naivety regarding legal processes ([18.1]–[18.4]).

The court noted other procedural issues, including her attorney's actions post-consultation. The appellant acted promptly upon learning of the requirement for notice, though there was about a month and a half delay in sending this notice after consultation with her attorney, which lacked sufficient explanation ([19]).

### Absence of Unreasonable Prejudice
The court determined that the SAPS was not unreasonably prejudiced by the delay in serving notice, referencing that the police had already investigated the matter extensively. The learned judge concluded that there was no substantive assertion from the respondent that the delay hindered their ability to mount a defense, a critical factor in assessing unreasonable prejudice ([20], [21]).

### Discretion to Grant Condonation
In the context of the misdirections evident in the lower court’s judgment, the Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized the learned judge’s balancing of Madinda’s right to have her claim heard against the interests of SAPS. The absence of unreasonable prejudice combined with her established 'good cause' satisfied the requisite legal framework for condonation, which the lower court failed to appropriately assess ([29]).

## C – Conclusion ([31])

The Supreme Court of Appeal determined that the learned judge of the High Court misdirected himself regarding both 'good cause' and unreasonable prejudice. The ruling was crucial as it upheld Madinda's right to have her claim assessed on its merits, concluding that she had sufficiently demonstrated both good cause for her delay and that no unreasonable prejudice faced the SAPS ([31]). 

Thus, the appeal was upheld with costs, allowing Madinda's claim to proceed to trial. The court's decision not only reinforces the judiciary's role in balancing individual rights against statutory requirements but also emphasizes the importance of fair access to justice, particularly in cases involving allegations of unlawful state action.
