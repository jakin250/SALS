Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 117

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History

The case revolves around Mr. Nathaniel Mashilo Masemola, a 90-year-old man with a significant history of involvement in the anti-apartheid struggle, notably through his membership in the African National Congress (ANC) since 1946. Mr. Masemola returned from exile to contribute to South Africa's transition to democracy, resulting in his application for a special pension under the Special Pensions Act 69 of 1996 (the Act) ([1]–[3]).

Mr. Masemola was awarded this pension in 1997 but was subsequently disqualified in 2008, following a fraud conviction in 2001, as stipulated by sections 1(7), 1(8), and 1(9) of the Act, which preclude any special pensioner convicted of certain enumerated offenses post-2 February 1990 ([3]). On 21 July 2011, Mr. Masemola received a Presidential pardon per section 84(2)(j) of the Constitution but nonetheless faced refusal from the Government Pensions Administration Agency (GPAA) to reinstate his pension ([4]).

After appealing to the Special Pensions Appeal Board (the Appeal Board) and receiving no positive decision, Mr. Masemola approached the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which ruled in his favor, reinstating his special pension and commanding the GPAA to pay it effective from the date of his pardon ([5]–[6]). The high court held that the GPAA's refusal to reinstate constituted an appealable decision ([6]). The GPAA and the Appeal Board subsequently appealed this ruling ([6]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions

  1. Disqualification under the Act: Did Mr. Masemola's conviction and subsequent Presidential pardon entitle him to reinstatement of his special pension despite the disqualifying provisions of the Special Pensions Act?
  2. Legal Validity of Presidential Pardon: Does a Presidential pardon effectively eliminate the legal framework that disqualifies individuals from receiving special pensions?
  3. Interpretation of the Lapsing Provision: How do the lapsing provisions introduced by sections 6A and 6Abis of the Act affect the eligibility for special pensions?

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon

The court relied heavily on the provisions of the Special Pensions Act, especially sections 1(8) and 1(9), which detail the disqualifications for receiving a pension based on criminal convictions. The lapsing provisions of section 6A, which negated new applications after a certain date without affecting benefits pending a determination, were pivotal to the legal arguments ([10]–[12]).

Furthermore, the Presidential pardon was scrutinized in relation to the precedent established by The Citizen 1978 (Pty) Ltd v McBride ([20]), especially its interpretation of amnesty provisions under the Promotion of National Unity and Reconciliation Act. The court noted the crucial distinction between amnesty and pardon, indicating that while the former expunges a conviction, the latter does not negate its historical existence ([20]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law

The Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized Mr. Masemola's claims through the lens of the legislative framework of the Special Pensions Act, emphasizing the statutory disqualifications that automatically triggered upon conviction ([19]). The court held that the lapsing provisions meant Mr. Masemola could not reapply or continue receiving the special pension, as the relevant part of the Act had lapsed well before his Presidential pardon.

Despite the sympathy for Mr. Masemola’s situation, the court emphasized the principle of legality, stating that allowing him to receive the pension would subvert the clear statutory disqualifications established by the legislature ([23]). The court further noted that while Mr. Masemola's counsel relied on the understanding of a Presidential pardon, the jurisprudence did not support an expansive interpretation that would overcome the explicit statutory barriers ([21]). The court differentiated between the effects of an amnesty and a pardon, affirming that there was no legal ground to recognize the pardon as restoring Mr. Masemola's eligibility for the pension under the existing laws ([20]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal, setting aside the high court's order that had reinstated Mr. Masemola's pension. The court concluded that the statutory disqualifications operated ex lege, leaving no room for reliance on the Presidential pardon to reinstate entitlement to the special pension ([25]).

In the broader context, this decision emphasizes the unwavering nature of statutory provisions and the importance of adherence to legislative frameworks in welfare entitlements, particularly for individuals who may have historical significance yet face legal barriers due to prior convictions. The ruling also underlines the implications for other similar cases, reaffirming the judiciary's stance on the principle of legality and legislative intent in welfare distribution ([23]).

This case marks an important affirmation of legal principles governing the intersection of legislative enactments and the scope of executive clemency, significantly affecting future claims under the Special Pensions Act.
