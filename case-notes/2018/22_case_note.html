Assign Services (Pty) Limited v National Union of Metalworkers of South Africa and Others [2018] ZACC 22

Constitutional Court 2018 [2018] ZACC 22

F – Facts

In the matter of Assign Services (Pty) Limited v National Union of Metalworkers of South Africa and Others, the Constitutional Court of South Africa was presented with an appeal stemming from the Labour Appeal Court regarding the interpretation of section 198A(3)(b) of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA). The question arose as to whether a worker placed by a temporary employment service (TES) for more than three months is deemed an employee of both the TES and the client or solely of the client.

The applicant, Assign Services (Pty) Limited, operates as a registered TES that provides workers to various businesses, including Krost Shelving & Racking (Pty) Limited, who was the client in this case ([1]–[10]). The first respondent, NUMSA (National Union of Metalworkers of South Africa), argued that when the deeming provision activates, the client becomes the sole employer, contradicting Assign's position that it retains a dual employer status for all intents and purposes post the three-month period ([14]–[15]).

The CCMA (Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration) initially ruled in favor of NUMSA, stating that the client becomes the sole employer upon the lapse of the three-month period. This decision, along with the Labour Court’s subsequent interpretation, led to the appeal presented in the Constitutional Court ([20]–[24]).

The appeal was heard on 22 February 2018 and decided on 26 July 2018, with the court ultimately rejecting Assign’s appeal and endorsing the Labour Appeal Court’s interpretation ([1]–[2]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue in this matter was the interpretation of section 198A(3)(b) of the LRA, specifically whether the section creates a dual employment relationship or a sole employer relationship upon the lapse of the three-month period for employees earning below the BCEA threshold ([1]–[2], [30]–[31]).

A subsidiary issue examined whether the appeal constituted a de facto ban on labour broking, as argued by Assign, and the implications this had for South Africa’s labour market ([38]–[39]).

R – Rules

The legal provisions at the center of this decision were:

  1. Section 198A(3)(b) of the LRA, which states that an employee contracted through a TES who earns below a specified threshold and works for a client for more than three months is deemed to be employed by that client ([1], [11]–[12]).
  2. Section 198(2), which defines the employee of a TES and establishes the legal relationship between the TES and the employee during the initial three-month period ([22]–[23]).
  3. Precedents set by earlier rulings in the Labour Courts, including the definition of employment relationships under section 213 of the LRA when dealing with statutory employment ([52]–[56]).

These rules necessitated careful interpretation concerning their implications for labour relations, particularly for workers engaged through TESs, which have historically been subject to scrutiny regarding worker rights and protections.

A – Application

The Constitutional Court, through Justice Dlodlo, analyzed the provisions of the LRA within the statutory framework and historical context. The court rejected Assign’s dual employer interpretation, asserting that the language utilized in section 198A(3)(b) was clear in creating a sole employer relationship after the threshold of three months was met. The use of the term "deemed" did not inherently indicate a retained status of dual employment but signified that the client firmly assumes responsibility as the sole employer for any employment law implications following the trigger of this section ([41]–[84]).

The court emphasized the objective of the 2014 Amendments, which aimed to elevate protections for vulnerable employees and mitigate the risks they face in the precarious employment landscape. The deeming provision, as argued, was not intended to perpetuate dual roles for employer liability but rather to clarify and secure the employment relationship for those who had been employed under a TES structure without longstanding protection ([66]–[70]).

In evaluating the implications, the court recognized that allowing a dual employment interpretation could result in complications regarding liability, clarity of operations, and, ultimately, protections afforded to workers. The amendments were meant to streamline employer responsibilities and bolster employee rights ([74]–[80]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded by affirmatively establishing that the correct interpretation of section 198A(3)(b) is that upon its activation, the employee is deemed solely an employee of the client for purposes of the LRA. As a consequence, the appeal was dismissed, implying that Assign's argument for a dual employer relationship was unfounded under the present statutory context ([83]–[84]).

The ruling has significant implications for the labour market in South Africa, affirming a clear delineation of employer responsibilities while addressing the vulnerabilities faced by placed employees. The ruling upholds the intent of the LRA and enhances the protection of workers within temporary employment gazetted under shifting employment frameworks.

This FIRAC analysis presents an overview of the key components and judicial reasoning of Assign Services (Pty) Limited v National Union of Metalworkers of South Africa and Others, contributing to a deeper understanding of the evolving legal landscape around temporary employment services in South Africa.
