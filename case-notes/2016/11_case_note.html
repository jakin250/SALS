Case Note: Economic Freedom Fighters v Speaker of the National Assembly and Others; Democratic Alliance v Speaker of the National Assembly and Others [2016] ZACC 11

Case Note: Economic Freedom Fighters v Speaker of the National Assembly and Others; Democratic Alliance v Speaker of the National Assembly and Others [2016] ZACC 11

FIRAC Analysis

F – Facts

The case involves two applications submitted by the Economic Freedom Fighters (EFF) and the Democratic Alliance (DA) regarding the conduct of President Jacob Zuma in relation to the Public Protector's findings on the Nkandla upgrades. The Public Protector had determined that the President derived undue benefit from non-security upgrades to his private residence at Nkandla and instructed him to take corrective actions, including reimbursing the state for unsupported expenditures ( [2]-[4], [28], [53]).

The National Assembly failed to act upon these remedial actions by the Public Protector, opting instead to absolve the President of any liability after conducting its own inquiries. This decision persisted for more than a year without compliance with the Public Protector's report ([3]-[12]). Dissatisfied, the EFF sought the Constitutional Court’s exclusive jurisdiction to enforce the remedial actions, while the DA’s application aimed to fortify the enforcement of constitutional accountability ([13]-[14], [24]).

I – Issues

The core legal questions include:

  1. Whether the Constitutional Court has exclusive jurisdiction to determine whether the President and the National Assembly failed to fulfill their constitutional obligations concerning the remedial actions mandated by the Public Protector ([15]-[17]).
  2. The binding nature of the Public Protector's remedial actions and whether the President’s non-compliance constituted a breach of his constitutional obligations ([5]-[14], [39], [40]).
  3. Whether the National Assembly's resolution to relieve the President of liability is constitutional or invalid ([10], [97]-[100]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal principles derive from various provisions of the Constitution of South Africa:

  1. Section 167(4)(e): This grants the Constitutional Court exclusive jurisdiction in matters involving failures by the President or Parliament to fulfill constitutional obligations ([15]-[24]).
  2. Section 182(1)(c): This provision empowers the Public Protector to take appropriate remedial action, which is deemed to be binding ([4], [76]-[82]).
  3. Section 83: Specifies the President's duty to uphold the Constitution, ensuring the effective use of state resources for the public good ([25]-[26]).
  4. Section 181 and Section 55: Establish the accountability of the Executive to the National Assembly, obligating it to oversee executive action meaningfully ([42], [84]-[85]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court analyzed the jurisdictional basis first by establishing that the matter pertains to obligations explicitly placed upon the President in the Constitution. The President’s failure to comply with the remedial action is viewed through the lens of both Section 83(b) and Section 182, highlighting a direct connection between non-compliance and an obligation to uphold constitutional values ([25]-[40]).

The Court determined the binding nature of the Public Protector’s remedial actions. It dismissed arguments suggesting the Public Protector's authority lacked the weight of a judicial officer, holding that her powers are sanctified by constitutional imperative, thereby rendering any defiance of her directives as unlawful ([63]-[74]).

The National Assembly's decision to exonerate the President, instead of facilitating compliance, contravened its constitutional mandate, indicating a failure to uphold the duty of oversight inherent in Sections 42(3) and 55(2) ([86]-[99]). It thus reinforced the principle that actions taken in defiance of the Public Protector’s findings without judicial challenge would be unlawful ([83]-[88]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court held that:

  1. Exclusive Jurisdiction: The Court has the exclusive authority to adjudicate the breaches by the President and the National Assembly ([1]-[2], [12]).
  2. Binding Nature of the Public Protector’s Remedial Action: The remedial actions imposed by the Public Protector are binding; the President's failure to comply with these recommendations constitutes a breach of constitutional duties ([83], [100]).
  3. Invalidation of the National Assembly's Resolution: The resolution by the National Assembly to relieve the President from compliance was deemed unconstitutional and invalid. This reaffirmed the essential oversight role that Parliament has in ensuring accountability within the executive ([10], [101]).

As a result, the Court made orders mandating compliance with the Public Protector's directive, establishing the importance of accountability mechanisms within South African governance and underscoring the integral relationship between constitutional obligations and the rule of law ([105]).

The case sets a significant precedent regarding the binding nature of the Public Protector's role and the accountability of the state organs to uphold constitutional mandates in South Africa. It underscores an evolving understanding of governance principles intertwined with constitutional supremacy and public accountability.
