Case Note: FIRAC Method for Republic of South Africa - Premier: Province of Mpumalanga v Die Hoofbestuur van die Vereniging van Bestuursliggame van Staatsondersteunde Skole: Oos-Transvaal

Supreme Court of Appeal 1998 1995 ([paras 2–3]). The court ordered the appellants to continue paying these bursaries until 31

Case Note: FIRAC Method for Republic of South Africa - Premier: Province of Mpumalanga v Die Hoofbestuur van die Vereniging van Bestuursliggame van Staatsondersteunde Skole: Oos-Transvaal

F – Facts:

The case involves an appeal by the Premier of Mpumalanga and the member of the Executive Council for Education against a judgment rendered on 1 December 1995 by D e Klerk J in the Transvaal Provincial Division. The judgment set aside a decision made by the second appellant (the member of the Executive Council) to discontinue all bursaries for school transport, hostel fees, and compulsory school fees for needy children attending state-aided schools ("Model C schools") in Mpumalanga, with effect from 1 July 1995 ([paras 2–3]). The court ordered the appellants to continue paying these bursaries until 31 December 1995 and directed the second appellant to pay the application costs on an attorney-and-own-client scale ([para 2]).

The bursaries were provided under section 32 of the Education Affairs Act (House of Assembly), 70 of 1988 ([para 3]). The respondent in this matter, the Executive Committee of the Association of Governing Bodies of State Aided Schools (Eastern Transvaal), sought relief under various sections of the Interim Constitution, claiming infringement of rights concerning security and neglect of needy children ([para 4]). They further contended that their members, being parents and children affected by the bursary discontinuation, had a legitimate expectation of consultation before such decisions are made ([paras 5–6]).

The second appellant contested the assertion that his decision infringed upon these constitutional rights, raising several contentions pertaining to procedural fairness and proper administrative action ([para 6]).

I – Issues:

The central legal questions raised in this case involve:

  1. Whether the decision made by the second appellant to discontinue bursaries constituted a violation of the constitutional rights of the affected children and parents under the Interim Constitution ([paras 6–7]).
  2. The determination of the court's jurisdiction to address these constitutional matters, specifically whether the appeal should not be heard in light of section 17 of Schedule 6 of the new Constitution ([paras 7–11]).
  3. Whether the appellants' conduct could be deemed procedurally fair or justifiable within the context of administrative law ([paras 10–15]).

R – Rules:

The legal authorities and provisions applicable to this case include:

  • Interim Constitution Act, 200 of 1993: The pertinent sections include:
  • Section 7(4): Ensuring that any person or association may seek relief in the case of any alleged infringement of fundamental rights ([para 4]).
  • Sections 24(b) and 24(d): Guarantees of procedural fairness and just administrative action ([paras 5, 6]).
  • Section 98(2)(a) and (3): Establishing the jurisdictional boundaries of the Constitutional Court in matters concerning constitutional rights and violations ([para 9]).
  • Sections 101 and 102: These sections relate to the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court and the ability of lower courts to refer matters to the Constitutional Court ([paras 11–13]).

The case law referenced includes Pudolph & Another v Commissioner for Inland Revenue, which examined the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court in relation to common law administrative principles ([para 15]).

A – Application:

In its deliberation, the court reviewed arguments from both sides regarding jurisdiction. Appellants argued the absence of jurisdiction for the Supreme Court to decide on constitutional issues, referencing sections of the Interim Constitution that emphasize exclusivity to the Constitutional Court on such matters ([paras 11–19]). However, the respondent contended that the case could still be adjudicated under common law principles, distinct from the constitutional aspects, thereby claiming the court's jurisdiction ([para 15]).

The court acknowledged the complexity surrounding jurisdictional interpretation under sections 101 and 102 of the Interim Constitution, weighing the interests of justice against potential overlaps in constitutional and non-constitutional issues ([paras 19–20]). Ultimately, the court recognized that any decision on constitutional matters must be approached cautiously to avoid jurisdictional conflicts with the Constitutional Court ([para 20]).

The court also contemplated provisions that might enable the appellants to obtain clarity on their appellate rights and actions regarding the potential interpretations and outcomes from the Constitutional Court ([para 18]).

C – Conclusion:

The Supreme Court of Appeal determined that it would be prudent to postpone the appeal sine die to allow the appellants to approach the Constitutional Court for clarification on jurisdictional concerns. Costs were reserved, and the order permits the respondent to re-enroll the appeal should the appellants not take necessary actions to finalize the matter within stipulated time limits ([para 21]). The decision implies a cautious judicial stance on overlapping jurisdictions and indicates an evolving landscape regarding administrative and constitutional law in South Africa.

This ruling underscores the need for clarity in the jurisdictional boundaries set forth by the Interim Constitution and highlights the proportionate interests of administrative law alongside constitutional protections.

References:

  • Education Affairs Act (House of Assembly), 70 of 1988
  • Constitution of the Republic of South Africa Act, 200 of 1993 (Interim Constitution)
  • Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996 (New Constitution)
  • Pudolph & Another v Commissioner for Inland Revenue, 1996(2) SA 886 (A)
