Constitutional Court

South African National Defence Union v Minister of Defence and Another

Constitutional Court of South Africa, CCT 27/98 Heard on: 25 March 1999 Decided on: 26 May 1999

Constitutional Court 1999 CCT 27/98

Constitutional Court of South Africa, CCT 27/98

Heard on: 25 March 1999
Decided on: 26 May 1999

FIRAC Method Case Note

F – Facts

On 25 November 1998, Hartzenberg J made an order declaring section 126B of the Defence Act, 44 of 1957, unconstitutional and invalid with respect to the prohibition of participation in public protest and joining trade unions by members of the South African National Defence Force (SANDF) ([1]). This landmark case arose when the South African National Defence Union (the applicant) sought the order to confirm Hartzenberg J's decision in the Constitutional Court. The applicant argued that the provision in the Defence Act infringed the rights guaranteed by the Constitution, particularly the rights to freedom of expression and freedom of association ([6], [19]).

The relevant sections in question prohibited not only the membership to trade unions as defined in the Labour Relations Act, but also restricted members from engaging in acts of public protest, under the criminal penalties that would be imposed for violations ([2], [13]). The applicant contended that these provisions severely limited the ability of armed forces members to express dissent on government policies and safeguard their interests as workers ([4], [9]).

I – Issues

The central legal question was whether the prohibitions embedded in section 126B of the Defence Act were constitutional given the protections afforded by the Bill of Rights, particularly the rights to freedom of expression ([6], [9]) and freedom of association ([19], [30]). The Court was tasked with determining if these prohibitions constituted justifiable limitations of these rights in terms of section 36(1) of the Constitution, which allows for reasonable and justifiable limitations within a democratic society ([10], [32]).

R – Rules

The case primarily referred to the following legal authorities and rules:

  1. Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996: Specifically, sections 16 (Freedom of Expression) and 23 (Rights of Workers) ([7], [19]).
  2. Section 36 of the Constitution: Framework for determining if rights may be limited ([10]).
  3. International Labour Organization Conventions: Conventions regarding the rights of workers in the armed forces ([26]).
  4. Previous case law: Various cases interpreting rights to freedom of expression and fair labour practices, including National Media Ltd and Others v Bogoshi ([7]), and international practices regarding military labour relations ([34]).

A – Application

The Court, led by O'Regan J, applied the constitutional principles to the facts of the case:

  1. Freedom of Expression: The Court found that the extensive prohibitions under section 126B(2)-(4) imposed a substantial infringement on the freedom of expression for members of the Defence Force, barring almost all forms of participation in public discourse and assembly ([6], [9], [12]). The definition of "acts of public protest" was deemed excessively broad and clumsy, undermining the clarity required for lawful restrictions on rights ([7], [14]).
  2. Freedom of Association: As for section 126B(1), the Court examined whether members of the Permanent Force qualified as "workers" under section 23 of the Constitution. The Court concluded that they did indeed fall within this classification, asserting their right to form and join trade unions ([20], [30]). The prohibition of union membership starkly contrasted with democratic principles, as it curtailed their constitutional rights without sufficient justification under section 36 ([30], [32]).
  3. Justifiability of Limitation: The Court focused on the importance and scope of the limitation posed by these provisions. It noted that while military discipline is necessary, the blanket ban was disproportionate to any perceived need to maintain order, suggesting that limited rights could be implemented without significantly undermining military discipline ([33], [35]).
  4. Severance and Constitutional Validity: The Court opted for a severance remedy, indicating that specific problematic phrases from section 126B could be struck, while maintaining other crucial prohibitions, which would align with constitutional standards ([15], [41]). This approach allowed the rulings to be constitutional without entirely discarding the legislative intent.

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court confirmed Hartzenberg J’s order with amendments, declaring section 126B(1) unconstitutional and invalid, thus permitting members of the SANDF to join trade unions. The ban on participation in public protests was also deemed unconstitutional, though with a specific approach to severance ([42]).

The judgment emphasized the necessity for the state to balance military discipline with the constitutional rights of individuals ([44]). By allowing these rights, the Court aimed to ensure that military personnel would not be treated as second-class citizens deprived of their rights ([47]). The respondents were ordered to pay the applicant’s costs, thereby underscoring the importance of the recognition and affirmation of constitutional rights within the armed forces ([43]).

For future implications, this case establishes a critical precedent reinforcing the constitutional rights of military personnel, suggesting that excessive prohibitions are not justifiable in a democratic society while setting a framework for appropriate limitations tailored to uphold military discipline without infringing fundamental rights.
