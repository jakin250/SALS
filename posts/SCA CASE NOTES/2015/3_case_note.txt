Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: Deutsches Altersheim Zu Pretoria v Roland Heinrich Dohmen (2015)

## F – Facts
The case concerns an appeal from the North Gauteng High Court regarding the eviction of Roland Heinrich Dohmen from the Deutsches Altersheim Zu Pretoria, a home for the aged ([1]). On 5 March 2007, the appellant (the home) entered into a written agreement with Mr. Dohmen. The agreement stipulated that the home would provide "board and lodging" along with care, in exchange for a monthly fee. The agreement highlighted the obligation of Mr. Dohmen's next-of-kin—specifically his son, Daniël Heinrich Dohmen, and daughter, Margretha Anna Botha—to act as guarantors for the financial obligations ([1]).

As a result of various issues both related to payment and conduct, the home's management issued a notice of termination to Mr. Dohmen on 21 September 2009, which would take effect on 31 October 2009 ([2]). Mr. Dohmen’s refusal to vacate his room led the appellant, on 5 March 2010, to initiate eviction proceedings in the Pretoria Magistrates’ Court under the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 ([2]). An eviction order was granted, which Mr. Dohmen appealed to the North Gauteng High Court, where his appeal succeeded, thus prompting the current appeal ([2]).

As the Supreme Court of Appeal was set to hear the case, it was revealed that Mr. Dohmen had passed away on 12 January 2015. The appeal was effectively rendered moot, sparking disputes over the implications for costs and whether the appeal should proceed ([3][5]).

## I – Issues
The key issues in the case are: 
1. Whether the appeal should be allowed to proceed despite Mr. Dohmen's death rendering the matter moot ([4]).
2. The appropriate allocation of costs now that the appeal is moot and the parties are primarily in dispute over costs incurred before his death ([8][10]).

## R – Rules
The relevant legal principles guiding the court’s decision include:
- The concept of mootness, particularly as outlined in **s 16(2)(a) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013**, which states that appeals may be dismissed if they hold no practical effect ([4]).
- Precedents like *Legal Aid South Africa v Magidiwana* emphasize that when a matter becomes moot, there are no live disputes that the court can adjudicate upon ([8]).
- The law governing costs, as seen in *Eisenstadt v Barone*, holds that a party who withdraws an appeal may be liable for costs incurred until that time, unless it can be demonstrated otherwise ([10]).
- The court has discretion in costs matters, as illustrated through various precedential cases, indicating that costs may not always align with strict traditional guidelines ([11]).

## A – Application
In its determination, the court first recognized the death of Mr. Dohmen rendered the dispute moot. As per the agreement's clause 9, which stated that the contract would terminate upon the lessee's death, the matter became academic with no live dispute or lis between parties remaining ([8]).

The court further evaluated the context of the appeal. Although the appellant sought to emphasize the public interest in addressing the legal principles established by the High Court, the judgment was found not to fall within the category of cases where merits may be adjudicated post-mootness. It was determined that the need for a decision did not subsist after Mr. Dohmen’s death ([7][8]).

On the question of costs, the court observed that while the appellant had initiated and maintained the appeal, its insistence on pursuing the matter after the death was unwarranted, particularly given their acknowledgment of mootness. In light of this, the court recognized that the respondents were justified in their need to defend against the appellant's claims ([12]).

Thus, the court concluded that two-thirds of the costs incurred up to the date of notice about mootness would be the burden of the appellant, as they had been the dominus litis, and one-third would be borne by the respondents, recognizing their part in prolonging the proceedings ([13]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately struck the appeal from the roll, signifying an acknowledgment of mootness stemming from Mr. Dohmen’s death ([9]). The court ordered the appellant to pay two-thirds of the respondents’ costs. This ruling underscores the importance of clarity in contractual obligations concerning eviction and illustrates how mootness shapes the extent of appellate jurisdiction. The case also reiterates the court's discretion over costs and establishes a precedent for how parties’ conduct can significantly impact cost responsibilities in moot proceedings ([13]).
