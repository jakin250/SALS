# FIRAC Case Note: Nong and Masingi v The State (787/2021) [2024] ZASCA 25

## F – Facts 
In this case, the appellants, Sello Nong and Thomas Masingi, were convicted in the Regional Court of Johannesburg on a charge of robbery with aggravating circumstances under the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 ([para 1]). Nong received a 12-year sentence while Masingi was sentenced to 15 years imprisonment. Following their conviction, the regional magistrate denied their request for leave to appeal against the conviction, although it granted leave concerning the sentence ([para 1]).

Subsequently, the appellants filed a petition for leave to appeal their conviction pursuant to section 309C of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977. This petition was dismissed by the Gauteng Division of the High Court ([para 2]). They subsequently sought special leave to appeal against the dismissal of their petitions, which was ultimately granted by the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 3]). 

The primary incident that led to their conviction involved a robbery at gunpoint. The complainant, Kwanele Arnold Dube, testified that he was robbed by two individuals, believed to be Nong and Masingi, one of whom brandished a toy gun ([paras 8, 9]). Dube managed to follow the assailants and sought the assistance of a colleague from the Community Policing Forum, Simphiwe John Mthembu, who helped apprehend the appellants ([para 9]).

## I – Issues
The key legal issue here revolves around whether the High Court erred in refusing the appellants leave to appeal their conviction based on their claim of reasonable prospects of success on appeal ([paras 6, 17]). The court also needed to consider the implications of the standard of proof required for appeal in criminal matters and the reliability of the identification evidence presented by the complainant and corroborated by Simphiwe and Constable Ndaonde ([paras 10, 13]).

## R – Rules
The court relied on specific statutory provisions and case law to determine the bar for reasonable prospects of success on appeal:

1. **Legal Framework**: Section 16(1)(b) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 prescribes that applications for leave to appeal must be based on the existence of reasonable prospects of success ([para 3]).
   
2. **Test for Reasonable Prospects**: Plasket AJA articulated the test for reasonable prospects of success, stating that it requires a court to make a dispassionate assessment of the facts and law, aiming for a determination where a court of appeal could reach a conclusion different from that of the trial court ([para 7]).

3. **Identification Evidence**: The court cited precedents such as *S v Mthetwa* and *S v Ooshuizen*, indicating that identification evidence must be carefully assessed for reliability, especially when it comes from a single eyewitness. It is outlined that a conviction can stand even on the evidence of a single witness, provided the witness is deemed credible ([paras 10, 11, 12]).

## A – Application
The court evaluated whether the High Court misapplied the principles surrounding the granting of leave to appeal against the conviction. The appellants contended that the trial court overlooked critical factors in its assessment of the complainant's identification reliability due to the incident's rapid nature and the apparent lack of opportunity for proper observation ([para 10]). 

However, the court noted that Dube's testimony was corroborated by Simphiwe and Constable Ndaonde, which lent weight to the complainant's account of the events ([para 12]). The reliability of the identification was deemed supported by the proximity during the incident and the corroborative evidence of the weapon (a toy gun) found in the possession of the appellants ([paras 14, 16]).

Ultimately, the court rejected the appellants' claim that there were reasonable prospects of success. It highlighted that their testimony did not substantially differ from that of the complainant and did not undermine the foundational evidence presented during the trial. Furthermore, the court found no misdirection on the part of the High Court in its prior decision to deny the appeal ([paras 16, 17]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the dismissal of the appellants' petition for leave to appeal against their conviction. The ruling affirmed that the appellants failed to demonstrate any reasonable prospects of success that would warrant an appeal ([para 18]). Consequently, the implications of this case reinforce the substantial weight placed on corroborated identification evidence, particularly in aggravated robbery convictions, while simultaneously confirming the procedural limitations surrounding appeals from the magistrate’s court to the higher courts in South Africa.
