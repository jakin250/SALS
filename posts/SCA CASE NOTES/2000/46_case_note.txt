# FIRAC Case Note

### Case Title: **Purser v Sales**  
**Case Nos:** 613/98, 614/98  
**Coram:** Hefer ADCJ, Marais, Zulman JJA, Mpati and Mthiyane AJJA  
**Heard:** 12 September 2000  
**Delivered:** 28 September 2000  

---

### **F – Facts**

The present appeals arise from two provisional sentence summonses issued in the Transvaal Provincial Division of the High Court of South Africa. In the first matter, **Alan Edward Sales** (the respondent), a British national residing in the UK, initiated proceedings against **Frank Albert William Purser** (the first appellant) for payment of £150,339.16 plus interest, based on a judgment rendered on 3 February 1997 by the Central London County Court ([para 1]). The second appeal involved sales issued against both Purser and his spouse, Jeanne Patricia Purser, for a sum arising from another judgment from the same court ([paras 1–2]).

The core dispute hinged on whether the English court possessed jurisdiction over the appellants, wherein they claimed that they were no longer residents of the United Kingdom at the commencement of proceedings in January 1989 ([para 8]). The appellants maintained that no contractual agreement provided for jurisdiction, and they had not submitted themselves to the jurisdiction of the English court ([para 9]). 

The court a quo established that the English court had jurisdiction as per its own legal standards, which were valid and appropriate as it was common ground that the agreement was entered within its jurisdiction ([para 10–11]). Ultimately, the court ruled that the appellants had indeed submitted to the jurisdiction of the foreign court as they did not objection during proceedings ([para 3]). 

### **I – Issues**

The pivotal legal questions addressed in this case were:
1. Whether the Central London County Court had competent jurisdiction over the appellants to enforce its judgments ([para 2]).
2. Whether the appellants' failure to object to the jurisdiction of the foreign court constituted submission to that jurisdiction, hence rendering the judgments enforceable in South Africa ([para 2]).

### **R – Rules**

The court relied on several legal principles regarding the recognition and enforcement of foreign judgments, particularly highlighting the relevant statutes and case law:
1. Enforcement of a foreign judgment in South Africa is conditional on the foreign court exercising jurisdiction recognized by South African law ([paras 11–12]).
2. The legal framework established in **Jones v Krok** stipulates that to enforce a foreign judgment, certain criteria must be met, including the foreign court having jurisdiction by the principles acknowledged by South African law ([para 11]).
3. As noted in **Mediterranean Shipping Co v Speedwell Shipping Co Ltd**, submission to jurisdiction can occur through conduct or agreements, and failure to object to jurisdiction can lead to an inference of consent ([para 13]).

Further, the court examined the specific procedural rules applicable under English law, particularly **Order 11** concerning the service of proceedings out of jurisdiction ([para 9]).

### **A – Application**

In applying the legal rules to the facts:
- The court found that while the respondent succeeded in obtaining the leave of the English court to serve documents in South Africa, the jurisdictional challenge rested on whether the appellants had effectively waived their rights by failing to object ([para 12]).
- The court highlighted the appellants' participation in the foreign proceedings, noting their acknowledgment of a defense without raising jurisdictional objections, which implied acceptance of the English court's jurisdiction ([para 14]).
- The appellants contended they had not signed the defense nor were informed of their right to object to jurisdiction, but the court dismissed these arguments, asserting they had legal representation and understood the proceedings' context ([para 20])—indicating that claims of lack of knowledge did not exonerate them from the implications of their actions ([para 22]).
- The court referenced the principles articulated in **Lubbe v Bosman**, stating that joining the merits without raising an objection leads to a deemed waiver of jurisdiction ([para 16]).

Ultimately, the court established that the appellants' decision to participate in the English court proceedings, even absent an express objection, constituted a form of tacit submission to the jurisdiction ([para 22]).

### **C – Conclusion**

The court dismissed the appeals, affirming the ruling of the court a quo that the appellants had submitted to the jurisdiction of the English court, thereby recognizing the validity of the foreign judgments and enforcing them in South Africa. This case reinforces the principles of jurisdiction and submission, emphasizing that failure to object can lead to significant legal repercussions. As such, parties must be cognizant of jurisdictional implications when engaging in cross-border legal disputes ([para 22]).

The implications of this ruling extend beyond the parties involved, as it clarifies the extent of submission doctrine in South African law and establishes the critical nature of jurisdictional objections in foreign judgments. The rulings serve as a reference point for future litigants and legal practitioners in similar circumstances concerning jurisdictional challenges and the enforcement of foreign court judgments.
