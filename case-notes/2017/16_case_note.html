FIRAC Case Note: Genesis Medical Scheme v Registrar of Medical Schemes and Another [2017] ZACC 16

Constitutional Court 2017 [2017] ZACC 16 #### F – Facts Genesis Medical Scheme (Genesi

FIRAC Case Note: Genesis Medical Scheme v Registrar of Medical Schemes and Another [2017] ZACC 16

F – Facts

Genesis Medical Scheme (Genesis) is a registered medical scheme governed by the Medical Schemes Act 131 of 1998 (MSA). In June 2013, the Registrar of Medical Schemes rejected Genesis's annual financial statements. The rejection was primarily based on alleged non-compliance with certain statutory provisions, specifically relating to the financial representation of the scheme's personal medical savings accounts (PMSAs) as liabilities rather than trust funds, following precedents set by the High Court in Registrar of Medical Schemes v Ledwaba NO (Omnihealth case). Genesis subsequently applied for judicial review of the Registrar’s decision, arguing it was materially influenced by an error of law ([1]–[3]).

The High Court ruled in favor of Genesis, declaring the Registry's decision unconstitutional, asserting that the PMSAs were the scheme's assets, not trust property. The ruling was appealed, and the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the Registrar's position, determining that PMSAs did constitute trust property ([3]–[4], [13]).

I – Issues

The case raised significant legal questions regarding:

  1. Whether PMSA funds were trust property under the Financial Institutions (Protection of Funds) Act (FIA), consequently to be treated as liabilities by Genesis.
  2. Whether the Registrar's rejection of Genesis's financial documents constituted an error of law that warranted judicial intervention ([1], [8], [20]).

R – Rules

The central statutory framework involved:

  • *Medical Schemes Act, 1998*: Sections 24, 30(1)(e), 35, and 38 outline conditions for medical schemes regarding deadlines for financial submissions and statutory obligations concerning financial soundness.
  • *Financial Institutions (Protection of Funds) Act, 2001*: Defines "trust property" and stipulates that such assets must be kept separate from an institution's assets, which affects how PMSAs are accounted for.
  • The Omnihealth judgment, which established precedent that PMSA funds should be deemed trust property, significantly influenced the reasoning employed by the Registrar and the courts ([7], [10], [26]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court analyzed the statutory provisions, focusing on:

  • The interpretation of the MSA and its implications for the fiduciary relationship between a medical scheme and its members. The court noted that PMSA funds should be recognized as liabilities but determined that professionally accrued interest on those funds could be retained by Genesis ([35]–[50]).
  • The court adopted a pragmatic view of the ongoing consequences that labeling PMSA funds as ‘trust property’ could impose on both constitutional governance and practical financial regulation ([55]–[59]).

In reviewing the Registrar’s rejection of Genesis's financial statements, the Constitutional Court found that the decision had indeed relied heavily on the flawed interpretation of the Omnihealth ruling. Furthermore, the majority opinion emphasized that a misclassification of PMSA funds could create unsustainable accounting demands, impacting the solvency of medical schemes this misclassification would create ([66]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court reversed the Supreme Court of Appeal's ruling, reinstating the High Court's declaration. It concluded that PMSAs, despite being labeled as liabilities in financial statements, do not constitute trust property, hence Genesis retains rights over such funds. The conflicting interpretations of the law, particularly stemming from Omnihealth, rendered the Registrar’s rejection invalid due to the lack of due consideration afforded to factual circumstances of the PMSAs ([61]–[64]). The decision underscores the necessity for clarity in legislative interpretation vis-à-vis administrative procedural adherence within the domain of financial health and member protections in medical schemes ([54]–[56]).

This case thus signifies a critical analysis of legal definitions and their implications on administrative processes, financial reporting, and stakeholder rights within medical schemes in South Africa.
