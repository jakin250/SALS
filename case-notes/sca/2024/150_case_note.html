Merryweather v Scholtz and Another (447/2023) [2024] ZASCA 150

Initially, Andrew and Nicholas instituted a civil action for damages against Oliver and his co-defendants following a criminal trial where Oliver, Joel Thackwray, and Liam Hechter were acquitted of charges related to the altercation ([5]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 150

F - Facts

This case pertains to an appeal lodged by Andrew Merryweather (the Appellant) against the dismissal of his delictual claim by the Full Court of the Western Cape Division of the High Court ([1]). The case originated from a troubling incident that occurred on 9 September 2006, when Andrew, along with his brother Nicholas and their friend Progress, became embroiled in a physical altercation with another group of young men, including Oliver Scholtz (the First Respondent) and his father, Gerard David Peter Scholtz (the Second Respondent). Andrew sustained severe injuries – a compression flexion type V fracture of the cervical spine – resulting in permanent partial paralysis and a reliance on a wheelchair ([4]).

Initially, Andrew and Nicholas instituted a civil action for damages against Oliver and his co-defendants following a criminal trial where Oliver, Joel Thackwray, and Liam Hechter were acquitted of charges related to the altercation ([5]). A default judgment was granted against Oliver but was subsequently set aside. During the trial, relying on precedents such as Mabaso v Felix, the trial court ruled that, since Oliver claimed self-defence, he bore the burden of proof regarding this defence's justification ([7]). Despite succeeding at the trial level on his claim against Oliver, Andrew was unsuccessful in seeking costs against Gerard, who was held to lack liability due to the absence of sufficient evidence ([1]).

The Full Court overturned the trial court's decision, asserting that Andrew had failed to substantiate his claim against Oliver with adequate evidence and establishing that Oliver's actions were proportionate in the context of self-defence ([2]). The court noted discrepancies in the accounts from witnesses and found that the trial court had improperly relied on inadmissible evidence, which affected its assessment of the overall merits of the case ([32]).

I - Issues

The pivotal legal issues in this appeal revolve around 1) the correctness of the Full Court's conclusion to dismiss Andrew's claim based on the alleged inadmissibility of evidence and perceived insufficient establishment of key factual elements regarding the attack, and 2) whether the appellate court should exercise its discretion to grant leave to appeal, assessing the presence of special circumstances warranting such a review ([3], [41]). The nature of the self-defence argument and the appropriate burden of proof remain central questions, alongside a cursory examination of the evidence base upon which the claims are asserted ([39]).

R - Rules

The legal framework governing this case includes principles of delictual liability, specifically the necessity for a claimant to prove each element of their claim, including the elements of fault (intention or negligence) and the causation of harm ([10]). The shift of the onus of proof in self-defence scenarios as established in Mabaso v Felix, which signifies that the party claiming self-defence must substantiate this claim, significantly influenced the rulings in both the trial and appellate courts ([7]). Furthermore, issues regarding the admissibility of evidence cited the Law of Evidence Amendment Act, particularly sections concerning the treatment of hearsay evidence and its provisional acceptance under certain conditions ([31]).

The judicial reasoning referenced precedents such as Westinghouse Brake and Equipment (Pty) Ltd v Bilger Engineering (Pty) Ltd, which encapsulate the standards for granting leave to appeal and the need for the presence of special circumstances, not merely a possibility of success ([5], [39]). The Full Court’s assessment included a comprehensive evaluation of witness credibility, the weight of conflicting narratives presented, and overarching theories of self-defence as they applied in the factual context ([33], [36]).

A - Application

In applying these principles, the Supreme Court of Appeal underscored that Andrew bore the burden of proving the specifics of his claim against Oliver, notably that Oliver employed excessive force exceeding what was reasonable in self-defence ([10]). The Full Court's analysis found critical inconsistencies in the testimonies, particularly around the nature of the altercation itself, the precise actions engaged by Oliver, and the contextual underpinnings of self-defence claimed ([20], [33]).

The reliance of the trial court on inadmissible evidence—specifically an affidavit from a witness who had not been called to testify—led to a finding of material irregularity, influencing the outcome of the case ([32]). The rejection of this evidence by the Full Court necessitated a reassessment of Andrew's claim, establishing that the overwhelming majority of evidence did not substantiate his allegations against Oliver ([34]).

In considering the prerequisite for special circumstances to warrant appeal, the court articulated that an appeal must transcend mere issues of factual dispute and must engage with substantial points of law or issues of significant public interest ([38]). The court concluded that Andrew's counsel had failed to establish such grounds, ultimately determining that the factual discrepancies and credibility issues did not merit the higher court's intervention and that Andrew’s appeal had no reasonable prospects of success ([40], [41]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that both the appeal and the cross-appeal were devoid of merit and consequently struck them from the roll with costs. The court firmly established that the Full Court's conclusion – that Andrew had not met the necessary burden of proof concerning his allegations against Oliver and that there were no special circumstances justifying further review – was sound ([42]).

This case reinforces the rigorous standards required in delictual claims, particularly concerning the evidentiary burdens placed upon claimants alleging self-defence. Furthermore, it illustrates the critical nature of admissibility and the effect of procedural irregularities upon the outcomes of civil trials in South African law. The judgment serves as a poignant reminder of the challenges that arise when factual disputes underpin legal claims, emphasizing the judiciary’s role in meticulously assessing credibility and the weight of evidence presented in court.
