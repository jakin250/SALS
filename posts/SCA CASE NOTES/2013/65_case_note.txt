Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: Topham v Member of Executive Committee for the Department of Health, Mpumalanga

**Neutral Citation**: Topham v Member of Executive Committee for the Department of Health, Mpumalanga (351/2012) [2013] ZASCA 65 (27 May 2013)

**Coram**: Mpati P, Brand and Cachalia JJA, Van der Merwe and Meyer AJJA

**Heard**: 10 May 2013  
**Delivered**: 27 May 2013

---

## F – Facts

The appellant, Mrs. Doreen Topham, engaged in a motor vehicle accident on 1 May 2006, which resulted in a right hip dislocation ([paras 1–3]). Following her admission to Rob Ferreira Hospital in Nelspruit, the respondent (the Member of the Executive Committee for the Department of Health, Mpumalanga) was responsible for the actions of the medical staff due to vicarious liability. Dr. Molete, an intern at the time, failed to diagnose the dislocated hip despite the appellant presenting with severe pain ([paras 3–4]). After being discharged, the appellant sought further treatment, and a diagnosis of her condition was confirmed on 9 May 2006 ([paras 3–4]).

The appellant claimed damages, asserting that Dr. Molete’s negligence in misdiagnosing her condition caused her to suffer from avascular necrosis of the femur head, leading to potential long-term health consequences ([paras 3, 4, 22]). The trial court, however, dismissed her claim, concluding that Dr. Molete had acted within the acceptable standard of care owing to the circumstances at hand ([para 4]). 

## I – Issues

Key legal questions arose regarding: 

1. Whether Dr. Molete’s actions constituted negligence under the circumstances, failing to adhere to the required standard of care expected from medical professionals when diagnosing trauma-related injuries ([paras 1, 22]).
2. The relationship between the alleged negligence (failure to diagnose) and the consequential injury (avascular necrosis) suffered by the appellant ([paras 22–23]).

## R – Rules

The case revolved around principles of medical negligence encapsulated in established precedents such as *Mitchell v Dixon* and *Van Wyk v Lewis* that outline the standard of care expected from medical practitioners ([para 6]). The legal principles governing causation focus on two distinct inquiries:
1. Factual causation evaluated through the "but for" test.
2. Legal causation, also known as remoteness of damage ([para 22]).

Furthermore, a rigorous clinical examination is mandated in cases of trauma, especially given the potential for serious complications like avascular necrosis following a hip dislocation ([paras 20, 21]).

## A – Application

The court critically evaluated Dr. Molete's adherence to standard medical procedures and the adequacy of his diagnostic methods. Evidence presented by the appellant’s expert witnesses, including Drs Van der Westhuizen and Kaiser, underscored the inadequacies in Dr. Molete's examination. They affirmed that the clinical signs of a hip dislocation should have been evident, necessitating further investigative steps beyond the inconclusive x-rays that were carried out ([paras 17, 18, 20]).

The court also highlighted Dr. Molete’s failure to follow through on his initial findings of severe pain and his reliance on an inferior quality x-ray, which he recognized had no diagnostic value.

In light of established medical norms and the expectations placed upon trauma physicians, the court concluded that Dr. Molete fell significantly short of the requisite standard of care. It determined that had he conducted a proper examination or adopted further diagnostic measures, the dislocation would likely have been identified, thereby preventing the subsequent complications faced by the appellant ([paras 21, 22]).

The court reaffirmed that the appellant successfully demonstrated both factual and legal causation linking Dr. Molete’s negligence to her injuries, refuting the trial court’s dismissal ([paras 22–23]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal found the trial court's decision untenable, thus overturning the dismissal of Mrs. Topham's claim against the Department of Health. The court concluded that Dr. Molete’s negligence in failing to properly diagnose the right hip dislocation led to significant health implications, specifically avascular necrosis of the femur head. Consequently, the respondent was held liable for damages resulting from this negligence. 

The court's judgment highlighted the crucial role of diligence and adherence to medical standards among professionals and the implications of failing to meet these standards not only for patient outcomes but also in terms of legal accountability ([paras 1, 24]). 

The final order mandated the respondent to pay damages along with costs, reflecting not only the resources expended by the appellant but also the necessity for thorough medical care in traumatic injury cases to uphold justice and equitable outcomes.
