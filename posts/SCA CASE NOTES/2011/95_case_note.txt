Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: Putco (Pty) Ltd v W M Mosholi (577/2010) [2011] ZASCA 95

## F – Facts

The case concerns Putco (Pty) Ltd (the appellant), which was held liable for damages exceeding R25,000 due to injuries suffered by Ms. Winnie Mina Mosholi (the respondent) during a motor vehicle collision [1]. The incident involved a bus operated by the appellant and a bakkie, where the respondent, a fare-paying passenger, sustained injuries. The trial court, presided over by Van den Heever AJ at the North Gauteng High Court (Pretoria), determined the trial on the issue of liability only. The issue of damages was to be adjudicated later [1].

The bus collided with the bakkie, leading to significant injuries and fatalities among passengers [4]. The trial court evaluated the credibility of witness testimonies from both parties, concluding that the evidence provided by Fernando Manuel, a passenger in the bakkie, was more believable than that of Stephen Seloane, the bus driver. Consequently, the trial judge attributed sole negligence for the collision to Seloane [2-3].

Furthermore, it was established that at the time of the accident, Seloane was acting within the course of his employment, effectively linking his actions to the appellant, hence implicating company liability for the damages claimed [4]. The Road Accident Fund (RAF) had previously settled the respondent's claim, paying R25,000, which was the statutory limit for passengers according to the Road Accident Fund Act, 56 of 1996 [4].

## I – Issues

The central legal issue for determination by the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether there were sufficient grounds to disturb the trial court's findings on the credibility of witnesses and the assessment of liability for the accident [6-7]. Specifically, the issues evolved around the following points:
1. Was the trial court justified in concluding that Seloane's negligence was the exclusive cause of the collision?
2. Did the trial court properly assess the credibility of the witnesses, particularly that of Mr. Fernando Manuel versus Mr. Stephen Seloane?
3. Was the RAF correctly found not to be a joint wrongdoer concerning the apportionment of damages?

## R – Rules

The legal framework for this case primarily revolves around principles of tort law, particularly those relating to negligence and liability, as well as the application of the Apportionment of Damages Act 34 of 1956. Sections of this Act outline the rules concerning joint wrongdoers and the grounds for apportioning damages [3].

Case law relevant to the trial court's credibility assessments, such as the standard applied in evidentiary evaluations (see S v Mafaladiso & andere 2003 (1) SACR 83 (SCA)), where the court underscored the importance of the trial judge's discretion in determining weight and credibility of witness testimony [7]. The decision in Mvumvu v Minister for Transport 2011 (2) SA 473 (CC) also plays a role, defining statutory limitations associated with claims under the RAF [4].

## A – Application

In applying the law to the facts, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the trial court's findings, emphasizing that the assessment of credibility should not be overturned lightly. The judgment reinforced the trial judge’s meticulous reasoning, highlighting his analysis of Seloane’s testimony, which demonstrated inconsistencies and inadequacies [6-7]. 

The court noted that Seloane could not adequately explain critical aspects of his driving leading up to the collision, such as whether he had flashed his vehicle's lights or braked before the bakkie crossed his path. The discrepancies in Seloane's accounts, paired with the credible testimony from Manuel, lent substantial weight to the trial judge's conclusion regarding Seloane's negligence as the sole cause of the accident [5-6].

Furthermore, the court evaluated the argument put forth regarding the RAF's status as a potential joint wrongdoer, noting that the trial judge's conclusions were consistent with the provisions of the Apportionment of Damages Act [3]. The absence of ambiguity in witness statements corroborated the trial court’s exclusive finding against Seloane.

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court's order that Seloane's negligence was the sole cause of the collision and that the RAF was not a joint wrongdoer under the terms of the Apportionment of Damages Act. The implications of this ruling serve to reinforce the principles regarding the credibility of witnesses and the significance of the trial court’s discretion in evaluating reliability and consistency in testimonies [8].

The judgment stands as an affirmation of lower court findings in negligence claims, emphasizing that credibility assessments are a crucial component of determining liability in traffic accidents. The firm legal footing regarding the exclusivity of fault placed upon Seloane also imparts crucial lessons for transport companies about their liabilities when employees act within the scope of their employment. 

In summary, the findings of the Supreme Court of Appeal were rooted firmly in the detailed evaluations conducted by the trial court, honoring the principle that a trial judge's credibility assessments are seldom to be disturbed. The dismissal of the appeal signifies a clear objective in safeguarding victim rights in negligence claims arising from road traffic incidents, consistent with prevailing tort law principles in South Africa.
