Supreme Court of Appeal

Consortium Consolidated Corporation (Pty) Ltd v Low N.O. (Case No: 384/96)

The case revolves around an agreement known as the "Swop Deal" between the deceased, William Andrew Low, and Consortium Consolidated Corporation (Pty) Ltd (the respondent).

Supreme Court of Appeal 1998

F – Facts

The case revolves around an agreement known as the "Swop Deal" between the deceased, William Andrew Low, and Consortium Consolidated Corporation (Pty) Ltd (the respondent). The appellants, Donald John Low, Elsie Edith Low, and Mara Odette Warren (the executors of the deceased's estate), sought to compel the respondent to deliver certain gold coins which had originally belonged to the deceased ([para 1]). The agreement, which was partly written and partly oral, established that:

  1. The deceased would deliver specific gold coins to the respondent.
  2. The value of these coins was agreed to be R732,500.
  3. The respondent would procure and deliver other coins (the "exchange coins") to the deceased ([para 2]).

The appellants claimed that the respondent had breached this agreement by failing to purchase and deliver the exchange coins, leading them to cancel the agreement and seek either the return of the deceased’s coins or the monetary equivalent ([para 4]). The trial court, however, ruled in favor of the respondent, denying the appellants' claims and asserting that the respondent had indeed delivered the exchange coins to the deceased ([para 8]).

I – Issues

The central legal issues in this case include:

  1. Whether the respondent failed to deliver the exchange coins to the deceased as stipulated in the contract ([para 10]).
  2. The burden of proof regarding the delivery of the coins—whether it rested on the appellants to demonstrate non-delivery or on the respondent to prove delivery ([para 10]).
  3. The applicability of a legal rule requiring cautious scrutiny of evidence from a party resisting a claim from a deceased estate ([para 12]).

R – Rules

The court relied on several legal principles and precedents:

  1. The rule that the burden of proof in civil matters lies with the party alleging a fact ([para 9]).
  2. The cautious approach established in Borcherds v Estate Naidoo ([1955] (3) SA 78 (A)), which requires a court to evaluate evidence given against a deceased estate with heightened scrutiny due to the deceased's inability to testify or provide contrary evidence ([para 12]).
  3. Judicial insights regarding the nature of contracts, specifically about breaches of contract and the terms implied therein (such as time being of the essence) ([para 11]).

A – Application

In the appeal, the court critically analyzed the testimony of both parties. The appellants contended that the trial judge erred in placing the burden of proof on them to demonstrate that the respondent had not delivered the exchange coins. They argued that the respondent, possessing the exclusive knowledge of the delivery, should have bees required to prove that delivery occurred ([para 10]).

Conversely, the respondent maintained that it did deliver the coins, supported by the testimony of Mark Andersen, the sole shareholder and executive director of the company. Andersen asserted that the deceased had personally checked and accepted the coins ([para 7]). The trial judge found Andersen's evidence credible, stating that even had he believed the onus was on the respondent, his conclusion about delivery would not have changed ([para 9]).

The court evaluated the character traits of the deceased, noting his secretive nature and history of eccentric behavior, which may account for the missing coins ([para 14]). Ultimately, the court found that the evidence provided by Andersen, coupled with the deceased's documented dealings with the respondent, was sufficient to establish that the exchange coins had been properly delivered to the deceased ([para 15]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal, affirming the trial court's decision, concluded that the appellants had not met their burden of proof to show non-delivery. Consequently, the appeal was dismissed with costs, establishing that the crucial question of delivery had been satisfactorily resolved in favor of the respondent based on credible testimony. This case underscores the importance of both parties' presentation of evidence in contractual disputes, especially when one of the parties is deceased, and reaffirms the legal principle that suspicion must be applied cautiously in assessing evidence from parties who alone bear the knowledge of transactions involving a deceased estate ([para 15]).

Court’s order: Appeal dismissed with costs.
