# FIRAC Case Note: Macingwane v Masekwameng and Others [2022] ZASCA 174

## F - Facts 
[1] The case centers on the National African Federated Chamber of Commerce and Industry (NAFCOC), which held a critical meeting on 31 July 2019, resulting in the dismissal of Sabelo Vusumzi Macingwane, the appellant and then-President of NAFCOC, following a motion of no confidence. The meeting was convened by Isaac Ntshireletsa Masekwameng, the first respondent, who acted as the National Chairperson of Provinces, citing a motion backed by two-thirds of Council members. Aggrieved by this action, Macingwane approached the Gauteng Division of the High Court, seeking to have the meeting deemed unlawfully convened and the resolutions taken at that meeting declared invalid [2].

The appellant's contention rested on two primary arguments: first, that the first respondent lacked the authority to convene such a meeting as the President alone held that power, and second, that the meeting contravened a previous High Court order [3]. The High Court dismissed Macingwane's application but granted him leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal [3].

## I - Issues 
[4] The central legal issue presented before the Court was whether the head office meeting held on 31 July 2019 was lawfully convened, thereby validating the resolutions taken therein. The resolution's legality hinged on the interpretation of specific clauses within the NAFCOC Constitution, particularly clauses 28.4.4 and 23.3, regarding meeting conventions and presidential removal processes [4].

## R - Rules 
[5] The rules governing this case were drawn from the NAFCOC Constitution and relevant legal principles pertaining to the interpretation of constitutions of voluntary associations. Clause 28.4.4 outlines the authority to convene meetings as vested in either the President or the National Chairperson of Provinces, while clause 23.3 sets forth the procedural requirement for presidential removal by a two-thirds majority at a specially convened meeting [5].

The court also referred to established rules of statutory interpretation, particularly from cases such as *Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality* [2012] ZASCA 13, emphasizing the importance of interpreting provisions based on their ordinary grammatical meanings unless such interpretations result in absurdities [21]. 

## A - Application 
[6] The Court held that the interpretation of clause 28.4.4 did not support the appellant's hierarchical approach which restricted the convention of meetings solely to the President or the Deputy President. The appellant argued that this clause suggested that only the President had empowering authority to call meetings unless unable or absent. The Court, however, found this interpretation would lead to an “insensible and unbusinesslike outcome” and was contrary to the plain language of the Constitution, highlighting the necessity of a collaborative approach amongst senior officials [24–26].

The appellant’s interpretation was assessed against the context of the NAFCOC Constitution and its purpose, which aimed to prevent past issues of the President neglecting to call meetings. The amendments in clause 28.4.4 expressly allowed a broader array of officials to convene meetings, effectively ensuring that the Council could function independently of any single executive’s whim. The Court opined that the clause's amendment aimed to enable quick responses to governance challenges without being bottlenecked by the President [29–33].

The Court ultimately agreed with the respondents’ interpretation, finding that the meeting was validly convened and that the removal of Macingwane adhered to organizational procedures as laid out in the Constitution [34]. 

## C - Conclusion 
[7] The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, confirming that the head office meeting was lawfully convened, and all resolutions adopted during that meeting remained in effect. This ruling underscores the principle that the interpretation of voluntary association constitutions should prioritize their explicit language and intent, favoring hierarchical inefficiencies and ambiguities that might lead to governance stagnation. The implications of this ruling reinforce the importance of proper procedural adherence within voluntary organizations and the need for clear constitutional governance structures to uphold democratic processes, thereby impacting similar scenarios in other voluntary associations [38].
