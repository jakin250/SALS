Source Folder: 2003

# FIRAC Case Note: Alexkor Ltd v The Richtersveld Community

### F – Facts
The appeal in **Alexkor Ltd v The Richtersveld Community** arose from a dispute regarding land restitution as per the **Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994**. The Richtersveld Community claimed that they had been unjustly dispossessed of their land—particularly a strip along the west coast of South Africa—due to past racially discriminatory practices. The Land Claims Court (LCC) dismissed this claim, primarily finding that while the community was recognized as a "community" for legal purposes, they did not prove their dispossession was legally actionable under discriminatory laws ([paras 1, 7, 8]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), however, overturned this ruling, establishing that the Richtersveld Community had maintained rights akin to ownership, including the right to exploit the land’s mineral resources, prior to a series of colonial actions that effectively dispossessed them beginning in the 1920s, notably following the discovery of diamonds. The SCA described these actions as amounting to racially discriminatory practices ([paras 9, 93]), which warranted restitution by the state, including Alexkor Ltd—an entity involved in diamond mining and wholly owned by the government ([paras 4, 6]).

### I – Issues
The main issues considered by the Constitutional Court included:
1. Whether the Richtersveld Community qualified as a "community" under the Act.
2. If the Community held established rights in land prior to British annexation.
3. The nature of the rights in land post-annexation and the legal consequences of such actions.
4. Whether the dispossession after 19 June 1913 was the result of racially discriminatory laws or practices ([paras 18-20, 92]).

### R – Rules
The Court relied on several statutory provisions:
- **Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994**: Particularly sections that recognize the entitlement to restitution for communities dispossessed after June 19, 1913 due to racially discriminatory practices ([para 6]).
- **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996**: Section 25(7), which echoes similar entitlements regarding land restitution ([paras 22, 37]).
- Precedent from other court cases indicating that the acquisition of sovereignty does not automatically extinguish indigenous land rights, but requires evaluation of existing rights and relevant legal practices ([paras 66, 68, 72]).

### A – Application
The Court analyzed the facts against these legal frameworks, emphasizing that the Richtersveld Community retained customary land rights under indigenous law prior to British annexation. The determination of whether their ownership was extinguished following annexation hinged on whether British legislative actions recognized or annulled these rights. The Community's historical usage and occupation of the land demonstrated traditional principles that supported their claim ([paras 60-62]).

The Court concluded that the legislation that followed the discovery of diamonds in the 1920s was discriminatory, as it failed to acknowledge or protect the Community’s rights under indigenous law while favoring registered landowners, typically white citizens. This failing to accord equal rights was found to be racially discriminatory and led to the dispossession of the Community ([paras 92-100]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court held that the Richtersveld Community was entitled to restitution, including ownership of the subject land and its resources. The degree of discrimination evidenced by state laws and policies substantively affected ownership rights, thus fulfilling criteria for restitution ([paras 102, 103]). The decision affirmed the Court’s broader interpretation of racial discrimination in land dispossession, implying that historical injustices required redress under the law, regardless of separate land legislative frameworks ([para 99]). 

The case sets a significant precedent for indigenous land rights claims in South Africa, reinforcing the principle that reformative legal action is needed to rectify historical injustices rooted in discriminatory practices. The implications extend beyond mere restitution, signifying a recognition of indigenous law as integral to South African legal identity, and mandating its application consistent with constitutional standards ([paras 51, 55]).
