Source Folder: 2010

**Case Note: Steyn v The State (444/09) [2010] ZASCA 49**

**FIRAC Method**

---

**F – Facts**  
[1] The appellant, Ms Mavis Pemella Steyn, faced conviction in the regional court on charges of forgery, uttering, and fraud, to which she pleaded guilty. For the first two counts, she received a two-year prison sentence, conditionally suspended for five years, while on the third count (fraud), she was sentenced to five years of imprisonment as per section 276(1)(i) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (the Act). The appeal arose from her sentence ([paras 1]).

[2] Initially, the trial court denied her petition for leave to appeal, prompting the high court (Van Zyl J and Voges AJ) to permit the introduction of new evidence from a social worker, Dr C C Wessels, regarding sentencing. The matter was redirected to the trial court as per section 309C(7)(d) of the Act for this further evidence to be heard ([para 2]).

[3] However, the trial court failed to document its findings regarding the new evidence as mandated by section 309B(5)(c)(ii) of the Act. This lack of recordal constituted a significant procedural irregularity, which was not fatal but was detrimental to the outcome of the initial hearings ([para 3], [para 6]).

[4] The high court reviewed the case without the required recordal of the trial court’s evaluations of the new evidence, ultimately refusing the appellant’s leave to appeal. The appellant subsequently filed another application for leave to appeal, claiming potential success in contesting her sentence ([para 4], [para 5]).

[9] The underlying facts of the case demonstrated that the appellant had manipulated insurance certificates to claim a loss of R42,000 from an insurance company while employed at an insurance brokerage. By January 2006, her fraudulent claim was lodged ([para 9]).

[10] At the time of sentencing, Ms Steyn was 30 years old, married, and a mother. With a monthly income of R2,500, her employers expressed readiness to re-employ her should she receive a short term of imprisonment. However, she was previously convicted of fraud and had failed to adhere to the conditions of her earlier suspended sentence ([para 10]).

[13] The probation officer, Ms Marinda Viljoen, described Steyn in a disparaging light, drawing on previous employer testimonies. In contrast, Dr Wessels provided a more sympathetic narrative of Steyn's troubled upbringing and family circumstances, although the two assessments starkly contrasted ([para 12], [para 13]).

---

**I – Issues**  
The central question before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the high court should have granted leave to appeal against the sentence. Specifically, the issue raised was the reasonable prospect of success in an envisaged appeal against the sentence imposed by the trial court ([para 8]).

---

**R – Rules**  
The court relied on the provisions of sections 309B(5)(c)(ii) and 309B(6) of the Criminal Procedure Act, which set out requirements for recordkeeping of findings related to further evidence presented in an appeal. The principle of functus officio, which restricts a court from revisiting matters once decided, was also pertinent, as it delineated the high court's authority in hearing the petition for leave to appeal ([para 6]).

The court noted precedents such as **S v Matshona** [2008] 4 All SA 68 (SCA) [para 8], and cited commentary on the Criminal Procedure Act to illustrate practices concerning the assessment of further evidence ([para 6]).

---

**A – Application**  
The court found the irregularities in the procedural handling of the further evidence unfortunate. However, it determined that these issues did not preclude a valid examination of whether a reasonable prospect of success existed for the appellant's appeal against sentence. The court highlighted that credibility findings were less critical in evaluating expert evidence by Dr Wessels than they would be in the context of eyewitness testimony ([para 8], [para 9]).

In assessing the evidence, the court acknowledged that Steyn’s troubling childhood and the challenges presented in her family life were factors to consider. Nevertheless, these mitigating circumstances were counterbalanced by the gravity of her offense, especially given her prior conviction for a similar fraud. The court concluded that her previous leniency in sentencing had not served its intended deterrent function, as evidenced by her repeat offenses and noncompliance with previous sentencing conditions ([para 14], [para 15]).

Ultimately, despite the procedural flaws and the different narratives provided by the probation officer and the social worker, the court did not identify sufficient grounds for altering the sentence, affirming that the imposed sentence aligned with norms for similar offenses ([para 15]).

---

**C – Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appellant’s application for leave to appeal against the refusal of her petition in the high court. The ruling confirmed that the procedural irregularities were not severe enough to undermine the substantive justice in her case. The court held that there existed no reasonable prospect of success in an appeal against the sentence, reflecting a clear alignment with the prevailing sentencing norms for such offenses and emphasizing the importance of adhering to procedural rules in judicial proceedings ([para 16]).
