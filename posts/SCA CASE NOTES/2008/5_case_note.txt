Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2008

## Case Note: Van Bosch v Charles (257/2007) [2008] ZASCA 5 (7 March 2008)

### FIRAC Method

**F – Facts**  
This case concerns an appeal against a judgment of the Cape Provincial Division, which had upheld an appeal against an order from the Magistrate’s Court that dismissed an exception taken by the defendant (subsequently referred to as “plaintiff”) against the appellant’s plea ([1]). On 18 April 2004, the plaintiff signed an offer to purchase property in Gordons Bay from the appellant, with a purchase price established at R600,000. The offer was accepted on 19 April 2004. A deposit of R60,000 was duly paid to the real estate agent, Seeff Properties, on April 20, 2004, with the agent holding it in a trust account pending transfer ([2]).

On April 22, 2004, the defendant's attorneys issued a letter to the plaintiff alleging non-payment of the deposit and failure to sign transfer documents, providing a seven-day timeframe for compliance ([3]). A subsequent letter on May 3, 2004, indicated that the contract was cancelled due to the breach by the plaintiff, with the seller reserving the right to claim damages ([3]). In response, the plaintiff contended in a letter dated 20 May 2004 that he had complied with payment, but indicated that he would not sign due to a perceived conflict of interest with the defendant's lawyers ([4]).

The plaintiff consequently instituted legal proceedings claiming the deposit and interest. He claimed the letters from the defendant’s attorneys constituted a repudiation of the agreement ([5]). The defendant denied this assertion ([6]). The agent agreed that the deposit was received but did not make a determination on payment to either party ([7]).

The plaintiff filed for an exception to the defendant’s plea, asserting that it disclosed no defence and requested the court to rule accordingly ([8]). The Magistrate dismissed the exception, leading to an appeal that was granted leave ([9]). The Cape High Court found the demand for a bank guarantee premature, asserting that the defendant’s plea did not disclose a valid defence ([10]).

**I – Issues**  
The central issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the defendant's plea disclosed a defence to the plaintiff’s claim or whether the demand for compliance and subsequent cancellation was premature in the context of the contractual obligations ([11], [12]). Specifically, questions arose regarding:
1. Whether a seller is entitled to demand a bank guarantee and related transfer costs when they were not yet in a position to lodge transfer documents ([12]).
2. Whether the defendant had effectively repudiated the contract by demanding performance ([20]).

**R – Rules**  
The court relied on established principles in contract law, particularly those surrounding the demand for bank guarantees. In **Hammer v Klein** (1951) 2 SA 101 (A), the court ruled that a seller cannot demand a bank guarantee prior to being able to lodge transfer documents with the Registrar of Deeds ([12]). Where contractual terms do not stipulate a time frame for payment, the seller must communicate when they intend to proceed with the transfer ([12]).

The rules governing exceptions in the Magistrate’s Court were also pertinent, particularly Rule 19(14) which allows a plaintiff to take exception where no defence is disclosed ([9]). The threshold for an exception is that it must be clear that the plea does not provide a legitimate defence ([9]).

**A – Application**  
The court analyzed the letters exchanged between the parties and the terms of the sales agreement. It found that the demand for a bank guarantee was indeed premature given that the defendant's attorneys were not in a position to lodge the transfer documents ([12]). The court also scrutinized the defendant’s assertion that the dissatisfaction from 22 April 2004 constituted a valid basis for cancellation. 

It was concluded that the court a quo had incorrectly focused solely on the demand for a bank guarantee, failing to evaluate all aspects of the defendant's plea and its implications for the plaintiff's rights ([14], [24]). Furthermore, the court addressed whether a refusal by the plaintiff to sign documents constituted a repudiation, reaffirming that the conduct and communications between the parties indicated that the plaintiff had not been informed that his actions were considered repudiation until the letters sent by the defendant’s attorneys ([21]).

Importantly, the argument posited by the defendant that the plaintiff’s refusal to sign the documents constituted a breach was deemed inadequate given that the requisite transfer documents had not been presented. The court underscored that, given the initial failure to perform, the steps taken by the defendant to cancel the agreement were premature ([25]).

**C – Conclusion**  
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the defendant's plea did contain a jurisdictional defence. In upholding the plaintiff's exception, the appeals court ruled that the defendant's demands undermined the contractual relationship established between the parties, thus the cancellation and subsequent denial of the plaintiff's claim was unfounded ([26]). 

The appeal was successful, with an award for costs against the defendant and the order of the court a quo being set aside, reinforcing the rights of the plaintiff to reclaim the deposit ([26]). This judgment clarifies the boundaries of contractual obligations concerning the demand for guarantees and the handling of breaches, reinforcing procedural and substantive legal safeguards for parties in real estate transactions. 

This decision serves as a critical precedent in South African contract law, affirming the need for clear communication and compliance with stipulated terms in the performance of contractual duties.
