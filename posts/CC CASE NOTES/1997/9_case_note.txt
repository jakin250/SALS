Source Folder: 1997

# FIRAC Case Note: Jaintilal Parbhoo and Others v Arnold Getz NO and The Master of the High Court [1997] CCT 16/97

## F - Facts

The case involved the applicants, former directors of Plymouth International (Pty) Limited, which underwent liquidation on October 15, 1996, under the Companies Act 1973. The first respondent, Arnold Getz, served as the liquidator, while the second respondent, The Master of the High Court, had prescriptive authority under the Companies Act. The case arose after a commission of enquiry was held into Plymouth's affairs. It was alleged that the directors had inflated debtor values to mislead creditors, specifically Commercial Union Trade Finance (Pty) Limited (Cutfin) into overpaying.

Prior to a scheduled meeting where the applicants would be compelled to give evidence, they filed an application in the Witwatersrand Local High Court. Their concern was articulated around the potential violation of their constitutional rights, notably their right to not self-incriminate, as guaranteed by Section 35 of the Constitution of 1996. The applicants argued that the provisions of Sections 415(3) and 415(5) of the Companies Act would subject them to self-incrimination during interrogation. The High Court (Southwood J) ruled in favor of the applicants, declaring these sections invalid regarding the admissibility of self-incriminating evidence.

The primary legal issue arose when Southwood J referred the matter to the Constitutional Court for confirmation, as stipulated by Section 167(5) of the 1996 Constitution, due to a legislative gap preventing enactment of specific referral procedures under section 172(2)(c) ([1]–[16]).

## I - Issues

The core constitutional issue revolves around the validity and constitutionality of the provisions of the Companies Act (Sections 415(3) and 415(5)). Specifically:
1. Are the provisions that deny individuals the right to refuse to answer questions on the grounds of self-incrimination consistent with the rights outlined in Section 35 of the 1996 Constitution?
2. Is the legislative framework surrounding the use of self-incriminating evidence consistent with constitutional guarantees regarding fair trial rights, particularly the right to a fair trial and protection against self-incrimination ([2]–[3])?

## R - Rules

The decision rests on the constitutional provisions of the 1996 Constitution:
- **Section 35(3)**: Guarantees the right to a fair trial, including the right not to be compelled to give self-incriminating evidence. This section explicitly outlines the rights of accused persons during trial proceedings.
- **Section 172(2)(a)** and **Section 167(5)**: Guide the process for the confirmation of orders of constitutional invalidity by the Constitutional Court, ensuring that any such declaration by a lower court requires confirmation to take effect.

The case **Ferreira v Levin NO** and Others; Vryenhoek and Others v Powell NO and Others, 1996 (1) SA 984 (CC), is precedent, affirming that similar provisions letting testimony be used in criminal prosecutions are unconstitutional as they violate rights guaranteed under the interim Constitution ([8]–[11]).

## A - Application

The Constitutional Court referenced the precedent set in Ferreira v Levin, noting the direct parallels between the disputes under Section 415 of the Companies Act and those handled under Section 417(2)(b) of the same Act, as examined in Ferreira v Levin. The core argument was that the rights enshrined in Section 35 of the 1996 Constitution echo those previous cases. Ackermann J determined that:
- The interpretation of the provisions of the Companies Act permitting compulsory self-incriminating testimony were inherently inconsistent with the rights to a fair trial outlined in Section 35.
- The constitutionally developed understanding of fair trial rights now included an express prohibition against compelling self-incriminating evidence, necessitating the invalidation of the offending parts of the Companies Act.

Importantly, the Court recognized the legislative vacuum regarding the procedures for the confirmation of invalidity orders, emphasizing the need to allow any relevant state bodies the opportunity to intervene in such matters. The Court underscored the need for a practical and immediate resolution to prevent legal uncertainty ([2]–[12]).

## C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court confirmed the order made by Southwood J, declaring Sections 415(3) and 415(5) of the Companies Act unconstitutional. The judgment held that no incriminating evidence given under the Companies Act could be used against the person in criminal proceedings, thus asserting constitutional rights to a fair trial and the protection against self-incrimination. This ruling has significant implications for corporate and liquidation proceedings moving forward, reinforcing the constitutional safeguards afforded to individuals, particularly in contexts where financial and corporate legal issues intersect with personal criminal liabilities. The case elucidates the necessity of legislative frameworks that respect constitutional guarantees and protect individuals' rights ([15]–[18]).
