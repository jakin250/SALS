# FIRAC Case Note: PFE International Inc v Industrial Development Corporation of South Africa Limited [2012] ZACC 21

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([1–8])

The case at hand involves an application for leave to appeal a decision by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which overturned a High Court ruling. The applicants, comprising of two corporations (PFE International Inc (BVI) and PFE International Inc (Liberia)), the company Van Dyck Carpets (Pty) Ltd, and two individual directors (Mehdy Zarrebini and Mehran Zarrebini), were engaged in a legal dispute with the Industrial Development Corporation of South Africa Limited (IDC) ([1]). 

The core issue arose when SAFYR (South African Fibre Yarn Rugs Limited), a subsidiary of the IDC, initiated legal proceedings against the applicants for the alleged breach of fiduciary duties by the Zarrebini brothers ([9–10]). Post-commencement of these proceedings, the applicants sought specific information held by the IDC to respond to requests for trial particulars through the Promotion of Access to Information Act 2 of 2000 (PAIA) ([11]). However, the IDC contended that access to the information should instead be sought through Rule 38 of the Uniform Rules of Court, arguing that PAIA was inapplicable due to ongoing civil proceedings ([12]) and cited section 7(1) of PAIA, which excludes its application under certain circumstances.

The High Court initially agreed with the applicants, determining that PAIA was applicable. Thereafter, the SCA reversed this determination, leading to the appeal to the Constitutional Court ([13–15]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([16–17])

The key legal question before the Constitutional Court was whether PAIA applies in circumstances where a request for access to information was made after legal proceedings had commenced and where access could ostensibly be sought under court rules—specifically Rule 38. More broadly, the case explores the interaction between statutory mechanisms for accessing information held by state entities and procedural rules governing civil litigation.

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([2–7], [18–22])

1. **Constitutional Framework**: Section 32 of the Constitution of South Africa guarantees the right to access information held by the state, which PAIA aims to give effect to ([2]). 

2. **Promotion of Access to Information Act (PAIA)**: Particularly, section 7(1) provides grounds under which PAIA does not apply, requiring that if access to certain records is requested for civil or criminal proceedings and is provided for in any other law, PAIA is rendered inapplicable ([19]).

3. **Uniform Rules of Court**: Specifically, Rule 38 governs the process for issuing subpoenas duces tecum, allowing parties to request documents for evidence in the context of ongoing legal proceedings ([22]).

4. **Case Law**: Previous judgments, including *Brümmer v Minister for Social Development* and *Mazibuko and Others v City of Johannesburg*, provide interpretative guidance on the balancing of access to information rights and procedural legislative frameworks ([3], [4]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([18–32])

In its analysis, the Constitutional Court emphasized the importance of the right to access information as guaranteed by the Constitution. It approached the interpretation of section 7 of PAIA restrictively to ensure that the right to access information is not unnecessarily curtailed ([18]). 

The Court concurred that the first two conditions of section 7(1)—that the request relates to civil or criminal proceedings and that it was requested after the commencement of those proceedings—were satisfied. The contentious aspect was whether Rule 38 represented "any other law" under section 7(1)(c) that could exclude PAIA's application.

Utilizing a purposive approach to statutory interpretation, the Court found that Rule 38(1) should not be interpreted narrowly. Instead, it should facilitate access to evidence even before a trial date is set, thus allowing a party to better prepare for trial ([25–30]). By adopting a more flexible interpretation of the Rule, the Court sought to promote the broader objectives of access to justice, reflecting the spirit of the Bill of Rights as mandated by section 39(2) of the Constitution.

Furthermore, the Court referenced prior judicial interpretations of Rule 38 that recognized its use outside strict constraints on witness attendance, thereby validating the use of subpoena duces tecum for accessing documents vital for trial preparation ([28–30]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([31–34])

The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the application for leave to appeal by the applicants, affirming the SCA’s decision that PAIA does not apply in this case. The Court confirmed that Rule 38 constitutes the relevant legal framework for accessing the necessary information requested by the applicants, as it effectively provides for such access within the context of ongoing litigation.

The ratio decidendi reaffirms the principle that procedural rules of court serve a facilitative role in the judicial process, and that reliance on PAIA for access to information held by state entities is restricted in cases involving ongoing civil proceedings. Furthermore, this case underscores the necessity of interpreting legislative provisions in alignment with constitutional rights, emphasizing transparency and accountability in public administration.

The implications of this judgment are significant for future cases involving access to information in the ambit of legal proceedings, guiding parties to utilize appropriate procedural channels rather than statutory requests under PAIA. Moreover, it reaffirms the critical role of judicial interpretation in balancing access rights with procedural integrity, ultimately promoting the broader goals of justice and fair trial rights laid out in the Constitution.
