# Case Note: Afriforum NPC v The Premier, Gauteng Province and Others (1000/2020) [2021] ZASCA 185

## F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History
The case arose from a decision made on 28 October 2020 by the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which dismissed an application by the appellant, Afriforum NPC, to set aside the approval of the 2020/21 annual budget of the City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality, approved by the third respondent, the administrator appointed in terms of s 139(1)(c) of the Constitution. Afriforum argued that this approval was unconstitutional and invalid ([1]). Prior to this ruling, the municipal council had been declared dysfunctional and dissolved by the Premier of Gauteng, effective from 23 March 2020, leading to the intervention of the administrator who was tasked with ensuring the municipality's continued functioning during this period of administrative control ([8]). The application in the high court had also involved procedural aspects, such as the late submission of heads of argument by the respondents, which was contested by the appellant but eventually granted by the court with costs ([2]–[4]).

## I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions
The central legal issue posed in this appeal was whether an administrator appointed under s 139(1)(c) of the Constitution possesses the authority to approve an annual budget for the municipality. This interpretation required the court to examine the constitutional provisions that govern the powers of both the municipal council and an appointed administrator ([5]).

## R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon
The court based its judgment on various sections of the South African Constitution, particularly s 139, which outlines the conditions under which provincial executives can intervene in local governance, and the procedural requirements specified in the Local Government: Municipal Finance Management Act 56 of 2003 (MFMA). Relevant provisions included:

- **Section 139(1)(c)**: Permitting dissolution of the Municipal Council to appoint an administrator.
- **Section 151(2)**: Clarifying that both executive and legislative authority rests with the municipal council.
- The MFMA provisions, especially those concerning budget approvals ([11]–[12], [15]).

Key case law included:
- **Mnquma Local Municipality v Premier of the Eastern Cape**, questioning the nature of a budget's approval process ([16]).
- **Fedsure Life Assurance Ltd v Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council**, discussing distinctions between legislative and executive functions ([19]).
- The court also referred to its prior judgments regarding the distinction between mootness and justiciability to affirm its reasoning ([6]).

## A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law
In applying the law to the facts, the court held that the administrator, during the period of provincial intervention under section 139(1)(c), was indeed empowered to approve the municipality's budget. The court distinguished between the obligations arising under different subsections of s 139, identifying that the specific circumstances leading to the administrator’s appointment fell under the broader powers of intervention designated by the Constitution.

The court's interpretation emphasized the following key points:
1. **Legislative Function vs. Executive Function**: The court found that the approval of a municipal budget is primarily administrative—it facilitates service delivery, rather than being purely legislative. It highlighted that the language of s 151(2) reflects that this authority is vested in the council but does not specifically exclude an administrator acting as a substitute during a crisis ([26]).

2. **Mootness and Justiciability**: Despite the budget's subsequent ratification by the reinstated council, the court chose to exercise its discretion to address the merits due to the legal implications of the constitutional interpretation required, indicating the broader significance for future local governance ([7]).

3. **Discretionary Powers**: The court affirmed that the provincial executive has discretion under s 139(1)(c) to determine whether to intervene, and once the administrator was appointed, it followed that his powers extended to necessary budget approvals to ensure municipal functions continued ([20]).

The court concluded that the purported legislative nature of a budget approval by an administrator as argued by Afriforum was mischaracterized and ultimately dismissed the appeal, reaffirming that the executive powers given to the administrator included budget approval ([29]).

## C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Afriforum's appeal, confirming that the administrator had the authority to approve the municipal budget under the powers conferred through s 139(1)(c) of the Constitution. The court's decision carries significant implications for the interpretation of the powers of municipal administrators during periods of intervention by provincial executives, clarifying that such powers are necessary to fulfill ongoing municipal services and governance, even in the absence of a functioning council.

The ruling emphasized the need for judicial interpretation of constitutional provisions to ensure continued and effective local governance and service delivery in South Africa, reinforcing the balance of powers during exceptional circumstances. Significantly, the court also established that budget approval should be viewed as within the scope of executive functions necessary for governance rather than purely legislative functions ([30]).
