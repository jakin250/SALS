Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# Case Note: Moyeni v De Vries and Others NNO [2020] ZASCA 128 (13 October 2020)

## FIRAC Method

### F - Facts
The case under review, *Moyeni v De Vries and Others NNO*, centers around the eviction of Mrs. Katie Moyeni (the appellant), a 73-year-old pensioner residing at Mooihoek Farm in Worcestershire, South Africa. The farm is owned by the De Vries Family Trust, whose trustees are the respondents in this matter ([1–2]). Mrs. Moyeni's late husband, Mr. Brown Moyeni, had been employed on the farm since 1994 and continued to live there until his passing in March 2014 ([3]–[4]).

Following Mr. Moyeni's death, the Trust served Mrs. Moyeni with a letter seeking to terminate her right of residence, specifying a deadline for her to vacate the premises ([4]). The Trust claimed that her right to reside on the farm was extinguished with her husband's death, since he was her link to the property under the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA) ([5]–[6]). Despite this contention, the appellant insisted that she had the right to remain on the farm as a protected occupier under ESTA, given her history of employment on the farm ([8]).

The Worcester Magistrate's Court ultimately ordered her eviction on 17 November 2017 ([10]). Mrs. Moyeni subsequently appealed this decision, which led to a review by the Land Claims Court, affirming the Magistrate's ruling ([1]–[2]). The appeal was based on an assertion that she qualified as a protected occupier under section 8(4) of ESTA, which stipulates that an occupier who has resided on the land for over ten years and has reached the age of 60 cannot be evicted without sufficient grounds ([11]).

### I - Issues
The central issue on appeal was whether Mrs. Moyeni could be classified as a 'protected occupier' under section 8(4) of ESTA, particularly given her age, length of residence, and claims of past employment on the farm. The consideration of this classification raised further legal inquiries into whether proper procedures had been followed in communicating her eviction ([11]), and whether the claims against her—namely breaches of housing rules—were substantial enough to warrant eviction ([13]).

Additionally, procedural matters regarding the late filing of the appellant's notice of appeal and heads of argument arose, inviting scrutiny regarding the application of rules governing appeals in South African law ([12]).

### R - Rules
The following legal provisions and case law were integral to the court's determination:

1. **Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA)**: 
   - **Section 8(4)**: Protects occupiers from termination of their rights if they have resided on the property for over 10 years and are 60 years or older, barring significant breach of rules ([14]).
   - **Section 10**: Outlines conditions under which eviction may occur, emphasizing that breaches must be material and that a balance between rights of landowners and vulnerable occupiers must be maintained ([15]).

2. **Case Law**: 
   - *Klaase and Another v Van Der Merwe NO and Others* and *Daniels v Scribante* were cited to demonstrate ESTAs role as legislation meant to protect vulnerable individuals (particularly women) in insecure housing situations ([17]–[19]).

3. **Plascon-Evans Rule**: This principle governs the evaluation of conflicting affidavits, stipulating that the version of the party opposing a motion is preferred unless it is untenable ([19]).

### A - Application
The Court applied the above rules to analyze the circumstances surrounding Mrs. Moyeni’s residency and employment history. Critical to the court's analysis was the acknowledgment of her past employment against the Trust’s claim that her residency was solely derived from her late husband ([13]).

The judges noted that the Trust had effectively conceded that Mrs. Moyeni had worked on the farm for significant periods, which aligned her with the definition of an 'occupier' under ESTA. The court highlighted that the prior magistrate erred in concluding that she possessed no individual claim to occupancy rights ([13]). The Trust was bound by admissions made in their affidavits which recognized her protected status and therefore could not justify eviction based on alleged breaches without presenting robust and uncontested evidence.

Furthermore, the Court addressed the procedural delays in filing for the appeal, acknowledging the circumstances surrounding her financial means and the late acceptance of her appeal, emphasizing that procedural justice must not come at the cost of substantive rights of vulnerable individuals ([12]). 

### C - Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Mrs. Moyeni's appeal, severing the Land Claims Court’s confirmation of her eviction. The previous eviction order was set aside entirely, with the Court determining that she did indeed qualify as a protected occupier under ESTA, effectively dismissing the eviction application ([21]). This decision reinforced the protective nature of ESTA for vulnerable individuals, especially pensioners like Mrs. Moyeni, while also highlighting the necessity of fair procedural adherence in eviction matters.

The Court mandated that the costs be borne by the attorneys responsible for the missteps in filing, reinforcing a commitment to uphold procedural integrity whiles protecting vulnerable subjects within the legal framework ([20]). This case prominently illustrates the balance between property rights and human dignity within South African law, reiterating the constitutional ethos aimed at safeguarding the rights of vulnerable populations.
