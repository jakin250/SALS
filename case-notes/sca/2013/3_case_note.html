Supreme Court of Appeal

Adidas AG & another v Pepkor Retail Limited (187/12) [2013] ZASCA 3

Adidas argues that the respondent’s footwear infringes its trademark rights, with the distinctiveness of the three stripes being well-established in South Africa due to extensive marketing and use since 1949 ([2], [3]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 3

F – Facts

The case of Adidas AG & another v Pepkor Retail Limited revolves around trademark infringement and passing off, centering on Adidas’ well-known three-stripe trademarks. The first appellant, Adidas AG, is a German corporation, while the second appellant, Adidas International Marketing BV, is a wholly-owned subsidiary based in the Netherlands ([1]). The respondent, Pepkor Retail Limited, a South African retailer, allegedly employed two and four parallel stripes on its sporting footwear, leading to claims of trademark infringement under section 34(1)(a) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 ([1], [4]).

Adidas argues that the respondent’s footwear infringes its trademark rights, with the distinctiveness of the three stripes being well-established in South Africa due to extensive marketing and use since 1949 ([2], [3]). After serving cease and desist letters to the respondent, the appellants applied for an interdict and ancillary relief when Pepkor continued to use the stripes ([7], [8]). The court of first instance dismissed the application, prompting the appeal ([1]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues can be identified as follows:

  1. Trademark Infringement: Whether the use of two and four stripe marks by the respondent so nearly resembles the three-stripe trademarks of the appellants as to be likely to deceive or cause confusion among consumers ([19]).
  2. Passing Off: Whether the respondent's actions constituted passing off its goods as those of the appellants, thereby misleading consumers ([28]).

R – Rules

The legal framework for addressing the issues involves the following statutory provisions and principles:

  • Section 34(1)(a) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 states that trademark rights are infringed by unauthorized use of a mark similar to a registered trademark, causing likely deception or confusion ([9]).
  • The principles established in Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd detail that the burden lies with the plaintiff (in this case, the appellants) to demonstrate a probability of confusion or deception, not requiring proof that every consumer would be misled ([20]).
  • For passing off claims, the plaintiff must prove that the get-up has become distinctive of their goods and that there is a likelihood of deception due to similarity of markings ([28][29]).

A – Application

Trademark Infringement

Regarding the allegations of trademark infringement, the Court evaluated the similarity of the respondent’s two and four stripe marks to the three-stripe trademarks registered by Adidas. The Court cited the importance of the impression made on the average consumer, noting that even slight similarities could lead to confusion ([20], [25]).

  1. Comparison of Marks: The Court found that the registered marks’ distinctiveness suggested any similar marks would likely cause confusion. Although the respondent's marks were not identical, their configuration and visual characteristics were sufficiently similar, thus satisfying the criteria for infringement under section 34(1)(a) ([19],[19]).
  2. Consumer Likelihood: The Court also emphasized consumer perception, noting that given the strong reputation of the Adidas trademarks, the likelihood of confusion increased rather than diminished ([24], [25]).

Passing Off

In assessing passing off, the Court noted the principles of implied representation, which depend on the likelihood of confusion among consumers ([28], [30]). The Court compared the relevant get-ups and the trademarks in question:

  1. Distinctiveness and Market Context: The appellants successfully established that the get-up of their footwear is well-known, and thus any adoption of similar features by the respondent was likely to mislead consumers regarding the origin of the goods ([29], [35]).
  2. Evidence of Confusion: While the respondent attempted to argue there was no clear evidence of actual confusion, the Court maintained that the absence of such evidence was not determinative; rather, the likelihood of confusion based on the similarities was sufficient for the passing-off claim to succeed ([27], [30]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, concluding that the respondent's use of two and four parallel stripes infringed the registered trademarks of the first appellant and constituted passing off. The Court held that the similarities were likely to lead to confusion among consumers, affirming the distinctiveness of the Adidas trademarks.

The implications of this ruling reinforce the protection of well-known trademarks, emphasizing the importance of consumer perception in both trademark infringement and passing off cases. The decision mandates that businesses take heed when adopting designs that could invoke associations with already well-established brands to avoid legal ramifications ([39]).

Key Orders:

  1. The appellants’ request for an interdict against the respondent’s use of the infringing marks was granted.
  2. The respondent was ordered to cease the production and sale of infringing goods and to account for damages and royalties as determined via an inquiry ([39]).

This case illustrates the safeguarding of intellectual property rights within the framework of trademark law and underscores the need for retailers to carefully evaluate their branding strategies to avoid infringing upon existing trademarks.
