Case Note: IN THE CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2007

# Case Note: Engelbrecht v The Road Accident Fund CCT 57/06

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

In this case, Renier Albertus Hermanus Engelbrecht (the applicant) sought to challenge the constitutionality of regulation 2(1)(c) of the regulations enacted under the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 (the Act) following a hit-and-run motor vehicle accident on 22 February 2002 ([1]–[4]). Engelbrecht was injured in a collision with an unidentified truck and subsequently faced difficulties in claiming compensation from the Road Accident Fund (the Fund) due to his late filing of the necessary affidavit with the South African Police Service ([3]–[8]). The Fund raised a special plea arguing that Engelbrecht’s claim was invalid because he failed to comply with regulation 2(1)(c), which required claimants to submit an affidavit within 14 days after being in a position to do so. Engelbrecht conceded that he did not comply with this regulation but contended that it was unconstitutional because it limited his right of access to the courts as protected by section 34 of the Constitution ([10]–[11], [12]).

The Cape High Court dismissed Engelbrecht's claim, indicating that regulation 2(1)(c) was constitutional ([13]–[14]). Engelbrecht then sought leave to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court, arguing that the regulation effectively nullified his claim and that its objectives could be addressed through less restrictive means ([15]–[18]).

### I – Issues

The primary constitutional issue before the Constitutional Court was whether regulation 2(1)(c) of the Road Accident Fund regulations infringed on Engelbrecht's right of access to the courts, as guaranteed by section 34 of the Constitution. The Court was tasked with determining the constitutionality of this regulation, particularly whether the 14-day filing requirement constituted a limitation of that right and whether such limitation was reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society ([11], [29]).

Subsequently, legal questions arose regarding whether there existed a justiciable claim in a hit-and-run context, the standing of regulation 2(1)(c) against common law, and whether the regulation adequately aligned with the purposes of the Act without infringing constitutional rights ([19]–[22], [39]).

### R – Rules

The relevant legal rules included:

1. **Constitutional Provisions**: The right of access to courts as established in section 34 of the Constitution, which guarantees that everyone has the right to resolve disputes through fair hearings.
2. **Regulations under the Act**: Regulation 2(1)(c) stipulating the conditions for a claimant's eligibility for compensation from the Fund, specifically requiring the submission of a detailed affidavit within a stipulated timeframe ([10]).
3. **Principles of Administrative Law**: The regulation must align with the legislative intent and objectives of the overarching statute, which, in this case, was to ensure access to compensation for victims of motor vehicle accidents ([26]). 
4. **Judicial Precedents**: The application of case law, especially decisions like *Makwetlane* and *Moise*, provided context on how limitations on access to courts were assessed ([17], [29]).

### A – Application

In analyzing the constitutionality of regulation 2(1)(c), the Court indicated that while regulations can impose requirements on claimants, they cannot completely bar access to courts for a justiciable claim ([28]–[40]). The Court examined the specific 14-day timeframe and found it excessively restrictive, particularly for individuals who might be unaware of these regulations. This concern resonated with the Court’s duty to uphold the principle of access to justice.

The ruling also noted that engaging in a limitations analysis necessitated weighing various factors, including the nature of the right affected and the purpose of the regulation. The potential benefits of combating fraud were acknowledged but deemed insufficient to justify the harsh limitations imposed by the regulation ([41]–[42]). The Court noted a lack of evidence to support a causal relationship between the regulation and its intended purpose, thereby questioning its proportionality and efficacy ([40]–[42]).

### C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court held that regulation 2(1)(c) was unconstitutional and invalid due to its unjustifiable limitation of the right of access to courts as protected under section 34 of the Constitution. The Court declared the regulation inconsistent with the Constitution and ordered its invalidation, allowing Gonzalez to pursue his claim against the Fund without being barred by the procedural timeline specified in the regulation ([43]–[47]).

This case is significant not only for its direct impact on Engelbrecht’s claim but also for its broader implications regarding the enforceability of claimant requirements under the Road Accident Fund framework, highlighting the necessity of ensuring that regulatory measures align with constitutional rights in an open democratic society. Thus, the Court granted Engelbrecht leave to appeal, dismissed the Fund's special plea, and remitted the case back to the Cape High Court for determination of the underlying merits of his claim ([46], [47]).

This ruling serves as a precautionary tale about the importance of legislative measures that do not inadvertently undermine the constitutional rights they aim to protect. The decision has broader ramifications for those seeking redress from statutory compensation schemes in South Africa, stressing the delicate balance required between regulatory control and individual rights.
