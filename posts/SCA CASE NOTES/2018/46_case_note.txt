Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: Minister of Home Affairs & others v Fireblade Aviation Ltd & others (13/2016) [2018] ZASCA 46

### F – Facts
The case arose from an application for leave to appeal against a judgment delivered by the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria. The primary parties involved included the Minister of Home Affairs (First Appellant), the Director-General of Home Affairs (Second Appellant), Fireblade Aviation Proprietary Limited (First Respondent), the South African Revenue Service (Second Respondent), and Denel SOC Limited (Third Respondent).

Fireblade sought judicial approval for the establishment of an international customs and immigration operation at O R Tambo International Airport. On 28 January 2016, the Minister granted the necessary approval and communicated this in writing to Fireblade. Subsequently, the Minister appeared to contradict this approval, claiming that no such approval had been granted, leading to misunderstandings concerning Fireblade’s project. Following a ruling on 27 October 2017 by Potterill J in the High Court favoring Fireblade, an application for leave to appeal was dismissed on 8 December 2017. A subsequent enforcement order was issued by Potterill J, but this was also appealed.

The full court dismissed the urgent appeal on 14 December 2017 [1–4].

### I – Issues
The key legal issue centered on whether the application for leave to appeal constituted an appeal against an original decision by a single judge or a request for special leave to appeal due to prior dismissal by two courts. The distinction was critical due to differing standards of review under the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, specifically between sections 16(1)(a)(i) and 16(1)(b) [1–5]. 

In addition, further points of contention raised by the applicants included:
1. Whether Fireblade's designation as a port of entry was relevant.
2. The legality of the Minister’s decision to reject Fireblade’s application after initially approving it.
3. Claims regarding the Minister's compliance with Section 217 of the Constitution.
4. An alleged failure of the High Court to address a counter-application regarding the interpretation of s 9A of the Immigration Act [7–10].

### R – Rules
The governing legal framework included the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, specifically sections 16 and 17, which outline the procedures for leave to appeal. The tests for granting leave to appeal under s 17(1) establish that the appeal must demonstrate reasonable prospects of success or entail a compelling reason for its consideration.

Moreover, South African case law relevant to evaluating the credibility of contested documents was also referenced. Notable precedents included *Da Mata v Otto* [1972] 3 SA 858 (A) and *MEC for Health, Eastern Cape v Kirland Investments (Pty) Ltd* [2014] 3 SA 481 (CC), which explore the treatment of contemporaneous documents in judicial disputes [6][8].

### A – Application
The court clarified that the correct classification of the leave to appeal application was under s 16(1)(a)(i) of the Act, not as a special leave under s 16(1)(b) as initially posited by the parties. Consequently, the standard for assessing the appeal was sufficiently robust, requiring the demonstration of reasonable prospects for success.

Upon analysis:
1. The court found that Potterill J's approval of the Fireblade application is well-supported by objective documents, which were neither challenged nor contradicted at the relevant time. The Minister’s subsequent contradictory claims were deemed inconsistent and legally ineffective against the initial approval granted [5–6].
  
2. The assertion regarding Fireblade’s operational designation was dismissed as it was clarified that the application related to the ad hoc provision of services rather than establishment as a new port of entry [7].

3. The claim concerning a rejection of Fireblade's application lacked merit. The lack of any judicial challenge to the Minister's earlier approval meant it remained valid and must be adhered to [8].

4. The allegations of a compromise of public procurement principles under s 217 of the Constitution was unconvincing as the actions of the Minister were seen as discharging statutory functions without necessitating procurement violations [9].

5. Finally, the High Court's non-consideration of the counter-application was justified given the points were non-disputed, and the appeal did not engage compelling arguments warranting further examination [10].

### C – Conclusion
The application for leave to appeal was ultimately dismissed with costs, with the court finding no reasonable prospects of success on appeal. The ruling elucidated the proper legal framework to assess the leave to appeal, affirming the robustness of the original High Court decision and the Minister’s inability to substantiate his claims against Fireblade [11–12]. This case reinforces the importance of procedural correctness and the adherence to lawful decisions within the framework of administrative law.
