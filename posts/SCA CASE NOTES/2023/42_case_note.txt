Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of Unica Iron and Steel (Pty) Ltd v The Minister of Trade and Industry and Another

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–6])

In March 2015, the respondents, the Minister of Trade and Industry (the Minister) and the Manufacturing Development Board (MDB), initiated legal action in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria against the appellants, Unica Iron and Steel (Pty) Ltd (Unica) and Mr. Mohamed Asif Qasim (Mr. Qasim). The claim involved the repayment of incentive grants amounting to R4,734,986.00 disbursed to Unica under the Small Medium Enterprise Development Programme (SMEDP) ([1]). 

The Minister oversees the Department of Trade and Industry (DTI), while the MDB operates under the Manufacturing Development Act 187 of 1993. The Minister implemented the SMEDP to provide financial support to qualifying entities. Unica, through an application from Mr. Qasim, had received these grants based on the fulfilment of specified conditions in the agreement, which were alleged to have been violated due to non-compliance with local environmental regulations ([2]).

In response to the court proceedings, the appellants formally challenged the authority of Rudman & Associates Incorporated (Rudmans), the attorneys representing the respondents, by filing a Notice in terms of Rule 7 of the Uniform Rules of Court. Rule 7 allows for the dispute of an attorney's mandate on notice to the party involved ([3]). The appellants contended that the State Attorney could not appoint private attorneys in districts where the State Attorney had an office, arguing that such action contradicted Section 8 of the State Attorney Act 56 of 1957. The high court nevertheless ruled that Rudmans were duly authorized and granted a declaratory order to that effect, dismissing the appellants' arguments ([6]).

The appellants were subsequently granted leave to appeal this order to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which considered whether the appeal was based on a "decision" as required under Section 16 of the Superior Courts Act ([8][10]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 8, 10, 13])

The central legal issue to be resolved in the appeal was whether the order issued by the high court constituted an appealable "decision" under Section 16(1)(a) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013. This determination hinged upon whether the order was final and definitive, thereby affecting the substantive rights of the parties involved ([10]). 

The refined question was posed: "Does the State Attorney, pursuant to the State Attorney’s Act 56 of 1957, have the power and authority to appoint and instruct an attorney from the private sector within the same district where the State Attorney is based?" ([8]). Furthermore, the court needed to discern if the appeal should proceed in the interests of justice, especially since the formulated legal question appeared largely academic and abstract in nature ([13]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 9, 10, 12])

The SCA's analysis was guided by precedents elucidating the meaning of a "decision" in the context of the Superior Courts Act and prior interpretations of statutory provisions relating to appealability. The landmark case, *Zweni v Minister of Law and Order* defined the criteria for a "judgment or order" as requiring three characteristics: it must be final in effect, definitive of the rights of the parties, and should dispose of a substantial portion of relief claimed in the main proceedings ([12]).

Further, the ruling in *DRDGOLD Limited and Another v Nkala and Others* offered clarity on the contextual nature of appealability under the Supreme Court Act, affirming that only decisions that meet the *Zweni* criteria are typically appealable. Nonetheless, exceptions arise if the interests of justice necessitate a review of an otherwise non-appealable order ([10][12]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 13–18])

The SCA engaged in a meticulous examination of the underlying facts to determine the appeal's legitimacy. The court noted that the order granted by the high court was interlocutory, and therefore, did not fit into the final or definitive framework required for appealability under Section 16 of the Superior Courts Act. It observed that the order failed to resolve substantive rights between the parties or to dispose of significant claims in the main action ([11][13]).

Furthermore, the court elaborated on the factual basis surrounding the authority allegedly granted to Rudmans. It established that the authority to act had emanated from an email instruction dated June 2, 2014, which supposedly signified the DTI's referral of matters to Rudmans. However, the court found that the correspondence did not provide sufficient evidence of formal authorization from the State Attorney. It was confirmed that Rudmans was appointed by the DTI, not the State Attorney, indicating a lack of evidentiary backing for the respondents' claims of authorized representation ([14][18]). 

In light of these findings, the court concluded that the formulated legal question was abstract and disconnected from the contested reality of the case. The court ultimately determined that the balance of interests did not favor advancing the appeal; thus, it was not in the interests of justice to treat the order as appealable ([13][18]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 19–19])

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the matter was to be struck off the roll, ruling that it did not engage with an appealable decision as contemplated under the relevant legal framework. The court’s reasoning emphasized that an appeal against interlocutory orders, which do not definitively resolve litigation or conclusively impact the merits, is generally impermissible. This ruling underscores the principle that only orders meeting specific legal standards or justified by compelling interests should be subject to appellate scrutiny ([19]). 

The implications of this decision are significant for the legal landscape, particularly concerning the authority of the State Attorney and the standards of proof required to establish attorney mandates in public sector litigation. Legal practitioners will keenly note the court's assertion against entertaining abstract legal questions, reiterating the judiciary's inclination to focus on fact-based inquiries directly affecting the parties’ rights rather than engaging with hypothetical or academic legal deliberations.
