Constitutional Court of South Africa Case CCT 43/13

Constitutional Court 2013 CCT 43/13

Facts

[1] The case involved an application for leave to appeal against a decision of the Supreme Court of Appeal that had overturned a declaratory order made by the North Gauteng High Court. The High Court had ruled that section 84(2)(k) of the Constitution of South Africa does not empower the President to confer senior counsel (SC) status on advocates. The applicant, Urmilla Roshnee Devi Mansingh, had successfully argued in the High Court that the power to confer such status was not included in the President's honours prerogative, resulting in the High Court ordering the President and the Minister of Justice to pay her costs. However, upon appeal from the General Council of the Bar (GCB) and the Johannesburg Society of Advocates (JSA), the Supreme Court of Appeal found that the President indeed had the power to confer SC status as an honour.

[2] The central point of contention was the interpretation of section 84(2)(k) of the Constitution, which states that “[t]he President is responsible for conferring honours.” Ms Mansingh, applicant and member of the JSA, emphasized that the case did not concern the merits of her application for SC status or the institution of silk itself.

[3] Historical context revealed that prior to 1994, the conferral of such honours was derived from the royal prerogative in the English law tradition. This historical framework was critical in understanding the legal powers conferred upon the President under the new constitutional regime established in 1996.

Issues

[8] The primary issue was whether the authority conferred to the President under section 84(2)(k) includes the power to bestow silk or SC status upon advocates. Related issues included examining the broader implications of this interpretation within the constitutional framework and whether such conferral constituted an “honour” in the requisite sense.

[13] Furthermore, the legitimacy of the interpretation provided by the Supreme Court of Appeal warranted examination, particularly exploring whether it aligned with constitutional principles and the intended scope of executive power granted to the President.

Rules

[15] The governing rule was the interpretation of section 84(2)(k) of the Constitution. The court turned to principles of constitutional interpretation, emphasizing a purposive and contextual approach that acknowledged both the text and the historical basis for the powers conferred. Citing precedents like Investigating Directorate: Serious Economic Offences and Others v Hyundai Motor Distributors [2000] ZACC 12, the court reinforced that linguistic understanding must be complemented by broader contextual meaning.

[24] The court also recognized that powers previously held as royal prerogatives were retained by the President, subject to constitutional constraints. It was noted that the codification of certain powers in section 84 would inherently carry the historical notions that framed their interpretation.

Application

[29] In applying these rules, the court found that the ordinary meaning of “honour” was broad enough to encompass the conferral of SC status, as it serves to designate a level of professional distinction based upon integrity and excellence in advocacy. The Supreme Court of Appeal had articulated that the granting of silk status represented acknowledgment by the state of the professional capabilities and esteem in which the recipient is held, hence qualifying it as an honour.

[36] In considering arguments raised against the interpretation of silk as an honour, the court reiterated that the constitutional text and the accompanying historical context did not preclude the treatment of SC status as an honour. Moreover, the potential implications for rights under sections 9 and 22 of the Constitution were deemed separate issues and not determinative of the President's powers under section 84(2)(k).

Conclusion

[38] Ultimately, the Constitutional Court upheld the Supreme Court of Appeal's ruling, determining that the President's authority to confer honours indeed included the power to grant SC status to advocates. The appeal was dismissed, solidifying the understanding that the institution of silk falls within the purview of honours that the President is constitutionally empowered to confer.

The implications of this ruling underscore the significance of the honours prerogative within a constitutional framework, drawing from historical prerogatives while maintaining adherence to modern constitutional principles. The decision marks a pivotal interpretation of the President's powers, ensuring the continuance of professional distinctions aligned with the evolving legal landscape in South Africa.
