Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 51

F – Facts

This case arises from a motor vehicle accident on 1 May 2010, involving Mr. Koos Jacobs, who sustained serious head injuries while being a passenger in a vehicle that collided. The injuries led to significant cognitive impairments, characterized by left hemiparesis and communication difficulties, impacting his occupational performance ([3]).

After the accident, Mr. Jacobs lodged a claim with the Road Accident Fund (RAF) on 18 January 2017, approximately seven years post-incident. This delay raised the defense of prescription from the RAF, which argued that sections 23 of the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 (RAF Act) governed the claim, mandating a claim submission within three years of the accident ([1], [4]). The RAF contended that Jacobs' claim had lapsed, as it should have been submitted by 30 April 2013 and summons served by 30 April 2015 ([3]).

After being appointed as curatrix ad litem for Mr. Jacobs on 28 November 2017, the appellant representative filed suit against the RAF, who responded with a special plea citing prescription based on the RAF Act ([2], [4]). The Eastern Cape Division of the High Court upheld this plea, concluding that the relevant provisions of the Prescription Act [68 of 1969] did not apply, and thus dismissed the claim ([2], [10]).

I – Issues

The central issue in this case was whether the prescription of claims under the RAF Act was governed exclusively by its provisions, specifically section 23, or whether section 13(1) of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 also applied, thus permitting an extension of time based on the claimant's incapacity due to mental unsoundness ([1], [2]).

An accompanying issue was whether the legislative framework discriminated against mentally incapacitated persons by failing to protect their right to claim due to the tight prescription framework established under the RAF Act ([11], [32]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal provisions at issue included:

  1. Section 23 of the RAF Act (Prescription of claims):
  • Prescribes that claims must be lodged within three years from the date of the cause of action, with specific exceptions for minors and persons under curatorship ([7]).
  1. Section 13(1)(a) of the Prescription Act:
  • Provides that prescription does not complete against creditors who are minors, insane, or under curatorship until one year after their incapacity ceases ([8]).
  1. Precedents:
  • Road Accident Fund v Smith NO ([12], fn 3): Affirmed the applicability of the Prescription Act to claims by mentally incapacitated individuals.
  • Road Accident Fund v Mdeyide ([21], fn 6): The Constitutional Court examined the relationship between the RAF Act and Prescription Act concerning prescription in claims, emphasizing a gap in legal protection for claimants.

A – Application

The court's analysis centered on the statutory construction surrounding section 23 of the RAF Act versus section 13(1) of the Prescription Act. The Supreme Court of Appeal reasoned that the specific wording in the RAF Act indicated an intention to exclusively govern claims for compensation arising from motor vehicle accidents and to limit the application of the Prescription Act in this context, indeed establishing specific guidelines about when claims would be considered prescribed ([23]-[34]).

The court highlighted the lack of retrospective application of the curatorship appointment to benefit Mr. Jacobs for the period prior to his appointment. His condition at the time of the accident did not retrospectively render him a person under curatorship, which was critical since his claim had already prescribed when curatorship was conferred ([5], [6]).

The appellant's reliance on the Smith precedent was deemed inapplicable because of the subsequent Mdeyide case that clarified the inconsistency between the two acts regarding prescription terms, effectively neutralizing the previous protection afforded to mentally incapacitated individuals under the Prescription Act unless they were previously recognized under curatorship by the relevant legislation ([27], [28]).

Thus, since the court ruled that the RAF Act's sections did not violate constitutional guarantees of access to courts, it concluded that erroneously extending prescriptive protections could lead to widespread legal uncertainty ([33]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, upholding the high court's ruling that the prescription of the claim by Mr. Jacobs was valid based on the applicability of section 23 of the RAF Act and not the Prescription Act ([2], [34]). The court held that the prescriptive period for claims under the RAF Act is explicitly covered within its own provisions, rendering section 13(1)(a) inapplicable.

The implications of this decision reinforce the stringent framework set by the RAF Act regarding claims, indicating a limited capacity for mentally incapacitated individuals to invoke protections under the Prescription Act. Furthermore, it accentuates the necessity for timely legal intervention in cases concerning individuals with mental incapacities to safeguard their rights and interests in the event of road accident claims. Thus, although this ruling closed pathways for certain protections previously held under the common law through the Prescription Act, it is consistent with legislative intent as noted in the historical context surrounding the enactment of the RAF Act ([29], [30]).

In summary, the court clarified and confirmed the legal landscape governing the prescriptive periods relevant to the road accident claims, establishing a precedent for future claims while reiterating the importance of prompt action in safeguarding potential claims for damages arising from road accidents. The outcome also underscores the asymmetrical exposure of particularly vulnerable groups whose claims may go unconsidered due to legislative intricacies in dealing with mental incapacities and the requirement for legal action within the constraints posed by prescription laws.
