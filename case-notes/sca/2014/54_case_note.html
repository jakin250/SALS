Litako & Others v S (584/2013) [2014] ZASCA 54

In the case of Litako & Others v S, the events under consideration occurred on 4 February 2007, at the White House Tavern in the Mmatau Village, North West Province.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2014 [2014] ZASCA 54

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

In the case of Litako & Others v S, the events under consideration occurred on 4 February 2007, at the White House Tavern in the Mmatau Village, North West Province. During the night, a robbery ensued in which the tavern owner, Ms. Christinah Sibanda, was robbed of R5 000 and a cellular telephone, while a patron named Mr. Godfrey Moleta Ngema was assaulted and another victim, Mr. Ben Seretse Motshwaedi, was fatally shot ([paras 1–2]).

Following the robbery, six individuals, including the five appellants—Moses Moshe Litako, Thabo Mbaola, Philemon Gumede, Greaterman Morongwa Mathlare, and Ndabuko Docus Simamane—were arrested and charged with multiple offences, including murder and robbery ([para 3]). The prosecution's case relied significantly on a statement made by the first appellant, Litako, to a magistrate, which implicated the others despite being exculpatory for himself ([para 4]). This statement became central to the trial and subsequent appeal.

The trial court found the appellants guilty mainly based on this statement, leading to severe sentences, including life imprisonment for murder ([paras 5–6]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues presented involved:

  1. Admissibility of the First Appellant's Statement: Whether the statement made by the first appellant could be admissible against the co-accused as per Section 3 of the Law of Evidence Amendment Act 45 of 1988 ([paras 26, 30, 31]).
  2. Common Purpose Doctrine: The applicability of the common purpose doctrine in convicting individuals based on the admissions of a co-accused without providing them an opportunity to contest those statements ([paras 30–31]).
  3. Fair Trial Rights: Whether the admission of the first appellant's statement violated the other appellants' constitutional rights, particularly the right to a fair trial under Section 35 of the Constitution of South Africa ([para 67]).

R – Rules

Key legal authorities and statutes relevant to this case included:

  • Section 3 of the Law of Evidence Amendment Act 45 of 1988: Allows for the admission of hearsay evidence if the interests of justice warrant it but does not explicitly permit admissions by one co-accused against another.
  • Constitution of South Africa, Section 35: Guarantees the right to a fair trial, which includes the right to challenge evidence and effectively defend oneself against charges.
  • Case Precedents:
  • S v Ndhlovu (2002): Discussed the admissibility of statements made by co-accused and the significant shift it represented in South African law.
  • S v Molimi (2008): Explored fair trial rights and how it intersects with the admission of co-accused's statements.

A – Application

In applying these rules, the court undertook a detailed examination of the historical context surrounding the admissibility of confessions and admissions within South African law. The court revisited the implications of Ndhlovu and Molimi, emphasizing that while the legal landscape had shifted, there were still substantial concerns regarding fairness when extra-curial statements were involved.

Specifically, the court noted that the primary basis for the appellants' convictions rested on the first appellant's statement, which was ruled admissible by the trial court against the others despite their inability to cross-examine or challenge that evidence effectively ([paras 28–32]). This reliance on the first appellant's statement without additional corroborative evidence created a precarious basis for the convictions, ultimately deemed inadequate ([para 68]).

The court further analyzed the risk of wrongful conviction stemming from the invocation of the common purpose doctrine, as applied in this case, where the co-accused had been bound by hearsay evidence without any established liability ([paras 30–31]), leaving substantial gaps in the prosecution's case.

C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal based on the admitted flaws in the trial court's handling of evidential matters. The court held that the extra-curial statement of the first appellant should not have been admitted against the co-accused due to the fundamental right to a fair trial and the inherent dangers of relying on such hearsay evidence ([paras 68–72]). Consequently, it set aside the convictions and related sentences against all appellants.

This case illustrates the delicate balance the courts must maintain between advancing justice and ensuring that fair trial rights are strictly observed, reaffirming that the interests of justice cannot override the fundamental rights guaranteed by the Constitution.
