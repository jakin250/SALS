Constitutional Court

Republic of South Africa (Andile Phillip Dyakala v City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality)

In Andile Phillip Dyakala v City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality ([1]), the applicant, Andile Phillip Dyakala, sought urgent relief from the Labour Court to declare the termination of his employment contract as unlawful, invalid, and of no effect.

Constitutional Court 2015

F – Facts

In Andile Phillip Dyakala v City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality ([1]), the applicant, Andile Phillip Dyakala, sought urgent relief from the Labour Court to declare the termination of his employment contract as unlawful, invalid, and of no effect. Dyakala served as the Group Chief Financial Officer of the City of Tshwane until March 9, 2015, when he received notice of termination from the respondent's attorneys. His suspension dated back to August 6, 2014, based on allegations of financial misconduct, including posting derogatory comments on Facebook aimed at the City Manager, Mr. Jason Ngobeni ([2]).

The disciplinary hearing, which commenced on August 18, 2014, experienced multiple postponements, and on March 9, 2015, while still ongoing, the respondent terminated Dyakala’s contract, citing a breakdown of the trust relationship ([4]). The applicant contended that the termination violated clause 18 of his employment contract, which mandated adherence to certain disciplinary procedures when dismissing an employee for alleged misconduct ([6]). He sought reinstatement, citing ongoing financial harm and reputational damage arising from the unlawful termination and the submission of allegations against him in media outlets ([8]–[10]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues raised in this matter relate to:

  1. Whether the termination of Dyakala's employment constituted a breach of contract and was thus unlawful ([1], [17]);
  2. Whether Dyakala was entitled to a disciplinary hearing as stipulated in his contract and relevant regulations, and if the failure to provide such constituted an infringement of his rights ([18], [21]);
  3. Whether the Labour Court had jurisdiction to reinstate Dyakala pending further disciplinary procedures ([19]).

R – Rules

Two sets of legal frameworks underpin the judgment. Firstly, the common law principles regarding employment contracts that stipulate a contract can only be terminated in accordance with its terms ([19]). In the present case, clause 18.2 of Dyakala's contract expressly required adherence to established disciplinary procedures before termination ([20]).

Secondly, the Local Government: Disciplinary Regulations for Senior Managers (2010) mandates a formal disciplinary hearing in cases of serious misconduct ([21]). As established in prior case law, particularly in Nyathi v Special Investigating Unit and Equity Aviation Services ([17]), an employee's entitlement to procedural fairness in disciplinary matters must be upheld, ensuring the right to defend against allegations is respected.

A – Application

The Court meticulously analyzed the respondent's decision to terminate the applicant's employment contract amidst an ongoing disciplinary hearing. It concluded that such a premature termination breached the contract of employment:

  1. Breach of Contract: By terminating Dyakala’s contract without completion of the disciplinary procedure specified in clause 18.2, the respondent violated the terms of the employment contract ([20]). The Court emphasized that an employer must adhere to its contractual obligations and procedures when terminating an employee on the basis of alleged misconduct.
  2. Lack of Justification: The termination notice stated a breakdown of trust as the reason, which was at odds with the ongoing disciplinary procedures whereby the applicant was yet to be declared guilty of any misconduct ([20]). The court noted that this contradicted the employer's obligation to follow the procedure prior to dismissal for wrongdoing, establishing that the cited reason lacked a justifiable foundation.
  3. Entitlement to a Hearing: Moreover, the Court reinforced that Dyakala had a right to a disciplinary hearing based not only on the employment contract but also according to the prescribed regulations. The premature termination disrupted this critical process and undermined the principles of fairness required in administrative actions both under common law and statutory provisions ([21]).
  4. Urgency of Relief: The Court found that Dyakala’s dignity and financial wellbeing were at stake, affirming that urgent relief was warranted due to ongoing reputational damage and the financial implications arising from the unlawful termination ([10], [14]).

C – Conclusion

The Labour Court ruled in favour of Dyakala, finding that the termination of his employment contract was unlawful and set it aside ([28]). The applicant was ordered to be reinstated pending adherence to the contractual termination procedures stipulated under clause 18.2. The Court emphasized that the principles of fairness and adherence to process must be upheld in employment matters, reiterating the importance of these legal protections for employees in South Africa’s labour context.

In essence, this judgment not only reinstated Dyakala but also reinforced the notion that procedural fairness in disciplinary actions is non-negotiable, serving as a precedent for similar cases involving premature dismissal in the public sector. The costs were awarded to the applicant, acknowledging the necessity of legal intervention to protect his rights ([28.3]).
