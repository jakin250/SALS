Case Note: DIE HOOGSTE HOF VAN APPèL
Source Folder: 2002

# FIRAC Case Note: Die Hoogste Hof van Appèl van Suid-Afrika

## F – Facts:
The legal dispute centers around a lease agreement between the appellant, Madelein Brisley (the tenant), and the respondent, Antoinette Drotsky (the landlord). Brisley failed to make full, timely rental payments for her townhouse, which was set at R3500 per month, paid in advance on the first day of each month. By January 31, 2000, she had not paid the January rent in full. This prompted Drotsky to terminate the lease and demand that Brisley vacate the premises within fourteen days. Following Brisley’s refusal to comply, Drotsky initiated eviction proceedings ([paras 1-3]).

The initial proceedings resulted in a court order for eviction, where the presiding judge, Vorster Wnde R, issued a ten-day notice for Brisley to vacate the property, but did not grant costs. Notably, Brisley’s claims of having an oral agreement allowing her to delay her payments and other defenses, including a claim for wrongful eviction under the South African Constitution, were put forth but ultimately not supported by evidence ([paras 2-4]).

## I – Issues:
1. **Shifren Principle**: Does the principle established in *SA Sentrale Ko-op Graanmaatskappy Bpk v Shifren*[1964] allow for a modification of contract terms only in writing, and does it apply to this eviction case?
2. **Bona Fides**: Is Drotsky's reliance on the non-variation clause in the lease agreement against the principles of good faith (bona fides) in the legal context of contract law?
3. **Constitutional Question**: Does section 26(3) of the South African Constitution, which provides that no one may be evicted without consideration of relevant circumstances, apply to private lease agreements such as this one?

## R – Rules:
1. **Shifren Principle**: The Shifren principle mandates that modifications to lease agreements must be in writing to be enforceable. The court emphasized the importance of certainty in contractual relationships, asserting that parties should be bound by their express terms, preventing casual or informal alterations ([paras 6-8]).
   
2. **Bona Fides**: Courts have previously acknowledged that if the enforcement of a contractual clause is unreasonable or acts contrary to good faith, it may not be enforced. The existing jurisprudence emphasizes the necessity of upholding contract terms unless a significant miscarriage of justice is evident ([paras 11-13]).

3. **Section 26(3) of the Constitution**: The court examined the constitutional provision, which mandates that no one can be evicted from their home without a court order, which considers all relevant circumstances. This section is interpreted to extend to private lease agreements, requiring courts to assess socio-economic conditions before granting eviction orders ([paras 35-39]).

## A – Application:
In applying the Shifren principle to this case, the court confirmed that the explicit terms in the lease mandating written modifications are binding. Brisley’s allegations of a verbal agreement granting her leniency on late payment were invalid, as they did not conform to the contractual requirement set forth in the lease for any alterations ([paras 4-10]).

The court also critically evaluated the bona fides argument presented by Brisley, which claimed that enforcing the lease's non-variation clause would be unjust. However, the court maintained that the signed lease agreement took precedence and that Brisley’s previous conduct (consistent late payments) could not amend agreed-upon terms ([paras 11-14]).

In resolving the constitutional question, the court determined that section 26(3) does indeed apply to private lease agreements. The court referenced the *Grootboom* case to illustrate that the constitutional obligation extends to private sectors, implying that the landlord must demonstrate legitimate grounds for evictions, including consideration of the tenant's socio-economic status ([paras 35-39]).

## C – Conclusion:
The court concluded that the lease terms and the Shifren principle must be upheld, rejecting Brisley’s appeal against the eviction order. The court noted that the integrity of the written contractual terms must be prioritized over alleged informal agreements, emphasizing that both parties had the freedom to establish the terms of their agreement. The court also affirmed the necessity of abiding by section 26(3) of the Constitution, affirming its application to private lease agreements, thereby mandating consideration of all relevant circumstances in eviction matters. Consequently, the appeal was dismissed with costs ([paras 43-44]).

### Implications:
This case reinforces the sanctity of written agreements and the Shifren principle within South African contract law, promoting certainty in commercial dealings. It highlights the obligations landlords have under the Constitution, ensuring that eviction processes are fair and consider tenants' human rights, thus advancing a more equitable approach in landlord-tenant disputes.
