Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note on *Bokoni Platinum Mines (Pty) Ltd v Abram Moropane* (/1035/2019) [2020] ZASCA 168

## F – Facts

The dispute began in 2013 when Bokoni Platinum Mines (Pty) Ltd (Bokoni), the appellant, commenced mining activities on Klipfontein Farm, leading to several agreements with local farmers, including Abram Moropane (the respondent). Each agreement stipulated a compensation of R54,000 for the loss of subsistence farming rights, to be paid in three annual installments of R18,000 ([paras 2–4]).

Moropane received the first installment but did not receive the subsequent payments, prompting him to sue Bokoni to recover the unpaid amounts. Bokoni's defense included denying the existence of a written agreement and asserting that Moropane was not entitled to compensation as he was not farming at the time the agreement was struck ([paras 5–6]). Subsequent amendments to Bokoni's counterclaim acknowledged a written agreement but contested its interpretation, asserting that compensation was contingent upon Moropane actively farming on the land ([paras 9–10]).

Bokoni sought to amend its plea and counterclaim several times. After a contentious procedural history including the recusal of the presiding officer, Magistrate Malebane, on grounds of bias ([paras 12–14]), his judgment refusing the amendment was later upheld by the High Court, which found that it was res judicata ([paras 15–16]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal questions before the Supreme Court of Appeal were:

1. Was the judgment of Magistrate Malebane, which dismissed Bokoni’s application for leave to amend its plea and counterclaim, res judicata?
2. Could the high court raise the issue of res judicata sua sponte (of its own accord)?
3. Did the recusal of Magistrate Malebane invalidate all aspects of the trial, including prior judgments and orders made during it? ([paras 3, 17])

## R – Rules

South African law provides that res judicata applies when a final judgment has been delivered on a matter, barring re-litigation of the same issue between the same parties ([para 16]). In the context of bias and recusal, the law mandates that the decisions rendered by an adjudicator who has exhibited bias are considered nullities ([paras 20–21]). The courts have established that bias vitiates the judicial process, which means any judgment rendered under such taint lacks legal standing and perspective of nullity applies to the entire proceedings ([paras 22–23]).

## A – Application

The court analyzed the implications of Magistrate Malebane’s recusal and the effects on the trial process. It considered whether the dismissal of the amendment application was nullified. The High Court's ruling, which maintained the judgment of Magistrate Malebane was binding and could not be set aside merely due to the recusal, was found to misapprehend the legal principle that bias infects all aspects of the trial ([paras 17, 20–25]).

The Supreme Court concluded that the recusal necessitated the setting aside of all prior judgments, including the dismissal of Bokoni's application to amend, treating the proceedings as null. The requisite analysis was therefore shifted from res judicata to the effects of the recusal on the procedural integrity of the trial, affirming that all orders made up until that point were invalid ([paras 24–26]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Bokoni’s appeal, ruling that the proceedings before Magistrate Malebane, including the judgment denying the leave to amend the plea and counterclaim, were void due to the magistrate's recusal. Consequently, the matter was remitted to the Limpopo Division of the High Court for a reevaluation of the application to amend the plea and counterclaim on its merits. The court also directed that each party bear its own costs due to the procedural missteps involved both in the High Court and in prior stages of the case ([para 26]).

The ruling reinforces the principle that bias invalidates procedural integrity and highlights the courts’ responsibility to ensure unbiased adjudication—an essential component of a fair judicial system ([paras 20, 24]).
