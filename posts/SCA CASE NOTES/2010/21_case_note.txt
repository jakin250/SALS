# FIRAC Case Note: De Lange v Absa Makelaars (262/09) [2010] ZASCA 21

### F – Facts
The case of **De Lange v Absa Makelaars (262/09)** arose from a dispute regarding the interpretation of a contract of employment between Johan de Lange (the Appellant) and Absa Makelaars (the Respondent), specifically concerning a clause (clause 16.6) that outlines the obligations of the parties when making determinations of legal liability to clients ([1]–[2]). The parties agreed that claims related to De Lange's liability would be tested through two designated claims (claims 4 and 10) stemming from alleged negligent financial advice he provided to clients ([3], [4]).

De Lange had worked for ABSA as a broker from 1995 to 2001, during which time he gave financial guidance that resulted in significant client losses. Following his advice, clients such as Mr. Loubser and Mr. Honiball experienced substantial financial detriment, leading ABSA to compensate them based on their claims. ABSA subsequently sought reimbursement from De Lange, citing clause 16.6, which limited their liability and outlined a right of recovery against the employee if deemed legally liable ([6]–[11]).

The trial court determined the claims in favor of ABSA without allowing De Lange an opportunity to present his side of the story during the investigation, leading to his appeal ([19], [31]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal question in this case concerned whether clause 16.6 of the employment contract required ABSA to afford De Lange a fair hearing (audi alteram partem) before making determinations about his liability ([1], [17]). Additionally, the court needed to decide whether the contract included a tacit term that obliged ABSA to consider De Lange's account of events as part of their liability assessment, especially post-employment ([20]).

### R – Rules
The court based its analysis on principles of contract interpretation, particularly those pertaining to tacit terms and the necessity of providing a fair hearing. The **officious bystander test** was cited as a standard for determining whether a tacit term should be imported into the contract, which states that a term can only be inferred if it was a necessary agreement between the parties ([21]).

Furthermore, the decisions in **Estate Milne v Donohoe Investments (Pty) Ltd** and **Perdikis v Jamieson** were referred to concerning the classification of ABSA's role and obligations under clause 16.6 ([18]). The *arbitrium boni viri* concept was also highlighted, indicating ABSA's discretion should be exercised reasonably, which would typically include fair procedural standards ([17]).

### A – Application
In applying these rules, the court found the interpretation of clause 16.6 was flawed as it failed to include the requirement to hear De Lange's version prior to making a decision on liability. The High Court's classification of ABSA as a "valuer" rather than an "arbitrator" mischaracterized its functional role, which was more akin to making a legal judgment than merely evaluating financial risks. The findings from the investigations initiated by ABSA were substantiated by their lack of proper procedural engagement with De Lange ([18]–[20], [29]).

The court emphasized that the failure to afford De Lange a right to present his version of events did not adhere to the principles of fairness required for a properly conducted investigation, leading to the conclusion that the tacit term as asserted by De Lange was indeed applicable and necessary to ensure fairness and efficacy in the contract ([31]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that ABSA had indeed failed to comply with the tacit term requiring it to afford De Lange a fair hearing during its decision-making process. As such, the claims against De Lange were dismissed based on the interpretation of clause 16.6, effectively reversing the High Court's decision and highlighting the importance of fair procedural practices in employment contracts. This ruling delineated the necessity of involving employees in matters that directly affect their reputations and legal responsibilities ([32], [33]).

The matter underscores a critical precedent regarding employment law in South Africa, articulating the need for procedural fairness in contractual interpretations and employer-employee relationships ([1]–[5], [30]).
