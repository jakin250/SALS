### FIRAC Case Note: The Public Servants Association v National Commissioner of the South African Police Service 

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–3])**  
This case involves the interpretation of regulation 24(6) of the South African Police Service Employment Regulations, which concerns the conditions under which the National Commissioner may retain an employee in an upgraded post without advertising the position. The appellant, the Public Servants Association, represents members from the national public service, while the respondent is the National Commissioner of the South African Police Service (the “Service”). Uncertainty arose regarding whether the regulation imposed a duty or merely gave the Commissioner the discretion to continue employing the incumbent in a higher-graded post after such a post's salary had been adjusted. The Commissioner originally sought a declaratory ruling from the High Court, which held in favor of the appellant, leading to this appeal ([paras 1–2]).

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 8–20])**  
The primary issue is whether regulation 24(6) provides the National Commissioner with discretion in employing an incumbent in an upgraded post without advertising, or whether it creates an obligation to do so if the specified conditions are met ([para 8]). A secondary issue concerns the implications of such an interpretation on the rights of employees under the Constitution of South Africa, particularly regarding fair labor practices ([para 10]).

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 4–7], [24–34])**  
The court analyzed the drafting of regulation 24, particularly the distinctions between the words 'must' and 'may' in legal context, drawing from established principles of statutory interpretation ([paras 4–7]). For example, the use of 'must' indicates a mandatory requirement while 'may' generally signifies discretion ([para 33]). Precedents such as *Jaga v Dönges NO* 1950 (4) SA 653 (A) emphasize that often, permissive language must be construed within the wider context of governing legislation ([para 24]). The court also referenced the applicable sections of the South African Police Service Act 68 of 1995, specifically regarding the Commissioner’s responsibilities and employment policies ([paras 13–22]).

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 15–20])**  
The court dissected the implications of interpreting 'may' in regulation 24(6). It determined that while the regulation uses permissive language, the circumstances surrounding employee retention—namely satisfactory performance and current duties—imply a duty upon the Commissioner. To not retain the employee could lead to dismissal, raising concerns of unfair labor practices as outlined in the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 ([para 15]). The court rejected the notion that the Commissioner had discretion to dismiss the incumbent merely because the post had been upgraded, arguing that such a conclusion would contradict legal principles safeguarding employee rights ([paras 15–20]).

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 21–22])**  
Ultimately, the court held that the National Commissioner is not just permitted but obliged to retain an incumbent employee in an upgraded post without advertising if the conditions of satisfactory performance and role fulfillment are met ([para 21]). Consequently, the court reversed the High Court's ruling, emphasizing the need for regulations that are consistent with fairness and equity as mandated by the Constitution ([para 22]). This ruling reinforces the commitment to protecting employee rights and ensuring fair labor practices in the South African public service context.
