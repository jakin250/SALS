### F – Facts

The case involves an application for leave to appeal from the North West High Court, Mafikeng, where Mr. Samuel Sampie Khanye and Mr. Victor Zandile Moyo were convicted along with five co-accused of multiple offenses, including murder, robbery with aggravating circumstances, and unlawful possession of firearms and ammunition ([1]). The Trial Court sentenced each applicant to life imprisonment for murder, 15 years for robbery, and six years for possession of firearms ([1]). 

The case arose from a violent incident on 3 August 2002, where Warrant Officer Dingaan Makuna was murdered after being shot in front of his daughter. The applicants were subsequently arrested and confessed to their participation through extra-curial statements collected during police interrogation. These statements, however, were contested as being made under duress ([5], [7]).

The Trial Court held that these statements, as well as the admissions made by the accused, were admissible and formed the basis for the prosecution's case ([8]). The principle of "common purpose" was pivotal in the Trial Court's conclusion, which resulted in conviction despite the applicants' assertions of duress ([12]). 

After the Trial Court's conviction and sentencing were upheld by a Full Bench appeal, the applicants sought further appeal, which was initially dismissed by the Supreme Court of Appeal ([14], [15]). However, the matter reached the Constitutional Court, where it faced a new interpretation of the admissibility of extra-curial statements against co-accused.

### I – Issues

The primary legal issue centered on whether extra-curial admissions made by one accused can be used against another in the context of common-law principles and constitutional protections ([20]). The Court also had to determine if the admissibility rulings previously made violated the constitutional rights of the accused to a fair trial ([22]).

### R - Rules

The Constitutional Court relied on several pivotal precedents and statutory provisions in their judgment. The leading case in this matter was *Mhlongo v S*, where it was established that extra-curial admissions made by one accused are inadmissible against a co-accused ([22]). Furthermore, they referenced section 219A of the Criminal Procedure Act, which maintains that an admission by an accused is only admissible against themselves and does not extend to others ([22]). The Court sought to uphold the common law position regarding the treatment of co-accused's statements, ensuring adherence to fundamental rights to a fair trial protected under Section 35 of the Constitution of South Africa ([20]).

### A – Application

In its application of the law, the Court analyzed the particulars of each applicant's case, particularly focusing on the evidentiary basis for their convictions. 

For Mr. Khanye, the Court noted that the only incriminating evidence against him was derived from the extra-curial statements of his co-accused, which had been deemed inadmissible ([23]). The Supreme Court reasoned that there was a lack of sufficient evidence directly linking him to the murder or the robbery, as mere presence at the scene, without further corroborative evidence, could not sustain a conviction ([27]).

Similarly, the Court found Mr. Moyo’s defense compelling, reiterating that without the co-accused statements, which had implicated him, there was no substantive evidence to warrant a conviction ([28]). The Court emphasized that while he had been with individuals suspected of the crimes, this was insufficient for establishing a common purpose in the absence of further incriminatory evidence against him.

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court unanimously upheld the applicants' appeal, finding that the reliance on inadmissible co-accused statements constituted a violation of their fair trial rights, ultimately leading to their wrongful convictions ([29]). The order was made to set aside all convictions and sentences, leading to their immediate release from custody. This ruling reinforces the constitutional standard concerning the admissibility of evidence among co-accused, emphasizing a commitment to fair trial rights and justice in the criminal process ([20], [29]). 

The implications of this judgment underscore the importance of ensuring that all evidence presented in court complies with constitutional norms and protection against the potential misuse of one accused's statements against another, thereby reinforcing individual rights within the South African judicial system.
