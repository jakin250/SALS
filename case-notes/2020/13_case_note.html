Beadica 231 CC and Others v Trustees for the time being of the Oregon Trust and Others [2020] ZACC 13

The franchise agreements were linked to lease agreements for premises owned by the Trustees of the Oregon Trust ([4]–[5]).

Constitutional Court 2020 [2020] ZACC 13

F - Facts

This case involves four close corporations (the applicants) that entered into franchise agreements with Sale’s Hire CC, a franchisor specializing in renting and selling tools and equipment. The applicants, former employees of Sale’s Hire, acquired their franchises under a black economic empowerment initiative financed by the National Empowerment Fund (the Fund) ([1]–[3]).

The franchise agreements were linked to lease agreements for premises owned by the Trustees of the Oregon Trust ([4]–[5]). The lease agreements allowed the applicants the option to renew for an additional five years, which they were required to exercise six months before the lease's termination date, which was set for July 31, 2016 ([6]–[8]). The applicants failed to provide written notice of their intent to renew the leases within the specified timeframe, prompting the Oregon Trust to demand their eviction ([10]–[11]).

The High Court ruled in favor of the applicants, declaring the leases had been validly renewed based on the principle of proportionality concerning public policy ([11]). The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned this decision, emphasizing the sanctity of contracts and questioning the applicability of the principle of proportionality in contract law ([12]). The applicants appealed to the Constitutional Court, raising public policy concerns, particularly invoking section 9(2) of the Constitution regarding substantive equality and the failure of the empowerment initiative ([13]).

I - Issues

The key legal questions before the Constitutional Court were:

  1. Whether the strict enforcement of the renewal clause of the lease agreements was contrary to public policy in the context of black economic empowerment and the principle of substantive equality as enshrined in the Constitution ([13]–[14]).
  2. The implications of the applicants' failure to exercise their renewal options properly on the enforcement of their contractual rights ([15]).

R - Rules

The judgment draws on several pertinent legal principles:

  • Pacta sunt servanda: Contracts must be upheld, reflecting constitutional values of freedom and dignity ([84]).
  • Public Policy: A court may refuse to enforce contractual terms if doing so contravenes values underlying the Constitution, particularly fairness, justice, and the principle of ubuntu ([79]).
  • Barkhuizen Test: Contractual terms are assessed for reasonableness, considering circumstances preventing compliance—a two-stage test assessing the enforceability of the clause itself and the fairness of enforcing it in the given circumstances ([32]–[37]).

A - Application

The Court evaluated whether the enforcement of the renewal clauses would undermine public policy. The applicants argued that their failure to comply with the renewal terms was due to a lack of sophistication and understanding of their legal rights, exacerbated by the dynamics of franchising and their prior dependent relationship with Sale’s Hire ([96]). However, the Court found their explanations lacking. It noted that all representatives of the applicants had prior franchise experience and should have understood the lease terms that were neither complex nor obscure ([94]).

Notably, the Court examined the broader implications of enforcing or invalidating the contracts related to the black economic empowerment initiative. While the potential failure of these businesses was grave, the Court emphasized that the failure to comply with the contractual terms ultimately rested with the applicants, who did not sufficiently demonstrate how their situation justified overriding those terms ([97]).

The Court concluded that allowing the applicants to escape their contractual obligations simply because of the gravity of the consequences would contravene the established legal principle of pacta sunt servanda and undermine the certainty necessary in contract law ([99]–[102]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court upheld the Supreme Court of Appeal's decision, concluding that the enforcement of the renewal clauses was not contrary to public policy. The applicants failed to demonstrate that their non-compliance was justified or that the consequences of enforcement engaged the principles of fairness or equality to the degree necessary to invalidate the contracts. The Court’s decision reasserts the importance of contract law stability while acknowledging the need for transformation aligned with constitutional values, particularly in economic empowerment contexts ([104]).

The ruling underscores the balance that must be struck between upholding contractual obligations and addressing historical inequalities, ultimately emphasizing the importance of personal accountability in contract performance. The implications of this decision serve as critical precedent in cases involving black economic empowerment initiatives, clarifying the role of public policy in judicial review of contractual terms without giving unqualified power to courts to sidestep strict contractual obligations based on perceived unfairness or harshness.
