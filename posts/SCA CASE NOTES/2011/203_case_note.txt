Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: Rosenberg v Nuco Chrome Bophuthatswana (Pty) Ltd [2011] ZASCA 203

### F – Facts
The case of *Rosenberg v Nuco Chrome Bophuthatswana (Pty) Ltd* arose from the appellant Martin Rosenberg’s dispute with the respondents, particularly concerning matters of costs following a previous motion. The factual background indicates a request for final relief in a motion that was persistently pursued by the appellant until the fifth day of the hearing before the North West High Court under Lever AJ ([para 1]). The appeal was subsequently brought on procedural grounds, requesting a referral to trial given the nature of the issues at stake ([para 1]).

At the appellate level, the court recognized that both parties came to an understanding that the matter should progress to trial ([para 1]). The contentious issue primarily revolved around the allocation of costs associated with the appeal, particularly concerning the conduct of the appellant and the second respondent's executors' claims that the appellant should be responsible for these costs due to his insistence on seeking final relief via motion ([para 2]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal question in this case was the appropriate allocation of costs following the appeal and the procedural legitimacy of the appellant's initial approach to the court by way of motion rather than trial ([para 2]). Specifically, the court needed to determine whether the appellant, who argued for the costs to be awarded in his favor, was justified given that the prior proceedings led him to require intervention from the appellate court to have the original order set aside ([paras 2, 3]). 

### R – Rules
In determining the outcome, the court relied on various judicial principles concerning costs in civil litigation. The general rule is that the successful party is entitled to recover costs, often framing this within the context of the “costs in the cause” doctrine – which means that costs are awarded to the successful party to follow the event ([para 3]). The court recognized that the appellant was compelled to appeal due to the legal barriers imposed by the lower court’s order, thereby establishing a basis for the entitlement to costs from the second respondents, the executors ([para 2]). 

Moreover, the case touches on procedural norms established under the Uniform Rules of Court regarding the progression from motions to trials, emphasizing that complicated matters should typically be dealt with in the latter format in order to facilitate a complete examination of the issues at hand ([para 1]).

### A – Application
In applying these rules, the court assessed the substantive elements of the appeal, concluding that the persistence of the appellant in seeking final relief through motion was not sufficient to deprive him of the costs of the appeal. The judges noted that the decisive aspect was the necessity for the appellant's intervention to set aside the order from the court a quo, which signifies the efficacy of his appeal ([para 2]). 

The appellate court's order reflects an understanding of the procedural missteps in the previous hearings and suggests a deliberate shifting of the matter to trial, which acknowledges the complexity of the issues that were inadequately addressed through the motion process ([para 1]). This judicial discretion aligned with broader principles of justice served to ensure equitable outcomes for parties involved in civil litigation, particularly in recognizing the necessity for procedural correctness in advancing legal matters effectively.

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the appellant, setting aside the previous order and issuing directives for a trial referral. The court held that the matter would proceed as follows: the notice of motion would stand as a simple summons, with specified timelines for declarations and pleas. Importantly, it ordered the second respondent (the executors) to pay the appellant's costs of the appeal, inclusive of two counsel fees, recognizing the appellant's necessity to seek judicial review to rectify the situation ([para 3]).

This decision underscores the essential legal principle that parties should not be penalized in costs merely due to their procedural choices, particularly when subsequent necessary actions are taken to address significant issues inadequately resolved at the earlier stages. The implications of this ruling extend to future cases, reinforcing judicial tendencies to grant costs to the party requiring enforcement of their legal rights through appeal—a clear indication of the court's commitment to ensuring fair and equitable access to justice within the South African legal framework.
