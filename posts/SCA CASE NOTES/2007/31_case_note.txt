Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2007

# FIRAC Case Note: McGregor v City of Johannesburg [2007] SCA 31 RSA

## F – Facts
The dispute arises from an interdict obtained by the City of Johannesburg (the respondent) against Bruce E. McGregor (the first appellant) and Corpcom Outdoor (Pty) Ltd (the second appellant), forbidding them from displaying an advertising sign and hoarding for contravening municipal by-laws ([1]). The advertising sign was authorized by the Eastern Metropolitan Local Council (EMLC) in a letter dated July 1, 1999, permitting its display from July 1, 1999, to June 30, 2002, under the 1995 by-laws ([2]). The 1995 by-laws were subsequently repealed by new by-laws (the 1999 by-laws), which were later replaced, effective December 1, 2001, by the 2001 by-laws ([2]–[3]).

The 2001 by-laws rendered the display of advertising signage on residential property unlawful ([4]), yet they included an exemption at clause 4(3) for signs that were lawfully displayed immediately before their enactment, provided they were maintained without alteration ([5]–[6]). The appellants contended that their sign was lawfully displayed on November 30, 2001, and thus fell within this exemption ([5]). The legal question was whether the promulgation of the 2001 by-laws extended the appellants' right to display the sign beyond the originally permitted timeframe of June 30, 2002 ([6]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issue pertains to the interpretation of the 2001 by-laws and whether they extend the original approval granted for the appellants to display their sign beyond its expiration due to the new regulatory framework ([7]). Specifically, the court had to determine whether clause 4(3) of the 2001 by-laws preserves the appellants' right to display the sign indefinitely despite the explicit expiration period stipulated in the original approval ([6]–[8]). 

## R – Rules
Key legal rules emerge from the interpretation of the municipal by-laws—especially clauses 4(3) and 5(26) of the 2001 by-laws. Clause 4(3) preserves existing rights by exempting any sign that was lawfully displayed right before the new by-laws commenced, provided it remains properly maintained ([8]). Conversely, clause 5(26) categorically prohibits advertising signs on residential property beginning December 1, 2001, thus marking the end of any rights associated with signs displayed under previous approvals ([4]).

The court draws upon statutory interpretation principles, especially regarding legislative intent to regulate signage effectively and maintain public order and safety ([7]–[8]). The interpretation must align with the overarching purpose of the by-laws, which is to consider community interests against those of individual businesses ([8]).

## A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal examined the context and legislative history of the by-laws. It noted that the exemption in clause 4(3) serves to preserve rights existing prior to new regulations rather than to expand or confer new rights ([8]). Theron AJA concluded that interpreting the exemption as granting perpetual rights would undermine the regulatory framework the new by-laws sought to implement ([9]).

The court rejected the appellants’ argument, asserting that granting extended rights based on non-compliance would lead to absurd outcomes contrary to the legislature’s intent. Moreover, the interpretation of clauses 4(3) and 5(26) revealed that while the former preserved existing rights under certain conditions, it did not amend the finite nature of the original approval ([10]), thereby upholding the City's decision to interdict the display of the sign post-expiration of the approval ([11]).

## C – Conclusion
The court dismissed the appeal, affirming the interdict and stating that, although the sign was lawfully displayed up to the expiration of the original approval on June 30, 2002, post this date, its continued display was unlawful under the new by-laws ([11]). The judgment reinforces the importance of municipal regulations in maintaining public order and aesthetics, marking a clear warning against the misinterpretation of legal exemptions within regulatory frameworks. The case illustrates the balance of competing rights of businesses against the municipality's duty to safeguard community interests ([8]–[11]). 

This ruling serves as a critical precedent regarding the interpretation of municipal by-laws and the limits of previously granted approvals in the context of new regulatory legislation.
