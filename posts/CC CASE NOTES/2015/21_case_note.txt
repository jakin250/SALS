Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: **De Vos N.O. and Others v Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development and Others** [2015] ZACC 21

### F - Facts
**Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–2, 4–5])**  
This case arose from a constitutional challenge to sections 77(6)(a)(i) and (ii) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 concerning the treatment of accused persons who are alleged to lack the mental capacity to comprehend legal proceedings. The first applicant, Ms. Anna-Marie De Vos N.O., represented individuals with intellectual disabilities, specifically Mr. Stuurman, who, at age 14, was charged with murder. The second applicant, Ms. Maria Stuurman, is his mother. Similarly, Mr. Snyders, the third applicant’s son, faced charges related to rape, with assessments determining both had limited understanding due to intellectual disabilities.

In the High Court, justifications for the constitutional invalidity of the provisions were sought following their compulsory institutionalization. The High Court ruled the provisions peremptory, infringing upon the accused's rights to freedom and security, especially for children ([para 9]). This case was then elevated to the Constitutional Court for confirmation of that order ([para 1]).

### I - Issues
**Legal Questions ([paras 13–17])**  
The court was tasked with determining:
1. Whether the application was premature given ongoing lower court proceedings.
2. Whether section 77(6)(a) is indeed peremptory.
3. The constitutionality of the provisions, particularly in relation to:
   - The compulsory hospitalisation and imprisonment of adults.
   - The institutionalisation of children with intellectual disabilities.
4. Whether any identified infringements could be justified under section 36 of the Constitution ([para 17]).

### R - Rules
**Relevant Legal Authorities and Precedents ([paras 20, 21, 60])**  
In addressing the case, the court referred to:
- **Section 12(1)** of the Constitution, which secures the right to freedom and security, asserting that such rights include safeguards against arbitrary detention and degradation.
- **Section 9(3) and (4)** pertaining to equality, especially regarding discrimination against individuals with mental health issues.
- The court distinguished provisions under **sections 77 and 78** of the Criminal Procedure Act, affirming that the former employs mandatory incarceration without discretion, whereas the latter accords some judicial flexibility, thus underpinning claims of arbitrariness and unfairness ([paras 8, 19]).
  
### A - Application
**Court Analysis ([paras 20–67])**  
The court critically evaluated section 77(6)(a) as compulsory, dismissing the respondent's arguments that it allowed for discretionary interpretation:
- The term "shall" in section 77(6)(a) was interpreted as prescriptive, thereby lacking judicial discretion ([paras 19–20]).
- The court referenced both **international human rights norms** and judgments, notably from the European Court of Human Rights, asserting that institutionalisation must not be merely for the presence of a mental illness but requires substantive justification ([para 30]).

In scrutinising the requirement for constitutional adherence, it was determined that both compulsory imprisonment and hospitalisation could contravene the rights guaranteed by the Constitution, particularly impacting children disproportionately, further compounded by poor facility provisions ([para 46]).

Additionally, the court held that mere resource constraints could not justify imprisonment in the absence of actual risk posed by the accused ([para 45]). A reading-in remedy was deemed appropriate for section 77(6)(a)(ii) to permit judicial discretion post-fact finding, thereby aligning with constitutional obligations ([para 67]).

### C - Conclusion
**Holding and Impact ([paras 69–69])**  
The Constitutional Court declared both provisions *constitutionally invalid* as they failed to provide adequate discretion for judicial officers and imposed compulsory detention without adequately justifying such measures:
1. Section 77(6)(a)(i) was invalidated regarding mandatory detention of adults and children with no discretion under resource constraints.
2. Section 77(6)(a)(ii) was invalidated for requiring institutionalisation irrespective of assessed needs, thus undermining individual rights under the Constitution ([para 69]).

The declaration of invalidity was suspended for 24 months, allowing Parliament the opportunity to amend the law, underscoring a balanced approach to uphold public safety and individual rights, as mandated by the Constitution ([para 69]). This ruling serves as a pivotal judicial affirmation of safeguarding the rights of mentally ill and disabled individuals within the legal framework, potentially reinforcing similar legislative reforms domestically and influencing broader international norms.
