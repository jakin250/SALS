Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note: Geallal Raghubar v The State (148/12) [2012] ZASCA 188

## F – Facts

The appellant, Geallal Raghubar, was charged with indecent assault in the Verulam Regional Court, KwaZulu-Natal on 18 February 2009, related to events alleged to have occurred between 2000 and 2004. The complainant, referred to as "P," was 14 years old at the time of the trial and was allegedly sexually assaulted when he was six years old. The charge claimed that Raghubar unlawfully and intentionally inserted his penis into P's anus. Following trial, Raghubar was convicted and sentenced to ten years' imprisonment. After his appeal was dismissed by the KwaZulu-Natal High Court, he sought further relief from the Supreme Court of Appeal.

The complainant provided testimony via an intermediary through a closed-circuit camera to shield him from the courtroom environment. Concerns emerged regarding the compliance of the trial court with the provisions laid out in sections 162, 163, and 164 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 regarding the competency of child witnesses. The magistrate’s assessment of P’s competency, based on limited questioning about his age and education, was met with skepticism. The court noted that the intermediate questioning employed was crucial, especially for young witnesses, and that proper procedures were not adhered to ([1]–[9]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues presented in this case revolved around:
1. **Compliance with Procedural Safeguards**: Whether the trial court adequately ensured that the complainant understood the nature and importance of the truth, as stipulated in sections 162, 163, and 164 of the Criminal Procedure Act ([3], [5]).
2. **Reliability of the Complainant's Testimony**: Whether the testimony of the complainant was admissible and reliable enough to sustain a conviction given the concerns regarding his competency and the absence of corroborative evidence ([10], [20]).
3. **Cautionary Rule Application**: Whether the trial court properly applied the cautionary rule concerning the evidence of a single child witness in sexual misconduct cases ([11], [13]).

## R – Rules

The primary legal standards relevant to this case include:
1. **Sections of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977**: Sections 162, 163, and 164 outline the methods by which witnesses, particularly children, may be evaluated for competency in testifying. These sections stipulate the need for a witness to understand the oath or affirmation they are under ([4]).
   
2. **Cautionary Rule**: Well-established jurisprudence dictates that the evidence of a single witness, especially a child in sexual assault cases, requires careful scrutiny due to the potential unreliability of such testimony, as established in cases like *R v Manda* and *S v J* ([11], [13]).

3. **Right to a Fair Trial**: The need to guarantee the accused’s right to a fair trial as enshrined in the South African Constitution, particularly regarding the admissibility of testimony from witnesses who may not fully comprehend the gravity and implications of their statements ([9]).

## A – Application

In analyzing the case, the Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized the trial magistrate’s approach to ascertaining P’s competency. The court held that the magistrate failed to adequately explore whether P could differentiate between truth and falsehood, merely relying on simplistic inquiries about his education and age. The appellate court emphasized the Constitutional Court's directives on the necessity of skilled questioning tailored to a child's comprehension level ([7], [9]).

Additionally, the court found significant gaps and contradictions in the complainant's testimony. P had recounted various incidents without providing clear timelines or consistency, which raised serious questions about reliability. The medical evidence also failed to confirm the occurrence of the alleged assault, with the findings being inconclusive and contradictory regarding the assertion of prior anal penetration ([14], [15]). The trial magistrate’s judgement appeared influenced by confirmation bias, disregarding contradictions while favoring the State's case.

The appellate court noted that the evidentiary burden was not met, cautioning against the dangers of convictions based on isolated witness accounts without corroboration. The criticisms of Raghubar’s defense were also noted to lack fairness, highlighting that the trial court had a responsibility to consider the evidence against the backdrop of innocence until proven guilty ([19]).

## C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, citing that the initial conviction could not be sustained due to grave misdirections by the trial court concerning both the procedural handling of child testimony and the credibility of the complainant’s evidence. The appellate court concluded that the evidential shortcomings precluded a conviction beyond a reasonable doubt. The implications of this ruling emphasize the judiciary's obligation to uphold procedural safeguards, particularly in sensitive cases involving minors and allegations of sexual misconduct, ensuring that the rights of the accused are preserved while maintaining justice for complainants. Thus, the appeal was granted, and Raghubar's conviction and sentence were set aside ([20]).

This case illustrates significant challenges in the adjudication of sexual offences involving child witnesses, reinforcing the need for careful procedural adherence and the cautious application of evidentiary principles to prevent miscarriages of justice.
