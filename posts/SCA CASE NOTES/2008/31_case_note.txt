# FIRAC Case Note: Casino Enterprises (Pty) Ltd (Swaziland) v The Gauteng Gambling Board (91/07)

**Neutral Citation**: [2008] ZASCA 31  
**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Date Delivered**: 28 March 2008  
**Judges**: Howie P, Mpati DP, Nugent JA, Hurt AJA, Snyders AJA

## F – Facts ([paras 1–5])

This case involves a protracted legal dispute between Casino Enterprises (Pty) Ltd, a Swaziland-based internet casino operator, and the Gauteng Gambling Board (the Board). The appellant operates its casino without a license in South Africa, despite advertising its operations within Gauteng through local radio stations. Following warnings from the Board regarding the illegality of such advertisements per the Gauteng Gambling Act 4 of 1995 and the National Gambling Act 7 of 2004, the broadcasters withdrew the ads.

In response, the appellant filed a motion in the Pretoria High Court in October 2004, seeking a declaratory order asserting that gambling transactions initiated by Gauteng residents at its casino occur in Swaziland and therefore do not infringe either provincial or national gaming regulations. Both parties submitted affidavits featuring expert opinions regarding the nature of internet gambling, culminating in a referral to trial, with the founding affidavit allowed to serve as a simple summons ([paras 6–8]).

Upon filing an amended declaration, the Board raised an exception on the grounds that the appellant failed to allege the existence of a required gambling license, effectively challenging the legitimacy of the appellant’s claim ([para 7]).

## I – Issues ([paras 8–9])

The core issues before the court include:

1. Whether the appellant’s gambling operations could be legally conducted without a South African casino license, given that the appellant is registered in Swaziland.
2. The validity of the Board’s exception, which contended the absence of a necessary allegation regarding the licensing requirements under both the Gauteng and National Acts ([para 9]).

## R – Rules ([paras 10–12])

The relevant legislative framework consists of the Gauteng Gambling Act and the National Gambling Act. The Gauteng Act explicitly stipulates that any gambling related advertisements must come from entities that hold valid licenses. The National Act further defines the parameters of "interactive games," mandating that no person may engage in or advertise gambling without a proper license.

The critical legal precedents stem from the interpretation of these acts, particularly the conditions under which conducting gambling operations is permitted ([paras 10–11]).

## A – Application ([paras 12–18])

The court's assessment revolved around whether the appellant needed to demonstrate the absence of a need for a license or whether the Board could challenge the sufficiency of the appellant’s facts. The judge noted a significant misconception in the argument against the need for a declaratory order based on the alleged absence of activities in Gauteng. The court established that a declaration was necessary if the appellant's claims were validated.

Further, the court found that the exception raised by the Board presented contradictions, as it did not adhere to the prescribed legal standards for an excipient. The Board's assertion that the appellant must allege the necessity of a South African license misapplied legal principles; if the appellant contended it did not need such a license, then its failure to allege otherwise should not constitute a basis for an exception.

The court's reasoning highlighted the absence of factual support for the Board’s inferences regarding the online gambling activities occurring within Gauteng, thus warranting a dismissal of the exception ([paras 13–16]).

## C – Conclusion ([paras 19–20])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded in favor of the appellant. The appeal was granted, and the previous court’s ruling was set aside, with the court affirming that the Board’s exception was dismissed. The judgment clarified that the appellant's assertions should be subjected to a proper trial where factual and legal determinations could be made comprehensively.

This ruling holds crucial implications for online gambling operations in South Africa, establishing that advertisements for foreign gambling operations do not inherently contravene local laws unless it can be demonstrated that such operations specifically breach existing provincial statutes. Additionally, the court suggested that future litigation might avoid trial methods if the parties could reach an agreement based on expert testimony and factual clarity ([para 20]).

This case note encapsulates the dispute's complexity while analyzing significant legal principles regarding licensing and the operational scope of gambling laws as applied to foreign entities. The attention to legislative text is crucial in understanding the ruling's foundation and its potential effects on online gambling regulation going forward.
