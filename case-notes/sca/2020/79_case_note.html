Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 79

F - Facts

The case of AfriForum NPC v Chairperson of the Council of the University of South Africa & Others revolves around the University of South Africa (UNISA) adopting a new language policy that replaced its dual-language medium of instruction, utilizing both Afrikaans and English, with an English-only medium. The policy was implemented following decisions made by the Council and Senate of UNISA, which were contested by AfriForum, representing concerns primarily for Afrikaans-speaking students ([2]–[4]).

AfriForum sought to challenge these resolutions on the grounds that they infringed on the rights enshrined in Section 29(2) of the South African Constitution, which provides for the right to receive education in the official language of one’s choice where such education is reasonably practicable ([48]). The organization contended that no feasibility study had been conducted to confirm the impracticability of offering instruction in Afrikaans alongside English, thus the changes imposed by the new language policy were unlawful and unconstitutional ([14], [31], [33]).

The Gauteng Division of the High Court previously dismissed AfriForum's application, leading them to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([2], [23]).

I - Issues

The issues presented to the Supreme Court of Appeal were threefold:

  1. Whether the decisions made by UNISA's Council and Senate contravened Section 29(2) of the Constitution, thereby infringing on the rights of Afrikaans-speaking students to receive education in their mother tongue ([26]).
  2. Whether the Senate had followed its own internal rules during the adoption of the new language policy, potentially breaching the principle of legality ([26]).
  3. Whether UNISA adequately consulted with the stakeholders most affected by the new policy, and if their review process was rational and justifiable ([26], [41]).

R - Rules

The legal rules relevant to this case primarily derive from the Constitution of South Africa, particularly Section 29(2), which asserts the right to receive education in one’s language of choice where it is reasonably practicable ([28]).

In addition to constitutional requirements, the review applied the principles established in University of the Free State v AfriForum which outlined that language policy decisions must not only prioritize operational efficacy but must also respect language rights without unjustifiable exclusion or discrimination ([30]). Furthermore, the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) was initially invoked by AfriForum but later abandoned due to a judicial interpretation that deemed the language policy decisions non-administrative ([3]).

A - Application

In considering whether UNISA's actions violated Section 29(2), the Court rigorously examined the procedural aspects of how the new language policy was adopted. The Supreme Court found that UNISA failed to provide substantial justification for the removal of Afrikaans from the list of languages of instruction, noting that insufficient evidence was presented to support claims of impracticability ([24], [34]). Key discrepancies pointed out included a lack of statistical data regarding the demands for Afrikaans instruction placed before decision-making bodies, which made it difficult to justify the perceived necessity for the language switch ([36], [41]).

The Court also scrutinized the claims that procedural irregularities had occurred during the Senate's decision-making process. It found that even though UNISA asserted compliance, the lack of accurate data undermined their position, and the absence of any legitimate consultation with stakeholders facilitated a breach of procedural rationality ([46], [47]).

Moreover, in addressing UNISA's arguments around cost-saving and efficiency, the Court noted that the rationale provided was not backed by comprehensive data or considered fairly against the potential viability of retaining Afrikaans instruction, thus failing to meet the thresholds of rationality and reasonableness expected under the Constitution ([44], [45]).

C - Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal held that the resolutions of UNISA's Council and Senate were unconstitutional and unlawful, primarily due to their failure to justify the removal of Afrikaans as a medium of instruction under Section 29(2) of the Constitution. The appeal was thus upheld, and directions were issued to restore Afrikaans as a medium of instruction, highlighting the necessity of diligent and balanced consideration of all stakeholders' rights in educational governance ([48]).

The implications of this ruling reaffirm the importance of regulatory compliance and justifications in language policies within universities, ensuring that practices not only reflect operational feasibility but also uphold constitutional rights of linguistic diversity. The decision serves as a crucial reminder for educational institutions of their obligations to recognize and accommodate cultural and linguistic rights in their policy-making processes ([27], [29]).
