# Case Note: G Smit v The State (144/08) [2010] ZASCA 84

## FIRAC Method Case Note

### F - Facts

On 24 February 2005, Gerhard Smit (the appellant) was convicted in a regional court of attempted rape and rape, receiving sentences of three years and ten years' imprisonment, respectively, to run concurrently. The charges stemmed from allegations made by a 16-year-old complainant concerning events that took place in the appellant’s home during the night of 3 to 4 September 2003, where both counts involved the same complainant, who was boarding with the appellant’s family ([1]).

The appellant denied his guilt, contending that any intimate contact had been consensual. He and the complainant had gone to a nearby hotel for drinks, returned home, and the complainant alleged she had been initially assaulted in a ‘Wendy house’ before being raped in the bedroom she shared with the appellant's siblings ([3]–[8]). The appellant claimed that the complainant had initiated sexual contact, which she later denied ([9]–[10]).

Initially attempted rape charges were characterized by the complainant's evidence, which the trial court accepted. However, the high court later dismissed the appeal against the convictions on 6 August 2007, and Smit's subsequent applications for leave to appeal were rejected. Eventually, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was approached, with an order to address the merits of the conviction ([1]–[2]).

### I - Issues

The primary issues for determination included:

1. Whether the trial court and the high court erred in accepting the complainant’s testimony as credible and reliable beyond a reasonable doubt ([12]).
2. Whether the actions described by the appellant constituted a plausible defense against the charges of attempted rape and rape ([12]).

### R - Rules

The legal framework applicable to this case included:

- **Credibility of Witnesses**: The standard of proof required in criminal cases is "beyond a reasonable doubt", necessitating that the court must scrutinize the credibility of witnesses closely (S v Boesak 2000 (1) SACR 633 (SCA)) ([2], [11]).
- **Definition of Attempted Rape**: To prove attempted rape, it must be established that there was a direct and immediate action towards the commission of the crime, as defined under South African law ([11]).
- **Consent**: The jurisprudence reflects that consent must be unequivocally given for sexual acts; any reasonable doubt as to consent must benefit the accused ([11], [12]).

### A - Application

The SCA engaged in a thorough analysis of both testimonies, ultimately scrutinizing the reliability of the complainant’s account against the appellant’s claims. The high court had accepted her testimony as "satisfactory in every material respect," but the SCA found this assessment inconsistent with the evidence presented ([14]). 

1. **Inconsistencies in the Complainant’s Testimony**: The court pointed out significant discrepancies in her account, particularly during cross-examination where her narrative evolved without logical foundation, suggesting embellishment or fabrication ([15], [19], [20]). Her failure to report the alleged rape immediately to her surroundings coupled with her behavior post-incident raised further doubts about her credibility ([21]–[24]).

2. **Credibility of the Accused**: In contrast, the court considered the appellant's version not as inherently improbable but rather consistent with the circumstances described. His story was corroborated by the testimony of his sister, Jolindi, which contradicted many aspects of the complainant's narrative ([18], [19]).

The court also highlighted that the context of sexual consent was crucial, noting that the nature of the interaction between the appellant and complainant appeared to align with consensual conduct, particularly in the absence of any protests during or immediately following the alleged incidents ([13], [21]).

### C - Conclusion

Ultimately, the SCA concluded that the conviction was unsafe due to the significant doubts surrounding the credibility of the complainant’s testimony and the lack of compelling evidence to support the charges. Thus, the appeal was upheld, and the prior convictions and sentences for attempted rape and rape were set aside ([27]).

The ruling signifies an essential application of the legal principles concerning the burden of proof in criminal law, emphasizing that any reasonable doubt must benefit the accused. This case underscores the necessity of thorough evidential highlights in cases involving such serious allegations, where individual freedoms are at stake and the integrity of judicial outcomes is paramount.
