Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: Molema v The State (555/10) [2011] ZASCA 62

## F – Facts [paras 1–26]
The case *Molema v The State* concerns an appeal by Isaac Molema (the appellant) against the refusal of the South Gauteng High Court (Mailula J and Mabesele AJ) to grant him leave to appeal his conviction and sentence for two counts of rape in the Regional Court of Krugersdorp. Allegations against Molema arose from two incidents occurring in June and December of 2006, where he was accused of unlawfully and intentionally engaging in sexual intercourse with Ziphora Mahlangu, aged 38, without her consent.

Molema pleaded not guilty but admitted to having sexual relations with Mahlangu, claiming the intercourse was consensual. The Regional Court convicted him on both counts, leading to a sentence of 15 years’ imprisonment, with the counts treated as one for sentencing purposes. Following this, Molema's applications for leave to appeal were denied at multiple judicial levels, culminating in his appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which assessed whether there were reasonable prospects of success for an appeal against his conviction and sentence.

## I – Issues [paras 1–9]
The primary legal question in this case is whether the appellant had reasonable prospects of success in an appeal against both his conviction and sentence. This encompasses several sub-issues:
1. Whether the trial court misjudged the credibility of the complainant's testimony.
2. Whether the magistrate's decisions reflected erroneous legal standards regarding the burden of proof in criminal cases.
3. The assessment of the mitigating and aggravating factors during sentencing, specifically regarding the nature of the crimes.

## R – Rules [paras 14–23]
The SCA relied on established principles of criminal law and procedure, particularly those embedded in the *Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977,* which sets out the criteria for appealing convictions and the burden of proof in criminal trials. It highlighted that the state must prove its case beyond a reasonable doubt and that findings of fact made by the trial court are presumptively correct unless shown to be vitiated by irregularity or incorrect examination of evidence.

Moreover, the case references principles related to the evaluation of evidence in sexual offense cases, particularly the need for courts to be sensitive to the issues of consent and the credibility of victims, as evidenced in *S v York* 2002 (1) SACR 111 (SCA). The SCA also reaffirmed the judicial duty to provide a clear message regarding the serious nature of rape offenses and the judicial stance against them, as noted in *S v Chapman* 1997 (2) SACR 3 (SCA).

## A – Application [paras 6–25]
The SCA evaluated the merits of the appeal by closely analyzing the evidence presented in the original trial. Mr. Schaefer, representing Molema, argued that the trial magistrate incorrectly assessed the credibility of the complainant, arguing that various discrepancies in her account undermined its reliability. Critically, Schaefer claimed that the trial court misconstrued the burden of proof and that the magistrate unfairly characterized Molema's defenses.

Despite this, the SCA found the trial court's evaluation of the evidence to be thorough and justified. The court noted that the magistrate had properly considered the credibility of witnesses, including the complainant, who had no motive to lie. It was emphasized that the appellant's defense was a bare denial of the accusations without substantial corroborating evidence to challenge the complainant's testimony.

In assessing the sentencing, the SCA weighed the factors the magistrate considered, including Molema’s age, family circumstances, and community ties against the aggravating factors tied to the nature of the crime, notably the exploitation of a position of trust between the appellant and complainant, who had been his employee and lived in his premises.

The court maintained that the 15-year sentence was not excessively harsh in light of the seriousness of the offenses and in the context of South Africa’s prevailing societal issues regarding violence against women.

## C – Conclusion [paras 25–26]
Ultimately, the SCA dismissed the appeal, concluding that there were no reasonable prospects of success in challenging either the conviction or the sentence. The court affirmed the magistrate’s findings and reinforced the message that the judicial system remains committed to a zero-tolerance policy on sexual offenses. The court’s decision underscores the importance of upholding convictions in cases where the evidence establishes guilt beyond reasonable doubt, while also ensuring that the victims’ narratives are taken seriously within the legal framework.

The appeal's dismissal serves both to validate the original findings of the trial court and to highlight the judiciary's role in safeguarding the rights and dignity of victims of sexual violence within the South African legal system.
