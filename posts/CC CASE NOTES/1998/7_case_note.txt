Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1998

# FIRAC Case Note: *Mello v The State* (CCT 5/98)

## F – Facts:
The case revolves around William Mello and Constantina Botolo (the appellants), who, along with two accomplices, were charged with dealing in and alternatively possessing dagga, a prohibited substance, under the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act (the Act) ([1]). The evidence presented during their trial in the Pretoria Magistrate Court revealed that several packages of dagga were discovered concealed in a truck driven by one of the accused, with the appellants as passengers ([1]). The Magistrate relied on the presumption created in section 20 of the Act, which states that if a drug is found near the accused, it is presumed they possess it unless proven otherwise. Consequently, the appellants were convicted based on this presumption ([1]).

The appellants subsequently appealed their conviction, which led to the Transvaal High Court referring the constitutional validity of section 20 to the Constitutional Court, citing potential inconsistency with section 25(3)(c) of the interim Constitution ([2]). The referral process itself faced delays, but the appellants were not incarcerated during this time ([3]). The Attorney-General later indicated that he accepted that section 20 was inconsistent with the Constitution ([4]). 

## I – Issues:
The primary issue brought before the Constitutional Court was the constitutionality of section 20 of the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act, particularly regarding whether it violates the right to a fair trial, which includes the presumption of innocence as stated in section 25(3)(c) of the interim Constitution ([2]). The court needed to determine if the presumption imposed by this section constituted a reverse onus, thereby infringing on the constitutional rights of the accused ([4]).

## R – Rules:
The rules concerning the presumption of innocence and the burden of proof are formulated under the interim Constitution. Section 25(3)(c) guarantees every accused person the right to be presumed innocent until proven guilty ([2]). Additionally, previous judgments in *S v Mbatha* and *S v Prinsloo* established that legal presumptions creating a reverse onus violate this fundamental right ([5]). The court must also evaluate whether any limitation of constitutional rights presented by legislation is reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society, as outlined in section 33(1) of the interim Constitution ([7]).

## A – Application:
The court's analysis focused on whether section 20 indeed imposed a reverse onus on the accused. It compared the language and implications of section 20 with similar presumption sections previously ruled unconstitutional in cases such as *S v Mbatha* and *Ntsele* ([6]), where the court voided provisions that shifted the burden of proof onto the accused. The court emphasized that such presumptions undermine the core of the presumption of innocence and shift the onus to the defendant to disprove guilt, consequently violating the right to a fair trial ([6]).

Furthermore, the court rejected any arguments that the presumption was proportionate or necessary for effective law enforcement. It asserted that the presumptions in question were couched in broad terms, leading to a significant risk of false convictions and disproportionately impacting innocent persons ([8]). The court concluded that section 20, like the invalidated provisions, did not meet the constitutional standards and was unjustifiable ([10]).

## C – Conclusion:
The Constitutional Court declared section 20 of the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 140 of 1992 inconsistent with section 25(3)(c) of the interim Constitution, rendering it invalid from the date of the judgment ([15]). The decision emphasized the importance of the presumption of innocence and reaffirmed the prosecution's burden of proof. The court ordered that the invalidation applied to all pending cases as of the decision date, reiterating that justice must be served for all individuals similarly positioned as the appellants ([15]). The ruling not only vindicated the appellants but also ensured that future legislative provisions would align with constitutional guarantees, reinforcing the principles of fairness and justice in the criminal justice system.
