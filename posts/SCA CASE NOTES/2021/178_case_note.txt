Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# FIRAC Case Note: Macsteel Tube and Pipe v Vowles Properties (680/2020)

**Neutral Citation:**  
Macsteel Tube and Pipe, a division of Macsteel Service Centres SA (Pty) Ltd v Vowles Properties (Pty) Ltd (680/2020) [2021] ZASCA 178 (17 December 2021).

### F – Facts 
The appeal stems from an interlocutory application for the amendment of the particulars of claim in a breach of lease agreement initiated by Vowles Properties (Pty) Ltd against Macsteel Tube and Pipe, a division of Macsteel Service Centres SA (Pty) Ltd ([1]). The lease agreement entered on 26 July 2009 required Macsteel to maintain the premises and prohibited subletting without Vowles' consent. In a claim filed on 11 December 2015, Vowles sought damages of R 1,567,096.03 for breach of the lease after its termination on 31 December 2012 ([2]).

Initially, Macsteel filed exceptions to the particulars of claim, leading to a July 2016 order whereby the original claim was set aside, and Vowles was to file amended particulars within 20 days ([3]). However, Vowles failed to comply promptly, resulting in multiple failed amendment attempts. After appointing new attorneys, Vowles lodged a Notice of Motion in June 2018 to amend its claim. Macsteel continued to oppose this motion, arguing that Vowles' amendment reintroduced a cause of action, essentially changing the defendant's identity ([5]–[7]).

On 24 October 2018, the regional court allowed Vowles to amend its particulars of claim, prompting Macsteel to appeal this decision on the grounds that the amendments violated prior court orders and introduced new parties into the claim ([7]).

### I – Issues 
The appeal raised several significant legal questions ([8]):
1. Whether the regional court made a definitive order regarding jurisdiction that cannot be altered. 
2. Whether the amendment constitutes a new cause of action or party due to prescription issues.
3. The appealability of the order allowing the amendment.

### R – Rules 
The relevant legal principles were largely derived from the Magistrates’ Court Act 32 of 1944, specifically Section 111, which allows amendments to claims before judgment as long as no party is prejudiced ([9]). Furthermore, numerous precedents articulate that amendments will generally be allowed unless shown to be mala fide or causing injustice that cannot be remedied by costs ([9]).

In _Zweni v Minister of Law and Order_, the Court articulated the nature of a "judgment or order," underscoring that an interlocutory order does not usually have final effect ([11]).

### A – Application 
The Court first addressed the appealability of the regional court's order, confirming that an order allowing amendment is typically not final, thus not appealable; it serves merely to set the stage for future proceedings ([12]).

On evaluating Macsteel's objections, the Court considered the legal arguments concerning jurisdiction based on the lease agreement ([14]–[15]). It found inadequate merit in the claim that the regional court had definitively determined jurisdictional issues. Furthermore, the analysis of the amendment's nature revealed that an augmentation of an existing claim does not equate to introducing a new cause of action, as established in precedents including _Moolman v Estate Moolman_ and _Foxlake Investments v Ultimate Raft Foundation Design Solutions_ ([16]–[19]).

Macsteel's assertions regarding naming a non-existent party were also dismissed. The regional court's conclusion that the descriptive changes aligned with the lease agreement and did not capture new parties upheld the validity of Vowles’ claim ([17]). Lastly, it was determined that any purported infringement of the July 2016 order did not negate Vowles’ right to proceed with amended particulars; Macsteel’s own actions indicated an understanding that the matter remained active and amendable ([21]–[22]).

Ultimately, the regional court's careful exercise of discretion, emphasizing the absence of prejudice to Macsteel, reaffirmed the general principle in civil procedure favoring amendments aimed at ensuring the ventilation of disputes ([23]–[27]).

### C – Conclusion 
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Macsteel’s appeal with costs, upholding the lower court's conclusion that the proposed amendments did not introduce a new cause of action or party, nor did they prejudice Macsteel’s ability to defend its interests. The judgment reaffirmed the process for amending claims under the Magistrates’ Court Act and stressed a judicial approach favoring the proper adjudication of disputes ([28]). This case illustrates the flexible nature of procedural law in civil claims while prioritizing equitable access to justice over rigid formalities.
