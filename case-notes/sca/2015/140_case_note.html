Supreme Court of Appeal

Tlhako v The State (129/15) [2015] ZASCA 140

The trial court did not inform the appellant of the intention to apply this Act in the event of a conviction, rendering the trial process contentious.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 [2015] ZASCA 140

F – Facts

[1] Gilbert Ngwako Tlhako (the appellant) was convicted on two counts of robbery with aggravating circumstances in the Polokwane Regional Court on 19 November 2003. The crimes, designated as counts 5 and 8, occurred in July and August 2001, respectively, involving the armed robbery of Mr. Manamela and his wife and Mr. Sebola and Ms. Madiba. The appellant and his accomplices threatened their victims with firearms, forced them to a cemetery, and stole various personal items and vehicles ([2]–[3]). The appellant was sentenced to an effective 30 years' imprisonment under the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 ([4]).

The trial court did not inform the appellant of the intention to apply this Act in the event of a conviction, rendering the trial process contentious. While the appeal against the convictions was dismissed, the court below deferred the sentencing issue to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) for reconsideration ([1]–[5]).

I – Issues

[6] The primary legal issue was whether the application of the sentencing provisions of the Criminal Law Amendment Act was appropriate, considering the appellant was not notified during the trial of the possibility of invoking these provisions. This failure could potentially constitute a breach of the constitutional right to a fair trial ([5]–[8]). Additionally, the fairness of the sentencing process itself was called into question, particularly regarding the harshness of the 30 years' imprisonment ([10]).

R – Rules

[7] The court referenced the provisions of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, which prescribes minimum sentences for serious crimes, alongside the constitutional right to a fair trial as enshrined in Section 35 of the Constitution of South Africa. The relevant case law reiterated that the prosecution must inform the accused of the intent to apply harsher sentencing provisions, allowing the accused to mount an appropriate defense ([7]–[9]).

The SCA also invoked principles from previous cases, such as S v Legoa, which emphasized that a just trial under the new constitutional framework demands “substantive fairness,” inherently inclusive of the sentencing process, as well as the obligation to inform the accused about potential sentencing enhancements ([7]).

A – Application

[8] The SCA concluded that the trial court misdirected itself by not alerting the appellant about the intended application of the Criminal Law Amendment Act, which constituted a fundamental unfairness in the trial proceedings ([9]). Given this procedural error, the SCA determined that upholding the original sentence of 30 years would not only be unjust but also shockingly excessive, thus meriting reconsideration ([10]).

The court acknowledged the appellant's personal circumstances, including his age and family responsibilities, but juxtaposed these with the serious nature of the crimes and their impact on society ([11]–[13]). It was noted that while the sentences must reflect the gravity of the offenses, they should not be excessively punitive to the point of devastation. In assessing the appropriate sentence, the SCA aimed for a balance between the interests of the appellant and societal protean concern over rising crime rates ([12]–[13]).

Consequently, the SCA ordered a reduction of each sentence to 10 years' imprisonment for both counts, highlighting that this reflected both the seriousness of the crimes committed and an understanding of the rehabilitative potential of the appellant ([14]).

C – Conclusion

[15] The SCA allowed the appeal, set aside the original sentences, and substituted them with a new sentence of 10 years’ imprisonment for each count, while declaring the appellant incompetent to possess a firearm. Further, the SCA ante-dated the imposition of these sentences to 15 December 2003, effectively considering the time already served ([15]).

This decision underscores the imperative for legal processes to respect the rights of the accused, particularly in relation to sentencing provisions, which highlight broader issues of procedural fairness in criminal justice, setting a significant precedent for future cases concerning excessive sentencing under the Criminal Law Amendment Act.
