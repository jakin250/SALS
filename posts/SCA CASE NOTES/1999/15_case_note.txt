# Case Note: Intercontinental Exports (Pty) Ltd v F T Fowles

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

[1] The appellant, Intercontinental Exports (Pty) Ltd (I.C.E.), sought default judgment against the respondent, Mr. Frank Fowles, in the Witwatersrand Local Division for rectification of a deed of suretyship. The application also requested payment of an alleged due amount, interest, and costs on an attorney-and-client scale ([1]). 

[2] Initially, the application was denied by Malan J, who also refused leave to appeal. Leave to appeal was later granted by the Chief Justice following a petition ([2]). During the appeal, the respondent did not appear ([2]).

[3] The deed of suretyship had inconsistencies, such as improper identification of the debtor and the terms concerning banking facilities, which were stated as not applicable to I.C.E., a company that did not operate as a bank ([3]–[5]). The particulars of claim elaborated that the company Security Depot (Pty) Ltd was indebted to I.C.E. for R2,178,844.43 due to goods ordered between 1996 and 1997, and that Fowles had entered into the suretyship as security for the company’s debts, not for his own ([6]).

[6] I.C.E. contended that the suretyship's misdescriptions stemmed from a mutual error. It sought to rectify the deed by changing the references from Fowles to the actual debtor, Security Depot ([6]).

### I – Issues

[12] The central legal issue was whether the suretyship agreement was formally valid as per Section 6 of the General Law Amendment Act 50 of 1956, which necessitates the identification of the creditor, principal debtor, and surety for the contract to be valid ([7]). If valid, this would affect the enforceability of the rectification sought by I.C.E. ([12], [21]).

### R – Rules

[7] Section 6 of the General Law Amendment Act stipulates that no contract of suretyship shall be valid unless in writing and signed by the surety. This includes all essential terms being present, particularly the identities of the creditor, principal debtor, and surety ([8]).

[9] Prior cases, such as *Fourlamel (Pty) Ltd v Maddison* and *Magwaza v Heenan*, establish that a contract null and void due to a lack of formal compliance cannot be rectified ([9], [10]). A well-rounded understanding of what constitutes formal validity and its implications for potential rectification is critical ([11]).

### A – Application

[15] The court examined the suretyship document itself to determine formal validity, noting that it clearly identified Mr. Fowles as the principal debtor in clause 1.2 and that the surety identified in clause 1.3 was also Fowles ([15]). The court disagreed with the trial court's finding that left identities blank; the mere existence of the identities sufficed for formal validity as long as they did not refer to the same person ([15], [17]).

[16] It was conceded by I.C.E.’s counsel that a deed of suretyship is invalid if the principal debtor and surety are the same entity. However, the court opined that in this specific case, the use of differing names (Mr. Frank Fowles for the principal debtor and Frank Turner Fowles for the surety) did not inherently denote the same person, addressing a potential point of confusion ([16], [17]).

[18] Further clarifications and examples from prior jurisprudence illustrated that as long as the relevant identities were maintained and differentiated within the suretyship, the contract could still be valid ([18]). Thus, on the grounds that the agreement identified the parties correctly, the court ruled in favor of the appellant ([21]).

### C – Conclusion

[21] The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that the suretyship was formally valid, allowing the rectification of the deed as requested by the appellant. In addition, the court acknowledged the complexities surrounding costs as stipulated in the suretyship, leading to a cautious discretion regarding attorney and client costs and ultimately awarding only party and party scale costs ([22]–[28]).

The implications of this judgment highlight the necessity for precision in drafting surety agreements. This case reinforces the judicial movement towards ensuring that technicalities do not obstruct the enforcement of the parties’ true intentions while also clarifying the limits of contractual agreements concerning costs. The court exercised significant discretion in deciding on the costs, emphasizing judicial direction over contractual stipulations.
