Supreme Court of Appeal

City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality v Engen Petroleum Ltd [2009] ZASCA 5 (10 March 2009)

The respondents asserted their entitlement to damages under subsections 67(3) and (4) of the Local Government Ordinance 17 of 1939 (T), which allows for compensation related to the permanent closure or diversion of streets ([3]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009 [2009] ZASCA 5 (10 March 2009)

FIRAC Analysis

F – Facts

This case stems from the significant alterations made to Grayston Drive in Sandton, Johannesburg, by the City Council between 1992 and 1994, which involved the construction of a flyover at the intersection with Katherine Street ([1]). The second respondent, Sandton Gate Service Station CC, operates a filling station adjacent to this intersection. Following the construction, Sandton Gate and Engen Petroleum Ltd filed suit against the City Council, alleging that the changes impeded vehicle access to their premises, resulting in financial losses ([2]–[3]).

The respondents asserted their entitlement to damages under subsections 67(3) and (4) of the Local Government Ordinance 17 of 1939 (T), which allows for compensation related to the permanent closure or diversion of streets ([3]). The City Council acknowledged procedural non-compliance with these statutory requirements, asserting no notice was necessary due to the lack of permanent closure or diversion of the road ([4]).

The High Court's inquiry determined whether the construction work amounted to a permanent diversion or closure as per section 67 of the Ordinance. The court found that although the construction did not close the road, it constituted a permanent diversion ([5]–[6]).

I – Issues

The primary legal question presented for the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the High Court correctly judged that the elevation of Grayston Drive constituted a permanent diversion within the meaning of section 67 of the Local Government Ordinance. Central to this discussion was the definition of "diversion" and whether it could include vertical changes, such as the creation of a flyover ([5]–[6]).

R – Rules

The relevant law is encapsulated within section 67 of the Local Government Ordinance, which outlines the requirements for the permanent closing or diversion of streets, including provisions for public notice and compensation for affected parties ([3]). The section necessitates that any anticipated adverse impacts on property owners, lessees, or occupiers due to changes in the road must be incidental to the proposed diversion or closure.

In interpreting the term "divert," the court referenced the prior case of Bellevue Motors CC v Johannesburg City Council, which established that traffic flow changes alone do not constitute a diversion if the street's original path remains ([8]). Conversely, definitions and contextual interpretations from various cases were scrutinized to determine if vertical modifications could also fit this operational framework ([10]).

A – Application

The appeal hinged on whether the elevation of Grayston Drive constituted a diversion under the interpretation of section 67. The City Council argued that the flyover did not change the road's directional path horizontally, thus not satisfying the criteria for a diversion ([7]). They relied on dictionary definitions that suggested diversion entails a lateral change ([10]–[11]).

However, the respondents argued that the conceptualization of "diversion" should extend beyond the horizontal plane to encompass vertical alterations affecting access. They posited that the construction materially altered access to the filling station, which constitutes the kind of financial harm section 67 was designed to address ([16]–[17]).

In deliberating, the court noted that the significant change in elevation resulted in a new configuration impacting the patrons' ability to access the filling station from the flyover, effectively diverting traffic, irrespective of the original horizontal pathway ([18]). The rationale established that while the outer lane remained accessible, the alterations materially changed the dynamics for motorists navigating the area.

Consequently, the ruling clarified that section 67's application involves both horizontal and vertical changes, provided they lead to a negative financial impact on adjacent business operations ([15]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court's ruling, affirming that the construction of the flyover constituted a permanent diversion within the purview of section 67 of the Local Government Ordinance, allowing for compensation to the affected businesses. The appeal was dismissed with costs, including those incurred for employing two counsel ([20]).

This decision sets a pivotal precedent in interpreting statutory provisions concerning infrastructure developments, reinforcing the importance of compensating affected property owners for both horizontal and vertical alterations that materially impact access and use. Moreover, the ruling underscores the necessity for municipalities to adhere strictly to procedural requirements in road diversions to mitigate potential liabilities.
