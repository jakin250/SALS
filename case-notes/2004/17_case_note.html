Constitutional Court

Bhe and Others v Magistrate, Khayelitsha and Others [2004] ZACC 10 (CCT 49/03)

The case revolves around the constitutionality of section 23 of the Black Administration Act 38 of 1927 ("the Act") and the application of the principle of male primogeniture in intestate succession under customary law ([1–3]).

Constitutional Court 2004 [2004] ZACC 10 (CCT 49/03)

F – Facts

The case revolves around the constitutionality of section 23 of the Black Administration Act 38 of 1927 ("the Act") and the application of the principle of male primogeniture in intestate succession under customary law ([1–3]). Three interconnected cases were heard together by the Constitutional Court: Bhe v Magistrate, Khayelitsha; Shibi v Mantabeni Freddy Sithole; and South African Human Rights Commission v President of the Republic of South Africa. The first case, Bhe, involved Nontupheko Maretha Bhe (the third applicant), representing her two minor daughters Nonkululeko (1994) and Anelisa (2001), following the intestate death of their father, Vuyo Elius Mgolombane ([9–10]). The second applicant, the Women's Legal Centre Trust, acted in the public interest to challenge the discriminatory effects of the legislation on female descendants and illegitimate children ([10–11]). Following the father's death, his father was appointed as the representative of the estate, a decision made in accordance with section 23 of the Act, which does not recognize the rights of the daughters to inherit ([12–15]).

The application sought to declare that the estate of the deceased be administered according to the principles of the Intestate Succession Act 81 of 1987, which affords equal inheritance rights irrespective of gender or marital status, unlike the provisions under the Black Administration Act ([19]).

In the Shibi matter, the applicant, Charlotte Shibi, sought similar consideration after the intestate death of her brother Daniel Solomon Sithole in 1995, which also fell under the scrutinized provisions of section 23. The High Court found that the appointments and distributions made under the Act were unconstitutional and subsequently issued an order declaring the relevant sections invalid ([21–27]).

I – Issues

  1. Is section 23 of the Black Administration Act unconstitutional under the South African Constitution, particularly concerning equality and human dignity?
  2. Does the application of the principle of male primogeniture within the context of customary law violate the rights to equality (section 9) and dignity (section 10) protected by the Constitution?
  3. If found unconstitutional, what remedial actions should be taken to ensure equitable administration of estates under customary law?

R – Rules

The ruling hinges on multiple provisions of the South African Constitution, including:

  • Equality Rights (Section 9): Guarantees equality before the law and prohibits discrimination on various grounds, including gender and birth.
  • Human Dignity (Section 10): Affirms that everyone has inherent dignity, and it must be respected and protected.
  • Rights of Children (Section 28): Places the best interests of the child as a primary consideration in all actions concerning children.
  • Section 39(2): Guides courts when interpreting legislation to promote the spirit, purport, and objects of the Bill of Rights.
  • Customary Law Recognition (Section 211): Supports the applicability of customary law, provided its provisions align with the Constitution ([41–42]).

A – Application

The Court established the unconstitutional nature of section 23 based on its discriminatory historical context. It emphasized that the provision effectively perpetuated apartheid-era laws that institutionalized racial and gender discrimination ([60–62]). Additionally, a significant focus was placed on the inherent injustice caused by the primogeniture rule, whereby women and illegitimate children had no rights to inherit, violating their dignity and equality under the Constitution ([93–94]).

Both Langa DCJ and Ngcobo J emphasized that the application of male primogeniture was fundamentally out of step with contemporary societal norms and must evolve to accommodate changing values concerning gender roles. The analysis concluded that the identification of rights to inherit based on gender or birth status constituted a direct violation of constitutional protections ([190–192]). The argument also addressed how traditional patterns have transformed due to economic and social developments, necessitating adjustments in customary law practices to remain relevant ([80–82], [222]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court held that section 23 of the Black Administration Act is unconstitutional and invalid, alongside its regulations and the male primogeniture rule, which discriminated against women and extra-marital children. The order also highlighted that intestate estates for deceased individuals previously governed by the Act should now be managed according to the provisions of the Intestate Succession Act.

The implications of this decision extend beyond immediate redistributive justice, advocating for legislative reform to address gaps in the application of law pertaining to indigenous systems. The judgment calls for immediate implementation of equitable standards in estate administration, thereby advancing the movement towards non-discrimination and equality as guaranteed by the Constitution ([136]).

In conclusion, this case heralded a landmark shift in the intersection of customary law, gender rights, and constitutional protections, affirming the court's role in developing law to reflect contemporary values while respecting the nuances of social customs ([117–120]).
