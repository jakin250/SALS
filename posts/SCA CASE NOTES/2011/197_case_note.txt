# Case Note: Claasen v Bester (872/10) [2011] 197 ZASCA

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–11])

In **Claasen v Bester**, the parties involved were farmers in the Free State, once friends, now embroiled in a legal dispute regarding the sale of a farm. Mr. Michiel Daniël Bester (the respondent) faced financial difficulties, prompting Mr. Evert Bakkes Claasen (the appellant) to purchase the farm to prevent foreclosure by the Land Bank. The agreement included a provision allowing Bester a right of first refusal or the right to repurchase the farm, although the specific terms were not documented in the deed of sale ([para 1]). 

Bester later sought a declaration that the sale was void or voidable while offering to repay the purchase price. Claasen, however, raised a special plea of prescription, arguing that Bester’s claim had lapsed ([paras 2–3]). The Free State High Court ruled against Claasen, finding that Bester's claim had not prescribed. This ruling led to Claasen's appeal ([paras 10–11]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 3–11])

The primary legal issue revolved around whether Bester's failure to recognize that the contract was void or voidable constituted a "fact" for the purposes of Section 12(3) of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 ([para 11]). This provision dictates that a debt is not deemed due until the creditor is aware of the identity of the debtor and the facts from which the debt arises. Key questions included:

1. Does a legal conclusion regarding the voidability or validity of a contract qualify as a "fact" under the Prescription Act ([para 10])?
2. When did Bester acquire knowledge of the necessary facts to trigger the running of prescription?

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 12–15])

The court relied on several judicial precedents concerning the Prescription Act:

1. **Drennan Maud & Partners v Pennington Town Board** 1998 (3) SA 200 (SCA) emphasized that “debt” refers broadly to “claim,” requiring recognition of the facts underlying the claim ([para 12]).
2. **Truter v Deysel** 2006 (4) SA 168 (SCA) clarified that a debt is deemed due when the creditor possesses a complete cause of action, encompassing all facts required to support a claim ([para 13]).
3. **Minister of Finance v Gore** 2007 (1) SA 111 (SCA) reiterated that the prescription period does not wait for a creditor to fully understand their legal rights; mere knowledge of the facts is sufficient ([para 14]).
4. The court further recognized that preceding rulings confirmed that knowledge of legal conclusions is not necessary for the prescription period to commence ([para 15]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 16–18])

The court assessed the timeline of Bester's knowledge regarding the enforceability of the "right" to repurchase the farm. On **3 March 2004**, Bester obtained a copy of the deed of sale and became aware that no enforceable price was stipulated for the buy-back provision ([para 16]). While Bester could believe the provision was enforceable, this belief did not affect the running of prescription. His knowledge of the absence of a price constituted the factual basis for his claim, fulfilling the requirements set forth in the Prescription Act ([para 16]).

The court dismissed Bester's argument that Claasen’s actions post-3 March 2004 could interrupt the running of prescription, citing Section 14 of the Prescription Act, which allows interruption only in acknowledgment of a debt. Since Claasen was not legally indebted to him, no further acknowledgment was possible ([para 17]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 18–19])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Claasen's appeal, affirming that the special plea of prescription was valid and that Bester's claim had prescribed by the time he issued summons on **14 December 2007** ([para 18]). The ruling underscored the distinction between the factual basis for a claim and legal conclusions about a claim's enforceability. The court concluded that knowledge of the legal status of a claim does not delay the commencement of the prescription period ([para 19]).

The implications of this judgment affirm the principles governing the Prescription Act and clarify that creditors need only be aware of the factual circumstances underlying their claims, not the legal conclusions arising from them. This case reinforces creditors' responsibilities to know the pertinent facts of their claims promptly to avoid the prescription of their rights.
