Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: Dormac Marine and Engineering (Pty) Ltd v CA Henneberry (305/10) [2011] ZASCA 63

**F – Facts**  
The case concerns an injury sustained by the respondent, Calmin Andrew Henneberry, while working on a shipping vessel at Dormac's Durban dry dock ([1]–[3]). Henneberry, an employee of an independent contractor, Camrod Engineering CC, claimed damages for bodily injuries when a chain block broke while lifting a steel plate, causing a severe injury to his left hand, which ultimately resulted in amputation ([7]). The trial in the KwaZulu-Natal High Court was conducted in two phases, separating the issues of liability and quantum as per Rule 33(4) of the Uniform Rules ([1]).

Dormac Marine and Engineering, the appellant and defendant, had contracted Camrod for its ship repair work, and the incident occurred due to alleged negligence on Dormac’s part in failing to provide proper and safe equipment ([1], [10]). The High Court found in Henneberry's favor, attributing liability to Dormac as the provider of the faulty equipment ([2]). Dormac's appeal was based on insufficient evidence of negligence and improper weight given to witness testimony ([28]).

**I – Issues**  
The primary legal questions arose from the findings on negligence and liability. The issues identified by the court included:  

1. Whether Dormac Marine and Engineering failed to provide safe equipment, thereby breaching its duty of care to Henneberry.
2. Whether the negligence, as claimed, was proven in light of conflicting accounts of the incident ([11], [30]).
3. The applicability of the res ipsa loquitur principle in inferring negligence from the circumstances surrounding the accident ([27]).

**R – Rules**  
The court applied established principles of negligence, emphasizing the twofold inquiry from *Kruger v Coetzee*, which requires a determination of (1) foreseeability of harm and (2) whether reasonable steps were taken to mitigate that risk ([31]). The case also referenced the principle of *res ipsa loquitur*, which suggests that certain incidents hint at negligence when the specific cause of an accident is unclear but implies a lack of care ([27]).

In determining whether Dormac maintained a duty of care, the court noted that Dormac admitted to the obligation to provide safe equipment but contested that the equipment was either faulty or outdated ([12], [32]). The relevant Occupational Health and Safety Act also implies standards that employers must comply with concerning workplace safety ([35]).

**A – Application**  
In reviewing the evidence, the court found discrepancies between the testimonies of Henneberry and the Dormac witnesses, particularly concerning the nature of the chain blocks used and circumstances of the incident ([22], [23]). While the respondent claimed the chain blocks were inappropriate and not adequately tested, Dormac's witnesses contended that the equipment was certified and in good working order at the time of use ([13], [24]).

Significantly, the court noted that the evidence indicated that the specific chain block tested post-incident had a known broken link and that it had not been defective during the time it was provided to Camrod’s workers ([21], [26]). The court concluded that the lack of credible evidence to definitively ascertain the fault of the chain block meant that the inference of negligence under *res ipsa loquitur* was improperly applied. It underscored that mere occurrence of an incident does not imply liability without substantial evidence supporting the cause of that incident ([27], [29], [40]).

**C – Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of Dormac Marine and Engineering, overturning the lower court's decision and granting "absolution from the instance" ([42]). The court concluded that Henneberry had failed to substantiate the claim of negligence against Dormac. As a result, the appeal was upheld with costs, excluding the costs associated with preparing an unnecessary volume of appeal records ([43]). The ruling highlighted the importance of immediate evidence substantiation, particularly in workplace injury cases, where the burden of proof lies firmly with the claimant to establish that the defendant's actions directly caused harm through negligence. 

This case underscores the judicious application of negligence principles within the context of workplace safety and operational liability, reaffirming that the mere existence of an accident does not automatically yield liability without robust foundational evidence.
