Arendsnes Sweefspoor CC v Dalia Marcelle Botha (471/12) [2013] ZASCA 86

The defendant raised a special plea asserting that the plaintiff's claim had prescribed under section 12 of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, contending that the summons had not been served within the prescribed three-year period, which would have ended on January 1, 2007.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 86

F – Facts

The respondent, Dalia Marcelle Botha (the plaintiff), sustained serious bodily injuries on January 2, 2004, while being transported by a cable car system operated by the appellant, Arendsnes Sweefspoor CC (the defendant). The incident involved the malfunction of the cable clamps, causing the cable car to flip over and her to fall approximately 30 meters to the ground. In response, the plaintiff instituted a claim against the defendant for damages.

The defendant raised a special plea asserting that the plaintiff's claim had prescribed under section 12 of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, contending that the summons had not been served within the prescribed three-year period, which would have ended on January 1, 2007. The service of the summons occurred on December 14, 2006, at the defendant’s registered office, where the sheriff served it to Mr. Pretorius, an employee of a restaurant operating on the same premises but not associated with the defendant.

The plaintiff argued that service was effective and interrupted prescription, asserting that the summons was served at the registered office. The defendant maintained that service was improper since it was not made upon a responsible employee and that the business had ceased operations prior to service.

I – Issues

The central issue on appeal was whether the service of the summons on December 14, 2006, was legally adequate and effective in interrupting the running of prescription, as per the requirements of the Uniform Rules of Court and the Prescription Act ([paras 2–5]).

R – Rules

The legal framework governing service of court processes is established in Rule 4 of the Uniform Rules of Court, which stipulates that for service upon a corporation, it may be done by delivering a copy to a responsible employee at its registered office or principal place of business. If no employee is available, service can be conducted by affixing a copy to the main door of the registered office ([paras 6–7]).

The Prescription Act, particularly section 15(1), states that the running of prescription is interrupted by the service of any process where the creditor claims payment, provided that the service conforms to legal requirements. If the service does not adhere to the said stipulations, the interruption lapses unless the debtor acknowledges liability ([paras 8–9]).

A – Application

The court examined whether service on Mr. Pretorius constituted valid service. It was accepted that the sheriff delivered the summons at the registered office via Mr. Pretorius, who was not an employee of the defendant. The defendant argued that service was invalid because Rule 4(1)(a)(v) specifically requires delivery to a responsible employee, or alternatively, attachment to the door if no such employee is present.

The court held that service on an employee of a different business at the registered office could still amount to substantial compliance with service rules. It drew on precedent from Brangus Ranching (Pty) Ltd v Plaaskem (Pty) Ltd and other cases indicating that service at a registered address—while preferable to be on a corporation’s employee—remains valid if the essence of service is fulfilled ([paras 11–29]).

The court also noted the importance of the corporation’s duty to maintain a responsible presence at its registered address, reaffirming that a corporation cannot evade liability by failing to ensure its registered office is staffed appropriately ([paras 20–30]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal confirmed that the service of the summons on December 14, 2006, was adequate and effectively interrupted the running of prescription. The court dismissed the defendant's special plea with costs, thereby affirming the lower court's ruling. The decision underscores the principle that substantial compliance with service rules is sufficient and that corporations must maintain accessibility for legal processes to uphold justice principles ([paras 20–21], [22–31]).

The implications of this judgment reinforce the necessity for corporations to ensure that their registered office is effectively staffed and accessible for legal processes, preventing them from alleging non-compliance when they have neglected their obligations to inform the registrar of any changes in their operational status.
