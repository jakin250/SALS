Kubheka and Another v The State (200/2020) [2021] ZASCA 25

Through its judgment delivered on 20 June 2019, the High Court upheld the convictions but drastically increased the sentences to five years for the first appellant and eight years for the second [4].

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 25

F – Facts:
The case concerns appellants Nhlanhla Arthur Kubheka (first appellant) and Armstrong Ngidi (second appellant), who were convicted in the Regional Division, North Gauteng, Randburg for theft of a cellular telephone and an iPod from a locked Mercedes Benz vehicle on 12 October 2017 ([1]). Following their conviction, both appellants received sentences of four years' imprisonment, with the first appellant's sentence including a two-year suspension contingent upon not being convicted of theft or a similar offence [1]. The second appellant was not granted any suspension. Both were declared unfit to possess firearms under s 103(1) of the Firearms Control Act 60 of 2000 ([1]). The appellants appealed to the High Court against their convictions and sentences. During the appeal hearing on 22 May 2019, the High Court raised the possibility of increasing their sentences ([2]).

Through its judgment delivered on 20 June 2019, the High Court upheld the convictions but drastically increased the sentences to five years for the first appellant and eight years for the second [4]. The High Court justified the increased sentences based on the careful planning, prevalence of the crime, and the necessity to protect societal interests, while seemingly downplaying the personal circumstances of the appellants [4]. Subsequently, the appellants sought leave to appeal the increased sentences, which was granted exclusively in relation to the sentence [5].

I – Issues:
The central legal issue arose from whether the High Court erred in increasing the sentences imposed by the regional court when there was no evident misdirection or failure of the regional court to exercise its discretionary power properly ([6]). Additionally, it revolved around whether the new sentences imposed by the High Court were proportionate to the nature of the crime committed ([10]).

R – Rules:
The Supreme Court of Appeal relied on established principles regarding a court’s power to intervene in sentences imposed by a lower court. Citing S v Bogaards, it reiterated that an appellate court may only overturn a sentence where there has been an irregularity leading to a failure of justice or if the lower court misdirected itself to the extent that its decision on sentence is vitiated, or that the sentence is so grossly disproportionate that no reasonable court could have imposed it ([6]). The court also referenced that no appellate court should interfere simply because it prefers a different sentence, as established in S v Malgas ([10]).

A – Application:
The Supreme Court of Appeal meticulously assessed the reasons provided by the High Court for increasing the sentences and noted that the High Court had failed to demonstrate any critical misdirection by the regional court. The trial court had accounted for relevant factors, including the personal circumstances of both appellants—a 44-year-old and a 46-year-old professional reputably contributing to society ([7]-[8]). It was emphasized that the regional court had applied the principles of sentencing, including rehabilitation, deterrence, and retribution [9]. The High Court's rationale for increasing the sentences was criticized for failing to acknowledge the significant personal circumstances of the appellants and not establishing that the regional court had inadequately balanced the interests of society and the appellants [10].

The Supreme Court observed that the appellants' actions were motivated more by greed than by immediate need, indicating that while the nature of the crime must be seriously considered, the imposition of direct imprisonment warranted scrutiny for its proportionality in light of past judicial precedents in similar theft cases ([11]).

C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appellants' appeal against the increased sentences and reinstated the original sentences imposed by the regional court, which were deemed appropriate and proportionate ([12]). The court concluded that no significant misdirection warranted the High Court's intervention, reinforcing the necessity for appellate courts to respect the sentencing discretion exercised by trial courts where it is adequately justified. This ruling underscores the principle of proportionality in sentencing and serves as a caution against hasty judicial increases in sentences without robust justification based on fundamental errors by lower courts.
