Supreme Court of Appeal

37 Case Note

[1] The appellant, the Moladora Trust, is the owner of the property described as the Remainder of the farm Wildebeeslaagte number 282, located in the district of Dr Kenneth Kaunda, North West Province of South Africa.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 37 (3 April 2024) Coram: Ponnan, Matojane a

FIRAC Case Note: Moladora Trust v Mereki and Others

Neutral Citation: Moladora Trust v Mereki and Others (189/2023) [2024] ZASCA 37 (3 April 2024)

Coram: Ponnan, Matojane and Weiner JJA
Heard: 15 March 2024
Delivered: 3 April 2024

F – Facts

[1] The appellant, the Moladora Trust, is the owner of the property described as the Remainder of the farm Wildebeeslaagte number 282, located in the district of Dr Kenneth Kaunda, North West Province of South Africa. The first, second, and third respondents (the Mereki children) occupy part of this property under the Extension of Security of Tenure Act No 62 of 1997 (ESTA). Their right to occupy the land arises from their deceased mother, Mrs. Mereki, who was employed on the farm and had a right of residence under section 3(4) of ESTA, which allows for presumption of consent for those who have continuously and openly resided on land for one year ([para 1]).

[2] The Moladora Trust initiated proceedings in the Land Claims Court (LCC) on 11 May 2022, seeking orders to compel the Mereki children to remove their livestock from the farm and to prevent them from returning without the trust's consent. Despite legal communications, the Mereki children did not respond or appear at the LCC hearing ([paras 3-5]).

[3] The LCC found that a tacit agreement existed, allowing the Mereki children to graze their livestock on the farm, arguing that the absence of a formal challenge or expressed denial of consent constituted tacit consent. This conclusion was drawn despite the trust's evidence indicating no assertion of tacit consent from the Mereki children and the absence of any reply or evidence disputing the trust's claims ([paras 4, 10-11]).

I – Issues

The central issue on appeal revolves around whether the LCC correctly determined that there existed a tacit agreement or consent allowing the Mereki children to keep their livestock on the Moladora Trust's land. The appeal particularly questioned the sufficiency of evidence supporting claims of tacit consent, the trust's right to terminate such rights, and whether the mere continuance of residence constituted consent as per the provisions of ESTA ([para 4]).

R – Rules

The court reviewed various provisions of ESTA relevant to the rights of occupiers. Section 3(4) establishes that continuous residence may imply consent unless proven otherwise. The court also invoked precedents addressing the necessity for clear evidence of either express or tacit consent in relationships involving land occupation:

  • Adendorff’s Boerdery v Shabalala and Others [2017] ZASCA 37.
  • Loskop Landgoed Boerdery (Pty) Ltd and Others v Petrus Moeleso and Others [2022] ZASCA 53.

The appeals court emphasized that the burden lies with the party alleging the existence of a tacit agreement to show unequivocal conduct suggesting consent ([paras 10-12]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal undertook a stringent analysis of whether the findings by the LCC were factually supported and legally sound. The court highlighted that the Mereki children had not advanced a defense of tacit consent, and all factual claims by the trust were unchallenged due to the mere absence of counter-affidavits or representations from the respondents. Furthermore, the court ruled that mere occupation does not automatically afford rights to engage in livestock grazing without expressed or tacit consent, a position supported by preceding case law ([paras 12-13]).

The court found that the LCC’s determination of a tacit agreement lacked a factual basis and that its reasoning was conjectural and unsupported by prior written communications betwixt the parties. Specifically, the contention that tacit consent might have been given after the death of Mrs. Mereki was deemed untenable, as rights of occupation did not extend to grazing rights without collated agreement ([paras 11-13]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the Moladora Trust, overturning the LCC's decision. It held that the findings of the LCC regarding tacit consent were not founded upon any viable evidence, thus undermining the legitimacy of their conclusion. The court mandated the immediate removal of all livestock belonging to the Mereki children from the trust's property within 30 days and prohibited further grazing without explicit consent. The ruling emphasizes the critical nature of clear and explicit agreements in land laws, reaffirming the necessity of evidential submissions in legal disputes involving land and occupancy rights ([paras 15]).

Implications: This case underscores the necessity for clear agreements regarding land use and occupation, mitigating reliance on tacit consent, particularly in dealings governed by ESTA. The decision clarifies that mere occupation does not suffice to infer consent for activities such as livestock grazing, thereby reinforcing the property rights of landowners.
