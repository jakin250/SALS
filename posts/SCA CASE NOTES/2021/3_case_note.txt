Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# Case Note: Municipality of Mhlontlo v TDH Tsolo Junction (1086/2019)

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

The matter at hand revolves around an appeal from the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court in Mthatha concerning the claim for damages for breach of contract instituted by TDH Tsolo Junction (Pty) Ltd ("the respondent") against the Municipality of Mhlontlo ("the appellant"). The appellant, in its capacity as a local municipality, invited proposals in July 2008 for the development of a property known as Tsolo Junction located in Tsolo, a town within its jurisdiction ([1]).

Following a bidding process in December 2008, the appellant issued a letter of appointment to the respondent, indicating its intention to engage the respondent for the development, contingent upon several conditions being met ([2]). The letter listed the essential documentation that needed to be submitted, including proof of insurance, a surety letter, and an occupational health and safety plan. It specifically stated that the appointment would only take effect once these conditions were satisfied ([2]). Subsequently, after accepting the appointment and engaging in negotiations, a Supply and Development Agreement was executed on 18 March 2009 ([3]).

On 25 February 2011, the respondent initiated legal action against the municipality for breach of the agreement, claiming damages amounting to R48,340,059. The appellant raised several special pleas, one of which claimed that the agreement was unenforceable due to the respondent's failure to meet suspensive conditions outlined in the earlier letter of appointment ([4]). The court a quo dismissed the special pleas, leading to the appeal heard on 9 November 2020 ([1]).

### I – Issues

The salient issue for determination in this appeal is whether the contract between the appellant and the respondent was rendered unenforceable due to the alleged non-fulfillment of the suspensive conditions articulated in the letter of appointment ([1]). Specifically, the question is whether the initial letter constituted a binding agreement that could impose conditions on the subsequent Supply and Development Agreement, or whether the latter agreement alone represented the complete and enforceable contract between the parties ([5]).

### R – Rules

The court relied on established principles of contract law, particularly concerning the validity and enforceability of contracts, the interpretation of written agreements, and the implications of suspensive conditions. It was noted that a suspensive condition is one that suspends the acquisition of rights until a certain event occurs ([7]). The judgment underscored that in interpreting contracts, the intention of the parties must be discerned from the written documents constituting the contract.

The principle that a contract must reflect the complete agreement of the parties was also central to the court's reasoning. Clause 15 of the Supply and Development Agreement explicitly stated that it represented the "whole agreement" between the parties, negating any earlier arrangements or understandings not included in the signed document ([7]). This clause is significant as it typically excludes the application of preliminary agreements unless mentioned expressly within the final document, as established in various precedents ([6]).

### A – Application

In addressing the matter, the court established that the letter of appointment was a preliminary communication, not intended to create a binding obligation on its own. The court noted that it explicitly stated that the effective appointment of the respondent was contingent on the subsequent signing of a contract that would outline the precise conditions ([6]). 

Evidence presented during the hearings indicated that the condition requiring the submission of specific documents was excluded from the final agreement during negotiations. The representatives of the respondent had clarified why those documents were not applicable, and this explanation was accepted by the appellant’s representatives ([7]). Hence, the court opined that the parties had mutually consented to forgo the submission of the documents listed in the appointment letter, which meant that the contract entered into on 18 March 2009 was independent of those conditions.

The appellant's argument, suggesting that the formal execution of the agreement was dependent on the satisfaction of the suspensive condition, was dismissed. The court reiterated that the argument lacked merit and that the final agreement was self-contained, reflecting the parties' consensus at the time of signing without reference to the conditions previously mentioned.

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court’s ruling that the supply agreement was enforceable despite the alleged non-fulfillment of the conditions specified in the letter of appointment ([8]). This judgment upholds the principle that a finalized contract, duly signed and agreed upon, supersedes preliminary negotiations and conditions unless explicitly included in the final agreement. The implications of this decision emphasize the importance of ensuring clarity in contractual terms and the binding nature of written agreements, reinforcing the standard that parties must clearly delineate their intentions in any contractual documentation.

In terms of costs, the court ordered that the appellant bear the costs of the appeal, including the costs of two counsel, reflecting the complexity and significance of the matter at hand ([9]). Thus, the ruling serves as a pivotal reference for future contract law cases in South Africa concerning the need for clarity and adherence to formalized contracts.
