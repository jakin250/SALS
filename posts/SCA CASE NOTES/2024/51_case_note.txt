# Case Note: City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality v Vresthena (Pty) Ltd and Others (1346/2022) [2024] ZASCA 51

**FIRAC Method**

## F – Facts
The litigation involves the City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality (the Appellant) and Vresthena (Pty) Ltd (the First Respondent), along with several other parties. The pivotal facts ([paras 2–4]) can be summarized as follows:

1. **Background**: The Zambezi Retail Park, a commercial property, has existed since 2006 as a sectional title scheme owned by Vresthena, which leases sections to various businesses. A Body Corporate, which manages the scheme, was elected on June 19, 2018.

2. **Electricity Supply and Payment**: The City provides electricity to the Retail Park through a single supply, billing the Body Corporate. Non-payment by the Body Corporate led the City to implement credit control measures, including disconnecting the electricity supply in January 2022. 

3. **Legal Action Initiated**: Vresthena filed an urgent application with the high court seeking an order compelling the City to restore electricity and reconsider its application for a separate electricity connection for its sections. 

4. **High Court Order**: The high court’s order granted Vresthena's request but did not address the consequences of ongoing non-payment. The City sought leave to appeal, which the court eventually permitted.

## I – Issues
The primary legal questions ([paras 1–7]) arising from this case are:

1. **Appealability of the Order**: Whether the high court's order is final or interim, and therefore subject to appeal.
   
2. **Condonation and Reinstatement**: Whether the City should be granted condonation for the late filing of its notice of appeal and if the appeal can be reinstated.

3. **Merits of the Application**: Whether the high court erred in granting an interim interdict reflecting a failure to consider the obligations of Vresthena to pay for services rendered and whether Vresthena had established a prima facie right to receive services without payment.

## R – Rules
The legal framework governing the case includes several key principles and statutes ([paras 8–19]):

1. **Appealability Criteria**: The Zweni triad establishes that an order is appealable if it is final in effect, definitive of the rights of the parties, and disposes of a substantial portion of the relief sought ([para 8]).

2. **Municipal Responsibilities**: The Constitution of the Republic of South Africa mandates municipalities to provide services sustainably while adhering to financial obligations ([paras 18–19]). Specific provisions include:

   - Municipality’s obligation to collect debts (s 96 of the Local Government Municipal Systems Act 23 of 2000).
   - Rights to disconnect services for non-payment (s 21 of the City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality Standard Electricity Supply By-laws 2013).

3. **Interdict Requirements**: For an interdict to be granted, the applicant must demonstrate a prima facie right and the absence of alternative remedies. 

## A – Application
The Court analyzed the appealability of the order and the merits of the case ([paras 12–34]):

1. **Finality of the Order**: The Court concluded that the high court's order, while labeled as interim, demonstrated finality (e.g., it compelled the City to restore services without a clear obligation for Vresthena to pay). The Court cited the chilling effect that would result from the order, compelling the City to act against its statutory obligations and interfering with its revenue collection ([paras 13–14]).

2. **Condonation for Late Appeal**: The Court found that the City provided reasonable grounds for the delay and demonstrated strong prospects of success on the merits ([para 16]).

3. **Merits of the Interdict**: The high court was deemed to have erred in favoring the respondents without adequately considering their obligation to pay for services, leading to an anomaly whereby services were continued despite significant arrears. The order failed to impose reciprocal payment obligations on the Body Corporate and its members ([paras 22–32]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal's ruling was clear and impactful:

1. **Holding**: The Court upheld the appeal, setting aside the high court’s order and dismissing Vresthena's application with costs. It granted condonation for the late filing and reinstated the appeal ([para 34]).

2. **Ratio Decidendi**: The Court emphasized the importance of ensuring that municipalities can carry out their constitutional responsibilities while collecting unpaid debts. Vresthena was found not to have a prima facie right to receive services without payment, solidifying the principle that non-payment by the Body Corporate constituted a valid basis for the City's actions ([paras 33–34]).

3. **Implications**: This ruling underscores the responsibilities of municipal entities in service provision and revenue collection, reinforcing the interdependence of payment obligations in public service delivery. It sets a precedent for the treatment of interim orders and representative actions involving sectional title schemes in South Africa's legal framework.
