Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2006

# FIRAC Case Note: *Minister of Environmental Affairs and Tourism v George* [2006] SCA 57 (RSA)

**F - Facts:**  
The appeal arises from a refusal by the Equality Court to refer a case concerning fishing rights to the High Court as a more suitable forum ([1]). In December 2004, the respondents, representing about 5000 artisanal fishers, lodged simultaneous applications in both the High Court and the Equality Court, claiming that the Minister of Environmental Affairs and Tourism (the "Minister") had failed to provide equitable access to fishing rights ([1]). The Minister sought a referral to the High Court, which was denied by NC Erasmus J of the Equality Court. The Minister did not seek leave to appeal against this refusal ([1]).

The Equality Court was established under the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000 (the "Equality Act"), designed to address issues of unfair discrimination ([3]). Upon filing, the court is tasked with determining whether to hear a case or to refer it to a more appropriate forum ([5]). The statutory provisions emphasize the court's jurisdiction, and the presiding officer is required to assess relevant factors, including the circumstances of the parties involved ([4]-[5]). The Minister's procedural breaches included the late filing of his appeal notice and a failure to demonstrate sufficient grounds for condonation ([2]).

**I - Issues:**  
1. Whether the Equality Court's refusal to refer the matter to the High Court constituted an appealable order ([2]).
2. Whether leave to appeal was required before appealing the matters decided in the Equality Court ([15]).

**R - Rules:**  
The Equality Act (s 16, s 20) establishes the framework within which equality courts operate, notably granting them discretion to refer cases to suitable alternative forums ([3], [5]). Section 20(3)(a) empowers presiding officers to decide if matters should be heard in the Equality Court or another institution, emphasizing that this does not constitute a judgment ([6], [8]). Section 23(1) entitles any aggrieved person to appeal against decisions of the Equality Court, but §19(1)(e) of the same Act mandates compliance with the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959 requirements for appeals ([15])—including the need for leave ([16]).

**A - Application:**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the Equality Court's decision not to refer the matter to the High Court did not amount to an appealable order because it represented a discretionary determination without the necessitation of an explicit order, reflecting the court's ongoing jurisdiction over the case ([6]-[8]). Moreover, even if a referral were misconceived, the discretion exercised by the Equality Court was not shown to have been misapplied, leading to the conclusion that the Minister’s appeal lacked merit ([9]-[10]).

Subsequently, the court addressed the issue of whether leave to appeal was required. The Minister asserted he had an unconditional right of appeal based on the provisions of the Equality Act; however, the court clarified that since the Supreme Court Act applies through necessary adjustments, leave was indeed mandated ([15]-[16]). Furthermore, there was an emphasis on the need for the Equality Act's underlying goal of promoting expeditious and informal processing of cases, which the current procedural state contradicted, complicating rather than simplifying access to justice for the fishers ([18]).

**C - Conclusion:**  
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal found the Minister's appeal incompetent for two primary reasons: the absence of a judiciable order from the Equality Court and the need for leave to appeal, which was not obtained ([14], [15]). As a result, the application for condonation was refused, and the appeal was struck from the roll with costs ([19]).

This ruling underscores the critical nature of procedural adherence within specialized courts and reaffirms the legislature's intent to maintain accessibility and efficiency in handling equality-related claims. The implications suggest a necessary care in procedural conduct in future equality-related cases to prevent similar procedural barriers.
