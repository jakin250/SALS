Supreme Court of Appeal

Hammond v The State

On 17 October 2003, Hammond was arrested alongside a co-accused in a police operation initiated from an anonymous tip-off.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2007 [2007] SCA 164 (RSA)

Neutral Citation: [2007] SCA 164 (RSA)

F - Facts

The appellant, Nicholas James Hammond, was originally convicted in March 2004 at a district magistrate’s court for dealing in Methcathinone (known as "Cat"), a substance classified as a dependence-producing drug under section 5(b) of the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 140 of 1992 (the Drugs Act). He was sentenced to 12 years' imprisonment, a conviction subsequently upheld by the High Court of Johannesburg in April 2006. Following this, Hammond appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal with the court's leave, despite the absence of the High Court's judgment due to a transcription error ([paras 1–2]).

On 17 October 2003, Hammond was arrested alongside a co-accused in a police operation initiated from an anonymous tip-off. Police officers, Sergeant Tickner and Inspector de Jager, were directed to apprehend individuals in possession of drugs at a service station near Gold Reef City in Johannesburg. Upon arrival, they discovered a sports bag containing over 3 kilograms of Methcathinone in the vehicle. During the trial, the co-accused was acquitted, while Hammond claimed the trial was unfair due to inadequate evidence from the State and alleged entrapment through police actions ([paras 2–4]).

Significantly, the credibility of police statements was called into question. Initially, the officers described an anonymous informant, later contradicted by supplementary statements identifying Captain Kukard, who had died before trial, as the informant. Further complicating matters, the presence of an Indian man named Yunus, found in the vehicle, was not disclosed initially ([paras 5–7]). During the trial, the prosecution asserted they had not employed a police trap tactic; however, evidence from Hammond indicated that officers had coaxed him into the drug deal ([paras 8–10]).

Hammond’s defense framed the police conduct as entrapping him, emphasizing that the presence of undercover agents distorted his opportunity to engage in a fair transaction ([paras 11–12]). His appeal argued that this conduct was in violation of section 252A of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which governs the admissibility of evidence procured during undercover operations or traps.

I - Issues

The primary legal questions addressed included:

  1. Whether the evidence relevant to the alleged police trap was admissible under section 252A of the Criminal Procedure Act, regarding the fairness of the trial based on the police conduct.
  2. Whether the conduct of the police went beyond merely providing an opportunity to commit the offense, thus warranting exclusion of the evidence on grounds of unfairness.
  3. Were the terms of the sentence just given the circumstances around entrapment and Hammond's role in the crime? ([paras 1, 10, 23–24]).

R - Rules

Key provisions discussed include:

  • Section 5(b) of the Drugs Act: Criminalizes dealing in specified drugs, including Methcathinone ([para 1]).
  • Section 252A of the Criminal Procedure Act: Allows for the use of undercover operations, providing that such operations do not exceed affording an opportunity to commit an offense. If police conduct violates this threshold, the court may exclude the evidence obtained from such conduct ([paras 20–21], 22).
  • S v Hayes [1998] 1 SACR 625 (O): A case where police practices raised substantial concerns regarding the accused’s right to a fair trial. This case emphasized the importance of examining police conduct in relation to its fairness and legitimacy ([para 23]).

A - Application

In assessing Hammond's appeal, the court acknowledged the particulars of police conduct. It scrutinized whether the conduct of the officers constituted entrapment and if that conduct compromised the fairness of the trial.

The judgment found that while the police did fail initially to disclose critical information regarding the informant's identity and the existence of an undercover agent, such missteps did not amount to fraud upon the court. Per section 252A, the crucial determination was if police actions exceeded merely providing opportunities for offense commission. The court argued that the evidence presented showed Hammond voluntarily engaged in arrangements to sell drugs, expressing a clear understanding of his actions and their implications ([paras 25–27]).

The situation was more complex with respect to the officers’ integrity; their misleading initial statements were condemned but didn’t compel a finding that the trial was fundamentally unfair. The court ultimately concluded that Hammond did not demonstrate that his rights were substantially violated and maintained a willingness to engage in illicit trade, regardless of who instigated his involvement ([paras 24, 27–28]).

In terms of sentencing, although the court acknowledged the gravity of drug-dealing offenses and the resulting societal harms, it deemed the 12-year imprisonment excessive, given that Hammond was coerced into one transaction involving the delivery of drugs. As such, this was mitigated by his apparent cooperation and the prior time he had spent incarcerated, leading to a reduction of the sentence to five years, with two years suspended ([paras 29–31]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal against conviction, determining that police conduct, while questionably managed, did not infringe on fair trial protocols as prescribed by law. The evidence relating to the operation that ensnared Hammond was deemed admissible under section 252A since the officers' conduct did not exceed merely providing an opportunity to commit the offense.

However, the appeal regarding the severity of the sentence was upheld, reflecting the court's recognition of mitigating factors including the entrapment dynamics and the appellant's openness during the trial process. The final sentence imposed was five years, with two suspended—asserting both accountability and mercy in light of the circumstances under which the appellant was charged ([paras 28–31]).

This case highlights ongoing discussions of police conduct within South African criminal law, particularly the balance between effective law enforcement and the rights of individuals in legal proceedings.
