Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: FIRAC for S v Okah [2018] ZACC 3

## F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([1]–[4])

In **S v Okah**, the respondent, **Henry Emomotimi Okah**, a Nigerian citizen and permanent resident of South Africa, faced multiple charges related to terrorism under the **Protection of Constitutional Democracy against Terrorist and Related Activities Act 33 of 2004** (the **Act**). He was implicated in two car bombings that occurred in Warri and Abuja, Nigeria, resulting in fatalities and extensive injuries ([1]–[2]).

The **High Court of South Africa**, Gauteng Local Division, convicted Okah on 13 counts related to terrorism, concluding that he masterminded and financed the bombings. However, the **Supreme Court of Appeal** later overturned four convictions, ruling that the High Court had overstepped its jurisdiction because the actions occurred outside South Africa, despite the Act's provisions allowing for some extra-territorial jurisdiction ([2]–[3]). The Supreme Court of Appeal reduced his sentence from 24 years to 20 years based on these findings ([2]).

The State appealed, seeking to restore all convictions, particularly those related to the Warri bombings. Concurrently, Okah appealed four issues, including his claimed exemption from culpability under section 1(4) of the Act, as well as irregularities in his trial regarding special entries under the **Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977** ([3]).

The case consolidated significant legal questions regarding jurisdiction under the Act, especially concerning the definition of "specified offence," and the applicability of section 1(4) that exempts actions taken in a legitimate struggle for self-determination, provided they comply with international humanitarian law ([4]).

## I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([4]–[5])

The primary legal issues presented to the **Constitutional Court** were as follows:

1. **Extra-territorial Jurisdiction**: Whether South African courts have the jurisdiction under section 15(1) of the Act to prosecute acts beyond the financing of terrorism which occurred internationally ([4]).
   
2. **Exemption Under Section 1(4)**: Whether Okah qualifies for exemption from prosecution under section 1(4) of the Act, which pertains to acts committed in the context of a legitimate struggle ([4]).

3. **Irregularities in Trial**: Whether the High Court improperly dismissed Okah's applications for special entries regarding alleged trial irregularities under section 317 of the **Criminal Procedure Act** ([4]).

## R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([5]–[19])

The court considered several critical statutory provisions and precedents:

1. **Protection of Constitutional Democracy against Terrorist and Related Activities Act 33 of 2004**:
   - Section 15(1): Outlines the jurisdiction of South African courts over "specified offences," which initially appeared to be narrowly defined ([8]).
   - Section 1(4): Provides an exemption for acts committed in the context of a struggle for national liberation, subject to international humanitarian law standards ([10]).

2. **Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977**:
   - Section 317: Grants courts discretion to record special entries concerning procedural irregularities, establishing the threshold for granting such entries ([11]).

3. **International Humanitarian Law**: Emphasising the obligation to distinguish between military objectives and civilian targets ([47]).

4. **Comity and Jurisdictional Principles**: The court reviewed doctrines around extra-territorial jurisdiction, drawing from both domestic and international jurisprudence that recognises broader grounds for asserting legal jurisdiction over acts impacting state interests ([16]).

## A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([18]–[55])

### Extra-territorial Jurisdiction
In assessing section 15, the court noted interpretative inconsistencies leading to an overly restrictive reading by the Supreme Court of Appeal, which limited "specified offences" to financing-related charges ([21]). The court rebuffed this interpretation, underscoring the necessity for a broader jurisdictional approach consistent with international obligations stemming from various anti-terrorism conventions ([35]). It held that both the Warri and Abuja bombings constituted terrorism under the Act's definitions, warranting jurisdiction ([33]).

### Exemption Under Section 1(4)
When addressing Okah’s claim for exemption under section 1(4), the court evaluated whether his actions met the criteria of being part of a legitimate struggle for national liberation and compliance with international humanitarian law. The court found insufficient evidence to substantiate his claims and confirmed that the bombings significantly violated humanitarian norms by deliberately targeting civilians, thus nullifying claims for exemption based on international law ([49]–[51]).

### Irregularities in Trial
Regarding claims for irregularities, particularly the failure to grant requests for special entries concerning the State’s alleged misconduct and his right to consular access, the court clarified that procedural safeguards must be maintained to ensure fairness. While it validated Okah's claim related to consular access due to failure to inform him of this right, it held that it did not significantly undermine the trial's overall fairness ([73]–[78]). The other irregularity claims were deemed without merit, failing to meet the burden of proof required for special entries ([56]–[69]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([90]–[91])

The **Constitutional Court** issued a comprehensive ruling reinstating all the convictions against Okah, concluding that South African courts possess the necessary jurisdiction under the Act to prosecute extraterritorial terrorism offences. It affirmed that Okah did not qualify for the section 1(4) exemption, condemning his actions as indiscriminate and violative of international humanitarian law, and decided that the trial's procedural irregularity regarding consular access, while acknowledged, did not warrant overturning his convictions ([91]).

The implications of this judgment extend beyond the case at hand, reinforcing the broad jurisdictional competency of South African courts in terrorism-related matters while delineating the strict boundaries of lawful struggle as established under international law. The decision further clarifies procedural standards in criminal trials, particularly surrounding the adjudication of irregularities and the necessity of informing defendants of their rights. This case thus serves as a critical precedent for future terrorism prosecutions within South Africa.
