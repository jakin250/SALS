# FIRAC Case Note: The State v Wouter Basson

**Court:** Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Case Number:** CCT 30/03  
**Date Heard:** November 4-5, 2003  
**Date Decided:** March 10, 2004  
**Judgment:** Ackermann J, Madala J, Mokgoro J, Mosenecke J, Ngcobo J, O'Regan J

---

## F – Facts

The case arises from the prosecution of Dr. Wouter Basson, a former employee of the South African National Defence Force who faced 67 charges, including murder, conspiracy, and drug offences linked to his role in the Civil Co-operation Bureau (CCB) between 1981 and 1989 ([2][3]). The charges stemmed from events occurring before the advent of democratic governance in South Africa in 1994. 

**Procedural History:** 

Basson was initially arrested in 1997 on charges related to the Medicines and Related Substances Control Act and fraud. After extensive legal wrangling, including a trial that commenced in 1999, he was acquitted on all charges in April 2002 ([7][8]). Following the acquittal, the state filed an application to the High Court, which reserved a question of law regarding the trial judge's failure to recuse himself, the admissibility of a bail record, and the correctness of quashing certain charges under the Riotous Assemblies Act. The High Court reserved one question for the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ([9][12]).

The SCA ultimately ruled that the issues regarding the trial judge’s recusal and the admissibility of evidence raised factual, not legal questions, and therefore struck these points from the roll ([12][13]). The state sought special leave to appeal this decision directly to the Constitutional Court, arguing multiple grounds, primarily around issues of bias and the interpretation of section 18(2) of the Riotous Assemblies Act, which deals with conspiracy to commit crimes.

## I – Issues

1. **Bias of the Trial Judge:** Whether the High Court's refusal to recuse itself on the grounds of alleged bias presents a constitutional matter ([15][24]).
2. **Admissibility of Evidence:** Whether the SCA correctly held that the admissibility of the bail record and its implications raised questions of fact versus law ([18][26]).
3. **Interpretation of Statutory Law:** Whether the interpretation of section 18(2) of the Riotous Assemblies Act regarding conspiracies to commit crimes beyond South Africa's borders raises a constitutional matter ([28][32]).
4. **Double Jeopardy and the Right to a Fair Trial:** Whether proceeding with a retrial after the state’s appeal could violate Basson's rights set out in sections 35(3)(m) and (d) of the Constitution ([61][62]).

## R – Rules

The governing provisions include:

1. **Section 167(3) of the Constitution:** This section limits the jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court to constitutional matters and issues connected with constitutional matters ([17]).
2. **Section 35(3) of the Constitution:** It grants accused individuals rights related to fair trials, including protection against double jeopardy ([62]).
3. **Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977:** Particularly sections 319 and 85, governing appeals from trial courts and objections to indictments ([9][10][28]).
4. **The Riotous Assemblies Act 17 of 1956:** Particularly section 18(2), concerning conspiracy to commit crimes ([28]).

## A – Application

The court undertook a thorough review of both the procedural history and substantive issues presented. 

1. **On the Bias Issue:** The Constitutional Court found that the question of judicial bias is a constitutional matter, recognizing the importance of impartiality in judicial proceedings ([21]). The Court articulated that both the state and the accused have a vested interest in an impartial trial, thereby allowing the state to appeal on these grounds ([24]).

2. **Regarding Evidence Admissibility:** The ruling on the bail record’s admissibility involved a discretionary judicial decision related to the fairness of a trial. The Court underscored that evaluating whether evidence can be admitted often involves assessments that incorporate both factual determinations and legal standards, affirming that such queries could elevate to constitutional matters depending on their scope ([26][28]).

3. **Interpretation of the Riotous Assemblies Act:** It was determined that the High Court’s interpretation of this Act had significant implications for the prosecutorial powers of the state regarding serious crimes, including potential war crimes. The interpretation could establish a constitutional breach if it prevents prosecution for offences that undermine constitutional values ([32][34]).

4. **Double Jeopardy Considerations:** The Constitutional Court noted that any retrial post-acquittal must consider the implications of double jeopardy as encapsulated in section 35(3)(m). The rights affirmed therein necessitate a careful balancing of the state’s duty to prosecute against the individual’s rights to a fair trial and finality ([61][62]).

## C – Conclusion

The Court ultimately concluded that the grounds upon which the application for special leave was sought raised constitutional matters as purported by sections 167(3) and 35 of the Constitution ([79]). It dismissed the state's application for direct appeal against the High Court’s judgment relating to bias while affirming that the questions regarding recusal of the trial judge, admissibility of the bail record, and interpretation of the Riotous Assemblies Act were constitutional matters deserving of review.

**Implications:** This ruling underscored the importance of judicial impartiality and fairness, shaping the relationship between prosecutorial authority and constitutional rights while setting precedents for the interpretation of statutes in alignment with international humanitarian law. The interplay between the state's obligation to prosecute and the rights of the accused thus remains a pivotal concern within South African constitutional law.
