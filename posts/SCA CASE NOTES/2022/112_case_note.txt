Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# Case Note: Maree v The State (Case No 422/2021) [2022] ZASCA 112

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–2])

The appellant, Mr. Jovan Maree, was convicted in the Parow Regional Court on four charges related to sexual offenses: rape (count 1), sexual assault (count 2), attempt to commit a sexual offense (count 3), and exposure of genital organs (count 4). The alleged incidents occurred on 22 September 2018 with the complainant, Ms. BM, a 23-year-old woman. The State claimed that Mr. Maree unlawfully and intentionally engaged in various sexual acts with Ms. BM without her consent, including finger penetration and physical assault.

After pleading not guilty, Maree was found guilty on all counts and sentenced to a total of ten years’ imprisonment, as counts 2, 3, and 4 were to run concurrently with the sentence for count 1. Following his conviction, Mr. Maree sought leave to appeal to the Western Cape Division of the High Court, which was denied. He then petitioned this Court as a final resort and was granted special leave to appeal the high court’s refusal of his application for leave to appeal.

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 2–3])

The primary legal issue at stake was whether the high court had erred in refusing Maree leave to appeal against his conviction and sentence. The secondary issue concentrated on the criteria for granting such leave: whether there existed a reasonable prospect of success in the appeal. This matter also invoked the jurisdictional limitations of the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which generally prohibits appeals directly from lower courts, necessitating that such cases be first addressed by the high court having jurisdiction.

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 3–4])

The court referred to Section 309(1)(a) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA), which articulates that no appeal may lie directly to the Supreme Court of Appeal from a lower court. The established principles surrounding appeals from regional courts emphasize that leave to appeal must first be sought from the trial court and, if refused, from the high court through a petition addressed to the Judge President (as reaffirmed in *S v Matshona* [2008] ZASCA 58). Furthermore, the court cited its own decisions, emphasizing that the review of the denial of leave to appeal is limited to whether reasonable prospects of success on the merits of the appeal exist.

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 5–20])

The court scrutinized the trial court’s evaluation of the evidence. It noted that the test for leave to appeal is not about reviewing the merits of the conviction but rather if the appellant establishes a reasonable chance of success on the potential appeal. The SCA assessed the competing narratives presented at trial, highlighting that the trial court found the complainant's testimony more credible. However, the SCA expressed concerns that the trial court may have misdirected itself in a few fundamental respects.

1. **Lack of Holistic Analysis**: The appellant argued that the trial court did not conduct a comprehensive analysis of the evidence and did not adequately weigh the various elements that resulted in the conviction ([para 16]).

2. **Credibility of Testimony**: The trial court classified the complainant as a single witness, necessitating cautious evaluation. Nevertheless, the SCA pointed out that this caution may not have been appropriately applied, raising questions about the reliability of her testimony and the corroboration considered by the trial court ([para 17]).

3. **Consideration of Evidence**: Further, the court noted the appellant's claims regarding the complainant’s demeanor after the incidents and the inconsistencies that arose in her testimony which were not sufficiently addressed by the trial court ([paras 18–19]).

In considering these points, the SCA established that the alleged shortcomings could lead to a reasonable conclusion different from that of the trial court, thus qualifying the requirement of a reasonable prospect of success.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 21–22])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of Mr. Maree, upholding his appeal against the high court’s decision to deny him leave to appeal his conviction and sentence. The court's judgment emphasized that there existed reasonable prospects that another court might arrive at different conclusions concerning both the merits of the conviction and the appropriateness of the sentence imposed. Consequently, the SCA ordered a remittance of the case back to the Western Cape Division of the High Court to grant Mr. Maree the opportunity to present his appeal.

The implications of this decision underscore the importance of thorough judicial scrutiny in sexual offense cases and the necessity of ensuring that all aspects of witness testimony are evaluated with due diligence. The case reaffirms the procedural safeguards designed to prevent miscarriages of justice in the appellate process, underscoring the role of higher courts in safeguarding individual rights and ensuring fairness in criminal proceedings.

This case note exemplifies the rigorous standards maintained by the judiciary in South Africa when addressing appeals, particularly in sensitive cases involving sexual offenses. Future implications for similar cases may emerge as judicial interpretations evolve in response to prevailing societal attitudes toward sexual assault and consent.
