Supreme Court of Appeal

Du Rand NO v Faerie Glen Renaissance Scheme

In regard to property rights, the court held that the amendments merely reflected the pre-existing legal realities of the Faerie Glen Renaissance Scheme and did not substantively change the rights of the appellants, thus not requiring their written consent as purportedly argued...

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009

F - Facts:
The case involves the Faerie Glen Renaissance Scheme, a sectional title development governed by the Sectional Titles Act 95 of 1986. The body corporate of this scheme sought to amend its management rules to incorporate the Housing Development Schemes for Retired Persons Act 65 of 1988, which the appellants—including trustees of the Ameva Trust—contended adversely affected their rights as unit owners ([1]–[2]). The Pretoria High Court, presided over by Visser AJ, granted the amendments, leading to this appeal ([1]–[2]). The appellants were the only owners opposing the amendments amid a general consensus that a unanimous resolution was unachievable ([2]).

I - Issues:
The legal questions before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:

  1. Whether the respondents' attempts to amend the management rules were procedurally valid under the relevant provisions.
  2. Whether the amendments infringed the appellants' property rights, requiring their written consent under section 1(3)(c) of the Sectional Titles Act ([2]–[3], [7]).

R - Rules:
Key legal frameworks referenced in the judgment include:

  1. Sectional Titles Act 95 of 1986, particularly sections 1, 1(3)(c), and 35(2)(a), which outline the processes for amending management rules and the definition of "unanimous resolution" ([3], [26]).
  2. Housing Development Schemes for Retired Persons Act 65 of 1988, specifically section 7, which regulates occupancy in retirement housing, establishing that occupancy is limited to "retired persons" or their spouses ([28]–[35]).

A - Application:
The court evaluated the appellants' arguments against the statutory backdrop. The appellants claimed that the meeting held to obtain a unanimous resolution was improperly constituted and that there was no demonstrable backing from a majority of the body corporate's members for the application([3]–[5]). The court counter-argued that the factual basis demonstrating that unanimity was unreachable was clear, given the ongoing animosity with the appellants ([5]–[6]).

In regard to property rights, the court held that the amendments merely reflected the pre-existing legal realities of the Faerie Glen Renaissance Scheme and did not substantively change the rights of the appellants, thus not requiring their written consent as purportedly argued under section 1(3)(c) ([22]–[35]). The amendments were deemed necessary to clarify the scheme's adherence to existing statutes relating to retirement housing, ultimately serving to protect the interests of both current and future owners ([22]).

C - Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the Pretoria High Court’s order. The court reasoned that the amendments did not adversely affect appellants’ rights as their concerns stemmed from misapplication of the law rather than substantive legal infringement ([24]–[25], [35]). The dismissal with costs reinforced the finding that the body corporate acted within legal parameters to achieve rule amendments ([24]–[23]). The outcome underscores the uphold of communal governance mechanisms within sectional title schemes, emphasizing that adherence to legal formalities does not compromise established property rights when legislative provisions already encompass those rights.

This case note serves to concisely summarize the decisive aspects of the Du Rand NO v Faerie Glen Renaissance Scheme judgment for academic citation, legal advisory purposes, or inclusion in formal case analyses.
