Source Folder: 2010

# FIRAC Case Note: Singh v BMW Financial Services (546/2009)

**Neutral Citation:** Singh v BMW Financial Services (546/2009) [2010] ZASCA 121 (30 September 2010)  
**Court:** The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Coram:** Mpati P, Cloete, Cachalia, Mhlantla JJA, Bertelsmann AJA  
**Heard:** 13 September 2010  
**Delivered:** 30 September 2010

## F – Facts
The appellant, Khoosial Singh, entered into an instalment sale agreement to purchase a BMW X5 4.41 automatic motor vehicle from the first respondent, BMW Financial Services (Pty) Ltd, via the second respondent, SMG Auto Durban (Pty) Ltd ([1]–[2]). Mr. Singh agreed with a sales representative at SMG that the vehicle would be ‘first registered’ in 2006, which was a crucial aspect of the transaction for Mr. Singh ([1]–[3]). The purchase price was R609,850, with Mr. Singh paying a deposit of R236,653.81 and financing the remainder through an agreement with BMW, which stipulated that it would retain ownership until the full obligations were settled ([2]).

The vehicle was delivered with temporary license plates on 31 December 2005. However, in early January 2006, SMG revealed that the vehicle had been registered multiple times in 2005, contrary to the agreement ([4]–[6]). This sparked a series of communications from Mr. Singh to SMG, seeking compliance with the original agreement. After receiving unsatisfactory responses, he instituted proceedings in the Durban High Court against both BMW and SMG on 11 May 2006, initially seeking specific performance—delivery of a vehicle registered for the first time in 2006 ([10]). 

As the proceedings took longer than expected, Mr. Singh amended his claim to seek cancellation of the contract and restitution of the payments made, arguing he had not received the vehicle he contracted for ([13]–[14]). The High Court originally granted this relief, agreeing that Mr. Singh was entitled to cancel the agreement due to its failure to meet an essential term ([14]).

The full court on appeal, however, reversed this decision, arguing that Mr. Singh had entered into two contracts: a sale agreement with BMW and an oral agreement with SMG for the vehicle's registration. Thus, they concluded that he could only claim for damages against SMG if the registration did not meet the terms of that separate contract ([15]).

## I – Issues
The key legal issues in this case were:

1. Was the requirement for the vehicle to be ‘first registered’ in 2006 an essential term of the sale agreement?
2. Did the delivery of a vehicle registered in 2005, despite the certificate of registration issued later, constitute a breach of contract, justifying cancellation and restitution?
3. Were the claims against the respondents correctly delineated between the purported separate contracts established by the full court?

## R – Rules
The court relied on various principles of contract law, including:

1. Interpretation of contracts: The principle that a term specifically stated in a contract is enforceable ([17]).
2. Essential terms: Terms deemed essential to the contract’s agreements can provide grounds for cancellation if breached ([14]).
3. Restitution: Legal concepts surrounding the restitution of unjust enrichment where one party is unjustly deprived to another ([21]).
4. Damages versus cancellation: Differentiating claims for damages from those justifying the cancellation of a contract due to essential term breaches ([22]).

Furthermore, the court had to consider the implications of a non-variation clause in the sale agreement, which limited the ability to introduce additional terms ([16]).

## A – Application
Cachalia JA, writing for the majority, disagreed with the full court's interpretation of the contractual obligations. He emphasized that the express term regarding the year of first registration was indeed essential to the contract between Mr. Singh and BMW ([17]). The fact that the vehicle had previously been registered before the contract's execution contradicted the contract's specifications. 

The court further analyzed the full court's reasoning that the delivery of the registration certificate—labelled as a ‘date liable for registration’—satisfied the agreed term. This argument was rejected, as the certificate failed to reflect the agreed terms candidly, and the history of prior registrations indicated a breach of the delivery obligations owed to Mr. Singh ([18]).

The court's rationale indicated that Mr. Singh's insistence on receiving a vehicle that had not been previously registered was neither trifling nor immaterial; rather, it was integral to the economic value and contractual expectations ([23]). 

Moreover, the court clarified that Mr. Singh's continued payment post-dispute could not be interpreted as an acceptance of the contract after the breach. He had the right to seek restitution without being penalized for fulfilling his financial obligations under the circumstances ([24]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Mr. Singh’s appeal, overturning the full court's judgment. The court found that the delivery of the vehicle as it stood constituted a breach of the essential terms of the sale agreement. As a result, the contract was declared cancelled. BMW was ordered to repay all amounts Mr. Singh paid under the contract, along with applicable interest, effectively acknowledging the principle of unjust enrichment ([30]). 

The implications of this case extend to clarity in contractual terms, ensuring that essential features such as the year of registration are not only agreed but are strictly adhered to in transactions involving goods where value may significantly fluctuate based on those characteristics. Furthermore, this ruling reinforces the importance of accurate representation in vehicle sales and the obligations of financial institutions alongside dealerships in such sales.
