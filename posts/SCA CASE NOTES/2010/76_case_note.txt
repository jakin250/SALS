Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

# FIRAC Case Note: Van Jaarsveld v Bridges (344/09) [2010] ZASCA 76

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([1–19])

This case arises from the claim for damages instituted by the respondent, Ms. Sunette Bridges, against the appellant, Mr. Deon van Jaarsveld, based on a breach of promise to marry. The parties were engaged on July 29, 2005, with the wedding date set for January 14, 2006. However, on December 4, 2005, Mr. van Jaarsveld, through a text message, informed Ms. Bridges that he could not proceed with the marriage due to a change in his feelings. His message was apologetic, expressing regret for the situation, and it was part of ongoing communications primarily conducted via text messaging ([12]–[13]).

In the aftermath of the repudiation, Ms. Bridges, who called herself a singer and had some media presence, claimed damages exceeding R1 million, asserting that she had incurred substantial emotional and financial losses due to the cancellation of the wedding plans. Initially, the court below awarded Ms. Bridges R110,000 for iniuria (sentimental damages) and close to R172,413 for contractual damages. Both parties received leave to appeal, although the primary focus of the appeal was limited to quantum ([1]–[3]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal broadened the scope of the appeal to include the nature of the breach as well as public policy considerations underlying the action for breach of promise to marry. The appeal raised deeper questions concerning whether such actions remain compatible with contemporary societal mores and public policy ([2]–[3]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([2–4])

The case presents several legal and constitutional issues, primarily:

1. **Contumacious Breach**: Whether Mr. van Jaarsveld’s refusal to marry constitutes a wrongful act in the delictual sense thus warranting sentiment damages under the actio iniuriarum ([3]).

2. **Public Policy**: The court’s obligation to reassess and possibly redefine the legal basis for actions relating to breach of promise to marry, consistent with changing social norms and the current interpretation of the Bill of Rights, which recognizes diverse intimate relationships ([2]–[3]).

3. **Contractual Damages**: Examination of whether an engagement, as a form of consensual arrangement, should have ramifications akin to those of a formal contract, particularly in light of modern societal expectations and the increasing acceptance of non-traditional intimate relationships ([4]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([3–8])

The court discussed various precedents and legal principles critical to resolving the case:

1. **Actio Iniuriarum**: A breach of promise to marry can lead to damages under this delictual action if the repudiation is deemed contumacious, as established in the case of *Guggenheim v Rosenbaum* 1961 (4) SA 21 (W) at 36 ([4]).

2. **Public Policy Considerations**: The court cited Davis J’s remarks in *Sepheri v Scanlan* 2008 (1) SA 322 (C), urging a re-evaluation of the action due to its rigid contractual framing and the need to reflect modern values and relationships ([3]).

3. **Contemporary Understanding of Engagement**: The ruling took into account the evolution of societal mores regarding marriage and the implications for potential claims for compensation stemming from a breach of engagement, acknowledging broader considerations set forth in the Constitution, particularly section 39(2) which emphasizes developing the law in line with justice ([6]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([19–30])

The court undertook a thorough analysis concerning the nature of Mr. van Jaarsveld's breach. It assessed both the emotional and financial claims made by Ms. Bridges:

1. **Evaluation of Contumacy**: The court found that Mr. van Jaarsveld's communication during the breakup did not demonstrate a contumacious attitude. The text messages were apologetic and reflected his sincere burden regarding the decision ([19]). The court determined that miscommunication with respect to public acknowledgment did not equate to legal injury, especially given Bridges' public persona ([20]).

2. **Financial Claims**: Ms. Bridges’ claims for financial damages, including loss of income due to her anticipated role as a housewife, were scrutinized. The court noted that Ms. Bridges had an established income and had already received R200,000 from a separate investment agreement, which undermined her claims of financial loss related to the engagement. Thus, the court adjusted the previously granted awards downward, ultimately noting that many claims were either speculative or failed to demonstrate adequate evidence ([25]–[27]).

3. **Overall Implications of Relationships**: The court emphasized the importance of recognizing how societal perceptions of engagements and marriages have evolved. It ultimately advocated for a dismissal of claims that stemmed from an outdated view of engagements as fundamentally contractual in nature, arguing the need for contemporary legal interpretations that reflect modern relationships ([3]–[6], [30]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([30])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal with costs, including those for two counsel, concluding that the lower court had erroneously awarded damages to Ms. Bridges ([30]). The court decisively stated that the claim should have been dismissed based on two primary grounds: the lack of contumacious conduct by Mr. van Jaarsveld, and the failure of Ms. Bridges to substantiate her financial claims adequately. 

The ruling has significant implications for the future of breach of promise claims; it reflects an increasing judicial willingness to reevaluate traditional legal doctrines in line with contemporary principles of justice and public interest. The court's recognition that engagements should not be treated as rigid contracts aligns with broader societal shifts towards recognizing diverse relationship forms and the manner in which personal relationships interface with law. This reassessment fosters potential future developments in family law, especially regarding the emotional and contractual dimensions of engagements and marriages in South Africa.
