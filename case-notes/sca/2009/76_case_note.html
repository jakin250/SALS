Supreme Court of Appeal

J P Kriel v The Legal Aid Board (138/08) [2009] ZASCA 76

The appellant, Jan-Paul Kriel, an attorney employed by the Legal Aid Board, was dismissed after a disciplinary enquiry on 9 September 2004.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009 [2009] ZASCA 76

F – Facts

The appellant, Jan-Paul Kriel, an attorney employed by the Legal Aid Board, was dismissed after a disciplinary enquiry on 9 September 2004. Kriel had been employed since 1 August 1999 and served as the head of the Pretoria Legal Justice Centre. His dismissal followed a series of disputes with Ms Flavia Isola, the Justice Centre Executive. Tensions arose over the demand for new service contracts, which Kriel and his candidate attorneys refused to sign. After Kriel accused Ms Isola of pressuring staff and acting unethically by abandoning prior candidates, he was suspended pending a disciplinary enquiry initiated in December 2002 ([paras 1-6]).

During the enquiry, chaired by a Labour Consultant, Kriel was found guilty of misconduct, which he contested, but the internal appeal was dismissed ([paras 6-10]). Kriel subsequently launched review proceedings in the High Court, seeking to challenge his dismissal under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA), claiming it constituted unlawful administrative action ([paras 8-9]).

I – Issues

The central issue in this case was whether Kriel’s dismissal constituted "administrative action" under PAJA, thereby enabling judicial review of the decision. The High Court initially dismissed Kriel’s application on the grounds that it lacked jurisdiction to entertain a review under PAJA, which Kriel contested as being based on a misinterpretation of relevant precedent ([paras 2, 8-9]).

R – Rules

The legal principles applicable to this case include:

  1. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) – Establishes grounds for judicial review and mandates the right to fair administrative action ([para 12]).
  2. Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA) – Governs employment disputes and particularly provides recourse for unfair dismissals ([paras 12, 21]).
  3. Constitutional Court Precedents:
  • Chirwa v Transnet Ltd – Clarified that dismissals from public sector employment do not amount to administrative actions subject to PAJA review; instead, they must be addressed within the LRA framework ([paras 13-14]).
  • Sidumo v Rustenburg Platinum Mines Ltd – Affirmed that judgments regarding disciplinary hearings do not constitute administrative actions ([para 8]).

Additional statutes that may apply include the Protected Disclosures Act 26 of 2000, which protects whistleblowers ([para 11]).

A – Application

The appeal court clarified that the High Court erred in dismissing the case for lack of jurisdiction, as the true question was whether Kriel's dismissal constituted administrative action. The court reaffirmed the precedent set in Chirwa, emphasizing that under current labour law frameworks, dismissals are to be addressed via the LRA, rather than being construed as administrative actions ([paras 9-14]).

Kriel attempted to distinguish his case from Chirwa by arguing that his dismissal involved issues of contract law and improper delegation of authority. However, the court maintained that such arguments do not change the fundamental nature of the dismissal as a contractual issue rather than administrative action. The court noted that the essence of employment termination—whether in the public or private sector—remains contractual, aimed at managing employee relations ([paras 15-18]).

Moreover, even the complaints regarding the alleged misconduct of Ms Isola and Kriel’s subsequent unprotected disclosures did not constitute grounds for invoking PAJA. The court remarked that Kriel could pursue remedies under the LRA for unfair dismissal but had incorrectly elected to proceed with PAJA review ([paras 19-22]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the dismissal of Kriel did not amount to administrative action under PAJA, and therefore, the High Court's dismissal of Kriel's application could not be disturbed based on statutory jurisdiction, as the dismissal itself was inherently a contractual matter governed by labour relations law. As such, the appeal was dismissed with costs, affirming that Kriel had left available remedies under the LRA unaddressed ([paras 23-24]).

This ruling underscores the delineation between administrative and employment law within the context of public sector employment, reinforcing the notion that employment disputes, including dismissals, should primarily be adjudicated under the LRA framework rather than under PAJA. The decision has important implications for the rights of public sector employees navigating disputes regarding dismissals and the legal avenues available for redress.
