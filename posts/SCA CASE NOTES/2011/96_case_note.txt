Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

### Case Note: Eskom Pension and Provident Fund v Krugel [2011] ZASCA 96

#### F – Facts

The case originates from the appeal of the **Eskom Pension and Provident Fund** (the Fund) against the decision of the **South Gauteng High Court** involving a complaint lodged by **Krugel Elizabeth Maria** (the first respondent) with respect to her entitlement to a portion of the pension benefits of her ex-husband, **Mr. PJ Krugel**, who had been a member of the Fund ([1]–[2]). 

Mr. Krugel resigned from his position at Eskom on **31 January 1993** and elected to defer his pension benefits, thus becoming a deferred pensioner ([2]). The couple divorced on **14 September 2001**, and a settlement agreement was executed, which included a provision entitling the first respondent to **25%** of Mr. Krugel's pension interest, payable once he became entitled to pension benefits ([2]–[3]).

However, the Fund refused to register the endorsement of the divorce decree, contending that Mr. Krugel had already resigned and thus no pension interest was associated with his membership in the Fund as per the provisions of the Divorce Act 70 of 1979 and section 37D of the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956 ([3]). Following the Fund’s refusal, the first respondent lodged a complaint under section 30A of the Pension Funds Act, leading to the adjudication by **De La Rey EM N.O.** (the second respondent), who ruled in her favor copying an inclusive approach to the definition of 'pension interest' ([3]–[4]).

The Fund subsequently filed for review of the second respondent's decision under section 30P of the Pension Funds Act, which was upheld by the High Court, prompting this appeal ([5]–[6]).

#### I – Issues

The primary legal question before the court was whether, under sections 7(7) and 7(8) of the Divorce Act, a non-member spouse is entitled to a portion of the pension funds of their ex-spouse when that spouse had resigned prior to the divorce while deferring their pension benefits ([6]–[7]). Specifically, the appeal scrutinized the interpretation of what constitutes a pension interest following a member’s resignation and the prerequisites for sharing such an interest post-divorce ([6]–[8]).

#### R – Rules

The relevant statutory provisions include:

1. **Divorce Act 70 of 1979**, particularly sections 7(7) and 7(8), which govern the treatment of pension interest in divorce proceedings:
   - **Section 7(7)** stipulates that a party's pension interest during divorce is deemed part of their assets, allowing for division.
   - **Section 7(8)** permits courts to order that pension interests be paid out to the non-member spouse upon the member spouse becoming entitled to such benefits ([9]).

2. **Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956**, specifically section 37D, which outlines the conditions under which pension funds can deduct amounts owed to non-member spouses:
   - **Section 37D(4)(a)** states that the portion assigned to a non-member spouse is deemed to have accrued on the date of the divorce ([8]).

The interpretation of these statutes was central to determining the definition of 'pension interest' and the implications of Mr. Krugel's resignation on the first respondent's entitlement ([10]–[12]).

#### A – Application

In its analysis, the court held that once Mr. Krugel resigned from his post and opted to defer his pension benefits, he ceased to have a 'pension interest' as defined under the Divorce Act, making it immaterial whether he had been a member of the Fund prior to his divorce ([12]). The definitions and provisions analyzed suggest that a pension interest pertains specifically to benefits accruing post-resignation, and thus the non-member spouse's claim must derive from a valid existing interest at the time of divorce ([10]).

The court closely examined sections 7(7) and 7(8) in conjunction with section 37D of the Pension Funds Act. It concluded that Mr. Krugel’s resignation meant that there was no pension interest to 'assign' to the first respondent, violating the terms set forth in the relevant statutes ([13]–[14]). The adjudicator’s ruling had improperly assumed that a deferred benefit constituted a pension interest for the purposes of section 7(8), which was erroneous based on the timing of Mr. Krugel's resignation and subsequent deferral ([12]–[15]).

Moreover, the court noted that despite the unfavorable ruling for the first respondent, avenues remained open to her through the divorce settlement, allowing claims upon the eventual payment of Mr. Krugel’s benefits after he reached pensionable age ([15]).

#### C – Conclusion

The appeal was upheld, with the Supreme Court of Appeal finding in favor of the Fund and setting aside the ruling of the High Court and the second respondent’s determination. The court concluded that under the applicable provisions of both the Divorce Act and the Pension Funds Act, Mr. Krugel lacked a pension interest at the time of the divorce ([16]).

This ruling clarifies essential aspects of divorce settlements involving pension interests, particularly concerning the timing of a member's resignation in relation to divorce and the interpretation of statutory definitions of 'pension interest.' The implications from this judgment reinforce the necessity for precise legal definitions and understanding in matters of family law and pension entitlements, potentially guiding future cases of a similar nature.
