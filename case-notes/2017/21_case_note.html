Constitutional Court

United Democratic Movement v Speaker of the National Assembly and Others [2017] ZACC 21

Rules of the National Assembly: Specifically, Rule 102 permits the Assembly to determine the procedure for voting unless otherwise provided by the Constitution ([para 65-66]).

Constitutional Court 2017 [2017] ZACC 21

F – Facts

The United Democratic Movement (UDM) filed an application in the Constitutional Court seeking clarity and declaratory relief regarding the procedure for voting on a motion of no confidence against the President of South Africa. Triggered by the President's dismissal of the Finance Minister and subsequent economic downgrade, the motion was scheduled for 18 April 2017. On 6 April 2017, the UDM requested the Speaker of the National Assembly to allow voting by secret ballot on the no-confidence motion, citing concerns over the freedoms of Members of Parliament to express their votes without fear of repercussions. The Speaker refused, claiming she lacked the authority to prescribe such a procedure, referencing a prior ruling in Tlouamma v Speaker of the National Assembly [2015] ZAWCHC 140. In response, the UDM, supported by various political parties, sought direct access to the Constitutional Court to resolve whether the Speaker had such a power under the Constitution and the Rules of the National Assembly ([para 1-19]).

I – Issues

The main legal issues presented in this case are:

  1. Does the Speaker of the National Assembly have the constitutional power to prescribe a secret ballot for a motion of no confidence in the President?
  2. Are the Rules of the National Assembly consistent with the Constitution regarding the voting procedure for such a motion?
  3. Is the prior decision in Tlouamma regarding the lack of an implied constitutional requirement for a secret ballot applicable in this case? ([para 12-19]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal provisions and principles include:

  • Constitution of South Africa:
  • Section 102, which stipulates the procedure for motions of no confidence.
  • Section 57, which grants the National Assembly the authority to determine its own procedures.
  • Section 19(3)(a) guarantees the right to vote in secret for legislative bodies.
  • Section 42(3) emphasizes the representative function of Parliament ([para 43-39]).
  • Rules of the National Assembly: Specifically, Rule 102 permits the Assembly to determine the procedure for voting unless otherwise provided by the Constitution ([para 65-66]).
  • Precedent: The case of Tlouamma, which held that there is no express requirement for secret ballots regarding motions of no confidence in the President ([para 16-18]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court analyzed the provisions of the Constitution and the National Assembly's Rules to establish whether the Speaker had the power to authorize a secret ballot. Central to the court's reasoning was the interpretation of Section 57, which allows the Assembly to control its proceedings, and Section 102’s requirement for the Assembly to vote on motions of no confidence.

The court recognized that:

  • The Constitution does not expressly prohibit voting by secret ballot, nor does it mandate one specific method. Therefore, it leaves it to the Assembly, through the Speaker, to decide on the appropriate voting procedure ([para 59-60]).
  • Additionally, the Assembly’s power to resort to either open or secret ballot enhances legislative accountability by allowing Members to vote according to their conscience, especially on sensitive matters such as a motion of no confidence against the President ([para 77-81]).

The court found that the factual context of the motion of no confidence—involving a potential threat to Members' careers—justified the need for a secret ballot to ensure freedom from coercion and undistorted expression of will ([para 61-82]).

The court also noted that the Speaker’s earlier refusal, based on her interpretation of the Tlouamma decision, was erroneous and constrained her decision-making powers unnecessarily ([para 89-92]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ruled in favor of the UDM, finding that the Speaker does have the constitutional authority to authorize a secret ballot in a motion of no confidence. The prior assumption that the Constitution and the Rules did not support such a procedure was incorrect. The court set aside the Speaker's earlier decision and remitted the decision regarding the mode of voting back to her for reconsideration, emphasizing the need for effective accountability mechanisms within the parliamentary framework. The court also ordered that costs be borne by the Speaker and President, acknowledging the involvement of multiple parties in the proceedings ([para 96-97]).

This case underscores the significance of democratic principles of accountability and responsiveness, affirming the necessity for legislative organs to act in accordance with the Constitution while maintaining the integrity of their procedures.
