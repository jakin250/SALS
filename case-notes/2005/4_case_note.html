Institute for Security Studies v The State (CCT 30/03)

Constitutional Court 2005 CCT 30/03

F – Facts

The case arises from an application for admission as amicus curiae submitted by the Institute for Security Studies (ISS), a research organization focused on human security issues in Africa ([1]). The ISS sought to provide perspectives on matters concerning international criminal law relevant to the case involving Dr. Basson, the respondent. Specifically, the ISS aimed to address the role of crimes against humanity in South Africa's legal context, the dismissal of conspiracy charges, and the extraterritorial implications of the constitutional duty to prosecute international crimes ([2]).

The respondent, Dr. Basson, did not oppose the ISS’s application "in principle" but raised the argument that the issues it sought to introduce were not pertinent because they had either been abandoned by the state or were previously settled by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ([3]). The ISS’s claims for relevance were grounded in its ongoing research and advocacy work regarding the Rome Statute of the International Criminal Court and its implications for war crimes and crimes against humanity ([2]).

The legal framework for such applications resides within the rules governing amicus curiae admissions, specifically rule 10, which stipulates conditions under which external parties may contribute to judicial proceedings ([5]).

I – Issues

The court faced several legal and procedural issues:

  1. Whether the ISS could be admitted as amicus curiae given the opposition of the respondent and the earlier dismissal of related charges by the state ([4]).
  2. The interpretation of rule 10 of the court's procedural regulations concerning the admission of amici and the extent of the court's discretion in evaluating the application ([6]–[9]).
  3. Whether the submissions proposed by ISS would provide new insights or perspectives that would assist the court as required under the relevant rules ([10]).

R – Rules

The key legal principles are derived from the court's procedural rules, particularly rule 10, which governs applications for amicus curiae status.

  1. An applicant may be admitted only with the written consent of all parties, or, in the absence of such consent, by application to the Chief Justice ([5]).
  2. The principles to consider for the admission include the relevance of the proposed submissions, their potential usefulness to the court, and whether they present new contentions distinct from those put forth by the parties ([7]–[9]).
  3. A recent case cited as precedent, Fose v Minister of Safety and Security, emphasized that the court retains control over amicus participation irrespective of the parties' consent ([9]).

A – Application

In assessing the ISS's application, the court analyzed the proposed submissions and their relevance. Notably, the ISS articulated that its contributions would provide unique insights into aspects such as:

  • The evolution of individual criminal responsibility under international law.
  • The duty of states to prosecute or extradite individuals charged with international crimes ([12]).

However, upon reviewing the state's submissions, which comprehensively addressed crimes under international law, the court noted that the core issues the ISS wished to discuss had already been covered ([13]). Thus, the contributions by the ISS appeared to echo arguments already presented by the state, suggesting that no new perspectives would be introduced ([14]).

Additionally, the court considered the implications of admitting the ISS, particularly in light of the balance of fairness required in criminal matters. The specter of potentially bolstering the state’s case against the respondent, Dr. Basson, was a significant concern, as it could undermine the principles of equality before the law and the right to a fair trial ([15]).

Ultimately, it was determined that the ISS’s application did not satisfy the requisite criteria, as its proposed submissions did not differ substantively from those put forth by the other parties and lacked the necessary novelty to warrant intervention as amicus curiae ([16]).

C – Conclusion

The court concluded that the ISS’s application for admission as amicus curiae was denied. This decision reaffirmed the necessity for an amicus to not only demonstrate a relevant interest in the proceedings but to present distinct arguments that would aid the court in its deliberations. The ruling underscores the court's commitment to maintaining fairness in criminal proceedings, ensuring that additional arguments do not inadvertently skew the trial process against the accused ([16]).

Overall, this case serves as a precedent for the rigorous standards required for the admission of amicus curiae, emphasizing the court's discretion in upholding the integrity of judicial processes. The refusal of the ISS's application reflects judicial vigilance concerning the balance of rights, procedural fairness, and the substantive contributions to court proceedings in complex legal contexts.

Citation

Institute for Security Studies v The State CCT 30/03 [2005] ZACC 11; 2005 (8) BCLR 795 (CC).
