Supreme Court of Appeal

Arjo Wiggins Ltd v Idem (Pty) Ltd and Registrar of Trade Marks

The first respondent, Idem (Pty) Ltd, previously held a trade mark for "Idem" for a variety of goods related to writing and stationery until its registration lapsed in 1993 ([para 1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2001

F – Facts

Arjo Wiggins Ltd (hereinafter referred to as "the appellant") is a company incorporated in the United Kingdom and is the registered proprietor of the trade mark "Idem" in South Africa for various goods under the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 (the "Act") ([para 1]). The registration was granted in 1972 and pertained to goods such as paper, cardboard, and stationery. The appellant operates in South Africa through its local distributor, Carbonless Papers Ltd (CPL) ([para 1]).

The first respondent, Idem (Pty) Ltd, previously held a trade mark for "Idem" for a variety of goods related to writing and stationery until its registration lapsed in 1993 ([para 1]). After the lapse, the appellant initiated legal proceedings against the respondent in 1997, claiming an interdict and royalties for trade mark infringement under section 34 of the Act, alleging unauthorized use of the Idem mark on its products ([para 2]).

In response, the respondent sought an expungement of the appellant's trade mark registration under section 27 of the Act, claiming a lack of bona fide use of the mark on the registered goods ([para 3]). The initial court proceedings resulted in the expungement of the Appellant's registration, except for “carbonless paper” ([para 4]).

Procedural History

The appellant appealed the decision partially upholding the expungement, challenging the finding regarding its bona fide use of the Idem mark for goods beyond carbonless copying paper ([para 4]).

I – Issues

  1. Does the appellant's use of the Idem mark on promotional goods constitute “bona fide use” in accordance with section 27 of the Trade Marks Act?
  2. If the answer is negative, does the respondent have a valid basis for the removal of the appellant's trade mark registration given the use proved was limited to carbonless copying paper?

R – Rules

The relevant statutory provisions include:

  • Section 27 of the Act: This provision states, in part, that a trade mark may be removed from the register on grounds of non-use unless bona fide use has been established ([para 1]).
  • Section 34 of the Act: Outlines actions constituting trade mark infringement ([para 2]).

Precedent Cases:

  • *Gulf Oil Corporation v Rembrandt Fabrikante en Handelaars (Edms) Bpk*: Established the principle that bona fide use must have the purpose of protecting, facilitating, or furthering trade in the goods concerned ([para 6]).
  • *McDonald’s Corporation v Joburgers Drive-Inn Restaurant (Pty) Ltd*: Reinforced the relevance of bona fide user principles across different legislative contexts ([para 6]).

A – Application

In evaluating the merits of the appellant’s claims, the court assessed whether Arjo's secondary use of the Idem mark on promotional items constituted bona fide use. The appellant argued that the mark's application to promotional goods distinctively identified them, thereby furthering trade in its primary products ([para 8]).

The judgment emphasized that bona fide use entails not just any use of a mark, but use that promotes the goods for which the trade mark is registered. The appellant's secondary use, while "commercial" in intent, did not meet the statutory requirement of being "in relation to" the specific goods of which the mark was registered ([para 11]).

Ultimately, the court determined that actual, intended trading activities in relation to specific goods were necessary to establish bona fide use as defined under the Act ([para 12]). The ruling in Rembrandt provides a clear delineation; absent genuine trading in a product category, use does not fulfill the statutory requirements ([paras 7–10]). Therefore, as Arjo's activities concerning promotional goods did not constitute bona fide trading, the objections concerning the expungement were substantial.

The court then turned to consider the question of whether the registration could be more narrowly defined or expunged, considering arguments for limiting “paper and paper articles” to more explicitly defined categories ([para 13]).

Conclusion from Application

The decision came to rest on these two core findings:

  1. The application of the Idem mark to promotional items did not constitute bona fide use under the Act.
  2. There was no demonstrable commercial coherence that warranted a further restriction of the registration beyond the proven use of carbonless copying paper ([para 21]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately ruled in favor of the appellant, establishing that it had successfully demonstrated bona fide use of its trade mark only in relation to carbonless copying paper. The lower court's decision to expunge the registration entirely was modified to maintain the registration for "paper and paper articles" more broadly, recognizing that the demonstrated use did not warrant a complete removal from the register ([para 23]).

The implications of this case reinforce the necessity for trade mark owners to substantiate claims of use with genuine commercial activity related to specific registered goods. The judgment emphasizes the legal interpretation of “bona fide use,” thereby setting a precedent for future cases under the Trade Marks Act ([para 22]).

Order

The appeal succeeded, with costs awarded to the appellant while the lower court's findings regarding bona fide use were affirmed ([para 23]).
