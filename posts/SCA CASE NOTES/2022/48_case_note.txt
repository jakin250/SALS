Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: Thabo Sindisa Kwinana and Others v Lulama Smuts Ngonyama and Others [2022] ZASCA 48

## F - Facts

The case revolves around an appeal by the Kwinana parties, comprising Thabo Sindisa Kwinana and the trustees of the Eyabantu Development Trust, against a decision made by Grenfell AJ in the High Court of Johannesburg that refused to rescind two default judgments previously granted in favor of the Ngonyama parties (Mr. Lulama Smuts Ngonyama and the trustees of the Khululekile Family Trust) ([1]–[2]). 

The genesis of the dispute traces back to an oral agreement purportedly entered into in 2005, wherein the Kwinana parties, acting as agents for the Ngonyama parties, were tasked with procuring a 6.5% shareholding in Eyabantu Capital Consortium (Pty) Ltd ([2]). On the basis of this agreement, a subsequent oral agreement in 2006 allegedly stipulated that the Eyabantu Trust would transfer 50% of all income derived from its 13% shareholding in Eyabantu Consortium to the Khululekile Trust ([3]). Following a failure to file a plea within the requisite time, the High Court granted default judgments against the Kwinana parties on April 15 and 17, 2019 ([4]).

The Kwinana parties eventually sought to rescind the judgments, arguing that the particulars of claim did not establish a cause of action ([10]). Their application, however, was dismissed, leading them to appeal this decision ([4]).

## I - Issues

The central issue for determination was whether the Kwinana parties had made a sufficient case for rescission of the default judgments granted by Dosio AJ under Uniform Rule 42(1)(a) ([10]). Specifically, it necessitated an examination of whether the court erred in denying the Kwinana parties the chance to present a defense due to their late-filing of a replying affidavit and the claim that the particulars of claim lacked a legal basis for requiring compliance ([12]).

Additionally, there were procedural questions regarding the application for intervention by Eyabantu Capital and the Eyabantu Consortium, who contended they had a direct interest in the outcome of the appeal, but were opposed by the Ngonyama parties ([5]–[6]).

## R - Rules

In adjudicating this matter, the court referred to the established legal standard for joinder in civil procedure, as articulated in *Pheko and Others v Ekurhuleni City*, which mandates that a party seeking to intervene must demonstrate a direct and substantial interest in the subject matter of the litigation ([7]). The court also referenced *Lodhi 2 Properties Investments CC v Bondev Developments (Pty) Ltd*, which clarified that a judgment cannot be rendered erroneous based solely on the subsequent emergence of a defense ([10]).

The judges also emphasized the procedural aspects of Uniform Rule 42(1)(a), which allows for the rescission of a judgment granted in the absence of a party only if it is shown that such judgment was erroneously obtained ([10]).

## A - Application

In their analysis, the court assessed the merits of the Kwinana parties' argument for rescission based on their claim that the particulars of claim did not disclose a cause of action against them ([11]). The judges underscored that these arguments were not originally presented in the founding affidavit of the rescission application and were instead introduced in a late-filed replying affidavit. This submission was not admitted by the court due to its untimeliness and the fact that it effectively constituted a new rescission application that prejudiced the Ngonyama parties ([12]).

Furthermore, the court found that the Kwinana parties failed to meet the procedural requirements for filing a replying affidavit, having neglected to respond to a notice under Uniform Rule 30 to have the affidavit set aside or to apply for condonation for its late filing ([12]). Thus, the court concluded that the Kwinana parties had not properly set forth a basis for challenging the default judgments.

In relation to the application for intervention, the court ruled that the intervening parties failed to demonstrate a legal interest in the subject matter of the appeal, viewing their interest as indirect and insufficient to merit intervention ([8]–[9]).

## C - Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal filed by the Kwinana parties, affirming the High Court’s decision not to rescind the default judgments ([13]). The ruling carried costs against the appellants, highlighting the procedural rigidity and requirements that litigants must adhere to in civil proceedings. The implications of this judgment underscore the importance of timely compliance with procedural rules and the challenges faced by parties attempting to rectify defaults without substantive grounds or appropriate procedural adherence.

The decision serves as a reminder of the necessity for parties to present their defenses adequately and to comply with timelines set forth by the court in order to safeguard their interests in litigation. Thus, the appeal not only clarifies procedural expectations in instances of a default judgment but also reaffirms the necessity of groundwork in civil claims as a precursor to any recourse via rescission.
