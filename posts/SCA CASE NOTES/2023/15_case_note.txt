Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note

## Case Title: Isedor Skog N.O. & Others v Koos Agullus & Others (797/2021) [2023] ZASCA 15 

**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Judges**: Petse AP, Molemela JA, Makgoka JA, Basson AJ, Goosen AJ  
**Heard On**: November 7, 2022  
**Delivered On**: February 20, 2023  

---

### F - Facts

[1] This appeal arose from the order of the Land Claims Court (LCC) dismissing an application by the appellants, trustees of the Rein Trust, for the eviction of occupants from the Rein Hill Estate, a property they own. The occupiers, comprising multiple families, had previously been employed by the Trust’s predecessor, Amen Trust, and resided on the farm in cottages constructed for former employees. Each cottage was occupied either by former employees or their families, with the nature of the construction varying between durable materials and temporary structures ([2–4]).

[2] Upon the end of the employment relationship on June 24, 2011, the Trust issued a notice to the occupiers to vacate the farm, which they ignored. Subsequent attempts to evict them through the Magistrates' Court also failed, primarily due to the lack of evidence regarding suitable lease agreements and the disputes over conduct leading to the perceived breakdown in the relationship between the Trust and the occupiers ([5–12]).

[3] In May 2018, the Trust issued new notices of termination of residence rights and subsequently filed for eviction in the LCC in June 2019, citing a breakdown due to claimed misconduct by the occupiers and non-payment of rent since the cessation of the employment relationship ([13–14]). The LCC, in its ruling, dismissed the eviction application, focusing on insufficient grounds and the allegation of collective misconduct without specific attributions to particular occupiers ([14–16]).

### I - Issues

[4] The appeal raised two primary legal questions:
1. Was the termination of the occupiers’ right of residence just and equitable under the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA)?
2. Did the prior judgment of the Magistrates' Court render the current eviction claim res judicata ([3], [12], [63])?

### R - Rules

[5] The applicable legal framework is primarily set forth in the ESTA, which outlines conditions under which an occupier's right of residence can be terminated, emphasizing that such termination must be just and equitable ([19]). Key provisions include:

- **Section 8**: Details lawful grounds for the termination of residence, requiring consideration of various factors including the fairness of the procedure followed ([20]).
- **Section 9**: Stipulates notice requirements for eviction applications ([28]).
- **Section 10-11**: Provide specific circumstances under which eviction can be ordered, including potential breaches of the relationship between landowners and occupiers ([28–29]).

In addition to ESTA, constitutional considerations regarding the right to housing (especially for vulnerable individuals) are pertinent, demonstrating the balance courts must strike between property rights and social welfare ([1]).

### A - Application

[6] The SCA's analysis began by recognizing the preceding LCC's failure to consider the seriousness of misconduct alleged against the occupiers, thereby misapplying the standards set in previous rulings (i.e., the Plascon-Evans principle) which operates under the assumption that disputes of fact favor the version presented by the respondents unless clear and credible objections exist ([24]).

[7] The Court noted the lack of compliance by the occupiers with established house rules, their failure to express a desire for dialogue with the Trust, and their inability to demonstrate accountability regarding the alleged misconduct ([24–42]). It ruled that the evidentiary basis for eviction—rooted in the irretrievable breakdown of the relationship and documented harmful behavior—met the just and equitable standards required by ESTA. The occupancy was characterized as untenable due to health risks and property damage ([31–41]).

[8] Regarding the res judicata claim, the Court found that the LCC’s earlier decision did not preclude the current proceedings, as the foundational issues relating to alleged misconduct and the justification for treatment had not been conclusively determined in the earlier magistrate's proceedings ([63–72]).

### C - Conclusion

[9] The SCA upheld the appeal, concluding that the eviction was justified and the LCC's refusal to grant it was erroneous both in fact and law. The Court stressed that the relationship between the Trust and the occupiers had fundamentally broken down due to chronic misconduct, justifying the Trust's actions in seeking eviction under ESTA. The appeal was granted, overturning the LCC's ruling and allowing eviction with sufficient time for the occupiers to vacate and the provision of emergency housing through municipal support ([78]).

### Implications

[10] This case reinforces the judicial anthem of balancing property rights with social justice, particularly in matters of eviction. It presents a clarion call for future considerations of the justness of evictions, ensuring that the conduct of all parties is scrutinized rigorously while upholding the rights of the vulnerable. Moreover, it sheds light on the procedural necessities to maintain transparency and accountability in eviction proceedings, particularly within the contextual framework of South Africa's historical injustices surrounding land and habitation ([49–60]).
