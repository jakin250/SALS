Case Note: REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2005

# FIRAC Case Note: Absa Bank Limited v Johann Jacobus Izak Stephanus Lombard

## F – Facts

[1] This case concerns an appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa, wherein Absa Bank Limited (the appellant) challenged a judgment issued by De Vos J in the Pretoria High Court. The trial court had dismissed the appellant's claim in reconvention for R51,796.60, alongside interest and costs.

[2] The dispute arose from a loan agreement executed in January 1988 between the respondent, Dr. Johann Jacobus Izak Stephanus Lombard, and the Trust Bank of Africa Limited, the appellant's predecessor. The respondent borrowed R259,995.60, which increased the principal debt to R260,000 after accounting for stamp duty. The agreement stipulated an interest rate of 15.55% per annum, subject to adjustment at the lender's exclusive discretion.

[3] Various clauses in the loan agreement allowed the lender to alter the interest rate, including a clause specifically stating the lender's discretion. Despite this, the respondent contended that the increased rates over the life of the loan exceeded what would be a reasonable adjustment, particularly as the interest rate varied significantly between 1988 and 1997.

[4] By May 1997, the respondent had paid a total of R701,055.71 in stop orders, believing that the incremental increases in interest were unjustified and that he had overpaid R187,130.51 relative to what he would have owed had the initial interest rate remained unchanged.

[5] The appellant countered with a claim in reconvention, asserting that the respondent still owed R51,796.60 based on valid variances to the interest rate made within its presumed bounds of discretion. The respondent's defence included allegations that adjustments to the interest rate were not executed in a reasonable manner.

[6] Subsequent judicial interpretation indicated that the discretion concerning interest adjustments must be exercised reasonably and in accordance with market conditions (as established in NBS Boland Bank Ltd v One Berg River Drive CC). This interpretation contributed to the respondent's withdrawal of his initial claim, tendering wasted costs prior to the trial.

## I – Issues

[16] The central legal questions before the court were:

1. Did the appellant and its predecessor reasonably exercise their discretion in not reducing the interest rate when the prime lending rate fell, despite choosing to raise it when rates increased? 
2. Was the onus of proving a reasonable exercise of discretion on the appellant, or did it rest with the respondent?

## R – Rules

[9] The relevant legal principle, as delineated in NBS Boland Bank Ltd v One Berg River Drive CC, is that while lenders may possess discretionary power regarding interest rate adjustments, such discretion must be exercised arbitrio boni viri, i.e., reasonably. Further, the case of Cohen v Louis Blumberg (Pty) Ltd identifies that waiving certain legal exceptions may place the burden of proof upon the party who waives them.

## A – Application

[20] In the court's assessment, the appellant's argument asserting that the discretion was not unreasonably exercised could not be sustained. Despite the appellant's claims of valid variations in interest rates, the court highlighted that the interest charges during the relevant timeframe resulted in excessive repayments, implying that had the interest been held at the initial margin above prime, the respondent would have adequately discharged his debt by January 1998.

[14] Testimony from the appellant’s sole witness did not satisfactorily rebut the respondent's assertion that the adjustments were unreasonably harsh, particularly in light of stable or diminished risk profiles over time. Consequently, the court found that the prima facie case provided by the respondent established grounds to question the appellant's exercise of discretion in varying interest rates.

[19] The arguments presented by the appellant about the burden of proof were also addressed, ultimately noting the trial court's appropriate determination that obligations to show the reasonableness of the interest adjustments were a function of the borrower’s objections to the extent of the lender's claims.

## C – Conclusion

[21] The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal and reaffirmed the lower court's judgment, concluding that the appellant had not exercised its discretion reasonably in adjusting the interest rates as it had. The broader implications of the ruling reinforce the jurisprudential stance that lenders must justify their adjustments to borrower costs under conditions of changing interest rates, thereby promoting fairness and transparency in lending practices.

This case thus highlights the judicial expectation that creditors must engage in fair lending practices and reinforces the implications of the duty to exercise discretion in good faith, encouraging a recalibration of bank practices with respect to interest rate adjustments.
