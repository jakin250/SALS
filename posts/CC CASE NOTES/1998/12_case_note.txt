Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1998

# FIRAC Case Note: Joaquim Augusto de Freitas and Another v Society of Advocates of Natal

**Court**: Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Case Number**: CCT 2/98  
**Heard on**: 21 May 1998  
**Decided on**: 15 September 1998  
**Judgment by**: Langa DP

---

## F – Facts

The case arises from a disciplinary proceeding initiated by the Society of Advocates of Natal (the Respondent) against Joaquim Augusto de Freitas (the First Applicant), who was found guilty of unprofessional conduct for accepting direct work from clients, contrary to the so-called "referral rule." The referral rule mandates that advocates must accept work only through attorneys ([1]). The High Court subsequently suspended de Freitas from practice for six months and dismissed a counter-application by the Independent Association of Advocates of South Africa (the Second Applicant), which sought to declare that its members were not bound by the referral rule and could thus accept work from the public directly ([1]).

The initial proceedings began in September 1996, initiated by the Respondent which sought to have de Freitas struck from the roll of advocates. The Second Applicant intervened, leading to its counter-application in April 1997 ([2]).

The crux of the applications is twofold: First, the constitutionality of section 7(2) of the Admission of Advocates Act, 1964, which permits disciplinary proceedings against non-members initiated by the Respondent; and second, whether the referral rule infringes the advocate's constitutional right to freely practice their profession ([3]).

## I – Issues

1. **The constitutionality of section 7(2) of the Admission of Advocates Act**: The First Applicant argues that this section is unconstitutional as it allows the Respondent to initiate disciplinary actions against advocates who are not its members ([3]).
  
2. **The constitutional validity of the referral rule**: The applicants claim that the referral rule violates their rights under sections 22 and 39(3) of the Constitution, violating their right to practice freely without unnecessary regulation by a competing profession ([11]).

## R – Rules

The relevant legal frameworks include:

- **Section 7(2) of the Admission of Advocates Act, 1964**: Authorizes the General Council of the Bar or any society of advocates to apply to the courts for the suspension or striking off an advocate from the roll ([6]).
  
- **Section 22 of the Constitution of South Africa, 1996**: Provides that every citizen has the right to choose their trade, occupation, or profession freely, subject to regulation by law.
  
- **Section 39(3) of the Constitution**: Asserts that this section does not deny the existence of rights or freedoms recognized under common law, customary law, or legislation, provided they are consistent with the Bill of Rights ([11]).

## A – Application

### Issue 1: Constitutionality of Section 7(2)

The First Applicant's argument that section 7(2) infringes constitutional rights was scrutinized. While it was asserted that the provision caused discrimination against non-members, it was clarified that section 7(2) did not grant the Respondent jurisdiction over non-members but merely provided standing to bring disciplinary matters before the court. The High Court unanimously found no substance in the claim that section 7(2) violates constitutional rights. The judges cited that prior jurisprudence supported the ability of the courts to discipline attorneys regardless of membership in a specific society ([8]-[10]). 

### Issue 2: Referral Rule

The Second Applicant claimed the referral rule infringes their constitutional rights by requiring advocates to rely on attorneys for work. This contention invoked sections 22 and 39(3) of the Constitution. However, the Court noted that the referral rule is well-established in South African legal practice and serves to delineate the roles between advocates and attorneys, thereby contributing to orderly legal practice ([11]-[12]). 

Moreover, the Court emphasized that the referral rule has a long-standing precedent and is subject to development by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA). Thus, it should not be decided by the Constitutional Court without prior adjudication by the SCA ([23]-[26]). The provision regulates conduct consistent with protecting the legal profession rather than infringing on rights.

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court denied the applications for leave to appeal, determining that both issues raised by the applicants were without merit. First, they did not demonstrate a constitutional infringement based on section 7(2) of the Advocates' Act. Secondly, the referral rule was found to be a necessary regulation that did not conflict with the constitutional right to practice ([28]). Furthermore, the Court highlighted that the SCA should initially adjudicate such matters involving the development of common law standards and the regulation of legal practice ([27]-[28]).

The judgment serves to clarify the separation of functions between the professions within the legal framework, illustrating the balance between regulation and the right to practice law in South Africa.
