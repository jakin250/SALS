Source Folder: 2000

# Case Note: Gerhardus Francois Janse Van Rensburg NO v Minister of Trade and Industry NO & Another

**Court:** Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Case Number:** CCT 13/99  
**Heard on:** 31 August 2000  
**Decided on:** 29 September 2000  
**Judgment by:** Goldstone J

---

## FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts
The case involves challenges against the constitutionality of specific provisions of the Consumer Affairs (Unfair Business Practices) Act 71 of 1988 (the Act), specifically sections 7(3) and 8(5)(a). The applicants are Gerhardus Francois Janse Van Rensburg NO, a trustee for Omega Trust Power Marketing CC. The latter is involved in promoting consumer power through collective bargaining. After an informal investigation, the Consumer Affairs Committee initiated formal investigations into the practices of the second applicant, leading to fears that the powers granted under sections 7(3) and 8(5)(a) might be used against them, even though they had not been exercised.

On 26 October 1998, the Transvaal High Court ruled against these sections, declaring them constitutionally invalid, with the declaration taking effect only after confirmation by the Constitutional Court. The applicants allowed their appeal to lapse, with only the Minister participating in the confirmation proceedings.

Following this, the Law Review Project (LRP) applied for amicus curiae status shortly before the hearing, seeking to argue in favor of the constitutional invalidity determined by the High Court ([1]–[10]).

### I – Issues
The main legal issues presented relate to whether:
1. Sections 7(3) and 8(5)(a) of the Act are constitutionally valid.
2. The declaration of unconstitutional provisions from the High Court should be confirmed despite the amendments to the relevant sections.
3. The provisions of section 8(5)(a) violate the right to lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair administrative action as outlined in section 33 of the Constitution ([11]–[20]).

### R – Rules
The relevant legal provisions involved include:
1. **Section 14 of the Constitution:** Guarantees the right to privacy, protecting individuals against arbitrary search and seizure ([8]).
2. **Section 36 of the Constitution:** Addresses the limitation of rights under the Bill of Rights, requiring justifiable and reasonable limitations based on critical factors ([7]).
3. **Section 33 of the Constitution:** Ensures every individual has a right to lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair administrative action ([18]).
4. Prior case law establishing precedents on constitutional validity, procedural fairness, and the necessity for legislative guidance in the exercise of administrative discretion ([18]–[26]).

### A – Application
**Section 7(3) Analysis:**
The Court noted that section 7(3), allowing for searches and seizures without judicial authorization, violated the right to privacy guaranteed in the Constitution. The original provision did not survive constitutional scrutiny and thus became moot upon amendment. The Court declined to confirm the invalidity because there was no current application of the previous law affecting any parties ([9]–[10]).

**Section 8(5)(a) Analysis:**
In evaluating section 8(5)(a), the Court affirmed the High Court's ruling that the Minister's discretionary powers, which included the ability to suspend alleged unfair business practices and freeze assets based solely on preliminary opinions without due process, failed to meet constitutional standards for administrative justice, particularly the requirements of procedural fairness. Key deficiencies identified included the absence of a pre-decision hearing and the lack of sufficient guidance governing the Minister’s discretion ([17], [29]).

The Court recognized that while unregulated administrative discretion can lead to abuses that violate citizens' rights, an objective framework is necessary for legitimate administrative action. Since the Minister's actions could cause irreparable harm to businesses without adequate pre-emptive safeguards, the section was ruled unconstitutional and unjustifiable under section 36 which governs limitations on rights ([24]–[26]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court confirmed the declaration of invalidity for section 8(5)(a) but took into account the necessity for legislative action to address the urgent nature of unfair business practices. Hence, the order of invalidity was suspended for twelve months, allowing Parliament time to amend the law in a manner that safeguards rights while enabling necessary regulatory measures against unfair business practices. The Court's holding reflects a balance between protecting public interests and ensuring due process in administrative action ([27]–[36]).

---

This case underscores the Court's commitment to uphold constitutional rights while recognizing the need for effective consumer protection frameworks, demonstrating the delicate balance between regulatory enforcement and individual rights.
