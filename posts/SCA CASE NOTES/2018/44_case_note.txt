Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis

### Case Title: The National Consumer Commission v Univision Services Association NPC (618/2017) [2018] ZASCA 44

### F – Facts

The appeal arises from a decision of the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, concerning a costs order related to complaints received by the National Consumer Commission (hereafter "the Commission"). The Commission, established under section 85(1) of the Consumer Protection Act 68 of 2008 (CPA), acted after receiving hundreds of consumer complaints about the first to eleventh respondents, collectively referred to as "Univision," which operates in the timeshare industry ([paras 1–2]).

The complaints primarily pertained to Univision's marketing and collection practices concerning 'points' sold as timeshare interests ([para 3]). In November 2014, the Commission referred these complaints to the National Consumer Tribunal (hereafter "the Tribunal") under section 73(2)(b) of the CPA. This referral was initiated with specific relief sought, including declarations against unconscionable conduct and false misrepresentation regarding the sale of timeshare points ([paras 6–7]).

However, just before the Tribunal hearing scheduled for November 9, 2015, the Commission submitted a notice of withdrawal, indicating it did not consent to pay costs pending the Tribunal's determination ([paras 9–10]). Univision, aggrieved by the withdrawal, subsequently sought a costs order against the Commission, arguing that it had pursued the complaint in a frivolous and vexatious manner ([paras 10–12]).

In November 2015, the Tribunal ordered that the costs be borne by each party, relying on section 147 of the National Credit Act 34 of 2005 (NCA), which prescribes that parties involved in a Tribunal hearing must bear their own costs under certain conditions ([para 11]). Univision sought a review of this decision from the High Court, which ultimately ruled that the Tribunal could, indeed, assess costs in light of the regulatory framework, contradicting the Commission’s standpoint ([para 16]).

### I – Issues

Central to this appeal is the determination of whether the Tribunal had the authority to award costs in situations where the referral to the Tribunal was withdrawn by the Commission. Specifically, the legal question is whether section 147 of the NCA precludes the Tribunal from granting a costs order against the Commission in these circumstances ([paras 1, 12, 24]).

Additionally, the case raises the question of the applicability of the regulations under the CPA and their compatibility with the statutory framework provided by the NCA, particularly in distinguishing between the roles and responsibilities of the Commission and consumer complainants under differing sections of the CPA ([paras 17, 22]).

### R – Rules

The relevant legal framework consists of the following:

1. **Consumer Protection Act 68 of 2008 (CPA)**: This Act governs the rights of consumers and outlines the obligations of suppliers, detailing the procedures for lodging complaints and seeking redress ([para 4]).

2. **National Credit Act 34 of 2005 (NCA)**: Particularly, section 147 stipulates that in hearings before the Tribunal, parties generally bear their own costs unless specific conditions allowing for cost orders against a party have been met ([paras 11, 24]).

3. **Regulations under both Acts**: These regulations, particularly regulations 19(2) and 25(4), outline the procedures for cost assessment in the context of applications before the Tribunal ([para 15]).

4. **Judicial Precedents**: The judgment in *Competition Commission of South Africa v Pioneer Hi-Bred International Inc* serves as a precedent, illustrating the limitations on cost orders against state organs like the Commission when acting in the public interest ([para 23]).

### A – Application

The appeal court conducted a thorough analysis of the powers of the Tribunal in light of the conflicting conclusions drawn by the lower courts regarding costs. The court emphasised that the Commission operates not merely as a litigant but as a state entity mandated to protect consumer interests ([para 17]). This distinction is critical because it suggests that the general practice of awarding costs in civil litigation does not neatly apply to cases involving the Commission.

The court examined the language of section 147 and noted that the Tribunal is limited in its powers to award costs, specifically when the complaint is made via the Commission’s intervention, primarily due to the Commission's role as a regulatory body ([para 21]). It reinforced that the regulations and the NCA work together, establishing a framework that restricts the Tribunal's powers to only those circumstances explicitly enumerated by the legislature.

The court further clarified that while private complainants could be subject to cost awards under varying circumstances, the Commission’s submission of withdrawal before the hearing does not constitute actionable grounds for imposing a cost order due to the public interest it serves ([paras 18, 22]). The court referenced the fundamental flaw in the court a quo’s reasoning, asserting that regulations should not extend the scope of the powers laid down in the NCA, therefore barring costs against the Commission in such proceedings ([para 21]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the Tribunal correctly determined it lacked the power to award costs against the Commission upon the withdrawal of its referral. It decided that section 147 expressly precludes a costs order in this context and that the lower court had erred in substituting its decision ([paras 24–25]). Consequently, the appeal was upheld, and the order of the court a quo was set aside.

The ruling reinforces the notion that regulatory bodies, when acting in the public interest, do not engage in litigation as a traditional party would. Therefore, the implications extend to how cost awards are approached in consumer protection matters, ensuring that these bodies can function without the hindrance of costs that would otherwise deter them from pursuing their mandated public duties.

---

This case note presents an analytical framework for understanding the judicial interpretation of costs in consumer protection contexts within South Africa. The decision underscores the legislative intent to protect consumer interests while also delineating the roles of regulatory versus private parties in legal proceedings.
