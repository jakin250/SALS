Malan v City of Cape Town [2014] ZACC 25

Constitutional Court 2014 [2014] ZACC 25

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The applicant, Johanna Malan (Ms. Malan), is a 74-year-old widow residing at 100D Sonderend Road in Manenberg, Cape Town, under a lease agreement with the City of Cape Town (the City) that began in 1979 ([1]–[5]). After her husband's death in 1982, she entered a lease agreement with the City. The City canceled the lease on 31 October 2008, citing rental arrears of R8,290.90 as of April 2008 and alleged illegal activities associated with the property. The City served notice of cancellation on 23 November 2008, allowing Ms. Malan until 31 December 2008 to vacate the property ([7]).

Despite receiving the notice, Ms. Malan continued to reside at the property. The City proceeded to seek an eviction order in the Western Cape High Court, arguing that she was an unlawful occupier due to the lease's cancellation for both rental arrears and the purported illegal activities occurring on the premises ([8]–[10]). The High Court ultimately granted the eviction, noting that Ms. Malan’s default on the rental obligation and failure to rectify performance justified the City’s actions ([15]).

Malan opposed the eviction, arguing that the cancellation of the lease was invalid due to lack of notice of the alleged illegal activities and that certain clauses of the lease agreement were unconstitutional ([12]–[13]). She asserted that the City was obliged to afford her the opportunity to rectify any breach before proceeding with eviction ([16]).

I – Issues

The core legal issues before the Constitutional Court included:

  1. Whether the applicant should be granted leave to appeal against the High Court's eviction order ([19]).
  2. The constitutionality of clauses 2, 24, and 28 of the lease, in regard to public policy and fundamental rights.
  3. Whether Ms. Malan was in breach of the lease agreement, justifying the City’s cancellation ([19]).
  4. The applicability and proper application of the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act (PIE) in this case ([19]).

R – Rules

The court primarily relied on the provisions of the South African Constitution, particularly section 26, which guarantees the right to adequate housing and protects individuals against arbitrary eviction ([3]). The court also considered relevant precedents established by past rulings regarding the interplay between public housing contracts and constitutional rights, notably the principles articulated in Jaftha v Hyman and Barkhuizen v Napier concerning fairness and the need for procedural protections within housing agreements ([23], [25]).

Furthermore, the court scrutinized clauses of the lease, particularly:

  • Clause 2: Permitting termination of the lease with one month's notice;
  • Clause 24: Allowing for cancellation in cases of involvement in illegal activities; and
  • Clause 28: Enabling summary cancellation for non-payment of rent without an opportunity to rectify the breach ([29], [32]).

These rules were balanced against the broader context of providing public housing and ensuring that tenants maintain their dignity and rights under the Constitution.

A – Application

The Constitutional Court found that the applicant's situation could not be adequately addressed by a simple application of contract law principles in the context of public housing. It emphasized that while the City had grounds to cancel the lease, procedural fairness required it to afford Ms. Malan the opportunity to rectify any alleged breaches of the lease ([34]).

  • Regarding Clause 2, the court highlighted that a mere notice period without cause could lead to arbitrary evictions and breach the lessee's security of tenure ([30]). Therefore, the application of this clause was deemed unconstitutional when it facilitated circumstances that undermined the protections afforded to vulnerable tenants.
  • In terms of Clause 28, the court ruled that allowing summary cancellation without providing an opportunity to remedy the breach violated the fundamental rights protected under section 26 of the Constitution, as the informality of contracts in public rental schemes does not excuse procedural inequities ([34]).
  • Concerning Clause 24, the court noted that reliance on a breach deemed by mere allegations (rather than through legal conviction) was problematic, especially since Ms. Malan was neither directly involved in nor had knowledge of the alleged illegal activities conducted by her family ([60], [76]).

Overall, the court concluded that the City had failed to demonstrate that it had adequately communicated concerns regarding illegal activities before pursuing cancellation and eviction, thereby undermining the applicant's rights ([150]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately concluded that Ms. Malan was entitled to appeal the High Court's eviction order. The court held that the City had failed to provide the procedural fairness required by both contract law and constitutional obligations, as it did not afford Ms. Malan the opportunity to respond to allegations of breach before cancellation ([87]). As such, despite grounds for cancellation based on the lease terms, the City was required to ensure any eviction was just and equitable, particularly given Ms. Malan's vulnerable status as an elderly tenant who had resided there for decades.

The appeal was granted, results in dismissal of the eviction order, and no costs were awarded, reflecting the importance of the constitutional issue at play and the applicant's significant public interest rights involved in the case ([87]). The ruling reinforced the essential nature of procedural protections in housing agreements, particularly those involving public entities, and underscored the judiciary’s role in ensuring adherence to constitutional standards in public housing administration.
