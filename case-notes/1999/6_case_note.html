Constitutional Court

The Premier of the Province of the Western Cape v The Electoral Commission

Despite various efforts to resolve the disagreement through correspondence, the parties failed to reach an agreement.

Constitutional Court 1999

FIRAC Analysis

F – Facts

The dispute arose from a determination made by the Electoral Commission concerning the number of seats in the Western Cape Provincial Parliament in preparation for the second democratic election in South Africa. On 17 March 1999, the Commission decided that the Western Cape would have 39 seats, calculated using a formula of one representative per 100,000 inhabitants (which yielded 39 seats, given a population of 3,956,875) ([paras 1–2]). However, the applicants—the Premier of the Province of the Western Cape and the Speaker of the Provincial Parliament—contended that, as per section 13 of the Constitution of the Western Cape 1998, the province was entitled to 42 seats ([para 2]).

Despite various efforts to resolve the disagreement through correspondence, the parties failed to reach an agreement. Consequently, on 20 May 1999, just 13 days before the election, the applicants approached the Constitutional Court for relief, asserting exclusive jurisdiction under section 167(4)(a) of the national Constitution, or alternatively seeking direct access pursuant to Rule 17 ([paras 3–5]).

I – Issues

The key legal issue was whether the number of seats allocated to the Western Cape Provincial Parliament, as stated in its provincial constitution, was legally valid in light of conflicting national legislation under section 105(2) of the national Constitution and the Electoral Act ([paras 6–8]). Specifically, it revolved around the interpretation of whether a provincial constitution could dictate the structure of its legislature in opposition to national legislative provisions designed to standardize such frameworks across different provinces.

R – Rules

The legal framework includes several constitutional provisions:

  1. Section 105(2) of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, which stipulates that a provincial legislature consists of between 30 and 80 members, determined by a formula provided in national legislation ([para 7]).
  2. Section 143(1) of the national Constitution allows provincial constitutions to diverge regarding legislative structures and procedures as long as they do not contravene the founding values and principles set forth in the national Constitution ([para 9]).
  3. Section 147(1)(a) establishes that, in the event of a conflict between national legislation and a provision of a provincial constitution regarding a matter requiring national legislation, the national legislation prevails ([para 7]).

The court was also directed to previous judgments asserting that provincial legislatures could create their structures as sanctioned by their constitutions, which could not be overridden by national legislation unless expressly stated ([para 10]).

A – Application

The court analyzed whether section 105(2) and the corresponding national legislation applied to the composition of the provincial legislature. Ensuing from the interpretation of section 143(1), it concluded that the provincial constitution could indeed dictate the number of legislature seats distinct from the national framework, provided that it complied with constitutional foundational values and principles ([paras 9–10]).

The court's reasoning highlighted that the provisions in questions were default provisions and did not impose restrictions on provincial constitutions already expressly determined by the province. The argument posited by respondents that having 42 seats would contravene the principle of equality was dismissed, as the proportional representation system ensured that voter strength was maintained ([paras 11–13]).

Ultimately, the court determined that the provision in the provincial constitution for 42 seats held greater legal weight than the national legislation’s determination of 39 seats, rendering the latter invalid ([paras 14–15]). The court affirmed the autonomy of provinces to shape their governance structures as enshrined in their constitutions while adhering to the overarching compliance with the national Constitution’s values.

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court issued a unanimous ruling invalidating the Electoral Commission’s determination of 39 seats as conflicting with the Western Cape's constitutional provision for 42 seats. The ruling underscored the authority of provincial constitutions in defining the framework of their legislative bodies, thereby solidifying a vital aspect of federalism within South Africa ([para 1]). The decision set a precedent that reinforced provincial autonomy while maintaining compliance with the principles of the national Constitution.

The court ordered costs to be paid by the respondents, signalling a recognition of the merit in the applicants’ position ([para 15]). This case is significant for its implications on the broader constitutional law realm, especially regarding the delicate balance between national standards and provincial autonomies in legislative structures.

In summary, The Premier of the Province of the Western Cape v The Electoral Commission reinforces the principles of constitutional supremacy while allowing for provincial legislative uniqueness, enhancing the framework of federalism in South Africa.
