# Case Note Using FIRAC Method: Hermanus Stephanus Pretorius v Coopers Theron Du Toit

## F – Facts

The case revolves around an appeal by Hermanus Stephanus Pretorius (hereafter "the appellant"), who sought to overturn a judgment in favor of Coopers Theron Du Toit (hereafter "the respondent"). The appellant was a partner in the accounting firm Coopers Theron Du Toit and claimed that upon his withdrawal from the partnership on 30 June 1992, certain amounts were due to him based on a dissolution agreement ([paras 2–4]).

The discontent stemmed from the interpretation of Clause 8 of the dissolution agreement which detailed how financial statements would be prepared and what debts could be written off. The respondent contended that the only debt applicable was that of N C K Hydraulics, while the appellant argued that other amounts should be considered as well. The trial court found against the appellant, basing its decision on credibility findings regarding the discussions held on 3 June 1992 before the dissolution ([paras 3–4]).

The key issue pertained to whether the discussions at that meeting encompassed the writing off of work in progress, as opposed to debts already acknowledged. Both parties provided evidence, but the court found the respondent's witnesses to be more credible. This decision was further complicated by correspondence, and lack of rebuttals by the appellant regarding the final deductions agreed upon ([paras 4–5]).

## I – Issues

The central issue on appeal was whether the trial court correctly interpreted the dissolution agreement, specifically the parameters for writing off debts, including work in progress and other deductions, following the appellant's withdrawal from the partnership. The crux of the matter lay in:

1. Whether a tacit agreement was reached on 3 June 1992 concerning the debts to be written off.
2. The credibility of witness testimonies regarding the discussions prior to the formal dissolution agreement.

Additionally, the appeal examined the provisions of the dissolution agreement, specifically the interplay between oral agreements made during the negotiations and the subsequently signed written terms, particularly in light of how these terms related to Clause 8.1 and its implications for later deductions ([paras 2–4, 8–11]).

## R – Rules

The decision of the court relied heavily on established contract law principles, including:

1. **Interpretation of Contracts**: The judgment assessed the interplay of oral and written agreements, in line with the principles established in *Johnston v Leal* 1980 (3) SA 927 (A) regarding the admissibility of prior discussions that clarify the intended contractual terms ([para 10]).

2. **Credibility of Witnesses**: The trial court's evaluation of witness credibility set the groundwork for upholding its findings. The court distinguished between the evidence presented by the appellant and respondent, siding with the latter due to perceived credibility and the likelihood of the narrative they presented ([paras 4–6]).

3. **Contractual Obligations**: The obligations of the parties under the dissolution agreement were dissected, determining whether the appellant had effectively waived certain rights or if the respondent had failed to meet their own obligations under said agreement ([paras 7–10]).

## A – Application

In analyzing the court's application of the legal principles to the facts, the court maintained that the discussions on 3 June had led to a form of tacit agreement regarding what debts were considered for writing off against the firm's income.

The court's findings emphasized that it was improbable for an experienced firm of accountants, represented by the respondent's witnesses, to overlook critical financial elements such as work in progress during discussions of this nature. Consequently, the arguments presented by the appellant regarding the exclusion of work in progress from the agreement on approvals were found to lack evidentiary support.

Furthermore, the court highlighted that the established consensus on the debt owed by NCK was not in contention; rather, it was the interpretation of other debts and work that remained central to the appeal. The written terms provided clarity that the financial consequences were to be determined via the method usually followed by the firm, which included taking account of work in progress and other debts stated ([paras 3–10]).

## C – Conclusion

The court upheld the trial court's decision, concluding that no deduction from the amount awarded to the respondent was warranted. The appellant's claims related to additional deductions were dismissed due to a lack of evidence and credibility in his assertions. The court additionally found merit in the argument that the oral agreement on work-related deductions made prior to the formal writing did not contravene the written dissolution agreement, as it served to clarify the terms rather than modify them materially. The appeal was dismissed with costs, reinforcing the trial court's findings as consistent and well-grounded in both fact and law ([paras 11–12]).

Through this case, the court reaffirmed the contractual duties of partners and the critical importance of precise communications in partnership agreements, especially during transitions such as a dissolution. The implications of this judgment highlight the intricacies involved in partnership dynamics and the need for clarity in financial arrangements amidst partnership changes.
