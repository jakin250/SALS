Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

### Case Note using FIRAC Method for **Minister for Transport and Others v Anele Mvumvu and Others**

**CCT 62/12**  
**[2012] ZACC 20**   
**Constitutional Court of South Africa**  

---

#### **F – Facts**: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–2])

In this case, the **Minister for Transport** and the **Road Accident Fund** (the applicants) petitioned the Constitutional Court of South Africa for an urgent order to extend the suspension period related to the invalidity of certain provisions of the **Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996** ([para 1]). The Constitutional Court had previously declared sections 18(1)(a)(i), 18(1)(b), and 18(2) of the Act to be unconstitutional but suspended the declaration of invalidity for **18 months** to allow Parliament time to remedy the defects ([paras 1–3]). The suspension was to expire on **17 August 2012**, and the applicants launched this application on **2 July 2012** upon realizing Parliament's inability to enact necessary amendments within this period ([para 2]).

The application came before the court on **14 August 2012**, where it was noted that the respondents did not oppose the relief sought. The court subsequently granted an order extending the suspension for an additional **six months** until **17 February 2013** ([para 2]).

#### **I – Issues**: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 3–4])

The primary issue in this case is whether the Constitutional Court has the jurisdiction to extend the period of suspension of a declaration of invalidity of legislation beyond the initial stipulated timeframe, particularly when Parliament had failed to amend the legislation within that period. Additionally, it raises the question of what constitutes a "just and equitable" order under Section **172(1)** of the Constitution, allowing for such an extension ([para 3]). The court must also consider the implications of failing to provide the extension as it relates to the financial stability of the Road Accident Fund and the fairness to affected parties ([para 12]).

#### **R – Rules**: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 5–6])

The court's authority to extend the suspension derives from **Section 172(1)** of the Constitution, which empowers the court to make any order that is just and equitable, including orders suspending the declaration of invalidity. This section indicates that, while a declaration of invalidity is necessary for any law or conduct inconsistent with the Constitution, the court can control the effective date of such declaration ([para 3]):

> "When deciding a constitutional matter within its power, a court must declare any law or conduct that is inconsistent with the Constitution invalid to the extent of its inconsistency and may make any order that is just and equitable" ([para 4]).

In **Zondi v MEC, Traditional and Local Government Affairs and Others**, the court specified that its power to reconsider suspensions is not confined to the initial declaration, establishing a precedent that the court may extend previously granted suspensions ([para 5]).

#### **A – Application**: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 7–14])

The court first examined the **sufficiency of explanation** provided by the applicants for their failure to meet the initial deadline. The applicants described a detailed process, indicating their commitment to drafting an amendatory bill shortly after the initial ruling. However, the drafting process proved to be more complex than anticipated, entailing extensive consultation and revisions following public feedback ([paras 7–9]). 

The court found the explanation adequate, noting the proactive steps the applicants had taken to address the issues. 

Next, the court evaluated the **justness and equitability** of extending the suspension. The applicants argued that if the declaration of invalidity took effect, it would impose an additional financial burden on the Road Accident Fund amounting to approximately **R2.263 billion**, threatening its stability ([para 11]). Conversely, they claimed that extending the suspension would limit the financial impact to **R1.3 billion**, making the situation more manageable while also allowing for the consideration of other legislative amendments beyond those initially invalidated ([para 12]).

Furthermore, the court assessed the implications of not extending the period. It emphasized the unfair consequences that would ensue by allowing the invalid provisions to take effect when numerous other relevant provisions remained unamended, perpetuating inequalities ([para 12]). 

Ultimately, the court was satisfied that extending the suspension for an additional six months was just and equitable, equipping Parliament with the necessary time to address the issues adequately ([paras 13–14]).

#### **C – Conclusion**: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 14])

The Constitutional Court ruled to extend the period of suspension by six months, thereby preventing immediate operational impacts from the declaration of invalidity pending legislative amendment. This decision reaffirms the court's position on exercising its powers to ensure a functional and equitable legal framework, particularly in matters affecting public welfare (i.e., road accident victims) and the fiscal health of public institutions ([para 14]). 

The implications of this ruling underscore the importance of legislative compliance with constitutional provisions while also highlighting the court’s flexibility and responsiveness to changing circumstances in ensuring justice and equity among affected parties. The decision illustrates the balance between maintaining legal accountability and allowing for legislative remediation in complex socio-economic contexts.
