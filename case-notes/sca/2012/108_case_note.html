Supreme Court of Appeal

Cipla Medpro (Pty) Limited v Aventis Pharma SA [2012] ZASCA 108

Cipla intended to market a generic version of Aventis' product, Taxotere, which resulted in Aventis alleging patent infringement in 2011.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2012 [2012] ZASCA 108

F – Facts

The present case involves two appeals regarding the validity of South African Patent No. 93/8936, registered in the name of Aventis Pharma SA, and its alleged infringement by Cipla Medpro (Pty) Limited and associated entities. The patent pertains to a pharmaceutical composition containing docetaxel, a cancer treatment agent, and its expiry date is set for 30 November 2013 ([paras 1–3]).

Cipla intended to market a generic version of Aventis' product, Taxotere, which resulted in Aventis alleging patent infringement in 2011. In May 2011, Aventis sent letters to Cipla demanding compliance to cease the exploitation of its patent before the patent's expiration ([paras 5–6]). Cipla, resisting these demands, sought to challenge the validity of the amended claim of the patent in court, filing an application for revocation of the amended claim based on claims of vagueness and lack of novelty. Concurrently, Aventis filed for an interim interdict against Cipla to prevent infringement pending the outcome of the uncertainty surrounding the patent ([paras 6–7]).

Both applications were dismissed by the Commissioner of Patents, leading to the current appeals ([para 7]).

I – Issues

The case presents multiple legal issues:

  1. Validity of Patent Claims: Whether the amendment of the original patent claim broadened its scope contrary to Section 51(7) of the Patents Act 57 of 1978 ([para 13]).
  2. Clarity of Claims: Whether the expired claims lack clarity, rendering them invalid under Section 61(1)(f)(i) of the Patents Act ([para 19]).
  3. Infringement and Contributory Infringement: The applicability of contributory infringement principles, specifically regarding Cipla's liability for indirectly inducing infringement by providing components that would be used unlawfully ([paras 29–34]).
  4. Public Interest: The relevance of public interest and access to affordable medications in the court's decision when evaluating the application for an interim interdict ([paras 42–46]).

R – Rules

The court relied on various legal principles and statutes in addressing the above issues:

  1. Patents Act, 1978: The Act allows for amendments to patent claims under Section 51 but restricts amendments that extend the scope of the patent ([para 13]). Section 61 outlines grounds for revocation, specifically regarding clarity and novelty of claims.
  2. Case Law: The leading case cited for the interpretation of patent specifications is Gentiruco A.G. v Firestone SA (Pty) Ltd, where the court emphasized interpreting the specifications based on ordinary language and intention conveyed by the patent ([para 14]).
  3. Contributory Infringement: The court referenced principles derived from international patent laws, indicating liability arises when one knowingly aids infringement ([para 31–34]). The decision in McKenzie v Van der Merwe was cited for the principle of aiding and abetting ([para 34]).
  4. Constitutional Principles: Section 27(1) of the South African Constitution guarantees access to healthcare, which was raised by the Treatment Action Campaign (TAC) as significant in the analysis of the public interest ([para 44]).

A – Application

The court undertook a detailed construction of the patent claims to determine whether the amended patent widened its scope. The analysis indicated that the amendment did not broaden the claims but merely clarified the arrangement of its components, thus maintaining its original intent ([paras 17–18]).

On the issue of clarity, the court criticized the lower Commissioner’s inconsistent assessment of the patent's clarity while also determining that the ordinary meaning of the claims was clear enough to avoid invalidation ([para 21]). The court navigated through expert testimonies, concluding that while experts may provide insights into sufficiency, clarity matters are primarily within judicial discretion ([para 22–25]).

The court extensively discussed the principle of contributory infringement by asserting that Cipla’s role as a supplier of components did constitute contributory infringement that intended to incite assistance in infringing the Aventis patent. It distinguished between manufacturing and ancillary actions, establishing liability for knowing participation in patent infringement ([paras 29–34]).

The court also weighed the implications of granting an interim interdict against public interest considerations linked to the affordability of medicine. It noted that while the TAC's arguments about public health access were valid, the statutory right of Aventis to enforce its patent must also be respected ([paras 44–46]). Ultimately, the court found that the potential harm to Aventis due to lost sales and unimpeded market entry outweighed Cipla’s arguments, leading to a favourable conclusion for Aventis regarding its request for an interdict ([paras 58–61]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded with a nuanced understanding of the interplay between patent rights and public interest, ultimately upholding the validity of the amendments made to the patent and granting Aventis the interim interdict against Cipla.

  1. First Appeal (Case No. 139/2012): Dismissed, affirming the lower court’s rejection of Cipla's application to revoke the amended patent ([para 62(1)]).
  2. Second Appeal (Case No. 138/2012): Upheld, issuing an interdict against Cipla from engaging in acts of infringement until the final resolution of the patent validity, with specified costs ([para 62(2)]).

The ruling illustrates a significant affirmation of patent rights over commercial competition, providing clarity on the interpretation of patent claims while accommodating the constitutional imperatives surrounding public health and access to medicines. The case sets a substantial precedent for future patent and pharmaceutical disputes in South Africa.
