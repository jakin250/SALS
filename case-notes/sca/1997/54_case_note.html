Supreme Court of Appeal

Case No 105/96

In the matter of Rowe v Rowe [2-6], the appellant, Gregory Stewart Rowe, and the respondent, Catharina Jakoba Rowe, were former spouses who engaged in a divorce proceeding that culminated in a settlement agreement granted by the erstwhile Witwatersrand Local Division.

Supreme Court of Appeal 1997

F – Facts

In the matter of Rowe v Rowe [2-6], the appellant, Gregory Stewart Rowe, and the respondent, Catharina Jakoba Rowe, were former spouses who engaged in a divorce proceeding that culminated in a settlement agreement granted by the erstwhile Witwatersrand Local Division. The divorce decree awarded custody of the couple's minor children, Marelize and Brendan, to the respondent while awarding custody of Raymond to the appellant. The appellant was obliged to pay for the maintenance of Marelize and Brendan.

After the divorce, Gregory Rowe initiated further proceedings, alleging that the respondent had fraudulently induced him to agree to the divorce and settlement by misrepresenting that he was the biological father of Brendan and Raymond. He claimed that this deception warranted the rescission of the divorce decree, which had been finalised based on the flawed agreement. He also sought alternative relief in the form of compensation, arguing that he had been enriched at the respondent's expense due to ongoing maintenance payments made under the belief that he was obliged to support his children.

The respondent, Catharina Rowe, responded with an exception to the particulars of claim, arguing that they disclosed no cause of action as they relied on invalid grounds for rescission — specifically, that the appellant sought to overturn an order he had himself procured, and that the issues raised had already been adjudicated in earlier proceedings.

Roos J upheld the exception, resulting in an appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([5-6]).

I – Issues

The central legal issues concern:

  1. Whether a party who has actively sought and obtained a court order can later seek its rescission based on allegations of fraud ([4-5]).
  2. Whether the claims made in the particulars of claim constituted a legitimate cause of action independent of the rescission of the divorce order ([5-6]).
  3. Whether the principles from previous case law concerning judicial fraud, particularly regarding whether fraud was committed during or outside the proceedings, apply to the current case ([9]).

R – Rules

The court’s assessment drew on various precedents:

  1. Florence v Florence 1948 (3) SA 71 (D), which held that a party could not seek rescission of an order obtained at their request based merely on extrajudicial fraud ([7-8]).
  2. Schierhout v Union Government 1927 AD 94 and Makings v Makings 1958 (1) SA 338, which affirm that fraud may provide grounds for rescission if it affects the judicial decision ([8-13]).
  3. Principles from other cases such as Huddersfield Banking Company Ltd v Henry Lister & Son Ltd and Gollach & Gomperts (1978) emphasize that agreements made under fraudulent pretenses could be rescinded, as long as the fraud pertains to essential elements critical to issuing the judgment ([10-12]).

A – Application

In applying these rules, the court determined that Roos J's reliance on Florence v Florence was misplaced, as the consent order was made in line with a settlement agreement in which both parties were equally involved. The appellant's claim inferred that he reasonably believed the respondent's fraudulent statements when entering the settlement; thus, his right to resile from the agreement remained intact notwithstanding the order's existence [10-11].

The judged fraud was indeed a matter affecting the court's judgment and not merely extrajudicial deception, as it was rooted in the representations made to the appellant, which misled him regarding paternity, leading directly to the settlement ([13]). Therefore, the main claim did not fail on the basis that Roos J asserted in the exception, particularly since the claims had not been conclusively adjudicated and were valid on their own merits.

The alternative claim for monetary compensation, separate from the rescission of the divorce decree, was correctly identified as disjointed from the claim for rescission and accordingly should not have been dismissed based on the same arguments [15]. The case law firmly established the ground that distinct claims regarding enrichment could proceed independently of the issue of rescission, invalidating Roos J’s dismissal of both claims based on the same grounds.

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, concluding that the exception raised by the respondent did not adequately form a proper basis for dismissing the claims made by the appellant. The court rectified the lower court’s order, stating unequivocally that the exception was dismissed with costs ([16]).

The significance of this case lies in clarifying that parties who are misled into settlement agreements through fraudulent representations retain the right to contest those agreements, even if they have previously invoked judicial authority to formalise them. This decision underscores the court's role in ensuring that justice prevails, particularly in matters involving allegations of deceit that undermine the integrity of judicial processes. The ruling thus highlights the judiciary's commitment to the accurate and honest representation of facts, reinforcing the principles underlying family law and the welfare of children involved in custody disputes.
