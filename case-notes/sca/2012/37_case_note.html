Supreme Court of Appeal

Sagren Perumal v National Director of Public Prosecutions

The NDPP sought a forfeiture order regarding the attached assets on 3 March 2011.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2012 1998 (POCA). On 12

FIRAC Method

F – Facts
This case stems from an ex parte application made by the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) pursuant to Section 38 of the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (POCA). On 12 March 2004, the NDPP secured a preservation order that targeted various assets associated with multiple appellants including Sagren Perumal and his family members, all alleged to be connected to unlawful drug activities. The order was publicized in the Government Gazette on 2 April 2004 ([1]).

The NDPP sought a forfeiture order regarding the attached assets on 3 March 2011. Of the properties involved, only the Kings Avenue house, an Iveco Eurotech truck, a Henred Freuhauf Platform truck trailer, and a Volkswagen Caravelle were in dispute in the appeal, as the remaining assets had already been returned to the appellants ([2], [5]).

The first appellant, Sagren Perumal, was claimed to be one of the largest drug dealers in the Durban area, with the NDPP alleging that the assets were either proceeds of drug trafficking or instruments of crime. The appellants argued against this, stating that they had not engaged in any unlawful activities and that the NDPP’s claims were unfounded ([4], [8]).

All appellants had familial ties. For instance, Sagren was married to Pushpaganthie Perumal, and the family lived together in the Kings Avenue house, registered under Rajambal Pillay, the mother ([2]). The NDPP's application faced significant opposition, leading to the appeal we now consider ([1], [24]).

I – Issues
The central legal questions revolved around the sufficiency of evidence to support the NDPP's claim that the assets in question were indeed the proceeds of unlawful activities, specifically drug trafficking, and whether the forfeiture order was appropriately granted based on the affidavits submitted ([6], [8]). A significant issue was whether the NDPP had successfully demonstrated the connection between the first appellant and the alleged illicit activities to justify the forfeiture ([29]).

R – Rules
The court interpreted the provisions of POCA, particularly Section 48, which states that a forfeiture order must be granted if the court is satisfied, on a balance of probabilities, that the property is either the proceeds of unlawful activities or an instrumentality of an offence. Importantly, existing criminal convictions or proceedings are not prerequisites for such a forfeiture ([6], [50]). This legal framework places emphasis on the property involved, rather than the individual accused of wrongdoing.

In applying the 'Plascon-Evans' principles, the court acknowledged that the undisputed allegations in the NDPP's founding affidavits, together with the appeals' responding allegations, must be assessed to determine if they establish, on a balance of probabilities, that the properties in question were acquired through unlawful means ([7], [28]).

A – Application
The court thoroughly evaluated the evidence presented by the NDPP against the appellants’ counterarguments. The evidence primarily comprised affidavits from various investigators who had surveilled and investigated the first appellant over several years. The investigators testified to the first appellant's alleged long-standing involvement in drug dealing, including previous arrests for possession of illegal drugs ([9], [10]).

However, upon reviewing the affidavits, the court found significant inconsistencies and insufficiencies. For example, the affidavits provided lacked clear, direct evidentiary links between the first appellant and specific drug transactions, as many relied on hearsay and speculative assertions. The NDPP's reliance on the testimony of individuals purportedly connected to the first appellant was deemed insufficient for reaching the necessary threshold of proof ([21], [24]).

Moreover, the third appellant, Rajambal Pillay, who had passed away prior to the hearing, provided an affidavit claiming legitimate sources of income that directly funded the property acquisitions. The evidence presented did not substantiate the NDPP’s assertion that her deceased husband's financial activities were illegitimate ([27], [29]).

The court concluded that the NDPP’s case did not meet the burden of proof necessary to justify the forfeiture. The indicators of a middle-class lifestyle among the family, along with the lack of direct evidence linking the first appellant to drug trafficking or affluent living funded by such activities, played a crucial role in the court's determination ([25], [28]).

C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the previous ruling and rejected the NDPP's application for forfeiture of the disputed properties, underscoring the respondent's failure to establish a credible case on the balance of probabilities. Thus, the appeal was upheld with costs, and the order of the KwaZulu-Natal High Court was set aside, leading to the dismissal of the NDPP’s application ([30], [31]).

The implications of this case highlight the significant burden placed on the state in forfeiture applications, emphasizing the necessity for clear, credible evidence establishing a direct connection between alleged criminal activities and the properties sought to be forfeited. Overall, it reflects the principles of justice and the protections against unjust deprivation of property.
