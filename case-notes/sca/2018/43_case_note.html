Supreme Court of Appeal

Dinnermates (Tvl) CC v Piquante Brands International (Pty) Ltd and Another [2018] ZASCA 43

The appellant, Dinnermates (Tvl) CC, is a close corporation involved in the manufacturing and selling of food products, particularly processed meat and cooked pepper products.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 43

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–7])

The appellant, Dinnermates (Tvl) CC, is a close corporation involved in the manufacturing and selling of food products, particularly processed meat and cooked pepper products. In 2010, Dinnermates applied for the registration of the trade mark "PEPPAMATES" along with a device of a pepper with a stalk in various classes. The first respondent, Piquante Brands International (Pty) Ltd, holds several registered trademarks incorporating the mark "PEPPADEW", which pertain to similar food products and are also registered in the same classes as the appellant's application.

The respondents opposed the registration of "PEPPAMATES" under sections 10(14) and 10(17) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, contending that the trade marks are likely to deceive or confuse consumers. After being unsuccessful before the Registrar of Trade Marks, Dinnermates appealed to the Gauteng Division of the High Court, where the court upheld the respondents' objections. Subsequently, Dinnermates sought leave to appeal the High Court's decision to the Supreme Court of Appeal, focusing solely on the issues under section 10(14) of the Act ([paras 1–2]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 8–11])

The core issue revolves around whether the appellant's proposed mark "PEPPAMATES" is so similar to the respondents' registered mark "PEPPADEW" that its use would likely deceive or cause confusion among the relevant consumers, as stipulated by section 10(14) of the Trade Marks Act. This primarily includes two interrelated questions:

  1. Does the similarity between the two marks create a likelihood of confusion or deception among average consumers in a competitive marketplace? ([para 10])
  2. Are the distinguishing characteristics of "DEW" and "MATES" sufficient to mitigate the risk of confusion related to the common prefix "PEPPA"? ([para 22])

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 12–14])

The court relied upon principles established in several key cases when considering the likelihood of confusion among consumers.

  1. Cowbell AG v ICS Holdings Ltd 2001 (3) SA 941 (SCA) emphasizes that the primary function of a trade mark is to indicate the origin of goods, with a determination of confusion constituting a value judgment ([para 11]).
  2. Orange Brand Services Limited v Account Works Software (Pty) Ltd 2013 [ZASCA 158], which clarifies that the focus in opposition proceedings is how marks may be used if both were registered, rather than current usage ([para 12]).
  3. The global assessment of confusion requires considering all relevant factors and viewing marks from the perspective of the average consumer ([para 13]).
  4. Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd 1984 (3) SA 623 (A) provides a framework for evaluating main features, overall impressions, and dominant characteristics of marks under review ([para 14]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 15–23])

The SCA meticulously analyzed the visual, phonetic, and conceptual similarities between "PEPPADEW" and "PEPPAMATES".

  • The respondents argued that the prefix "PEPPA" is a distinctive element underpinning their mark, and its inclusion in the appellant's mark establishes a high probability of confusion. They noted that the average consumer remembers "PEPPADEW" and could easily confuse it with "PEPPAMATES," even momentarily ([paras 16–17]).
  • On the other hand, Dinnermates contended that "PEPPA" is derivative of the word "pepper" and is thus descriptive—a fact reflected in various other registered trademarks containing "PEPPA". They argued the suffixes "DEW" and "MATES" serve to differentiate the two marks sufficiently. The court noted that visually and conceptually, the marks are dissimilar, with "MATES" and "DEW" representing distinct semantic content ([paras 19–22]).
  • The court further ruled that granting a monopoly over "PEPPA" could restrict other entities from lawfully utilizing a common term related to a widely recognized food product. The analysis recognized that consumers often perceive trademarks holistically rather than by dissecting specific elements. The likelihood of confusion was deemed low, particularly in the context of the distinctive appearances and overall impressions created by each mark ([paras 21–23]).

The SCA concluded that the full court's decision had erred by failing to consider these significant distinguishing features adequately.

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 24–25])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Dinnermates' appeal, ruling in favor of the registration of the "PEPPAMATES" mark. The court rejected the respondents' claims of confusion, determining that the distinguishing elements of the marks significantly reduce the potential for consumer deception.

In doing so, the court underscored the principle that a descriptive or commonly used element cannot underpin exclusivity, particularly when the overall impression of the marks diverges considerably. Consequently, the judgment reinforces the balance between protecting intellectual property rights and ensuring fair competition in the marketplace, allowing multiple traders to operate without infringing on one another's rights.

The implications of this ruling affirm the significance of substantive trademark examination processes in relation to public perceptions, ensuring that descriptive elements cannot be monopolized to the detriment of fair competition among businesses in the food industry and beyond ([paras 24–25]).

Thus, the appeal was upheld with costs associated with the employment of two counsel, and the previous court's order was set aside, allowing the appellant's marks to proceed to registration.
