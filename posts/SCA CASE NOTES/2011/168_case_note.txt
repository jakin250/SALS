Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: Thabo Mofutsanyana District Municipality v Steyn-Enslin & Vennote (639/2010) [2011] ZASCA 168

**F - Facts**  
The appellant, **Thabo Mofutsanyana District Municipality** (the "Municipality"), appealed against a decision of the Free State High Court (Mocumie J) which upheld an exception raised by the first, second, and third respondents (collectively the "Respondents") regarding the Municipality's particulars of claim ([1]). The Municipality sought an order mandating the Respondents, who were registered regional services levy payers, to furnish a proper statement of accounts, engage in the debatement of such accounts, and provide other substantiating documents following their alleged default in paying regional service levies ([1]–[5]).

The primary contention from the Respondents was that the Municipality lacked the legal authority to demand accounts or estimations of levies from defaulting levypayers, arguing that such power resided with the **Commissioner of the South African Revenue Service** under the **Regional Services Council Act 109 of 1985** ([1], [3]). The Municipality further contended that if it indeed lacked such power, the common law should be developed under **section 39(2)** of the **Constitution** of the Republic of South Africa to confer this authority ([4], [5]).

The Municipality's particulars of claim explicitly demanded a reflective statement of account under various conditions along with stipulations for the payment of outstanding amounts ([2]). However, the high court ruled there was no valid cause of action as the demand was unsupported by law, ultimately denying the Municipality the relief sought, but allowing for a chance to amend its claim ([5]).

**I - Issues**  
The appeal raised several pertinent legal questions:

1. **Whether the Municipality had a legal basis to demand accounts, debate accounts, and other financial documents from the Respondents?**
2. **If not, should the common law be developed under section 39(2) of the Constitution to enable the Municipality to make such demands?**
3. **Was it appropriate to construe the demand for a statement of account as one for a 'return' as outlined in the regulations?** ([6]).

**R - Rules**  
The applicable legal rules and precedents in this case stemmed from the Regional Services Council Act, particularly sections pertaining to the powers and responsibilities of municipal councils in relation to regional services levies. Specifically, **regulation 13(1)** of the Act states that a council cannot compel the production of documents or books related to the levy ([8]). 

Additionally, relevant to the constitutional argument was **Section 39(2)** of the Constitution, which permits the development of common law to promote the spirit, purport, and objects of the Bill of Rights ([6]). Previous case law, including **Carmichele v Minister of Safety and Security** 2001 (4) SA 938 (CC), cautioned against judicial overreach into legislative prerogatives ([13]).

**A - Application**  
The court applied the existing statutory framework critically in determining the Municipality's appeal. It noted the strict prohibition in regulation 13(1), which curtailed the Municipality’s power to demand financial documents from levypayers ([8]). Moreover, the court emphasized that while the Municipality was potentially entitled to required returns under regulation 9(4), it did not plead for such returns in its particulars of claim. This failure to frame the issue correctly weakened the Municipality’s position on appeal ([10]).

The judges further evaluated the Municipality's alternative argument for the development of common law and firmly concluded that there was no need for judicial intervention since the statutory language was clear and provided sufficient mechanisms for levies assessment—namely, by the Commissioner ([13], [14]). The court expressed that inviting judicial reform in this context would be unwarranted, as it risks undermining legislative intent.

Ultimately, the appeal failed because the foundational argument related to the statutory authority was insufficient, leading the court to uphold the high court's decision ([14], [15]).

**C - Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the Municipality’s appeal, reaffirming the jurisdictional limitations imposed by regulation 13(1) and clarifying that the common law does not need to be developed for the Municipality to exercise powers that legally do not exist ([15]). This case thereby reinforces the boundaries delineated by the legislature concerning municipal powers over regional services levies and emphasizes the necessity for statutory actions to align with explicit legislative frameworks. The Municipality was permitted to consider amending its particulars of claim within 30 days, preserving the potential for revising its approach to compliance with the statutory requirements ([15]). The implications of this case highlight the interplay between legislative authority and municipal governance in South Africa, stressing the importance of adhering to established legal procedures when seeking compliance from defaulting levypayers.
