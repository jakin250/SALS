# Case Note: Ahmed and Others v Minister of Home Affairs [2018] ZACC 39

## FIRAC Case Note

### F - Facts
The case of **Ahmed and Others v Minister of Home Affairs** ([1]) revolves around the legality of **Immigration Directive 21 of 2015**, issued by the Director-General of the Department of Home Affairs. The applicants—Tashriq Ahmed, Arifa Musaddik Fahme, Kuzikesa Jules Valery Swinda, and Jabbar Ahmed—are asylum seekers whose applications for visas under the **Immigration Act 13 of 2002** were refused based on the Directive's provisions, which mandated that all applications from asylum seekers for temporary and permanent residence visas be rejected ([1], [2]). The context stems from a prior court ruling, the **Dabone order** ([5]), which had allowed asylum seekers to apply for residence permits without canceling their asylum seeker permits or needing a valid passport.

In 2016, the Department issued Directive 21, contradicting the prior court ruling and effectively reinstating the requirement that asylum seekers could not apply for visas until their asylum claims were formally recognized ([7]). This Directive led to conflicts regarding visa applications from individuals like Ms. Fahme, who sought to apply for a visitor's visa; and Mr. Swinda and Mr. Ahmed, who applied for critical skills visas ([9], [10]).

The applicants argued that the Directive was inconsistent with both the **Immigration Act** and the **Refugees Act** and requested the High Court to declare it invalid and to compel the reconsideration of their visa applications ([11]). 

### I - Issues
The key legal questions raised in this case include:

1. Are asylum seekers, whose applications for refugee status have been refused, eligible to apply for temporary and permanent visas under the **Immigration Act**?
2. Is **Immigration Directive 21 of 2015**, which imposes a blanket ban on asylum seekers from applying for visas, valid in light of the relevant legislation ([15])?

### R - Rules
The court relied on multiple legislative texts and principles, including:

1. **Immigration Act 13 of 2002**: It defines the conditions under which visas can be applied for, specifically noting that all foreigners, including asylum seekers, may apply for temporary residence permits ([28]).
   
2. **Refugees Act 130 of 1998**: This Act distinguishes between asylum seekers and recognized refugees, establishing specific rights for each category ([24], [25]).

3. **Directive 21 of 2015**: Issued by the Director-General, this directive was determined to be ultra vires as it contradicted the established legal framework ([44], [46]).

4. **Case Law**: The judgments in relevant cases, such as the **Dabone order** and other precedents that establish asylum seekers' rights to apply for residence permits, were also evaluated ([13], [14]).

### A - Application
The court evaluated the entire structure of South Africa's immigration and refugee laws, concluding that:

- The Directive's interpretation—that asylum seekers could not apply for permits—was contrary to the purpose of both the **Immigration Act** and the **Refugees Act** ([50],[51]). 
- The court stated that the inability to apply for visas would unfairly penalize individuals based on their asylum status, placing them in a state of prolonged uncertainty without legal means to acquire residency ([52],[53]).
- The court highlighted that the Directive imposed a blanket prohibition inconsistent with the Immigration Act's expectations regarding foreign applications and failed to provide room for exemptions as allowed under section 31(2)(c) of the Act ([67]).

### C - Conclusion
The Constitutional Court held that:

1. **Leave to appeal was granted**, and the appeal was upheld, declaring that the blanket ban imposed by **Immigration Directive 21 of 2015** was inconsistent with the **Immigration Act** and thus invalid ([3],[4]).

2. The Directive was found to hinder asylum seekers from applying for permanent residency permits without valid justification, thereby breaching their fundamental rights as outlined in both the **Immigration Act** and **Refugees Act**.

3. Importantly, the court's decision reaffirmed that asylum seekers are entitled to apply for visas and permits and directed the Department to comply with established legislative protocols, illustrating a significant step towards upholding the rights of vulnerable groups within the framework of South African law ([62],[63]).

Additionally, the court ordered that there would be no costs awarded, acknowledging the shared responsibility for the situation among the parties involved ([66]). 

### Implications
The ruling carries substantial implications regarding the treatment of asylum seekers in South Africa, reaffirming their rights amidst ongoing legislative changes and addressing systemic barriers to achieving lawful residency. By clarifying the applicability of immigration law to asylum seekers, the decision promotes inclusivity and fairness within governmental processes, aligning administrative practices with constitutional values of justice and dignity for all individuals ([22],[23]).
