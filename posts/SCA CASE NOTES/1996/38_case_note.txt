Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1996

# FIRAC Case Note: Cronshaw & Another v Coin Security Group (Pty) Ltd

**Case Citation**: Cronshaw v Coin Security Group (Pty) Ltd [1996] ZASCA 26; 1996 (3) SA 64 (A)

**Court**: Supreme Court of South Africa (Appellate Division)

**Date Heard**: 19 March 1996  
**Date Filed**: 28 March 1996

---

## F – Facts [paras 2–3]

The case centers around William Graham Cronshaw (the first appellant), who had previously been employed by Coin Security Group (the respondent) under a restraint of trade provision. Following his resignation, Cronshaw began working for Fidelity Guards Holdings (the second appellant), a direct competitor of Coin. Consequently, Coin instituted proceedings seeking interim interdicts against both Cronshaw and Fidelity, specifically seeking to prevent Cronshaw from continuing his employment with Fidelity during the pendency of the trial to resolve the enforcement of the restraint of trade.

The court a quo, presided over by Barkhuizen AJ, granted these interim interdicts, ruling against Cronshaw and Fidelity. Leave to appeal against these interdicts was initially refused on the grounds that the interim orders were non-appealable. However, upon petitioning to the Chief Justice, leave to appeal was granted, leading to the matter being considered by the Supreme Court.

---

## I – Issues [paras 4–5]

The primary legal issue presented before the Appellate Division was whether the granting of an interim interdict is an appealable order, particularly in light of the precedents set in *African Wanderers Football Club (Pty) Ltd v Wanderers Football Club* 1977 (2) SA 38 (A). Furthermore, the court needed to address whether the distinction drawn by Cronshaw's counsel between the current case and *African Wanderers* was valid, given the differences in procedural history and the granting of leave to appeal in the present case.

---

## R – Rules [paras 6–12]

The court extensively referenced the decision in *African Wanderers* to support its jurisprudential approach to defining the nature of interim interdicts. It reiterated the distinction between interlocutory and final orders, establishing that an order’s appealability generally hinges on whether it disposes of an issue in the main action.

Furthermore, the court examined the historical context and the legal principles regarding the appealability of interim interdicts as dictated by various statutes, particularly the provisions of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959. The court highlighted the balanced consideration of potential prejudice against the interests of justice, where the rights of parties may vary significantly depending on the classification of a judicial order as either final or interlocutory.

Key authorities cited included *Westinghouse Brake & Equipment (Pty) Ltd v Bilger Engineering (Pty) Ltd* 1986 (2) SA 555 (A) and *Zweni v Minister of Law and Order* 1993 (1) SA 523 (A), which discussed the nature and effects of judgments.

---

## A – Application [paras 10–15]

In applying the rules to the facts at hand, the court reaffirmed the primacy of *African Wanderers* in determining the appealability of an interim interdict. It validated the original finding that Howard J did not intend to make a definitive ruling regarding the rights underlying the interdict, suggesting that this sort of interim measure remains interlocutory in nature. Thus, the court found compelling that the interim relief sought by Coin did not conclusively determine the broader issues associated with the ongoing litigation.

The Court also engaged critically with the argument concerning the irretrievability of the harm suffered due to interim interdicts. It acknowledged that while such harms may occur, they do not automatically validate early appeals, citing several prior authorities that established necessary procedural safeguards. The court emphasized that allowing appeals against interim orders could lead to protracted legal proceedings that would undermine the very purpose of interim interdicts—to maintain the status quo and prevent irreversible harm until the merits can be conclusively determined.

Moreover, the court pointedly noted that the issue of appealability turned on the absence of a definitive intention in the initial order and how freedom of action during ongoing litigation could create procedural havoc if stayed.

---

## C – Conclusion [paras 16–15]

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of South Africa (Appellate Division) ruled that the appeal was to be struck from the roll, affirming that the granting of an interim interdict was not an appealable order. This decision confirmed the ratio decidendi established in *African Wanderers*, reinforcing that while interim interdicts may impose significant burdens on respondents, the legal framework must prioritize procedural clarity and the efficient resolution of disputes.

The ruling underscored the critical distinction between interlocutory and final orders within judicial contexts, ensuring that appeals are reserved for judgments that substantively affect the rights of parties in finality. The implications of this decision suggest a commitment to maintaining judicial efficiency while still addressing the substantive concerns of parties in restraint of trade disputes, thereby serving as a significant precedent in South African commercial law.
