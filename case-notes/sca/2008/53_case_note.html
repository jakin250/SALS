Supreme Court of Appeal

Koumantarakis v Mystic River (172/07) [2008] 53 ZASCA (14 May 2008)

In the matter of Koumantarakis Group CC v Mystic River Investment 45 (Pty) Ltd, the appellant (Koumantarakis Group CC) appealed a decision by the Durban and Coast Local Division (Madondo AJ) regarding the enforcement of a property sale agreement ([para 1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2008 [2008] 53 ZASCA (14 May 2008)

F – Facts

In the matter of Koumantarakis Group CC v Mystic River Investment 45 (Pty) Ltd, the appellant (Koumantarakis Group CC) appealed a decision by the Durban and Coast Local Division (Madondo AJ) regarding the enforcement of a property sale agreement ([para 1]). The appellant and the first respondent entered a written agreement on 30 May 2006, concerning the sale of an immovable property described as Erf 301, Portion 16, Springfield Park, for R12 million ([para 3]). The purchase price required a deposit of R1 million secured by a bank guarantee acceptable to the seller, to be lodged within three days of fulfilling a suspensive condition ([para 4(a)]). Additionally, a guarantee for the remaining R11 million had to be provided within 45 working days ([para 4(b)]).

The appellant requested an extension of the due diligence period but was denied ([para 6]). The purchaser attempted to comply with the guarantee provision by instructing Standard Bank to prepare a guarantee in accordance with the agreement; however, the seller rejected the guarantee, asserting that it was revocable and thus unacceptable ([para 11]). The seller subsequently cancelled the agreement, claiming that the purchaser had failed to present an acceptable guarantee ([para 12]). The purchaser sought enforcement of the agreement, leading to the initial judgment by the court below, which found in favor of the seller and dismissed the case with costs ([paras 1, 18]).

I – Issues

The appeal raised two primary legal questions ([para 20]):

  1. What is the meaning and scope of Clause 3.2 of the agreement of sale, specifically concerning the bank guarantee?
  2. Did the seller act reasonably in rejecting the bank guarantee provided by the purchaser?

R – Rules

The court examined various principles governing the interpretation of contractual terms, particularly those relating to guarantees in property sales. Reference was made to Mouton v Mynwerkersunie and Hermes Ship Chandlers (Pty) Ltd v Caltex Oil (SA) Ltd, where the contextual interpretation of the term "guarantee" was underlined ([paras 22–23]). The court emphasized that the purpose of a guarantee in real property transactions is typically to facilitate payment upon transfer, rather than to serve as security ([para 24]).

Furthermore, the seller's discretion in accepting or rejecting the guarantee was rooted in the requirement that such decisions should be made based on reasonable grounds, as established in Herbert Porter & Co. Ltd v Johannesburg Stock Exchange ([paras 40–41]). The objective test for reasonableness necessitated that the grounds for rejection be justifiable in an impartial context ([para 39]).

A – Application

In assessing Clause 3.2, the court noted that it only stipulated a guarantee "acceptable to the seller" without expressly requiring that the guarantee be irrevocable ([paras 28–29]). The court rejected the seller's argument that the timing of guarantee provisions implied a necessity for an irrevocable guarantee. The requirement was not explicitly stated in the contract, suggesting that the standard practice of issuing revocable guarantees sufficed ([para 33]).

The testimonies of witnesses from the appellant’s side illustrated that the guarantee's withdrawal clause was standard in the banking sector and commonly found in similar transactions ([paras 35–38]). Counterarguments from the seller regarding the acceptability of the guarantee were scrutinized; the court concluded that the rejection was not based on sound rational grounds and that the reasons provided appeared exaggerated ([paras 43–48]).

In this respect, the seller's assertion that unpredictable elements might invalidate the guarantee did not withstand scrutiny, as the bank would require evidence to justify any withdrawal that could encumber the transfer of property ([paras 44–49]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the seller acted unreasonably in rejecting the purchaser's bank guarantee. It ruled that the guarantee provided complied with the contractual obligations set forth in the agreement of sale, thereby declaring that the seller was improperly entitled to cancel the agreement ([paras 49–50]).

As a result, the appeal was upheld, the initial judgment was set aside, and an order was issued for the transfer of the property, with directions for compliance and the payment of costs ([para 50]). The ruling reaffirms the necessity of clear, precise language in contracts regarding guarantees and underscores the importance of reasonableness in the exercise of discretionary powers by parties in a contractual relationship.
