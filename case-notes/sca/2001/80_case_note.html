Devraj Govender v. The Minister of Safety and Security

Case Citation: Devraj Govender v. The Minister of Safety and Security, Case No. 342/99, [2001] ZASCA 32 (1 June 2001).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2001 [2001] ZASCA 32 (1 June 2001). ## F - Facts The appellant, D

Case Citation: Devraj Govender v. The Minister of Safety and Security, Case No. 342/99, [2001] ZASCA 32 (1 June 2001).

F - Facts

The appellant, Devraj Govender, brought an appeal against a ruling of the Durban and Coast Local Division of the High Court, where the learned trial judge, Booysen J., had absolved the respondent, the Minister of Safety and Security, from liability for damages arising from serious injuries sustained by Govender's minor son, Justin. The injuries occurred when Inspector Cox, a police officer, shot Justin, resulting in paralysis.

The facts are not disputed: Justin, along with two friends, stole a BMW car in Durban on June 16, 1995. During a police pursuit initiated after the vehicle was reported stolen, the police attempted to apprehend the suspects. Justin, then 17 years old and unarmed, fled after being ordered to stop, prompting Inspector Cox to fire a warning shot and subsequently attempt to apprehend him by shooting at his legs. The shot resulted in severe injury to Justin, fracturing his spine and leaving him paraplegic.

Cox contended that his actions were justified under section 49(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (the Act), which permits law enforcement to use force to prevent suspects from fleeing. The central legal question revolves around whether the use of lethal force was justified under these circumstances, considering the constitutional rights afforded to individuals, including fleeing suspects.

I - Issues

The critical legal issue in this case pertains to the interpretation of section 49(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act, specifically regarding:

  1. Whether Inspector Cox’s use of lethal violence was justified to prevent Justin from fleeing while balancing the constitutional rights to life, dignity, and physical integrity as protected under the Interim Constitution (specifically sections 9, 10, 11, and 25).
  2. How to interpret "reasonably necessary" force under section 49(1) in light of the constitutional framework, particularly regarding proportionality between the nature of the force used and the threat posed by the suspect.
  3. Whether the existing legal standard for justifying the use of force needs to be aligned with contemporary constitutional values and judicial interpretations that prioritize human rights.

R - Rules

The relevant statutory provision is section 49(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which states:

> “If any person authorized under this Act to arrest or to assist in arresting another, attempts to arrest such person and such person...flees...the person so authorized may, in order to effect the arrest, use such force as may in the circumstances reasonably necessary to overcome the resistance or to prevent the person concerned from fleeing.”

The court referenced constitutional principles from the Interim Constitution (Act 200 of 1993), particularly the rights to life, dignity, and the presumption of innocence, alongside international standards and comparative case law such as Tennessee v. Garner, 471 U.S. 1 (1985), and various precedents from Canada and the European Court of Human Rights. The Constitutional Court's approach in Investigating Directorate: Serious Economic Offences v. Hyundai Motor Distributors (Pty) Ltd was also cited, emphasizing the need for legislation to be interpreted within the context of the Bill of Rights.

A - Application

The court meticulously examined the circumstances under which Inspector Cox discharged his weapon and the statutory criteria for using lethal force. Olivier JA underscored that while the theft of a motor vehicle is indeed a serious crime, Justin was an unarmed minor at the time of the shooting. A key aspect of the ruling involved its analysis of whether Justin posed an immediate threat to the officer or the public.

The judgment reformulates the test for the use of force: it should incorporate considerations of proportionality not only regarding the severity of the crime but also the nature of the threat posed by the suspect. The court emphasized that using deadly force must meet the constitutional standard of being "reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society", thus seeking to balance state interests with individual rights.

Olivier JA articulated that the existing interpretations had established a rather permissive standard, which failed to adequately protect the rights of suspects. It was concluded that the threshold for using lethal violence must be higher, asserting that force is only justified if the suspect poses an immediate threat of serious bodily harm.

Applying this enhanced proportionality assessment, the court found that the shooting of Justin was unlawful. The inference drawn was that no justification existed for firing at a fleeing suspect who posed no spectral danger to the officer or the public and was an unarmed minor at the time.

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal found in favor of Devraj Govender, reversing the ruling of the High Court. The court concluded that Inspector Cox's actions were not justified under section 49(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act, thus establishing that the shooting of Justin constituted a violation of his constitutional rights. The court ordered that the matter should be returned to the lower court for the quantification of damages owed to the appellant, acknowledging the profound implications of unlawfulness stemming from the use of excessive force in a law enforcement context.

In this landmark ruling, the judiciary redefined the bounds of lawful conduct under the statute, reinforcing the commitment to constitutionally enshrined rights while addressing the need for equitable applications of force by police officers, ultimately impacting future interactions between law enforcement and civilians in South Africa.
