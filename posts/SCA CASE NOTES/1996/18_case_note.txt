# Case Note: David Michael Critchell v The State ([1996] ZASCA 21)

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 2–21])

David Michael Critchell (the appellant) was charged with two counts of culpable homicide and one count of attempted murder due to a shooting incident on 19 August 1988. Critchell, a security manager employed at the Sandton City complex, was convicted in a regional court on all three counts. The first count involved the death of Mr Neville Lalla, while the second count concerned the death of security guard Mr Kopeng Motimele, with the attempted murder charge involving Mr Joel Ramushu.

Initially, the appellant received severe sentences: eight years for Lalla’s homicide (with three years conditionally suspended), two years for Motimele’s homicide, and two years for attempted murder (with one year conditionally suspended). The total effective sentence was six years, which was later appealed. The Witwatersrand Local Division upheld the convictions but reduced the total effective imprisonment to two years. The appellant, seeking further redress, appealed those convictions and the altered sentences to the Supreme Court of South Africa ([paras 2–6]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 7–19])

The case presents several legal issues, notably whether:

1. The appellant was justified in using lethal force in his attempt to detain Lalla, specifically whether he acted in self-defense or under a misapprehension regarding the threat he faced.
2. The trials’ findings on the credibility of witnesses presented a sufficient basis for rejecting the appellant's account of events.
3. The appellant had the requisite mens rea for attempted murder concerning Mr Ramushu.
4. The appeal court adequately considered the circumstances surrounding the shooting, particularly regarding the reasonableness of the appellant’s actions leading to the fatal shootings ([paras 7–19]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 15–18])

The legal framework underpinning the case comprises multiple statutory provisions and case law:

1. **Culpable Homicide and Attempted Murder**: Governed primarily by common law, the elements of both charges require evidence of negligence (culpable homicide) and intention, whether direct or in the form of dolus eventualis (attempted murder).
   
2. **Section 49 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977**: Provides that a private citizen may use reasonable force to effect an arrest, permitting lethal force only to prevent a threat to life or serious bodily harm. This provision is critical to determining whether the appellant’s actions were lawful.

3. **Case Law**: The ruling draws from precedent set in *S v Nsubane* 1985 (3) SA 677 (A), concerning the treatment of culpable homicide and the nature of justifiable actions taken during an arrest attempt ([paras 7–15]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 12–19])

The court meticulously evaluated the evidence presented during the trial, focusing on the credibility of witnesses such as Ms Aletha Carswell and Mr Dietlof Maré. Despite the appellant's claims of self-defense, the testimony established that Lalla was in a position of surrender when the appellant opened fire, contradicting the notion that Critchell faced an immediate threat. 

The magistrate’s assessment regarded the appellant’s version as implausible, particularly noting that multiple shots fired into a purportedly surrendering individual cannot constitute reasonable force as contemplated under Section 49. The court accepted that Critchell acted with awareness of the potential consequences of his actions, satisfying the mens rea requirement for attempted murder regarding Ramushu. Furthermore, when evaluating the negligence standard for the culpable homicide count associated with Motimele’s death, it found that Critchell acted recklessly, having failed to ascertain who was behind him before firing [paras 12–19].

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 20–21])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court upheld the appellant's convictions, asserting that the evidence presented convincingly indicated that Critchell acted outside the bounds of lawful self-defense and that he used excessive and unreasonable force in his capacity as a security manager. The court maintained that the reduced sentence of two years was appropriate, reinforcing the need for accountability in the use of lethal force by individuals tasked with security responsibilities. 

The ruling highlights the careful balance between enforcing the law and protecting civil liberties, particularly regarding the use of force in apprehending suspected criminals. The decision serves as a pivotal reference for future cases dealing with the scope of permissible actions by security personnel and the necessary vigilance against misuse of authority in law enforcement contexts [paras 20–21].
