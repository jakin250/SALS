Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: Minister of Agriculture v C M De Klerk (747/2012)

**Case Citation:** Minister of Agriculture v C M De Klerk (747/2012) [2013] ZASCA 142 (30 September 2013)

**Coram:** Navsa ADP, Cachalia and Majiedt JJA, Van der Merwe and Meyer AJJA

---

## F - Facts

The case arose from a dispute concerning the sale of immovable property following a transaction between **Carola Maria de Klerk** (the seller) and the **National Department of Land Affairs** (the buyer), represented by the **Minister of Agriculture and Land Affairs** (the first appellant) and the **Deputy Director: Finance in the Head Land Claims Commission** (the second appellant) ([1]). This transaction was prompted by claims lodged by the beneficiaries of the **MANALA MGIBE CPA**, seeking restitution under the **Restitution of Land Rights Act** 22 of 1994 ([2]).

A written agreement for the sale was made on **22 August 2008**, involving properties owned by de Klerk. The **purchase price** was set at **R3,700,000**, with specific arrangements for payment detailed in the contract ([4]). The funds were to be deposited into the conveyancer's trust account, specifically appointed to manage this transaction ([3]).

Despite receiving a deposit of R1,850,000 and subsequent payments, the conveyancer, **Sechele Inc.**, misappropriated a portion of the funds intended for de Klerk, resulting in a total outstanding balance of **R1,001,971.50** after the registration of transfer ([5-9]). De Klerk only received partial payments, primarily **R650,000** at the registration of the transfer and subsequent smaller amounts ([8]). After numerous complaints, it was found that the conveyancer had misappropriated funds before they were due to de Klerk under the sale agreement ([8]–[9]).

The first instance court (Hiemstra AJ) ruled in favor of de Klerk, ordering the Department and the Deputy Director to pay her the outstanding amount, concluding that the conveyancer had acted primarily as the agent of the buyer, thus specifying that the payments had not been made to de Klerk or her agent ([11]). 

## I - Issues

The key legal questions addressed in this case included:

1. **Agency**: Whether the conveyancer acted as the agent of the seller or the purchaser during the transaction, particularly in receiving payment ([12]).
2. **Discharge of Obligation**: Whether the payment made to the conveyancer constituted valid discharge of the purchaser's obligation to pay the seller for the properties as per the terms of their agreement ([12]).

## R - Rules

The court's findings relied upon several legal principles concerning agency and contractual obligations:

- A principal-agent relationship implies authority to act on behalf of another, which is critical in determining payment obligations ([14]).
- The parties' intentions as articulated in the deed of sale guide the interpretation of agency, including clauses that specify obligations concerning payment ([17]).
- Case law, including **Baker v Probert** and **Verbeek v Maher**, was referenced, establishing precedents regarding the nature of agency authority in commercial transactions ([12], [13], [34]).

Clause 4 of the deed detailed the payment structure, emphasizing that **payment to the conveyancer was contingent upon the completion of certain obligations by the seller**, leading to the consensus that a successful payment by the purchaser was reliant on conveyancer actions being aligned with the seller's best interests ([17]-[20]).

## A - Application

Upon evaluating the facts against governing legal principles, the court ruled that the express terms of the deed indicated that the conveyancer operated as an agent of the purchaser—not the seller—when receiving payments. The arrangement did not confer authority upon the conveyancer to accept payments on behalf of the seller, which led to the conclusion that payments made to the conveyancer did not absolve the purchaser's obligation towards the seller ([11], [16], [20]).

The court also emphasized that misappropriation occurred before the seller had fulfilled her obligations under the contract, thus reinforcing that failure to pay the seller was a breach of contractual duty by the purchaser ([8], [45]). It was determined that unless explicitly granted, a party cannot consider payments made to a third party (in this case, the conveyancer) as discharging their own obligations under the contract ([11], [21]).

The conclusion teetered on the complications surrounding the sufficiency of the purchaser's fulfillment of obligations per the sale agreement, as established in the trial ([28], [29]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held the following:

1. The **first appellant's appeal** was dismissed, affirming the lower court's finding regarding their liability to de Klerk ([22]).
2. The **second appellant's appeal** was upheld, meaning they were not liable as part of the transaction due to their limited involvement as defined in the deed ([22]).
3. The court replaced the lower court's order with a directive to the purchaser alone, as the conveyancer was not appointed to receive payment for the seller; thus, payments could not be equated to discharges of obligation to the seller ([22]).

The implications of this decision highlight the significance of clearly delineating agency roles in real estate transactions, especially regarding financial dealings managed by third-party conveyancers. Moreover, this case underscores the necessity for parties engaged in similar agreements to ensure that all payment terms are explicitly stated in contracts to protect against misappropriation and to clarify discharge of obligations.
