Khumalo v Twin City Developers (328/2017) [2017] ZASCA 143

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 143

F – Facts

The case concerns an appeal against a costs order issued by the Land Claims Court in an urgent application initiated by the appellants, Gweje Khumalo and July Joseph Magubane ([1–2]). Both appellants are long-time occupiers on a farm, Damascus, in Mpumalanga, who had resided there since the 1970s and 1980s, respectively. Their occupancy arose from a historical arrangement permitting them to keep livestock on the land ([2]). The farm was sold to the first respondent, Twin City Developers (Pty) Ltd, which intended to engage in agricultural activities, including keeping buffaloes, as authorized under the Animal Diseases Act 35 of 1984 ([2–4]).

Subsequently, buffaloes were introduced to the farm, leading the appellants to express concerns regarding the potential danger posed by these animals to their families and livestock, considering they believed this constituted a form of constructive eviction ([4–5]). The appellants subsequently brought an urgent application for an interdict to prevent their unlawful eviction amid ongoing eviction proceedings initiated by the respondents ([5]).

The Land Claims Court ultimately dismissed the application, labeling it as "opportunistic" and "mischievous," while also asserting that it lacked jurisdiction over the relief sought, specifically regarding the alleged constructive eviction ([8–9]). As a result of the dismissal, the court ordered the appellants to pay the respondents’ costs, including the costs of employing two counsel ([8–9]).

I – Issues

The appeal raised significant legal questions:

  1. Whether an appeal against a costs order can proceed when the merits of the underlying case remain unchallenged ([13]).
  2. Whether there existed exceptional circumstances warranting the consideration of the appeal concerning costs in accordance with section 16(2)(a) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 ([13–16]).
  3. Whether the Land Claims Court exercised its discretion judicially in awarding costs against the appellants ([13]).

R – Rules

The relevant rules and legal principles included:

  1. Section 16(2)(a) of the Superior Courts Act, which allows for the dismissal of appeals where the decision sought will have no practical effect, except under exceptional circumstances ([16]).
  2. The principle that costs follow the result, but special considerations in the context of social interest litigation and the established practice of the Land Claims Court indicate that costs should not generally be awarded unless exceptional circumstances arise ([17–18]).
  3. The court's discretion to award costs must be exercised judiciously and in light of all relevant circumstances, including the socio-economic status of litigants ([19]).

A – Application

The court began by affirming its ability to hear the appeal centred on costs, emphasizing that the absence of a challenge to the merits did not preclude examination of the case’s facts relevant to costs ([14–15]).

Next, the court assessed whether exceptional circumstances existed based on the criteria in section 16(2)(a). In doing so, the court scrutinized the underlying nature of the litigation, noting that it involved issues of significant social interest stemming from the historical context and rights encapsulated in ESTA ([16–20]).

Importantly, the court declared that the appellants' characterization of their circumstances as tantamount to constructive eviction was not frivolous or vexatious, contradicting the respondent's assertions ([37–38]). The court elaborated that the factors contributing to the vulnerability of the appellants and the chilling effect that an adverse costs order could have on similar litigants were significant considerations that had not been adequately engaged by the Land Claims Court ([31–49]).

Consequently, the SCA concluded that the adverse costs order constituted an exceptional circumstance as it threatened the appellants' livelihoods and undermined their ability to assert their rights under ESTA ([50–51]).

C – Conclusion

The appeal was upheld, with the Supreme Court of Appeal deciding to set aside the costs order made against the appellants and emphasizing the need to protect vulnerable litigants seeking to assert their land rights ([50–51]). The implications of this ruling are notable; it not only reinstated the principle that costs orders in social interest litigation should be approached cautiously, particularly in the context of historical injustices but also reinforced the judiciary's commitment to equity and justice in land tenure disputes.

Thus, the case underscores the imperative of judicial sensitivity to the historical and socio-economic realities of litigants engaged in land disputes, thereby fostering an environment where such litigants can pursue their rights without the paralyzing fear of adverse costs.

Order
The appeal is dismissed with costs ([52–64]).
