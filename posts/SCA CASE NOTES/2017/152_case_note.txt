Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note: Red Coral Investments (Pty) Ltd v Cape Peninsula University of Technology ([2017] ZASCA 152)

### F – Facts
In *Red Coral Investments (Pty) Ltd v Cape Peninsula University of Technology* ([1]), the appellant, Red Coral Investments (Pty) Ltd (Red Coral), instituted a claim for R758,630.12 against the Cape Peninsula University of Technology (CPUT) in the Western Cape Division of the High Court, Cape Town. The claim was based on an alleged oral agreement wherein CPUT—acting as the agent for the National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS)—agreed to pay Red Coral for providing rental accommodation to students funded by NSFAS ([1]–[4]).

CPUT responded with an exception, asserting that the particulars of claim lacked the necessary averments to sustain a cause of action, arguing that NSFAS was legally precluded from entering into such an oral agreement as stipulated by the National Student Financial Aid Scheme Act 56 of 1999 (the Act) ([3]). The High Court upheld the exception, and although it allowed Red Coral to appeal, the appellant was late in lodging the appeal record, hence the appeal lapsed ([1]).

Red Coral sought condonation from the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) for its non-compliance with procedural requirements and reinstatement of the appeal. CPUT opposed this application, contending that the appeal itself lacked merit ([2]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal questions before the court were:
1. Whether the alleged oral agreement between Red Coral and CPUT, acting as agent for NSFAS, was legally valid under the provisions of the Act ([6]).
2. Whether Red Coral's particulars of claim contained the necessary averments to sustain its claims based on delict and unjustified enrichment ([5]).
3. Whether the application for condonation for the late lodging of the appeal could be granted in the absence of prospects of success ([2]).

### R – Rules
The relevant legal principles concerning the validity of contracts and statutory compliance were primarily drawn from the National Student Financial Aid Scheme Act 56 of 1999 ([6]–[15]). The Act serves as a statutory framework governing the relationships and obligations of NSFAS, designated public colleges, and service providers.

Notably, the SCA cited:
- The principle of ultra vires, highlighting that any agreements made outside the statutory powers conferred by the Act are deemed invalid ([8]).
- Provisions of the Public Finance Management Act 1 of 1999, which assert that the State will not be bound by unauthorized transactions by public institutions ([7]).
- The necessity for agreements involving NSFAS to be written and formalized, as per sections 19 and 20 of the Act ([10]).

### A – Application
In its analysis, the SCA reviewed whether CPUT had acted within its statutory authority in facilitating Red Coral's claim. It emphasized that the legislative framework imposed strict limitations on the types of agreements NSFAS could enter into and that such agreements must be formalized in writing ([12]–[14]).

The court found that:
- The Act did not define "service provider," but the functions of NSFAS were clearly restricted to those outlined in the Act, which did not encompass the entering into oral agreements or payments to third-party service providers like Red Coral ([9]–[13]).
- The alleged oral agreement would undermine the statutory purpose of the Act, which primarily aims to provide financial aid to students through regulated processes ([14]).

Consequently, the SCA concurred with the High Court's ruling that the particulars of claim were devoid of sufficient legal basis to establish a cause of action against CPUT, as Red Coral's claims hinged on a legally untenable agreement ([16]).

As to the condonation application, the SCA held that since Red Coral demonstrated no prospects of success on appeal, there was no justification to grant condonation for the late filing ([17]).

### C – Conclusion
The SCA dismissed Red Coral’s application for condonation and reinstatement of its appeal with costs. The court confirmed that the High Court was correct in upholding the exception raised by CPUT, underscoring that the allegations of an oral agreement did not fall within the statutory framework established by the National Student Financial Aid Scheme Act. This ruling illustrates the importance of statutory compliance in public sector contracts and emphasizes the judicial commitment to uphold legislative intentions that protect public funds ([17]).

The case serves as a critical reminder of the need for service providers engaging with institutions like NSFAS to carefully adhere to statutory provisions, ensuring that all agreements are properly documented and within the legal scope of authority established by their governing legislation.
