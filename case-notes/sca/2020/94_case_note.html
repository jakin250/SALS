Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 94

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The appellant, Kedumetse MacWilliam Ngakantsi, a constable in the Stock Theft Unit of the South African Police Service (SAPS), was charged in the Vryburg Regional Court with one count of corruption under section 4(1) of the Prevention and Combating of Corrupt Activities Act 12 of 2004 (the Corrupt Activities Act) ([para 2]). The State alleged that in January 2012, Ngakantsi sold a stray cow for R3,000, which constituted a corrupt act benefiting himself. The regional court ultimately convicted him of this charge and sentenced him to five years’ imprisonment ([para 2]).

During the trial, Ngakantsi failed to appear in court on two occasions: October 8, 2015, and April 18, 2016. His non-appearance was attributed to serious health issues, as he was undergoing treatment for psychosis and severe major depressive disorder, conditions certified by his medical practitioners ([para 3]). The trial court, however, found that his failure to appear was wilful, leading to his conviction on two counts for contravening section 72(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (the CPA), for which he was fined R200,000 on each count ([para 4]).

Following the dismissal of his application for leave to appeal by the regional court, Ngakantsi petitioned the North West Division of the High Court, which also refused his petition on February 13, 2019 ([para 5]). Subsequently, he sought special leave to appeal from the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) concerning the convictions, and sentences imposed for the contraventions of the CPA and corruption ([para 5]).

I – Issues

The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the North West Division of the High Court should have granted Ngakantsi's petition for leave to appeal his convictions and sentences under section 309C of the CPA ([para 6]). This necessitated an evaluation of whether there existed reasonable prospects of success in a potential appeal against the dismissed convictions and the imposed sentences ([para 6]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal provisions include section 72(2) and (4) of the CPA, which govern the consequences of an accused's failure to appear in court following a warning. Section 72(4) states that an accused may be found guilty unless they can demonstrate a reasonable possibility that their failure to appear was not due to fault on their part. This had been addressed in the precedent established in S v Singo [2002] (2) SACR 160 (CC) ([para 7]). Further, the standard for assessing if leave to appeal should be granted is based on whether there are reasonable prospects of success in the anticipated appeal, as confirmed by S v Khoasasa [2003] (1) SACR 123 (SCA) and S v Tonkin [2013] ZASCA 179 ([para 6]).

A – Application

The court evaluated whether the medical evidence and personal circumstances presented by Ngakantsi could establish a reasonable prospect that a court might conclude his non-appearance was not attributable to willful neglect ([para 8]). The argument was bolstered by the psychiatric evaluations he provided, which diagnosed him with conditions that could arguably justify his absence from court ([para 3]).

Additionally, the court considered the appropriateness of the sentences imposed. Ngakantsi’s defence contended that the fines exceeded statutory limits and that considering the circumstances—specifically, his status as a first offender and the absence of disciplinary action from his employer—there was potential for a more lenient sentence ([para 9]).

The assessment concluded that there was indeed sufficient justification to argue for reasonable prospects of success in an appeal, particularly in relation to the fines imposed, which could be perceived as excessive ([para 10]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Ngakantsi's appeal, determining that the North West High Court had erred in refusing to grant him leave to appeal ([para 11]). As a result, it set aside the previous order and granted Ngakantsi the leave to appeal to the full court of the North West Division of the High Court concerning the convictions for contravening section 72 of the CPA and the sentence imposed for corruption under section 4 of the Corrupt Activities Act ([para 11]).

This ruling underscores the principle that an accused's mental health and circumstances should be duly considered in judicial proceedings, particularly regarding the imposition of sentences and failures to appear in court. It also reaffirms the judiciary’s commitment to ensuring that individuals have appropriate avenues for contesting convictions that may appear to lack just foundation in established legal standards ([para 11]).

The rulings have significant implications for similar future cases, particularly concerning the standards applied when assessing an accused's capability to represent themselves effectively in court, and how personal circumstances may lend credence to appeals against seemingly harsh penalties.
