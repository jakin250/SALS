Constitutional Court

Garreth Anver Prince v The President of the Law Society of the Cape of Good Hope

The appellant, Garreth Anver Prince, a follower of the Rastafari religion, sought to be admitted as an attorney.

Constitutional Court 2003 CCT 36/00

Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa
Case Number: CCT 36/00
Judgment Date: 25 January 2002
Judges: Ngcobo J, Chaskalson CJ, Ackermann, Kriegler, Sachs, Mokgoro JJ, Yacoob J

F - Facts

The appellant, Garreth Anver Prince, a follower of the Rastafari religion, sought to be admitted as an attorney. He met all academic requirements except a mandatory period of community service outlined in the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979 ([1]). The Law Society of the Cape of Good Hope, however, refused to register his contract for community service due to his prior convictions for cannabis possession and his intention to continue using cannabis, which he claimed was a requirement of his religious practice ([2]-[4]). The Law Society argued that individuals who declare a permanent intention to violate the law cannot be deemed fit and proper persons to practice law ([2]).

Prince challenged the constitutionality of the prohibition on cannabis possession as prescribed by the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 140 of 1992 and the Medicines and Related Substances Control Act 101 of 1965. He argued that these laws infringed upon his rights to religious freedom, dignity, and equality ([6]).

The appellant's claims were dismissed in both the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal ([6]), leading to the present appeal, focusing on the intersection of cannabis-related legislation and religious practice.

I - Issues

The central issue in this case is whether the prohibition on cannabis use and possession, as it pertains to Rastafari religious practices, violates Prince's constitutional rights. Specifically, the questions are:

  1. Does the prohibition on cannabis use impact the freedom of religion as guaranteed by sections 15 and 31 of the Constitution?
  2. Is the limitation on this right justifiable under section 36 of the Constitution, especially in the context of the government's interest in combatting drug abuse? ([4], [5], [41]).

R - Rules

The relevant statutory provisions include:

  • Attorneys Act 53 of 1979: mandates community service for attorney admission.
  • Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 140 of 1992: prohibits the use and possession of cannabis; does not allow exemptions for religious use ([22]).
  • Medicines and Related Substances Control Act 101 of 1965: similarly prohibits cannabis outside regulated purposes.

Key constitutional provisions cited:

  • Section 15(1): guarantees the right to freedom of thought, conscience, religion, and belief.
  • Section 31(1): protects the rights of cultural, religious, or linguistic communities to practice their religion.
  • Section 36: allows limitation of rights; sets a proportionality test requiring that limitations on rights must be reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society ([37], [45]).

A - Application

The court determined that the laws in question do indeed limit the appellant's religious rights. The practice of using cannabis is central to Rastafari belief, which is recognized as a legitimate religion under South African constitutional law ([15], [16]).

However, the state maintained that prohibiting cannabis serves a pressing governmental interest in the war on drugs and addressing social harm. The justification for the prohibition was deemed essential due to the perceived risks associated with drug abuse, which the appellant did not contest ([44]).

The court examined whether there were less restrictive means to achieve the same governmental objectives. Ngcobo J concluded that an exemption for religious use could be constructed, thus showing the prohibition's overbreadth in not accommodating the religious practices of Rastafari ([78]-[84]).

The majority opinion cited the difficulty of enforcing the prohibition fairly, emphasizing that blanket restrictions on cannabis use rendered legitimate religious exercise impossible. The court noted the need for mechanisms to create a regulated exemption for Rastafari ([155]-[172]).

However, the dissent (Sachs J) argued that the majority underspecified the potential for limitations that do not undermine law enforcement and the government's obligation to limit freedoms without infringing on individual rights unnecessarily ([148]-[150], [164]-[173]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court upheld the appeal, declaring sections of the Drugs Act and the Medicines Act unconstitutional in their failure to provide for religious exemptions concerning cannabis use for Rastafari. The ruling mandated legislative change to incorporate such exemptions while suspending the declaration of invalidity for twelve months to allow Parliament sufficient time to amend the statutes. This decision exemplifies a commitment to accommodating religious practices while recognizing the state's legitimate harmful drug prevention goals ([85], [90], [93]).

The ruling carries significant implications not only for Rastafari but also for broader religious freedoms in South Africa, aligning with constitutional encouragement for tolerance and diversity. The court's decision illustrates the balance between enforcing public health and safety laws while respecting individual rights and minority religious practices.
