# FIRAC Case Note: DPP and Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development v Phillips

**Case Citation:**  
The DPP and Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development v Phillips (803/11) [2012] ZASCA 140 (28 September 2012)

**Court:**  
Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Coram:**  
NAVSA, CLOETE, SHONGWE, TSHIQI JJA, and PLASKET AJA

**Heard:**  
3 September 2012

**Delivered:**  
28 September 2012

## Facts [1–20]

In this appeal, the Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP) and the Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development, as appellants, sought to overturn a High Court decision that had stayed the prosecution subsequent to Mr. Andrew Lionel Phillips's acquittal in a regional magistrate's court. Mr. Phillips had been arrested on 2 February 2000 and faced numerous charges, including keeping a brothel and various breaches related to prostitution, perjury, and immigration law violations ([5]).

The trial commenced in January 2004 but faced multiple delays, exacerbated by a change of prosecutors and extensive pre-trial proceedings. By November 2006, after lengthy proceedings, the DPP's case was closed, with Mr. Phillips applying for a discharge, which was ultimately refused. The defence was scheduled to begin in June 2008, but in the interim, the DPP's appeal under section 310 of the Criminal Procedure Act (CPA) was filed, leading to disputes regarding the record of the proceedings and substantial delays in its filing ([6–9]).

Phillips's legal representatives sought both the dismissal of the appeal and a permanent stay of the prosecution, arguing that the prolonged delay violated his constitutional right to a fair trial under section 35(3)(d) of the Constitution, which guarantees the right to a trial without unreasonable delay ([2]).

The High Court, presided over by Satchwell J, found for Phillips, noting that the DPP's failure to efficiently prosecute the appeal had created an inordinate delay over the years, leading to a violation of Phillips's right to a fair trial ([3],[19-20]). The DPP and the Minister subsequently appealed this decision.

## Issues [22–24]

1. **Jurisdiction of the High Court**: Did Satchwell J, sitting alone, have the jurisdiction to hear the application for a permanent stay of prosecution?
2. **Delay Attribution**: Was the delay in prosecuting the appeal attributable solely to the DPP, or could it also be ascribed to Phillips's legal representatives?
3. **Nature of Relief**: Was the relief sought by Phillips—permanent stay of prosecution—adequately framed and substantiated in light of the procedural history?

## Rules [25–27]

The relevant legal rules included:

- **Section 310 of the CPA**: Governs appeals against acquittals and requires a statutory framework to assess any substantive legal questions arising from the acquittal or procedural decisions made during the criminal trial.
- **Section 35 of the Constitution**: This section outlines the right to a fair trial, including the right to a trial that begins and concludes without unreasonable delay.
- **Common Law Principles on Fair Trial**: Derived from precedents such as *Sanderson v Attorney-General, Eastern Cape* (1998) 2 SA 38 (CC) and *Bothma v Els* (2010) 2 SA 622 (CC), which emphasize the implications of delay on the fairness of trials and highlight the responsibilities of the state in prosecutorial efficiency and accuracy.

## Application [28–57]

In her judgment, Satchwell J stressed that the DPP bore the primary responsibility for the delays and inefficiencies. The court assessed the significant timeline of events, highlighting that over eleven years had passed since the arrest of Phillips, with considerable time elapsing after his acquittal before the appeal could be meaningfully pursued ([59]).

The DPP attributed delays to Phillips's legal team, arguing that they insisted on an exhaustive record that was outside the statutory requirements of a section 310 appeal. However, the court countered that the DPP could have filed a limited record as they insisted was sufficient for the appeal, thus abdicating their responsibility ([35–37]).

Satchwell J further noted that the societal interest in prosecuting serious offenses must be balanced against the accused's rights. She underscored the increased prejudice Phillips faced due to delays, including ongoing social stigma and the risk of double jeopardy should the prosecution succeed in reopening the case ([20], [64]).

The court concluded that any appeal seeking to overturn Phillips's acquittal would constitute a significant infringement of his constitutional rights under section 35(3)(m) concerning double jeopardy ([20]), and this recognition was critical in justifying the order for a permanent stay of prosecution.

## Conclusion [58–61]

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the DPP and Minister's appeal, affirming the High Court's decision to permanently stay the prosecution against Phillips. The court emphasized the necessity for timely administration of justice and the implications of inordinate delays on the accused's rights. 

This case serves as a compelling reminder of the balance between prosecutorial authority and the constitutional rights of accused individuals, and it highlights the imperative of efficiency within the prosecutorial framework to ensure justice is both done and seen to be done. The decision reinforced the principle that ongoing delays, particularly under circumstances as complex and lengthy as those present in Phillips's circumstances, warrant judicial intervention to meet constitutional standards of justice.
