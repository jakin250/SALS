Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of **General Council of the Bar v Mansingh** (417/12) [2013] ZASCA 9

## F – Facts
The case **General Council of the Bar v Mansingh** commenced in the North Gauteng High Court, where U. R. Devi Mansingh (the first respondent), a practising advocate affiliated with the Johannesburg Society of Advocates (JSA), sought a declaratory order asserting that section 84(2)(k) of the Constitution did not empower the President to confer the title of senior counsel (commonly referred to as ‘silk’) to advocates. The application included various respondents: the President of the Republic, the Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development, the General Council of the Bar (GCB), the JSA, the Independent Association of Advocates of South Africa (IAASA), and the Law Society of South Africa ([1]-[2]). 

While the Law Society took an ambivalent position in the initial proceedings, the court a quo approved Mansingh's application, ruling in her favor and thereby indicating that the conferral of ‘silk’ was not within the President's authority under the cited section ([3]). The decision led to an appeal by the GCB and the JSA ([3]). The primary concern of the appeal rested on an interpretation of section 84(2)(k), which states that the President is responsible for "conferring honours" ([4]).

## I – Issues
The pivotal legal issue in this case revolves around whether the President’s power to "confer honours" under section 84(2)(k) of the Constitution encompasses the authority to appoint advocates as senior counsel ([4]). The appellants maintained that the title of senior counsel is a traditional mark of professional recognition, thereby constituting an honour. In contrast, Mansingh contended that the section did not authorize such conferral. Secondary issues included whether this power could be inferred from section 84(1) as an auxiliary function of the President as Head of State ([5]-[6]).

## R – Rules
The relevant rule arises from the interpretation of section 84(2)(k) of the Constitution, which is analyzed through a purposive lens considering both linguistic and historical contexts ([10],[12]). The court followed specific interpretive methodologies previously adopted by the Constitutional Court, indicating that the meaning of constitutional text must reflect its object and purpose without imposing a strained interpretation ([10]). 

The court highlighted various precedents, including **South African Airways v Aviation Union of South Africa** and **Investigating Directorate: Serious Economic Offences v Hyundai Motor Distributors**, establishing that interpretation must comply with the language while being cognizant of historical contexts ([10],[12]).

## A – Application
In analyzing the provision historically, the court found that the institution of senior counsel has roots in British colonial traditions, where conferral was tied to royal prerogative ([13],[14]). Consequently, senior counsel appointments were treated as honours, thereby falling within the ambit of section 84(2)(k). The appellants provided several historical references, including **Attorney General for Dominion of Canada v Attorney General for the Province of Ontario**, stated conclusions on honour conferrals as not merely administrative acts but as indicators of professional eminence recognized by the state ([15]-[16]). 

Mansingh's assertion concerning the absence of senior counsel amid existing national orders on the Presidency's website was challenged by the appellants, arguing that such exclusions do not disqualify the standing of silks as honours under section 84(2)(k) ([29]). The court also rejected the argument that the President's power to confer such honours excludes other categories of professionals, underscoring historical distinctions between legal practitioners and other trades ([31]).

The court scrutinized the broader contextual implications of the honour, asserting that the establishment of senior counsel continues to be relevant even in contemporary paradigms ([34]). 

## C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, concluding that the President indeed possesses the authority to confer the title of senior counsel under section 84(2)(k) of the Constitution. It found no substantial grounds to diverge from the historically established recognition of senior counsel as an honour, highlighting the President’s role as a marker of professional esteem within the legal profession. The court dismissed Mansingh’s application, thereby validating the tradition of conferring ‘silk’ as both an honour and a recognition of professional merit ([34],[35]).

### Implications:
This judgment reinstates the significance of the senior counsel institution within the South African legal framework, affirming the authority of the President in such appointments. Additionally, it underscores the imperative to interpret constitutional texts with respect to both historical context and existing legal traditions. The ruling reinforces the importance of professional recognition mechanisms while highlighting a need for ongoing discourse regarding the role and relevance of such institutions within a modern constitutional framework.
