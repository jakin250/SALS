Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note: Pitso and Others v Chabeli Molatoli Attorneys Incorporated [2024] ZASCA 94

## F – Facts

In this case, Mrs. Polo Susan Pitso, the first appellant and executrix of the estate of the late Mr. Likano John Pitso, sought to terminate her mandate with the respondent, Chabeli Molatoli Attorneys Incorporated ([1]). The respondent had previously assisted Mrs. Pitso in the administration of her late husband's estate following his intestate death in November 2021. 

On 18 November 2021, a written mandate and fee agreement was concluded between Mrs. Pitso and the respondent, allowing the respondent to manage the estate's administration, with a stipulated fee of 3.5% ([5]). However, Mrs. Pitso expressed concerns about the respondent's interim invoices in April 2022, alleging they far exceeded the total available funds for settling estate debts ([6]).

On 28 April 2022, Mrs. Pitso terminated the mandate and appointed a different law firm, Seleka Attorneys Incorporated, to replace the respondent ([8]). The respondent responded by filing an urgent application in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, seeking an order to declare the termination invalid and to prevent Mrs. Pitso from terminating the mandate without court approval ([9]). The high court initially dismissed the respondent's application for lack of urgency, but upon further proceedings before Ndlokovane AJ, the termination was deemed unlawful despite the established principle that a mandate may be revoked at will ([10]).

## I – Issues

The central issue in this appeal was whether Mrs. Pitso had the right to terminate the mandate with Chabeli Molatoli Attorneys Incorporated and if that termination could be deemed unlawful ([1]–[2]). Additionally, the legitimacy of the high court's orders, particularly regarding the termination of the agency and the implications on Mrs. Pitso's position as executrix, were under examination ([10], [14]).

## R – Rules

The case touches upon fundamental principles of agency law, particularly the mandate's terminability at the will of the principal, as established in *Liberty Life Group Ltd and Others v Mall Space Management CC t/a Mall Space Management* ([12]). For a party to succeed in obtaining a final interdict, they must demonstrate a clear right, an apprehended injury, and that no other remedy is available ([11]). 

Furthermore, in terms of public policy, compelling a principal to retain an agent against their will is generally viewed as contrary to legal principles, and if there is any prejudice arising from the termination, it can only give rise to claims in damages ([12]). Thus, the common law principles exercising agency and the conditions for final interdicts are the legal frameworks that form the basis for resolving the issues at hand.

## A – Application

The Court of Appeal thoroughly examined whether Mrs. Pitso had acted within her rights to terminate the mandate. Relying on the *Liberty Life* case, the Court emphasized the longstanding principle that a principal is entitled to revoke a mandate at will, hence rejecting the respondent's claim that Mrs. Pitso had unlawfully terminated her agency ([12]). 

The Court noted Mrs. Pitso's expressed dissatisfaction with the respondent's invoices and her assertion that she was not in a proper state of mind when signing the original mandate ([6], [10]). However, the court found no legal basis in the application for a final interdict, noting the absence of proof that Mrs. Pitso acted against the interests of the estate or any creditors ([14]). The Judge also determined that the claim was merely for payment of fees, which did not necessitate court intervention to compel Mrs. Pitso to retain the services of the respondent ([13]).

As for the requirements of a final interdict, the respondent failed to establish the necessary legal criteria for its case, undermining its claims under the outlined standards ([11]). The President and Justices recognized that further complicating factors, such as the failure to produce necessary supporting material, also contributed to the dismissal of the high court's ruling ([12], [14]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that Mrs. Pitso was within her rights to terminate the mandate with the respondent, affirming that the exercise of a principal’s right to revoke an agent's mandate is legally protected ([15]). The court overturned the high court's finding, dismissing the application with costs and declaring the cross-appeal as fatally defective due to lack of merit ([2], [15]). The implications of this ruling reassert the concept that agents cannot compel principals to continue an agency relationship against their will, which serves both to uphold the autonomy of the principal and to clarify the boundaries of agency law in South Africa. This case reinforces the legal doctrine on agency termination and sets a precedent for handling similar disputes, thereby shaping future judicial outcomes related to agency agreements and executorship.
