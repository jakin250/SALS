Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: *Rayment and Others v Minister of Home Affairs and Others; Anderson and Others v Minister of Home Affairs and Others* [2023] ZACC 40

### F – Facts:  Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–20])

The case involved two applications for the confirmation of a constitutional invalidity order made by the Western Cape Division of the High Court, Cape Town. The first application, referred to as the Rayment application, involved Tereza Rayment and five others, including applicants who represented their minor children ([paras 1–4]). The second application, referred to as the Anderson application, involved Richard William Anderson and three others, with similar reference to minor children ([para 2]).

The applicants were foreign nationals who previously held spousal visas under section 11(6) of the Immigration Act 13 of 2002 (“Immigration Act”). These visas were valid only as long as the marital relationships persisted. Upon termination of these relationships, the visas ceased to be valid. The applicants, residing in South Africa, were active and supportive parents to their children, some of whom were South African citizens or permanent residents. The breakdown of these relationships exposed the applicants to the risk of being forced to leave South Africa, separating them from their children, which could significantly hinder their ability to fulfill parental obligations ([paras 6–16]).

The High Court ruled that certain sections and regulations of the Immigration Act were unconstitutional as they unjustifiably limited the rights of the applicants under sections 10, 28, and 36 of the Constitution, affecting their dignity and the well-being of their children ([para 20]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–20])

The primary legal questions revolved around the constitutionality of specific provisions in the Immigration Act and associated regulations. Hence, the relevant issues included:

1. Whether sections 10(6), 11(6), and 18(2) of the Immigration Act, and regulation 9(9)(a) of the Immigration Regulations were inconsistent with the Constitution ([para 3]).
2. Whether the existing provisions imposed an unjustifiable limitation on the applicants' rights to dignity and family life, particularly in relation to their children ([paras 19–20]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 1–20])

The court referred primarily to the South African Constitution, particularly sections:
- **Section 10**: Right to dignity.
- **Section 28**: Rights of children, affirming that a child's best interests are paramount in all matters affecting them.
- **Section 36**: Justification of limitations on rights, assessing the reasonable and justifiable nature of such limitations in an open and democratic society.

The court also invoked significant precedents, mainly:
- **Dawood v Minister of Home Affairs** [2000] ZACC 8, which addressed the rights related to family life and the unjustifiable limitation thereof.
- **Nandutu v Minister of Home Affairs** [2019] ZACC 24, which highlighted similar issues regarding the rights of spouses and children in immigration contexts ([paras 51–89]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 21–99])

The Constitutional Court assessed the provisions of the Immigration Act in light of the constitutional rights implicated. The Court found that the provisions effectively marginalized foreign parents of South African children, adversely impacting their ability to maintain familial ties and economic stability ([paras 51–75]).

1. **Section 10(6)(b)**: The requirement to leave South Africa to apply for a change of status was invalidated as it unjustifiably hindered the right to family life and dignity. The court determined the limitations imposed were neither rational nor justifiable, failing to serve a legitimate governmental purpose ([paras 90–108]).

2. **Section 18(2)**: This provision, which prevented foreign nationals from working under a relative’s visa, was also struck down. The Court emphasized that preventing individuals from working when they have parental obligations is irrational and undermines both the rights of the parent and child ([paras 94–109]).

Collectively, these provisions limited not only the applicants’ rights but also infringed upon the rights of their South African children, who stood to lose familial support, thus violating their rights under the Constitution ([paras 73–99]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 100–132])

The Constitutional Court upheld the High Court's ruling that the relevant sections of the Immigration Act and regulations were unconstitutional and invalid. The declared invalidity was subject to a 24-month suspension to allow Parliament to rectify the constitutional defects.

1. **Key Findings**:
   - The provisions unjustifiably limited the rights to dignity and parental responsibilities under sections 10 and 28 of the Constitution.
   - The requirement for foreign nationals to leave the country to obtain new visa status while having parental obligations was deemed irrational and discriminatory.

2. **Implications**:
   - This ruling serves as a significant affirmation of the protection of rights for foreign nationals in spousal relationships, particularly highlighting the implications for children of such relationships.
   - The decision also underscores the need for legislative bodies to craft laws that align with constitutional principles governing dignity and family life.

3. **Future Considerations**: Should Parliament fail to amend the identified provisions within the stipulated period, the reading-in effect as prescribed by the Court would become final, affirming greater rights for non-South African parents of South African children ([paras 126–132]).

This judgment is pivotal in shaping immigration law as it relates to family rights and exemplifies the judiciary's role in ensuring compliance with constitutional mandates, particularly concerning vulnerable populations such as children ([paras 100–132]).
