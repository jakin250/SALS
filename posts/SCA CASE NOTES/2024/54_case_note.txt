Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# Case Note: KB & Another v Minister of Social Development (Case no 462/23) [2024] ZASCA 54

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The case revolves around a challenge to **Section 294 of the Children’s Act 38 of 2005** (the Act) concerning surrogacy. The First Applicant, **KB**, and the Second Applicant, **HBB**, are a married couple who have faced significant medical difficulties in conceiving children. They had a son born via a surrogacy agreement using donor gametes, as both KB suffered from uterine complications and HBB had undergone a vasectomy followed by cancer treatment ([paras 2–4]).

After their son’s birth, they wished to use three remaining fertilized embryos to have another child through surrogacy. However, **Section 294** mandates a genetic link between the child to be born and at least one of the commissioning parents, a requirement KB and HBB contested ([paras 1, 10]). They sought to challenge this provision on constitutional grounds, arguing it violated the rights of their already born child by limiting his ability to have a genetically linked sibling ([paras 4–6]).

The **High Court** dismissed their application, stating that the requirement for a genetic link was constitutionally valid and that the appellants failed to demonstrate an infringement of rights ([paras 8–9]). The couple appealed this decision to the **Supreme Court of Appeal**.

### I – Issues
The primary issue at stake was whether **Section 294 of the Children’s Act** is constitutional concerning the rights of children under the **Constitution of South Africa**, specifically the rights to dignity, equality, and the best interests of the child ([paras 10, 13]). The court had to consider whether a constitutional right exists for an already born child to have a genetically linked sibling through surrogacy and if the challenged provision was inconsistent with the constitutional rights of the child ([paras 10–12, 21]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework referenced the **Constitution of South Africa**, particularly:
- **Section 9** – Right to equality
- **Section 10** – Right to dignity
- **Section 28** – Children’s rights, which emphasizes the best interests of children ([paras 10, 22])

Additionally, the court addressed the **separation of powers** doctrine, emphasizing that courts should respect the legislative intent unless the law is demonstrably unconstitutional ([paras 9, 10, 26]). Precedential rulings from the **Constitutional Court**, notably in **AB and Another v Minister of Social Development**, were also fundamental in establishing the legal background and purpose of **Section 294** ([paras 18–20]).

### A – Application
The court meticulously analyzed the legislative purpose of **Section 294**, identifying that it intends to establish a genetic relationship between the child born via surrogacy and at least one of the commissioning parents, thereby safeguarding the child's self-identity ([paras 17, 20, 21]). 

Counsel for the appellants argued there should be an allowance for a genetic link between siblings, but the court countered that the statute specifically protects the unborn child’s interests, not those of the already born child. It was concluded that the right to have a genetically linked sibling is not recognized constitutionally. The burden of proof required to demonstrate a violation of constitutional rights and establish that such rights exist was not met by the appellants ([paras 13–20, 24, 30]).

Moreover, the court articulated that no irrationality in the legislative choice was present, maintaining that the legislative intent aligns with the public good. This aligns with established principles regarding legislative interpretation, which dictate a need to consider the text, context, and purpose of laws in light of constitutional mandates ([paras 23–24, 26]).

### C – Conclusion
The **Supreme Court of Appeal** ultimately dismissed the appeal, affirming the constitutionality of **Section 294** of the Children’s Act. It reinforced that the legislative measure serves a significant function in establishing a genetic link for the protection of children born through surrogacy. The court clarified that without a constitutional finding of invalidity, the legislative text could not be altered by reading words into it ([paras 32–34]). As a result, each party was ordered to bear its costs adequately, emphasizing the need for individuals to challenge laws without the deterrent of onerous costs ([paras 28–30]).

This decision underscores the boundaries within which constitutional rights are interpreted and the deference given to legislative provisions designed to uphold the welfare of children.
