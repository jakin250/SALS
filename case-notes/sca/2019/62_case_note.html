Department: Transport, Province of KwaZulu-Natal v Ramsaran & Others [2019] ZASCA 62

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 62

F – Facts

This case involves the Department: Transport, Province of KwaZulu-Natal (hereinafter "the department"), which initiated legal proceedings against nine officials of the department, including S Ramsaran, JS Naidoo, and others, alleging their involvement in a fraudulent scheme resulting in the loss of R7,123,704.91 ([1]). The combined summons was issued on 25 November 2014, and the defendants responded with a notice on 3 March 2015, questioning the authority of the plaintiff's attorneys based on the Treasury Regulations promulgated under the Public Finance Management Act 1 of 1999 ([2]).

The matter escalated, resulting in the respondents applying for an order to have the summons declared a nullity and the action set aside, which the department opposed ([3]). On 4 December 2015, the High Court granted the department a period of 30 days to rectify or explain the circumstances underlying the purported Power of Attorney authorizing their attorneys to act ([4]). The department's subsequent affidavits, filed out of time and without a condonation application, resulted in the court striking the matter from the roll and ordering the department to pay the costs incurred ([4]).

On 16 November 2016, the court ruled against the department, leading to the present appeal ([5]). During the proceedings, the respondents issued a notice on 6 August 2018, abandoning their previous judgment but reserving the issue of costs ([6]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) were:

  1. Whether the abandonment of the judgment by the respondents extinguished the department’s appeal rights and the operative provisions of the High Court's judgment ([6]).
  2. The implications of the respondents' failure to tender costs within their notice of abandonment ([8]).
  3. Whether the department could recover costs associated with the appeal following the abandonment ([9]).

R – Rules

The court relied upon various rules and statutory provisions in reaching its decision:

  1. Uniform Rule 41: Specifically, sub-rules 1 and 2, which govern the abandonment of judgments and the associated costs ([8]).
  2. Relevant case law:
  • Durban City Council v Kistan established that abandonment removes the lis between the parties, leaving the court with no recourse to underlying arguments unless the substantive issues are still relevant ([7]).
  • Subsequent cases demonstrated that an abandonment without a cost tender permits the receiving party to apply for costs ([9]).

A – Application

In evaluating the issues, the SCA noted that by abandoning the judgment, the respondents effectively conceded the Department's entitlement to relief on appeal. The court clarified that an appeal lies against the substantive order, not the rationale behind it ([6]). It emphasized that the mere abandonment did not uphold the previous judgment; therefore, the merits of that judgment did not warrant further adjudication ([6]).

The court recognized that the absence of a tender for costs in the respondents' notice implied that the department maintained the right to seek recovery. The court assessed the procedural integrity of the department’s ability to recover costs incurred up to the abandonment date and reiterated procedural precedents allowing the department to either bring the matter back before the court specifically for costs or to seek an order directly linked to the abandonment ([9]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA ultimately dismissed the department's appeal but ruled that costs up to 6 August 2018 were to be paid jointly and severally by the first, third, fourth, sixth, and eighth respondents. The court maintained the notion that while the substantive appeal failed, the department's right to recover certain costs was affirmed, ensuring that procedural fairness was upheld and demarcating the boundaries of respondent responsibilities in cases of abandonment ([10]).

Holding and Implications

The case illustrates the importance of procedural compliance in legal proceedings, particularly regarding representations made by public departments, the necessary tender of costs in abandonment cases, and the rights and remedies available to parties in similar circumstances.
