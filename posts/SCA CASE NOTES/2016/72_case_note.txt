Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Masemola v Road Accident Fund (256/2015) [2016] ZASCA 72

## F – Facts

On **1 November 2011**, Debbie Mahlaku Masemola (the appellant/plaintiff) was involved in a motor vehicle accident while a passenger in a vehicle on Mmameng road, Limpopo Province, South Africa. The collision resulted in various injuries, including head trauma and soft tissue injuries ([1]). The Road Accident Fund (RAF), the defendant/respondent, conceded to 100% liability and committed to covering Masemola’s past and future medical expenses, as mandated by s 17(4)(a) of the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 ([2]).

The disagreement arose regarding the percentage to be deducted for pre-morbid contingencies in calculating Masemola’s future loss of earnings. An actuarial report provided by Johan Sauer Actuaries and Consultants was to serve as the basis for this calculation, alongside a report from Industrial Psychologist Louis Linde ([2]). Upon reviewing the case, the **Gauteng Division of the High Court**, represented by De Klerk AJ, erroneously misconceived the issue of the trial and inaccurately stated the contingency percentage deduction as 15% rather than 10% as proposed ([3]).

Following the court's ruling on **18 August 2014**, which led to an erroneous award of R406 000 for future loss of earnings ([3]), Masemola’s legal team sought to appeal the decision due to an apparent miscalculation and legal misunderstanding regarding the agreed deduction ([5]). The application for leave to appeal was lodged after a delay, leading to subsequent applications for condonation due to the late filing ([5]). The RAF maintained a non-settlement stance, further complicating the litigation ([7]).

## I – Issues

The core legal questions in this appeal were as follows:

1. **What was the appropriate contingency percentage rate to deduct for pre-morbid future loss of earnings?** ([6])
2. **Did the trial court misdirect itself in its calculations and the resulting award for future loss of earnings?** ([6])

These issues revolved around the trial court's interpretation of expert reports and its discretion in evaluating contingency percentages, which may have unjustly affected the damages awarded to Masemola.

## R – Rules

The court relied on established legal principles governing delictual claims and the assessment of damages. Key rules considered include:

- **Subjective Estimation of Contingency Percentages**: The determination of contingency deductions is inherently subjective, and appellate courts avoid substituting their estimates unless the trial court has materially misdirected itself ([14]). The precedent in *Shield Insurance Co Ltd v Booysen* 1979 (3) SA 953 (A) emphasizes this principle ([14]).
  
- **Duty of the Trial Court**: The trial court must consider various factors when exercising its discretion regarding contingency rates, including potential future incapacity or limitations arising from injuries sustained ([15]).
  
- **Appellate Review Standards**: The appellate court exercises restraint, interfering with trial court findings only in cases of gross miscalculation or misapplication of discretion ([14]) ([15]).

## A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal critically assessed the trial court's error in the application of the contingency percentage. The trial court calculated a 15% deduction despite evidence indicating that a 10% deduction, in line with expert recommendations, was more appropriate ([17]). 

Significantly, the Linde report notably described Masemola as an individual with aspirations of pursuing a career in security services which was potentially achievable barring the accident. The court recognized her previous attempts to recover professionally post-accident, which included part-time work despite her injuries ([12]–[17]). The evidence from the actuarial report supported a 10% deduction rather than 15%, thus indicating that the trial court placed insufficient weight on Masemola's potential earning capacity had the accident not occurred ([16]–[17]).

Furthermore, the court highlighted the inherent flaw in the trial court's calculations, leading to an unjustifiably low award. Notably, the court pointed out that the RAF's refusal to settle was inefficient and wasteful in the context of the claimant's protracted wait for resolution, stressing the need for efficient justice ([9]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, recognizing the errors made by the trial court. The court ordered that the **pre-morbid contingency deduction** for future loss of earnings be revised from 15% to a more appropriate **10%**, effectively increasing the potential damages award for Masemola. The court emphasized the importance of efficiency in resolving claims and admonished the RAF for prolonging the litigation unnecessarily. 

The appellate judgment concluded with a mandate for costs to be borne by the RAF, including expenses for the employment of two counsel, thereby reinforcing the principle of compensating the litigant for the undue burden caused by the appeal ([19]). The implications of this decision underscore the judiciary's role in ensuring fair and accurate compensation for victims of delicts and the importance of adhering to expert recommendations in such assessments.
