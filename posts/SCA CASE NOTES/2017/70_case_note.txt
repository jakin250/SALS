Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note: Machete v The State (167/17) [2017] ZASCA 70

## F – Facts

In the case of **Machete v The State** (167/17) [2017] ZASCA 70 ([paras 1–2]), the appellant, Thabo William Machete, was charged with robbery with aggravating circumstances in the Regional Court of Tzaneen. Following his conviction, he was sentenced to fifteen years' imprisonment. Machete appealed this conviction to the Limpopo Division of the High Court, which upheld the conviction but reduced the sentence to eight years. Eventually, Machete sought special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA).

The principal evidence against Mr. Machete was the testimony of the complainant, Mr. Mabeka, who claimed to have recognized him during the assault as he made his way home from a tavern ([para 5]). However, Mabeka provided vague details regarding his prior acquaintance with Machete, primarily conveying that his brother-in-law had introduced them without clarifying the context or the identification method. The robbery took place at night, and the only light source was the tavern, raising questions about Mabeka's ability to properly identify Machete, particularly since he indicated he could not recognize the other two robbers ([para 6]). 

The proceedings revealed significant inconsistencies between Mabeka's account and that of the investigating officer regarding the circumstances surrounding Machete's arrest ([para 7]). The trial magistrate appeared to disregard Machete's detailed defense, and the validity of the complainant’s identification was notably tenuous.

## I – Issues

The central issue in this appeal was whether Mr. Machete was properly identified as one of the robbers who attacked and robbed Mr. Mabeka ([para 5]). This issue encompasses broader questions about the reliability of eyewitness testimony in criminal cases, particularly when the identification comes from a single witness and is not corroborated or reinforced by other evidence.

Furthermore, there was a procedural question regarding the authority and duty of the appellate court when the prosecution itself seeks to overturn a conviction, calling into question how the High Court dealt with the prosecution's admission that the evidence was insufficient to establish the appellant's guilt beyond a reasonable doubt ([para 4]).

## R – Rules

The legal principles that the SCA relied upon in its decision included the standard of proof in criminal cases, as established in **S v E** 1995 (2) SACR 547 (A), which mandates that guilt must be proven beyond a reasonable doubt ([para 4]). The Court noted that it is open to a court of appeal to uphold a conviction despite the prosecution expressing reservations regarding the evidence, but such a stance should be adopted only after thorough consideration ([para 4]).

Moreover, the court emphasized that the prosecution's role is not merely to secure a conviction at all costs but to present its case fairly, including making appropriate concessions when warranted ([para 4]).

## A – Application

In applying the law to the facts, the SCA thoroughly examined the evidence presented during the trial, particularly focusing on the precarious nature of the complainant's identification of Machete ([paras 3–4]). The prosecution’s admission that the evidence was insufficient to satisfy the required standard of proof significantly influenced the court's analysis.

The SCA expressed astonishment at the High Court's decision to maintain the conviction despite the prosecution's concession that the evidence did not adequately support Machete's identification as one of the robbers ([paras 3–4]). This pointed to a fundamental oversight in the High Court's reasoning process, as it failed to engage with or scrutinize the prosecution's reservations regarding the evidence ([para 3]). 

As the complainant's testimony was not corroborated by other witnesses or reliable identification methods and featured significant inconsistencies, the SCA concluded that the identification itself was fundamentally flawed. The inconsistencies between Mabeka’s account and Machete’s defense indicated that Machete was treated unfairly, undermining the integrity of the trial ([paras 6–7]).

Ultimately, the SCA found that the prosecution's decision to request the conviction's dismissal on the basis of insufficient evidence was justified, and it took the view that the High Court had misapplied its evaluative responsibilities ([para 4]). Thus, the SCA determined that the evidence, even when viewed in light of the prosecution's argument, did not sustain a conviction, warranting the appeal's upholding.

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Mr. Machete's appeal, resulting in the annulment of his conviction and sentence ([para 1]). The SCA's ruling underscored the necessity for strong, corroborated evidence in criminal identification cases, particularly when relying on a single witness. The Court's decision reflected a critical stance on the responsibilities of prosecution and the importance of fair trial principles, emphasizing that even in cases where the legal framework allows for ambiguity, justice must ultimately be served through rigorous adherence to evidentiary standards ([paras 4–5]).

The implications of this case extend beyond the particulars of Machete's conviction, prompting a reevaluation of how identification evidence is treated in criminal trials, particularly in instances where the prosecution expresses doubt about its own evidence. The judgment serves as a reminder that the presumption of innocence must prevail, and that the burden of proof lies firmly with the prosecution to demonstrate guilt beyond reasonable doubt.
