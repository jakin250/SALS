Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

### FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title**: White v City of Cape Town (918/10) [2011] ZASCA 212 (29 November 2011)

**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Judges**: Ponnan, Snyders, Leach, Majiedt JJA, and Petse AJA

**Heard**: 10 November 2011

**Delivered**: 29 November 2011

---

#### F - Facts

[1] This case revolves around a water tariff policy adopted by the City of Cape Town, which the appellant, Mr. Desmond White, alleged discriminated against flat dwellers such as himself. The matter had substantial implications for residents in multi-unit dwellings who were charged higher tariffs compared to single dwelling residents. By the time the appeal was heard, the specific tariffs in question had already expired, leading the court to question whether the appeal had any practical effect.

[3] In June 2009, Mr. White applied to the Western Cape High Court for declaratory orders against the City. He sought to demonstrate that the City had contravened sections 74(3), 74(1)(a), and 74(1)(b) of the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000. He argued that flat dwellers were charged significantly more for water than their single dwelling counterparts, and that these charges were both unfair and discriminatory. He also sought declarations that the applicable tariff policies for solid waste removals were unfair and discriminatory.

[5] The application was dismissed on 31 May 2010 with costs by Judge Thring, though leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal was granted. Notably, the City emphasized that the tariff policy being contested would only be in effect until 30 June 2010, raising the question of whether the case had become moot by the time the appeal was heard ([6]).

[9] Mr. White had confined his claims to declaratory relief regarding the policies in question for the financial year 2009/2010, asserting that the City had the option to amend these tariffs follow the budget meeting. Still, his argument failed to address how a ruling in his favor would affect future tariffs or similar cases.

---

#### I - Issues

[2] The central issue presented before the court was whether the sought-after declaration would yield any practical effect, as outlined in section 21A(1) of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959. 

[11] Specifically, the court had to determine if the circumstances of the case constituted a live controversy requiring adjudication, or whether it had lapsed into an abstract, moot query, thus allowing for dismissal under the parameters established in prior judgments.

---

#### R - Rules

[2] Section 21A(1) of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959 provides:

> "When at the hearing of any civil appeal... the issues are of such a nature that the judgment or order sought will have no practical effect or result, the appeal may be dismissed on this ground alone."

[7] The court relied on previous cases such as *Coin Security Group (Pty) Ltd v SA National Union for Security Officers & others* 2001 (2) SA 872 (SCA), emphasizing a longstanding principle against courts making determinations on abstract or academic issues ([8]). 

[10] Reference was made to *National Coalition for Gay and Lesbian Equality & others v Minister of Home Affairs & others* 2000 (2) SA 1 (CC), noting that a case is moot when it no longer presents a live controversy that requires legal resolution, which is essential for avoiding advisory opinions.

---

#### A - Application

[7] The court considered the appellant’s assertions juxtaposed with the City’s responses regarding the expiration of the tariff policy, highlighting that any ruling would ultimately lack effect once the policy ceased to be. The City’s position suggested a commitment to revising tariff structures annually, further asserting that a decision against the current tariff would not assist future litigants in similar situations ([9]).

[12] In assessing appellant’s arguments against the costs, the court noted the disproportionate burden on the City in defending against claims that lacked practical relevance. It was explained that maintaining balance in resource allocation for public entities was vital to ensure effective governance and service delivery, making the costs order reasonable ([14]).

[15] The court ultimately found that the appellant’s appeal did not present a legitimate controversy, and therefore it was appropriate to strike the appeal from the roll, reinforcing judicial efficiency and the principle that courts should not engage in academic disputes. 

---

#### C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal struck the appeal from the roll with costs, concluding that it lacked the practical effect required for adjudication as outlined in section 21A of the Supreme Court Act ([15]). The court's unanimous decision reiterated the importance of courts engaging only in real and relevant disputes, emphasizing the need for judicial economy in light of the increasing burden on court resources. This case serves as a pertinent reminder about the nature of legal challenges and their actual implications on lived realities, particularly in the context of public policies affecting diverse groups within society.
