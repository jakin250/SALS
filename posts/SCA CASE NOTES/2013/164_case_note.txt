Case Note: SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: Thunder Cats Investments 92 (Pty) Ltd v Nkonjane Economic Prospecting and Investment (Pty) Ltd

**Neutral Citation:** Thunder Cats Investments 92 (Pty) Ltd v Nkonjane Economic Prospecting and Investment (Pty) Ltd (847/12) [2013] ZASCA 164 (26 November 2013).

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Coram:** Navsa ADP, Malan, Shongwe, Wallis JJA et Meyer AJA

## F – Facts

The case revolves around the winding-up of Nkonjane Economic Prospecting and Investment Pty Ltd (the company), in which Thunder Cats Investments 92 Pty Ltd and Turquoise Moon Trading 8 Pty Ltd (the appellants) are shareholders who, alongside Bosasa Operations Pty Ltd and Bosasa Youth Development Centres Pty Ltd (the respondents), each hold 25% of the shares ([1]-[2]). The relationship between the shareholders has significantly deteriorated, resulting in an inability to operate effectively, leading the High Court to order liquidation based on the ground that such an action is "just and equitable" under Section 81(d)(iii) of Companies Act 71 of 2008 ([2]). 

Interestingly, the company's primary asset—a significant shareholding in Ntsimbintle Mining Pty Ltd—remains solvent, valued at approximately R132 million ([1]). The appellants contested the winding-up, arguing that it was precipitated by the respondents’ desire to exit the company. They also pointed out that the shareholders’ agreement stipulates that a deadlock does not justify winding-up ([6]-[10]). The High Court, however, found that the relationship breakdown was irretrievable, justifying the liquidation decision ([2]-[34]).

## I – Issues

The principal legal issues can be distilled as follows:

1. **Applicability of Section 81 of the Companies Act:** Whether the application for winding-up on the basis of "just and equitable" grounds associated with the breakdown of relationships among shareholders aligns with the provisions under Section 81(d)(iii) of the Companies Act 71 of 2008 ([3], [4]).

2. **Deadlock Clause Interpretation:** Whether the deadlock clause in the shareholders' agreement precluded either party from seeking winding-up, and the implications of such a clause on the just and equitable standard set by the Act ([10]-[12]).

3. **Clean Hands Doctrine:** Whether the appellants’ contention that the respondents approached the court with unclean hands effectively barred the winding-up claim, and how this might influence judicial discretion ([27]-[30]). 

## R – Rules

The court referred primarily to the Companies Act 71 of 2008, particularly Section 81, which establishes the framework for winding-up companies that are solvent ([4]-[5]). 

1. **Section 81(d)(iii):** This provision allows for the winding-up of a company where it is established to be "just and equitable," broadening the grounds available for initiating such proceedings ([4]).

2. **Interpretation of Shareholders’ Agreements:** The conventional principles governing the interpretation of contracts, including relevant case law on "just and equitable" standards, feature heavily in the ruling, drawing on precedents such as *Ebrahimi v Westbourne Galleries Ltd* to explore equitable considerations in shareholder relationships ([20]).

3. **Clean Hands Doctrine:** This equitable maxim posits that a party seeking judicial assistance may be denied relief if they have acted unethically regarding the subject of the action ([27]-[29]).

## A – Application

The court, applying the above rules, considered the facts surrounding the breakdown of the relationship between the shareholders and identified that the dysfunction at the company was exacerbated by a long history of failed meetings and litigation ([21]-[26]). The ruling emphasized that the underlying malaise was not merely a symptom of a deadlock but a broader failure of trust and cooperation necessary for the company's governance.

While the appellants argued that the deadlock clause in the shareholders' agreement prevented winding-up on those grounds, the court clarified that this did not preclude consideration under the broader scope of "just and equitable" as presented in Section 81(d)(iii) ([10]-[12]). 

Furthermore, the court placed substantial weight on the irretrievable breakdown of the relationship, supported by communications demonstrating an ongoing impasse and disinterest from the appellants towards negotiations ([22],[26]). The court noted that the essence of partnership-like relationships in corporate contexts requires a level of trust that had dissipated ([18]). As a result, the court found that the winding-up order was justified.

The argument regarding clean hands was also critically examined; although the respondents had contributed to the conflict, the court found that such blame was not solely theirs ([29]). Ultimately, the lack of clean hands was insufficient to eliminate the applicants' right to petition for winding-up, particularly in light of the continuing dysfunction and continued impasse ([28]-[30]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court's decision to liquidate Nkonjane Economic Prospecting and Investment Pty Ltd, ruling that the breakdown of relationships among shareholders constituted a valid ground for winding-up under Section 81(d)(iii) of the Companies Act 71 of 2008 ([34]). The court confirmed that equitable considerations allow for such orders even when both parties share fault regarding the breakdown of relations, emphasizing that the need for effective governance and mutual cooperation lies at the heart of successful company management. This decision may have significant implications for similar cases in corporate law, particularly concerning the interpretation of shareholders' agreements and the application of equitable principles in determining "just and equitable" remedies. 

Thus, the appeal was dismissed with costs, and the court amended the High Court's punitive costs order regarding the recusal application made by the appellants ([35]-[36]).
