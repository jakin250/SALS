Supreme Court of Appeal

Raath v Nel (473/2011) [2012] ZASCA 86

[2] The respondent, J J G Nel, a game farmer from Mokopane, sued the appellant, Dr R Raath, an anaesthetist, due to complications arising from a failed intubation preceding a back operation.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2012 [2012] ZASCA 86

F – Facts

[1] This case involves an appeal against a damages award granted by Rabie J in the North Gauteng High Court, Pretoria. The appeal, with Rabie J's leave, primarily concerns claims for future medical expenses, loss of income and earning capacity, and general damages.

[2] The respondent, J J G Nel, a game farmer from Mokopane, sued the appellant, Dr R Raath, an anaesthetist, due to complications arising from a failed intubation preceding a back operation. The failed intubation resulted in extensive medical issues, including a prolonged stay in intensive care, significant physical rehabilitation, and severe psychological distress, leading to a divorce and suicidal tendencies.

[3] The respondent was a self-made entrepreneur whose business operations included Koos Nel Auto (Pty) Ltd, of which he was the sole shareholder until 1 April 2001 when he transferred his shares to the Koos Nel Trust for estate planning purposes. Consequently, the claims were based on losses allegedly incurred by the company due to the respondent's inability to manage the business effectively during his recovery from the failed intubation.

I – Issues

[8] The legal issues at the core of this appeal encompass whether the respondent personally suffered any loss due to the alleged damages stemming from the failed intubation. The appellant argued that any losses pertained not to the respondent but to Koos Nel Auto and the Koos Nel Trust post-establishment on 1 April 2001. The case also questioned the principles surrounding the separateness of an entity (like a trust) from its beneficiaries and the concept of personal loss in delictual claims.

R – Rules

[9] Citing the principles from previous jurisprudence, the court listed the prerequisites for claiming damages. Key cases, particularly Rudman v Road Accident Fund, establish that for shareholders, a company’s loss does not automatically equate to personal loss unless sufficient evidence is provided to demonstrate that the personal financial situation of the shareholder has been negatively impacted.

[11] The court underscored the legal doctrine concerning the distinct nature of trusts as entities bereft of full legal personality, emphasizing that the operational and fiduciary structure of a trust serves to separate personal and trust assets.

A – Application

[17] The court evaluated the claims for loss of income and earning capacity, differentiating the period before and after the sale of shares to the trust. Evidence of financial loss was presented, notably through the financial auditor’s calculations, demonstrating the anticipated versus actual income for Koos Nel Auto.

[19] The bench found merit in the respondent’s claims for loss sustained before the formation of the trust. The financial report indicated tangible losses attributable directly to the respondent's incapacitation that were rooted in his personal estate.

[20] Furthermore, the court upheld the award for future medical expenses necessitated by the respondent's depressive state post-operative complications, corroborated by psychiatric evidence indicating the respondent would require lifelong treatment.

C – Conclusion

[23] The appeal was partially upheld, reducing the awarded damages linked to loss of earning capacity suffered by the Koos Nel Trust post-establishment, while recognizing personal losses sustained prior to that date. The court reaffirmed the finding awarding future medical expenses and general damages, illustrating the profound impact of the failed intubation on the respondent's life.

The appeal signified a critical analysis of the connection between the personal losses of entrepreneurs operating through corporate entities and reiterated the imperative for clear demarcation of damages when considering trusts and corporate structures in personal injury claims.
