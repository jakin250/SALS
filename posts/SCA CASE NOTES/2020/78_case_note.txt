# Case Note: FIRAC Method  
## Van Huyssteen and Others v Pepkor Speciality (Pty) Ltd and Another (334/2019) [2020] ZASCA 78  

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–9])  
In this case, the appeal concerns the absence of an annexure in an interim order granted by the Western Cape Division of the High Court. The appellants—Mr Abraham Johannes van Huyssteen, Mr Bernard Eugene Mostert, Mr Michael Brown, Mr Gert Christoffel Claassens, and Mr David van Niekerk—were key management personnel of Tekkie Town (Pty) Ltd, a successful retail business specializing in branded footwear ([para 2]).  

On 29 August 2016, these appellants, collectively referred to as key management, exchanged their shares in Tekkie Town for shares in Steinhoff International Holdings NV, which significantly valued at R3 257 250 000 ([para 3]). The exchange agreement stipulated a suspensive condition requiring that they enter into written service agreements with Tekkie Town, which were subsequently amended to include restraint of trade provisions ([para 4–5]).  

Following the share exchange, several structural changes led Tekkie Town to become a wholly-owned subsidiary of Pepkor Holdings Limited and later, the Tekkie Town business was sold to Pepkor Speciality (Pty) Ltd ([para 6]).  

In May 2018, the former shareholders filed a summons against Steinhoff, alleging misrepresentation in the inducement of the share exchange ([para 7]). Eventually, the appellants resigned from their positions in mid-2018 and subsequently began operating a competing business named Mr Tekkie ([para 9]).  

The respondents, Pepkor Speciality (Pty) Ltd and Pepkor Holdings Limited, sought an interim interdict against the appellants to enforce the restraint clauses of their agreements, which the high court granted ([paras 10–13]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1, 10, 20])  
The primary legal issue presented to the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the absence of the envisaged annexure rendered the order from the high court meaningless or unjust, and consequently, whether the appeal concerning that order should be entertained ([para 1]).  

Additionally, the court had to determine whether the interests of justice required intervention despite the interim nature of the order, as the appeal did not satisfy the criteria for a final decision as set out in *Zweni v Minister of Law and Order* ([para 20]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 17, 18, 21])  
The court drew from a variety of legal principles and precedents to evaluate the appeal's merits:

1. **Zweni Test**: This established that a “judgment or order” must be final in effect, definitive of the parties' rights, and must dispose of a substantial portion of the relief claimed ([para 17]).
2. **What constitutes appealability**: As articulated in *S v Western Areas Ltd* and further developed in *Philani-Ma-Afrika v Mailula*, the focus should also consider whether the interests of justice warrant appellate intervention, even in circumstances where an order might not be appealable under traditional definitions ([para 18, 19]).
3. **Civil contempt**: The court relied on the standard for civil contempt set out in *Fakie NO v CCII Systems (Pty) Ltd*, highlighting the requirements to establish proof beyond a reasonable doubt in cases of contempt ([para 26]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 21–29])  
The court critically assessed the ambiguity surrounding the absence of the annexure and its impact on the clarity and enforceability of the interim order ([paras 21–22]). The appellants contended that the lack of an annexure made the order unjust and unclear, with potential implications for enforcing the restraint of trade clauses ([paras 21–22]). However, the court found that the order imposed sufficient clarity regarding what the appellants were restrained from doing: being involved in any competing business stocking or selling specific footwear items dated 1 October 2016 ([paras 25, 24]).  

The court concluded that although an annexure would provide more specificity, it did not alter the fundamental requirements imposed on the appellants by the order ([para 25]). The implications noted by the court emphasized that civil contempt would require proof beyond reasonable doubt—an evidential burden on the respondents to show the appellants had caused any breach of the interdict ([para 26–27]). 

Ultimately, the court held that the interim order was not meaningless or unjust, thus negating the appellants' reliance on the interests of justice as grounds for appeal ([para 28]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 28–34])  
The Supreme Court of Appeal struck the matter from the roll with costs, concluding that the appeal did not warrant further examination given that the interim order was sufficiently clear and enforceable despite the absence of the annexure ([paras 28, 34]). The court reiterated the necessity for clarity in orders affecting litigants, but simultaneously affirmed that the specificities provided within the order's context were adequate to ensure fairness and compliance.

This case reinforces the principles surrounding appealability, the nature of interim orders, and the balance between the interests of justice against definitive legal frameworks, particularly within the context of competition law and restraints of trade. It further illustrates how courts may maintain the integrity of judicial orders while still ensuring that potential ambiguities are minimized through contextual interpretation rather than reliance on supplementary documents. 

Overall, the decision exemplifies a commitment to both upholding substantive legal rights and maintaining judicial efficiency in the face of procedural disputes.
