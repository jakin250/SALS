Supreme Court of Appeal

Brompton Court Body Corporate v Khumalo

The appellant in this case, Brompton Court Body Corporate, is the body corporate of the Brompton Court sectional title scheme ([para 1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018

F - Facts

The appellant in this case, Brompton Court Body Corporate, is the body corporate of the Brompton Court sectional title scheme ([para 1]). The respondent, Christina Fundiswa Khumalo, owned a sectional title unit in the same scheme. Disputes emerged between the parties, leading to the referral of the matter to arbitration as per their mutual agreement ([para 1]).

During the arbitration, the respondent claimed various amounts in levies, repairs, damages for loss of rental income, and defamation. Specifically, she sought credit for levies and charges, as well as costs for repairs and damages due to the inability to rent her unit caused by the appellant's conduct ([para 2]). In response, the appellant counter-claimed for outstanding amounts owed to it by the respondent ([para 2]).

An arbitration award was issued on 21 December 2012, where the arbitrator allowed part of the respondent's claims but ultimately found that she owed the appellant R135,099.48 after subtracting the awarded amounts of R20,000 for repair costs and R27,750 for loss of rental income, thus granting the appellant a final award of R87,349.48, along with interest and costs ([para 3]).

Subsequently, on 26 March 2014, the appellant sought to have the arbitration award made an order of court under section 31 of the Arbitration Act 42 of 1965. The respondent challenged this application, asserting that the claim had prescribed under the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 ([para 4]). The High Court found in favor of the respondent, ruling that the claim had indeed prescribed, leading the appellant to seek redress at the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 4]).

I - Issues

The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the defence of prescription was correctly upheld by the court a quo ([para 4]). Specifically, the court was tasked with determining:

  1. Whether the arbitration award constituted a new debt, thereby triggering a new period for prescription to begin.
  2. If not, what implications this had on the appellant's claim, especially in relation to section 13(1)(f) of the Prescription Act ([para 11]).

R - Rules

The application of prescription in this case revolves around the provisions of both the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 and the Arbitration Act 42 of 1965. Key points of law included:

  • Section 11 of the Prescription Act establishes various periods of prescription for different kinds of debts. Specifically, debts typically prescribe after three years ([para 4]).
  • Section 13(1)(f) provides that if a dispute has been subjected to arbitration, the running of prescription may be delayed for a year after the arbitration proceedings conclude. This provision does not create a new debt forward but simply postpones the completion of the prescriptive period of an existing debt ([paras 7-8]).
  • The Supreme Court emphasized that arbitration awards do not create new debts but confirm existing liabilities, which serve to liquidate or affirm previously disputed debts ([para 6]).

The court further referenced past decisions such as Electricity Supply Commission v Stewarts & Lloyds of SA (Pty) Ltd and Makate v Vodacom Ltd, to delineate the concept of a "debt" within the context of the Prescription Act ([para 11]).

A - Application

In analyzing the legal framework, the Supreme Court of Appeal held that the arbitration award did not create a new debt as per the general principle. Instead, the award served to crystallize the existing debts owed by the respondent to the appellant ([para 6]). Thus, the court established that the three-year prescription period started at the point when the debts originally became due, not when the arbitration award was issued ([para 11]).

The court also noted that the respondent did not satisfy her burden of proof concerning the assertion that the debts had prescribed. Specifically, she failed to provide dates or evidence of when the various debts from the appellant became due, leading the court to conclude that the defense of prescription was not valid in this case ([para 16-17]).

The Supreme Court further referred to section 13(1)(f) of the Act, asserting that it was misapplied by the lower court. The respondent incorrectly framed the argument as if it provided for a one-year period for the arbitration award rather than addressing the suspension of prescription in relation to the underlying debt ([para 15]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the decision of the court a quo, ruling that the appellant's application to have the arbitration award made an order of court should not have been dismissed on the grounds of prescription. The court concluded that the arbitration award did not create a new debt, and therefore the commencement of the prescription period was unaffected by it ([para 19]).

The implications of this ruling underscore the importance of understanding how an arbitration award affects existing obligations and the related prescription timelines. The failure of the respondent to prove the necessary facts regarding the debts' due dates contributed to the court's decision, highlighting the necessity for parties to maintain thorough records of such matters in disputes.

The court's order was that the appeal was upheld with costs, providing a clearer interpretation of the application of prescription in contexts involving arbitration awards and underlining the necessity for accurate legal argumentation when raising the defense of prescription ([para 19]).
