# Case Note: Thembalethu Sam v The State (343/2007) [2008] ZASCA 9

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The appellant, Thembalethu Sam, was convicted in the regional court of Adelaide, Eastern Cape, on four counts: robbery with aggravating circumstances (15 years), unlawful possession of an unlicensed semi-automatic firearm (15 years), unlawful possession of ammunition (9 months), and attempted murder (6 years). The total cumulative sentence was 36 years and 9 months, but the regional court ordered some sentences to run concurrently, resulting in an effective sentence of 25 years in prison ([1]).

The appellant appealed the sentence for unlawful possession of the firearm to the Eastern Cape High Court, which dismissed the appeal on grounds that the sentencing provisions of the Criminal Law Amendment Act were applicable. Following this dismissal, Sam appealed again to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) with leave ([2]).

It was agreed that the appellant was indeed in possession of a semi-automatic pistol during the robbery of the Adelaide branch of Standard Bank, where he and others stole R64 700. Furthermore, he had made a formal admission in the regional court that the firearm was semi-automatic. His testimony revealed that he was aware of how to handle the weapon during the robbery, which included an incident where he fired at a complainant who attempted to apprehend him ([3]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issue revolves around the applicability of the sentencing provisions of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 to the appellant's conviction for unlawful possession of a semi-automatic firearm. The key question is whether these provisions mandate a minimum sentence of 15 years for unlawful possession or if they can be challenged based on substantial and compelling circumstances ([4]).

Additionally, the appellant contended that the interpretation of the Act by the lower courts, particularly regarding what constitutes unlawful possession, was flawed. He drew on precedent, including S v Sukwazi and several other similar cases, arguing that the intention behind the legislation was misapplied ([4], [5]).

### R – Rules
The relevant statutory framework includes the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, specifically section 51(2), which provides that a court must impose a minimum sentence of 15 years for specified offences unless substantial and compelling circumstances justify a lesser sentence ([5]). 

Additionally, Part II of Schedule 2 outlines offences related to the unlawful possession of firearms, including automatic and semi-automatic firearms, and addresses the legislative intent concerning firearm possession and sentencing ([5], [6]).

The SCA also referred to established judicial principles regarding statutory interpretation, which state that a court's role is to ascertain the legislative intent. Words used in the enactment must be given their ordinary meaning, departing from this only in cases of glaring absurdity ([8]).

### A – Application
The court affirmed that once it is established that a semi-automatic firearm was unlawfully possessed, the sentencing provisions of the Criminal Law Amendment Act apply. The court indicated that the explicit language of the statute—using “shall” regarding sentencing—suggested mandatory compliance with the minimum sentence of 15 years for first offenders, unless mitigating factors are established ([6], [7]).

The appellant's challenges were scrutinized against precedents that had interpreted similar legislative provisions. The court distinguished its previous judgments, particularly S v Sukwazi, emphasizing that the question was not whether the offence constituted a new category but rather how the specific offence fits within the existing framework of the Criminal Law ([9],[12]).

The court noted that while the appellant asserted that he was not aware of the semi-automatic nature of the firearm, his admission in court undermined this claim. Given the factual context—that he used the firearm during the robbery and was knowledgeable about its function—the court found no compelling reason to deviate from the minimum sentencing provisions established in the law ([13],[15]).

Moreover, the SCA highlighted that the nature of the firearm was relevant to assessing the severity of the crime and the appropriateness of the 15-year minimum sentence. It pointed out that legislative intent often focuses on the firearms most frequently associated with violent crimes, justifying the stringent penalties associated with the possession of semi-automatic weapons ([17]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming that the sentencing provisions of the Criminal Law Amendment Act were correctly applied in the case of unlawful possession of a semi-automatic firearm. The court held that the principles established in Sukwazi were inapposite to the specifics of this case and found no grounds to render the mandatory minimum sentence disproportionate or unjust. 

The implication of this decision emphasizes the courts' commitment to imposing stringent penalties in cases involving firearms, aligning with legislative intent to combat violent crime. The judgment also clarifies the interpretation of the minimum sentencing legislation in relation to existing offences, thereby providing guidance for future cases involving similar charges ([18]). 

The final order was that the appeal was dismissed ([18]).
