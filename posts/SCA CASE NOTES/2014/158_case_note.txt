Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: Kekana v The State (629/2013) [2014] ZASCA 158

### F – Facts ([1]–[8])
Mr. David Kekana (the appellant) was charged with murder and arson in the South Gauteng High Court after he pleaded guilty to both counts. The appellant locked his wife in a bedroom and set their house on fire following a violent argument, leading to her death in hospital days later. The trial court found that Kekana acted with premeditation based on the facts detailed in his guilty plea. He was sentenced to life imprisonment for murder and five years for arson, which were ordered to run concurrently. Following an unsuccessful appeal in the High Court, the appellant sought leave to appeal the life sentence in the Supreme Court of Appeal.

### I – Issues ([3]–[4])
The appeal raised two primary legal issues:
1. Whether the trial court erred in convicting the appellant of premeditated murder instead of a lesser charge based on an impulsive attack.
2. Whether the trial court misapplied the criteria for identifying substantial and compelling circumstances that could justify a deviation from the prescribed minimum sentence under section 51(1) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997.

### R – Rules ([10]–[12], [18], [22])
The court examined the legal standard for establishing premeditation, referring to the precedent in *S v Raath* [2009] ZASCA, where it was asserted that the State must demonstrate a period of contemplation between the intent to kill and the act itself. Additionally, the court referenced *S v Malgas* [2001] ZASCA, establishing that a court of appeal would only interfere with a sentence if a material misdirection is found or if the sentence is shockingly disproportionate relative to the established legal standards.

### A – Application ([13]–[19], [21], [23])
In its deliberation, the court found that the facts indicated a clear intention to cause harm rather than an impulsive act of anger. The appellant's actions demonstrated planning: he locked his wife in and actively procured petrol to carry out the act. The court concluded this evidenced a definitive premeditated act rather than a spontaneous reaction to provocation. 

On the issue of substantial and compelling circumstances, the appellant argued that his guilty plea, expressions of remorse, and status as a first-time offender warranted a lighter sentence. However, the court held that his actions — specifically the brutal nature of the attack and his refusal to assist the victim afterwards — outweighed any mitigating factors. The absence of genuine remorse, as illustrated by his conduct post-offence and the continuing domestic violence context, further validated the imposition of the life sentence described within legislative parameters.

### C – Conclusion ([24])
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, holding that the trial court had correctly characterized the murder as premeditated and found no substantial and compelling circumstances warranting a deviation from life imprisonment. This decision underscored the seriousness of domestic violence and the court’s commitment to addressing such crimes with appropriate severity. The ruling affirms the legal significance of demonstrating premeditation in violent crimes and the court's role in upholding stricter sentencing guidelines to deter future domestic violence offenses.
