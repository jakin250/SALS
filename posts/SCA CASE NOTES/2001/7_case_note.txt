# FIRAC Case Note on Desmond James Thompson v. South African Broadcasting Corporation

## F – Facts:
The case reports on the procedural history of an appeal concerning costs related to damages awarded by the Cape High Court to the South African Broadcasting Corporation (SABC), the respondent ([1]). The initial ruling mandated that Desmond James Thompson, the applicant/appellant, pay damages to the SABC and also the costs of the trial ([1]). Subsequently, on 29 November 2000, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) allowed Thompson's appeal, setting aside the lower court's ruling and replacing it with an order of absolution from the instance; however, it did not award any costs to Thompson, requiring him to bear his own trial costs ([1]). Unhappy with this decision, Thompson filed an application for reconsideration concerning the costs order, arguing that since the costs were not previously debated during the appeal, the original order should be viewed as provisional and open to reconsideration ([2]). The SCA received comprehensive written submissions from both parties and considered the merits of Thompson's arguments ([2]).

## I – Issues:
The primary legal question revolves around the validity and justification of the costs order made by the SCA, specifically whether the order to disallow Thomson's costs was appropriate given the circumstances of the case ([2]). This includes considerations of whether the factual findings from the lower court, which labeled Thompson as an "unreliable and dishonest witness," were correctly addressed and if the appellate court had the jurisdiction to amend these findings post-judgment without oral argument ([3]–[7]).

## R – Rules:
The SCA referred to various precedents and legal principles regarding judicial costs orders. The cornerstone ruling cited was Estate Garlick v. Commissioner for Inland Revenue, which established that a costs order could be reconsidered if the issue had not been fully argued during the appeal ([2]). Furthermore, the court referenced the imperative criteria established in Firestone South Africa (Pty) Ltd v. Gentiruco AG concerning the limits of a court’s authority to amend its orders and the distinction between an amendment to a judgment and a correction due to clerical errors ([5]). Additionally, the principle from S v Wells, which permits modifications to judgments as long as the substance remains unchanged, also played a significant role in the SCA's analysis ([5]).

## A – Application:
The SCA rigorously analyzed the arguments presented by Thompson, primarily focusing on the presumption of correctness pertaining to the factual findings made by the trial court ([4]). The appellate court underscored that factual determinations made by trial judges are generally esteemed as accurate unless disproved, which was not successfully accomplished by Thompson’s arguments. It was noted that while the applicant’s appeal did contest the trial court’s findings, he failed to raise concerns specifically pertaining to the costs in his heads of argument, leading the court to conclude that he implicitly accepted those findings ([4]–[7]). 

Thompson asserted that the lack of oral argument concerning the costs indicated an implicit understanding that such an order was provisional and thus amendable ([2]). However, the SCA rejected this interpretation, reaffirming that oral argument supplements written submissions and cannot be used as a basis to entirely reopen matters already concluded ([6]–[7]). 

The Court emphasized that the trial judge’s discretion regarding costs, particularly in light of findings of dishonesty, was correctly exercised; thus, the appellate court was justified in upholding the original cost ruling ([8]). In considering the comprehensive submissions, the SCA concluded that the findings supporting the costs order were fully justified, and no grounds existed for an amendment ([9]).

## C – Conclusion:
The SCA ultimately dismissed Thompson's application for reconsideration of the costs order, reaffirming that the initial decision to disallow costs was valid. The court’s holding clarifies that dissatisfaction with a judgment does not automatically warrant its revisiting, especially when factual findings are accepted without dispute during critical phases of the proceedings ([10]). This case illustrates the importance of thorough engagement with all legal issues in written submissions and the weight courts place on findings of dishonesty in the evaluation of costs. Additionally, the judgment served to affirm the discretionary authority of trial judges in cost matters and the need for parties to adequately present all arguments during the appeal process to avoid adverse orders.
