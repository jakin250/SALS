# Case Note on Similanie v Kuswayo

**Case No:** 613/2010  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Neutral Citation:** Similanie v Kuswayo (631/2010) [2011] ZASCA 79 (27 May 2011)  
**Coram:** Navsa, Cloete, and Malan JJA  

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The case centers on a property transaction involving the mining town of Nababeep, previously owned by the O’Kiep Copper Company, which donated the land to the Namakwa municipality after ceasing operations ([1]). On 17 November 2003, the municipality donated one of the houses to the respondents, Leon and Regina Kuswayo. Subsequently, on 6 November 2006, Mr. and Mrs. Similanie (the appellants) purchased this house for R50,000 with a R15,000 deposit ([1]).

At the time of the sale, the property was still under the respondents' name, having been registered only on 22 May 2007. A restrictive condition against alienation was attached to the title deed, which prohibited the sale of the property within five years from the date of donation ([1]). The respondents claimed that they were unaware of this condition during the sale agreement and offered to return the deposit to the appellants, asserting the agreement's invalidity due to the title's restrictive condition ([2]).

The appellants argued that the Namakwa municipality, which benefitted from this condition, had waived its rights, citing an affidavit from Ms. Jolene Faro of the municipality, which indicated that the municipality chose not to exercise its right of pre-emption ([2][10]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues arising in this case include:
1. Whether the condition in the title deed constituted a valid ground for declaring the sale agreement void.
2. The validity of the waiver alleged by the appellants and whether it effectively negated the restrictive condition against alienation.

These questions necessitate an examination of the interplay between contractual obligations and statutory prohibitions associated with property rights ([5-6]).

### R – Rules
The court relied upon various legal principles, including:
- The notion that a prohibition against alienation, if registered against the title deed, can render a sale void, as held in *Strauss v De Villiers* 1981 (2) SA 163 (NC) ([6]).
- Distinction between the validity of an agreement (the legal capacity to contract) and its enforceability ([6]).
- The enforceability of a waiver of a condition, where the waiver can occur informally, particularly if a condition is for the benefit of a specific party—in this case, the municipality ([9]).

Additionally, reference was made to the legal principle allowing for waiving conditions that constitute an encumbrance on property, as long as the waiver has been effectively communicated ([10]).

### A – Application
In applying the law, the court analyzed the conditions surrounding the sale agreement between the appellants and the respondents. The key considerations included:
- Both parties were unaware of the restrictive condition when entering into the sale agreement, thereby raising questions regarding the validity of their contractual obligations ([6]).
- The court acknowledged that while the title deed included a prohibitive condition, this condition was imposed for the benefit of the municipality. As such, the municipality had the authority to waive it ([10]).
- The court found that the letter from Ms. Faro clearly illustrated the municipality's intent to waive its rights, explicitly stating that the conditions would fall away, despite the absence of a formal council resolution ([11]).

The court rejected the respondents’ claims that the waiver was not retrospective and not proven without a formal resolution. It emphasized that the waiver's retrospective effect was immaterial and that any objections should have been raised proactively by the respondents in their answering affidavit ([11]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, ruling in favor of the appellants and finding that the agreement of sale between them and the respondents was indeed valid. The court clarified that the condition against sale was waivable, and such a waiver had effectually been executed by the Namakwa municipality ([12]).

The decision reinforced the principle that a registered condition intended for the benefit of a municipal authority could be waived, supporting the notion that both contractual integrity and property rights could coexist where municipal conditions are concerned. This ruling has significant implications for real estate transactions involving municipal regulations and may influence future cases regarding property rights and alienation conditions ([12]).

In summary, the judgment in *Similanie v Kuswayo* clarifies the enforceability of property sales under the influence of municipal regulations, highlighting the importance of awareness and the ability of municipalities to relinquish their rights tacitly.
