Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: Bouwer and Another NNO v Master of the High Court, Pretoria [2023] ZASCA 135

**F – Facts**  
The case at hand concerns an appeal filed by Willem Francois Bouwer and Annali Christelle Basson, acting as co-curators bonis for Ms Johanna Helena Josina van Dyk, against the Master of the High Court, Pretoria ([1]–[2]). A court order issued on 8 June 2018 found the patient incapable of managing her affairs due to unsound mind. Appellants were tasked with the patient's estate management under the Administration of Estates Act 66 of 1965 (the Act). 

On 1 July 2019, the first curator submitted an annual curator’s account detailing the income for the period of 2018/2019, which included assets realised from the patient’s estate: an Absa current account and funds obtained from the sale of a vehicle and debt collection ([5]–[6]). Total income reported was approximately R1.3 million, with fees calculated based on a 6% remuneration tariff applying to all income collected. 

The respondent contended that certain realised assets should not be considered income and insisted on recalculating fees based on a lower capital figure, leading to a disputed impasse between the curators and the Master ([6]).

**I – Issues**  
The primary legal issues revolve around the proper categorization of realised capital assets and the applicability of the remuneration tariff for curators to those assets ([7]–[8]). Specifically, the court needed to address:

1. Whether the proceeds from realised capital assets should be classified as income in the curator’s account.
2. Whether a curator is entitled to a 6% fee on these proceeds or only on income generated subsequently through investment of the realised assets ([8]).

**R – Rules**  
The relevant legal framework is composed of the Administration of Estates Act 66 of 1965, particularly sections 83 and 84; and regulations 7 and 8 promulgated under the Act ([1]–[3]). These provisions outline:

- **Section 83** mandates curators to lodge complete accounts annually, detailing administration and separating income from capital ([3]).
- **Section 84** provides for remuneration that could either be defined by any existing will or according to a prescribed tariff if no remuneration is specified; utilizing the pragmatic tariff for curators who fulfill their duties ([12]).
- **Regulation 7** outlines the format of accounts curators must follow, specifically requiring that realised capital be recorded under the income section after being converted to currency and confirming the need for a separate capital account ([4]).

**A – Application**  
The court evaluated whether the Respondent's instruction to categorize the proceeds from capital assets as capital rather than income contradicted the provisions outlined in the regulations. It determined that **once capital assets are realised, they lose their identity as such and must be reported as income** ([10]).

The court articulated that the regulations’ requirements are mandatory and lack any provision for a ‘third category’ of asset classification as advocated by the Respondent ([10]). Furthermore, the court reinforced the understanding that following the realisation, these assets no longer exist as capital assets, and thus should be reflected under the income heading. 

Regarding the remuneration issue, the court concurred with the appellants that the entity of the 6% tariff applies to all income collected in the annual curator’s account, irrespective of its previous status as a capital asset ([15]–[16]). The court rejected the respondent's position that only income from the eventual investment of realised capital assets should attract fees. The court clarified that denying full remuneration would effectively underpay curators for their essential duties and would contradict legislative intentions, as the structure was designed to fairly compensate those overseeing a patient's estate([16]).

**C – Conclusion**  
In conclusion, the appeal was upheld with costs being awarded to the appellants, which included the engagement of two counsel where applicable ([25]). The court declared that:

1. The proceeds from the Absa current account, the vehicle's sale, and debts collected are correctly classified as income ([25]).
2. Curators are entitled to a fee of 6% on all funds reflected under the annual curator’s income account, regardless of original asset status ([25]).

This ruling reinforced the principles associated with estate management and clarified the requirements for financial reporting by curators, ensuring that the roles and compensations align with legislation. The decision ultimately serves to protect the integrity of the administration of estates in cases of individuals declared incapable of managing their own affairs.
