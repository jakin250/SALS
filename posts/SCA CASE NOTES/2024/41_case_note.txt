Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# Case Note: Makwakwa and Others v Minister of State Security 

**Neutral Citation:** Makwakwa and Others v Minister of State Security (1316/2022) [2024] ZASCA 41 (5 April 2024).

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–8])

The appellants, Thabo Makwakwa, Independent Media (Pty) Ltd, and Independent Online SA (Pty) Ltd, appealed against a High Court decision that confirmed an interim interdict prohibiting them from publishing an intelligence report compiled by the State Security Agency (SSA). The report, which Mr. Makwakwa obtained unlawfully, purportedly detailed the United States’ interest in the political dynamics of the African National Congress (ANC). 

On December 22, 2021, the Minister of State Security, represented by the Deputy Minister, initiated ex parte proceedings at the High Court to impose an urgent interdict against the appellants without prior notice. The High Court initially granted the interdict asserting that Makwakwa had unlawfully obtained and was in possession of the report, which could harm national security.

A return date was set for February 24, 2022, for the appellants to show cause why the interim interdict should not be made permanent. The Minister later sought confirmation of the interdict, supported by an affidavit from the Deputy Director-General of the SSA.

In response, the appellants contended that the report was not pertinent to national security but rather revealed improper involvement of the SSA in the ANC's factional disputes. The High Court ultimately confirmed the interim interdict on the grounds that the report’s classification warranted protection, thus sustaining the interdict pending the appeal.

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 18–19])

1. Did the Minister of State Security observe requisite good faith in the ex parte application?
2. Was the classification of the report decisive in establishing its non-disclosure?
3. Did the content of the report merit protection from publication due to national security concerns?

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 38–43])

The case draws upon established principles regarding ex parte applications articulated in *Schlesinger v Schlesinger* and restated in cases like *Phillips v National Director of Public Prosecutions*. The requirement for full disclosure of all material facts in ex parte hearings is paramount; any non-disclosure could lead to rescission of the order. Additionally, the constitutional provisions regarding open justice dictate that courts must scrutinize classified documents to assert whether they warrant protection from disclosure to the public (*Independent Newspapers v Minister for Intelligence Services*). 

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 18–49])

The Supreme Court of Appeal articulated multiple fundamental issues surrounding the initial decision made by the High Court. In scrutinizing whether the Minister acted in good faith, it was evident that crucial material facts were not disclosed, particularly regarding the classification of the report. The testimony suggesting that the report was "Top Secret", rather than "Secret", constituted a significant misstatement that undermined the basis of the ex parte application ([paras 22–24]).

The Court outlined that the lower court's assertion that the report’s classification prevailed and denied its jurisdiction to examine the report further misapplied the law. Relying heavily on the *Masetlha* precedent, the Court underscored that just because a document is classified does not automatically preclude judicial scrutiny or adjudication regarding its content and its potential public disclosure ([paras 38–42]). 

In concluding, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that the Minister had failed to meet the burden of proof regarding national security risks associated with the report’s publication. The contents of the report, centered around foreign interest in ANC politics, did not disclose sensitive information that could jeopardize state security, leading to the ultimate decision to discharge the interim interdict as the appellants had made a compelling case against its classification ([paras 47–49]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 49–50])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, concluding that the High Court erred in confirming the interim interdict due to failure to properly scrutinize the classification and the misleading nature of the evidence presented during the ex parte proceedings. The Court further emphasized the necessity of full and honest disclosure in ex parte applications. 

The implications of this judgment underscore the balance between state security interests and the public's right to information, particularly in democratic societies where transparency is critical. The decision reiterates the judiciary's role in ensuring that executive claims of national security are substantiated and that mere classification cannot abdicate the court's oversight function in assessing restrictions imposed on free expression and publication. 

**Order:** 
1. The appeal is upheld with costs, including the costs of two counsel.
2. The interim interdict granted on December 22, 2021, is discharged with costs.
