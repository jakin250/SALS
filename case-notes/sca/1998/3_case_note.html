Case Title: Bradley Cecil Jasmin v The State Cases Nos: 89/92, 87/92, 83/92 Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa Coram: Eksteen, Howie et Scott JJA

Supreme Court of Appeal 1998

Case Title: Bradley Cecil Jasmin v The State

Cases Nos: 89/92, 87/92, 83/92

Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

Coram: Eksteen, Howie et Scott JJA

F – Facts

The appellants were charged with a series of felonies stemming from events that transpired on January 18 and 19, 1990, including two counts of murder (Pillay and Malangathi), multiple counts of robbery, theft, and unlawful possession of a firearm ([paras 2–10]). Specifically, the charges arose after two bank robberies were committed, first at the Permanent Building Society in Tongaat, followed by another at the Natal Building Society in Port Shepstone, where a shot was fired during the robbery ([paras 2–4]).

The sequence of events culminated in the murder of two traffic inspectors who attempted to apprehend the appellants ([para 6]). Evidence included witness descriptions of the robbers, multiple police reports, and the recovery of firearms linked to the victims, ultimately leading to the appellants' arrests ([paras 7–10]).

In total, the first appellant was convicted on counts including robbery and murder, while the second and third appellants faced similar charges with additional convictions for theft ([paras 10–12]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions concerned the admissibility of the appellants' statements made to a magistrate, the sufficiency of the evidence linking the appellants to the crimes, and the appropriateness of the sentences imposed ([para 13]). The court also had to determine the credibility of an accomplice's testimony against the appellants and whether their convictions were substantiated by the weight of the evidence ([paras 30–34]).

R – Rules

The court relied on legal principles surrounding accomplice testimony, which require that such evidence be approached with caution, particularly given the potential for bias ([paras 14–30]). The necessity for corroboration in this context was underscored by the need for such evidence to be consistent and credible ([paras 30–33]). Furthermore, the rights to a fair trial as enshrined in the Constitution were also pertinent, impacting the admissibility of statements and the consideration of judicial precedent regarding capital punishment, especially since the death penalty was later found unconstitutional by the Constitutional Court ([paras 36–37]).

A – Application

The court evaluated the evidence against the appellants, focusing on the testimony of accomplice Rochelle Koopman, whose account illustrated the detailed orchestration of the criminal activities ([paras 16–24]). While Koopman’s reliability was scrutinized, the court found her testimony consistent with other evidential elements, including fingerprints and the recovery of vehicles and firearms ([paras 30–34]).

The court considered the appellants' narratives, which were contradicted by Koopman’s insights and the physical evidence. It concluded that a reasonable jury could find the appellants guilty based on the evidence provided ([paras 32–36]).

Regarding the death sentences, which were challenged, the court noted the invalidation of the death penalty in South Africa, necessitating a remittal for re-sentencing ([para 37]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the convictions of all three appellants based on the substantial evidence linking them to the crimes committed. However, the court ruled against the death sentences, which were set aside due to their inconsistency with the current constitutional framework. The matter was remitted for the imposition of competent sentences for the murder convictions ([paras 37–37]).

This case underlines significant issues regarding the admissibility of statements, the reliability of accomplice evidence, and the evolving landscape of capital punishment in South Africa, reinforcing the necessity for judicial systems to adapt to constitutional changes.
