Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title: Sim Road Investments CC v Morgan Air Cargo (Pty) Ltd**  
**Neutral Citation:** [2010] ZASCA 081 (27 May 2011)  
**Case No:** 024/10  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Coram:** Harms DP, Lewis, Bosielo, Seriti JJA and Petse AJA  
**Heard:** 05 May 2011  
**Delivered:** 27 May 2011  

## F – Facts

The appellant, **Sim Road Investments CC** ("Sim Road"), owned an agricultural holding known as plot 35 Pomona Estates in Kempton Park. The respondent, **Morgan Air Cargo (Pty) Ltd** ("Morgan Air"), purchased this property at an auction conducted by the auctioneers, Venditor, on **17 May 2006**. Morgan Air paid a deposit of **R200,000** and an auctioneer's commission of **R182,400** ([para 1]).

Morgan Air later sought cancellation of the agreement and a refund based on claims of **fraudulent misrepresentation** by Sim Road and, alternatively, on the basis of **mistake** ([para 1]). The **North Gauteng High Court**, presided by Murphy J, ruled in favour of Morgan Air, leading Sim Road to appeal ([para 1]).

The auction was promoted through advertisements incorrectly labeling the property as commercial rather than agricultural. The title deed stipulated that the land could exclusively be used for agricultural purposes ([paras 3-4]). Mr. Morgan, a director of Morgan Air, was attracted to the property by its advertised designation for light industrial use, which was necessary for his business ([para 5]). 

Despite this, the auctioneer failed to inform Morgan that the property was agricultural prior to the auction process. Following the auction, upon discovering the true zoning of the land, Morgan Air sought to rescind the contract ([paras 7-19]). Sim Road and the auctioneer, however, refused to accept the cancellation, defending the validity of the contract based on standard clauses stating that the property was sold **voetstoots** (as is) and that the purchaser acknowledged reliance solely on their judgement and not on any representations made ([paras 8-9]).

## I – Issues

The case presented significant legal issues regarding:

1. **Fraudulent Misrepresentation:** Whether the misrepresentation regarding the property’s zoning constitutes fraudulent misrepresentation actionable by Morgan Air ([para 1]).
2. **Mistake:** The legal implications of unilateral mistake as it pertains to the constitutionality of the contract and its rescission ([paras 20-21]).
3. **Effect of Exclusion Clauses:** The implications of the voetstoots clause and additional disclaimers in the contract on the nature of liability stemming from misrepresentations ([paras 22-23]).

## R – Rules

The relevant legal rules applied include:

1. **The Law of Misrepresentation:** A representation that induces a party to enter into a contract can void that contract if the representation is fraudulent, negligent, or innocent, provided it is material ([para 22]).
2. **Provisions of Voetstoots Clause:** A voetstoots clause can protect a seller from claims related to misrepresentations; however, it does not exempt a party from liability for fraudulent conduct ([para 24]).
3. **Concept of Unilateral Mistake:** The law recognizes that a unilateral (or one-sided) mistake, particularly regarding a material term, can render a contract voidable. However, in cases of fraud, the affected party may rescind the contract without the need to prove a unilateral mistake ([para 21-25]).

## A – Application

The court applied these principles notably in evaluating the actions of Sim Road and its agents. 

### Finding of Fraud
The court emphasized that **Sim Road** knew the advertisement was misleading and that its purpose was to lure a buyer into a purchase that was not consistent with the agricultural zoning of the property ([paras 16-26]). It established that the misrepresentation was both material and directly correlated to the inducement of Morgan Air’s purchase decision.

### Examination of Evidence
Despite defendants' claims that statements made by the auctioneer had clarified the zoning before the auction began, no credible testimony from the auctioneer was provided, which raised doubts about the validity of this defense ([paras 18-19]). The absence of affirmative evidence supporting a correction of the advertisement was crucial in sustaining Morgan Air's claims of being misled.

### Conclusion on Mistake
Although the lower court ruled based on a unilateral mistake, the Supreme Court determined that the fundamental issue was malicious misrepresentation by Sim Road, which effectively voided the exclusionary clauses that would typically govern liability and accountability for errors ([paras 20-24]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal by Sim Road, upholding the lower court's order for repayment to Morgan Air based on fraudulent misrepresentation ([para 26]). The ruling reinforces critical legal precedents that fraudulent conduct cannot shield a party from liability in a contractual context, regardless of any disclaimers or exclusion clauses. This judgement clarifies the interplay between misrepresentation and economic consequence, advocating for fairness in the contractual arrangements surrounding property transactions. 

In essence, the decision illustrates the court's commitment to ensuring that deceptive practices do not compromise the integrity of contractual obligations, particularly in real estate dealings. The implications of this ruling extend beyond the parties involved, contributing significantly to case law regarding misrepresentation and contractual fairness in South Africa.
