# Case Note: FIRAC Method - Total Support Management (Pty) Ltd v Diversified Health Systems (South Africa) (Pty) Ltd

## F – Facts 
The case revolves around an arbitration dispute related to a written agreement concluded on 25 October 1995 between Interpharm Integrated Pharmaceutical Benefit Management (Pty) Ltd and Total Support Management (Pty) Ltd (TSM), alongside Mr. G W Slabbert, as sellers, and Diversified Health Systems (South Africa) (Pty) Ltd (DHS) as the purchaser ([1]–[2]). Following the agreement, Interpharm ceded its claims against DHS to TSM ([1]). The agreement contained an arbitration clause (clause 30), stipulating that disputes would be resolved by an agreed-upon arbitrator or one appointed by the President of the Law Society of the Transvaal if the parties could not agree ([2]).

A dispute arose regarding TSM’s liability for certain payments (totaling about R20 million), leading to an arbitration proceeding. A senior attorney, Mr. P E B Reynolds, was appointed as the arbitrator since no agreement was reached on a mutual choice ([3]–[4]). The arbitration took place in September 1999, resulting in a judgment delivered on 21 October 1999, dismissing both parties' claims and imposing costs against them ([4]).

Following the arbitration, TSM and Slabbert filed an application to the Transvaal Provincial Division of the High Court to set aside the arbitrator’s award under section 33(1) of the Arbitration Act 42 of 1965 ([5]). The High Court dismissed the application with costs, granting leave to appeal ([5]).

The core issues during the arbitration included the fulfillment of payment conditions under the agreement: TSM’s entitlement to R20 million and related interest ([9]–[10]). DHS counterclaimed on various grounds, including overpayments made under a mistaken belief ([10]). Ultimately, the arbitrator dismissed all claims and counterclaims, including allegations of conduct that could constitute misconduct under the Act ([11]).

## I – Issues 
The central issues presented for review were:
1. Whether the arbitrator, Mr. Reynolds, committed misconduct by dismissing the appellants' claims based on errors and negligence ([12]).
2. Whether the role of Milo, the arbitrator's assistant, represented misconduct or a gross irregularity, which would invalidate the arbitration award ([12]).

## R – Rules 
The pertinent legal provisions include:
- **Section 33(1) of the Arbitration Act 42 of 1965**: This allows for the setting aside of an arbitration award on the grounds of an arbitrator's misconduct or gross irregularity in proceedings ([14]).
- **Judicial Precedents**: The court relied heavily on precedent, particularly *Dickenson & Brown v Fisher's Executors* where misconduct requires evidence of "wrongful or improper conduct" on the part of the arbitrator ([15]). Subsequent rulings affirm that bona fide mistakes by an arbitrator do not constitute misconduct ([17]–[21]).

- **Constitutional Principles**: While the appellants attempted to invoke section 33(1) of the Constitution relating to administrative action, the court distinguished arbitration, which arises through private consent rather than public authority ([22]–[25]).

## A – Application 
The court evaluated the appellants' claims against these standards. On the first ground of review, the appellants presented several points suggesting that the arbitrator had erred in determining facts crucial to the set-off from DHS. However, the court found that the arbitrator had conducted a thorough evaluation of evidence and that any mistakes made did not rise to the level of misconduct. In particular, it emphasized the established legal principle that errors, even gross ones, are not sufficient to demonstrate misconduct unless indicative of bad faith or dishonesty ([29]–[36]).

On the second ground, concerning the alleged overreach of Milo, the court concluded that while Milo had played a significant role in drafting the original award, the actions were not indicative of a usurpation of authority. The second respondent maintained control over the arbitration proceedings and did not abdicate his duties in favor of the assistant. The alleged complexities and the agreement to involve an assistant did not amount to a violation of the core responsibilities of an arbitrator ([37]–[47]).

## C – Conclusion 
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the findings of the arbitrator and the High Court. The court concluded that:
1. No misconduct or gross irregularity was found in the arbitrator's conduct.
2. The errors identified did not rise to the level that would render the arbitration award void under section 33(1) of the Arbitration Act.

The judgment reinforced the concept that arbitration serves as a private law process governed by the parties’ agreement rather than public administrative norms. Consequently, the implications extend to the understanding of arbitration’s autonomy and the limited grounds for judicial interference, establishing critical precedents regarding the integrity and autonomy of arbitral awards ([48]–[49]). 

This ruling has broader implications for the arbitration landscape within South Africa, emphasizing the importance of adhering strictly to established legal principles while ensuring arbitrators maintain their independence without undue influence from assistants or other parties ([48]–[49]).
