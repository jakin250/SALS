Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2007

### Case Note: Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service v Trend Finance (Pty) Ltd [2007] SCA 105 (RSA)

**FIRAC Method**

---

#### F – Facts
[1] In this case, the Supreme Court of Appeal addressed a provisional order concerning costs related to an appeal between the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (the Appellant) and two respondents: Trend Finance (Pty) Ltd and Trend Gear Enterprises (Pty) Ltd. The matter centered on the Commissioner’s obligation to repay amounts paid by Trend concerning two consignments and an order requiring the Commissioner to bear the costs of the proceedings in the court a quo. The court had previously issued a provisional costs order, directing Trend to pay half of the Commissioner’s costs, while dismissing Trend’s cross-appeal with costs.

[2] Trend’s counsel conceded that the cross-appeal was unsuccessful and acknowledged the correctness of the costs awarded to the Commissioner; however, he sought a more favorable costs order regarding the primary appeal. Conversely, counsel for the Commissioner advocated for the finalization of the provisional order. The appeal included two principal issues: the Commissioner’s liability to refund amounts for two consignments and the directive for the Commissioner to pay Trend’s costs at the lower court. The Commissioner had mixed success, failing on the refund issue but prevailing on the trial costs.

#### I – Issues
[3] The core legal questions involved:
- Whether the Commissioner should be held responsible for repaying amounts associated with the two consignments in question.
- How the court should adjudicate costs given the distinct outcomes in the two severable issues of the appeal (as noted in [3]).

[4] Additionally, there was a procedural query on how costs should be apportioned given the mixed success of the parties (where the Commissioner won on costs but lost on the repayment issue).

#### R – Rules
[5] The legal principles governing the award of costs are paramount in this case. The court referred to precedents that establish the guidelines for awarding costs regarding distinct or severable issues. Notably, in **Protea Assurance Co Ltd v Matinise** [1978] (1) SA 963 (A) at 978A-C, it was asserted that where a party succeeds on separate issues, the court may opt for partial costs orders reflective of that party's level of success.

[6] Furthermore, in cases where both parties achieve relative success, as laid out in the judgment, it may be equitable to have each party bear its own costs or make equal contributions toward the other's costs ([5]).

#### A – Application
[7] In applying these principles, the court undertook a thorough examination of the issues at hand. It acknowledged that the balance of success favored Trend concerning the arguments about the refund of the amounts associated with the consignments, especially since the sum in question (R900,000) was significant and the time expended on oral and written arguments reflected the importance of this matter. 

[8] The court asserted that the initial provisional order inadequately recognized Trend’s success on the salient issue of retaining the funds. The concessions made by Trend were contingent upon the outcome regarding the Commissioner’s unconditional victory on the issue of costs for the trial, which ultimately shaped the course of costs awarded in the appeal ([6]).

[9] As the Commissioner had only succeeded in one aspect of the appeal—namely, the cost issue—the court deemed it appropriate to adjust the provisional costs order. The ultimately restorative decision reflected that the Commissioner, despite his partial success, should bear three-quarters of Trend’s costs in the appeal to align with the principles of fairness and proportionality established in prior jurisprudence.

#### C – Conclusion
[10] The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the provisional order concerning costs was inadequate given the specific circumstances of the case and Trend's success in resisting the majority of the Commissioner’s appeal. The court modified the order so that the Commissioner was to pay three-quarters of Trend’s costs of the appeal, along with the costs of Trend’s application for variation of the provisional order ([8]).

[11] This judgment elucidates the court’s approach to cost awards in mixed outcomes, reinforcing the principle that success should attract costs in a manner that reflects the true balance of victory between the parties, which serves both fairness and judicial efficiency ([7]).

This case note serves not only as a summary of the ruling but also encapsulates the court's reasoning on the pertinent legal principles governing costs in civil appeals. The decision affirms the court's commitment to ensuring equitable outcomes while navigating the nuances of complex litigations involving distinct yet interconnected legal issues.
