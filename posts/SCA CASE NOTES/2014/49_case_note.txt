Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: Murabi v Murabi (893/12) [2014] ZASCA 49

## F – Facts

In *Murabi v Murabi*, the Court addressed the validity of marriages between the appellant, T[…] M[…] M[…], and the deceased R[…] R[…] G[…] M[…], and the first respondent, N[…] C[…] M[…], against a backdrop of complex customary and civil marriage laws ([paras 1–2]). The appellant claimed that she entered into a customary marriage with the deceased on 1 November 1970, which was subsequently registered in 1991. Meanwhile, the first respondent argued that her civil marriage to the deceased on 2 August 1995 was valid and that the appellant had never married the deceased ([paras 4–5]).

The appellant's assertions were founded on the contention that a *lumalo* had been paid to her family, whereas the first respondent claimed that no such payment had occurred and raised questions about the legitimacy of the appellant’s marriage status at the time. The appellant also admitted to being married to another man, F[…] R[…], when the deceased married the first respondent, complicating the matter further ([paras 6–8]).

The initial ruling by the Limpopo High Court dismissed the appellant's claims, deciding that she had not sufficiently proven her customary marriage and declaring the first respondent the only legitimate spouse ([para 9]). On appeal, matters focused on whether the customary marriage was valid and if the first respondent’s civil marriage could coexist with it ([paras 12–13]).

## I – Issues

The primary issues central to this case included: 

1. **Validity of the Customary Marriage**: Was the appellant lawfully married to the deceased in accordance with customary law principles and whether the registration of that marriage provides sufficient proof of its existence ([paras 1, 10])?
   
2. **Validity of the Civil Marriage**: Is the civil marriage between the deceased and the first respondent valid given the appellant's claims of having a customary marriage with the deceased ([paras 1, 10])?

## R – Rules

The case was primarily governed by several legal statutes and previous court decisions:

- **Recognition of Customary Marriages Act 120 of 1998**: Section 2(1) offers that a marriage recognized at customary law is deemed valid, whereas section 4(8) indicates that a certificate issued under this Act constitutes prima facie proof of a customary marriage ([para 10]).
  
- **Black Administration Act 38 of 1927**: Section 22(1) places restrictions on contracting a marriage while engaged in a subsisting customary union ([para 10]). 

- **Marriage and Matrimonial Property Law Amendment Act 3 of 1988**: Section 1 specifies that a man cannot contract a civil marriage if he is already a partner in a customary union with another woman ([paras 10, 17]).

The Court also referenced the case of *Road Accident Fund v Mongalo; Nkabinde v Road Accident Fund* to underline the significance of fraud in questioning the reliability of legal documents ([para 14]).

## A – Application

The Court undertook a careful assessment of the procedural facts and statutory provisions. The appellant's argument rested heavily on the assumption that her registered customary marriage provided undeniable proof of its validity. The court found that in absence of compelling evidence claiming fraud, the registration certificate indeed constituted prima facie evidence of the marriage ([paras 13–14]).

In evaluating the validity of the civil marriage to the first respondent, the Court determined that since the appellant's customary marriage was deemed valid, the deceased could not contract a civil marriage under section 1(1) of the Marriage and Matrimonial Property Law Amendment Act. This finding aligned with previous judgments that stated marriages contracted in violation of existing unions are void ([para 17]).

Moreover, the previous High Court's assertion that the appellant approached the court with "unclean hands" was addressed critically. The appeal demonstrated that while the appellant had not disclosed all facts about her marriage background, the first respondent did not adequately substantiate their claims about the fraudulence of the appellant's registration ([para 11, 19]).

## C – Conclusion

The *Supreme Court of Appeal* ultimately upheld the appeal, declaring the customary marriage between the appellant and the deceased valid and the civil marriage with the first respondent null and void. The ruling emphasized that a man cannot simultaneously partake in a civil marriage while engaged in a subsisting customary union, reflecting not only legal standards but also customary law principles ([paras 20–21]).

The implications of this case underscore the intersection of customary law and statutory regulations in South African marital law. The decision established clear precedence around the legitimacy of marriages based on registration and the structure of polygamous contexts within customary frameworks, including necessary declarations required for civil marriages to be legally binding when customary unions exist. Thus, this ruling serves to reinforce the integrity of existing customary unions against the risks posed by challenges through civil marriages, thereby bolstering the legal protections afforded to customary marriages within South African law.
