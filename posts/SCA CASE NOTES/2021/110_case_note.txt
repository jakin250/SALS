Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# FIRAC Case Note: The MEC for Health & Social Development, Gauteng v TM obo MM

**Neutral Citation:** The MEC for Health & Social Development, Gauteng v TM obo MM (380/2019) [2021] ZASCA 110

---

## F - Facts
The appellant, the Member of the Executive Council for Health and Social Development of the Gauteng Provincial Government (MEC), was involved in a delictual action instituted by the respondent, TM, representing her minor child, MM, who suffered acute profound hypoxic ischemia during birth at Charlotte Maxeke Johannesburg Academic Hospital (CMH) on 28 August 2010. The MEC was deemed liable by the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which made an order for damages. The MEC subsequently appealed this decision.

Ms TM alleged that the hospital staff failed in their duty of care by not performing a timely caesarean section (C-section) and in various respects including inadequate staffing, management of resources, failure to have a second operative theatre available, and the absence of interim measures to assist the foetus's condition ([1]-[2], [6]).

The trial revealed numerous periods of downtime wherein the theatre at CMH was unused, and the C-section for TM was only performed over two hours after the doctors decided it was necessary ([12], [21]). The key decision at trial turned on whether this delay and the mismanagement of theatre resources constituted negligence ([10], [56]). 

The appeal issues revolved around the alleged negligence concerning the promptness of medical treatment, the adequacy of hospital resources, and decisions made regarding admissions and prioritizations of patients throughout the day ([6]-[19], [41]). 

## I - Issues
The legal questions at stake included:
1. Whether the MEC could be held liable for negligent conduct that occurred prior to Ms TM's admission to CMH.
2. Whether the alleged sub-optimal management of operating theatre resources constituted negligence.
3. The determination of appropriate triage in decision-making during the allocation of theatre resources when multiple patients required C-sections ([6], [17]).

## R - Rules
The court applied established principles of delictual liability, focusing on the existence of a legal duty of care owed by the MEC to Ms TM once she was admitted to the hospital ([18], [41]). The necessary criteria for negligence included:
- The establishment of a duty of care, grounded in the obligation to provide adequate medical services ([42]).
- Contributory negligence was not applicable here as the respondent was claiming on behalf of her child, MM.
- The parameters for reasonable medical practice concerning the timing and implementation of C-sections, referencing local and international guidelines that recommend a performance window of under one hour once a decision has been made ([34], [50]).

Legal precedents such as *AN v MEC for Health, Eastern Cape* and *Soobramoney v Minister of Health (Kwazulu-Natal)* were referenced to frame the duties and standards expected within the context of public healthcare obligations ([17], [22], [36]).

## A - Application
The court primarily focused on three grounds of appeal concerning alleged negligence. 

1. **Duty of Care Prior to Admission**: The court held that the duty of care arose when TM was admitted to the hospital, ruling out potential negligence claims prior to arrival ([19]).

2. **Failure to Use Theatre Efficiently**: The MEC's failure to utilize the single theatre effectively within outlined downtimes did not establish negligence under the circumstances presented. Testimonies indicated that logistical issues, including staffing and equipment availability, hindered operational capacity ([20], [22]).

3. **Triage Decision Making**: The prioritization of surgical operations based on perceived urgency was scrutinized. The court acknowledged that while the regulation of admitting patients is crucial, decisions made by medical staff—particularly junior doctors—must be considered within the broader context of medical standards and liabilities ([28], [30]).

Ultimately, the court noted that TM's treatment did not meet the standard of care expected, particularly with the delays in administering substantive medical attention. However, it ruled that there was no definitive causal link between the hospital’s alleged negligence and MM's injuries ([50]-[56]).

## C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the MEC, concluding that the respondent had failed to meet the burden of proof in demonstrating negligence on the part of the hospital staff and that even if negligence were present, it had not been proven that the injury sustained by MM would have been averted had proper protocols been followed. Therefore, the high court's ruling was set aside, and the plaintiff's claim was dismissed with costs awarded to the appellant ([50], [128]).

This decision reinforces the complexities surrounding claims of medical negligence in public healthcare settings and emphasizes the importance of establishing causation in addition to proving that negligence occurred. The implications of this case highlight the nuances of operational management in healthcare contexts, particularly under resource constraints ([6], [41]).
