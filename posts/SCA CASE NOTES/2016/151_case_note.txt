Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Snyers v Mgro Properties (Pty) Ltd

**Neutral Citation:** Snyers v Mgro Properties (Pty) Ltd (20816/2014) ZASCA 151 (30 September 2016)  
**Coram:** Mhlantla, Leach, Willis, Zondi and Mathopo JJA  
**Heard:** 19 November 2015  
**Delivered:** 30 September 2016  

## F – Facts

This case arises from an eviction dispute concerning Mr. Abraham Snyers (first appellant) and his family from a farm owned by Mgro Properties (Pty) Ltd (first respondent) and Mouton Citrus (Pty) Ltd (second respondent). Snyers had been a farm laborer since November 1981 and was later promoted to senior foreman. His tenure on the farm was linked to his employment, meaning it would terminate if his employment ended ([2]).

In November 2010, after Mgro Properties and Mouton Citrus acquired the farm, Snyers entered into a new employment agreement. This agreement reiterated that his right to reside on the farm depended on his employment status ([3]). Following a turbulent relations and issues with management, Snyers resigned on 17 December 2010, which he asserted was under duress due to misrepresentations regarding his pension ([4]). 

On 13 January 2011, Snyers referred a constructive dismissal dispute to the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA) concurrent with his termination of employment ([5]). However, on 7 March 2011, the respondents served a notice of eviction to Snyers, mandating him to vacate the property within two months ([6]). Snyers contested the eviction, claiming he was awaiting the CCMA's determination of his labor dispute, asserting that the eviction notice was invalid while the dispute was pending ([8]).

Despite the CCMA's ruling against him on 1 June 2011, stating it lacked jurisdiction due to a late referral, Snyers refused to vacate the premises. The respondents subsequently escalated the matter by serving eviction notices on his wife, Katrina Snyers ([11]). In the Land Claims Court, the court upheld the eviction, a decision that was appealed by Snyers to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([12]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues presented in this case are:

1. Whether the eviction notices served by the respondents to Snyers were valid given the pending labor dispute before the CCMA.
2. The applicability of the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA) in protecting Snyers' right to residency while his labor dispute was unresolved.
3. The implications of the right to family life under section 6(2)(d) of ESTA concerning the eviction of family members of the laborer ([15]).

## R – Rules

The legal framework includes the following statutes and case law:

### 1. **Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA)**
   - Section 8 deals with the termination of the right of residence, providing that an occupier's right may not be terminated if there is an unresolved labor dispute ([16]).
   - Section 9(2)(a) stipulates that eviction orders can only be made if the right of residence has been terminated in accordance with section 8.
   - Section 6(2)(d) entitles occupiers to family life, stating that eviction of one spouse while the other remains is undesirable ([23]).

### 2. **Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA)**
   - Section 190(1) specifies the date of dismissal and the process for referring disputes concerning unfair dismissal ([9]).

### 3. **Case Law**
   - **Klaase & others v Van der Merwe NO & others** ([1]) established precedent concerning the necessity for disputants to remain equitable and just in eviction proceedings.
   - **Karabo & others v Kok & others** underscored that any termination must be resolved in accordance with the LRA before action can be taken under ESTA ([19]).

## A – Application

In determining the validity of the eviction notices, the court focused on the unresolved labor dispute between Snyers and the respondents. The South African Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that since Snyers' right to residence was anchored in the employment relationship, any eviction proceedings must respect the outcome of the labor dispute.

The Court analyzed section 8(2) and (3) of ESTA, stating that if a labor dispute was pending (in this context, a claim for unfair dismissal), an evictee's right to reside on the property must remain intact until the dispute is resolved. Thus, the notices served on Snyers while his CCMA matter was unresolved were deemed invalid ([18]).

Furthermore, the court reflected on the familial implications of evictions. The protection of family life under section 6(2)(d) of ESTA necessitated a reconsideration of the eviction order against Mrs. Snyers, as it would infringe on her rights to remain with her family while her husband was still on the farm. Citing precedents that emphasized minimizing family separation, the court underscored that an injustice could not be inflicted upon the family ([23]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Snyers’ appeal, ruling that the eviction notices served by the respondents were invalid as they undermined the provisions of ESTA, particularly in light of the ongoing labor dispute. The court also highlighted the importance of family unity, stating that any eviction order against Mrs. Snyers was inappropriate while her husband remained. This case illustrates the necessity for landowners to navigate evictions with regard to labor disputes and family rights, reinforcing the protective measures aimed at vulnerable occupiers in the context of South African housing laws. 

Consequently, the order of the court a quo was set aside, and the application for eviction was dismissed with costs ([24]).
