# Case Note: Le Roux v Zietsman and Another [2023] ZASCA 102

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts 
In July 2011, the respondents, Christiaan Frederik Zietsman and Ester Petronella Zietsman (collectively “the respondents”), purchased a guesthouse in Tzaneen, Limpopo from the appellant, Jan Pieter Le Roux, for R1,300,000 ([2]). Following the transfer of the property on 30 September 2011, the respondents commenced occupation shortly thereafter. A few months into their occupation, heavy rains caused extensive leaking throughout the roof of the guesthouse, leading to significant property damage, including soaked furniture and thus prompting the respondents to obtain loans amounting to R241,281.76 for repairs ([2]–[3]).

The respondents filed a claim in the Regional Court of Limpopo, alleging damages based on fraudulent non-disclosure and misrepresentation related to known latent defects of the roof ([3]). The respondents claimed R241,281.76 for repairs and another R102,725.04 for loss of income incurred during the repair period ([3]). They emphasized that the appellant had assured them that the roof leaks had been repaired but failed to inform them of the true extent of the defects ([4]).

The regional court ruled in favor of the respondents, awarding R167,480.23 for repairs and R68,038 for loss of income, and noted the appellant's duty to disclose such defects originally ([4]).

### I – Issues 
The core legal question before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the appellant, with full knowledge of the latent defects in the guesthouse’s roof and aware of the intended commercial use of the property, fraudulently failed to disclose such defects to the respondents prior to the sale, thus inducing the sale under false pretenses ([8]).

### R – Rules 
The court centered its analysis on the principles governing fraudulent non-disclosure and misrepresentation in the realm of delict. A fraudulent non-disclosure claim can arise where a seller, with knowledge of latent defects, fails to inform the buyer, under circumstances indicating intent to induce the buyer into the purchase ([1]). According to established precedents, the following must be proven for a fraudulent misrepresentation claim:
1. The seller was aware of the defect at the time of sale;
2. The seller deliberately failed to disclose the defect to the purchaser (dolo malo); and 
3. The intent was to induce the purchaser to conclude the sale ([1]).

Case law supports that defects are considered latent if they would not be discoverable upon ordinary inspection ([1]).

### A – Application 
The court analyzed the evidence in conjunction with the legal standards for fraudulent misrepresentation and non-disclosure. The testimonies from the respondents provided credible evidence of their observations regarding leaks prior to the sale, including the assurance from the appellant that the issues had been resolved. The corroborative expert testimony by civil engineer Mr. Dirk Rosslee indicated underlying structural flaws, which contradicted the appellant's claims of a minor and manageable leak ([10]–[13]). 

As established by both the regional and high courts, the inference drawn from the evidence was that the appellant possessed knowledge of the serious defects that would likely have prompted any reasonable seller to disclose such information proactively ([12]–[19]). Notably, the appellant’s assertion that he was unaware of the broader implications of the roof defects was deemed implausible, especially given the extensive and long-standing nature of the repairs that had been attempted ([21]–[22]). Mr. Rosslee's findings directly refuted the appellant's testimony, particularly highlighting that the leaking was a pre-existing, chronic issue rather than one that had developed post-sale ([14]–[19]).

The court concluded that the appellant's claims were insubstantial and disingenuous, particularly in light of his prior occupancy and involvement in maintenance ([20]–[22]). The findings supported the claim of fraudulent misrepresentation as the omissions from the appellant were deemed intentional, aimed at facilitating the sale.

### C – Conclusion 
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately concurred with the lower courts, affirming the ruling that the appellant had indeed fraudulently misrepresented the state of the roof and failed in his duty to disclose significant defects ([22]). The appeal was dismissed with costs, reinforcing the legal standards concerning fraudulent non-disclosure and misrepresentation in property transactions, especially highlighting the seller's affirmative duty to disclose any known defects that could materially affect the buyer’s decision to purchase. The implications of this ruling emphasize the extent of liability sellers hold in property transactions concerning latent defects and reinforce the necessity for disclosure to protect buyers from fraudulent practices ([23]).
