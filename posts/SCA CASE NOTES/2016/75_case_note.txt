Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Registrar of Medical Schemes v Genesis Medical Scheme (238/2015) [2016] ZASCA 75

## F – Facts
This case arose from a dispute regarding the treatment of funds allocated to members' Personal Medical Savings Accounts (PMSAs) in the financial statements of Genesis Medical Scheme (the respondent). The Registrar of Medical Schemes (the first appellant) rejected Genesis Medical Scheme's Annual Financial Statement (AFS) for the year 2012 on the grounds that it inaccurately reported the PMSA funds as part of its own assets rather than as trust property held on behalf of its members ([1]–[3]). The rejection stemmed from prior court interpretations, notably from *Registrar of Medical Schemes v Ledwaba NO (Omnihealth)*, which had established that PMSA funds should be treated as trust property ([4]).

Genesis contested this interpretation, asserting that the Registrar's decision represented an erroneous legal conclusion influenced by the complexities of previous judgments ([6]–[7]). The court a quo agreed with Genesis, viewing the Registrar's rejection as legally flawed, and thus it overturned the decision. The Registrar and the Council for Medical Schemes subsequently appealed this ruling ([2]).

## I – Issues
The principal legal question addressed in this case involved the proper characterization of PMSA funds under South African law—specifically, whether these funds constituted "trust property" in terms of the *Financial Institutions (Protection of Funds) Act 28 of 2001* (FI Act), and how they ought to be reflected in the AFS of medical schemes as delineated by the *Medical Schemes Act 131 of 1998* (MSA) ([2]–[4], [23]). The divergent judicial interpretations from the *Omnihealth* case and its implications for PMSA funds prompted conflict in the lower courts regarding their nature and treatment under relevant legislation ([3]–[8]).

## R – Rules
The relevant statutory frameworks guiding this case include:
1. **Medical Schemes Act 131 of 1998**: Particularly, section 35(9)(c) which states that the amounts credited to members' PMSAs are considered liabilities of a medical scheme. While section 26 prohibits claims on a scheme's assets beyond those related to transactions concerning members ([10], [35]).
2. **Financial Institutions (Protection of Funds) Act 28 of 2001**: outlines guidelines for the management and protection of trust property by financial institutions, defining "trust property" and requiring such funds to be held separately from the institution's assets ([41]).

Judicial precedents, notably the *Omnihealth* verdict, further informed the interpretation of PMSA funds, ruling them as trust property, which led to the implications challenged by Genesis in the current case ([4], [38]).

## A – Application
The court's analysis revolved around the interpretation of statutory provisions regarding PMSAs. The Registrar contended that PMSA funds could not be trust property since they were reported as liabilities within the scheme's financial obligations, requiring a corresponding asset to match each liability—a fundamental principle of accounting ([20], [27]). The registrar's argument relied heavily on the legislative framework that explicitly articulated how contributions are to be managed and the rights of members concerning these funds ([24]).

Chief Justice Cachalia, dissenting, argued that the PMSA funds should be treated as the medical scheme's assets, emphasizing that the MSA does not explicitly categorize PMSA as trust property and highlighted the lack of statutory language to that effect ([35]–[38]). Conversely, Judge Willis, in a concurring judgment, drew on the FI Act’s provisions defining trust property to assert that PMSA funds are indeed trust assets, maintaining that these funds are specifically set aside for members’ future healthcare expenses, thereby justifying their ring-fencing from other assets in the event of insolvency ([55]–[58]).

The core difficulty emerged from reconciling the statutory requirement for medical schemes to maintain sound finances with the assertion that PMSA funds must be accounted for separately as trust property. This paradox drew criticism from professionals in the field of finance and accounting who asserted that attempting to manage PMSA funds as both trust property and scheme assets would create significant operational complications ([28], [67]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately ruled in favor of the Registrar of Medical Schemes, reversing the lower court's decision and upholding the rejection of Genesis Medical Scheme's AFS based on the interpretation of PMSA funds as the scheme's assets and liabilities ([1], [2], [78]). The decision reinforced the interpretation established in *Omnihealth*, indicating that PMSA funds must be reflected in a scheme’s financial records in alignment with their legal classification, thus ensuring that they met statutory requirements for financial accountability and transparency.

The implications of this ruling are profound, potentially impacting the operational frameworks of medical schemes in South Africa, particularly concerning the treatment of members' savings accounts and their protections during financial distress. This case highlights the intricate interaction between constitutional principles of justice and public interest against the backdrop of financial regulation in the healthcare sector.
