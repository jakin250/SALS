Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title: Centre for Child Law v Director of Public Prosecutions, Johannesburg and Others [2022] ZACC 35**

**Coram:** Mhlantla J (unanimous)

**Heard on:** 3 March 2022  
**Decided on:** 29 September 2022  

---

## F – Facts

The case arose from a review application involving four children who tested positive for cannabis during a school-sanctioned drug test. Each child was alleged to have been in possession of cannabis, violating the *Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 140 of 1992* ([para 6]). They were initially brought before the Krugersdorp Magistrates’ Court and agreed to participate in diversion programs. However, their subsequent failure to comply with these programs led to their referral for compulsory residential diversion ([para 7]).

Upon review, the High Court held that the provisions governing compulsory residential diversion programs under section 41 of the *Child Justice Act 75 of 2008* did not allow for this type of sanction for Schedule 1 offences ([para 8]). The proceedings revealed that children, if convicted for the use or possession of cannabis, would face the stigma of a criminal record, which carried severe implications ([para 40]).

The High Court further examined the constitutionality of section 4(b) of the *Drugs Act*, which criminalizes the use and possession of cannabis by children, and found a significant disparity in the treatment of children compared to adults, who were no longer criminally prosecuted for similar actions due to the ruling in *Prince* ([paras 12-16]).

None of the State respondents opposed the confirmation application brought by the Centre for Child Law, which participated as amicus curiae. The applicant urged that it was vital to declare the impugned law unconstitutional as it entailed undue and harmful implications for children's rights ([paras 4, 21]).

---

## I – Issues

1. **Constitutional Validity**: Is section 4(b) of the *Drugs Act* unconstitutional insofar as it criminalizes the use and/or possession of cannabis by a child? ([paras 12-13])
2. **Impact on Children**: Does criminalizing such behavior infringe upon a child's rights under the Constitution, specifically the right to dignity and the best interests of the child? ([paras 11-12, 21])
3. **Justifiability**: If there is a limitation on children’s rights, is that limitation justifiable under section 36 of the Constitution? ([para 64])

---

## R – Rules

The key legal frameworks and precedents relied upon in this case include:

- **Constitution of South Africa**:
  - **Section 28**: Enshrines children’s rights, stipulating that the best interests of the child must be paramount in all matters concerning them ([para 2]).
  - **Section 9**: Prohibits unfair discrimination and ensures the right to equality ([para 17]).
  
- **Child Justice Act 75 of 2008**: Establishes a framework for the prosecution, diversion, and rehabilitation of children who commit offences ([para 16]).

- **Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 140 of 1992**: Criminalizes the use and possession of various narcotics, including cannabis ([para 14]).

- **Prince v Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development**: A pivotal case that determined the unconstitutionality of criminalizing cannabis use for adults, establishing a precedent that significantly influenced the current case's analysis regarding age discrimination ([paras 22-23]).

---

## A – Application

The Court applied the principles related to the best interests of the child, emphasizing that the criminal system is not an appropriate mechanism for dealing with children caught using or possessing cannabis. Instead, the Court noted that social systems should provide supportive interventions ([paras 30-31]).

### Criminalization Impact

The Court considered the harsh realities of exposing children to the criminal justice system, such as the potential for imprisonment and the long-lasting implications of having a criminal record ([paras 38-40]). The High Court’s analysis highlighted that placing children in the criminal justice system can exacerbate their vulnerability and expose them to further negative experiences, countering the purported benefits of deterrence through criminalization ([paras 59-62]).

### International Law Considerations

The Court evaluated international standards regarding the treatment of children, referencing the United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child (UNCRC) and its position on the best interests of the child, which supports non-punitive, rehabilitative measures over punitive responses ([paras 44-52]).

### Limitation Analysis

In its section 36 analysis, the Court engaged in a balancing exercise weighing the need to protect children from substance use against the potential harm of criminalizing their behavior. It concluded that less restrictive means, such as rehabilitation and social support systems, were available and would better serve the child’s interests compared to criminal sanctions ([paras 64-80]).

---

## C – Conclusion

The Court confirmed the High Court's declaration that section 4(b) of the *Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act* is unconstitutional as it infringes upon children's rights, particularly their best interests and dignity. The order declared that no child should face arrest or prosecution for violating this section and mandated a shift to social interventions ([para 100]). 

The Court emphasized that this judgment does not absolve children of consequences for drug use but rather insists on addressing such behaviors through supportive frameworks rather than criminal sanctions ([para 98]). The implications of this case extend beyond immediate legislative reform, underlining the necessity for a paradigm shift in how legal systems respond to children's interactions with drugs in a manner consistent with their developmental needs and rights.

**Ultimately**, the confirmation of the invalidity illustrates a progressive interpretation of children's rights within South African law, aligning them with global standards and emphasizing the need for protective, rehabilitative approaches in lieu of criminal penalties.
