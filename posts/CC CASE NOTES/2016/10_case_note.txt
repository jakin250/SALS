Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Links v MEC for Health, Northern Cape

## F – Facts ([1]–[10]; [11]–[13])
The applicant, Dirk Links (the applicant), sought leave to appeal the dismissal of his claim for damages against the Member of the Executive Council, Department of Health, Northern Cape Province (the respondent). The applicant's claim arose from an incident dated 26 June 2006, when he dislocated his left thumb while working as a cleaner. After receiving initial treatment and being fitted with a plaster cast, he returned to the hospital multiple times due to severe pain, ultimately leading to a fasciotomy and amputation of his thumb on 5 July 2006.

The applicant alleged that he was not informed of the decision to amputate or the reasons behind it, leading him to claim that he was unaware of the medical negligence until about September 2006, when he understood that he had permanently lost the use of his left arm. In November 2006, he sought legal advice, which led to a protracted delay in instituting his claim, which was ultimately served on the respondent on 6 August 2009. However, the respondent contended that the claim had prescribed, as it was filed after the three-year statutory period without proper notice under the Legal Proceedings Act.

## I – Issues ([11]–[24])
The primary legal issue was whether the applicant's claim had prescribed by the time he served summons on the respondent. Central to this determination was the interpretation of section 12(3) of the Prescription Act, which stipulates when a debt is deemed "due." Specifically, the court needed to ascertain whether the applicant had the requisite knowledge of the facts from which the debt arose — that is, whether he was aware of the negligence that led to his injuries by 5 August 2006.

Accompanying this question were secondary issues related to the interpretation and application of the provisions under the Legal Proceedings Act concerning the required notice of the intention to sue an organ of state.

## R – Rules ([15]–[28])
The relevant legal rules were outlined primarily within the Prescription Act and the Legal Proceedings Act. Section 12(1) of the Prescription Act states that "prescription shall commence to run as soon as the debt is due." Subsection 12(3) further clarifies that a debt is deemed due only when the creditor has knowledge of the identity of the debtor and the pertinent facts from which the debt arises.

Additionally, section 3 of the Legal Proceedings Act mandates that claimants give written notice to the relevant organ of state within six months of the debt becoming due, failing which the claim is subject to prescription.

The court relied on several precedent cases, such as *Truter v Deysel* to interpret "debt due" as encompassing when the entire set of facts necessary for a cause of action is established. The court also referenced *Road Accident Fund v Mdeyide* to emphasize the constitutional dimensions of the right of access to justice per section 34 of the Constitution.

## A – Application ([29]–[50])
The court meticulously examined whether the applicant had the necessary knowledge of the facts surrounding his claim by the critical date of 5 August 2006. Although the applicant had knowledge of the amputation of his thumb, he did not comprehend the causative factors related to his medical treatment. The court found that the applicant lacked understanding concerning the potential negligence leading to the amputation.

Evidence presented suggested that during his consecutive hospital stays, there was a breakdown in communication regarding his treatment, ultimately leading to his delayed understanding of the cause of his injuries. This included testimonies from medical professionals and implications from the hospital records that highlighted a lack of adequate information provided to him regarding the implications of the swelling and subsequent loss of function.

Critically, the court established that the applicant could not have reasonably been expected to acquire knowledge about the negligence, as both the High Court and Full Court had previously overlooked this fundamental question. As such, the respondent failed to demonstrate that the applicant was aware of all material facts by the prescribed date, indicating possibly erroneous application of the prescription rules.

The court also noted the applicant's reliance on the failed efforts of the Legal Aid Centre to support his claim and indicated that the applicant had approached them promptly after understanding the medical implications of his treatment.

## C – Conclusion ([51]–[58])
The Constitutional Court upheld the applicant's appeal, dissolving the prior judgments of the High Court and Full Court. It ruled that the applicant's claim had not prescribed by the time of service of the summons as the requisite knowledge of the underlying facts from which the debt arose had not been established by the critical date. The court emphasized the vital nature of ensuring that claimants, particularly in vulnerable positions, are not deprived of their right to seek redress due to procedural failings.

Furthermore, the court condemned the handling of the matter by the Legal Aid Centre, recognizing systemic issues that may impede access to justice for indigent claimants like the applicant. The court's ruling reaffirmed the need for clarity in legislation and the protection of individual rights within the context of prescriptive periods, aligning with the intentions of justice as enshrined in the South African Constitution.

In sum, the judgment underscored the intersections of procedural law, access to justice, and the requisite legal protections for individuals seeking redress in instances of alleged medical negligence. This case also highlights the need for courts to consider the realities faced by individuals in navigating complex legal frameworks, particularly in contexts involving statutory reporting requirements.
