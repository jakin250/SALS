Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

**FIRAC Case Note: South African Rail Commuter Corporation Ltd v Thwala (661/2010) [2011] ZASCA 170**

**F – Facts:**  
The respondent, Almmah Philisiwe Thwala, a 54-year-old woman, initiated a lawsuit against the appellant, the South African Rail Commuter Corporation Limited, following injuries sustained at the Village Main Station, Johannesburg, on 1 June 2007. During her daily commute, Thwala boarded a heavily crowded train (Train 9705) due to a civil service strike. As the train approached her stop, she was jostled by other passengers disembarking and subsequently fell onto the platform, where she was trampled.

Thwala claimed to have sustained soft tissue injuries to her neck and arm, and a head injury causing a brief loss of consciousness. Following the incident, she reported her injuries to station officials, but a critical point of contention arose regarding whether the train was in motion or stationary at the time of the incident. Testimonies from officials indicated that Thwala had stated the train was stationary; however, she contended that she had communicated the opposite.

The trial was conducted in the South Gauteng High Court, where the judge found the appellant liable for the injuries sustained by Thwala due to negligence. The appellant subsequently appealed this decision, contesting the lower court's findings ([paras 1–9]).

**I – Issues:**  
The primary legal issues presented in this case revolve around:  
1. Whether the South African Rail Commuter Corporation was negligent in their duty of care to Thwala.
2. Whether the appellant failed to take reasonable precautions to ensure passenger safety, contributing to the injuries sustained by Thwala.
3. Determining the factual basis of the incident—was the train in motion or stationary when Thwala fell? ([paras 4–17]).

**R – Rules:**  
The legal principles applicable to this case center around delictual liability, specifically the obligations of a service provider to ensure the safety of patrons. The established test for delictual liability involves three criteria ([para 11]):
1. A reasonable person in the defendant's position would foresee the possibility of their conduct causing harm.
2. The defendant would take reasonable steps to avert such risk.
3. The defendant failed to take those steps.

Under South African law, the burden of proof for establishing negligence rests on the claimant. Additionally, the case law (e.g., *Kruger v Coetzee* and *Shabalala v Metrorail*) provides that a claimant must demonstrate the reasonable measures that could have been taken to prevent negligence ([para 11]).

**A – Application:**  
The court analyzed the evidence presented, focusing particularly on testimonies and the credibility of accounts regarding the train's motion at the time of the incident. The court below had accepted that the overcrowding of the train was foreseeable but ruled against Thwala's assertion that the train was in motion when she fell. 

The appeal court found the lower court's factual determination erroneous, concluding that Thwala did not adequately establish that the train was moving when she fell, which ultimately was a necessary condition for proving negligence on the part of the appellant. The failure to meet this burden meant that the foundational basis for liability was not satisfied ([paras 13–19]).

The testimony from the train officials was deemed credible, supported by documented evidence from the Railway Occurrence Reporting (Liability) Report, which indicated that the train was stationary. The appeal court concluded that the alleged negligence linked to overcrowding did not bear a direct causal connection to Thwala’s injuries, as her specific claims regarding a moving train were unsubstantiated ([paras 10–16]).

**C – Conclusion:**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the appellant, overturning the previous judgment and granting 'absolution from the instance'—meaning that Thwala's claim was dismissed with costs. The court emphasized that the absence of proof regarding whether the train was in motion at the time of the incident extinguished Thwala's claims of negligence. The ruling underscored the critical nature of the burden of proof in negligence claims and established that, without sufficient evidence to support negligence, liability cannot be imposed ([paras 19]).

This case serves as a salient illustration of the intricate relationship between factual determinations and legal liability, particularly in public transport contexts where safety and negligence claims are concerned.
