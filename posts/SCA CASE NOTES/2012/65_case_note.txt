Source Folder: 2012

# Case Note: Midstream Home Owners Association v Shoprite Checkers (424/2011); Heritage Hill Home Owners Association v Shoprite Checkers (435/2011) [2012] ZASCA 65 (21 May 2012)

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The appellants in the case, comprised of various homeowners' associations and corporations from Midstream Estates, sought to challenge the construction and use of an access point for the Shoprite Checkers distribution centre on Brakfontein Road, Centurion. This access point was constructed as part of a broader development which included significant investment, totaling approximately R5 billion, following the 1999 development plan that anticipated three access points to the facility. However, only two access points had been approved under the Tshwane Town Planning Scheme, which included a “line of no access” for construction ([1–3]).

The contention arose from the assertion that Shoprite Checkers operated outside the confines of the established town planning scheme by constructing a new access point without the necessary permissions. The appellants asserted that this construction breached the line of no access stipulated initially when the township was established ([13]). They sought both interim and final interdicts against Shoprite Checkers, pending a reevaluation of the permissions granted by the City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality ([1]).

### I – Issues
The central issues arising from these appeals included:
1. Whether the construction of the access point by Shoprite Checkers violated the established “line of no access” according to the Tshwane Town Planning Scheme, as alleged by the appellants.
2. Whether the decision by the Municipality to approve the access point constituted administrative action under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA), thereby obligating the Municipality to afford the appellants a right to be heard prior to approval ([27–32]).
3. The implications of the interpretations of the relevant municipal laws, particularly those concerning revocation of access limitations under the Town Planning and Townships Ordinance 15 of 1986 ([14–19]).

### R – Rules
In adjudicating the matter, the Court relied on several legal principles and statutory frameworks:
- **Tshwane Town Planning Scheme, 2008**: Specifically, clauses related to access prohibition and the processes for granting permissions for land use alterations ([6–19]).
- **Town Planning and Townships Ordinance 15 of 1986**: This ordinance offers a framework for the Municipality's authority to prepare, amend, and enforce planning schemes ([14]).
- **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000**: This act requires that administrative action affecting rights and legitimate expectations afford affected parties due process and an opportunity to make representations ([31]).

Relevant case law was also considered, including:
- **Baron & Jester v Eastern Metropolitan Local Council** concerning public procedural fairness requirements in zoning and planning matters ([15]).

### A – Application
In its judgment, the Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed the “line of no access” established by the Municipality and whether its implied revocation could be established through subsequent approvals granted to Shoprite Checkers. It noted that the construction of the access point aligned with the procedural requirements established under municipal law, particularly referencing the Municipality’s council resolutions that facilitated streamlined application processes ([20–25]).

The Court concluded that while the line of no access was initially established, the approval processes undertaken by the Municipality constituted valid permissions for the access point construction, thereby negating claims of illegality on the part of Shoprite Checkers ([26]). Moreover, the Appellants' argument regarding administrative justice did not substantiate a claim to standing or a right to a hearing, as the decision to grant access did not infringe upon their rights or legitimate expectations ([30–32]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, thereby affirming the decision of the lower court. The judgment reinforced the principle that municipal authorities have wide discretion in regulating land development and access rights and highlighted the importance of procedural compliance in administrative decisions ([1], [33]). The court's ruling serves as a significant precedent, underscoring the limited recourse available to interested parties concerning municipal planning decisions where procedures are adhered to, and it delineates the boundaries of procedural rights in the context of land use and urban planning. 

Further, the implications extend to how homeowners and associations engaged in urban residential planning must actively monitor municipal developments and processes to uphold their interests within the framework of applicable laws and regulations. The outcomes of this case firmly establish the necessity of proactive engagement and the importance of understanding the mechanisms and authorizations inherent within town planning schemes.
