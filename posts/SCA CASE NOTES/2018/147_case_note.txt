Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: Gavin Charlton Harvey NO v Georgina Elizabeth Crawford NO (1016/2017) [2018] ZASCA 147

## F – Facts
[1] This case revolves around the interpretation of a Notarial Deed of Trust executed by Mr. Louis John Druiff in January 1953, which was amended at a later date. The Trust Deed provides for the distribution of a trust fund among certain beneficiaries, primarily the biological children of the donor and their descendants, upon the death of the donor. At the time the Trust Deed was executed, the donor had four children, three of whom had their own children. 

[3] One daughter, Ms. Dulcie Helena Harper, adopted two children after the donor's death, sparking uncertainty over their inheritance rights. When it became clear that the Trust Deed did not explicitly mention adopted children, Ms. Harper filed an application in the Western Cape Division of the High Court seeking to establish that the terms used in the Trust Deed—namely, "children," "descendants," "issue," and "legal descendants"—should include her adopted children ([para 4]). 

[4] The appellants argued that the exclusion of adopted children would amount to unfair discrimination. The respondents, consisting of the trustees of the Trust and the Master of the High Court, contested this application on various grounds, including that the donor's intent was to include only biological descendants. The court a quo found in favor of the respondents, leading to the appeal [para 10].

## I – Issues
[6] The principal issues for determination included:

1. Whether the terms "children," "descendants," "issue," or "legal descendants" as employed in the Trust Deed should encompass adopted children.
2. Whether the exclusion of adopted children from the trust's benefits constitutes unfair discrimination in violation of Section 9(4) of the Constitution.
3. Whether the court should vary the Trust Deed pursuant to Section 13 of the Trust Property Control Act in light of the donor’s possible intent at the time of execution.

## R – Rules
[14] The interpretation of the Trust Deed necessitates examining the legal framework existing at the time of its execution. Section 71(2) of the Children's Act 31 of 1937 defined adopted children as legitimate children of adoptive parents, but also allowed that they would not inherit property unless expressly included in the trust or will [para 17]. 

[22] Principles of testamentary freedom (freedom of testation) indicate that individuals possess the right to dictate the terms of their legacies unless those terms contravene public policy. The judiciary is generally reluctant to interfere with a testator's express wishes unless a strong public policy argument arises [para 40]. 

[24] The applicable judicial principles dictate that the grammatical meaning of trust documents should be interpreted in context, considering all relevant factors, including the intent of the donor at the time of the trust's creation [para 25].

## A – Application
[30] The court assessed the language of the Trust Deed to determine if it inherently excludes adopted children. It took note of Ms. Harper's prior discussions with the donor regarding her struggles with pregnancy, thus implying the potential that adopted children were a consideration in the donor's mind [para 33]. However, the court ultimately found that the explicit terms used in the document aligned with biological descendants, given the historical context of adoption laws at the time [para 48].

[50] The court also evaluated whether the absence of explicit language concerning adopted children indicated an intention to exclude them. Citing precedents such as Cohen v Roetz NO, the court argued that specific language regarding biological heirs was typical in similar documentation from that era and demonstrated an intended exclusivity to blood relations [para 50].

[70] The argument that the exclusion of adopted children violated public policy was not upheld, as the case presented did not substantiate a public interest argument strong enough to override testamentary freedom. The court distinguished between public and private trusts and emphasized that the latter, particularly family trusts, are often treated with more leniency regarding testamentary visits [para 62].

## C – Conclusion
[75] The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, confirming the lower court's ruling that Ms. Harper's adopted children did not fall under the categories defined in the Trust Deed. The court underscored the importance of interpreting testamentary documents according to their explicit terms and historical context, asserting that altering such a deed based on contemporary ideals of fairness was unjustified. The decision reinforced the principle of testamentary freedom, suggesting that the trustee's intent must take precedence, provided it does not contravene public policy [para 75].

This ruling highlights the tension between evolving societal values and established legal provisions in the realm of trusts and estates, particularly with respect to adopted children’s rights. The implications reflect ongoing debates within constitutional law on equality and non-discrimination, in the light of private testamentary autonomy.
