Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# Case Note: Duwayne Esau and Others v Minister of Co-operative Governance and Traditional Affairs and Others (611/2020) [2021] ZASCA 9

## F – Facts
[1] On 15 March 2020, South Africa declared a national state of disaster in response to the COVID-19 pandemic under the Disaster Management Act 57 of 2002 (DMA). The objective of this declaration was to regulate the country's response to the pandemic and prevent the spread of SARS-CoV-2, which had caused significant health and economic challenges ([1–18]). Various regulations were promulgated during this period.

[2] The appellants in this case, comprising eight individuals, sought judicial review of regulations issued by the Minister of Co-operative Governance and Traditional Affairs (the COGTA Minister) relating to lockdown measures. Specifically, they contested the validity of the regulations made under level 4, arguing that they were unconstitutional and infringed upon fundamental rights without sufficient justification ([2–6]).

[3] The Western Cape Division of the High Court dismissed their application, leading to an appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ([3]). The SCA was asked to determine multiple issues regarding the legal effect of decisions made by the National Coronavirus Command Council (NCCC), the procedural fairness of the regulations, and the constitutionality of specific provisions within those regulations ([2]).

## I – Issues
[4] The central legal issues before the SCA included:

1. Whether the policy decisions made by the NCCC were justiciable, and if so, whether they had lawful authority to make such decisions ([4]).
2. Whether the regulations promulgated by the COGTA Minister adhered to the procedural fairness requirements of the DMA and the Constitution ([5]).
3. The constitutional validity of specific restrictions on fundamental rights imposed by the level 4 regulations ([6–8]).

## R – Rules
[5] The applicable legal rules stem from the DMA, which empowers the Minister to make regulations in response to a disaster, provided such regulations are consistent with sections 26 and 27 of the Act. Section 27 specifically outlines the powers for creating regulations to manage risks and impacts of a disaster ([9–17]).

[6] Key judicial principles include the following:

1. **Separation of Powers**: Courts may not interfere with executive decisions merely because they disagree with them. The legality of the decision is to be assessed, not its wisdom ([6]).
2. **Procedural Fairness**: The provisions of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) require that administrative actions affecting the public must be procedurally fair ([76–83]).
3. **Limitation of Rights**: Section 36 of the Constitution permits the limitation of fundamental rights, provided that such limitation is reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society ([107–110]).

## A – Application
[7] The SCA applied the above rules to the facts of the case as follows:

1. **Justiciability of NCCC Decisions**: The court concluded that the NCCC's deliberations, despite being policy-oriented, were ultimately subject to judicial review once they were given legal effect through the COGTA Minister's regulations. The court held that these decisions did not infringe separation of powers principles ([34–39]).

2. **Procedural Fairness**: The court examined the public participation process that preceded the promulgation of the level 4 regulations. The COGTA Minister had consulted broadly, allowing for public comment. The court found that the Minister appropriately weighed public inputs in formulating the regulations, indicating a procedurally fair process ([63–100]).

3. **Constitutionality of Regulations**: The appellants contended that certain regulations infringed fundamental rights. The court recognized that regulations limiting movement and economic activity imposed valid restrictions due to the urgent need to combat the pandemic. The COGTA Minister's actions were deemed rational and proportional in the context of the disaster. However, regulation 16(2)(f), which restricted exercise to three forms and a time-limited window, was considered overly restrictive and irrational, leading to its declaration as invalid. The prohibition on over-the-counter sale of hot food was also found unconstitutional as it lacked a rational basis and proportionality ([142–147]).

## C – Conclusion
[8] The SCA dismissed the appeal in part, affirming the constitutionality and legality of most level 4 regulations while declaring regulation 16(2)(f) and items 1 and 2 of Part E of Table 1 invalid due to their unconstitutional nature and lack of legal justification. The appeal's limited success led the court not to award costs, aligning with the principle established in Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources and Others ([158–161]).

The judgment underlines the delicate balance between the need for emergency responses during a national disaster and the protection of constitutional rights, reaffirming the role of the judiciary in scrutinizing executive action, even during crises.
