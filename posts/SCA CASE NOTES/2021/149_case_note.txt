# Case Note: HAL obo MML v MEC For Health, Free State [2021] ZASCA 149 

**FIRAC Case Note**

### F – Facts 
On 1 May 2005, Ms HAL, the appellant, was admitted to Thebe Hospital in Harrismith, Free State, during the late stages of her pregnancy. She gave birth to her son, MML, on 2 May 2005. Despite a seemingly normal delivery, MML later exhibited signs of neurological regression, diagnosed as cerebral palsy, and a magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scan in August 2014 revealed he suffered from hypoxic ischemic encephalopathy (HIE) — a brain injury resulting from prolonged oxygen deprivation. 

On 2 September 2014, HAL initiated legal proceedings against the MEC for Health, Free State, asserting that MML’s injury resulted from negligence during the intrapartum phase due to inadequate monitoring, resulting in failure to detect foetal distress. The MEC denied liability, leading to a lengthy trial in the Free State Division of the High Court, where the appellant’s case was dismissed with costs. She was granted leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA).

The trial was complicated by the absence of complete hospital records, and expert witnesses provided evidence based on limited documents and assertions by HAL. The appellant argued that the injury occurred during the delivery, supported by expert testimony. 

### I – Issues 
The central legal issues revolved around:

1. **Medical Negligence**: Whether the hospital staff's failure to monitor HAL and her unborn child adequately constituted negligence ([para 19]).
2. **Causation**: The determination of when and how MML's brain injury occurred and whether it could be attributed to the hospital's conduct ([para 73]).
3. **Weight of Evidence**: The reliability of expert testimony based upon potentially disputed facts and limited records ([para 66]).
4. **Adverse Inference**: The implications of the missing medical records and whether the absence warranted an inference of negligence ([para 81]).

### R – Rules 
The court relied upon several statutory and judicial principles including:

1. **Standard of Care**: The healthcare professionals involved are expected to meet a standard of care set by a reasonably competent professional, with particular regard to the established maternity care guidelines ([para 56]).
2. **Causation in Negligence**: Establishing a causal link between the defendant's negligence and the resultant injury, necessitating a balance of probabilities showing that an injury likely occurred due to the defendant's conduct ([paras 82, 148]).
3. **Adverse Inference from Missing Records**: The absence of appropriate medical records may lead to a presumption of negligence if it can be shown that such records typically demonstrate the standard of care expected ([para 81]).
4. **Expert Testimony Standards**: Experts must provide opinions based on reasonable evidence, and their conclusions should withstand scrutiny in light of established facts. Opinions based on hearsay may be deemed inadmissible without appropriate substantiation ([para 204]).

### A – Application 
In examining the evidence, the SCA highlighted several factors:

1. **Credibility of the Appellant**: The High Court found HAL to be an unreliable witness due to inconsistencies in her testimony regarding the birth, monitoring, and subsequent events, rendering her case less persuasive ([para 71]).
   
2. **Expert Evidence**: The court scrutinized the qualifications and credibility of the expert witnesses, focusing on the inadequacies of their reliance on HAL's statements, given the unavailability of comprehensive hospital records. The judgments emphasized that expert opinions lacking a solid factual basis should not be accepted ([para 210]).
   
3. **Causation Links**: Despite the assessments of experts, the trial judge's determinations were underpinned by the conflicting accounts of the timing of the brain injury, coupled with the evidence that no immediate signs of distress were recorded at birth ([para 72]). The adverse inference drawn from the hospital's failure to produce records was challenged but ultimately failed to sway the court, as no direct causal link could be established ([para 160]).
   
4. **Health Record Policy and Legal Implications**: The SCA acknowledged that health facilities have a statutory duty to maintain comprehensive medical records and that failure to do so could prejudicially affect claims of negligence ([paras 119-120]). The absence of such records in this case was deemed a neutral point, not strong enough to substantiate a claim against the hospital ([para 80]).

### C – Conclusion 
Ultimately, the SCA upheld the High Court's decision to dismiss the appeal. The judgment concluded that the appellant failed to establish the breach of duty by the hospital and the requisite causation link between the alleged negligence and MML's injuries. The Court affirmed that the absence of reliable evidence rendered the claims speculative. The implications of this decision underscore the critical importance of maintaining proper medical records and the challenges faced in litigating medical negligence claims where records are missing. 

The order was made for the appeal to be dismissed with costs, highlighting the responsibilities of healthcare providers in proper documentation and care standards ([para 84]).
