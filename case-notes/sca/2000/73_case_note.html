Supreme Court of Appeal

Van der Berg v Coopers & Lybrand Trust (Pty) Ltd & Others [2000] 466/98

[2] The statement accused Van der Berg of manipulating attorneys, claiming that his conduct was improper and constituted a breach of professional duty.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2000 1985 (1) SA 654

F – Facts

[1] The appellant, J Van der Berg, is a senior advocate based in Cape Town. The respondents include Eileen Fey and Michael Lane, who serve as joint trustees in the insolvent estate of Jurgen Harksen, and the companies Coopers & Lybrand Trust (Pty) Ltd and James Lane Trustees (Pty) Ltd. The case originated from a defamation claim instituted by Van der Berg against Lane and Fey, arising from a statement made during a condonation application involving the trustees and various attorneys.

[2] The statement accused Van der Berg of manipulating attorneys, claiming that his conduct was improper and constituted a breach of professional duty. The defamatory statement allegedly appeared in Lane's affidavit during ongoing legal proceedings concerning Harksen’s estate, which had been sequestrated on 16 October 1995. Fey endorsed Lane's affidavit, leading to their participation and subsequent liability in the defamation action.

[3] The Cape of Good Hope Provincial Division, under Judge Cleaver, ruled in favor of the respondents, asserting that the defamatory statement was published on a "privileged occasion" and relevant to the judicial proceedings at hand. The appellant was subsequently granted leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal, resulting in the present case.

I – Issues

[19] The appeal brought forth three main issues for determination:

  • (a) Whether the respondents successfully proved their defense of qualified privilege regarding the defamatory statement.
  • (b) Should the appellate court determine the vicarious liability of Republic Trustees for Lane’s publication of the defamatory statement.
  • (c) What is the appropriate quantum of damages if the appellant prevails in the appeal.

R – Rules

[21][24] The rules governing the defense of qualified privilege in defamation claims are well established. A defamatory statement made during legal proceedings may enjoy qualified privilege if it is relevant to the proceedings. The respondents bore the onus to demonstrate that the statement in question was indeed relevant, as set out in cases such as Joubert v Venter 1985 (1) SA 654 (A) and National Media Ltd v Bogoshi 1998 (4) SA 1196 (SCA).

[17] Should the respondents establish the defense of qualified privilege, this protection could be overcome by demonstrating malice on the part of the trustees. The court noted that malice could entail an improper or indirect motive and referenced Basner v Trigger 1946 AD 83 in articulating the malice standard.

[18] The principle of vicarious liability stipulates that an employer may be liable for the actions of an employee when those actions occur in the course of employment, as indicated in Minister van Polisie v Gamble 1979 (4) SA 759 (A).

A – Application

[36] The central question of whether Lane's statement was relevant to the condonation application was assessed against the criteria for qualified privilege. The court found that the defamatory statement was speculative and did not serve to advance the trustees' position. On this basis, the court articulated that the statement failed to meet the relevance threshold necessary to qualify for the defense of privilege, emphasizing that no link existed between the allegations made and the defendants’ legal struggles, rendering the remarks merely derogatory.

[42] Furthermore, considering the vicarious liability of Republic Trustees, the court noted that the lack of evidence regarding the degree of control Republic had over Lane's actions failed to absolve them of responsibility. The court adhered to the view that Lane’s roles and responsibilities within the company did not negate the relationship of employer-employee concerning the defamatory act, thereby establishing grounds for vicarious liability.

[44-49] In assessing damages, the court took into account the seriousness of the defamatory content and the relatively limited scope of its publication. There was no indication that the defamatory allegations led to a tangible decline in the appellant's professional stature, aligning with principles expressed in previous cases regarding compensations for non-pecuniary damages. Ultimately, the court awarded R30,000 as damages, factoring in the lack of prior attempts at an apology and the nature of the deed's impact.

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the appellant, overturning the lower court's decision by concluding that the respondents' defense of qualified privilege was not established. The court also found Republic Trustees vicariously liable for Lane's defamatory remarks. The court provided compensatory damages of R30,000, illustrating the importance of both protecting professional reputations and maintaining the integrity of legal proceedings. The judgment reflects a balance between the right to freedom of expression and the right to reputation, reinforcing the boundaries within which legal professionals operate in the conduct of their duties.
