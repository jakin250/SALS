## FIRAC Case Note: Wiese v Government Employees Pension Fund and Others ([2012] ZACC 5)

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History

This case revolves around the plight experienced by non-member spouses married in community of property whose partners are members of the Government Employees Pension Fund (GEPF). The applicant, Mathilda Louisa Wiese, was married to Cornelius Johannes Marx, a member of the GEPF. Following their divorce in 2008, Wiese was awarded a 25% share of Marx's pension interest, as outlined in their settlement agreement. However, Wiese's ability to realise her share was impeded by the Government Employees Pension Law (GEPL), which permitted realisation of pension benefits only upon specific exit events (e.g., retirement or dismissal) ([paras 1–12]).

Wiese initiated her case in the Western Cape High Court, challenging the GEPL's provisions as inconsistent with section 9(1) of the Constitution, which guarantees equality before the law. The High Court ruled in her favour, declaring the GEPL unconstitutional as it did not afford non-member spouses the same rights as those under the Pension Funds Act (PFA). The court suspended the declaration for twelve months to allow Parliament to amend the law ([paras 13–14]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions

The core constitutional question was whether the provisions of the GEPL that restricted a non-member spouse's access to their pension interest upon divorce constituted discrimination in violation of the equality clause (section 9(1) of the Constitution) ([paras 3–4]). Further, the application included questions regarding the appropriate remedy for the found constitutional invalidity ([para 14]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon

The Court analyzed the legislative landscape, particularly the GEPL and its amendment provisions. Section 9(1) of the South African Constitution was crucial for the determination of equality rights. The PFA, particularly section 37D, which allows for a clean break upon divorce, provided a comparative framework to assess the GEPL's deficiencies ([paras 8–9], [14]).

Precedent users include previous decisions that have shaped constitutional jurisprudence on equality and rights connected to divorce, such as **Malachi v Cape Dance Academy International (Pty) Ltd and Others** ([2010] ZACC 24) and **Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources and Others** ([2009] ZACC 14) which underscored the importance of ensuring constitutional compliance in legislation ([paras 26–29]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law

The Court highlighted the differences between the treatment of non-member spouses under the GEPL compared to the PFA. They observed that the GEPL effectively froze the pension benefit of non-member spouses upon divorce while the PFA allowed for immediate realisation. The legislative amendments introduced by Parliament post the High Court's ruling were deemed sufficient to eliminate the grievances highlighted by Wiese. The clean-break principle was effectively integrated into the GEPL via the GEPL Amendment Act ([para 24]) ([paras 21–23]).

The application of mootness principles further complicated the issue, as the legislative change rendered Wiese's substantive claims regarding the GEPL's invalidity unnecessary for determination. Therefore, the Court refrained from ruling on the substantive issues but addressed the matter of costs ([paras 20–24], [28–30]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications 

The Constitutional Court concluded that the GEPL amendment sufficiently remedied the constitutional defect identified by the High Court, thereby rendering the applicant's claims moot ([para 23]). The primary holding of the Court revolved around costs, where it concluded that Wiese was entitled to costs against the Minister of Finance due to the failure to timely amend the discriminatory legislation, which forced her to seek judicial intervention ([paras 30–31]).

The implications of this judgment extend beyond the individual case, reaffirming the necessity for legislative compliance with constitutional principles, particularly concerning equality and non-discrimination in financial arrangements following divorce. The judgment serves as a reminder of legislative responsibility in amending laws to reflect the democratic principles enshrined in the Constitution, directly impacting the rights of vulnerable parties in marital contexts ([paras 17–19]).
