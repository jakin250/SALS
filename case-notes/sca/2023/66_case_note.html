FIRAC Case Note for Cooper N O and Another v Curro Heights Properties (Pty) Ltd (1300/2021) [2023] ZASCA 66

F – Facts
The case involves an appeal from the Western Cape Division of the High Court regarding the validity of a land sale agreement between the joint liquidators of Nomic 151 (Pty) Ltd (in liquidation) and Curro Heights Properties (Pty) Ltd. The first and second appellants in this case are Chavonnes Badenhorst St Clair Cooper N O and Sumiya Abdool Gafaaf Khammisa N O, acting as liquidators for Nomic. The respondent, Curro Heights, was represented by its sole director, Mr. Rhett Molyneux ([paras 2–3]).

A written sale agreement was concluded on 8 April 2016, whereby the liquidators sold land, which included discrepancies about the measurements and erf numbers. Following some disputes over payment, the liquidators cancelled the initial agreement due to Curro's failure to pay a deposit. Subsequently, on 14 November 2016, a second sale agreement was executed. However, the agreement still featured inaccuracies concerning the erf number of a "ring road" that provided access to subdivided erven ([paras 5–6]).

Despite the liquidators' attempts to rectify the inaccuracies through addendums, significant complexities arose concerning the "merx" (subject matter of the sale). Curro later expressed concerns over portions extending into another development, suggesting an informal arrangement regarding subdivision. Ultimately, following a series of delays and failures to address these outstanding issues, the appellants sought declaratory relief from the court regarding the validity of the agreement ([paras 8–12]).

I – Issues
The central issue on appeal was whether the written sale of land agreement was null and void ab initio due to non-compliance with section 2(1) of the Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981, and for the want of consensus regarding the merx ([paras 1, 3]). Specifically, the court needed to assess:

  1. Whether the agreement complied with the statutory requirements of section 2(1) of the Act.
  2. Whether there was consensus regarding the subject matter of the sale, given the disputes arising post-agreement concerning the erf numbers and intended land parcels ([paras 1, 16]).

R – Rules
The Supreme Court of Appeal's analysis focused on the statutory requirements laid out in the Alienation of Land Act 68 of 1981, particularly section 2(1), which stipulates that no alienation of land shall have force or effect unless it is contained in a deed of alienation signed by the parties ([para 15]).

The court reiterated that the essential material terms of the contract must be clearly defined and included within the written agreement for validity. The existence of consensus was addressed, where consensus involves agreement on the essential elements of the contract, among which the merx is critical ([paras 16–19]).

Case law was referenced to delineate the principles governing rectification of agreements as a remedy when a mistake occurs in the representation of the parties' true intentions ([para 14]).

A – Application
The court determined that the lack of proper documentation led to non-compliance with section 2(1) of the Act. It concluded that all material terms required to constitute a valid contract of sale—including the correct identification of erf numbers and the implications of the subdivision—were not adequately addressed in the written agreement.

The court noted that there was no direct reference to the anticipated subdivision in the original agreement or addendum and emphasized that any discussions regarding subdivision made after the agreement was insufficient to create consensus or rectify the contract’s terms ([paras 17–20]).

An evaluation of the parties' intentions revealed that both sought to complete the sale of erf 19565; however, Curro's realization of the implications related to the Nurture Park development introduced uncertainty. This resulted in the conclusion that there was a lack of agreement as to the fundamental qualities of the subject matter, which is pivotal for a valid sale ([paras 12, 14]).

C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, declaring the written sale agreement and its addendum void ab initio due to non-compliance with section 2(1) of the Alienation of Land Act and a lack of consensus regarding the merx. The court’s order set aside the prior judgment of the High Court and mandated that the respondent pay the appellants' costs ([paras 21]).

This ruling underscores the legislative intent of the Alienation of Land Act to ensure complete clarity and consensus in contracts concerning land transactions, strict adherence to formalities, and the implications of erroneous documentation on contractual validity. The case serves as a significant reminder of the requirement for precision and thoroughness in contractual agreements, especially in real estate, where material terms affect both parties' rights and obligations.
