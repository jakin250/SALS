Supreme Court of Appeal 2015

FIRAC Case Note: Maykent (Pty) Ltd v Trackstar Trading 20 (Pty) Ltd (1036/2013)

F – Facts

The case involves a dispute concerning a construction contract between the appellant, Maykent (Pty) Ltd (Maykent), a franchisee of Kentucky Fried Chicken, and the respondent, Trackstar Trading 20 (Pty) Ltd (Trackstar), a building contractor. Maykent contracted with Trackstar in February 2006 for alterations and additions to a fast-food outlet in New Town, Johannesburg. The completion date was initially set for 30 June 2006 but extended to 15 September 2006 due to variation orders causing delays ([para 1]).

Payment under the contract, totaling approximately R1.28 million, was to be made in stages, with a final invoice submitted by Trackstar amounting to R985,423. Despite demand for payment, Maykent did not settle the amount, prompting Trackstar to initiate proceedings in the Gauteng Division of the High Court. The summons was served on 5 September 2008 ([paras 2–3]).

In the litigation, Maykent raised several defenses, including a claim that no payment was owed due to the absence of a final completion certificate, which was allegedly required under the JBCC 2000 Principal Building Agreement. Maykent also filed a counterclaim for damages amounting to R2,122,206 for losses incurred due to delays ([paras 3–4]).

The trial court found in favor of Trackstar, determining that Trackstar was entitled to the payment amount cited in the final payment certificate issued by Maykent’s appointed agency, Tertius Rabe Property Services CC (TRE), shortly before the trial commenced. The court ordered Maykent to pay interest from the date of the summons ([paras 4–5]).

I – Issues

Central to this case were two primary legal issues ([paras 5–9]):

  1. Did the absence of a certificate of final completion under the JBCC contract create a valid defense for Maykent in contesting Trackstar's claim for payment?
  2. Was the imposition of interest from the summons date justified given the timeline of events, including the issuance of the final payment certificate five years after the summons was served?

R – Rules

The court relied upon the legal principles inherent in contract law, specifically those addressing the enforceability of contractual obligations and the relevance of contractual terms to the interpretation of duties owed between parties ([paras 5–6]). Key rules considered included:

  • JBCC 2000 Principal Building Agreement terms, particularly clause 34.5, which stipulates that a final payment certificate can only be issued after a final completion certificate has been provided ([para 6]).
  • The principles of agency law, establishing that TRE acted as Maykent's agent in conducting business with Trackstar, thereby binding Maykent to the actions of TRE ([para 9]).

A – Application

The court focused on the practicalities of the agreement and the parties’ subsequent conduct. It determined that although Maykent argued the JBCC contract was binding, the evidence suggested that the parties did not operate under its terms during the contract execution. Mr. Stears, a representative of TRE, indicated a lack of awareness regarding the JBCC contract's application, suggesting the parties had effectively set it aside ([para 7]).

Furthermore, the court noted that the delay in issuing the final payment certificate, while relevant, did not negate Trackstar’s entitlement to payment, as Maykent was aware of the amount due from January 2007. The court found that Maykent had not established a causal connection between any alleged damages and Trackstar's performance. Consequently, Maykent’s defenses based on the lack of a final completion certificate lacked merit ([para 8]). The court affirmed that Trackstar was owed the amount in the final certificate and was entitled to interest from the summons date, as Maykent had known of its liability ([paras 9–10]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Maykent's appeal with costs, reinforcing that the failure to issue a final certificate did not excuse Maykent from its obligation to pay Trackstar. The court concluded that the procedural steps taken in the litigation—including the acknowledgment of the amount owed—rendered the summons valid and not premature ([paras 10–11]). This judgment underscores the importance of clear contractual terms and adherence to agreed-upon procedures in construction contracts, highlighting the implications for future disputes in similar contexts.

This case illustrates a pivotal interpretation of contractual obligations where form may not always dictate substance, reaffirming principles of agency and the enforceability of construction-related agreements.

References:

  • Maykent (Pty) Ltd v Trackstar Trading 20 (Pty) Ltd (1036/2013) [2015] ZASCA 14 (17 March 2015).
  • JBCC 2000 Principal Building Agreement, clause 34.5.
