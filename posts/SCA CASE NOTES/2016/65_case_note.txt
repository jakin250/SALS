Source Folder: 2016

# Case Note: Gayiya v S (1018/15) [2016] ZASCA 65

### FIRAC Analysis

#### F – Facts

The appellant, Charles Vuyo Gayiya, was charged with multiple offenses, including kidnapping, assault with intent to cause grievous bodily harm, murder, possession of a firearm without a licence, and possession of ammunition without a licence. The events giving rise to the charges occurred on 29 September 1998. Gayiya pleaded guilty to some charges while pleading not guilty to others. During the regional court proceedings, the magistrate did not appoint assessors, which is mandatory under the Magistrates’ Courts Act 32 of 1944 when an accused is tried for murder, unless the accused requests otherwise ([1]–[5]).

Despite the procedural irregularity regarding the lack of assessors, the trial proceeded, leading to Gayiya's conviction for multiple counts after he made formal admissions and chose not to testify. Following his sentencing, Gayiya sought to appeal both the convictions and the sentences imposed upon him, which was initially delayed, and during the appeal process, it was argued that the absence of assessors constituted a fundamental procedural error ([6]–[8]).

#### I – Issues

The primary legal issue revolved around whether the trial court was properly constituted, given the absence of assessors as mandated by section 93ter(1) of the Magistrates’ Courts Act. The specific constitutional question here was if the trial could proceed without the required assessors, and whether any purported waiver of this right by the accused post-verdict could rectify the initial procedural defect ([8]–[11]).

#### R – Rules

The governing statutory provision was section 93ter(1) of the Magistrates’ Courts Act 32 of 1944, which explicitly requires that a judicial officer in a regional court dealing with murder charges shall be assisted by two assessors unless the accused requests a trial without them. Furthermore, the legal precedent from **R v Price** established that failure to adhere to mandatory procedural requirements results in an improperly constituted court, rendering any verdict void ([7]–[10]).

The judgment also referenced **Green v Fitzgerald & others** to assert that deviations from statutory requirements cannot be waived or cured through subsequent proceedings or agreements, emphasizing the strict necessity of a properly constituted court for valid judgments ([11]).

#### A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal assessed the procedure followed during the trial against the statutory provisions and noted that the regional magistrate failed to inform Gayiya of his right to have assessors before he pleaded. This omission rendered the trial fundamentally flawed, as the legal requirement for assessors is not discretionary but obligatory in murder cases ([8], [11]).

The court emphasized that even though Gayiya indicated a lack of need for assessors post-verdict, this post hoc acknowledgment did not remedy the procedural defect, as the trial’s validity was predicated upon the prior conformity to legal requirements. Given the circumstances, the Court found itself compelled to uphold the appeal based on the flawed constitution of the initial trial and ruled that the defect was irreparable irrespective of subsequent acknowledgments by the accused ([10]–[12]).

#### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal allowed the appeal, thereby setting aside Gayiya's convictions and sentences due to the failure to constitute the court correctly as required by legislation. The court’s decision reinforced the importance of adherence to procedural law and asserted that judicial processes must strictly comply with legal statutes to ensure the integrity of convictions. The implications of this ruling stress the necessity for trial courts to uphold procedural requirements, particularly in serious criminal matters such as murder, to avoid miscarriages of justice. The case serves as a pivotal reminder of the inalienable rights granted to defendants under the law and the critical role of assessors in ensuring a fair trial ([12]).
