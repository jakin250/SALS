Supreme Court of Appeal

Nkengana v Schnetler (65/09) [2010] ZASCA 64

Shortly after the agreement was concluded, the appellants faced difficulty in adhering to the payment schedule.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2010 [2010] ZASCA 64

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case arises from a written deed of sale dated 22 December 2000 between the appellants, Joseph Mncendisi Nkengana and Nombeko Felicia Nkengana (collectively referred to as the "appellants"), and the first respondent, Stephanus Van der Walt Schnetler, (hereinafter referred to as "the respondent"). The appellants purchased a property situated in Uitenhage for a total purchase price of R260,000, payable in four instalments ([2]).

Shortly after the agreement was concluded, the appellants faced difficulty in adhering to the payment schedule. Subsequently, an oral agreement emerged, whereby the appellants consented to pay the monthly instalments of the respondent’s bond with Standard Bank until they could meet the initial purchase price obligations ([3]). By February 2007, the appellants contended they had paid R238,054.83 towards the purchase price and requested transfer of the property upon payment of the outstanding balance of R21,945.17 ([4]).

Disputes arose concerning the precise terms of the oral agreement, especially relating to the payments made by the appellants and whether these constituted sufficient performance under the initial written agreement. The respondent contested that the payments made were insufficient and insisted that the oral agreement implied a temporary variation to their obligations ([6]).

The appellants initiated an application for specific performance due to the lack of response from the respondent following their tender ([4]). This application was dismissed by the initial court, and further appeal to the full court of the Eastern Cape High Court also resulted in a dismissal, leading to the appellants’ ultimate appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([1]).

I – Issues

The primary legal question before the court was whether the appellants' tender of performance, as stated in their application for specific performance, was sufficient to warrant the enforcement of the original contract ([8]). Specifically, the issues included:

  1. Did the oral agreement alter the appellants' obligations under the written contract of sale?
  2. Was the appellants' final tender sufficient performance to claim specific performance of the deed of sale?
  3. Should the dispute over past payments and the terms of the oral agreement have been resolved to determine the appellants' right to specific performance?

R – Rules

The court's analysis was informed by several legal principles:

  1. A tender must consist of the full amount owed to be legally recognized in a claim for specific performance ([13]).
  2. The obligation to perform under a contract remains until cancelled; a party that has fulfilled their obligations may demand performance from the other ([12]).
  3. An oral agreement may validly vary a written contract, provided both parties consent ([3], [6]).
  4. Specific performance can be enforced as long as the conditions stipulated in the agreement are satisfied and are lawful ([12]).

Furthermore, established case law, such as Farmers' Co-operative Society v Berry 1912 AD 343, undergirds the principle that a party ready to perform may compel the other party to fulfill their contractual obligations ([12]).

A – Application

The court's examination focused on the appellants' final tender, wherein they asserted that their cumulative payments exceeded the purchase price. The final tender was determined to include R21,945.17, the bond's outstanding amount, and an additional tender of R67,860, leading to a total tender of R270,087.89 which surpassed the agreed price of R260,000. This was significant because it indicated that, independent of past payment disputes, the appellants had, at the very least, complied with the requirements for tender necessary for specific performance ([13]).

Regarding the disputes surrounding the oral agreement, the court found it unnecessary to conclusively resolve these due to the determination that the tender itself had satisfied the requirements for enforcement of the initial contract. The argument that some payments constituted "occupational rent" rather than reductions of the purchase price did not negate the appellants' entitlement to claim specific performance based on the final tender made ([6], [8], [16]).

The court also addressed procedural aspects, notably the issue of whether new matters introduced in a replying affidavit could be considered. The court ruled that such submissions were permissible if they directly responded to assertions raised by the respondent and did not substantially alter the case being made ([10]). The court found that the respondent had effectively waived any reliance on the terms of the original contract due to accepting bond payments in place of cash instalments, nullifying any claim for delay-related mora interest ([14]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appellants were entitled to specific performance of the original contract of sale, ordering that the respondent complete the transfer of the property upon satisfaction of their tender ([18]). The court dismissed the further appeal against the lower courts' decisions, establishing strong precedent regarding the permissibility of oral agreements modifying written contracts and clarifying tender requirements for specific performance in South African contract law.

The holding underscores not only the principle of contractual fidelity but also the adaptability of contractual obligations through mutual agreement, emphasizing that such variations should still remain enforceable, provided proper tender is made ([18]). This case therefore reinforces the significance of evaluating both the sufficiency of performance under tender conditions and the enforceability of agreed-upon modifications to existing contracts.
