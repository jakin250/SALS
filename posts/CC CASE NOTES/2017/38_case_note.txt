Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note

## Case Title: Niekara Harrielall v University of KwaZulu-Natal
**Neutral Citation:** [2017] ZACC 38  
**Coram:** Jafta J (unanimous decision)  
**Decided on:** 31 October 2017  

### F - Facts

This case involves an appeal by Niekara Harrielall ("the applicant"), a prospective medical student, against the University of KwaZulu-Natal ("the University"), regarding her unsuccessful application for admission to the MBChB (Bachelor of Medicine and Bachelor of Surgery) program ([1–2]). The applicant's prior application was rejected in 2015, prompting her to enroll in a Bachelor of Medical Science (Anatomy) course to enhance her admission prospects for the following year ([2]). In 2016, she applied as a mature student, a specific category within the University’s admission policy that allows 20% of total applicants to be chosen based on specific criteria ([2–3]).

The applicant met the criteria for one of the three categories of mature students, specifically category (a), but faced fierce competition for limited spots (10 places) against 161 candidates ([4]). The University chose candidates based on academic qualifications, resulting in her rejection ([5]).

Dissatisfied, she sought a review in the KwaZulu-Natal High Court to set aside the University’s decision, asserting that it failed to apply its own policy appropriately ([5–6]). The High Court dismissed her application, a decision upheld by the Supreme Court of Appeal; however, her appeal on costs became the central issue before the Constitutional Court ([6–7]).

### I - Issues

The main legal questions in this case involved:

1. Whether the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal properly applied the **Biowatch principle** regarding costs in constitutional litigation ([10, 17]).
2. Whether the applicant's situation constituted a constitutional matter under the **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act** (PAJA) and substantive constitutional rights, specifically the right to access further education as defined under Section 29(1) of the Constitution of South Africa ([17, 19]).

### R - Rules

The legal authorities and precedents relevant to this case are:

1. **Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources** [2009] ZACC 14, which established a principle that unsuccessful litigants against the State in constitutional matters should not be burdened with costs to avoid deterring the exercise of constitutional rights ([10, 12]).
  
2. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act** (PAJA) governs the review of administrative actions, reinforcing constitutional guarantees found in Section 33 of the Constitution of South Africa, which entails the right to administrative justice ([17, 19]).

3. Jurisprudence from **Affordable Medicines Trust v Minister of Health** [2006] ZACC 3, which outlines exceptions to the Biowatch principle, emphasizing that litigants who engage in abusive or frivolous litigation may be liable for costs ([12–14]).

4. **Pharmaceutical Manufacturers Association of South Africa v President of the Republic of South Africa** [2000] ZACC 1, which highlights the obligation of courts to control public power exercised by the state or its organs ([18]).

### A - Application

The Constitutional Court began by affirming that the rejection of the applicant's admission did not present sufficient grounds for upholding the merits of the applicant’s appeal, as it was clear her application was evaluated against the University’s policy and determined fairly ([9]). However, the court focused on examining how the lower courts handled the costs associated with the review application.

The High Court's failure to recognize the application of the **Biowatch principle** constituted a significant judicial error. They had applied the ordinary rule of costs following the result, thus imposing costs on the unsuccessful applicant in a constitutional matter ([10, 11]). The Supreme Court of Appeal compounded this error by erroneously stating that the constitutional issues were not implicated ([16]). The court established that a review under PAJA constitutes a constitutional issue as it is rooted in the rights guaranteed by Section 33 ([17]).

The court noted that the applicant's need for access to education was tied into larger constitutional imperatives outlined in Section 29, thus reinforcing the argument that the Biowatch principle should apply to shield her from costs ([19]).

In addressing prior cases, the court reiterated that the constitutional framework necessitates vigilance against decisions that may dissuade potential litigants from pursuing legitimate constitutional claims ([12, 15]).

### C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately ruled in favor of the applicant regarding the costs orders from the High Court and Supreme Court of Appeal while refusing to entertain the merits of her appeal concerning the admission decision. The court stated:

1. Leave to appeal was granted with respect to costs, setting aside the previous costs orders imposed by lower courts.
2. The court did not make an order for costs in its own proceedings pertaining to this case.

The implications reinforce that the **Biowatch principle** is vital for ensuring that litigants, particularly in constitutional matters against state organs such as a university, are not deterred by the risk of incurring costs. This judgment further elucidates the importance of ensuring access to educational opportunities and the role of courts in protecting essential constitutional rights against the backdrop of administrative decisions ([20, 21]).
