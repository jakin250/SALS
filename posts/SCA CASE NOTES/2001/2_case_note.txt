# FIRAC Case Note: RAPPORTEERBAAR

**Case Reference:** RAPPORTEERBAAR  
**Case Number:** 565/98  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Judges:** Smalberger, Vivier, Scott, Mpati, Melunsky  
**Hearing Date:** 16 February 2001  
**Judgment Date:** 22 February 2001  

---

## F – Facts

The Transvaal Law Society ("the respondent") initiated proceedings through a notice of motion, under Rule 6 of the Uniform Rules of Court, in July 1997, seeking the removal of the appellant, A.W.F. Middelberg, a practising attorney, from the role of attorneys of the Transvaal Provincial Division. The respondent also sought additional relief. After prolonged proceedings, the appellant was removed on 5 October 1998 by a court comprising Judges Van Coller and Combrinck, and the requested additional relief was granted. On 19 October 1998, the appellant filed a notice of appeal without first seeking or obtaining leave to appeal.

The court limited the initial argument to the question of whether it had jurisdiction to consider the proposed appeal in the absence of leave. The determination of this question would affect the proceedings' future course. The statutory framework under which the appeal was contemplated included Section 20(1) of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959, which provides for appeals against decisions or orders of the provincial division in civil matters ([paras 1–2]).

## I – Issues

The core legal issue presented to the court was whether the appellant’s request constituted a valid civil appeal to the Supreme Court without prior leave as mandated by the relevant statutory provisions. Specifically, this involved determining if the original proceedings constituted "civil proceedings" as defined by the statutory frameworks that govern appeals, particularly given the requirements set forth in Section 20(4) of the Act ([paras 2–5]). 

Additionally, the court sought to clarify the interpretation of what constitutes "civil proceedings," as the distinction could either allow or disallow the appeal's merits based on the absence of previously sought leave ([paras 6–8]).

## R – Rules

The legal rules implicated in this case were derived from the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959, particularly:

1. **Section 20(1):** This section grants the court the authority to hear appeals against judgments or orders of Provincial or Local Divisions in civil matters.
2. **Section 20(4):** This section stipulates that no appeal shall lie from a decision of a Provincial or Local Division in civil proceedings unless:
   - Directed by the full court of that division with special leave from the appeal division.
   - With leave of the court against whose order the appeal is noted or, if such leave is denied, with leave from the appeal division ([paras 3–5]).
3. **Section 21:** This section expands on the court's jurisdiction concerning appeals and likely provides fallback jurisdiction for cases not uniquely covered by Section 20 ([paras 3–4]).

The precedent cases, particularly **Prokureursorde van die Oranje Vrystaat v Louw** [1989] 1 SA 310 (O), examined the nature of applications for the removal of attorneys, emphasizing their classification as civil proceedings. The ruling in this case aligned with earlier landmark decisions such as **Kalil v Decotex (Pty) Ltd and Another** [1988] 1 SA 943 (A) and **Westinghouse Brake & Equipment (Pty) Ltd v Bilger Engineering (Pty) Ltd** [1986] 2 SA 555 (A), which significantly addressed the interpretation of civil proceedings within the statutory framework ([paras 8–12]).

## A – Application

The court assessed whether the application for the removal of the appellant was indeed "civil proceedings" under Section 20(1) and, more critically, whether the requisite leave to appeal was obligatory. After examining the statutory wording and the nature of the discovered legal proceedings, the court concluded that the proceedings were civil in nature, notwithstanding any characteristics that may render them sui generis ([paras 12–15]).

It was further established that the implications of the decisions in **Solomon and Jackson** and **Hassim (also known as Essack)** recognized the necessity for strict adherence to appeal protocols and leave requirements. Thus, in the absence of leave sought from the lower court or this court as specified in Section 20(4), this court possessed no jurisdiction to hear the appeal. The ruling from **Prokureursorde van die Oranje Vrystaat v Louw** was affirmed as appropriate, asserting that these regulations were not merely procedural hurdles but required conditions for the court's authority to proceed ([paras 5–15]).

The court contended that failure to comply with the procedural requirements was evident and directly impacted the entitlement to an appeal. The court also emphasized the care that appellants should exercise in ensuring compliance with prevailing legal principles and procedural requirements ([paras 16–18]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that there was no competent appeal before it due to the appellant's failure to obtain the required leave to appeal as mandated by the relevant provisions of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959. Consequently, the appeal was dismissed, and the court ordered that the matter be removed from the roll with costs. The implications of the ruling emphasized the essential need for adherence to procedural norms within legal practice, reinforcing that compliance with statutory requirements is fundamental, not only to the appellant's case but also in upholding the integrity of legal processes ([paras 17–19]).

Overall, the case reaffirmed the statutory framework's role in delineating the boundaries of judicial authority, highlighting the necessity for clear procedural compliance to safeguard judicial resources and uphold the rule of law in civil proceedings.
