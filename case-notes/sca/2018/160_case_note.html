Tembu Convenience Centre CC v City of Johannesburg (270/2018) [2018] ZASCA 160 (28 November 2018)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 160 (28 November 2018)

FIRAC Case Note

F - Facts

The case relates to an appeal by Tembu Convenience Centre CC (the first appellant) and its sole member, John Zakhele Radebe (the second appellant), against the City of Johannesburg, the Johannesburg Development Agency (Pty) Ltd (the first and second respondents), and the Johannesburg Road Agency (Pty) Ltd (the third respondent) regarding the alleged permanent closure and diversion of Mooki Street, Orlando East, Soweto, due to the creation of dedicated bus lanes ([paras 1–2]).

The appellants contended that the establishment of these bus lanes and their associated structures between 2008 and 2009 adversely affected their business by causing a significant loss of customer access to the first appellant’s convenience store and service station, which is located at the northwest corner of the intersection of Mooki Street and the Soweto Highway ([para 4]). The street prior to the establishment of the bus lanes featured a broken median line that allowed vehicles to cross, enabling access to the garage. Following the changes, vehicles travelling in a westerly direction or entering Mooki Street from the south were required to navigate an alternate route to access the garage ([paras 5–6]).

The appellants initiated legal proceedings seeking damages, framing their claims around the argument that the modifications constituted a permanent closure and diversion of the street in violation of section 67 of the Local Government Ordinance 17 of 1939 (Gauteng), which mandates certain procedural requirements for street closures ([para 8]). The respondents denied that the changes amounted to a permanent closure and contended that the closure of the inner lane for non-bus traffic fell under section 66, which allows for the permanent closure of a street for particular classes of traffic ([paras 9–10]).

The High Court ruled against the appellants, stating that the changes did not constitute a closure within the meaning of section 67 of the Ordinance, and the appellants subsequently appealed this decision ([para 3]).

I - Issues

The central issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the changes made to Mooki Street constituted a "permanent closure or diversion of a part of that street" as defined by section 67 of the Local Government Ordinance 17 of 1939 ([paras 1, 3]). This raised additional questions regarding the interpretation of "closure" and "diversion" in the context of municipal actions concerning street modifications and the impact on traffic flow.

R - Rules

The pertinent legal provisions underpinning the case are found in the Local Government Ordinance 17 of 1939. Specifically, the following sections were discussed:

  • Section 67 relates to the permanent closure or diversion of streets and requires local authorities to notify potential claimants affected by such closures and allows for compensation claims ([para 8]).
  • Section 66 permits local authorities to close parts of streets for specific classes of traffic, ensuring that alterations do not necessarily signify a closure for all traffic ([para 9]).

For precedential context, the court referred to:

  • Bellvue Motors CC v Johannesburg City Council 1994 (4) SA 339 (W), which held that a mere change in traffic flow did not amount to a diversion ([para 11]).
  • Rustenburg Local Municipality v Mwenzi Service Station CC [2015] 1 All SA 315 (SCA), clarifying the application of section 67 and emphasizing that an alteration in traffic flow did not amount to closure ([para 12]).

A - Application

In assessing the application of law to the facts, the court analyzed whether the physical barriers implemented in creating the bus lanes constituted a closure as claimed by the appellants or merely regulated traffic in a manner compliant with the respondents' authority under the Ordinance. The appellants argued that the physical separation created a definitive barrier impacting their access; however, the court maintained that:

  • The separation created by physical barriers, such as rumbling blocks and crash barriers, was secondary to the fundamental issue of whether the street remained open to any traffic type. The bus lanes did not prevent all classes of traffic from using the street; they merely restricted specific traffic classes from entering certain lanes ([para 14]).
  • The court reinforced previous rulings that the enforcement of traffic regulations, whether through physical barriers or signage, does not equate to a closure of the street ([para 17]) and highlighted that roads can remain open for some traffic while enforcing rules about others.

Thus, the court aligned with precedents that established that the traffic flow modifications—while impactful—did not comprise a legal closure in the sense outlined in section 67 because Mooki Street continued to serve as a thoroughfare for other traffic classes ([paras 20]).

C - Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court's ruling that the establishment of dedicated bus lanes on Mooki Street did not amount to a permanent closure or diversion as per section 67 of the Local Government Ordinance. The court confirmed that such regulatory measures aimed at enhancing public transport, even with resulting access difficulties for some businesses, were legally permissible under section 66, which allows for the closure of a street for particular classes of traffic.

The decision not only clarifies the definitions and practical applications of “closure” and “diversion” under the Ordinance but also reinforces the legal standing of local authorities in modifying traffic regulations for public transport systems, underlining the balance between urban development objectives and the protection of individual business interests ([paras 20]). The case upholds that legislative provisions must be interpreted in a manner that permits functionality and regulation of urban spaces without falling into the trap of unnecessarily stringent definitions that could impede public transport innovations.

In light of the above considerations, the appeal was dismissed with costs, including the costs associated with the employment of two counsel, solidifying the judicial interpretation regarding municipal powers and the implications for affected businesses in urban planning contexts ([para 3]).
