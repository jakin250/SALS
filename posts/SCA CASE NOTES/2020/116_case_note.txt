# Case Note: Municipal Employees’ Pension Fund and Others v Chrisal Investments (Pty) Ltd and Others [2020] ZASCA 116

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([1–10])

In the case before the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa, the Municipal Employees’ Pension Fund (MEPF) made an investment to acquire a 55% stake in a shopping centre business operating three shopping centres. This acquisition was completed for R550 million alongside a co-ownership agreement (COA) that provided a structured framework for the management and financial governance of the enterprise ([1]).

The co-owners were three entities (Chrisal Investments, Takou Investments, and Procprops 60, collectively referred to as the Adamax co-owners), which had initially owned the joint enterprise. Following the acquisition, the dispute arose when the Adamax co-owners sought to invoke the *actio communi dividundo*, a legal remedy permitting the division of common property, seeking the sale of the jointly owned properties ([2]).

The MEPF contested the applicability of the *actio communi dividundo*, arguing that the agreements (specifically the sale agreement and COA) had established a relationship of bound co-ownership that precluded the unilateral termination of the co-ownership of the shopping centres. The Gauteng Division of the High Court ruled in favor of the Adamax co-owners, thereby permitting the application of the *actio*. The MEPF appealed this decision, asserting that the high court misinterpreted the nature of the agreements and the rights arising from them ([3]–[4]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([2, 17, 18])

The principal legal issue before the court was whether the co-ownership established by the COA constituted free co-ownership, which would allow the invocation of the *actio communi dividundo*, or bound co-ownership, which precluded the invocation of this remedy. Specifically:

1. **Is the right to invoke the *actio communi dividundo* available to the co-owners of the shopping centre business?**
2. **Does the nature of the relationship established by the agreements create a situation of bound co-ownership?** ([2], [18]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([20–27])

The court primarily examined the principles governing co-ownership, distinguishing between free and bound co-ownership as articulated by prominent legal scholars ([20]). The determination hinged on whether the rights and obligations arising from the COA dictated the character of the ownership.

1. **Free Co-Ownership**: Characterized by the sole relationship between the co-owners being one of joint ownership, where any co-owner may request partition unilaterally ([20]).

2. **Bound Co-Ownership**: In contrast, this arises from an additional legal relationship, such as a partnership or contractual agreement that plays a role in regulating obligations concerning the property. Here, no co-owner can demand partitioning during the existence of that relationship ([21]–[22]).

3. The court also referenced the significant case law, including *Robson v Theron*, to underscore the limitations prescribing the availability of the *actio communi dividundo* in contexts of bound co-ownership ([39]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([48–63])

In its analysis, the court distinguished the obligations and relationships dictated by the COA from the mere ownership of the underlying properties ([48]). It determined that:

1. **Nature of the COA**: The central agreement characterized the relationship as one of joint management and operation of a business, which bore the hallmarks of a partnership. As such, the nature of the co-ownership could not be treated as free co-ownership ([57]–[62]).

2. The court rejected the notion that merely because co-owners could invoke *actio communi dividundo* at any point automatically characterized their relationship as free co-ownership. Instead, it emphasized that the context and agreements governing the ownership dictated the legal character of co-ownership ([56]–[61]).

3. Throughout the interpretation of the agreements, the court elucidated the importance of the comprehensive terms contained within the COA, which aimed to regulate their ongoing commercial relationship and excluded unilateral termination through the common law remedy of *actio communi dividundo* ([63]).

4. It further highlighted that any attempt by the Adamax co-owners to sidestep the terms of the COA in favour of the *actio* undermined the fundamental agreements governing their relationship ([26]–[63]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([64])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the high court erred in its characterisation of the co-ownership relationship as one amenable to the invocation of the *actio communi dividundo*. The judgment held the following substantive outcomes:

1. The nature of the relationship established by the COA was one of bound co-ownership, reflecting a contractual relationship that governed the rights and duties of the parties involved far beyond mere property ownership ([63]).
   
2. The appeal was upheld, the high court's order was set aside, and the application for the partition of the properties was dismissed, emphasizing the binding nature of the agreements made in relation to the co-ownership of the Letting Enterprise ([64]).

### Implications

The ruling reinforces the legal understanding of co-ownership in commercial dealings, particularly highlighting the paramount importance of agreements establishing the relationship between co-owners. It potentially sets a precedent for future disputes concerning the operation and management of jointly owned enterprises in South Africa, indicating that contractual obligations may trump common law rights where the nature of ownership is expressly specified in agreements. 

Overall, this judgment underscores the necessity for clear and comprehensive agreements when entering into co-ownership arrangements, ensuring that the rights and responsibilities of parties are adequately defined and protected within the commercial context.
