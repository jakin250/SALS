Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

## FIRAC Case Note: Speaker of the National Assembly and Others v New Nation Movement NPC and Others

### F – Facts

This case arises from a prior decision of the Constitutional Court in *New Nation Movement II*, where the court declared that the Electoral Act 73 of 1998 was unconstitutional, as it only permitted eligible South Africans to stand for election through their membership of a political party. This ruling was made on 11 June 2020, and the court suspended the declaration of invalidity for a period of 24 months to allow Parliament to amend the legislation ([1]–[2]).

The suspension period expired on 10 June 2022. On 26 April 2022, Parliament (represented by the Speaker and the Chairperson of the National Council of Provinces) sought an extension of this suspension for an additional six months, until 10 December 2022. The application was supported by the Minister of Home Affairs and the Electoral Commission, while it was opposed by the first and second respondents, New Nation Movement NPC and Ms. Chantal Dawn Revell, who argued that the application was dilatory and demonstrated a lack of political will to effect needed changes ([5]–[7]).

The application was met with further complications, as the respondents filed counter-applications on 3 June 2022, after the objections period had closed ([8]–[9]). Given the urgency of the matter, this case was determined without oral argument ([8]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal question for the court was whether it should grant an extension of the suspension period, considering the surrounding circumstances, including the prior failure of Parliament to take timely action to amend the Electoral Act as mandated ([9]).

Secondary issues included:
1. Whether the counter-applications by the first and second respondents should be entertained despite their late submission.
2. The implications of delaying the implementation of electoral reforms on the rights of independent candidates and the upcoming elections ([10]–[22]).

### R – Rules

Key legal principles involved in this case included:
- The power of the Constitutional Court to extend declarations of invalidity under section 172(1)(b) of the Constitution, which allows for just and equitable remedies ([60]).
- Prior rulings, such as *Electoral Commission v Minister of Cooperative Governance* and *Ex parte Minister of Social Development*, which stress that extensions should not be granted as a matter of course, particularly not at the last moment ([40], [60]).
- The requirement for Parliament and the Executive to demonstrate good faith efforts to comply with the court's orders and the interests of justice in facilitating compliance ([24]).

### A – Application

In its judgment, the court considered the reasons proffered by Parliament for the delay, noting the complexities of the legislative process and the involvement of multiple stakeholders, including the Minister who had not introduced the necessary Bill in a timely fashion ([61]–[64]). 

The court assessed the urgency of the application and acknowledged that while the situation was primarily produced by Parliament’s own delays, it nonetheless had to act decisively to prevent a breach of the court’s order ([59]–[60]). 

The arguments from New Nation and Revell highlighted substantial concerns regarding adequacy of public participation and the implications for independent candidates’ rights. However, the court found that Parliament had provided a sufficient plan to address the constitutional defects within the requested extension timeframe, thus allaying some of these concerns ([62]–[70]).

The court ultimately determined that while Parliament had not conducted itself in an ideal manner, the overriding interests of justice warranted the granting of the extension. Therefore, the court extended the suspension of the declaration of invalidity until 10 December 2022, allowing time for the necessary amendments to the Electoral Act to be made ([72]).

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ruled unanimously to grant the extension of the suspension period until 10 December 2022, allowing Parliament sufficient time to amend the Electoral Act. The judgment emphasized the necessity of compliance with court orders for the functioning of South Africa’s constitutional democracy. The decision reinforces the court’s responsibility to ensure upcoming elections are conducted in a manner compliant with constitutional standards, particularly regarding the rights of independent candidates ([73]–[81]).

The ruling has significant implications, reinforcing legislative accountability and the constitutional imperatives surrounding electoral reform, especially in light of the upcoming 2024 elections, when independent candidates must have the opportunity to participate effectively ([66], [70]). This case sends a clear message to Parliament about the necessity of timely action in legislative reforms to avoid breaches of constitutional obligations.
