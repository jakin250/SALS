Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2008

# FIRAC Case Note: **Wightman v Headfour (Pty) Ltd (66/2007)** [2008] ZASCA 6

### F – Facts:
The appellant, John Cecil Wightman, is a building contractor who entered into a construction agreement with Headfour (Pty) Ltd (the first respondent) for work on property owned by the first respondent in Hout Bay, represented by its director, Archar Head (the second respondent) ([paras 1–2]). Disputes over payment and alleged defective workmanship arose, leading to the first respondent taking possession of the property and employing other contractors to continue the work ([paras 2–5]). On 17 August 2004, Wightman filed an application for restitution and ejectment, claiming spoliation given that he had not authorized the entry of the other contractors ([paras 5, 30]).

The Cape High Court initially dismissed Wightman’s application in March 2005, although he was granted leave to appeal ([para 7]). The appeal was subsequently dismissed by the Full Bench in September 2006, leading to the present appeal before the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 7]).

### I – Issues:
The principal legal question is whether the respondents unlawfully despoiled the appellant of his possession of the property, thereby constituting spoliation ([para 8]). This encompasses several sub-issues: 
1. **Possession** - Did Wightman retain possession of the property despite handing over duplicate keys?
2. **Dispute of Fact** - Was there a real, genuine or bona fide dispute of fact regarding Wightman’s alleged possession and the circumstances surrounding the delivery of the duplicate keys ([para 11])?

### R – Rules:
The law concerning spoliation dictates that a person in peaceful possession may not be dispossessed without their consent unless through unlawful means ([para 27]). Key legal precedents cited include Nino Bonino v De Lange 1906 TS 120, which articulates that dispossession may be achieved without violence or fraud as long as it is against the consent of the possessor. The court also referenced the principles established in *Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd* [1984] 3 SA 623 and *Ripoll-Dausa v Middleton NO* [2005] 3 SA 141, dictating that where there are disputes, the applicant’s version must be upheld unless the opposing party's account shows a real and bona fide dispute ([paras 12–13]). 

### A – Application:
The Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed the facts surrounding the appellant's possession and the nature of the agreements made between the parties. The court found that although the appellant had provided duplicate keys to allow inspections, the original keys remained with him, demonstrating an intention to retain ultimate control over the property ([paras 26–28]).

Moreover, the court emphasized that the respondents' actions, specifically allowing others to occupy the property while denying the appellant access, amounted to unlawful possession ([paras 28–30]). The court concluded that there were no genuine disputes of fact that could warrant rejecting the appellant’s version, including his assertion of having maintained possession through control measures like having a guard at the site ([paras 20–22]).

### C – Conclusion:
The court held in favor of the appellant, determining that he had been unlawfully despoiled of his possession on 12 July 2004, when he was denied entry to the property despite having rightful possession ([para 30]). It thus overturned the lower court's judgment, providing clear directives for restoring possession back to the appellant contingent on security being provided by the respondents ([paras 31–32]). The ruling underscores the importance of protecting the rights of possessors against unlawful dispossession and clarifies that possession may not be equated with the mere handing over of keys, particularly under circumstances suggesting intent to maintain control ([paras 27–29]). 

This judgment illustrates crucial principles in property law, particularly in relation to builder’s liens and the protections available to parties in possession against spoliation.
