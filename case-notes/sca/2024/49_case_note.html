FIRAC Case Note

Case Title: Gensinger and Neave CC & Others v Minister of Mineral Resources and Energy (223/2023) [2024] ZASCA 49
Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Date: 15 April 2024
Coram: Mokgohloa, Mbatha and Weiner JJA and Coppin and Bloem AJJA
Heard: 16 February 2024

F – Facts ([paras 1–6])

The appellants, a group of four entities holding site and retail licences under the Petroleum Products Act 120 of 1977, operate petroleum outlets in the central business district of Matatiele. In May 2019, two new applicants, the respondents, sought similar licences to retail petroleum products in the same vicinity. The appellants objected, arguing that the new outlet would ‘cannibalise’ their existing sales, contravening the efficacy and viability requirements stipulated in the Act.

Despite these objections, the Controller of Petroleum Products approved the respondents' applications on 8 March 2021, prompting the appellants to appeal this decision to the Minister of Mineral Resources and Energy within the designated period. The appellants asserted that their appeal should suspend the Controller’s decisions pending its determination. However, the Controller and respondents contended that the Act did not provide for such a suspension.

Fearing operational impacts from the new retail outlets before the appeal could be resolved, the appellants sought interdictive relief in the KwaZulu Natal Division of the High Court. The High Court dismissed the application, ruling that the appellants lacked the necessary standing, as they were not ‘directly affected’ by the Controller's decision—this set the stage for the appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal.

I – Issues ([paras 1, 6])

The case presents two critical legal questions:

  1. Whether the holders of site and retail licences are 'directly affected' by the Controller’s approval of new site and retail licences in their area, as per section 12A of the Petroleum Products Act.
  2. Whether an appeal to the Minister against the Controller’s decision suspends that decision pending the resolution of the appeal.

R – Rules ([paras 11–12, 18–30])

The legal framework governing this case comprises the Petroleum Products Act and its associated regulations. Section 12A(1) establishes the right to appeal for any person "directly affected" by the Controller's decisions. The Act aims to ensure efficient operation and commercial viability within the petroleum industry, promoting economic opportunities and transformation as outlined in sections 2B and 2C.

Concerning administrative decisions, the common law principle applies: an appeal generally suspends the decision unless expressly stated otherwise. This presumption holds unless the legislation explicitly indicates it does not apply. Relevant case law highlights that standing can arise from direct commercial interests affected by administrative decisions.

A – Application ([paras 15–30])

The court employed a purposive interpretation of the term ‘directly affected’ in section 12A, contrasting the objection stage—which allows broad public participation—with the appeal stage, restricted to those with a direct stake in the outcome. The appellants argued that their existing operations would be undermined by the new competitors, thereby qualifying as “directly affected."

The appellants successfully demonstrated, through expert testimony and previous Ministerial decisions, that the establishment of competing outlets would negatively impact their sales volumes, job sustainability, and overall business viability. The court found the evidence substantiated their claims of imminent material loss, fulfilling the criteria of direct affectation.

On the issue of suspension, the court recognized the absence of provisions within the Act that override the common law principle and upheld the notion that an appeal to the Minister suspends the Controller’s decision pending finalization. Hence, the appellants’ appeal was deemed to have suspended the issuance of the new licences, reinforcing the need for judicial discretion in regulating competitive fairness and economic considerations in the petroleum sector.

C – Conclusion ([paras 31–35])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the appellants, affirming that they are 'directly affected' by the Controller's decision to issue new retail licences. It was declared that the common law's automatic suspension of administrative decisions upon appeal remains applicable under the Petroleum Products Act. Consequently, the appellants retained their right to appeal, effectively suspending the Controller's decisions regarding site and retail licences pending the outcome of their appeal. The court ordered costs against the respondents and returned the application regarding interim interdicts to the High Court for further consideration.

In summary, the decision emphasizes the principles of administrative law, particularly regarding the rights of parties affected by administrative decisions, reinforcing the importance of economic viability and competitive fairness in regulatory frameworks. The verdict not only affirms principles of standing but also reasserts the significance of the common law's role in administrative procedure, furthering the broader goals of sustainable economic development in South Africa's petroleum industry.
