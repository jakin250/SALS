FIRAC Case Note: J E Mahlangu and Another v Minister of Police [2021] ZACC 10

F – Facts

This case involves an application for leave to appeal against the ruling of the Supreme Court of Appeal, specifically regarding the liability of the Minister of Police for the unlawful detention of the applicants, J E Mahlangu and I T Mailela N.O. (as the representative of the deceased estate of Mr Phanie Johannes Mtsweni). The unlawful detention arose from an incident on 25 May 2005, where four family members were brutally murdered, resulting in the arrest of Mr Mahlangu on 29 May 2005 without proper basis or the advising of his rights as mandated by the Constitution ([1]-[4]).

Following his arrest, Mr Mahlangu was subjected to torture in order to extract a false confession regarding his involvement in the murders. This forced confession led to the police arresting Mr Mtsweni the next day, and both were denied bail at their first court appearance on 31 May 2005 based on this confession. Their situation worsened as their prosecutor was misled by the police, relying on the false confession without knowledge of its dubious origins ([5]-[8]). Both applicants were detained until their release on 10 February 2006, after the actual perpetrators were arrested and subsequently convicted ([8]-[10]).

The applicants filed for damages due to their wrongful arrest and detention, claiming amounts to cover both emotional trauma and loss of income ([10],[11]). The High Court initially granted limited damages for the period of their detention leading up to their first court appearance but denied claims for the full detention period ([12]). The applicants appealed, which led to the Supreme Court of Appeal determining that the Minister was not liable for the entirety of the detention after the first court appearance ([15]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions arose regarding the liability of the Minister of Police for damages resulting from the applicants' detention. The specific issues discussed include whether the unlawful arrest and the subsequent false confession impacted the entirety of the detention period and whether the applicants’ failure to apply for bail absolved the Minister from proving the lawfulness of the subsequent detention ([1]).

R – Rules

This case hinged on the constitutional rights afforded by Section 12(1) and Section 35(1)(c) of the South African Constitution, which safeguard individuals from arbitrary deprivation of freedom and ensure the right to a fair trial ([21]). The court adhered to principles of delictual liability established in cases such as Zealand and Woji, which articulate that once unlawful detention is shown, the onus shifts to the state to justify such detention. Previous rulings emphasized that conduct that violates an individual's rights, especially in the context of unlawful arrest and detention, can give rise to a claim for damages ([32]-[34]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court found that the unlawful arrest of both applicants initiated a chain of violations that led to their subsequent detentions. Importantly, it pointed out that despite the Magistrate's remand orders, the police had a continuous obligation to disclose all relevant facts, including the manner in which the confession was obtained ([34]-[38]). The court analyzed the previous ruling from the Supreme Court of Appeal and concluded that shifting the burden of proof onto the applicants undermined established jurisprudential principles and created a novel and erroneous test ([46]).

The Court dismissed the argument that a failure by the applicants to apply for bail justified the Minister's non-liability after the first court appearance. The continuous concealment of the false confession's nature and the officers' misconduct directly connected to the unlawful detention reinforced the applicants' claims for the entirety of the detention period, which was unduly prolonged due to police misconduct ([35]-[42]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court held that the Supreme Court of Appeal erred in its decision to grant limited liability to the Minister for the damages incurred by the applicants during their period of unlawful detention. It recognized the severity of the police’s misconduct in both the initial arrest and subsequent actions that perpetuated the unlawful detention. The appeal was thus upheld, awarding Mr Mahlangu R550,000 and Mr Mtsweni R500,000, along with costs and interest from the High Court's judgment date. The decision not only illustrates the importance of accountability in law enforcement actions but also sets a precedent reaffirming the protection of fundamental rights under the Constitution ([49]-[57]).

This case serves as a crucial reminder of the state's responsibility in safeguarding individual freedoms and the overarching principle that unlawful acts by the police cannot be overlooked, especially in matters of personal liberty.
