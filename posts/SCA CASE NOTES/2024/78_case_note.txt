Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# Case Note: Botha N O and Others v Jonker and Others (1003/2022) [2024] ZASCA 78

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts

The case involves the liquidation of Jonker Products CC, initiated by the Land and Agricultural Bank of South Africa (the Land Bank), which launched a provisional winding-up application on 23 July 2020 ([para 3]). Following various proceedings, a final order of liquidation was granted on 29 October 2020, and several liquidators were appointed on 21 October 2020, with letters of appointment issued by the Master on 9 November 2020 ([para 3]). The liquidators summoned the first meeting of creditors on 6 May 2021 without obtaining the necessary consent of the Master, as required by section 78(1) of the Close Corporations Act, 69 of 1984 (the Close Corporations Act) ([para 4]).

The meeting was plagued by procedural irregularities, including the issuance of faulty notices regarding the first meeting. Consequently, the respondents were not present, leading to the Land Bank being the sole creditor who proved a claim ([para 4]). After requesting the requisite consent from the Master ex post facto, the liquidators were informed that there was no provision permitting such consent after the meeting had been held ([para 6]). The respondents sought legal recourse, resulting in the Free State Division of the High Court declaring the first meeting invalid and all resolutions adopted therein set aside ([para 10]).

The liquidators' counter-application argued that the Master’s consent was a mere formality and that even if absent it should not invalidate the meeting ([para 9]). On 30 March 2022, the High Court affirmed the invalidity of the meeting, emphasizing the peremptory nature of the consent requirement ([para 10]). The leave to appeal was granted, limited to specific orders of the High Court ([para 13]).

### I – Issues

Two central legal issues were at stake:

1. **Whether the liquidators acquiesced to the High Court's order, thereby waiving their right to appeal.** This preliminary query considered the conduct of the liquidators in summoning a subsequent meeting per the High Court's directives and their admission of the transformative nature of the initial judicial decision ([para 14-17]).

2. **The substantive question of the interpretation of section 78(1) of the Close Corporations Act** regarding whether the Master has the authority to grant retrospective consent for a meeting convened beyond the statutory time frame ([para 14, 19]).

### R – Rules

The court examined the relevant legal framework:

- **Section 78(1) of the Close Corporations Act**, which mandates that a liquidator must summon a meeting of creditors not later than one month post a final winding-up order, except with the Master's consent ([para 27]). This provision is pivotal as it stipulates the procedural requirements critical for the legitimacy of the meeting.

- **Precedents** such as *Capitec Bank Holdings Limited and Another v Coral Lagoon Investments 194 (Pty) Ltd and Others* ([2021] ZASCA 99), and similar interpretative rules from *Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality* ([2012] ZASCA 13), which underscore the necessity of interpreting statutes purposefully and in context ([para 26]).

- Additional statutory provisions governing liquidation processes, such as the 1973 Companies Act and the Insolvency Act, underpinning procedural compliance ([para 19]).

### A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal delved into various aspects regarding the interpretation of the statutory framework governing liquidations. First, the court found that the liquidators did not waive their right to appeal, as the evidence did not indicate clear acquiescence, and their actions contesting the High Court’s findings suggested an intention to uphold their appeal ([para 17]).

Regarding section 78(1), the court acknowledged that the text allows for the Master to grant consent either before or after the meeting is summoned, but pertinent considerations must include that the consent cannot bar retrospective validation of actions already taken during an invalid meeting ([para 39-40]). The majority opinion clarified that to interpret the section as precluding post-meeting consent would unduly restrict the Master’s powers to oversee the liquidation process effectively, suggesting they may retain the capacity to validate meetings held in good faith despite procedural errors ([para 38]).

The dissenting opinion, however, stressed the need for strict adherence to statutory consent requirements, arguing that the interpretation providing for post-facto consent could weaken the Master's regulatory role and potentially lead to uncertainty during liquidations ([para 46-61]).

### C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the High Court's findings regarding the invalidity of the first meeting of creditors due to the lack of prior consent from the Master. It clarified that while the court would not permit the Master to grant consent ex post facto after actions had already been taken within a meeting, it allowed that consent could permissibly be obtained post-summoning but prior to holding the meeting ([para 41]).

The judgment therefore holds significant implications for the regulation of liquidation processes within South African company law, emphasizing the balance between procedural integrity and administrative practicality in close corporation liquidations. The decision highlights the essential need for liquidators to act in compliance with statutory requirements while ensuring that creditor interests are adequately represented and protected. The costs of the appeal were directed to be borne by the liquidators, further reinforcing accountability within the liquidations ([para 41]).

These proceedings emphasize the critical need for liquidators to obtain the proper authorizations as required, showcasing the interpretative nuances of corporate governance frameworks in liquidations under South African law.
