Supreme Court of Appeal

Esquire Consulting and Marketing CC v Sea Glades Holdings (Pty) Ltd

Citation: Esquire Consulting and Marketing CC v Sea Glades Holdings (Pty) Ltd (1315/2016) [2017] ZASCA 167 (30 November 2017)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 167 (30 November 2017) Court: Supreme Court

Citation: Esquire Consulting and Marketing CC v Sea Glades Holdings (Pty) Ltd (1315/2016) [2017] ZASCA 167 (30 November 2017)

Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

Coram: Ponnan, Bosielo, Leach and Mathopo JJA and Ploos van Amstel AJA

F – Facts

The case revolves around a dispute regarding the zoning of erf 3306 Sea Vista, situated within the Kouga Municipality, and its intended use for business purposes ([1]). The appellants, comprising property owners and trustees of trusts in Marina Village, sought to interdict the respondents from operating a restaurant on erf 3306, claiming that the property's use was unlawful under the current town planning scheme ([2], [3]).

In December 2015, the appellants filed an application to the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court for such an interdict, alleging that a restaurant was under construction in violation of the zoning by-laws. The appellants targeted the first and second respondents—who were developing the property—alongside the fourth respondent, Eastern Cape Liquor Board, for issuing a liquor license without proper zoning ([3], [4]).

The property originally formed part of a larger agricultural-zoned area, and the respondents had applied for simultaneous subdivision and rezoning to facilitate development. While subdivision was approved in December 2001, the zoning for certain erven, including erf 3306, was deferred for further details ([5], [6]). This led to subsequent applications for rezoning, with approval finally granted on 23 September 2004 ([10], [11]).

Despite ongoing development activities on the property, including infrastructure installation and canal construction, disputes between the developers and local landowners regarding rights to the moorings led to tensions ([12], [13]). The appellants argued they had not been made aware of the zoning for business purposes until 2015, leading to their application for an interdict to prevent the establishment of the restaurant ([14]).

The court of first instance dismissed the appellants' application for an interdict, leading to their appeal ([4]). The complexities of the case involved interpretations of the Land Use Planning Ordinance 15 of 1985 (LUPO), specifically regarding zoning regulations and what constituted 'utilization' of land for business purposes ([19]).

I – Issues

The key legal issues in this appeal were:

  1. Zoning and Utilization: Whether erf 3306 was lawfully zoned for business purposes under the LUPO, and if so, whether the two-year utilization requirement of section 16(2)(a)(i) of LUPO applied ([1], [14]).
  2. Interdict on Grounds of Illegality: Whether the appellants had established a clear right to prevent the respondents from constructing the restaurant due to alleged violations of the zoning scheme ([14], [24]).

R – Rules

The court relied upon several legal principles and precedents, including:

  • Land Use Planning Ordinance 15 of 1985 (LUPO): Specifically, sections concerning simultaneous applications for subdivision and zoning (sections 16(2)(a), 22(1)), and provisions regarding the confirmation of subdivisions (section 27) ([6], [8]).
  • Principles of Administrative Law: The administrative decisions of the municipality must be respected unless legally challenged, particularly where no issues were raised against the prior council decisions approving the developments ([8]).
  • Utilization Definition: Under LUPO, the definition of 'utilization' includes improvements for authorized land use, not just active business use ([20]).
  • Precedent: Referenced Cool Ideas 1186 CC v Hubbard & another 2014 (4) SA 474 (CC) regarding the requirements for zoning and subdivision under LUPO ([16]).

A – Application

The court undertook a thorough analysis of the procedural history and facts leading to the approval of sowohl subdivision and subsequent rezoning applications. The appellants contended that the initial resolution to subdivide did not lawfully include erf 3306, as zoning was deferred ([16], [18]). However, the court found that the 2004 resolution on zoning was, in effect, an extension of the original application process and thereby valid.

The court also evaluated the substantive arguments concerning 'utilization'. The appellants’ claim that the property had not been used as a shop or restaurant until November 2015 was juxtaposed against evidence of ongoing construction and infrastructure development on the erf that satisfied the business use criteria within the two-year period following the 2004 approval ([19], [20], [23]).

Ultimately, the court concluded that the respondents had indeed undertaken steps to utilize erf 3306 for business purposes as authorized, which meant that no violation of the zoning laws had occurred. Therefore, the appellants failed to demonstrate a clear legal right to the interdict they sought ([24]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court's ruling ([24]). It established that the process for rezoning and subdivision complied with LUPO, and the actions undertaken by the respondents constituted sufficient utilization of the property for business purposes. The dismissal reaffirmed the significance of valid municipal decisions and the requirements for establishing grounds for interdicts in land-use disputes.

This case underlines the necessity for clarity in municipal zoning procedures and emphasizes that once a property is rezoned, the improvement or utilization of said property aligns with permitted business activities is legally protected unless expressly contested in accordance with procedural norms.
