Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

### Case Note: Sabdia N O and Another v Soma and Another (845/2023) [2024] ZASCA 174

### FIRAC Analysis

#### F – Facts

The case revolves around the executors of the estate of the late Dr. Mahamed Faruk Sabdia, specifically the first and second appellants, Shiraz and Riaz Sabdia. Dr. Sabdia initiated review proceedings against the first respondent, Aniel Kanjee Soma, in 2006 regarding a property in Marabastad, Pretoria, but passed away on 5 November 2013 before the case concluded. Following his death, the executors were appointed and represented the estate in ongoing litigation, which included an eviction application brought by Soma against the estate.

The eviction application was contested successfully, resulting in a cost order awarded on a punitive scale in favour of the executors, who were represented by their firm, Mothle Jooma Sabdia Incorporated (MJS). Subsequently, in September 2019, MJS submitted a bill of costs to the Taxing Master for taxation. However, the Taxing Master disallowed professional fees claimed by Shiraz Sabdia, contending that the executor's remuneration encompassed all work performed, which excluded remuneration for services rendered in a professional capacity (paras [2]–[4]).

The executors challenged this decision, leading to review proceedings that were dismissed by the Gauteng Division of the High Court. Their appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal was predicated on discontent with this dismissal, arguing that the High Court misinterpreted the executor's remuneration entitlement as stipulated in the Last Will and Testament of Dr. Sabdia and the Administration of Estates Act [paras [5]–[6]].

#### I – Issues

The case presents critical legal questions regarding:

1. Whether an executor, who is also an attorney acting in a professional capacity, is entitled to receive remuneration for legal services rendered on behalf of the estate, despite provisions in the Last Will and Testament.
2. Whether the Taxing Master had the discretion to disallow such fees based on the interpretation of the executor’s duties and the applicable statutes ([paras [1]–[2]]).

#### R – Rules

The legal authorities relevant to these issues include:

1. **Section 51(1)(a) of the Administration of Estates Act 66 of 1965**: This section mandates that executors are entitled to remuneration as prescribed by the deceased in their Will.
2. **Estate Fawcus v Van Boeschoten and Lorentz [1934] TPD 94**: This case established that executors who are also attorneys cannot claim additional fees for professional services rendered on behalf of the estate.
3. **Nedbank Limited v Gordon N.O and Others [2019] ZAGPPHC 460** and **Die Meester v Meyer en Andere 1975 (2) SA 1 (T)**: These cases further elucidated the nature of executor's remuneration and its boundaries ([paras [8]–[9], [20]]).

The principles of testamentary freedom and fiduciary duty also underpin the examination, highlighting the extent to which a testator's intention can dictate the entitlement of remuneration.

#### A – Application

In deciding the case, the Supreme Court of Appeal emphasized the paramount importance of the specific language used in Dr. Sabdia's Will. The remuneration clause distinctly allows executors to bill for professional services rendered, thus contradicting the previous rulings which were primarily based on the precedents established in the Fawcus case that do not account for explicit consent outlined in the will. 

The court applied interpretative principles stemming from **Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality [2012] ZASCA 13**, which asserts that the interpretation of legal documents must consider context, clarity, and the document's purpose ([paras [13]–[15]]). It asserted that the remuneration clause explicitly sanctioned the charging of professional fees for legal work performed, which was overlooked by the High Court.

The judges noted that Dr. Sabdia intended to ensure that his executors could charge for their professional services, even when serving dual roles, defeating the rationale behind the Taxing Master’s initial ruling ([paras [16]–[19]]). They distinguished this matter from prior cases by highlighting that the will itself had provisions enabling professional fee retention, thus falling under the exception to the general rule established in Fawcus.

#### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal determined that the High Court's interpretation was flawed, particularly in its rigid adherence to the precedents without recognizing the unique circumstances presented by Dr. Sabdia’s Last Will. The Court ruled in favor of the executors, asserting their right to professional fees as explicitly allowed in the Will. Thus, it overturned the Taxing Master's decision and directed that the bill of costs be re-enrolled for appropriate taxation on an attorney-client scale.

The implication of the judgment reinforces the principle of testamentary freedom, affirming that testators have the authority to provide for executors in such a manner that their professional contributions are recognized monetarily, even when they fulfill fiduciary roles ([paras [22]–[25]]). This case underscores the importance of clear testamentary provisions and their role in defining the limits of executor compensation within the framework of South African law.
