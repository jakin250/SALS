Supreme Court of Appeal

The Chairperson, Eastern Cape Gambling and Betting Board and Another v Vukani Gaming Eastern Cape (Pty) Ltd and Others [2021] ZASCA 180

The matter pertains to three consolidated applications for special leave to appeal against the judgment and order of the full court of the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, Grahamstown.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 180

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–5])

The matter pertains to three consolidated applications for special leave to appeal against the judgment and order of the full court of the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, Grahamstown. The primary parties involved include Vukani Gaming Eastern Cape (Pty) Ltd (“Vukani”), which holds a significant position as one of the two licensed route operators for limited payout machines (LPMs) in the Eastern Cape province, and the Eastern Cape Gambling and Betting Board (the “Board”) that issued a request for proposals (RFP) for independent site operator licenses.

In June 2015, the Board commissioned a study that suggested licensing an additional 4000 LPMs, leading to the publication of the RFP in September 2017. Following hearings, the Board awarded licenses to several applicants, including the Golden Palace group, which sought special leave to appeal against Vukani's subsequent application to review the decision of the Board issuing the RFP and awarding licenses. The full court found that the Board failed to comply with Regulation 59(3) of the Eastern Cape Gambling Regulations, which deemed the RFP unlawful, compelling Vukani to seek a rectification of this judgment in the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 2–5]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 6–11])

The issues central to the appeal arise from the interpretation of Regulation 59(3) of the Eastern Cape Gambling Regulations. The first issue, termed the “saturation question,” involves understanding the requirement for the Board to affirmatively show that licensing would not lead to over-saturation of LPMs within the province. Here, the court was tasked with determining whether “satisfaction” is to be measured subjectively by the Board or whether such satisfaction should be evaluated objectively against the province's realities ([para 7]).

The secondary issue, referred to as the “timing question,” queries at what stage compliance with the stipulations of Regulation 59(3) is necessary: at the issuance of the RFP or only at the time when licenses are granted. The applicants contended that compliance with regulatory requirements was necessary only at the license granting stage, while Vukani asserted that these requirements applied at the RFP phase. Conclusively, Vukani’s review challenges both the interpretation of over-saturation and the legality of the RFP process ([paras 8–9]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 12–33])

The decision predominantly hinges on Regulation 59(3) of the Eastern Cape Gambling Regulations, mandated by s 80 of the Eastern Cape Gambling and Betting Act 5 of 1997. The relevant paragraph specifies prerequisites for the Board’s power to issue additional licenses, notably requiring an assessment of whether the issuance would lead to an over-saturation of LPMs.

Precedents cited include Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality, which underscores the need for a contextual interpretation of statutory provisions ([para 13]), and Capitec Bank Holdings Limited v Coral Lagoon Investments 194 (Pty) Ltd, which reinforces the necessity of understanding terms based on the statutory framework and the provisions' intended outcomes ([para 13]).

In applying these interpretative guidelines, the court emphasized the essentiality of establishing a rational basis for the Board’s conclusions concerning over-saturation, alongside satisfying regulatory conditions required for licensing LPMs ([paras 18–23]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 34–44])

The court found Vukani's interpretation flawed upon evaluating the requirements of Regulation 59(3). In particular, it recognized that the statutory provision mandates an aggregative assessment of saturation across the province rather than localized assessments, thereby permitting the licensing authority to conclude that additional machines would not precipitate an over-saturation condition ([paras 32–34]).

The Board’s reliance on the earlier study was found to be within reasonable bounds, despite identified flaws, as it afforded a rational basis to inform its decision-making process. The criticisms raised by Vukani’s expert were deemed insufficient to undermine the study fundamentally. Notably, the study’s conclusion was not assessed as wholly irrational or devoid of evidence that could potentially substantiate the issuance of an RFP ([para 44]).

Thus, as the Board met its obligations under Regulation 59(3) and the appeal successfully argued that the full court erred in its judgment, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeals and dismissed Vukani’s review ([paras 48–49]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 47–49])

The Supreme Court of Appeal granted special leave to appeal, upheld the appeals of the Golden Palace and Spin and Win applicants, and dismissed Vukani’s review of the RFP. The court concluded that the Board had sufficiently complied with the criteria set out in Regulation 59(3), particularly regarding the saturation assessment conducted on a province-wide basis.

This judgment clarifies critical aspects of regulatory compliance in the gambling licensing framework in South Africa, underscoring the Board’s discretion in interpreting over-saturation while ensuring its determinations remain justified within existing legal parameters. The decision resolves pivotal questions surrounding the application of regulatory standards and sets a precedent for future assessments relating to gambling licenses, reinforcing the need for balancing regulatory oversight while accommodating market demands in the gambling sector ([paras 47–49]).

The implications of this ruling extend to regulatory bodies, licensed operators, and stakeholders within the Eastern Cape and beyond, emphasizing the importance of sound analytical frameworks and the merits of substantial evidence when addressing issues of saturation within contingencies of the gambling industry.
