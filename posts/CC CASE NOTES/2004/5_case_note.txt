Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2004

## FIRAC Case Note: Samuel Kaunda and Others v The President of the Republic of South Africa

### F – Facts
The case involves 69 South African citizens, the applicants, who were arrested in Zimbabwe on various charges after allegedly planning to provide military assistance in Equatorial Guinea ([1]). The applicants were concerned about the possibility of being extradited to Equatorial Guinea, where they feared an unfair trial and a potential death sentence ([3]). They initially approached the High Court in Pretoria seeking orders that would compel the South African government to intervene on their behalf and protect their constitutional rights during their detention ([3–4]).

### I – Issues
The central legal issues presented by the applicants included:
1. Whether the South African Constitution imposes a duty on the government to seek diplomatic protection for its citizens detained abroad and the implications of such a duty regarding fair trials and treatment ([6], [12]).
2. Whether the applicants could compel the government to act on their behalf and take steps to prevent their extradition to Equatorial Guinea ([4], [17]).

### R – Rules
The court's considerations were guided by several legal rules:
1. **Constitutional Provisions**: Sections 7(2) and 3(2) of the South African Constitution, which mandate the state to "respect, protect, promote and fulfil the rights in the Bill of Rights" and recognizes the rights, privileges, and benefits of citizenship ([31], [4]).
2. **International Law Principles**: Customary international law regarding diplomatic protection, as expounded in the Barcelona Traction case, posits that states have the right but not the obligation to protect their nationals against breaches of international law by foreign states ([23–24], [47]).
3. **Judicial Precedents**: The court analyzed previous rulings, particularly the Mohamed case, which highlighted the obligation of the government to prevent the death penalty for its citizens and considered the nature of government duties in foreign relations ([46–50]).

### A – Application
In addressing the issues, the court examined whether the applicants could assert a right to diplomatic protection under the Constitution. The application focused on whether South Africa has a responsibility to act on behalf of its nationals facing potential gross violations of human rights in a foreign state ([66–67]).

1. **Duty to Protect**: The court noted that while the Constitution does not explicitly stipulate a right to diplomatic protection, the government does have a duty to consider requests for protection as a reflection of its commitment to human rights. This includes the obligation to make representations on behalf of its citizens facing serious violations abroad ([102–104], [90–91]).

2. **Urgency and Necessity**: The court emphasized the significant risk the applicants faced if extradited to Equatorial Guinea. It noted that the South African government is not obligated to extradite the applicants but must act to protect them from potential human rights violations if they seek such protection ([85–86], [90–93]).

3. **Manner of Intervention**: The court underscored the government's broad discretion in foreign affairs and diplomatic relations. It posited that while the government has an obligation to consider such protection requests, it remains the prerogative of the executive to determine when and how to act, based on foreign policy considerations ([105–106], [210–212]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately concluded that the applicants' claims lacked the legal basis for forcing the government to take specific actions regarding diplomatic protection and extradition. It held that while the applicants have a right to request governmental assistance under their constitutional rights, whether or not the government acts on those requests falls under the scope of executive discretion ([144], [152], [207]).

1. **Holding**: The application for leave to appeal was granted; however, the appeal was dismissed, affirming the High Court's order ([145], [144]). The court clarified that while South African nationals may seek protection, the state's decisions in these matters are subject to constitutional review but retain significant leeway in diplomatic affairs without the court imposing specific obligations upon the government to act in particular ways in foreign relations ([10–11]).

2. **Implications**: The judgment clarifies the limits of constitutional rights for citizens abroad, emphasizing the complex interplay of domestic and international law, and asserts the significance of executive discretion in diplomatic matters ([108, 130]). The implications also extend to how future claims for diplomatic protection may be structured, highlighting the need for clarity in claims and the timing of requests for intervention ([267–268]).
