Source Folder: 2004

# Case Note: Jabulane Billyboy Sithole v The State [2004] ZASCA 90

---

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts

The appellant, Jabulane Billyboy Sithole, was convicted in the regional court of Villiers, Free State, for dealing in 160 kilograms of dagga. The regional magistrate valued the dagga at R160,000, which invoked the minimum sentence provisions of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997. However, the magistrate also found substantial and compelling circumstances, thereby sentencing Sithole to seven years’ imprisonment. Following an unsuccessful appeal in the High Court, Sithole sought relief from the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) regarding both his conviction and sentence ([paras 1-3]).

The arrest occurred on November 15, 1999, during a police roadblock on the N3 highway. Inspector Masondo sensed a strong smell of dagga upon approaching Sithole’s vehicle, leading to its search, which yielded multiple bags of dagga concealed within. During the trial, Sithole claimed ignorance of the contents, arguing that the car had been taken without his knowledge by his nephew, Themba Mlambo, who had loaded the dagga as part of another task. This account was contradicted by the physical evidence and the testimony of the inspector, leading to the regional magistrate’s conclusion that Sithole was guilty beyond a reasonable doubt ([paras 2-6]).

### I – Issues

The legal issues presented to the SCA included:

1. Whether the appellant was properly convicted of dealing in dagga based on the evidence presented.
2. The appropriate interpretation of "value" in the context of minimum sentence legislation, and whether the value of the seized dagga exceeded the R50,000 threshold required for a minimum sentence ([paras 12-16]).
3. Whether the substantial and compelling circumstances justified the imposition of a lesser sentence than the minimum ([para 20]).

### R – Rules

The legal framework included several key statutes and precedents:

1. **Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 140 of 1992**: Section 5(b) makes it an offense to deal in dependence-producing substances, including dagga. 
2. **Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997**: This act dictates minimum sentences for drug offenses, specifying that a minimum of 15 years applies when the value exceeds R50,000 unless there exist substantial and compelling circumstances ([para 1]).

Notably, the precedent in *S v Legoa* established that "value" must be determined based on market conditions relevant to the dealer, not merely the potential street value of the substance ([paras 13-14]).

### A – Application

The SCA undertook a thorough analysis of the regional and high court's proceedings regarding the appellant's conviction. The Court found the evidence presented at trial sufficient to support the conviction. It evaluated the improbabilities in Sithole’s narrative, notably his claimed lack of awareness regarding the dagga's presence, suggesting that this was untruthful. Consequently, it agreed with the magistrate’s credibility assessment of the appellant and the police inspector ([paras 5-11]).

Turning to the value of the seized dagga, the SCA criticized the prior courts for valuing the dagga at R1 per gram, emphasizing that the appropriate valuation should take into account the bulk nature of the drugs and the market context at the time of seizure. They concluded that the State had not adequately proven the required R50,000 threshold as the value of 160 kg of dagga could only be accurately assessed regarding its potential as a bulk sale, not as individually packaged distributions ([paras 12-19]).

### C – Conclusion

The SCA dismissed the appeal against the conviction, reaffirming the findings on the appellant's credibility and the evidence supporting his culpability. However, the Court found merit in the appeal against the sentence, setting aside the magistrate's seven-year sentence due to the misapplication of the minimum sentencing legislation regarding the value of the dagga. Consequently, they imposed a new sentence of four years' imprisonment, considering the appellant's personal circumstances and the nature of the offense, including prior case comparisons ([paras 20-21]).

**Order**:
1. The appeal against conviction is dismissed.
2. The appeal against sentence succeeds.
3. The sentence imposed by the regional court is set aside, and the appellant is sentenced to four years’ imprisonment.

**Judges**: E Cameron, Conradie JA, Van Heerden JA ([paras 24-25]).
