# Case Note: Baloyi v Public Protector and Others [2020] ZACC 27

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–6])

This case centers around Louisah Basani Baloyi (the applicant), a former Chief Operations Officer in the Office of the Public Protector (the first respondent). Baloyi was employed on a five-year fixed-term contract starting from February 1, 2019, which included a six-month probation period. Upon the conclusion of the probation period, Baloyi received a letter on October 21, 2019, from Vussy Mahlangu (the third respondent), announcing the termination of her employment due to unsatisfactory performance.

Baloyi applied to the High Court for urgent relief, arguing that her termination was unlawful and contravened her constitutional rights under Section 181(2) of the Constitution. She sought a declaratory order to invalid the termination, along with a review of the decision based on alleged constitutional and contractual violations. The High Court dismissed her application on jurisdictional grounds, claiming it should have been brought before the Labour Court. Baloyi subsequently sought leave to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court regarding the jurisdictional issue.

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 9–11])

The case primarily raises the question of whether the High Court holds jurisdiction over claims pertaining to the unlawful termination of a fixed-term employment contract. Specifically, it examines if Section 157(1) of the Labour Relations Act (LRA) confines the Labour Court’s exclusive jurisdiction over such matters, thereby excluding the High Court. Additionally, the court faced the jurisdictional challenge regarding the nature of the claims raised by Baloyi—whether they were strictly labour-related or included constitutional claims that might fall within the High Court's remit.

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 12–16, 21–30])

The Constitutional Court, in interpreting the LRA, cited precedent cases such as Gcaba v Minister for Safety and Security [2009] ZACC 26 and Chirwa v Transnet Limited [2007] ZACC 23, which address the jurisdictional distinction between the Labour Court and the High Court. The Court noted that the exclusive jurisdiction of the Labour Court is limited to disputes expressly arising under statutes, primarily the LRA and its associated provisions. 

The principles elucidated in these cases clarified that although the Labour Court had exclusive jurisdiction over specific employment disputes, concurrent jurisdiction exists under Section 157(2) of the LRA and Section 77(3) of the Basic Conditions of Employment Act (Employment Act) concerning violations of fundamental rights arising from employment relations.

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 34–49])

The Constitutional Court analyzed Baloyi's claims, determining that they had dual bases: contract law and public law. The crux of Baloyi's case rested on whether her dismissal constituted a simple breach of contract or something more significant that implicated her constitutional rights. The Court noted that the High Court misinterpreted the jurisdictional boundaries set forth in previous rulings, incorrectly categorizing Baloyi's claims as purely labour-related.

The Court stated that while the LRA provides specific remedies for unfair dismissal, it does not extinguish other common law rights that may arise from the employment relationship. Since Baloyi asserted that her contract was terminated outside the terms agreed upon, her claim for breach of contract fell within the purview of the High Court’s jurisdiction. The Court emphasized that claims must be interpreted based on the factual allegations and legal basis provided in the applicant's pleadings.

Furthermore, the Court concluded that Baloyi’s claim of unlawful termination based on the public protector’s failure to meet constitutional obligations also falls within the High Court’s jurisdiction. Thus, the Court held that Baloyi was not compelled to confine her claims to the Labour Court despite the potential for some claims to be exclusively adjudicated there.

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 50–52])

The Constitutional Court upheld Baloyi’s appeal and concluded that the High Court erred in asserting that it lacked jurisdiction to hear the case. The Court ordered a remittal of the matter to the High Court for a hearing de novo, signifying that the lower court would consider the merits of Baloyi's claims without bias from earlier proceedings. Additionally, the Court awarded costs in favor of Baloyi, positioning her against the first respondent for her successful jurisdictional challenge. 

This ruling has significant implications, as it delineates the boundaries of jurisdiction between the High Court and Labour Court, reaffirming that employment-related claims can arise under both the common law and constitutional frameworks, thereby granting claimants greater flexibility in seeking redress.
