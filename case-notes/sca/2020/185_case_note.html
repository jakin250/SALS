Eskom Holdings SOC Limited v Resilient Properties (Pty) Ltd and Others; Eskom Holdings SOC Limited v Sabie Chamber of Commerce and Tourism and Others; Thaba Chweu Local Municipality and Others v Sabie Chamber of Commerce and Tourism and Others

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020

F – Facts

This matter involves a trilogy of appeals concerning Eskom Holdings SOC Limited's (Eskom) decisions to interrupt bulk electricity supplies to two municipalities, Emalahleni Local Municipality (ELM) and Thaba Chweu Local Municipality (TCLM), due to non-payment of electricity debts ([1]-[4]). Eskom is a state-owned entity responsible for supplying electricity across South Africa and is the only licensed entity eligible to sell electricity to municipalities ([11], [12]).

Resilient Properties (Pty) Ltd, Changing Tides 91 (Pty) Ltd, and other businesses, reliant on electricity primarily from ELM, sought relief against Eskom's decision, asserting that they had fulfilled their obligations to the municipality but suffered from interrupted electricity supply due to the municipalities’ failures to pay their debts to Eskom ([7]-[9]). The municipalities faced severe financial stress, leading to their designation as delinquent in fulfilling their obligations ([2]).

Eskom's rationale for the interruptions was to mitigate escalating debt. It announced planned interruptions during designated times, allowing for public representations, yet largely ignored the stakeholders' concerns ([18]-[19]). Legal action followed in the High Court, where separate applications sought to contest the legality and constitutionality of Eskom's decisions ([20]).

The High Court found that Eskom acted unconstitutionally by not adhering to their obligations under sections 40 and 41 of the Intergovernmental Relations Framework Act 13 of 2005 (IRFA), which require all state organs to resolve disputes amicably before litigation ([28]-[36]).

I – Issues

The main legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:

  1. Whether Eskom's interruption of electricity constituted a legitimate exercise of its powers under section 21(5) of the Electricity Regulation Act 4 of 2006 (ERA) without a court order.
  2. Whether the dispute between Eskom and the municipalities fell within the ambit of intergovernmental disputes as outlined in section 41 of the Constitution and whether they adhered to the necessary cooperative governance principles.
  3. Assessing if Eskom's actions were irrational and whether they took into account relevant considerations regarding public welfare and service delivery.
  4. **Whether the Thaba Chweu Local Municipality should bear the costs awarded against them in the High Court for the litigation initiated by the Chambers ([38]-[40]).

R – Rules

Several key legal principles and legislative provisions were invoked in addressing the issues surrounding Eskom's actions, including:

  • Section 21(5) of the ERA, which permits the supply interruption due to a customer’s failure to pay ([49])-([50]).
  • Section 41 of the Constitution, mandating cooperative governance amongst all spheres of government. This section asserts that all state organs must exhaust alternative dispute resolution mechanisms before resorting to the courts ([41]-[62]).
  • Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA), particularly section 6(2)(e)(iii), which provides grounds for reviewing decisions that fail to consider relevant factors ([90]).
  • Various Municipal Acts that impose duties on municipalities to provide essential services, including electricity, to their residents ([28]-[34]).

A – Application

The court evaluated whether Eskom adhered to both robust legislative frameworks and constitutional mandates in executing its power to interrupt supply. It confirmed that Eskom's decisions to disrupt electricity were grounded in escalating debt, but concluded that these were implemented without proper adherence to cooperative governance principles outlined in the Constitution and IRFA ([61]-[80]).

The court highlighted that Eskom's actions effectively penalized municipalities and indirectly their residents, contradicting the constitutional obligation to provide basic services. The court convincingly stated that Eskom could not invoke self-help measures without first exhausting available remedies in resolving disputes with the municipalities ([39]-[82]).

The engagements Eskom had with local authorities were insufficient, lacking the robust mediation processes required by law. The court's conclusions aligned with the directive principles of cooperative governance, emphasizing the need for collaborative efforts over unilateral actions ([62]-[64]). The court firmly established that Eskom’s interpretation of section 21(5) as permitting broad interruptions, without court oversight, was incompatible with overarching constitutional values pertaining to service delivery and community welfare ([45]-[58]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Eskom's appeals in both Eskom I and Eskom II, underpinning the significance of adhering to the Constitution and legislations governing intergovernmental relationships, especially within municipal contexts ([93]).

However, it upheld the appeal in Thaba Chweu Local Municipality, emphasizing that the high court had erred in its costs order against the municipality, which had remained largely passive in the litigation ([97]-[108]). The judgment underscores a vital legal precedent that in cases involving state organ interactions and their obligations, cooperative governance principles must prevail to protect the functional capacity and responsibilities of local governments and avoid punitive measures that disrupt essential services for communities.

This decision affirms that state entities must engage constructively to resolve financial disputes, rather than resorting to measures that undermine constitutional obligations and citizens' rights to basic services, reflecting the court's commitment to uphold constitutional values and cooperative governance ([93]-[96]). The implications of this case extend beyond Eskom and the municipalities involved, reinforcing the need for accountable intergovernmental relations in South Africa's constitutional framework.
