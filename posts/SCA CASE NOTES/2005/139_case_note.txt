Source Folder: 2005

# FIRAC Case Note: Inventive Labour Structuring (Pty) Ltd v Dennis Corfe ([2005] ZASCA 92)

### F – Facts

The case centers on a deed of suretyship involving the appellant, Inventive Labour Structuring (Pty) Ltd, and the respondent, Dennis Corfe. In September 2004, the appellant initiated an action in the High Court at Pietermaritzburg seeking rectification of the suretyship agreement and payment of R240,119.93, inclusive of interest and costs ([1]). The appellant contended that on 5 August 2003, Corfe executed a deed of suretyship that mistakenly identified him as the principal debtor, instead of D & R Distributors CC, the actual debtor ([2]). 

The respondent failed to defend the action, prompting the appellant to apply for a default judgment alongside the requested rectification of the contract. However, the court a quo, presided over by Theron J, declined to grant rectification based on precedent, specifically citing **Republican Press Ltd v Martin Murray Associates CC** and **Nuform Farmwork and Scaffolding (Pty) Ltd v Natscaff CC** ([3]). With leave, the appellant appealed this decision.

### I – Issues

The central legal issues presented in this case are:

1. Whether the deed of suretyship was valid per the formal requirements mandated by the General Law Amendment Act 50 of 1956 ([4]).
2. If invalid, whether rectification of the deed could be granted to reflect the common intention of the parties, particularly given the mistake in identifying the principal debtor ([12]).

### R – Rules

The case is governed primarily by the **General Law Amendment Act 50 of 1956**, specifically section 6, which stipulates that:

- No contract of suretyship entered into post-enactment shall be valid unless recorded in a written document signed by the surety ([4]).
  
The interpretation of “terms” in this context includes the identification of the creditor, principal debtor, and surety. Additionally, insights from established precedents illuminate that if a contract can be construed in multiple ways, a court should favor an interpretation that preserves validity, as noted in previous rulings such as **Intercontinental Exports (Pty) Ltd v Fowls** ([5]-[10]) and **Nedbank Ltd v Van Zyl**.

### A – Application

In assessing whether the suretyship was valid, the court noted that while the document did state the identities of the creditor, principal debtor, and surety, the identical names of the surety and principal debtor created ambiguity. This dual interpretation risked invalidating the contract if the court determined that Corfe could not stand surety for his own debt ([9]). 

However, the alternative interpretation—that the surety and principal debtor were distinct entities with identical names—would preserve the contract's validity under the statutory requirements. Drawing from **Intercontinental Exports**, the court underscored a presumption favoring the valid interpretation of contracts, thereby establishing that the suretyship was valid “[11]”.

Regarding the rectification issue, the court acknowledged the common intention of both parties to ensure Corfe acted as surety for D & R Distributors CC, for which the incorrect identification in the deed constituted a factual mistake justifying rectification. The appellate court elucidated that the basis for rectification was adequately demonstrated and the refusal by the court a quo was erroneous ([12]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the rectification of the deed of suretyship should be granted. The ruling clarified that the contract could be construed as compliant with the formal requirements of the General Law Amendment Act, and thus valid, serving to reflect the parties’ original intent ([14]). 

The court overturned the earlier decision, stipulating that rectification would involve substituting “Dennis Corfe” with “D & R Distributors CC” in the suretyship agreement. Furthermore, it ordered the payment of the stipulated sum along with interest, while determining no costs were to be awarded against the respondent due to the absence of opposition and the prior misunderstanding of applicable legal precedent ([16]).

This ruling illustrates the importance of accurately capturing the parties' intentions in legal agreements, especially in matters concerning suretyship, and reinforces the courts’ willingness to correct written agreements to align with the genuine intent of contracting parties.
