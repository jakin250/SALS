Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# CASE NOTE: Matshivha v The State (656/12) [2013] ZASCA 124 (23 September 2013)

## F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([1]–[3])

In the case of *Matshivha v The State*, Jeffrey Khathutshelo Matshivha (the appellant) faced charges of rape and murder in the Limpopo High Court at Thohoyandou. The charge of rape pertained to an incident involving a seven-year-old girl (the complainant) in Magau Village on 5 January 2000. The State alleged that Matshivha raped the complainant, and alternatively, contravened section 14(1)(a) of the Immorality Act 23 of 1957 by having sexual intercourse with a minor under 16. Additionally, he was charged with the murder of Mukilasi Gideon Ramavhoya (the deceased), alleging that Matshivha stabbed him on 22 July 2000.

Matshivha pleaded not guilty to both counts but was found guilty and subsequently sentenced to life imprisonment for rape and 45 years for murder. The appeal focused primarily on the state’s evidence regarding identification and the administration of oaths concerning child witnesses.

The appeal record revealed ambiguity concerning whether the procedures followed complied with sections 162 and 164 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, specifically regarding the administration of the oath for the child witnesses, the complainant, and her brother ([3]).

## I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([3]–[5])

The key issues raised in this case involved:

1. **Compliance with Statutory Requirements**: Did the trial court properly follow the procedural dictates of sections 162 and 164 of the Criminal Procedure Act concerning the admission of child witness evidence? Specifically, was there an adequate enquiry into the witnesses' understanding of the oath, thereby ensuring their evidence was admissible?

2. **Reliability of Witness Evidence**: Considering the reliance on the identification evidence of the complainant and her brother, was this evidence sufficient to sustain a conviction, particularly in view of the cautionary rule applied to child witnesses?

3. **Evidentiary Issues in Murder**: Given the contradictory nature of the eyewitness testimony concerning the appellant’s involvement in the deceased’s stabbing, was there sufficient credible evidence to maintain the conviction for murder?

## R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([8]–[11])

The legislative framework pertinent to this case includes:
- **Sections 162 and 164 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977**: These sections govern the requirement for witnesses to take an oath and the necessary conditions under which a child’s testimony can be admitted without an oath.

- **Case Law**: The court cited precedents emphasizing the procedural nuances necessary when handling child witnesses. In particular, the judgement referred to *Director of Public Prosecutions, Transvaal v Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development & others*, where the court stressed the need for a reliable understanding of truth-telling by child witnesses for their evidence to be deemed admissible ([12]).

- **Cautionary Rule**: It was necessary for the trial court to apply the cautionary rule regarding the evidence of a single child witness, which reinforces the need to scrutinize such evidence carefully before relying on it as a basis for conviction ([22]).

## A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([11]–[35])

The Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized the trial court’s handling of child witness testimony and observed that the procedure employed was flawed. The trial judge did not conduct an adequate enquiry to establish whether the witnesses understood the nature of the oath or could distinguish between truth and falsehood ([12]–[14]). 

The court concluded that without compliance with the statutory provisions, the evidence of the complainant and her brother lacked the necessary legal status, rendering it inadmissible. The identification evidence of the complainant was particularly tenuous, as it was unclear whether she had a clear opportunity to observe the perpetrator, especially considering the circumstances of the incident, namely the time of night, the lack of adequate illumination, and the emotional distress of the child at the time. Further, her brother's attempts to identify the appellant were hampered by his inability to see the assailant’s face ([16]–[23]).

The discrepancies in the identification process were highlighted, including the fact that the complainant’s subsequent claims regarding her assailant’s identity (identified as "Godi" during her initial accounts) could not firmly link the appellant to the crime. Thus, the court concluded that there was insufficient evidence to uphold the rape conviction ([25]).

Turning to the murder charge, the appellate court again found contradictions in the witness testimonies. The bartender’s narrative was not consistently supported by other evidence, with conflicting accounts impeding the reliability of his testimony. The court emphasized that the appellant's version—which involved a struggle leading to the deceased's accidental fall against a window—was reasonable and warranted serious consideration, particularly given the discrepancies in the medical evidence regarding the wound ([26]–[35]).

As a result of these evidentiary issues and procedural failures, the Supreme Court concluded that both convictions were unsustainable.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([36])

The Supreme Court of Appeal allowed the appeal and set aside both the convictions and sentences for rape and murder. The court identified a significant failure related to the administration of the oath to child witnesses, leading to an inability to rely on their testimony. The decision underscores the paramount importance of procedural adherence in criminal trials, especially those involving vulnerable witnesses like children.

The ruling also emphasizes the necessity for careful judicial assessment regarding the reliability of evidence presented in cases hinging on identification and the implicit rights of the accused to receive a fair trial based on credible evidence. In broader terms, this decision reaffirms the legal standards required for the prosecution to establish guilt beyond a reasonable doubt and serves as a reminder of the potential tragic consequences of flawed evidentiary procedures.

The case has implications for future cases involving child witnesses, reinforcing the need for structured and careful judicial enquiry into their capacities to understand and convey the truth. This decision serves as guidance for lower courts, ensuring the rights of individuals to a fair trial are protected against erroneous convictions based on unreliable witness testimony.
