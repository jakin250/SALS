# Case Note: Andrew Machele and Others v William Marofane Mailula and Others

**Court**: Constitutional Court of South Africa   
**Case No**: CCT 99/08  
**Judgment Date**: 26 March 2009  
**Citation**: [2009] ZACC 7  

---

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts

The matter arose from an urgent direct access application by Andrew Machele and 61 other applicants for leave to appeal against a High Court eviction order ([1]–[5]). The applicants were residents of Angus Mansions, a block of flats in Johannesburg, and included vulnerable groups such as people with disabilities, elderly individuals, and children. Mr. William Marofane Mailula, the first respondent, obtained an eviction order from the South Gauteng High Court on 5 November 2008, which authorized his eviction of the applicants ([3]–[4]).

The eviction was contested on two fronts: the validity of the sale of the property from Philani-ma-Afrika (68th applicant) to Mr. Mailula, the purported owner, was in dispute ([9]–[10]). Furthermore, concerns were raised regarding the lack of regard for the provisions of section 26 of the Constitution, which ensures the right to adequate housing and protects against arbitrary evictions, as well as the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE) ([13]–[15]).

The High Court had granted Mr. Mailula the right to evict the applicants, despite their appeal against the order, leading to this urgent application for a suspension of the eviction order ([3]–[5]).

### I – Issues

The main legal issues considered by the Constitutional Court included:

1. **Whether the applicants had the right to appeal against the High Court's interim execution order concerning the eviction, given the provisions of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959** ([18]–[23]).
2. **Whether the case raised a constitutional matter, thereby justifying the intervention by the Constitutional Court** ([26]).
3. **Whether the applicants demonstrated a risk of irreparable harm if the eviction order was executed before the final determination of the appeal** ([27]–[33]).

### R – Rules

The legal framework involved multiple key provisions:

- **Section 26 of the Constitution**: This section provides that everyone has the right to access adequate housing and protects against arbitrary eviction ([13]).
- **PIE (Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998)**: Designed to ensure that any eviction process considers fairness and constitutional values ([15]).
- **Section 20 of the Supreme Court Act**: Governs the conditions under which appeals against interim orders can be made ([18]–[19]).

Historical references were made to the case of *Port Elizabeth Municipality v Various Occupiers* [2004] ZACC 7, which emphasizes the necessity of interpreting legal provisions in light of constitutional values ([14]).

### A – Application

The Court undertook a thorough analysis of whether an interim execution order could be appealed. It became evident that while interim orders are generally seen as non-appealable, this principle could be circumvented where a constitutional issue was raised and irreparable harm demonstrated ([19]–[24]).

1. **Constitutional Matter**: The Court affirmed that an eviction from one’s home is inherently a constitutional issue, as it invokes rights protected under section 26 ([26]). The conditions under which eviction orders are issued were scrutinized to ensure compliance with constitutional safeguards.

2. **Irreparable Harm**: The Court found that the applicants would indeed suffer irreparable harm if the eviction order were executed. This harm was not trivialized despite the applicants not being considered the "poorest of the poor." Losing one's home represents a dignity-related infringement that transcends socio-economic status. Conversely, the Court noted that Mr. Mailula's claims of irreparable harm were minimal and could not offset the severe consequences for the applicants ([29]–[33]).

3. **Interim Execution Order Appeal**: The Court ultimately decided to suspend the eviction order, recognizing the need for preventing a judgment that could lead to significant injustices while the appeal was adequately reviewed ([37]).

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court, therefore, ordered that the execution of the eviction order granted by the High Court be suspended pending the final determination of the appeal in the Supreme Court of Appeal. Furthermore, it referred the question of the appealability of the interim execution order to the Supreme Court of Appeal for further adjudication ([40]). The decision marked an important affirmation of the constitutional right to housing, reinforcing the need for courts to adhere strictly to constitutional principles in eviction matters. This case illustrated the delicate balance between property rights and the protection of vulnerable communities within the South African legal landscape. 

The ruling serves not only as a precedent for similar eviction cases but also underscores the essential role of the judiciary in safeguarding human rights against arbitrary state and private actions.
