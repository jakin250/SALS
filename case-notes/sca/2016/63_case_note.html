Neutral Citation: Nova Property Group Holdings v Cobbett (20815/2014) [2016] ZASCA 63 (12 May 2016)

Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

Coram: Maya AP, Majiedt, Mbha JJA, Plasket and Kathree-Setiloane AJJA

Heard: 1 March 2016

Delivered: 12 May 2016

FIRAC Analysis

F – Facts

This matter involves an appeal concerning the statutory right of access to the securities registers of three companies—Nova Property Group Holdings Ltd, Frontier Asset Management & Investments (Pty) Ltd, and Centro Property Group (Pty) Ltd—by Moneyweb (Pty) Ltd and Mr. Julius Peter Cobbett. Cobbett, a financial journalist, was commissioned by Moneyweb to investigate and report on the shareholding structures of these companies, which were linked to the controversial Sharemax property syndication scheme ([1]–[2]).

Cobbett submitted requests to the Companies on 24 July 2013 for access to their securities registers per s 26(2) of the Companies Act 71 of 2008 (the Companies Act). When his requests were refused, Moneyweb initiated proceedings in the Gauteng Division of the High Court to compel compliance with the request ([3]). As the Companies continued to delay, they launched an interlocutory application seeking certain documents from Moneyweb to interrogate the motives behind the access request, citing concerns that Moneyweb was executing a 'sinister agenda' against them ([4]–[5]).

The High Court partially granted the Companies' application for discovery but did not grant the full relief sought, leading to the appeal ([5]–[6]). The court ruled on the proper interpretation of s 26(2) of the Companies Act, ultimately determining its implications for the rights of access to the securities register ([6]).

I – Issues

The appeal raised two main legal issues:

  1. Is the interlocutory order of the High Court appealable? Given the nature of the application, the court had to determine whether the order met the criteria for appealability as defined under existing precedents (Zweni v Minister of Law and Order) and subsequently adjusted by Moch v Nedtravel (Pty) Ltd and Philani-Ma-Afrika v Mailula & others ([7]–[8]).
  2. Does s 26(2) of the Companies Act confer an unqualified right of access to the securities register? This question required the court to analyze the scope and nature of the right of access described in the Companies Act and whether it is subject to limitations or conditions concerning the motives behind the request ([7]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal frameworks include:

  • Companies Act 71 of 2008, specifically s 26(2), which establishes the right to access a company's securities register.
  • Promotion of Access to Information Act 2 of 2000 (PAIA), which outlines the general framework for accessing information but was argued by the Companies to impose limitations on s 26(2).
  • Case Law: The court referenced several cases, including:
  • Zweni v Minister of Law and Order [1992] ZASCA 197, regarding the criteria for appealability ([8]).
  • Bernstein & Others v Bester NO & Others [1996] ZACC 2, discussing the transparency and accountability obligations of companies ([16]).
  • Previous conflicting decisions on the interpretation of access rights, including La Lucia Sands Share Block Ltd v Barkhan and Bayoglu v Manngwe Mining (Pty) Ltd ([9]).

A – Application

The court applied the aforementioned legal principles by first affirming the appealability of the order, emphasizing the importance of resolving legal uncertainties around s 26(2) concerning the interpretation of rights and public interest ([10]–[11]).

In its interpretation of s 26(2), the court concluded that it confers an unqualified right of access to securities registers, indicating that the legitimacy of the requester’s motives was irrelevant to providing access. The court noted that both the text of the Companies Act and its legislative history suggest that the right intended by Parliament was absolute, without burdening it with applicable motives or the provisions of PAIA ([15]–[20]).

The court also noted that the companies' reliance on PAIA provisions to justify refusals lacked substantive grounding, reaffirming that PAIA's grounds of refusal do not apply to direct requests under s 26(2) ([22]–[28]). The court criticized the Companies for attempting to import limitations based on motives or subjective considerations into this statutory right, prioritizing the principles of transparency and the public's right to information ([36]–[48]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, emphasizing that s 26(2) of the Companies Act provides an unequivocal right of access to any person requesting it, thereby reinforcing the rights to freedom of expression and access to information ([49]). The Companies had failed to demonstrate any legitimate basis for withholding the requested documents, and their objections were characterized as attempts to restrain press freedom under the pretext of protecting company interests ([50]).

The judgment establishes that accessing information about company securities is pivotal for journalistic integrity, transparency, and accountability, rejecting any argument that privileges the Companies’ subjective concerns over statutory rights.[^1]

[^1]: Nova Property Group Holdings Ltd v Cobbett (20815/2014) [2016] ZASCA 63.
