Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: Notito v The State (123/11) [2011] ZASCA 198

### F – Facts 

The case revolves around Ndoyiso Paulos Notito, the appellant, who was convicted in the Free State High Court on multiple accounts of rape (seven counts), theft (eight counts), and indecent assault (two counts). The incidents giving rise to these charges occurred over two years, from May 2005 to August 2007, across various locations in the Free State province, including Welkom, Kroonstad, and Odendaalsrus ([1]–[2]).

The modus operandi of the appellant involved targeting vulnerable women, convincing them that he was a prophet, and alleging that they had been bewitched. He would request personal items, such as hair from their head, armpit, and pubic areas, as part of a supposed healing process. This often led to sexual assault, during which the women would lose consciousness or be otherwise incapacitated ([2]).

During the trial, the State presented testimonies from several complainants, medical evidence, and procedural information related to the arrest and identification of Notito. In the end, the trial court convicted the appellant, imposing a cumulative sentence of 120 years, which was later reduced to an effective 28 years of imprisonment by taking into account the cumulative effect of the sentences ([3]–[4]).

Leave to appeal was granted specifically regarding the convictions for rape (counts 1, 2, 6, 9, and 14), theft (count 3), and indecent assault (count 17) ([4]).

### I – Issues

The key legal questions raised on appeal included:

1. Whether the State proved its case beyond a reasonable doubt regarding the charges of rape in counts 1 and 2, specifically concerning the identification of the appellant as the offender ([5]).
2. Whether the charge of theft (count 3) had been proven ([5]).
3. Whether the complainants had given tacit consent through their conduct regarding the charges of rape (counts 6, 9, and 14) and indecent assault (count 17) ([5]).

### R – Rules

The legal framework applied in the case involves several principles pertinent to criminal law and specifically sexual offences:

- The definition of rape under the common law served as the basis for the charges. At the time of the alleged offenses, a male person could be convicted of rape if he unlawfully and intentionally had sexual intercourse with a female without her consent ([6]).
- Consent must be free, voluntary, and consciously given. If the complainant is incapable of consenting due to unconsciousness or prevailing misconceptions, the validity of consent is nullified ([6], [9]).
- The law emphasizes that circumstantial evidence must be assessed in its totality, requiring that inferences drawn must align with all established facts ([16]).

Significant precedents cited include *R v K* ([6], [8]) and *S v Reddy* ([16]), which elucidate the nature of consent and the assessment of circumstantial evidence in sexual assault cases.

### A – Application

1. **Counts 1 and 2 (Rape)**:
   The court evaluated the evidence of the complainants, particularly focusing on whether they provided consent. The first complainant, Ms. Diemo Nkoata, testified that she lost consciousness and regained awareness to find physical signs indicating sexual intercourse had occurred ([10]–[12]). The court noted that the absence of consent was clear since the complainant was incapacitated, thus supporting the trial court's conviction based on the circumstantial evidence regarding the appellant being the only male in the vicinity ([17]).
   
2. **Count 3 (Theft)**:
   Similar to the issue of identification in the rape charges, the court considered Ms. Kutu's testimony about her belongings being taken after interacting with the appellant ([18]–[22]). The court emphasized that the circumstantial evidence established a connection between the appellant and the theft, affirming the trial court's conviction.

3. **Counts 6, 9, 14 (Rape) and Count 17 (Indecent Assault)**:
   The appellant's defense of tacit consent was prominently challenged. The complainants testified under similar deceptive pretenses as prior cases, asserting that they did not provide real consent, which aligned with the established legal standards defining valid consent ([25]–[39]). The court reinforced the notion that duress, fraud, or deception nullifies the existence of consent ([44]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal confirmed the trial court's convictions on all counts, asserting that the State had sufficiently established the appellant's guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. The judgments reinforced critical legal principles surrounding consent, identifying the modus operandi of the accused and evaluating circumstantial evidence as a cohesive narrative.

The implications of this judgment reaffirm the importance of protecting vulnerable individuals from sexual offences, emphasize the significance of consent, and the necessity for courts to provide thorough examinations of evidence in rape cases. Consequently, the appeal was dismissed in its entirety, and the appellant's convictions and sentences were upheld ([47]).
