Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note

**Title**: Phasha v Minister of Police (70/2016) [2017] ZASCA 006  

**Court**: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  

**Date Heard**: 21 February 2017  

**Date Delivered**: 10 March 2017  

**Coram**: Ponnan, Theron, Petse, Zondi and Van der Merwe JJA  

---

## F – Facts

The appellants, sixteen individuals, sought leave to appeal against a decision of the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which had dismissed their claims for damages from the Minister of Police due to alleged unlawful arrest, detention, and assault ([1]). These individuals were employed by the Makhado Municipality and were part of the South African Municipal Workers Union (SAMWU). On 15 August 2011, a national protected strike was initiated by SAMWU, and regulations concerning picketing were established ([2]).

On 23 August 2011, amidst a breakdown in communication regarding the authorized route for protestors, approximately 400 workers decided to march to the civic center. The police were deployed and blocked the march, leading to tensions and altercations. This resulted in several arrests, including the arrest of the eleventh applicant, Resimati Samuel Hlongwane, who was already facing issues related to a medical condition ([4]-[10]).

Throughout the subsequent trial in the magistrate's court, the evidence was split. The police witnesses, including Captain Kroucamp, testified that disturbance and unlawful actions occurred at the civic center, justifying arrests under the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, specifically sections relating to public violence ([14]). Conversely, the applicants presented their narrative denying any unlawful actions, claiming they were wrongfully arrested and assaulted ([6]-[10]).

The trial court ultimately sided with the police's version, dismissing the applicants' claims. The applicants appealed to the High Court, which affirmed the trial court's findings ([1]-[12]). It was upon this unfavorable outcome that the applicants sought special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal, relying on section 16(1)(b) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 ([1]).

---

## I – Issues

The primary issue for determination was whether the applicants provided sufficient grounds to warrant special leave to appeal, which typically requires demonstrating special circumstances ([13]). The specific legal questions revolved around:

1. Whether there were substantial points of law raised that merited the appeal.
2. Whether the applicants showcased meaningful prospects of success against the factual findings of the lower courts ([13]-[14]).

---

## R – Rules

The application for leave to appeal is governed by the principles explained in *Westinghouse Brake & Equipment (Pty) Ltd v Bilger Engineering (Pty) Ltd* 1986 (2) SA 555 (A), which stipulates that special circumstances are required to grant leave for appeal, particularly when the matter holds significant legal or public relevance ([13]). Furthermore, the principles from *S v Boesak* 2000 (3) SA 389 (SCA) clarify that the considerations for leave to appeal differ from those applied during an actual appeal ([13]).

Under section 40(1)(a) and (b) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, police officers are authorized to arrest without a warrant if a member of the police has reasonable grounds to suspect that an offense is being committed, particularly public violence in this context ([14]).

---

## A – Application

The court analyzed the evidence mainly presented during the trial and the credibility of both the police and the applicants. The magistrate had made factual determinations based on the testimonies provided, which were upheld by the appellate court. The Supreme Court of Appeal reiterated the importance of adhering to the factual findings of lower courts unless there was material misdirection ([12]).

The court found the police's version of events credible and consistent, particularly regarding the justification for the arrests, viewing the applicant's denial of any unrest as improbable. The allegations of assault made by applicants were found largely unsupported by medical evidence, casting doubt on their credibility ([15]). Furthermore, inconsistencies in the testimonies of the applicants concerning their involvement in the protests and their treatment during the arrest were noted ([15]).

The court concluded that the factual findings of the trial court were not only supported by credible evidence but that the applicants had failed to show any significant legal question or special circumstances that would warrant an appeal. Consequently, the likelihood of success based on the factual disputes was deemed poor, which is crucial when considering an application for leave to appeal ([14]-[16]).

---

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the application for special leave to appeal, concluding that the applicants did not present a case for exceptional circumstances that would merit further scrutiny. The court underscored that the factual findings from the trial were robust and that the applicants' claims of wrongful arrest and assault lacked credible evidence. Costs were awarded in favor of the respondent, as is customary in cases where applications for leave to appeal fail ([17]).

**Implications**: This judgment highlights the stringent evidential requirements for claims of unlawful arrest and detention, particularly in public order situations. It underscores the court's reliance on the factual findings of trial judges, emphasizing that the prospects of success in an appeal must be substantial to warrant further judicial consideration. This case reaffirms the challenges applicants face in cases based primarily on conflicting evidence where the trial court has thoroughly weighed the testimonies presented.
