Andrew Barney August v The State (962/2022) [2023] ZASCA 170

The appellant, Andrew Barney August, was convicted in the Magistrates’ Court of Kimberley on 20 September 2006 for dealing in drugs, specifically 100 Mandrax tablets, under section 5(b) of the Drugs and Drugs Trafficking Act 140 of 1992.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 170

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The appellant, Andrew Barney August, was convicted in the Magistrates’ Court of Kimberley on 20 September 2006 for dealing in drugs, specifically 100 Mandrax tablets, under section 5(b) of the Drugs and Drugs Trafficking Act 140 of 1992. He was sentenced to four years’ imprisonment on 4 October 2006. The conviction stemmed solely from evidence obtained through entrapment, as he sold drugs to an undercover agent ([1]).

Following his conviction, August applied for leave to appeal, which was denied by the trial court on 30 March 2007. His subsequent petition to the Northern Cape High Court was also refused on 10 June 2008, and a further application to this Court was similarly dismissed on 10 September 2009. Although leave to appeal was granted on 10 March 2011, the appellant did not timely prosecute the appeal, attributing the delay to the negligence of his appointed Legal Aid attorney ([2]–[4]).

Over 12 years after the initial leave to appeal was granted, the appellant applied for (i) condonation for the late filing of the notice of appeal and (ii) permission to adduce further evidence in the appeal against his conviction. The delay in filing the appeal was substantial, with the appellant having completed his sentence long before the current proceedings ([4]).

I – Issues

The pivotal issues in this case included:

  1. Whether the appellant could obtain condonation for the late filing of the notice of appeal given the significant delay and the reasons provided for it ([5]).
  2. Whether the appellant’s application to introduce further evidence was properly grounded and relevant to the intended appeal ([4]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal framework included:

  1. Section 252A of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA):

This section regulates the admissibility of evidence obtained through entrapment and sets forth conditions under which such evidence may be excluded if the conduct of law enforcement goes beyond merely providing an opportunity to commit an offence ([12]), ([14]).

  1. Section 35(5) of the Constitution:

This provision mandates the exclusion of evidence obtained in violation of constitutional rights if its admission would render the trial unfair or be detrimental to the administration of justice ([28]).

  1. Legal Principles Regarding Condonation:

Condonation for non-compliance with procedural requirements is addressed in various cases, stipulating that it is an indulgence not granted lightly. Factors to consider include the nature of relief sought, extent and cause of delay, the impact on the administration of justice, and the importance of the issue being raised ([9]–[10]).

A – Application

The court analyzed the appellant's application for condonation by considering several factors:

  1. Inordinate Delay:

The delay of over 12 years was judged to be excessive. The appellant's failure to provide a satisfactory explanation for this prolonged period—including neglect on the part of his attorney—was deemed inadequate. The court noted that while litigants may rely on legal representation, they are also obliged to engage actively in their legal processes ([32]).

  1. Prospects of Success:

The court determined that the prospects of success on the actual appeal were minimal. The appellant’s contention that entrapment evidence should have been excluded was countered by the argument that the conduct of law enforcement was justified under section 252A of the CPA. The admission of the evidence was unlikely to be reversed on appeal as the conduct in question merely provided an opportunity for the appellant to commit the offence ([31]).

  1. Nature of the Further Evidence:

The evidence that the appellant sought to introduce was considered not only to potentially contradict the prosecution’s narrative but also as insufficient. Any arguments relating to the lack of authorisation for the entrapment operations were deemed irrelevant since the core inquiry was whether the conduct went beyond that of merely providing an opportunity ([41]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal refused the application for condonation due to the substantial delay, inadequate explanations for the same, and poor prospects of success in the appeal. Consequently, the appeal was struck from the roll ([46]).

The ruling reinforced the principles concerning the necessity for timely appeals and the rigorous scrutiny applied to the justification of entrapment evidence under South African law. Ultimately, the decision underscored the importance of judicial efficiency and the significant value placed on finality in criminal proceedings. The resolution of the matter illuminated the balance between individual rights within the criminal justice system and the imperative for effective law enforcement.

References: Andrew Barney August v The State (962/2022) [2023] ZASCA 170; Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977; Constitution of the Republic of South Africa.
