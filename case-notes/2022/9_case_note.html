FIRAC Case Note: Municipal Employees Pension Fund and Another v Mongwaketse [2022] ZACC 9

F – Facts

The case originated from a dispute involving Ms. Dineo Innolentia Mongwaketse, who was employed by the Ngaka Modiri Molema District Municipality as its chief audit executive and sought membership in the Municipal Employees Pension Fund (MEPF). Mongwaketse's employment contract, signed in February 2012, was fixed for five years with a provision for potential renewal, which was conditional upon satisfactory performance. Her application for MEPF membership was submitted without explicit mention of her fixed-term status ([2]–[3]).

In November 2014, Mongwaketse received a statement indicating that her membership benefits would only reference her contributions, causing her to question her membership eligibility, eventually revealing her ineligibility as a fixed-term employee. After extensive communication regarding her status, the Municipality acknowledged this error in September 2015, requesting her withdrawal from the MEPF and a refund of her contributions, which had been improperly collected since her employment commencement ([4]–[6]).

On March 1, 2017, Mongwaketse filed her grievance with the Pension Fund Adjudicator, asserting entitlement to the return of all contributions made to the MEPF, which culminated in the adjudicator ruling in her favor on November 10, 2017. The Adjudicator determined that Mongwaketse had never qualified for membership and consequently ordered MEPF to refund her contributions totaling R856 489.94, less the amounts already reimbursed ([8]–[12]).

Following the Adjudicator’s decision, the MEPF initiated review proceedings in the High Court, arguing that Mongwaketse was not a "complainant" as defined by the Pension Funds Act. The High Court upheld the Adjudicator's findings, which the MEPF then unsuccessfully contested before the Supreme Court of Appeal ([13]–[20]).

I – Issues

The core legal questions addressed by the courts involved:

  1. Whether Mongwaketse’s grievance constituted a "complaint" and whether she qualified as a "complainant" per the definitions in section 1 of the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956 ([29]).
  2. Whether the Pension Fund Adjudicator possessed jurisdiction to grant relief on grounds of unjustified enrichment ([29]).
  3. The interpretation and application of the definitions of "complaint" and "complainant" concerning Mongwaketse’s situation ([41]).

R – Rules

The court's analysis relied primarily on the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956, particularly:

  • Definitions: The terms "complainant" and "complaint" defined in Section 1. A "complainant" includes any person who is, or claims to be, a member or former member of a fund, beneficiary, or any person with an interest in a complaint ([41]).
  • Jurisdiction: The Adjudicator’s powers to investigate and adjudicate complaints as specified in sections 30D and 30E of the Act, allowing for various forms of relief, including unjustified enrichment claims ([57]).
  • Unjustified Enrichment Principles: Established within South African common law, specifically the condictio indebiti, which permits a party to recover payment made without cause ([64]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court reaffirmed the findings from the lower courts, concluding that:

  1. Mongwaketse did not become a member of the MEPF and therefore could not claim under the rights of membership as defined. The MEPF improperly admitted her due to her fixed-term employment status, contradicting the fund rules, which led to her being classified outside the category of eligible members ([31]–[39]).
  2. The interpretation of "complaint" was extended to include grievances arising from the fund's ultra vires conduct, thereby allowing Mongwaketse's claim to stand despite the MEPF’s contestation ([41]–[56]). The court determined that Mongwaketse’s claim was rooted in the misapplication of the fund’s rules concerning her eligibility for membership, qualifying her grievance as an administrative issue subject to the Adjudicator's jurisdiction.
  3. The Adjudicator's order to refund Mongwaketse was upheld based on the principles of unjustified enrichment since the MEPF benefited from contributions it was not entitled to receive, and the Municipality had erroneously withheld contributions owed to Mongwaketse ([64]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court affirmed the ruling of the Supreme Court of Appeal, thus reaffirming the legitimacy of the Adjudicator's decision. The court ruled that Mongwaketse had the right to recover her contributions, signaling significant implications for pension fund governance, particularly regarding the standards of eligibility and the importance of lawful admission to membership. The ruling emphasized statutory adherence by pension funds and reinforced the need for proper procedures in handling membership applications and the respective rights of potential members ([71]–[72]). The case sets a precedent concerning the interpretation of complainant status within pension fund law, ensuring broader access to justice for individuals wrongfully admitted to fund membership.
