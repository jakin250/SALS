Supreme Court of Appeal

Gordon v Department of Health: KwaZulu-Natal

Case Citation: Gordon v Department of Health (337/2007) [2008] ZASCA 99 (17 September 2008).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2008 [2008] ZASCA 99 (17 September 2008). Coram: Scott, Cloe

Case Citation:
Gordon v Department of Health (337/2007) [2008] ZASCA 99 (17 September 2008).

Coram:
Scott, Cloete, Mlambo, Maya JJA et Leach AJA.

F – Facts

[1] This case arises from an appeal against the judgment of the Labour Appeal Court (LAC) which dismissed the appellant’s claim against the Department of Health of KwaZulu-Natal. The Labour Court had earlier dismissed the appellant’s claim on the grounds that his non-appointment did not constitute unfair discrimination.

[2] On 11 April 1996, the respondent advertised the position of Deputy Director: Administration at Greys Hospital, Pietermaritzburg. The appellant, a white male with extensive experience, and Mr. Z Mkongwa, a black male also with experience, applied for the position. The selection panel recommended the appellant for the role based on his demonstrated competencies. However, the Provincial Public Service Commission overruled this recommendation and mandated the appointment of Mkongwa, citing the necessity to promote representivity within the public service based on the racial dynamics of South Africa.

[4] The appellant, aggrieved by his non-appointment, sought relief in the Labour Court, contending that the decision was discriminatory and an unfair labour practice. He claimed protective promotion from the effective date he should have been appointed.

[5] The Labour Court concluded that promoting the appellant would not fulfil the constitutional goal of equality and transformation and dismissed his claim. The LAC subsequently dismissed the appeal based on the failure to join Mr. Mkongwa in the proceedings, reasoning that his interests were directly affected by a potential finding of unfair discrimination.

I – Issues

[6] The primary issues before the court included:

  1. Whether the non-joinder of Mr. Mkongwa was fatal to the appellant's claim.
  2. Whether the appointment of Mr. Mkongwa constituted an unfair labour practice under Item 2(1)(a) of Schedule 7 of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995.
  3. The interpretation and application of affirmative action measures as provided in section 8(3)(a) of the Interim Constitution.

R – Rules

[7] The legal rules pertinent to the case stem from various sources:

  1. Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995, specifically Item 2(1)(a) concerning unfair discrimination and labour practices.
  2. Interim Constitution of South Africa:
  • Section 8, which outlines equality and the acknowledgement of measures designed to advance previously disadvantaged persons.
  1. Relevant case law concerning non-joinder principles and the rights of parties in dispute where third-party interests are affected.

A – Application

[11] The court first addressed the non-joinder issue articulated by the LAC. Mlambo JA posited that the LAC's reliance on previous decisions failed to duly distinguish the unique circumstances of this case. Unlike the cited cases, the appellant's claim did not seek to directly challenge Mkongwa's employment but was rooted in alleging unfair discrimination against himself. Consequently, the LAC's conclusion was termed incorrect.

[12] Examining the unfair discrimination claim, the court reiterated that while the appellant was discriminated against on racial grounds, the pivotal question was whether this discrimination was fair or unfair. The respondent's defence rested on the premise that the appointment of Mr. Mkongwa was justified as an affirmative action measure aiming to promote equality, as enshrined in both the Interim Constitution and the Labour Relations Act.

[13] The court emphasized that the term “measures” in section 8(3)(a) denoted structured, coherent policies rather than isolated or ad hoc appointments. Citing cases like Stoman v Minister of Safety and Security, Mlambo JA underscored that affirmative action must be rooted in rational and well-structured plans to be considered legitimate and constitutional. The appointment of Mr. Mkongwa lacked such a foundation, as it appeared arbitrary and whimsical rather than part of a coherent policy aimed at systemic equality.

C – Conclusion

[28] The Supreme Court of Appeal found merit in the appellant's claims and overturned the decisions of the Labour Court and the LAC. It was declared that the appointment of Mr. Mkongwa constituted an unfair labour practice under the Labour Relations Act because the decision to not appoint the appellant was unreasonable and amounted to unfair discrimination.

The court ordered that the appellant be compensated for the earnings he lost during the duration of this unfair appointment, with the obligation on the Department of Health to pay him the difference in wages due from the date he should have been appointed to the date of his retirement.

Moreover, the case established critical principles regarding the necessity for affirmative action measures to be rooted in coherent policies, emphasizing that mere references to transformation without structured plans would violate the Constitutional imperative of fairness in the workplace.

Implications

This ruling could potentially set a precedent for similar cases where candidates claim unfair discrimination; it emphasizes the need for employers to have clearly defined affirmative action policies that withstand judicial scrutiny. Further, the case reiterates the necessity for inclusion of all affected parties in disputes involving potential adverse effects on their positions, thus reinforcing principles of fairness and justice in employment practices.
