Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note

### Case Title: LA Group (Pty) Ltd v Stable Brands (Pty) Ltd and Another [2021] ZASCA 161

### F – Facts

In the matter before the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa, the case numbered 650/2020 centered around a dispute involving trademark registrations. The appellant, LA Group (Pty) Ltd (hereafter 'LA Group'), challenged the first respondent, Stable Brands (Pty) Ltd (hereafter 'Stable Brands'), regarding certain trade mark registrations. Specifically, the appeal pertained to the removal of a trademark registration, more precisely the trademark identified as 'POLO PONY & PLAYER DEVICE', initially cataloged under the registration number '2013/31832' for goods in class 9. The case unfolded against a backdrop of established trademark law in South Africa and involved procedural history where an initial order was granted on 22 February 2022, following a counter-application by Stable Brands for the removal of the contested trademark from the register ([paras 1–2]).

On 12 October 2022, both parties brought to the attention of the registrar of the Court that a 'patent error' existed in the previous order. The designation '2013' was incorrect and needed to be amended to '2014'. The parties amicably consented to this correction, given the public nature of the order impacting the trade mark register ([paras 1–2]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issue presented was whether the Supreme Court of Appeal had the authority to amend its previous order to correct a patent error, specifically addressing the implications of such an amendment on the integrity of the public trade mark register ([para 2]). The issue also raised questions about the procedural propriety of amending a court order after it has been granted, which is pertinent to maintaining the reliability of legal records ([para 2]).

### R – Rules

The legal framework governing this case includes provisions under the Trade Marks Act, particularly section 27(1)(a), which outlines the grounds for the removal of trademarks from the register. The appellate court referenced previous case law to underpin its determination, notably Firestone South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Genticuro A.G. [1977] 4 All SA 600 (A), which illustrates judicial precedent on correcting clerical or patent errors in court orders ([para 2]). 

Additional cases such as Afgri Grain Marketing (Pty) Ltd v Trustees for the time being of Copenship Bulkers A/S (in liquidation) and Others [2019] ZASCA 104 and The Gap Inc v Salt of the Earth Creations (Pty) Ltd and Others [2012] ZASCA 68 provided necessary context that supports the premise that amendments to judicial orders concerning public records are permissible to correct obvious errors ([para 2]).

### A – Application

Upon reviewing the submissions from both parties regarding the patent error in the order, the Court applied the established precedent allowing for the correction of clerical errors. The Court recognized that the amendment of the year from '2013' to '2014' was not merely formalistic; it was crucial for the accuracy and reliability of the trade mark register, which serves public interests in trademark law ([paras 1–3]). 

The Court's analysis emphasized that the correction would not prejudice any party and was necessary to maintain the correctness of the records in the register. This perspective aligns with the legislative intent to provide certainty in trademark ownership and rights. 

In confirming the amendment, the Court showed deference to the principle of legal accuracy, highlighting that the misstatement posed a potential for confusion within the sphere of trademark rights, an area where precision is paramount ([para 2]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal, finding merit in the application for amendment, ordered that paragraph 2(c) of the original order be corrected to reflect '2014' instead of '2013'. This case underscores the Court's commitment to ensuring that judicial orders accurately reflect the reality of legal rights, particularly when such orders impact public registers ([para 3]).

The ratio decidendi emphasizes the judiciary's role in maintaining the integrity of legal mechanisms like trademark registries, thereby reinforcing public confidence in the legal system. This ruling also highlights the procedural avenues available for correcting clerical errors, ensuring that such corrections do not undermine the authority or reliability of prior legal decisions. The implications of this case affect the broader landscape of trademark law and underscore the necessity for precise legal documentation, which is essential for the continued functioning and efficacy of trademark protections in South Africa ([paras 2–3]).
