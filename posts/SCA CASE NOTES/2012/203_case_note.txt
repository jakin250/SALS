# Case Note: Basson v Niemann (150/2012) [2012] ZASCA 203

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–7])
The case centers around a former partnership of attorneys in Pretoria, comprising Johan George Wilhelmus Basson (the appellant) and the four respondents, who were his partners—Benito Hitler Niemann, Jacob Jacobus de Klerk, Deon Storm, and Francois Charl Le Roux. Following a disagreement over financial dealings with a client involving Anglo-Euro Company (Pty) Ltd, the partnership was dissolved in 1999, leading to allegations of financial misconduct against Basson. The respondents contended that Basson had paid clients' deposits to Anglo-Euro without proper authorization, resulting in their financial liability to the investors. 

They filed a claim against Basson in the North Gauteng High Court in December 2009, seeking an order for compensation regarding partnership debts. Basson countered with a special plea of prescription under the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, which stipulated that the respondents’ claims had prescribed since they had knowledge of the relevant facts more than three years before filing the suit ([paras 7–9]). The High Court dismissed Basson’s special plea. 

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 8–10])
The primary legal question on appeal was whether the respondents’ claims had indeed prescribed, thus barring them from recovering their alleged losses. Specifically, the core issues were:

1. When did the respondents acquire the requisite knowledge of the facts underpinning their claims against Basson?
2. Did that knowledge occur within the three-year period mandated by the Prescription Act, which begins when a debt is deemed due ([para 7])?

Basson asserted that the respondents had knowledge of the material facts necessary to initiate their claims by 2006, while the respondents argued that they only learned of the relevant facts in May 2007.

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 11–16])
The central legal principle derives from the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, particularly sections 12(1) and 12(3). Section 12(1) establishes that a claim is prescribed after three years from when the debt is due, whereas section 12(3) specifies that prescription does not commence until the creditor is aware of the identity of the debtor and the facts that give rise to the debt. This provision includes the notion that a creditor is deemed to have such knowledge if it could be acquired through reasonable care.

The respondents relied upon section 12(2) of the Prescription Act, which states that if a debtor wilfully prevents the creditor from knowing about the existence of the debt, the period of prescription does not begin until the creditor becomes aware of it ([para 8]). The court also referenced the arbitration decisions and the precedent in *Hirschowitz Flionis* ([para 17]), as these addressed similar issues around the duty of attorneys to act transparently with their partners and clients regarding financial dealings.

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 18–26])
Upon examining the facts, the court underscored that the respondents had ample information suggesting that Basson acted improperly well before May 2007. Evidence presented showed that the respondents were aware of allegations made by investors regarding the improper payments shortly after they occurred in 1999. The court noted that:

1. The existence of litigation initiated by investors (the Visscher claim) as early as December 1999 should have alerted the respondents to their potential liability and the necessity to investigate Basson’s actions ([para 12]).
2. Despite being informed of adverse arbitration outcomes in 2005 and 2006, the respondents failed to act diligently, thereby allowing the prescription period to begin ([para 26]).
3. The assertion that the respondents lacked requisite knowledge was inconsistent with their conduct of making payments to settle claims, indicating an acknowledgment of their liabilities ([paras 20–24]).

The court concluded that the respondents had not exercised due diligence, and as such, they could be deemed to have known the facts that gave rise to their claims much earlier than they alleged, validly triggering the prescription period.

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 27–31])
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Basson’s appeal, concluding that the respondents’ claims had indeed prescribed prior to their initiating action in December 2009. The court emphasized that the respondents had knowledge of the relevant facts well beyond the three-year prescription period established by law and had failed to act within that timeframe.

The court’s decision reaffirmed the principle that a claim may not be enforced if the claimant had knowledge of the relevant facts that would allow them to pursue such claims but failed to do so dutifully and timely ([para 30]). The implications of this ruling highlight the importance of prompt legal action and investigation in matters involving alleged financial misconduct, especially in professional partnerships. 

Ultimately, the court ordered the dismissal of the respondents’ claims with costs, thereby solidifying the standard of due diligence applicable to partners in legal and financial contexts ([para 31]).
