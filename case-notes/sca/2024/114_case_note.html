FIRAC Case Note: Independent Regulatory Board for Auditors and Others v East Rand Member District of Chartered Accountants and Others [2024] ZASCA 114 (22 July 2024)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 114 (22 July 2024) --- #### F – Facts: Relevant

FIRAC Case Note: Independent Regulatory Board for Auditors and Others v East Rand Member District of Chartered Accountants and Others [2024] ZASCA 114 (22 July 2024)

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–11])

The case arose from an appeal against the Gauteng Division of the High Court's order, which set aside decisions by the Independent Regulatory Board for Auditors (IRBA) concerning fees and penalties imposed on registered auditors. The IRBA, established under the Auditing Profession Act 26 of 2005 (the Act), is tasked with regulating the auditing profession to protect public interest ([para 1]).

The IRBA had issued notices in 2019 and 2020 setting various assurance fees applicable to the financial years 2020 and 2021. Among its decisions was the withdrawal of a 50% fee concession previously allowed for auditors over 65. The East Rand Member District of Chartered Accountants (the respondent) challenged these fees in two applications based on the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) and the principle of legality ([paras 6–10]).

The High Court treated the actions of the IRBA as administrative and held them to be ultra vires, meaning the Board acted beyond its legal authority. It imposed a series of findings against the Board, including substantive procedural unfairness, which led to the appeal ([paras 12–17]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 18–20])

The main issues raised in the appeal include:

  1. Whether the IRBA's decisions constituted executive or administrative action.
  2. Whether the first application by the respondent was time-barred under section 7 of the PAJA.
  3. Whether the Board had the authority to impose assurance fees and tax practitioner fees.
  4. The necessity of a consultative process preceding the imposition of fees.
  5. Whether the failure to gazette the assurance fees in the 2021 financial year meant those obligations ceased.

These legal questions implicate both the statutory framework governing the actions of the IRBA and the principles of procedural fairness enshrined in the PAJA ([para 18]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 20–37])

The court analyzed the definitions of executive and administrative action, particularly referring to the framework established in Minister of Defence and Military Veterans v Motau ([para 21]), which emphasized that the source of the power, its constraints, and the appropriateness of judicial review are critical in distinguishing between the two types of action.

Key statutory provisions cited include:

  • Auditing Profession Act 26 of 2005: Sections detailing the IRBA's powers, particularly sections 4 (functions of the Board) and 8 (prescribing fees).
  • Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA): Sections 3 (procedural fairness) and 7 (time limits for review).
  • Relevant case law, including Esau and Others v Minister of Co-operative Governance and Traditional Affairs and Grey's Marine Hout Bay for clarifying the nature of administrative action and the requirement of publication for legal effect ([paras 20–36]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 38–108])

The court concluded that the IRBA's decisions regarding the fees were administrative rather than executive, thus more rigorously subject to judicial scrutiny under the PAJA. It upheld the High Court's finding that the time limitation for the first application did not bar the appeal, reasoning that the respondent filed within 180 days of the unlawful actions being gazetted, as required by PAJA ([paras 36–39]).

The court affirmed that the IRBA overstepped its authority by applying a percentage fee structure for Category C assurance work, which was not supported by the legislation. It emphasized that the Act only allows for recovery of costs directly associated with specific inspections, which invalidated the Board's approach ([paras 44–70]).

Additionally, the court addressed procedural unfairness, finding that while consultation was not strictly required before imposing fees, the IRBA needed to afford those affected a meaningful opportunity to make representations about significant increases in fees as necessitated under PAJA ([paras 79–92]). This respect for procedural fairness aims to enhance the quality and legitimacy of administrative decision-making ([para 92]).

The High Court's finding regarding the IRBA's failure to gazette the assurance fees in the 2021 financial year was also supported. The court clarified that obligations to pay prescribed fees cease when such fees are not formally published ([paras 95–98]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 109–110])

The court's decision partially upheld the appeal, remitting certain decisions for reconsideration by the IRBA with an emphasis on ensuring procedural fairness as outlined in PAJA. This highlights the necessity for regulatory bodies to conform not only to the letter of the law but also to the procedural rights of those affected by their decisions ([para 109]).

Ultimately, the holding signifies an important judicial endorsement of transparency and accountability in administrative action, reinforcing the critical role of public participation in regulatory processes. The outcome mandates the IRBA to recalibrate its approach to fee prescription, ensuring compliance with legal standards while balancing fiscal responsibilities within the auditing profession ([para 110]).

This comprehensive analysis, structured under the FIRAC method, encapsulates the essential legal findings and highlights the implications of the court's ruling on the independence and operational integrity of the IRBA within statutory confines.
