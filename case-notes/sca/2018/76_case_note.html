Supreme Court of Appeal

Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality & Others v Thupetji Alexander Thubakgale & Others (125/2018) [2018] ZASCA 76

This case arises from the decisions made by the Gauten Division of the High Court concerning the housing rights of residents in the Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 76

F – Facts

This case arises from the decisions made by the Gauten Division of the High Court concerning the housing rights of residents in the Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality. Following an application by the respondents on 15 December 2017, Justice Teffo issued an order compelling the Municipality to provide housing to the first and third to 134th respondents (hereafter referred to as "the residents") at Tembisa Extension 25 or an agreed location before 31 December 2018, with additional obligations to register titleholders by 31 December 2019 and provide progress reports every three months ([1]).

On 8 February 2018, the Municipality was granted leave to appeal against the specific deadlines set by the High Court. The Municipality argued that the requirements lacked a proper factual foundation, suggesting instead deadlines of 31 December 2021 and 31 December 2022 ([2]). During the appeal hearing on 2 May 2018, it became apparent that the Municipality had made progress since the order was issued. The project manager estimated that housing would be ready by 30 June 2019, contradicting the previous court's deadlines ([3]).

Responses to the Municipality's updated report indicated disagreement from the residents, who maintained that the Municipality could meet the original deadline ([4]). Nonetheless, the court concluded that since the original deadlines were no longer feasible, they could not be upheld.

I – Issues

The primary legal issue in this case revolves around the enforceability of the deadlines established by the High Court concerning the provision of housing. Specifically, the court had to determine whether the original dates set for compliance (31 December 2018 for housing provision and 31 December 2019 for registration) were realistic and achievable, in light of the Municipality's updated progress report and the evidence available ([2][4]).

Additionally, the case touches on broader constitutional issues relating to the right to housing as guaranteed under the South African Constitution and the obligations of municipalities to provide housing for their residents.

R – Rules

The court's reasoning draws extensively on constitutional principles, particularly section 26(1) of the Constitution of South Africa, which guarantees the right to adequate housing. This section places an obligation on the state to take reasonable legislative and other measures to progressively realize this right ([1]). The court also referenced previous case law regarding the enforceability of housing rights and the parameters within which municipalities operate when fulfilling their obligations under this constitutional framework.

The High Court's order must align with the realities of the situation on the ground, which the appeal court highlighted in evaluating the feasibility and reasonableness of the dates imposed.

A – Application

In analyzing the competing arguments, the court gave weight to the Municipality's progress report, which indicated that the housing could realistically be completed by 30 June 2019, even though the residents contended that the original 31 December 2018 deadline was feasible. The absence of substantial evidence negating the Municipality's claims meant the court could no longer uphold the original deadlines, which were determined to be 'incapable of fulfilment' ([4]).

The court endorsed a more practical timeline, substituting the High Court's original deadlines with new dates reflecting the Municipality’s latest assessment. The substituted order required the Municipality to provide houses by 30 June 2019 and to register residents as titleholders by 30 June 2020, thereby balancing the residents' rights with the practicalities of housing provision within municipal capacities ([6]).

Moreover, the court noted the Municipality's acceptance of liability for the costs of the appeal, reinforcing its commitment to rectifying the situation despite the complexities involved in delivering on its housing commitments ([5]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the Municipality's appeal, recognizing the necessity for deadlines that align with actual capacity and condition on the ground. The court ultimately replaced the original order with revised deadlines, allowing for the provision of housing by 30 June 2019 and registration by June 2020 ([6]).

The case underscores the delicate balance between the enforcement of constitutional housing rights and the practical realities of municipal service delivery. It reinforces the principle that judicial orders must be feasible, thereby preserving the functionality of government and acknowledging the challenges faced in realizing socio-economic rights within a framework of limited resources. This case is indicative of an evolving jurisprudence that appreciates both legal rights and practical realities in the realm of public service obligations.
