Supreme Court of Appeal

S B LOMBO v AFRICAN NATIONAL CONGRESS (2002) SCA 17/2001

In November 1993, S B Lombo (the appellant) initiated a civil action for damages against the African National Congress (ANC) and the South African Communist Party (SACP) for alleged unlawful detention and assault from January 1986 to August 1991 ([1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2002

F – Facts

In November 1993, S B Lombo (the appellant) initiated a civil action for damages against the African National Congress (ANC) and the South African Communist Party (SACP) for alleged unlawful detention and assault from January 1986 to August 1991 ([1]). The appellant contended that he had been unlawfully detained and mistreated in various countries across Africa during this period, claiming a total of R6,135,812.00 in damages ([1]).

The case arose in the context of the ANC’s armed struggle against the apartheid government. The appellant alleged that he was detained first in Botswana, then in Zambia, Tanzania, and Angola, where he suffered mistreatment, torture, and deprivation of his property ([3]–[6]). The ANC contested the claims, asserting that he had not been detained as alleged and claiming that any assaults were unintentional acts by rogue elements within the organization ([10]–[11]).

The trial judge, Hurt J, absolved both the ANC and the SACP from liability on the basis of the special plea of prescription, concluding that the appellant failed to establish an acknowledgment of liability by the ANC that could interrupt the prescription period ([2][18]). The appellant subsequently appealed the ruling against the ANC only ([2]).

I – Issues

The primary issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal were:

  1. Whether the appellant's claims for assault, unlawful detention, and deprivation of property had prescribed under the Prescription Act 68 of 1969.
  2. Whether the appellant had established that he was unlawfully detained, and if so, whether such detention was lawful at any point during the timeframe relevant to the appeal ([27][28]).
  3. The applicability of the common law maxim lex non cogit ad impossibilia in the context of the appellant's claims given his detention status ([21]–[22]).
  4. The admission and relevance of documentary evidence regarding the conditions in ANC detention camps and the appellant's treatment ([14][29]).

R – Rules

The court relied on several legal principles, statutes, and precedents:

  1. The Prescription Act 68 of 1969, particularly sections 11(d) and 13(1), which govern the prescription periods for claims and the interruptions of such periods ([15][23]).
  2. Common law principles regarding wrongful detention and the requirement of justifiable reasoning for detention as established in Hurley and Another v Minister of Law and Order 1986 (3) SA 568 (A).
  3. The principle that each act of assault or maltreatment constitutes a separate cause of action that prescribes independently ([19]).
  4. Judicial discretion in the application of the law based on circumstances specific to a case, such as the conditions under which the ANC operated during the armed struggle ([48]–[49]).

A – Application

Upon examining the evidence, the court found that numerous claims regarding assaults and maltreatments were prescribed because they arose over three years before the summons' service date of 22 November 1993 ([30][28]). The appellant failed to demonstrate any acknowledgment of liability by the ANC that would interrupt the prescription ([17]).

On the issue of the unlawful detention claim, the court evaluated when the appellant was first detained. The appellant contended he was detained in Botswana. However, the court found this assertion uncorroborated against the weight of evidential testimonies indicating his detention actually commenced in Dakawa, Tanzania ([34]).

The legality of the appellant’s initial detention was scrutinized, and while the court found that a reasonable suspicion existed justifying the ANC's detention of the appellant as a suspected spy, it also considered whether the appellant was afforded a trial within a reasonable timeframe ([43][46]). The ANC's inability to provide a timely hearing was noted, though ultimately the appellant’s refusal to accept a subsequent trial negated potential claims of wrongful detention for the period post-November 1990 up to his release in August 1991 ([51]).

In examining the appellant's claims, the court emphasized the significant evidence from the ANC suggesting that the appellant's continued detention was justifiable based on the suspicion of espionage, supported by prior discrepancies in his documented biographies ([37][40]–[41]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appellant's appeal. The claims for assault, maltreatment, and deprivation of property were ruled prescribed. While the court found that the appellant had a valid claim for unlawful detention for the period between November 1990 and August 1991, his refusal to participate in trials offered post-abduction resulted in the conclusion that the ANC's continued detention was lawful during that time ([53]).

The court recognized the unfortunate circumstances of the appellant's situation and the substantive evidence indicating probable mistreatment while in captivity, yet reiterated the procedural constraints of the law. Thus, the appeal was dismissed with costs, closing a significant chapter of litigation involving the legacy of the anti-apartheid struggle and its aftermath ([54]).
