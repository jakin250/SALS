Constitutional Court

The Affordable Medicines Trust and Others v The Minister of Health of the Republic of South Africa

The applicants sought judicial relief against provisions of the Medicines and Related Substances Act 101 of 1965, particularly subsection 22C(1)(a) and various regulations that govern the issuance of licenses to dispense medicines.

Constitutional Court 2005

F – Facts

This case pertains to an application for leave to appeal concerning a constitutional challenge to a licensing scheme imposed by the South African government that restricts health care providers, including medical practitioners and dentists, from dispensing medicines without a specific license issued by the Director-General of Health ([1]–[2]). The applicants, namely the Affordable Medicines Trust, the National Convention on Dispensing, and Dr. Mabata Norman Mabasa, argued that they had the right to dispense medicines without a license based on prior legislation and that the new requirements infringed their constitutional rights ([2]).

The applicants sought judicial relief against provisions of the Medicines and Related Substances Act 101 of 1965, particularly subsection 22C(1)(a) and various regulations that govern the issuance of licenses to dispense medicines. The broader legislative context reveals that prior to the licensing scheme, dispensing was minimally regulated under the now-repealed section 52 of the Health Professions Act 56 of 1974, which simply required notification rather than formal licensing ([12]–[13]).

Before the High Court, the applicants narrowed the scope of their challenge to focus specifically on the phrase "on the prescribed conditions" in subsection 22C(1)(a), arguing it conferred arbitrary discretion upon the Director-General ([4]). The primary concerns revolved around the restrictions on dispensing conditions and the requirements linking licenses to specific premises ([3], [19]–[20]).

I – Issues

The core legal issues in this case include:

  1. Whether the discretion given to the Director-General under subsection 22C(1)(a) is too broad and therefore unconstitutional ([24]–[25]).
  2. Whether the requirement of linking a license to dispense medicines to specific premises violates the principles of legality and the constitutional rights of practitioners ([24]–[26]).
  3. Whether regulations connected to the licensing framework infringe upon constitutional rights regarding the practice of a profession under section 22 of the Constitution ([59]–[60]).

R – Rules

The rules applicable in this matter derive from the South African Constitution and relevant legislative frameworks:

  • Section 22 of the Constitution guarantees citizens the right to freely choose their profession and permits regulation of the practice of a profession, but does not explicitly guarantee the practice itself ([57]–[58]).
  • The Doctrine of Legality mandates that all public powers exercised must originate from a lawful source and must not exceed the authority conferred by law ([49]-[50]).
  • Pharmaceutical Manufacturers Association of South Africa v President of the Republic of South Africa establishes that decisions stemming from executive discretion must be rationally related to a legitimate purpose or policy ([75]).

The judgment also references unlawful delegation of legislative powers, stipulating that the powers must be exercised within the framework set by Parliament ([30]).

A – Application

The Court assessed the validity of the applicants' arguments against the established objectives of the licensing scheme. It noted that the Minister of Health's regulations aimed to address historically poor dispensing practices that posed health risks and aimed to improve safety standards in dispensing medicines ([18]–[22]).

Regarding the phrase "on the prescribed conditions", the Court determined that broad discretion is permissible under the Constitution as long as it is guided by the objectives to ensure safety and efficacy in medicine dispensing ([32]–[39]). The applicants' claims that this provision resulted in arbitrary power did not stand, as it was conditioned by necessary checks that aligned with the public interest ([39]). The regulation that linked licenses to specific premises was also upheld, reflecting a judicious balance that did not deter the exercise of the profession but rather ensured medicinal safety and public health ([41]–[54]).

However, the Court found merit in the applicants' challenge to specific regulations, particularly sub-regulatory provisions that could be interpreted as unduly limiting the rights of practitioners. Notably, provisions that required the Director-General to consider the geographic presence of pharmacies as a criterion for dispensing licenses were found to distort the intended public policy. The Court declared these sub-regulations unconstitutional as they were ultra vires the Medicines Act's empowering statutes ([118]–[123]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court partially upheld the appeal, declaring that while the primary provisions of subsection 22C(1)(a) and other regulations generally aligned with constitutional standards, certain provisions of sub-regulation 18(5)(a), (c), (d), and (e) were declared unconstitutional for exceeding the scope of legal authority. The judgment emphasized that regulations must support and enhance public health objectives without unreasonably limiting the right to choose and practice a profession ([141]).

Notably, given the constitutional nature of the matter, the Court declined to award costs against the applicants, reiterating the necessity for caution in chilling future constitutional challenges ([138]–[140]). Thus, while the authorities maintain a framework for the licensure of dispensary practices, the balance must ensure the protection of fundamental rights and legitimate regulations supporting public health interests.

This ruling affirms the power of the Director-General to impose conditions on dispensing licenses but also underscores the necessity of accountability and justifications for such conditions that do not unduly impede medical practitioners' rights.
