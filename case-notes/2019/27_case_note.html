Constitutional Court 2019 [2019] ZACC 27 #### F – Facts The case arises from an urgent

FIRAC Case Note: New Nation Movement NPC and Others v President of the Republic of South Africa and Others [2019] ZACC 27

F – Facts

The case arises from an urgent application for direct leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court against a judgment of the High Court of South Africa, Western Cape Division, delivered on 17 April 2019 ([1]). The applicants—New Nation Movement NPC, Chantal Dawn Revell (a representative of Khoi and San communities), GRO (a nascent trade union), and Indigenous First Nation Advocacy SA PBO—sought a declaration that provisions of the Electoral Act 73 of 1998 are unconstitutional due to the exclusion of independent candidates from standing for election to the National Assembly and Provincial Legislatures ([3–4]). The application was filed on 18 April 2019, with the subsequent hearing focused solely on whether the matter was urgent ([1]).

At the High Court level, the applicants sought three orders: to declare section 57A and schedule 1A of the Electoral Act unconstitutional; to declare the Act itself unconstitutional for lacking provisions that regulate independent candidacy; and to direct Parliament to amend the electoral system before the upcoming 2019 elections. They later amended their relief request to seek changes "as soon as possible" instead of before the elections ([4–5]). The respondents included the President, Minister of Home Affairs and Electoral Commission, of whom the first and fourth respondents did not oppose the application ([3]).

Despite the urgency claimed by the applicants, the Constitutional Court ultimately found that sufficient grounds for emergency relief had not been established ([8]). Various reasons contributed to this finding, including the potential for the applicants to seek alternative remedies post-elections ([11]). The background also highlighted logistical challenges, as over sixty million ballots had been processed just days prior to the 2019 elections ([17]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue was whether it is constitutionally permissible to prevent eligible South Africans from standing for election to the National Assembly and Provincial Legislatures outside of party lists ([1]). The matter raised significant constitutional questions regarding the right to participate in free and fair elections, the validity of the current electoral framework, and whether independent candidates could justly contend for public office under South African law ([12]).

Additionally, procedural concerns were also evaluated, particularly regarding the urgency of the application, and whether the matter warranted determination prior to the elections ([10–11]).

R – Rules

The ruling drew upon several constitutional principles and statutory rules, particularly focusing on the constitutional right to vote as bestowed by section 19 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa. This section guarantees every citizen the right to vote in elections for the National Assembly and Provincial Legislatures. The rules governing urgent applications were also crucial, referencing Rule 12 of the Constitutional Court's rules, which allows the Chief Justice to dispense with ordinary procedures in urgent cases requiring expedited relief ([6–7]).

Notably, the Court emphasized that urgency must be substantiated with clear justification and cannot be a mere assertion ([9]). Precedents such as Kaunda v President of the Republic of South Africa and South African Informal Traders Forum v City of Johannesburg contributed to the Court’s understanding of urgency ([8]).

A – Application

In evaluating the urgent application, the Court found that the changes sought by the applicants did not necessitate a immediate resolution ahead of the elections ([11]). The amendment of their relief claim suggested a lack of need for urgent adjudication, as they did not assert that any relief sought would become irrelevant post-elections. The Court highlighted that there remained alternative avenues available for the applicants, including post-election reviews of the electoral process ([12–13]).

The assertion that new ballot papers should be printed to include independent candidates was dismissed due to a lack of articulate reasoning and prior acknowledgment from the applicants that substantive changes would not be possible prior to the scheduled elections ([15–16]). Furthermore, the practical implications of postponing elections, particularly given that voting had already commenced, weighed heavily against the urgency of the application. Challenges related to whether Parliament could reconvene to amend the electoral framework were also underscored, raising concerns about the constitutional legitimacy of such actions ([18]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded that the application for direct appeal did not demonstrate the urgency required to warrant expedited consideration. As such, the matter was postponed to 15 August 2019 ([21]). The ruling accentuates the importance of the electoral framework's integrity and the necessity of procedural fairness, especially in cases with serious implications for democratic processes.

The case demonstrates that while constitutional rights must be preserved and potentially amended electoral regulations pursued vigorously, they must also be contextualized within practical and procedural realities. This judgment underscores the delicate balance between urgency in legal claims and the legitimacy of the electoral framework, establishing clear parameters for future cases concerning electoral reforms and independent candidacy rights. The implications of a ruling in favor of the applicants could have had profound effects on the constitutionality of past and future elections under the current legislative framework.
