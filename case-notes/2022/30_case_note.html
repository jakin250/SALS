Constitutional Court 2022 [2022] ZACC 30

F – Facts

This case arose from a series of incidents involving the National Union of Metalworkers of South Africa ("NUMSA") representing 41 employees (hereafter referred to as "the Appellants") who were dismissed by Marley Pipe Systems (SA) (Pty) Ltd (hereafter referred to as "the Respondent"). The events occurred on 14 July 2017, during an unprotected strike initiated by NUMSA members dissatisfied with a wage increase agreement communicated on 13 July 2017 ([para 3]). Amid the strike, there was a violent incident in which Mr. Ferdinand Christiaan Steffens, the head of human resources for the Respondent, was brutally assaulted by striking employees, sustaining significant injuries ([para 4]).

After a disciplinary hearing, all 148 involved employees were dismissed for two counts of misconduct: the assault on Mr. Steffens and participation in the unprotected strike ([para 5]). The Labour Court upheld these dismissals, awarding damages to the Respondent. The Appellants contended that dismissals were unfair ([para 6]).

An appeal was lodged with the Labour Appeal Court, which confirmed the Labour Court's decision, thus prompting further appeal to the Constitutional Court ([para 10]). This appeal specifically concerned the 41 employees who were neither positively identified as perpetrators of the assault nor were they present at the scene during the violence ([para 9]) but based their guilt on the doctrine of common purpose as established by the Labour Appeal Court ([para 11]).

I – Issues

The central issue for determination was whether the Labour Appeal Court's application of the doctrine of common purpose was appropriate, particularly concerning the implicit imposition of a duty on bystanders to dissociate themselves from unlawful actions to avoid liability ([para 14]). Further consideration was also given to the constitutional implications of the dismissals vis-à-vis the right to fair labour practices as enshrined in section 23 of the South African Constitution ([para 15]).

R – Rules

The law surrounding common purpose stems from several key cases, emphasizing that to establish complicity, the following must be proven: presence at the scene, knowledge of the wrongdoing, intention to assist the wrongful act, and some act of association with the conduct of the perpetrators ([para 17]). The court cited S v Mgedezi [1989] and Dunlop Mixing and Technical Services [2019] as foundational authorities establishing these criteria ([paras 17–19]).

The Labour Appeal Court's requirements indicated that mere presence at the scene during violent conduct does not automatically bind an individual to the actions of a group; rather, they must have intended to assist in the wrongdoing ([para 23]). The legal principles surrounding dismissals for misconduct hinge upon ensuring that they are substantively fair per sections 23(1) and 23(2) of the Constitution ([para 15]).

A – Application

In applying the doctrine of common purpose, the Constitutional Court critically analyzed the finding of the Labour Appeal Court that the Appellants had not adequately distanced themselves from the violence, suggesting they should have intervened to prevent the assault ([paras 24–25]). The Court noted that prior legal pronouncements required actual evidence of individual wrongdoing to substantiate guilt, and mere association with a group implicated in violence does not qualify as complicity without further evidence ([para 36]).

By examining the facts surrounding the unprotected strike, the Court reasoned that the 40 employees had not been shown to have engaged in any collective intent to harm Mr. Steffens. Instead, their conduct mainly consisted of marching, chanting, and carrying placards, acts that were insufficient to establish a common purpose in the assault ([paras 24–26]). The critical distinction was drawn between those who had actively engaged in the assault and the purely bystander Appellants, leading to the conclusion that the Labour Appeal Court had misapplied the principles of common purpose ([para 33]).

Consequently, the Court found the Labour Appeal Court's expectation that bystanders must intervene or distance themselves from the assault lacked legal grounding, as the absence of action did not imply complicity or intent ([para 24]). This distinction upheld the necessity for individual culpability versus collective guilt.

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded that the Labour Appeal Court erred in affirming the dismissals based on the doctrine of common purpose regarding the Appellants. They were found not guilty of the assault on Mr. Steffens, leading to the central holding that the dismissals were substantively unfair due to a lack of requisite proof of individual complicity ([para 40]).

The Court ordered the remittance of the case back to the Labour Court for reconsideration of sanctions regarding the charge of participating in the unprotected strike, to be adjudicated by a differently constituted bench ([para 40]). The decision highlighted the vital importance of safeguarding individual rights in the workplace, especially in contexts involving collective actions like strikes, reinforcing that legal obligations to intervene do not extend to passive bystanders.

This case elucidates the principles of fair labor practices and the careful balancing that must be achieved when adjudicating issues of collective misconduct without infringing on the rights of individuals who may not have engaged in unlawful conduct.
