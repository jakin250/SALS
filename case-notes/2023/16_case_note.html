Ashebo v Minister of Home Affairs and Others [2023] ZACC 16

On July 7, 2022, Ashebo was arrested for violating the Immigration Act by illegally entering and residing in South Africa.

Constitutional Court 2023 [2023] ZACC 16

F – Facts

The case involves Beneyam Deselegn Ashebo, an Ethiopian national who unlawfully entered South Africa from Zimbabwe on June 11, 2021, after fleeing persecution in Ethiopia due to his political and religious beliefs. He claims his family was killed for these beliefs, prompting his escape to South Africa, where he arrived without a passport and did not use official ports of entry due to fears of arrest and deportation ([paras 4–5]).

On July 7, 2022, Ashebo was arrested for violating the Immigration Act by illegally entering and residing in South Africa. During his arrest, he informed authorities of his status as an asylum seeker and his attempts to apply for asylum, which were hindered by the closure of the Refugee Reception Office (RRO) during the COVID-19 pandemic ([paras 5–6]). Despite his assertions, the detention officer dismissed his claims, asserting that he was in the country for economic reasons. Ashebo alleges that he was made to sign documents without understanding their contents due to a lack of interpretation support ([para 6]).

Ashebo filed an urgent application in the High Court challenging his detention and seeking to prevent his deportation until his asylum status was definitively resolved. The High Court struck the application from the roll for lack of urgency and imposed costs on Ashebo, prompting him to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court ([paras 1, 2, 8, 10]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues presented to the Constitutional Court consisted of two salient questions:

  1. Whether an illegal foreigner, upon expressing the intention to apply for asylum, is entitled to be released from detention pending the resolution of their asylum application ([paras 28–31]).
  2. Whether the applicants had sufficient grounds to challenge their unlawful detention whilst awaiting deportation under the Immigration Act and procedures under the Refugees Act ([paras 11, 28, 29]).

R – Rules

The court relied on several legislative and constitutional provisions, notably:

  1. Section 12(1) of the Constitution: This provision guarantees the right to freedom and security of the person, including protection against arbitrary detention ([para 11]).
  2. The Refugees Act 130 of 1998: Particularly, Section 2, which embodies the principle of non-refoulement, asserting that no person may be forcibly returned to a country where they face persecution ([para 30]).
  3. The Refugees Amendment Act 11 of 2017: Section 21(1) establishes procedural requirements for asylum seekers, underscoring the importance of timely applications and the necessity for interviews by a Refugee Status Determination Officer (RSDO) ([paras 18, 36]).
  4. Regulations promulgated under the Refugees Act, which dictate the processes surrounding asylum applications, particularly the expectations for an applicant's prompt declaration of their status and presentation before authorities ([para 39]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court found that Ashebo's detention was unlawful given his expressed intention to apply for asylum. It determined that once an illegal foreigner indicates this intention, they must be afforded the opportunity to apply for refugee status, and their detention pending an asylum application could render their continued detention unlawful ([paras 29–31, 46–56]).

In assessing Ashebo's circumstances, the court noted that while he had illegally entered South Africa, his situation invoked the protections under the Refugees Act and the obligation of the state to facilitate his asylum process. The court also highlighted that failure to assist Ashebo following his application for asylum constituted a breach of his rights ([paras 57–58]). The respondents had not taken reasonable steps to allow him to apply for asylum after his declaration, thereby failing to uphold the statutory requirements ([para 58]).

The ruling underscored that the principle of non-refoulement protects asylum seekers and obligates the state to act when they express their intention to seek asylum. The Court indicated that while Ashebo may not be entitled to immediate release merely upon expressing this intention, he must be provided a lawful mechanism to apply for asylum ([paras 59–60]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court granted leave for direct appeal, effectively ruling in Ashebo's favor. The court set aside the High Court's order and declared that Ashebo could not be deported until he had been given an opportunity to establish good cause for his illegal entry and his asylum application had been resolved ([para 61]). The implications of this ruling are significant: it reinforces the right to apply for asylum and the state's responsibility to facilitate such applications, aligning with international standards for the protection of refugees. The respondents were further ordered to take all reasonable steps to implement the order, failing which Ashebo was to be released from detention unless legally detained under the Criminal Procedure Act. The decision not only impacts Ashebo's case but also sets a precedent for future asylum seekers facing similar circumstances under South African law ([paras 61–62]).
