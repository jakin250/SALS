Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

### FIRAC Case Note

**FIRAC Case Note: Raduvha v Minister of Safety and Security [2016] ZACC 24**

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–12])**

The case at hand involves the Applicant, Michell Joyce Raduvha, a 15-year-old girl who claims she was wrongfully arrested and detained by members of the South African Police Service (SAPS) on April 6, 2008. This incident occurred when police officers attempted to arrest her mother, Mrs. Raduvha, for violating a protection order. Michell intervened to prevent her mother’s arrest, prompting the officers to arrest her under section 40(1)(j) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977.

Following her arrest, Michell was detained for approximately 19 hours until she was released the next day, with the Public Prosecutor subsequently declining to prosecute. Michell and her mother then filed separate civil claims against the Minister of Safety and Security, asserting unlawful arrest and detention. The High Court dismissed their claims, a decision that was upheld by the Full Court of the Gauteng Local Division. Following the rejection of her petition to the Supreme Court of Appeal, Michell sought a leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court.

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 28–31])**

The key issues the Constitutional Court needed to address included:

1. Whether the term "detention" as referred to in section 28(1)(g) of the Constitution encompasses "arrest".
2. Whether Michell's arrest and detention were lawful under the circumstances.
3. Whether section 28(2) of the Constitution imposes an additional jurisdictional requirement on lawful arrests under section 40(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act.
4. If successful, whether the Court should determine the amount of damages payable to Michell.

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 32–56])**

The Court’s analysis revolved around key provisions of South African law and relevant constitutional principles. Specifically:

- **Section 28(1)(g)** and **Section 28(2)** of the Constitution affirm the rights of children to not be detained except as a last resort, and that a child's best interests are of paramount importance in all matters concerning them.
- **Section 40(1)** of the Criminal Procedure Act provides police officers the discretion to arrest individuals, using "may" to denote that such action is not mandatory.
- Jurisprudence such as *Minister of Safety and Security v Sekhoto* and *Duncan v Minister of Law and Order* articulate that the discretion conferred on police officers must be exercised in good faith and not arbitrarily, bearing in mind the overarching principles of dignity and freedom embedded in the Bill of Rights.

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 57–72])**

The Court delved deeply into the interpretation of the global context and statutory framework concerning police powers and the rights of minors. It highlighted that:

- An arrest is distinctly separate from detention as per the Constitution, illustrated by the separate treatment of these two processes in the legal system.
- By conceding that Michell posed no real threat to the police or public, the officers violated their obligation to evaluate whether an arrest was necessary, particularly given Michell's status as a minor. The Court asserted that police must consider the child's best interests, as mandated by both the Constitution and international human rights standards concerning juvenile justice.
- The analysis raised the question of whether section 28(2) adds an extra layer to the requirements necessary for lawful arrests under section 40(1). The Court concluded that it complicates the matter of an arrest, which must encompass the child's best interests as an inherent part of police discretion.

The failure of the officers to act accordingly, negating proper consideration of Michell’s circumstances, resulted in the unlawful nature of both her arrest and detention.

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 73–76])**

The Constitutional Court unanimously upheld Michell’s appeal, concluding that her arrest and subsequent detention were unlawful under sections 28(1)(g) and 28(2) of the Constitution. The Court ordered the Minister of Police to compensate Michell for damages she suffered, remitting the case to the Gauteng Local Division to determine the quantum of damages payable.

This judgment carries significant implications for the treatment of minors within the justice system, underscoring that police must prioritize a child's best interests. The Court not only affirmed the importance of protecting children from unnecessary harm but also reinforced the need for a sensitive approach to law enforcement actions involving minors. Overall, this case establishes critical precedent that shapes child law jurisprudence in South Africa, demanding adherence to both constitutional mandates and international obligations concerning the rights of juveniles.
