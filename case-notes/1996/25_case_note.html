FIRAC Case Note: Key v The Attorney General, Cape of Good Hope Provincial Division and Another

Constitutional Court 1996 1993 ([1]). Following an order issued under section 6

FIRAC Case Note: Key v The Attorney General, Cape of Good Hope Provincial Division and Another

F – Facts

The case involves Mr. M.R. Key, the applicant, who is facing criminal charges in the Cape of Good Hope Provincial Division arising from the activities of Tollgate Holdings Ltd, a company that experienced a collapse in early 1993 ([1]). Following an order issued under section 6(1) of the Investigation of Serious Economic Offences Act No 117 of 1991 (the "Act"), law enforcement officials conducted searches of Key's residence and office, seizing several documents ([1]). Key contended that the prosecution's case against him is fundamentally based on evidence obtained from these searches and subsequent disclosures to investigative accountants due to sections 6 and 7 of the Act ([1]).

In August 1994, prior to the scheduled trial, Key initiated urgent motion proceedings where he sought a declaration that sections 6 and 7 of the Act were unconstitutional and ineffective in relation to the evidence seized ([1]). The case was subsequently referred to the Constitutional Court for determination of the constitutional validity of these sections ([1]).

I – Issues

The primary issues in this case focus on:

  1. Whether sections 6 and 7 of the Act are unconstitutional ([1]);
  2. Whether evidence obtained via searches authorized by these sections should be considered inadmissible in the pending criminal proceedings against Key ([1]).

Additionally, the court considered whether Key could constitutionally challenge the validity of sections 6 and 7 regarding conduct before the Constitution's enactment on April 27, 1994 ([3]).

R – Rules

The court relied on several legal precedents and constitutional provisions, notably:

  • Section 13 of the Constitution: This section guarantees the right to personal privacy, including the right from searches of one’s person, home, and property, and the seizure of private possessions ([2]).
  • Du Plessis and Others v De Klerk and Another: This case established that the Constitution does not retroactively apply to conduct occurring before its enactment ([4]). It concluded that unlawful acts prior to April 27, 1994 cannot become lawful due solely to the emergence of the Constitution.
  • The court also referenced R. v. James; R. v. Dzagic, which involved similar issues surrounding search and seizure under a statutory framework that was amended post-application of the charter ([5]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court applied the aforementioned principles extensively to Key's case. It determined that the search and seizure conducted under the Act occurred prior to the Constitution coming into effect. Consequently, the court found that the events which formed the basis of Key's grievance could not be construed as violating his constitutional rights because those rights did not exist at the time of the conduct ([6]).

It was reiterated that if the court were to accept that sections 6 and 7 were unconstitutional, it would not necessarily render the evidence obtained thereunder inadmissible in the criminal trial ([12]). Instead, the admissibility of evidence is contingent upon a separate analysis by the trial court, which must consider whether introducing the evidence respects the right to a fair trial as enshrined in the Constitution ([12]).

Furthermore, the court distinguished Key’s situation from the Park-Ross case, where there had been an agreement on the admissibility of evidence based on the constitutional challenge of the Act. Here, there was no prior agreement, making the precedential weight of Park-Ross inapplicable ([9]).

C – Conclusion

The court concluded that no orders would be made regarding the constitutionality of sections 6 and 7 of the Act. It held that evidence obtained prior to the enactment of the Constitution from lawful searches under the Act could not be automatically considered inadmissible in the forthcoming criminal trial ([16]). The trial judge will need to determine on a case-by-case basis the fairness of admitting such evidence, maintaining the Constitution's focus on substantive fairness and justice in legal proceedings ([12]).

The court also declined to make any orders relating to costs due to the consensual nature of the referral and the broader implications of its ruling, reflecting a comprehensive judicial approach to balancing the rights of the accused against the public interest in prosecution ([18]).
