The Lion Match Company Limited v Paper Printing Wood & Allied Workers Union

Supreme Court of Appeal 2001

F – Facts

[1] The appellant, The Lion Match Company Limited, manufactures and distributes safety matches and has its factory in Durban. The first respondent, Paper Printing Wood & Allied Workers Union, is a trade union registered under the Labour Relations Act 28 of 1956. The union was recognized by the appellant for its employees at the Durban factory. Despite the repeal of Act 28 of 1956 by Act 66 of 1995, the parties agreed that the former Act governed their dispute.

[4] The dispute arose when the second to 218th respondents, all employees at the Durban factory, were dismissed by the appellant on 22 August 1996 after a work stoppage intended to coerce the appellant into transferring the employees from the company’s pension fund to the union’s provident fund. Following unsuccessful attempts to resolve the issues, the first respondent applied to the Regional Director of Manpower for KwaZulu-Natal to establish a conciliation board to address the alleged unfair dismissal.

[7] The relevant statutory provisions include Section 35 of the Labour Relations Act, which outlines the procedure for establishing conciliation boards, specifically requiring trade unions to submit a written application signed by an office-bearer, along with a certificate of compliance with the union’s constitution.

[10] The first respondent’s application, submitted on 28 October 1996, included a compliance certificate signed by Acting Branch Secretary Lucky Mhlongo, certifying adherence to the union's constitution. Following this, a conciliation board was established on 5 December 1996 but failed to resolve the dispute in its meeting on 15 January 1997.

[13] On 17 March 1997, the first respondent and employees instituted proceedings in the industrial court for declaring the dismissals unfair, at which point the appellant raised a special plea, asserting that the certificate of compliance was false and thereby nullified the industrial court's jurisdiction.

[19] The appellant’s application for a declaratory order was argued before Jappie J on 24 November 1998 but was dismissed on 4 August 1999, leading to the present appeal.

I – Issues

[24] The key legal issue on appeal revolves around whether the application for a declaratory order was brought within a reasonable time and the implications of any delay on the validity of the application for a conciliation board established under the Labour Relations Act.

[25] Specifically, the court needed to determine whether the appellant could validly challenge the conciliation board’s authority and the special plea regarding the alleged invalidity of the application, despite the passage of time since its initiation.

R – Rules

[7] The statutory framework governing this dispute is primarily Section 35 of the Labour Relations Act, which stipulates the procedures for establishing conciliation boards and the requirements for applications submitted by trade unions. Section 46(9) indicates that disputes regarding unfair labour practices must be referred either to an industrial council or, where none exists, to a conciliation board.

[25] The legal precedents that were significant include the principle that a delay in bringing a review application can result in a forfeiture of the right to complain about the alleged irregularity. This principle was supported by case law, such as Wolgroeiers Afslaers (Edms) Bpk v Munisipaliteit van Kaapstad (1978) and Mamabolo v Rustenburg Regional Local Council (2001), which emphasize the necessity of timely action in judicial review.

A – Application

[25] In applying these principles, the court recognized that the appellant’s challenge against the jurisdiction of the industrial court should be treated as a review application. The delay of at least five months in contesting the validity of the conciliation application was deemed significant and unreasonable, particularly since the appellant had actively participated in the conciliation process up until the board’s failure to reach a resolution.

[27] The court noted that the appellant had received prior notice of the conciliation board's establishment and had engaged in discussions with its representatives, indicating acquiescence to the process and any potential irregularities that they later sought to exploit as grounds for disputing jurisdiction.

[28] The ruling emphasized that the delay caused prejudice to the dismissed employees, undermining the appellant’s argument that the actions of the first respondent prior to the dismissals constituted a breach of its constitution. Furthermore, any alleged noncompliance was dismissed as a procedural issue of form rather than substance, confirming the legitimacy of the union's application.

C – Conclusion

[34] Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court's decision with costs. The court's ruling underscores the importance of timely challenges in labour law and clarifies that participation in statutory processes precludes parties from later contesting their validity without adequate explanation for any delays. The implications of this ruling extend to how labour disputes are managed and the significance of procedural compliance within statutory frameworks, reinforcing the principles of good faith and promptness in the resolution of employment conflicts.
