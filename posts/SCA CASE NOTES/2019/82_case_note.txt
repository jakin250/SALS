Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

### FIRAC Case Note for *Mochebelele v Director of Public Prosecutions, Gauteng & Others* (377/2018) [2019] ZASCA 82 

#### F – Facts
The appellant, Mr. Reatile Thabo Mochebelele, a national of Lesotho, appealed against a judgment from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg, which overturned a previous order of the Randburg Magistrate that had discharged him according to section 10(3) of the Extradition Act 67 of 1962 ([1]–[2]). Mochebelele was convicted of bribery in Lesotho and sentenced to five years’ imprisonment after having fled to South Africa ([2]). The Lesotho government requested his extradition based on a treaty between the two nations, citing the need to enforce the sentence ([3]) and executed a request for extradition on 25 February 2010 ([4]). 

Mochebelele's extradition process was delayed, partly due to his application for refugee status, which was denied ([2]–[5]). The magistrate, in a subsequent hearing, accepted that the extradition request was valid but discharged Mochebelele on the grounds that a political persecution basis was pending in another court, specifically regarding his refugee status ([19], [20]).

The Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP) challenged this discharge in the High Court, which ultimately ruled that the magistrate acted beyond his powers by considering extraneous factors and ordered Mochebelele to be detained pending a decision by the Minister of Justice regarding his extradition ([20]).

#### I – Issues
The central issues in this matter revolved around:
1. Whether the magistrate had the authority to consider extraneous factors during an extradition inquiry as stipulated under the Extradition Act.
2. The appropriateness of the High Court's decision to substitute its order for that of the magistrate directly, rather than remitting the matter back to the magistrate for further proceedings ([20]–[26]).

#### R – Rules
The relevant provisions from the Extradition Act are critical in addressing the above issues:

- **Section 10 of the Extradition Act**: Details the magistrate’s powers and the conditions under which an individual can be discharged or committed to prison—specifically, that the magistrate must determine whether there is sufficient evidence for prosecution in the requesting state, without discretion to consider political factors ([14], [17]).
  
- **Section 11 of the Extradition Act**: Outlines the Minister's powers, including decisions on humanitarian grounds to refuse extradition, which are not within the magistrate's jurisdiction ([15], [18]).

- **Case precedents**: In *Director of Public Prosecutions: Cape of Good Hope v Robinson* ([25]), it was established that a magistrate does not have the authority to consider whether extradition might infringe upon constitutional rights, which must be assessed by the Minister under section 11.

#### A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the magistrate had erred in considering the pending refugee application as a valid reason to discharge Mochebelele. The court underscored that the statutory provisions of the Extradition Act strictly outline the powers granted to the magistrate, emphasizing that the consideration of humanitarian factors must rest solely with the Minister ([22]–[23]). 

The court determined that the magistrate’s duty was confined to evaluating the validity of the extradition request based on the criteria set forth in section 10. Hence, even if there were substantial concerns regarding Mochebelele's situation in Lesotho, these could not legally influence the magistrate's decision ([21]).

Furthermore, regarding the appeal from the DPP, the court confirmed the High Court's decision to substitute its order rather than returning the matter to the magistrate, as the inquiry had already satisfied the necessary conditions established in section 10(1) ([29], [30]). 

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal by Mochebelele. The court affirmed the High Court's ruling that the magistrate had acted outside the purview of his authority by considering extraneous matters, positively concluding that Mochebelele was liable for extradition based on the evidentiary findings ([32]).

The ruling serves as a clear reiteration of the delineations of powers between the magistrate and the Minister concerning extradition matters while reinforcing that procedural and substantive determinations regarding extraditions must adhere strictly to legislative frameworks. The ruling reflects the court's commitment to ensuring that the statutory scheme is applied without the improper introduction of political considerations by judicial actors. 

Thus, Mochebelele was ordered committed to prison, awaiting the Minister’s decision concerning his extradition to Lesotho, which highlights the judicial commitment to upholding the strictures of the Extradition Act ([32]). The implications of this case underscore the necessity for an organized and unambiguous approach to extradition proceedings, particularly in contexts involving potential political ramifications.
