Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2009

# FIRAC Case Note: Municipal Manager: Qaukeni Local Municipality v F V General Trading CC

**Case Citation:** Qaukeni Local Municipality and F V General Trading (324/08) [2009] ZASCA 66 (29 May 2009)

---

## F – Facts

The appeal revolves around the legality of a municipal services procurement contract between the Qaukeni Local Municipality (the Second Appellant) and F V General Trading CC (the Respondent). The Second Appellant, situated in Eastern Cape, had accepted a tender from the Respondent to serve as a refuse collector, leading to an oral agreement from November 2005 to 30 June 2006. 

As the oral agreement neared expiration, there was an invitation from Mr. Cezula, the then municipal manager, to the Respondent to submit a budget for the continuation of services for another year. The Respondent submitted what was described as an itemized quotation, leading to a new contract (‘ZEV 2’) being signed on 25 June 2006. This contract stipulated monthly payments of R351,350, with a provision for automatic renewal unless notification of termination was provided six months prior to the expiration date ([1-6]).

Conflict arose when the new municipal manager, Mr. Fihlani, attempted to terminate the contract effective 30 June 2007, without following the statutory procedures, which triggered urgent legal proceedings from the Respondent. Initially, these proceedings sought to declare the termination unlawful, but the Second Appellant countered by arguing that the contract itself was invalid due to non-compliance with statutory procurement processes ([7-10]).

The Mthatha High Court ruled in favor of the Respondent, holding the contract to be valid, prompting the appellants to appeal the judgment ([2-3]).

---

## I – Issues

The primary legal questions presented in the appeal were:

1. Was the contract between the Second Appellant and the Respondent valid despite the alleged breaches of statutory requirements for municipal contract procurement?
2. Did the court err in concluding that the appellants had not justified the termination of the contract and in dismissing the counter-application to declare the contract null and void ([2-3], [10]).

---

## R – Rules

The court examined several statutory provisions relevant to municipal procurement. Specifically, these included:

- **Section 217(1) of the Constitution**: Mandates that organs of state, including municipalities, must procure services in a manner that is fair, equitable, competitive, and cost-effective.
- **Municipal Systems Act (No. 32 of 2000)**: Requires municipalities to implement a supply chain management policy that promotes transparency and competition.
- **Municipal Finance Management Act (No. 56 of 2003)**: Establishes that all procurement processes for contracts exceeding R200,000 must adhere to competitive bidding processes ([11-12]).

The court also referenced several precedents highlighting the consequences of non-compliance with procurement regulations, underlining that contracts awarded without adherence to such regulations are subject to nullity ([15-16]).

---

## A – Application

The court analyzed the validity of the contract ‘ZEV 2’ against the backdrop of these statutory requirements. It noted that:

- The Qaukeni Local Municipality did not follow its statutory obligations, as it had neither a functioning supply chain management policy in place nor conducted a competitive bidding process. This disregard for prescribed processes contravened the principles of transparency and competition fundamental to public procurement ([13-14]).

- The argument presented by the Respondent, asserting that the failings of the municipality meant it must enforce the contract nonetheless, was considered unfounded. The court reinforced that these statutory provisions are imperative and not discretionary; failure to comply results in an invalid contract ([17-20]).

Moreover, the court noted that refuse collection is indeed a municipal service subject to these procurement requirements, dismissing the Respondent's attempts to categorize it differently ([21-22]).

In addressing the appellants' capacity to seek a declaration of invalidity, the court reaffirmed that public bodies are entitled—and indeed obligated—to challenge the lawfulness of their own administrative acts, particularly when those acts involve the contract of public funds ([23-24]).

---

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal reversed the decision of the High Court, finding that the original contract 'ZEV 2' was void ab initio due to the procedural failings in its procurement. Consequently, the termination attempts made by the appellants were valid, as no lawful contract existed post-30 June 2007. 

The court's ruling emphasized the importance of adherence to statutory procurement processes to maintain the integrity of public procurement and protect public resources. The appeal was allowed with costs, which included a stipulation that the record of appeal would not include costs for unnecessary documentation ([28-32]).

This case reaffirms the stringent compliance required in public procurement, serving as a significant precedent in ensuring that municipal contracts are subjected to necessary regulatory oversight to prevent maladministration and promote accountability in the use of public funds.
