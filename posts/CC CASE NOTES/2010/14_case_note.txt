# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title:** Stuttafords Stores (Pty) Ltd and Others v Salt of the Earth Creations (Pty) Ltd and Others  
**Court:** Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Case Number:** CCT 59/10  
**Judgment:** [2010] ZACC 14  
**Decided on:** 2 September 2010  

---

## F – Facts  
[1] This case arises from an application for leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court against an order of the Full Court of the North Gauteng High Court, Pretoria. The applicants include Stuttafords Stores (Pty) Ltd, Stuttafords International Fashion Co (Pty) Ltd, and various entities of The Gap, while Salt of the Earth Creations (Pty) Ltd and others respond as the respondents. The appeal sought to reconsider the controversial recollection of Judge Basson’s main judgment, delivered on 28 May 2007, from which the applicants alleged bias arose.

[2] The applicants contended that Judge Basson’s judgment mirrored Salt's heads of argument instead of demonstrating original judicial reasoning, leading to a perception of bias. Upon refusal of Judge Basson to recuse himself, an appeal was lodged with the Full Court, which subsequently dismissed the appeal, citing the matter became academic due to Basson's retirement. Notably, the Full Court found no compelling reason to indicate bias arising from adopting heads of argument.

[4] The trademark dispute had been ongoing for over a decade in which The Gap, having lost the trademark to Salt due to expungement by the Supreme Court of Appeal on grounds of non-use, sought to reposition itself in the South African market through partnerships with local retailers. Salt then attempted passing-off claims against The Gap and Stuttafords, tying the main judgment to Judge Basson's perceived bias. The recusal of the judge was contested post-main judgment.

[6] The Full Court expressed its disapproval towards adopting the heads of argument verbatim yet did not equate this practice with bias. Additionally, further developments—such as Basson's retirement—rendered the recusal dispute moot despite ongoing appeals regarding the original merits of the trademark matter.

## I – Issues  
[3] The primary legal question was whether the Constitutional Court should grant leave to appeal the Full Court's decision on recusal and, by extension, address the validity of the main judgment concerning perceptions of bias arising from Judge Basson's extensive reliance on the written submissions of Salt’s counsel.

## R – Rules  
[4] The applicable legal framework includes established doctrines regarding judicial bias and the recusal of judges, primarily focusing on the principles enunciated in prior judgments. The courts have emphasized the necessity for judges to provide original reasoning in their judgments, deterring mere adoption of arguments presented by parties as it could foster perceptions of bias. The relevant precedents from previous cases, including *Van Wyk v Unitas Hospital* and *Independent Electoral Commission v Langeberg Municipality*, guide the analysis of judicial conduct and issues.

## A – Application  
[5] The Court examined the implications of Basson J's conduct, highlighting that approximately 1890 lines of the main judgment are effectively reproductions of Salt's counsel's submissions, with only a minimal portion reflecting the judge's independent reasoning. Although some reliance on counsel's arguments is permissible, the Court underscored that judges should express their reasoning in their own words to uphold the integrity of judicial processes and avoid potential biases.

[9] The Constitutionally defined interest of justice weighs heavily in the Court's proceedings, signifying that even if an error occurs in judicial reasoning, the resolution must serve the broader interest of justice, wherein the Court ultimately concluded that adjudicating the recusal could not resolve the material issues at hand. It noted that practical effects of dismissing the appeal outweighed the theoretical exploration of bias by Basson J.

## C – Conclusion  
[13] The application for leave to appeal was dismissed as it was deemed not in the interests of justice. The court held that reaching a verdict on recusal based on Basson J's judgment itself was moot and would not affect the substantive issues pending between the parties. Importantly, the constitutional implications of judicial conduct and independence were reiterated, albeit the Court refrained from providing definitive views on the bias issue due to the peculiar procedural circumstances leading to Basson's retirement, leaving potential for future disputes over the principles involved.

---

**Ratios Decidendi:** The judgment reinforces the notion that judicial independence must be upheld through original reasoning to mitigate perceptions of bias while also delineating the practical implications when contested judgments become academic due to procedural developments.

**Implications:** This case underscores the importance of judicial accountability and the necessity for judges to maintain independence in their decisions, shaping future conduct and expectations within the judicial system. The distinction between perceived bias and substantive judicial error forms a critical area for future legal discourse in the context of judicial recusal and integrity.
