Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

### Case Note: Madiba v The State (497/2013) [2014] ZASCA 13

#### F – Facts
The appellant, Maposa Frans Madiba, was convicted by the Limpopo High Court of multiple serious crimes, including attempted rape, kidnapping, rape, and murder. The court, presided over by Hetisani J, sentenced him to 10 years’ imprisonment for attempted rape, 15 years for kidnapping, life imprisonment for rape, and 35 years for murder, with the sentences ordered to run concurrently ([paras 1–2]). The case arose from an incident where Madiba broke into the home of L[…] M[…], who was asleep with her young children. He made an explicit sexual advance towards L[…] before physically assaulting her. During the struggle, L[…] managed to escape, leaving her children behind. Madiba subsequently kidnapped her daughter, R[…], whose body was later discovered, leading to further charges against him ([para 3]).

The trial court’s decision sparked an appeal process. Madiba sought to overturn his convictions for attempted rape and rape while also appealing against the severity of his sentences ([para 2]).

#### I – Issues
The case raised several pertinent legal questions:

1. **Conviction:** Was the conviction for attempted rape justifiable based on the evidence presented? Specifically, had Madiba’s actions reached a legal threshold to constitute an attempt at rape?
2. **Rape Conviction:** Did the evidence substantiate the conviction for the rape of R[…] without direct proof of penetration?
3. **Sentencing:** Were the sentences imposed by the trial court appropriate, or did they reflect a material misdirection that warranted appellate intervention? Were there substantial and compelling circumstances present that could reduce the life sentence for the rape conviction? ([paras 4–14]).

#### R – Rules
This case engaged the principles established in prior legal precedents, notably the standard of proof in criminal attempts as articulated in **R v B** 1958 (1) SA 199 (A). Here, Schreiner JA elucidated that an attempt occurs when an assailant assaults a woman intending to engage in intercourse against her will, even if there is no direct effort for penetration ([para 5]).

Regarding the sentencing framework, the **S v Malgas** 2001 (1) SACR 469 (SCA) case was relevant. It established that a court can only adjust sentences where there is a material misdirection or where the sentencing disparity is shockingly inappropriate ([para 10]).

#### A – Application
In evaluating the evidence for the attempted rape conviction, the court found that Madiba’s actions satisfied the attempted rape criteria articulated in **R v B**. The court highlighted that his intention was sufficiently clear through both his verbal advances and his actions ([paras 5-6]). Consequently, Madiba's initial argument was ultimately conceded by his legal counsel.

For the rape of R[…], the court analyzed circumstantial evidence, including witness testimonies and a post-mortem report pointing to the state of the victim's body at discovery, indicating significant injury and blood. The court underscored that although constraints existed in the direct proof of penetration, the totality of evidence led to the reasonable inference that the rape occurred ([paras 6–9]).

Concerning sentencing, the Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that Hetisani J had misdirected himself by incorrectly aggregating the sentences despite a life sentence being imposed, leading to a gross misrepresentation of the actual time to be served ([paras 10–11]). The court agreed on revised sentences for attempted rape and kidnapping, reducing them to five and six years respectively, reflecting what the appellate court deemed appropriate given the circumstances ([paras 12–13]). The court upheld the life sentence for the rape of R[...] as there were no compelling circumstances justifying a lesser sentence; the brutality of the crime, particularly against a three-year-old, held paramount importance ([paras 13–14]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeals against Madiba’s convictions for attempted rape and rape but partially upheld the appeal regarding sentences. The revised sentences were established: 5 years for attempted rape, 6 years for kidnapping, life imprisonment for the rape of R[…], and 35 years for murder, with all sentences to run from the original imposition date of 4 May 2009. This case reaffirmed the importance of clear evidence in serious sexual offences and established accountability in sentencing where misdirection occurs, affirming judicial oversight in providing justice ([paras 15]).
