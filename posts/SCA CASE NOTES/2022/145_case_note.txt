Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# Case Note: Deon Nel v Petrus Jacobus de Beer & Another (406/21)

### FIRAC Method

#### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–7])

This case arises from a dispute about the validity and enforceability of a right of pre-emption contained in a lease agreement between Deon Nel (the appellant) and Petrus Jacobus de Beer and his estate (the respondents). The lease, entered into on 20 November 2008, allowed Nel to lease five separately registered farms from the de Beers for a period of three years ([para 2]). The agreement included a right of pre-emption that granted Nel the first option to purchase the properties before the lease's expiration.

Despite this, the de Beers, without notifying Nel, entered into a sale agreement with the Fanie Trust for the sale of two of the farms covered by the lease ([paras 3–4]). Following this, Nel expressed his desire to exercise his pre-emption right, triggering legal proceedings when his offer to purchase was ignored by the de Beers. His claim in the Gauteng Division of the High Court was initially dismissed on grounds that the pre-emption clause was not properly structured, leading Nel to appeal the decision ([paras 7–15]).

#### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 16–16])

The primary legal issues identified were as follows:
1. Whether Deon Nel and his brother, Marius Nel, were co-lessees under the lease agreements.
2. Whether the right of pre-emption was triggered by the sale of only two of the five farms.
3. Whether Nel properly exercised his right of pre-emption.
4. Whether the omission of certain clauses in his offer to purchase constituted a counter-offer.
5. The appropriate remedy for the breach of the right of pre-emption by the de Beers ([para 16]).

#### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 17–38])

The court referenced several key legal principles regarding rights of pre-emption. It reiterated the distinction between a right of pre-emption and an option to purchase, citing *Owsianick v African Consolidated Theatres* as establishing that a grantee of a right of pre-emption must be offered the property before the seller can sell to a third party ([para 17]). 

The court analyzed the interpretation of lease agreements, invoking the principles set forth in *Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality* regarding contractual interpretation, emphasizing that meaning should be derived from the context and overall intent of the agreement ([para 22]). 

Furthermore, the court discussed case law demonstrating that the sale of a portion of pre-emption property generally constitutes a breach of the right of pre-emption, referencing *McGregor v Jordaan* ([para 28]) and principles from both South African domestic law and international precedents regarding similar contractual obligations ([para 29]).

#### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 18–38])

The court first resolved the co-lessee issue by examining testimonies from both Nel brothers. It concluded that Marius Nel was not a co-lessee, as he did not sign the lease and behaved more like a sub-lessee ([para 21]). This determination permitted Deon Nel to exercise his right of pre-emption unilaterally.

On the question of whether the pre-emption right was triggered, the court determined that the phrasing within the pre-emption clause did not confine the sale to a requirement that all farms be sold simultaneously. It held that the right could be invoked upon the sale of any individual farm as opposed to the whole ([para 27]). This reasoning aligned with broader notions of fairness within the interpretation of the lease's intent.

The court also examined Nel's attempt to exercise his pre-emption right, ruling that he had made a valid offer in response to the sale of Portions 6 and 11. The assertion that his offer was a counter-offer due to clause omission was rejected; the court maintained that no actual offer had been made to Nel in the first place ([para 34]).

As to the remedy, the court underlined that specific performance—forcing the respondents to adhere to the original terms—was appropriate given the circumstances, particularly due to the respondents being aware of Nel's pre-emption right when selling to the Fanie Trust ([para 36]). The decision reinforced that rights of pre-emption are enforceable, aligning with established jurisprudence on the subject.

#### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 38])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that Deon Nel’s appeal succeeded, setting aside the High Court's order and granting specific performance as a remedy ([para 38]). The court's ruling confirmed the validity of the pre-emption right and underscored the obligation of property sellers to honor such rights, warning against the undermining of contractual agreements. The implications signal a strong judicial commitment to uphold pre-emption rights, enhancing legal predictability for contractual parties in agrarian and real estate matters in South Africa. 

This decision emphasizes the necessity for proper communication and adherence to contractual obligations, particularly when multiple agreements converge around essential rights such as pre-emption. Thus, practitioners and contracting parties alike are reminded to remain vigilant in honoring existing rights and ensuring clarity in agreements to avoid legal entanglements and disputes.
