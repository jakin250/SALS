Source Folder: 2007

# Case Note: MINISTER OF SAFETY AND SECURITY v MOHOFE [2007] SCA 21 (RSA)

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
[1] On March 12, 2001, near Bree and Rissik Streets in Johannesburg, Inspector Gerson Nemengaya, a plain-clothes police officer, observed three men who appeared to have committed a robbery, with at least two armed suspects. Upon confronting them, he announced his presence and ordered them to stop, yet two fled while Mr. Banyana Sibeko, one of the suspects, fired a shot at him. Although Nemengaya dived for cover, the bullet struck an innocent bystander, Mr. Johannes Mohofe, who later died from his injuries. The police took Sibeko into custody following the incident.

[2] The respondent, Mathume Mohofe, the decedent's mother, sought damages from the Minister of Safety and Security, claiming vicarious liability for Nemengaya’s alleged negligent actions, arguing that his decision to announce his presence escalated the situation and directly contributed to her son’s death, despite the fact that it was Sibeko who fired the fatal shot.

[3] In the trial court, presided over by Schwartzman J, the judge found in favor of the respondent, determining that Nemengaya owed a legal duty to protect Mohofe and had acted wrongfully and negligently, thereby holding the State vicariously liable. Subsequently, the Minister of Safety and Security appealed the decision.

---

### I – Issues
[2] The central legal question before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the Minister of Safety and Security could be held delictually liable for the actions of Inspector Nemengaya. Specifically, the court needed to ascertain whether Nemengaya’s conduct amounted to negligence that caused Mohofe’s death, despite the intervening action of Sibeko.

---

### R – Rules
[4] The principles regarding negligence are governed primarily by the test established in *Kruger v Coetzee* [1966] 2 SA 428 (A), which requires assessing whether a reasonable person in the defendant’s position would have foreseen the possibility of injury to another and whether they took reasonable steps to avoid such harm. The court must consider the surrounding circumstances, including potential risks versus benefits of the conduct in question. 

[5] The court referenced the criteria of objective foreseeability and the necessity of duty in determining negligence (see *Cape Town Municipality v Paine* [1923] AD 207). Furthermore, in *Herschel v Mrupe* [1954] 3 SA 464 (A), it was noted that a reasonable person might foresee harm but opt against precautionary action depending on the balance of risks involved.

---

### A – Application
[9] In applying the negligence test, the court scrutinized Nemengaya's decision to alert the suspects to his presence, weighing the risks of potential harm to bystanders against the imperative of duty he had to protect the public and apprehend armed criminals. The court acknowledged his training and standard procedures that compelled him to announce his presence.

[10] The court recognized that while it was objectively reasonable for Nemengaya to foresee the possibility that Sibeko might retaliate, this did not conclusively render his actions negligent. There were substantial reasons to speculate that the suspects might surrender when confronted by police. Dismissing the argument that he should have covertly pursued the suspects, it underscored that failure to act could have jeopardized public safety even further.

[12] Furthermore, the court contended that the decision to call out was consistent with established police protocols aimed at minimizing public danger. The court underscored that the reasonable officer would weigh the gravity of the risk of a bystander being shot against the necessity of arresting potentially dangerous suspects.

[16] Ultimately, the court noted that Nemengaya acted in a manner reflective of a reasonably trained officer under similar circumstances, emphasizing that his actions should be viewed through the lens of urgency and necessity.

---

### C – Conclusion
[17] The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the trial court's decision, concluding that Inspector Nemengaya did not act negligently. It held that his actions, derived from established police procedures and grounded in a duty to protect the public, were suitable given the circumstances faced. The court found that the inherent risks of confronting fleeing suspects did not outweigh the necessity of vigilant police action in a crowded area. 

The order of the lower court was set aside, and the appeal was upheld with costs, leading to the dismissal of the plaintiff's claim ([18]). This ruling reaffirms the necessity of diligent, yet proactive policing in public safety, recognizing the complex balance between immediate response and the inherent risks of armed confrontations.
