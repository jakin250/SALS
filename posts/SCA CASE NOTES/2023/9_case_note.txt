Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: DRDGOLD Limited and Another v Nkala and Others (688/2016) [2023] ZASCA 9

## FIRAC

### F – Facts

In a significant class action certification case, the Gauteng Division of the High Court was asked to certify a class action on behalf of former underground mineworkers who developed silicosis or tuberculosis, illnesses linked to silica dust exposure ([paras 1–4]). The application was initially addressed against thirty-two mining companies, culminating in a unanimous order from a specially constituted court of three judges, wherein the class action was certified ([para 2]). Academy-school approval included two specific classes: one for individuals afflicted with silicosis and another for those with pulmonary tuberculosis, with the case categorically aimed at holding the mining companies liable for negligence due to their failure to mitigate the health risks associated with prolonged silica dust exposure ([paras 5–6]).
  
Additionally, the respondents sought a declaratory order pertaining to the transmissibility of claims for general damages concerning estates of deceased claimants, which the court expanded into a broader declaration not limited to class actions as the respondents proposed ([paras 11–12]). As the litigation progressed, the mining companies' liability claims shifted due to a settlement agreement, limiting the certification to six companies and the silicosis class only ([para 12]).

### I – Issues

The core legal inquiries presented before the Supreme Court of Appeal were twofold: (1) Did the High Court's decision regarding the class action certification and the declarator constitute an appealable 'decision' under Section 16(1) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013? ([paras 13–14]); and (2) Did the interests of justice warrant that these orders be classified as appealable, permitting a review by the Supreme Court of Appeal despite their interlocutory nature? ([paras 32–39]).

### R – Rules

The judicial analysis rested on statutory provisions of the Superior Courts Act, particularly Section 16(1)(a), which stipulates the grounds for appealability, emphasizing the necessity of a definitive ‘decision’ from the High Court as defined by precedent ([paras 16–17]). Drawing from precedent in *Zweni v Minister of Law and Order* (1993), the following attributes characterize an appealable judgment: it must be final, definitive of the parties’ rights, and substantial regarding the relief sought ([paras 19–20]). Additionally, considerations around the ‘interests of justice’ laid out in prior rulings outlined that even if a decision does not meet the rigid Zweni requirements, it might still warrant appeal based on the circumstances of the case ([paras 25–28]).

### A – Application

Analyzing the certification order, the court determined that it lacked the requisite finality and definitive quality; it was merely a procedural measure aimed at facilitating the class action's conduct, thus failing to substantiate a claim of appealability under the Zweni framework ([paras 31–36]). The court addressed the appellants' assertion of potential prejudice due to participation in the certification order, finding that the appeal's advantages were insufficient to merit deviating from customary appellate principles, particularly given the options available to the trial court under procedural rules to manage such claims ([paras 32–33]).

As for the declarator, the court ruled that this, too, did not constitute a ‘decision’ under Section 16(1). It lacked specificity in defining the rights of individual claimants since substantive claims would only emerge in the second phase of the class action, underscoring its non-definitive status as a ruling and thus its non-appealability ([paras 36–39]). Furthermore, considerations relating to the socio-economic status of potential class members and the overarching urgency to expedite the main class action led the court to conclude that the interests of justice did not support granting appeal rights in this instance ([para 39]).

### C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal ruled that both the certification and the declaratory order were not appealable decisions under Section 16(1) of the Superior Courts Act. Even though leave to appeal had been granted, the absence of definitive judgment elements meant the Court lacked jurisdiction to entertain the appeal. The matter was struck from the roll, with costs awarded to the respondents, inclusive of counsel charges for the complexity and significance of the case ([paras 40–41]). The decision reinforces the principle of finality in court orders and the strict requirements governing appellate jurisdiction, highlighting the balancing act courts must navigate between procedural efficiencies and substantive justice in class action litigation.
