Source Folder: 2010

# FIRAC Case Note

## Case Title: Samancor Group Pension Fund v Samancor Chrome (452/09) [2010] ZASCA 77

### F – Facts

The present case revolves around the appeal lodged by the **Samancor Group Pension Fund** (the **Appellant**) against a decision made by the South Gauteng High Court related to a determination by the **Pension Funds Adjudicator** concerning the disability benefits of **Gerhardus Swanepoel** (the **Fourth Respondent**). Mr. Swanepoel had been employed by **Samancor Chrome** (the **First Respondent**) as a security officer until his employment ended in December 1997 due to medical issues. Following medical evaluations indicating his unsuitability for work, Mr. Swanepoel expressed a preference for receiving disability benefits, culminating in a formal complaint lodged with the Pension Funds Adjudicator on 13 May 2002 ([paras 1–9]).

The Adjudicator issued a determination on 15 February 2005, ordering **Samancor** to submit a disability claim on behalf of Mr. Swanepoel and mandating payment of due benefits once approved ([paras 9–10]). However, **Samancor** did not adhere to this determination and delayed implementation, alleging confusion regarding liability following a subsequent transaction involving the sale of its chrome division ([paras 12–13]).

On 12 May 2008, **Samancor** filed an application in the high court seeking to set aside the adjudicator's determination, claiming a failure to submit within the six-week timeframe mandated by **section 30P(1)** of the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956 ([paras 14, 19]). The high court initially granted condonation for the late application and set aside the adjudicator's determination, with Mr. Swanepoel prevailing as entitled to compensation under rule 6.5 of the Fund's rules ([paras 2, 19]).

### I – Issues

The central issues in this case are:

1. Whether the **high court** was correct in granting **Samancor** condonation for its late application to set aside the adjudicator's determination.
2. Whether the rights of **Samancor** to appeal the adjudicator's decision were perempted due to its acquiescence in the initial ruling and subsequent attempts to implement it, thus addressing the implications of prejudice to the Pension Fund and Mr. Swanepoel ([paras 3, 24]).

### R – Rules

The legal framework upon which this case is built primarily resides in **section 30P(1)** of the **Pension Funds Act**, which allows an aggrieved party to approach the high court within six weeks of an adjudicator's determination for relief. Additionally, the principles of peremption, as articulated in *Gentiruco AG v Firestone SA (Pty) Ltd* [1972] and further cited in *Natal Rugby Union v Gould* [1999], establish that a litigant's conduct may lead to a waiver of rights to appeal if they show unequivocal acquiescence in the judgment ([paras 25–26]).

The high court is empowered to govern its own procedures and exercise discretion in condonation applications, as established in *Toyota South Africa Motors (Pty) Ltd v Commissioner, SARS* [2002] ([para 20]). Furthermore, decisions concerning the interpretation of the perceived fairness of adjudicator determinations are grounded in substantive administrative law ([para 24]).

### A – Application

Upon analyzing the high court's decision to approve **Samancor's** late application, the Supreme Court of Appeal underscored serious deficiencies in the consideration of prejudice to the **Pension Fund** and the impact of **Samancor's** prolonged delay ([paras 21–24]). The Court highlighted that **Samancor** had effectively acknowledged its obligation to adhere to the adjudicator's order by attempting partial compliance after the determination was issued. Thus, its conduct demonstrated acquiescence, thereby perempting its right to further contest the decision ([paras 25–26]).

In assessing the issue of condonation, the Supreme Court of Appeal found the high court had failed to address the correspondingly adverse effects of its ruling on the **Pension Fund**, which had legitimate interests tied to the adherence and compliance with the insurance arrangements and timely claims ([paras 22-24]). The importance of timely action was paramount in preserving rights to pursue claims against insurers, as underscored by the stipulations of the **Pension Funds Act** and its provisions for member benefits ([paras 18–19]).

The Supreme Court asserted that the earlier ruling not only overlooked critical factors including potential prejudice but also misapplied the law relating to peremption and adjudicator determinations ([para 27]).

### C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal set aside the high court's order, ruling that **Samancor's** application for condonation should have been dismissed without further consideration, primarily based on the lack of timely action and established acquiescence to the adjudicator's decision. The appeal was therefore upheld, and **Samancor** was ordered to pay costs resulting from the appeal, emphasizing the principles of justice and accountability within the pension fund framework. This case underscores key principles regarding the necessity for parties to adhere to procedural timelines under statutory frameworks and the importance of timely claims processes to safeguard benefits for all involved ([paras 1, 28]).
