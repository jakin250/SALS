Source Folder: 2006

# FIRAC Case Note: Samsudin v De Villiers Berrange NO [2006] SCA 79 (RSA)

## F – Facts
This case arises from an appeal against a final order of sequestration granted by the Pietermaritzburg High Court concerning the joint estate of the appellants, Dato' Samsudin Bin Abu Hassan and Datin Mellaney Venessa Samsudin. The respondent, P De Villiers Berrange, acted as the provisional liquidator of NRB Holdings Limited (in provisional liquidation) and initiated ex parte sequestration proceedings against the appellants on December 17, 2003, which led to the granting of a provisional sequestration order ([1]).

The respondent alleged that NRBH had a liquidated claim exceeding R50 owing to it from the first appellant due to the misappropriation of shares in Mitrajaya Holdings Berhad ([25]). The facts also revealed a complex background involving substantial financial dealings, including a loan to NRBH and a resultant investment in shares, which the first appellant allegedly mishandled, ultimately resulting in significant losses for the creditor ([3]-[32]).

Notably, on December 5, 2003, the first appellant took steps to transfer funds from South Africa to Malaysia, actions perceived by the respondent as attempts to evade debt ([10], [42]). The High Court dismissed the reconsideration applications brought by both appellants, confirming the provisional sequestration ([2]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal questions involved in this case include:
1. Whether the respondent established the requirements for a final sequestration order under Section 12(1) of the Insolvency Act 24 of 1936 ([1]).
2. Whether the provisional sequestration order was justified without notifying the appellants, raising questions about due process and procedural fairness ([12], [50]).
3. The implications of alleged material non-disclosures by the respondent in the application for the provisional order ([14]).

## R – Rules
The relevant statutory provisions include:
- **Section 9(1) of the Insolvency Act 24 of 1936**: This section permits a creditor with a liquidated claim of R50 or more against a debtor who has committed an act of insolvency to petition for sequestration ([24]).
- **Section 12(1) of the Insolvency Act**: Under this section, the court may sequestrate a debtor’s estate if it is satisfied regarding the creditor's claim, the debtor's insolvency, and the advantages to the creditors from sequestration ([24]).
- **Sections 8(a) and 8(d) of the Insolvency Act**: These sections detail the acts of insolvency that can justify sequestration, including the intention to evade creditors ([38]).

In the case law, the principle established in **Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd** serves as a precedent for determining disputes of fact arising in affidavits ([32]).

## A – Application
The court analyzed the sufficiency of evidence presented by the respondent to justify the sequestration order. It assessed the allegations of misappropriation of the MITRAJAYA shares as well as the details surrounding the financial actions of the first appellant, determining that these constituted sufficient grounds for establishing a liquidated claim ([26], [35]).

The appellate court also considered the legitimacy of dispensing with the requirement of notifying the appellants about the application for the provisional order. The justification rested on the urgency of the matter, where the respondent posited that notifying the appellants could lead to asset dissipation, warranting the urgency of the ex parte application ([12], [13]).

Moreover, the court found that the first appellant's attempts to transfer funds out of the country were acts intended to prejudice creditors, satisfying the elements of Section 8(d) of the Insolvency Act ([41]).

The court determined that the first appellant had effectively misappropriated NRBH's assets, as evidenced by a lack of authority to dispose of the shares, invalidating his claims to a legitimate business arrangement ([30]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court’s decision to grant the final sequestration order. The court concluded that:
1. The respondent successfully established a liquidated claim against the first appellant as per Section 9(1) of the Insolvency Act.
2. The first appellant had committed acts of insolvency under Section 8(d), demonstrating intent to prejudice creditors.
3. It was appropriate to dispense with service of the application on the appellants, considering the circumstances of urgency and the evidential basis of the respondent's claims ([47], [49]).

Consequently, the appeal was dismissed with costs, anchoring the legal principles around the nadir of procedural fairness against the exigent circumstances faced in insolvency claims ([53]). This case reaffirms the court's authority in insolvency proceedings while balancing the necessity for procedural fairness with the protection of creditors' rights.
