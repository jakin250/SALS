Freedom Stationery & Others v Hassam & Others (921/2017) [2018] ZASCA 170

The original application was dismissed by the High Court, which ordered that shares belonging to the dissenting minority be purchased at a judicially determined value ([12]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 170

F – Facts

The case revolves around a dispute concerning the shareholding and management of four companies namely, Freedom Stationery (Pty) Ltd, Oyster Plastics (Pty) Ltd, SA File Manufacturers (Pty) Ltd, and Moss Ridge Properties (Pty) Ltd, collectively referred to as the Freedom Group ([2]). The shareholders included various family trusts and individuals, primarily related. A significant event unfolded in 2010 when certain shareholders alleged oppressive and unfairly prejudicial conduct by the majority shareholders, leading to an application under Section 252 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973 ([8]).

The original application was dismissed by the High Court, which ordered that shares belonging to the dissenting minority be purchased at a judicially determined value ([12]). Despite initially opting not to oppose the application, the trustees of the Mahomed Zukeria Hassam Trust (the M Z Hassam Trust) subsequently sought to challenge the order, arguing that it was invalid due to alleged breaches of their rights outlined in the Articles of Association ([14]). The High Court ruled in favor of the M Z Hassam Trust, leading to an appeal from the other parties ([16]).

I – Issues

  1. Whether the trustees of the M Z Hassam Trust were entitled to rescission of the Section 252 order on the grounds that it was erroneously granted in their absence ([16]).
  2. Whether the relief sought by the respondents was within the powers granted under Section 252 of the Companies Act, or whether it contravened provisions in the Articles of Association of the Freedom Group companies ([8], [24]).

R – Rules

  1. Section 252 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973: Established the framework for applications concerning oppressive or unfairly prejudicial conduct by majority shareholders.
  2. Uniform Rule 42(1)(a): Stipulates that a court may rescind or vary an order or judgment that was erroneously granted in the absence of a party affected ([18]).
  3. Judicial Precedents: The court relied on established case law interpreting both Section 252's applicability and the procedural strictures of rescinding orders, including Firestone South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Genticuro AG 1977 (4) SA 298 (AD) and Lodhi 2 Properties Investments CC v Bondev Developments (Pty) Ltd 2007 (6) SA 87 (SCA) ([16], [18]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal addressed whether the M Z Hassam Trust had established grounds for rescission. The court highlighted that a party claiming rescission under Rule 42(1)(a) must demonstrate that the order was not just procedurally erroneous but also that they had not participated in the proceedings that led to the order ([19], [25]).

The court noted the M Z Hassam Trust's prior knowledge and understanding regarding the potential outcome of the order. They chose not to participate in the Section 252 application, indicating an acceptance of the judicial proceedings' nature. Thus, they could not challenge the validity of the order on grounds of procedural error, as they should have anticipated potential outcomes ([32]).

The M Z Hassam Trust's claim that a previous sale agreement had been invalidated due to the failure to adhere to the Articles of Association was critically dismissed. The court clarified that such invalidity could not alone permit rescission, given the overarching acceptance of the Section 252 order's potential consequences ([19], [24]).

The court also evaluated the relationship between the companies within the Freedom Group, determining that the intertwined business activities implied that the M Z Hassam Trust should have been aware that shares, including those owned by the other companies, would be at stake in potential purchases ([29]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately ruled in favor of the applicants, setting aside the High Court's order and dismissing the M Z Hassam Trust's action with costs. The court reiterated that the M Z Hassam Trust's decision not to engage in the initial action represented a conscious acceptance of the risks involved, thereby negating their claim for rescission on procedural grounds. This ruling affirms the importance of litigation finality, underlining the principle that parties must actively protect their interests during judicial proceedings ([33]).

The court's decision not only upheld the Section 252 order but also clarified procedural standards regarding rescission claims under Rule 42, emphasizing that merely claiming an alleged invalidity later does not provide sufficient grounds for overturning final judgments when parties have the opportunity to participate.
