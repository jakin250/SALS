Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# Case Note: Green Willows Properties v Rogalla Investment Company (20436/14) [2015] ZASCA 133

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–10])

In June 2006, a written contract was established between the appellant, Green Willows Properties 215 (Pty) Ltd (the Purchaser), and the respondent, Rogalla von Biberstein Investment Company (Pty) Ltd (the Seller), concerning the sale of a piece of undeveloped land in Plettenberg Bay (the property) for R13,250,000. Green Willows intended to develop the property by constructing 80 residential units, necessitating rezoning approval from the local authority, Bitou Municipality ([paras 1–2]).

The contract included clauses outlining conditions for final written approval of the development, specifically the need for a resolution from the Municipality that would allow for the construction of the proposed units ([para 2]). Following the contract's execution, on 18 December 2006, the Municipality passed a resolution that Green Willows contended did not meet the standard of "final written approval" as stipulated in their contract, leading to a dispute over whether the conditions established in the contract had been fulfilled ([paras 1–6]).

After the trial in the KwaZulu-Natal High Court, the presiding judge, Pillay J, ruled in favor of Rogalla, determining that the final written approval had been granted and that Green Willows was liable for the outstanding amount of R3,250,000. Green Willows subsequently appealed this decision ([paras 6–10]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 11–17])

The primary legal issue in this appeal is whether the December resolution from the Municipality constituted final written approval of the development project as required per the contract between Green Willows and Rogalla. This interpretation involved assessing the meaning of "final written approval" in the specific context of the contract ([paras 11–12]).

Additionally, two ancillary issues arose regarding the validity of the approval process itself, focusing on whether:
1. A necessary departure from the rezoning scheme had been obtained as required by the Land Use Planning Ordinance (LUPO).
2. Condition (e) of the May resolution regarding the ownership and management of the access road had been sufficiently addressed ([paras 17–22]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 8, 11, 21, 25, 26])

In determining the parameters of final written approval, the court analyzed the obligations outlined in the contract between the parties and reviewed precedents regarding the interpretation of municipal resolutions and their legal standing. A reference was made to the judgment in *Oudekraal Estates (Pty) Ltd v City of Cape Town & others* 2010 (1) SA 333 (SCA), which supports the principle that an administrative act remains valid until set aside ([para 8]).

On the issue of recusal, the standard set forth in *President of the RSA & others v South African Rugby Football Union & others* 1999 (4) SA 147 (CC) was applied, which requires that a party must demonstrate that a reasonable person would conclude that the judge had not maintained an impartial stance towards the adjudication of the case. This involved interpreting whether comments made by a judge during proceedings could reasonably be seen as exhibiting bias ([paras 25–27]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 12–23, 24–29])

The court noted the significance of the evidence presented regarding the January resolution's interpretation. Testimonies from municipal and town planning experts confirmed that the December resolution indeed constituted final written approval necessary for the contract's conditions. The court asserted that the resolution, which set out the necessary conditions for proceeding with construction, fulfilled the precedent established in the May resolution, demonstrating substantial compliance with the contract ([paras 11–16]).

The court dismissed Green Willows’s arguments regarding the requirement of a departure for the approval of bulk area and management of the access road. It concluded that the Municipality's approval satisfied the contractual requirements, with evidence indicating that the resolution was contingent on the submission of a comprehensively developed site plan that met the conditions laid out ([paras 18–23]).

On the recusal issue, the court reaffirmed that the judge had expressed a willingness to consider further evidence. Hence, there was no basis for concluding that any bias had influenced the proceedings, dismissing Green Willows's claim for recusal as unfounded ([paras 24–29]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 29])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the lower court's judgment, affirming that the December resolution constituted the necessary final written approval. This ruling established the binding effect of municipal resolutions in the context of contractual obligations for property development. The appeal was dismissed with costs, reinforcing the decision's authority ([para 29]).

The implications of this case underscore the importance of clear contractual conditions regarding local authority approvals in property transactions. It illustrates that developers must ensure compliance with municipal requirements while providing adequate documentation to secure their contractual rights.

The court's effective handling of recusal claimed emphasizes the expectation that judges should remain impartial throughout the proceedings, reinforcing judicial integrity amidst claims of perceived bias.
