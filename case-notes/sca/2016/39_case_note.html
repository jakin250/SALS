Supreme Court of Appeal

Pro Tempo Akademie CC v C S van der Merwe obo H van der Merwe (20853/2014) [2016] ZASCA 39

In this case, the appellant, Pro Tempo Akademie CC, owned and operated a school for children with learning disabilities, including the minor student, Jacobus Hercules Van der Merwe (Jaco).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 39

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

In this case, the appellant, Pro Tempo Akademie CC, owned and operated a school for children with learning disabilities, including the minor student, Jacobus Hercules Van der Merwe (Jaco). On September 2, 2009, while engaged in play activities in the school’s playground, Jaco became impaled on a steel dropper that was erected to support saplings planted in the area ([1]). The dropper extended 60 centimeters above the ground alongside a 30-centimeter-high sapling. The school facilitated various games like cricket and rugby, where it was common for students to run near the dropper ([2]).

The circumstances surrounding the incident were uncertain. Jaco, who had been feeling unwell that day with apparent disorientation, indicated that he "sat on the dropper" but could not clearly remember the events leading to the injury ([3]). Witnesses, including staff members, testified to their observations but had no definitive explanation of how the incident occurred ([6–8]). Following the incident, Jaco required surgical intervention for significant injuries to his rectum and bladder [[1]–[4]].

The Gauteng Division of the High Court initially ruled in favor of Jaco's mother, Ms. Cornelia van der Merwe, establishing that Pro Tempo was liable for negligence without addressing the issue of quantum at that stage ([1]). The court found that the presence of a protruding steel dropper in a playground setting constituted a hazardous condition, especially considering Jaco's age and vulnerability due to his learning disabilities. The Appellant appealed this decision, claiming that public policy considerations implied that liability should not be extended in such unique circumstances ([12], [21]).

I – Issues

The central legal issues in this appeal revolved around:

  1. Whether the Gauteng Division of the High Court correctly determined that Pro Tempo was liable for negligence in causing Jaco’s injuries ([1], [13]).
  2. The relevance and application of public policy considerations to the determination of liability in this context ([14]).
  3. The interpretation of "wrongfulness" as a key element in claims of delictual liability, particularly concerning the requirements for establishing negligence ([15], [21]).

R – Rules

The legal principles applied in this case drew upon established jurisprudence concerning negligence and tort liability, including:

  1. Negligence Test: The court referred to the principles established in Kruger v Coetzee ([11]), where negligence is defined through a multi-faceted analysis involving foreseeability of harm and the reasonable steps a prudent individual would take to prevent such harm. This was foundational in evaluating whether Pro Tempo exhibited negligence in its actions concerning Jaco's injury.
  2. Public Policy in Negligence: The court noted the importance of public policy in determining the existence and extent of liability. In Hawekwa Youth Camp & another v Byrne ([16]), the court emphasized that the reasonableness of imposing liability must be considered alongside public policy norms.
  3. Case Law: The court relied heavily on Transvaal Provincial Administrator v Coley ([9]), which addressed liability when children engaged in play around potentially dangerous objects. This precedent underscored that placing hazardous objects in areas where children play could create a duty to protect those children from foreseeable harm.
  4. Constitutional Considerations: Section 28(1)(b) of the Constitution, granting every child the right to appropriate care, further influenced the court's thinking about the responsibility of educational institutions in safeguarding children's welfare ([21]).

A – Application

In applying these legal principles, the court affirmed the findings of the lower court regarding the factual circumstances surrounding Jaco’s injury. The evidence indicated Jaco's impalement resulted from proximity to the unsecured steel dropper, which was not appropriately placed considering the children’s activities ([2], [12]).

The court found that the school had a duty to anticipate that children might run and potentially fall onto hazardous protruding objects in their environment. Citing Coley, the court maintained that creating dangerous situations in areas meant for child play constituted negligence. The judge confirmed that the circumstances showed foreseeability of harm to children, which, combined with the reasonable actions a prudent school authority should undertake, led to a finding of negligence ([21]).

On the issue of public policy, the court found that arguments against liability—asserted on the grounds of Jaco's unique actions—were insufficient. Acknowledging that Jaco exhibited impulsive behavior characteristic of children with learning disabilities, the court emphasized that the responsibility fully encompassed the institution's duty to provide a safe environment for all students ([12], [21]). Such public policy principles reminded the court of its obligations under constitutional rights and the peculiar vulnerabilities of the children it served ([15], [19]).

The arguments leveraged by the appellant claiming extraordinary or unique circumstances were dismissed as not establishing a valid basis for excluding liability ([14], [22]). The court's analysis confirmed that public policy considerations favored protecting vulnerable children within the educational context, thereby warranting the upholding of liability against Pro Tempo.

C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court's ruling that Pro Tempo Akademie CC was liable for Jaco's injuries due to negligence. The court's reasoning highlighted the established principles of foreseeability and the duty of care owed to children in educational settings, particularly vulnerable populations with learning disabilities. This decision re-affirmed the significance of public policy and constitutional protections when considering the duties of educational institutions ([22]). The case sets a crucial precedent emphasizing the responsibility of schools to ensure that their premises are safe for all students, thereby strengthening the legal framework protecting children's rights in South Africa.

The Appellant was ordered to cover the costs of the appeal, including the costs of two counsel, underscoring the court's firm stance on the matter of institutional neglect ([22]).
