Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

## FIRAC Case Note: Tadvest Industrial (Pty) Ltd and Another v A Hanekom & Others

### F – Facts
The case before the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa involves a review of taxation concerning costs awarded to the first and second respondents, Anthea Hanekom and Stuurman Hanekom, by the court after the dismissal of their application for leave to appeal ([1]-[2]). The legal dispute revolved around the consequences of a purported settlement agreement where the respondents allegedly waived their rights to claim costs in exchange for various obligations from the applicant, Tadvest Industrial (Pty) Ltd ([3]).

The taxation process was initiated following a costs order granted to the respondents, which included the expenses relating to the litigation concerning land occupation ([2]). The applicant challenged the validity of the costs claims based on an assertion that the respondents had settled their rights to claim costs through a written settlement agreement. The respondents countered, claiming that they had not waived their rights and that the applicant had not complied with its obligations under the agreement ([3]-[6]). 

The taxing master confirmed the costs without delving into the disputed claims of payment or the alleged waiver, leading to the applicant’s request for a stated case and a review of the taxation ([8]-[12]). The taxing master stated that his role was to enforce existing court orders related to costs rather than assess underlying agreements between parties ([12]).

### I – Issues
The core legal issues presented in this case involve:
1. Whether the taxing master had the authority to consider the alleged waiver of costs in light of the settlement agreement ([15]-[19]).
2. Whether the taxing master’s decision to finalize the taxation, despite the applicant's objections, was lawful and within his jurisdiction ([12]-[20]).
3. The implications of the alleged waiver on the entitlement to costs as claimed by the respondents under the order of the Supreme Court ([2]-[4]).

### R – Rules
Relevant legal rules and precedents inform the court’s approach in this case, including:
- **Rule 17(3)** of the Supreme Court of Appeal’s rules, which limits the scope of review of taxation to challenges regarding specific items and not to the validity of a costs order itself ([16]).
- The constitutional principles espoused in *President of the Republic of South Africa and Another v Gauteng Lions Rugby Union*, clarifying that a taxing master's role is to ensure a reasonable fee arrangement as per court orders, rather than to adjudicate on disputed aspects of those orders ([17]).
- The precedent established in *Berman & Fialkov v Lumb*, which asserts that questions regarding the validity of agreements related to costs must be adjudicated by a court, not a taxing master ([18]).

### A – Application
The Supreme Court applied these legal principles to ascertain whether the taxation process was appropriate given the circumstances of the case. The applicant contested the taxing master’s authority, arguing that there were substantial unresolved issues regarding the enforceability of the costs order linked to the purported waiver presented by the respondents ([8]-[10]). 

Ultimately, the court concurred with the taxing master’s position that such matters were beyond his remit. It reaffirmed that the taxing master's function is reactive to costs orders made by courts and that he does not have the jurisdiction to question the validity or enforceability of such orders or agreements ([15]-[20]). The emphasis was placed on the principle that the appropriate avenue for disputing the waiver and its implications for costs lay in a formal court setting rather than in the taxation process ([18]-[19]).

### C – Conclusion
The court ultimately dismissed the review of taxation with costs, affirming that the taxing master acted within his jurisdiction, and rejected the applicant's arguments regarding the waiver and the obligations of the respondents under the settlement agreement ([20]). The judgment delineates the boundaries of a taxing master’s authority and underscores the necessity for parties to resolve substantive disputes regarding costs waivers through established legal channels, rather than within the taxation framework. 

This case emphasizes the procedural rigor in cost recoveries following litigation and the importance of clear agreements in settlement contexts, ultimately serving as a reference point for future cases involving taxation and costs disputes in South Africa's legal landscape.
