Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Mojapelo v The State (574/2014) [2016] ZASCA 22

### F – Facts
The case revolves around the circumstances leading to the murder of Mr. Albert Mojapelo, whose body was discovered on 28 September 2005, next to his red Toyota bakkie in Orange Farms. The appellants, Tinky Sophie Mojapelo (the first appellant) and Antoinette Nkhesani Masuku (the second appellant), were charged along with an accomplice, Mr. Orlando Mandoza, who absconded before trial ([paras 2-3]). The two appellants were convicted in the South Gauteng High Court by Satchwell J of murder, conspiracy to commit murder, and other offenses on 18 June 2009 ([para 3]). The appellants initially sought to appeal their convictions to a full bench of the North Gauteng High Court, but their appeal was dismissed ([para 4]).

The evidence against the appellants rested primarily on the testimony of Mr. Sakhele Malwane, an accomplice who was warned per Section 204 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([para 5]). He alleged that the first appellant conspired to murder her husband with the second appellant’s encouragement. Malwane stated he witnessed discussions regarding the murder, payment to Mandoza, and ultimately, the events of the night of the murder, where he claimed to have been forced to drive the deceased to a location where he was executed ([paras 6-8]).

In the trial court, critical issues emerged regarding Malwane's credibility, with Satchwell J finding inconsistencies in his testimony and labeling him as an accomplice ([paras 13-15]). The trial court also relied upon statements made by the first appellant to the deceased’s sister, Ms. Makgato, as additional incriminating evidence ([para 11]).

### I – Issues
The principal legal issue on appeal was whether the State had proven beyond a reasonable doubt that the appellants were guilty of murder, relying solely on the uncorroborated and disbelieved testimony of an accomplice ([para 2]). Specifically, the court needed to determine whether Malwane's allegations constituted sufficiently credible evidence to support the convictions given the uncertainties surrounding his truthfulness and the absence of corroborating evidence.

### R – Rules
The Supreme Court of Appeal highlighted several critical rules regarding the reliability of accomplice testimony:

1. **Caution in Convictions**: Courts must approach evidence from accomplices with caution due to the risk of falsely implicating innocent individuals ([para 16]).
2. **Corroboration Requirement**: While corroboration of an accomplice's testimony is not a strict legal requirement, when it is sought, it must directly implicate the accused ([para 18]).
3. **Credibility Evaluation**: There must be some reliable evidence establishing a link between the accused and any alleged conspiracy or crime committed ([para 19]).
4. **Discharge Application**: A trial court must discharge an accused when the evidence presented does not reasonably support a conviction ([para 22]).

Relevant case law, including *S v Francis* and *S v Eyssen*, underscores the importance of corroborative evidence in cases involving accomplices and the special dangers associated with convicting based on such testimony ([para 17]).

### A – Application
In reviewing the trial court's findings, the Supreme Court noted that the trial judge, Satchwell J, had found Malwane's evidence to be “bizarre” and full of inconsistencies. The finding that Malwane could not be trusted as a credible witness was central to the appeal ([paras 13-15]). 

Despite Satchwell J recognizing substantial flaws in Malwane’s account, including discrepancies about his involvement and the nature of payments made, the trial court nonetheless accepted parts of his testimony as reliable. This led to a significant misdirection because the core of the State's case relied on Malwane's flawed narrative without any corroborating evidence, such as cellular phone records that could establish communication between the appellants and Mandoza ([paras 9, 20]).

The absence of such corroboration significantly undermined the case against the appellants. The appellate court observed that the test for a prima facie case was not met as there was no credible evidence implicating the appellants in any conspiracy or the murder itself. Furthermore, the court found Satchwell J's rationale for convicting based on alleged statements made by the first appellant after the murder to be unfounded and insufficient to maintain a conviction given the broader context of unreliable evidence ([paras 24-25]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that the State had failed to prove the guilt of both appellants beyond a reasonable doubt. The convictions and sentences imposed were set aside, reflecting a critical assessment of the evidentiary thresholds required in criminal law, notably concerning accusations hinging on uncorroborated accomplice testimony ([paras 1, 24]). The implications of this judgment reaffirm the necessity for robust and credible evidence in securing convictions, particularly in cases involving serious allegations such as murder.

In sum, the court highlighted the fundamental principle of fair trial rights, ensuring that no individual is convicted based on unreliable or unsupported evidence, thereby reaffirming the high threshold required for criminal convictions in South African law.
