Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note: Robert Paul Serné N O and Others v Mzamomhle Educare and Others (588/2023) [2024] ZASCA 152

### F – Facts

This case involves an appeal against a decision by the Western Cape Division of the High Court, which originally dismissed an application for the eviction of several unlawful occupiers from the immovable property known as Erf 22933 Kraaifontein, situated at 74 Grootboom Avenue, Wallacedene, Cape Town ([para 2]). The appellants, who are the trustees of the Mzamomhle Foundation Trust, had successfully purchased the property in 2011 on behalf of the Mzamomhle Educare, represented by Ms. Margaret Noxolo Ngaleka. The Trust also constructed an Early Childhood Development Centre (ECDC) on the property ([paras 4–5]).

Following Ngaleka's death in 2016, her descendants, Bongeka Mqolombeni and Siphokazi Mqolombeni, contested the Trust's ownership of the property, claiming they inherited it from their mother. They alleged that Ngaleka was mislead into signing documents concerning the donation and subsequent leasing of the property to the Trust ([paras 6–12]). An application to vacate was served on them in late 2018, leading to the eventual legal proceedings ([para 8]).

The high court dismissed the eviction application on multiple grounds, including consideration of hearsay evidence provided by the second respondent, Mqolombeni, which led to significant legal debate over ownership and the evidentiary burden ([para 15]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal questions involve the following ([para 14]):
1. **Ownership Rights**: Did the Trust validly acquire ownership of the property, and did Mqolombeni have grounds to dispute this ownership?
2. **Validity of the Lease**: Was there a valid lease between the Trust and Mzamomhle Educare, and did the lease expire, thereby affecting the respondents' right to occupy the property?
3. **Application of the Rei Vindicatio**: Did the Trust establish its claim for eviction under the principle of *rei vindicatio*, which requires the owner to prove ownership and the possession of the property by another party?
4. **Constitutional Considerations**: Were the rights of the respondents, particularly concerning housing and children's education as enshrined in the South African Constitution, adequately considered in the eviction proceedings?

### R – Rules

The legal framework applicable to this case includes:
- **Principle of Rei Vindicatio**: As established in *Chetty v Naidoo*, the owner must merely prove ownership and that the property is held by another party, with the onus on the latter to establish any right to continue holding the property ([paras 27–28]).
- **Law of Evidence**: The admissibility of hearsay evidence is governed by the Law of Evidence Amendment Act 45 of 1988. Generally, hearsay is inadmissible unless it meets certain criteria under section 3 of the Act. The court may admit it if it serves the interests of justice ([paras 20–21]).
- **Constitutional Rights**: Sections 25 (property), 26 (housing), and 28 (children’s rights) of the Constitution are important in considering any potential infringement of rights arising from eviction applications ([para 30]).

### A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed whether the high court correctly applied these legal principles. It concluded that the high court had erred in its reliance on hearsay testimony that did not robustly challenge the Trust's ownership of the property. The evidentiary weight of Mqolombeni's claims was found lacking, particularly given that she provided information absent of corroborating evidence and basing her claims on her mother's alleged, but uninformed, perceptions of the transaction ([paras 22–23]).

The court highlighted that the respondents’ counterclaims concerning wrongful acquisition of ownership and the validity of the lease did not establish a legal right to continue occupying the property. Ownership by the Trust was undisputed based on registered transfer documents, and the lease had effectively expired ([paras 25–31]). As a result, the appellants established the necessary claim for eviction, invoking the rei vindicatio to reclaim possession of their property.

In addressing constitutional implications, the Court emphasized that the eviction was not from a residence but a commercial property, thereby indicating that the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act (PIE) was inapplicable. The Trust had intentions to continue the operation of the ECDC, thereby ensuring no adverse impact on the education of children ([paras 31–32]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately found that the high court wrongly considered factors relevant to an eviction application without adequately applying the correct substantive law. The Court reversed the high court's ruling, ordering the respondents to vacate the property within five days and authorized the sheriff to carry out the eviction if necessary ([para 34]). The judgment underscores the limited scope of hearsay evidence in property disputes and reaffirms the principle that registered ownership stands until legally challenged. Additionally, it illustrates the balance that must be struck between protecting property rights and considering constitutional imperatives, especially in cases involving non-residential properties.
