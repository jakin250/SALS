Supreme Court of Appeal

Surmon Fishing (Pty) Ltd v Compass Trawling (Pty) Ltd (162/2008) [2008] ZASCA 142

The present appeal arises from the granting of certain long-term hake fishing rights by the Minister of Environmental Affairs and Tourism under section 18 of the Marine Living Resources Act 18 of 1998 ([para 1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2008 [2008] ZASCA 142

F – Facts

The present appeal arises from the granting of certain long-term hake fishing rights by the Minister of Environmental Affairs and Tourism under section 18 of the Marine Living Resources Act 18 of 1998 ([para 1]). Due to the capital-intensive nature of deep-sea trawling and the small tonnages of fish allocated to various permit holders, a joint venture agreement was formulated in 1999 between Blue Continent Products (Pty) Ltd and Azanian Fishing (Pty) Ltd. This agreement enabled the pooling of their fishing rights for better economic viability ([paras 1–2]).

The first appellant, Surmon Fishing (Pty) Ltd, subsequently joined this joint venture. To exploit their fishing rights, Blue Continent purchased a trawler, the MFV Compass Challenge, for R 20.5 million and later sold it to Compass Trawling (Pty) Ltd for R 33.6 million, with loans facilitated by Blue Continent ([para 3]).

On 13 August 2007, Foodcorp (Pty) Ltd made an offer to Surmon to purchase its hake rights, which triggered the obligations under Clause 9.3 of the General Terms and Conditions (GTC) governing the joint venture ([para 5]). Surmon informed Compass Trawling of the offer on 27 August 2007.

On 12 September 2007, a board meeting of Compass Trawling saw four of six directors vote in favor of purchasing Surmon’s hake rights based on the Foodcorp offer. However, the second appellant, Sam Montsi, contested the meeting’s validity, attempting to convene a shareholders’ meeting to overrule the board’s decision ([paras 6–7]).

The conflict intensified when Surmon's attorneys claimed that Compass Trawling’s decision was invalid, leading Surmon to accept Foodcorp’s offer, thereby triggering a legal impasse. An application for declaratory relief was launched, where the Cape High Court ruled in favor of Compass Trawling, affirming the validity of the board's resolution to purchase Surmon's rights ([paras 8–9]).

I – Issues

Two primary legal issues arose in this case:

  1. Applicability of Clause 7.10 of the GTC: Whether Clause 7.10, which necessitated a vote of more than 66.6% of directors for certain decisions, applied to the board of Compass Trawling for the approval of the acquisition ([para 10]).
  2. Validity of the Board Decision: Whether the required majority for the board's decision was attained and whether Surmon could unilaterally accept the Foodcorp offer ([paras 10–11]).

R – Rules

The rules relevant to resolving these issues include:

  1. Clause 7.10 of the GTC: It stipulates that decisions of the management board require a majority of more than 66.6% of members present and voting ([paras 9–10]).
  2. Articles of Association of Compass Trawling: These indicate that decisions of the board require a simple majority vote ([para 15]).
  3. Shareholders Agreement: This agreement further clarifies voting protocols, stating that resolutions not passed unanimously are deemed rejected unless passed by a majority vote ([para 15]).
  4. Case Law: Reference is made to Fundstrust (Pty) Ltd (in Liquidation) v Van Deventer 1997 (1) SA 710 (A), emphasizing the need for contextual understanding in interpreting statutory provisions and contracts ([para 12]).

A – Application

The Court, upon interpretation of Clause 7.10, favored the position that 66.6% should equate to a majority greater than two-thirds. The trial court initially accepted that four out of six votes constituted a valid majority. However, upon appeal, it was contended that this interpretation was misplaced, as the use of “more than” 66.6% necessitated a stricter standard ([paras 11–13]).

The appeal court clarified that Compass Trawling, as a separate corporate entity, was governed by its articles of association and the shareholders agreement. The board of directors acted within the framework provided by the company's governance documents and was not bound by the GTC's voting requirements, which pertained to the management of the joint venture ([para 16]).

The critical aspect emerged from Clause 8 of the agreement of assignment, which stated that if there were conflicts between the GTC and the shareholders' agreement, the latter would prevail. The court determined that this clause fundamentally undermined the appellants' contention that Clause 7.10 applied, thereby reinforcing the trial court’s finding that Compass Trawling had lawfully resolved to acquire Surmon's rights ([para 17]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, upholding the lower court's ruling that Compass Trawling’s board had validly passed the resolution to purchase Surmon’s fishing rights. The judgment clarified that the procedural norms of the joint venture did not extend to the corporate governance of Compass Trawling and that the directors’ decision was within their authority under the articles of association.

The implications of this ruling underscore the importance of clearly defined corporate governance structures and the precedence of shareholders' agreements over general terms relating to joint ventures. This case reinforces the principle that companies must operate within their defined regulatory frameworks and that voting procedures must be interpreted contextually based on their specific terms ([paras 17–18]).

The appeal was dismissed with costs, solidifying the judicial affirmation of Compass Trawling's rightful exercise of its corporate powers in the procurement of the hake fishing rights ([para 18]).
