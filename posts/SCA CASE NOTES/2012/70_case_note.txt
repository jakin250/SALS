Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# Case Note: Keyser v State [2012] ZASCA 70

## FIRAC

### F – Facts
Heinrich Carl Keyser, a 35-year-old married man, was arrested at Johannesburg International Airport on December 10, 2004, after arriving from Sao Paulo, Brazil. Upon his arrest, Keyser was charged with importing 6.5 kg of cocaine, violating Section 5(b) of the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 140 of 1992. He was found to be in possession of a rucksack containing clothing saturated with cocaine, alongside a shampoo bottle concealing a cocaine-filled condom. Keyser was sentenced to 20 years in prison by the regional magistrate ([paras 1–2]). 

In his appeal against both the conviction and sentence, Keyser raised two primary grounds. Firstly, he argued that the trial was fundamentally flawed because the charge included references to Sections 20 and 21 of the Act, which impose a reverse onus that had been declared unconstitutional. Secondly, his counsel contended that the State failed to prove his guilt beyond a reasonable doubt ([paras 3–6]).

Keyser's defense argued that he was an innocent courier exploited by unknown individuals. The State established that he had engaged in suspicious activities, including an extended stay in South America, uncertainty regarding job interviews there, and arrangements to carry a rucksack to South Africa ([paras 7–14]). 

Critical points in the evidence included the testimony of Inspector Mars, who arrested Keyser after noticing a strong chemical odor and discovering cocaine in the rucksack. Keyser's contradictory claims regarding his knowledge of the bag's contents and his intended payment for carrying it were pivotal in the case ([paras 19–23]).

### I – Issues
The primary issues before the court were:
1. Whether the inclusion of unconstitutional reverse onus provisions in the charge sheet constituted a fatal irregularity affecting the fairness of the trial ([paras 3–5]).
2. Whether there was sufficient evidence to prove beyond a reasonable doubt that Keyser knowingly participated in drug trafficking, rather than being an unwitting courier ([paras 6, 23]).

### R – Rules
The court relied on established principles of criminal law regarding the evidential burden of proof, which dictates that the State must prove the accused's guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. Furthermore, the court considered precedents addressing the implications of declaring reverse onus provisions unconstitutional, particularly:
- **Daniels v S** [2012] ZASCA 71, where it was elucidated that convictions could only be overturned on procedural irregularities if they resulted in a failure of justice ([paras 4, 6]).
- The criteria for inferential reasoning as discussed in **S v Reddy and Others** 1996 (2) SACR 1 (A), emphasizing that a judge must thoroughly evaluate the credibility of both the State's and defense's evidence before determining guilt ([paras 23]).

### A – Application
On examining the evidence as a whole, the court determined that despite the irregularities concerning the charge, they did not materially affect the conduct of the trial or Keyser's ability to mount a defense ([paras 5, 25]). The key aspect was whether the evidence unequivocally pointed towards Keyser's guilt.

The court addressed the appellant's claims of innocence, identifying various implausibilities surrounding his defense. These included:
- The lack of credible explanations regarding how and why he ended up carrying the rucksack filled with drugs.
- The suspicious circumstances around his travel, including payments for airfare and accommodation by individuals he had tenuous connections to, which contradicted his assertion of ignorance ([paras 24, 30]).

Moreover, the court noted that the appellant's admissions to Inspector Mars, coupled with the significant volume of cocaine—indicating a serious intent rather than mere negligence—supported the conclusion that he was not an unwitting courier ([paras 20, 30]). Ultimately, the appeal against the conviction failed due to the overwhelming evidence of guilt ([paras 27]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Keyser's conviction and sentence, dismissing his appeal. The court maintained that while the charge contained references to some unconstitutional provisions, this did not equate to a failure of justice as the evidence overwhelmingly demonstrated Keyser's guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. The court's ratio decidendi emphasized the need for careful evaluation of the entire context in cases where the appellant claims a failure of justice based on procedural irregularities. The implications of this case highlight the stringent evidentiary requirements in drug trafficking cases and the courts' inclination to emphasize public safety over individual claims of innocence in the face of substantial evidence ([paras 27-32]).
