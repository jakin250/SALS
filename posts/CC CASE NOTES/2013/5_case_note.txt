Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: Hattingh v Juta (CCT 50/12) [2013] ZACC 5

### F – Facts ([paras 1-9])
In this case, the three applicants—Michael Hattingh, Edwina Junita Hattingh, and Pieter Hattingh—sought leave to appeal a decision made by the Supreme Court of Appeal that upheld a judgment by the Land Claims Court, which ordered their eviction from a cottage located on a smallholding owned by Laurence Edward Juta in Stellenbosch ([1], [2]).

The cottage, originally two separate units, was converted by Juta to accommodate Mrs. Magrieta Hattingh, the mother of the applicants, and her family. Initially, her right to occupy the cottage was based on her employment with Juta as a domestic worker until 2005. Disputes arose regarding the basis of the applicants' residence, with Juta claiming an oral agreement limiting their stay, while the applicants argued they had a right protected under the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA). The Land Claims Court ruled that Mrs. Hattingh's right to family life, as articulated in section 6(2)(d) of ESTA, did not extend to adult children who were not dependants ([7]-[12], [15]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal affirmed this view, concluding that the right to family life was confined to spouses and dependants, prompting the applicants to seek relief from the Constitutional Court ([19], [36]).

### I – Issues ([paras 24-25])
The key issue before the Constitutional Court was the interpretation of section 6(2)(d) of ESTA, specifically whether it encompasses the applicants as part of Mrs. Hattingh's family, and whether her right to family life would be infringed by their eviction ([24]).

Additionally, the Court considered the broader implications of this case, particularly as it pertains to the rights of occupiers under ESTA and the impact of eviction on vulnerable families in South Africa, thus raising questions concerning the balance of property rights and the rights of the occupiers ([24], [30]).

### R – Rules ([paras 30-41])
The relevant legal framework comprises the Extension of Security of Tenure Act, particularly section 6(2)(d), which grants occupiers the right to family life without prejudice to the rights of the landowner. This provision must be read in conjunction with sections 5 and 6(1) of ESTA, which reaffirm the rights of occupiers while emphasizing the need for just and equitable considerations in balancing the interests of both the owner and the occupier ([31], [41]).

Statutory definitions and relevant precedents concerning the meaning of "family" and "culture" were pivotal, including the understanding that familial associations may extend beyond the nuclear family, as iterated in cases such as *Dawood* ([34]-[36]) and the balancing acts described in previous case law ([32]-[33]).

### A – Application ([paras 34-42])
The Court evaluated whether Mrs. Hattingh's familial context could extend to include her adult children within the ambit of "family life" under section 6(2)(d). Zondo J pointed out that the term “family” in ESTA should not strictly apply to only dependants but may encompass additional family members as long as their presence does not impinge on the landowner's rights ([34]).

In conducting the balancing exercise, the Court identified several factors that weighed in favor of eviction, including:

1. Juta’s willingness to accommodate Mrs. Hattingh and her specific needs concerning health.
2. The applicants’ independent status and admission that they were seeking alternative housing.
3. The competing rights of Juta as landowner to reallocate the space for practical purposes ([42]).

Despite the acknowledgment of acute housing shortages in the region, the Court concluded that the limitation of Mrs. Hattingh’s right to family life would thus not infringe upon her rights, leading to the determination that eviction would be just and equitable ([42]).

### C – Conclusion ([paras 45])
The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal but upheld the Supreme Court of Appeal's decision to dismiss the application of the Hattingh family. Juta’s needs as a landowner and the credible assertion that Mrs. Hattingh could continue to live independently with recognized assistance deemed the eviction of the applicants justifiable. 

The order stipulated that the eviction should be adapted to grant a reasonable time frame for the applicants to vacate the property while emphasizing the necessity for a balanced interpretation of rights under ESTA. Consequently, the Court underscored the critical need for compassion and justice in dealing with vulnerable households living on others' land, ultimately demonstrating a nuanced understanding of familial rights within the South African constitutional context ([45]).

---

This case serves as a pivotal development in the interpretation of familial rights and the balance of property law, reinforcing the need for equitable resolutions in land disputes rooted in South Africa's historical injustices.
