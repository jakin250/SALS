Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2008

# FIRAC Case Note: DINGAAN HENDRIK NYATHI v MEMBER OF THE EXECUTIVE COUNCIL FOR THE DEPARTMENT OF HEALTH, GAUTENG

## F – Facts
The case of *Nyathi v MEC for the Department of Health, Gauteng* ([1]–[92]) revolves around the application by applicant Dingaan Hendrik Nyathi, who suffered serious injuries due to alleged medical negligence while receiving treatment in state hospitals. After successfully obtaining a High Court order for interim damages, which the state failed to comply with, he challenged the constitutionality of **Section 3** of the State Liability Act ([3]). This section forbids the execution or attachment of state property to satisfy a court order, asserting that such a provision rendered court judgments against the state unenforceable. Nyathi passed away before the case was heard, leading to his wife replacing him as the applicant ([4]). The matter was heard on an urgent basis due to the applicant's deteriorating health and the state's failure to comply with court orders ([19]–[20]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issues presented in this case are:
1. Does Section 3 of the State Liability Act implicitly limit the rights to equality, dignity, and access to courts as guaranteed by the South African Constitution ([40], [47])?
2. If there is a limitation, is this limitation reasonable and justifiable under Section 36 of the Constitution ([49])?
3. What is the appropriate remedy for the applicant in light of the constitutional allegations ([89])?

## R – Rules
This case is grounded in several crucial legal principles:
- **Sections 34, 165(5), and 195(1)(f)** of the Constitution, which guarantee the right to access the courts, state accountability, and the binding nature of court orders ([24]).
- **Section 3 of the State Liability Act** (Act No. 20 of 1957), which prohibits the attachment of state property in satisfying judgments against it; this provision was challenged as unconstitutional ([3], [4]).
- The **test of rationality** from case law such as *Prinsloo v Van der Linde*, establishing that differentiation must serve a legitimate governmental purpose ([140]), and the possibility of justifiable limitations on rights under Section 36 of the Constitution ([49]).

## A – Application
**Limitation of Rights:** The court assessed whether Section 3 limits rights guaranteed by the Constitution ([45], [47]). It was argued that it unfairly distinguishes between private and state debtors, thereby contravening the principle of equality under Section 9. The Court acknowledged the unequal treatment of judgment creditors against the state compared to those against private entities. It also highlighted that the inability to attach state assets leads to practical impediments for creditors seeking enforcement ([40], [47]).

**Reasonableness and Justification of Limitations:** Examining whether the limitation was justifiable, the court reflected on governmental interests in maintaining service delivery and preventing disruptions ([49]). Despite a persuasive argument for accountability, the court determined that the need to preserve essential state functions justified the existence of Section 3. Nevertheless, it underscored that such justification does not absolve state officials from their duties to comply with court orders ([51], [146]).

**Remedies:** The Court emphasized that available legal remedies, such as a mandamus compelling compliance with the order, were overlooked by the applicant ([148], [149]). Although underscoring the potential ineffectiveness of those remedies in practical terms, it refused to declare Section 3 unconstitutional based on the non-exhaustion of available avenues.

## C – Conclusion
The Court ultimately decided to confirm the High Court’s order declaring Section 3 of the State Liability Act unconstitutional. However, it suspended the declaration for twelve months to afford Parliament time to rectify the legal framework facilitating attachment and execution against the state ([91]). This decision marks a vital precedent for ensuring state accountability while balancing public interest considerations, reaffirming that while state interests must be preserved, the judiciary maintains an essential role in safeguarding individual rights ([92]).

The implications of this ruling underscore a shift towards improved accountability in state litigation practices while necessitating legislative reform to ensure compliance with judicial mandates.
