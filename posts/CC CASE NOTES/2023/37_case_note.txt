Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

### FIRAC Case Note: *The Ad Hoc Central Authority for the Republic of South Africa and Another v Koch N.O. and Another* [2023] ZACC 37

#### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–35])

The case involves a 6-year-old girl, referred to as E, who was born in the United Kingdom (UK) to British nationals. Upon the diagnosis of her mother's terminal illness, E travelled to South Africa (SA) in September 2019 with her parents, intending to return to the UK the following month. However, after undergoing surgery, E's mother could not return to the UK and decided to remain in SA with E, asserting that the father could not provide adequate care. E's father sought her return to the UK through the UK Central Authority, which initiated proceedings under the Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction (Convention) ([paras 1–12]).

Prior to the proceedings initiated by the Ad Hoc Central Authority (AHCA) in SA, the mother and her sister filed an application to confer parental rights on the aunt. The High Court ruled in favor of returning E to the UK, but subsequent appeals led to the Supreme Court of Appeal upholding the aunt's appeal based on an alleged grave risk of psychological harm to E ([paras 13–35]).

#### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 36–65])

The key legal issue revolves around the interpretation of Article 13(b) of the Hague Convention, particularly its application regarding the threshold for "grave risk" of psychological harm or placing the child in an intolerable situation. The case presents questions about the balancing of the child's best interests against the principles of the Convention ([paras 36–49]).

Other issues include:
1. Whether the circumstances of E's situation warranted an exception under Article 13(b);
2. The weight of the evidence regarding the potential risks to E if returned to her father in the UK ([paras 50–65]).

#### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 66–100])

The court primarily referenced international conventions and legal precedents regarding child abduction and custody rights, particularly emphasizing:
1. The Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction, specifically Articles 12 and 13(b);
2. South African legal principles pertaining to the best interests of the child, as enshrined in section 28(2) of the Constitution;
3. Judicial precedents including *Sonderup v Tondelli* and others addressing custody and return procedures in similar contexts ([paras 66–85]).

The court also noted the significance of historical rulings in establishing the standard of proof required to meet the threshold of "grave risk" ([paras 86–100]).

#### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 101–130])

The court critically assessed the evidence presented by both parties. It examined expert testimonials, particularly from psychologists who evaluated E's psychological state and her potential for developing attachments. The majority found that the Supreme Court of Appeal had overstepped its jurisdiction by incorrectly applying the *Plascon-Evans* principle, leading to an inappropriate assessment of the evidence ([paras 101–120]).

The dissenting opinion emphasized the ongoing risks involved if E were to return to the UK, outlining the potential psychological harm she might experience by being displaced from her established caregiving environment. The court applied a forward-looking approach to determine if E's return would expose her to serious harm ([paras 121–130]).

#### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 131–145])

The Constitutional Court ruled to uphold the appeal, ordering E's return to the UK under conditions that would safeguard her welfare, thus restoring her father's rights and disrupting the aunt's custody claim. The court underscored that the child's long-term interests must be evaluated within the jurisdiction of her habitual residence, promoting the policy against self-help in parental disputes. It further acknowledged the vital role of the UK social services in mitigating potential risks ([paras 131–145]).

The implications of this ruling extend to future Hague Convention proceedings, emphasizing the need for expeditious legal frameworks to manage international child abduction cases effectively and align them with constitutional rights regarding the best interests of the child. The ruling also delineates the nuanced balance between protecting children and upholding the legal framework established by international law ([paras 145]).

---

This case highlights critical legal principles relevant to international child abduction and emphasizes the importance of optimizing legal proceedings to protect children's rights without unnecessary delays.
