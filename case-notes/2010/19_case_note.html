Case Title: Camps Bay Ratepayers' and Residents' Association v Harrison and Others

Constitutional Court 2010 [2010] ZACC 19 Decision Date: 4 November 2010 --- ##

Case Title: Camps Bay Ratepayers' and Residents' Association v Harrison and Others

Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa
Case Number: CCT 18/10
Judgment: [2010] ZACC 19
Decision Date: 4 November 2010

F - Facts

The case involves an application for leave to appeal against the decision of the Supreme Court of Appeal concerning the municipality's approval of building plans on property owned by the first respondent, Gerda Yvonne Ada Harrison. The origins of the dispute trace back to September 2004, when Ms. Harrison acquired the previously modest cottage on her property at the intersection of Geneva Drive and Blinkwater Road, Camps Bay, Cape Town. On 24 February 2005, the City of Cape Town approved the original plans for a three-storey house which led to the demolition of the existing structure and the commencement of construction on 17 March 2005 ([1]–[2]).

Following objections from the applicants, the Camps Bay Ratepayers’ and Residents’ Association and PS Booksellers (Pty) Ltd, the City approved revised plans (the September 2005 plans) on 8 September 2005. The applicants subsequently filed a High Court application to interdict Ms. Harrison from further construction, arguing that the plans violated specific conditions imposed by the title deed and zoning regulations. Meer J granted the interdict, citing that certain structures labeled as boundary walls were, in fact, retaining walls, which violated the 3.15-meter setback requirement ([3]–[5]).

Despite subsequent modifications leading to the September 2007 plans which eliminated some objections, the applicants again sought to challenge the City’s approval of these later plans in the High Court based on the same grounds, alleging that the City did not consider their objections adequately. The High Court ultimately dismissed the application, leading to an appeal, which was also rejected by the Supreme Court of Appeal. This prompted the current application to the Constitutional Court ([11]–[22]).

I - Issues

The primary legal issues in this case revolved around:

  1. The interpretation and application of section 7(1) of the Building Act concerning the obligation of municipal authorities to consider objections to building plans.
  2. Whether the City acted unlawfully by approving plans that allegedly contravened title deed restrictions and zoning regulations concerning property height and setbacks.
  3. The procedural validity of the introduction of new objections outside the 180-day period prescribed by the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA) and whether such grounds could be entertained by the courts ([26]–[64]).

R - Rules

Several legal principles and statutes underpin the Court's findings:

  1. Building Act: Section 7(1) stipulates the local authority's responsibilities regarding the approval of building plans, particularly concerning recommendations by building control officers and considerations of public objection ([13]–[14]).
  2. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA): Section 7 establishes a 180-day period for lodging review applications, during which time legal challenges to administrative actions must be initiated. Extensions are possible but must meet substantive criteria ([25]).
  3. Constitutional Rights: The issues raised also invoked rights under sections 33 and 25 of the South African Constitution—specifically, the right to just administrative action and the right to property.

A - Application

The Court examined the legal arguments presented by both parties regarding the interpretation of section 7(1)(b)(ii) of the Building Act, which concerns whether a local authority must refuse to grant approval if the proposed building will derogate from the value of adjacent properties. The Court noted a divergence in interpretation of this section between the judgments of the Constitutional Court in Walele v City of Cape Town and the Supreme Court of Appeal in this matter. The Constitutional Court reaffirmed that the local authority holds a broad discretion to refuse approval based on potential detriment to neighboring property values, which places the onus on objectors to sufficiently show such potential impacts ([27]–[40]).

On the applicants' procedural arguments regarding the introduction of new review grounds, the Court ruled that the Supreme Court of Appeal correctly concluded these were not raised within the prescribed time frame and thus could not be introduced in reply without proper justification ([51]–[62]).

Furthermore, the Court clarified the interpretations of unrestricted title deed conditions, asserting that the City, as the approval body, acted within its jurisdiction in its decision-making process ([67]–[74]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the application for leave to appeal on all grounds, indicating that the applicants failed to meet the interests of justice criterion necessary for the court to intervene. The Court held that:

  1. The Supreme Court of Appeal's interpretation of section 7(1) of the Building Act and the implications thereof on the applications raised were sufficiently reasoned and did not constitute a violation of any constitutional rights.
  2. No unlawful conduct was found on the City's part in approving the contested plans, as the legal requirements had been met satisfactorily.

The implications of this ruling reaffirm the notion that local authorities have significant discretion in administrative matters related to urban planning and zoning, thereby enhancing the stability of local governance processes in the context of property development disputes ([75]–[77]).
