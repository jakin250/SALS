# FIRAC Case Note: Panayiotou v Shoprite Checkers (248/2016) [2017] ZASCA 12 

## F – Facts 
The case arises from an application made by the first respondent, Shoprite Checkers (Pty) Ltd, for a liquor store licence in the Rivonia Village shopping centre, where it operated a supermarket licensed to sell table wine ([1]). The Gauteng Liquor Board (the Board) initially refused the licence on 14 April 2014, citing concerns over competition and potential monopolistic behaviour in violation of the Gauteng Liquor Act 2 of 2003 (the Act) ([2]). Andreas Panayiotou, the appellant, objected to the licensing of Shoprite, arguing that it would adversely impact his existing liquor store located 150 meters away ([1]).

Subsequently, Shoprite sought judicial review of the Board's decision, leading the Gauteng Local Division of the High Court, Johannesburg, to set aside the refusal, direct the Board to issue the liquor licence, and provide for conditions under which the licence should be issued ([3]). The High Court's decision was not immediately appealed by either Shoprite or the Board, although Panayiotou's application for leave to appeal was granted ([5]). 

On 28 October 2015, following the compliance with statutory requirements, the Board issued the liquor licence to Shoprite, which began trading shortly thereafter ([4]). By December 2015, Panayiotou moved for an interdict to prevent Shoprite from operating under the newly granted licence; however, this interdict was denied ([4]). Panayiotou’s subsequent appeal against the High Court's decision raised questions about the practical effect of the appeal given that the licence was already issued and trading had commenced ([6]).

## I – Issues 
The primary legal issue before the court revolved around whether the appeal filed by Panayiotou could be dismissed on the grounds that it would have no practical effect due to the issuance of the liquor licence ([7]). A necessary sub-issue involves whether the appeal pertained to any discrete legal issue of public importance that would warrant judicial review despite the absence of practical consequences from the appeal ([8]).

## R – Rules 
The court's adjudication was guided by section 16(2)(a) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which stipulates that an appeal may be dismissed if the decision sought will have no practical effect or result ([7]). Key legal principles were derived from prior case law, notably Oudekraal Estates (Pty) Ltd v City of Cape Town & others, which asserts that even unlawful administrative acts may produce legally valid consequences so long as they are not judicially set aside ([7]). Furthermore, precedents indicate that while courts can decline to hear an appeal under this provision, they may exercise discretion to address questions of public importance ([8]).

## A – Application 
In applying the rules to the current scenario, the court recognized that the issuance of the liquor licence to Shoprite constituted a fresh administrative act, fully compliant with the procedural requirements under the Act; consequently, this act had not been challenged ([6], [7]). The unanimous agreement amongst the justices reflected that the existing licence permitted Shoprite to trade lawfully, thereby rendering Panayiotou's appeal devoid of practical consequences ([7]).

Additionally, despite Panayiotou's counsel asserting potential grounds for appeal, the court noted the absence of any submission identifying a legal issue of public significance for which a determination could be warranted ([8]). The ruling emphasized that the appellate judiciary must avoid unnecessary burdens from matters lacking in practical implications, a view echoed by previous decisions emphasizing judicial economy ([8]).

## C – Conclusion 
The court ultimately dismissed the appeal, concluding that it had no practical effect or result per section 16(2)(a) of the Superior Courts Act. As such, the appeal would not change the licensing situation pertaining to Shoprite, which had already commenced its operations under the granted licence ([9]). The ratio decidendi pivoted on the critical finding that the license’s existence rendered further judicial review moot and underscored the legal principle that unchallenged administrative acts continue to hold validity ([8], [9]). This case reinforces the principle of judicial economy and clarifies the procedural posture for future cases involving similar issues, ensuring that courts conserve resources by declining cases that exert no substantial effect or modify existing facts.

In terms of implications, the judgment signals the importance of timely responses to administrative decisions and effectively highlights the necessity for all parties to proactively engage with new developments during litigation. The order, dismissing the appeal with costs, underscores the court's stance on the viability of administratively sanctioned actions within the bounds of statutory compliance ([9]).
