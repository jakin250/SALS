FIRAC Case Note: Afriforum NPC v Minister of Tourism and Others; Solidarity Trade Union v Minister of Small Business Development and Others [2021] ZASCA 121

F - Facts

In early 2020, the COVID-19 pandemic prompted the South African government to declare a national state of disaster on 15 March 2020 under the Disaster Management Act 57 of 2002 (DMA) ([1], [3]). This declaration led to the promulgation of regulations allowing ministers to issue directions to manage the effects of the pandemic ([4], [8]). Acting under the authority granted by these regulations, the Minister of Tourism issued a direction on 6 April 2020, creating a Tourism Relief Fund aimed at offering financial assistance to small, micro, and medium enterprises (SMMEs) within the tourism sector ([1], [19]).

The Fund was established with an allocation of R200 million and was intended to provide once-off capped grants of up to R50,000 to eligible businesses negatively impacted by COVID-19 ([6], [19]). Notably, eligibility was tied to criteria established under the Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment Act 53 of 2003, particularly the requirement for applicants to possess a specific B-BBEE status ([20], [36]).

The cases emerged when two applicants, Afriforum NPC and Solidarity Trade Union, claimed that the race-based eligibility criteria were discriminatory and sought to review and set aside the Minister's direction ([2], [30]). The High Court dismissed their applications, and they subsequently sought appeals from the Supreme Court of Appeal ([2], [30]).

I - Issues

The principal issue in the appeals was whether the Minister was legally obligated to include B-BBEE criteria in the eligibility for funding from the Tourism Relief Fund as per section 10(1)(e) of the B-BBEE Act, or whether her direction could be viewed as unlawful due to her misinterpretation of statutory obligations ([31], [46]). Additionally, the appeals raised the question of whether the Minister's exercise of power constituted administrative action subject to review under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([31], [34]).

R - Rules

The legal framework relevant to this case includes:

  1. Disaster Management Act 57 of 2002 (DMA): This Act allows for the management of disasters through specific regulatory powers vested in the government ([8], [12]).
  2. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA): This Act defines 'administrative action' and outlines the grounds for judicial review ([31]).
  3. Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment Act 53 of 2003 (B-BBEE Act): Particularly section 10(1)(e), which mandates that organs of state must apply relevant codes of good practice when determining eligibility for granting incentives and financial assistance to promote economic empowerment ([36], [42]).

Judicial precedents such as Esau and Others v Minister of Co-operative Governance and Traditional Affairs and Grey’s Marine Hout Bay (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Public Works were also considered, emphasizing the distinction between administrative and public powers ([34], [52]).

A - Application

The Court embarked on a detailed analysis to ascertain whether the Minister's direction constituted administrative action within the ambit of PAJA. It noted that if the Minister’s directive was administered in a manner consistent with bureaucratic norms, it would fall within the scope of PAJA ([34]).

The Supreme Court found that the Minister had believed herself legally bound to incorporate B-BBEE requirements in the eligibility criteria; however, this misinterpretation of the law resulted in an error of law that materially influenced her decision-making process ([54]). The court juxtaposed the purposes of the DMA and the B-BBEE Act, clarifying that while both statutes aim for transformative objectives, their scopes are distinct and their applications should not overlap unless expressly provided for by law ([44], [46]).

The Court likewise suggested that the Minister's reliance on the B-BBEE Act was misplaced, as the purpose of the funds derived from the Tourism Relief Fund was to mitigate the economic consequences of the pandemic, rather than to further B-BBEE objectives. Thus, the inclusion of race-based criteria represented an unlawful overreach ([54], [56]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court ultimately upheld the appeals, declaring the Minister's directive unlawful on the grounds of misapplying the statutory requirements of the B-BBEE Act, leading to a material error of law. It asserted that while the Fund had already disbursed all allocated finances, a declaratory order was warranted due to the significant public interest involved and the implications for future administrative actions in disaster management ([55], [56]).

The Court directed that the Minister was not obliged to recover any disbursed funds from the beneficiaries who received support through the Fund, balancing accountability with fairness to the benefited enterprises ([55], [56]).

This case serves as a critical reminder of the necessity for precision in statutory interpretation by public officials and the importance of adhering to legitimate objectives when exercising statutory powers. The judgment underscores the judiciary's role in safeguarding administrative justice and preventing the misuse of power, particularly in contexts affected by racial and economic equity considerations.
