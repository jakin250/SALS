Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: Notyawa v Makana Municipality and Others [2019] ZACC 43 

## F – Facts
The case revolves around Mr. Mbulelo Paul Gladstone Notyawa (the applicant), who applied for the position of municipal manager in November 2014 following a public advertisement issued by the Makana Municipality (first respondent). Despite being shortlisted and interviewed, Notyawa's appointment was initially resolved by the municipal council in March 2015. However, the Member of the Executive Council for Co-operative Governance and Traditional Affairs, Eastern Cape (second respondent), later expressed dissatisfaction with this appointment, citing non-compliance with the prescribed qualifications under the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 ([paras 12-15]).

Notyawa initiated a review application in the High Court in July 2015 to set aside the MEC's refusal to appoint him, but failed to advance the case to a hearing ([paras 16-20]). This failure culminated in a 23-month delay, which was central to the High Court's decision. Eventually, the High Court dismissed Notyawa's application for condonation of the delay due to its unreasonableness and the absence of satisfactory explanations for inaction ([paras 21-30]). The High Court also deemed that the underlying issue had become moot following municipal elections and subsequent legislative restrictions on managerial appointments, leading to a lack of prospects for success in the case ([paras 27-29]).

## I – Issues
The primary issue for determination was whether it was in the interests of justice to grant Notyawa leave to appeal the High Court's refusal to condone his delayed review application ([para 1]). A secondary issue was the characterization of the decisions by the Municipality and the MEC—specifically, whether these constituted administrative or executive action under the legal framework, particularly the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA) and the legality principle derived from the Constitution ([paras 34-36]).

## R – Rules
The applicable legal rules stem from both statutory provisions and case law. Section 54A of the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act mandates the careful appointment of municipal managers with prescribed qualifications ([para 3]). The Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA) governs administrative actions, requiring an applicant to demonstrate a "reasonable period" for bringing applications ([para 22]). 

In the context of delay, the principles established in case law, including **Khumalo v MEC for Education, KwaZulu-Natal** and **Asla Construction**, outline a two-stage approach for evaluating delay applications under PAJA ([paras 46-52]). These decisions emphasize that a failure to intuitively explain delays, along with practical implications and prospects for success, critically inform the courts’ discretion to condone delays.

## A – Application
The High Court applied the legal principles of PAJA, assessing Notyawa's explanations for delay as insufficient and failing to cover the entire span of 23 months ([paras 23-30]). The court also examined the substantive merits of the initial review application, concluding that Notyawa would likely not succeed due to a lack of requisite qualifications as per the Systems Act—a determination that justified the dismissal ([para 28]).

On appeal to the Constitutional Court, Notyawa's claim was further scrutinized. Justices noted that the High Court had exercised its discretion appropriately, considering the implications of the delay on service delivery and affirmatively concluded that the impugned decisions had not demonstrated a compelling illegality that warranted overlooking such delays ([paras 49-52]). The Court found that while there is discretion to overlook delays, such discretion must be exercised with an acute awareness of constitutional compliance, which was not sufficiently established in Notyawa's case ([paras 52-54]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately concluded that it was not in the interests of justice to grant leave to appeal. The rationale was based primarily on the determination that the High Court had not exercised its discretion judicially but appropriately; the delay was excessive and unexplained, and prospects for success were minimal ([paras 54-56]). The Court dismissed Notyawa's application with no order as to costs, aligning with principles established in **Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources**, recognizing that public interest considerations in constitutional matters warrant leniency in cost orders against unsuccessful applicants when they are engaging with state bodies ([para 55]).

Thus, the verdict reflected not only adherence to principles of legal reasoning but also highlighted the necessity for timely judicial action in administrative justice, emphasizing the Court's commitment to ensuring that local governance operates effectively under prescribed legal frameworks.
