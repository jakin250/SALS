Source Folder: 2016

# Case Note: Snyders and Others v de Jager [2016] ZACC 53

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

In this case, the applicants, Karel Snyders, Sofia Snyders, and their minor children (collectively referred to as the "Applicants"), brought an application for contempt against the respondent, Louisa Frederika de Jager ([paras 1–2]). The underlying conflict arose out of several prior judgments relating to the occupation of a residential property located in Kriel, Mpumalanga. Following a judgment from the Supreme Court of Appeal on September 30, 2015, which ordered the eviction of the Snyders family from their home, the respondent executed this eviction on October 1, 2015, without prior notice ([para 2]).

Consequently, on October 16, 2015, the Constitutional Court issued an interim order directing Ms de Jager to restore peaceful possession of the property to the Snyders family pending the outcome of their appeal ([para 3]). Despite this order, Ms de Jager failed to restore possession within the prescribed timeframe, indicating that the new occupant, Mr. Willem Breda, refused to vacate the premises. Ms de Jager attempted to persuade Mr. Breda to move back to a different accommodation, which he declined, leading her to contemplate legal action for eviction but ultimately deciding against it due to perceived low prospects of success ([paras 4–5]).

The Snyders subsequently brought a contempt application, asserting that Ms de Jager’s failure to comply with the interim order amounted to contempt of court ([para 5]). Ms de Jager countered that her actions were bona fide and intended to comply with the court's order ([para 6]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal question before the court was whether Louisa de Jager could be held in contempt of court for failing to comply with the interim order issued on October 16, 2015, requiring her to restore the Snyders family's peaceful possession of their home. Specifically, the court needed to determine if Ms de Jager acted with **mala fides** or if her failure to comply was due to a bona fide belief that she was not in breach of the court's order ([para 7]).

### R – Rules

The legal principles governing contempt of court require the demonstration of **mala fides** — that is, the individual's willful disobedience of a court order ([paras 7–8]). The court identified a key element of contempt as the necessity for the party alleged to be in contempt to have acted in bad faith, which denotes an intention to disregard the court's authority or its orders ([para 8]). The court also recognized the complexity of compliance when the order does not explicitly delineate the required actions, thus leaving room for reasonable interpretations ([para 8]). 

### A – Application

The court, under Zondo J, analyzed the actions of Ms de Jager in light of the evidence presented. Ms de Jager had taken steps attempting to comply with the court's order by asking Mr. Breda to vacate the property and even sought guidance from the Registrar of the Constitutional Court regarding further actions she might take, which supported her claim of bona fides ([para 6–8]). 

The court concluded that her failure to restore possession was not indicative of an intention to defy the court's order, as there was no evidence suggesting Ms de Jager wished to retain Mr. Breda in the house against the court's decision. Instead, her inability to execute a successful eviction was a reflection of the challenges she faced, which included Mr. Breda's refusal to comply ([para 9]). Thus, the court found that without demonstrating **mala fides**, contempt could not be established, leading to the dismissal of the contempt application ([para 10]).

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court, in a unanimous decision, dismissed the application for contempt against Louisa de Jager, ruling that she did not act in bad faith in her attempts to comply with the court order ([par 11]). The court ordered that each party bear their own costs, reflecting a consideration of justice and equity, particularly given the socio-economic context of the Snyders family as farm workers living in challenging conditions ([paras 10–11]). This judgement underscores the judiciary's intent to uphold the principle that contempt must involve an element of willful disobedience and reinforces the importance of constructive compliance with court orders.

**Court Holding:** The application for contempt is dismissed, and no costs order is made.

**Implications:** This case highlights the parameters of what constitutes contempt of court, emphasizing the requisite element of mala fides in such applications, which is crucial in maintaining judicial respect and authority, especially in contexts where compliance is complex and contested.
