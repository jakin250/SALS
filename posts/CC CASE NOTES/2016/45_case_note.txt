Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# Case Note: Lawyers for Human Rights v Minister in the Presidency and Others [2016] ZACC 45

## F - Facts

The constitutional challenge in **Lawyers for Human Rights v Minister in the Presidency** arose from **Operation Fiela-Reclaim**, which was launched in response to violence against non-South African nationals during 2015. The operation involved police and military forces conducting widespread searches and arrests without warrants in communities across the nation ([1]).

The applicant, **Lawyers for Human Rights (LHR)**, represented several individuals detained during these operations. They launched an urgent application in the High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Division, six weeks after the conclusion of the operation, alleging that it was conducted in violation of various statutes, including the South African Police Service Act 68 of 1995, the Defence Act 42 of 2002, the Refugees Act 130 of 1998, and the Immigration Act 13 of 2002 ([2]). The High Court ruled that LHR's application was inappropriate due to the absence of evidence for further illegal actions and denied the application with costs ([3]).

Subsequently, LHR sought leave to appeal the costs order against it, which was ultimately dismissed by both the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal. The case was then brought before the Constitutional Court, focusing on the appropriateness of the costs awarded by the High Court ([4]–[5]).

## I - Issues

The primary legal issues were:

1. **Constitutionality of Costs Orders**: Whether the High Court’s costs order was constitutionally justified, especially in light of LHR's role of representing clients asserting constitutional rights against state actors ([6]).

2. **Application of Legal Principles**: The applicability of established legal principles related to costs in constitutional litigation, particularly referencing **Biowatch Trust v Registrar: Genetic Resources** [2009] ZACC 14, in determining if circumstances warranted an adverse costs order against a litigant ([8]).

3. **Discretion of the High Court**: Whether the High Court exercised its discretion properly in declaring the application for urgent relief as manifestly inappropriate and awarding costs against LHR ([12]–[13]).

## R - Rules

The Court applied the following legal principles regarding costs in constitutional litigation:

1. **General Rule on Costs**: The general rule mandates that the unsuccessful party in litigation bears the costs, unless exceptional circumstances dictate otherwise ([13]).

2. **Biowatch Principle**: The principles established in **Biowatch** indicate that costs should generally not be awarded against unsuccessful litigants when the case involves a genuine constitutional issue and pertains to rights against state parties. This principle is intended to encourage litigants to assert constitutional rights without the fear of heavy costs ([15]).

3. **Discretion of Courts**: Courts exercise discretion in awarding costs; they must consider the conduct of the parties, the nature of the proceedings, and whether the application was vexatious or frivolous. A costs order is justified where the court determines that the litigant acted improperly or with ulterior motives ([18]).

## A - Application

The Constitutional Court analyzed the facts of the case against the principles of costs in constitutional litigation:

1. **Nature of Urgency**: The Court acknowledged that while LHR's application raised constitutional issues regarding dignity and privacy, the application’s belated nature (six weeks post-operation) rendered its urgency problematic. The High Court deemed the timing and manner of bringing the application as inappropriate, as it had become academic by then, with no imminent threat of future unlawful raids ([22]).

2. **Discretionary Powers of the High Court**: The High Court's discretion was scrutinized. The Constitutional Court concluded that, despite the constitutional importance of LHR's claims, the High Court acted within its rights to impose a costs order. LHR's failure to substantiate the urgency justified the High Court’s decision ([24]).

3. **Constitutional Implications of Costs Orders**: The Court emphasized that while adverse costs orders can chill the willingness of parties to assert their constitutional rights, the High Court’s ruling was justified in the context of LHR’s conduct, which it found to be inappropriate. As such, the High Court’s application of the guidelines from **Biowatch** was seen as appropriate, allowing for costs to be awarded based on the specifics of the situation ([25]).

## C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court dismissed LHR's application for leave to appeal against the costs order. It found that the High Court had exercised its discretion within legal bounds and did not act in a manner that warranted interference. The dismissal was aligned with the judicial principle that litigants must respect both the timing and manner of legal challenges, particularly in the realm of constitutional rights. The ruling underscored the importance of managing court processes judiciously and established a clear guideline for future litigants regarding the consideration of costs in constitutional matters. Overall, the Court reaffirmed that the principles from **Biowatch** must not be exploited to escape accountability for improper procedural conduct ([28]).
