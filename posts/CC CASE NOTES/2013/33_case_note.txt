Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# Case Note: **Minister of Police & Others v Premier of the Western Cape & Others [2013] ZACC 33**

## FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts (paras [1]–[10])

This case arises from a conflict among different organs of state regarding the powers of the Premier of the Western Cape to appoint a commission of inquiry into the inefficiency of policing in Khayelitsha, a township in Cape Town. The applicants, which include the Minister of Police, the National Commissioner of the South African Police Service (SAPS), and various high-ranking police officials, contested the legitimacy of this commission, claiming it overstepped constitutional boundaries.

The controversy has its origins in complaints delivered to the Premier on 28 November 2011 by the Women’s Legal Centre (eighth respondent) on behalf of civil society organizations, outlining serious deficiencies in police effectiveness, citing high crime rates and inefficiencies in policing. The complaints indicated a lack of visible policing, inadequate victim support, and a breakdown in relations between the community and the police.

Following a series of correspondences attempting to address these concerns, the Premier appointed a commission of inquiry on 22 August 2012. The applicants responded by applying to the High Court for an interdict to halt the commission's activities, asserting the Premier lacked constitutional authority to issue subpoenas to police members. The High Court ruled in favor of the Premier, leading to the appeal before the Constitutional Court.

### I – Issues (paras [11]–[16])

The principal legal issue is whether the Premier of the Western Cape possesses the constitutional authority to appoint a commission of inquiry that includes subpoena powers over members of the South African Police Service, particularly in light of concerns regarding existing cooperative governance principles and intergovernmental relations defined under Chapter 3 of the Constitution.

The applicants argue that:

1. The complaints submitted to the Premier did not constitute the jurisdictional facts necessary for the appointment of the commission.
2. The relevant constitutional provisions (sections 206(3) and 206(5)) do not grant the Premier authority to appoint a commission with coercive powers against police officials.
3. The Premier failed to meet her constitutional obligations under intergovernmental relations before establishing the commission.
4. The terms of reference for the commission were overly broad and vague.

### R – Rules (paras [1]–[10], [11]–[18])

The Court's analysis rests on several constitutional provisions:

1. **Sections 206(3) and (5)** of the Constitution, which outline provincial powers to monitor police efficiency and conduct inquiries into police-community relations.
2. **Section 41** of the Constitution, which mandates cooperative governance among state organs.
3. The **Western Cape Provincial Commissions Act, 1998**, which provides legislative backing for the powers assigned to the Premier regarding the establishment of commissions.
4. Judicial precedents, notably *Doctors for Life* [2006] ZACC 11, establishing the exclusive jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court in disputes impacting governance structure and powers among state organs.

### A – Application (paras [26]–[51], [58]–[70])

The Constitutional Court analyzed the Premier’s authority under the relevant constitutional provisions. It determined that while the powers bestowed on provincial executives related to policing are limited, they include the authority to establish commissions of inquiry for accountability purposes.

The court pointed out that the existence of police inefficiencies in Khayelitsha, evidenced by the complaints received, justified the establishment of the commission. The Court found that:

1. The commission's role was crucial in ensuring accountability and transparency about police actions, which resonated with the constitutional obligation to protect citizens' rights.
2. The argument that the subpoena powers of the commission constitute control over the police service mischaracterizes the nature of oversight and accountability, which is fundamentally different from operational control.
3. The commission’s coercive powers are necessary to compel testimony and gather evidence, ensuring the effectiveness of investigative functions mandated by section 206(5).

Regarding the Premier's compliance with Chapter 3 obligations, the Court determined that sufficient consultation efforts had been undertaken prior to establishing the commission. Therefore, the claims of various breaches of cooperative governance lacked merit.

The Court also dismissed concerns regarding the vagueness of the commission’s terms of reference, asserting they clearly defined the inquiry's scope and aligned with the specified complaints regarding police inefficacy in Khayelitsha.

### C – Conclusion (paras [71]–[73])

The Constitutional Court concluded that the Minister and the National Commissioner’s application to declare the Premier's appointment of the commission of inquiry unconstitutional and invalid was without merit. The Premier acted within her constitutional authority when forming the commission to investigate police inefficiency. The Court emphasized the need for holistic mechanisms to ensure police accountability and provided a clear affirmation of the province's legislative competence concerning policing matters.

The application for direct access was denied, and the initial appeal against the High Court’s decision was dismissed. Additionally, the Social Justice Coalition, having raised pertinent issues regarding police conduct, was granted costs, reinforcing the recognition of the role civil society plays in promoting accountability and justice. 

---

This case establishes critical precedents regarding the balance of powers among national and provincial authorities, particularly in the context of policing, and affirms the necessity of accountability mechanisms in safeguarding citizens' rights. Through its comprehensive ruling, the Court underlined the importance of cooperative governance and the functional integrity of provincial oversight in matters affecting public safety and community welfare.
