Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: Minister of Health and Another v Alliance of Natural Health Products (South Africa) [2022] ZASCA 49

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–12])

This case arises from an appeal following a decision made by the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, where the respondent, The Alliance of Natural Health Products (South Africa) (the Alliance), challenged the validity of regulations promulgated by the Minister of Health (the Minister) under the Medicines and Related Substances Act 101 of 1965 (the Act) ([para 1]).

The Minister, in conjunction with the South African Health Products Regulatory Authority (the Authority), issued the General Regulations on 25 August 2017 (GN 859) as part of their efforts to regulate not only traditional medicines but also complementary medicines and health supplements ([para 2]). After several years of public consultation, the Alliance sought declaratory orders, contesting the regulations on the grounds that they exceeded the Minister’s rule-making powers as outlined in the Act and were, thus, ultra vires. The High Court partially upheld this challenge, leading to the present appeal concerning the validity of the regulations under the ultra vires principle ([para 8]).

### Key Legislative Context

The Act delineates four categories for regulation: medicines, scheduled substances, medical devices, and in vitro diagnostic devices. Critically, it provides that the definition of ‘medicine’ is limited to substances intended for therapeutic purposes, thus establishing a clear boundary for regulatory powers ([paras 3–4]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 7–12])

The primary legal issue before the court was whether the regulations made by the Minister were ultra vires, meaning they purported to regulate substances that did not fall within the definition of ‘medicines’ as stipulated in the Act, thereby exceeding the Minister’s conferred powers ([para 7]). The Alliance maintained that these regulations impermissibly regulated complementary medicines and health supplements, which were not classified as ‘medicines’ or ‘scheduled substances’ according to the Act's definitions ([para 7]).

Additionally, the case raised several procedural concerns, notably whether the Minister had adequately considered public comments on the draft regulations and whether the regulations themselves were irrational or procedurally unfair ([para 8]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 19–21, 23–24])

The court referenced the definition of 'medicine' as set forth in the Act, which explicitly states that a medicine must be used or marketed for therapeutic purposes ([para 19]). The central tenet of this definition, as agreed upon by the parties, is that it must always pertain to therapeutic efficacy ([para 20]).

In support of the concept of ultra vires, the court relied upon precedents such as *Savoi and Others v National Director of Public Prosecutions and Another* [2014] ZACC 5, which discussed the implications of an abstract challenge to regulations that seemed constitutionally unsound. The judgment established the facet of standing for applicants challenging administrative decisions on the grounds of legality ([para 13]).

Furthermore, the court considered the implications of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA), determining that the regulations constituted administrative action requiring a procedural fairness evaluation ([para 15]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 26–28])

The Supreme Court of Appeal noted that the jurisdiction of the Minister was strictly framed by the Act. It concluded that the regulations in question extended their regulatory reach into areas beyond those designated explicitly as ‘medicines’ or ‘scheduled substances’. As such, the new definitions for complementary medicines did not align with the legislative intent and scope delineated in the Act ([para 26]).

The court ultimately agreed with the argument presented by the Alliance, determining that the regulations were ultra vires since they encompassed many substances that were not ‘medicines’ under the Act ([para 26]). The court stressed that this breach of authority was significant enough to warrant invalidation, reinforcing the need for regulations to remain within the legislative framework's scope ([para 26]).

With respect to procedural arguments, the court upheld the appellants' assertions that comments on regulations had been considered, but noted that any substantive irrationality claims remained largely unaddressed within the confines of the appeal ([para 11]). 

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 28–29])

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the regulations were ultra vires and thus invalid to the extent that they improperly governed substances outside the categorization of ‘medicines’ or ‘scheduled substances’ as defined in the Act ([paras 26–27]). The appeal was dismissed with costs, including costs for two counsel, reaffirming the need for the Minister to re-evaluate and amend the regulations specifically to comply with statutory mandates ([para 29]).

In modifying the order of the lower court, the Supreme Court indicated that the declaration of invalidity would be suspended for a year to allow the Authority time to rectify the regulatory framework while emphasizing the public interest in regulating complementary medicines and health supplements temporarily ([para 28]). This judgment underlines the judiciary's role in ensuring administrative actions adhere to statutory boundaries and highlights the importance of public consultation in the regulatory process, suggesting future evaluations of regulatory frameworks will be crucial for compliance with existing statutory provisions.

---

This case exemplifies the intricate balance that must be maintained between regulatory powers and statutory boundaries in South African administrative law, particularly in the evolving field of health products regulation. It offers crucial insights into how the courts can intervene when executive power may overreach its mandate, establishing a vital precedent for future regulatory challenges.
