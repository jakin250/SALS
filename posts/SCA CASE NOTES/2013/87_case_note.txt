Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: **Emergency Medical Supplies and Training CC v Health Professions Council of South Africa** [2013] ZASCA 87

### F – Facts
The appellant, Emergency Medical Supplies and Training CC (trading as EMS), challenged the decision of the Professional Board for Emergency Care Practitioners (the Board), which withdrew its accreditation to train emergency care practitioners. EMS had originally obtained accreditation in 1999 to conduct training for four Basic Ambulance Assistance (BAA) courses, three Ambulance Emergency Assistance (AEA) courses, and one Critical Care Assistance (CCA) course. However, by 2004, it was discovered that EMS had expanded its operations to offer 11 BAA courses, five AEA courses, and two CCA courses without the requisite approval from the Board ([1]–[4]).

Faced with complaints about the standard of training, inadequate facilities, and poor student performance, the Board conducted investigations, which included examining the results of students in November 2006. The subsequent findings prompted the Board to revoke EMS's accreditation ([4]–[16]).

EMS appealed this decision under section 20 of the Health Professions Act 56 of 1974 (the Act). The core issue was whether the appeal was wide—allowing for review of the entire matter—or narrow, restricted to whether the decision was correct based on the existing record ([2], [5], [6]).

### I – Issues
The case presented the following central legal questions:

1. Is the appeal properly before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) given the procedural history, including an earlier appeal that was struck from the roll?
2. Does section 20 of the Health Professions Act afford a wide or a narrow appeal? If it is wide, can the appellant rely on additional review grounds, such as bias and conflict of interest, or is it limited solely to the merits of the original decision ([3], [6])?

### R – Rules
The applicable legal framework includes:

1. **Health Professions Act 56 of 1974**: Section 20 provides a right of appeal against decisions made by the council or professional boards, though its interpretation regarding the nature of the appeal—whether wide or narrow—was central to the decisions made.
   
2. **Precedents**:
   - **Health Professions Council of SA v De Bruin [2004] 4 All SA 392 (SCA)**: Established that appeals under section 20 of the Act are confined to the evidence before the board, categorizing it as a narrow appeal.
   - **De Beer v Raad vir Gesondheidsberoepe van Suid-Afrika 2007 (2) SA 502 (SCA)**: Reinforced the notion that appeals under section 20 are not review processes but strictly focused on the correctness of the decisions made ([8], [9]).

### A – Application
The SCA addressed the procedural issue first, concluding that the case was appropriately before the court. It dismissed the appellant's argument regarding res judicata, stating that the earlier appeal did not result in a definitive conclusion on the issue of whether the appeal was wide or narrow, as it had been struck from the roll without a ruling on the merits ([5], [10]).

On the substantive issue of whether the appeal under section 20 is wide or narrow, the court followed the precedents established in *De Bruin* and *De Beer*, asserting that such appeals should be treated as narrow, focused on the merits based on evidence available from the Board's record ([10]–[11]). The court dismissed the reasoning from the lower courts, stating there were sufficient records for consideration, thus enabling the conclusion that EMS had contravened provisions of section 16(1) of the Act during its operations ([17]).

The court assessed the merits of the case, scrutinizing the conduct of EMS that included running courses beyond its approved accreditation and the poor performance of its students in board-set examinations. The Board's decision to revoke EMS's accreditation was upheld as lawful and justified based on evidence about inadequate training standards ([12]–[24]).

### C – Conclusion
The SCA dismissed the appeal, confirming that section 20 of the Health Professions Act establishes a narrow appeal confined to evaluating the merits of decisions based solely on the evidence presented to the Board. The implications of this ruling reinforce the delineation between appeals and reviews in South African administrative law, ensuring adherence to accreditation standards in emergency medical training ([4], [24]).

The judgment serves as a significant precedent regarding the interpretation of appeal rights under the Health Professions Act, affirming the Board's role in maintaining educational standards within the medical profession and protecting public interests.
