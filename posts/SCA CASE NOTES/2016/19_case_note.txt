Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

## FIRAC Case Note: Zwane v The State (700/15) [2016] ZASCA 19

### F – Facts
[1] The appellant, Mr. Ernest Vusi Majazi Zwane, was arrested on 17 November 2000, facing charges of murder, attempted murder, and robbery with aggravating circumstances. The incidents occurred in KwaMashu Township, Durban, at approximately 19:30 on 31 July 2000, when the deceased, Mr. Bafana Emmanuel Zondi, was shot multiple times. On 11 March 2002, the trial court convicted Zwane of murder, sentencing him to life imprisonment on 18 March 2002, with no reference to minimum sentencing provisions.

[2] After initially appealing only the sentence, the appellant was later granted leave to appeal against both his conviction and sentence by the Supreme Court of Appeal. The substantive issues revolved around the sufficiency of evidence linking Zwane to the crime and whether the trial court improperly relied on the mandatory sentencing provisions under Section 51 of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 without advising the appellant of these provisions.

[3] The state’s case hinged primarily on the testimonies of six witnesses, including the deceased's brother, Nene, who knew the appellant well. Nene identified Zwane and another, Bongani Kwenyama, as the assailants. Other witnesses' accounts varied in detail but corroborated the rapid sequence of the crime observed from well-lit surroundings reaching a consensus that multiple individuals were involved in the shooting.

### I – Issues
[3] The primary legal issues at the heart of this appeal are:
1. Whether there was sufficient evidence to identify the appellant as one of the assailants responsible for the murder of the deceased ([3]).
2. Whether the trial court erred in its application of Section 51 of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 as it relates to sentencing, specifically regarding the failure to inform the appellant of the potential for a life sentence under these provisions ([24]).

### R – Rules
[19] The evidentiary standards applicable to single witness testimony are governed by Section 208 of the Criminal Procedure Act, which allows for convictions based on the testimony of a single competent witness, emphasizing the necessity for such testimony to be substantially satisfactory ([20]). This principle was underscored in *Stevens v S* [2005] 1 All SA 1 (SCA) and *S v Mahlangu* [2011] ZASCA 64; 2011 (2) SACR 164 (SCA), which clarified that while single witness testimony can suffice for conviction, it must be approached with caution and assessed against credibility factors ([21]).

[23] Section 51 provides that a court must impose a life sentence for murder unless "substantial and compelling circumstances" are present. Critically, in *S v Ndlovu* [2003] (1) SACR 331 (SCA), it was established that the implications of this provision must be communicated to the accused at the commencement of the trial so as to allow for an adequate defense ([25]). 

### A – Application
[16] The trial court's credibility finding that favored the state witnesses, particularly Nene's, was central to the conviction. The court found that Nene had close visibility and considerable time to identify Zwane as one of the assailants who shot the deceased at the tuck shop. The discrepancies between Nene's statements and testimonies were deemed non-material and were supported by the context of a traumatic event; hence, the court was satisfied that the identification was reliable ([22]).

[17] The appellate court also addressed claims regarding the admissibility of the appellant's warning statement. It acknowledged that the appellant had contended he was not apprised of his rights adequately before providing this statement. However, the court found that Zwane was aware of his rights and had previously indicated his knowledge to the investigating officer, undermining his claims of coercion during the statement’s procurement ([18]).

[24-25] In relation to sentencing, the appellate court recognized the omission in the procedural requirements pertaining to the disclosure of minimum sentencing directives during the trial phase. The absence of this vital information precluded the appellant from understanding that a life sentence could be imposed, thus falling foul of the principles established in *Makatu v S* [2013] ZASCA 149 ([26]). Therefore, the court deemed that the sentence of life imprisonment was improperly imposed and amended it to 20 years, considering the appellant's age, potential for rehabilitation, and the circumstances surrounding the murder.

### C – Conclusion
[26] Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal against conviction, affirming the trial court's findings of guilt based on the available evidence. However, it conceded that the sentence of life imprisonment could not stand due to procedural failures concerning the disclosure of minimum sentencing guidelines. Consequently, the court set aside the original sentence and imposed a new sentence of 20 years’ imprisonment, which was antedated to align with the original sentencing date of 18 March 2002. This decision reflects a broader commitment to uphold fair trial principles while also addressing the appellant's position as a young offender. 

The case illustrates significant legal standards pertaining to the admissibility of evidence from single witnesses and procedural requirements in imposing minimum sentencing provisions, reinforcing the importance of adhering to due process in criminal proceedings.
