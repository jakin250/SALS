Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: Sekoko Mametja Incorporated Attorneys v Fetakgomo Tubatse Local Municipality (Case No. 60/2021) [2022] ZASCA 28

### F – Facts

The case revolves around a dispute between Sekoko Mametja Incorporated Attorneys (the appellant) and the Fetakgomo Tubatse Local Municipality (the respondent) concerning the legality of a tender awarded for debt collection services. The municipality initially published an invitation for tenders, which Sekoko Attorneys responded to and was awarded the tender along with three other bidders ([para 2]). Subsequent interactions led to the municipality cancelling the tender award, claiming Sekoko Attorneys had submitted a non-responsive bid due to failures related to tax compliance, specifically a lack of a valid Tax Clearance Certificate and a valid Central Supplier Database (CSD) report ([paras 3–4]).

Sekoko Attorneys opposed the municipality's review application and counterclaimed for payment of R438,260.30, covering services rendered between January and May 2018 ([para 4]). Notably, the municipality did not contest the counterclaim but argued that the tender award was void ab initio, thereby justifying a refusal to pay for services rendered under the allegedly unlawful contract ([para 5]).

In the Limpopo Division of the High Court, the court found the tender award invalid under section 172(1)(a) of the Constitution, which led to the dismissal of Sekoko's counterclaim despite acknowledging that the appellant had indeed performed satisfactorily under the contract ([paras 6–7]). Sekoko Attorneys appealed the dismissal of its counter-application, focusing solely on whether it was entitled to a remedy under section 172(1)(b) of the Constitution, seeking a just and equitable order despite the tender’s invalidity ([paras 8–9]).

### I – Issues

The principal issues before the court were:
1. Whether the High Court erred in its dismissal of Sekoko's counterclaim despite the municipality's acknowledgment of the services rendered and the absence of any legal dispute regarding the quality of those services ([paras 7–9]).
2. Whether section 172(1)(b) of the Constitution allows for a just and equitable remedy in the context of an unlawful tender that may nonetheless give rise to rights for services performed ([paras 9–12]).

### R – Rules

The legal framework governing the case includes:
- **Section 172 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996**, which deals with the powers of courts regarding unconstitutional actions. Specifically:
  - **Section 172(1)(a)** mandates courts to declare any law or conduct inconsistent with the Constitution invalid.
  - **Section 172(1)(b)** grants courts the authority to make any order that is just and equitable in cases of invalidity ([paras 6, 8]).
  
Key precedent cases referenced for guidance included:
- *Steenkamp NO v Provincial Tender Board of the Eastern Cape*, which introduced the principle that remedies for administrative failures must be just and equitable to the parties involved ([para 10]).
- *Bengwenyama Minerals (Pty) Ltd v Genorah Resources (Pty) Ltd*, which highlighted that unfairness arising from administrative actions can warrant equitable remedies ([para 11]).
- *Buffalo City Metropolitan Municipality v Asla Construction (Pty) Ltd*, which underscored the need for courts to consider whether the municipality should benefit from its own delay in contesting the validity of the services performed ([para 13]).

### A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the High Court had indeed erred in dismissing Sekoko Attorneys’ counterclaim without evaluating whether a just and equitable remedy could be contemplated under section 172(1)(b) of the Constitution ([para 8]). Although the tender was declared invalid, the court emphasized that it had still operated effectively in the interim; therefore, the municipality benefitted from the services rendered ([para 14]). 

The court noted that the municipality had neither disputed the quality of the services provided nor opposed the appeal, effectively conceding that Sekoko had performed satisfactorily ([para 14]). It underscored the need for a remedy that acknowledges the municipality's non-dispute and Sekoko's incurred expenses for providing those services, concluding that it was both just and equitable to order the municipality to pay the amount owed under the terms of the unlawful tender ([paras 15–16]).

### C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, recognizing that the High Court had failed to apply the correct legal principles regarding the just and equitable remedy available under section 172(1)(b) ([para 17]). The order dismissing the counterclaim was set aside, and the municipality was ordered to pay Sekoko Attorneys R436,250.30 plus interest from November 26, 2019, to the date of payment, without any order as to costs due to the straightforward nature of the case ([para 17]). This case establishes a significant precedent on the remedies available in instances of administrative irregularity, reinforcing the principle that courts should seek equitable solutions even in the face of legally invalid contracts.
