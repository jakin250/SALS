Constitutional Court

Minister of Home Affairs and Another v Tsebe and Another; Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development and Another v Tsebe and Another [2012] ZACC 16

In two consolidated applications before the Constitutional Court of South Africa, the applicants were various government officials, including the Ministers of Home Affairs and Justice, who sought to appeal against a decision from the South Gauteng High Court.

Constitutional Court 2012 [2012] ZACC 16

FIRAC Method

F – Facts ([1]–[4], [6]–[10])

In two consolidated applications before the Constitutional Court of South Africa, the applicants were various government officials, including the Ministers of Home Affairs and Justice, who sought to appeal against a decision from the South Gauteng High Court. The respondents were Emmanuel Tsebe and Jerry Ofense Phale, both Botswana nationals who faced murder charges in Botswana. The respondents sought orders to prevent their extradition or deportation to Botswana unless the Government provided written assurances that the death penalty would not be imposed or executed.

Mr. Tsebe was accused of murdering his partner in 2008 and fled to South Africa following the allegations. Mr. Phale faced similar accusations in 2009. Both were arrested in South Africa on warrants issued by Botswana. The High Court found in favor of Tsebe and Phale, ruling that extradition in the absence of sufficient assurances regarding the death penalty was unconstitutional (Tsebe and Another v Minister of Home Affairs and Others; Pitsoe v Minister of Home Affairs and Others 2012 (1) BCLR 77 (GSJ)).

I – Issues ([17], [19])

The primary legal question before the Constitutional Court was whether the South African government had the power to extradite or deport individuals to Botswana in the absence of assurances that they would not face the death penalty if convicted of murder. This raised fundamental issues regarding the constitutional obligations of the government under section 7(2) of the Constitution, which mandates the state to "respect, protect, promote and fulfill the rights in the Bill of Rights."

Additionally, the case examined whether the precedential ruling in Mohamed and Another v President of the RSA and Others [2001] ZACC 18 bound the court in this instance.

R – Rules ([25]–[30])

The case heavily relied on section 7(2) of the South African Constitution, which mandates the state’s duty to respect and protect the rights of individuals. The rights implicated included the right to life (section 11), the right to human dignity (section 10), and the right not to be treated in a cruel, inhuman, or degrading way (section 12).

Furthermore, the decision referenced international law regarding extradition, specifically the Extradition Treaty between South Africa and Botswana, which allows either party to refuse extradition if the death penalty might be imposed.

The rule established in Mohamed was also pivotal, which stated that the state could not extradite or deport an individual if it facilitated the risk of a death sentence being carried out, thus violating constitutional protections.

A – Application ([24], [46]–[74])

The court determined that the government’s action to extradite both Tsebe and Phale without the necessary assurances from Botswana posed a substantial risk of infringing upon their rights, specifically regarding the potential imposition and execution of the death penalty. The Constitutional Court found that the brutal nature of the allegations, combined with Botswana’s laws mandating death for murder without extenuating circumstances, indicated a real risk of the death penalty if extradition were to proceed.

In its analysis, the court emphasized that the state had a constitutional obligation not only to prevent practice of the death penalty but also to ensure that no individual could be transferred to another jurisdiction where they faced a real risk of death as punishment. The ruling in Mohamed was underscored as being applicable and binding, highlighting that the absence of assurances from Botswana rendered any extradition attempts unconstitutional and therefore unlawful.

C – Conclusion ([75])

The Constitutional Court dismissed the appeal by the government, affirming the High Court's ruling that it is unconstitutional to extradite or deport individuals to a country where they face a real risk of receiving the death penalty without the necessary assurances. The court maintained that this principle safeguards individuals' rights as guaranteed under the South African Constitution and reflects the state's obligation to uphold these rights universally, regardless of the individuals' immigration status or legal circumstances. The decision thus reinforced the broader commitment to human rights and dignity enshrined in the Constitution, emphasizing that no legal or international obligation should override these foundational principles.

The court ordered the applicants to pay the costs incurred by the respondents, setting a significant precedent regarding the interplay between domestic law and international human rights obligations and the government's responsibilities in extradition matters.
