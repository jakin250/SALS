Supreme Court of Appeal

Ferrostaal GmbH and Another v Transnet Soc Ltd t/a Transnet National Ports Authority and Another (Case no 1194/2019)

In December 2016, FMA was placed under business rescue, during which its business rescue practitioner suspended its obligation to pay rental to Transnet ([para 3]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 2022 ([para 2]). In December 2016

F – Facts

The case concerns an appeal heard by the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa regarding the business rescue proceedings of Ferromarine Africa (Pty) Ltd (FMA), a company partially owned by Ferrostaal GmbH (the first appellant) and Atlantis Marine Projects (Pty) Ltd (the second appellant) ([paras 1-2]). FMA entered a lease agreement with Transnet Soc Ltd t/a Transnet National Ports Authority (Transnet), which provided access to port facilities and included an option to renew the lease before its expiry in 2022 ([para 2]).

In December 2016, FMA was placed under business rescue, during which its business rescue practitioner suspended its obligation to pay rental to Transnet ([para 3]). Transnet, as the only independent creditor, opposed a series of proposed business rescue plans (BRPs), citing that they did not adequately protect its interests as a major creditor, especially regarding approximately R40 million in arrears ([para 6]). The second BRP was published in July 2019, which again failed to receive Transnet’s approval, and its negative vote subsequently led FMA’s shareholders to seek court intervention pursuant to section 153(1)(b)(i)(bb) of the Companies Act 71 of 2008 ([paras 5-7]). The high court reviewed the matter and ultimately decided to dismiss the application, concluding that Transnet's vote was not inappropriate and could not be set aside ([para 8]).

I – Issues

The primary issue was whether the high court erred in refusing to set aside Transnet's vote against the revised BRP, which the appellants argued was both unreasonable and inappropriate under the parameters established by section 153(7) of the Act ([paras 11-12]).

R – Rules

The key legal framework involved included the Companies Act, specifically sections 153(1)(a) and (b), which provide grounds for contesting a creditor's vote where it is deemed inappropriate ([para 12]). Furthermore, section 153(7) dictates that a court's determination must consider the interests of dissenting creditors, the provision made for their interests in the proposed plan, and a fair estimate of the return in a liquidation scenario. The overarching goal of the business rescue process is to facilitate the recovery of financially distressed companies while balancing stakeholders' rights and interests ([para 12]).

Precedent from past cases such as FirstRand Bank Ltd v KJ Foods CC further outlines how courts should interpret and apply these rules ([paras 16-17]).

A – Application

In applying the law, the court analyzed whether the high court made a reasonable value judgment in considering the specifics of FMA's situation, emphasizing that the interests of Transnet as a major creditor were not adequately addressed in the BRP ([paras 19-24]). The court noted that the BRP did not provide for the immediate repayment of arrear rental during the head lease, deferring it instead to the potential future negotiations or extensions, which Transnet found commercially unviable ([paras 20-22]).

The court examined the relationship and contractual obligations between FMA and Transnet, reiterating that FMA's reliance on contingencies for future revenues did not serve to mitigate Transnet's financial risks. It also emphasized that the lack of clarity around rental payments and inability to ensure future solvency undermined the BRP's viability ([paras 25-36]).

The comparison with FirstRand was highlighted; unlike KJ Foods, FMA lacked stakeholders with tangible employment interests, making the business rescue's implications differ greatly ([para 19]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the lower court’s decision, confirming that Transnet's vote against the BRP was not inappropriate given the substantial uncertainties surrounding the plan's viability and the lack of protections for Transnet's interests. The court emphasized the importance of a balanced approach in business rescue that considers the weight of all relevant factors ([paras 37-39]). The appeal was dismissed with costs, affirming the cautious approach needed when dealing with business rescue proceedings where creditor interests are at stake ([para 40]).

Implications

This case underscores the intricate balance that must be maintained between restructuring efforts for distressed entities and protecting the interests of significant creditors. The court's decision reinforces the principle that business rescue plans should be demonstrably viable and adequately safeguard creditor rights to maintain the integrity of the process as structured by the Companies Act.
