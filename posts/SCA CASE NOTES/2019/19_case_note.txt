Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

## FIRAC Case Note: Tadvest Industrial (Pty) Ltd v Anthea Hanekom & Others [2019] ZASCA 19

### F – Facts

The case originates from eviction applications launched by the property owner, Tadvest Industrial (Pty) Ltd (formerly known as Old Abland (Pty) Ltd), against the Hanekom and Jacobs families from two properties located in Stellenbosch ([1]). The initial applications for eviction were brought before the Magistrates’ Court for the District of Stellenbosch, leading to separate eviction orders for the respective families ([1]–[4]).

In both cases, the Magistrates’ Court also ordered the appellant to pay R80,000 to assist the respondents with relocation and obtaining alternative housing. Following these orders, the appellant appealed to the Land Claims Court (LCC), which dismissed its appeals but upheld the respondents’ cross-appeals against eviction, thus setting aside the eviction orders ([4]–[5]). The LCC permitted the appellant to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), leading to the appeals being recorded and later heard together ([5]–[6]).

Prior to the SCA hearing, the court posed questions regarding the LCC's jurisdiction to grant leave to appeal and whether this could be considered valid, highlighting crucial legal issues regarding the jurisdiction of the LCC and the SCA ([6]). Following the hearing, the SCA determined that even if the LCC granted such leave, it did not possess the authority to do so, thus rendering the appeals invalid due to lack of jurisdiction ([7]–[21]).

### I – Issues

Several pivotal legal issues emerged during these proceedings:

1. Did the Land Claims Court (LCC) have the jurisdiction to grant leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal?
2. If the LCC lacked this jurisdiction, did the order granting leave amount to a nullity?
3. If the order was indeed a nullity, did the Supreme Court of Appeal have jurisdiction to consider the merits of the appeal? ([6]).

### R – Rules

The case engages various legislative provisions and judicial precedents that define the jurisdiction of the courts:

1. **Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013**: Particularly, Sections 16(1)(b) and (c) require special leave from the SCA for appeals from high court decisions and decisions of courts of similar status, like the LCC.
   - **Section 16(1)(b)** specifies that appeals from high court decisions require special leave.
   - **Section 16(1)(c)** allows for appeals from LCC decisions, provided leave is granted by the SCA or the LCC, which must align with Chapter 22 of the Restitution of Land Rights Act.
   
2. **Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994** affirms the limited powers of the LCC, stating it possesses only the powers of a High Court concerning civil matters ([10]–[11]).

3. **Case Law**: The judgment references **Snyders v De Jager** ([8]), emphasizing that jurisdiction is strictly defined by statute, reinforcing the hierarchical structure of the court system. 

### A – Application

The SCA analyzed the statutory provisions, establishing that the LCC's powers do not extend to granting leave to appeal to the SCA when acting as an appellate court. The LCC can only grant leave to appeal against its decisions in line with its status as a court limited to the powers of a High Court ([10]–[11]).

The court rejected arguments that the leave granted by the LCC was sufficient for jurisdiction as it conflicted with the established precedent and statutory limits ([12]–[21]). It identified a critical distinction between the LCC acting in an appellate capacity and in its role of automatic review, as seen in Snyders, where the distinction was pivotal in determining jurisdiction ([17]–[20]).

Consequently, since the LCC lacked the statutory authority to grant leave, its order was deemed a nullity, and as such, the SCA could not exercise jurisdiction over the appeals ([21]). The court further noted that the appellant’s request for special leave was informally made and therefore not in line with procedural requirements outlined in S v Potgieter ([22]).

### C – Conclusion

The SCA concluded that the appeals did not possess a valid legal basis due to the LCC's lack of jurisdiction to grant leave to appeal, classifying the order granting leave as a nullity. Consequently, the appeals were struck from the roll, and each party was ordered to bear its own costs ([23]). This judgment signifies a reaffirmation of the structured statutory framework defining judicial authority, underscoring the importance of jurisdiction in appellate processes.

### Implications

The ruling clarifies the limits of the LCC's powers and reinforces the necessity for strict adherence to jurisdictional protocols in appeals. This case serves as a benchmark highlighting the importance of statutory interpretations and the implications in judicial review and appeals in South African law, particularly in matters related to land rights and evictions. The decision emphasizes the necessity for parties to be cognizant of jurisdictional requirements, which are crucial for the valid pursuit of appeals in the South African legal landscape.
