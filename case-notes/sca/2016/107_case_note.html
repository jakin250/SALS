Supreme Court of Appeal

City of Cape Town v Khaya Projects (Pty) Ltd

The appeal arose after the Western Cape Division of the High Court dismissed the City's application for a declarator that Khaya engaged in unconstitutional conduct due to defective housing construction.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 107 (26 July 2016) ## F - Facts The case emanate

Neutral Citation: City of Cape Town v Khaya (Pty) Ltd (158/15) [2016] ZASCA 107 (26 July 2016)

F - Facts

The case emanated from a housing development project at Witsand, a township in Atlantis, Cape Town, initially developed to address the housing needs of low-income groups ([1]–[2]). The City of Cape Town (the City), as an organ of state, entered into a development agreement with Peer Africa (Pty) Ltd (Peer Africa), which subsequently contracted Khaya Projects (Pty) Ltd (Khaya) to execute the construction work. The City alleged that Khaya had failed to adhere to its supposed constitutional obligations regarding the construction of housing as stipulated under Section 26 of the South African Constitution ([3]).

The appeal arose after the Western Cape Division of the High Court dismissed the City's application for a declarator that Khaya engaged in unconstitutional conduct due to defective housing construction. The City contended that permitting such defects would perpetuate poor building practices across low-cost housing projects ([3]–[4]). The case raised three procedural issues: the practical effect of the order sought, the City’s standing to declare the arbitration between Khaya and Peer Africa lapsed, and the issue of costs regarding the amicus curiae ([4]).

The arbitration between Khaya and Peer Africa was pending, with Khaya asserting non-payment and the City alleging that various houses built were defective or presented safety issues. It highlighted that any claims regarding the defects would have prescribed ([12]–[15]).

I - Issues

  1. Constitutional obligations: Did Khaya bear constitutional obligations regarding housing construction under Section 26 of the Constitution? What is the extent of such obligations? ([4]).
  2. Subsidarity principle: Should the City not have first exhausted all available contractual and statutory remedies before claiming constitutional relief? ([17]).
  3. Locus standi and standing in arbitration: Had the arbitration relating to the defects in the construction lapsed, and did the City have standing to pursue the court action despite not being a party to the arbitration process? ([36]).
  4. Costs: Should the City be responsible for the costs incurred by the amicus curiae? ([44]).

R - Rules

  1. Constitutional Framework: Section 26 of the Constitution guarantees the right to adequate housing. Section 39(2) also stipulates that when interpreting the Bill of Rights, courts must promote the values of human dignity and equality.
  2. Doctrine of Subsidiarity: The principle elucidated in Mazibuko v City of Johannesburg allows parties to first rely on existing statutory frameworks before invoking constitutional rights ([18]).
  3. Arbitration Act 42 of 1965: Sections relevant to the timeliness of awards and the standing of parties involved.
  4. Case Law: Cited decisions include Rail Commuters Action Group v Transnet Ltd and Allpay Consolidated Investment Holdings v Chief Executive Officer, South African Social Security Agency that clarify the constitutional duties of state organs and their limitations on private entities ([6], [23]).

A - Application

The court analyzed whether Khaya, as a private company, could incur constitutional obligations akin to those of the City. It was concluded that while the City has a constitutional obligation to provide housing, Khaya’s responsibilities lay within the confines of its contractual obligations to Peer Africa, making it not an organ of state ([20], [29]).

Furthermore, the doctrine of subsidiarity dictated that the City should first pursue remedies based on the existing regulations or contractual provisions before seeking relief through constitutional declarations. The court noted that the City had not adequately pursued remedies in arbitration, nor could it claim standing in that proceeding due to its lack of a direct contractual relationship with Khaya ([17], [36]).

On the matter of costs related to the amicus curiae, the court highlighted that an amicus does not constitute a party with direct interest and, traditionally, should not incur costs awarded against the parties in litigation ([44]). Hence, the High Court's order for the City to cover the amicus expenses was deemed inappropriate.

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the City's appeal on the grounds that Khaya was not a subject of constitutional obligations when it constructed the housing units, thereby upholding the principles of subsidiarity. The court's ruling reinforced the requirement for enforcing remedies under existing statutory frameworks before invoking socio-economic rights as baselines for legal obligation. The appeal regarding the costs of amicus curiae was also dismissed, aligning with established legal precedents regarding the role of amici in court proceedings ([1]–[45]).

This decision has far-reaching implications for future engagements involving private construction entities under state-funded projects, clarifying the boundaries of constitutional obligations in such contexts while emphasizing the necessity for adequate statutory adherence before seeking constitutional redress.
