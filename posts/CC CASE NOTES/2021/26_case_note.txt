Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# FIRAC Case Note: Union for Police Security and Corrections Organisation v South African Custodial Management (Pty) Ltd and Others [2021] ZACC 26

### F – Facts
The case involves the **Union for Police Security and Corrections Organisation** (the Applicant), a registered trade union pursuant to the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 ([para 2]). The first respondent, **South African Custodial Management (Pty) Ltd** (SACM), operates within a public-private partnership with the South African Department of Correctional Services. The second to fifth respondents, including **Kensani Corrections Management (Pty) Ltd**, **Royal Mnandi (Pty) Ltd**, **Tec-Tron Maintenance (Pty) Ltd**, and **JFE Security**, are subcontractors to SACM ([para 3]).

The dispute originated from an audit report created by a Task Team formed to address employment-related concerns at the **Kutama Sinthumule Correctional Facility**. The Applicant contended that findings and recommendations in the report were binding on the respondents for implementation ([para 5]). Subsequently, the Applicant sought to enforce these obligations through the Labour Court ([para 7]).

The Labour Court, however, upheld an exception by the respondents, ruling it lacked jurisdiction because the relevant documents constituted a collective agreement under section 24 of the LRA, which mandates referring such disputes to the **Commission for Conciliation, Mediation, and Arbitration (CCMA)**, thus dismissing the application with no costs orders ([paras 8–9]). An appeal attempt to the Labour Appeal Court failed ([paras 10–11]). The Applicant then sought leave to appeal to the **Constitutional Court**.

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues addressed in this case were:
1. Whether the Labour Court erred in characterizing the documents as a collective agreement and thereby, whether it had jurisdiction to adjudicate.
2. Whether the Labour Court’s decision to impose a costs order against the Applicant adhered to established principles in labour law that typically disallow costs following the result in labour disputes ([paras 12–13]).

### R – Rules
Key legal principles cited included:
1. **Section 24 of the Labour Relations Act** (LRA), which establishes that disputes regarding the interpretation or application of collective agreements must be arbitrated unless explicitly allowed for judicial review ([para 8]).
2. The **frequent precedent** established by the **Constitutional Court**, notably in **Zungu v Premier of the Province of KwaZulu-Natal**, reinforces that, in labour matters, costs do not typically follow the result ([para 24]), emphasizing constitutional and statutory imperatives rooted in **Section 23** (labour rights) and **Section 34** (fair hearings) of the Constitution ([paras 26–29]).

### A – Application
The **Constitutional Court**, under **Khampepe J**, analyzed the appeals separately. The court found that the Labour Court was correct in its jurisdictional assessment and that the Applicant's claim fell squarely within the enforcement of a collective agreement, thus the Labour Court did not err in dismissing the appeal for want of jurisdiction ([paras 21–22]). 

However, on the issue of costs, the court found that the Labour Court failed to follow the correct approach, simply defaulting to the rule that costs follow the result without addressing the merits or reasoning needed to justify a departure from established principles. This lack of scholarly assessment violated sections of the LRA, hindering access to justice for unions representing workers ([paras 35–40]).

### C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Constitutional Court denied leave to appeal on the merits, affirming the Labour Court’s interpretation of jurisdiction over collective agreements ([paras 41–42]). However, the court upheld the appeal regarding the costs order, stating the Labour Court’s reasoning was inadequate and misaligned with the established aphorism that costs do not follow the result in labour disputes. The costs order was thus set aside, and the court concluded there would be no order as to costs in their proceeding, aligning with the principles of fairness and access to justice in labour matters ([para 43]).

### Implications
This case reaffirms the foundational principle that adverse costs should not deter parties from pursuing legitimate labour disputes, thereby enhancing workers' rights as secured under the Constitution and reaffirming the accessibility of labour courts as protective forums in South African law. The judgment serves as a critical reminder to lower courts on the application of the discretion surrounding costs in labour matters.
