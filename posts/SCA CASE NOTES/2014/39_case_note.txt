Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: Capricorn District Municipality v SANCO (237/2013)

**F – Facts**  
[1] This matter arises from an appeal against an order by the North Gauteng High Court (Legodi J), which mandated Capricorn District Municipality (the first appellant) and Lepelle-Nkumpi Local Municipality (the second appellant) to repair and replace all leaking water pipes and defective water meters in Lebowakgomo Zone A, Limpopo, within twelve months. The court also required that, pending completion of the repairs, both municipalities charge water consumers a flat rate of R70 per household per month for services rendered from September 12, 2011, and R50 per month retroactively from August 2009 until September 11, 2011.  
[3] As the dedicated water service authority, the Capricorn District Municipality oversees water provision, while the Lepelle-Nkumpi Local Municipality acts as the provider. Over the years, the local authority cited infrastructural issues as the source of water leakages and shortages in Zone A. Residents claimed that inflated water accounts were primarily due to faulty meters and estimate billing practices by municipal employees. The South African National Civic Organisation (SANCO), acting on behalf of the community, presented a case showcasing egregious billing discrepancies, exemplified by a scenario wherein an elderly resident received a bill of R5000 in one month—a claim of consumption that appeared implausibly excessive.  
[4] After unsuccessful protests and lack of adherence to grievance procedures established for disputing billing issues, SANCO launched this application on September 12, 2011, requesting interdicts to compel repair and replacement activities by the municipalities.

**I – Issues**  
[2] Central to the appeal are questions regarding the competency of the High Court's orders under the principles of legality and the separation of powers. The appellants argue that judicial intervention in municipal executive and legislative matters violates the separation of powers doctrine. They also assert that the flat rates imposed contravene established legal frameworks that govern water tariffs and billing, raising concerns about the adherence to the principles of equitable treatment of service users.

**R – Rules**  
[10] The legal framework relevant to this appeal includes constitutional mandates that guarantee the right to access sufficient water ([3] Constitution §27(1)(b)). Municipalities possess the executive authority and must comply with the regulations set forth by the Water Services Act 108 of 1997 and the Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000. These Acts direct municipalities to develop and implement equitable tariff policies for water services that reflect actual service usage, operational costs, and ensure sustainability ([11] Systems Act §74(2)). In Mazibuko & Others v City of Johannesburg & Others, the Constitutional Court underscored that a municipality's decisions in tariff setting are generally legislative acts and therefore not subject to judicial review unless criteria are substantially ignored ([20] Mazibuko). 

**A – Application**  
[18] The court finds that the High Court's mandatory orders were fundamentally flawed. The imposition of a flat rate for water tariffs contravened municipal obligations under the Systems Act, which delineates how tariffs must be set and collected in an equitable manner ([21]). The court rejected the argument that the municipalities conceded to faulty infrastructure verification through the consulting report that indicated possible issues but not definitive incapacitation. It stressed that the municipalities had proactively commissioned an engineering study to inform necessary repairs, indicating their commitment to resolving these systemic issues through their legislative and executive powers rather than through judicial compulsion ([23-24]). Moreover, the court pointed out that the High Court's intervention essentially circumvented the municipalities' rights to manage their fiscal and organizational resources, an affront to the separation of powers principle.

**C – Conclusion**  
[27] The Supreme Court of Appeal thus overturned the earlier decisions of the High Court, ruling that the mandatory interdicts were not competent and set aside the order in favor of SANCO. This decision reaffirms the legislative and executive authority of municipalities in matters of water supply and billing, underscoring the vital principles of legality and the separation of powers ([24]). It highlights the necessity of following proper procedural channels for addressing municipal grievances while reiterating that constitutional rights to service delivery must be upheld without infringing on local governance structures. In consequence, the appeal was upheld with costs, including the fees for two counsel, emphasizing the appropriateness of due process and adherence to established legal frameworks in municipal governance.
