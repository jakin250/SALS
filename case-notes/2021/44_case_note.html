Using the FIRAC Method: Van Zyl N.O. v Road Accident Fund [2021] ZACC 44

Constitutional Court 2021 [2021] ZACC 44

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])

On May Day 2010, Mr. Koos Jacobs suffered serious injuries in a motor vehicle accident, resulting in severe mental incapacity ([1]). He was unable to manage his affairs or lodge a claim against the Road Accident Fund (RAF). On January 18, 2017, his mother filed a claim on his behalf, approximately seven years post-accident. Subsequently, a court appointed Ms. Phillipa Susan van Zyl N.O. as his curatrix ad litem on November 28, 2017, and she initiated legal action against the RAF for damages on March 8, 2018 ([2]).

In accordance with section 23(1) of the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 (RAF Act), claims must be instituted within three years of the cause of action, thus by April 30, 2013, in this case ([3]). However, sections 23(2)(b) and (c) of the RAF Act provide specific protections against prescription for persons with mental disabilities under certain conditions, which Mr. Jacobs, due to his incapacity, failed to meet ([4]). The legal question arose as to whether Mr. Jacobs’ delayed claim could be protected from prescription.

In the Eastern Cape High Court, the RAF raised a special plea of prescription, which was upheld, leading to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) affirming this judgment without considering constitutional issues ([5]-[7]). The SCA reasoned that the applicant should have managed the situation by securing Mr. Jacobs’ timely detention under mental health legislation or appointing a curator, which would have suspended the running of prescription per section 23(2)(c) of the RAF Act ([8]). The case reached the Constitutional Court, emphasizing the broader implications for dignity and access to justice for individuals with disabilities ([9]-[10]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 21-21])

The singular issue for determination was whether Mr. Jacobs’ claim could be saved from the prescription under section 23 of the RAF Act, especially given his incapacitated status. This leads to four sub-questions:

  1. Does section 23 of the RAF Act provide suspension of prescription for individuals like Mr. Jacobs?
  2. Is the prescription period for claims against the RAF Act governed solely by section 23, or does section 13(1) of the Prescription Act offer supplementary protection?
  3. Does the impossibility principle from common law apply to suspend the running of prescription in Mr. Jacobs’ case?
  4. Is the Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) applicable, affecting the interpretation of these issues? ([21]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 22–24])

The key legislative frameworks at play are:

  • Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996: Sections 23(1), (2)(b), and (2)(c) outline the prescription of claims and the limited exceptions for mental incapacity.
  • Prescription Act 68 of 1969: Particularly section 13(1)(a), which allows for the extension of prescription periods for persons incapacitated by mental or intellectual disability.

The courts also relied upon the precedent set in Mdeyide II ([10]), which discussed the relationship between the RAF Act and the Prescription Act, concluding that the latter is excluded in matters governed by the RAF Act. Furthermore, principles of common law regarding incapacity and the impossibility principle, including the maxims lex non cogit ad impossibilia and contra non valentem agere non currit praescriptio, provide foundational support for the analysis ([16]-[19]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 40-62])

In applying these legal rules, the Constitutional Court analyzed whether the provisions of sections 23(1) and 23(2) of the RAF Act implicitly excluded individuals with disabilities unable to meet the specific conditions needed to suspend prescription. The court established that while section 23 offers explicit protections, these are limited in scope and do not accommodate all forms of mental incapacity. This creates a substantial gap in legal protection for individuals like Mr. Jacobs who were not formally detained or under curatorship.

The court acknowledged that the impossibility principle should apply, meaning a claim should not prescribe while a person is incapable of asserting their rights due to circumstances beyond their control. The court ultimately concluded that prescription did not commence against Mr. Jacobs until after the appointment of his curator ad litem, given that he lacked the capacity to act on his own ([76]-[77]). The court emphasized the need for a compassionate interpretation of the law that promotes justice and human dignity, aligning its conclusions with the obligations imposed by the CRPD, thereby highlighting the need to harmonize local law with international human rights obligations ([78]-[85]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 86-90])

The Constitutional Court concluded that the Prescription Act does not apply to claims under the RAF Act. However, in Mr. Jacobs’ case, the common law principles of impossibility and incapacity effectively shielded his claim from prescribing due to his mental incapacity. This ruling is significant as it reinforces the interpretation of legislative provisions in light of human rights principles, particularly for vulnerable persons who are denied access to justice due to their circumstances. The court ordered the dismissal of the RAF's special plea with costs, which includes costs of two counsel, thus asserting a protective stance for similarly situated claimants against the harsh consequences of prescription under the RAF Act ([87]-[90]).

This ruling not only aids claimants with mental disabilities but serves as a precedent for future cases concerning prescription limitations in light of incapacity, ensuring that justice is increasingly accessible to the most vulnerable groups within society.
