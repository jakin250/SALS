Daniels v Scribante and Another 2017 ZACC 13 (CCT 50/16)

After maintenance was performed, Daniels sought to make essential improvements to her home, including leveling floors and installing indoor plumbing.

Constitutional Court 2017 CCT 50/16

FIRAC Analysis

F – Facts:
The applicant, Yolanda Daniels, has been a domestic worker at Chardonne Farm near Stellenbosch for the last 16 years, living there with her minor children. The first respondent, Theo Scribante, manages the farm while the second respondent, Chardonne Properties CC, owns it. In 2014, Scribante attempted to evict Daniels by tampering with her dwelling’s door and cutting off her electricity. Following her appeal for restoration, the Stellenbosch Magistrate’s Court issued an interim order requiring Scribante to repair the door and restore her electricity supply, an order which was complied with.

After maintenance was performed, Daniels sought to make essential improvements to her home, including leveling floors and installing indoor plumbing. These enhancements were necessary for basic human habitation. Scribante and Chardonne Properties refused her requests, seeking to compel her to halt her improvement efforts, leading Daniels to approach the Stellenbosch Magistrate’s Court for recognition of her right to make these improvements under the Extension of Security of Tenure Act (ESTA). The court denied her request, asserting that occupiers under ESTA must obtain the owner's consent before making any improvements. This ruling was affirmed by the Land Claims Court and subsequently the Supreme Court of Appeal, denying her leave to appeal.

Ultimately, Daniels sought permission to appeal to the Constitutional Court.

I – Issues:

  1. Should leave to appeal be granted?
  2. Does ESTA confer upon an occupier the right to make improvements to their dwelling?
  3. If so, is consent from the owner needed for these improvements?
  4. If consent is not required, can an occupier execute improvements irrespective of the owner’s preferences?

R – Rules:
The relevant legislation includes:

  • Extension of Security of Tenure Act (ESTA) (Act 62 of 1997), particularly sections 5 and 6, which outline the rights of occupiers regarding dignity and habitation.
  • Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996, particularly section 25(6) concerning the right to secure tenure, and section 26, which guarantees the right to housing.

Key case law referenced:

  • Hattingh v Juta [2013] ZACC 5, emphasizing a purposive understanding of the rights of occupiers under ESTA.
  • Port Elizabeth Municipality v Various Occupiers [2004] ZACC 7, providing a direct context for balancing property rights with occupier rights.

A – Application:
The court conducted a historical and contextual analysis of land rights and the socio-economic conditions of occupiers under ESTA. In its judgment, Madlanga J emphasized that denying the right to improvement violates the essence of human dignity, intrinsic to the rights of occupiers. The court highlighted that the history of land dispossession necessitates robust legal protection for occupants, particularly in light of past injustices.

The interpretation of sections 5 and 6 of ESTA, as well as their relationship with the Bill of Rights, was crucial. Notably, the court disagreed with the lower courts’ conclusion that an occupier's right to improve their dwelling is solely contingent upon the owner's consent. Instead, the majority held that engagement between the occupiers and the landowner should be meaningful and aim toward a balance of interests, reinforcing the right to improve one’s living conditions essential for human dignity.

While the court affirmed the owner's rights, it clarified that those rights must be balanced with the occupier’s rights under ESTA. The court held that, since the proposed improvements were necessary for human habitation and dignity, consent from the owner was not a legitimate barrier to their implementation.

C – Conclusion:
The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal and upheld Daniels’ right to make necessary improvements to her dwelling at Chardonne Farm without needing the owners’ consent. It declared these improvements essential for maintaining human dignity and reiterated the need for meaningful engagement between owners and occupiers regarding home improvements.

The court’s ruling sets a significant precedent for the rights of occupiers under ESTA, emphasizing the necessity of improving living conditions for marginalized individuals residing on land they do not own, thereby aligning statutory interpretation with the constitutional commitment to dignity, equality, and freedom.

The implications of this ruling extend to the treatment of occupiers across South Africa, especially in rural contexts, laying down key principles on the balance of property rights and the rights of individuals living in conditions of potential indignity. The judgment underscores the importance of practical engagement to foster equitable living conditions for all individuals, consistent with the values enshrined in the Constitution.

Citations

  • Extension of Security of Tenure Act (ETA) 62 of 1997.
  • South African Constitution (1996).

This case highlights the judicial commitment to addressing the legacies of historical injustices and affirming the rights of vulnerable communities in our contemporary legal landscape.
