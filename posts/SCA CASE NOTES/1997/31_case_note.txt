Source Folder: 1997

# Case Note: Die Hoogste Hof van Appèl v. Heavystone Lebopa (390/96)

## FIRAC Method Case Note

### F – Facts
The appellant, Heavystone Lebopa, was the second accused during a trial in the Regional Court of South Transvaal. Alongside his younger brother (first accused) and another individual, Frans Mnguni (third accused), all were convicted of murder and robbery, receiving sentences of 10 years for each charge ([para 1]). The appellant appealed to the Transvaal Provincial Division against his conviction for murder and the sentences imposed, which were dismissed ([para 2]). Under the Chief Justice’s consent, he is now appealing solely against the murder conviction ([para 3]).

The events leading to the appeal occurred on February 4, 1994, around 18:00, when the appellant and three accomplices planned to commit robbery at a sports ground in Actonville. The group, armed with weapons—including a loaded .22 pistol and a toy revolver—encountered two victims in a parked vehicle and attempted to rob them. During the altercation, the first accused struck the male victim (the complainant) multiple times, while the deceased (an accomplice) drew a firearm and, in the subsequent struggle, was shot dead by the first accused ([paras 3-4]).

Witness testimonies revealed conflicting narratives, particularly regarding the appellant's role during the confrontation. The complainant reported a fight with the deceased, and although the appellant was noted to be present, the details of his actions during and after the shooting became pivotal in understanding his culpability. Subsequent actions by the appellant, including collecting money from the female complainant post-shooting, were contentious as they implicated him in the robbery and potentially in an association with the criminal intentions of his accomplices ([paras 5-10]).

### I – Issues
The critical legal questions before the court centered on:
1. Whether there was sufficient evidence to establish the appellant’s mens rea regarding the murder charge.
2. Whether the appellant could be considered to have participated in the common purpose of the robbery, thereby sharing liability for the murder committed by another party ([paras 10-11]).

### R – Rules
The court primarily relied on well-established legal principles concerning joint criminal enterprise as noted in *S v Daniek* and *S v Malinga*, which articulate the conditions under which a participant can be held criminally liable for acts committed by co-perpetrators. Under *S v Nkombani*, the liability of a socius criminis is based on his own mens rea; he must foresee the possibility of violent outcomes during the execution of a common purpose to be culpable for murder ([paras 11-12]).

### A – Application
In applying these legal standards to the facts, the court underscored the importance of the appellant’s awareness and actions during the robbery. Evidence indicated that the appellant actively engaged in planning the crime and was cognizant of the weapons present, suggesting he anticipated potential violence. The appellant's behavior post-shooting—conducting himself in a manner analogous to that of an accomplice—was interpreted as tacit approval of the violent act committed by the first accused ([paras 10-16]).

The court analyzed witness testimonies, recognizing inconsistencies, particularly emphasizing the complainant's inability to definitively recount the appellant's whereabouts during the shooting. Nevertheless, given the overarching context of shared criminal intent and the predicting of fatal consequences stemming from the robbery, the court held that the appellant must have foreseen the potential for violence and death as inherent risks of their criminal undertaking. 

Furthermore, it was concluded that the appellant's post-offence actions demonstrated his acceptance of the consequences of the group’s primary objective as he participated in both the robbery and the handling of the deceased's body ([paras 13-16]).

### C – Conclusion
The appellant’s conviction for murder was ultimately upheld. The court determined that the appellant possessed dolus eventualis, having recognized and accepted the potential for deadly outcomes from their robbery attempt. This decision reinforces the legal principle that in a joint enterprise, all parties can be held accountable for the actions stemming from their collective criminal intent. The appeal was dismissed, reaffirming the convictions and sentences of the lower courts ([para 17]).

The implications of this ruling underscore the judicial stance on equal culpability in collaborative criminal endeavors, driving home the importance of assessing each participant's intent and awareness during the commission of crimes, notably in violent settings.
