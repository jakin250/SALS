Speaker of the National Assembly and Another v Land Access Movement of South Africa and Others [2019] ZACC 10

In July 2016, this Court invalidated the Amendment Act due to procedural deficiencies, notably a failure to adequately facilitate public participation as mandated by section 72(1)(a) of the Constitution.

Constitutional Court 2019 [2019] ZACC 10

FIRAC Method

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–13])

The case arises from issues stemming from the land restitution process in South Africa, a critical mechanism designed to address historical injustices related to land ownership due to racially discriminatory laws. The Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994 established a system for dispossessed individuals and communities to reclaim land or receive equitable compensation. However, by 2014, a significant backlog of unresolved claims existed, prompting Parliament to enact the Restitution of Land Rights Amendment Act in 2014, which aimed to reopen the claims process until June 2019.

In July 2016, this Court invalidated the Amendment Act due to procedural deficiencies, notably a failure to adequately facilitate public participation as mandated by section 72(1)(a) of the Constitution. The Court prohibited the Commission on Restitution of Land Rights from processing claims lodged under the repealed Amendment Act from July 2014 until December 1998, leading to a backlog of over 20,000 unresolved claims. The interdict resulted in the creation of “interdicted claims,” which could not be addressed until appropriate legislative action was taken.

In March 2018, the Speaker of the National Assembly and the Chairperson of the National Council of Provinces petitioned the Court for an extension of the original 24-month period granted for re-enactment of the Amendment Act. This extension was intended to facilitate the completion of the necessary legislative process. However, the urgency of these matters was underscored by the opposition from several applicants (the LAMOSA respondents), who asserted that past injustices required judicial resolution as Parliament had failed to act within the stipulated timeframe.

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 14–20])

The principal issues before the Court were:

  1. Whether the Court could extend the period outlined in its earlier order given Parliament’s failure to enact the required legislation within the original timeframe.
  2. If so, whether granting an extension would be just and equitable under the constitutional framework.

Additional considerations included the procedural implications of untimely legislative action by Parliament and the balance between judicial oversight and legislative authority regarding land restitution claims.

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 31–63])

Key legal principles emerged from notable precedents, including Sibiya v Director of Public Prosecutions and Teddy Bear Clinic for Abused Children v Minister of Justice, both of which established that extensions may be granted if they align with the interests of justice and if sufficient explanations for prior delays are provided. The Court underscored that its remedial powers under section 172(1)(b) of the Constitution allow it to deliver just and equitable orders, including extending timeframes for compliance.

The case heavily referenced the earlier decision, Land Access Movement of South Africa v Chairperson, National Council of Provinces (LAMOSA I), which emphasized the constitutional obligations of Parliament to facilitate public involvement and announced that the commission could not proceed with processing claims under the invalidated Amendment Act pending the presence of a new law.

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 24–60])

The Court first addressed the "functus officio" doctrine, determining that it retained the authority to reconsider its own prior order because the original ruling made provisions for future applications regarding the processing of interdicted claims. The majority opinion highlighted the need for Parliament to provide an adequate explanation for its legislative inaction. Notably, the Court found that the legislative processes had experienced significant undue delays with insufficient justification from Parliament.

In its analysis, the Court weighed the competing interests of various stakeholders in the land restitution process, noting that granting the extension requested by Parliament would perpetuate the stalling of a critical process while the urgency of land restoration and societal equity remained. Granting the extension would exacerbate the wait faced by those individuals awaiting resolution of their claims, thereby failing to serve the overarching goals of justice and equity foundational to the Constitution.

In granting the counter-application by the LAMOSA respondents, the Court ruled against the blanket extension sought by Parliament, citing the need to protect the rights of those holding interdicted claims, emphasizing that these claims could only be processed once old claims had been settled or if proper applications were made to the Land Claims Court.

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 61–67])

The Court dismissed the application for an extension by the Parliamentary applicants, thereby lifting the interdict and reinstating the operator's obligation to process the interdicted claims as outlined previously under section 172 of the Constitution. It upheld the counter-application from the LAMOSA respondents, standing firm on the position that no new claims could be processed until the old claims had been adjudicated.

This judgement reinforces the imperative of accountability within Parliament regarding land restitution, shedding light on the delicate balance between legislative authority and judiciary oversight in ensuring that restorative justice principles underlie land reform initiatives. The ruling reflects South Africa's broader commitment to addressing the legacies of apartheid and highlights the judiciary's role as an arbiter of constitutional rights in relation to historical injustices.

In practical terms, the judgement served to enhance the efficacy of land claim processing, potentially accelerating resolutions that had been significantly delayed while ensuring that future parliamentary actions adhered to constitutional mandates aimed at safeguarding public involvement in the legislative process.
