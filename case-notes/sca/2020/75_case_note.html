Supreme Court of Appeal

Oranje Watersport CC v Dawid Kruiper Local Municipality and Others (397/2019) [2020] ZASCA 75

On December 3, 2015, the Municipality resolved to alienate the Property by public tender, determining a market-related sale price of R2,080,000.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 75

FIRAC Method Case Note

F – Facts

This case involves Oranje Watersport CC (the "Applicant"), which has leased a property known as Erf 15747, Olivier Park, Upington (the "Property") from the Dawid Kruiper Local Municipality (the "First Respondent") for 17 years. The latest lease commenced on July 26, 2013, and was set to expire at the end of June 2018 ([2]).

On December 3, 2015, the Municipality resolved to alienate the Property by public tender, determining a market-related sale price of R2,080,000. Two bids were submitted: the Applicant bid R2,080,000, while the second respondent, Upington Hotel (Pty) Ltd (“Upington Hotel”), bid R2,400,000. The Municipality’s Bid Evaluation Committee did not consider the Applicant’s bid as it was deemed non-responsive due to the absence of a required bank guarantee proving the financial capability of the applicant ([3]–[5]). On April 8, 2016, the Municipality awarded the tender to Upington Hotel ([4]).

Following this, the Applicant sought to interdict the transfer of the Property to Upington Hotel and to review the Municipality's decision ([6]). The Northern Cape Division of the High Court dismissed this review application on July 6, 2018. The Applicant subsequently sought leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([7]).

I – Issues

The critical legal issues for determination by the court centered on:

  1. Was Upington Hotel’s bid responsive, specifically regarding compliance with the special conditions of the tender?
  2. Did the Municipality comply with the provisions of section 14 of the Local Government: Municipal Finance Management Act 56 of 2003 (the “MFMA”) while deciding to alienate the Property? ([9]).

R – Rules

The rules pertinent to this case include:

  1. Local Government: Municipal Finance Management Act 56 of 2003, Section 14: This section outlines the restrictions on a municipality's ability to dispose of capital assets, necessitating public deliberation about the economic and community value of the asset ([14]).
  2. Tender Regulations: The special conditions in the Municipality's tender process required that bids include guarantees from registered financial institutions verifying the bidder's qualifying financial status ([10]).
  3. Case Law:
  • The court referred to Claassen’s Dictionary of Legal Words and Phrases, emphasizing that a "guarantee" must assure payment in specific terms ([12]).
  • The case of Mayfair South Townships (Pty) Ltd v Jhina provided a judicial interpretation of "guarantee," defining it as assurance regarding payment ([12]).

A – Application

The court examined the responsiveness of Upington Hotel’s bid. It determined that the offered letter from Nedbank did not fulfill the requirements of a guarantee as outlined in the tender's special conditions ([11]). The lack of clarity and assurance pertaining to the bidder's ability to fund the intended development rendered the bid non-responsive.

Regarding compliance with the MFMA, the court found that the Municipality failed to adequately assess and document the economic and community value of the Property before its alienation, as required by section 14(2)(b) of the Act ([15]–[17]). The requirement for public disclosure and deliberation was emphasized, highlighting the transparency needed in the process.

The court concluded that the failure to follow due procedure under the MFMA constituted a jurisdictional defect, thus invalidating the award of the tender and the ensuing sale ([18]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal granted leave to appeal and ultimately ruled in favor of the Applicant. The court's order set aside the Municipality’s resolution to sell the Property and the tender award to Upington Hotel. Any contract entered into as a result of these decisions was declared invalid and of no effect, with the Municipality ordered to bear the costs ([1] & [18]).

This judgment reaffirms the importance of adherence to statutory requirements in public procurement processes, particularly in ensuring the transparency and accountability of municipal decisions affecting public assets.
