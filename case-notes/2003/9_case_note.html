Sipho Shongwe v The State [2003] CCT 45/02

Constitutional Court 2003 CCT 45/02

FIRAC Method

F – Facts ([paras 1–5])

The case concerns Sipho Shongwe, the applicant, who sought direct access to the Constitutional Court of South Africa following his conviction in the Pretoria High Court for serious offences including murder, attempted murder, and robbery resulting from a cash-in-transit robbery on 31 July 1997. On 1 March 2001, he was sentenced to two life imprisonment terms along with additional sentences. His appeals to the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal were denied.

In the Pretoria High Court, the conviction relied heavily on the testimony of a state witness regarding Shongwe's involvement in the robbery, corroborated by the applicant's admissions and a police pointing out during the night of 19 to 20 March 1998. Shongwe contended that his rights to a fair trial were violated, particularly due to an alleged breach of an assurance made by police to his attorney that any questioning would first involve his attorney, which did not occur prior to his arrest. His application to the Constitutional Court, however, was deemed to have been improperly filed.

I – Issues ([paras 3–5])

The primary legal issues raised in this case include:

  1. Whether the applicant’s fundamental right to a fair trial was infringed due to the admission of certain evidence in his trial.
  2. Whether the applicant's application for direct access to the Constitutional Court was proper under the applicable procedural rules, specifically Rule 17, which provides for direct access to the Court under exceptional circumstances.
  3. The appropriate standard and procedure for appealing convictions where a significant constitutional matter is raised after the expiry of the appeal period.

R – Rules ([paras 4–5])

The relevant rules and legal authorities cited include:

  • Rule 17(1): This rule allows for applications for direct access to the Constitutional Court but is explicitly stated to not serve as an appeal procedure.
  • Rule 18(1): This rule outlines the procedure for seeking leave to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court when a constitutional matter has been decided by a court other than the Supreme Court of Appeal.
  • The Court referenced past decisions, such as Van der Spuy v General Council of the Bar of South Africa and Bruce and Another v Fleecytex Johannesburg CC, to emphasize that direct access should not be erroneously conflated with appeal procedures.

A – Application ([paras 3–5])

The Court analyzed the procedural missteps of the applicant, indicating that despite the substantial nature of the constitutional issue surrounding the right to a fair trial, the application for direct access was impermissible as it did not align with the intended use of Rule 17. The Court highlighted the necessity for proper adherence to procedural requirements, particularly since Shongwe was represented by counsel, meaning that a transition of his application from Rule 17 to Rule 18 was not justified. The Court invoked the principle that a legitimate and timely application for condonation would need to accompany any late appeal, particularly in cases where constitutional rights are involved.

Additionally, the Court underscored that, although concern for constitutional rights is paramount, this must be balanced against the need for finality in litigation. The Court expressed reluctance to entertain applications that bypass established procedural safeguards.

C – Conclusion ([paras 5–6])

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court dismissed Shongwe's application for direct access, not only because he utilized the wrong procedural route but also due to the absence of any valid condonation application for an appeal against his conviction, which had long expired. The dismissal reinforces the Court's position on the importance of procedural correctness and the limitations of direct access applications within the ambit of constitutional litigation. The holding illustrates the necessity for applicants to observe procedural channels and the implications of failing to do so, particularly where significant constitutional rights are purportedly at stake.

The case exemplifies the stringent requirements placed upon appellants within the South African judicial framework when contending with criminal convictions, ensuring that procedural propriety is upheld as a foundational element in the resolution of such matters.
