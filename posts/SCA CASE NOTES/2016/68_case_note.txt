# Case Note - Abbott v Overstrand Municipality (99/2015) [2016] ZASCA 68

## FIRAC Case Note

### F - Facts
In **Abbott v Overstrand Municipality** ([1]–[6]), the appellant, David Willoughby Abbott, owned property adjacent to the Klein River in Hermanus, Western Cape. After acquiring the property in 1982 and erecting a dwelling in 1989, Abbott alleged that his home suffered damage due to flooding, which he attributed to the Overstrand Municipality's failure to take preventative actions concerning the estuary's berm management. In July 2014, Abbott sought judicial review of the municipality's refusal to intervene to prevent flood damage, invoking the **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000** (PAJA). His application sought the setting aside of the municipality's decision and called for the reconsideration of its actions regarding protecting his property from flooding ([6]).

### I - Issues
The legal issues in this case revolve around whether the Overstrand Municipality had the legal obligation to prevent flooding damage to Abbott’s property and whether it had unlawfully failed to make a decision regarding flood management ([10]–[11]). Additionally, there were questions pertaining to the establishment of a previous practice of flooding management that could support Abbott's claim of legitimate expectation regarding the municipality's responsibilities ([32]).

### R - Rules
The case relied on several legal principles, especially those codified in PAJA, where administrative action is defined as the taking of a decision or failure to take a decision by an organ of state in pursuing public duties, as emphasized in **Bato Star Fishing (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Environmental Affairs and Tourism** ([12]) and further detailed in sections of PAJA that govern the scope and grounds for review of administrative decisions ([13]).

The municipalities’ powers and duties are also governed under Section 156 of the Constitution of South Africa, which delineates the authority concerning local government matters and specifically excludes responsibilities for managing flood risks in the estuary unless such authority has been conferred by legislation ([14]–[16]). Furthermore, the relevant environmental statutes, including the **National Environmental Management Act 107 of 1998** (NEMA) and the **Nature Conservation Ordinance 19 of 1974**, were examined concerning their scope on managing the estuary ([17]).

### A - Application
The Court, led by Fourie AJA, undertook thorough legal scrutiny, ultimately concluding that the municipality lacked the legal authority to intervene in the manner that Abbott suggested since the Constitution and national legislation did not assign any such duty to the municipality to control the flooding ([24]). The appellant's contention that a historical practice existed, whereby the municipality would undertake river berm breaches for flood management, was not supported by credible evidence ([22]–[30]). The court highlighted significant discrepancies in Abbott's position regarding the frequency and cause of flooding events at his property ([25]–[27]).

Further, the applicant's claim of a legitimate expectation was deemed unsubstantiated because the necessary factual basis to validate such an expectation was absent ([32]–[34]). The court ruled that, regardless of the existence of a purported practice, there was no legal duty for the municipality to undertake the actions Abbott demanded, and thus, the review application could not succeed under PAJA.

### C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Abbott's appeal, affirming the High Court's ruling that the Overstrand Municipality was not legally required to take preventative measures against flooding as claimed by the appellant ([35]). The ratio decidendi focused on the municipality’s lack of statutory authority over berm management and the insufficiency of evidence regarding an established practice, thereby grounding the ruling in principles of administrative law and local governmental powers as structured by the South African Constitution. This case elucidates the stringent requirements for a clear legal obligation or authority to warrant judicial review against administrative actions in the context of local government.

#### Implications
The decision underscores the importance of clear statutory provisions governing local government's responsibilities and the challenges of invoking the doctrine of legitimate expectation when past practices do not align with current legal frameworks. It serves as a crucial precedent concerning the limitations of municipal power in environmental management within South Africa.
