Supreme Court of Appeal

The Chairperson of the Municipal Appeals Tribunal, City of Tshwane & others v Brooklyn and Eastern Areas Citizens Association & others ([2019] ZASCA 34)

In September 2015, the fourth appellant, Caliber 651 (Pty) Ltd, submitted an application under section 56 of the Town Planning and Townships Ordinance 15 of 1986 for the rezoning of Erf 908, Brooklyn, from ‘Residential 1’ to ‘Special’ ([1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 34)

F – Facts

In September 2015, the fourth appellant, Caliber 651 (Pty) Ltd, submitted an application under section 56 of the Town Planning and Townships Ordinance 15 of 1986 for the rezoning of Erf 908, Brooklyn, from ‘Residential 1’ to ‘Special’ ([1]). The application faced objections from several parties, including the Brooklyn and Eastern Areas Citizens Association (BEACA) and the University of Pretoria. The Municipal Planning Tribunal approved the rezoning on 18 May 2016, and the decision was communicated via letter dated 8 June 2016 ([1]–[2]).

BEACA lodged an internal appeal on 2 August 2016 against the Municipal Planning Tribunal’s decision. In response, the developer raised various points in limine questioning the validity of the appeal, ultimately leading to BEACA submitting a second appeal by 19 October 2016 to address these concerns. On 31 January 2017, the Municipal Appeals Tribunal dismissed BEACA's appeals as invalid, stating that they were incorrectly filed under the Ordinance rather than the applicable procedures under the Tshwane Land Use Management By-Law 2016 ([3]).

Subsequently, BEACA sought a review of the Tribunal’s ruling from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which found in favor of BEACA, setting aside the Tribunal's decision and remitting the matter back to the Appeals Tribunal with specific directions ([5]). The appellants then sought to appeal this decision ([6]). During the course of appeal proceedings, it emerged that the construction of the building had progressed significantly, culminating in its completion and occupation ([7]–[8]).

I – Issues

The principal legal questions arising from the case included:

  1. Mootness: Whether the appeal was moot given that the construction had been fully completed and occupied while the appeals were still pending ([8]–[10]).
  2. Piecemeal Adjudication: Whether the court could adjudicate on the appeal without resulting in fragmented or piecemeal litigation of the underlying merits of the rezoning decision ([19]–[20]).
  3. Appeal Procedures: Whether BEACA's internal appeals were validly lodged under the applicable statutory regime, specifically considering the transition from the Ordinance to the By-Law and SPLUMA ([4]–[5]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal frameworks and precedents considered include:

  1. Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013: Section 16(2)(a) regulates moot appeals, indicating that a court may dismiss an appeal if the sought decision would have no practical effect ([8]).
  2. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA): Relevant particularly in assessing the justness and equity of administrative decisions and potential remedies ([20]).
  3. Spatial Planning and Land Use Management Act 16 of 2013 (SPLUMA) & Tshwane Land Use Management By-Law of 2016: Governs the procedural validity of planning appeals, indicating the supremacy of SPLUMA over prior legislative frameworks where inconsistencies exist ([38]).

Precedential cases discussed include Tecmed Africa v Minister of Health ([11]), concerning the parameters of mootness, and several instances wherein the court has historically dealt with the merits even in cases where newer developments rendered previous litigation moot ([12]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal examined the implications of the completed building on the mootness of the appeal. The court explored whether the development's status would effectively negate the significance of the proceeding ([8]–[12]). It determined that the existence of a fully tenanted building did not undermine the respondent's interest in contesting the legitimacy of the rezoning due to its constitutional implications and the enduring impact of land use decisions ([10]–[11]).

Moreover, the court recognized the procedural complexities arising from the transition between statutory regimes (the Ordinance to the By-Law under SPLUMA). The court found that despite procedural missteps by BEACA, the intrinsic aim of the legislative framework was to facilitate public involvement in land-use decisions, therefore, the intention to appeal facilitated sufficient compliance with the procedural requirements ([52]).

In evaluating the piecemeal adjudication objection, the court found that resolving the appeal would not lead to fragmented litigation, nor would it extend the overall process unduly. It clarified that the appeal would address the specific legality of the internal appeals rather than adjudicating on the merits of the rezoning itself, thus fitting within the permissible scope of administrative review processes ([20]–[21]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal largely on the grounds that the issues raised were not rendered moot despite the building's completion. It affirmed that the proceedings were within the court's jurisdiction to adjudicate on the merits of the procedural irregularities surrounding the internal appeals ([18]–[34]).

The court emphasized that the substantive rights of the parties required clarification, particularly how procedural irregularities could affect future land use adjudications in light of SPLUMA. Thus, directions were issued for the Appeals Tribunal to reassess BEACA’s first internal appeal properly. The appellants were jointly and severally ordered to pay BEACA's costs ([58]). This ruling stressed the value of maintaining procedural integrity while facilitating equitable access to judicial review processes in land use governance.

In summary, the case reinforces the principles that procedural missteps should not inherently invalidate an aggrieved party’s right to appeal and highlights the significant judicial review capacity within municipal land use frameworks. It serves as a pivotal reference point for future disputes involving procedural compliance under potentially conflicting legislative regimes.
