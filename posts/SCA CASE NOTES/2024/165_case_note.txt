Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title**: Masiteng v Minister of Police (944/2023) [2024] ZASCA 165 (04 December 2024)

## F – Facts

The matter arises from an application for reconsideration under s 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 ([1], [3]). The applicant, Ntjanyana Daniel Masiteng, suffered unlawful arrest and detention by members of the South African Police Services (SAPS) on 29 September 2019 in Warden, Free State, where he was held for 42 hours without a court appearance ([2], [5]). The regional court awarded him R30,000 in damages, which he found insufficient given the circumstances of his detention, citing inhumane conditions and humiliation during the arrest ([8], [7]).

Masiteng appealed to the Free State Division of the High Court, which upheld the regional court’s ruling and dismissed his appeal with costs on 12 May 2023 ([2], [9]). Following a petition for leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal, which was similarly dismissed on 10 August 2023, Masiteng sought reconsideration from the President of the Supreme Court, who referred the matter back for reconsideration ([3], [4], [21]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues to resolve were whether:
1. There are exceptional circumstances that warrant the granting of leave to appeal against the original award of damages for unlawful detention ([3], [20]).
2. The damages awarded of R30,000 are fair and reasonable in relation to the applicant's experience and suffering ([1], [18]).

Additionally, a secondary issue concerned the applicant's contention regarding the costs awarded against him, raised under the Biowatch principle, asserting that as he sought to vindicate constitutional rights, he should not be penalized with costs ([11], [22]).

## R – Rules

The court applied the legal framework established by the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, particularly s 17(2)(f), which allows for reconsideration in exceptional circumstances where a grave failure of justice might otherwise occur ([4]). The principles guiding compensation for unlawful arrest and detention are sourced from [Tyulu][8] and [Ak v Minister of Police][13], emphasizing that awards should not be seen as mechanical calculations but should factor in elements such as the nature of the police conduct, the context of the arrest, and the resultant impact on the individual's dignity and liberty.

Furthermore, the Biowatch principle ([12]) establishes that a successful party in constitutional matters may not incur costs against them, particularly when challenging state actions impacting rights, which imposes a duty on courts to assess costs orders judiciously.

## A – Application

The court, through Judge Molefe, determined that Masiteng had not demonstrated exceptional circumstances to merit the reconsideration of his leave to appeal ([20]). It emphasized that the high threshold required had not been met, as the applicant failed to introduce any new arguments verging on unconventional circumstances that warranted revisiting the prior decisions ([21]).

The analysis reiterated that the assessment of damages must consider the totality of circumstances rather than surface-level comparisons to other cases. It was determined that the original R30,000 award was appropriately reflective of the infringement of personal liberty, aligning with the judicial discretion exercised ([10], [19]). The court acknowledged the emotional harm incurred by Masiteng but maintained that the damages awarded adequately fulfilled the compensatory purpose of providing a solatium for his violation ([18]).

In terms of costs, the court found the arguments regarding the Biowatch principle misplaced, reiterating that the claim was fundamentally delictual, and thus, the principle does not alter the costs order outlined by the high court ([22], [23]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the application for leave to appeal with costs ([24], [25]). The court's holding reaffirmed the importance of judicial discretion in assessing damages for unlawful arrest and detention while underscoring the stringent requirements for establishing exceptional circumstances justifying appeal reconsideration. The ruling encapsulated a broader affirmation of the delicate balance between individual rights and judicial economy in the context of claims against the state, thereby contributing to the evolving jurisprudence surrounding state accountability in unlawful detentions. Furthermore, it clarified the application of costs principles in litigations aimed at vindicating constitutional rights, offering guidance on the treatment of such cases in future judicial considerations.
