Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note: MEC for Local Government, Environmental Affairs and Development Planning, Western Cape Province v. Minister for Mineral Resources [2012] ZACC 10

## F – Facts
The case involves two judgments delivered by the Constitutional Court of South Africa, specifically regarding costs orders stemming from cases CCT 102/11 and CCT 103/11 ([1]). In the first case (CCT 102/11), the MEC for Local Government and Environmental Affairs was a respondent. The Court ordered the Minister for Mineral Resources to pay the costs of Swartland Municipality and the MEC, including costs for two counsel ([1]). In the second case (CCT 103/11), a similar costs order was issued in favor of the City of Cape Town against the Minister for Mineral Resources ([1]).

Post-judgment, on April 16, 2012, the MEC filed an application under Rule 29 of the Constitutional Court Rules, invoking Rule 42 of the Uniform Rules ([2]). The MEC contended that both orders contained "patent errors" due to mischaracterization of his involvement ([3]–[4]). Specifically, in case CCT 102/11, the MEC argued that although he was cited as a respondent, he did not oppose the appeal ([3]). However, he asserted that in case CCT 103/11, he was wrongly excluded from the cost order that should have included him as he successfully opposed part of the appeal ([4]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal questions presented by the MEC were as follows:

1. Did the Constitutional Court err in including the MEC in the costs order of CCT 102/11 when he did not oppose the appeal?
2. In CCT 103/11, was there an error in the costs order by excluding the MEC, who argued successfully on a portion of the case concerning the applicability of the Land Use Planning Ordinance ([5]–[9])?

## R – Rules
The Court's decision relied on the interpretation of Rule 42 of the Uniform Rules, which allows for the variation or rescission of judgments when there is a patent error or ambiguity ([2], [5]). The rule stipulates:

- The court may rescind or vary an order due to “an ambiguity, or a patent error or omission” ([2]).
- A party affected by such discrepancies must notify all other parties ([2]).

The case law cited included **Firestone South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Genticuro A.G.** and others, which recognize the court's authority to amend its orders under specific conditions ([5]).

## A – Application
Regarding CCT 102/11, the Court concluded that the cost order mistakenly included the MEC as a party since he did not participate in the appeal ([6]). The acknowledgment of factual claims uncontested ensured clarity, allowing the Court to amend the order and exclude the MEC from the costs responsibility ([6]).

In CCT 103/11, the MEC's contention that he should have been included in the costs order fell short of the requisite standard. The Court held that while he presented valid arguments regarding the LUPO issue, the MEC's claims did not constitute a complete success in the appeal, especially considering his attempts to cross-appeal failed ([8]). Thus, it was deemed equitable for each party (MEC and Minister for Mineral Resources) to bear their own costs, resulting in no amendment of the costs order in case CCT 103/11 ([9]).

## C – Conclusion
The Court issued two key decisions: 

1. It amended the costs order in CCT 102/11 to remove reference to the MEC, as his inclusion had been erroneous ([10]).
2. It refused the MEC's application to vary the costs order in CCT 103/11 based on the premise of partial success ([10]). 

The implications of this ruling underscore the importance of accurately categorizing parties in legal proceedings and affirming the principle that parties should only bear costs commensurate with their involvement and success in the dispute. The case highlights the application of procedural rules aimed at ensuring just outcomes in the realm of administrative law and local governance.

In summary, the Court affirmed the clarity of its previous judgments while exercising caution in cost allocations reflective of the respective parties' arguments and successes in court. This ruling maintains legal precedents and procedural integrity in subsequent administrative law matters.
