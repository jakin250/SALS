Constitutional Court 2007

Case Note: M M Van Wyk v Unitas Hospital and Dr G E Naudé

F – Facts

The case involves three applications that are interrelated, primarily focusing on the right of the applicant, M M Van Wyk, to access a medical report that she believed could substantiate a claim for damages against Unitas Hospital following her husband's death while a patient there ([1]–[2]). Van Wyk argued that this report, authored by Dr Naudé, who treated her husband and served as the director of the intensive care unit, was essential for her legal proceedings ([2]).

Van Wyk approached the Johannesburg High Court seeking an order for the release of the report under Section 50(1)(a) of the Promotion of Access to Information Act 2000 (PAIA), which provides a right to access records from private bodies when required for the protection of any rights ([3]). The High Court initially ruled in her favor, granting her access to the report. However, this decision was appealed by the hospital, resulting in a ruling from the Supreme Court of Appeal that denied her access to the report ([4]).

After an eleven-month delay, Van Wyk sought leave to appeal this ruling in the Constitutional Court, along with a condonation for the late filing of her application ([5]–[6]). Her delay was partly attributed to confusion over court dates, but ultimately, the court found her explanation unconvincing and lacking substance, leading to the striking of her application from the roll ([6]–[9]).

I – Issues

The case raised several legal and constitutional issues:

  1. Constitutional right of access to information: Is the applicant entitled to the medical report under both PAIA and the Constitution, which guarantees access to information necessary for exercising or protecting rights? ([3], [21]).
  2. Condonation for late filing: Should the Constitutional Court grant condonation for the applicant’s late application for leave to appeal, considering the inordinate delay and lack of reasonable explanation? ([20], [34]).
  3. Mootness: Is the issue raised in the appeal moot, given that the applicant had subsequently gained access to the report through discovery in ongoing litigation? ([27], [34]).

R – Rules

Key regulations and statutory provisions forming the foundation of the court's analysis included:

  • Constitution of South Africa: Section 32(1)(b), which guarantees everyone the right of access to information held by others necessary for the protection of rights ([3]).
  • Promotion of Access to Information Act 2000: Particularly Section 50(1)(a), which mirrors the constitutional provision and obligates private bodies to grant access to records upon request for the exercise or protection of rights ([3], [21]).
  • Constitutional Court Rules: Rule 11 governs the handling of applications to the court, outlining how and when applications may be disposed of, including provisions for oral argument versus written argument ([14], [16]).

Relevant case law influences included considerations from previous constitutional rulings emphasizing the importance of justiciability even in the face of mootness, subject to the principles of justice ([29], [31]).

A – Application

The Court's analysis is multi-faceted:

  1. Right of Access: Although the constitutional significance of the right to access information is critical, the Court found that the matter's core issue had become moot by the time it reached them. Van Wyk had already accessed the report through the discovery process in her other ongoing claim against the hospital and thus could not show that a live issue concerning the right of access remained ([27], [34]).
  2. Condonation Analysis: The Court applied the ‘interests of justice’ standard for granting condonation. Factors considered included the nature and importance of the relief sought, the extent and cause of delay, and the applicant's rationale for not fulfilling timelines ([20]). The Court found Van Wyk’s explanation inadequate, determining that the inordinate delay and failure to convincingly explain her circumstances rendered her late application unjustifiable.
  3. Mootness and Public Interest: While the amicus curiae submitted that resolving issues of access to information is generally of public interest, and delays in the litigation process often render them moot by the time of decision, the Court ultimately concluded that addressing moot issues should not come at the cost of fairness to the parties involved. The historical delays, coupled with the applicant’s lack of action until faced with adverse cost implications, affirmed the decision to view the matter as effectively moot ([29], [30]).
  4. Finality in Litigation: The Court stressed the importance of finality in litigation, contending that long delays instill reasonable expectations that adverse decisions have become unassailable, thereby justifying the refusal of condonation on these grounds ([31], [34]).

C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Constitutional Court dismissed both the application for condonation regarding the late filing and the application for leave to appeal ([36]). The ruling underscored significant principles around the rights to access information, the conditions under which the courts may grant condonation for delayed appeals, and the functional necessity of finality in the litigation process.

The note ends with the decision that no costs order would be imposed, acknowledging the unwarranted burden of costs on the applicant, especially in light of the history of the case ([36]). The judgment serves as a critical reference point that affirms the courts' reluctance to entertain matters that have become moot, especially when they concern delays not reasonably explained, thereby reinforcing the necessity for timely litigation practices within the framework of constitutional rights.
