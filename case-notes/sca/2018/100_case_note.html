Moyo v The Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development & others (387/2017); Sonti v The Minister of Justice and Correctional Services & others (386/2017); [2018] ZASCA 100

The appellants contended that these provisions infringed their rights to freedom of expression and fair trial, arguing that the law disproportionately affected their ability to engage in legitimate political discourse and protest ([paras 4-9]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 100

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The appeals in question stemmed from challenges by General Alfred Moyo and Nokulunga Primrose Sonti, both of whom were charged with violations under the Intimidation Act 72 of 1982. Moyo was accused of making threatening statements that allegedly incited fear during a community meeting concerning police conduct. Sonti faced similar accusations in the context of allegedly threatening a complainant to withdraw criminal charges against another individual. Both appellants sought declarations that sections 1(1)(b) and 1(2) of the Act were unconstitutional ([paras 1-3]).

The appellants contended that these provisions infringed their rights to freedom of expression and fair trial, arguing that the law disproportionately affected their ability to engage in legitimate political discourse and protest ([paras 4-9]).

The Gauteng Division of the High Court ruled against them, finding that while section 1(2) infringed fair trial rights, the infringements were justified due to the State's interest in combating intimidation ([para 1]). The judgment was appealed ([para 1]).

I – Issues

The core legal issues presented in this case included:

  1. Constitutionality of Section 1(1)(b): Does the provision unjustifiably infringe upon the right to freedom of expression under section 16(1) of the Constitution by criminalizing expressions which invoke subjective feelings of fear regardless of subjective intent? ([paras 4-5]).
  2. Constitutionality of Section 1(2): Does this provision create an unconstitutional reverse onus of proof, infringing on the rights to be presumed innocent and to remain silent under section 35(3) of the Constitution? ([para 7]).

R – Rules

The Court's analysis drew upon several constitutional principles:

  1. Freedom of Expression (Section 16 of the Constitution): Guarantees the right to express oneself freely, with limitations for certain designated categories (e.g., incitement of violence) ([para 24]).
  2. Right to a Fair Trial (Section 35): Ensures the presumption of innocence until proven guilty and the right of the accused to remain silent ([para 35]).
  3. Limitation Clause (Section 36): Allows for the limitation of rights under certain circumstances, provided the limitations are reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society ([paras 38-39]).
  4. Legal Precedents: The court referenced prior cases emphasizing the need for legislative interpretations that align with constitutional protections while ensuring that equitably applied standards do not lead to overreach or punishment for lawful behavior ([paras 20-40]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed both sections within the context of constitutional scrutiny:

  1. Section 1(1)(b): The majority opinion declared the section excessively broad and capable of encompassing not just "true threats" but also politically charged dialogue without intent to intimidate. The statute's language created potential for criminalizing political speech and expression that may not meet the threshold of true intimidation ([paras 31-34]). Ultimately, the court concluded that the chilling effect of this provision on freedom of expression warranted declaring it unconstitutional.
  2. Section 1(2): The court examined whether it imposed a reverse onus that compromised the presumption of innocence. It found that it placed undue pressure on accused individuals to disclose their defenses prematurely, infringing their right to silence and protection against self-incrimination ([paras 54-65]). Here, the Court found a direct conflict with section 35(3)(h) of the Constitution, concluding that this provision could not be justified under the limitation clause ([para 72]).

Various dissenting opinions highlighted legitimate state interests in combating intimidation and expressed concerns about the consequences of invalidating the sections, emphasizing that existing threats to public safety should be carefully weighed against individual rights ([paras 84-86]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court ultimately held that:

  1. Section 1(1)(b) of the Intimidation Act is unconstitutional, as it unjustifiably infringes on freedom of expression and fails to limit itself to true intimidatory conduct ([para 46]).
  2. Section 1(2) is also unconstitutional for creating a reverse onus that undermines the presumption of innocence and compulsively infringes the accused's right to silence and fair trial ([para 82]).

While Moyo's appeal was dismissed, Sonti's was upheld, leading to a retrospective declaration of invalidity for both contested sections. The order signaled an opportunity for remedy in ongoing cases affected by these provisions, affirming the court's role in safeguarding constitutional rights over unjust legislative frameworks ([para 3]).

The implications of this judgment highlight the critical balance between protecting public safety and maintaining the sanctity of constitutional rights, emphasizing the need for legislative caution in the criminalization of political and social expressions in a democratic society.
