Constitutional Court

Schubart Park Residents’ Association v City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality

Residents engaged with City officials seeking temporary accommodation, but negotiations failed.

Constitutional Court 2012 [2012] ZACC 26 ## F - Facts The dispute in this case arose

Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa
Case Number: CCT 23/12
Judgment Date: 9 October 2012
Citation: [2012] ZACC 26

F - Facts

The dispute in this case arose from the urgent removal of residents from Schubart Park, a residential complex in Pretoria, managed by the City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality (the City). Originally built in the 1970s for civil servants, the complex had fallen into disrepair, and by 2011, approximately 700 families lived there under deteriorating conditions ([2]–[3]). Following protests by residents concerning their living conditions on 21 September 2011, which escalated into violence and fires within the property, the police, along with city officials, evacuated block C of Schubart Park and denied residents access to return ([3]–[4]).

Residents engaged with City officials seeking temporary accommodation, but negotiations failed. The next day, an urgent application was filed in the North Gauteng High Court seeking permission for residents to return to their homes ([5]–[6]). The High Court dismissed the application for immediate return but mandated that temporary accommodation be arranged ([8]–[9]). Subsequent High Court orders stipulated that at a later stage, residents could agree to accept a tender for temporary accommodation, which raised concerns about eviction under section 26(3) of the Constitution, which protects against eviction without a court order ([10]).

Eventually, the residents sought leave to appeal after their requests were dismissed in lower courts, arguing their eviction was unlawful ([10]–[11]). They sought orders for immediate re-occupation, restoration of water and electricity, and punitive costs against the City ([10]).

I - Issues

The primary legal issues in this case were:

  1. Was the initial dismissal of the applicants' re-occupation application justified, or did it amount to unlawful eviction under section 26(3) of the Constitution?
  2. Did the High Court’s subsequent orders provide adequate constitutional relief under section 38 of the Constitution?
  3. What constitutes “appropriate relief” to ensure the applicants can return to their homes safely and legally?

These issues revolved around the interplay of property rights, due process in eviction procedures, and the obligations of the state under socio-economic rights provisions ([22]–[30]).

R - Rules

The Constitutional Court’s decision relied heavily on the following legal principles:

  • Section 26(3) of the Constitution: Prohibits eviction from homes without a court order made after considering relevant circumstances ([13]).
  • Common law of spoliation: The right to restoration of possession even for unlawful occupants, highlighted in cases like Tswelopele and Rikhotso, which guide the available remedies to address unlawful dispossession and emphasize immediate possession restoration ([23]–[25]).
  • Section 38 of the Constitution: Allows courts to grant “appropriate relief” for rights infringed under the Bill of Rights, prompting a need for proportional remediations ([18], [28]–[31]).

A - Application

Based on the factual matrix and the applicable legal framework, the Constitutional Court analyzed the High Court’s orders ([36]–[40]). The Court upheld that the condition of the buildings justified immediate safety concerns, but dismissed the notion that this justified permanent evictions. The initial order's ambiguity regarding eviction and re-occupation was recognized as problematic. The Court highlighted the fundamental right of residents not only to be protected from arbitrary eviction but also to be consulted in the re-occupation process.

The assessment put forward was that while the dismissal order was made in urgent circumstances, it did not legally remove the residents' rights to ultimately return ([36]–[42]). Further, the tender arrangement imposed by the City limited the residents’ rights excessively, placing them in a position where accepting a tender was the only means to avoid suffering long-term homelessness, which contradicted existing constitutional protections ([40]).

This led the Court to conclude that a declaratory order was warranted; it mandated that the residents had the right to re-occupation of their homes as soon as feasible and necessitated meaningful engagement between the City and residents to facilitate this return ([53]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court held that the High Court's orders did not constitute lawful grounds for eviction as articulated by section 26(3) of the Constitution. It established that residents had the right to be restored to their homes, emphasizing that any forced removal under urgent safety pretenses could only be temporary, not permanent ([52]–[53]). The verdict mandates the City to engage meaningfully with the residents for a concrete plan that includes identification of residents, restoration timelines, and alternative accommodation, under judicial oversight to ensure compliance and transparency ([53]). This decision underscores the judiciary's role in enforcing socio-economic rights and the necessity of municipal accountability in housing provisions.
