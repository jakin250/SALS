Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2007

### FIRAC Case Note: Menqa & Another v Markom & Others [2007] SCA 172 (RSA)

#### F – Facts
The case revolves around the sale in execution of residential property, specifically erf 23584 Maitland, Cape Town, that took place on 17 November 2003. The first appellant, Patrick Markom, purchased the property from a deceased estate in 1995. At the time of purchase, the property was occupied by Jules Tromp under a lease that the executor of the deceased estate terminated. Following a personal injury claim against Markom, a default judgment was issued against him on 19 November 1999, which later formed the basis for the sale in execution ([paras 1–2]).

Markom became aware of this judgment only four years later, on 13 November 2003, when a notice of sale in execution was served. Despite obtaining an urgent interim order to stay the sale, the execution proceeded, and the property was sold for R110,000 to the second appellant, Desmond Menqa, who subsequently sold it to Owen Peter Roux for R490,000 ([paras 3–6]).

The Cape High Court, presided over by Zondi AJ, ruled that the sale in execution was invalid because the warrant had been issued by a clerk of the Court without the necessary judicial oversight, violating the ruling in *Jaftha v Schoeman; Van Rooyen v Stoltz* [2005] (2) SA 140 (CC) ([para 7]).

#### I – Issues
The principal legal issues in this case pertain to the validity of the sale in execution under section 66(1)(a) of the Magistrates’ Courts Act 32 of 1944, focusing on judicial oversight and the constitutional protections afforded to debtors, specifically regarding their right to adequate housing under section 26(1) of the Constitution. Critical considerations include:

1. Was the warrant of execution validly issued in compliance with judicial oversight?
2. Can the subsequent sale in execution be validated under section 70 of the Act despite its invalidity?
3. What implications arise from potential unjust enrichment in the context of the sale and transfer of property? ([paras 8–10], [22–23]).

#### R – Rules
The rules applicable to this case include the following legislative and judicial provisions:

1. **Section 66(1)(a) of the Magistrates’ Courts Act 32 of 1944**: Requires judicial oversight for the issuance of execution orders against immovable properties ([paras 8–9]).
2. **Section 70 of the Magistrates’ Courts Act**: States that sales in execution shall not be impeached against a bona fide purchaser for value without notice of defects ([para 11]).
3. **Jaftha v Schoeman; Van Rooyen v Stoltz (CC)**: This case ruled that section 66(1)(a) constituted an infringement of the right to adequate housing, necessitating judicial oversight to safeguard against arbitrary deprivation of property ([paras 8–10], [47]).
4. **Common Law Principles**: Distinctions regarding the validity and enforceability of sales in execution, particularly when procedural irregularities affect legitimacy ([paras 46–48]).

#### A – Application
In applying these rules, the court found that the lack of judicial oversight in the issuance of the warrant rendered it invalid, hence the subsequent sale in execution could not stand. The judgment underscored the imperative need for adherence to constitutional safeguards intended to prevent violations of property rights of debtors ([paras 12, 20]). The court emphasized that applying section 70 in this instance would contradict the essence of the *Jaftha* ruling, which aimed to protect vulnerable debtors from unjust loss of home ([paras 21–23]).

The judges considered arguments regarding factual knowledge and good faith from Menqa and Roux, recognizing that despite their lack of bad faith, the invalidity of the initial sale rendered any transfer of ownership ineffective. The principle of rei vindicatio allowed Markom to reclaim ownership of the property as the sale was a nullity from the outset ([paras 19, 43]).

Moreover, the court highlighted concerns of unjust enrichment, noting that while Markom's right to reclaim ownership must be balanced with Menqa's financial involvement in the property, these considerations would be better suited for subsequent judicial scrutiny rather than immediate relief ([paras 50–51]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled that the Cape High Court was correct in declaring the sale in execution invalid and prohibiting the registration of the property transfer to Roux. The court rejected the arguments regarding the applicability of section 70 to validate the transaction, reaffirming the critical importance of judicial oversight in sales in execution to protect constitutional rights. 

The order from the court below was modified: the confirmation of the section relating to the registration of Markom as the owner was set aside, indicating that all financial claims and the implications of ownership will need resolution in future proceedings. This judgment reinforces the legal framework governing sales in execution, emphasizing the blend of equitable principles and constitutional protections in the realm of property law ([paras 27, 48–51]).
