# Case Note: Zwane and Another v The State (426/13) [2013] ZASCA 165

**FIRAC Method**

## F – Facts ([paras 1–3])

Tshepo Bongani Zwane and Amos Nkosinathi Zwane (the appellants) were convicted by the Pretoria Regional Court on nine counts of robbery with aggravating circumstances. The first appellant faced an additional count of receiving stolen property, as a competent verdict for the theft of a motor vehicle. Each appellant received a sentence of 15 years’ imprisonment on the robbery counts, to run concurrently, with an additional three years for the first appellant on the receiving stolen property count.

An appeal to the North Gauteng High Court was denied, prompting this further appeal, granted by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA). It is noteworthy that part of the record of the proceedings was reconstructed due to the unavailability of transcriptions from the original plea proceedings and the first three State witnesses' evidence, but it was agreed that this reconstruction accurately reflected the proceedings ([para 2]).

The robbers perpetrated an armed robbery at a Pick ‘n Pay supermarket in Centurion on 6 February 2006. During the incident, employees were overpowered, tied up, and robbed of cash and other valuables. The appellants were arrested in possession of a Volkswagen (VW) Golf, which contained items identified as part of the stolen goods, about 40 hours after the robbery. The first appellant claimed to have borrowed the car from the late Mr. Dan Tshabalala, and evidence was presented from Mrs. Mathlaba, the widow of Mr. Tshabalala, verifying this claim ([paras 3, 4]).

## I – Issues ([paras 4–10])

The central legal issues in this case are:

1. The adequacy of circumstantial evidence in establishing the appellants' guilt for the robbery.
2. The applicability of the doctrine of recent possession concerning the stolen goods found in the VW Golf.
3. Whether the trial court's rejection of the first appellant's explanation constituted a reasonable inference of guilt. 
4. The sufficiency of evidence against the second appellant, whose only link to the crime was his presence in the vehicle ([para 10]).

## R – Rules ([paras 4–12])

Key legal principles and precedents that guided the Court's judgment included:

- **Doctrine of Recent Possession**: It is a well-established principle that possession of recently stolen property can support the inference that a person is a thief or is implicated in the theft, especially if the items are of a type that can easily be disposed of ([para 11]).
  
- **Importance of Inferences**: As noted in *S v Mtsweni* (1985)(1) SACR 590 (A), false evidence or a denial by an accused should not automatically imply guilt; it must be evaluated alongside all available evidence, lest speculative conclusions be drawn.
  
- **Credibility Determinations**: Observations by the trial court regarding witness credibility are generally given significant deference by appellate courts, as the trial court is in the best position to evaluate the demeanor and reliability of witnesses ([para 9]).

- **Right to Silence**: The failure to inform suspects of their right to remain silent can impact the admissibility of their statements to the police, following the precedent set in *S v Orrie* (2005)(1) SACR 63 (C) regarding the constitutional rights of arrested persons ([para 8]).

## A – Application ([paras 4–13])

In considering the evidence, the SCA found two central points that weakened the State's case:

1. **Insufficient Identification**: None of the robbery victims identified the appellants as the perpetrators. The State relied primarily on circumstantial evidence, particularly the possession of stolen items found in the vehicle. However, this alone did not fulfill the burden of proof required to establish guilt beyond a reasonable doubt ([para 4, 10]).

2. **Falsehoods and Explanations**: The trial court had declared the first appellant's explanation for being in possession of the stolen vehicle as false beyond reasonable doubt. However, the SCA reasoned that this falsity, in absence of corroborative evidence of complicity in the robbery, could not serve as the sole basis for a conviction. The first appellant's claim regarding borrowing the car, supported by Mrs. Mathlaba's testimony, was deemed credible and consistent with the available evidence, thus undermining the conviction on count ten ([paras 5, 13]).

Regarding the second appellant, his sole connection to the crime stemmed from his presence in the vehicle, and his choice not to testify against him led to the conclusion of insufficient evidence for his conviction ([para 10]).

## C – Conclusion ([paras 12–14])

The SCA ultimately held that the cumulative evidential failings meant the State had not proved the appellants’ guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. The doctrine of recent possession could not stand alone without further corroborative evidence implicating the appellants in the robbery itself. Consequently, the court concluded:

- Both appellants’ convictions and sentences were set aside, underlining the significance of due process and the presumption of innocence. 

This case emphasizes critical considerations for circumstantial evidence, the maintenance of proper procedural standards, and the need for judicial caution in evaluating the sufficiency of an evidentiary basis for a conviction. The judgment reinforces the principle that mere possession of stolen goods, in conjunction with false explanations, does not automatically entail guilt without a broader context establishing involvement in the commission of the crime ([paras 14]).
