# Case Note: Republic of South Africa (SCA)

**Case Citation**: [2003] ZASCA 55; 2004 (5) SA 600 (SCA)  
**Case Number**: 653/2002  
**Judgment Date**: 19 September 2003  
**Coram**: Marais, Farlam, Navsa, Cloete JJA et Jones AJA

---

### FIRAC Case Note

#### F – Facts

The case arises from a series of claims made by the master and crew of the MT 'Argun', which were brought before the Cape Provincial Division under the Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983 ([1]–[4]). 

1. In **Case No. AC127/99**, the master and crew, who served on board the MT 'Argun' upon her arrival in Cape Town on 25 May 1999, sought claims for unpaid wages and costs due to repatriation ([1]).
   
2. **Case No. AC134/99** involved a similar action by the previous crew from 25 July 1995 to 31 January 1996, claiming unpaid wages ([2]).
   
3. A third claim, **Case No. AC4/2002**, was instituted by the first twenty-one plaintiffs of the earlier action for wages owed during their service on board from 1 July to 13 October 1999 ([3]).

The vessel was initially arrested on 14 July 1999 for the first case and on 23 July 1999 for the second ([4]). The sheriff sought a court order concerning the reimbursements for costs of preserving the vessel, and this initial application was dismissed by the High Court but granted upon appeal ([6]–[7]). On 21 June 2002, it was held that the earlier arrests had lapsed, yet the actions were allowed to proceed ([8]).

The High Court ultimately ruled that the defendant vessel, which is the appellant in this case, was to pay various amounts, including capital, interests, as well as costs associated with preservation ([9]).

#### I – Issues

The appeal presented six primary legal issues:

1. **Lapsing of Actions**: Did the first and second actions lapse when the arrests under which they were instituted lapsed? ([10]).

2. **Execution of Judgments**: Were the judgments in the first and second actions in rem enforceable against the appellant? ([10]).

3. **Interest on Claims**: Was it appropriate for the court to award interest from the end of each month for which wages were claimed? ([10]).

4. **Rate of Interest**: Was the award of interest at the rate of 15.5% lawful? ([10]).

5. **Inclusion of Costs**: Were the sheriff's preservation costs correctly included in the respondents' costs order? ([10]).

6. **Costs of Additional Volume**: Should the respondents bear the costs for the additional volume of the appeal record? ([10]).

#### R – Rules

This case is evaluated under various legal provisions and precedents relevant to maritime and admiralty law. Key legal rules examined include:

1. **Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983**: Particularly provisions on actions in rem and the necessity of an arrest for such actions ([12], [20]).

2. **Common Law Principles**: English admiralty law principles regarding maritime liens and the execution of judgments in rem, supported by case law, including *The MV Andrico Unity* ([13], [19]) and *Republic of India v Indian Steamship Co Ltd (No 2)* ([16]).

3. **Discretionary Powers of the Courts**: The standard for granting interest and costs is guided by a broad discretion under s 5(2)(f) of the Act, allowing courts to decide what is just ([38]).

#### A – Application

1. **Lapsing of Actions**:
   The court concluded that the lapsing of the arrests did not automatically lead to the lapse of the action in rem. Following the principles derived from English law, particularly the procedural and personality theories regarding actions in rem, it was determined that the law applicable did not necessitate a continuing arrest for the action to persist ([34]).

2. **Judgments in Rem**:
   Since the court ruled that the actions were not lapsed, the enforceability of the judgments that resulted from these actions remained intact. Therefore, the judgments could be executed against the appellant vessel without necessitating new proceedings ([35]).

3. **Interest Claims**:
   The court noted that a lack of evidence regarding what constituted a just interest rate led to upholding the High Court’s award of interest from the end of each month claimed ([39]).

4. **Rate of Interest**:
   The assessment of the interest rate consistent with what is deemed fair by the courts was also found appropriate, thus maintaining the 15.5% rate set by the lower court ([40]).

5. **Inclusion of Costs**:
   The court affirmed that the sheriff's preservation costs are permissible to be included in costs of suit under the Act, thus validating the trial court's ruling on this matter despite arguments about their separation from general suit costs ([46]).

6. **Costs of Additional Volume**:
   The court found against the appellant, confirming that the expenses related to the additional volume were necessary for the appeal, reaffirming the principle that all documents relevant to court proceedings must be included ([50]).

#### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the rulings of the lower court. The judgments in rem against the appellant were held enforceable, and the actions did not lapse despite the lapsing of arrests ([55]). The implications of this ruling extend to the necessity for continued practicality in maritime claims, reinforcing that procedural nuances such as the existence of maritime liens and the utility of actions in rem are central to their enforceability. The court's considerations demonstrate an alignment with both legislative intent and common law principles, aiming to protect the rights of seafarers in claims of unpaid wages while facilitating the operational integrity of admiralty jurisdiction.
