Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

## Case Note: Mount Amanzi Share Block Ltd v The Body Corporate of Windsor Heights Sectional Title Scheme & Others (537/2016) [2017] ZASCA 38

### F – Facts
The appellant, Mount Amanzi Share Block Limited, is a share block company operating a timeshare scheme on a housing estate. The first respondent, The Body Corporate of Windsor Heights Sectional Title Scheme, administers a housing estate that is located on land within the boundaries of Mount Amanzi. The remaining respondents are unit owners and tenants within Windsor Heights ([1]–[2]).

The estates, Windsor Heights and Mount Amanzi, were developed by De Wildt Trust starting in 1988, and due to geographical limitations, they could not connect to municipal utilities. Consequently, a notarial deed of servitude established Mount Amanzi's right to supply utility services to Windsor Heights, obliging Windsor Heights to pay annual levies, as determined by Mount Amanzi ([2]–[3]). 

Disputes arose regarding an increase in levies imposed by Mount Amanzi, which the respondents challenged as unilateral, leading to claims of spoliation as access to Mount Amanzi's facilities was restricted. The respondents sought court intervention, claiming unlawful interference and an order restoring access to facilities and services ([4]–[6]).

In the Gauteng Division of the High Court, the court ruled in favor of the respondents on the basis of an ongoing dispute of fact regarding levy payments and access rights, granting a temporary interdict and restoration of access ([4]–[5]). Mount Amanzi appealed against this ruling.

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues can be outlined as follows:
1. Whether the denial of access to the Mount Amanzi facilities and interference with services constituted spoliation.
2. Whether the increase in levies was validly determined and enforceable under the notarial deed of servitude.
3. Whether the court a quo correctly found that a genuine dispute of fact precluded a decision on the merits ([4]–[5], [17]–[24]).

### R – Rules
Several legal principles and precedents apply in this case:

1. **Spoliation**: Spoliation refers to the unlawful deprivation of a person’s possession or rights without a legal order. The party claiming spoliation must establish they were in peaceful possession and were unlawfully disturbed ([6], [20]).

2. **Discretionary Power**: The exercise of a discretionary power must be conducted arbitrio bono viri—reasonably and honestly. This principle stems from common law and is supported by case law such as *NBS Boland Bank Ltd v One Berg River Drive CC* and *Absa Bank Ltd v Lombard* ([30]–[31]).

3. **Irresoluble Disputes of Fact**: A dispute is considered genuine when it is raised clearly and significantly by the disputing party. A bare denial may not be sufficient if it lies within the knowledge of the person making the assertions as outlined in *Wightman t/a JW Construction v Headfour (Pty) Ltd* ([39]).

### A – Application
In examining the claim of spoliation, the court found that although there were temporary access restrictions imposed by Mount Amanzi, these were justified based on a prior agreement, thereby negating the claim of spoliation related to recreational facilities ([1]–[16]). The evidence indicated that access was not completely denied and had not been implemented at the time of filing, contradicting the basis for the spoliation application ([17]–[20]).

Regarding the calculation of levies, Mount Amanzi presented detailed evidence supporting the reasonableness of the levies and the necessity of these expenses for services provided. The court noted that the respondents had not adequately established a bona fide dispute of fact as the challenges against the levies relied heavily on bare denials ([23]–[47]). The levies were assessed consistent with the contracting provisions outlined in the servitude, allowing Mount Amanzi to determine levies at its discretion.

The supposed compromise reached in earlier meetings to cap the levies was disputed, with Mount Amanzi asserting it was unenforceable without documentation. This point was crucial as it underscored the ongoing obligation on the respondents to comply with the validly imposed levies, and engagement in discussions regarding their basis ([25]–[30]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal found in favor of Mount Amanzi, noting that:
1. The claim of spoliation was not established due to insufficient evidence of unlawful denial of access.
2. The levies imposed were valid, having been determined according to the provisions of the notarial deed.
3. The court a quo misdirected itself by concluding that an irresoluble dispute of fact existed and failing to fully consider the evidence provided by Mount Amanzi.

Consequently, the appeal was granted with costs; the order of the court a quo was set aside, affirming that the Body Corporate of Windsor Heights is liable for the budgeted levies as determined by Mount Amanzi ([49]). The implications of this judgment reinforce the importance of adherence to agreed terms in servitude arrangements and clarify the legal standards surrounding disputes regarding the enforcement of service levies in similar contexts.
