FIRAC Case Note: Malele v The State (723/2016) [2017] ZASCA 173

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1-10])

The case originated from a tragic event that occurred on 26 February 2013, where Mr. Silvesta Jossefa Marcia was arrested by officers of the South African Police Service stationed at the Daveyton Police Station. During the arrest, Mr. Marcia became handcuffed to a steel bench in the rear of a police van. As the officers perceived a threat from a gathering crowd, the vehicle departed with Mr. Marcia still attached to the bench, dragging him approximately 200 meters along the road. After arriving at the police station, Mr. Marcia, who was conscious upon arrival, was placed in a holding cell but died a few hours later.

The State’s case against the four applicants—members of the police—was that Mr. Marcia died from injuries sustained during this dragging incident and subsequent assaults in the holding cells. The applicants contended that they were unaware of Mr. Marcia being dragged and denied any wrongdoing.

Initially, the Gauteng Division of the High Court convicted the applicants of murder, with the court asserting that they knowingly participated in the unlawful action through the doctrine of common purpose. Each applicant received a 15-year prison sentence. Applications for leave to appeal were denied by the lower court and by two judges of the Supreme Court of Appeal, prompting the applicants to appeal to the President of the Supreme Court in accordance with s 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013. The President, Mpati P, referred the matter for reconsideration, highlighting concerns about the application of the common purpose doctrine ([paras 1–10]).

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 7-10])

The critical legal issues under examination were whether the applicants' convictions for murder rightly employed the doctrine of common purpose and whether the lower court adequately substantiated its findings regarding the applicants' mens rea (intent) in relation to the events surrounding the death of Mr. Marcia. The inquiry also revolved around the sufficiency of evidence to support the conviction of all the accused, particularly in light of varying degrees of involvement and knowledge about Mr. Marcia’s treatment.

Furthermore, it focused on whether the trial court had appropriately considered the distinctions in the actions and awareness of the individual applicants, specifically regarding their engagement in the alleged assaults in the police holding cells and their responses during the dragging incident ([paras 7-10]).

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 9-10])

The court's examination was guided by principles established in previous legal precedents pertaining to the doctrine of common purpose, notably S v Mgedezi & others [1989] 1 SA 687 and S v Thebus & another [2003] 2 SACR 319 (CC). These cases outline the requirements for liability under common purpose, including the necessity for an agreement to commit a crime and evidence of intentional and concerted action among the accused.

In addressing the mens rea requirements, the court looked to the established legal standards for proving intent, particularly dolus eventualis, which necessitates that an individual foresees the possibility of harmful consequences arising from their conduct ([paras 9-10]).

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 8-11])

In examining the application of common purpose, Mpati P raised 'grave doubts' regarding the trial court's conclusions. He noted the lack of definitive evidence linking the actions of the applicants to the death of Mr. Marcia beyond the threshold of reasonable doubt. The court critically evaluated the testimony of the single witness to the alleged assault in the holding cells, pointing out the witness's inability to identify specific individuals involved in the act.

Moreover, the court acknowledged the actions of the fourth applicant, who attempted to alert the driver of the police vehicle to stop, suggesting a distinction between his conduct and that of his co-accused. This brought into question whether the applicants adequately fulfilled the requirements of common purpose as articulated in previous judgments.

Mpati P emphasized the necessity for the State to prove all prerequisites of liability beyond a reasonable doubt and considered whether there was a reasonable prospect that a different court could overturn the murder convictions, thus warranting the applicants’ request for reconsideration ([paras 8-11]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 11-13])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that there was sufficient concern regarding the prior court’s application of the law, particularly the doctrine of common purpose and the various degrees of involvement exhibited by the applicants. The court determined that there were reasonable prospects for a different court's reconsideration of the murder convictions, leading to the decision to grant the applicants leave to appeal.

The implications of this ruling underscore the necessity for meticulous examination in cases involving complex interactions and multiple defendants, particularly in upholding the principles of justice and the protection of individual rights within the legal system. The case also highlights the evolving application of the common purpose doctrine in the context of police conduct and accountability for unlawful actions ([paras 11–13]).

This judgment thus serves as a pivotal moment in South African jurisprudence, reinforcing the standards for evidence and intent required for serious criminal convictions under the doctrine of common purpose. It illustrates the court's commitment to ensuring fair trials and the correct application of legal principles, particularly in high-stakes cases involving state agents.
