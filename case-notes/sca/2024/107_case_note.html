Phoenix Salt Industries (Pty) Ltd v The Lubavitch Foundation of Southern Africa (330/2023) [2024] ZASCA 107

Golden Hands Property Holdings (Pty) Ltd (hereafter "Golden Hands") was involved as a co-principal debtor and surety alongside Lubavitch under the same loan agreement ([2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 107

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The present case arises from an appeal against a decision by the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg, which dismissed Phoenix Salt Industries (Pty) Ltd's (hereafter "Phoenix Salt") application for the recovery of R2,886,005.20 plus interest and costs from The Lubavitch Foundation of Southern Africa (hereafter "Lubavitch") ([1]–[2]). The origins of the claim date back to a loan agreement executed on August 12, 1994, which mandated Phoenix Salt to settle a mortgage indebtedness of R5.2 million incurred by Lubavitch to Nedbank ([2]–[4]).

Golden Hands Property Holdings (Pty) Ltd (hereafter "Golden Hands") was involved as a co-principal debtor and surety alongside Lubavitch under the same loan agreement ([2]). Both the loan and a subsequent sale agreement that provided for the sale of properties in exchange for the loan amount were meant to facilitate a property development intended to aid Lubavitch’s financial recovery ([5]–[6]).

The primary contention in this case revolves around Clause 9 of the loan agreement, which stipulated non-variation clauses concerning any amendments, cancellations, or waivers of obligations ([7]). On July 25, 2017, Phoenix Salt demanded repayment of the outstanding loan balance, prompting the subsequent litigation ([8]).

Both parties presented differing accounts regarding the nature of the agreement and whether the subsequent conduct constituted a waiver of the repayment obligation. Rabbi Menachem Lipskar, representing Lubavitch, asserted that assurances from the Krok Brothers (representatives of Phoenix Salt) indicated a waiver of rights to enforce repayment against Lubavitch, thereby suggesting a change in the obligations set out under the agreement ([10]).

I – Issues

The primary issues before the court were:

  1. Whether Phoenix Salt, through the Krok Brothers, waived their right to recover the remaining loan amount from Lubavitch, and
  2. If such a waiver was valid despite the existence of non-variation clauses in the loan agreement ([15]).

The court had to consider whether a waiver could be established through the conduct of the parties, specifically in light of the non-variation clauses which explicitly required that any amendments or cancellations be in writing ([13]–[14]).

R – Rules

The court primarily relied on the following legal principles:

  1. Waiver is recognized as a voluntary abandonment of an existing right, which can occur either explicitly or through conduct that demonstrates an intention to relinquish that right ([15]).
  2. Non-variation clauses, which typically serve to preserve the integrity of contracts by requiring amendments to be documented in writing, do not universally preclude waiver, particularly when such waivers do not constitute variations to the contract itself ([16]–[19]).
  3. The Plascon-Evans rule, which dictates that in motion proceedings the version of the respondent must be accepted where it is credible and supported by admissible facts, guided the court's examination of the differing accounts provided by the parties ([14]).
  4. The context surrounding contractual agreements, including the relationship between the contracting parties, is relevant to the interpretation of the agreement ([24]–[26]).

A – Application

Applying these rules, the court evaluated the evidence presented by both parties. The Krok Brothers’ actions suggested that they had not actively pursued the recovery of the loan amount since at least 2003, indicating a possible waiver of the right to claim repayment ([11]). The court emphasized that the non-variation clauses, while enforceable, did not specifically govern the abandonment of contractual rights.

In analyzing the specific wording of Clause 9, the court noted that the non-variation clauses referred explicitly to modifications or cancellations of the contract's terms but did not address the potential for waiver. Thus, the court concluded that a waiver could validly occur without breaching these clauses ([17]–[19]).

Furthermore, the relationship between Phoenix Salt and Lubavitch was examined. The Krok Brothers had acted as benefactors to Lubavitch, and their assurances that the loan would be repaid through Golden Hands pointed towards a deliberate abandonment of efforts to enforce repayment directly from Lubavitch ([21]).

C – Conclusion

The court dismissed the appeal, confirming the high court’s finding that Phoenix Salt had indeed waived its right to enforce repayment of the loan against Lubavitch. The determination highlighted that the waiver did not contravene the non-variation clauses of the contract because it did not alter the terms of the agreement itself but represented a relinquishment of rights. The implications of this ruling reinforce the principle that while parties can establish strict guidelines for variations to agreements, the potential for waiver remains a distinct and legally recognized option.

As per its order, the appeal was dismissed with costs, reinforcing the prevailing legal stance on waivers and non-variation clauses within contractual agreements ([28]).

This judgment reiterates the importance of examining both the written terms of contractual obligations and the surrounding context to ascertain the intentions and resultant conduct of the parties involved. It illustrates the delicate balance courts must maintain between the sanctity of written contracts and the realities of relational dynamics that often influence contractual adherence.
