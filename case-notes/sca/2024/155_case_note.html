Nkomazi Local Municipality v The Valuation Appeal Board for the District of Ehlanzeni & Others (615/2023) [2024] ZASCA 155

Initially, the property was valued at R33,236,726 in 2008, a value that notably increased to R1,064,880,000 after several objections ([paras 6–8]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 155

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The Nkomazi Local Municipality (the Municipality) is situated in Mpumalanga and is authorized under section 2 of the Local Government: Municipal Property Rates Act 6 of 2004 (MPRA) to levy property rates within its jurisdiction ([para 2]). The Valuation Appeal Board (VAB), constituted under Mpumalanga Provincial Notice 148 of 2018, oversees valuation appeals ([para 2]). Leopard Creek Share Block Limited (Leopard Creek), the third respondent, owns a large property consisting of residential and recreational areas, detailed as „Portion 20 of the Farm Riverside 173, JU” ([para 4]).

Initially, the property was valued at R33,236,726 in 2008, a value that notably increased to R1,064,880,000 after several objections ([paras 6–8]). In a subsequent appeal that commenced in July 2019, testimony was provided by several expert witnesses regarding the property’s valuation. However, the VAB dismissed the appeal without adequately evaluating the evidence provided by Leopard Creek's experts and failed to substantiate its preference for the Municipality's valuation ([paras 11, 15]).

Leopard Creek challenged the VAB's decision in the Mpumalanga Division of the High Court, arguing the lack of reasons for the VAB’s decision constituted bias and irrationality ([para 18]). The high court agreed and set aside the VAB's decision, leading the Municipality to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 20]).

I – Issues

The key legal questions raised in this appeal included:

  1. Did the VAB properly assess and evaluate the evidence of the expert witnesses?
  2. Did the VAB provide sufficient reasons for dismissing the evidence of Leopard Creek’s expert witnesses?
  3. Could the Supreme Court of Appeal substitute its judgment for the VAB's evaluation of evidence?

([para 20]).

R – Rules

The legal analysis in this case was premised on administrative law principles, especially those concerning the rationality of administrative decisions and the obligation to give reasons as mandated under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA). The precedent set in Carephone (Pty) Ltd v Marcus NO outlined that a decision-maker must possess a rational basis connecting the evidence to their conclusion ([para 22]).

According to sections 45(1) and 46 of the MPRA, the VAB is required to determine property value based on market conditions—specifically, what the property would realistically fetch between a willing seller and a willing buyer ([para 17]). The court highlighted the VAB's failure to comply with these statutory obligations robbed Leopard Creek of its right to a fair process in administrative decision-making ([para 25]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal evaluated whether the VAB performed its duty by properly assessing evidence and providing rationale for its decision. It recognized that an administrative body must engage with expert opinions deeply when determining substantial matters, like market valuation ([para 23]). The court found that the VAB not only failed to evaluate the evidence but also did not substantiate its preference for the Municipality's expert, resulting in a decision that was arbitrary and unreasonable ([para 26]).

The court further stressed that merely being informed about the context does not absolve the VAB from its duty to provide reasoned decisions, affirming that each case must be assessed on its merits regardless of general knowledge of proceedings ([para 24]).

The court noted, however, that while the high court had grounds for its decision, it overstepped by determining which expert method should prevail—something which was rather the VAB’s responsibility ([para 31]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the Municipality’s appeal, agreeing that the high court rightly set aside the VAB’s decision on the grounds of irrationality due to non-compliance with procedural fairness outlined in the MPRA. The court emphasized the importance of transparency in decision-making, particularly in valuing property for rates ([para 32]).

This case serves as a pivotal reminder that administrative bodies must thoroughly evaluate all submitted evidence while providing sufficient reasoning for the conclusions they reach, ensuring that any decision is made within the bounds of the law, thereby fostering confidence in administrative justice ([para 28]).

Implications

This ruling reinforces the significance of adequate justification in administrative decisions and underscores the obligation of tribunals like the VAB to provide clear reasoning for their decisions as part of maintaining fairness in administrative processes. It establishes a precedent for future cases involving administrative reviews, where failures to engage critically with evidence could result in the invalidation of decisions.

This ruling also clarifies the boundaries of judicial intervention in administrative matters, affirming that although courts can review the manner in which decisions are made, they cannot substitute their judgments for those of the administrative bodies unless a legal error is demonstrably present.

The standard for reviewing administrative actions, and the requirement that agencies must articulate their decision-making processes, remains a cornerstone of upholding fair administrative practices in South Africa.
