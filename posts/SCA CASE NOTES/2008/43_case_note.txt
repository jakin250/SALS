Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2008

# FIRAC Case Note: Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development v Moleko (131/07) [2008] ZASCA 43

## F - Facts
The respondent, Sekele Michael Moleko, a magistrate in Engcobo, instituted a claim for damages in the Transkei High Court against three appellants: the Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development, the Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP), and the Minister of Safety and Security, alleging malicious prosecution ([paras 1-2]). 

On January 16, 2002, Moleko presided over a bail application involving two accused charged with armed robbery and hijacking, who had been remanded in custody since September 2001. Notably, one of the accused had been hospitalized, and the other was present in court. Despite the DPP's office informing Moleko of the serious nature of the charges under Schedule 6 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 and the previous denial of bail, Moleko postponed the hearing and ordered their release on warning without leading any evidence ([para 3-5]). Consequently, the State sought a review of Moleko's decision, leading to the subsequent prosecution against him for defeating the course of justice ([paras 6-7]).

After being acquitted, Moleko sued the appellants for malicious prosecution. The trial court found in favor of Moleko, stating that he had proven malicious prosecution, which resulted in damages ([para 2]).

## I - Issues
1. **Malicious Prosecution**: Did the three appellants instigate or support the malicious prosecution of Moleko?
2. **Reasonable Cause**: Did the appellants have reasonable and probable cause to initiate the prosecution against Moleko?
3. **Malice**: Did the actions of the DPP exhibit malice or animus injuriandi regarding Moleko's prosecution?
4. **Judicial Independence**: What are the implications of prosecuting a judicial officer for actions taken in the course of their duties, particularly concerning the independence of the judiciary?

## R - Rules
To prove a claim for malicious prosecution, a plaintiff must establish: 
(a) The defendants set the law in motion (instigated the proceedings);
(b) The defendants acted without reasonable and probable cause;
(c) The defendants acted with malice (animus injuriandi);
(d) The prosecution has failed ([para 8]).

Regarding judicial officers, Section 17 of the Transkei Penal Code protects them from criminal liability for acts or omissions in good faith in their judicial functions ([para 6]). The DPP must evaluate the reasonable grounds for prosecution, which includes a subjective honest belief in the defendant’s guilt, supported by reasonable evidence ([para 20]).

The test for malice is whether the prosecutor acted with an intention to injure or with knowledge that the prosecution was baseless ([para 61]).

## A - Application
### Instigation of Proceedings ([paras 8-11])
The trial judge found that the state actors were intertwined in instigating the prosecution; however, the appellants argued that the DPP initiated the prosecution based solely on police recommendations. This was a key area of contention, particularly about the role of the police in gathering evidence versus the DPP's decision-making authority. The court suggested the police's role did not automatically render them liable.

### Absence of Reasonable and Probable Cause ([paras 19-25])
The DPP, represented by Ms Neveling, testified that material evidence in the form of affidavits and Moleko's own warning statement led her to believe a crime had occurred. However, upon examining the evidence at hand, the court concluded that Ms Neveling failed to exercise prudence. Her decision was based on inaccurate interpretations of Moleko's actions and statements made in his defense. She did not adequately consider the context in which Moleko made his decisions, including his concerns regarding the health of the accused and the pressure presented to him by the public prosecutor to expedite proceedings ([paras 34-46]).

### Evidence of Malice ([paras 61-65])
While the DPP believed she had reasonable grounds for prosecution, the court emphasized her neglect to investigate key discrepancies in the evidence, which indicated a lack of due diligence. This demonstrated an inherent recklessness regarding the potential impact of the prosecution on Moleko. The court ultimately found that her actions constituted animus injuriandi because they were directed toward Moleko’s potential harm without reasonable basis ([paras 66-67]).

### Judicial Independence ([paras 52-56])
The court grappled with the implications of prosecuting a judicial officer. It underscored that judicial independence is crucial but reiterated that judges are not entirely immune from prosecution when acting outside their judicial mandate. The prosecution of Moleko posed a critical intersection between maintaining judicial independence and ensuring accountability for judicial misconduct ([para 54]).

## C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the appeals by the first and third appellants succeeded while the appeal by the DPP was dismissed. The court ordered the DPP to pay costs, emphasizing that although Moleko's decision was perhaps irregular, it was not made with malicious intent. The court found that reasonable grounds for prosecution were lacking and that the claim for malicious prosecution had been substantiated. The ruling also highlighted the necessity for caution when prosecuting judicial officers, reaffirming the delicate balance between judicial accountability and independence ([para 67]).

The implications of this judgment emphasize the need for prosecutorial discretion to be exercised with a comprehensive understanding of the judiciary's role and the significance of reasonable cause, especially when it involves figures within the judicial system. The court's ruling may serve as a precedent in cases involving the accountability of judicial officers and the appropriate boundaries of prosecutorial authority in relation to judicial conduct.
