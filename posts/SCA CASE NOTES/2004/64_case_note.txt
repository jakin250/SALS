Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2004

# Case Note: Price Waterhouse Coopers Inc v National Potato Co-operative Ltd 

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([1]–[37])

This appeal in the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa centers on a dispute regarding the validity of a champertous agreement between the National Potato Co-operative Ltd ("the Co-operative") and a third party, Farmers Indemnity Fund (Pty) Ltd ("FIF"), which was invoked as a defense by the appellants, Price Waterhouse Coopers Inc and others ("Price Waterhouse"). 

The Co-operative, registered under the Co-operatives Act, No 91 of 1981, initially hired Collett, Du Toit & Associates (Pty) Ltd ("CDA") to investigate alleged irregularities committed by its former general manager. Following CDA's draft report in late 1997, which indicated that significant irregularities occurred, Price Waterhouse resigned as the Co-operative's auditor at the annual general meeting. In March 1998, investigations shifted focus to a potential lawsuit against Price Waterhouse, which necessitated financing due to cost constraints.

Consequently, on 17 April 1998, the Co-operative agreed to sell its right to sue Price Waterhouse to FIF for a 50% share of any successful claim proceeds. FIF was established shortly prior, being a shelf company of which a lawyer associated with the Co-operative was the beneficiary. As financing became essential, the Co-operative later entered an "assistance agreement" with FIF, which included provisions for FIF to fund the litigation in return for 45% of the claim's proceeds.

In November 1999, the Co-operative filed an action against Price Waterhouse, claiming damages for breach of contract amounting initially to R283,490,742.19, later adjusted to R353,890,045.72. During the trial in 2002, Price Waterhouse raised the legality of the financing arrangements as a defense, claiming that the arrangement constituted a champertous agreement contrary to public policy. The trial court dismissed these arguments.

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([20]–[22])

The primary issue before the court concerned whether the financing arrangement made between the Co-operative and FIF constituted a champertous agreement that was contrary to public policy and whether this could serve as a valid defense against the Co-operative's claim. The analysis encompassed three key questions:

1. What constitutes public policy concerning financial support in litigation from a third party?
2. Is the agreement between FIF and the Co-operative, where FIF funds the litigation in exchange for a percentage of the proceeds, contrary to public policy?
3. If the agreement is deemed champertous and contrary to public policy, does this illegality provide a defense against the Co-operative's claim?

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([23]–[52])

The ruling drew upon various precedents regarding champerty and maintenance within South African law, acknowledging that, traditionally, such agreements were considered void as they encouraged speculative litigation. The court referred to foundational cases such as *Sasfin (Pty) Ltd v Beukes* 1989 (1) SA 1 (A), which highlighted that agreements contravening public policy should be void if they manifest impropriety or public harm.

The constitutional framework laid out in Section 34 of the South African Constitution, guaranteeing the right to access the courts for legal resolution, was also taken into account. Post-1994 developments in case law indicated a shifting perspective on public policy in relation to financial assistance for litigation—which had become increasingly permissive, as seen in the enactment of the Contingency Fees Act 66 of 1997, legitimizing contingency fee agreements with regulatory controls.

The judgment drew parallels with evolving English law, emphasizing that inherent fears surrounding abuses associated with champertous arrangements may have diminished due to strengthened judicial oversight and ethical practices within the legal profession.

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([46]–[52])

The court applied the principles laid out in its analysis, concluding that the alleged champertous agreement between the Co-operative and FIF should not be viewed generally as invalid or contrary to public policy. The judgment noted that the Co-operative had employed due diligence in seeking financing and that the arrangements did not exhibit characteristics that would encourage frivolous or vexatious litigation.

Furthermore, it was determined that the illegality of the financing agreement, even if it were found to exist, did not inherently provide a defense against the Co-operative’s claim. The ruling drew on established legal principles that any association of illegality between the litigant and a third-party financer should not preclude the litigant's access to justice, emphasizing that the right to a fair hearing is paramount.

The court reiterated that any litigation relating to claims arising from such financing agreements should focus on the bona fides of the claim itself, underscoring the need for courts to separate illegality of financial arrangements from the legitimacy of the actual claim presented.

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([53]–[64])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Price Waterhouse's appeal, solidifying that the agreement between the Co-operative and FIF did not contravene public policy. Therefore, it ruled that champerty, as alleged by Price Waterhouse, could not serve as a valid defense against the Co-operative’s claim. 

The court's decision emphasized the evolving nature of public policy in relation to litigation financing, highlighting the importance of access to justice and reinforcing the constitutional protection provided under Section 34. The decision further reflects a significant shift towards recognizing financial support arrangements for legal actions, ensuring that such arrangements, when conducted in good faith, facilitate rather than undermine the administration of justice.

The holding reinforces the principle that the merits of the claim must be focused upon, devoid of distractions regarding the financing mechanisms employed, and sets a precedent for future cases involving similar financial arrangements in the context of litigation. This case could significantly impact how third-party litigation financing is perceived under South African law, fostering a more supportive legal environment for litigants reliant on external financial assistance.
