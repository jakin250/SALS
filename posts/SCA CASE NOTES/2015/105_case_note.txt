# Case Note: **Dr F Kluever v Michiel Jacobus De Goede** (20198/2014) [2015] ZASCA 105

## FIRAC Method Analysis

### F – Facts
The case involves Michiel de Goede, a promising young rugby player who suffered a severe injury during a match on 5 April 2007, resulting in a ruptured patella tendon ([1]–[5]). Initially misdiagnosed by Dr Khwitshana as a sprained knee, the severity of the injury was only identified five days later when Dr Thiart, an orthopaedic surgeon, diagnosed the rupture ([1]).

On 13 April 2007, Dr Felicia Kluever, the first appellant, performed surgery to repair the damaged tendon. Post-surgery, Michiel's recovery was plagued by complications, and concerns regarding his rehabilitation were raised but not adequately addressed by the medical staff ([4]–[5]; [12]–[13]). Following further assessments by physiotherapists, it was discovered that the patella position was incorrect—specifically, a high riding patella was identified by Dr de Vlieg, leading to a second corrective surgery in September 2008 ([6]–[4]).

Consequently, Michiel brought a suit against the appellants, claiming negligence on the part of Drs Kluever and Bhawani, who performed the surgeries. The third appellant, the Minister of Defence, was held vicariously liable since both doctors were employees of the SANDF during their respective actions ([5]) and [6].

### I – Issues
The primary issues addressed by the court were:
1. Whether Dr Kluever and Dr Bhawani exhibited negligence during the surgical procedures performed on Michiel.
2. Whether Michiel contributed to his injury through his actions during rehabilitation ([8]–[9]; [35]).

### R – Rules
The court relied on established legal principles concerning medical negligence, notably:
- **Mitchell v Dixon**: A medical practitioner is obliged to exercise reasonable skill and care, and is liable for any negligent actions ([23]).
- **Van Wyk v Lewis**: Reasonable care is assessed against the standard of skill and diligence prevalent within the medical profession at the time ([24]).
- **Whitehouse v Jordan**: Not all errors in judgment qualify as negligence; it depends on whether the error falls within the capabilities of a competent professional ([25]).

Expert testimony indicated that the failure to adhere to accepted medical standards and practices during surgery constituted negligence. The principles regarding the engagement of expert witness testimony were highlighted in **Louwrens v Oldwage** and **Medi-Clinic v Vermeulen**, wherein the logical foundation underpinning expert opinions was deemed crucial for determining negligence ([26]–[28]).

### A – Application
The court performed a thorough analysis of the facts against the standard of care expected from medical professionals. Several lapses were identified in Dr Kluever's methodology, particularly:
- Reliance on an inaccurate diagnosis without full examination of Michiel's condition or any imaging studies prior to the surgery ([12]–[30]).
- Inappropriate measurement and placement of the patella during surgery, relying on a method deemed insufficient by expert witnesses, which culminated in the high riding patella ([9], [30]).

Despite the appellants arguing that the repairs were undertaken in accordance with professional standards, the judge found that evidence overwhelmingly supported Michiel’s claim of negligence by both Kluever and Bhawani. The failure to rectify recognized complications post-surgery also demonstrated negligence ([40]).

In regard to contributory negligence, the appellants posited that Michiel's activities damaged the initial repair ([36]–[39]). This argument was dismissed by the court, which found no supporting evidence for such claims, particularly given Michiel's lack of mobility immediately post-surgery and the nature of activities undertaken during rehabilitation ([38]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court's findings on both negligence and the absence of contributory negligence by Michiel. The evidence showed that Michiel’s disability was caused primarily by negligent surgical practices and subsequent failures to identify and address post-operative complications. Thus, Kluever and Bhawani were found liable, with the Minister of Defence held vicariously responsible for their actions ([40]).

This ruling reinforces the legal expectations for medical practitioners to adhere to established standards of skill and care, especially when managing sports-related injuries—a critical area given the potential for long-term impacts on athletes' careers. The case exemplifies the legal principles guiding medical negligence claims in South Africa, reiterating that medical treatment must not only be executed with care but also include continuous monitoring and adequacy in response to complications ([5], [6]).
