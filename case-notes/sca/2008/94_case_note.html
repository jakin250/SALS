Masstores (Pty) Ltd v Murray & Roberts Construction (Pty) Ltd (573/2007) [2008] ZASCA 94

The core of the claim revolved around multiple alleged breaches of duty by Murray & Roberts, including failure to adhere to legal and safety regulations, and general negligence in executing the contracted work.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2008 [2008] ZASCA 94

FIRAC Method Case Note

F – Facts ([paras 1-4])

The appellant, Masstores (Pty) Ltd, a wholesaler, engaged the first respondent, Murray & Roberts Construction (Pty) Ltd, for extension and alteration works at its store located in Struben’s Valley, Roodepoort. A fire incident occurred during the execution of the work, allegedly due to the negligence of employees of a subcontractor, which resulted in the destruction of the warehouse and its contents. Consequently, Masstores pursued a breach of contract claim against Murray & Roberts, seeking damages amounting to R169,365,175 for the loss incurred.

The core of the claim revolved around multiple alleged breaches of duty by Murray & Roberts, including failure to adhere to legal and safety regulations, and general negligence in executing the contracted work. In response, Murray & Roberts filed an exception to Masstores’ particulars of claim, asserting that their liability was precluded by clause 9.2.7 of the building contract, which exempted them from liability for physical loss or damage to existing structures for which they were engaged to undertake alterations or additions.

I – Issues ([paras 5-6])

The pivotal legal question in this appeal was whether clause 9.2.7 of the contractual indemnity provisions effectively exempted Murray & Roberts from liability for negligent and grossly negligent breaches of the building contract. This required a careful interpretation of the contractual language and the surrounding context to determine if the clause was ambiguous and, if so, how it should be construed.

R – Rules ([paras 6-10])

The interpretation of the contractual provisions rested upon established contract law principles, which require that the words of a contract be construed in their ordinary meaning, considering the overall context of the contract. Notably, a relevant principle observed in prior case law is that exemption clauses must be read narrowly and cannot exclude liability for negligence unless clearly stated (South African Railways & Harbours v Lyle Shipping Co Ltd 3 SA 416 (A)).

In this case, the court referenced earlier decisions that highlighted the necessity of extra caution when interpreting clauses that form exceptions to general liability under contracts. The court stressed that ambiguity in an exemption clause could impact its enforceability.

A – Application ([paras 11-29])

The court examined the specific wording of clause 9.2.7, which stated that the employer (Masstores) indemnified the contractor (Murray & Roberts), thereby raising questions about the exclusion of liability. Masstores contended that the clause did not preclude claims based on negligence. Furthermore, Masstores raised four key ambiguities within this clause, particularly concerning the terms "indemnify and hold harmless", the juxtaposition of clauses 9.1.2 and 9.2.7, and the implications of differing phrases regarding loss.

The court found that the indemnity language was not limited to third-party claims. Rather, it included claims arising from direct actions, including those relating to the damage caused by the contractor. This interpretation was reinforced by the absence of specific references to negligence as an excluded ground for liability in a manner analogous to relevant case precedents (First National Bank of Southern Africa Ltd v Rosenblum 4 SA 189 (SCA)).

Upon analyzing the broader structure and context of the contract, the court determined that clause 9.2.7 was not intended to exempt Murray & Roberts from liability for negligent conduct. The rational distribution of risks in the contract indicated that the contractor had responsibility for the works up until practical completion, but retained no liability for pre-existing structures.

Furthermore, the court noted the significance of the lack of any provision in clause 9.2.7 that explicitly exempted negligent actions, contrasting it with other clauses that allowed for such an exemption. The court concluded that the parties’ intention, as reflected in the contractual framework, did not support a claim of indemnity against the contractor for negligent damage to the existing structure.

C – Conclusion ([paras 30-31])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the decision of the High Court, affirming that clause 9.2.7 did indeed preclude any action by Masstores against Murray & Roberts for negligent or grossly negligent conduct leading to the destruction of the warehouse and its contents. The appeal was dismissed with costs, including the arrangements concerning two counsel.

This decision emphasizes the critical importance of precise language in contractual agreements and delineates the interpretative boundaries within which courts must operate concerning clauses that seek to limit liability for negligence. The affirmation of the High Court’s ruling reinforces the legal principle that exemption clauses cannot be construed to remove all potential liability unless expressly and unambiguously articulated. The ruling thus serves as a significant precedent in the interpretation of liability clauses within construction contracts in South Africa.
