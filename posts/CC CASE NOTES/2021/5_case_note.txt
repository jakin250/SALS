# FIRAC Case Note: Freedom Under Law NPC v Minister of Social Development and Others [2021] ZACC 5

## Facts
[1] This case emerged following a previous ruling on 17 March 2017, granting *Freedom Under Law (FUL)* the right to intervene in an application initially brought by the *Black Sash Trust*. The original respondents included high-ranking officials and bodies of South Africa's social welfare system, including the *Minister of Social Development* and *Cash Paymaster Services (Pty) Limited (CPS)*.

[2] The case traces its roots to *Allpay II*, where a tender awarded to CPS by the *South African Social Security Agency (SASSA)* was declared invalid, but the effects were suspended for 12 months, with a directive for SASSA to take over the payment of social grants. SASSA's initial assurance to manage grant payments from April 2017 proved to be unfounded, leading to delays in payments to millions of vulnerable citizens.

[3] After the assurance was revealed to be false, further applications by *Black Sash Trust* and FUL sought urgent judicial intervention to prevent the imminent disruption of social grants. On 17 March 2017, the court extended the suspension and mandated strict requirements for SASSA and CPS to ensure ongoing compliance with the regulations for social grant payments.

[6] On 30 November 2019, FUL raised concerns about compliance with the court's previous order, citing access issues for auditors appointed by SASSA to CPS’s financial records. Allegedly, CPS had under-reported its profits significantly, necessitating a legal bid to secure full disclosure of financial records to enable accurate verification of profits made under the unlawful contract.

[9] FUL's application sought various forms of compliance from CPS, demanding access to financial documents, timely reporting to the National Treasury, and determination of all profits derived from the controversial contract. The intention behind FUL's motion was to ensure that sufficient oversight was conducted around social grants, ensuring accountability and protection of beneficiaries' rights.

## Issues
[8] The primary legal issues raised in this case pertained to:
1. Whether CPS was obligated to furnish RAIN and SASSA with all necessary documentation for audit verification.
2. The extent of judicial authority to compel ongoing compliance with an earlier court order regarding the management of social grants and financial transparency.
3. The constitutional implications regarding the right to social security, and the duty of government entities to adhere to compliance standards laid out by the court.

## Rules
[5] Key legal principles and precedents considered in the judgment included:
1. **Right to Social Security:** Under section 27 of the *Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996*, the State is mandated to take reasonable legislative and other measures to achieve the progressive realization of the right to social security.
2. **Judicial Review:** The judiciary retains the capacity to supervise the compliance of public entities with court orders, particularly when these orders involve the rights of vulnerable populations.
3. **Accountability and Transparency:** Statutes like the *Social Assistance Act 13 of 2004* impose strict obligations on government entities to protect personal data and ensure compliance with conditions regarding the spending of public funds.

## Application
[13] The Constitutional Court highlighted non-compliance by CPS in response to previous rulings, particularly regarding the provision of documentation necessary for the audit verification. The court underscored that the financial oversight demanded was critical in determining the legitimacy of payments made and profits accrued under an invalid contract, potentially amounting to significant wrongful gains.

[14] The Court addressed the concerns for transparency and accountability, asserting that the provision of financial documents was not just a bureaucratic formality but a necessity fundamentally tied to the protection of grant beneficiaries. The failure of CPS to cooperate hindered the audit process and called into question the proper handling of social grants.

[15] Given the categories of relief sought by FUL, the court determined that since CPS's non-compliance remained unchallenged by the other parties, the portions of the proposal calling for immediate compliance with the provision of documents (as outlined in paragraphs 1 to 7 of FUL’s application) were granted, emphasizing the necessity of completing the verification process.

## Conclusion
[15] The Constitutional Court ultimately ordered CPS and related parties to adhere to the stipulated timelines for document submission and audit verification, demonstrating the judiciary's commitment to protecting the rights of social grant beneficiaries. The court deferred the more contentious issues regarding the determination and repayment of profits to a later date, pending the completion of the necessary financial evaluations, thus ensuring a thorough evaluation of claims regarding CPS's unlawful profits.

This judgment accentuates the underlying constitutional imperatives guiding the South African social security framework, the judiciary’s role in enforcing accountability, and safeguarding the welfare of the vulnerable populace. The ruling thus sets important precedents addressing compliance with judicial orders in the context of public interest and the effective delivery of social services.
