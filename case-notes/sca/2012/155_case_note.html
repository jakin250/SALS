Supreme Court of Appeal

Sheryl Cwele & another v The State (671/11) [2012] ZASCA 155

The prosecution's case revolved around the recruitment of a third individual, Tessa Beetge, whom the appellants allegedly induced to travel to South America to collect and transport cocaine back to South Africa.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2012 [2012] ZASCA 155

F – Facts:

The case at hand involves two appellants, Sheryl Cwele and Frank Nabolisa, charged with drug trafficking under South African law. The appellants were specifically accused of contravening section 5(b) of the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 140 of 1992, along with alternative charges involving conspiracy related to drug trafficking ([paras 1–2]).

The prosecution's case revolved around the recruitment of a third individual, Tessa Beetge, whom the appellants allegedly induced to travel to South America to collect and transport cocaine back to South Africa. The evidence against the appellants included testimonies from Tessa’s mother, as well as communications exchanged between Sheryl and Tessa, implicating Sheryl in the recruitment process and in facilitating the arrangement ([paras 3–10]).

Tessa was arrested on June 13, 2008, in Brazil, found in possession of 9.25 kg and 1.025 kg of cocaine, drawing the attention of South African authorities ([paras 6–7]). Communication records established a timeline in which Sheryl had communicated to Tessa that her work involved collecting items for Frank, further asserting the nature of their arrangement ([para 21]). Ultimately, the trial court convicted both appellants, imposing a sentence of 12 years’ imprisonment, which was appealed by both Sheryl and Frank ([para 2]).

I – Issues:

  1. Conviction Validity: Did the trial court err in convicting the appellants based on the circumstantial evidence of their involvement in the drug trafficking operation?
  2. Sentence Amount: Was the 12-year sentence imposed on the appellants appropriate considering the seriousness of the offence and the aggravating factors involved in drug trafficking?
  3. Admissibility of Evidence: Was the trial court correct in excluding certain intercepted communications that might have substantiated the prosecution's case further?

R – Rules:

  1. Criminal Law Principles: The standard of proof in criminal cases requires that the State must establish the guilt of the accused beyond a reasonable doubt. This includes reliance on circumstantial evidence where direct evidence is not available ([para 19]).
  2. Sentencing Guidelines: Under the minimum sentence legislation, particularly section 51(2) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, a minimum sentence of 15 years applies unless substantial and compelling circumstances warrant deviation ([paras 27–28]).
  3. Admissibility of Evidence: The Electronic Communications and Transactions Act, 25 of 2002 governs the admissibility of electronic communications in court ([para 12]).

A – Application:

The court evaluated the case by considering circumstantial evidence as a whole, confirming that the facts presented pointed to a reasonable inference that Sheryl and Frank were aware of and participated in the drug trafficking scheme ([para 20]). The court noted that Sheryl's communications with Tessa regarding the supposed "work" in Turkey were highly suspect given that Sheryl had assured Tessa that she was to bring back a package for Frank, which she did not deny despite the lack of explicit detail about its contents ([para 22]).

The absence of both appellants’ testimonies allowed for an inference of guilt based on the circumstantial evidence, particularly given Sheryl's role in recruiting Tessa, which the court found to demonstrate a significant degree of involvement in the drug trafficking operation ([para 25]).

As for sentencing, the court determined that the gravity of the crime warranted an increase in the original sentence of 12 years to 20 years, noting that the initial trial court underestimated the impact of drug trafficking on society. The court considered the high street value of cocaine involved in the case and the significant role the appellants played in its smuggling operation ([para 36]).

C – Conclusion:

The Supreme Court of Appeal, therefore, dismissed the appeals against the convictions of both appellants, upholding the trial court’s findings that there was sufficient circumstantial evidence to support their involvement in drug trafficking. Additionally, the court increased the sentences for both appellants from 12 years to 20 years’ imprisonment, concluding that the severity of the crime justified the harsh penalty, reflecting society's need for protection from the deleterious effects of drug trafficking ([para 37]). The ruling emphasizes the courts' commitment to impose stringent sanctions against those involved in drug trafficking, balancing the need for deterrence with individual circumstances in sentencing.
