Supreme Court of Appeal

Republic of South Africa in the Supreme Court of Appeal

On September 21, 1994, Lungile Lennox Ntamo (the deceased) was killed by members of the South African Police Service (SAPS) in Tsomo, Transkei.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2002

F – Facts ([paras 1–12])

On September 21, 1994, Lungile Lennox Ntamo (the deceased) was killed by members of the South African Police Service (SAPS) in Tsomo, Transkei. Following this incident, his spouse, Nosisekho Joyce Ntamo, along with their six minor children, filed a lawsuit against the Minister of Safety and Security (the appellant), who represented the police officers involved, seeking damages for loss of support.

The trial was held in the Transkei High Court, where the parties agreed to separate issues of liability and quantum as per Rule 33(4) of the Uniform Rules of Court. The High Court found in favor of the respondents on the merits regarding liability. Subsequently, the appellant, having admitted that his employees shot the deceased, contended that the shooting was justified as an act of self-defense and public safety. His argument hinged on establishing that the officers were acting within their lawful duties during the encounter with the deceased.

The deceased had been a passenger on a bus en route to Umtata when an altercation occurred with other passengers. Armed with a handgun, he brandished it at one passenger and subsequently engaged the police when they arrived on the scene. Despite the police claim that the deceased had discharged his firearm, inconsistencies in eyewitness accounts and the physical evidence presented raised questions about the justification of the officers' lethal actions.

I – Issues ([paras 3–12])

The primary legal issue before the court was whether the shooting of the deceased by the police officers was justified. Specifically, the court needed to determine:

  1. Was the use of lethal force by the police warranted under the circumstances?
  2. Did the appellant successfully meet the onus of proving that the officers were in imminent danger that justified their actions?

These issues involved a comprehensive examination of the facts surrounding the encounter, the credibility of witness testimonies, and the legal standards regarding justification in the use of lethal force by law enforcement.

R – Rules ([paras 3, 7, 12])

In adjudicating this case, the court focused on several pertinent legal principles:

  1. Onus of Proof: The appellant bore the burden of proving that the fatal shooting was justified, following precedents such as Mabaso v Felix 1981 (3) SA 865 (A) and Ferreira v Ntshingila 1990 (4) SA 271 (A).
  2. Justification in Use of Force: The legality of police officers discharging their firearms is predicated on the necessity of the action for self-defense or the protection of the public. The court referenced the need for clear evidence that the officers were under threat that warranted such extreme measures.
  3. Credibility of Evidence: The court highlighted the importance of consistent and credible accounts from witnesses, especially when a life is taken. The presence of contradictions in the testimonies of the police officers significantly influenced the court's decision.

A – Application ([paras 7–12])

The court systematically analyzed the evidence presented at trial, focusing on the dynamics of the confrontation between the police and the deceased. Although the appellant asserted that the police acted to protect themselves and the public, several key findings emerged:

  1. Conflicting Testimonies: The testimonies from the police officers appeared inconsistent regarding the number of shots fired, the sequence of events, and whether the deceased had indeed fired his weapon at the time of the shooting. This ambiguity raised reasonable doubt about the officers' perception of threat.
  2. Physical Evidence: Forensic findings indicated discrepancies in the number of cartridges recovered and the narrative provided by the police witnesses. The existence of only a singular cartridge from the deceased's firearm raised additional questions regarding the legitimacy of the police's claims of having acted in self-defense.
  3. Absence of Public Threat: There was no substantive evidence that innocent bystanders were in imminent danger from the deceased at the time the officers discharged their firearms. The court noted this absence as a critical factor in determining the unlawfulness of the shooting.

Given these evaluations, it became clear to the court that the appellant had not met the onus of proving that the use of lethal force was justified, thus concluding that the police action constituted an unlawful killing.

C – Conclusion ([paras 12])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming the lower court's findings and holding that the police's shooting of the deceased was unjustified. The court's ruling highlighted the significant legal principles concerning police conduct and the justification for the use of lethal force. The case underscores the paramount importance of credible evidence and the protection of citizens against unlawful state actions.

This decision serves as a clear precedent regarding police accountability and the necessity of strict adherence to legal standards when force is utilized. The implications extend beyond the immediate parties involved, reinforcing principles of justice and the rights of individuals against state violence in the exercise of law enforcement duties.
