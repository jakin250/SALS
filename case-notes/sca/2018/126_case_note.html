Supreme Court of Appeal

Sentinel Retirement Fund v Masoanganye (1003/2017) [2018] ZASCA 126

The case involves an appeal from the Gauteng Division of the High Court regarding the entitlement of a pension benefit under the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956 ("PFA") following a restraint order under the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 ("POCA").

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 126

F – Facts

The case involves an appeal from the Gauteng Division of the High Court regarding the entitlement of a pension benefit under the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956 ("PFA") following a restraint order under the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 ("POCA"). The first appellant, Sentinel Retirement Fund, is a pension fund, and the second appellant, Gary Meyer, is its legal manager. The first respondent, Richard Masoanganye, was appointed as curator bonis by the National Director of Public Prosecutions ("NDPP"), who had previously prosecuted Linda Martin Ndebele (the second respondent) for racketeering and other criminal charges, resulting in a conviction.

Ndebele, a member of the pension fund, became entitled to a lump sum withdrawal benefit after terminating his services with a prior employer. The NDPP obtained a provisional restraint order against Ndebele, which prohibited him from dealing with any "realisable property." Masoanganye, as curator bonis, sought to attach Ndebele's pension benefit, which the Fund contended was protected under section 37A(1) of the PFA from any form of attachment or execution ([1]–[6]).

The high court ruled in favour of the curator and NDPP, leading Sentinel to appeal on the grounds that the pension benefit could not be subjected to execution under a restraint order ([2]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue in the appeal revolves around whether the payment of Ndebele’s pension benefit can be ordered to a curator bonis pursuant to a restraint order under POCA, in light of the protections afforded to pension benefits under section 37A(1) of the PFA ([1], [8]). Specifically, does a restraint order constitute an "attachment" or "execution" within the meaning of section 37A(1) of the PFA?

R – Rules

The legislative framework for this case includes two pivotal statutes:

  1. Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956 (PFA): Particularly, section 37A(1), which states that pension benefits are protected from attachment or execution under a court order, except as permitted by the Act itself. This section establishes that benefits are only reducible under the Income Tax Act and the Maintenance Act, and under specific provisions of the PFA ([8]).
  2. Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (POCA): This Act allows for restraint orders to be issued to prevent an accused from dealing with their property pending legal actions, which may lead to confiscation. Section 26 of POCA pertains to the restraint of property identified as "realisable" ([9], [11]).

Additionally, prior case law such as Van Heerden & another v National Director of Public Prosecutions & another has established that restraint orders can be viewed as forms of execution within the meaning of section 37A(1) ([12]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal applied a comprehensive interpretation of section 37A(1) of the PFA, emphasizing the protective nature of pension benefits, which underpins social security policy. The Court thoroughly analyzed whether the restraint order issued against Ndebele's assets, including his pension benefit, could constitute an attachment.

The Court found that restraint orders prevent the defendant from dealing with property, thus amounting to an attachment as specified in the PFA. The order cannot simply be brushed aside as it operates to protect the property until a confiscation order is confirmed. Therefore, the protection afforded by section 37A(1) applies, as the restraint order constitutes a form of execution under a court order ([10]–[11]).

Further, the distinction between the property being owned by the Fund and the member’s right to the benefit was emphasized. Benefits held in the Fund do not legally belong to the member until disbursal, which means they cannot be considered "realisable property" that can be restrained by the NDPP ([14]).

Critically, the Court disagreed with the high court's conclusion that Ndebele lost any rights to the benefit upon it becoming payable. The Supreme Court's analysis established that the benefit would remain an asset of the Fund until disbursed to the designated account controlled by the curator ([15]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal of the Fund regarding the payment of pension benefits to Ndebele directly, affirming that such payments could be directed to the curator bonis instead. The Court ruled that a payment to a curator does not divest the defendant of ownership; it simply involves the curator managing the funds in accordance with the law and the terms of the restraint order. Therefore, the prior ruling by the high court was upheld. The appeal was dismissed, but with the court relieved from paying costs in light of the significant legal issues raised ([21]–[22]).

This case signifies critical interpretations of pension fund protections and the balance of rights under restraint orders, potentially influencing similar future cases in South African law. The rulings emphasize that pension benefits are not mere assets to be treated lightly under restraint conditions, aligning with the broader objectives of social security policy.
