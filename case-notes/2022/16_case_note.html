Constitutional Court 2022 [2022] ZACC 16

F – Facts

The present case stems from an application concerning the right of Fidelity Security Services (Pty) Limited (Fidelity) to apply for new possession licences for firearms, following the lapse of their previous licences. Fidelity, a significant security service provider in South Africa, is responsible for the possession of over 8,500 firearms through its employees ([1]–[2]).

Fidelity had nominated Mr. SG Yssel as the responsible person under section 7 of the Firearms Control Act 60 of 2000 (the Act). Following Yssel's departure, the new responsible person, Mr. JC Wentzel, discovered that around 700 possession licences for firearms had not been renewed timely. These licences subsequently lapsed by operation of law in accordance with section 28 of the Act ([3]–[5]).

In April 2016, Fidelity attempted to renew the expired licences over the cut-off period outlined by the Act, leading to refusal by the police. Fidelity's subsequent High Court application sought various remedies, including an order to compel the police to accept late renewal applications or new applications for firearms. The High Court denied the application, relying on the precedent set in Minister of Safety and Security v South African Hunters and Game Conservation Association (SA Hunters), which it interpreted as precluding the possibility of new applications following the lapse of a licence ([6]–[8]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), however, reversed the High Court’s decision. It asserted that the Act did not expressly forbid new applications following the expiration of a licence and that the Commissioner had a duty to process such applications ([9]–[11]).

I – Issues

The principal legal issue revolves around whether a gun owner whose possession licence has expired is entitled to submit a new application for a possession licence under the Firearms Control Act whether the expiration constituted a permanent loss of the right to apply anew ([8]).

Also raised were broader constitutional implications regarding the interpretation of the Act and the statutory duties of public officials, with significant potential effects on firearm regulation and ownership rights ([13]).

R – Rules

The Firearms Control Act 60 of 2000 specifically governs the licensing and possession of firearms. Notable provisions include:

  • Section 3: Prohibits the possession of a firearm without a valid licence.
  • Section 24: Details the process and timing required for renewal of firearm licences, mandating an application at least 90 days before expiry.
  • Section 28: States conditions under which a licence may terminate, including failure to renew.
  • Sections 13 to 20: Outline different forms of possession licences and the authorities' responsibility to issue them, emphasizing the criteria for application ([27]–[35]).

Precedential cases, particularly SA Hunters, provided guidance on interpreting these provisions, notably emphasizing the obligatory nature of licence possession and the consequences for noncompliance ([36]).

A – Application

The Court concluded that, upon examining the Act's language, there is no express prohibition against a firearm owner applying for a new possession licence following the lapse of a previous licence. The interpretation should be guided by principles of statutory interpretation, acknowledging the importance of legislative intent without imposing restrictions that are not present in the Act ([54]).

The Court also argued that allowing new applications contributes positively to the balance of firearm ownership regulation while protecting the owners' property rights under section 25 of the South African Constitution. It acknowledged distinctions between possession and ownership, noting that ownership does not cease merely when a licence expires, thus allowing for the potential recovery of possession through lawful avenues such as new applications ([38]–[57]).

Ultimately, the Court highlighted that the Registrar retains discretion to assess any new applications and that the interpretations employed by the SCA and affirmed by the Constitutional Court align with both statutory provisions and underlying constitutional principles ([67]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court unanimously dismissed the State parties' appeal, holding that firearm owners are permitted to apply for new possession licences even after their previous licences have terminated. The Court reiterated that its findings align with the aims of the Firearms Control Act while acknowledging the rights of firearm owners as encapsulated in the Constitution ([71]).

The judgment underscores the necessity of clear legislative guidelines in firearm possession and regulation, and asserts the important balance between public safety, the lawful enjoyment of property rights, and the State's regulatory functions. As a result of the dismissal, the applicants were ordered to pay Fidelity’s legal costs, reflecting the principles enshrined in Biowatch Trust v Registrar Genetic Resources concerning the allocation of costs when governmental entities are involved ([69]–[70]).
