Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note: Van Huyssteen N.O. and Another v Milla Investment and Holding Company (Pty) Ltd

## F – Facts

The case revolves around a dispute concerning a lease agreement for premises at the Cape Gate Lifestyle Centre in Brackenfell, Cape Town, where Tekkie Town conducted its business ([para 1]). The first and second appellants, Abraham Johannes van Huyssteen and Marius Roelof van Huyssteen, are trustees of the Sport City Trust, which had taken over the operation of Tekkie Town. The respondent, Milla Investment and Holding Company (Milla), claimed unpaid rental from the Trust, alleging the rental amount due was based on a different arrangement than what the Trust contended ([paras 1–2]).

Originally, Mr. Van Huyssteen entered into a written lease agreement with Cape Gate, the original owner of the shopping centre, in August 2005 ([para 4]). This agreement allowed for a rental escalation of 9% per annum, among other conditions ([para 5]). In May 2006, Cape Gate sent an offer for a new lease agreement encompassing larger premises, leading to the creation of an amended offer that included changes made by Mr. Van Huyssteen ([paras 6–7]).

However, this amended offer was never countersigned by Cape Gate's representative, Mr. Odendaal, preventing its formal acceptance. Nevertheless, the Trust gained beneficial occupation on 3 July 2006, and business operations began on 1 August 2006, with invoices based on the terms of the original lease being issued until October 2007, when a dispute arose regarding rental payments. The Trust ultimately asserted an oral agreement predicated on the original lease terms, which Milla disputed, asserting the terms of the amended offer governed the leasing situation ([paras 9–11]).

The high court found in favor of Milla, rejecting the Trust's claim based on an alleged oral agreement and applying the doctrine of quasi-mutual assent to conclude there was a tacit acceptance of the amended offer. The court ordered the Trust to make rental payments amounting to R1,283,328.49, which prompted the current appeal ([paras 1, 13]). 

## I – Issues 

The central issue before the court revolved around the application of the doctrine of quasi-mutual assent, particularly whether the high court correctly concluded that a binding lease agreement existed based on the amended offer despite its lack of acceptance by Cape Gate ([para 15]). More specifically, the legal questions included:
1. Did the high court properly apply the doctrine of quasi-mutual assent in finding that the amended offer constituted a binding lease agreement?
2. Was there a sufficient basis for the appellants to assert an oral agreement consistent with the original lease terms, and did the high court err in not recognizing this?

## R – Rules 

The court relied primarily on the law of contract, particularly the doctrine of quasi-mutual assent, which asserts that a party may be bound by the terms of an agreement even if it has not explicitly consented to those terms, provided the conduct of both parties suggests an agreement was reached. This doctrine is elaborated upon through the "battle of the forms" approach, as discussed in various case law, including *Ideal Fastener Corporation CC v Book Vision (Pty) Ltd* [2001] and *Sonap Petroleum (SA) (Pty) Ltd v Pappadogianis* [1992] ([paras 19–22]).

Moreover, the court discussed earlier cases that established the standard of proof necessary for a party claiming the existence of a contract, noting that it is generally incumbent upon the party asserting the contract to prove its existence ([para 26]). 

## A – Application 

The application of these legal principles involved detailed reliance on the facts surrounding the exchange of communication between the parties, particularly the overlooked amended offer. The court analyzed whether the actions taken by Cape Gate and the Trust following the amended offer indicated a meeting of minds regarding the terms of the lease ([paras 15–19]). 

Key evidence included the nature of the rental payments made and the understanding of both parties regarding the tenancy. The court scrutinized the credibility of witnesses, particularly focusing on Mr. Van Huyssteen's testimony and the circumstances that led to the Trust operating under the assumption that a binding lease was in effect ([paras 13, 14]). 

Ultimately, while the high court found the conduct of Milla's representative, Mr. Odendaal, indicated tacit acceptance of the amended lease terms, this finding was challenged on the grounds that it unjustifiably inverted the contractual position between the parties ([paras 25–26]). The court emphasized that for quasi-mutual assent to apply, the conduct of the party denying the contract must have misled the other party into a reasonable belief that an agreement existed. The Trust maintained that no such understanding was reached, and the essential terms differed significantly from what Milla sought to enforce. 

## C – Conclusion 

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal, setting aside the high court's judgment in favor of Milla. It found that the high court had misapplied the doctrine of quasi-mutual assent and that Milla failed to satisfy its burden to prove the existence of a binding contract based on the amended offer ([paras 1, 27]). 

The implications of this ruling emphasize the necessity for clear acceptance in contractual agreements, particularly concerning modified terms. The case illustrates the importance of ensuring that all parties involved have a tangible understanding and agreement on the terms underpinning their relationship, serving as a cautionary tale for both landlords and tenants alike regarding the complexity of lease negotiations under South African law.
