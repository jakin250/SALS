Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# Case Note: Minister of Safety and Security & another v Swart (194/11) [2012] ZASCA 16

## F – Facts
The events leading to the present case occurred in the early hours of 27 May 2007, when the respondent, Johannesburg-based police officer Johann François Swart, was arrested without a warrant by Constable Jonathan Daniëls (the second appellant) under suspicion of driving under the influence of intoxicating liquor. The police had received a report of an accident in the Hex Pass area and, while checking the scene, encountered the respondent, who requested their assistance with his vehicle that had gone off the road ([paras 1–4]). Despite the respondent's assertions that he was not driving when the accident occurred and that the engine of the vehicle was not running, Daniëls attempted to arrest Swart, who resisted the attempt ([para 4]). Subsequently, other officers arrived to assist in subduing the respondent, leading to his eventual arrest on the basis that he was suspected to have been driving under the influence of alcohol ([para 5]).

After the arrest, a blood sample was taken from the respondent, revealing a blood alcohol level of 0.04 grams per 100ml, which is below the legal limit ([paras 2, 5]). The charges against him were subsequently withdrawn. Following his detention, the respondent brought a civil action against the appellants in the Magistrates’ Court in Worcester for damages, claiming the arrest and detention were unlawful. His initial claim was dismissed, but an appeal to the Western Cape High Court resulted in an award of R50,000 in damages ([para 2]). The appellants sought to appeal this decision ([paras 2-5]).

## I – Issues
The case presents several key legal issues: 
1. Whether the arresting officer (Daniëls) had reasonable grounds to suspect that Swart was driving under the influence of intoxicating liquor, thereby justifying an arrest without a warrant under s 40(1)(b) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([paras 11, 16]).
2. If the arrest was deemed unlawful, whether the High Court's awarding of damages was appropriate in terms of quantum ([paras 24–25]).

## R – Rules
The legal framework governing the arrest without a warrant is articulated in section 40(1)(b) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which stipulates that a peace officer may arrest without a warrant anyone whom they reasonably suspect of committing an offence listed in Schedule 1 of the Act ([para 16]). To establish the lawfulness of an arrest under this provision, it is essential that:
1. The officer must be a peace officer.
2. The officer must possess a suspicion of the commission of an offence.
3. That suspicion must be grounded in reasonable facts ([para 17]).

The case of *Duncan v Minister of Law & Order* (1986) and *Minister of Safety & Security v Sekhoto & another* (2011) illustrates the necessity for a reasonable basis for suspicion, where the reasonableness of the officer's belief is assessed objectively ([para 19]). Additionally, the onus rests on the arresting officer to justify the legality of the arrest, as articulated in *Minister of Law & Order & others v Hurley & another* (1986) ([paras 19-20]).

## A – Application
The court scrutinized the beliefs held by the second appellant at the time of the arrest. It was noted that the suspicions raised by Daniëls were based solely on the fact that Swart smelled of alcohol and that his vehicle had gone off the road ([paras 21-22]). The court emphasized that such a determination did not present sufficient grounds for a reasonable suspicion of intoxication. Notably, there was a lack of evidence indicating that Swart displayed traditional physical signs of being under the influence, such as slurred speech, unsteadiness, or bloodshot eyes ([paras 21-23]).

The court held that further inquiries were warranted by the arresting officers, given the circumstances surrounding the incident, such as the dark and dangerous road conditions, that could account for the vehicle veering off the road without the element of drunkenness. The conduct of the respondent—running towards the vehicle and requesting assistance in a coherent manner—further indicated that he was not impaired at the time ([para 22]) .

Consequently, the court concluded that the suspicion held by the second appellant did not meet the requirement of being based on reasonable grounds, rendering the arrest unlawful ([para 23]).

In examining the quantum of damages, the court considered the aggravating factors, particularly the respondent's position as a senior police officer and the humiliation suffered due to the manner of arrest and subsequent taunting in custody by other officers ([paras 24-25]). The original award of R50,000 was upheld.

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal by the appellants, affirming the High Court's decision that the arrest of the respondent was unlawful and was not supported by reasonable suspicion founded on the required legal standards ([para 25]). The court elaborated on the implications for police conduct, reiterating that the mere odor of alcohol is insufficient to substantiate an arrest for driving under the influence, particularly when other circumstances could plausibly explain the situation. This judgment serves as a significant reminder of the protections against unlawful arrests and the need for police to adhere to reasonable standards before exercising their arrest powers under the law. The implications extend beyond individual cases, emphasizing police accountability and the necessity of a thorough investigation prior to making an arrest.
