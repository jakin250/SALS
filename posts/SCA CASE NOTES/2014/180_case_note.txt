# Case Note: PMG Motors v Firstrand Bank (866/2013) [2014] ZASCA 180

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 2–5])

The case concerns the appeal of PMG Motors Kyalami (Pty) Ltd and PMG Motors Westville (Pty) Ltd (both in liquidation) against an order granted by the South Gauteng High Court, Johannesburg in favor of Firstrand Bank Ltd, Wesbank Division. Wesbank sought confirmation of the cancellation of floor plan agreements and repayment of three sums of money under the condictio indebiti following the liquidation of the two dealerships ([paras 2–5]).

Wesbank entered into floor plan agreements with the dealerships for the financing of vehicles, retaining ownership until full payment was made. After canceling the agreements on January 22, 2009, Wesbank collected the vehicles from the dealerships with their consent ([paras 2–4]). Upon liquidation, Wesbank made payments to the liquidators, which it later contended were made under a mistake of law, asserting the amounts were not owed ([paras 5]). Subsequently, Wesbank initiated an application under the condictio indebiti to recover those payments, leading to the first-instance court ruling in its favor, which included finding that the agreements were effectively canceled before liquidation commenced ([paras 5, 6]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1, 6, 16])

The appeal raised five key issues:

1. **Jurisdiction**: Whether the High Court had jurisdiction to entertain the application due to the liquidation of the dealerships.
  
2. **Section 84(2) of the Insolvency Act**: Whether this section applied to the claim, specifically regarding the return of property and payments made regarding canceled agreements.
  
3. **Factual Disputes**: Whether there were material disputes of fact that warranted a referral to oral evidence or dismissal of the application.
  
4. **Condictio Indebiti Claim**: Whether the claim ought to have been initiated as an action instead of application proceedings given the need for adjustments concerning the amounts paid.
  
5. **Section 111 Objection**: Whether Wesbank's objection to the liquidators' account under Section 111 of the Insolvency Act precluded it from seeking relief before this objection was resolved ([para 1]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 7–29])

Key legal principles and sections relevant to the case include:

- **Insolvency Act 24 of 1936**: Particularly S84(2), which involves the return of property delivered under a transaction deemed an instalment agreement and stipulates conditions regarding payments made for returned property under such agreements.

- **Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959**: Defines jurisdiction in relation to a court's ability to hear matters based on where entities reside or operate ([para 8]).

- **Common Law Principles**: The condictio indebiti, which allows for the recovery of payments made under a mistake, was central to Wesbank's claim ([para 6]).

The court also considered previous case law regarding corporate residence (e.g., **Steytler NO v Fitzgerald**; **Bisonboard Ltd v K Braun Woodworking Machinery**), emphasizing that a company's principal place of management determines jurisdiction and the implications of liquidation on corporate entities ([paras 8–11]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 7–33])

1. **Jurisdiction** ([paras 6–15]): The court affirmed that jurisdiction was established based on the residence of the dealerships prior to liquidation. It was determined that, despite the liquidation, the principles governing the 'residence' of a company remained intact, allowing the High Court to proceed with the matter.

2. **Application of Section 84(2)** ([paras 24–29]): The court held that Section 84(2) did not apply as the agreements were cancelled prior to the liquidation. Hence, there was no extant agreement under which the claim could be made, leading to the conclusion that the payments made were indeed made indebite.

3. **Factual Disputes** ([paras 16–23]): The court found that any perceived dispute regarding the delivery of cancellation letters was not substantial enough to warrant a referral to oral evidence. It confirmed that the agreements were effectively canceled before liquidation commenced, aligning with Wesbank's claims.

4. **Proceedings Type** ([paras 30]): The court concluded that since Section 84(2) did not apply, the argument that the claim should have been brought as an action rather than by application was irrelevant.

5. **Section 111 Objection** ([paras 31–32]): The court stated that the objection to the account did not prevent Wesbank from pursuing its claim via the condictio indebiti. The claim did not hinge on the outcome of the objection, allowing Wesbank to seek recovery without delay.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 33])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, confirming that the High Court had the necessary jurisdiction, that Section 84(2) of the Insolvency Act did not apply to claims arising from canceled agreements prior to liquidation, that there were no substantive factual disputes meriting oral evidence, and that the proceedings followed were appropriate.

The ruling reinforces the interpretations of jurisdiction in cases relating to companies in liquidation, clarifies how the condictio indebiti operates post-liquidation, and validates the authority of courts to adjudicate matters relating to corporate entities even when under liquidation. The decision also emphasizes the necessity for clear, substantial affirmations of fact in contesting applications, especially when concerning the timing of actions and agreements in liquidations ([para 31]). 

This crucial case demonstrates the complexities of insolvency law, specifically the interplay between corporate agreements, jurisdictional authority, and statutory provisions. The implications could influence future practices regarding claims made in the context of post-liquidation proceedings for similar financial claims in South Africa.
