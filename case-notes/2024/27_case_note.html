Charnell Commando and Others v City of Cape Town and Another [2024] ZACC 27

The case at hand revolves around the eviction of a group of residents from properties located on B[…] Street in Woodstock, Cape Town, following the purchase of the property by Woodstock Hub, a property development company.

Constitutional Court 2024 [2024] ZACC 27

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–12])

The case at hand revolves around the eviction of a group of residents from properties located on B[…] Street in Woodstock, Cape Town, following the purchase of the property by Woodstock Hub, a property development company. The residents, collectively referred to as the B[...] residents, had occupied the property under various lease agreements for many years, some tracing back to generational leases from their grandparents' era. The sale of the property for development aimed at creating high-rental units ignited concerns about gentrification and spatial apartheid in these historically marginalized inner-city areas.

Despite engagements between the B[...] residents and the City of Cape Town regarding temporary accommodation options following the eviction ordered by the High Court, the City confirmed it had no obligation to provide emergency accommodation within the inner city. The High Court ultimately ruled in favor of the B[...] residents, declaring the City's housing program unconstitutional for its failure to adequately plan for emergency housing needs amid gentrification pressures. This decision was contested by the City, leading to an appeal at the Supreme Court of Appeal, which narrowed the focus to the reasonableness of the City’s housing measures.

The Constitutional Court heard fresh arguments from both parties concerning the City’s obligations under section 26 of the Constitution, which encapsulates the right to housing, culminating in a judgment that underscored the interdependence of housing rights and the legacy of apartheid spatial policies ([paras 1–11]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 12–15])

The case presents several significant legal issues, including:

  1. Whether the City of Cape Town's constitutional duty to provide temporary emergency housing extends to specific locations, particularly in relation to the entrenched community ties of the B[...] residents.
  2. The reasonableness of the City’s decision not to prioritize emergency accommodations in the inner city amidst the ongoing gentrification.
  3. The delineation of the government’s responsibilities and the extent of judicial scrutiny permissible over municipal decisions concerning housing program implementations ([paras 12–15]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 16–37])

  1. Constitutional Provisions: Section 26(1) guarantees the right to access adequate housing. Section 26(2) obligates the state to take reasonable legislative and other measures to achieve the progressive realization of this right. Section 26(3) protects against arbitrary eviction.
  2. Case Law: The judgment relies heavily on precedent from Government of the Republic of South Africa v Grootboom [2000] ZACC 19; City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality v Blue Moonlight Properties 39 (Pty) Ltd [ZACC 33]; and Occupiers of 51 Olivia Road [2008] ZACC 1, which affirm the necessity of providing adequate emergency housing and show how municipal programs must be evaluated against the constitutional standard of reasonableness and necessity.
  3. International Standards: The court also weighs international instruments such as the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (ICESCR) and its General Comments, which underscore the importance of adequate housing in relation to community ties and social services ([paras 16–37]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 38–95])

The Constitutional Court scrutinized the City’s approach to emergency housing, specifically its lack of a tailored policy to address the unique context of urban gentrification in Woodstock and Salt River. The Court found the City’s blanket refusal to provide emergency housing within the inner city to be unreasonable, given the historical and socio-economic significance of the area for the B[...] residents.

Drawing from the rationale in Grootboom, it asserted that while the City is not obligated to provide housing in a specific location, it must account for the realities that displacement imposes on the ability of residents to maintain connections to work, education, and community services. The judgment emphasized the need for the City to engage with the B[...] residents meaningfully, acknowledging their longstanding ties to the area and the detrimental effects of moving them significant distances away.

Further, the Court noted the City's failure to plan adequately for emergency housing options while favoring social housing development at the expense of immediate housing needs, reinforcing the interconnectedness of socio-economic rights and the need for affirmative action to mitigate spatial inequality exacerbated by gentrification ([paras 38–95]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 96–116])

The Constitutional Court held that the City of Cape Town's implementation of the National Housing Programme was unconstitutional for failing to adequately address the urgent housing needs arising from the gentrification pressures in Woodstock and Salt River. Key implications of the ruling include:

  1. The Court mandated the City to develop a reasonable Temporary Emergency Accommodation Policy consistent with its constitutional obligations within a specified timeframe.
  2. The judgment articulated the necessity for local governmental bodies to consider community contexts in housing policies, thus demonstrating judicial commitment to addressing historical injustices linked to spatial apartheid.

The ruling affirms the principle that the right to housing is not merely a policy concern but a constitutional imperative requiring active measures to safeguard the dignity and rights of vulnerable populations ([paras 96–116]).
