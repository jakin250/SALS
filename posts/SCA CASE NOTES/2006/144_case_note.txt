Case Note: REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2006

# Case Note: FIRAC Method for Republic of South Africa v Soomar [2006] SCA 172 (RSA)

## F – Facts

This case emerges from a complex series of events involving allegations of a conspiracy orchestrated by the appellants against the respondents. The first respondent, a business person in Durban, and the second respondent, a company involved in refining, bottling, and selling edible oils in Ciskei, instituted action in October 2001 against multiple appellants, including several companies and individuals, claiming damages of R1,476,000 from the first respondent and R46,633,000 from the second respondent ([2]–[3]).

The essence of the plaintiffs' case revolves around complaints that from June 1993 until April 2001, the defendants engaged in a conspiracy aimed at damaging the second plaintiff’s business. The allegations accused the defendants of various forms of misconduct, including falsely reporting to law enforcement and tax authorities, leading to unwarranted criminal charges and unjustified financial assessments against the second plaintiff. As a consequence of the alleged conspiracy, the second plaintiff claimed significant financial losses and reputational damage ([3]–[4]).

The appellants filed a special plea of prescription arguing that the claims were barred because all relevant events had occurred more than three years prior to the commencement of the action ([4]). The court a quo found against the appellants, determining that the claims had not prescribed due to the ongoing nature of the alleged conspiracy ([6]–[8]).

## I – Issues

The fundamental legal issue in this case was whether the claims brought by the respondents had prescribed under the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, particularly given the nature of the ongoing conspiracy alleged. Specific sub-issues included ([4]–[6]):
1. Whether the events giving rise to the claims were distinct or part of a continuing wrong.
2. The impact of the alleged conspiracy on the determination of when the claims became actionable for purposes of prescription.

## R – Rules

The court relied on the provisions of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, specifically Section 12(1) and Section 12(3), which state:
- A debt is deemed due once the creditor is aware of the identity of the debtor and the facts giving rise to the debt ([9]).
- Prescription for damages claims commences only when the debtor is known to the creditor and the facts arise ([10]).

Additionally, the judgment referenced principles from tort law, particularly those related to actionable conspiracy and malicious prosecution ([7]–[8]).

## A – Application

In applying the law to the case, the court considered the nature of the conspiracy alleged by the plaintiffs. The court stated that the claims could not be viewed in isolation; they were part of a continuing conspiracy that culminated in various significant events throughout the alleged period ([6]–[7]). The judge noted that the criminal proceedings and the civil actions taken by the appellants effectively masked the injuries suffered by the respondents, delaying the accrual of the claims until the conclusion of the conspiratorial actions ([8]).

The judge drew on English law principles regarding actionable conspiracy but ultimately concluded that the continuing nature of the wrongful acts negated the prescriptive period until the unlawful activities had ceased or had been adequately resolved ([11]–[12]). The court further found that the claims were only actionable once the criminal prosecution was concluded, thereby reinstating the prescriptive period and asserting that the appellants' pleas of prescription. However, the claim for malicious prosecution within the broader context of unlawful competition was also determined to be inextricably linked to the timing of the alleged wrongs ([20]–[24]).

The court dedicated significant analysis to differentiate between claims arising from continuing wrongful conduct and those arising from discrete, completed acts. It concluded that the claims for losses directly arising from the conspiracy did not prescribe as they were interwoven with ongoing actions by the appellants ([12]–[20]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that the pleas of prescription raised by the appellants should be upheld concerning the claims for loss of profits and reputational damage, based on the reasoning that the respondents had sufficient knowledge of the facts preceding the three-year prescription period. Consequently, the court reversed the findings of the court a quo and ruled in favor of the appellants ([30]–[31]).

The implications of this ruling extend to the interpretation of the prescriptive periods for conspiracy-related torts in South Africa, establishing a clearer boundary as to how the ongoing nature of conspiratorial conduct may influence the prescription of claims under South African law. The judgment serves to reinforce the importance of knowledge and the timing of claims in the context of complex legal disputes involving multiple parties and allegations of conspiracy.
