# Case Note: Ocean Echo Properties 327 CC v Old Mutual Life Assurance Company (South Africa) Limited (288/2017) [2018] ZASCA 09

## F – Facts  
The case revolves around two appellants, Ocean Echo Properties 327 CC ("Ocean Echo") and Mr. Angelo Giannaros, who faced a legal challenge from Old Mutual Life Assurance Company (South Africa) Limited ("Old Mutual"). The proceedings stemmed from a lease agreement dated 11 November 2008, which saw Old Mutual renting out commercial premises located at Shop 3 Cartwrights Corner, Cape Town, to Ocean Echo. Mr. Giannaros entered a deed of suretyship on 29 October 2008, making him responsible for Ocean Echo's obligations under the lease ([1]–[3]).

Old Mutual issued summons asserting that Ocean Echo was in arrears on rental payments. In response, the appellants contended that the lease was "tacitly terminated" when they vacated the premises in December 2011, claiming they had settled all due payments by that date ([3]). They further argued that Old Mutual had acknowledged a tacit lease with the subsequent occupant, Ms. Nandipha Solomon, which should absolve them from liability for any arrears ([3]).

Old Mutual filed an exception to the appellants' plea, arguing that the termination of the lease was ineffective due to specific clauses prohibiting alterations unless in writing ([4]). The initial ruling by Le Grange J upheld the exception, striking out the appellants' plea and granting judgment to Old Mutual ([5]–[6]).

## I – Issues  
The legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:
1. Whether the appellants' plea adequately disclosed a defense against Old Mutual's claims for arrears ([4]).
2. The validity of an implied or tacit termination of the lease agreement amidst strict clauses concerning modification and termination, particularly in light of Old Mutual's non-variation clause ([8]).
3. Whether the lower court erred by not allowing the appellants the opportunity to amend their plea, following the granting of the exception ([8]).

## R – Rules  
Key legal principles established in connection with this case included:
- The role of exceptions: An exception, as a procedural practice, disposes of a pleading without necessarily ending the action itself. The court is generally obliged to allow an opportunity for amendment unless there are compelling grounds not to do so ([8]).
- The burden of proof rests on the excipient (the party filing the exception) to demonstrate there is no possible interpretation of the plea that could disclose a valid defense ([9]).
- Legal precedent holds that oral agreements can effectuate cancellation of a contractual obligation without varying the contract's terms. The distinction between cancellation and modification is critical; a tacit agreement can operate in futurum, meaning it cancels future obligations while preserving past ones ([11], [15]).
- Non-variation clauses do not eliminate the possibility of tacit agreements, as existing contracts can be terminated or modified through mutual agreement, even if not formalized in writing ([14]).

## A – Application  
In the appeal, the Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized the previous judgments critically. The court noted that the lower court's ruling effectively deprived the appellants of a fair trial by striking out their plea without allowing for amendment. Ponnan JA emphasized the necessary balance between the contract's written requirements (particularly regarding consent) and the mere notion of a tacit agreement acknowledging the termination of obligations ([8], [10]).

The court also highlighted that the tacit termination plea, while elliptical, was still capable of supporting a valid defense if considered within the broader context of the parties' conduct, notably the acceptance of rent from a third party, Ms. Solomon ([8], [10]). It found that the plea, although poorly articulated, presented reasonable grounds for interpretation that could sustain a defense based on the alleged tacit termination ([10], [15]).

Furthermore, the exception proceedings required stringent scrutiny as Old Mutual was obliged to prove, on every plausible interpretation, that the appellants had no defense available. The court concluded that this burden was not met ([9], [10]).

## C – Conclusion  
The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the appeal by Ocean Echo and Giannaros should be upheld. The court determined that the exception filed by Old Mutual did not meet the necessary standards to strike the appellants' plea and that the lower court had erred in dismissing the defense without allowing it to be amended. The court resolved that a tacit agreement could sufficiently operate to terminate future obligations under the lease, thus preserving defenses related to past obligations.

The final order set aside the previous judgment and dismissed the exception with costs, allowing the appellants the opportunity to present their defense at trial ([17]). The implications of this ruling underscore the judicial preference for allowing parties the right to amend pleadings and the intricate balance between strict contract clauses and equitable principles allowing for tacit agreements.
