Mgwenya v Kruger (1060/16) [2017] ZASCA 102

Following the High Court's initial dismissal of his review application, Kruger successfully appealed to the Full Court, which set aside the disciplinary committee’s ruling ([paras 3-4]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 102

F - Facts

This case concerns an appeal from the decision of the North West Division of the High Court (the NWHC), wherein Vernon Xavier Kruger, the first respondent, challenged the fourth appellant, the Apostolic Faith Mission Church of South Africa (the Church), regarding his disciplinary hearing. Kruger, a pastor with the Church, was found guilty of various misconduct charges in 2012, which led to his immediate termination as a pastor effective from 23 January 2013 ([paras 2-3]).

Following the High Court's initial dismissal of his review application, Kruger successfully appealed to the Full Court, which set aside the disciplinary committee’s ruling ([paras 3-4]). However, before the appeal was heard at the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), Kruger died on 27 January 2017. Consequently, his son was substituted as the executor of his estate in the appeal ([paras 4-5]).

The primary contention in the appeal was whether it would have any practical effect, given the death of the first respondent, which led to the questioning of the appeal's merit and relevance based on Section 16(2)(a)(i) and (ii) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 ([para 1]).

I - Issues

The central legal issue before the SCA was whether the appeal could proceed given the circumstances of Kruger’s death, specifically considering whether a judgment on appeal would have any practical effect ([para 6]). Additionally, the appellants claimed that failing to hear the appeal could unfairly burden the Church with costs awarded in Kruger’s favor, raising the question of whether "exceptional circumstances" existed justifying consideration of costs under Section 16(2)(a)(ii) of the SC Act ([para 7]).

R - Rules

The relevant rules derive from Section 16 of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which states:

  1. Section 16(2)(a)(i): At the hearing of an appeal, if the issues are such that the decision sought will have no practical effect or result, the appeal may be dismissed on this ground alone.
  2. Section 16(2)(a)(ii): Exceptions to the first provision require that, in deciding whether a decision would not have practical effect, the court generally must not consider costs unless "exceptional circumstances" are presented ([para 5]).

The judgment also referred to previous case law defining "exceptional circumstances," identifying it as something out of the ordinary and rare, which deviates from the general rule ([para 8]).

A - Application

The SCA began its analysis by affirming that, following the death of Kruger, there were no longer live issues between the parties, rendering any potential decision moot. The appellants’ acknowledgment of this situation was pivotal ([para 6]). Furthermore, the SCA scrutinized the appellants’ argument regarding the implications of the costs orders made in favor of Kruger, ultimately concluding that no grounds existed to establish "exceptional circumstances" warranting a departure from the statutory framework ([para 10]).

The cases cited by the appellants, specifically MV Ais Mamas Seatrans Maritime v Owners, MV Ais Mamas & Another and Oudebaaskraal (Edms) Bpk en Andere v Jansen van Vuuren en Andere were found to be distinguishable on the facts, as they involved material evidence and extensive proceedings unlike the summarized procedural history in this case ([para 9]).

Moreover, the SCA maintained that the costs arguments presented by the appellants amounted to a plea for mercy without sufficient legal grounds, failing to demonstrate any truly exceptional circumstances ([para 11]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, backing its decision on the inadequacy of live issues, the failure to demonstrate exceptional circumstances under Section 16(2)(a)(ii), and the principle that the appellants’ pursuit of their appeal was ultimately of no consequence. Therefore, their obligation to bear the costs was affirmed, reflecting the court's discontent with their decision to continue the appeal despite evident mootness. The ruling thus reinforces the importance of ensuring that appeals have substantive legal consequences ([paras 11-12]).

This case underlines the significance of clarity regarding the procedural and substantive prerequisites for hearings at appellate levels, particularly where changes in the parties’ circumstances may impact the viability of a case.
