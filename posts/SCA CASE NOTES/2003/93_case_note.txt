# Case Note Using the FIRAC Method: The Minister of Justice v Firstrand Bank Ltd

**Case Title**: Minister of Justice v Firstrand Bank Limited  
**Citation**: [2003] ZASCA 45; (2003) 2 All SA 651 (A)  
**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Coram**: Howie P, Scott JA, Zulman JA, Brand JA, Cloete JA  
**Heard**: 1 September 2003  
**Delivered**: 25 September 2003  

---

## F - Facts ([1]–[3])

In this case, the Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development (the appellant) challenged a High Court decision that had set aside his directive to appoint a fifth provisional liquidator, Mr. E.M. Motala, to Retail Apparel Group Limited (the company). The first respondent, Firstrand Bank Limited, sought and obtained an order for the provisional liquidation of the debtor company due to its insolvency. Following this, the Master of the High Court appointed four provisional liquidators. The Minister intervened, citing section 371(3) of the Companies Act 61 of 1973, believing it empowered him to direct the Master's appointment of a provisional liquidator. The High Court subsequently reviewed this appointment, leading to the Minister's appeal.

## I - Issues ([4]–[4], [22]–[22])

The primary legal issue in this case revolved around the interpretation and application of section 371(3) of the Companies Act. Specifically, the court had to determine whether the section's provisions apply solely to liquidators as defined within the Act or if they also extend to provisional liquidators. The further question was whether the Minister’s directive and resulting appointment were legally valid.

## R - Rules ([8]–[12], [13]–[18])

The court evaluated several sections of the Companies Act:

- **Section 371(1)** stipulates that any person aggrieved by the appointment of a liquidator, or the refusal to accept a nomination can appeal to the Minister.
- **Section 368** outlines the Master’s authority to appoint provisional liquidators without the involvement of the nomination process required for final liquidators.
- **Section 364** details the nomination and appointment protocol for liquidators, emphasizing that only persons duly nominated can be appointed as liquidators.
- The Act does not extend the Minister's powers, as described in section 371, to provisional liquidators, which forms the fulcrum of the court's analysis.  

## A - Application ([22]–[25])

In applying the relevant sections, the court noted that the terms ‘liquidator’ and ‘provisional liquidator’ have distinct roles under the Companies Act. The court emphasized that section 371 specifically addresses grievances pertaining to the appointment of a liquidator, not provisional liquidators. The ruling highlighted that while provisional liquidators can be appointed in urgent scenarios, their appointment does not necessitate the same procedural safeguards as the appointment of a final liquidator.

The Minister's argument that an informal nomination process could suffice was rejected. The court underscored that the proper procedure must be followed for such appointments, invoking constitutional principles of administrative justice found in sections 33(1) and 39(2) of the Constitution, but clarifying that these do not override the explicit legislative framework governing liquidations.

## C - Conclusion ([25]–[26])

The court concluded that the Minister's directive to appoint a provisional liquidator was invalid because it did not align with the statutory requirements set forth in the Companies Act. It reaffirmed the importance of adhering to the nomination process and announced that section 371(3) could not apply to provisional liquidators as argued by the Minister.

Overall, this case emphasizes the need for strict compliance with the procedures established by the Companies Act concerning the appointment of liquidators. The appeal was dismissed with costs, affirming the lower court's ruling and clarifying the limits of the Minister's authority in the context of provisional liquidations.
