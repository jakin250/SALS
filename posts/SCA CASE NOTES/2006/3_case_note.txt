# FIRAC Case Note: Republic of South Africa - R Green and Another v The State [2006] SCA 3 (RSA)

## F – Facts

The case under consideration involves Robert Green and Bheki Mashaba, the appellants, who are charged with robbery with aggravating circumstances, specifically the use of firearms. The alleged robbery occurred on September 9, 2005, in Nelspruit, where the appellants are accused of stealing R7,276,150 from four victims ([1], [9]). Initially, the appellants applied for bail, but the regional court magistrate dismissed their application, asserting that they failed to substantiate the existence of exceptional circumstances that would warrant bail in the interests of justice ([2]).

The magistrate's decision was subsequently upheld by Bosielo J in the Pretoria High Court. The court articulated that there were no exceptional circumstances justifying their release ([3]). The substance of the State's case against the appellants included eyewitness accounts, the video evidence from closed-circuit television, and physical evidence such as fingerprints found on a getaway vehicle. Despite various challenges brought by the defense, including claims of coercion related to witness statements, the magistrate maintained that the evidence collected by the State indicated a strong prima facie case against the appellants ([18], [20]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues presented in this appeal are:

1. Whether the magistrate properly exercised discretion in refusing bail by not finding that exceptional circumstances existed to justify the release of the appellants ([3], [21]).
2. Did the magistrate err in dismissing the appellants’ requests for access to key evidentiary materials, such as the closed-circuit television footage and fingerprint reports, which could impact the determination of bail ([13], [22])?

## R – Rules

The court relied on several critical legal provisions outlined in the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (as amended). The relevant statutory rules include:

- **Section 60(1)**: Provides that an accused in custody is entitled to be released on bail unless the interests of justice dictate otherwise.
- **Section 60(3)**: Mandates that if the court lacks sufficient information to make a decision regarding bail, it must require that information to be presented.
- **Section 60(11)(a)**: States that in cases involving Schedule 6 offenses, such as robbery with firearms, a court shall refuse bail unless exceptional circumstances justify release ([4], [8]).
- The precedent set in **S v Dlamini**, which upheld the constitutionality of the provision requiring the establishment of exceptional circumstances for granting bail ([7]).

## A – Application

In reviewing the case, the SCA underscored that the magistrate's decision was predicated on the evidence presented by Superintendent Molapo, which had significant weaknesses exposed during cross-examination. The magistrate had noted that while certain aspects of Molapo's testimony appeared questionable, other evidence presented a strong case against the appellants ([20], [23]).

However, the SCA found that the magistrate acted prematurely in dismissing the bail application without first compelling the State to produce critical evidence, specifically the closed-circuit television footage that could clarify the appellants' involvement ([23]). The court emphasized that the duty of the presiding officer during bail hearings encompasses a more proactive role in investigating the evidence, which was lacking in the magistrate's proceedings ([23]).

The appellants argued that the State's failure to provide access to the video and fingerprint evidence constituted a violation of their rights, impacting the legitimacy of the court's decision regarding bail. The SCA concurred, ruling that the magistrate should have invoked section 60(3) to order the production of this essential evidence before making a determination on bail ([23], [24]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the magistrate's order dismissing bail could not stand due to an erroneous exercise of discretion. The court set aside the lower court's ruling and ordered that the State grant the defense access to the necessary evidence, thus allowing for a proper reassessment of the bail application ([25]). This ruling underscored the importance of robust evidentiary support in the judicial process and reaffirmed the role of the judiciary in balancing the rights of the accused with the interests of justice.

The implications of this judgment extend to the procedural rights of accused persons in South Africa, emphasizing the necessity for transparency and thoroughness in bail hearings, especially in serious offenses like robbery involving firearms. The verdict establishes a critical precedent concerning the obligations of the State in disclosing evidence in bail applications, aligning with constitutional protections under Section 35.
