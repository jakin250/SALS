Supreme Court of Appeal

N S v J N (506/2021) [2022] ZASCA 122

The case involves an appeal against a decision from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, regarding parental rights over a minor child, D, born on May 4, 2018.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 122

F – Facts

The case involves an appeal against a decision from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, regarding parental rights over a minor child, D, born on May 4, 2018. The appellant (father) and respondent (mother) were the unmarried biological parents, with the child residing with the mother in Malaysia since birth ([para 2]). A functional arrangement regarding parenting was in place until travel restrictions due to COVID-19 disrupted this arrangement in March 2020. The mother had visited South Africa with the child in March 2020, and upon her return, allowed the father to care for D until March 21, 2020. Due to the imposition of lockdowns, the father refused to return the child to Malaysia, which prompted various legal applications between the parties ([paras 2-4]).

In September 2020, the father sought an urgent order for an investigation into the child's best interests and aimed to retain primary care of the child ([para 4]). Conversely, the mother sought the child's return to Malaysia, resulting in an order from Judge Mosopa in October 2020, favoring the mother's application and mandating the return of the child ([para 5]).

Subsequent court proceedings found the father refusing to comply with orders, leading to further applications and counter-applications between the parties. The application ultimately arrived before Judge Lamprecht AJ, who on November 11, 2020, dismissed the father's applications and set aside prior orders by Sardiwalla J ([paras 6-9]).

The appellant sought leave to appeal this decision, citing ongoing disputes regarding the child's care and the need for a Family Advocate's report, even after the mother and child relocated to Malaysia ([paras 10-16]).

I – Issues

The central legal issue in this case revolves around whether the appeal should be heard given that the respondent and the child had already left South Africa, raising questions of mootness ([paras 11-19]). Specifically, the court considered:

  1. Whether the orders made by Lamprecht AJ were dispositive of the ongoing parental rights issues.
  2. If the appeal, due to a lack of practical effect, should be dismissed under Section 16(2)(a)(i) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013.
  3. The consideration of whether it is in the interests of justice for the court to hear an appeal despite the absence of a live controversy ([paras 13-15]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal rule is encapsulated in Section 16(2)(a)(i) of the Superior Courts Act, which permits a court to dismiss an appeal when the issues do not result in any practical effect or result ([para 13]). The courts acknowledge the futility of adjudicating matters that would yield no meaningful resolution and refer to prior jurisprudence emphasizing that appeals are meant to settle concrete disputes rather than addressing abstract questions ([paras 13-14]).

Additionally, the court referenced the leading cases of SA Metal Group v The International Trade Administration Commission and Normandien Farms v South African Agency for Promotion of Petroleum Exportation and Exploitation to affirm judicial efficiency and the need to avoid hypothetical adjudications ([paras 13, 14]).

A – Application

The court analyzed whether the appeal had been rendered moot by the relocation of the respondent and child to Malaysia. It concluded that any order made at this stage would hold no practical effect, as the father’s ability to compel the return of the child or to determine primary residency had effectively been nullified. The court noted that the appellant had acknowledged that the proceedings surrounding Part B could be re-enrolled at any time, even without the Family Advocate's report ([paras 17-19]).

The court underscored the principle that even if the appellant argued the case had merit, the absence of the child and mother rendered it academic. It emphasized that continuing down this path could lead to inefficient judicial resource allocation and a proliferation of piecemeal appeals ([paras 18-19]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal based on the principle outlined in Section 16(2)(a)(i) of the Superior Courts Act, confirming that the appeal posed no real issues that warranted court intervention ([para 22]). The court held that the appellant’s actions did not serve the best interests of the child and were deemed vexatious, leading to a punitive costs order against him for his refusal to comply with prior court orders and escalating legal disputes ([paras 20-21]).

Consequently, the appeal was dismissed with costs, including the costs of two counsel to be paid on an attorney-and-client scale, signaling the court's disapproval of the litigation tactics employed by the appellant ([para 22]). This case serves as a critical reminder of judicial economy and the necessity for appeals to address substantive live controversies.
