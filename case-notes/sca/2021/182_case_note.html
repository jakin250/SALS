Supreme Court of Appeal

City of Cape Town v The South African Human Rights Commission (Case no: 144/2021)

The appeal arises from an interim order granted by the Western Cape Division of the High Court, Cape Town, during a national state of disaster due to the COVID-19 pandemic.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 2002 ([para 1]). In July 2020

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–6])

The appeal arises from an interim order granted by the Western Cape Division of the High Court, Cape Town, during a national state of disaster due to the COVID-19 pandemic. This national state of disaster commenced with a "hard lockdown" from 26 March to 30 April 2020, during which evictions were severely curtailed under the Disaster Management Act 57 of 2002 ([para 1]).

In July 2020, a widely circulated video depicted Mr. Bulelani Qolani, a resident of an informal settlement, being forcibly removed from his dwelling by officials of the City of Cape Town. This incident prompted the South African Human Rights Commission (SAHRC) to file an urgent application, seeking an interdict against the City to prevent evictions without a court order during the national disaster ([para 2]).

The application included claims of interim relief (Part A) and a comprehensive review of the City's actions and constitutional validity of the Anti-Land Invasion Unit's (ALIU) operations (Part B). Additional intervening parties included the Economic Freedom Fighters (EFF) and the Housing Assembly, who sought further relief regarding confiscated building materials and compensation for personal possessions lost as a result of the evictions ([para 3]).

The High Court ruled in favor of the respondents, granting interim relief in August 2020, including prohibiting unlawful evictions and mandating judicial oversight ([para 4]). The City of Cape Town sought leave to appeal this order but was initially denied, leading to the present appeal with the leave of this Court ([paras 5-6]).

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 7–9])

The principal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal revolve around the appealability of the interim orders issued by the High Court. Specifically, the questions are:

  1. Do the interim orders granted by the High Court have a final effect, allowing for an appeal?
  2. Would permitting the appeal lead to irreparable harm or a grave injustice to the City of Cape Town?
  3. Are the claims for compensation final in nature, thus permitting judicial review at this stage?

The background of these issues emphasizes the judicial principle that appeals on interim orders are generally disallowed unless they meet particular criteria set out in previous case law, notably Zweni v Minister of Law and Order ([para 8]).

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 8–11])

The Court relies on established principles from South African jurisprudence regarding the appealability of interim orders, notably:

  1. Zweni v Minister of Law and Order ([1993] 1 SA 523 (A)): The criteria for appealability stipulate that decisions must be final, definitive of the rights of the parties, and dispose of a substantial portion of the relief sought ([para 8]).
  2. Cloete v S; Sekgala v Nedbank Limited ([2019] ZACC 6): Emphasizes the obligation of courts to avoid piecemeal adjudication and consider whether allowing the appeal would lead to wasted resources or prolonged litigation ([para 9]).
  3. National Treasury and Others v Opposition to Urban Tolling Alliance ([2012] ZACC 18): This case outlines the necessity for consideration of irreparable harm against longer-term judicial efficiency and justice ([para 10]).

The rules emphasize that for interim orders, appealability is rarely available unless there is significant injustice—specifically, the potential for irreparable harm if the appeal is not heard ([para 11]).

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 12–19])

The Court first evaluated whether the High Court’s orders possessed a final effect. It identified that the orders primarily addressed interim relief pending the resolution of Part B of the application, which would consider the constitutionality of the City’s actions concerning evictions ([para 12]).

The Court articulated that the interim orders were not final; rather, they would continue to be subject to review in the subsequent proceedings of Part B. Consequently, they did not fulfill the finality necessary to warrant an appeal ([paras 16-19]).

In assessing whether the City would suffer irreparable harm, the Court affirmed that oversight of eviction processes is a legal requirement, thus not constituting a basis for irreparable harm if the City is required to adhere to constitutional mandates while executing evictions ([para 16]). The Court noted that allowing the appeal could promote conflicting judgments, which is discouraged under the constitutional mandate for coherence and judicial efficiency ([para 17]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 20–34])

The Court concluded that the appeal must be dismissed concerning the first four paragraphs of the High Court’s formulation, affirming that none of the relief sought was final and that any potential harm or injustice was insufficient to merit the extraordinary remedy of an appeal ([para 34]).

However, the Court reversed the High Court's order regarding the return of building materials and compensation for personal possessions, determining that this was improperly categorized as interim when it had immediate and definitive effects on the affected parties ([para 34]). The Court underscored the necessity of establishing clear proof and justification for claims of confiscation before any compensation could be awarded ([paras 24-25]).

Ultimately, the ruling clarifies the boundaries regarding the appealability of interim orders in South African law while ensuring that the rights of individuals against unlawful eviction are also protected through the broader context of legal oversight and constitutional fidelity. Such rulings reflect ongoing tensions in balancing municipal governance and human rights, particularly in contexts of disaster and economic distress ([paras 32-34]).
