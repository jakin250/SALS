# Case Note: African Information Technology Bridge 1 (Pty) Ltd v The MEC for Infrastructure Development, Gauteng Province

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–17])

In this case, the appellant, African Information Technology Bridge 1 (Pty) Ltd (AITB 1), was involved in a dispute with the Member of the Executive Council for Infrastructure Development, Gauteng Province (MEC). The core issue was whether valid contracts were formed between AITB 1 and the MEC in relation to three tenders—GC 1417/05/2006, GC 1418/05/2006, and GC 1419/05/2006—for six projects ([para 1]).

The company originally known as Crestwell Trading 9 (Pty) Ltd changed its name to AITB 1 in June 2006 ([para 3]). The initial business focus had shifted from general trading to information technology. The Gauteng Department of Transport and Public Works initiated three tenders following a successful pilot project wherein AITB worked in conjunction with iNathi Technology Holdings ([para 6]).

Notably, AITB 1 had connections to African Bridge, leading to potential confusion due to their similar names ([paras 3–10]). Tenders were awarded to "AITB/Inathi JV", but subsequent documents often addressed African Bridge rather than AITB 1 ([para 12]). After issues arose concerning the execution of the tenders, the department realized that its contracts had inadvertently involved AITB 1, not African Bridge, and negotiations failed to resolve the issue.

The North Gauteng High Court previously examined the claims, finding some in favor of AITB 1 while dismissing the majority, leading to this appeal ([paras 1, 17]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1, 18–22])

The primary legal question before the Court of Appeal was whether valid contracts existed between AITB 1 and the MEC at the time of the tender awards. A secondary issue ensued concerning the nature of the mistakes asserted by the MEC, specifically whether a unilateral error (mistake in persona) could negate the formation of a valid contract. Furthermore, the appeal also addressed if the subsequent agreements constituted a counter-offer recognized under the law ([paras 1, 19–36]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 21–35])

The court’s analysis was grounded in principles of contract law, highlighting that a meeting of the minds is essential for contract formation ([para 21]). When considering unilateral mistakes, the court referred to significant precedents, including *National and Overseas Distributors Corporation (Pty) Ltd v Potato Board*, where the difficulty of establishing a unilateral mistake was addressed ([para 22]). The criteria for establishing a valid mistake center on whether the mistake was reasonable and communicated ([paras 22–24]).

Furthermore, the court discussed the concept of "justus error," identifying that a fundamental mistake could render a contract void ab initio, drawing from cases like *Bird v Sumerville* and *Sonap Petroleum (SA) (Pty) Ltd v Pappadogianis* ([paras 23–25]). The findings were confirmed by the lack of awareness by the ME regarding AITB 1’s identity and the interplay of their presented documentation ([paras 28–29]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 18–41])

The court addressed the MEC's claim of a unilateral mistake and concluded that this mistake was indeed justus, as AITB 1 was aware that it had submitted a bid under its name ([para 22]). The court underscored Tucker’s deliberate tactic to present AITB 1 as African Bridge, misleading the department into believing that it was contracting with a familiar entity ([paras 30–34]).

The department’s intentions, as reflected in their documents and ongoing communications, showed a consistent belief that they were engaging with African Bridge, reinforcing the notion that the mistake was both significant and reasonable. AITB 1's actions contributed to this confusion, thus solidifying the MEC's position that a contract never came into effect due to the fundamental nature of the error ([paras 35–40]).

Additionally, the court critically assessed whether the letters dated 6 July 2006 could be interpreted as a valid counter-offer to AITB 1. It concluded that these documents still addressed African Bridge, indicating that the MEC did not genuinely consider AITB 1 as a contracting entity until the error's recognition post-award ([paras 36–41]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([para 41])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court's findings that the contracts concerning tenders 1417 and 1418 were void ab initio due to a fundamental mistake by the MEC. The court emphasized that this mistake, depicting AITB 1's deceptive manoeuvers, negated any valid contract formation ([para 41]). The case underscores the fundamental nature of clarity in the contracting process, especially within public procurement, where misunderstandings can lead to significant legal consequences. As a result, AITB 1 bears the costs of the appeal, reinforcing clear standards for tender and contract validity in public administration.
