Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# Case Note: Department of Correctional Services & another v POPCRU & others (107/12) [2013] ZASCA 40

### FIRAC Case Note

---

### F – Facts

This case arises from the dismissal of male correctional officers from the Department of Correctional Services (the Department) for refusing to cut their dreadlocks, which they maintained for religious and cultural beliefs. The respondents—five individuals, all members of the Police and Prisons Civil Rights Union (POPCRU)—were employed at Pollsmoor Prison, Cape Town, and were dismissed in June 2007 after they refused to comply with a Corporate Identity Dress Code that prohibited dreadlocks. Prior to the appointment of the second appellant, Area Commissioner Pollsmoor, there had been no reported issues regarding the respondents' hairstyles ([paras 1–2], [5]).

The Department's dress code, particularly clause 5.1.2.3, specifically banned dreadlocks for male officials; however, until the Area Commissioner’s appointment in January 2007, enforcement was lax. Following his appointment, the Area Commissioner convened a meeting emphasizing compliance with the dress code and security concerns, leading to written directives requiring all officers to comply with the dress code ([paras 3–5]).

The respondents cited their dreadlocks as expressions of their Rastafarian faith and Xhosa cultural identity ([paras 6–7]). Their refusal to cut their hair led to a suspension on 2 February 2007, and upon their non-compliance with a disciplinary hearing, they were formally dismissed ([paras 8–9]).

The core of the respondents' claim was that their dismissals were automatically unfair as they constituted discrimination based on religion, culture, and gender, as defined by section 187(1)(f) of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA) ([paras 10–11]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal questions presented in this case include:
1. Was the dismissal of the respondents discriminatory and automatically unfair under the LRA?
2. Did the Department’s dress code constitute unfair discrimination based on religion, culture, and gender in contravention of section 1 of the LRA?
3. Is there any justification for the dress code, in light of the respondents’ rights to practice their religion and culture? ([paras 1–2], [17], [18]).

### R – Rules

The relevant legal provisions and precedents cited in the judgment include:
- **Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995**: Section 187(1)(f) defines a dismissal as automatically unfair if the reason is connected with discrimination on various grounds, including religion, culture, and gender.
- **Constitution of South Africa, 1996**: Section 9(1) guarantees equality before the law and prohibits discrimination on various grounds, including religion and culture.
- **Harksen v Lane NO**: This case establishes that discrimination is presumed to be unfair unless the employer can prove otherwise ([paras 22–23]).

### A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the findings by the Labour Court and the Labour Appeal Court which indicated that the respondents' dismissals were automatically unfair for several reasons:
- The court noted that the Department’s dress code disproportionately affected male officers by forbidding them from expressing their religious and cultural identities through their hairstyles while not imposing the same restrictions on female officers ([paras 15–16]).
- The court emphasized that merely stating a neutral policy was insufficient to justify practices that effectively punished adherence to sincerely held religious and cultural beliefs ([paras 19–21]).
- The Department failed to establish that the dress code was an inherent requirement of the job, noting that no evidence indicated that the respondents' dreadlocks had any negative impact on their performance or safety ([paras 24–25]).
- Furthermore, it was held that the punitive consequences of the policy constituted an invasion of the respondents' dignity, thereby undermining their right to practice their faith ([paras 22–24]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, confirming that the dismissals were automatically unfair as they constituted multifaceted discrimination based on religion, culture, and gender. This decision emphasizes the importance of adequately accommodating employees' religious and cultural identities in workplace policies. The ruling reaffirms the significant intersection of workplace rights and constitutional protections, affirming the principle that a dress code should not inhibit the expression of one’s identity in a manner that undermines their rights and dignity ([paras 25–26]).

---

This case has implications not only for the correctional services and related sectors but also for broader workplace policies across all industries in South Africa, highlighting the necessity for inclusivity and accommodation of diverse cultural and religious expressions within employment practices.
