THE LASER TRANSPORT GROUP (PTY) LTD & ANOTHER v ELLIOT MOBILITY (PTY) LTD & ANOTHER (835/2018) [2019] ZASCA 140

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 140

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])

This case arose from a tender process conducted by the Department of International Relations and Co-operation (DIRCO) concerning removal and insurance services for household goods and vehicles of DIRCO officials. On 11 March 2015, DIRCO issued a tender invitation (DIRCO 13/2014/15) which the appellants, The Laser Transport Group (Pty) Ltd and Gin Holdings (Pty) Ltd, responded to by submitting a bid priced at R117 673 286.00. The first and second respondents, Elliot Mobility (Pty) Ltd and Neo Thando/Elliot Mobility, also submitted their bid at a significantly higher price of R215 882 882.00 ([paras 2–4]).

The appellants expressed concern over delays in announcing the tender award and were eventually informed on 6 August 2015 that the tender had been cancelled and would be re-advertised due to complaints about exclusionary criteria allegedly affecting Previously Disadvantaged Movers. The cancellation prompted the appellants to claim that DIRCO’s actions violated procurement regulations ([paras 5–11]).

Upon re-tendering under new terms (DIRCO 05/2015/16), both the appellants and respondents again submitted bids. Ultimately, the respondents were awarded the tender despite the appellants scoring higher in functionality assessments. The contract was awarded on 3 November 2015 and subsequently implemented ([paras 7–10]).

Dissatisfied with the award, the appellants challenged the judgments made by DIRCO in both tender processes, asserting that the award should have been made in their favor due to their lower bid and higher score ([para 9]). The Gauteng Division initially granted the appellants relief but was later overturned by a Full Court that dismissed their application and ordered them to pay costs ([paras 13–14]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 15–22])

The appeal predominantly focused on whether the awarding of the tender to the respondents was lawful despite the appellants scoring higher and offering a lower bid. The main legal issues raised included:

  1. Whether the cancellation of the first tender was justified according to the regulations established under the Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act 5 of 2000 (PPPFA).
  2. Whether DIRCO could lawfully award the tender to a higher-priced bidder when a lower-priced bid scored higher.
  3. Determining if the appeal was moot given that the contract period with the respondents was nearing its conclusion.

The court deliberated upon whether the appeal warranted consideration under section 16(2)(a)(i) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which allows dismissal of appeals deemed to have no practical effect ([paras 15–18]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 17–19])

The relevant legal framework includes the PPPFA, particularly sections elucidating the obligations of state organs in tender processes, which stipulate that contracts should generally be awarded to those scoring the highest points unless objective criteria justify an alternative award ([para 12]). The legal discourse incorporated principles established in past judgments that assert courts should refrain from adjudicating moot cases unless a matter involves a significant legal question of public interest ([para 18]).

Key references included:

  • PPPFA, Section 2(1)(f): Mandating that tenders be awarded to the highest-scoring tenderer unless justified otherwise ([para 10]).
  • Minister of Justice and others v Estate Stransham-Ford: Established bases for courts to exercise discretion in moot appeals if they have broad public significance ([para 18]).
  • Geldenhuys and Neethling v Beuthin: Stressed that courts exist for redressing actual infringements of rights, not for academic discourse ([para 17]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 20–22])

In assessing whether the appeal was moot, the court highlighted that adjudicating the merits of the case would yield no practical outcome due to the imminent expiration of the contract awarded to the respondents. It concluded that reinstating the appellants' tender would not be viable since the tender was designed to commence in 2015 under certain preconditions that had since changed dramatically ([para 21]).

The court rejected the appellants' argument that a favorable decision would retroactively extend the contract or reinstate their position, emphasizing that such an outcome would contravene the original terms of the procurement process. They further articulated that despite constitutional concerns related to public procurement, the specific case was not of a nature that warranted intervention under the exceptional grounds applicable to moot appeals ([paras 20–22]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 1–4, 22–23])

The Supreme Court of Appeal, adhering to the principles established under section 16(2)(a) of the Superior Courts Act, dismissed the appeal on the grounds of mootness, concluding that the issues presented bore no practical outcome due to the impending expiry of the tender contract. The court emphasized the purpose of conserving judicial resources and refraining from addressing speculative or academic issues ([paras 1, 22]).

The judgment solidified the understanding that tenders may lawfully be awarded despite a lower scoring bid under the scrutiny of objective criteria, thus affording DIRCO discretion in procurement as long as there are lawful grounds for their decisions. The ratio decidendi elucidated that legal disputes involving procurement must establish not only procedural irregularities but also demonstrate a tangible practical effect on the parties involved for judicial intervention ([paras 22–23]).

The implications of this ruling reinforce the rigorous adherence required to the principles of procurement law, emphasizing both the procedural safeguards for equity in procurement and the importance of operational discretion for public entities like DIRCO, thereby already affecting similar future procurement disputes.
