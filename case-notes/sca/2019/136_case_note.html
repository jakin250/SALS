Case Title: Scholtz v NDPP (69/18) [2019] ZASCA 136 (1 October 2019)
Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

F – Facts

The case concerns an appeal against a confiscation order issued by the Northern Cape Division of the High Court against multiple appellants (first to seventh appellants) under section 18 of the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (POCA). The appellants were convicted of corruption and money laundering, culminating in a confiscation order for R59,806,982.00 ([paras 2-3]).

Following the convictions dated 6 December 2016, the appellants sought to appeal against the confiscation order. Despite an arrangement with the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) to file for leave to appeal by February 6, 2017, the actual application for leave was submitted late on October 10, 2017. This delay was approximately 10 months after the confiscation order and 8 months after the agreed deadline, leading to the NDPP opposing the application for condonation for the late filing ([paras 3-4], [9]).

The explanation for the delay involved miscommunication between the appellants' attorney, Mr. Du Plessis, and a candidate attorney, Mr. Pino, who was tasked with filing the applications. The appellants contended that their applications were improperly delayed because the availability of the presiding judge at the time was not properly communicated ([paras 5-6], [9]).

The NDPP countered that the confiscation order was effectively not final and therefore not appealable. He argued that there would be no prejudice to the appellants as the confiscated amount was placed in a suspense account pending the outcome of the appeal ([para 7]).

I – Issues

The case raised the following critical legal issues:

  1. Whether the court should grant condonation for the late filing of the application for leave to appeal against the confiscation order ([para 1]).
  2. Whether the appellants demonstrated a reasonable prospect of success in their appeal concerning the interpretation of the confiscation order ([paras 9-10]).

R – Rules

In considering the application for condonation, the Supreme Court of Appeal indicated that several factors should be weighed, such as the degree of lateness, the explanation for it, the importance of the matter, potential prejudice to parties involved, and the prospects of success ([para 8]). The applicable legal precedents guiding this analysis include:

  • Melane v Santam Insurance Co Ltd 1962 (4) SA 531 (A)
  • Uitenhage Transitional Local Council v South African Revenue Service 2004 (1) SA 292 (SCA) ([para 8]).

For the second issue, the court referenced legal principles regarding the interpretation of agreements, particularly emphasizing the objective construction, which aligns with established case law such as:

  • KPMG Chartered Accountants (SA) v Securefin Ltd & another [2009] ZASCA 7
  • Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality [2012] ZASCA 13 ([para 12]).

A – Application

In applying these rules to the case, the SCA noted that the delay in filing was approximately ten months, and the appellants failed to provide adequate justification for this lack of compliance. The explanation centered around the alleged unavailability of the judge and miscommunication with the candidate attorney. However, the court found that the lack of timely filing was not excusable on these grounds, as the availability of the judge was not a precondition for filing the application ([para 9]).

Regarding prospects of success, the appellants argued primarily that the judge made an error in interpreting an agreement related to the confiscation order. The SCA found the terms of the settlement agreement were clear and did not support the appellants' claims. It emphasized that the State’s burden included proving that proceeds from crime were actually received by the entities involved. The court recognized no reasonable basis to interpret "any of them" as "each of them," rejecting the argument that proportional reduction of the confiscated amounts was warranted if it was not proven that all received proceeds ([paras 10-14]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the application for condonation, asserting that the appeal presented neither compelling reasons nor prospects of success that warranted further consideration. The final order struck off the application for leave to appeal and emphasized adherence to procedural timelines as integral to the justice system ([para 15]). This ruling underscores the critical importance of compliance with court procedures and the weight of clear, unambiguous contractual language in legal interpretation.

In terms of practical implications, this case reinforces the necessity for timely and proactive legal representation and warns of the repercussions of failing to comply with judicial timelines, particularly in matters involving substantial sums and public interest, such as those implicated in POCA.
