Supreme Court of Appeal

Duncan v The Minister of Environmental Affairs and Tourism [2009] ZASCA 168

The appellant, Glen Duncan, is a commercial fisherman who sought a long-term fishing licence for traditional line fish under the Marine Living Resources Act 18 of 1998 (MLR Act).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009 [2009] ZASCA 168

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The appellant, Glen Duncan, is a commercial fisherman who sought a long-term fishing licence for traditional line fish under the Marine Living Resources Act 18 of 1998 (MLR Act). On 4 November 2005, he applied for this licence, which the Chief Director of the Department of Environmental Affairs and Tourism refused, stating that Duncan failed to demonstrate access to a 'suitable line fish vessel' ([1]). This decision was upheld by the Minister of Environmental Affairs and Tourism when Duncan's appeal was rejected ([1]). Consequently, Duncan applied for judicial review in the Cape High Court, but the application was dismissed ([2]). The crux of Duncan's application is that he had a legitimate expectation to be granted the licence based on his history in the fishing sector and previously granted licences ([6]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal were:

  1. Legitimate Expectation: Whether Duncan had a legitimate expectation to acquire a long-term fishing licence based on prior approvals and participation in the industry ([13]–[15]).
  2. Right to be Heard: Whether Duncan was entitled to be heard regarding his appeal before the adverse decision was made ([21]).
  3. Compliance with Administrative Procedures: Whether the decisions made by the Chief Director and Minister were compliant with the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([24]).

R – Rules

The court referenced various legal principles, including:

  • Legitimate Expectation: Originally meant to ensure procedural fairness, the substantive application of legitimate expectations remains unsettled in South African law ([13]–[15]). The requirements for a legitimate expectation to be deemed 'legitimate' include clarity of representation, inducement by a decision-maker, reasonableness, and lawfulness of the representation ([15]).
  • PAJA: Section 6(2) outlines procedural requirements and grounds for review, stating that decisions should not be taken for ulterior purposes or be so unreasonable that no reasonable decision-maker would take them ([24]).

A – Application

Analysis of Legitimacy

The court assessed Duncan's claim of legitimate expectation against the standards required in South African administrative law. The judgment noted that Duncan’s various claims—his length of involvement in the fishing sector, prior licensing success, and the representations in support of his application—lacked the necessary attributes to constitute a legitimate expectation ([17]–[20]). The court emphasized that merely being involved in the fishing sector did not establish a clear and unambiguous expectation that would compel granting a licence, especially given the regulatory changes and environmental considerations ([19]).

Right to be Heard

While Duncan's argument shifted towards procedural fairness, asserting that he should have been notified of changes in the suitability criteria for vessels, the court concluded that sufficient opportunity had been provided for him to make representations ([22]). The policy document clearly laid out the requirements for a suitable vessel, and Duncan had previously acknowledged that the Endeavour did not meet these qualifications. His submissions had been considered, and he was afforded the opportunity to argue his case, negating the need for further procedural rights ([22]).

PAJA Compliance

The court found that the decisions of the Chief Director and the Minister were founded on reasonable grounds, being directly connected to the state of marine fish stocks and environmental considerations. The court dismissed all claims alleging irrationality or ulterior motives ([24]). Given the evidence of significant consultation and the rationale for the policy changes, there was no basis for asserting that the decisions were flawed or unreasonable.

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, concluding that Duncan failed to establish a legitimate expectation to receive a long-term licence. Furthermore, he was afforded fair procedural rights under PAJA, with decisions made in light of legitimate environmental concerns ([25]). This case highlights the complexities surrounding the doctrine of legitimate expectations and affirms the importance of strict adherence to environmental regulations in licensing decisions, underscoring the necessity for fishery management to prioritize sustainable practices. The ruling reinforces that mere participation in a regulated industry does not guarantee entitlement to rights without compliance with the established criteria.
