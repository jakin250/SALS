# Case Note: Jacob Gedleyihlekisa Zuma v Democratic Alliance and Economic Freedom Fighters (Case no 1028/2019) [2021] ZASCA 39

## F - Facts

The case of ***Jacob Gedleyihlekisa Zuma v Democratic Alliance and Economic Freedom Fighters*** involves a complex interplay of legal issues regarding the allocation of state funds for private legal representation. The appellant, Jacob Gedleyihlekisa Zuma, served as President of South Africa from May 2009 until February 2018 and was implicated in various corruption charges stemming from actions performed during his tenure ([para 1]).

In 2006 and 2008, before the broader public scrutiny of Zuma's legal battles, he submitted requests to the State Attorney for assistance with legal representation at state expense, with the understanding that he would refund the state if he were found guilty ([para 7], [para 9]). However, the scope and legal foundation of the state’s decision to cover these costs came under scrutiny, particularly after a series of public undertakings by the state, which indicated that a total of approximately R15.3 million was expended on Mr Zuma's legal fees ([para 12], [para 14]).

The **Democratic Alliance (DA)** and the **Economic Freedom Fighters (EFF)**, as respondents, launched applications in the High Court seeking to review the legality of the state’s payments towards Zuma's private legal costs, claiming that these expenditures were unlawful and lacked any legal basis under the State Attorney Act 56 of 1957 ([paras 16-17]).

The High Court supported the DA and EFF's assertions, declaring the state’s liability for Zuma's personal legal costs as invalid and ordering Zuma to repay the funds, which framing was upheld by the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 18]).

## I - Issues

The principal legal issues in this case include:

1. **Interpretation of the State Attorney Act 56 of 1957**: Whether the provisions of s 3(1) and s 3(3) of the Act authorise the state to cover Zuma's private legal costs related to his personal criminal prosecution ([paras 19-30]).

2. **Timing of the Applications**: Did the DA and EFF bring their applications within a reasonable time limit as stipulated under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000, thus rendering them admissible for review? ([para 19], [para 20]).

3. **Repayment of State Funds**: Is it just and equitable to require Zuma to repay public funds expended on his legal representation? ([para 44])

4. **Costs and Allegations Against the Judiciary**: Should punitive costs be awarded against Zuma due to unfounded allegations made against the judiciary during the proceedings? ([paras 47-52]).

## R - Rules

The legal principles applied by the court include:

1. **State Attorney Act 56 of 1957**: Sections 3(1) and 3(3) delineate the powers and functions of the State Attorney ([para 43]). The functions are limited to performing legal work on behalf of the government and do not extend to funding private legal costs for individuals, even if they once held high office ([paras 32-36]).

2. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000**: This statute requires that administrative justice be conducted in a manner that is fair and reasonable. It sets a timeframe for review applications that can be subject to interpretation concerning what constitutes a reasonable time ([para 19]).

3. **Principles of Just and Equitable Remedies**: The court must consider whether repayment of public funds is just and equitable, which entails accountability for the abuse of public resources ([para 45]).

4. **Judicial Integrity**: Courts maintain that accusations of bias against the judiciary must be substantiated; failure to do so can lead to punitive costs as a means of upholding judicial integrity ([para 52]).

## A - Application

Upon reviewing the facts, the Supreme Court of Appeal found:

1. **Interpretation of the Statutory Provision**: The court affirmed that s 3(1) and s 3(3) of the State Attorney Act were misapplied in Zuma’s case. The state’s decision to fund his private legal representation did not satisfy the statutory requirements of serving the government’s legal interests ([para 43]). The Supreme Court confirmed that the transactions involved were ultra vires the Act and thus unlawful.

2. **Reasonableness of Timing**: The court dismissed the argument that the DA and EFF’s applications were not brought within a reasonable timeframe. It concurred with the High Court that the knowledge of the state’s payments lacked transparency and was only clarified post-2018, establishing a basis for the applications ([paras 20-30]).

3. **Just and Equitable Remedies**: The court upheld that repayment was necessary to reaffirm public accountability and restore the integrity of public finances, particularly given Zuma’s prior undertaking to repay the attorney fees if found guilty ([para 44]).

4. **Punitive Costs**: In addressing the allegations against the judiciary, the court emphasized that Zuma’s unfounded claims not only lacked evidence but also imperiled the credibility of the judicial system. Thus, the imposition of punitive costs was justified to vindicate the judiciary’s integrity and deter similar future conduct ([paras 48-52]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Zuma's appeal with costs, reinforcing key legal principles regarding the boundaries of public expenditure on personal legal representation. The ruling was pivotal in asserting the rule of law, reinforcing that while public officials may engage in legal disputes, utilizing public funds for personal legal battles, especially concerning allegations of corruption, is impermissible ([para 52]). This case underscores significant implications for accountability and transparency in the use of state resources, establishing a precedent that reinforces stringent checks on the interface between public office and personal legal liabilities. 

In conclusion, the court’s ruling is a robust affirmation of constitutional adherence to the principles of legality and the necessity of responsible governance in the use of public funds. The findings, particularly concerning punitive costs, serve as a warning against politicized attacks on judicial integrity and highlight the judiciary's role as a custodian of public interest and accountability.
