Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note: Coca-Cola Beverages Africa (Pty) Ltd v Competition Commission and Another [2024] ZACC 3

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–16])

The case arises from an application for leave to appeal by Coca-Cola Beverages Africa (Pty) Ltd (CCBA), a major player in the South African beverage industry, against the Competition Appeal Court's decision. The origin of the dispute stems from a merger that occurred on 10 May 2016, which combined four independent bottlers into a single entity, Coca-Cola Beverages South Africa (Pty) Ltd (CCBSA). As part of the approval process, the Competition Tribunal imposed conditions on the merger focused on preserving employment levels, especially due to high unemployment rates in South Africa.

Under the conditions of approval, CCBA committed to maintaining the number of employees and refraining from retrenching bargaining unit employees as a direct result of the merger for a specified duration. However, following the merger, economic conditions deteriorated, and CCBA faced numerous challenges, leading it to notify the Competition Commission of potential retrenchments. The Food and Allied Workers Union (FAWU) raised concerns that retrenchments initiated by CCBA were in breach of the conditions stipulated during the merger approval.

The Competition Tribunal initially ruled in favor of CCBA, concluding that it had substantially complied with the merger conditions. However, this decision was overturned by the Competition Appeal Court, which deemed that the Commission had found apparent breaches related to retrenchments.

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 34–34])

The critical issues presented for consideration were as follows:
1. Is the Constitutional Court's jurisdiction engaged based on the substantive legal questions raised in the application?
2. Is it in the interests of justice to grant leave to appeal?
3. What is the nature and standard of review regarding the rule 39(1) and (2) procedures of the Commission?
4. What is the test for establishing whether post-merger retrenchments are causally linked to the merger events?
5. Did the Competition Appeal Court overstep its bounds in overturning the Tribunal’s factual findings?

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 47–56])

The court heavily referenced the Competition Act 89 of 1998, particularly sections 12A and 16, which outline the criteria governing mergers and the Tribunal's powers concerning compliance with merger conditions. The legal principles regarding the causation test in the context of labor relations and the nuances of merger-specific retrenchments were essential to the court's reasoning. Particularly, the case law referenced included:

- **Aveng Trident Steel**: Provided the necessary tests for determining causation in dismissals.
- **BB Investment Company**: Established a standard for evaluating whether job losses post-merger were "merger-specific" and reaffirmed the importance of establishing a clear causal nexus.
- **Sidumo**: Addressed the permissible scope of regulatory frameworks affecting administrative review powers under the Constitution.

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 57–92])

The Constitutional Court undertook a detailed review of the lower courts' findings, scrutinizing how they approached the tests for causation and compliance under the merger conditions. 

1. **Nature of Review**: The court identified that the appeal stemmed from a special statutory review as outlined in rule 39 of the Commission Rules, emphasizing that the review process should assess whether the firm had substantially complied with the merger conditions rather than merely evaluating the Commission's decision-making process for reasonable and procedural fairness.
   
2. **Causation Test**: The court criticized the Competition Appeal Court's reliance on a "some nexus" standard, insisting that compliance with merger conditions required a stricter causal connection between retrenchments and the merger activities. The Tribunal's approach of evaluating the "true reason" for retrenchments through probabilities was upheld as correctly aligned with relevant case law.

3. **Factual Findings**: The Tribunal's analysis was deemed cogent and not subject to undue interference by the Competition Appeal Court. The evidence presented by CCBA regarding market conditions, including the adverse economic impacts and resulting operational requirements, was found to support the Tribunal's conclusion.

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 93–95])

The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal, overturning the judgment of the Competition Appeal Court and restoring the Tribunal's original decision. It concluded that the Competition Appeal Court mischaracterized the nature of the review, misapplied the legal tests concerning causation, and improperly interfered with factual determinations of the Tribunal.

The ruling has significant implications for competition law in South Africa, clarifying the standards of proof and operational compliance that firms must meet to avoid punitive repercussions under merger conditions. The judgment fortified the Tribunal's role as a specialized adjudicator with the requisite expertise to evaluate mergers' compliance with public interest provisions effectively.

In essence, the court articulated that while economic incentives following a merger may create a context for operational adjustments, actual causal connections must be robustly established to warrant any punitive actions, hence defending the balance between regulatory oversight and corporate operational freedom.
