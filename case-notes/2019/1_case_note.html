Constitutional Court 2019 [2019] ZACC 1 Facts The case involved Florette Kayamba

Case Note: Firac Method - Mulowayi v Minister of Home Affairs [2019] ZACC 1

Facts
The case involved Florette Kayamba Mulowayi and Nsongoni Jacques Mulowayi, who fled from the Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) and became permanent residents in South Africa in 2011. They had three children in South Africa, two of whom were recognized as South African citizens by birth; however, their youngest child, Gaddiel, who was born in 2017, was not granted citizenship despite being registered in accordance with the Births and Deaths Registration Act 51 of 1992. The couple had renounced their Congolese citizenship based on the information they received from the Department of Home Affairs, stipulating a five-year waiting period for citizenship applications. Additionally, on 24 October 2016, their applications for citizenship were rejected under regulation 3(2)(a) of the South African Citizenship Act 88 of 1995, which stated a qualification period of ten years for permanent residents seeking naturalization ([4]-[8]). The Mulowayis argued that the regulation imposed unreasonable conditions leading to their statelessness and filed a challenge against it in the High Court, where they sought to have it declared unconstitutional.

Issues
The central issues before the Constitutional Court were:

  1. Whether to grant condonation for the late application.
  2. Whether the Court had jurisdiction and should grant leave to appeal against the suspension of the declaration of invalidity of regulation 3(2)(a).
  3. The nature of an appropriate remedy regarding the High Court's suspension order and the constitutionality of regulation 3(2)(a) ([20]-[21]).

Rules
The legal framework included:

  • South African Citizenship Act 88 of 1995, particularly section 5(1) concerning naturalization and the conditions therein.
  • Regulations on the South African Citizenship Act, particularly regulation 3(2)(a), which stipulated a ten-year period for applications.
  • The Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000, specifically section 6(2), which allows for the review of administrative decisions.
  • South African constitutional law, particularly section 28(1)(a), which guarantees the right of a child to a nationality ([4]-[10], [12]).

Application
The Constitutional Court analyzed several key arguments:

  • The applicants contended that the rejection of their citizenship applications was improper, as it had been made without the Minister's direct involvement, thereby contravening section 5 of the Citizenship Act ([12]-[13]). The Court acknowledged the inconsistency between the ten-year requirement in the regulation and the five-year requirement under the Act, stating the regulation exceeded the authority provided by the Act and was thus invalid ([16]-[19]).
  • The High Court's suspension of the declaration of invalidity was scrutinized. The Court identified an erroneous belief that its order required confirmation by the Constitutional Court as the basis for the suspension, arguing that declarations of invalidity regarding regulations do not necessitate such confirmation ([28]-[34]). Thus, the validity of regulation 3(2)(a) was a direct implication of the applicants’ circumstances, which rendered the suspension impractical and severely affected Gaddiel's rights ([32]-[34]).

Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ruled in favor of the Mulowayis, granting condonation and ultimately upholding their appeal. The Court rejected the High Court's suspension of the declaration of invalidity regarding regulation 3(2)(a) as it lacked a legal basis, affirming that the regulation was unconstitutional. The order was thus set aside, reinstating the High Court's declaration of invalidity without the suspension. The judgment recognizes the critical rights at stake for children like Gaddiel, supporting the principle of providing nationality and identity regardless of their parents' situations. There was no order as to costs ([35]-[36]).

In essence, this case reaffirms the need for administrative regulations to align with statutory frameworks and the Constitution, emphasizing the necessity of effective remedies for individuals affected by erroneous governance in citizenship matters.
