Source Folder: 1996

# Case Note Using the FIRAC Method: T A P Duplessis N O and Harry Kaplan N O v Rolfes Ltd (Case No: 500/94)

## F – Facts 
The appellants, T A P Duplessis N O and Harry Kaplan N O, are the joint liquidators of R McCarthy (Pty) Ltd ("McCarthy"). On 17 June 1988, McCarthy entered a contract with the City Council of Johannesburg to refurbish and extend the Johannesburg Civic Theatre ([para 2]). A relevant aspect of this contract, Clause 15, permitted the nomination of subcontractors, and on 2 November 1990, Rolfes, a consortium member, was appointed to undertake substantial work through two subcontracts (the "sub-contracts") ([para 2], [para 3]). 

After various delays and outstanding work, McCarthy was provisionally wound up on 6 May 1993, and the liquidators were appointed ([para 4]). Retention funds totalling R885,708.69 and unpaid architect's certificates amounting to R605,985.21 were contentious points ([para 4]). On 11 November 1993, Rolfes brought an application to the Witwatersrand Local Division, aimed at securing a declaratory order that the liquidators had elected to proceed with the sub-contracts, along with an order to pay the outstanding amount ([para 5]).

On 30 March 1994, the Court a quo ruled in favor of Rolfes, asserting that the liquidators had indeed elected to proceed with the sub-contracts ([para 6]). The liquidators appealed this decision, leading to the present case ([para 6]).

## I – Issues 
The primary legal issue in this appeal is whether the finding of the Court a quo—that the liquidators had elected to continue the subcontract agreements—was correct ([para 6]). The implications of this finding are significant: if an election did occur, Rolfes would hold a preferential claim against the estate as an administrative cost rather than a mere concurrent creditor ([para 6]). Rolfes contended that an election transpired either on 2 July 1993 (termed "direct election") or as an indirect outcome of the liquidators' indicated intention to continue with the main contract in August 1993 ([para 7]).

## R – Rules 
The legal framework governing this issue stems primarily from insolvency principles within South African law, particularly the common law concerning the rights of liquidators and trustees to elect whether or not to abide by executory contracts post-insolvency ([para 25]). Under common law, a liquidator is not bound to perform unexecuted contracts entered into by the insolvent, unless they believe such performance serves the interests of the general body of creditors ([para 25]). 

Case law such as *Bryant and Flanagan (Pty) Ltd v Muller* and *Thomas Construction (Pty) Ltd (In Liquidation) v Grafton Furniture Manufacturers (Pty) Ltd* establishes that contracts remain valid post-liquidation and can be elected to, under certain statutory conditions ([para 26]). Furthermore, an unequivocal election to continue with a contract carries obligations for a liquidator to notify the other party and could result in expenses of administration ([para 30]).

## A – Application 
The court’s analysis centered around whether the correspondence on and after 2 July 1993 demonstrated a "direct election" by the liquidators. The key piece of correspondence was a letter sent by Bryden, representing the liquidators, which indicated that further confirmation was needed before any tasks could proceed ([para 11]). The court found that this did not establish a clear election, noting that Rolfes had attempted to impose an election upon the liquidators and that the response from the liquidators did not affirmatively indicate such an election ([para 12]).

On the topic of "indirect election," the court did recognize the liquidators' decision in August 1993 to continue with the main contract. However, the conclusion drawn by the Court a quo that this implied a simultaneous decision to honor all obligations under the sub-contracts was scrutinized. The liquidators contended that they were yet to evaluate the financial viability of completing the subcontracts, thus negating any assertion that an automatic decision to honor them had been made ([para 26], [para 30]).

The court also indicated that the contract statuses of the main contract and the sub-contracts were independent and that obligations stemming from one did not automatically transfer to the other, thereby rejecting Rolfes's argument of a necessary inference based on the liquidators' decisions regarding the main contract ([para 34]).

## C – Conclusion 
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the liquidators' appeal and set aside the order of the Court a quo. The court found that the liquidators had not made an election to continue with the sub-contracts, either explicitly or implicitly ([para 41]). Thus, Rolfes remained a concurrent creditor rather than a preferred claimant for administration costs. This judgment underlines the need for clear, unequivocal actions from liquidators in circumstances of insolvency, specifically regarding the intentions to abide by executory contracts ([para 41]). 

The implications of this decision reinforce the independence of contracts post-liquidation and the necessity for creditors, particularly subcontractors, to recognize their placement within the hierarchy of claims against an insolvent estate ([para 34], [para 41]).
