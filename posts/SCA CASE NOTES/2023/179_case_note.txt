Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: Hough & Bremner and Another v The Road Accident Fund (1024/2022) [2023] ZASCA 179

## F – Facts
1. This case arises from a legal dispute regarding a fee agreement between Hough & Bremner Inc (H&B), a firm of attorneys, and their client, Anita Ernesto Chiau (Ms Chiau) ([1–2]). The matter involved a delictual claim initiated by Ms Chiau against the Road Accident Fund (RAF) relating to damages from a motor vehicle accident that occurred on 20 July 2015 in Mpumalanga ([3]).

2. The fee agreement in question was executed on 11 September 2015, prior to the action's institution. The high court concluded that it constituted a contingency fee agreement (CFA) under the Contingency Fees Act 66 of 1997, which it determined did not meet the formal requirements outlined in the Act ([2–4]).

3. On 7 March 2022, a settlement was reached between Ms Chiau and the RAF, wherein the RAF accepted liability for her damages, amounting to R1,034,470.20, and additionally promised to cover future medical expenses and provide funds to settle legal costs ([4–5]). The trial was initially scheduled for 14 March 2022, but was subsequently postponed due to questions raised by Legodi JP regarding the validity of the fee agreement ([5–6]).

4. During the proceedings, the high court issued directives demanding information about the fee agreement, which led H&B to assert that the fee agreement did not constitute a CFA, indicating that fees would be paid irrespective of the litigation outcome ([7–9]). However, the high court nonetheless reviewed and set aside the fee agreement, while also refusing to make the settlement an order of the court ([10–11]).

5. The high court’s judgment included several findings adverse to H&B, even though it was not formally a party to the proceedings, leading to claims regarding violations of due process ([12–15]). Therefore, H&B and Ms Chiau appealed the decision and sought to reverse the high court's rulings related to the fee agreement and the settlement ([1–2]).

## I – Issues
1. The crucial questions before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) included whether the high court erred in concluding that the fee agreement constituted a CFA that contravened the provisions of the Contingency Fees Act ([1–3]).

2. Additionally, there were questions concerning the appropriateness of orders issued by the high court that were not specifically sought by the parties involved, particularly regarding the implications of these orders on H&B despite it not being a party to the initial proceedings ([10–12]).

3. The case further explored whether the high court was empowered to issue the contested orders against H&B and to amend the terms of the settlement agreement without affording the parties a hearing ([12–13]).

## R – Rules
1. The principle governing contingency fee agreements is encapsulated within the Contingency Fees Act 66 of 1997, which provides specific formal requirements for such agreements to be legally enforceable ([13–15]).

2. Precedent from prior appeals, such as *Mucavele v MEC for Health, Mpumalanga Province* ([2023] ZASCA 129) and *Majope and Others v The Road Accident Fund* ([2023] ZASCA 145), established that valid fee agreements do not necessarily constitute CFAs merely on the grounds that they stipulate payment only upon case conclusion ([1–2],[12]).

3. The *Fischer v Ramahlele* ([2014] ZASCA 88) judgment emphasized that it is the responsibility of parties to frame their disputes appropriately, and a court should not take unilateral action that alters the dispute's character without hearing from both sides ([14]).

## A – Application
1. The SCA scrutinized the high court's determination that the fee agreement was a CFA and found significant procedural and substantive errors. The court noted that the high court had overlooked H&B's clear assertions regarding the non-CFA nature of their agreement, which aligned with established precedents ([13–14]).

2. The SCA criticized the high court for issuing far-reaching orders against H&B, emphasizing that such actions required the opportunity for H&B to be heard, which was not afforded ([12–13]). The decisions made by Legodi JP invalidated not only H&B's rights regarding its fees but also wrongly imposed obligations on a party not present in the proceedings ([10–12]).

3. The SCA reaffirmed the interpretation that a fee structure allowing for payment upon resolution of a case does not automatically constitute a CFA that undermines an attorney's right to remuneration. It reiterated that even if the fee structure contained flaws, it did not invalidate the separate settlement agreement reached between Ms Chiau and the RAF ([15]).

4. The analysis extended to the settlement agreement, wherein the high court failed to recognize that an invalidated fee agreement did not necessitate the nullification of the settlement agreement itself, which had distinct legal validity ([14–15]).

## C – Conclusion
1. The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal lodged by H&B and Ms Chiau, reversing the high court’s order. It reinstated the settlement agreement reached on 7 March 2022 as a valid order of the court ([16]).

2. The SCA underscored the importance of due process and the rights of parties not formally included in proceedings. It highlighted that the jurisdiction of the court must be exercised within proper procedural bounds, protecting the interests of all parties involved in litigation ([12–16]).

3. The ramifications of this decision stress the need for courts to adhere strictly to procedural fairness and respect the separateness of fee agreements from settlement agreements in litigation, which influences future cases involving attorneys' fee structures under the Act ([12–16]). 

This decision thus contributes significantly to clarifying the law surrounding CFAs and attorney-client agreements within the South African legal framework.
