# Case Note: City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality v Glofurn (Pty) Ltd (136/2023) [2024] ZASCA 101

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts 
The appeal before the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa revolves around a dispute between the City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality (the City) and Glofurn (Pty) Ltd (Glofurn) concerning the implementation of credit control measures, particularly the threat to disconnect Glofurn's electricity supply. The facts were largely undisputed and can be summarized as follows ([para 1–7]).

Glofurn had two electricity accounts: a post-paid account (account number 20[...]) that had been closed as of March 1, 2022, and a pre-paid account to which Glofurn was migrated, designated as account number 50[...]. Despite this migration, Glofurn continued to make payments toward the closed post-paid account, which inadvertently remained in credit to the amount of R400,000.00 by June 2022. Meanwhile, the City issued an invoice indicating that Glofurn was in arrears (R766,457.81) on the new account, a claim that Glofurn disputed.

In response to the City's actions, Glofurn formally lodged a dispute under sections 102 and 95 of the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 (the Systems Act), denying any debt owed. As the City threatened to disconnect electricity services, Glofurn launched an urgent application in the high court seeking an interdict against such measures, claiming that the dispute had not been adequately resolved ([paras 2–6]).

The high court granted Glofurn an interim interdict, preventing the City from disconnecting electricity until the dispute was resolved, prompting the City’s appeal against this ruling ([para 7]).

### I – Issues
The principal issues for determination were: 
1. Whether the City was entitled to implement credit control measures against Glofurn due to a purported dispute lodged under section 102(2) of the Systems Act remaining unresolved ([para 1]).
2. Whether the high court correctly found that the City’s Credit Control and Debt Policy was a nullity in the face of its failure to be enacted as a by-law, thereby affecting its enforceability against Glofurn ([paras 10–12]).

### R – Rules 
The legal framework governing this dispute is primarily established by the Systems Act, particularly sections 95 and 102. Section 102(2) mandates that municipalities cannot enforce debt collection measures if a dispute exists regarding the amount claimed ([paras 13–17]). The City's Credit Control and Debt Management Policy further details the compliance requirements for electricity supply and dispute resolution processes ([paras 15–16]).

Additionally, the case law elucidating administrative law principles, particularly the notions expressed in **Oudekraal Estates** and **MEC for Health, Eastern Cape v Kirland Investments** are pertinent, as they address the conditions under which administrative actions are deemed valid prior to being set aside ([para 27]).

### A – Application 
The court thoroughly analyzed both parties' arguments concerning the existence and resolution of the dispute. It concluded that the City’s claim that the dispute had been resolved was unfounded due to the presence of ongoing unresolved issues that entitled Glofurn to seek further relief ([paras 19–22]). 

In the high court, the determination of the status of the policy was reached without the necessary review, as the policy was argued to be unenforceable until properly enacted as a by-law, a condition stipulated by section 98 of the Systems Act. The absence of a formal review precluded the City from asserting that the policy could be treated as effective against Glofurn ([paras 19–27]).

The court also scrutinized the conduct of the City’s representatives in their failure to adequately investigate Glofurn's grievances, which highlighted an unjustified haste in the decision to implement credit measures without appropriate dispute resolution. Such conduct was seen as neglecting the City’s responsibilities under the Systems Act and the associated policy ([para 22–24]).

### C – Conclusion 
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the City’s appeal, affirming the high court's ruling in favour of Glofurn's interdict. The judgment underscored that an unresolved dispute invokes specific protections against unilateral credit control measures, as stipulated in the Systems Act. Consequently, Glofurn's rights were upheld - the necessary processes were not followed, and the City failed to adhere to the proper standards of administrative conduct.

The court's decision further clarified that policies adopted by municipalities require legislative backing to be enforceable, affirming the high court's findings as valid and just under the circumstances ([paras 26–29]).

This case serves as a crucial reference point regarding the enforceability of municipal policies and the mandatory processes involved in resolving disputes related to municipal services, reiterating the essential role of adherence to statutory processes in local governance.
