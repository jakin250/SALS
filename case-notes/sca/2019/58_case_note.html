Mphephu v Mphephu-Ramabulana & Others [2019] ZASCA 58

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 58

F – Facts

Procedural Background and Context ([1–4])
The appellant, Masindi Clementine Mphephu, initiated review proceedings in December 2012 before the Limpopo Division of the High Court against several respondents regarding the identification and recognition of the first respondent, Regent Toni Peter Mphephu-Ramabulana, as the King of the Vhavenda. The proceedings were complicated by a series of contested claims over the traditional leadership of the Vhavenda community, which led to points in limine being raised concerning the judicial process and procedural compliance under the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003 and its amendments.

The appellant claimed a right to precedence in the succession due to her familial ties — her father, Mr. Dimbanyika Mphephu, was installed as chief in 1994 but passed away in 1997, after which the first respondent was identified by the Mphephu-Ramabulana Royal Family Council (the eighth respondent) ([1–4]).

Historical Claims and Commissions ([5–9])
The historical context spans from an application for the first respondent’s recognition as king in 2003, dismissal of claims, and eventual constitutional provisions that established resolutions for traditional leadership disputes. The Commission on Traditional Leadership Disputes and Claims was created to address claims like those of the first respondent and the complexities surrounding the ===Kingship/Queenship of the Vhavenda community.
The high court initially dismissed the review application, prompting an appeal.

I – Issues

Legal Questions ([10–12], [19–27])
The central legal issues can be delineated as follows:

  1. The legitimacy of the points in limine raised by the respondents, questioning jurisdiction and procedural preconditions based on the Framework Act — primarily, whether claims had prescribed and whether the high court had jurisdiction to hear the matter without the formal lodging of a dispute with the Commission.
  2. The potential discrimination embedded in the criteria for succession to traditional leadership positions in the context of customary law, including gender discrimination that may conflict with principles enshrined in the Bill of Rights.
  3. The constitutionality of the provisions of ss 2A and 2B concerning the framework for traditional leadership and their relationship to ensuring compliance with gender equality.

R – Rules

Legal Framework ([3–4], [14–18])
The case engaged several key legal principles, primarily arising from:

  • Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003, as amended, which stipulates processes for the recognition and identification of traditional leaders and the pivotal role played by the Commission.
  • Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA), which addresses the reviewability of administrative actions and was central to the court's authority to adjudicate conflicts regarding decisions made by the state and customary institutions.
  • Constitutional Review Norms: The South African Constitution provides guidelines for adherence to human rights and traditional customs ([4], [14]). Relevant sections include s 211, which recognizes customary law, and provisions aimed at gender equality found in the Bill of Rights.

A – Application

Court's Reasoning and Decision on Points in Limine ([12–24])
The Supreme Court of Appeal highlighted that the high court misjudged its jurisdiction to review the identification and recognition decisions made under customary law ([14]). It affirmed the judiciary’s role in adjudicating customary matters, recognizing that courts could intervene without prior lodging of disputes with the Commission when issues of rights and compliance with the Constitution are at stake.

Furthermore, the court evaluated the constitutionality of the criteria leading to the identification of the first respondent as king, recognizing the implications of gender biases implicit in the traditional customs. This was aligned with the principles of progressive adaptation of customary law as mandated in section 2A(4)(c) of the amended Framework Act which is designed explicitly to combat gender discrimination ([29–32]).

The court concluded that the procedure followed for identification was flawed, given that the eighth respondent’s decision did not reflect the requisite compliance with either substantive laws or principles of equality ([33–36]). The application revealed a systemic bias that fails to uphold constitutional values.

Review of Recognition and Referral for Further Proceedings ([40–44])
The court set aside the decisions recognizing the first respondent as king, determining that entire processes needed re-evaluation under expert guidance from traditional councils as established by the Framework Act. This included the necessity for appropriate inquiries into the evidence and legitimacy of succession claims. The decision underlined the need for comprehensive remedies reflecting the intricate dynamics of gender and lineage within Vhavenda customs ([43]).

C – Conclusion

Outcome and Implications of Ruling ([40–46])
The Supreme Court sustained the appeal and mandated that the prior decisions to identify and recognize the first respondent be reviewed and set aside. The matter was referred back to the Limpopo Division of the High Court for further adjudication on the merits, underscoring the necessity to resolve outstanding customary disputes before any future identifications of traditional leadership occur.

Crucially, the ruling highlighted the evolving relationship between customary law and constitutional values, creating a precedent for similar cases regarding gender representation in traditional leadership ([46]). It charted a clear pathway toward adapting customs to align with constitutional mandates, fostering a more inclusive environment for advancement in traditional leadership dynamics across South Africa.

The detailed structure presented allows for academic citation and serves as a foundational understanding of the complexities involved in this landmark case, exemplifying the balance between traditional practices and constitutional compliance.
