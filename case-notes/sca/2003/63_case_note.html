Supreme Court of Appeal

Wimpie Barnard v The State

[1] The appellant, Wimpie Barnard, a 34-year-old clerk employed by the complainants, pleaded guilty to 30 counts of theft involving an amount of R30,069.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2003

F – Facts

[1] The appellant, Wimpie Barnard, a 34-year-old clerk employed by the complainants, pleaded guilty to 30 counts of theft involving an amount of R30,069. Following his plea, he was sentenced to five years' imprisonment. Despite seeking to appeal his sentence before the Transvaal Provincial Division, his appeal was unsuccessful; leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal was subsequently granted.

[2] Barnard's role in his employer's business included bookkeeping duties and the oversight of a safe containing cash, indicative of the trust vested in him. His systematic theft went undetected for 15 months. When confronted about the theft, Barnard promised to repay the stolen funds, but his employment was terminated, and his final salary of R3,000 was withheld.

[3] Post-incident, Barnard reported alleged VAT irregularities related to his employer to the South African Revenue Services (SARS) and joined the Mineworkers Union, which assisted him in recovering the withheld salary. While an investigation was initiated by SARS, it did not conclude before his trial.

[4] In mitigation, Barnard attempted to present evidence regarding his current employment and proposed a repayment plan of R700 per month. A report was submitted, recommending correctional supervision under s 276(1)(h) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (the Act).

[5] The State brought forth evidence from the complainants, emphasizing the indignation felt due to Barnard's whistleblowing and the concealment of his thefts.

[6] The trial court's findings regarding sentencing included the seriousness of theft by an employee in a position of trust, Barnard's apparent lack of remorse, and concerns surrounding the deterrent effect of correctional supervision.

I – Issues

[9] The crux of the issue was whether the trial court exercised its discretion judiciously in imposing a five-year sentence of direct imprisonment. This raises broader questions about the proportionality of sentencing and the balance between punishment and rehabilitation. An appeal court generally refrains from overriding a sentencing court's discretion unless a substantial misdirection can be established.

R – Rules

[10] The court emphasized that a misdirection justifying interference by an appeal court must be significant enough to demonstrate that the trial court failed to exercise its discretion appropriately. Mlambo AJA highlighted the standard applied in sentencing, drawing from precedent that includes s 276 of the Act, which allows for various sentencing options, including correctional supervision and direct imprisonment.[8]

[12] The court referred to principles laid out in S v Sadler 2000 (1) SACR 331 (SCA), asserting that in cases of theft, particularly by trusted employees, the law expects severe consequences, and direct imprisonment is a necessary option.

A – Application

[10-21] In its analysis, the Supreme Court of Appeal identified several misdirections by the trial court. Mlambo AJA argued that the trial court's attribution of Barnard's whistleblowing to a lack of remorse was flawed, as it did not adequately ascertain Barnard's motivations for his actions post-theft discovery. Barnard had signaled willingness to repay and engaged in communication regarding a repayment arrangement, which was disregarded by the trial court.

[12] It was noted that the trial court failed to explore sentencing options provided under s 276, specifically the potential for correctional supervision under s 276(1)(i). Mlambo AJA underscored that while theft was serious, Barnard's restitution efforts, his role as a first offender, and his circumstances warranted consideration in sentencing.

[16-23] The court took into account Barnard's eventual full repayment of the stolen sum, interpreting it as an indication of his willingness to rectify his actions. This was pivotal, particularly as the appeal court convened as if for new sentencing, unaffected by previous dispositions, leading to the conclusion that a nuanced sentence could better reflect both societal and individual interests.

C – Conclusion

[23] The appeal was successful, with the Supreme Court of Appeal setting aside the initial sentence. In its place, the court imposed a modified sentence of five years' imprisonment under s 276(1)(i), which allows for conversion to correctional supervision. This decision underscores the principle that while accountability for misdeeds is critical, the justice system must also accommodate both the rehabilitative needs of offenders and the importance of deterrence in society.

This case illustrates the delicate balance courts must maintain between punishment and the potential for rehabilitation, especially when an offender demonstrates a willingness to make amends. The ruling has important implications not just for the specific case, but for setting precedents regarding sentencing in theft cases involving trusted employees.
