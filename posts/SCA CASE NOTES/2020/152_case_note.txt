Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# Case Note: The Minister of Home Affairs and Others v Jose and Another (169/2020) [2020] ZASCA 152

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The case revolves around citizenship applications made by the respondents, Joseph Emmanuel Jose ("Joseph") and Jonathan Diabaka "Junior" ("Junior"), both born in South Africa to Angolan nationals who fled to South Africa in 1995. Their family was granted refugee status in 1997 under the Refugees Act 130 of 1998. However, in January 2014, their refugee status was withdrawn, compelling them to apply for Angolan passports to remain legally in South Africa ([paras 6–8]).

By 2017, after unsuccessful attempts to secure South African citizenship under section 4(3) of the South African Citizenship Act 88 of 1995 ("Citizenship Act"), the respondents applied to the Gauteng Division of the High Court for an order compelling the Minister of Home Affairs and other appellants ("the Department of Home Affairs" or "DHA") to grant their citizenship. Specifically, they asserted that they met all four requirements of section 4(3) of the Citizenship Act, which include being born in South Africa of non-citizen parents and having registered their births according to the Births and Deaths Registration Act 51 of 1992 ([paras 10–11]).

The High Court, presided by Yacoob J, ordered the DHA to grant the applications within ten days. The DHA was granted leave to appeal only concerning whether it was competent to order the Minister to grant rather than merely consider the applications for citizenship ([paras 12–13]).

### I – Issues
The key legal questions addressed in this case include:
1. Whether the Department of Home Affairs had a duty to grant citizenship to the respondents given that their applications met the statutory requirements stipulated under section 4(3) of the Citizenship Act ([para 14]).
2. Whether the High Court could issue a directive compelling the Minister to grant citizenship as opposed to merely considering the applications anew ([para 16]).
3. Whether the DHA's appeal had substantive merit based on factual and legal grounds concerning the respondents' applications ([paras 22–24]).

### R – Rules
The primary statute governing this case is the South African Citizenship Act 88 of 1995. Section 4(3) specifies the conditions under which a child born in South Africa to non-citizen parents may apply for citizenship. These conditions include:
- Being born in South Africa.
- Being born of non-citizen parents who have not been admitted for permanent residence.
- Continuous residence in South Africa until adulthood.
- Registration of birth in accordance with the Births and Deaths Registration Act ([paras 3–5]).

Furthermore, the case cites recent jurisprudence from the Constitutional Court clarifying that citizenship questions are legal determinations rather than discretionary decisions subject to the Minister's approval, as articulated in **Chisuse and Others v Director-General, Department of Home Affairs and Another** ([2020] ZACC 20) ([paras 22–25]).

### A – Application
The court analyzed whether all requirements under section 4(3) had been satisfied and found that they were undisputedly met. The appellants' contention that the respondents had not properly applied for citizenship was dismissed due to a lack of prescribed application forms, and it was noted that the DHA had no records indicating any procedural irregularities linked to the birth registration ([paras 17–20]).

Moreover, the court underscored the necessity of tangible evidence or documentation when contesting factual allegations made under oath. The DHA's failure to provide such evidence raised doubts regarding its claims that the applications were improperly filed ([paras 18–20]). This led the court to conclude that the Minister had effectively been bound to grant citizenship given the fulfillment of statutory conditions ([paras 14–15], [22]).

The court invoked precedents and specifically mentioned that the absence of application forms does not negate the legitimacy of the respondents’ claims to citizenship, reinforcing the idea that citizenship is a legal right when statutory criteria are fulfilled ([paras 24–25]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, emphasizing that further remittances to the Minister were unnecessary as the requirements for citizenship were indisputably met. The ruling underscored the legal nature of citizenship claims and the inadequacy of the DHA's procedural defenses ([para 34]).

The implications of this judgment extend to the responsibilities of public officials to uphold the rule of law in citizenship matters, stressing that bureaucratic processes cannot negate citizens' rights when legal criteria for citizenship are satisfied. This ruling serves as a vital precedent reinforcing the judicial oversight in matters of statutory citizenship applications and the principle that entitlement to citizenship must be recognized promptly once legal conditions are met ([paras 25–30]).

The court also sanctioned a punitive costs order against the DHA, reflecting its disapproval of the conduct exhibited during the appeal process, thereby highlighting that state officials must act within the framework of justice and fairness ([paras 31–34]). 

This case thus emphasizes that citizenship is a key aspect of belonging and identity in South Africa, and its deprivation can have profound effects on individuals and families.
