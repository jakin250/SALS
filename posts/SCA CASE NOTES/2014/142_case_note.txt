Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

### FIRAC Case Note: D[...] S[...] v The State (769/13) [2014] ZASCA 142

---

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1-3])**

In the matter of *D[...] S[...] v The State*, the appellant was convicted on two counts of indecent assault and one count of rape by the regional court in Welkom. The incidents giving rise to the charges occurred between 2005 and 2007, impacting a young complainant who was related to the appellant by marriage. The appellant received a five-year prison sentence for the first count of indecent assault, a ten-year sentence for the second count, and a fifteen-year sentence for rape, with all sentences ordered to run concurrently, hence an effective sentence of fifteen years. The appellant initially pursued leave to appeal against his convictions and sentences, which was dismissed by both the magistrate and the Free State High Court, leading to his eventual appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA).

The SCA was tasked with examining the legality and correctness of the convictions specifically concerning the claims of indecent assault. The appeal focused particularly on the evidence provided by the complainant, who was a single 12-year-old witness and had been only six years old at the time of the alleged incidents ([para 8]).

---

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1-2])**

The main legal question was whether the evidence was sufficient to support the convictions for indecent assault. Specifically, the SCA had to determine the reliability of the complainant’s testimony as a single witness, the evidentiary weight of her accounts, particularly as they pertained to the first count, and the implications of the testimony of the forensic social worker on the second count of indecent assault ([paras 8, 10]):

1. Was the evidence of the complainant sufficient to uphold the conviction for the first count of indecent assault?
2. Did the conviction for the second count of indecent assault have sufficient corroboration to warrant upholding?

---

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 9-14])**

The court primarily relied on the Sexual Offences Act 32 of 2007, which governs matters related to sexual offenses, to contextualize the defining elements of indecent assault ([para 1]). In evaluating the evidence, the SCA referenced the established principles regarding the treatment of single-witness testimony and the necessary corroboration required to substantiate a conviction beyond reasonable doubt.

The court noted that while the complainant's testimony contained certain discrepancies, particularly regarding the second count, it also found support in the professionally assessed data provided by the forensic social worker. Her findings, framed within a ‘multi-dimensional framework’ for evaluating child sexual abuse allegations ([para 13]), provided crucial corroborative material that evaluated the complainant's credibility.

The rules of evidence regarding hearsay were also relevant, as the court considered how the testimonies and evaluations by the forensic social worker influenced the determination of the case ([para 13]). 

---

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 9-14])**

In applying the legal rules to the facts, the SCA scrutinized the first count of indecent assault, ultimately reasoning that although the appellant's conduct was distasteful, it did not reach the legal threshold for indecent assault as defined by the statutes. This led to the conclusion that the conviction for this count was erroneous ([para 9]).

Conversely, the second count was upheld through a detailed analysis of the complainant's testimony and the forensic social worker’s assessment. The complainant described the assault vividly, including sensory details that supported her claims regarding the experience of ejaculate ([para 10]). While there was a noted inconsistency regarding how she described the appellant's actions during the assault, the overall credibility of her testimony was deemed adequate when considered alongside the forensic evidence.

Crucially, the court weighed the appellant's testimony as lacking in credibility, which further solidified the case against him for the second count. The SCA found that the comprehensive view of the evidence—including corroborative assertions from the forensic social worker—made it beyond reasonable doubt that the second count’s conviction was justified ([para 14]).

---

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 15])**

The SCA ultimately upheld the appeal regarding the first count of indecent assault, setting aside both the conviction and the associated sentence, while dismissing the appeal concerning the second count. The ratio decidendi emphasized the multifaceted nature of evaluating child witness testimonies, and the significance of corroborative evidence in sexual offenses, particularly when involving a vulnerable, young complainant.

This judgment underscores the importance of meticulous evidential assessment in cases surrounding sexual offenses and the complexities involved with relying on single-witness accounts. The implications serve to guide future cases, particularly surrounding the evaluation of child testimonies and the role of expert evidence in supporting or contesting such claims ([para 15]).
