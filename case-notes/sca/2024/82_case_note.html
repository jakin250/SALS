Supreme Court of Appeal

AfriForum v Economic Freedom Fighters & Others (1105/2022) [2023] ZASCA 82

The appellant, AfriForum, is a civil rights organization in South Africa, primarily advocating for minority rights, with a membership of approximately 265,000 individuals.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2023] ZASCA 82

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–7], [36–64])

The appellant, AfriForum, is a civil rights organization in South Africa, primarily advocating for minority rights, with a membership of approximately 265,000 individuals. It has taken a stand against acts it considers hate speech, consolidating its focus with the launch of a complaint under the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000 (the Equality Act) ([para 3]).

AfriForum's grievance stems from the singing of the song “Dubula ibhunu” (translated as “Kill the Boer”) by members of the Economic Freedom Fighters (EFF) political party, specifically its president, Julius Sello Malema, and MP Mbuyiseni Ndlozi ([para 4]). The complaint alleged that the lyrics advocate violent incitement towards white farmers, attributing this call to historical grievances regarding land dispossession ([para 5]).

The Gauteng Division of the Equality Court dismissed the case but allowed AfriForum to appeal ([para 7]). The EFF’s defense included arguments against the application of issue estoppel, highlighting that the EFF had not been involved in previous related litigation involving Malema’s singing of the song ([para 36]).

Follow-up procedures involved an application for the recusal of Acting Justice Keightley, based on alleged prior bias, which AfriForum initiated several days after the judgment was reserved ([paras 8–10]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 11–19], [52–107])

  1. Recusal of Justice Keightley: The key issue was whether Justice Keightley should recuse herself based on perceived bias due to prior comments made regarding AfriForum, and whether these remarks constituted a reasonable apprehension of bias ([paras 12–26]).
  2. Hate Speech Complaint: The court faced the central issue of whether Mr Malema’s singing of “Dubula ibhunu” and Dr Ndlozi’s chanting of “Shisa lamabhunu” constituted hate speech as defined by s 10(1) of the Equality Act. This inquiry required an assessment of both the context of the speech and the intentions behind it ([paras 52–105]).
  3. Application of Legal Standards: The court needed to determine if the Equality Court properly applied the legal standards set out in both domestic and constitutional law regarding freedom of expression, hate speech, and the interplay between potentially conflicting rights, notably the rights to dignity and equality versus freedom of speech ([paras 52–87]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 20–61])

The court referenced several key legal principles and statutory provisions, including but not limited to:

  1. Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000 - Particularly s 10(1), which prohibits hate speech by prohibiting the publication or advocacy of messages inciting hatred based on race or ethnicity ([para 56]).
  2. Constitutional Framework:
  • s 16 of the Constitution - Guarantees freedom of expression but excludes the advocacy of hatred that incites harm ([para 52]).
  • s 19 of the Constitution - Protects political participation and expression ([para 53]).
  1. Precedents:
  • The court cited Qwelane v South African Human Rights Commission regarding the balance between freedom of expression and the prohibition of hate speech ([para 52]).
  • Masuku and SARFU were referenced for their elucidation on the presumption of judicial impartiality and the requirements therein for recusal ([paras 20–31]).

A – Application: Analysis of how the Court Applied the Law ([paras 62–105])

Recusal Application:

  • The court considered that AfriForum did not demonstrate a concrete case of bias that met the stringent standards required for recusal. The relevant comments made by Justice Keightley were interpreted in a broader context, independent of the ongoing adjudication. The remarks reflected personal viewpoints rather than definitive bias against AfriForum ([paras 28–34]).

Hate Speech Inquiry:

  • The court engaged in a detailed two-part analysis of s 10(1):
  1. Identification of Prohibited Grounds: The court found that the term "boer" as used in “Dubula ibhunu” could not be strictly regarded as based on prohibited grounds without considering context. The analysis highlighted that the reasonable person would recognize the intent behind the song and its historical significance ([paras 91–104]).
  2. Intention to Incite Harm: The court further ruled that Mr Malema’s delivery of the song was a form of political expression linked to the EFF's agenda and did not meet the threshold of hate speech. It emphasized the importance of context in determining the implications of the lyrics, asserting that they served political expression rather than incitement to violence ([paras 92–104]).

The court balanced the competing rights and concluded that the want to declare the song as hate speech would encroach upon essential political expression rights guaranteed by the Constitution, which must be safeguarded, particularly in a vibrant democracy ([paras 104–106]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 106–109])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed both the recusal application and AfriForum’s appeal with costs, thus upholding the Equality Court's original dismissal of the hate speech claim against the respondents. The court's ratio decidendi underscored the necessity of context in evaluating politically charged statements, which influences interpretations of potential hate speech.

The ruling reiterates the protection afforded to political expression under s 16 and s 19 of the Constitution, affirming that the potential for inflammatory or offensive language does not automatically fault the speaker as inciting hate or violence ([paras 108–109]). Furthermore, it reaffirms the principles of judicial impartiality, demonstrating the high threshold required to challenge a judge's participation in proceedings.

This decision has far-reaching implications, potentially setting a precedent for how similar future cases involving allegations of hate speech, particularly in politically sensitive contexts, may be adjudicated in South Africa, emphasizing the essential balance between free speech and the prohibition of hate speech in a diverse constitutional democracy.
