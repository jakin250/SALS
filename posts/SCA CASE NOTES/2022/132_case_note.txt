Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: *The Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service v Airports Company South Africa* (Case no 785/2021) [2022] ZASCA 132

## F – Facts
The case pertains to a dispute between the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (SARS) and the Airports Company South Africa (the taxpayer) regarding an additional tax assessment for the taxpayer’s 2011 year of assessment. SARS conducted an audit from December 2015 to February 2016, leading to a Letter of Audit findings issued on 8 February 2016, which proposed disallowing deductions related to corporate social investment expenditure, certain allowances, and imposing understatement penalties ([para 3–4]). 

The taxpayer lodged an objection on 12 May 2016, disputing only the disallowance of the corporate social investment expenditure and not opposing the other adjustments made by SARS ([para 5]). Following unsuccessful settlement discussions and alternative dispute resolution proceedings, the taxpayer sought permission on 6 September 2019 to amend its objection in order to include disputes over the disallowed allowances and penalties ([para 6–8]).

SARS denied this request, citing that the Tax Administration Act 28 of 2011 and the associated tax court rules did not permit an amendment to an objection after the period prescribed ([para 9]). Consequently, the taxpayer applied to the tax court, relying on Uniform rule 28(1) and tax court rule 42(1) for the plea to amend ([para 9–11]).

## I – Issues
The case raised two main legal questions: 

1. Is it permissible for a taxpayer to amend the grounds of objection against an additional assessment issued by SARS after the specified periods have lapsed according to the tax court rules?
2. Is such an order regarding the amendment of an objection appealable? ([paras 1–2])

## R – Rules
The legal framework relevant to this case includes:

- **Tax Administration Act 28 of 2011**: This Act governs procedural aspects regarding tax assessments and objections ([para 1]).
- **Tax Court Rules**: Specifically, rule 7, which outlines the process for lodging objections, and rule 10(3), which restricts the introduction of new grounds of objection once an objection has been disallowed ([para 15]).
- **Uniform Rules of Court**: Rule 28(1) stipulates the amendment of documents filed in connection with court proceedings, while rule 42(1) allows for application of High Court rules when tax court rules lack specific provisions ([para 10–11]).

Judicial precedents, including *Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service v Brummeria Renaissance (Pty) Ltd* and *Metcash Trading Ltd v Commissioner, South African Revenue Service and Another*, were also pivotal as they outline the nature of objections as part of an administrative process rather than judicial ([para 23–24]).

## A – Application
The court first analyzed the applicability of the amendment sought by the taxpayer. It determined that uniform rule 28(1) concerns only amendments to pleadings once legal proceedings have commenced, and inquiries were made into whether the objection could be classified as such ([paras 13–21]). The court concluded that an objection to an assessment is distinctly part of the pre-litigation administrative process, meaning it does not constitute a pleading that can be amended under the Uniform Rules ([para 21]).

Furthermore, the inability to amend the objection was underscored by the necessity of adhering to the time frames established in the Tax Administration Act which ensure certainty and finality in tax assessments. The court reasoned that allowing an amendment after the objection period would contravene established standards regarding the finality of assessments ([para 23–24]).

Regarding the appealability of the order, the court found the tax court’s ruling to be fundamentally flawed and thus an appealable decision could not be dismissed as interlocutory due to its erroneous nature ([paras 25–27]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld SARS's appeal, ruling that the tax court wrongly allowed the taxpayer to amend its notice of objection. It explicitly articulated that objections are administrative, not judicial, and the request for amendment was outside permissible bounds as defined by the Tax Administration Act and tax court rules. The order of the tax court was set aside, and the appeal was dismissed with costs, reinforcing the importance of adherence to procedural timelines and the principles of finality in tax assessments ([paras 1–2, 27]).

The decision serves to clarify the procedural limitations governing tax objections and underscores the need for adherence to statutory timelines, thereby maintaining the integrity of the tax collection process while balancing taxpayer rights within a regulated framework.
