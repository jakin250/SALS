# Case Note: Republic of South Africa (SCA) – FIRAC Method

## F – Facts
In *Republic of South Africa* (Cases No. 475/2002), the appellant, Gregory Joseph Paola, owns two adjacent erven in McMahon Avenue, Umgeni Heights, directly situated above the trust property owned by the first and second respondents, Jaivadan Jeeva N.O. and Tarulata Jeeva N.O. ([1–2]). The trust property, lower in elevation, has proposed developments which would substantially obstruct the appellant’s previously unobstructed view, causing a reduction in the value of his property. The appellant contended that the decision made by the third respondent, the North and South Central Local Council, to approve the building plans on the trust property was invalid for three primary reasons: (a) the proposed construction would derogate the value of the appellant’s property, breaching section 7(1)(b)(ii)(aa)(ccc) of the National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act 103 of 1977 (the "Act"); (b) the council's failure to appoint a building control officer to assess the plans before approval; and (c) the plans violated local Town Planning Regulations by not providing the required five-meter rear space ([6–10]).

## I – Issues
The central legal issues in this case were: 
1. Whether the absence of a building control officer constituted a jurisdictional fact necessary for the local council’s approval of the building plans ([13]).
2. Whether the approval of the plans undermined the appellant’s property value, amounting to a derogation of value per the provisions of the Act ([19]).
3. Whether the council's interpretation of the rear space regulations was consistent with the intention of the Town Planning Regulations ([24]).

## R – Rules
The court referenced several statutory provisions and principles in reaching its conclusions:
- **National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act 103 of 1977**: Sections 5, 6, and 7 outline the necessity for local councils to appoint a building control officer and consider their recommendations for plan approvals ([9]).
- **Town Planning Regulations**: Regulation 19 requires a minimum rear space of five meters between any dwelling and the rear boundary ([10]).
- Precedent laws cited include *SA Defence and Aid Fund v Minister of Justice* [1967] 1 SA 31 (C) regarding the requirement of jurisdictional facts for the exercise of statutory power and *Rajah and Rajah (Pty) Ltd v Ventersdorp Municipality* [1961] 4 SA 402 (A) concerning the implications of administrative irregularities on the validity of decisions ([11–15]).

## A – Application
In analyzing the first issue regarding the absence of a building control officer, the court determined that the local council had not fulfilled necessary jurisdictional facts prior to approving the plans ([14–16]). The court emphasized that the statutory language of the Act mandates these appointments; hence, the approval was invalid. The council's argument that expert advice was available and the appellant was not prejudiced was rejected ([13–15]).

Regarding the second issue of value derogation, the appellant successfully argued that the planned construction would significantly diminish the market value of his property by obstructing views ([19–23]). The court held the interpretation of "value" within the context of section 7(1) should reflect its ordinary meaning, which intrinsically includes market views. It further clarified that deprivation of a view could result in value loss independent of any servitudes or rights of prospect.

On the rear space issue, the court rejected the respondents' assertions that the trust property’s configuration changed the identity of the rear boundary and reaffirmed that the Town Planning Regulations provide clear definitions that must be adhered to consistently, irrespective of planning design ([24–26]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the appellant, holding that the approval of the building plans by the third respondent was invalid due to the lack of a building control officer and the failure to comply with statutory requirements regarding insufficient rear space ([27]). The decision underscores the importance of adhering to established procedural safeguards within the building approval process and reinforces the legal standing of property owners to oppose developments that may derogate from their properties’ values. The ruling has implications for future construction approvals, stressing that irregularities in meeting statutory conditions cannot be treated as mere administrative oversights if they affect the validity of the decision.
