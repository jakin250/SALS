Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: The Council for Medical Schemes v Genesis Medical Scheme (20518/14) [2015] ZASCA 161

## F – Facts

In **Genesis Medical Scheme**, the first respondent, Ms Nicola Joubert, was a member and sought coverage for medical expenses related to her daughter, Roxanne, who sustained a severe leg injury in a motor-cross accident on 13 January 2008 ([paras 1–4]). The injury resulted in a compound fracture classified medically as a "sub-total amputation," requiring multiple surgical interventions at private hospitals ([para 4]).

Initially, Genesis reimbursed related expenses, but disputes arose regarding the necessity and costs for certain prostheses, specifically concerning Genesis’s payment obligations under its rules versus the statutory framework established by the **Medical Schemes Act 131 of 1998** (the Act) ([paras 5–6],[10]). After a series of misunderstandings involving treatment coding and the nature of the prostheses, Genesis reversed previously approved reimbursements and limited coverage to R30,000 for an internal prosthesis ([paras 10–12]).

Ms Joubert filed a complaint to the Registrar of Medical Schemes, which ruled in her favor. Genesis appealed to both an Appeal Committee and the Council's Appeal Board, ultimately leading to judicial review in the Western Cape High Court ([paras 3, 19, 22]). The court a quo found in favor of Genesis, stating it was not obliged to cover certain costs due to its rules that limited reimbursement for external devices to public health facilities ([para 19]).

This prompted the Council for Medical Schemes and the Registrar to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 3, 20]).

## I – Issues

The central issues arising from the case include:

1. Whether the rules of Genesis Medical Scheme allowed the limitation of its payment obligations for prescribed minimum benefits (PMBs) to only those incurred in public hospitals ([paras 16, 35]).
2. Whether the statutory obligations imposed by the Act supersede the specific contractual stipulations in the Genesis Medical Scheme rules ([paras 29, 42]).
3. The legitimacy of the Appeal Board’s ruling that constrained reimbursement to state hospital costs ([para 46]).

## R – Rules

The applicable legal authorities include:

1. **Medical Schemes Act 131 of 1998**:
   - Section 29 mandates medical schemes to cover costs for PMBs ([paras 26, 38]).
   - Section 32 states that rules shall be binding between the scheme and its members, potentially forming a contractual obligation ([para 35]).

2. **Regulations under the Act**:
   - Regulation 8 outlines obligations for medical schemes to pay for PMB treatments in full, without deductibles when provided at designated service providers ([para 27]).
   - It further specifies that no limitation applies to reimbursements for services obtained at public hospitals ([para 29]).

3. **Precedents**:
   - The case **Nimed Medical Aid Society v Sepp** (1989) emphasizes the contractual nature of medical scheme rules, underscoring the relationship between members and their schemes ([para 36]).

## A – Application

The court analyzed the interaction between Genesis’s internal rules and its statutory obligations under the Act. Genesis contended that its members contracted terms through the established rules, which limited obligation to treatment received at public institutions ([paras 34, 42]). However, the court noted the statutory framework requires coverage for PMBs irrespective of the provider, aiming to prevent shifting costs to public healthcare ([paras 40–44]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the appeal ruling improperly limited Genesis's liability to the level of services rendered at public hospitals, contrary to the statute's purpose ([paras 45, 46]). Instead, the law obliges the scheme to fully cover costs incurred for treating PMB conditions, thereby directly rejecting Genesis’s argument that it could enforce cost limitations through its contractual rules.

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal, rescinding the previous court's decision and clarifying that Genesis is required to reimburse the full costs incurred for PMB treatment without regard for whether the treatment occurred in a public or private facility ([para 47]). 

### Implications
This ruling reinforces the statutory obligations of medical schemes, emphasizing that regulations designed to protect public interests cannot be overridden by internal rules that may impose limitations on coverage. The decision underscores the principle of ensuring access to necessary medical treatment for scheme members, irrespective of the geographical or institutional context, ultimately enhancing the degree of protection for vulnerable populations within the healthcare system. 

The court’s directive to amend the Appeal Board's ruling by striking down the "public hospital" limitation further affirms that the statutory mandates are designed to serve as minimum standards for medical scheme operation ([para 47]). Henceforth, all medical schemes must align their rules to reflect these obligations, providing comprehensive coverage consistent with the PMB regulations under the Act.
