# Case Note: **68 Wolmarans Street Johannesburg (Pty) Ltd and Others v Tufh Limited** ([2024] ZASCA 48)

**Neutral Citation**: 68 Wolmarans Street Johannesburg (Pty) Ltd and Others v Tufh Limited (1263/2022) [2024] ZASCA 48 (15 April 2024)

### FIRAC Case Note

## F – Facts

The dispute arose from a loan agreement executed between Tufh Limited (Tufh) and 68 Wolmarans Street Johannesburg (Pty) Ltd (Wolmarans) concerning the financing of real estate. On 5 March 2020, Tufh initiated an application against Wolmarans for the payment of R4,897,004.22, foreclosure of a mortgage bond, as well as associated costs and interest ([1]). This followed a previous judgment from Senyatsi J of the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg, which had dismissed Tufh's earlier application on 17 September 2021 ([2]). Subsequently, Tufh was granted leave to appeal by a full court comprising Justices Matojane, Molahlehi, and Strydom, which reversed the initial ruling and ruled in favor of Tufh on 13 December 2022 ([3]).

The substantive issue concerned Wolmarans’ failure to settle municipal charges as required under clauses 17 and 18 of the loan agreement, which defined obligations regarding payment of rates and taxes and specified events of default for non-compliance ([8, 9]). Since 30 October 2015, Wolmarans had fallen into significant arrears, neglecting not only its payments to the City of Johannesburg (CoJ) but also its obligations to provide proof of such payments to Tufh ([14], [16]). 

Despite ongoing disputes with the CoJ regarding specific billing issues, Wolmarans failed to pay for taxes, rates, and other associated charges without dispute, prompting Tufh to demand compliance ([18], [24]). The full court found that Wolmarans’ inability to pay its debts constituted an event of default under their agreement.

The applicants sought special leave to appeal from the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) on 5 December 2022, arguing that the enforcement of the loan agreement would be unconscionable and contrary to public policy ([33]). 

## I – Issues

1. **Bona Fide Dispute**: Did Wolmarans have a bona fide dispute with the CoJ concerning municipal services despite admitting non-payment of undisputed debts?
   
2. **Risk to Security**: Was Tufh’s security compromised due to the existence of suretyships from other parties involved?

3. **Public Policy**: Would the enforcement of the loan agreement, particularly concerning municipal payments, be unconscionable and contrary to public policy?

## R – Rules

The full court relied upon several legal principles in contract law, including the doctrine of pacta sunt servanda, which emphasizes that contractual obligations freely undertaken must be honored ([28]). It also referenced critical principles regarding public policy in A B and Another v Pridwin Preparatory School ([47]) and Beadica 231 CC and Others v Trustees for the time being of the Oregon Trust ([51]). The judgment from the full court reiterated that public policy necessitates upholding contracts unless specific, enforceable grounds for invalidation exist ([53]).

It is also worth noting that the legal status of municipal accounts under the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act No 32 of 2000 and the CoJ's Credit Control and Debt Collection By-laws empowered municipalities to pursue debts due irrespective of ongoing disputes ([39], [40]).

## A – Application

The SCA's analysis underscored that the failure by Wolmarans to pay any municipal service charges was a clear breach of the loan agreement, thus activating the provisions allowing Tufh to demand full repayment ([30], [56]). The court found there was no merit to the argument that Wolmarans had a valid dispute regarding payments owed for services rendered from the CoJ, since the dispute pertained specifically to water and electricity, while other charges remained unpaid—which were undisputed ([36], [37], [46]).

As for Tufh's security position, it maintained that while having suretyships, such security was compromised by years of outstanding debts, as the municipal charges take precedence under Section 118(3) of the Systems Act ([56]). The argument that Tufh's conduct of enforcing the agreement put its security at risk was rejected; the court held that Tufh was merely adhering to the contractual obligations set forth in the agreement ([55]).

## C – Conclusion

The application for special leave to appeal was dismissed by the SCA on 15 April 2024. The court held that the applicants failed to demonstrate reasonable prospects of success on appeal and did not present any special circumstances warranting review ([60]). This ruling reinforced the importance of contractual compliance in commercial transactions, especially concerning the mitigation of financial risks. The implications of this decision highlight the responsibilities of borrowers in maintaining payments despite disputes and uphold the principle that municipalities can enforce payment of services rendered even amidst contested billing.

The judgment also reiterates a vital tenet of contract law: that freedom of contract must be honored, and parties must adhere strictly to their agreements unless compelling public policy considerations suggest otherwise. The consequences for Wolmarans are notable, with potential ramifications for how future disputes regarding municipal billing and loan agreements are handled. 

This case underscores the balance between contractual obligations and the critical role of public policy in enforcing those agreements in South African law.
