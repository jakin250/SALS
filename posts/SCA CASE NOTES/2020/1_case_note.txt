# Case Note: Oosthuizen & Another v The State (180/2018) [2020] ZASCA 1

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

The case of **Oosthuizen & Another v The State** centers around the appeal submitted by the first and second appellants, Willem Jakobus Albertus Oosthuizen and Theo Martinus Jackson, against adverse decisions made by the Gauteng Division of the High Court (sitting as the Mpumalanga Division) in Middelburg. The trial court initially convicted the appellants for multiple offences, including assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm and kidnapping, alongside charges related to defeating the ends of justice. The trial court sentenced them to varying terms of imprisonment. 

On 2 December 2019, the Supreme Court of Appeal initially issued an order that upheld the appeal and set aside the trial court's order, substituting the convictions and sentences. An error occurred regarding the antedating of the sentences, which were incorrectly documented as being from 25 August 2017 instead of the intended date of 27 October 2017, as stipulated in Section 282 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([paras 1–2]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal question before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the court had the authority to correct a patent error within its own judgment, specifically regarding the antedating of sentences. The court examined whether it was within its remit to amend its previous ruling based on the identification of this error ([paras 1–2]).

### R – Rules

The pertinent legal rule underpinning this appeal includes Section 282 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which allows for the specification of the date from which a sentence may be antedated. Additionally, the court referenced the principle established in **Firestone SA (Pty) Ltd v Gentiruco AG** 1977 (4) SA 298 (A) at 307D, which outlines the conditions under which a court can correct a patent error in its judgment ([paras 1–2]).

### A – Application

In this case, the Supreme Court of Appeal, guided by the procedural norms established in the **Firestone** case, reviewed the original intention behind its order made on 2 December 2019. The court acknowledged the clerical error concerning the incorrect dates assigned to the sentences and clarified that the correct antedate should indeed be 27 October 2017, aligning with the legislative requirements set forth in the Criminal Procedure Act. The court found that such a correction was necessary to reflect its true intention and to ensure the proper application of justice ([paras 1–2]).

### C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal allowed the appeal, reaffirming the convictions and formalizing the amendment to the judgment to reflect the correct antedating of sentences. The amended order served not only to rectify a clerical oversight but also to substantiate the court’s dedication to the rule of law, ensuring that sentence provisions meet legislative standards. The ruling underscores the Supreme Court's commitment to the integrity of its judgments, thereby reinforcing the legal framework within which such judicial corrections are permissible ([paras 1–2]).

---

This case analysis demonstrates the procedural mechanisms available to higher courts in South Africa to amend their own judgments, emphasizing the importance of accuracy in judicial administration. The ruling affirms that courts can correct errors to uphold the intent of justice, ensuring that legal outcomes are not only fair but also procedurally sound.
