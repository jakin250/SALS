# Case Note using FIRAC Method

**Case Title:** Transnet Ltd and Inter Waste (Pty) Ltd v SA Metal Machinery Company (Pty) Ltd  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Coram:** Howie P, Zulman JA, Cameron JA, Mlambo JA, Nkabinde AJA  
**Date Delivered:** 29 November 2005  
**Reportable Case No:** 147/05

---

## F - Facts

[1] The case arose from a tender process held by Transnet Limited, a wholly state-owned public transport company through its National Ports Authority of South Africa (NPASA). NPASA invited tenders for a two-year contract for the removal of galley waste from ships in Cape Town harbour, with Inter Waste (Pty) Ltd being the successful tenderer and SA Metal Machinery Company (Pty) Ltd (the respondent) being an unsuccessful tenderer.

[2] Following the contract award, the respondent formally requested copies of various documents, including the completed tender document of Inter Waste, under the Promotion of Access to Information Act 2 of 2000 (the Act). NPASA responded that the requested document contained confidential commercial information and thus denied access without further specification from the respondent.

[3] After unsuccessful correspondence, the respondent applied to the High Court demanding access to the entire tender schedule 'without deletions'. Inter Waste did not oppose this application but rather provided the information to support NPASA’s assertion that disclosure should be denied. The High Court granted substantial relief to the respondent, ordering the production of the complete document (excluding deleted information).

[4] The appellant, alongside Inter Waste, appealed this decision, challenging the grounds of the refusal of access based on sections of the Act, particularly sections 36 and 37.

[5] NPASA had provided a blanked-out version of the schedule that revealed no detailed pricing information necessary for assessing the successful tender. The core issue revolved around whether the disclosure of this pricing information would cause commercial harm to Inter Waste.

## I - Issues

[6] The primary legal issues in this case were:  
1. Whether the specific pricing information sought by the respondent, the components of the successful tenderer's price, constituted confidential information which, if disclosed, could cause identified harm to the third party (Inter Waste).
2. The interpretation of the statutory ground for refusing access under section 36(1)(c) of the Act, particularly the phrase "could reasonably be expected to" cause harm.
3. Whether the court has discretion under section 82 to refuse access despite the failure of the public body to justify its refusal as per the provisions of the Act.

## R - Rules

[7] The case examined several legislative provisions:
- **Promotion of Access to Information Act 2 of 2000:** This Act provides the procedural framework and rights pertaining to access to information held by public bodies. Sections 36(1)(b) and (c) outline the grounds for refusing access based on the potential harm to third parties.
- **Constitution of South Africa, Section 32:** This section guarantees the right of access to information held by the state, effectively mandating transparency in government dealings.
- **Precedents:** Certain case law appeared relevant concerning the interpretation of disclosure obligations and the balancing of public interest against commercial confidentiality. The appellant relied on principles drawn from both South African and Canadian law regarding confidentiality and public access to information.

## A - Application

[8] The court undertook a detailed analysis of the provisions of the Act concerning the request for disclosure. It concluded that the information was supplied in confidence and assessed whether the subsequent expectation of harm was reasonable. The court established that disclosure would not necessarily harm Inter Waste's commercial interests, particularly given the context of the tender process and the information already being in the public domain.

[9] The court further examined the interpretation of section 36(1)(c), ultimately siding with the respondent's interpretation that the reasonable expectation of harm included a likelihood of probable harm, rather than merely possible harm. By stating that a prudent person would not consider the loss of competitive advantage as simply a possibility, but a probable consequence, the court reinforced the need for a balanced interpretation of potential harm.

[10] Deliberating on the appellant's argument regarding a discretionary power under section 82 to deny access, the court found this claim untenable. It affirmed that the legislature intended access to information to prevail unless demonstrable harm was established.

## C - Conclusion

[11] The court dismissed the appeal and upheld the High Court's decision, thereby affirming the right of the respondent to access the tender information, albeit with the caveat that the details of the rates would involve a reasonable expectation of potential harm that was not sufficiently demonstrated by the appellant.

[12] The court's ruling underscored the importance of transparency and the public's right to access information held by public bodies, particularly in matters involving public procurement. The judgment affirmed that obligations under the Act must be stringently adhered to and that public bodies must substantiate claims of confidentiality and harm with concrete evidence.

[13] The case has significant implications for the interpretation of access to information laws, balancing commercial confidentiality and the public's right to access state-held information, setting a precedent that reinforces the judiciary's active role in safeguarding transparency in public procurement practices.
