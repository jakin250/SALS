Supreme Court of Appeal

Naidoo v EP Property Projects (Pty) Ltd [2014] ZASCA 97

This case involves a dispute over ownership of an undeveloped coastal property (Portion 14 of the farm Sea View 28) in the Nelson Mandela Bay Metropolitan Municipality ([1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2014 [2014] ZASCA 97

FIRAC Method Case Note

F – Facts

This case involves a dispute over ownership of an undeveloped coastal property (Portion 14 of the farm Sea View 28) in the Nelson Mandela Bay Metropolitan Municipality ([1]). The dispute is primarily between Reginald Tobias Marais (the second appellant) and EP Property Projects (Pty) Ltd (the first respondent). EP acquired the property in 1980 and was represented by its director, Gerald John Blignaut ([2]). Blignaut was the sole shareholder until he sold his shares to Alex Campbell Stevenson, which placed the Stevensons in control of EP's assets and affairs ([2][3]).

In 2005, after internal disputes within EP, Blignaut sought to convene a meeting to remove the Stevensons as directors. To prevent this, the Stevensons and EP obtained a court order that included a stipulation barring Blignaut from representing EP ([4][5]). In 2006, Blignaut attempted to act against this order, attempting to transfer the property to Tobias, prompting an interdict sought by EP ([6][7]).

Following a court-mediated arbitration agreement, the main issue of ownership was set to be decided by an arbitrator ([8]). Despite Tobias not attending the arbitration, he was represented by legal counsel during the initial proceedings ([10]). However, the arbitrator eventually ruled that the agreement Tobias relied upon for ownership was fraudulent and reaffirmed EP as the rightful owner ([11][12]).

The arbitration award prompted EP to seek its confirmation through the Western Cape High Court, where Tobias opposed it on multiple grounds including the alleged lack of jurisdiction of the arbitrator, and argued that the arbitration was tainted by irregularities ([15][16]). The High Court denied these claims and awarded costs against Tobias and his funder, Naidoo ([22][23]).

I – Issues

The key issues to be resolved in this matter included:

  1. Whether the arbitrator had jurisdiction, given the claims of irregularity in the proceedings ([15][16]).
  2. The legality and appropriateness of the arbitration award based on arguments of misconduct and irregularity during the arbitration process ([19][20]).
  3. The correctness of the High Court’s decision to impose costs against Naidoo, who had a financial interest in the litigation ([20][21]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal framework for the arbitration dispute was primarily governed by the Arbitration Act 42 of 1965. The Act provides for various grounds upon which an arbitration award may be reviewed or set aside, particularly under section 33, which outlines conduct by arbitrators that could constitute misconduct or gross irregularity ([33]).

Judicial precedent reinforced the principle of limited court intervention in arbitration matters. Key cases include Clark v African Guarantee and Indemnity Co Ltd ([32]), which asserted courts' reluctance to interfere with arbitration awards as a matter of respecting party autonomy in electing for arbitration.

A – Application

In reviewing the arbitrator’s jurisdiction, the court emphasized that objections should have been raised before or during the arbitration proceedings ([24][25]). The court found that Tobias had effectively acquiesced to the arbitrator's authority by participating fully in the initial stages of arbitration without raising jurisdictional issues. The court cited precedent indicating that litigants cannot selectively assert claims of bias or jurisdiction after the outcome ([26]).

The High Court decision was characterized as thorough, examining each ground for contesting the arbitration outcome ([19][20]). Notably, attempts to argue extensive misconduct on the arbitrator's part were dismissed, as the court found no compelling evidence of bias or failure to provide a fair hearing. Tobias’ withdrawal from proceedings followed by his legal representatives’ decision was interpreted as a tactical choice, which undermined claims of misconduct ([36][38]).

The court found no merit in the complaint regarding delivery of the arbitral award, stating that, under the circumstances, procedural irregularities did not substantially affect the outcome ([18]).

Regarding the cost order against Naidoo, the court considered her role and control over the litigation as indicative of a commercial interest, justifying the costs order against her as appropriate ([22][40]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Tobias’ appeal, thereby upholding the High Court’s ruling that the arbitrator had jurisdiction and that the arbitration process had not been compromised by misconduct. The court confirmed that the arbitrator's findings about the fraudulent nature of the ownership conveyed to Tobias stood firm, reaffirming EP's ownership of the property ([43]).

The case reinforces the importance of participating parties to a dispute to raise all relevant objections early in proceedings and reflects the binding nature of jurisdictional agreements in arbitration. Furthermore, the court's decision on costs illustrates the potential accountability of funders involved in litigation, especially when they take an active role in directing the proceedings.
