Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

# FIRAC Case Note: Ackermans Limited v Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service

**Citation:** Ackermans Ltd v Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service [2010] ZASCA 131 (1 October 2010)

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

---

### F – Facts:
The case arises from two appeals by Ackermans Limited and Pep Stores (SA) Limited against the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (CSARS), following a denial of deductions for contingent liabilities by the South Gauteng Tax Court ([1]). On 1 March 2004, Ackermans sold its retail business as a going concern to Pepkor Ltd for a total of R1,129,440,402, which included a substantial amount of liabilities, specifically R329,440,402 ([2]). 

The liabilities included three contingent liabilities totaling R17,174,777: 
1. A post-retirement medical aid benefit obligation of R9,880,666,
2. Employee bonus obligations of R6,394,111,
3. Repair obligations under property leases amounting to R900,000 ([2]).

The appellants claimed that by selling the business at a reduced price due to these liabilities, they effectively incurred expenditure equal to the amount of these liabilities, thereby entitling them to a deduction under section 11(a) of the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962 ([5]).

### I – Issues:
The key legal questions were:
1. Did Ackermans incur "expenditure" as contemplated in section 11(a) of the Income Tax Act when it agreed to a lower purchase price due to contingent liabilities it transferred to Pepkor?
2. Was the manner of discharging the purchase price by Pepkor relevant to establishing whether Ackermans incurred actual expenditure ([6])?

### R – Rules:
The legal framework involved primarily section 11(a) of the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962, which allows for deductions of expenditures and losses that have been "actually incurred" in producing income. Precedents and interpretations from the courts regarding what constitutes "expenditure," including established definitions and principles about conditional liabilities and deductions, were pivotal to the court's assessment ([6], [8]). 

The appellant's arguments relied on cases like *SIR v John Cullum Construction Co (Pty) Ltd* 1965 (4) SA 697 (A), and foreign authorities including *Hancock v General Reversionary & Investment Co Ltd* [1919] 1 KB 25, and various Australian cases that discussed expenditures made to relieve a taxpayer from future obligations ([5], [6]).

### A – Application:
The court analyzed whether the contingent liabilities constituted "expenditure actually incurred." Ackermans claimed that because it reduced the purchase price due to the contingent liabilities, it effectively incurred expenditure ([5], [11]). However, the court rejected the argument that such a contractual arrangement equated to an actual financial outlay. The judges emphasized that the sale agreement did not create any obligation for Ackermans to pay Pepkor thus no actual liability was incurred ([8], [11]).

The ruling cited that even if the economic consequence resulted in a lesser purchase price, this did not translate into a legal liability or expenditure. Additionally, the argument that the net effect of the transaction resulted in a definable financial loss was dismissed as it did not meet the established definitions of "expenditure" in terms of South African tax law ([10], [12]). 

The court also considered the implications of denied deductions, clarifying that Pepkor could claim the deductions when the liabilities became unconditional, thus undermining the appellants' claims of unfair treatment ([9]).

### C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal affirmed the decisions of the lower court, holding that Ackermans did not incur real expenditure as defined under section 11(a) of the Income Tax Act in the context of the transaction ([12]). Consequently, both appeals were dismissed with costs awarded to the respondent. This judgment clarifies the limitations of what constitutes expenditure under tax law, particularly in scenarios involving complex sales transactions and contingent liabilities, maintaining a stringent interpretation of the applicable legal standards. 

The implications of the ruling are significant for similar future transactions, emphasizing that mere reductions in purchase prices due to liabilities are insufficient grounds for claiming tax deductions unless actual cash flows or legal obligations are evidenced ([12]).
