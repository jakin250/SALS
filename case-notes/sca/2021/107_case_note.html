Case Note: J E L v The Ad Hoc Central Authority for the Republic of South Africa and Others [2021] ZASCA 107

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 107 FIRAC Case Note --- F – Facts T

Case Note: J E L v The Ad Hoc Central Authority for the Republic of South Africa and Others [2021] ZASCA 107

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

This case arose from an application for leave to appeal against a judgment of the Western Cape Division of the High Court, which ordered the return of three minor children from South Africa to Thailand under the Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction (1980) (the “Convention”). The applicant, J E L, removed her children (A, aged 12, and twins K and M, aged 9) from Thailand without the consent of the second respondent, T C L, their father, in December 2019 ([1–2]).

The couple, married in the United States on February 26, 2007, had divorced in Thailand in June 2018, where a Compromise Agreement had granted both parents joint custody ([3]). Post-divorce, the applicant relocated within Thailand, leading to limited contact between the second respondent and the children ([4]).

Faced with ongoing difficulties in securing visitation rights and contact, the second respondent sought the return of the children after learning of their removal. The High Court, under Judge Desai, found the removal wrongful under Article 3 of the Convention and examined the application of the Article 13(b) defense. The applicant argued that a return to Thailand would expose the children to grave risk of harm based on allegations of sexual molestation and domestic violence ([5, 9]). The High Court dismissed the applicant’s defenses, ordered the children’s return, with conditions ensuring ongoing support and monitoring ([9]).

Following this, the applicant sought leave to appeal, arguing violations of the rights of the children and herself as a mother and raising concerns about their safety if returned to Thailand ([1, 2]).

I – Issues

The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the applicant had established reasonable prospects of success on appeal, particularly concerning her defense under Article 13(b) of the Convention. Specifically, the court needed to determine:

  1. Did the applicant sufficiently demonstrate that returning the children to Thailand would expose them to grave risk of physical or psychological harm?
  2. Did the High Court erroneously weigh the evidence regarding the allegations of abuse and the implications of the return order on the children's welfare? ([1, 10]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal authorities and precedents included:

  1. Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction (1980): Particularly Articles 3 (wrongful removal) and 13(b) (grave risk defense).
  2. Children’s Act 38 of 2005: Implementing the Convention in South African law.
  3. Pennello v Pennello (Chief Family Advocate As Amicus Curiae) 2004 (3) SA 117 (SCA): This case established the onus on the party resisting the return to prove defenses under Article 13 of the Convention on the balance of probabilities ([7, 10]).

In essence, Article 13(b) allows for a refusal to return a child if the person opposing return shows there is a grave risk of harm to the child upon return ([7, 10]).

A – Application

In evaluating the application, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court's assessment that the applicant had not discharged the burden imposed under Article 13(b). It noted that the serious allegations of sexual molestation were vague and contested, suggesting they could be better examined in Thailand by the appropriate authorities ([10, 12]). Counsel for the applicant failed to articulate potential specific harms that may arise should the High Court’s order be implemented.

The court observed that adequate protective measures were imposed by the High Court, including arrangements for psychological support and maintenance during the transition ([11, 12]). The High Court had structured the return in a manner intended to mitigate any risks perceived by the applicant, thus demonstrating foresight in ensuring the safety and well-being of the children.

Importantly, the Court reiterated that the best interests of a child removed from their habitual jurisdiction typically favor their return to that jurisdiction to allow the local authorities to properly adjudicate custody matters ([13]).

The Supreme Court found that the applicant's repeated claims of potential harm appeared speculative and were insufficient to override the presumption favoring return under the Convention ([14, 15]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the application for leave to appeal, affirming the High Court's order for the return of the children to Thailand. The Court concluded that the applicant did not present reasonable grounds to believe that the children would face grave harm upon their return ([15]). The ruling reinforced the principle that Hague Convention objectives prioritize children’s welfare and the enforcement of parental rights through established legal channels rather than unilateral actions. The dismissal, while emphasizing the protection necessary for the children, indicated a robust judicial reliance on cooperative international legal standards, ensuring that parents respect the jurisdictional authority in custody disputes.

The implications of this decision reaffirm the judiciary's role under the Convention in fostering international cooperation for child welfare, indicating that assertions of risk must be substantiated with clear evidence rather than allegations alone. Each party bore their own costs, reflecting the challenging nature of custody disputes and the shared interest in the welfare of the children involved ([16]).
