Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# Case Note: United Democratic Movement and Another v Lebashe Investment Group (Pty) Ltd and Others (1032/2019) [2021] ZASCA 4

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
This case arose from a letter dated 26 June 2018, sent by the first appellant, the United Democratic Movement (UDM), and its leader, Bantu Holomisa, to the President of South Africa. The letter accused various respondents of unlawful actions related to the Public Investment Corporation (PIC) and requested an investigation into these allegations. The letter was also published on the UDM website, prompting the respondents—Lebashe Investment Group (Pty) Ltd, Harith General Partners (Pty) Ltd, Harith Fund Managers (Pty) Ltd, and individual respondents—to seek an interim interdict against the appellants for what they claimed was defamatory content ([paras 1–2]).

On 16 July 2018, Tlhapi J of the Gauteng Division of the High Court granted an interdict prohibiting the appellants from repeating certain allegations against the respondents. The interdict ordered the appellants to cease and desist from making further publications regarding the allegations and mandated the removal of the original letter from their website and social media platforms ([para 3]).

Despite the appellants receiving permission to appeal from the High Court on 26 August 2019, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ultimately struck the appeal from the roll on the grounds that the order was not appealable, even though leave to appeal had been granted ([paras 4–5]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issue in this case centered on whether the interim interdict issued by the High Court was appealable. The SCA had to determine if the order had a "final effect" or if it was merely interim, thereby not meeting the standard for appealability under common law. Furthermore, even if the order was interim, the court had to consider whether the interests of justice warranted the appeal being heard ([paras 1–2], [4–5]).

Additional issues related to the constitutional rights of the appellants were raised, particularly concerning their freedom of expression and political rights, highlighting whether these rights were irreparably harmed by the interim order ([para 19]).

### R – Rules
The SCA relied on several legal principles regarding the appealability of interim orders, notably outlined in cases like *City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality v Afriforum* and *Zweni v Minister of Law and Order*. The general requirement for an order to be appealable is that it must be final in effect, definitive in the rights of the parties, and have the effect of disposing of a substantial portion of the relief claimed. However, the Constitutional Court in *Afriforum* emphasized that the interests of justice could permit appealability even in cases involving interim orders under specific circumstances ([paras 7–8], [10]).

In considering the merits of the appeal, the court examined factors such as whether irreparable harm would result if the appeal was not heard and whether the interim order had a substantial immediate effect ([paras 8–9], [24]).

### A – Application
The SCA found that the interim interdict did not possess the attributes of finality required for appealability. While the appellants argued that their constitutional rights were infringed, the court highlighted that the order was temporary and did not preclude them from expressing their views in Parliament or elsewhere. The court emphasized that mere allegations of harm were insufficient; instead, there needed to be compelling reasons demonstrating the order’s impact on irreparable harm, which was not established in this case.

The SCA also questioned the necessity of the interim order after the allegations had already been made public and noted that the proceedings were ongoing in the trial court, which was competent to resolve the disputes satisfactorily. The court concluded that the delay in processing the matter was not a grounds for appeal, given that no extraordinary circumstances prevented the regular progress of the action ([paras 24–26]).

### C – Conclusion
In conclusion, the SCA held that the appeal was struck from the roll with costs awarded to the respondents. The court determined that the interim interdict was not appealable as it lacked the final characteristics necessary for such a proceeding, and the interests of justice did not dictate that the court should entertain the appeal. Ultimately, this judgment underscores the adherence to legal principles regarding interim orders and emphasizes the balancing act between protecting reputational interests and safeguarding rights to free expression, particularly relating to political discourse ([paras 26–27]).

This case serves as a significant touchstone regarding the broader principles affecting interim interdicts and the conditions under which appeals can be heard in South African law, particularly relating to political expression and accountability.
