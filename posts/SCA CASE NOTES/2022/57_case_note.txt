Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# Case Note: Minister of Police and Another v Erasmus (366/2021) [2022] ZASCA 57 

## FIRAC Method 

### F - Facts
The case arises from the events surrounding the arrest and subsequent detention of Edward Alberto Erasmus from 4 to 19 May 2016 ([1]). On 4 May 2016, police received a report regarding a housebreaking and theft at a property in Port Elizabeth, and upon arrival at the scene, found Mr. Erasmus with items believed to be stolen ([4]-[5]). Police arrested Mr. Erasmus after verifying sufficient grounds for suspicion based on witness statements.

Mr. Erasmus was charged with housebreaking and theft, offences classified under Schedule 1 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA) ([5]). Following his arrest, he was detained until his first court appearance on 5 May 2016. At his appearance, his rights were explained, and he opted for representation by a Legal Aid lawyer. The magistrate postponed the case to 12 May 2016 for the bail application process ([6]), consequently remanding Mr. Erasmus in custody.

On 12 May, a bail application was made, during which Mr. Erasmus was unable to post the R500 bail set by the magistrate. Thus, he remained in custody until the matter was struck off the roll on 19 May 2016 due to the absence of the police docket, leading to his release ([7]). Mr. Erasmus subsequently instituted a claim for damages against the Minister of Police and the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) in the Port Elizabeth Regional Court.

The regional court initially found that the first period of detention was lawful but that the second was unlawful, awarding Mr. Erasmus R250,000 for damages ([2]). Dissatisfied, both parties appealed. The Eastern Cape Division of the High Court upheld Mr. Erasmus's cross-appeal for damages related to his unlawful arrest and detention, resulting in increased compensation and prompting further appeal by the appellants to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([3], [8]).

### I - Issues
The primary legal issues in this case center around two areas: the liability of the Minister of Police and the NDPP concerning the unlawful arrest and detention of Mr. Erasmus, particularly whether the police actions during both periods of detention were lawful ([9]-[10]). 

1. Was the arrest and initial detention of Mr. Erasmus on 4 May 2016 and subsequent detention post-court appearance unlawful?
2. Were the police and prosecutors liable for malicious detention during the second period of detention from 5 May to 19 May 2016?
3. What damages, if any, are appropriate for the unlawful arrest and detention?
  
### R - Rules
The case primarily relies on the principles of delict (tort law) in South Africa, particularly the action for damages arising from *actio iniuriarum* – the malicious deprivation of liberty ([11]). The key rules derived from previous case law include:

1. **Malicious Detention**: Liability for malicious detention arises if the defendant instigates the detention without reasonable or probable cause or with an intent to injure (animus iniuriandi) ([11]).
2. **Wrongful Detention**: Both wrongful and malicious deprivation of liberty are actionable under South African law, where wrongful deprivation occurs if the defendant or their agents illegally detain the plaintiff ([12]).
3. **Legal Provisions**: According to Section 60(11)(b) of the CPA, an accused charged with schedule 5 offences (due to prior convictions) may be detained until the court determines bail, placing the onus on the accused to demonstrate their entitlement to bail.

### A - Application
In analyzing the legality of the arrest and detention, the court noted that the police had sufficient reason to arrest Mr. Erasmus based on witness statements and evidence at the scene of the crime ([5]). Thus, the first period of detention (4-5 May 2016) was deemed lawful by the regional court and subsequently scrutinized in appeals ([10]).

For the second period of detention (5-19 May 2016), the court focused on whether the police and the NDPP acted unlawfully. It found that no evidence supported the claim of malicious intent or wrongful detention by the police or the NDPP ([12], [14]). The police officer's decision not to oppose bail was consistent with their obligation under the CPA. The magistrate's decision to remand Mr. Erasmus was attributed to his inability to post bail rather than any wrongful conduct by the police ([14]-[16]).

The Supreme Court emphasized that the NDPP could not be held liable unless it was shown that they acted without reasonable cause or with an intent to harm ([17]). As the evidence from prosecutors demonstrated reasonable cause to oppose bail owing to the serious nature of the charges, the court found that they had acted appropriately, undermining claims of liability ([16]).

In discussing damages, the court acknowledged the high court's award was excessive regarding the initial period of detention, resulting in a revised award of R25,000 instead of R50,000, corresponding to the circumstances of his detention which lasted just over 20 hours in unpleasant conditions ([17]).

### C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal regarding the NDPP's and the Minister's liability for the second period of unlawful detention and reduced the damages concerning the first period. The Court's ruling underscored the separation of responsibilities between police and prosecution in instances of detention, confirming that as long as judicial processes are appropriately followed and there are legitimate grounds for detaining an accused, liability may not attach to initial detentions even if later judicial processes fail ([19]). The judgment highlighted the need for clear evidence of both wrongful and malicious actions to secure liability in cases of detention and emphasized the principle that prosecutorial discretion exercised reasonably and in good faith should not expose prosecutors to liability. 

**Order**: The appeal was granted with costs, the previous judgments were set aside, and liability for arrest and detention was affirmed only in relation to the first period for the amended award ([19]).
