# Case Note: AD & Another v DW & Others

**Court:** Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Case No.:** CCT 48/07  
**Citation:** [2007] ZACC 27  
**Judgment Delivered by:** Sachs J  
**Date of Judgment:** 7 December 2007

---

## FIRAC Case Note

### F - Facts

On 14 November 2004, a newborn, referred to as Baby R, was discovered abandoned in a veld in Roodepoort, South Africa ([1]). Following her abandonment, Baby R was placed in the foster care of DW and CW, an American couple residing in South Africa and managing a sanctuary for children. The applicants, AD and DD, who were friends of the foster couple and also American citizens, visited and formed a bond with Baby R, seeking to adopt her if possible ([1]–[2]).

Upon seeking legal advice, the applicants were informed that South African adoption policies effectively barred foreign nationals from adopting without meeting stringent local requirements. Consequently, they sought an order from the Johannesburg High Court granting them sole custody and guardianship of Baby R to facilitate her adoption in the United States ([2]–[3]). The High Court dismissed the application, emphasizing the necessity for the Children's Court to determine the best interests of the child, citing the Children’s Care Act, which governs adoption processes ([4]). 

The applicants subsequently appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which upheld the lower court's decision ([5]–[6]). The Supreme Court of Appeal's majority expressed concern about the implications of bypassing the Children's Court, reflecting a preference for the principle of subsidiarity, which prioritizes local placement of children before considering inter-country adoption ([8]–[9]).

Following the SCA's dismissal, the applicants sought leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court, which granted them an audience ([11]). The matter was approached with additional input from the Centre for Child Law and government departments involved in child welfare ([11]).

### I - Issues

The constitutional issues at the heart of this case include:

1. **Jurisdiction of the High Court:** Whether the High Court possesses the jurisdiction to grant an order of sole custody and sole guardianship with a view to facilitate an adoption in another country ([18]).
2. **Interpretation of Best Interests:** How section 28(2) of the Constitution, which asserts that a child's best interests are paramount, should be interpreted within the context of inter-country adoption ([18]).
3. **Application of the Principle of Subsidiarity:** Consideration of whether the principle of subsidiarity, which advocates local placement of children, serves as a bar to granting custody orders for foreign adoptions ([21]).

### R - Rules

The decision draws on several legal rules and established precedents, including:

- **Children’s Care Act 74 of 1983:** The governing statute concerning adoption in South Africa, which mandates that the Children's Court is the primary authority for adoption matters ([4]).
- **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa:** Particularly section 28, which protects the rights of children and stipulates that their best interests are of paramount importance ([12]).
- **Fitzpatrick v Minister of Welfare and Population Development:** This case established that no blanket prohibition exists preventing foreign nationals from adopting South African children and underscored the flexible interpretation of the best interests of the child ([23]).
- **Hague Convention on the Protection of Children and Co-operation in Respect of Inter-country Adoption:** Identifying the normative framework for international adoptions which requires that countries consider local placements as a priority before considering inter-country adoption ([36]).

### A - Application

In their analysis, the Constitutional Court examined the circumstances surrounding the applicants' attempts to adopt Baby R. It emphasized that the Children’s Court is equipped to handle adoption proceedings through a structured framework aimed at ensuring children's best interests, which the High Court deemed an appropriate route ([29]). The Court noted that using the High Court for sole custody and guardianship risked undermining the protective legal framework established to safeguard children’s welfare ([30]).

The majority emphasized that while the High Court retained jurisdiction regarding children, exercising that jurisdiction through proper legal mechanisms—specifically within the Children's Court—was paramount to ensuring adherence to both the Constitution's provisions and international norms on child welfare ([31]).

Regarding the principle of subsidiarity, the judgment emphasized that while this principle must be respected, it should not serve as an absolute barrier against inter-country adoption. The focus required a holistic evaluation of Baby R's interests, countering strict interpretations of legal residency or nationality restrictions ([36]–[40]).

### C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court found for the applicants, granting leave to appeal and substituting the Supreme Court of Appeal's decision with an order reflecting the terms of settlement reached during proceedings. This included an expedited application for adoption in the Children’s Court and recognized Baby R as having been abandoned, thus clearing the way for her adoption by the applicants ([[62]–[63]). 

This ruling affirms the parameters within which inter-country adoptions may be adjudicated in South Africa while reinforcing the critical nature of prioritizing the child’s best interests. It underscores the necessity for a structured legal framework to ensure that children's rights are protected, including the standards established under international law. Therefore, the decision has far-reaching implications, shaping future inter-country adoptions and the operational effectiveness of South African child welfare laws.
