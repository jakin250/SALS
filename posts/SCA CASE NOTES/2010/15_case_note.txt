# FIRAC Case Note: **Kwikspace Modular Buildings v Sabodala Mining Company** (173/09) [2010] ZASCA 15

**F – Facts**:  
The case arises from a contractual dispute between Kwikspace Modular Buildings Limited (the Contractor) and Sabodala Mining Company SARL (the Principal). On 30 December 2006, the Contractor entered into a written contract with the Principal for the supply and installation of an accommodation village in Senegal. The contract was defined by various documents, including special conditions and general conditions of contract, the latter of which were based on the Australian Standard General Conditions of Contract AS 2124 – 1992 ([2]).

At the core of this dispute were performance guarantees issued by Nedbank, each in the amount of R2,651,254, which the Principal sought to call upon following disputes regarding payment that arose during contract performance. On 24 October 2008, the Principal notified the Contractor of its intention to convert the provided performance guarantees into cash. The Contractor sought an urgent interdict to prevent this action, claiming that the performance guarantees were not in line with the contract’s provisions regarding security ([5]).

Initially, the Johannesburg High Court granted an interim interdict which temporarily restrained the Principal from demanding payment under the performance guarantees. However, this interim relief was contested and the final interdict was ultimately denied by the High Court. The Contractor then appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([5]-[16]).

**I – Issues**:  
The principal legal questions in this case revolve around:
1. Whether the Contractor could interdict the Principal from calling upon the performance guarantees based on the terms of the building contract, specifically General Condition 5.5, which governs the conditions of calling in such security ([1], [6]).
2. Whether the contractual terms provided a basis for limiting the right of the Principal to present performance guarantees that were otherwise unconditional ([1], [6]).

**R – Rules**:  
The court's examination involved interpreting relevant Australian contract law principles, especially in relation to the interpretation and enforcement of performance guarantees. Key rules and precedents cited included:
- **General Condition (GC) 5**: Dictated terms regarding security, contract performance, and specified conditions under which security could be converted ([3], [14]).
- **Case Law**: The court referred to *Wood Hall Ltd v Pipeline Authority* and subsequent Australian cases which established that a contract can impose conditions on the right of a Principal to call on performance guarantees, even if the guarantees are stated to be unconditional ([6], [9]-[12]).
- The principle that a contractor may not be unjustly deprived of contractual protections set out within the building contract, against the freedom to call upon performance guarantees ([9], [22]).

**A – Application**:  
In its judgment, the court engaged with the Contractor's proposition that GC 5.5 incorporated a necessary condition requiring the Principal to demonstrate a legitimate claim to call upon the guarantees, rather than simply allowing for a bona fide assertion of entitlement. The court emphasized that the underlying contract's provisions would indeed define the parameters of the Principal’s right to call the guarantees ([18]-[20]). 

The court rejected the Contractor’s argument that a tacit term should be incorporated to mandate the Principal's requirement to specify grounds for the call on the guarantee. It concluded that existing contractual stipulations adequately provided for the functionality and interdependencies agreed upon by both parties ([19]-[20]). Here, the court cited Australian standards where it was adjudged that unless defined fraud or clear unconscionable conduct exists, the rights under performance guarantees are typically enforceable ([18], [21]).

Moreover, despite the controversies expressed over the timing and manner in which the Principal intended to exercise its rights, the court found no compelling reasons to accept the Contractor's substantive arguments preventing the calling of the guarantees, particularly because the Principal had established claims of non-payment substantiated under GC 42 ([15]), and that disputes over valuations could not preclude the Principal's entitlements.

**C – Conclusion**:  
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, solidifying the notion that a contractually backed performance guarantee does indeed confer substantive rights that operate unfettered by claims of dispute unless explicitly stipulated otherwise. The holding clarified the capacity of a Principal to act upon unconditional guarantees provided clear conditions of entitlement under the contract as interpreted within the framework of relevant case law from Australia, thereby reinforcing the binding nature of such performance obligations ([23]).

The implications of this case underscore the importance of clarity within contractual terms and performances guarantees in construction and related contracts, illustrating the court's inclination to uphold the enforceability of clearly articulated rights against the backdrop of contractual performance dynamics.
