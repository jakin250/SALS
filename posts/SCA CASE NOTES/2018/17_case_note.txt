# CASE NOTE: BANNISTER’S PRINT v D & A CALENDARS CC (1078/2016) [2018] ZASCA 17

## FIRAC Method Case Note

### F – Facts

[1] This case arises from a protracted dispute between Bannister's Print (Pty) Ltd (the Appellant) and D & A Calendars CC (the First Respondent), alongside Darryl Albert Bannister (the Second Respondent), both entities associated with the Bannister family. Tensions among family members resulted in litigation over financial claims, leading to a series of actions commenced by Print in April 2011 against Calendars and Darryl.

[2] The initial action involved Print seeking payment, while Calendars countered with its claims. Darryl, representing Calendars, engaged Mr. M Strauss from Ian Levitt Attorneys to handle the litigation. Due to the turnover of staff at the firm, Mr. Marc Lieberthal was assigned to the case without having been properly admitted as an attorney, an issue later acknowledged by Mr. Levitt.

[3] In February 2013, a purported settlement agreement emerged from communications between Lieberthal and Darryl. The agreement proposed the withdrawal of claims and counterclaims. Darryl, under the impression that an agreement was being finalized, made amendments to a draft and signed it, only to find that his amendments were not incorporated into the finalized document submitted to court.

[4] The court accepted the agreement as valid despite irregularities, and enforcement procedures were initiated by Print, leading to a writ of execution being issued against Calendars following the alleged settlement.

[5] In July 2013, Darryl and Calendars sought a declaratory order from the South Gauteng High Court, asserting that the agreement was void owing to fraud—specifically, that Lieberthal had forged Darryl's initials. This finding held that the settlement did not reflect the true consensus between the parties, leading Satchwell J to declare the agreement a nullity, a position disputed by Print, resulting in the appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal.

### I – Issues

[6] The central legal issue pertains to whether a party can be held bound to an allegedly fraudulent agreement when the representation leading to that agreement was formed by a forger (Lieberthal in this case), despite indications that the party purportedly making the representation had the authority to do so via agency principles.

[7] Specifically, the court needed to ascertain if notions of estoppel could apply, whereby a party may be precluded from denying the validity of an agreement based on representations made by its agents, even when those representations are shown to be fraudulent.

### R – Rules

[8] The legal principles surrounding agency, particularly apparent authority and estoppel, were pivotal in this case. The judgment referenced several precedents, most notably MEC v Kruizenga [2010] 4 SA 122 (SCA) and Absa Bank Ltd v Moore [2017] 1 SA 255 (CC), to clarify how courts traditionally assess the authority of legal representatives.

[9] The court reiterated the principle that "fraud unravels all" but distinguished this notion by emphasizing the specific circumstances in which it applies. The court acknowledged that while fraud can void contracts, a party misled by representations that an agent had authority to act may still be bound to the agreement they believed to be valid.

[10] Furthermore, the SCA clarified the difference between actual authority, where the agent has the explicit power to act on behalf of the principal, and ostensible authority, where the principal's conduct may create a reasonable belief in a third party regarding the agent's authority.

### A – Application

[11] In making its determination, the SCA closely examined the circumstances surrounding Lieberthal's unauthorized actions. It specifically noted that although Darryl had engaged with Lieberthal, the resulting document that appeared to settle the case was a forgery and, therefore, an unequivocal nullity.

[12] The court rejected Print's estoppel argument, noting that the reliance on the forged document did not align with the principles established in the Kruizenga case. Print had not substantiated a reasonable belief that Lieberthal had the authority to represent Calendars definitively, as their reliance was on a fraudulent instrument rather than the actor's conduct.

[13] The distinction made in the ruling stressed that the genuine intention of the parties must be discerned from their conduct and the documentation presented. It found that Darryl’s amendments to the draft were not incorporated into the final agreement which thus did not reflect any consensus or agreement from Calendars’ side.

[14] Furthermore, the SCA recognized that Darryl had been effectively misled by Lieberthal’s actions, emphasizing that a forged document meant that no legitimate agreement existed between the parties to be enforced.

### C – Conclusion

[15] Consequently, the SCA upheld the lower court's declaration that the agreement was devoid of legal validity due to the fraud committed by Lieberthal. The ruling reaffirmed the principle that fraudulent actions of an agent cannot bind the principal unless there has been a valid meeting of minds that complies with contract law principles. 

[16] The court dismissed Print’s appeal, upholding the findings of Satchwell J regarding the nullity of the agreement and confirming that the parties' true intention was not reflected in the forged agreement.

[17] As for costs, the SCA ruled that they should be awarded in favor of Calendars and Darryl, reflecting the circumstances surrounding the litigation's complexity and the necessity for two counsels.

[18] This decision serves as a significant cautionary tale regarding legal authority and the implications of forged documents within contract law. It delineates the limits of estoppel and reinforces that agency cannot protect a party against fraudulent misrepresentation by their own legal representatives.
