Constitutional Court 2023 Ledla Structural Development (Pty) Ltd and Others v Special Investigating Unit [2023] ZACC 8

Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa

Neutral citation: Ledla Structural Development (Pty) Ltd and Others v Special Investigating Unit [2023] ZACC 8

Coram: Kollapen J, Madlanga J, Majiedt J, Mathopo J, Mhlantla J, Mlambo AJ, Theron J, Tshiqi J

Judgment: Mhlantla J (unanimous)

Heard on: 24 May 2022
Decided on: 10 March 2023

F - Facts

The case revolves around Ledla Structural Development (Pty) Ltd (Ledla) and its directors, Rhulani Lehong and Kgodisho Norman Lehong, who challenged a decision by the Special Investigating Unit (SIU) concerning a procurement contract for personal protective equipment (PPE) amidst the COVID-19 crisis ([1–3]).

In March 2020, the Gauteng Department of Health reported a PPE shortage amounting to R1.7 billion. Subsequently, under urgency due to the pandemic, the Department deviated from standard tender procedures for procurement ([3–5]). The SIU found that the contracts awarded to Ledla, which replaced another contractor, Royal Bhaca, were procured unlawfully, with significant irregularities in the process, including the involvement of politically connected individuals ([6–12]).

In August 2020, the SIU sought to invalidate the contract between Ledla and the Department and sought preservation orders for the funds paid, claiming they were the proceeds of unlawful activities ([14–15]). The Special Tribunal ruled against Ledla, declaring the contract unlawful and ordering the forfeiture of R38.76 million to the state ([22]).

The applicants sought leave to appeal the Tribunal's decision, arguing that the Tribunal lacked jurisdiction and that they were denied the right to a fair hearing and due process ([24–28]).

I - Issues

The primary legal issues raised in the appeal included:

  1. Whether the Special Tribunal is a court with the power to conduct legality reviews and issue preservation and forfeiture orders ([39]).
  2. The appropriateness of such a determination in light of the right to a fair trial under section 34 of the Constitution ([40]).
  3. The legal standing of the applicants to challenge the procedural validity of the Tribunal’s orders ([40]).

R - Rules

The Court's analysis is founded on several key legal principles and provisions:

  • Section 166 of the Constitution: It defines courts, including the High Court and other prescribed entities, establishing whether the Special Tribunal qualifies as a court ([48]).
  • Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA): It stipulates the rights to judicial review of administrative actions, defining tribunals in a manner that may exclude the Special Tribunal from review authority on administrative actions due to its nature ([53]).
  • SIU Act: Section 8(2) confers jurisdiction upon the Special Tribunal to hear civil proceedings stemming from the investigations by the SIU, indicating a broad scope of powers ([64]).

A - Application

The Court carefully evaluated the nature and function of the Special Tribunal:

  • It concluded that, while the Tribunal engages in judicial-like processes and is composed of judges, it did not fit the criteria of a court as defined in section 166. Its establishment and operational framework suggested an ad hoc nature rather than one akin to statutory courts ([50–52]).
  • Despite not being a court, the Tribunal was empowered under the SIU Act to conduct legality reviews due to the broad language in its provisions. The review authority conferred upon it was consistent with remedial powers intended to combat corruption within state procurement processes ([66–70]).
  • The Tribunal also appropriately exercised its discretion to issue preservation and forfeiture orders, as these powers were aligned with its mandate to safeguard state interests in preventing corruption and maladministration ([71–72]).

C - Conclusion

The Court ruled that the Special Tribunal is not a court as delineated in the Constitution; however, it possesses jurisdiction to conduct legality reviews and grant preservation and forfeiture orders. The appeal by Ledla was therefore dismissed with costs, including the costs of two counsel, reaffirming the Tribunal’s authority and underscoring the systemic need to address procurement corruption, particularly evident during the pandemic ([75–76]).

Implications

This judgment clarifies the legal status and powers of the Special Tribunal, reinforcing its role in ensuring accountability within public procurement frameworks, especially in crisis contexts. It asserts the necessity for transparent governance mechanisms and the oversight functions of state bodies in curbing corruption.
