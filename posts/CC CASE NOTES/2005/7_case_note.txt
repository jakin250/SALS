Source Folder: 2005

# Case Note: Laugh It Off Promotions CC v South African Breweries International (Finance) B.V.

## FIRAC Case Note

### F - Facts
This case examines the tension between the right to freedom of expression, as protected by section 16 of the South African Constitution, and the protection of intellectual property, specifically regarding registered trade marks under section 34(1)(c) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993. The applicant, Laugh It Off Promotions CC (hereafter referred to as "Laugh It Off"), is a close corporation engaged in producing T-shirts that use altered imagery and wording of well-known brands for the purpose of social commentary ([1, 4, 5]). The respondent, South African Breweries International (Finance) B.V. (now known as SABMiller), is the owner of the trade marks in question, particularly the well-regarded "CARLING BLACK LABEL" brand ([5, 8]).

The applicant's T-shirts featured modifications of the respondent's branding, altering phrases such as "Black Label" to "Black Labour" and "Carling Beer" to "White Guilt," promoting a critical narrative surrounding race and exploitation in South Africa ([9, 10]). The respondent initiated legal action against Laugh It Off in the Cape High Court, asserting that the applicant's use of its trade marks constituted an infringement that was likely to cause material detriment to the distinctive character and reputation of the marks under section 34(1)(c) of the Act ([12-15]).

After the High Court granted an interdict against Laugh It Off, which it appealed, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) eventually upheld the decision, albeit with amendments ([16-17]). Laugh It Off subsequently sought leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court, which accepted the application due to the constitutional implications involved ([27]).

### I - Issues
The legal issues central to this case are:
1. Whether the applicant's use of the respondent's trade marks constituted an infringement under section 34(1)(c) of the Trade Marks Act ([18]).
2. The balancing act required between the protection of intellectual property and the right to freedom of expression under section 16 of the Constitution, particularly in the context of artistic and social commentary ([43, 48]).
3. Whether the manner in which Laugh It Off appropriated the trade marks amounted to parody, and if so, how that factor played into the determination of unfair advantage or detriment ([62-64]).

### R - Rules
The relevant statutory provisions include:
- **Section 16 of the Constitution**: Provides that everyone has the right to freedom of expression, which includes freedom of the press, the right to receive or impart information, freedom of artistic creativity, and academic freedom ([2]).
- **Section 34(1)(c) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993**: Prohibits the unauthorized use of identical or similar trade marks in such a manner that would likely be detrimental to their distinctive character or reputation, even in the absence of confusion ([3]).

Case law addressing the balance between free expression and trade mark protections, particularly the Supreme Court of Appeals' decisions, also informed rule application, including the need for substantial detriment to be demonstrated for successful claims ([19]).

### A - Application
The Constitutional Court, led by Justice Moseneke, analyzed the application of the law to the facts. The court first assessed whether the applicant's conduct constituted an infringement as defined under the Trade Marks Act. It found that the applicant had admitted to utilizing the trade marks in relation to its commercially sold T-shirts without authorization ([22-23]).

While considering the constitutional aspect, the court criticized the SCA for failing to adequately account for Laugh It Off’s freedom of expression. Moseneke J contended that the lower court should have balanced the interests of the applicant's expressive rights against SAB's proprietary rights. The judgment noted that while trade marks enjoy protection, the enforcement of such rights should not suppress legitimate commentary and criticism integral to democratic discourse ([43-45]).

On the issue of potential detriment, the court revisited the SCA's assertion that the message “black labour white guilt” carried an inherently negative association with the respondent's brand. However, it found insufficient evidence that this would lead to a substantial loss in the respondent's market share or damage to its reputation ([55-59]). Thus, the court held that the applicant's actions were largely expressive rather than exploitative.

### C - Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ruled in favor of Laugh It Off, asserting that the appeal against the SCA’s judgment should be upheld. The court emphasized the necessity of protecting freedom of expression — especially in the context of societal critique — and determined that the respondent had failed to establish a likelihood of significant economic detriment to its trade marks ([68-69]). The ratio decidendi underscores that, while businesses have vested interests in protecting their brand images, the law does not enable them to suppress expressions critical of their practices, particularly when such expressions are articulated via parody, which holds substantial social value in a democratic context.

This landmark ruling not only reinforces the protections afforded to free expression in South Africa but also sets a precedent on the permissible scope of artistic critique against established commercial enterprises, particularly in the context of societal issues like race and exploitation ([108-110]).
