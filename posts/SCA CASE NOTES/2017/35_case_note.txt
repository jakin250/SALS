Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note: *Cathay Pacific Airways Ltd v Lin & another* [2017] ZASCA 35

## F – Facts
[1] This appeal arises from contempt of court orders made against Cathay Pacific Airways Ltd (the first appellant) and its employee Shirley Jones (the second appellant) by Spilg J in the Gauteng Local Division of the High Court. The controversy began when the respondents, Mr. Hai Lin and Ms. Ruihong Weng, sought urgent relief after their three children were denied entry into South Africa and were about to be deported back to Hong Kong based on allegations that they possessed fraudulent permanent residency permits ([2]–[4]).

[2] On 26 July 2014, the children arrived at OR Tambo International Airport where they were stopped by immigration officers. The parents, promptly realizing the severity of the situation, engaged attorney Mr. Ashraf Mohamed Essop, who telephonically contacted Judge Wright, on urgent duty, at around noon. Judge Wright issued a 'telephonic interdict' prohibiting Cathay Pacific from boarding the two younger children on flight CX748 due to depart shortly thereafter ([5]–[8]).

[3] However, due to multiple miscommunications—most notably, that the interdict was not effectively communicated to Cathay Pacific—both younger children boarded the flight. Judge Wright repeated his attempts to intervene throughout the day, subsequently issuing a series of orders, including a second order directing Cathay Pacific to return the children after they had left South Africa ([9]–[11]).

[4] After non-compliance, the respondents sought to hold Cathay Pacific and its employees (including Ms. Jones) in contempt of court. The motion was presided over by Spilg J, who ultimately found both appellants in contempt of court for failing to comply with the orders issued by Judge Wright ([18]–[20]).

## I – Issues
[5] The core issues the court addressed included:
1. Under what circumstances is a court empowered to grant an order made telephonically, and what requirements must be satisfied for such an order to be effective?
2. Whether the respondents proved that the appellants were duly cited in the proceedings and that the court orders were properly brought to their notice.
3. The liability for contempt of court of employees of a company when neither the employees nor the company were cited as parties to the respective orders ([3]).

## R – Rules
[6] The court identified several critical legal principles regarding civil contempt:
1. An applicant must establish that a court order was made; that the order was served; that there was non-compliance; and that the non-compliance was wilful and mala fide. Proof beyond a reasonable doubt is required for these elements ([26]).
2. Urgent applications can dispense with normal procedural forms under Uniform Rule 6(12)(a). However, any resulting court order must always be recorded in writing ([29]).
3. A party must be cited in order for a contempt of court finding to be permissible unless it can be shown that they aided or abetted the disobedience of the order ([40]).

## A – Application
[7] The court conducted a thorough analysis of the events leading up to the contempt findings:
- The first order was inadequately communicated. The evidence suggested substantial urgency justified the telephonic interdict, but the appellants did not receive the order before the flight's departure because it was not documented and due to faulty communication regarding service to Cathay Pacific ([30]–[32]).
- Regarding the second order, it was issued while the children were already in international airspace, thus beyond the jurisdiction of South African courts. The court ruled that no contempt could be imposed for non-compliance when the order was practically unenforceable and not served correctly ([36]).
- The third order, similar in circumstance to the second, was also not valid for the same reasons, and Ms. Jones was not properly cited in the initial proceedings for contempt. Consequently, there was no legal basis for attributing knowledge of the orders to her or finding her in contempt ([38]–[40]).

## C – Conclusion
[8] The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the contempt findings against both appellants were legally untenable, leading to the following rulings:
1. The appeals against the convictions of civil contempt were upheld.
2. The orders imposed by Spilg J were set aside, concluding that Cathay Pacific and Ms. Jones were not in contempt as they were not effectively served with the orders.
3. The court recognized the procedural missteps made during the initial contempt hearing, particularly the failure to adequately cite the appellants.
4. The costs order against Cathay Pacific was deemed justified due to the airline’s non-appearance in earlier hearings, but the punitive costs orders were set aside due to procedural impropriety ([43]–[48]).

This case underscores the necessity for meticulous adherence to procedures in urgent court applications, particularly concerning the communication and documentation of court orders. The balancing of judicial efficiency with the rights of the parties affected by court orders remains an essential consideration in judicial proceedings.
