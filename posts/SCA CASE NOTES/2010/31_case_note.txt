Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH
Source Folder: 2010

# FIRAC Case Note: MEC KwaZulu-Natal v Yengwa (147/09) [2010] ZASCA 31 

## F – Facts

The case revolves around an appeal concerning the validity of a cost order stemming from a dispute over the appointment of M S Yengwa as the Municipal Manager for Umvoti Municipality ([paras 1-2]). The position was advertised in February 2007, requiring candidates to possess a recognized Bachelor's degree in Public Administration or relevant fields, as stated in regulation 38(1) of the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 ([paras 2-3]).

Yengwa, lacking the requisite degree but deemed by the council as qualified, was appointed despite opposition from the appellant, the Member of the KwaZulu-Natal Executive Council for Local Government, Housing and Traditional Affairs ([paras 3-4]). Following the resolution to appoint Yengwa, the appellant filed an urgent application in the KwaZulu-Natal High Court, seeking to declare the appointment void due to non-compliance with the requirements prescribed in regulation 38(1) ([paras 5-7]).

However, shortly after the application was submitted, Yengwa indicated that he could not accept the appointment for personal reasons, effectively making the appellant's desired relief moot ([paras 6-8]). Despite this, the appellant pursued the matter, which had transformed into a dispute over costs ([para 9]).

The High Court, presided over by Van Heerden AJ, recognized the absence of a substantive matter but ultimately declared regulation 38(1) invalid, proceeding to award costs against the councillors who voted for Yengwa's appointment ([paras 10-11]). The appellant contested the costs award but later modified his position by not seeking costs against the councillors, instead proposing that all parties bear their own costs ([paras 12-13]).

In delivering judgment, the Supreme Court of Appeal noted the High Court's decision as inappropriate in the absence of a live issue needing resolution and criticized the appellant for pursuing a futile appeal ([paras 11-14]). Consequently, the court made a partial order in favor of the appellant but required him to pay costs to the councillors, emphasizing proper intergovernmental relations ([paras 14-15]).

## I – Issues

1. Whether the High Court appropriately issued a declaration of invalidity regarding regulation 38(1) when the substantive basis for the litigation had fallen away, and there was no longer a live issue between the parties ([paras 9-11]).
2. What implications the appellant's conduct in insisting on costs had on the court’s decision regarding cost awards against councillors ([paras 12-14]).

## R – Rules

The relevant rules and authorities that guided the court's decision included:

1. Section 21A of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959, which stipulates that appeals regarding cost orders are only entertained in exceptional circumstances, referencing past cases such as *Biowatch Trust* and *Freedom of Expression Institute* ([para 1]).
2. Regulation 38(1) of the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000, which outlines qualifications necessary for the appointment of a Municipal Manager ([paras 2-3]).
3. The principle established in *Swartbooi & others v Brink & others*, which protects councillors from personal liability for costs incurred while performing their functions ([para 13]).
4. The statutory protection under section 28(1)(b) of the Local Government: Municipal Structures Act 117 of 1998 ([para 13]).
5. Judicial policy which suggests that constitutional issues should only be determined when they have a practical impact on parties involved ([para 11]).

## A – Application

The court applied the law to the facts by first establishing that, since substantive issues had diminished due to Yengwa’s withdrawal from the post, there was no active dispute requiring resolution regarding the validity of the regulation. The court highlighted that the appellate bench should not engage in academic disputes that lack practical implications for the parties involved ([para 11]). 

Moreover, the court underscored the significance of adhering to established precedents regarding costs, particularly the refusal to hold councillors personally liable, thus affirming their protection under the legislative provisions cited ([paras 12-13]).

In addressing the costs awarded by the High Court, the court expressed disapproval of the appellant’s decision to persist in seeking costs against the councillors in light of established protections, deeming this pursuit futile and detrimental ([para 12]). Instead, the appellate court shifted to a focus on appropriate cost-sharing between government entities to encourage alternative dispute resolution, ultimately ordering that the appellant bear the costs of the second to the thirteenth respondents due to his inappropriate conduct ([paras 14-15]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal partially upheld the appeal by overturning the High Court’s declaration of invalidity concerning regulation 38(1) of the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000, noting that there was no longer a live issue warranting such a declaration ([paras 11-15]). The court further held that the appellant would bear the costs for the councillors, emphasizing that political bodies should avoid unnecessary litigation against one another and encouraging more amicable resolutions to disputes between state entities ([para 14]). 

The ruling reinforces the principle that litigation should only pertain to substantive issues that have an actual impact on the parties involved and reiterates the protections afforded to local government officials from personal liability when acting within their official functions ([para 13]). This case illustrates the judicial restraint concerning unnecessary constitutional declarations without practical implications and highlights the significance of responsible conduct in public office.
