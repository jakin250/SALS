Case Title: Mabaso v National Commissioner of Police & another (1222/2017) [2019] ZASCA 43 Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa Judgment Delivered: 29 March 2019 Coram: Navsa AP, Van der Merwe, Makgoka JJA and Mokgohloa AJJA, Eksteen AJJA

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 43 Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal of So

Case Title: Mabaso v National Commissioner of Police & another (1222/2017) [2019] ZASCA 43
Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Judgment Delivered: 29 March 2019
Coram: Navsa AP, Van der Merwe, Makgoka JJA and Mokgohloa AJJA, Eksteen AJJA

F - Facts

[1] The appellant, Timothy Mabaso, submitted a legal notice to the National Commissioner of Police on 10 June 2015, asserting a claim for damages against the Minister of Police. This claim arose from an alleged assault during his detention on 20 February 2015 and was pursued under Section 3(1)(a) of the Institution of Legal Proceedings Against Certain Organs of State Act 40 of 2002 (the Act) (para [1]). The notification was served timely within a six-month window as dictated by Section 3(2)(a) of the Act, seeking R400,000 for general damages and loss of earnings.

[2] Following this notice, the Commissioner did not respond to either the initial legal notice or a subsequent demand for a decision sent on 13 July 2015. Due to the lack of response, Mabaso then initiated court proceedings on 2 December 2015. He sought a declaratory order compelling the respondents to make a decision regarding his claim, provide reasons for that decision, and review the respondents' non-decision, which he claimed was unlawful under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) (paras [3]–[5]).

[3] The Western Cape Division, Cape Town dismissed his application with costs, concluding that the law did not impose an obligation on the organ of state to respond to a claim notice before litigation commenced. Mabaso appealed this decision, which the court granted permission to proceed (para [2]).

I - Issues

[4] The primary legal question addressed by the court was whether an organ of state is required to make a decision on a claim after receiving notice under Section 3 of the Act, and if thus required, to provide reasons for the decision before litigation commences (para [2]). This question encompasses broader issues of statutory interpretation and the rights of claimants in relation to state obligations in the context of legal proceedings.

R - Rules

[5] The legal framework of the case is governed principally by the Institution of Legal Proceedings Against Certain Organs of State Act 40 of 2002, particularly Section 3 which stipulates the requirement of notice before legal proceedings can be initiated against an organ of state (paras [1] and [18]). The court also referenced PAJA regarding the review of administrative action, which may impose duties on state actors under certain circumstances (para [28]).

[6] Furthermore, the court considered the precedential value of earlier cases, including Minister of Safety and Security v De Witt [2009] ZASCA 103 and others which contextualize the necessity of prior notice in providing state organs the opportunity to investigate claims before being embroiled in litigation (para [11]).

A - Application

[7] The court undertook an interpretive analysis of Section 3, establishing that there is no explicit mandate requiring an organ of state to respond to a claim notice or make a decision before the expiration of the stipulated period (para [21]). The justices evaluated arguments presented by the appellant citing constitutional provisions, PAJA, and comparative legislation (such as the Road Accident Fund Act) to support his claim that an implied duty exists for state organs to respond (paras [22]–[38]).

[8] The court rejected these arguments, observing that the purpose of Section 3 fundamentally serves the interests of the state, affording it time to investigate claims (para [13]). Critical to the court's reasoning was the absence of any implication in the statute necessitating a decision prior to litigation. The appeal was thus positioned against established interpretations that the legislature had deliberately crafted procedural protections for state organs, recognizing their operational realities (para [49]).

C - Conclusion

[9] The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, affirming that there is no statutory obligation for an organ of state to make a decision regarding a claim prior to litigation (para [52]). The judgement clarifies that while the right to institute claims was preserved, the procedural framework under Section 3 does not support the imposition of a duty to respond. This decision underscores the importance of interpreting statutory provisions within the context of state functions and the rights of claimants. Consequently, the ruling holds significant implications for future claims against state organs, particularly in defining the boundaries of claimant rights in relation to administrative duties of the state (paras [50]–[52]).

The appeal was dismissed with no order as to costs, reinforcing the position that the issue at hand raised significant questions of public importance, despite the adverse outcome for the appellant (paras [51]–[52]).
