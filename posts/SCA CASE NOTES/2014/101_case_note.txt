Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: **Zuma v Democratic Alliance** (836/2013) [2014] ZASCA 101

## F – Facts

This case arises from protracted litigation involving Jacob Gedleyihlekisa Zuma, the President of South Africa (the Appellant) and the Democratic Alliance (the First Respondent), centered around the prosecutorial decision not to charge Zuma. The underlying issue related to the office of the National Director of Public Prosecutions (the Third Respondent) and the enforcement of a previous ruling by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ([para 1]).

At the heart of the dispute is the interpretation of the SCA's previous order in *Democratic Alliance v Acting National Director of Public Prosecutions* (2012) 3 SA 486 (SCA), where the DA sought access to the record justifying the NDPP’s decision to discontinue prosecution against Zuma. The DA required this information for purposes of judicial review ([para 2]), asserting that the prosecutorial conduct lacked transparency and accountability ([para 40]).

The ANDPP claimed confidentiality over certain internal documentation and submissions made by Zuma ([para 11]), which had contributed to the decision to discontinue the prosecution ([para 8]). The DA countered, arguing that the order did not allow for blanket confidentiality concerning documents vital to scrutinizing the prosecutorial process ([para 36]).

When the DA's demands for compliance with the SCA’s order were not fully met, it sought a court order compelling the production of recordings and transcripts related to the decision, as well as internal memoranda that do not breach confidentiality. The High Court initially ruled in favor of the DA, which provoked Zuma's appeal ([para 28]).

## I – Issues

1. **Did the confidentiality claims raised by Zuma regarding the audio recordings and related documents impede the DA’s right to access relevant materials for judicial review?** ([para 4])
   
2. **What standard applies to determine whether material is confidential, and does this extend to internal documents generated by the NDPP in relation to its prosecutorial decision?** ([para 42])

3. **Did the NDPP and Zuma’s legal representation adequately assert or substantiate claims of confidentiality that would prevent disclosure?** ([para 19])

## R – Rules

The primary legal authority in this case is the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, especially section 34, which guarantees the right to a fair trial and public hearings. Additionally, the principles established in the first appeal, specifically regarding the review of prosecutorial decisions and the standards for disclosing lawful documentation, guide this case ([paras 7–9]).

**Key Precedents:**
- *Democratic Alliance v Acting National Director of Public Prosecutions* served as the benchmark ruling dictating the obligation of the NDPP to disclose materials for review ([para 2]).
- *Tetra Mobile Radio (Pty) Ltd v MEC, Department of Works* (2008), which discusses balancing transparency and confidentiality, also contributes essential guidance on the matter ([para 36]).

## A – Application

The court examined the NDPP's lack of a specific legal claim concerning confidentiality and the absence of a credible response to the DA’s challenges to the said claims. Particularly problematic for Zuma's case was the NDPP's assertion that confidentiality was a blanket prohibition without adequate substantiation for each document in question ([para 19]).

The SCA reaffirmed the necessity of the legitimacy behind confidentiality allegations. It noted that internal documentation discussing the decision to discontinue prosecution is fundamental to uphold public trust in the constitutional body responsible for upholding the law ([para 40]). Here, the court emphasized that the NDPP must provide transparency as a constitutional body devoid of fear or favor in its actions ([para 40]).

The court ultimately found that the audio recordings constituted distinct evidence separate from the written or oral representations and should, therefore, be disclosed ([para 32]). Zuma's counsel conceded the lack of basis for preventing the release of these recordings and could not establish that they should remain confidential ([para 30]).

## C – Conclusion

The SCA dismissed Zuma's appeal, emphasizing the responsibility and duty of the NDPP to present documentation justifying its prosecutorial decisions without invoking unfounded claims of confidentiality. The ruling highlighted the necessity of fostering trust in public institutions through transparency, thus reaffirming the DA's standing and right to access relevant materials ([para 42]).

The court's holding elucidates the implications for future prosecutorial decisions: the expectations for accountability and the importance of public access to information governing the prosecutorial process. The implications extend to ensuring that the integrity of legal processes in South Africa is maintained, which is vital for the rule of law. Hence, the court mandated compliance with its previous order while imposing costs on Zuma and the NDPP for the failure to comply prior ([para 42]).
