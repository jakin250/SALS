Case Title: Shoprite Checkers (Pty) Ltd v Mafate (903/2021) [2023] ZASCA 14 (17 February 2023) Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa Coram: Petse AP and Mocumie, Carelse, Nhlangulela, and Chetty AJJA Heard: 08 November 2022 Delivered: 17 February 2023

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 14 (17 February 2023) Court: The Supreme Co

Case Title: Shoprite Checkers (Pty) Ltd v Mafate (903/2021) [2023] ZASCA 14 (17 February 2023)
Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Coram: Petse AP and Mocumie, Carelse, Nhlangulela, and Chetty AJJA
Heard: 08 November 2022
Delivered: 17 February 2023

F – Facts

The case revolves around a tragic incident that occurred on 15 October 2014 when Ms Nolunga Mkhwanazi, employed at Checkers Hyper in Edenvale, sustained severe injuries after falling from a cage being lifted by a forklift. As a consequence, she suffered permanent mental incapacity and was unable to institute legal proceedings in her own name ([2]).

On 1 February 2017, Mr Cecil Tshepo Mokopane Mafate, a practicing attorney, was appointed as her curator ad litem to manage her affairs and initiate legal claims against Shoprite Holdings Limited. Initially, proceedings were instituted against Shoprite Holdings on 22 February 2017, but after facing pleas of misjoinder and non-joinder, the curator withdrew the action in June 2018. New proceedings against Shoprite Checkers (the correct defendant) were instituted on 15 October 2018, after acknowledging the true identity of the debtor ([3], [4]). Shoprite Checkers raised a special plea of prescription, arguing that the claim had prescribed before the summons was served ([4], [5]).

It was agreed that Ms Mkhwanazi did not have knowledge of the true debtor until the curator's appointment, which led to the assertion that prescription commenced only after this date ([7], [9]). The Gauteng High Court dismissed the special plea of prescription, holding that sections 12 and 13 of the Prescription Act were not mutually exclusive and emphasized access to courts as a constitutional right ([8]). The appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal challenged this interpretation.

I – Issues

  1. Does the appointment of a curator ad litem for a person with mental incapacity mean the relevant impediment referred to in section 13(1) of the Prescription Act ceases to exist?
  2. Can a curator appointed on behalf of a person with mental incapacity invoke section 12 of the Prescription Act if neither the curator nor the person under curatorship had knowledge of the debtor's identity until after the relevant date?

R – Rules

The main statutory provisions at issue are from the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 ([12]–[17]).

  • Section 12: Outlines when prescription begins, specifying that a debt is deemed due when the creditor has knowledge of the identity of the debtor and the facts from which the debt arises ([15]).
  • Section 13: Provides that the completion of prescription is delayed for certain groups, including individuals with mental incapacity and those under curatorship. The period of prescription does not complete before one year after the relevant impediment ceases to exist ([13], [16]).

In previous case law, such as Mohlomi v Minister of Defence and Uitenhage Municipality v Molloy, principles were established highlighting the importance of time limits in litigation while balancing the rights of creditors and debtors ([26], [27]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal examined whether the permanency of Ms Mkhwanazi's mental incapacity meant that the impediment outlined in section 13(1) persisted, thereby precluding the operation of the prescription clock. The court found that the curator's appointment did not eliminate Ms Mkhwanazi's mental incapacity, which continued to affect her ability to manage her affairs or initiate legal proceedings ([30], [34]).

The court emphasized that Ms Mkhwanazi's condition was permanent, thus her legal ability to enforce her claim remained compromised. Consequently, the legal impediment she faced did not cease simply because a curator was appointed; the key question was whether the curator had the requisite knowledge to interrupt the running of prescription. The curator only became aware of the correct defendant upon the filing of special pleas by Shoprite Holdings, and thus, the court held that the prescription did not begin to run until that moment ([36]).

Ultimately, the court reiterated that the interpretation of the Prescription Act must align with principles of access to justice and not unduly penalize claimants who are incapacitated. The findings echoed those of the High Court, reinforcing that the sections of the Prescription Act function together rather than in exclusion ([18], [28]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming the High Court's decision that the special plea of prescription was served incorrectly against the curator's claims. The court held that sections 12 and 13 of the Prescription Act are not mutually exclusive, thus a curator appointed for someone with mental incapacity may invoke protections under both sections ([39]). The outcome signifies a nuanced approach to cases involving mental incapacity, ensuring that individuals trapped by their conditions still have recourse to legal avenues for redress.

This ruling serves to clarify the application of prescription law as it pertains to vulnerable individuals, highlighting the courts' role in promoting access to justice and reinforcing the protective measures enshrined in South African law. The implications extend beyond this case, setting a precedent regarding the interpretation of statutory provisions affecting incapacitated individuals in civil claims.
