Source Folder: 2013

### FIRAC Case Note: Ngewu v Post Office Retirement Fund and Others

#### F – Facts

The case of *Ngewu v Post Office Retirement Fund and Others* ([1]–[3]) involves the constitutional challenge pertaining to the right to equality as enshrined in section 9(1) of the South African Constitution. The applicants, Phumla Ruth Patricia Ngewu and the Women’s Legal Centre Trust, sought to rectify a legal discrepancy that prevented divorcees of members of the Post Office Retirement Fund (referred to as 'the Fund') from claiming their share of pension interest upon divorce, akin to the entitlements granted to ex-spouses of members of pension funds governed by the Pension Funds Act and the Government Employees Pension Law.

Phumla Ngewu was married to Mawethu Ngewu in community of property in 1980. Upon their divorce on 27 July 2007, she was awarded 50% of her ex-husband's pension interest, but could only receive her share when the benefit accrued to him. The Rules of the Fund did not provide for immediate realization of this interest at the point of divorce ([5]–[6]). 

In September 2010, following difficulties with the application of the Fund’s rules, Ms Ngewu lodged a challenge in the Western Cape High Court to deem the Fund's rules and the Pension Funds Act unconstitutional due to the absence of provisions allowing for an immediate payout of pension interests upon divorce ([6]).

The high-profile case of *Wiese v Government Employees Pension Fund* ([8]) led to an amendment that recognized similar claims for ex-spouses under the Government Employees Pension Law. In a striking development, the legislative process set in motion by the Fund suggested changes to the Post Office Act, leading to the postponement of its judicial review ([12]–[14]). However, by February 2013, prior to the hearing, the applicants contended that proposed amendments did not resolve the constitutional deficiencies, arguing for a judicial remedy of 'reading-in' to the Post Office Act ([14]).

#### I – Issues

The primary legal question is whether the differentiation in the treatment of divorcees of members of the Fund compared to those under the Pension Funds Act is inconsistent with the guarantees of equality and equal protection before the law under section 9(1) of the Constitution ([1]). 

Specifically, the issue arose concerning:
1. The rational justification for the distinction made between the two categories of beneficiaries.
2. The adequacy of proposed amendments to remedy the identified constitutional deficiency.
3. The appropriateness of judicial intervention, particularly through a reading-in of provisions to the Post Office Act to ensure compliance with constitutional standards ([17]).

#### R – Rules

The Court relied upon the principles laid out in the Constitution, specifically section 9(1), which guarantees equality and prohibits unfair discrimination. The precedent set in *Harksen v Lane NO* and *Prinsloo v Van der Linde* ([17]) underscored the necessity of rational justification for legislative differentiation. The Court examined whether the distinction between the treatment of former spouses of members of pension funds was reasonable and justifiable in an open democracy, as well as whether it constituted a discrimination that could not withstand legal scrutiny.

Further, *Wiese v Government Employees Pension Fund* was pivotal, illustrating how legislative amendments can cure constitutional defects. The nature and obligations of legislative change and the necessity for Parliament to engage in rectifying these disparities were also addressed ([10]) ([17]).

#### A – Application

The Court held that the differentiation between former spouses of members of the Fund and those governed by the Pension Funds Act was arbitrary and devoid of a rational basis ([17]). This stark contrast in treatment effectively created an unconstitutional situation. The Court's analysis demonstrated that the substantive rights of Ms Ngewu and countless others in similar positions had been compromised due to legislative oversight.

The suggested amendments to the Post Office Act were found wanting as they failed to incorporate the essential 'clean break' principle that would allow immediate realization of pension interests. The Court underscored the urgency and necessity of remedying the constitutional defect to respect the fundamental rights of individuals subjected to such legislative gaps ([16]–[19]). 

Consequently, the Court determined that a declaration of invalidity of the concerning sectionsof the Post Office Act was warranted due to their inconsistency with section 9(1) of the Constitution. However, it instituted a suspended declaration of invalidity for eight months, allowing Parliament the opportunity to rectify this legislative flaw ([18]).

The reading-in remedy proposed by the applicants aimed to incorporate the provisions of section 24A of the Government Employees Pension Law into the Post Office Act as section 10F. This would enable the former spouses to claim their pension interests immediately upon divorce, thereby aligning the Fund’s regulations with constitutional mandates ([15]).

#### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately held that the rules regarding the Post Office Retirement Fund were unconstitutional, given their failure to provide equal rights to divorcees in contrast to other pension fund members. The Court's ruling emphasized the need for legislative action to prevent violating the equality clause contained in the Constitution. The declaration of invalidity of the offending sections of the Post Office Act was suspended for eight months, obligating Parliament to cure this deficiency.

Should Parliament fail to enact appropriate amendments within the stipulated timeframe, the Court indicated it would execute a reading-in of necessary provisions to ensure compliance with constitutional requirements. This ruling not only affirmed the rights of Ms Ngewu but also reinforced the principle of equality before the law in South African jurisprudence ([16]–[21]). 

The judgment is significant for its affirmation of constitutional values and the proactive role the judiciary can play in addressing legislative failings. It compels the legislative body to act urgently in matters of social justice, particularly in circumstances where individuals' rights hinge on timely reforms. The outcomes of this case have broader implications for similar legal frameworks in South Africa, necessitating vigilance in legislative compliance with constitutional rights. 

In conclusion, *Ngewu v Post Office Retirement Fund and Others* serves as a pivotal case reinforcing the necessity for equality in the treatment of divorced spouses under pension policies and highlights the judiciary's role in safeguarding fundamental rights when legislative bodies falter in their constitutional duties.
