# Case Note: E GABAATLHOLWE and N RAMMUTLE v THE STATE

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Case No:** 96/2002  
**Judges:** Scott, Farlem JJA and Heher AJA  
**Date of Hearing:** 15th November 2002  
**Date of Judgment:** 28th November 2002

---

### FIRAC Method Case Note

**F – Facts**  
[1] The appellants, E GABAATLHOLWE and N RAMMUTLE, along with a third accomplice, were policemen convicted of serious charges including rape and kidnapping. The trial presided by Labuschagne J and assessors concluded with life sentences for both appellants, alongside additional sentences of ten years imprisonment for the other related offenses. Initially, the appellants' application for leave to appeal was denied both by the Court a quo and subsequently by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) upon petition. However, the trial judge, at the request of the first and third accused, recorded a special entry under section 317 of the Criminal Procedure Act 1977. The entry detailed a request made by the defense to call Stanley van der Westhuizen as a witness, who was present during the incident and purportedly had a statement that could be pivotal to the defense’s case ([paras 1–2]).

[2] Van der Westhuizen was reported by the defense to have claimed that the complainant had consumed alcohol but was not incapacitated. His evidence was not elicited by the State, which instead made him available as a witness for the defense. The trial court ultimately dismissed the defense's request to subpoena Van der Westhuizen, reasoning that his testimony was not essential to just adjudication ([paras 3–4]).  

[3] The complainant, 21 at the time, testified that on the night of 26 March 1999, she was taken to the Bundu Inn by her stepfather to meet Van der Westhuizen, where they engaged in drinking. The events escalated to a point where, after being induced to leave the tavern with police officers, she was assaulted and raped ([paras 9–10]). 

[4] Counsel for the defense argued that Van der Westhuizen’s testimony regarding the complainant's sobriety was crucial and stated that such evidence would affect the trial court’s judgment on the credibility of the complainant ([paras 18–22]).

---

**I – Issues**  
[1] The primary legal issue before the court was whether the trial court erred in refusing the defense’s application to call Van der Westhuizen as a witness, thereby breaching their duty under section 186 of the Criminal Procedure Act, which mandates a court to subpoena witnesses whose evidence is deemed essential for a just decision ([paras 1, 4–6]).

[2] A secondary issue was the relevance of Van der Westhuizen's potential testimony regarding the complainant's consumption of alcohol, which the defense contended would impact the assessment of the complainant's credibility and the concept of consent ([paras 22–26]).

---

**R – Rules**  
[1] The court relied on section 186 of the Criminal Procedure Act 1977, which stipulates that a court must summon witnesses if their evidence is essential to a just decision ([para 2]). This section requires that the necessity of calling a witness must be assessed per the evidence available to the court at any point in the trial.

[2] Legal precedents established that trial judges maintain considerable discretion concerning the necessity of a witness's evidence. Interference from appellate courts in such matters would be confined to instances where it is clear the trial court failed to consider whether the evidence was essential ([para 8]).

---

**A – Application**  
[1] In applying the law, the SCA emphasized that the appraisal of whether evidence is essential is generally left to the discretion of the trial judge. The court observed that the refusal to summon Van der Westhuizen should not be interfered with lightly and that greater latitude is given to trial judges when assessments are made prior to the defense case ([paras 7–8]).

[2] The SCA reasoned that Van der Westhuizen's testimony, which did not provide unequivocal evidence, fell short of being characterized as essential. His statement on the complainant's drinking was ambiguous and not definitive enough to substantially affect the outcome of the case. Notably, various aspects of conduct observed by others—including her ability to engage actively and seek help—contradicted claims of significant intoxication ([paras 23–26]).

---

**C – Conclusion**  
[1] The SCA concluded that the trial court had not erred in its refusal to mandate the subpoena of Van der Westhuizen, affirming that the trial judge's discretion in this regard was appropriately exercised based on the context of the case ([para 27]). The court did not accept the defense's argument that Van der Westhuizen's testimony would have materially shifted the case's outcome but noted that the complainant's account remained credible in light of the surrounding circumstances ([paras 28–29]).

[2] The appeal was ultimately dismissed, underscoring the importance of the trial judge’s function as an administrator of justice in evaluating what constitutes essential testimony and reaffirming the principle that trial courts enjoy a considerable degree of discretion in managing evidentiary matters during criminal proceedings ([para 29]).
