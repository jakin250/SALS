Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: Posthumus v The Road Accident Fund (20024/2014) [2015] ZASCA 40

**F - Facts**

The appeal arose from a motor vehicle accident that occurred on 20 May 2003, involving Mrs. Petronella Posthumus and Mr. Pierre Posthumus, who were passengers in a minibus driven by Mr. Andries Maritz. The vehicle capsized on the N14 near Sannieshof, leading to bodily injuries sustained by both passengers. The Posthumus couple initiated a claim for damages against the Road Accident Fund (RAF), alleging that the driver of a parked vehicle, identified as a 'bakkie', was negligent for leaving its headlights on and blinding Mr. Maritz, leading to his loss of control over the minibus ([1]).

At trial, it was uncontested that Mr. Maritz had acted negligently. The pivotal issue was whether the parked bakkie contributed to the accident through its headlights distracting Mr. Maritz. Despite evidence provided by Mr. Maritz and his wife supporting the presence of the bakkie, the trial court ultimately concluded that no bakkie was present and attributed full liability to Mr. Maritz. This decision was based on a misapprehension of the facts, particularly concerning the presence of the bakkie ([2]–[4]).

After the trial court's ruling in October 2010, the Posthumuses sought leave to appeal, which was granted in August 2011. The appellate process, however, experienced significant delays, and judgment was rendered by the full court in April 2013, which led to further division in its decision regarding the bakkie's presence and negligence ([4]–[5]). 

A critical aspect of this case was the tragic backdrop of both appellants' deaths prior to the appeal, compelling their estates to proceed with the case ([7]).

---

**I - Issues**

The primary legal issues confronted by the appellate court were:

1. Whether the trial court erred in finding that the bakkie was not on the scene at the time of the accident and, consequently, whether the driver of the bakkie could be held negligent for leaving his headlights shining into the roadway ([5]); 
2. Whether the actions of the driver of the bakkie could break the chain of causation concerning the RAF’s liability under the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 (the Act) ([19]).

The broader implications of establishing a duty of care for motorists with respect to their headlights also emerged as a significant matter for the court ([15]).

---

**R - Rules**

The court applied principles from South African delict law regarding negligence. The standard for establishing negligence requires:

1. A reasonable foresight of harm arising from one’s conduct;
2. A failure to take necessary precautions against that harm ([15]).

In determining whether a defendant, the driver of the bakkie, acted with negligence, the court cited previous cases that have examined similar issues, noting there was scant direct precedent addressing the negligence of a driver contributing to an accident by failing to dim headlights, although acknowledging a common understanding in the driving community about the dangers posed by dazzling lights ([16]).

Statutory reference to the Act was also critical. Section 17(1) stipulates liability for damages arising from driving a motor vehicle, while Section 20(2) attributes similar liability to parties who leave vehicles unattended but in an operable condition. This delineation was vital in understanding the RAF's obligations ([19]).

---

**A - Application**

The court analyzed the evidence presented at trial, determining that the trial court had errantly concluded that no bakkie was at the scene ([4][13]). Once the appellate court established the presence of the bakkie and its bright headlights, it found that Mr. Kgantsi’s failure to dim the lights was causally negligent, thus likely contributing to the accident. The court ruled that Mr. Maritz's statement about being blinded was credible and supported by his wife’s testimony ([10][14][18]).

Further discussion centered on the responsibility of drivers in mitigating hazards created by their vehicles. The court decreed that keeping headlights on while parked near the road at night, particularly on a curve, was inherently negligent as it posed an unreasonable risk to other road users. The ruling emphasized a reasonable person’s obligations to foresee potential dangers and take measures to prevent accidents ([17][18]).

Ultimately, the court accepted the applicability of the RAF’s liability provisions concerning the driver of the bakkie under the existing legal definitions and the facts of the case. Thus, the court found that the initial trial findings limiting the RAF’s liability were erroneous ([19]).

---

**C - Conclusion**

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appeal by the Posthumus estate was meritorious, overturning the previous decisions of the lower courts regarding the bakkie's presence and negligence. The court ordered that the RAF's liability could not be limited as initially asserted by the trial court, affirming that the actions of the bakkie's driver constituted negligence and directly contributed to the accident. The judgment not only rectified the liability in this case but also provided critical clarification regarding the duties of vehicle operators in preventing risk to others on the road. Consequently, the order of the full court was substituted with a ruling that articulated the RAF’s comprehensive liability for the Posthumuses’ damages ([23]).

The ruling underscores significant legal precedents about the expectations placed on motorists and the implications of negligent actions that result in road accidents. The court emphasized a broader expectation for vehicle operators to take reasonable steps to avert hindrances stemming from their vehicles, thereby refining the contours of negligence within the context of South African law.
