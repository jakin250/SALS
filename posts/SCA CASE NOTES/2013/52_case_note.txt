Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: Kadoma Trading (Pty) Ltd v Noble Crest CC

**Neutral Citation**: Kadoma Trading (Pty) Ltd v Noble Crest CC (452/12) [2013] ZASCA 52 (28 March 2013)

**Coram**: MAYA, SHONGWE, PILLAY JJA, ERASMUS and SWAIN AJJA

**Heard**: 1 March 2013

**Delivered**: 28 March 2013

### F - Facts

The case concerns an appeal from the Western Cape High Court regarding the interpretation of section 26(7) of the Close Corporations Act 69 of 1984 (the Act), which deals with the effects of the deregistration and subsequent restoration of a close corporation. The respondent, Noble Crest CC, operated a franchise business under the brand 'Dents ‘N All' and entered into a sale and franchise agreement with the appellant, Kadoma Trading 15 (Pty) Ltd, on 21 October 2010 and 13 January 2011, respectively ([paras 1–2]).

Payments totaling R1,750,000 were stipulated, with an initial payment of R1,500,000 made by the appellant on commencement of trade in February 2011. Following dissatisfaction with the services provided, the appellant cancelled the franchise agreement on 9 March 2011. The respondent, unaware of its own deregistration on 16 July 2010 due to non-compliance with annual returns, denied the allegations of breach and later claimed that re-registration on 12 April 2011 cured any previous invalidity of the agreements ([paras 3–5]).

The appellant opposed the respondent's claims, asserting that the agreements were void ab initio since they were with a deregistered entity. Following a local court ruling favoring the respondent, the appellant sought to appeal ([para 8]).

### I - Issues

The primary legal issue at the heart of the appeal is whether the sale and franchise agreements entered into by the parties during the respondent’s deregistration were validated by the latter's subsequent restoration under section 26(7) of the Act ([paras 1, 9]). Moreover, the appellant argued whether such restoration provided a legal personality retroactively, allowing for the enforceability of the agreements within common law ([para 10]).

### R - Rules

The relevant rule from the Close Corporations Act is section 26(7), which states that upon restoration of registration, the corporation shall be deemed to have continued its existence from the date of deregistration as if it had never been deregistered. This section has no direct precedents in terms of interpretation but draws from analogous provisions in the Companies Act 61 of 1973, particularly sections 73(6)(a) and 73(6A), which provide for similar outcomes following restoration ([paras 11–12]).

The court also referenced past decisions, including **Miller v NAFCOC Investment Holding Co Ltd** and **Insamcor (Pty) Ltd v Dorbyl Light & General Engineering (Pty) Ltd**, which interpreted provisions relating to corporate deregistration and restoration with regard to retroactive validation of acts performed by the corporation during deregistration ([paras 10, 13–14]).

### A - Application

The court acknowledged the appellant’s position regarding the invalidity of contracts with an entity that lacked legal capacity due to deregistration. However, the court emphasized that the critical question involved the effects of the re-registration and how it operates under section 26(7). 

The court held that restoration imbues the entity with legal personality retrospectively, thus validating all acts performed during the period of deregistration, inclusive of the contracts in question. Such validation protects parties who act in good faith when engaging with a deregistered entity, thereby avoiding a complete nullification of agreements made under that status ([paras 15–19]).

Further, the court addressed concerns about potential prejudices to third parties, asserting that the legislative design intended to preserve the continuity of the corporation’s existence and its obligations, allowing necessary administrative recourse for those not wishing to be adversely affected by restoration ([paras 15, 19]).

### C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the interpretation of section 26(7) endorsed by the court below was accurate. This interpretation supports the notion that the corporation is deemed to have continued its existence and validates all legal relationships established during the deregistration period. Therefore, the appeal was dismissed with costs on an attorney-and-own-client scale, reflecting the enforceability of the franchise agreements despite the initial challenges presented due to the respondent's deregistration. The ruling reinforces the legislative framework that governs corporate existence, promoting stability in commercial relationships even in light of administrative lapses ([paras 20–22]).
