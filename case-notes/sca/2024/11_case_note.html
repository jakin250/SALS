Muravha v Minister of Police (179/2022) [2024] ZASCA 11

In the subsequent appeal, it emerged that the entire trial record was lost, complicating the appellant’s pursuit of justice.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 11

FIRAC Method

F - Facts

The appellant, Lufuno Muravha, initiated a civil suit against the Minister of Police, seeking damages of R900,000 for a shooting incident that occurred on 8 August 2014 in Limpopo Province. Muravha alleged that he was shot by a member of the South African Police Service (SAPS) during a protest while he was trying to assist in removing individuals who had sought refuge inside his motor car scrapyard workshop ([1]). The trial court dismissed Muravha’s claim with costs, leading him to seek an appeal ([1]).

In the subsequent appeal, it emerged that the entire trial record was lost, complicating the appellant’s pursuit of justice. While the trial court had accepted the evidence of the police that Muravha had been involved with the protesters, Muravha contested these findings, asserting that the circumstances and actual events had been misrepresented ([2]–[4]). The appellant filed the notice of appeal 17 months after leave was granted, with the record of appeal submitted over two years later, containing only the pleadings and the judgment ([5]–[6]). The full court dismissed the appeal, maintaining this record was sufficient.

Muravha subsequently sought leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ([12]). The procedural history highlighted significant delays and an inadequate attempt to reconstruct the missing trial record, further intensifying the issues surrounding the fair trial rights of the appellant ([14]–[15]).

I - Issues

The pivotal legal questions in this appeal centered on:

  1. Whether the appellant could be afforded a fair trial in the absence of a complete trial record.
  2. Whether the full court had correctly assessed the circumstances surrounding the dismissal of the appeal, especially when the lost record prevented a proper evaluation of the evidence.
  3. The implications of the trial court’s reliance on evidence concerning the credibility of witnesses without having the complete record ([6]–[20]).

R - Rules

The judgment is primarily anchored in the constitutional provisions ensuring the right to a fair trial, particularly section 34 of the Constitution of South Africa, which guarantees that everyone has the right to have their disputes resolved in a fair public hearing ([20]). Precedents such as Schoombee v S ([7]) outline that the responsibility for ensuring an adequate record rests with both the appellant and the State. The courts have articulated the necessity of a complete trial record as a fundamental aspect of the right to appeal.

The full court's judgment also highlighted the obligation to reconstruct trial records when they have been lost, emphasizing that proper procedural steps need to be followed to ensure that fairness is preserved ([14]).

A - Application

The SCA evaluated how the absence of a complete trial record affected the appellant's right to appeal and highlighted substantial procedural shortcomings. The full court's reliance on the limited record was deemed erroneous since it could not effectively reconcile conflicting witness accounts, an assessment that was crucial to the trial's outcome ([18]–[21]). Furthermore, the SCA noted that the trial court's credibility findings were made without a complete understanding of the evidence due to the missing transcripts, thus raising questions about the fairness of the trial process. The court found that the challenges faced by the full court—stemming from the absence of the record—fundamentally undermined its decision to dismiss the appeal ([19]).

C - Conclusion

Ultimately, the SCA concluded that the full court made a significant misdirection by deciding the appeal without the benefit of the complete trial record, effectively rendering the appellant's right to a fair trial unexercisable. The appeal was upheld, and the matter was remitted to the trial court to start de novo before a different presiding judge ([22]). Each party bore its own costs for this appeal, reflecting the court's recognition of the procedural missteps that led to the situation ([21]). The ruling emphasizes the importance of maintaining comprehensive and accessible trial records as a crucial component of a fair judicial system, underlining that attempts to reconstruct lost records must be thorough and timely in order to uphold the fundamental rights enshrined in the Constitution.
