Kelbrick & Others v Nelson Attorneys & Another (307/2017) [2018] ZASCA 55

The case originates from a property transaction in which the appellants, Leonie Logie Kelbrick and the Van den Berg couple, sold their immovable properties to a property developer, Headline Trading 124 CC t/a Status Homes (hereinafter "Status Homes").

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 55

F – Facts ([paras 1–29])

The case originates from a property transaction in which the appellants, Leonie Logie Kelbrick and the Van den Berg couple, sold their immovable properties to a property developer, Headline Trading 124 CC t/a Status Homes (hereinafter "Status Homes"). The agreement was executed on 4 September 2006, but Status Homes was unable to fulfill its contractual obligations, leading to its eventual liquidation and sequestration of its sole member ([para 1]). The appellants filed a claim for damages against the first respondent, Nelson Attorneys, who had prepared the sale agreements and acted as conveyancers.

The crux of the dispute arose from a special plea of prescription raised by the first respondent. The court a quo upheld this plea, concluding that the appellants had a completed cause of action by 27 July 2008, starting the prescription period, which expired on 26 July 2011. The appellants countered this ruling, arguing that their claims had not yet arisen since the construction could not commence until the removal of restrictive conditions on the title deeds, which only occurred on 26 August 2008 ([paras 1–6]).

I – Issues ([paras 15–25])

The key legal issue presented to the court was whether the appellants' claims against the respondent for negligence had prescribed, specifically addressing:

  1. When did the prescription period begin to run concerning the appellants' claims under the Prescription Act 68 of 1969?
  2. Did the appellants have actual knowledge of the facts constituting a complete cause of action against the respondent by the alleged prescription commencement date ([paras 15–25])?

R – Rules ([paras 16–17])

The applicable legal framework includes:

  • Prescription Act 68 of 1969; particularly § 12, which states that prescription begins when a debt is due ([para 16]).
  • The definition of "debt due," as clarified in Truter & another v Deysel, indicates that a debt is due when the creditor possesses a complete cause of action against the debtor ([para 17]).

It is further established that a creditor must have knowledge of the debtor’s identity and the facts from which the debt arises for the debt to be deemed "due" ([para 16]).

A – Application ([paras 18–29])

  1. The court examined whether the appellants had acquired a complete cause of action by the time the prescription period was claimed to have commenced on 27 July 2008. The respondent claimed that by that date, the appellants must have realized that no construction was forthcoming and, therefore, they suffered damages ([paras 15–24]).
  2. Contrarily, the court highlighted that the construction’s commencement was contingent upon the removal of the restrictive conditions, which only occurred after the alleged commencement date of the prescription period ([paras 19–22]). The evidence indicated confusion and uncertainty surrounding the development until the restrictive conditions were finally removed.
  3. The court found the claim that the appellants had sufficient knowledge to pursue a claim against the respondent by the alleged cut-off in July 2008 unsubstantiated. The ongoing legal efforts of the respondent to remove the restrictions provided reasonable assurance to the appellants that the development project would proceed ([paras 23–25]).

C – Conclusion ([paras 29])

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the respondent's special plea of prescription was not established, thus allowing the appeal. The initial judgment was set aside, reinstating the appellants' claims ([para 29]). The court underscored that the case illustrates the importance of the creditor’s knowledge concerning the facts leading to action, asserting that the understanding of the actions and intentions behind legal proceedings should be closely examined to ensure just outcomes in claims of negligence. The ruling also serves as clarifying guidance on the handling of special pleas in separation of issues under the Uniform Rules of Court bracketing that these matters often intertwine and should ideally be dealt with concurrently to avoid confusion and facilitate judicial efficiency ([para 29]).

References:

  1. Prescription Act 68 of 1969.
  2. Truter & another v Deysel [2006] ZASCA 16; 2006 (4) SA 168 (SCA).
  3. Gericke v Sacks 1978 (1) SA 821 (A).
  4. Van Staden v Fourie 1989 (3) SA 200 (A).
  5. Santam Ltd v Ethwar 1999 (2) SA 244 (SCA).
