Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: OCA Testing and Certification South Africa (Pty) Ltd v KCEC Engineering and Construction (Pty) Ltd and Another [2023] ZASCA 13

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History
[1] This case revolves around an arbitration award in a dispute between OCA Testing Inspection and Certification South Africa (Pty) Ltd (hereafter "OCA Testing") and KCEC Engineering and Construction (Pty) Ltd (hereafter "KCEC Engineering"). OCA Testing claimed payment for services provided under three separate agreements, alleging a total amount due of R2 603 729.44 for non-destructive testing services rendered from May to August 2018 ([paras 3–6]). 

[2] KCEC Engineering, while acknowledging receipt of services, disputed liability on grounds of late delivery of the services, alleging overpayment, and claiming liquidated damages due to OCA Testing's breach of contractual obligations ([paras 7–9]).

[3] The arbitrator, Justice N P Willis, found in favor of KCEC Engineering, dismissing both the claims made by OCA Testing and the counterclaims of KCEC Engineering. He concluded that OCA Testing had breached the first agreement and failed to deliver a "Certificate of Conformity" (CoC), resulting in the dismissal of its claim ([paras 9–10]). Furthermore, he found the defense to the claims under the second and third agreements unsustainable yet failed to address these agreements in the final award's conclusion ([paras 12–14]).

[4] OCA Testing subsequently sought to set aside the arbitration award in the Gauteng High Court, which ruled in favor of KCEC Engineering, affirming the arbitrator's decision ([paras 14–16]). OCA Testing was granted leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 18]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions
[5] The principal legal question in this appeal was whether the arbitrator's failure to adequately address the claims arising from two out of three independent agreements constituted a "gross irregularity" pursuant to section 33(1) of the Arbitration Act 42 of 1965. Specifically, did the arbitrator's omission prevent a fair trial of the issues involved in OCA Testing’s claims? ([paras 1, 19]).

[6] Additionally, the case questioned the extent to which the court could intervene in the arbitration award based on alleged errors by the arbitrator that did not affect the outcome ([para 28]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon
[7] The case primarily relied on section 33(1) of the Arbitration Act 42 of 1965, which provides grounds for setting aside an arbitral award ([para 20]). Subsections (a) and (b) of this provision are relevant, addressing situations where an arbitrator has misconducted themselves or has committed gross irregularities ([para 20]).

[8] Precedents such as *Telcordia Technologies Inc v Telkom SA Ltd* and *Palabora Copper (Pty) Ltd v Motlokwa Transport and Construction (Pty) Ltd* were discussed to clarify what constitutes an irregularity. It was noted that an incorrect judgment or misinterpretation of evidence does not, in itself, equate to a gross irregularity unless it compromises the fairness of the proceedings ([paras 22–24]).

[9] Furthermore, the legal threshold for setting aside an arbitrator's award aligns with whether the arbitration process prevented a fair resolution of matters, as highlighted in *Goldfields Investment Ltd v City Council of Johannesburg* ([para 22]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law
[10] The Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized the arbitrator's decision-making process, particularly focusing on the claim generated from the second and third agreements ([para 24]). Notably, the arbitrator had acknowledged that the amounts were derived from independent agreements and had found KCEC Engineering's defenses regarding them to be baseless. Yet, he paradoxically failed to determine the fate of those claims in his award ([paras 24–28]).

[11] The court underscored that the arbitrator’s omission amounted to a failure to engage with critical issues affecting OCA Testing’s claims - specifically, whether the claims from the second and third agreements were legitimate. This fault was viewed as a gross irregularity that significantly impacted the integrity of the arbitration process ([para 28]).

[12] The Supreme Court also highlighted the importance of an arbitrator adequately determining all issues presented in a composite claim. The failure to do so not only constituted an oversight but also deprived OCA Testing of a fair trial regarding a substantial portion of its claim ([paras 28–32]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications
[13] The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that the arbitrator committed a gross irregularity by failing to address the claims related to the second and third agreements appropriately. As such, the appeal was upheld, and the court set aside the parts of the arbitration award concerning these agreements ([paras 34–36]).

[14] The court ruled that the dispute regarding the residual claims should be remitted to a new arbitrator for reconsideration, stating that allowing the same arbitrator to revisit the issues would not be appropriate given the established lack of confidence following the irregular conduct ([paras 34–35]).

[15] This judgment underscores the judiciary's role in ensuring that arbitration processes are conducted with due fairness and that parties are afforded an opportunity to have their claims fully evaluated. The ruling illustrates a commitment to safeguarding the integrity of arbitration as a dispute resolution mechanism while balancing the judicial non-interference principle inherent in arbitration law ([paras 34–40]).
