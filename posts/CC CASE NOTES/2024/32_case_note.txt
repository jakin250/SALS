Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note: Mohlaba and Others v Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs and Others

## F – Facts

This case arises from an application by traditional community members, accomplished by various respondents, aimed at challenging the constitutionality of section 25 of the Limpopo Traditional Leadership and Institutions Act 6 of 2005 (hereinafter referred to as the "Limpopo Act"). The core facts are as follows:

1. The applicants comprise former members of traditional communities across several traditional authorities in Limpopo ([paras 3–4]). The twelfth applicant, Nkuzi Development Trust, is a non-profit entity focused on land rights and community support ([para 4]).

2. Section 25 of the Limpopo Act grants traditional councils the authority to levy traditional council rates upon taxpayers within the traditional area concerned, conditional upon approval from the Premier. Non-payment of these levies could lead to punitive actions under customary laws ([para 1]).

3. The High Court earlier declared section 25 unconstitutional, asserting that only legislative bodies possess the power to impose taxes ([para 8]). Traditional levies, as outlined, were classified as taxes under South African constitutional law.

4. The case was subsequently appealed to the Constitutional Court for confirmation of the high court's order, along with an application for direct leave to appeal regarding the costs order, which the respondents contested ([para 1]).

5. Evidence presented revealed that traditional authorities rely on such levies for various community needs and that failure to pay often entailed significant consequences for community members ([paras 6–7]).

## I – Issues

The constitutional matters arising in this case include:

1. **Condonation**: Whether the court should grant condonation for the late filing of the application for confirmation and leave to appeal directly ([paras 9–10]).

2. **Unconstitutionality of section 25**: Whether section 25 of the Limpopo Act is unconstitutional, as it confers tax-imposing authority upon traditional bodies that the Constitution reserves for legislative bodies ([para 9(b)]).

3. **Costs Order**: Whether to grant leave to appeal against the High Court’s order which required each party to bear its own costs ([para 9(c)]).

## R – Rules

The legal provisions and precedents underpinning this case include:

1. **Constitutional Authority on Taxation**: The South African Constitution delineates taxation powers strictly to legislative entities (sections 77, 228, and 229). There is a requirement for legislative processes for any imposition of taxes ([paras 15–19]).

2. **Judicial Precedent**: The landmark rulings in _Fedsure Life Assurance Ltd v Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council_ and _Shuttleworth v South African Reserve Bank_ established that taxation powers should not be delegated to administrative or non-elected entities ([paras 15–18]).

3. **Constitutional Jurisprudence**: Recent judgments (e.g., _Casino Association_) have reiterated that the taxing power is not only exclusive to elected representatives but is also subject to stringent procedural safeguards established in the Constitution ([para 20]).

## A – Application

In addressing the above issues, the Constitutional Court undertook the following analyses:

1. **Condonation Grant**: The court deemed the delay in filing to be reasonable and justifiable, noting that the applicants sought counsel from the same legal team throughout the litigation process. The court found the potential prejudice against the applicants outweighed any inconveniences caused by the delay, hence condonation was justified ([para 11]).

2. **Constitutionality of Section 25**:
   - The Court reaffirmed that traditional leaders lack constitutional authority to impose taxes, a power limited to elected legislative bodies ([para 21]).
   - The Court noted that the levies in question share characteristics with taxes, being compulsory, uniformly imposed, and aimed at supporting services without specifying direct benefits to individuals ([paras 24–40]).
   - With these findings, the court held that section 25 contravenes the constitution, solidifying that tax imposition should remain strictly within legislative jurisdiction ([para 38]).

3. **Costs Order Appeal**: The Court analyzed the costs decision by the High Court, expressing that successful litigants asserting constitutional claims against state entities are generally entitled to their costs unless compelling reasons are provided otherwise. The court identified that the absence of such justification rendered the earlier costs order flawed. Consequently, the Court ordered that the State respondents bear the applicants' costs, which includes the engagement of two counsels ([paras 44–50]).

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded with the following holdings:

1. **Confirmation of High Court's Invalidity Order**: The High Court’s order declaring section 25 unconstitutional and hence invalid was confirmed ([para 62]).

2. **Condonation Granted**: The court accepted the late application filings, recognizing the extenuating circumstances faced by the applicants.

3. **Revised Costs Order**: The costs order was modified to favor the applicants, with the State respondents instructed to cover the costs, including the expenses related to engaging two counsels ([para 62]).

Ultimately, the Court's ruling affirmed critical constitutional principles regarding the powers of taxation and set a precedent ensuring that only duly elected bodies maintain authority in levying taxes, reinforcing democratic accountability in governance. The implications of the ruling are profound, emphasizing the necessity of aligning traditional leadership structures with constitutional norms while preventing potential exploitation in traditional communities.
