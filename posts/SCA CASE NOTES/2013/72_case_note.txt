Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: Buthelezi v Ndaba (575/2012) [2013] ZASCA 72

## F - Facts

This case arose out of allegations of medical negligence against Dr. Gabriel Buthelezi, a specialist gynaecologist and obstetrician, stemming from a surgical procedure he performed on Ms. Priscilla Ndaba on 13 February 2006. The procedure in question was a total abdominal hysterectomy (TAH). Post-surgery, Ndaba began experiencing significant urinary incontinence, which subsequent medical examinations revealed was due to a vesico-vaginal fistula — a medical condition defined as a hole between the bladder and vaginal wall (¶ [1]).

Following these complications, Ndaba initiated legal proceedings against Buthelezi in the KwaZulu-Natal High Court, claiming damages attributed to the alleged negligence during the operation. At this preliminary stage, the court was requested to determine the issue of liability before addressing the quantum of damages, which was to be deferred (¶ [2]).

At trial, expert testimony was presented from two medical professionals. Prof. R.W. Green-Thompson, advocating for Ndaba, argued that Buthelezi's negligence during the surgery had resulted in the development of the fistula. Conversely, Prof. L. Snyman testified for Buthelezi, contending that the evidence did not conclusively infer negligence (¶ [2]–[4]).

The trial court, presided over by Jappie J, ultimately ruled in favor of Ndaba, finding that Buthelezi had indeed been negligent during the procedure. This prompted Buthelezi's appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal, where he sought to overturn the earlier judgment (¶ [2]).

## I - Issues

The central legal issue in this case was whether Dr. Buthelezi acted negligently during the performance of the total abdominal hysterectomy, thus giving rise to Ms. Ndaba's injury. The specific questions included:

1. Was there sufficient evidence to conclude that injuries sustained by Ndaba were a direct result of negligence during the surgery?
2. How should the conflicting expert opinions presented at trial influence a determination of negligence?
3. Did the trial court properly evaluate the expert testimonies and the underlying reasoning behind those opinions? (¶ [14]–[15]).

## R - Rules

In determining negligence within the medical field, the court typically applies the standard of care expected of a reasonable practitioner in the same field, as articulated in *Van Wyk v Lewis* (1924) AD 438. The principles guiding negligence within medical contexts were reiterated, emphasizing that an adverse outcome does not alone establish negligence—the standard requires demonstrating that the practitioner failed to meet the requisite duty of care (¶ [15]).

Moreover, the court highlighted the complexity of medical procedures which often allow for a range of outcomes. The *res ipsa loquitur* doctrine, which infers negligence based on the nature of the occurrence, was criticized as inappropriate in a context where the mechanisms leading to injury remain ambiguous (¶ [16]).

Statements from established medical literature were included to bolster the understanding of acceptable risks associated with hysterectomies, specifically that the formation of a fistula can occur irrespective of negligence (¶ [13]).

## A - Application

The appeal court undertook a detailed evaluation of the expert testimonies presented during the trial. Prof. Green-Thompson's analysis suggested that a failure to protect the bladder during surgery inevitably pointed to negligence; however, this inference was scrutinized as overly simplistic and lacking a rigorous evidentiary basis. The court noted that Green-Thompson's conclusions appeared to be derived more from an inference of negligence from the occurrence of the fistula rather than concrete evidence of Buthelezi's failure to adhere to the standard of care (¶ [16]).

Conversely, Prof. Snyman emphasized the inherent risks associated with hysterectomies, underscoring that fistulas can develop despite careful surgical techniques. His testimony was further supported by international publications identifying bladder injuries as recognized complications resulting from such operations. This perspective was deemed more consistent with the complexities of surgical outcomes and the standard practices within the medical community (¶ [17]).

The appeal court also noted Buthelezi's own testimony, which acknowledged the unexpected nature of the surgical complication. The judgment emphasized that such acknowledgment did not equate to an admission of negligence, echoing the sentiment expressed in *Hucks v Cole* (1968) and *Castell v De Greef* (1993), where merely experiencing an adverse outcome does not suffice to establish a breach of duty (¶ [15]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, concluding that Ndaba had not satisfactorily proven that Buthelezi's actions constituted negligence during the hysterectomy. The court set aside the original judgment, dismissing Ndaba's claim with costs. This decision reinforced the notion that medical practitioners are not held liable for every adverse outcome that transpires during surgery, stressing the need for clear, cogent evidence of negligence rather than speculative assertions (¶ [18]).

The implications of this ruling underscore the need for a rigorous examination of expert opinions in medical negligence cases and affirm the critical importance of the burden of proof resting with the claimant to establish negligence via compelling evidence rather than mere inference from unfavorable outcomes.
