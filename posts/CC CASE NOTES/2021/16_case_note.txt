# FIRAC Case Note: National Union of Mineworkers obo Violet Masha and Others v SAMANCOR Limited (Eastern Chrome Mines) and Others

**Neutral Citation**: National Union of Mineworkers obo Violet Masha and Others v SAMANCOR Limited (Eastern Chrome Mines) and Others [2021] ZACC 16

**Coram**: Mogoeng CJ, Jafta J, Khampepe J, Madlanga J, Majiedt J, Mhlantla J, Pillay AJ, Theron J, Tlaletsi AJ, and Tshiqi J

**Judgment**: Mhlantla J (unanimous)

### **F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1-4])**

The **National Union of Mineworkers (NUM)** filed an application on behalf of five of its members—**Violet Masha, Vincent Pholwane, Nkipilili Siqalane, Father Mhlongo, and Moses Khoza**—who were dismissed from their employment with **SAMANCOR Limited (Eastern Chrome Mines)**, the first respondent. The dismissals stemmed from allegations of misconduct for working under unsafe conditions after verbal and written instructions from their supervisor, **Mr. Madikwane**, to cease work until necessary safety measures were implemented.

On **19 October 2015**, Mr. Madikwane observed the employees working despite prior instructions concerning safety. After a disciplinary hearing, the employees were dismissed for failing to comply with these instructions. The case was submitted to the **Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA)**, where an arbitrator ruled in favor of the employees, citing inconsistencies in the application of disciplinary measures.

SAMANCOR challenged this ruling in the **Labour Court**, which sided with SAMANCOR, leading to an appeal by NUM to the **Labour Appeal Court**. The Labour Appeal Court reversed the Labour Court’s decision, deeming the dismissals fair and ordering NUM to bear the costs of the proceedings. NUM subsequently sought leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court on both the merits of the dismissal and the costs orders.

### **I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 5-7])**

The following issues emerged for consideration:

1. **Condonation**: Should condonation be granted for the late filing of the appeal?
2. **Jurisdiction**: Does this Court have jurisdiction to hear the case, given the constitutional implications related to fair labour practices?
3. **Interests of Justice**: Is it in the interests of justice to grant leave to appeal?
4. **Dismissal Fairness**: Was the dismissal of the employees both procedurally and substantively fair?
5. **Appropriate Remedy**: What is the appropriate remedy regarding the cost orders issued by the Labour Appeal Court?

### **R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 8-12])**

The Court referenced several key principles and cases, including:

- The constitutional right to **fair labour practices** as enshrined in **section 23(1)** of the Constitution of South Africa, which asserts that everyone has the right to fair labour practices.
- The precedent set in **Zungu v Premier of the Province of KwaZulu-Natal** ([2018] ZACC 1), which established that cost orders should generally not apply in labour matters unless justifiable reasons are provided.
- The **principles of consistency in disciplinary actions** established in various labour law cases, including *Dunlop Mixing and Technical Services* and *Impala Platinum*.
- The discretion in awarding costs as elaborated in *Dorkin*, asserting that costs in labour matters should be awarded judiciously to avoid deterring parties from approaching courts with legitimate disputes.

### **A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 13-20])**

**Condonation**: The Court found that NUM provided a satisfactory explanation for the delay of approximately 20 days in seeking leave to appeal. The lateness was not deemed wilful or a disregard for court rules, and the Court was satisfied that no significant prejudice would result to SAMANCOR from granting condonation.

**Jurisdiction and Interests of Justice**: The Court recognized that the matter involved constitutional issues related to the rights of employees to fair labour practices, thus asserting its jurisdiction. It assessed the merits of the appeal against the backdrop of past rulings emphasizing the importance of employees' rights in the labour context.

**Merits**: In evaluating whether the dismissals were fair, the Court upheld the Labour Appeal Court's ruling that the employees had engaged in misconduct by working under unsafe conditions, undermining the authority of their supervisor and the safety regulations in their industry. Thus, the Labour Appeal Court's decision to uphold the fairness of the dismissals was deemed justified.

**Costs**: The Court criticized the Labour Appeal Court for failing to provide reasons for its costs orders and for not adhering to established principles concerning costs in labour matters. It concluded that the absence of any unreasonable conduct by NUM warranted the overturning of the costs orders, aligning with the principles outlined in *Zungu* and *Dorkin*.

### **C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 21-24])**

The Constitutional Court ruled as follows:

1. Condonation for late filing was granted.
2. Leave to appeal on the merits of the dismissal was refused.
3. Leave to appeal against the costs orders of the Labour Appeal Court was granted.
4. The appeal against the costs orders of the Labour Appeal Court was upheld, and the costs orders were set aside, with no order as to costs in the Constitutional Court.

The **ratio decidendi** highlights the importance of justifying costs orders in labour-related cases and reaffirmed the right to fair practices as a constitutional safeguard. This ruling underscored the necessity for legal consistency in disciplinary measures applied to employees in the workplace, facilitating equitable treatment and protecting employees' rights to seek redress without the fear of punitive costs. This judgment serves to reinforce the protections afforded to workers under South African labour law and accentuates the need for transparent judicial reasoning in the context of costs. 

Moreover, it suggests a cautious approach for employers in disciplinary actions and costs, aiming to safeguard employees from undue disadvantage in litigation processes. 

Through this case, the Court reiterated the critical function of unions and the judiciary in safeguarding constitutional rights within the workplace, contributing to the ongoing discourse on labour rights and justice in South Africa.
