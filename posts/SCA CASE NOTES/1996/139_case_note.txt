# FIRAC Case Note: RAPPORTEER

**Case Title:** RAPPORTEER  
**Case Number:** 218/96  
**Court:** Supreme Court of South Africa (Appellate Division)  
**Judges:** Hefer, Howie, Plewman AJJ  
**Hearing Date:** 21 November 1996  
**Judgment Date:** 27 November 1996  

---

### F – Facts

The appellant, **Georgie Petrus Kellerman**, contested the validity of his conviction and sentencing by a district court regarding two charges of fraud ([para 2]). The original convictions occurred on **21 September 1993**, and the appellant filed a notice of appeal on **11 October 1993**. Legal representation was obtained, and by **4 March 1994**, the appeal was scheduled for hearing on **23 May 1994**. The dynamics changed when the state informed Kellerman’s counsel on **13 April 1994** that it intended to seek a sentence enhancement ([para 4]).

The appeal hearing was postponed, and, finally, on **9 November 1994**, the appellant’s legal team withdrew the appeal ([paras 3-4]). However, a notification indicated that the appeal would still be heard on **16 March 1995**. Contrary to this, both parties addressed the appellate court on whether the court had the authority to increase the sentence ([para 4]). In a judgment rendered on **9 May 1995**, the court concluded to impose a harsher penalty on Kellerman ([para 7]).

Kellerman’s primary argument was centered on **Section 310A of the Criminal Procedure Act**, which he contended rendered the state's application for sentence enhancement without jurisdiction ([para 3]). The core of his argument hinged on the introduction of this section in 1990, which granted the state the right to appeal a lower court's decision and, implicitly, terminated the established practice of allowing the state to apply for a sentence enhancement when the accused appealed ([paras 5-6]).

### I – Issues

The central legal questions addressed in this case are:

1. **Jurisdiction**: Whether the court had the authority to enhance the sentence given the procedural withdrawal of the appellant’s appeal ([para 5]).
2. **Interpretation of Section 310A**: Does Section 310A abrogate or limit the state's ability to seek sentence enhancements when an accused withdraws their appeal? ([para 9])
3. **Appellate Authority and Practice**: What are the implications of withdrawing an appeal concerning the court's authority to raise a sentence meritoriously? ([para 6])

### R – Rules

The relevant legal framework for this case can be synthesized from:

1. **Section 310A of the Criminal Procedure Act** (Act No. 51 of 1977) establishes the rights of the Prokuror-generaal (Director of Public Prosecutions) to appeal against a judgment from a lower court and outlines the procedural requirements for such appeals ([para 10]).
   
2. **Common Law and Precedent**: Jurisprudence, including cases like **R v Coetzer**, **R v Swanepoel**, and **S v Du Toit**, established the practice where the state could seek sentence enhancement under certain conditions during the appeal process ([paras 7-8]).

### A – Application

The Supreme Court examined the applicability and potential conflict of Section 310A with the pre-existing common law practice. In doing so, the court assessed Kellerman's argument that the state could no longer seek a harsher sentence after the withdrawal of an appeal. The court referenced **S v Du Toit** and **S v Kirsten** to establish that the historical practice remained pertinent, and the power of an appellate court to enhance a sentence was not abated by Section 310A ([paras 11-12]).

The court ruled that Section 310A did not infringe upon the substantive rights of the state but instead modified the procedural mechanism by which they could exercise those rights. Hence, whilst the state could not operate under the antiquated practice post-1990, it was sufficiently empowered by the new regulation to act in cases where the appellant had filed a notice of appeal, effectively maintaining its right to seek enhancements ([para 12-13]).

The court maintained that since Kellerman's withdrawal of the appeal was null and void in this context—considering that the state’s advancement for raising the sentence was legally valid—the appeal against the enhancement was thus affirmed ([para 14]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court concluded that the appellate court indeed retained jurisdiction to enhance Kellerman’s sentence despite the procedural withdrawal of his appeal. Consequently, it affirmed the district court's decision to impose a heavier sentence. The courts underscored the vital role of maintaining public trust in law enforcement and the need for accountability within the police force, highlighting that misconduct by an officer carries significant implications for societal trust ([paras 23-24]). 

Furthermore, the ruling reinforced the significance of the procedural guidelines introduced by Section 310A while clarifying that historic practices may still inform judicial reasoning under specific circumstances. This case thereby serves as a pivotal reference for the procedural boundaries of prosecution appeals in South African law.
