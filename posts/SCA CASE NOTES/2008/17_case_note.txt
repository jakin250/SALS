Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2008

# FIRAC Case Note

### Title: Raliphaswa v Mugivhi (236/2007) [2008] ZASCA 17

### F – Facts: 
The appellant, Thinandavha Gerson Raliphaswa, challenged a judgment from the Thohoyandou High Court where he sought damages for defamation and indecent assault against the first and second respondents, Tshambengwa Thomas Mugivhi and M M Chibambu, both members of the South African Police Service (SAPS). The appellant alleged that on 28 April 2003, while driving past an informal market, he was summoned by the respondents who addressed him derogatorily as ‘tsotsi’. The appellant resisted a demand for search, prompting the respondents to conduct a physical search that allegedly included indecently touching him ([1]–[7]).

The respondents denied the allegations, asserting that they had merely intended to search his vehicle due to suspicions about the appellant's behavior. They claimed that the search was confined to the vehicle's boot and corroborated their accounts with minimal evidence. A key element of the trial involved the failure to call a sympathetic witness, a police reservist who had supposedly criticized the respondents after the incident. The trial court found for the respondents ([2]–[10]).

The appellant's claims were supported by the testimony of Mr. Khakhu, who corroborated the appellant's version of events but with minor discrepancies ([14]). The court highlighted contradictions within the respondents' testimonies, undermining their credibility ([11]–[13]).

### I – Issues:
The primary legal questions in this case revolve around: 
1. Whether the actions of the first and second respondents constituted defamation through their use of the term ‘tsotsi’ and the conduct during the search.
2. Whether the trial court's adverse inference regarding the failure to produce the reservist witness was justified under the factual circumstances.
3. The assessment of damages in light of the appellant’s status and the seriousness of the offenses claimed ([15]–[21]).

### R – Rules:
The court relied upon various legal principles and precedents in its deliberation:
- To establish defamation, the appellant needed to prove that the words used were defamatory, that they were published, and that they were used without lawful justification ([16]) (Independent Newspapers Holdings Ltd v Suliman [2004] 3 All SA 137 (SCA)).
- An evidential presumption arises that defamatory statements are made unlawfully, shifting the burden to the respondent to demonstrate justification ([17]) (National Media Ltd v Bogoshi 1998 (4) SA 1196 (SCA)).
- The provisions governing searches under the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, particularly Section 22(b), mandate that searches without consent or a warrant require reasonable belief in probable cause to justify the infringement of personal privacy rights ([19]). 
- The assessment of damages follows the principle that compensation should be just and fair, considering past judicial awards for similar acts ([21]).

### A – Application:
The court found that the use of the term 'tsotsi' was indeed defamatory as it implied dishonesty, which the respondents failed to contest properly ([16]). The humiliating nature of the situation, amplified by the fact that it occurred in a public setting, heightened the seriousness of the defamation claim. The court viewed the appellant's evidence as credible, particularly when corroborated by Khakhu’s testimony ([14]).

In addressing the adverse inference drawn from the failure to call the reservist, the court reasoned that this inference was unjustified as the appellant had no opportunity to obtain the witness's name or testimony due to police obstruction ([15]). The court posited that an adverse inference must apply equally to both parties when the witness is available to both but stressed that it should not detract from the appellant's established case ([15]).

On the matter of the indecent search, the court noted that the respondents had no reasonable suspicion justifying their actions, which constituted an invasion of the appellant’s rights under the Criminal Procedure Act. The court highlighted that mere noise and parking behavior described by the respondents did not meet the threshold of reasonable suspicion needed to authorize a search ([18]).

In assessing damages, the court accounted for the appellant’s standing in the community as a sheriff and community leader, acknowledging the public nature of the incident which resulted in reputational damage. However, it concluded that the case did not constitute one of the more severe categories of injuria, ultimately determining that R25,000 was a just compensation for both the defamatory act and the assault ([21]).

### C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal reversed the initial ruling of the Thohoyandou High Court, establishing that the first and second respondents were indeed liable for both defamation and the unlawful search resulting in indecent assault. The court awarded R25,000 in damages and criticized the lower court's costs order against the appellant as unsubstantiated ([22]).

The ruling underscores the importance of lawful justification for police actions, the presumption of intent in defamation claims, and the proper procedure in evaluating evidence when witnesses can impact credibility assessments. Ultimately, this decision reinforces the protection of individual dignity and the responsibilities of law enforcement to act within legal boundaries.
