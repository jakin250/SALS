Case Title: Stopforth and Veenendaal v Minister of Justice and Others Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa Case Number: 317/97 and 316/97 Judgment Date: 27 September 1999 Judges: Mahomed CJ; Olivier JA; Melunsky, Farlam, and Madlanga AJJA

Supreme Court of Appeal 1999

Case Title: Stopforth and Veenendaal v Minister of Justice and Others
Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Case Number: 317/97 and 316/97
Judgment Date: 27 September 1999
Judges: Mahomed CJ; Olivier JA; Melunsky, Farlam, and Madlanga AJJA

F – Facts

The appeals concern Darryl Garth Stopforth and Lennerd Michael Veenendaal, South African citizens affiliated with right-wing Afrikaner organizations, namely the Afrikaner Weerstandsbeweging (AWB) and the Orde Boerevolk. In or around 1989, they engaged in a conspiracy to commit acts of terrorism in South West Africa (SWA), now Namibia, including an attack on the UN offices in Outjo on 10 August 1989, which resulted in the deaths of a UN security guard and a South West African policeman ([2]–[4]).

After their arrest in SWA in September 1989 and subsequent escape to South Africa, the Namibian government sought their extradition on charges including murder ([4]–[6]). The South African Magistrate Roux determined their extradition was warranted on 30 April 1992, but Stopforth and Veenendaal did not appeal that decision or follow procedural requirements for appeal later ([6]–[9]). In November 1996, they applied to the Truth and Reconciliation Commission (TRC) for amnesty under the Promotion of National Unity and Reconciliation Act 34 of 1995 ([10]–[11]).

Subsequently, they brought motion proceedings challenging the extradition order issued by the South African Minister of Justice, seeking both suspension of the order pending the TRC’s decision on their amnesty applications and an alternative request for the extradition process to be revisited before the Magistrate ([12]–[13]). The lower court dismissed both applications, leading to this appeal ([12]–[13]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue is whether the TRC Committee has jurisdiction to grant amnesty for acts committed in SWA, particularly concerning whether such acts were “associated with a political objective” as defined in the TRC Act ([16]). Related questions involve the applicability of the TRC’s provisions to conflicts not classified within South Africa’s historical context and whether the acts committed by the Appellants fall under the amnesty provisions of the TRC Act ([14]–[17]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal authorities are:

  1. Promotion of National Unity and Reconciliation Act 34 of 1995:
  • Section 18 outlines the conditions under which amnesty may be granted.
  • Sections 20(1) and 20(2) define acts eligible for amnesty, particularly emphasizing acts associated with a political objective, which must arise from conflicts internal to South African society ([17]–[18]).
  1. Extradition Act 67 of 1962:
  • It governs the conditions and procedures for extradition between South Africa and foreign jurisdictions and establishes that crimes committed abroad must generally have a connection to South Africa to be extraditable ([5]).
  1. Case Law:
  • Secretary for Inland Revenue v Sturrock Sugar Farm (Pty) Ltd and others provide guidance on statutory interpretation with special regard to intent behind the legislation ([21]).

A – Application

The court carefully interpreted the provisions of the TRC Act, concluding that the jurisdiction of the Amnesty Committee is strictly limited to offences linked to the South African historical context of internal conflict. It noted that the acts committed by the Appellants were in support of a foreign conflict, namely in SWA, which is outside the purview defined by the TRC Act, as the statute is designed to address the conflicts of the past that saw South Africans divided ([23]–[24]).

The Appellants attempted to argue that their actions could be included within the broader framework of political actions; however, the judgment emphasized that the intent and impact of their actions were misaligned with the intended reconciliation objectives of the TRC Act ([17]–[25]). Moreover, it was noted that the crimes for which extradition was sought were not actionable in South African courts because they did not constitute extraterritorial offences, further complicating their claim for amnesty under the TRC ([35]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal rejected the appeals from both Stopforth and Veenendaal, holding that the TRC Committee lacked jurisdiction to grant amnesty for the acts committed in SWA in 1989, as these acts did not fall within the scope of South African internal conflicts that the TRC Act aimed to address ([36]). Furthermore, it affirmed the lower court's decision stating that their extradition was lawful given the absence of appeals against the magistrate's ruling. The court concluded that to suspend the extradition order pending TRC adjudication would be futile.

Holding: The appeals by Stopforth are dismissed with costs, and the appeal by Veenendaal is struck off the roll due to his status as a fugitive from justice ([40]).

Coordinates with case law to provide a comprehensive view of the complex issues regarding amnesty, jurisdiction, and extraterritoriality, establishing essential precedents regarding the limits of the TRC's authority and the extradition procedures in South African law.
