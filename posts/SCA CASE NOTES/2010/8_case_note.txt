# FIRAC Case Note: National Director of Public Prosecutions v King (86/09) [2010] ZASCA 8

### F – Facts
The appellant, the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP), appealed against a ruling from the North Gauteng High Court that had ordered the NDPP to provide a “motivated index” detailing every document denied to the respondent, David Cunningham King, within parts B and C of the police docket ([paras 8-10]). King faced serious criminal charges, including fraud, tax evasion, and money laundering, involving substantial monetary claims from the South African Revenue Services (SARS) ([paras 6-7]). The original indictment was served in 2005, and a long procedural history marked by delays and interlocutory proceedings preceded the appeal. The NDPP argued that King was not entitled to a motivated index detailing the withheld documents ([paras 12-27]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal question before the court was whether an accused person is entitled as of right to receive a comprehensive description of each document in parts B and C of the police docket, along with the specific reasons for denying access to such documents, to facilitate a fair trial ([paras 3-4]). 

### R – Rules
The legal principles governing the case derive mainly from the constitutional right to a fair trial as outlined in section 35(3) of the Bill of Rights. The court also referenced the precedent established in Shabalala v Attorney-General of Transvaal, which mandates that certain information deemed incriminating or exculpatory must be disclosed to the accused to uphold the fair trial rights ([paras 1, 4, 11]). The doctrine of litigation privilege, which creates a general rule that one cannot access an adversary’s documents, has been modified in criminal cases through Shabalala, allowing for access to relevant information necessary for a fair trial ([paras 1, 2]).

### A – Application
In its assessment, the court evaluated whether King could demonstrate that access to the motivated index was necessary for his fair trial. The NDPP maintained that much of the documentation in parts B and C was irrelevant or privileged, arguing that it had fulfilled its obligations under Shabalala to disclose information beneficial to King’s defence ([paras 27-29]). King’s legal team argued that an assumption existed that all documents were potentially relevant simply by being included in the docket; however, the court found that this assumption was unfounded without concrete evidence linking the documents to the charges against him ([paras 30-32]).

Moreover, King’s reference to foreign jurisprudence was deemed unavailing as the principles applicable in those jurisdictions did not translate directly to South African law. The court highlighted that while it may be impractical for the NDPP to provide a motivated list of documents, the duty to disclose remains contingent on their relevance to King’s case ([paras 34-35], [40-41]).

### C – Conclusion
The court concluded that King had not established a reasonable requirement for the motivated index, emphasizing that his fundamental right to a fair trial would not be compromised by the NDPP’s refusal to provide such a document. The court upheld the NDPP’s appeal, effectively dismissing the lower court's order ([paras 48-49]). The ruling further reinforced the legal stance that the right to a fair trial does not equate to an unlimited right to access all information in the prosecution’s possession; rather, it is more about ensuring that the accused has adequate information to prepare a meaningful defence. 

Thus, the significance of this ruling lies not only in its application of existing legal principles but also in clarifying the operational limits of disclosure obligations in the context of ongoing criminal proceedings.
