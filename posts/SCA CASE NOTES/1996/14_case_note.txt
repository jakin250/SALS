Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1996

# FIRAC Case Note: Azwihangwisiluckson Ramavhale v. The State

**Case Title**: Azwihangwisiluckson Ramavhale v. The State  
**Court**: Supreme Court of South Africa (Appellate Division)  
**Case No**: 208/95  
**Judges**: Hefer, Grosskopf, and Schutz JJA  
**Date Heard**: 27 February 1996  
**Date Delivered**: 18 March 1996  
**Judgment**: Schutz JA

## F – Facts

The appellant, Azwihangwisiluckson Ramavhale, was convicted of murder and sentenced to 20 years in prison by the Venda Supreme Court for the killing of Azwinndini Ronneth Rabali on 4 June 1992 ([paras 2–3]). The facts of the case are undisputed in that the appellant shot the deceased, but he claimed to have acted in self-defence. 

The incident occurred at the appellant's home in Thohoyandou at approximately 22:30. The appellant fired four shots from a 6.35mm pistol, all of which struck the deceased, who died at the scene ([para 2]). The deceased was found lying outside the lounge, with his body dragged to the location by the appellant, who claimed he was attempting to get the deceased to the hospital ([paras 4–5]).

Key evidence included a hunting knife near the deceased, a lack of blood on it, and no evidence from eyewitnesses due to the absence of the appellant in court ([paras 4–5]). The police discovered a panga beside the house the following morning, which was not tested for prints owing to dew ([para 5]). The appellant's vehicle was found positioned so that it could easily escape, suggesting a quick exit ([para 5]).

The State's case relied on circumstantial evidence and claimed contradictions in the appellant’s statements and the testimony of a witness, Wilson Nembudani, who provided hearsay evidence regarding the deceased's intentions prior to his death ([para 6, 18]). 

The appellant did not testify, but his narrative asserted that he believed the deceased posed an imminent threat as he forcibly entered his residence armed with a knife, supported by accomplices outside ([para 15]).

## I – Issues

The legal issues presented in this case involve questions of self-defence and the admissibility of hearsay evidence. Specifically, the key questions include:

1. Did the appellant act in legitimate self-defence?
2. Were the hearsay statements made by Nembudani appropriately admitted, and if so, did they violate the appellant's fair trial rights?
3. How did the reliance on hearsay without direct evidence impact the overall validity of the appellant's conviction? ([paras 17, 24])

## R – Rules

The foundational rules at play in this case involve self-defence principles under South African law, specifically where an individual may use reasonable force to protect themselves from imminent harm. The rules also include statutory provisions on hearsay evidence, particularly sections that govern its admissibility under Proclamation 37 of 1991 (Venda). 

1. **Self-Defence**: The appellant bore the onus of proving that he acted in self-defence. According to South African case law, self-defence must be proportional and necessary ([para 54]).

2. **Hearsay Evidence**: The admissibility of hearsay is governed by strict standards, where it is generally inadmissible unless its inclusion serves the interests of justice, as established in S v Dyimbane and other jurisprudence ([paras 24, 28]).

3. **Credibility and Reliability**: Assessments of witness credibility must be made judiciously, especially in criminal proceedings, to ensure fair trials and just outcomes ([para 30]).

## A – Application

The court scrutinized the self-defence claim by considering the immediate circumstances surrounding the shooting. The evidence, particularly circumstantial, suggested doubt regarding the imminent threat posed to the appellant at the time of the shooting. The court noted that the appellant's version—being attacked by multiple individuals while armed—needed thorough evidence to support its credibility ([paras 54–55]).

The admission of Nembudani’s hearsay evidence was critically analyzed. The trial judge accepted this evidence and concluded that it implied the deceased had a motive to visit the appellant, thus undermining the appellant's self-defence claim. However, the appellate court found significant shortcomings in how the court below handled the evidence, especially in allowing the hearsay to establish an element of the deceased's intent without appropriate cross-examination opportunities for the appellant ([paras 17, 29, 41]).

The court emphasized the prejudicial nature of admitting the hearsay evidence late in the proceedings, which significantly compromised the appellant's ability to challenge the reliability of that evidence or mount a proper defence ([paras 41–44]). The complexities surrounding the evidence of the knife and the circumstances under which the deceased's vehicle was found were also crucial to determining whether the appellant's version of events could reasonably stand ([paras 48–52]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court found substantial irregularities concerning the admission of hearsay evidence that ultimately denied the appellant a fair trial. The appellate judgement concluded that the evidence supporting the murder conviction was compromised by these irregularities and the State's failure to meet its burden of proof beyond reasonable doubt.

Thus, the appeal was granted, and the conviction and sentence were overturned, highlighting the importance of procedural integrity in ensuring justice ([paras 54–55]). The ruling serves as a critical reference for issues surrounding self-defence and the rigorous standards required for admissibility of hearsay in the judicial process.
