Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Khobane v S (887/15) [2016] ZASCA 124

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–8])

In *Khobane v S*, the appellant, Donald Khobane, was employed as an ATM consultant at Nedbank, responsible for managing nine ATMs in Johannesburg ([para 2]). Following his abrupt departure from work on July 30, 2009, a cash shortage exceeding R3 million was discovered in the ATMs under his control. Khobane was arrested on August 19, 2009, and later confessed—under dubious circumstances—to removing the cash, influenced by a self-styled "spiritual healer" ([paras 3–4]).

After a trial including legal representation, Khobane was convicted of theft on September 4, 2012, and sentenced to 15 years’ imprisonment. Importantly, the charge sheet did not mention the potential minimum sentence under the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 (the Act), which mandates a minimum sentence of 15 years for similar offenses ([paras 4–6]). Following the dismissal of his application for leave to appeal, Khobane petitioned the High Court, which was also denied ([para 8]). After further legal proceedings, the Supreme Court of Appeal eventually granted him leave to appeal against the sentence ([para 8]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–2, 9–10])

The primary legal issues in this case revolve around the proper application of minimum sentencing provisions under the Act and the implications of not informing Khobane of these provisions during the trial. The main questions posed were:

1. Did the omission of the potential minimum sentence in the charge sheet and failure to warn the appellant compromise his right to a fair trial? ([para 13])
2. Should the court reconsider the appropriateness of the sentence, particularly in light of the bizarre circumstances surrounding Khobane’s actions at the time of the offense? ([para 16])

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 9–12, 14–15])

The judgment referenced significant legal precedents regarding minimum sentencing and the rights of the accused. Notably, in *S v Malgas*, the Supreme Court emphasized that minimum sentences should normally be imposed unless there are "truly convincing reasons" for a different response ([para 10]). Furthermore, the court acknowledged the importance of giving proper warnings to an accused regarding potential minimum sentences, as this practice is essential for ensuring a fair trial ([paras 11–12]).

The principles enunciated in *S v Legoa* and *S v Ndlovu* were also cited, which highlighted the accused's right to be informed of the charges and the consequences involved ([para 14]). The case of *Khoasasa* clarified the procedure for appeals following the refusal of a leave to appeal, which again brought into question whether Khobane had reasonable prospects of success on appeal given the peculiar circumstances of his case ([paras 18–21]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 11–22])

In its analysis, the court found substantial grounds to reconsider the imposed sentence. The lack of notification regarding the minimum sentence posed a significant concern, particularly since this could affect the accused’s understanding of the charges and the seriousness of the potential penalties ([paras 11–12]). Moreover, the court underscored that the trial magistrate had not sufficiently engaged with the details of Khobane's defense, particularly his psychological state under the influence of the “spiritual healer” ([para 16]).

The court also expressed regret that due procedural fairness was not observed in the initial sentencing, stating that the magistrate's failure to inquire whether Khobane was aware of the risk of a 15-year sentence compounded the fairness issue ([paras 16–17]). Consequently, the justices found reasonable prospects of a different outcome upon appeal regarding the original sentence. They emphasized that while theft was a common law crime, the statutory minimum greatly altered the expectations surrounding potential sentencing ([para 15]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 22–23])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Khobane’s appeal, concluding that the refusal of the lower court to grant leave to appeal was erroneous. The order was set aside and the matter was remitted back to the Gauteng Local Division for reconsideration of the sentence ([para 23]). The court emphasized the importance of procedural fairness and the necessity to inform an accused of potential sentencing ramifications. This decision highlights a broader commitment to ensuring that defendants’ rights are upheld within the criminal justice system, particularly in cases involving significant sentences that diverge from public expectations regarding common law offenses.

In sum, *Khobane v S* serves as a critical reminder of the necessity for judicial rigor in ensuring that defendants are aware of the full scope of the charges they face, especially as they relate to significant statutory sentencing provisions. The implications of the ruling extend to not only Khobane's case but potentially all future cases where minimum sentencing statutes apply.
