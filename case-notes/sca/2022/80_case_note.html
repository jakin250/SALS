Supreme Court of Appeal

Peermont Global (North West) (Pty) Ltd v Chairperson of the North West Gambling Review Tribunal and Others and Two Other Cases [2022] ZASCA 80

Legislative Framework: The North West Gambling Act 2 of 2001 and the National Gambling Act 7 of 2004 regulate gambling in South Africa ([paras 6–8]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 80

F – Facts

The case involves three related appeals concerning the granting of bingo licenses by the North West Gambling Board to Jonoforce (Pty) Ltd and Latiano 560 (Pty) Ltd. Peermont Global (North West) (Pty) Ltd (Peermont), a competitor and objector, sought to challenge the legality of these decisions based on procedural fairness and the impact of the licensing on its nearby casinos.

  1. Legislative Framework: The North West Gambling Act 2 of 2001 and the National Gambling Act 7 of 2004 regulate gambling in South Africa ([paras 6–8]).
  2. Application Process: On October 2, 2015, the North West Gambling Board invited applications for bingo licenses through a public notice, which included a Request for Applications (RFA) detailing procedural requirements ([paras 18–27]).
  3. Peermont's Involvement: Peermont objected to the licensing on the grounds that it was unfairly denied access to the amended RFA and that crucial parts of the applications were not made available for public inspection. Moreover, Peermont contended that electronic bingo terminals (EBTs) would negatively affect its nearby casinos ([paras 21–27]).
  4. Licenses Awarded: After public hearings where Peermont made objections, the Board awarded licenses to Jonoforce and Latiano on June 10, 2016 ([paras 20–22, 36]). Peermont's internal review application was dismissed by the Tribunal and subsequently by the High Court, leading to this appeal.

I – Issues

The legal issues at stake include:

  1. Procedural Fairness: Was Peermont denied procedural fairness in the licensing process, particularly concerning access to the amended RFA? ([paras 23–24, 36]).
  2. Definition of Bingo: Did the EBTs comply with the statutory definition of bingo as per the North West Act? ([paras 45–56]).
  3. Impact Assessment: Did the Board adequately consider the potential adverse impact of the new bingo operations on Peermont's casinos? ([paras 66–70]).

R – Rules

The following rules and legislative provisions were pertinent to the case:

  1. Hearing & Review Powers: The Board’s authority to grant licenses is outlined in the North West Gambling Act, which mandates public notice and opportunities for objection ([paras 8–12]).
  2. Public Inspection Requirements: Section 32 of the North West Act requires that documents relating to applications must be available for public inspection, with specific exceptions for confidential information ([paras 40–41]).
  3. Definition of Bingo: The statutory definitions provided under the National Gambling Act and the North West Act specify how bingo should be conducted, including the permissible use of EBTs ([paras 53–56]).
  4. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act: The principles enshrined within PAJA, particularly regarding procedural fairness and the legality of administrative action, were invoked ([paras 23–27]).

A – Application

Procedural Fairness

The court evaluated Peermont's claims of procedural unfairness by determining whether Peermont was adequately informed about the licensing criteria. The Board's policy that required interested parties to procure the amended RFA within a specified period was upheld. The court found no obligation on the Board to provide the RFA beyond this point, concluding that Peermont bore the responsibility for failing to request the amended document on time ([paras 24–35]). Moreover, it was stated that Peermont’s participation in public hearings and its subsequent objections demonstrated its capacity to engage meaningfully with the process despite not having the amended RFA initially.

Definition of Bingo

In interpreting the definition of "bingo" under the North West Act, the court found that the EBTs could be classified under the broader definition allowing for electronic variations of the game. Unlike cases concerning traditional bingo practices, the current legislative context was said to embrace technological developments, supporting the notion that the EBTs could still constitute bingo, thereby deeming the licenses lawful ([paras 45–56]).

Consideration of Economic Impact

While Peermont argued that the introduction of bingo operations would adversely impact its own casinos, the Board’s decision-making process was characterized as reflective of a comprehensive socio-economic analysis. The Tribunal’s assessment, which noted that the potential economic benefits outweighed Peermont's concerns, was upheld. The court emphasized that the Board had appropriately balanced the various relevant factors, including public interest and empowerment of historically disadvantaged individuals, in arriving at its decision ([paras 66–70]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed all three appeals, affirming the decisions of both the High Court and the Tribunal. The ruling reiterated the legal validity of the licensing process undertaken by the North West Gambling Board, emphasizing that Peermont’s procedural rights were not unduly infringed, and the Board acted within its legal and regulatory mandate. The judgment also reinforced the position that economic transformation initiatives must be weighed against individual corporate interests within the gambling industry ([paras 71–72]).

This case serves to clarify the standards required for procedural fairness in administrative licensing processes and the adaptability of statutory definitions in responding to technological innovations within traditional gaming frameworks. Additionally, it underscores the necessity for regulatory bodies to balance competitive fairness with broader socio-economic objectives.

End of Case Note
