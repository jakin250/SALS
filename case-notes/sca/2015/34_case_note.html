Supreme Court of Appeal

The South African Hang and Paragliding Association v Bewick (1010/2013) [2015] ZASCA 34

The first appellant is the South African Hang and Paragliding Association (SAHPA), and the second appellant is the South African Civil Aviation Authority (SACAA).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 [2015] ZASCA 34

F – Facts

The first appellant is the South African Hang and Paragliding Association (SAHPA), and the second appellant is the South African Civil Aviation Authority (SACAA). The respondent, Mrs. Diane Bewick, a radiographer from Tyneside, UK, sustained severe injuries during a tandem paragliding flight for which she had paid ([1]).

In April 2004, while on holiday in Cape Town, Bewick expressed a desire to participate in tandem paragliding. The flight took place on 12 April 2004 at Hermanus, during which a wing collapse occurred shortly after takeoff. As a result of the incident, Bewick sustained horrendous injuries that left her paralyzed ([2]). She subsequently sued multiple parties, including the pilot, SACAA, and SAHPA, for damages totaling R25 million. Before the trial commenced, she settled with the pilot and his employers, proceeding only against the two appellants ([3]).

The High Court ruled in favor of Bewick, establishing that tandem paragliding for reward was illegal, and the appellants failed in their duty to prevent the illegal activity. They were held liable jointly and severally for any damages Bewick might prove ([3]).

I – Issues

  1. Legality of Tandem Paragliding for Reward: Was the act of tandem paragliding for remuneration illegal at the time of the accident?
  2. Wrongfulness: Did the appellants’ failure to halt the illegal activity constitute wrongful conduct in delict?
  3. Causation: Was there a causal link between the appellants' alleged omission and the harm Bewick suffered?

R – Rules

The foundation of delict involves establishing wrongfulness, negligence, and causation. In this case, the question of wrongfulness depends on the existence of a legal duty stemming from public policy and statutory requirements. As cited in Gouda Boerdery BK v Transnet 2005 (5) SA 490 (SCA), it was necessary for the respondent to prove facts establishing the legal duty ([5]).

The principal statutory sources governing civil aviation include the Civil Aviation Act and the corresponding regulations ([12]). Specifically, the Air Navigation Regulations and Civil Aviation Regulations from 1997 indicated that tandem paragliding for reward may have been illegal, as per several provisions ([10]–[11]).

A – Application

Legality of Tandem Paragliding

To navigate the legality, the court examined the evidence presented, particularly noting that the nuances of legal status were debated within both SAHPA and SACAA. While Bewick's arguments about illegalities were based on SAHPA’s Operations Manual and Civil Aviation Regulations, the court recognized the ambiguity surrounding whether paragliding was categorized correctly as an ‘aircraft’ under the regulatory framework ([10]–[12]). The appellants contended that the law did not prohibit tandem paragliding, as the majority within the paragliding community believed commercial operations were safe and intended to be legalized ([9]).

Wrongfulness

The court a quo had found a duty of care owed to Bewick based on public accountability, suggesting that SACAA's negligence in failing to enforce regulations was a breach of its constitutional obligations to ensure safety ([24]). However, the Supreme Court of Appeal questioned the reasonableness of imposing liability given the widespread perception that tandem flights were safe. The judgment in Minister of Safety and Security v Van Duivenboden 2002 (6) SA 431 (SCA) was relevant when considering whether a legal duty should result in civil liability ([24]).

Causation

Following the High Court's ruling, the application of the ‘but-for’ test to establish causation was scrutinized. The court emphasized that without definitive evidence linking the absence of intervention by SAHPA and SACAA directly to the accident, causation could not be established. The possibility that Bewick could have still been injured without the appellants' failure to act posed significant challenges to her claim ([35]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately ruled in favor of the appellants, overturning the High Court's decision. It held that there was insufficient evidence to prove the illegality of tandem paragliding for reward at the relevant time and that the appellants' omission did not meet the threshold of legal wrongfulness ([38]). The court noted that imposing liability under these circumstances would not only be unreasonable but could lead to boundless liability for various conceivable accidents linked by loose assumptions of negligence. The appeal was upheld with costs, dismissing Bewick's claims against the appellants ([38]).

The implications of this ruling are significant, as they reinforce the importance of established legal frameworks governing new recreational activities, emphasizing the need for clear regulations regarding safety and liability in the context of increasingly popular adventure sports.
