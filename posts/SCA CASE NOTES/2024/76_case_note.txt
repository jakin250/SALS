Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note: City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality v Malvigenix NPC t/a Wecanwin and Others (90/2023)

## F – Facts
The City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality (the City) appeals against a judgment of the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which ordered it to reverse property rates levied on certain properties within Lombardy Estate based on invalid valuation rolls ([1]). This dispute originated from a 2016 application by Lombardy Development (Pty) Ltd and 13 property owners, leading to a High Court decision that declared the City’s 2012 supplementary valuation roll and 2013 general valuation roll invalid ([2]–[4]). The High Court found that these rolls improperly re-categorized residential properties as ‘vacant,’ consequently leading to substantial increases in rates for the affected property owners, with some facing increases of up to 700% ([5]).

The judgment issued by Tuchten J was subsequently upheld by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ([2]–[3]). Following the successful claim in Lombardy, the respondents in the present case—Wecanwin and its members—sought to be treated equally under the Lombardy judgment, requesting refunds for excess rates charged on the basis of their properties being categorized as vacant by the invalid rolls ([10]).

In response, the City contended that the High Court’s order was only applicable to the specific parties involved in the Lombardy case, arguing that Wecanwin must initiate review proceedings to achieve similar relief ([12], [14]). Wecanwin argued that the Lombardy judgment was a judgment in rem, and thus applicable to all affected properties within the Lombardy Estate, relieving them of further obligations to litigate ([11], [20]).

## I – Issues
The key legal issues in this appeal include:
1. Whether the judgment in the Lombardy case, deemed a judgment in rem, applies to property owners not party to that case ([14]).
2. Whether Wecanwin was required to initiate review proceedings to obtain relief similar to that granted to the plaintiffs in Lombardy ([14]–[15]).

## R – Rules
The court primarily relied on principles established in prior cases and statutes, notably:
- **Judgment in Rem vs. Judgment in Personam**: The distinction is crucial for determining the scope of the court's ruling as it relates to all property owners versus just the parties in the litigation (City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality v Lombardy Development (Pty) Ltd and Others, 2018) ([9]).
- **Oudekraal Principle**: Relates to the validity of actions performed based on an initially invalid act and the need to assess whether the invalidity of a first act renders subsequent acts invalid ([12], [19]).
- **Local Government: Municipal Property Rates Act 6 of 2004**: Governs the valuation and categorization of properties for rating purposes, empowering municipalities to levy different rates based on property use ([4]).

The constitutional provisions under Section 229 allow municipalities to impose rates on properties, aligning with the principles of fairness and equity outlined in the Rates Act ([4]).

## A – Application
The SCA's analysis focused on clarifying the misinterpretations by the City regarding the nature of the High Court's judgment. The court reiterated that the decision in Lombardy, being a judgment in rem, applies universally to all properties categorized within the subject matter discussed ([15]–[16]). This interpretation means that the invalidation of the property categorization automatically rescinded the imposition of the increased rates on all affected properties, including those owned by Wecanwin members.

Further, the SCA rejected the City’s reliance on the Oudekraal principle, emphasizing that validity once declared invalid does not necessitate further review proceedings for affected stakeholders ([20]–[22]). The court drew from precedents emphasizing that when an invalid categorization is set aside, all related actions—such as the imposition of rates—are rendered equally invalid ([19]).

The court also elaborated on the City's obligation to rectify its unlawful decisions, asserting that the burden of correcting such invalidities lies primarily with the municipality itself, not with the affected individuals ([24]–[26]). Furthermore, it highlighted that public functionaries must act proactively to remedy unlawful conduct ([26]).

## C – Conclusion
The appeal by the City of Tshwane was dismissed, reaffirming the High Court's order directing the City to refund Wecanwin for the overcharged rates based on the invalid valuation rolls ([28]). The court concluded that Wecanwin members were entitled to the same relief as the Lombardy applicants since the declared invalidity and setting aside of the valuation rolls encompassed all properties within the Lombardy Estate, thereby relieving them of initiating independent review proceedings. The judgment emphasizes the duty of local governments to ensure lawful conduct in the administration of property rates and to respond appropriately to judicial rulings ([26]–[27]).

The implications of this ruling underscore the importance of adhering to lawful procedures in municipal governance and the necessity for municipalities to promptly rectify unlawful rates categorization to uphold principles of equity enshrined in the Constitution ([25]).
