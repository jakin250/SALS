Supreme Court of Appeal

Afgri Grain Marketing (Pty) Ltd v Trustees for the Time Being of Copenship Bulkers A/S (in Liquidation) and Others

Copenship initiated a security arrest application under section 5(3)(a) of the Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983 (AJRA) to secure claims totaling USD 6,372,593.78 against Afgri arising from the charter party ([paras 2, 21]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 Afgri Grain Marketing (Pty) Ltd v Trustees for the Time Being of Copenship Bulkers A/S (in Liquidation) and Others 797/2018 [2019] ZASCA 67 (29 May 2019)

Neutral Citation: Afgri Grain Marketing (Pty) Ltd v Trustees for the Time Being of Copenship Bulkers A/S (in Liquidation) and Others 797/2018 [2019] ZASCA 67 (29 May 2019)

FIRAC Analysis

F – Facts

The disputes in this case arise from a charter party agreement executed on 21 August 2008 between Copenship and Afgri, concerning the charter of a vessel, MV ‘Fonarun Naree’, for shipments of maize. The shipment process commenced with an explosion occurring aboard the vessel while loading, leading to subsequent claims by the consignee (National Cereals & Produce Board) against the vessel, alleging cargo damage ([paras 7-10]).

Copenship initiated a security arrest application under section 5(3)(a) of the Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983 (AJRA) to secure claims totaling USD 6,372,593.78 against Afgri arising from the charter party ([paras 2, 21]). An initial order was granted by the Gauteng Division of the High Court ex parte on 21 February 2018, allowing for the arrest of Afgri’s funds in specific bank accounts ([paras 2-3]).

Afgri subsequently contested this order, arguing that Copenship failed to provide substantial grounds for the arrest and that the arrest itself was an abuse of process. It further argued that the ex parte application did not adequately disclose material facts that would justify such an arrest ([paras 3-4]).

Following the dismissal of Afgri's application for reconsideration of the arrest order by Weiner J, Afgri sought leave to appeal ([para 5]). As a result, various procedural entanglements arose, leading Copenship to seek urgent relief regarding the status of the arrest order pending appeal ([paras 4-5]).

I – Issues

The primary issue was whether the respondents had established the requisite account of a "genuine and reasonable need for security" for the arrest under section 5(3)(a) of the AJRA ([para 21]). Additionally, the appropriateness of the terms stipulated in the arrest order, particularly concerning future funds and the broader implications for Afgri's operational finances, were under scrutiny ([paras 17-19, 60]).

R – Rules

The arrest under the AJRA is contingent upon an applicant demonstrating:

  1. Existence of a maritime claim enforceable through an action in personam against the property owner.
  2. A prima facie case regarding the enforceability of said claim.
  3. A genuine and reasonable need for security for the claim ([para 1]).

Intricacies relating to the reconsideration procedure were also explored, wherein the court would assess the merits based on all material presented, despite the original application having been made ex parte ([paras 11-14, 60]).

Noteworthy precedents cited included:

  • MV Orient Stride, which outlined the principles governing genuine apprehensions of non-payment for security applications.
  • Bocimar NV v Kotor Overseas Shipping Ltd, reaffirming that evidential burdens rest on the applicant to validate claims of non-payment apprehensions based on factual underpinnings ([paras 21, 22]).

A – Application

In analysing whether Copenship had a genuine and reasonable need for security, the court found them deficient in substantial evidence. The claims made were largely speculative.

  1. Refusal to Provide Security: Copenship's claim that Afgri's refusal to provide security indicated an unwillingness to satisfy a future award lacked specificity and was not substantiated by links to broader financial reliability ([paras 24-26]).
  2. Shipping Industry Context: The appellant’s claims regarding financial strain in shipping were deemed irrelevant as Afgri was not a shipping company but rather a commodity trader in grain, thus circumstantially immune to shipping industry fluctuations ([paras 25-26]).
  3. Financial Analysis: Afgri’s financial statements demonstrated substantial receivables and profitability, fundamentally undermining Copenship’s arguments regarding insolvency or inability to satisfy any future award ([paras 37, 60]).
  4. Delay in Seeking Security: The decade-long gap since the initial causative incidents further weakened Copenship's claim. Such protracted delays, without justifiable explanation, were counterproductive to establishing urgency or a need for security ([para 28]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal found that Copenship had not demonstrated a genuine and reasonable need for security, thereby failing to meet the necessary criteria for a security arrest. As a result, the appeal was upheld, and the High Court’s original order was set aside, instructing that costs be borne by the applicants, including expenses for two counsels [[para 62]].

Implications: This ruling reinforces the essential scrutiny that security arrest applications must undergo, emphasizing the need for substantive, fact-based grounds to justify any interference with the financial resources of defendants in maritime claims. It also highlights judicial caution in interpreting maritime claims within the broader economic context of the involved parties.
