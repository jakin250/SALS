Doorewaard and Another v The State [2020] ZASCA 155

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 155

F - Facts

The appeal in Doorewaard and Another v The State concerns the conviction of Pieter Doorewaard and Philip Schutte on charges of murder, kidnapping, intimidation, theft, and pointing of a firearm by the North West Division of the High Court. The case revolved around the death of 15-year-old Mathlomola Jonas Mosweu, referred to as 'the deceased.' The main evidence against the appellants came from a single witness, Mr Sibongile Pakisi, who alleged that the appellants assaulted and ultimately threw the deceased from a moving bakkie on 20 April 2017. The appellants denied these allegations, asserting that the deceased had jumped out of the bakkie while being transported to a police station. The trial court found the appellants guilty despite serious inconsistencies in Mr Pakisi's testimony and the inadequacies of the police investigation ([paras 1–2], [24], [39],[115]).

I - Issues

The central legal questions addressed by the Supreme Court of Appeal included:

  1. Mutually Destructive Evidence: Was Mr Pakisi's evidence credible enough to convict the appellants in light of the coherent defence presented by them? ([paras 22–24]).
  2. Single Witness Rule: Did the trial court properly apply the legal principles concerning the evidence of a single witness, notably the requirement for corroboration? ([paras 22-23]).
  3. Criminal Investigation and Procedural Fairness: Was there a failure by law enforcement to investigate the matter adequately, impacting the reliability of the State’s evidence? ([paras 46–49]).
  4. Onus of Proof: Did the prosecution meet its burden to prove the guilt of the appellants beyond a reasonable doubt? ([paras 22, 43]).

R - Rules

The court relied heavily on established rules of criminal law regarding the evidence of a single witness as per s 208 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977. This section allows for a conviction based solely on the testimony of a competent witness, provided that the court is satisfied with the witness's credibility and reliability. The court reiterated the principle established in S v Mkohle concerning the need to scrutinize contradictory evidence and the necessity of corroborative evidence in light of the serious charges ([paras 22-25]).

The court also referenced the presumption of innocence, emphasizing that the onus of proof lies with the prosecution to establish guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. The standards for assessing the credibility of a single witness were underscored through case law, particularly the need to correlate witness testimony with available corroborating evidence ([paras 22-35], [72-74]).

A - Application

During its analysis, the Supreme Court of Appeal identified significant contradictions within Mr Pakisi's testimony, highlighting that his account of events was inconsistent and not corroborated by objective evidence. For example, Mr Pakisi did not seek immediate medical assistance even after alleging considerable physical harm, and crucial witnesses like police officers who could have corroborated his claims were not called to testify. The court also observed that forensic investigations were inadequately conducted, including the failure to test for blood found at the scene. The court further noted that the appellants had no prior history of misconduct in their dealings with the police, which factored into their credibility ([paras 22-32], [76-84]).

The court concluded that the appellants’ version of events was more plausible, particularly given a lack of direct evidence proving that the deceased had been thrown from the bakkie as alleged by the prosecution. The police investigation's shortcomings were critical to the assessment of the evidence and contributed to the court's determination that the prosecution had failed to meet its burden of proof ([paras 43-44], [83-84]).

C - Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, finding that the State had not proved its case beyond a reasonable doubt. The convictions and sentences imposed by the trial court were set aside, and both appellants were found not guilty and discharged. The ruling underscored the paramount importance of rigorous evidential standards and highlighted the impact of a flawed police investigation on the fairness of the trial process ([paras 44–44], [70], [83-84]).

The case illustrates vital principles surrounding the evaluation of witness credibility, the need for corroborative evidence in serious criminal cases, and the implications of investigatory failings in the judiciary's assessment of guilt.
