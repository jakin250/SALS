# Case Note: Rohde v The State (Case No 815/2019)

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1-3])
In the case of **Rohde v The State** (Case No 815/2019) [2021] ZASCA 134, the background to the appeal involves the tragic death of Susan Francis Rohde, the wife of Jason Thomas Rohde, CEO of a real estate company, who was found dead in their hotel suite bathroom on 24 July 2016 during a company conference in Stellenbosch ([para 1]). The appellant was charged with the murder of his wife and with defeating or obstructing the course of justice ([para 2]). Specifically, count 2 alleged that he manipulated the crime scene to suggest suicide,[para 2]; after a protracted trial in the Western Cape Division of the High Court, the trial court convicted him on both counts and sentenced him to an effective 20 years imprisonment ([para 2]). The appellant's subsequent application for leave to appeal was granted by the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 3]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 3-4])
The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal revolved around whether the deceased had died due to homicide by manual strangulation and/or smothering or whether her death was a suicide by hanging ([para 3]). This determination hinged on the evaluation of forensic evidence, particularly the opinions presented by expert pathologists regarding the cause of death. The central question was whether the respondent proved beyond a reasonable doubt that the deceased had been murdered, or if a reasonable possibility existed that she had committed suicide ([para 3]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 67-68])
The court relied upon established legal principles regarding the burden of proof in criminal cases, which requires that, to sustain a conviction, the prosecution must prove all elements of the crime beyond a reasonable doubt. Relevant case law that informs these principles includes *S v Malgas* 2001 (1) SACR 469 (SCA), which discusses the necessity for courts to be guided by prescribed minimum sentences and the conditions under which these sentences may be departed from ([para 90]). Furthermore, the court addressed procedural issues regarding the admissibility of expert testimony, emphasizing the right to a fair trial under the Constitution of South Africa ([para 68]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 82-93])
In analyzing the evidence, the court evaluated the credibility and reliability of the pathologists’ testimonies. Dr. Coetzee-Khan, the principal forensic pathologist, provided compelling evidence that the deceased's injuries were consistent with homicide, specifically manual strangulation, and noted the absence of marks indicative of suicide. Despite the appellant’s claims of self-defense, the unanimous opinion from the prosecution's expert witnesses concluded that the observed neck injuries were consistent with strangulation rather than an accident or suicide ([paras 52-56]).

The trial court's reliance on its observations regarding a pillowcase to conclude that the deceased had been smothered was critiqued by the Supreme Court of Appeal. The court emphasized that any substantive findings should be based on evidence presented in court and not merely on the trial court's own observations, which had not been properly put to the witnesses ([para 83]). The inconsistency of the forensic pathologists’ interpretations raised reasonable doubt about the cause of death, leading the appellate court to question the finding of smothering and the associated injuries resulting from it. 

In assessing the severity of the crime, the Supreme Court of Appeal reiterated the implications of domestic violence and the necessity of appropriate sentencing. Ultimately, while the convictions were upheld, the sentences were adjusted to reflect the correct interpretation of events, leading to a lesser sentence due to the absence of evidence supporting the brutal nature of the alleged offenses beyond manual strangulation ([paras 87-93]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 94-95])
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Rohde’s convictions for murder and assault but revised the sentences imposed by the trial court. Rohde was sentenced to 15 years for the murder and 3 years for the charge of obstructing justice, with the latter sentence running concurrently ([para 94]). This decision underscored the importance of weighing forensic evidence carefully and managing assumptions made by trial courts in their evaluations. 

The court's judgment serves as an essential precedent that impacts the interpretation of expert testimony and its admissibility, emphasizing that judicial observations must remain grounded in the evidence presented. Furthermore, it reiterates the need for a clear differentiation between proven facts and speculative narratives, particularly in cases involving domestic violence. The implications of this ruling extend beyond Rohde's individual case, as it establishes a standard for future adjudications concerning domestic violence cases and the evidentiary requirements necessary to substantiate claims of self-defense or suicide versus homicide.
