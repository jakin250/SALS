# Case Note: *Open Horizon Ltd v Carnilinx (Pty) Ltd* [2022] ZASCA 75

## FIRAC Case Note

### Facts

The appeal in *Open Horizon Ltd v Carnilinx (Pty) Ltd* arises from a judgment of the Gauteng Division of the High Court of Pretoria, where Hughes J dismissed the appellant's application concerning the infringement of its registered PACIFIC trade marks ([1]–[2]). The appellant, Open Horizon Ltd, sought an interdict against the respondent, Carnilinx (Pty) Ltd, regarding its use of various ATLANTIC marks—specifically ATLANTIC, ATLANTIC WAVE, ATLANTIC MENTHOL, ATLANTIC BREEZE, ATLANTIC BLUE, ATLANTIC CORAL, ATLANTIC APPLE CRUSH, and ATLANTIC CHERRY CRUSH—which the appellant contended were confusingly similar to its own PACIFIC trade marks registered under class 34, covering tobacco and cigarettes ([2]–[3]).

Open Horizon Ltd holds several PACIFIC marks, the oldest registered in November 2003. In contrast, Carnilinx applied to register the ATLANTIC marks starting in June 2012 ([3]). The core of the dispute focuses on whether the respondent's use of the ATLANTIC marks constitutes an infringement under section 34(1)(a) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 (the “Act”) or amounts to unlawful competition ([4]–[5]).

### Issues

The central legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the ATLANTIC marks used by the respondent were likely to cause confusion or deception with the registered PACIFIC marks of the appellant, thereby constituting trademark infringement under section 34(1)(a) of the Act. Additionally, the case examined whether the respondent's conduct constituted unlawful competition, despite the initial claim focused on statutory infringement ([6]–[8]). A secondary, procedural issue arose concerning the appellant's amendment to include the claim for unlawful competition and whether sufficient grounds for this amendment had been established ([20]–[23]).

### Rules

The court relied on the principles embodied in section 34(1)(a) of the Trade Marks Act, which states that trademark rights are infringed by the unauthorized use of a mark that is identical or similar enough to cause confusion ([4]). It cited previous rulings reinforcing that the query is limited to comparing the marks themselves rather than external factors impacting consumer perception ([5]).

Important precedents addressed included *Plascon-Evans Paints (TVL) Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd* for the appropriate comparative approach, and *Lucky Star Ltd v Lucky Brands (Pty) Ltd* for the necessity of an objective similarity comparison, excluding extraneous matter ([5]–[6]). Furthermore, in relation to unlawful competition, the importance of a sound evidential basis for such a claim was highlighted, particularly post-amendment of the pleadings ([24]–[26]).

### Application

The court engaged in an objective comparison of the PACIFIC and ATLANTIC marks. The ruling emphasized that while the marks evoke oceanic concepts, they are distinct names of different geographical oceans that do not present evident phonetic or visual similarities ([10]–[19]). The court found that the average consumer, when encountering the ATLANTIC brand, would not confuse it with the PACIFIC marks due to their distinct identities. Consequently, the appellant's claim was deemed insufficient to demonstrate the requisite level of confusion necessary for a successful trademark infringement claim ([19]).

For the unlawful competition claim, the court noted that the appellant failed to present adequate evidence in support, particularly as the claim emerged after the original application, thereby lacking cohesiveness with previously submitted evidence. The court also reaffirmed that without proving a likelihood of confusion or deception relevant to the respondent's actions, the unlawful competition claim could not stand, echoing prior judicial caution against overly elastic interpretations of the unlawful competition doctrine ([22]–[27]).

### Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appeal must be dismissed, affirming the lower court's findings that the respondent’s ATLANTIC marks did not constitute an infringement of the PACIFIC marks nor unlawful competition ([28]). The court upheld the vital principle that trademarks do not grant monopolistic control over concepts or ideas but require demonstrable confusion or deception between marks to warrant infringement. This ruling underscores the necessity for robust evidence and clear arguments in claims of unlawful competition, especially where they surface late in proceedings. The judgment reinforces existing legal standards concerning trademark protection and underscores the importance of clearly defined claims in both statutory and common law contexts.
