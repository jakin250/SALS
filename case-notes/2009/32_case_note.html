Head of Department: Mpumalanga Department of Education v Hoërskool Ermelo [2009] ZACC 32

The dispute arises from the language policy of Hoërskool Ermelo (the school), which had for many years provided instruction exclusively in Afrikaans.

Constitutional Court 2009 [2009] ZACC 32

F – Facts

The dispute arises from the language policy of Hoërskool Ermelo (the school), which had for many years provided instruction exclusively in Afrikaans. The Head of Department (HoD) of the Mpumalanga Department of Education (first applicant) and the Minister for Education (second applicant) contested the legality of the school's language policy following an incident wherein 113 learners seeking instruction in English were denied admission. The issue came to a head in January 2007 when the HoD revoked the authority of the school's governing body to set its language policy and appointed an interim committee to adopt a new parallel medium language policy, allowing for both Afrikaans and English instruction ([1]-[24]).

The school and its governing body (first and second respondents) sought to challenge this action, arguing that it undermined their constitutional rights and authority as prescribed under section 6(2) of the South African Schools Act [84 of 1996] (Schools Act). Following protracted legal disputes, the matter escalated to the Constitutional Court for review after the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the school's position ([3]).

I – Issues

The following legal issues arose for determination:

  1. Authority of the HoD: Did the HoD possess the legal authority under section 22 of the Schools Act to revoke the governing body’s power to determine the language policy? ([41])
  2. Withdrawal Grounds: If so, did the HoD do so on reasonable grounds and in a procedurally fair manner?
  3. Appointment of the Interim Committee: Did the HoD have the power to appoint an interim committee under section 25 of the Schools Act?
  4. Lawfulness of the Interim Committee's Action: Did the interim committee carry out its mandate in a lawful and procedurally fair manner?
  5. Constitutionality and Equity: How do the provisions of the Constitution, particularly section 29(2), govern the school’s language policy amidst the historical legacy of education inequality? ([42])

R – Rules

The ruling relied heavily on statutory interpretation of the South African Schools Act and constitutional principles encapsulated in sections 29(1) and (2) of the Constitution of South Africa, which guarantee the right to basic education in the language of one’s choice where practicable. Key statutory provisions include:

  • Section 6(2) of the Schools Act: Grants the school governing body the authority to determine its language policy, subject to constitutional and relevant statutory compliance.
  • Section 22(1): Empowers the HoD to withdraw governing body functions on reasonable grounds but requires procedural fairness.
  • Section 25(1): Enables the HoD to appoint persons to perform functions if a governing body fails in its duties ([63]-[68]).

The case also referenced precedent from Minister of Education, Western Cape v Governing Body, Mikro Primary School, where the court found that section 22 allowed for the withdrawal of functions, but subsequent interpretations limited this to functions derived from allocations under section 21 of the Schools Act ([30]).

A – Application

The court began by addressing the breadth of the powers granted to the HoD under section 22(1). It found that while the HoD has the authority to intervene, such intervention must be grounded in reasonable justifications and procedural fairness ([69]-[74]). The court critically assessed that while the HoD acted to ensure educational access during an imminent crisis, the revocation of the language policy was executed without the proper legal grounding and circumvented the lawful authority of the governing body.

Importantly, the HoD's decision to appoint the interim committee to enact a parallel medium policy was deemed unlawful since it was not based on any substantiated failure of the governing body to perform its duties as prescribed under section 25 of the Schools Act ([84]-[89]). The court reiterated that any decision regarding language policy must reflect the constitutionally mandated principles of equity and the right to receive education in a chosen language ([72]-[78]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded that:

  1. The HoD had the authority to revoke the governing body’s function concerning the school’s language policy but failed to do so lawfully and fairly.
  2. The interim committee's actions were deemed unlawful due to the manner of its appointment, effectively rendering any new language policy void and without legal consequence.
  3. The ruling underscores the necessity for public education institutions to adhere not only to their governing statutes but also to the constitutional imperatives of equality, non-discrimination, and inclusivity in language policies ([93][106]).

The court granted orders obliging the governing body to reassess its language policy in light of prevailing constitutional mandates, reinforcing the duty of administrative bodies to consider and rectify historical inequalities in educational opportunity ([107]). Thus, while the appeal by the applicants was dismissed, the court highlighted broader implications for schools regarding their societal responsibilities alongside statutory rights ([94]-[102]).

In finality, the ruling sought not only to restore educational equity but also to promote an environment of inclusivity and responsiveness, marking a significant moment in the ongoing struggle for equitable educational rights in post-apartheid South Africa ([100]).
