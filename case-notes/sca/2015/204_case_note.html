Director of Public Prosecutions, Gauteng v Pistorius (96/2015) [2015] ZASCA 204

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 [2015] ZASCA 204

F – Facts

The case revolves around the tragic death of Reeva Steenkamp, who was shot and killed by Oscar Pistorius on 14 February 2013 at his home in Silver Woods Country Estate, Pretoria. Pistorius, a well-known Paralympic athlete, was charged with her murder but was ultimately convicted of culpable homicide, leading the Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP) to appeal the conviction ([para 1]–[3]).

Pistorius contended that he shot Steenkamp believing she was an intruder. The shooting followed a series of sounds from the bathroom, which he interpreted as a potential intrusion. He fired four shots through the locked door of the toilet cubicle, leading to Steenkamp’s death shortly thereafter ([para 2]–[17]). This case drew unprecedented media attention and public scrutiny ([para 3]).

The trial court, led by Judge Masipa, found that Pistorius did not have the intention to kill (dolus directus) and ruled that he acted negligently, resulting in a verdict of culpable homicide with a sentence of five years ([para 19]). Following this conviction, the DPP reserved questions of law under Section 319 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA), focusing specifically on the legal principles surrounding 'dolus eventualis' and the application of circumstantial evidence ([para 20]).

I – Issues

  1. Whether the trial court correctly applied the principles of dolus eventualis in relation to the facts and conduct of the accused, including questions of error in objecto ([para 20]).
  2. Whether the trial court appropriately conceived and applied the legal principles related to circumstantial evidence and multiple defenses ([para 20]).
  3. Whether the trial court's acceptance of the accused's alternative version of events was legally justified and indeed reasonably possibly true ([para 20]).

R – Rules

The court analyzed the definitions and applications of dolus eventualis versus dolus directus in the context of murder, highlighting that dolus eventualis involves an intention resulting from the awareness of the risk of death but continuing with the act regardless. The Euclidean case precedent clarified that the intent does not require knowledge of the victim's identity ([paras 25]–[32]).

Further, the significant test was articulated in the decision-making surrounding circumstantial evidence, necessitating a totality approach where the inference drawn must be consistent with all evidence, and nothing may be ignored ([paras 34]–[35]). The court also addressed legal processes under sections 319 and 322 of the CPA, which provide the framework for reserved questions of law by the State ([paras 5]–[24]).

A – Application

In its assessment, the Supreme Court of Appeal critiqued the trial court's interpretation of dolus eventualis. It noted that the lower court had erroneously indicated that Pistorius's assessment of the situation—believing an intruder was present—excluded the possibility of dolus eventualis because he did not foresee Steenkamp as being behind the door ([paras 28]–[32]).

The SCA emphasized that the trial court misapplied the subjective foresight requirement; the key issue is not the accused’s awareness of the victim’s identity but whether he acted with a conscious disregard for the potential consequences of his actions ([paras 29]–[30]). The inference to be drawn from the evidence was that, given the choice to fire a lethal weapon, the possibility of causing death should have been evident to him. The court also highlighted the trial court's failure to account for significant circumstantial evidence that could influence the understanding of Pistorius's intention when firing the weapon, such as the type of ammunition used ([paras 39]–[40]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court ultimately reversed the trial court's decision, concluding that Pistorius acted with dolus eventualis—meaning he foresaw the possibility of death when he fired his weapon and reconciled himself to that possibility. Thus, his conviction for culpable homicide was set aside and replaced with a conviction for murder. The matter was referred back to the trial court to determine an appropriate sentence ([para 58]).

This case set a significant precedent in South African criminal law concerning the interpretation of intent in murder cases, underlining the importance of accurately applying legal principles concerning culpability and the handling of circumstantial evidence. Further, it reflects the judiciary's role in ensuring justice is served appropriately in cases of severe public interest and indeed highlighted how differing judicial perspectives can lead to varied outcomes in the justice process.
