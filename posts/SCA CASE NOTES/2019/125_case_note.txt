Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: Mosalakae & Others v Matlala & Others (267/2018)

## F - Facts

This appeal arises from a dispute regarding the shareholding structure of the fifth appellant, Gundo Investments (Pty) Ltd (Gundo), following an application for rectification under section 115 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973 ([1]–[2]). The respondents, consisting of various entities, sought to establish themselves and the third and fourth appellants as equal shareholders of Gundo. The Gauteng Division of the High Court initially upheld their application, prompting the current appeal ([1]).

The background to the case traces back to 1997 when various groups of black women were invited to invest in Black Economic Empowerment (BEE) initiatives. The entities involved, including the appellants and respondents, were supposed to hold equal shares in Gundo in consideration of their respective membership fees of R2 000 each ([2]–[4]).

As Gundo aimed to acquire a stake in Phumelela Gaming and Leisure Ltd through Dihla Investments (Pty) Ltd (Dihla), they faced a challenge in fulfilling the financial requisites for share acquisition ([5]–[7]). After several meetings and the emergence of disputes over the interpretation of their agreement, the original shareholders' investment details became contentious ([8]–[20]).

The appellants, primarily relying on an alleged agreement, argued that their shareholdings increased through a rights offer predicated on their payment to Dihla ([33]–[35]). However, the respondents contended that valid procedures were not followed, and the prior share structure must remain intact, which led to escalating disputes over ownership rights and the legitimacy of the altered share register established in December 2007 ([25]–[26]).

## I - Issues

The primary legal issues to be determined by the Supreme Court of Appeal include:

1. **Proof of Agreement**: Did the appellants manage to prove the terms of the agreement under which their shareholdings were purportedly increased? ([1]–[2]).
   
2. **Equity Claims**: Were the claims of the appellants to a larger shareholding valid under the Companies Act, given the shared contributions and actions of all involved parties? ([39]–[40]).

3. **Rectification and Shareholding**: How should the share register reflect the contributions made by parties, given the conflicting claims of ownership and prior resolutions? ([31]).

4. **Validity of Rights Offer**: Was there a valid rights offer made and accepted that could substantiate the alteration of shareholding in favor of the appellants? ([41]–[46]).

## R - Rules

The legal framework for resolving these issues primarily includes:

- **Section 115 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973**: This section empowers the court to rectify the register of shareholders, allowing for adjudication on the title of individuals to shares and determining necessary equitable outcomes([31]). The court's jurisdiction under this section is broad and grounded in principles of equity ([32]).

- **Precedent Cases**: The case cites Gafoor & another NNO v Vangates Investments (Pty) Ltd, which supports the notion that the exercise of the court’s discretion in matters of rectification should be equitable ([32]).

- **Contract Law Principles**: The judgment references contract law, asserting that any rights offer requires the certainty of terms similar to other contracts ([41]–[44]). Missing elements like share pricing or quantity can invalidate claims of acceptance and execution.

## A - Application

In applying the law to the facts, the court thoroughly examined the alleged rights offer made by the appellants ([39]). The court noted that the appellants' claims hinged on the payments made in November 2002 and an assertion that these constituted acceptance of an offer to increase their shareholdings ([40]). However, the court found that there was no valid, recognized acceptance communicated to Gundo, and the procedural requirements for formalizing a rights offer were not met, thus nullifying any claims of increased ownership based on that right ([45]).

It was observed that the appellants failed to prove the existence of necessary resolutions authorizing changes to share allocations ([36]–[37]). The court highlighted discrepancies in the appellants' narratives, noting that they lacked consistent explanations regarding the increase in shareholding. Furthermore, the adjustments made to the share register were considered improper, lacking transparency and proper authorization ([26]).

Ultimately, the court ruled out the possibility of the appellants holding a larger shareholding compared to the original contributions made by all parties, emphasizing the necessity of maintaining equity among shareholders ([38], [40]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the decision of the Gauteng High Court ([48]). The court concluded that the appellants had not adequately proven their claims regarding the purported increase in shareholding and that their actions did not conform with company law mandates concerning rights offerings. Notably, they failed to demonstrate compliance with statutory requirements or provide sufficient evidence to support their assertions ([48]).

This case reflects critically on the importance of clear communication, compliance with corporate governance norms, and equitable treatment of shareholders in corporate structures, particularly within entities formed under the auspices of BEE initiatives. The outcome reinforces the judiciary's role in ensuring that corporate regulations protect the interests of all stakeholders and uphold principles of fairness in business dealings.
