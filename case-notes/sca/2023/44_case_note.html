Case Title: W[…] v Williams-Ashman N O and Others (823/2022) [2023] ZASCA 44 (31 March 2023) Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa Coram: Saldulker, Mbatha, Molefe JJA, Kathree-Setiloane AJA, Unterhalter AJA Heard: 23 March 2023 Delivered: 31 March 2023

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 44 (31 March 2023) Court: The Supreme Court

FIRAC Case Note

Case Title: W[…] v Williams-Ashman N O and Others (823/2022) [2023] ZASCA 44 (31 March 2023)
Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Coram: Saldulker, Mbatha, Molefe JJA, Kathree-Setiloane AJA, Unterhalter AJA
Heard: 23 March 2023
Delivered: 31 March 2023

F - Facts

The appellant, J[…] M[…] W[…], was the former spouse of N[…] J[…] W[…] (née C[…]), who died shortly after their divorce. They were married on 11 June 2011, and on 24 October 2016, a decree of divorce was granted to them after they acknowledged their marital breakdown. On 8 December 2016, less than three months post-divorce, N[…] W[…] committed suicide. Before their marriage, N[…] executed a will bequeathing her entire estate to J[…] M[…] W[…]. However, the operation of section 2B of the Wills Act 7 of 1953 (the Wills Act) prevented the appellant from inheriting as it disqualified him due to the timing of N[…] W[…]’s death relative to their divorce. Under this provision, if a person dies within three months post-divorce, their will is enforced as if the former spouse predeceased them, unless the will clearly shows an intent to benefit the former spouse. As a result, N[…] W[…] was deemed to have died intestate, and her parents inherited her estate ([1–4]).

The appellant challenged the constitutionality of section 2B, arguing it infringed his rights under section 25(1) (protection against arbitrary deprivation of property) and section 34 (right to access to courts) of the South African Constitution. He contended that the provision effectively showed an arbitrary deprivation of property as it fettered N[…] W[…]’s freedom of testation and limited proof of testamentary intention to only what was articulated in her will ([4–5]).

The High Court ruled against the appellant, asserting that while section 2B did result in a deprivation of the right to inherit, it did not constitute an arbitrary deprivation within the meaning of the Constitution ([7]).

I - Issues

The central issues to be determined in the appeal were:

  1. Whether the appellant was deprived of property, in accordance with section 25 of the Constitution, as a result of section 2B?
  2. If he was deprived, whether this deprivation lacked sufficient justification, rendering it arbitrary under section 25?
  3. Was the restriction on regarding external evidence to ascertain testamentary intent procedurally unfair, infringing section 34 of the Constitution? ([8]

R - Rules

The court's analysis hinged on:

  • Section 2B of the Wills Act, which states any will executed by a person, who dies within three months of a divorce from their spouse, is treated as if the former spouse predeceased them unless the will explicitly states otherwise ([2–4]).
  • Section 25(1) of the Constitution, providing protection against arbitrary deprivation of property.
  • Section 34 of the Constitution, which enshrines the right of access to courts.

Judicial precedent from First National Bank of SA Ltd t/a Wesbank v Commissioner, South African Revenue Service & Another was utilized to establish that deprivation is not arbitrary if it serves a legitimate purpose and has sufficient justification ([11], [17]).

A - Application

The court began by assuming, in favor of the appellant, that his prospective right to inherit could constitute property under section 25. This led to a key consideration of more significant implications associated with section 2B. It was noted that the provision's intent was to forestall complications from divorces, thereby protecting the deceased’s intentions and allowing them time to amend their wills post-divorce.

The judgment underscored the legitimacy of the legislative purpose behind section 2B, aimed at preventing former spouses from unwittingly benefiting under outdated wills ([18–20]). The challenge of arbitrariness was carefully dissected, with the court determining that while the appellant had a legitimate claim to argue the limitation imposed by the paramountcy carve-out (restricting how testamentary intent could be proven), this limitation was justified. The express limitation allowed the deceased's intentions as expressed in a validly executed will to triumph, rooted in the principle that wills provide the most authoritative indications of intent ([22–27]).

As such, the restriction was not shown to lack sufficient justification and refrain from infringing procedural fairness as it established a justifiable framework for adjudicating testamentary intents ([25–29]). The court responded to arguments regarding the inconsistency of treatment amongst different marital regimes, ultimately stating that these claims were not substantiated within the founding affidavit, concluding that the appeal could not prosper on these grounds ([33–34]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, agreeing with the lower court’s determination that while the appellant experienced deprivation of property, such deprivation constituted neither an arbitrary act nor procedural unfairness as defined by the Constitution. Consequently, the appellant’s challenge against section 2B of the Wills Act was unsuccessful, reaffirming the statute’s constitutional validity and its purpose of ensuring testamentary clarity post-divorce. The court ordered the appellant to cover the costs incurred by the third and fourth respondents, which included the costs for counsel, acknowledging the private nature of the dispute regarding the inheritance ([1–2], [36]).

This case emphasizes the balance between individual freedoms in testamentary disposition and the legislative intent to uphold finality in personal relationships, demonstrating the court's deference to statutory frameworks designed to address societal complexities in inheritance matters.
