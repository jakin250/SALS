Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: Shange v S (613/2016) [2017] ZASCA 51

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The appellant, Mr. Lucky Vincent Shange, was originally convicted of multiple serious offenses, including murder, robbery with aggravating circumstances, and possession of a firearm without a license, on February 8, 2000, in the Durban Regional Court ([1]). Following his conviction, the KwaZulu-Natal High Court confirmed the convictions and imposed life sentences for the murder and robbery charges, along with a two-year sentence for the firearms offense ([1]). 

Despite having been granted leave to appeal to the KwaZulu-Natal High Court, the appeal was ultimately dismissed on November 8, 2001 ([2]). Shange was incarcerated for approximately 17 years following his convictions. In 2015, he applied for special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which was granted on September 8, 2015. 

At the hearing on May 2, 2017, a crucial issue arose concerning the absence of assessors during his trial, as mandated by the Magistrates' Courts Act 32 of 1944. Counsel for the appellant argued this point—initially not included in written arguments—and the State conceded that the absence of assessors invalidated the convictions ([3], [4]).

### I – Issues
The paramount issue in this case revolves around the legal validity of the convictions given the regional magistrate's failure to comply with statutory requirements regarding the use of assessors in a murder trial. Specifically, the question was whether the trial court was properly constituted in the absence of assessors as dictated by s 93ter(1) of the Magistrates’ Courts Act ([4]).

### R – Rules
The relevant legal provision under consideration is s 93ter(1) of the Magistrates’ Courts Act 32 of 1944. This provision mandates that a regional magistrate must be assisted by two assessors when presiding over a murder trial unless the accused explicitly requests otherwise ([4]). The SCA has previously ruled in S v Gayiya 2016 (2) SACR 165 (SCA) that non-compliance with this provision renders the court improperly constituted and necessitates the setting aside of convictions and sentences ([5]). This is in accordance with s 324(a) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which deems judgments or convictions invalid if there is a failure to adhere to necessary procedural requirements.

### A – Application
In its analysis, the SCA identified that the appellant was unrepresented during the trial and had not been informed of his right to have assessors. The magistrate's failure to summon assessors as required, coupled with the absence of any indication in the record that Shange or his co-accused were given a choice regarding the presence of assessors, constituted a flagrant violation of the statutory requirement ([6]). Given that the State did not contest this argument, the court concluded that the failure to comply with the requirements of the Magistrates’ Courts Act rendered the entire trial process fundamentally flawed, thus invalidating the convictions ([7]).

The court emphasized the peremptory nature of the requirement that assessors assist during a murder trial, noting that the judicial officer’s discretion to proceed without them only arises upon a specific request from the accused, which was absent in this case ([6]).

### C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the SCA upheld the appeal, ruling that Shange's convictions and sentences were to be set aside due to the improper constitution of the trial court. Consequently, the appellant was ordered to be released from custody immediately ([3], [7]). This case underscores the critical importance of procedural safeguards in the administration of justice, particularly concerning the rights of the accused in serious criminal proceedings. The case reaffirms the necessity for courts to adhere strictly to statutory provisions to ensure the integrity of judicial processes and the protection of defendants' rights. 

This judgment not only rectifies a specific miscarriage of justice for Shange but also highlights broader implications about the enforcement of legal standards in South Africa's criminal justice system.
