Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 1997

# FIRAC Case Note: National Union of Mine Workers v Black Mountain Mineral Development Company (PTY) Ltd

**Case Citation:** National Union of Mine Workers v Black Mountain Mineral Development Company (PTY) Ltd (Supreme Court of Appeal, 25 March 1997)

**Coram:** Mahomed CJ, F H Grosskopf, Nienaber, Marais, Scott JJA

### F - Facts

The respondent, Black Mountain Mineral Development Company (PTY) Ltd, operates Black Mountain Mine in Namaqualand and employs approximately 1,300 workers ([para 1]). In August 1991, negotiations between management and the National Union of Mine Workers (NUM), representing the workers, commenced regarding wage increases, sparked by a deadlock that evolved from initial negotiations starting in June 1991 ([paras 1-2]). The negotiations ultimately failed, leading the workers to strike on 24 August 1991, after which management dismissed 396 striking workers on 20 September 1991—offering reinstatement under conditions that matched their pre-dismissal terms.

Subsequent court proceedings began when the NUM sought an order under Section 46(9) of the Labour Relations Act 28 of 1956 for a declaration of unfair dismissals and reinstatement of its members ([paras 3-4]). The Industrial Court dismissed the NUM's application on 1 February 1993 ([para 3]). The NUM appealed to the Labour Appeal Court, which dismissed the appeal ([para 4]). 

With leave to appeal, the core issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the dismissals constituted an unfair labour practice, particularly under the jurisprudence established in National Union of Metal Workers of South Africa v Vetsak Co-operative Ltd ([para 5]).

### I - Issues

The primary legal issue was whether the dismissals of the striking workers constituted an unfair labour practice under the Labour Relations Act ([para 6]). Secondary considerations included the legitimacy of the employer's ultimatum issued on 17 September 1991 and whether management's actions at the negotiations meeting on 19 September constituted an unfair labour practice ([paras 27-28]).

### R - Rules

The court's analysis drew on prior jurisprudential principles established in *National Union of Metal Workers of South Africa v Vetsak Co-operative Ltd* ([para 6]), where it was acknowledged that:

1. The right to strike is integral to collective bargaining, and normally, dismissal for participating in a lawful strike is not permissible.
2. An employer may dismiss striking employees if the negotiation process reaches a stalemate; however, this must be evaluated on a case-by-case basis considering fairness to both parties ([para 30]).
3. Fairness functions as the ultimate standard, implying both parties must act in good faith and engage in reasonable negotiations.

Further reference was made to the distinction between dismissal due to misconduct and that arising from lawful strike action, as drawn from *National Union of Mine Workers and Others v Free State Consolidated Mines (Operations) Ltd* ([para 30]).

### A - Application

**Assessment of the Ultimatum:**
The court found that the ultimatum issued by the employer on 17 September was justified given the context—including the prolonged negotiations and financial hardships faced by the mining operation ([para 36]). By that point, the strike had persisted for 25 days, and significant progress had not been made toward a resolution of the wage dispute ([para 34]).

Furthermore, the employer took reasonable steps not only to negotiate through multiple meetings but also to manage the implications of the strike on operations. The court noted that while both sides were inflexible in their positions, the employer was not to be faulted for delivering an ultimatum to end the strike and preserve operational ability ([paras 34-36]).

**Negotiation Dynamics:**
The respondents’ refusal to backdate the wage offer during negotiations was characterized as a legitimate strategy rather than a punitive measure as argued by the NUM ([paras 39-43]). The court emphasized that Botes’s statements must be understood within the broader context of bargaining practices. No evidence convincingly demonstrated that backing down would have obviated the dismissals or materially changed the outcome of negotiations given the strikers’ intransigence ([para 44]).

### C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that the dismissals of the workers did not constitute an unfair labour practice under the Labour Relations Act. Both the delivery of the ultimatum and management's negotiating tactics were deemed permissible given the financial context and the failure of negotiations ([para 46]). 

The ruling secured a vital precedent emphasizing that while the right to strike is protected, it does not negate the employer’s right to ultimately make operational decisions necessary for the company’s survival. The implications of this ruling resonate throughout labor relations law, reinforcing the importance of good faith negotiations while providing clarity on the limits of strike actions. 

The appeal was dismissed with costs, establishing a reaffirmation of the prevailing interpretation of lawful strikes and associated labor practices in South Africa ([para 46]).
