Supreme Court of Appeal

BW Brightwater Way Props (Pty) Ltd v Eastern Cape Development Corporation

This case concerns the validity of a lease agreement promulgated on 20 December 2016 between the appellant, BW Brightwater Way Props (Pty) Ltd (hereinafter referred to as "Brightwater"), and the respondent, the Eastern Cape Development Corporation (ECDC).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 BW Brightwater Way Props (Pty) Ltd v Eastern Cape Development Corporation (1235/2019) [2021] ZASCA 47 (19 April 2021)

Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Case No: 1235/2019
Neutral Citation: BW Brightwater Way Props (Pty) Ltd v Eastern Cape Development Corporation (1235/2019) [2021] ZASCA 47 (19 April 2021)
Coram: Zondi, Molemela, Schippers JJA, Kgoele and Eksteen AJJA

F - Facts [1-4]

This case concerns the validity of a lease agreement promulgated on 20 December 2016 between the appellant, BW Brightwater Way Props (Pty) Ltd (hereinafter referred to as "Brightwater"), and the respondent, the Eastern Cape Development Corporation (ECDC). This lease allowed Brightwater to operate Portions A and B of the Remainder of Farm 31, Coffee Bay, Mqanduli, also recognized as the Ocean View Hotel, for a period of 20 years at a monthly rental of R32,000 [1].

Following a breach of the terms by ECDC, Brightwater initiated proceedings in the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court in July 2017 seeking validation of the lease, eviction of unlawful occupants from the property, and formal registration of the lease [2]. However, on 26 July 2019, the High Court dismissed this application while upholding ECDC’s counter-application. The court declared the lease agreement constitutionally invalid due to ECDC's failure to comply with the requisite transparent procedures mandated by the Public Finance Management Act 1 of 1999 and the Preferential Procurement Framework Act 5 of 2000 regarding property disposal [3-4].

After several proceedings and amendments, including a supplementary order declaring that the constitutional invalidity of the lease would not divest Brightwater of its rights under the agreement, the matter reached the Supreme Court of Appeal [4].

I - Issues [15-20]

The central legal issue before the Supreme Court was whether the High Court's order, declaring the lease constitutionally invalid while preserving Brightwater’s rights under the agreement, was competent. This necessitated an examination of:

  1. The validity of the lease agreement under statutory procurement principles.
  2. The implications of the court’s powers under section 172(1)(b) of the Constitution as to whether it has the discretion to preserve accrued rights despite a declaration of invalidity.

The evaluation hinged significantly on whether the High Court’s actions essentially allowed Brightwater to maintain future rights under an agreement deemed invalid, thereby raising questions about the legality and enforceability of such an arrangement [15-20].

R - Rules [16-28]

The Supreme Court relied on several legal principles derived from constitutional and administrative law, specifically:

  1. Section 172(1) of the Constitution: It mandates that when a court engages with constitutional matters, it must declare any inconsistent law or conduct invalid (s 172(1)(a)), and it possesses the discretionary power to craft any order that is deemed just and equitable (s 172(1)(b)) [16].
  2. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA): This was initially considered by ECDC in support of its counter-application, highlighting administrative obligations that underpin the validity and lawfulness of governmental actions [3].
  3. Case Law:
  • State Information Technology Agency SOC Limited v Gijima Holdings (Pty) Ltd [2017] ZACC 40: This case delineated the scope of judicial discretion relating to just and equitable remedies following the invalidation of contracts [25-27].
  • Buffalo City Metropolitan Municipality v Asla Construction (Pty) Ltd [2019] ZACC 15: Illustrating how courts may preserve accrued rights post-declaration of invalidity, this case also reinforced that while existing rights can be safeguarded, such orders do not establish new rights premises under an invalid agreement [25-28].

A - Application [21-30]

In applying the law to the present facts, the Supreme Court undertook a critical examination of the High Court's use of discretion under section 172(1)(b). The Court held that while the High Court could establish safeguards to uphold accrued rights, it could not extend these to create future rights under an agreement declared void ab initio. This determination was pivotal in assessing whether Brightwater was entitled to ongoing occupancy throughout the full duration of the lease despite its invalidity.

Counsel argued that maintaining Brightwater's rights as per the High Court order effectively invalidated the declaration of unconstitutionality, leading to semantic inconsistencies and potential misinterpretation of legal principles [28-30]. The Supreme Court ultimately concluded that the preservation of rights under section 172(1)(b) should not create an entitlement for further rights under a contract that had been expressly invalidated.

C - Conclusion [30-34]

The Supreme Court of Appeal found in favor of the ECDC, concluding that the order made by the High Court declaring the lease agreement constitutionally invalid could not concurrently allow Brightwater to benefit from future rights under that contract. The Court emphasized that contracts void ab initio cannot confer enforceable rights, thus upholding the constitutional doctrine of legality while also providing guidance on the application of the discretion vested by section 172 of the Constitution.

This case has significant implications for public procurement law and the enforceability of contractual obligations arising from unconstitutionally established agreements. Further legal discourse may be necessary to clarify how courts can judiciously wield their discretion in similar situations while balancing the need for accountability and lawful governance.

Orders:

  1. The cross-appeal succeeded.
  2. Brightwater was directed to bear ECDC's costs related to the appeal and the cross-appeal, including the costs for two counsel where applicable.
  3. The previous orders of the High Court were set aside in favor of a declaration of invalidity without preserving future rights under the contractual relationship.

This case illustrates the nuanced intersection between procurement law, contract validity, and the discretionary powers of courts in upholding constitutional principles in public governance.
