Source Folder: 1996

# Case Note: Colin Venter & Others v Credit Guarantee Insurance Corporation of Africa Limited & Others

**Case No:** 88/94  
**Court:** Supreme Court of South Africa (Appellate Division)  
**Judgment Delivered:** 13 May 1996  

## FIRAC

### F – Facts
The case arose from the South Eastern Cape Local Division, where the first respondent, Credit Guarantee Insurance Corporation of Africa Limited (CGICA), claimed R65,000 from the three appellants: Colin Venter, David Cohen, and Bay Nominees (Pty) Ltd. The claim was based on an alleged undertaking made by attorney Mr. Myers on behalf of the appellants ([para 2]).

The facts reflect that Contour Engineering (Pty) Ltd incurred a debt of R65,057 to NTC Limited (the second respondent) concerning goods sold. NTC had signed a cession of book debts in favor of CGICA, which covered NTC against losses up to 75% under an insurance policy ([para 5]). Contour entered liquidation, and CGICA sought to recover losses. During the proceedings, a proposed compromise was presented by the third appellant, which CGICA contingently supported unless opposed by NTC. An undertaking for payment of shortfalls was allegedly reached after discussions involving Myers and Wallace of CGICA ([paras 8-9]).

The trial court dismissed CGICA's claims against Venter but found Cohen and Bay Nominees jointly liable for R63,323 after several amendments to pleadings and applications to intervene by NTC ([paras 11-12]). The case also involved substantial procedural history involving complex applications to amend pleadings and oppose interventions, culminating in several cost orders.

### I – Issues
1. **Authorization of Myers:** Whether Mr. Myers was authorized to bind Venter to the agreement is central to the appeal. The appellants claimed that Myers lacked authority to conclude an agreement on behalf of Venter and that there was confusion regarding who represented whom during the negotiations ([paras 10-11]).

2. **Existence of the Shortfall:** Whether CGICA successfully established the existence and amount of the shortfall arising from the collection of book debts under the agreement ([paras 11-12]).

3. **Consensus of Contracting Parties:** Whether there was a valid consensus regarding the agreement entered into by the appellants, specifically concerning who was correctly identified as the contracting party ([para 18]).

4. **Validity of the "Secret Agreement":** Whether the agreement constituted a secret deal that could be declared void for being contrary to public policy or good morals ([para 23]).

5. **Costs and Judicial Discretion:** Whether the trial court exercised proper discretion in its orders relating to costs ([paras 5.1-5.3]).

### R – Rules
The relevant legal principles derive from contract law, specifically concerning the authority of agents (Kearney v Minister of Finance (1972) 1 SA 581 (A)), consensus and contractual obligations (Goldman v A Z B C 1954 (3) SA 173 (A)), and public policy concerning secretive arrangements (Sasfin (Pty) Ltd v Beukes 1989 (1) SA 1 (A)).

The court relied on the requirements of proving a shortfall in obligations under a guarantee agreement, the necessity of authority for representative actions, and the public policy doctrine underlying contracts that are allegedly contra bonos mores ([paras 11-12], [23]).

### A – Application
#### Authority of Myers
The court found that the evidence suggested Myers was acting on behalf of the appellants, particularly Venter, given the coordinated discussions leading to the agreement. The court ruled against the appellants' argument by closely analyzing the correspondence exchanged between the parties ([paras 10-11]).

#### Shortfall Establishment
Following the interpretive principles from *Jaga v Dönges* and *Swart v Cape Fabrix*, the court undertook a purposive interpretation of the shortfall clause in the agreement. It concluded that the phrase "should collection of book debts not realise R65,000" implicitly meant the requirement of actual payments to be made from these collections before determining a shortfall, thereby supporting CGICA's claim of R63,323 as evidence showed no payments were made ([paras 12-15]).

#### Consensus on Contracting Parties
The court highlighted inconsistencies in Wallace's testimony regarding representation while reaffirming that consensus was reached with CGICA as a principal party. Wallace's actions in failing to mention NTC during negotiations supported that only CGICA was intended to be the party to the agreement ([paras 19-21]).

#### Secret Agreement
The court dismissed the notion of an immoral agreement by emphasizing the lack of any illegal motive behind CGICA's negotiations and the absence of a duty to disclose the agreement as it did not prejudice the creditors. It determined that public policy favored the freedom of contract unless violation was unequivocally evident, which was not the case here ([para 24]).

### C – Conclusion
The court upheld CGICA's claim against Cohen and Bay Nominees, confirming the amount due under the agreement and emphasizing the appellant's failure to establish any legal grounds against liability. The cross-appeal concerning Venter's liability was granted, acknowledging the lack of evidence that Myers acted with proper authority in binding Venter. Additionally, the costs orders were amended to reflect only Venter's responsibility for specific claims, while maintaining the discretion exercised by the trial court regarding the remaining orders. The judgment thereby emphasizes the importance of clear authority in contractual matters, alongside the judicial preference for upholding contractual obligations unless proven otherwise ([paras 26-41]).
