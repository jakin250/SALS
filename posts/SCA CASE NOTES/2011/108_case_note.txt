Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: The Public Protector v Mail & Guardian Ltd (422/10) [2011] ZASCA 108

## F – Facts
The case involves an appeal by the Public Protector against a court order that mandated a review of the Public Protector's investigation and report regarding allegations raised in a series of articles published by the Mail & Guardian (M&G). The articles disclosed alleged improprieties related to the management of public funds, specifically a matter known as "Oilgate." Following these publications, members of the National Assembly requested the Public Protector to investigate the allegations concerning misconduct by the African National Congress (ANC), Imvume Management, and public entities involved, particularly PetroSA. The Public Protector, Adv M Mushwana, conducted an investigation and concluded that there was no improper conduct. The M&G and its journalists then brought review proceedings against this conclusion, which the North Gauteng High Court upheld.

The appeal concerns the adequacy of the investigation conducted by the Public Protector and whether it was conducted with an open and searching mind. Crucially, the Public Protector's report was characterized by the court as deficient in its obligation to thoroughly investigate the alleged connections between public money and party funding.

## I – Issues
1. Whether the investigation conducted by the Public Protector into the alleged misconduct was adequate and thorough.
2. Whether the Public Protector correctly interpreted the scope of his investigatory powers under the Constitution and the Public Protector Act 23 of 1994.
3. Whether the High Court's order to set aside specific paragraphs of the Public Protector’s report was justifiable and appropriate.

## R – Rules
The pertinent legal framework governing the role and powers of the Public Protector includes:
- The Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996, particularly § 182, which outlines the powers and functions of the Public Protector.
- The Public Protector Act 23 of 1994, specifically sections 6 and 7, delineating the scope of investigatory powers and the responsibilities of the Public Protector to probe allegations of impropriety in state affairs.
- The principles governing judicial review, indicating that decisions made by the Public Protector can be subject to review if found to lack rationality or fail to meet constitutional or statutory mandates.

## A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) evaluated the Public Protector’s approach to the investigation against the legal provisions that govern his functions. Key findings included:

- **Inadequate Investigation**: The SCA found that the Public Protector's investigation was not conducted with an open and enquiring mind. There was a failure to fully explore the relationship between the advance payments made to Imvume and the alleged funding to the ANC. Thus, the inquiry focused narrowly, which obstructed a comprehensive understanding of the allegations.

- **Legal Misinterpretation**: The Public Protector's interpretation of his mandate to investigate was deemed too restrictive. The SCA highlighted that the Public Protector has a proactive duty to investigate any conduct that might relate to impropriety concerning public money, regardless of whether the parties involved are public entities. This broadened understanding is crucial, especially in matters involving significant public interest.

- **Failure to substantiate Findings**: The report's conclusions, which discredited the reputations of M&G and its journalists, were not supported by adequate investigations. The SCA indicated that the findings were based largely on insufficient inquiry into the actual conduct of the parties involved and failed to consider relevant evidence and documents.

## C – Conclusion
The SCA upheld the High Court's decision to set aside certain paragraphs of the Public Protector’s report due to a lack of adequate investigation. However, it refrained from mandating a specific course of action for the Public Protector's subsequent inquiry, emphasizing that the nature of such an investigation is best determined by the Public Protector in the context of the evidentiary landscape at hand. Consequently, it was ordered that paragraphs 2 and 3 of the previous order be set aside, but the appeal was dismissed, maintaining the principle that public confidence in the integrity of state institutions must be upheld through thorough and effective oversight.

The implications of this case underscore the critical role of the Public Protector in ensuring accountable governance, reinforcing that investigations must be conducted with earnestness, transparency, and a commitment to unearthing the truth in matters of public concern. The findings in this case contribute significantly to the jurisprudence surrounding the Public Protector's duties and the standard of investigations expected from such independent institutions.
