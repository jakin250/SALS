# Case Note: Umgungundlovu District Municipality v Amaraka Investments 37 (Pty) Ltd [2020] ZASCA 52

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts ([paras 1–3])

The case originated from a dispute between Umgungundlovu District Municipality (the appellant) and Amaraka Investments 37 (Pty) Ltd (the respondent) regarding a contract for the provision of sewerage services. The contract was concluded on 31 October 2011. However, the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court, led by Nkosi J, later declared this contract unconstitutional, citing non-compliance with the procurement procedures required by the Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000. Despite declaring the contract invalid, Nkosi J suspended this invalidity, allowing the appellant to continue using the services until compliance with procurement procedures was achieved, which posed a central question regarding the retrospective effect of this suspension ([paras 1–2]).

The respondent, having completed its contractual obligations and seeking payment of R13,050,776.55, initiated summary judgment proceedings. The appellant acknowledged the amount due but contested the enforceability of the contract, arguing that the suspension of the contract did not apply retrospectively, thus refusing payment for the period from contract inception to the declaration of invalidity on 11 April 2018 ([paras 2–4]).

### I – Issues ([paras 2–5])

The key legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether Nkosi J's order, suspending the invalidity of the contract, operated retrospectively from the date of contract conclusion (31 October 2011) until the date of the order declaring it invalid (11 April 2018). The resolution of this issue hinged on interpreting the specific language of Nkosi J's order and its intended effect ([paras 2, 5]).

### R – Rules ([para 6–8])

The court considered relevant statutes and legal principles concerning constitutional validity and procurement processes, especially Section 172(1)(b) of the Constitution, which empowers courts to declare laws invalid and to make just and equitable orders. The case highlighted precedents related to similar situations where courts sought to balance the need for adherence to legal norms with the intended fairness to parties who acted in good faith under invalid contracts ([para 7]).

Additionally, prior case law, such as *Allpay Consolidated Investment Holdings (Pty) Ltd v The Chief Executive Officer, South African Social Security Agency and Others* [2014] ZACC 12, was referenced, emphasizing the principle that courts aim to mitigate harsh consequences of declaring contractual rights invalid, particularly when the aggrieved party has acted without impropriety ([para 8]).

### A – Application ([paras 14–19])

The court elaborated on the necessity of interpreting the Nkosi J order within the broader context of his judgment, not merely its wording. The appeal court reasoned that the interpretation of Nkosi J’s order should consider the intention behind the suspension and the judicial context surrounding it. The court felt that the clear intention was to prevent divestment of the respondent's rights due to the municipality's self-created predicament ([paras 14–15]).

The appellant's contention that the suspension rendered the contract invalid retroactively was consistently countered. The court emphasized that this interpretation would not align with Nkosi J's objective of ensuring equity. Furthermore, the judgment illuminated Nakosi J’s emphasis on the respondent’s substantive rights, aiming to protect them despite the constitutional invalidity finding ([paras 7, 18]).

Thus, the court affirmed that the judgment of the court a quo, which indicated that the suspension's effect was indeed retrospective, was the correct interpretation of Nkosi J's ruling; the contract remained enforceable for services rendered during the period before the declaration ([paras 18–19]).

### C – Conclusion ([paras 19–20])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the interpretation of Nkosi J's order by the court a quo was correct and upheld the enforceability of the contract for the period it was in effect prior to the invalidation order. This decision reinforced the notion that parties who have acted in good faith should not be denied their rights and remedies resulting from contracts, even when they face constitutional challenges. The court dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming the obligation of the municipality to remit payment to Amaraka Investments for services duly rendered under the contract ([para 20]).
