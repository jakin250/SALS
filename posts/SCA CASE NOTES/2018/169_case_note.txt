# Case Note: Minister of Home Affairs v Ali (1289/17) [2018] ZASCA 169

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa addressed the right to citizenship by naturalisation for children born in South Africa to non-South African parents ([1]). The first respondent, Miriam Ali, alongside five others, all born in South Africa and having lived there continuously, sought to apply for citizenship under Section 4(3) of the Citizenship Amendment Act 17 of 2010 (the "Act") but were denied by the Minister of Home Affairs due to an interpretation of the Act that excluded retrospective application ([1]–[12]).

The core facts are as follows:
- Respondents were born in South Africa between 1996 and 1998 to parents who were not South African citizens or permanent residents.
- Their births were registered under the Births and Deaths Registration Act 51 of 1992.
- They remained in South Africa, having no significant ties to their parents' countries ([4]–[8]).

Despite fulfilling the criteria set out in Section 4(3), the Minister interpreted this provision to apply only to children born after its enactment on January 1, 2013 ([11]). As a result, the Minister failed to promulgate necessary regulations for applications as mandated by Section 23 of the Act, prompting the respondents to seek legal redress in the Western Cape Division of the High Court ([12]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issues revolved around:
1. **Retrospective Application**: Whether Section 4(3) could be applied to individuals born before its enactment.
2. **Discretion of the Minister**: Whether the high court's order for the Minister to accept applications on affidavit infringed on legislative processes and the doctrine of separation of powers.
3. **Constitutional Rights**: Whether the denial of citizenship applications constituted an infringement of the constitutional rights of the respondents ([10]–[19]).

### R – Rules

The court referenced several important legal statutes and precedents:
1. **Citizenship Amendment Act 17 of 2010**: Specifically, Section 4(3) outlines the criteria for citizenship by naturalisation for children born in South Africa to non-citizens.
2. **Interpretative Principles**: The principles regarding retrospective laws cited from *Nkabinde & another v Judicial Service Commission & others* ([14]), and the distinction between retrospective and retroactive laws.
3. **Constitutional Provisions**: Sections 38(a), (b), and (d) of the South African Constitution, granting standing to individuals to enforce their rights and those of others, and the rights to just administrative action.
4. **Judicial Precedent**: References to cases like *Savoi and others v NDPP* and *Head of Department: Mpumalanga Department of Education v Hoërskool Ermelo* were utilized to support arguments regarding the rights of applicants and judicial authority to ensure fair administrative action ([21]–[23]).

### A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal applied the law by rejecting the Minister's interpretation of Section 4(3) as overly restrictive since it illegally prohibited eligible applicants just because they were born prior to the enactment date ([18]). Counsel for the Minister conceded to the correctness of these claims, acknowledging that the respondents fulfilled the jurisdictional requirements stipulated in the Act ([18]).

The court analyzed whether the high court's orders were overly broad or constituted an infringement of the separation of powers. It reasoned that the Minister's lack of action in promulgating the necessary regulations effectively barred the respondents from applying for citizenship, creating an unreasonable situation that warranted judicial intervention ([23]). The court emphasized that the order allowing applications by affidavit was not an overreach but a necessary and equitable remedy given the Department's inaction. The court clarified that the retrospective application of the law did not infringe upon any vested rights, reinforcing the applicants' right to justice ([13]–[27]).

Moreover, the court dismissed the notion that the high court overstepped its boundaries, arguing that the principle of separation of powers should not prevent substantive justice, especially where individuals face constitutional harm due to administrative delay ([20]–[22]).

### C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the Minister's appeal, affirming the high court's orders. The appeal was dismissed with costs, including those incurred by employing two counsel ([27]). The court's ruling confirmed that:
1. Section 4(3) of the Act applies retrospectively to those who meet the criteria, regardless of their birth date.
2. The Minister must accept applications for citizenship on affidavit in light of the absence of promulgated regulations.
3. The court intervened rightly to prevent the infringement of the constitutional rights of children born in South Africa.

This case underscores the importance of administrative accountability and the necessity for legislative frameworks to ensure that individuals can exercise their rights in accordance with constitutional provisions. The implications for policy and practice within the Department of Home Affairs are significant, necessitating a reevaluation of how citizenship regulations are interpreted and applied in practice ([1]–[27]).
