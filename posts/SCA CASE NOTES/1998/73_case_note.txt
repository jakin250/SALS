Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 1998

# FIRAC Case Note: Ensign Bickford (South Africa) (Proprietary) Limited & Others v AECI Explosives and Chemicals Limited

### F – Facts
The case at hand involves an appeal related to the infringement of South African Letters Patent No 79/3210, which pertains to an invention for a “Low-energy fuse consisting of a plastic tube the inner surface of which is coated with explosive in powder form” ([para 2]). The appellants (Ensign Bickford (South Africa) Proprietary Limited, Bulk Mining Explosives Proprietary Limited, and Dantex Explosives Proprietary Limited) were the defendants in two consolidated infringement actions against the respondent, AECI Explosives and Chemicals Limited, the patent’s proprietor ([para 2]). The patent was granted under the Patents Act No 57 of 1978, claiming a priority date from an earlier Swedish application ([para 3]). 

The inventors were employees of Nobel Nitro A B of Sweden, a notable entity in the explosives field, while the appellants were parties associated with the manufacture and distribution of products related to the patented invention ([para 3]). The patent representative initially acted as a licensee before acquiring it specifically for litigation purposes ([para 4]). 

Fuses known as shock tubes, critical to industries such as mining, were introduced in the early 1970s as an innovative and safe method for initiating explosions ([para 5]). The shock tubes had evolved over the years, with various versions and improvements being test-marketed by previous patent holders, indicating an ongoing commercial interest in the technology ([para 6]).

During the trial, evidence was presented to substantiate claims of patent infringement and revocation based on grounds of non-patentability, lack of inventiveness, and ambiguity in claims ([paras 8-9]). The plaintiffs, largely relying on expert testimony from Dr. Viljoen, were ultimately favored by the lower court, which ruled against the defendants’ claims ([para 9]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues raised in this appeal concern:

1. **Validity of the patent**: Did prior uses or sales of similar products (specifically, two products known as HD Nonel and an over-extruded shock tube) invalidate the patent by failing the novelty requirement? ([para 8])

2. **Inventiveness**: Did the claimed invention represent a sufficiently innovative step, or was it obvious to a person skilled in the art at the relevant date? ([para 21])

3. **Clarity of claims**: Were the claims in the patent sufficiently clear and unambiguous, or did they warrant revocation based on failure to comply with statutory requirements? ([para 9])

### R – Rules
The relevant legal frameworks guiding this case include:

1. **Patents Act No 57 of 1978**: This governs the patentability requirements, including novelty (s 25(1)), inventive step (s 25(10)), and clarity of claims (s 61(1)(f)(i)) ([para 22]). The key sections address what constitutes a patentable invention and the standards for evaluating whether an invention is non-obvious and hence qualifies for patent protection.

2. **Common Law Precedents**: The court relied on established principles from previous cases, including the notable principle from *Power Steel Construction (Pty) Ltd v African Batignilles* that differentiates the permissible boundaries of a claim from what is discussed in the specification ([para 14]). 

3. **Expert Evidence**: The assessment of novelty and inventiveness often necessitates expert testimony to clarify whether what is claimed as an innovation was, from a technical standpoint, obvious ([para 27]).

### A – Application
In judging the appeal, the court meticulously examined whether prior art or existing products negated the novelty of the patent. The appellants' argument centered on two primary products: the HD Nonel, which had various shortcomings, and the over-extruded product that they contended predated the patent in question ([para 17-20]). The court stated that the defendants failed to demonstrate that the specifics of these products constituted prior use that would invalidate the patent ([para 20]). 

The court also analyzed the inventive step requisite. The learned judge in the trial court’s decision highlighted that while certain components of the invention were known in the industry, the unique combination of elements in the claimed invention must be assessed in totality, focusing on whether overcoming issues led to practical, commercial success ([para 27-34]). 

Further analyzing the evidence, the court determined the defendants did not present compelling arguments supporting their claim of obviousness. Notably, while observations regarding the commercial success of E-B America’s products were referenced, they lacked substantive weight in challenging the inventive character of the patent ([para 30-32]).

The clarity of claims was less prominently muted in the appellants' argument; however, the necessity for the claims to be distinctly framed within the specifications and how they represent the unique invention was reiterated by the court ([para 35]).

### C – Conclusion
The appeal was ultimately upheld, with the Court finding insufficient evidence of novelty and inventive step, resulting in the revocation of the patent in question ([para 38]). The reasoning articulated by PLEWMAN JA reflected a thorough examination of the technical and commercial context surrounding the case, concluding that the claimed invention did not provide the inventive step required under the Patents Act ([para 36]). The case underscores the threshold challenge patentees face in distinguishing their claims within a highly competitive technological domain while aligning those claims with statutory requirements of novelty and inventiveness. 

As this patent had expired during the proceedings, the patentee was precluded from remedying the insufficiencies through amendments, emphasizing the importance of robust initial patent drafting to withstand subsequent legal scrutiny.
