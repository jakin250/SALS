Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: Stemmet and Another v Mokhethi and Another (681/2022) [2023] ZASCA 127

### F – Facts
The appeal concerns a claim initiated by Tseliso James Mokhethi and Makweleng Naomi Mokhethi (the respondents) against Gideon Jakobus Petrus Stemmet and Elaine Stemmet (the appellants) regarding latent defects in a property sold by the appellants to the respondents. The respondents viewed the property in Fichardt Park, Bloemfontein, on two separate occasions, leading to an agreement of sale concluded on 24 May 2013, for a purchase price of R1,290,000. Following the transfer of the property on 22 July 2013 and assuming occupation, the respondents discovered severe defects, including structural cracks and issues with windows and cornices, several months later ([paras 1-3]).

On 24 June 2014, the respondents lodged a claim with Absa Bank, their mortgage lender, which covered the property for insurance purposes. The claim was declined on 12 August 2014, based on the assertion that the defects were old and caused by natural movements of the clay soil ([paras 4-5]). The respondents issued summons against the appellants on 19 July 2017, claiming damages amounting to R128,423.26 due to fraudulent non-disclosure or concealment of defects ([para 6]).

The appellants responded with a special plea of prescription, arguing that the respondents were aware of the defects by June 2014, thus rendering the claim prescribed before the summons was issued ([paras 7-8]). The magistrate’s court found in favor of the respondents, dismissing the special plea. The appellants appealed this decision, leading to a split verdict in the Free State High Court before the matter proceeded to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 11-13]).

### I – Issues
The primary issue before the court was the determination of whether the respondents had acquired the necessary knowledge of the defects and the debt, as mandated by the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, to trigger the commencement of prescription ([para 16]). Specifically, the court needed to ascertain whether the respondents knew both the existence of the defects and the identity of the debtor, the appellants, by June 2014, thus allowing the prescription period to start running ([paras 17-20]).

### R – Rules
Key legal principles referenced include:

1. **Prescription Act 68 of 1969**, particularly sections 12(1), 12(2), and 12(3):
   - Section 12(1) states that prescription begins to run when the debt is due.
   - Section 12(2) indicates that if the debtor wilfully prevents the creditor from recognizing the existence of the debt, prescription is delayed until such awareness occurs.
   - Section 12(3) requires that a creditor must know both the identity of the debtor and the facts from which the debt arises for the prescription period to commence.

2. **Legal precedents** including *Truter and Another v Deysel* and others, asserting that the running of prescription is not postponed until the creditor is aware of all the details, but instead when the creditor possesses sufficient facts to instigate action ([paras 13-14]).

### A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately ruled that the respondents had, by June 2014, acquired sufficient knowledge regarding the defects to activate the prescription period. The Court noted that the respondents were aware that structural issues had begun to manifest, with cracks indicated during testimony and visual evidence presented ([paras 10-15]).

The majority opinion found that while the respondents may not have known the precise cause of the defects immediately, their knowledge of the symptoms (such as structural cracks and jamming of doors) constituted possession of the minimum necessary facts ([paras 20-24]). The Court emphasized that the presence of evidence suggesting attempted cover-ups of the issues pointed towards fraudulent misrepresentation by the appellants, facilitating the emergence of a valid cause of action ([para 25]).

Conversely, the majority ruling of the High Court had pointed to the necessity of knowing the cause of the defects as a prerequisite for invoking prescription, an interpretation the Supreme Court rejected, asserting that such a requirement would unduly complicate the application of the Prescription Act ([paras 14-25]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal of the appellants, with the full court’s order set aside and the magistrate’s ruling reinstated (ordering the dismissal of the respondents’ claim). The ruling underscored the importance of recognizing that mere knowledge of the defects, alongside the identity of the debtor, suffices to trigger the commencement of prescription under South African law. This case reinforces legal principles around the awareness required for prescription and clarifies boundaries on the factual knowledge requisite to initiate legal claims under the Prescription Act ([paras 26-27]). 

#### Implications
This judgment provides critical insights into the operation of the Prescription Act, particularly concerning latent defects in property sales and the obligation of purchasers to seek redress when they suspect fraudulent behavior. It emphasizes that knowledge of symptoms is crucial for claiming against sellers, thus influencing future claims regarding fraud and disclosure in real estate transactions.
