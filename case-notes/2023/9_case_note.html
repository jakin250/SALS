Minister of Water and Sanitation and Others v Lotter N.O. and Others; Minister of Water and Sanitation and Others v Wiid and Others; Minister of Water and Sanitation and South African Association for Water User Associations [2023] ZACC 09

This case involves three consolidated applications for leave to appeal stemming from a judgment of the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which had overturned a judgment from the High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Division, Pretoria.

Constitutional Court 2023 [2023] ZACC 09

Following FIRAC Structure

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–6])

This case involves three consolidated applications for leave to appeal stemming from a judgment of the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which had overturned a judgment from the High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Division, Pretoria. The central issue relates to the interpretation of sections 25(1) and (2) of the National Water Act 36 of 1998 (hereinafter "the Water Act"). The applicants, which include the Minister of Water and Sanitation and various Deputy Director-Generals, contested whether water use entitlements could be “transferred” or traded to third parties under the Water Act.

In the first matter, Lötter N.O., the Doornkraal Business Trust sought to purchase a water use entitlement from Britzkraal Properties (Pty) Ltd, which it had been denied by the Director-General. The same rationale was followed in the second matter, Wiid, where three contracts involving water use entitlements encountered a similar refusal based on the interpretation of the Water Act. The third matter dealt with the South African Association for Water User Associations (SAAWUA), which sought a declaratory order regarding the meaning of sections 25(1) and (2).

The primary contention arose when the applicants argued that the Water Act explicitly prohibits the transfer of water use entitlements to third parties, and thus the Director-General’s refusal was justified. Historically, from 1998 to January 2018, the Department of Water and Sanitation allowed trading in these entitlements until a policy change occurred, prompting litigation.

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 6–17])

The issues for determination can be encapsulated as follows:

  1. Transfer of Water Use Entitlements: Does the Water Act permit the transfer of water use entitlements obtained under the Act to third parties?
  2. Charging Fees for Transfers: If transfer is permitted, does the Water Act sanction the charging of fees for such transfers?
  3. Constitutional Considerations: If the interpretation of the Act allows for such transactions, does it conflict with constitutional imperatives, particularly regarding equality and access to water as stipulated in sections 9 and 27 of the South African Constitution?

The case raises significant statutory interpretation questions concerning the permissible scope of actions by water user entitlement holders and the state’s interests in preventing the commodification of water resources.

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 1–40])

The primary statute under consideration is the National Water Act. The relevant provisions include:

  • Section 25(1): Authorises a water management institution to allow temporary use of water by the holder for different or similar purposes on another property.
  • Section 25(2): Clarifies that a holder may surrender a water use entitlement to facilitate a license application, explicitly permitting only modifications respecting entitlements on the same or different properties under certain conditions.
  • Section 2 of the Water Act: Establishes the Act's purpose of ensuring equitable access to water resources while redressing past racial and gender imbalances.

Judicial precedents, including interpretations from previous cases such as Cool Ideas 1186 CC v Hubbard [2014] ZACC 16, and statutory interpretation principles, are employed to elucidate the intent behind the legislative provisions.

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 19–36])

In its judgment, the Constitutional Court analyzed the grammatical structure and purpose of sections 25(1) and (2). The Court highlighted that the ordinary grammatical meaning of section 25(1) must be preserved, allowing for the interpretation that the second part does indeed permit temporary usage by a third party, contradicting the applicants’ narrower interpretation that restricted usage to the entitlement holder themselves.

The Court also considered the implications of denying the transferability of water use entitlements. The legislative intent, as articulated in section 2, was to address historical inequalities, and a rigid interpretation restricting transfers could potentially exacerbate existing disparities in water access among communities.

Moreover, the Court examined arguments against fee charging for transfers and concluded that the Water Act does not expressly prohibit such practices. The broader interpretation of the legislative framework, coupled with applicable sections like 29(2) acknowledging private compensation arrangements, gives credence to allowing fees in transactions involving water entitlements.

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 37–40])

The Constitutional Court dismissed the appeals, effectively upholding the Supreme Court of Appeal's interpretation that sections 25(1) and (2) allow for the trading and transfer of water use entitlements under specific regulatory conditions. The Court concluded that:

  1. Water use entitlements could be transferred or traded to third parties under section 25.
  2. Fees could be charged for these transfers if they are conditioned appropriately under the Water Act.
  3. This interpretation aligns with constitutional mandates aimed at eradicating historical injustices and promotes equity in water access.

The implications of this decision extend to potential legislative reforms, as it highlights the need for the Water Act to more explicitly regulate the commodification and trading of water resources, ensuring equitable access aligns with broader constitutional principles. The judgment not only clarifies the statutory framework but also prompts a necessary reevaluation of policies affecting water distribution in South Africa, bringing the discourse into alignment with both legislative intent and constitutional values.
