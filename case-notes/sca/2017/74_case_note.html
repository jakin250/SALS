Supreme Court of Appeal 2017

FIRAC Case Note: Ramoba v The State (1301/2016) ZASCA 74 (1 June 2017)

F - Facts:
The appellant, Solly Ramoba, was convicted on various counts including robbery involving aggravating circumstances and attempted murder by a regional court in Tzaneen on 21 July 2004. The appeal focused particularly on three counts of unlawful possession of firearms (counts 11, 12, and 13) stemming from a robbery at the Oasis Mall on 6 January 2003 ([1–2]).

During that incident, a gang, armed with firearms, robbed a security vehicle transporting cash, resulting in injuries to both security personnel. Ramoba, identified as being part of the gang, did not possess any firearms during his arrest but was seen carrying stolen money containers ([5–6]). Following the robbery, the police pursued the gang and found an R4 rifle and a Norinco pistol in the abandoned getaway vehicle ([9–10]).

Despite the initial conviction, Ramoba appealed on the grounds that there was insufficient evidence to sustain a conviction for joint possession of the firearms ([4]).

I - Issues:

  1. Was there sufficient evidence to support a conviction for joint possession of firearms as per the legal standards in South Africa, particularly concerning counts 11, 12, and 13?
  2. Did the trial court err in its application of the joint possession principles, particularly based on the case of S v Khambule?
  3. Was the cumulative effect of the imposed sentence of 52 years’ imprisonment appropriate or shockingly disproportionate? ([4–20]).

R - Rules:
The legal framework pertinent to this case involves:

  • Joint Possession: According to S v Nkosi and S v Khambule, joint possession does not require each individual to physically possess the firearm but necessitates a shared intention (animus) to control the item ([11]).
  • Principle of Common Purpose: This legal doctrine allows for the inference of joint possession under specific circumstances, particularly when individuals in a group intend to use firearms to facilitate an offense ([12–13]).
  • Cumulative Effect of Sentences: An appellate court can interfere with a sentence if it is disturbingly inappropriate or induced by misdirection as established in S v Salzwedel ([21]).

A - Application:
The court critically evaluated the evidence regarding joint possession. For count 11 (the Norinco pistol), it was established that there was no direct evidence linking Ramoba to the possession of the firearm found in the Isuzu bakkie. The absence of evidence regarding who placed the firearm there or whether Ramoba was aware of its presence implied that the conviction on this count was unwarranted ([14–15]).

Conversely, for counts 12 and 13 (the R4 and R5 rifles), independent witness testimonies indicated that Ramoba was directly involved and had at one point carried an automatic rifle during the robbery, which constituted direct involvement in the act ([16–20]). Hence, the principles of joint possession applied in this scenario were sufficiently substantiated.

Regarding sentencing, the court found that an effective 52-year sentence was excessive, particularly noting that the offences stemmed from a singular event, with all stolen items ultimately recovered intact. Taking into account Ramoba’s age at sentencing (33) and the nature of the crimes, this raised concerns about the appropriateness of the length of the sentence ([21–28]).

C - Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Ramoba’s appeal against conviction for count 11 (unlawful possession of the Norinco pistol), finding insufficient evidence to support that charge. However, the court confirmed the convictions for counts 12 and 13 (possession of R4 and R5 rifles).

The court also reduced the overall sentence from 52 years to an effective 28 years, reflecting a more reasonable application of sentencing principles concerning the nature of the offences and the circumstances surrounding the robbery. This decision illustrates the balance courts must strike between upholding convictions for serious crimes and ensuring that sentences are proportionate to the conduct in question ([1–4, 28]).

The implications of this case reinforce the need for sufficient evidence in establishing joint possession and highlight the judiciary's role in assessing the cumulative severity of sentences.
