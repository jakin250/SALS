Source Folder: 2010

# Case Note: Malachi v Cape Dance Academy International (Pty) Ltd and Others

## FIRAC Structure

### Facts

The case before the Constitutional Court of South Africa involves Tatiana Malachi, who is the applicant, and Cape Dance Academy International (Pty) Ltd and House of Rasputin Properties (Pty) Ltd as the first and second respondents, respectively. This matter reached the Court following a provisional order made on 24 August 2010 that directed the Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development and the employers to pay half of Malachi’s costs ([para 1]). 

Malachi was arrested and detained under provisions of the Magistrates’ Courts Act which were later contested for their constitutionality. Upon being informed of the potential unconstitutionality of these provisions, the employers agreed to her immediate release and subsequently did not contest her efforts to declare the provisions unconstitutional, thus framing their litigation stance as non-adversarial with respect to Malachi ([paras 6.2–6.3]).

The Minister, representing the government, was criticized for failing to amend or repeal the offending statutory provisions, which remained unaddressed for over 15 years ([para 8]). The matter culminated in considerations of whether the provisional order should become final and if costs should be imposed on the parties involved.

### Issues

The key legal questions before the Court were primarily concerned with the appropriate allocation of costs and the constitutional obligations of the parties involved. Specifically:

1. Should the provisional order regarding the sharing of costs between the employers and Malachi be made final? ([para 7]).
2. Who should ultimately bear the costs associated with the proceedings, considering the constitutional implications of the contested law? ([paras 8–9]).

### Rules

The Court referenced several pertinent legal precedents and statutory provisions in reaching its conclusions:

1. **Constitutional Obligations**: The Minister bears the responsibility of ensuring that laws within his domain comply with the Constitution, mandating amendments or repeals of unconstitutional provisions ([para 8]).
2. **Biowatch Principle**: The decision in *Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources, and Others* establishes that in cases involving constitutional challenges, costs may often fall on the losing party in relation to the government’s failure to ensure legislative compliance ([para 7]).
3. **Condonation for Late Filing**: The Court invoked provisions allowing for condonation (for the late filing of representations), underscoring the need for fairness regardless of procedural delays ([paras 5–6.2, 6.3]).

### Application

In applying these legal rules, the Court noted that while the employers initially initiated the arrest of Malachi, their acquiescence to her subsequent release and decision to not contest the constitutional challenge positioned them favorably regarding costs. Their engagement was characterized as collaborative rather than adversarial, significantly influencing the Court's stance on costs liability ([para 7]). 

The Minister’s prolonged inaction over the unconstitutionality of the provisions was a pivotal consideration. The Court underscored the Minister's regular constitutional obligations to intervene against laws contravening constitutional rights, which directly impacted the cost distribution ([paras 8, 10]). This led the Court to resolve that it would not be just to penalize the employers by imposing costs upon them. Consequently, the Minister was held responsible for the costs incurred by Malachi due to the unconstitutional legislative framework, aligning with the principles established in *Biowatch*.

### Conclusion

The Constitutional Court delivered a significant judgment, ultimately discharging the provisional order for costs against the employers and ordering that the Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development pay Malachi’s costs ([para 10]). The ruling reinforces the expectation placed on the state to rectify unconstitutional laws timely and signifies the Court’s commitment to ensuring that affected parties are compensated fairly when governmental negligence leads to constitutional disputes. This case underscores the importance of the Minister's role in safeguarding constitutional compliance and delineates the repercussions of failing to fulfill these duties, both in terms of legislative accountability and the financial liability that follows.

In summary, this judgment serves as a crucial reminder of the balance of responsibilities among governmental entities and private individuals, particularly in contexts where constitutional rights are at stake.
