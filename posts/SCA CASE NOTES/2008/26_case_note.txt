# Case Note: Transnet Ltd v Maela (39/07) [2008] ZASCA 26

### FIRAC Method

## F – Facts

The case of *Transnet Ltd v Maela* revolves around a personal injury claim stemming from an incident on one of Transnet's trains. The respondent, Arlindo de Assis Januario Maela (the plaintiff in the court a quo), alleged that on the night of June 23, 2004, he was the victim of a robbery while traveling as a passenger on a train operated by the appellant (Transnet). During the incident, he claimed that he was robbed of his cell phone and money, and ultimately thrown from the moving train. The appellant counters this narrative, presenting a differing account of the events.

The High Court, under Judge Msimeki AJ, accepted the respondent's version of events and found the appellant liable for damages due to negligence, citing a failure in ensuring safety for its passengers ([para 1]). The primary question before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the trial court erred in its factual findings, particularly rejecting the testimony of a security officer for the appellant, Mr. Mzobanzi Ceba, while favoring that of the respondent ([para 2]).

## I – Issues

The central legal issues raised in this case included:

1. **Factual disputes:** Whether the trial court was correct in accepting the respondent's evidence over that of the appellant’s security officer, especially given the discrepancies in their accounts of the incident ([para 2]).
   
2. **Negligence and liability:** Whether the appellant (Transnet) had fulfilled its legal duty to act without negligence regarding passenger safety, and if the respondent had sufficiently established that the appellant’s negligence led to his injuries ([paras 2-2]).

## R – Rules

The court relied on principles surrounding negligence and delictual liability, particularly in the context of railway operations. Key rulings referenced included:

1. **Duty of Care:** It was acknowledged that Transnet owed a legal duty to its passengers to act without negligence, a fact conceded by the appellant’s counsel ([para 2]).
   
2. **Foreseeability:** The court recognized that harm to passengers from criminal activities was foreseeable, thus imposing a duty to take reasonable precautions ([para 2]).

3. **Assessing Evidence:** The evaluation of witness credibility and the weight of conflicting testimony were critical to determining liability ([para 12]).

## A – Application

In applying these legal principles, the Supreme Court found that the trial judge had erred in rejecting the security officer's evidence. The court scrutinized the credibility of both witnesses through a detailed analysis of their testimonies ([para 12]). 

1. **Credibility of Witnesses:** The Supreme Court questioned the trial judge's assessment of Mr. Ceba as a "poor witness" and scrutinized the relevance of discrepancies in his reports and testimonies. It noted that Mr. Ceba’s evidence did not present a significant enough contradiction to undermine its credibility ([para 8-9]).

2. **Probability of Events:** The court found the respondent's behavior—jumping out of a moving train—implausible, given that he had the option to alight at the next station ([para 10]). The chances of Mr. Ceba fabricating his account were also seen as low since he had reported the incident almost immediately after it occurred ([para 11]).

3. **Established Facts vs. Testimony:** The court pointed out the inconsistencies in the respondent's version of events, particularly regarding the timeline of the robbery happening between Dunswart and Benoni stations yet claiming to be thrown from the train at Apex station ([para 11]). Hence, the established facts aligned more closely with Mr. Ceba's testimony.

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court ultimately concluded that the court a quo had made errors in assessing the credibility of witnesses and the implications of the evidence presented. It reversed the decision of the High Court, holding that the appellant was not liable for the injuries sustained by the respondent ([para 14]). The order of the court a quo was set aside, and a dismissal of the action with costs was ordered, thus alleviating Transnet from liability in this instance. The decision underscores the importance of careful assessment of evidence in cases involving conflicting testimonies and raises questions about the thresholds for establishing negligence in personal injury claims involving public transportation.
