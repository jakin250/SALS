Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# Case Note: Kham and Others v Electoral Commission and Another [2015] ZACC 37

## FIRAC Method

### F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–20])

This case arises from a dispute regarding eight by-elections held in the Tlokwe Local Municipality between August and December 2013. The first three applicants, formerly members of the African National Congress (ANC), became independent candidates following a fall-out with their party. The other five applicants joined them in contesting various wards, all standing under a common banner against corruption. All applicants were unsuccessful, losing to candidates from the ANC in the mentioned by-elections. 

The applicants initially challenged the results of the by-elections before the Electoral Court, arguing that the outcomes were affected by irregularities in voter registration. After their application was dismissed, the applicants petitioned the Constitutional Court for leave to appeal, seeking to have the by-election results set aside and new elections ordered ([paras 1–3]).

The applicants asserted that voters were registered in wards where they did not reside, and claimed that the Electoral Commission (IEC) failed to comply with various procedural requirements. Notably, they cited that the IEC delivered incomplete voters' rolls, lacking residential addresses, which materially hampered their election campaigns. While some irregularities were acknowledged by the IEC in their own investigations, they maintained that these did not affect the overall results ([paras 4–14]).

The Electoral Court, siding with the IEC, found that the merits did not warrant setting aside the election results, thus reinforcing the applicants' resolve to appeal to the Constitutional Court ([paras 15–20]).

### I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 21–23])

The case raises several fundamental legal issues:

1. **Leave to Appeal**: Should leave to appeal be granted?
2. **Jurisdiction**: Did the Electoral Court possess jurisdiction to determine the applicants' complaints?
3. **Procedural Exclusivity**: Were the applicants confined to the remedies stipulated under the Electoral Act and the Municipal Electoral Act?
4. **Electoral Irregularities**: Were there, in fact, significant electoral irregularities affecting the outcomes?
5. **Free and Fair Elections**: Were the by-elections conducted in a manner consistent with constitutional standards for free and fair elections?
6. **Relief**: What legal remedy is appropriate should the by-elections be found not to have been free and fair?

### R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 30–36])

The Constitution of South Africa, particularly section 19, guarantees every citizen the right to free and fair elections, emphasizing the constitutional duty imposed upon the Electoral Commission (section 190) to manage elections. Key statutory frameworks include the Electoral Act, which instructs the IEC on voter registration procedures, and the Municipal Electoral Act, outlining regulations for municipal by-elections. 

Relevant case law includes *New National Party of South Africa v Government of the Republic of South Africa* [1999] ZACC 5, which outlines the definition and importance of free and fair elections. The principles elucidated in *August and Another v Electoral Commission and Others* [1999] ZACC 3 also provide context on the imperative of compliance with electoral processes to uphold democratic integrity.

### A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 37–93])

The Court, upon establishing its jurisdiction and the grounds for the appeal, meticulously assessed claims of electoral irregularities. It scrutinized the allegations regarding non-compliance related to voter registration, highlighting the IEC's failures to ensure only eligible voters were registered within the appropriate wards. The Court recognized that significant irregularities had indeed occurred, such as the late submission of electoral rolls lacking vital addresses and thus obstructing candidates' ability to campaign effectively ([paras 53–68]).

In evaluating whether the by-elections had been free and fair, the Court applied a dual standard. It stressed the need not only for procedural fairness but also substantive equality in the electoral process, referencing both constitutional and international norms of democratic participation. The repeated failures of the IEC in this case collectively undermined the elections' legitimacy ([paras 82–89]).

The Court concluded that while the IEC's own investigations revealed certain irregularities, they appeared insufficiently substantial to alter the final outcomes per se. However, the systematic issues pointed to a broader concern regarding electoral integrity and compliance with constitutional mandates ([paras 90–95]).

### C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 96–127])

The Constitutional Court held that the by-elections conducted in Tlokwe were not free and fair due to the grave electoral irregularities demonstrated, including improper voter registrations and procedural lapses in providing the candidates with the requisite electoral rolls. Consequently, the Court set aside the results of the December 2013 by-elections and mandated that fresh by-elections be held in accordance with the Local Government: Municipal Structures Act 117 of 1998 ([paras 97–118]).

In ordering an equitable remedy, the Court asserted the primacy of maintaining electoral integrity and safeguarding democratic rights over practical concerns regarding costs or logistical challenges posed by a new election. This decision underscores the necessity for electoral bodies to adhere strictly to constitutional mandates, thereby enforcing accountability ([paras 119–127]).

The implications of this ruling reflect a commitment to reinforcing democratic processes in South Africa. By mandating fresh elections, the Court not only upheld the applicants’ rights but also reinforced the broader electorate's rights to participate in genuinely fair electoral processes, setting a precedent for future litigations concerning electoral integrity and adherence to constitutional standards.
