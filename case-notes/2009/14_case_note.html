Constitutional Court

using FIRAC Method: Trustees for the Time Being of the Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources (CCT 80/08) [2009] ZACC 14

The case at hand centers around a dispute concerning costs awards in a constitutional context, particularly between the non-governmental organization The Biowatch Trust (Biowatch) and the governmental bodies responsible for the regulation of genetically modified organisms (GMOs).

Constitutional Court 2009 [2009] ZACC 14

F – Facts

The case at hand centers around a dispute concerning costs awards in a constitutional context, particularly between the non-governmental organization The Biowatch Trust (Biowatch) and the governmental bodies responsible for the regulation of genetically modified organisms (GMOs). Biowatch, an environmental watchdog, sought information from the Registrar for Genetic Resources—a governmental entity—under the provisions of the Genetically Modified Organisms Act 15 of 1997, and in pursuance of its rights under section 32 of the Constitution, which provides for the right of access to information held by the state ([1]–[4]).

The High Court ruled in favor of Biowatch, confirming its right to information. However, despite this success, the court opted not to award costs to Biowatch, citing the ineptitude of its requests for information and suggesting a lack of precision in its legal arguments as a basis for this decision ([4], [14]). Additionally, costs were imposed on Biowatch for Monsanto’s intervention; the court deemed that Biowatch's claims compelled Monsanto—an entity opposing the disclosure of certain information—to defend its interests in court ([4], [5]). Following unsuccessful appeals to the Transvaal Provincial Division (Full Court) and the Supreme Court of Appeal, Biowatch sought leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court, effectively questioning the High Court's approach to costs awards in constitutional litigation ([4], [6]).

I – Issues

The Constitutional Court was tasked with addressing various legal and constitutional questions concerning the judicial approach to costs awards in constitutional litigation. The primary issues included:

  1. Whether costs awards in constitutional cases should pivot on the status of the litigants or the substantive issues at hand ([14], [15]);
  2. The appropriate general approach for costs in litigations involving the state and private parties ([14]);
  3. The treatment of costs where the state fails to fulfill its constitutional and statutory responsibilities while litigation involves private parties ([14]);
  4. The principles guiding appellate courts in reviewing costs awards ([14]).

R – Rules

The judgment draws from the principles established in Affordable Medicines Trust v Minister of Health, where the court affirmed that the unsuccessful party in constitutional litigation against the state is generally not ordered to pay costs, thus minimizing the chilling effect on potential litigants seeking to enforce their constitutional rights ([21], [22]). The judgment in Ferreira v Levin also elucidates the necessity for flexibility and a case-by-case approach in discerning costs awards, emphasizing that such awards should reflect the public interest as well as the relevant conduct of the parties involved ([7], [8]).

Furthermore, the court referenced provisions in the Constitution (section 32, providing for the right to access information, and section 24, concerning the right to a healthy environment) as foundational for evaluating the ongoing implications of costs awards in this specific context ([10], [11]). The court established that constitutional considerations must guide the determination of costs and that courts should not lightly deviate from established norms that protect the right to litigate without the fear of adverse costs orders.

A – Application

Justice Sachs, delivering the judgment, emphasized the need for a pivot away from examining the status of parties toward the substantive implications of the issues raised in litigation. This assessment argued for an understanding of how costs affect public interest litigation ([15], [16]). Acknowledging the significant contributions of public interest organizations to the jurisprudence of the court, Sachs J noted that imposing adverse costs could dissuade such entities from pursuing constitutional litigation, thus adversely impacting societal interests ([16], [20]).

The court then considered the ramifications of the High Court's decisions, underscoring that Biowatch, being successful in its claims against the state, warranted an award of costs in line with the general rule that mandates the state to bear costs in such cases. The High Court's rationale for denying costs based on Biowatch's procedural ineptness was critiqued as not appropriately weighted against the constitutional character of the litigation ([38], [44]).

In relation to the costs awarded to Monsanto, the court found that since the core of the dispute involved the state’s failure to fulfill its obligations, imposing costs on Biowatch owing to its relationship with Monsanto was inappropriate. The court highlighted that such decisions misaligned with the true nature of the litigation ([53], [56]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately determined that the costs decisions by the High Court were unsustainable. The court upheld Biowatch's appeal, concluding that the governmental authorities must bear the costs incurred by Biowatch in the High Court and in the Constitutional Court. Additionally, the order requiring Biowatch to pay costs to Monsanto was overturned due to the mischaracterization of the litigation as a dispute primarily between the private parties ([60], [61]). Justice Sachs's judgment established key precedents regarding the approach to costs in constitutional litigation, affirming that the fears of financial repercussions should not obstruct access to justice, particularly in matters involving public interest and constitutional rights. This ruling has significant implications for future public interest cases, offering a renewed framework for costs awards that prioritizes constitutional scrutiny and equitable access to judicial remedies.
