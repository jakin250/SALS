Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

### Case Note: Van den Heever v Taljaard (289/2017) [2018] ZASCA 22

#### F – Facts

[1] The case revolves around the appellant, Mr. Johannes van den Heever, who is the registered owner of a property purchased from Sunset Point Properties 212 CC (Sunset Point) in August 2010. The transfer of this property occurred on 11 February 2011. After the transaction, Sunset Point was placed under winding-up, prompting the first and second respondents, Louis Marius Taljaard and Chebo Chaza, to pursue a court order declaring the transfer void on the grounds of compliance with the Companies Act 61 of 1973.

[2] The liquidators of Sunset Point contended that the sale was void under § 341 of the Companies Act, which addresses dispositions of property by companies that are unable to pay their debts. The appellant opposed this motion and filed a counter-application seeking validation of his purchase under § 341(2) of the same Act.

[3] The procedural history includes an earlier dismissal of winding-up proceedings by a magistrate, followed by the appellant acquiring the property without knowledge of the ongoing liquidation issues. Subsequently, the appeal against the winding-up proceedings was upheld, leading to the assertion that the transfer had occurred while Sunset Point was under deeming liquidation.

[4] Important details include prior financial disputes related to Sunset Point, notably concerning a deposit given by Mr. Gordon Anthony Jones, presenting heightened issues regarding the corporation's solvency.

[5] After the transfer, creditors—including Mr. Musolwa, who had applied for urgent interdicts regarding the transfer—raised concerns over the propriety of the transaction, suggesting undue advantage accrued to the appellant at the expense of other creditors ([paras 2-12]).

#### I – Issues

[6] The central issues in this case consist of:

1. Whether the transfer of property to the appellant constituted a void disposition under § 341(2) of the Companies Act 61 of 1973 due to the winding-up of Sunset Point.
   
2. Whether the appellant could succeed in validating the purportedly void transaction on grounds of adherence to a court order as specified in the Insolvency Act 24 of 1936.

3. The nature of the circumstances under which the order for transfer was obtained, specifically whether any collusion or reprehensible conduct occurred to prejudge other creditors ([paras 18-28]).

#### R – Rules

[7] Relevant legal principles include:

- **Section 341(2) of the Companies Act 61 of 1973** stipulates that dispositions made after commencement of the winding-up are void unless sanctioned by the court.
- **Insolvency Act 24 of 1936**, specifically § 2, defines "disposition" and outlines exclusions for transfers made in compliance with a court order.
- **Case precedent** involves the interpretation of reasonable expectations of parties acting in good faith, as articulated by cases like *Dabelstein and others v Lane and Fey NNO* 2001 (1) SA 1222 (SCA) and *Sackstein en Venter NNO v Greyling* 1990 (2) SA 323 (O) ([paras 18-23]).

#### A – Application

[8] The Court assessed the validity of the transfer under expertise from cases interpreting the Companies Act and Insolvency Act. It recognized that the appellant's claim for validation rested on the assertion that disposition occurred within the framework of compliance with a court order ([para 19]).

[9] Despite the appellant's arguments regarding ignorance of the ongoing liquidation and claims to good faith, the Court discerned a pattern of benefits having been conferred to Mr. Musolwa at the expense of other creditors, highlighting a lack of transparency ([paras 22-26]).

[10] The Court also considered the implications of Mrs. Bierman’s actions, from facilitating the sale to executing preferential payments, which collectively undermined the rights of other creditors. It recognized that the appellant, while potentially unknowing at some points, had been duly warned regarding risks concerning the winding-up proceedings ([paras 26-29]).

[11] Ultimately, the Court determined that the circumstances of the transfer were fundamentally flawed due to the elements of collusion and prejudice against other creditors, thereby dismissing the validation of the transaction as unjust and inequitable.

#### C – Conclusion

[12] The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the transfer of the property was indeed a void disposition under § 341(2) of the Companies Act as it had transpired while Sunset Point was deemed to be under winding-up. The judgment thus dismissed the appeal and reaffirmed the principle that the protection of creditors must prevail over individual transactions perceived to be unfairly advantageous. 

[13] The Court emphasized the importance of equitable treatment among creditors and maintained that the collapse of the transaction reflected poorly on conduct exhibited during the winding-up proceedings. The judgment implies a crucial precedent for future cases involving corporate insolvency and reinforces the legal scrutiny surrounding transactions that occur during such delicate financial periods ([paras 30-32]).
