Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: MC Denneboom Service Station CC and Another v Phayane [2014] ZACC 29

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History

[1] This case arose from an application for leave to appeal against a judgment of the North Gauteng High Court, Pretoria, which ordered the eviction of MC Denneboom Service Station CC (the first applicant) and Mr Nola Elison Chiloane (the second applicant) from certain immovable property owned by Mr Molefe Ian Phayane (the respondent) ([1]).

[2] The property in question, Erf 22104, situated at 95 Tsamaya Road, Mamelodi, was previously owned by Mr Chiloane and his wife, who were married in community of property. Following Mr Chiloane's sequestration in 1992, the property was sold in public auction in 2009 to Mr Phayane, who obtained transfer in May 2010 ([2]–[3]).

[3] Subsequently, the applicants contested Mr Phayane's claim of ownership through an action in the High Court, seeking to have the sale declared null and void, asserting that Mr Chiloane remained the rightful owner. However, procedural inaction followed as they did not pursue a trial date ([4]).

[4] In September 2012, Mr Phayane filed for eviction, claiming that the applicants were unlawful occupants of the property. The applicants, acknowledging their occupation but disputing Mr Phayane’s ownership, argued that he had failed to comply with the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE) ([5]).

[5] The High Court (per Cambanis AJ) ordered the ejection of the applicants and individuals working under them, except for residential occupants. They then sought leave to appeal, claiming the order was ambiguous and wrongfully authorized the eviction of Mr Chiloane, a residential occupant ([6]–[7]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions

[6] The key legal issues before the Constitutional Court included:

1. Whether the High Court's order ambiguous in authorizing the eviction of Mr Chiloane, who is a residential occupant.
2. Whether the eviction proceedings appropriately complied with PIE in terms of residential occupants.
3. The relationship of the eviction of juristic persons to the provisions of PIE and constitutional rights to housing ([10]–[11]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon

[7] In adjudicating this matter, the court applied several pertinent legal principles:

1. **Section 26(3) of the Constitution**: Protects individuals from being evicted from their homes without a court order made after considering all relevant circumstances ([16]).
2. **Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998**: Regulates the eviction of unlawful occupiers, emphasizing the necessity of compliance before eviction orders. It delineates between residential and commercial occupants ([16]–[17]).
3. **Legal Precedents**: 
   - In **Ndlovu v Ngcobo** and others, the Supreme Court of Appeal held that PIE does not apply to the eviction of juristic persons or those not using a property for residential purposes ([17]).
   - Past judgments interpreting the nature of lawful occupation and the rights afforded under PIE were also referenced ([8], [9]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law

[8] The court acknowledged that the High Court's order was ambiguous, particularly regarding whether it allowed the eviction of Mr Chiloane as a residential occupant, thus risking an infringement of his constitutional right to housing ([14]).

[9] The court concluded that while the High Court had legitimate grounds for evicting non-residential occupants, the problematic wording inadvertently extended to Mr Chiloane, violating protections afforded by PIE ([16]).

[10] The Constitutional Court determined that Mr Phayane had not complied with PIE concerning Mr Chiloane, who qualified as an unlawful occupier but also as a residential occupant entitled to the protections of the Act ([16]), thus necessitating judicial notice of the High Court's failure in applying PIE correctly.

[11] The respondent’s argument that PIE did not apply to commercial occupants was found valid and aligned with precedents, affirming that Mr Phayane’s eviction process for individuals not using the property as home did not require PIE compliance ([18]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications

[12] The Constitutional Court held that leave to appeal was only warranted concerning the amendment of the High Court's order. The appeal regarding ambiguity was granted, particularly protecting Mr Chiloane, whose status as a residential occupant required an explicit exclusion from eviction processes set forth by non-residential specifications ([19]).

[13] Consequently, the Court amended the High Court's order to clarify the eviction aimed only at non-residential occupants, hence fostering compliance with PIE and upholding constitutional guarantees ([20]).

[14] This case underscores the safeguarding of residential rights under PIE, reinforcing the importance of clarity in judicial orders to prevent unintended infringements of constitutional rights, particularly the right to housing. Consequently, it illustrates the continuous evolution of case law surrounding evictions and the necessity of aligning such orders with statutory requirements to uphold the rights established by the Constitution.

[15] The ruling emphasizes the duty of courts to ensure adherence to PIE in all eviction matters concerning residential occupants and sets a benchmark for future adjudications concerning similar rights.
