# Case Note: Qwelane v South African Human Rights Commission [2021] ZACC 22

### FIRAC Method

#### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–9])
The case arises from an article authored by Jonathan Dubula Qwelane published in the *Sunday Sun* on 20 July 2008, which expressed offensive views about the LGBT+ community. The article sparked extensive complaints, ultimately leading the South African Human Rights Commission (SAHRC) to institute proceedings against Qwelane in the Equality Court, alleging hate speech under section 10(1) of the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000 (the Equality Act). The Equality Court ruled in favor of the SAHRC, finding Qwelane’s statements constituted hate speech. Qwelane subsequently challenged the constitutionality of section 10(1) in the High Court, claiming it was overbroad and vague. Following the High Court's ruling, the Supreme Court of Appeal affirmed that section 10(1) was unconstitutional, leading to this appeal to the Constitutional Court for confirmation of the order of constitutional invalidity.

#### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 31–32])
The main legal questions posed for determination included whether section 10(1)(a) of the Equality Act is constitutionally valid, particularly regarding the inclusion of the term “hurtful.” Additionally, the issues regarding the proper interpretation of key terms like "hurtful," "harmful," and "to incite harm," alongside whether the provision is vague and constitutes an unjustifiable limitation on the right to freedom of expression, were pivotal to the court's inquiry.

#### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 49–55])
The court engaged deeply with sections 16 and 36 of the Constitution. Section 16 guarantees the right to freedom of expression, while section 36 outlines the parameters under which rights may be limited. The court also referenced previous case law, such as *Khumalo v Holomisa* [2002] ZACC 12 and *Islamic Unity Convention v Independent Broadcasting Authority* [2002] ZACC 3, which elucidate on the nature of speech that can constitute hate speech and the importance of balancing freedom of expression with the need to protect vulnerable groups. The court acknowledged the international legal framework related to hate speech, including provisions from the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR) and comparable foreign jurisprudence.

#### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 94–114])
In its reasoning, the Constitutional Court emphasized the necessity of a clear, objective standard for interpreting hate speech. The court found that the vague nature of the term “hurtful” could lead to an unreasonable limitation on freedom of expression as it set a low threshold for liability. It concluded that while "harmful" could be understood as causing significant emotional or psychological harm, the inclusion of “hurtful” in the definition of hate speech excessively encroached on free speech rights. Consequently, the court validated the need to excise the word "hurtful" from section 10(1) to preserve constitutional compliance. 

In terms of Qwelane’s statements, the court meticulously analyzed the context of his declarations, indicating his words indeed advocated hatred and vilified the LGBT+ community. This assessment drew upon both the societal context at the time of the utterances and the significant hurt caused to the community, leading to the conclusion that his behavior constituted hate speech as defined by the refined provisions of the Equality Act post-remedial interpretation.

#### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 162–194])
The court confirmed the Supreme Court of Appeal’s declaration of constitutional invalidity concerning the inclusion of the word “hurtful” in section 10(1) of the Equality Act, ruling it unconstitutional and invalid. The court suspended the operation of this declaration for 24 months, allowing Parliament time to remedy the defect. Furthermore, the court upheld the finding that Qwelane’s article did constitute hate speech and thus sustained the SAHRC’s complaint. This ruling underscores a critical balance between the protection of marginalized communities from hate speech and the safeguarding of the fundamental right to freedom of expression, reiterating the court's commitment to the transformative ideals enshrined in the South African Constitution.

The implications of this ruling signal a significant reinforcement of the legal framework against hate speech in South Africa, particularly in light of the vulnerabilities faced by the LGBT+ community. By excising the vague provisions and clarifying the bounds of reasonable expression, this decision aims to foster a more tolerant society while reaffirming the judiciary’s role in upholding constitutional rights.
