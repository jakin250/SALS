Nu Africa Duty Free Shops (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Finance and Others; Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service v Ambassador Duty Free (Pty) Ltd and Others; Minister of Finance v Ambassador Duty Free (Pty) Ltd and Others [2022] ZACC 31

Facts The three cases (CCT 29/22, CCT 57/22, and CCT 58/22) were consolidated for adjudication concerning alleged constitutional violations related to amendments to the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 and the Value-Added Tax Act 89 of 1991.

Constitutional Court 2023 [2022] ZACC 31

Facts

The three cases (CCT 29/22, CCT 57/22, and CCT 58/22) were consolidated for adjudication concerning alleged constitutional violations related to amendments to the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 and the Value-Added Tax Act 89 of 1991. The applicants include Nu Africa Duty Free Shops (Pty) Ltd, the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (SARS), and the Minister of Finance, while the respondents include several duty-free retailers and associated political figures ([1]–[4]).

The High Court had previously held that certain provisions of the Customs and VAT Acts delegated plenary legislative powers to the Minister, which violated the doctrine of separation of powers ([6]–[10]). Amending Schedules to these Acts had introduced a quota system limiting the amounts of alcohol and tobacco that diplomats could purchase duty-free, which sparked allegations of irrationalities and procedural unfairness ([30]–[34]).

Significant background factors included prior abuses of the duty-free system by diplomats, resulting in a substantial loss of revenue for the state ([8]–[9]). The amendments were implemented following consultations led by SARS and DIRCO (Department of International Relations and Cooperation), which were asserted to have inadequately considered the input from retailers ([25]–[31]) and failed to establish a clear procedure for administering the new rules ([196]).

Issues

Essential legal issues in this case revolve around:

  1. Whether the provisions of the Customs and VAT Acts delegating authority to the Minister to amend Schedules constituted an unconstitutional assignment of plenary legislative power.
  2. Whether the amendments conflicted with section 77 of the Constitution, which governs “money Bills”.
  3. Whether the process followed by the Minister and the Commissioner was procedurally fair and rational ([70]).

Rules

The relevant legal principles considered in this case include:

  1. The doctrine of separation of powers as entrusted by Chapters 1 and 2 of the Constitution, especially concerning legislative versus executive powers ([92]).
  2. South African case law establishing the prohibition against granting plenary legislative powers to the Executive, including Executive Council and Smit ([75]–[92]).
  3. The definition of "money Bills" as articulated within section 77 of the Constitution, specifically those bills which impose national taxes, levies, or duties ([165]).
  4. Constitutional requirements for administrative action and their societal purpose in ensuring fairness and rationality through the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([108]).

Application

The Court, in assessing the amendments to the Customs and VAT Acts:

  1. Acknowledged that mere legislated empowerment allowing the Minister to amend provisions did not inherently violate the Constitution, provided that such amendments were justifiable and within reasonable limits.
  2. Clarified that the delegation was permissible in the context of administrative expedience, necessitated by the immediacy of fiscal regulations, thus affirming the legitimacy of temporary measures targeting egregious abuses ([100]–[101]).
  3. Critically appraised the procedures leading to the establishment of the quota system and concluded that the lack of genuine consultation with affected retailers constituted a failure of procedural fairness, rendering the amendments unconstitutionally arbitrary ([221]–[223]).
  4. Evaluated the rationality of the quotas set. The court found that while the intent behind introducing quotas aimed at curbing abuse was legitimate, the lack of empirical grounding for the specified figures rendered the amendments irrational ([111]).

Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately ruled against upholding the High Court's declaration of constitutional invalidity concerning the relevant Customs and VAT provisions. It noted that while certain procedural inadequacies had tainted the amendment process, the overarching framework and goals were constitutionally valid ([148]–[149]). The outcome highlights the necessity for legislative clarity in delegated powers and the intricate balance between effective governance and constitutional adherence.

The follow-up for this ruling required suspending the effects until adequate consultation and review could be executed, reflecting the need for administrative reforms without retroactive damage to the state's fiscal integrity ([275]).

Implications

The ruling reinforces the imperative that legislative amendments must not only serve public interests but also adhere strictly to constitutional mandates on rights, procedures, and accountability, particularly in the context of revenue measures affecting public interests ([147]–[149]).
