Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1999

### FIRAC Case Note: **National Coalition for Gay and Lesbian Equality v Minister of Home Affairs** (CCT 10/99)

**F – Facts:**

The case involves a constitutional challenge to **section 25(5)** of the **Aliens Control Act 96 of 1991**. This statutory provision allows the immigration of spouses of permanent South African residents but explicitly excludes same-sex partners from its benefits ([1]). The appellants, consisting of fourteen individuals and the **Commission for Gender Equality**, argued that this exclusion discriminated against them on the grounds of sexual orientation, violating their rights under section 9 of the Constitution ([2]). The Cape of Good Hope High Court found the provision unconstitutional, allowing for a twelve-month suspension for Parliament to amend the law ([2]).

The respondents—**the Minister of Home Affairs**, the **Deputy Minister**, and the **Director-General of Home Affairs**—initially resisted the High Court's ruling, claiming a lack of grounds for appeal due to the political sensitivity surrounding immigration and potential impacts on societal norms ([4], [5]). The High Court had denied their request for a postponement to file further affidavits concerning their rationale against same-sex partnerships ([7], [8]).

The case culminated in appeals from both parties to the Constitutional Court, with the appellants seeking fuller validation of the High Court's findings and the respondents challenging the entire judgment ([4]).
  
**I – Issues:**

The principal constitutional questions arose from the appeal:

1. **Discrimination**: Does section 25(5) of the Aliens Control Act violate the equality rights of same-sex partners by allowing immigration rights only to heterosexual spouses?
2. **Judicial Remedies**: If the court finds the provision unconstitutional, may it read in words to amend the statute and achieve compliance with the Constitution? ([1], [58]).

**R – Rules:**

The court's analysis relied on several critical legal principles:

- **Constitutional Right to Equality**: Section 9 of the 1996 Constitution safeguards individuals from discrimination based on sexual orientation, affirming that everyone is equal before the law and entitled to equal protection ([30]).
- **Limitation and Justification**: According to section 36(1) of the Constitution, any limitation of rights must be justifiable in an open and democratic society, necessitating a balance of interests ([58]).
- **Reading In**: Jurisprudence confirms the permissible judicial practice of "reading in" additional words to amend a provision found to be unconstitutional, ultimately ensuring that legislative intent aligns with constitutional values ([61], [70]).

**A – Application:**

The Constitutional Court examined whether section 25(5) constituted an unfair discrimination against same-sex partners by:

1. Evaluating **the impact of the exclusion**: The court recognized that the lack of recognition for same-sex partners created a distinct disadvantage, reinforcing harmful stereotypes and undermining the dignity of individuals denoted by their sexual orientation ([39], [43]).
  
2. Assessing **the legislative intent behind the exclusion**: The provision was determined to focus solely on heterosexual unions, ignoring a substantial class of individuals sharing similar or identical familial structures ([40], [41]). The court applied an objective theory of unconstitutionality allowing those affected by the constitutional breach (same-sex partners) to challenge the discrimination on behalf of their South African counterparts ([28]).

3. Analyzing **the respondents' arguments**: The court dismissed claims that the state had an unqualified right to regulate family immigration, emphasizing that such actions could not circumvent constitutional rights. Additionally, it found no rational basis between the exclusion and any state interest in protecting the institution of marriage ([27], [59]).

Ultimately, the court concluded that the lack of protection for same-sex partnerships severely limited the rights of those partners, which constituted both unfair discrimination based on sexual orientation and a violation of dignity rights ([57], [80]).

**C – Conclusion:**

The Constitutional Court upheld the High Court’s ruling that section 25(5) was unconstitutional due to its discriminatory nature towards same-sex life partnerships ([81]). The court reversed the High Court's suspension order, emphasizing immediate compliance with constitutional standards by **reading in** "or partner, in a permanent same-sex life partnership" after "spouse" in the provision, facilitating equal immigration rights for both heterosexual and same-sex partners. This reading in was framed as both just and equitable ([88]). The ruling underscored that equal dignity and protection under the law must extend to all relationship forms within an evolving legal landscape, reinforcing the requirements set out in the Constitution for an inclusive society ([98]). 

The order was reflective of a decisive step towards substantive equality for all citizens, aligning with contemporary standards of justice and inclusivity.
