Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2004

# FIRAC Case Note: Mkontwana v Nelson Mandela Metropolitan Municipality

## Facts
The case involves three separate applications challenging the constitutionality of provisions in the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 and related ordinances concerning the burden of municipal consumption charges ([1]–[4]). 

- **Mkontwana Case:** 
  Nokuthula Phyllis Mkontwana purchased a property, requiring payment of outstanding municipal consumption charges before transfer could occur. She encountered significant debts incurred by prior occupants, leading the municipality to demand over R10,000 before issuing a necessary certificate for transfer ([19]).

- **Bissett Case:**
  Peter William Bissett and co-applicants experienced similar issues when attempting to sell their property, being informed by the Buffalo City Municipality of debts exceeding R14,000, despite conflicting statements regarding the actual amount owed ([20]).

- **WLD Application:** 
  This broader application included several parties, including the Transfer Rights Action Campaign, raising issues of various occupants’ (e.g., tenants, unlawful occupants) liabilities regarding outstanding consumption charges ([22]).

The South Eastern Cape Local Division of the High Court declared section 118(1) of the Act unconstitutional, leading to its confirmation appeal in the Constitutional Court ([3], [4]) and the application for direct access by other parties ([8]).

## Issues
The core legal issues include:

1. **Interpretation and Constitutionality:** Can sections 118(1) and 50(1)(a) of the Municipal Systems Act and related ordinances be interpreted to apply only to charges due by the owner?
2. **Arbitrariness of Deprivation:** Do the provisions lead to arbitrary deprivation of property, violating section 25(1) of the Constitution?
3. **Procedural Fairness:** Are these provisions procedurally fair regarding the obligations on municipalities to inform owners of outstanding charges ([25]–[26])?

## Rules
The case hinges on several statutory provisions and constitutional principles:

1. **Section 25(1) of the Constitution:** No one may be deprived of property except by law of general application, which may not permit arbitrary deprivation.
2. **Section 118(1) of the Municipal Systems Act:** A registrar of deeds may not transfer property unless a certificate is produced confirming all municipal service-related charges for the preceding two years have been paid ([31]).
3. **First National Bank v Commissioner (FNB case):** Established that deprivation of property could occur without the loss of ownership, including limitations such as inability to transfer, constituting "deprivation" under section 25(1) ([32]).

## Application
### Constitutionality Analysis
- **Deprivation Established:** The Court recognized that the provisions in question effectively deprive owners of their ability to transfer property if outstanding consumption charges are due. The extent of this deprivation was evaluated by considering the significant amounts charged, which may exceed the property's value and inhibit transfer for extended periods ([34]–[37]).

- **Arbitrariness Assessment:** 
  - The Court applied the framework outlined in the FNB case, particularly examining the relationship between the means (the deprivation) and the purpose (municipal financial security). The Court found sufficient reasoning exists to justify limitations intended to assure municipal debts are paid, primarily borne by property owners to encourage responsible management of municipal services by tenants ([54]–[56]).
  
- **Differential Treatment:** The Court dismissed claims of unequal treatment under section 9(1) as the law does not treat all owners equally; it creates distinctions based on whether they occupy the property or not ([68]–[70]).

### Procedural Fairness
- The lack of ongoing communication from municipal authorities regarding consumption charges was deemed a procedural shortcoming. The Court mandated that municipalities must provide owners with statements of account on request, enhancing transparency and fairness in dealings ([66], [67]).

## Conclusion
The Court ultimately upheld the constitutionality of section 118(1), determining that its remedies encourage responsible ownership and do not constitute arbitrary deprivation of property under the Constitution. It held that the significant connection between the law's purpose and the property—enhancing municipal financial sustainability—affords sufficient reason for the deprivation. The appeal against the High Court's earlier ruling was dismissed, thereby reaffirming the law's validity while underscoring the need for municipalities to maintain clear and open communication regarding consumer charges and their implications for property transfers ([75]).

**Order:** The Court ordered that the application for confirmation of the invalidity of section 118(1) and section 50(1)(a) be dismissed. A declaration was made that municipalities have an obligation to supply monthly account statements to property owners upon request ([75]). 

This decision underlines the balance between encouraging prompt municipal debt payment while protecting property owners' rights, encapsulating the dynamics of property, tenants, and municipal law in post-apartheid South Africa.
