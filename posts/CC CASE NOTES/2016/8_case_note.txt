Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Democratic Alliance v Speaker of National Assembly and Others

### I. Facts
The case before the Constitutional Court of South Africa involves the Democratic Alliance (applicant) against the Speaker of the National Assembly and the Chairperson of the National Council of Provinces, and the Government of the Republic (respondents). This dispute arose following the President's State of the Nation Address on 12 February 2015. During the address, members of the Economic Freedom Fighters (EFF) unexpectedly interjected, questioning the President about the repayment for state funds used for renovations at his private residence in Nkandla. The Speaker, attempting to maintain order, ordered the removal of the EFF members after they refused to comply. The removal was executed, allegedly using police forces, raising concerns over the constitutional implications surrounding the arrest of Parliament members for interrupting proceedings ([paras 1–8]).

The Democratic Alliance subsequently sought a declaration from the High Court that section 11 of the Powers, Privileges and Immunities of Parliament and Provincial Legislatures Act 4 of 2004 (the Act) was constitutionally invalid. This section authorizes the arrest of any person causing a disturbance in Parliament. The High Court found that this provision violated members’ constitutional rights to free speech under sections 58(1)(a) and 71(1)(a) of the Constitution, which ever prominently guarantee immunity from criminal liability concerning statements made in Parliament. The court declared section 11 invalid to the extent that it applied to members of Parliament ([para 6]).

### II. Issues
The key issues before the Constitutional Court revolved around whether:
1. Section 11 of the Act infringes on the parliamentary privilege of free speech established by sections 58(1) and 71(1) of the Constitution.
2. The interpretation of the term "person" in section 11 includes members of Parliament, and whether the application of this section impinges on their rights.
3. There are any permissible means by which the privilege of free speech could be limited through section 11 of the Act ([paras 10–12]).

### III. Rules
The court relied on several statutory provisions and legal principles, chiefly:
- **Sections 58 and 71 of the Constitution** guarantee freedom of speech and specific immunities to members of the National Assembly and the National Council of Provinces, respectively ([para 2]).
- **Section 11 of the Act**, which enables the arrest of persons creating disturbances in Parliament, was scrutinized for its implications on free speech rights.
- The standard principles of statutory interpretation were employed, emphasizing that legislation should be read within the broader constitutional framework, ensuring no absurdity arises from its application ([paras 19, 23]).
- **Case law** such as *Dikoko v Mokhatla* and *S v Zuma* was referenced to underline the constitutional protection of parliamentary privileges and the scope of permissible limitations to these rights ([para 11]).

### IV. Application
The majority judgment penned by Madlanga J held that "person" in section 11 broadly encompasses members of Parliament. The court noted that the ordinary meaning of statutory language, coupled with the context and purpose of the Act, supports this interpretation. By enabling the arrest of parliamentarians during legislative sessions, section 11 inherently threatens their constitutional right to conduct parliamentary debates freely ([paras 19–20]).

The judgment further asserted that the broad and ambiguous definition of "disturbance" under section 11 could encroach upon acceptable parliamentary discourse, which may include vigorous debate and dissent, characteristic of a democracy ([para 44]). The majority concluded that the potential for criminal liability stemming from section 11 embodies a 'chilling effect' on the essential freedoms provided to parliamentary members ([para 41]).

Consequently, the court held that although parliament may define rules governing its procedures, it cannot create an external legislative framework, such as that in section 11 of the Act, which undermines its own immunities and privileges—a core element of which is the freedom of speech in deliberative processes ([para 52]).

Nugent AJ concurring, while agreeing on the overarching violation of free speech rights, highlighted concerns regarding the precise interpretation of "arrest" under the Act, advocating for a broader definition that encapsulates any form of restraint ([para 64]).

Conversely, Jafta J, in his dissent, posited that the interpretation of "person" in section 11 should exclude members of Parliament. This view was based on the distinct definitions and operational contexts of members within the Act, arguing for a narrower interpretation that would sidestep constitutional conflicts, maintaining that the power to discipline members should reside strictly within parliamentary rules ([para 82]).

### V. Conclusion
The court determined that section 11 of the Powers, Privileges and Immunities of Parliament and Provincial Legislatures Act 4 of 2004, as it applies to members of Parliament, violates their constitutional rights, particularly regarding free speech provisions. Thus, the constitutional declarations of invalidity made by the High Court were upheld, and the section was ordered to be interpreted to exclude members, affirming the necessity for Parliament to remain self-governing in matters of internal discipline without external legislative encumbrances ([paras 60–63]).

This decision reinforces the autonomy and inviolability of parliamentary debate within South Africa's constitutional democracy, emphasizing that any limitations on speech must be carefully constructed to uphold the integrity of legislative discourse while maintaining essential democratic principles.
