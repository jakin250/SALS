Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# Case Note: Premier: Limpopo Province v Speaker of the Limpopo Provincial Legislature and Others [2012] ZACC 3

## F – Facts

This case arises from the decision of the Constitutional Court in *Premier, Limpopo Province v Speaker of the Limpopo Provincial Government and Others* (Limpopo I) ([1]). The issues at stake pertain to the constitutionality of several legislative acts that authorize provincial legislatures to manage their own financial affairs. The Premier of Limpopo had referred the Financial Management of the Limpopo Provincial Legislature Bill ([2]) to the Court, expressing doubts over the legislature's competence to legislate concerning its financial management.

In *Limpopo I*, the Court stated that while Parliament possesses plenary legislative powers, provincial legislative powers are limited to those explicitly outlined in section 104 of the Constitution ([2]). The Court noted that financial management of provincial legislatures is not listed in either Schedule 4 or Schedule 5, suggesting that provincial legislatures require specific national legislation or constitutional provisions for such powers ([3]).

Following the ruling in *Limpopo I*, attention was drawn to similar financial management legislations enacted by five other provinces: 

1. Financial Management of the Eastern Cape Provincial Legislature Act
2. Financial Management of the Free State Provincial Legislature Act
3. Financial Management of the Gauteng Provincial Legislature Act
4. Financial Management of the Mpumalanga Provincial Legislature Act
5. North West Provincial Legislature Management Act 

The Court noted that while none of the provincial legislatures initially responded to the invitation regarding the constitutionality of their financial management legislation, the Court maintained the authority to assess these statutes due to the overarching principle of constitutional supremacy ([5]).

The procedure culminated with the provincial Speakers and Members of the Executive Councils (MECs) being urged to submit responses regarding their statutes' constitutional validity and potential remedies ([7]–[8]).

## I – Issues

The key legal and constitutional issues to be resolved in this case include:

1. Are the financial management statutes enacted by the provinces unconstitutional?
2. If the North West Act extends beyond financial management, can its unconstitutional provisions be severed from the Act?
3. Should the Court suspend its order of invalidity concerning these statutes? ([7]).

## R – Rules

The relevant legal authorities that govern the issues at hand are found primarily within the South African Constitution, specifically section 104, which delineates the legislative authority of provincial legislatures. It provides that a provincial legislature can legislate on any matter listed in Schedules 4 or 5 or can be assigned powers through national legislation. The Financial Management of Parliament Act (FMPA) was also heavily referenced, as its provisions directly relate to the scope of the legislative competencies of the provincial legislatures ([2]–[4]).

The Court's ruling in *Coetzee v Government of the Republic of South Africa* ([23]) provided a crucial precedent regarding the test for severability, indicating that unconstitutional provisions may be severed if they can be extracted without undermining the statute's main objectives ([22]).

In addressing the remedy for unconstitutional statutes, section 172(1) of the Constitution informs the Court's discretion to declare any law that contradicts the Constitution as invalid ([20]).

## A – Application

Upon thorough deliberation, the Court found that, apart from the North West Act, the financial management statutes from the Eastern Cape, Free State, Gauteng, and Mpumalanga were unconstitutional ([16]–[17]). This determination was grounded in the similarities between these Acts and the Limpopo Bill, which had already been deemed unconstitutional in *Limpopo I*. The ruling emphasized that none of the parties disputed the unconstitutionality, simplifying the Court's task ([17]).

Conversely, the North West Act invited additional complexity due to its broader scope, addressing not just financial management but also internal legislative planning and administration ([19]–[20]). The Court examined whether the unconstitutional provisions could be severed without undermining the Act's overarching objectives, ultimately determining that such severance was impractical given the Act's dual focus ([26]).

On the issue of whether to suspend the declaration of invalidity, the Court weighed factors including potential disruptions to the functioning of provincial legislatures. Parties, including parliamentary representatives and the Gauteng MEC, advocated for an 18-month suspension to allow time for appropriate legislative fixes ([31]–[32]). The Minister opposed this, asserting that the PFMA would fill any legislative vacuum that might arise immediately. However, the Court acknowledged the legislative complexities and necessity for consultation and deliberation among parties to address these constitutional deficiencies adequately.

In arriving at a decision, the Court reiterated the need for a balance between interests in good governance and immediate constitutional remedies ([39]).

## C – Conclusion

The Court ultimately declared the Financial Management of the Eastern Cape, Free State, Gauteng, Mpumalanga, and the North West Acts unconstitutional and asserted that the declarations of invalidity would be suspended for 18 months from the date of judgment. This period would allow the provincial legislatures and Parliament to organize legislative solutions to rectify the defects ([49]).

The Court ordered the provincial legislatures to submit a report by September 9, 2013, detailing steps taken to remedy the identified constitutional issues ([49]). The ruling underscores the necessity for adherence to constitutional frameworks in provincial governance and sets an important precedent for future legislative actions relating to financial management in provincial contexts.
