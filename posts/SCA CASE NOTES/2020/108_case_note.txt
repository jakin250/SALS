### Case Note: Khan v Shaik (641/2019) [2020] ZASCA 108

#### FIRAC Method

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–3])**
The case of *Khan v Shaik* revolves around a dispute regarding the division of the fruits of a universal partnership between the appellant, Zorah Banoo Khan, and respondent, Salim Mohamed Shaik. The appellant initiated proceedings seeking a declaratory order to establish the existence of a universal partnership, which was claimed to have been dissolved when they parted ways. The court a quo dismissed her application, concluding that her claim had prescribed as it was instituted six years after the termination of the partnership and six years post the termination of their domestic consortium. The court’s dismissal was based primarily on the stipulations of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, sections 10(1) and 11(d), which dictate a three-year prescription period for such claims ([paras 1–4]).

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 4–6])**
The primary issues addressed by the court include:
1. What characterizes the rights of partners in a universal partnership?
2. Is a claim for the division of the fruits of a universal partnership a personal right or a real right?
3. Is the claim considered a debt under the Prescription Act?
4. How is the beginning of the prescription period determined in relation to claims in a universal partnership?
5. Is a court order required for the termination of a universal partnership? ([paras 5-6]).

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 6–8])**
The Supreme Court of Appeal relied on previous case law to clarify the legal nature of partnerships. In particular, the court referenced *Butters v Mncora*, which outlines the essentials of proving the existence of a universal partnership under the Roman-Dutch law framework. The distinction between real and personal rights was elucidated through *Absa Bank Limited v Keet*, indicating that a personal right typically entails a claim against a specific individual rather than a property right. Additionally, the Prescription Act serves as the governing statute, specifically outlining the periods of prescription regarding debts ([paras 6-10]).

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 9–30])**
1. **Character of Rights**: The court affirmed that a partnership involves a pooling of resources and responsibilities, thus granting partners personal rights to demand accounting and share profits. Therefore, the existence of the partnership must be proven based on active contributions and mutual intent, rather than a mere cohabitative relationship ([paras 9-12]).

2. **Personal vs Real Rights**: The court clarified that a claim for a partnership's profits is fundamentally personal, as it entails a request for accounting rather than ownership of specific assets. This distinction is crucial because it dictates the applicable frameworks under the Prescription Act ([paras 12-13]).

3. **Debt Under the Prescription Act**: The Supreme Court established that claims arising from partnerships fall into the category of debts under the Prescription Act. As such, the prescription period begins to run from the moment the claim becomes due, which is coincident with the termination of the partnership ([paras 14-16]).

4. **Determining Prescription Commencement**: The court noted that the prescription period for a partnership claim begins at the moment of termination of the partnership. In this case, the termination coincided with the cessation of the domestic consortium in 2009. With the claim initiated in 2015, the court concluded that the three-year prescription period had expired ([paras 17-21]).

5. **Court Order Requirement**: The argument that a court order is necessary for the termination of a partnership was dismissed. The law allows for dissolution via mutual consent or by the natural conclusion of dealings, as evidenced in the present case ([paras 29-30]).

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 31])**
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the lower court’s ruling, concluding that Khan's claim had indeed prescribed as it was initiated six years post-termination of the partnership, well beyond the three-year limitation imposed by the Prescription Act. The court emphasized that not only does the nature of a claim in a universal partnership hinge on personal rights, but the prescription triggers upon partnership dissolution. This decision has significant implications for similar future disputes regarding the cessation of partnerships and claims arising therein, asserting the necessity for timely action on claims following termination to ensure legal recourse ([paras 31]).

#### Order of the Court
The appeal was dismissed with costs, affirming the lower court's ruling ([Order]).
