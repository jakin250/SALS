Supreme Court of Appeal 1997

F – Facts

In The State v D B T Shabalala and S Sikhakhane, both appellants were convicted in the Witwatersrand Local Division for participation in a robbery and related crimes on December 17, 1992, at the Jabulani Civic Centre in Soweto, which resulted in the murder of Constable Malefetsane Rudolf Makate ([para 2]). Durante the incident, 18 Mossberg shotguns and 2 9mm pistols, along with ammunition, were stolen. Accused 1 (Shabalala) was found guilty of robbery with aggravating circumstances, unlawful possession of firearms, and ammunition. He received 20 years, 4 years, and 2 years for these counts respectively, with the latter two sentences running concurrently, resulting in a total of 24 years ([para 2]).

Accused 2 (Sikhakhane) was also convicted on all counts, receiving death sentences for the murder and robbery charges, with similar concurrent sentences for unlawful possession ([paras 3-4]). Shabalala's appeal against his sentences was limited to counts 2, 3, and 4, while Sikhakhane appealed his convictions and sentences for counts 1 and 2 under section 316A of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([para 3]).

The case was based primarily on witnesses’ testimony, including the girlfriend of Accused 1, who testified to the events leading up to the robbery, the presence of firearms in the accused’s room, and the subsequent actions of both accused ([paras 4-6]). Notably, Sikhakhane's written confession played a significant role, albeit contested due to allegations of police coercion ([paras 12-15]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the court included:

  1. Whether the evidence presented at trial was sufficient to support the convictions of both appellants on counts 1 and 2, considering the claims of duress and the admissibility of Sikhakhane's confession ([paras 6-9]).
  2. The appropriateness of the sentences imposed on both appellants, particularly the death sentences imposed on Sikhakhane, in light of evolving constitutional standards and justice practices following the establishment of the new constitution ([paras 26-27]).

R – Rules

The court relied on several statutory and case law principles:

  1. Section 316A of the Criminal Procedure Act, which governs the appellate procedure for criminal cases ([para 3]).
  2. The principles regarding the admissibility of confession statements, particularly the situations under which they could be deemed involuntary due to police misconduct ([para 10]).
  3. Constitutional provisions that came into force post-1994, notably regarding the unconstitutionality of the death penalty, as established in S v Makwanyane ([paras 26-27]).

A – Application

The court applied the law to the evidence before it. It scrutinized the testimonies provided by key witnesses, including Ngemane and various police officers, ultimately determining their credibility. Ngemane's evidence was deemed reliable and consistent with the events leading to the robbery ([para 22]).

The court recognized the confessional statement issued by Sikhakhane, evaluating the claims of coercion against detailed witness statements that countered those claims ([para 15-19]). Despite the assertions of duress, the court found that the evidence presented during the trial did not support the notion that the confession was compelled, leading to its admissibility and use as a key piece of evidence in corroborating co-accused involvement ([para 21]).

In regards to sentencing, while the accumulated evidence demonstrated Shabalala's involvement to some extent, the court found misdirection in viewing him as a co-conspirator in the original planning of the robbery, leading to a reduction of his sentence from 20 to 15 years for robbery ([para 25]). Conversely, the death sentences handed to Sikhakhane contravened new constitutional standards, requiring the court to set those aside and remit the case for re-sentencing ([para 27]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the convictions of both appellants on counts 2, 3, and 4, affirming the critical role of witness testimonies and confessions in the prosecution's case. However, it modified Shabalala's sentence, reducing his imprisonment to 15 years for robbery due to identification of judicial misdirection. The court unanimously found the death sentences imposed on Sikhakhane unconstitutional and ordered that he be resentenced on those counts ([paras 26-28]). This case underscores the significant advancements in South African criminal law reflecting changes in constitutional principles and the essential protection of individual rights against improper State conduct in judicial processes.
