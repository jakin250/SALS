# Case Note: Mulaudzi v The State (768/2015) [2016] ZASCA 70

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–2])
The appellant, Marcus Nndateni Mulaudzi, along with three co-accused, was charged in the Limpopo Local Division of the High Court, Thohoyandou, with three counts: (a) murder; (b) robbery with aggravating circumstances; and (c) attempted murder. On 22 August 2006, Mulaudzi was convicted of murder and robbery, while he was acquitted of the attempted murder charge. He received a life sentence for murder and a ten-year sentence for robbery ([para 1]).

Mulaudzi sought to appeal his conviction, a request initially refused by the High Court but later granted leave by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA). The appeal was heard on 5 May 2016 and resulted in a decision to uphold the appeal and set aside the convictions and related sentences, with reasons to be provided later ([para 2]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 1–2])
The principal issue in this appeal was whether the State proved beyond a reasonable doubt that the appellant was guilty of murder and robbery, given that the only material evidence against him came from an accomplice whose credibility was in serious doubt ([paras 1–2]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 11–12])
The court relied heavily on the established legal principles concerning the evidence of accomplices. The cautionary approach was underscored by referencing *S v Johannes* ([para 11]) and *R v Ncanana* ([para 12]), which highlight the particular scrutiny that must be applied to the evidence provided by accomplices. Specifically, the court emphasized that:

1. An accomplice's testimony carries inherent risks of falsehood and should be corroborated when it implicates a co-accused.
2. The evidence of a single witness, when that witness is an accomplice, must be clear and satisfactory in every material respect.

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 10–16])
The court identified several misdirections in the trial court's judgment. Foremost among these was the failure to consider all evidence presented in the trial, particularly the appellant's countervailing evidence against that of the first accused, who was found to be an accomplice and a single witness ([para 10]).

The SCA noted that the trial court did not adequately apply the cautionary approach towards the first accused's evidence despite its recognition of his untruthfulness in other respects. The court revisited the principles outlined in *S v Van der Meyden*, emphasizing that all evidence must be evaluated to reach a conclusion ([para 10]). Furthermore, the court found discrepancies in the first accused's narrative. His attempts to exculpate himself indicated a tendency to falsely implicate the appellant without credible support from other evidence ([paras 11–14]).

Ultimately, the SCA concluded that the first accused's testimony was inherently untrustworthy and was not sufficiently corroborated. As a result, the State had not met the requisite burden of proof; the merit of the appellant's defense was not outweighed by the compromised testimony of the accomplice ([para 16]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 16–17])
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, noting that the evidence garnered against Mulaudzi was insufficient to sustain a conviction for murder and robbery due to the unreliable nature of the sole incriminating witness, the first accused. The court found that the shortcomings in the witness's narrative materially detracted from its trustworthiness. Consequently, the court concluded that the State failed to establish guilt beyond a reasonable doubt, leading to Mulaudzi’s acquittal ([paras 16–17]).

This case reiterates the critical importance of evaluating accomplice evidence meticulously to avoid wrongful convictions, particularly in serious criminal cases. The ruling signals a robust commitment by the SCA to uphold principles of fair trial and the presumption of innocence, particularly when the integrity of evidence is compromised.
