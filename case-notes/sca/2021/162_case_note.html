Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 162 #### F – Facts The appellant, Edwin Hubert v

FIRAC Case Note: Van der Merwe v Bonnievale Piggery (Pty) Ltd [2021] ZASCA 162

F – Facts

The appellant, Edwin Hubert van der Merwe, is a former seller of slaughtered pig carcasses supplied by Bonnievale Piggery (Pty) Ltd, the respondent ([1]). The legal dispute originated when Bonnievale Piggery initiated a claim for R1,196,868.84, which represented the outstanding balance due for carcasses supplied to van der Merwe, alongside claims for interest and short payment ([1]).

Van der Merwe defended the claim by arguing that he was overcharged for these carcasses and asserted that Bonnievale Piggery had failed to comply with the National Credit Act 34 of 2005 by extending credit without being a registered credit provider ([2]). He also counterclaimed breach of contract and delict for unlawful competition, alleging the use of confidential information to undermine his business ([2]).

The matter was tried before Parker J in the Western Cape Division of the High Court, where the issues were separated as per Rule 33(4) of the Uniform Rules of Court for the adjudication of liability and damages ([3]). The trial court granted judgment in favour of Bonnievale Piggery, dismissing van der Merwe’s counterclaims with costs ([5]).

The full court later upheld Bonnievale Piggery’s appeal, concluding that the relationship between the parties ended in July 2012 when price negotiations broke down, invalidating van der Merwe’s counterclaims for breach of contract and unlawful competition ([7]).

I – Issues

The core legal issues in this case revolved around:

  1. Whether the parties had mutually agreed on a price for carcasses, which ultimately caused the termination of the contractual relationship.
  2. The validity of van der Merwe's counterclaims based on alleged breaches of contract and delictual claims for unlawful competition.
  3. Whether the trial judge misapplied the legal standards governing contractual obligations and unlawful competition ([21–22], [36–41]).

R – Rules

The court applied various legal principles relevant to contract law and unlawful competition, including:

  • The necessity for a consensus or agreement on price to form an enforceable contract, referencing Letaba Sawmills (Edms) Bpk v Majovi (Edms) Bpk [1993] 1 All SA 359 ([28]).
  • Requirements under the National Credit Act for credit providers ([2]).
  • The principles of wrongful interference derived from Schultz v Butt [1986] 2 All SA 403 ([41]).
  • The test for wrongfulness in competitive conduct as outlined in Phumelela Gaming and Leisure Limited v Gründlingh [2006] ZACC 6 ([42]).

A – Application

The court found that there was no enforceable agreement because the parties failed to reach a consensus on pricing, which was essential for the continuation of their business relationship ([21], [31]). Van der Merwe conceded that he had accepted the prices charged by Bonnievale Piggery in prior instances despite dissatisfaction, establishing a precedent of acceptance ([23]).

As to the counterclaims, the court assessed the merits of van der Merwe's argument concerning unlawful competition and the alleged misuse of confidential information. It determined that he failed to show wrongfulness, specifically that Bonnievale Piggery did not engage in conduct intended to undermine his business relationships. Evidence showed that van der Merwe's price claims relied on hearsay and lacked substantial proof of wrongdoing or a contractual breach ([44]).

The trial court’s determination of liability against Bonnievale Piggery was deemed flawed. The court emphasized that merely having access to confidential information does not automatically confer a contractual interest deserving protection ([34–35]). Additionally, the interrelationship of the agreements was not enough to demonstrate a clear breach as articulated by van der Merwe ([30], [39]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded by affirming the full court's judgment which dismissed van der Merwe's appeal and affirmed the decision of the trial court in favor of Bonnievale Piggery for the outstanding amount due for carcasses, plus costs ([45]). The court elucidated that the breakdown in negotiations was a significant factor in the termination of the contracts and that van der Merwe had not substantiated his counterclaims for breach of contract and unlawful competition ([7], [8]).

The broader implications entail a reaffirmation of contract law principles on the necessity for mutual agreement, particularly concerning terms that are essential to a contract’s enforceability. The case illustrates the judiciary's commitment to upholding the necessity for evidence-based claims in contractual disputes and emphasizes the importance of established legal standards governing competition and business practices in the marketplace.
