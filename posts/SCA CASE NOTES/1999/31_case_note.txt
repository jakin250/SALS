# Case Note: FIRAC Method

## Case Title: Tradax Ocean Transportation SA v mv “Silvergate” properly described as mv “Astyarnax” and Others

**Case No:** 107/97  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Judgment Delivered:** 28 May 1999  
**Coram:** Nienaber, Marais, Plewman, Streicher JJA, Farlam AJA

---

### F – Facts

In this case, the appellant, Tradax Ocean Transportation SA, had previously lodged an appeal which was dismissed on 24 May 1999. The central dispute arose from the failure of the lower court to award costs associated with the qualifying expenses for three expert witnesses: Mr Tasiopoulos, Mr Verhoeven, and Mr Nakazawa ([1]). The third respondent, Astyanax Compañia Naviera SA, cross-appealed against this omission, asserting a clear entitlement to these costs for the services rendered by the experts ([2]). The appellant did not contest this cross-appeal, indicating a level of concurrence with the claims made by the third respondent.

The procedural history indicates that there was significant reliance upon expert testimony in determining the issues before the court, emphasizing the importance of expert witnesses in maritime litigation. Their qualifying fees had not been addressed in the initial judgment, leading to the cross-appeal.

### I – Issues

The primary legal issue in this case was whether the third respondent was entitled to recover the costs associated with the qualifying expenses of its expert witnesses from the appellant, despite the oversight by the lower court ([1]–[2]). The matter raised questions about the recovery of litigation costs and the appropriate directions that should be given regarding expert evidence fees in the context of maritime law.

### R – Rules

The court relied on previous legal principles regarding the awarding of costs in litigation, particularly those relating to expert witness fees. The governing rule focuses on the entitlement of a party to recover costs directly associated with expert testimony when such testimony is deemed necessary for the case ([2]). The absence of such an order in the original judgment was viewed as an oversight rather than a conscious decision to deny those costs.

The rules governing costs in the context of expert witnesses are rooted in common law principles and are reiterated in case law that outlines the conditions under which such fees may be considered recoverable ([1], [2]).

### A – Application

In applying the law to the facts, the court noted that the judge in the court a quo had simply overlooked the third respondent's right to claim the qualifying expenses of expert witnesses, an issue on which the appellant did not oppose ([2]). The court concluded that the conclusions in the lower court's judgment should be varied to include an award for these expert witness expenses. The judges formulated an order which recognized the entitlement to recover such costs, emphasizing the necessity of fair legal proceedings in which parties could recover expenses directly incurred in securing requisite expert evidence ([3]).

The application of this ruling underscores the principle that oversight in judicial decisions can be rectified upon appeal, and that parties involved in complex litigation, such as maritime cases, should not suffer from procedural oversights that impede their entitlement to recoverable costs.

### C – Conclusion

The court allowed the cross-appeal, instructing that the original order be amended to include the qualifying fees and expenses for the three expert witnesses ([3]). This decision reaffirmed the importance of properly accounting for all costs in litigation, especially regarding expertise required to inform the court's decisions in complex matters. The significance of the ruling lies in its clarification that costs associated with expert witnesses are integral to the equitable resolution of disputes, and care must be taken to ensure that such costs are not overlooked.

The implications of this ruling extend to future maritime litigation cases, reinforcing the necessity for courts to be meticulous about including all recoverable costs in their orders, thereby ensuring litigants are fully compensated for the expenses they legitimately incur in the pursuit of their legal rights.
