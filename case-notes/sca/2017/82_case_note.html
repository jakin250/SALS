Case Note: Director of Public Prosecutions, Gauteng v Grobler [2017] ZASCA 82

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–8])

The case arises from an appeal by the Director of Public Prosecutions, Gauteng (the Appellant), challenging the decision of the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, which had determined the case against Morne Grobler (the Respondent) on a question of law pertaining to sentencing. Grobler was initially charged in the Regional Court with multiple counts, including three counts of rape, and various other offences related to child pornography and sexual grooming.

The complainant, a ten-year-old girl, resided with the Respondent (her stepfather) and her mother. The charges stemmed from multiple alleged incidents of sexual abuse occurring between September and November 2009, when the mother was absent. The Regional Court convicted Grobler on six of the seven counts, sentencing him to life imprisonment on the three counts of rape. However, the High Court subsequently reviewed these convictions and sentences. While it upheld the findings concerning the other charges, it reduced Grobler's rape convictions to sexual assault, significantly decreasing his sentence to ten years' imprisonment, five of which were suspended. This decision sparked the current appeal, with the Appellant asserting that the High Court wrongly considered whether the complainant had 'consented' to the acts as pertinent to sentencing, effectively contravening section 57(1) of the Sexual Offences Act.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1, 19-21])

The central legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the High Court erred in law by imputing consent from the conduct and perceived willingness of a child under the age of 12 regarding sexual acts, which, under section 57(1) of the Sexual Offences Act, she could not legally consent to. Furthermore, it addressed whether such imputed consent could validly inform the High Court's decision in mitigating Grobler's sentence.

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 12-13, 20-21])

The primary legal framework was provided by the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 and the Sexual Offences Act 32 of 1997. Section 57(1) of the Sexual Offences Act unequivocally states that individuals under the age of 12 cannot consent to sexual acts under any circumstances. This statutory provision underscores the principle that any semblance of consent derived from a minor's behavior or conduct cannot be utilized as a mitigating factor in the determination of a sentence for sexual offences against them.

The court also referenced prevailing jurisprudence regarding the seriousness of sexual offences, particularly where children are victims, emphasizing the necessity of strong disapproval and deterrence in sentencing to reflect the gravity of such acts. Significant case law, including S v Jansen and N v T, were highlighted to reinforce the view that sexual abuse of minors demands stringent sentences, irrespective of the offender's personal circumstances.

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 24-30])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the High Court erred in its legal reasoning by taking the alleged acquiescence of the complainant into account when considering an appropriate sentence. By doing so, it contravened the express provisions of section 57(1) of the Sexual Offences Act, which precludes any notion of consent from a victim under the age of 12, rendering the issue of the complainant’s supposed willingness irrelevant.

In determining the appeal, the court indicated that the High Court's consideration of the respondent's personal circumstances, including being a first-time offender and having spent 18 months in custody, had been overstated. The court stated that the High Court had adopted an incorrect legal principle in deciding sentence mitigation based on erroneous inferences about consent. Accordingly, it recognized this approach as a question of law and hence within the ambit of appeal under section 311 of the Criminal Procedure Act.

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 29-34])

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the appeal was upheld, and the decision of the High Court was set aside. The case was remitted back to the High Court for a proper reconsideration of the sentence in light of the correct legal principles articulated in the judgment. The Court emphasized that the issue of consent, particularly in relation to minor victims, must be handled with extreme caution, reinforcing the societal imperative to protect vulnerable individuals, namely children.

The ruling underscored the legal stance that any suggestion of consent from a child victim in sexual assault cases is inherently flawed and should not be permitted to influence judicial outcomes, thereby supporting the legislative framework aimed at safeguarding children from sexual abuse.

This case serves as a significant precedent reinforcing the strict non-consent provisions for minors and reiterates the justice system’s commitment to addressing sexual crimes against children with the appropriate severity, reflecting societal condemnation and fostering deterrence.
