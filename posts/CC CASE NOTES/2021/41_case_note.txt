Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# FIRAC Case Note: Union for Police Security and Corrections Organisation v South African Custodial Management (Pty) Ltd and Others [2021] ZACC 41

### F – Facts
The case before the Constitutional Court of South Africa involved the **Union for Police Security and Corrections Organisation** (the Applicant), a registered trade union, which challenged a judgment by the Thohoyandou Local Division of the High Court ([1]) that upheld two exceptions raised by the respondents: **South African Custodial Management (Pty) Limited**, **Kensani Corrections Management (Pty) Limited**, and **Johan Wagenaar**. 

The primary dispute arose from an alleged denial of access to the **Kutama Sinthumule Correctional Centre** (Kutama) for the purpose of exercising organizational rights as stipulated in a pre-existing rights agreement, which had been executed on **August 11, 2015**. Despite a notice to terminate this agreement given on **December 18, 2017**, the denial of access occurred within the agreed notice period ([5]).

Initially, the Applicant sought R2 million in constitutional damages for the alleged infringement of rights protected under sections **23(1)**, **23(2)**, and **17** of the Constitution ([6]). In later amendments to their particulars of claim, the Applicant modified their approach, seeking “appropriate relief” as envisaged in section **38** of the Constitution, without specifically claiming constitutional damages.

The second claim focused on alleged defamation, wherein the Applicant asserted that a written response from the respondents regarding the denied access conveyed false and harmful meanings about the union’s legitimacy and competence ([7], [8]). Additionally, the Applicant sought damages for emotional distress experienced by its members as a result of the access denial, amounting to **R108 million** ([9]).

The respondents raised three exceptions against the latest amended particulars of claim, of which the High Court upheld the first and third exceptions ([13]). Subsequent leave to appeal was denied by both the High Court and Supreme Court of Appeal, prompting the Applicant’s appeal to the Constitutional Court.

### I – Issues
The key legal issues presented were:
1. Whether the principle of **subsidiarity** precluded the Applicant from directly invoking constitutional rights in light of the Labour Relations Act, 66 of 1995 ([1]);
2. Whether the High Court erred in ruling on the defamation claim when it had become functus officio after granting a prior amendment ([3]);
3. The significance of constitutional interpretation regarding the alleged defamatory matter ([18]).

### R – Rules
Relevant legal principles include:
- **Section 23** of the Constitution stipulates the right to fair labour practices, including the right to form and join trade unions ([6]);
- **Section 38** allows individuals to approach courts for appropriate relief if their rights have been infringed ([6]);
- The principle of **subsidiarity**, as discussed in _My Vote Counts NPC v Speaker of the National Assembly_ [2015] ZACC 31, indicates that constitutional rights should initially be pursued through relevant legislation unless the legislation lacks adequacy ([1], [14]);
- The defamation standards as established in _Le Roux v Dey_ [2011] ZACC 4, where publications should be assessed through the constitutional lens, highlighting the interplay of dignity and expression ([18]).

### A – Application
In addressing the first issue, the Court examined the applicability of the principle of subsidiarity regarding claims grounded in the Labour Relations Act. The Applicant contended that since the Labour Relations Act did not comprehensively encapsulate  the issues at hand, the principle should not apply nor limit their constitutional invocation ([14], [15]). However, the Court concurred that alternative statutory avenues available for redress undermined the urgency of the constitutional claim, thus rejecting the Applicant’s argument.

On the defamation issue, the Court reiterated that the interpretation of allegedly defamatory statements should occur under a constitutional framework. Despite the Applicant’s assertions regarding the harmful meanings conveyed, the Court found the allegations insufficient to show how the constitutional lens would alter the evaluation of the intents and meanings of the statements ([19]). It ultimately determined that the lack of clarity in the claim failed to meet the required threshold, asserting that merely labeling a matter as constitutional does not engage jurisdiction ([21]).

Lastly, regarding the functus officio argument, the Court found that the prior judgment had not exhausted the issue at hand, allowing the High Court to still rule on matters regarding potential defamation ([17]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court, in a unanimous judgment led by **Madlanga J**, refused leave to appeal, concluding that the interests of justice did not favour the applicant due to their lack of reasonable prospects of success. The judgments against the Applicant regarding costs were set aside, with the parties ordered to bear their own costs across all levels ([23], [25]).

This case highlights the intricate balancing of constitutional rights against statutory frameworks and reaffirms the necessity for claimants to navigate appropriate legal avenues before turning to constitutional law. It underscores the vitality of procedural correctness and the need for practitioners to ensure robust, well-founded claims before invoking constitutional provisions. The implications of this ruling affect future labour relations disputes, particularly in how unions approach claims centered around constitutional rights and defamation in the context of labor practices.
