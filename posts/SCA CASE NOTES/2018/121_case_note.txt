Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: Bhola & others v The State [2018] ZASCA 121

## F – Facts
In the late afternoon of 25 February 2013, three male individuals, namely Aubrey Thamsanqa Bhola (first appellant), Nkosingiphile Patrick Mnthungwa (second appellant), and Lazarus Khoza (third appellant), attempted to rob a jewellery store in Piet Retief. Their efforts were thwarted when the wife of the store owner activated the alarm system, prompting them to flee the scene without any stolen goods ([1]).

The suspects were subsequently arrested the following day after police received details of their getaway vehicle. They were charged with attempted robbery with aggravating circumstances. The trial court convicted all the appellants and sentenced Mr Mnthungwa and Mr Khoza to 15 years' imprisonment each, while Mr Bhola, due to previous convictions, received a harsher 20-year sentence ([1], [2]).

The appellants unsuccessfully sought leave to appeal their convictions and sentences at the trial level but were granted leave to appeal their sentences by the Gauteng Division of the High Court. This appeal was dismissed, leading to subsequent petitions for special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([2], [3]).

During the trial, both the second and third appellants were represented by legal counsel, while the first appellant conducted his own defense. They all denied the allegations against them ([5]). The evidence presented indicated a coordinated effort involving a fourth unidentified female accomplice, who entered the store prior to the attempted robbery ([4]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issue in the case was whether the offence of attempted robbery with aggravating circumstances falls within the mandatory sentencing regime outlined in section 51(2) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 ([6], [7]). Specifically, the court needed to determine:
1. Whether the minimum sentences prescribed by section 51(2) were applicable to the crime committed by the appellants.
2. If not, whether the sentences imposed by the trial court were nonetheless appropriate given the circumstances of the crime and the personal circumstances of the appellants ([6], [8]).

## R – Rules
The relevant legislation, the *Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997*, specifically section 51(2), stipulates mandatory minimum sentences for certain crimes as enumerated in Part II of Schedule 2. The clear statutory language indicates that only offences explicitly included in that part are subject to prescribed minimum sentences, leaving out attempted robbery with aggravating circumstances, which is included in Part I of Schedule 2 only in relation to murders committed during such attempts ([6], [7]).

Judicial precedent, notably *S v Nkosi*, recognized that attempted robbery is not encompassed within the mandatory sentences outlined in the Minimum Sentences Act. The court in *Nkosi* indicated that lower courts have misapplied the Act when trying to impose minimum sentences for attempted robbery ([6], [9]).

## A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal examined whether the trial court misapplied section 51(2) by erroneously classifying the attempted robbery charge as one that attracted mandatory minimum sentencing. It concluded that the provisions of the Minimum Sentences Act did not apply to the offence of attempted robbery with aggravating circumstances, marking a significant misdirection by the trial court ([7], [10]). 

Despite the State's assertion that the serious nature of the crime justified the sentences initially imposed, the SCA argued that such conclusions were flawed due to the trial court's misapplication of the minimum sentencing regime. The court highlighted that although the crime involved the use of firearms and threats of violence, none of the individuals present suffered physical harm during the attempted robbery, thus the sentences required a fresh examination ([11], [12]).

The court considered the fact that all three appellants were first-time offenders, with the second and third appellants having significantly lesser roles in the plan to perpetrate the crime. Their personal circumstances were taken into account, especially noting their family obligations and time spent awaiting trial, which contributed to the decision to impose lighter sentences than originally mandated by the trial court ([13], [15]).

The first appellant's previous convictions were particularly noted, establishing a basis for a harsher penalty compared to his co-appellants. The court stated that his significant prior criminal history justified a longer sentence but ultimately found that the trial court's initial imposition exceeded the Regional Court's jurisdiction given the absence of applicable minimum sentences ([14], [10]).

## C – Conclusion
In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal against the sentences imposed by the trial court, significantly reducing their lengths to 13 years for Mr Bhola and 8 years each for Mr Mnthungwa and Mr Khoza. The sentences were also antedated to reflect the period already served since their arrest on 7 April 2014 ([16]). 

The case underscored the necessity for trial courts to accurately interpret statutory frameworks and the importance of proportionality in sentencing, especially regarding offences where actual injury was not inflicted. The ruling not only corrected a misapplication of the law but also reinforced principles of fairness and justice in the South African legal system.
