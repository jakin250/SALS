# Dexion Europe Limited v Universal Storage Systems (Proprietary) Limited  

**Case No:** 500/2000  
**Judgment Delivered:** 6 September 2002  
**Coram:** Harms, Schutz, Scott, Cameron JJA, Heher AJA  

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts

In this copyright infringement case, the appellant, Dexion Europe Limited ("Dexion"), is the copyright owner of four technical drawings associated with its Speedlock racking system. This racking system consists of various interconnected components designed for the efficient storage of goods ([1]).

The respondent, Universal Storage Systems ("Universal"), was initially a sub-licensee of Dexion's licensee, Brazier, until the termination of that sub-licence in 1993. Upon being denied a direct license from Dexion, Universal sought to create its own racking system that could compete with Dexion's product. Following legal advice, Universal attempted to design an interchangeable racking system known as the "Unirack" system by obtaining new tools and returning the previous Dexion tools ([2]).

Dexion did not contest Universal's attempts to produce competitive systems but rather alleged infringement concerning the indirect use of its technical drawings through tooling derived from Dexion’s design ([4]). Dexion sought to establish both direct and indirect infringement, although it later conceded the lack of evidence for direct copying. Thus, the matter pivoted on whether Universal engaged in indirect copying of Dexion’s drawings ([7]).

### I – Issues

The legal questions at stake in this matter include:

1. Whether Universal indirectly copied Dexion's copyright-protected technical drawings, utilizing tools derived from those drawings, thereby infringing Dexion's copyright.
   
2. Whether the exception provided in the Copyright Act 98 of 1978 section 15(3A)(a) applies to Universal's actions in developing its Unirack system, given that the original designs were available for reproduction under certain circumstances ([3]).

3. The contractual obligations arising from the sub-licence agreement between Dexion and Universal, particularly in light of Dexion's claims that it had enforceable rights against Universal due to breach upon termination ([16]).

### R – Rules

The core legal frameworks invoked in this case include:

- The Copyright Act 98 of 1978, particularly sections 2(1)(e), regarding copyright in artistic works, and section 15(3A)(a), detailing the conditions under which copyright may not be infringed for three-dimensional reproductions ([3]).
  
- Principles of copyright infringement as established in Galago Publishers (Pty) Ltd v Erasmus 1989 (1) SA 276 (A), necessitating a dual inquiry: demonstrating objective similarity between the works and a causal connection to establish copying ([4]).

- The jurisprudential guideline from previous cases on indirect copying, including the need for circumstantial evidence when direct evidence is unavailable ([10]).

### A – Application

The court's analysis began with the assessment of evidence regarding the causal connection relating to copying. Dexion attempted to assert that Universal had indirectly copied its designs by using tooling that replicated its technical drawings. However, Dexion's direct infringement claim was compromised as it voluntarily conceded the lack of evidence supporting it ([8]).

To substantiate the claim of indirect copying, Dexion argued that Universal’s use of tooling derived from its designs constituted infringement. Yet, Universal produced counter-evidence indicating its tools were independently designed, leveraging standard practices in engineering without reliance on Dexion’s drawings ([14]).

Regarding the footplate, the court held that Dexion failed to establish that Universal had copied this element from an unauthorized source. Notably, evidence indicated that Universal had indeed returned to using its own tools for production, which did not significantly mirror Dexion's copyrighted designs ([9]). The court also noted that essential features like the bracing and hooks were made from generic materials and practices, with the similarities often attributable to commonplace engineering elements rather than Dexion's copyrighted designs ([11]-[12]). 

Moreover, the legal threshold for establishing copyright infringement necessitated that Dexion demonstrate more than circumstantial evidence of access to its designs; rather, it required clear proof of copying, which was absent in this case ([10]).

Regarding Dexion's contractual claim, the court found that the sub-licence's terms did not extend protection to Dexion without explicit acceptance of the contractual benefits, akin to the distinction between a landlord and a sub-tenant, thus affirming the previous ruling [16-17].

### C – Conclusion

The court ultimately dismissed Dexion's appeal, affirming that Universal had not infringed upon the copyright of the technical drawings through indirect copying. The reasoning centered on the understanding that tools do not independently breach copyright protection as they do not constitute a reproduction of drawings, but rather an intermediate step in the manufacturing process ([18]). Furthermore, Dexion's contractual claims could not be sustained due to the lack of a direct legal connection with Universal, reinforcing the necessity of clarity and enforceability within contractual arrangements.

This judgment results in significant implications for the applicability of copyright protections in cases involving indirect copying and elaborates on the limitations of technical drawings as subject to reproductions in competitive markets. Such clarity is essential for entities in technology and design sectors to navigate copyright law effectively while pursuing innovation and market competition.
