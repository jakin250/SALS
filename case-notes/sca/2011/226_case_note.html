Supreme Court of Appeal

Eye of Africa Developments (Pty) Ltd v Nicola Carolyn Shear ([2011] ZASCA 226)

Initially, the Appellant sought environmental authorization from the Gauteng Department of Agriculture, Conservation and Environment (GDACE) to develop a mixed-use estate, which included a golf course that would use grey water from a sewage facility for irrigation ([6]–[9]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2011 [2011] ZASCA 226)

F – Facts

The case pertains to a dispute between Eye of Africa Developments (Pty) Ltd (the Appellant) and Nicola Carolyn Shear (the Respondent) concerning the amendment of an environmental authorization granted for the development of a golf estate. The Respondent, owner of Shearwood Farm, relied entirely on water from a borehole located on her property, which was essential for her family's and animals' sustenance ([1]–[5]).

Initially, the Appellant sought environmental authorization from the Gauteng Department of Agriculture, Conservation and Environment (GDACE) to develop a mixed-use estate, which included a golf course that would use grey water from a sewage facility for irrigation ([6]–[9]). Although the Appellant’s original application was refused, it later received authorization on appeal, subject to conditions aimed at ensuring that no alternative water sources were used for irrigation ([10]–[12]).

Due to delays in development and insufficient grey water production, the Appellant requested permission to temporarily use alternative water sources, such as boreholes, leading to a letter from Dr. Cornelius at the GDACE on 25 July 2008, which purported to amend the initial authorization. The Respondent contested this amendment, claiming it was unlawful and detrimental to her rights, as it would jeopardize her sole source of water ([13]–[16]).

The High Court reviewed the GDACE decision to allow this amendment and upheld the Respondent's position, leading to the Appellant’s appeal ([1]–[2]).

I – Issues

  1. Was the amendment to the environmental authorization validly executed under applicable environmental laws?
  2. Did Dr. Cornelius possess the authority to amend the Record of Decision (ROD) as claimed?
  3. Does the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA) require the Respondent to exhaust internal remedies before approaching the court?
  4. What is the appropriate legal remedy for the Respondent, considering the validity of the amendment? ([19]–[28]).

R – Rules

The court primarily evaluated the amendment process described in the regulations under the National Environmental Management Act 107 of 1998 (NEMA), specifically Regulations 40, 41, 44, and 45, which outline the procedures for amending environmental authorizations. These regulations stipulate that an amendment initiated by the competent authority requires public notification and an opportunity for representation ([20]–[25]).

Furthermore, the definition of "administrative action" as set out in PAJA §1 was examined to determine whether the letter issued by Dr. Cornelius constituted an exercise of public power. Under PAJA, only decisions taken by state organs or entities exercising public functions that adversely affect individuals could be reviewed ([27]–[28]).

Additionally, the principles established in relevant precedents such as Kimberley Junior School v Head, Northern Cape Education Department and Ferndale Crossroads Share Block (Pty) Ltd v Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality informed the court's understanding of the necessity for jurisdictional facts in valid administrative actions ([26]).

A – Application

The court found that Dr. Cornelius did not have the proper authority to amend the ROD as the processes outlined in NEMA had not been followed. There was no formal application as required by Regulation 41, and the necessary public participation or notifications to affected parties were absent, rendering the purported amendment invalid ab initio ([21]–[25]).

Moreover, the court determined that despite all parties operating under the assumption that the 25 July 2008 letter constituted a valid amendment, it was essentially an internal communication rather than a formal administrative decision that had external legal effects. Therefore, it could not meet the necessary threshold set by PAJA, and the Respondent was, consequently, not required to exhaust internal remedies before bringing the case to court ([28]).

Regarding the appropriate relief, the court acknowledged that the Respondent had a direct interest in the determination of the amendment's validity due to its potential impact on her water source. A review application was deemed unsuitable as the amendment did not constitute valid administrative action, justifying the Respondent's action for a declaratory order instead of a review ([29]–[31]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the Appellant's appeal and upheld the High Court's ruling. The purported amendment of the environmental authorization was declared invalid, resulting in the order that the Appellant and GDACE jointly bear costs of the application ([36]).

This decision underscores the critical importance of adhering to statutory procedures for amending environmental authorizations, illustrating how failure to comply can result in significant legal ramifications. The ruling also clarifies the application of PAJA concerning what constitutes administrative action, reinforcing the necessity for proper authorization in environmental governance to protect the rights of affected parties like the Respondent in this case.
