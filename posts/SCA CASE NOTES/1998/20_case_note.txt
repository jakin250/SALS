# Case Note: REPUBLIEK VAN SUID-AFRIKA
**In die Hooggeregshof van Appèl van Suid-Afrika**  
**Saaknommer: 103/96**  
**Hof: Smalberger, Howie, Olivier, Schutz A R R en Farlam Wnd A R**  
**Verhoordatum: 16 Maart 1998**  
**Uitspraak gelewer: 25 Maart 1998**

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
[2] On 4 April 1995, a written agreement was made between the Member of the Executive Council for Land Affairs, Housing, and Local Government of the then Transvaal Province (now Limpopo Province) and the Member of the Executive Council for Local Government of the then Eastern Transvaal Province (now Mpumalanga). This agreement aimed to establish a transitional council known as the Groblersdal/Motetema and Tafelkop Transitional Council, which included Groblersdal, Motetema, the residential area of Tafelkop, and rural areas Moutse 2 and 3 as the constitutive areas of the proposed transitional council.

[3] Groblersdal and Motetema were urban areas under the control of local government bodies, including the present respondent. Groblersdal is situated in Mpumalanga, while Motetema falls under Limpopo Province. The rural areas Moutse 2 and 3, also located in Mpumalanga, were managed by the Regional Service Council of Hoëveld. Although Tafelkop incorporates a residential region, it primarily constitutes a rural area situated in Limpopo and was administered by the Premier of Limpopo.

[4] The respondent was dissatisfied with the establishment of the transitional council, expressing willingness to tolerate the inclusion of Motetema but objecting to the inclusion of Moutse 2 and 3 as well as Tafelkop. On 15 May 1995, the respondent urgently approached the then Provincial Division of the High Court of South Africa for an interim interdict. Both appellants and several other respondents were summoned, leading to an order nisi from the court, compelling the respondents to show cause why the agreement should not be proclaimed under the provisions of the Local Government Transition Act 209 of 1993 ("the Act").

[5] The order was subsequently reinforced by the High Court (per Van Dyk R), with specific leave granted for appeal to the present court on two grounds: the interpretation and scope of section 10(3)(a) of the Act, as applicable to the factual situation before the lower court and whether the principle of *audi alteram partem* was properly applied concerning the prevailing circumstances.

### I – Issues
[6] The key legal issues pertain to:
1. The interpretation of section 10(3)(a) of the Local Government Transition Act in its application to the factual matrix that was presented before the lower court.
2. Whether the principle of *audi alteram partem* was correctly observed in the underlying factual circumstances of the case.

[7] A secondary concern raised during the appeal was whether the appeal was fundamentally about costs, as both parties were queried regarding any practical outcomes of the court's ruling beyond this aspect.

### R – Rules
[8] The relevant rule in question includes section 21 A of the High Court Act 59 of 1959 (as amended by section 7 of the Judicial Matters Amendment Act 104 of 1996), which stipulates conditions for dismissing an appeal when the dispute lacks practical significance. This article outlines a clear framework requiring courts to consider whether an appeal will yield tangible effects or merely address theoretical or hypothetical issues.

[9] Furthermore, the *audi alteram partem* rule emphasizes the right to a fair hearing, obliging courts to ensure that all parties have an opportunity to present their case adequately.

### A – Application
[10] The court analyzed the implications of the Local Government Transition Act, particularly section 21 A's intent to alleviate the burden on appellate courts by excluding appeals lacking practical relevance.

[11] The court deduced that on the date for ratification of the order nisi, the substantive dispute was no longer alive between the parties. It noted that the appellants were no longer pursuing the proclamation of the agreement, acknowledging that it had already lapsed.

[12] A significant factor considered was the explicit withdrawal of intentions to proclaim the disputed agreement and that the thirty-day period for such proclamations per section 7 A had expired. Consequently, any potential harm concerning the transitional council's formation had dissipated.

[13] The court articulated that any remaining disagreements regarding Tafelkop's status were irrelevant to the proceedings in light of the current administrative transfer of Groblersdal to Limpopo Province, which rendered the current appeal speculative.

[14] The arguments presented suggested that a decision on the merit of the appeal would not establish any binding precedent, as the court a quo had not addressed Tafelkop's qualifications as a legitimate "local authority" under the comprehensive definition of the Act.

[15] The conclusion emphasized that neither party presented sufficient evidence that a decision in the current case would yield any practical effect, reiterating that the matter was primarily speculative.

### C – Conclusion
[16] The court concluded by dismissing the appeal on grounds that it lacked any practical outcome or relevance as stipulated under section 21 A of the High Court Act. The judicial panel articulated that the initial order nisi had lost any substantive significance due to the lapsing of the agreement and changes in the administrative allocations of areas involved.

[17] In considering the costs, the court exercised discretion, determining that both parties bore some responsibility for prolonging the appeal without tangible merit and therefore ruled that each party should bear their own costs.

[18] The implications of this ruling signify the necessity for litigants to ensure the ongoing relevance of their matters before the courts and to consider the practical outcomes lest they engage in protracted, speculative litigation that offers no real resolution. The case profoundly illustrates the application of statutory provisions aimed at maintaining judicial efficiency and discouraging appeals devoid of substantive issues.
