Case Note: REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2002

### FIRAC Case Note: **REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA v HARRY JAMES JOSHUA** ([2002] ZASCA 14)

#### F – Facts:
The appellant, Harry James Joshua, was tried in the Cape Provincial Division on multiple counts, including three counts of murder and two counts of attempted murder. The events leading to the charges began on 23 May 1994, when Joshua's wife was robbed at knifepoint in Delft, Cape Town. After learning of the robbery from his wife, the appellant decided to confront the suspected robbers. Armed with a shotgun, he, accompanied by a neighbor, proceeded to a nearby sports ground where he encountered a group of youths, including Marlin Mohammed, who had allegedly participated in the robbery.

During the confrontation, Joshua demanded the return of his wife’s purse. This led to the shooting of Marlin and two other youths, Fabian Rossouw and Mervyn du Plessis, all aged 17. Joshua later pursued another youth, Ivan Mootjie, firing upon him as well. While the trial court acquitted him on the murder charge related to Marlin, he was convicted for the murders of Fabian and Mervyn, as well as the attempted murder of Mootjie. Joshua received a cumulative sentence of 15 years’ imprisonment, leading to his appeal against both the convictions and the sentences imposed ([1]–[2]).

#### I – Issues:
The primary legal issues in this case revolve around the following:
1. Whether the appellant had a justified claim for self-defense during the shootings of Fabian and Mervyn, as dictated by his honest perception of an imminent threat ([18]).
2. The appropriateness of the trial court's classification of the appellant's conduct leading to the convictions, and whether it constituted murder or culpable homicide, especially regarding the events surrounding the alleged imminent threats ([29]–[33]).

#### R – Rules:
The court applied established principles regarding **mens rea** and the test for **putative private defence**, referencing **S v De Oliveira** which clarified that in cases of putative private defence, the scruples of unlawful conduct do not negate culpability. It also noted that if the accused held an erroneous belief regarding a threat, this could negate the intent to kill (dolus), leading only to culpable homicide ([29]).

Further, the court highlighted that the determination of self-defense requires an objective assessment of whether a reasonable person in the accused's position would have acted similarly ([29]).

#### A – Application:
In applying the aforementioned rules to Joshua’s case, the court meticulously examined the facts surrounding each shooting. The findings regarding the distances from which the youths were shot suggested they were not posing an immediate threat at the time of their deaths ([20]–[28]).

- **For Fabian and Mervyn**: The court established that by the time Joshua shot these individuals, they were likely fleeing or turning away, indicating that the exigency for self-defense no longer existed. Thus, the convictions of murder were adjudged inappropriate when the evidence suggested they were not actively trying to harm Joshua ([30]). The court concluded that his belief, albeit sincere, was not aligned with an objective fact that justified lethal response ([30]).

- **For Ivan Mootjie**, the court determined that his injury arose under similar circumstances where Joshua lacked the requisite intent to justify a murder charge, concluding that, at worst, the appellant could be guilty of culpable homicide due to his belief of imminent danger being unfounded ([32]).

Based on the overall evidence, the trial court misapplied the legal standards regarding self-defense and the corresponding intent (dolus) leading to murder convictions, particularly through the lens of the sequences of events that transpired ([18], [29]).

#### C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal determined that the appellant's belief that his life was in danger was erroneous and therefore excluded the requirement of dolus. Consequently, the convictions for murder related to the deaths of Fabian and Mervyn were altered to culpable homicide, and the attempted murder charge regarding Ivan Mootjie was dismissed entirely. 

The resultant sentences were recalibrated to 5 years’ imprisonment for each of the culpable homicide counts, successfully appealing against the initial dual murder convictions, although the appellant remained sentenced to an effective term of 15 years for the other convictions ([34]–[36]).

This ruling is significant as it underscores the importance of the context in assessing claims of self-defense, particularly when evaluating an accused's belief in imminent threat against objective realities, reiterating the principles governing mens rea within the spectrum of self-defense in South African law.
