Source Folder: 2005

# Case Note: Greys Marine Hout Bay (Pty) Ltd & Others v Minister of Public Works & Others [2005] ZASCA 87

## FIRAC Structure

### F – Facts
The dispute arose in Hout Bay, where the quayside is a vital area for fishing and related commercial activities. The appellants—Greys Marine Hout Bay (Pty) Ltd, Hout Bay Yacht Club, and C-Craft Close Corporation—occupy adjacent premises used for fishing and boat repair ([1]–[2]). The state owns the quayside, with leasing authority vested in the Minister of Public Works ([3]). 

In October 2001, the Minister granted a lease for a previously undeveloped portion adjacent to the appellants' properties to Bluefin Holdings (Pty) Ltd—established to support historically excluded fishermen—to construct a fish-processing facility and restaurant ([4]–[5]). The appellants claimed this development would disrupt their operations and impede access ([6]). 

They sought to challenge the Minister's decision in the Cape High Court but were unsuccessful, prompting their appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([6]–[7]).

### I – Issues
1. **Did the Minister's decision to grant the lease to Bluefin constitute administrative action under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)?** 
2. **Did the Minister's decision adversely affect the rights or legitimate expectations of the appellants, thereby requiring procedural fairness?**
3. **Was the Minister's decision arbitrary or irrational as per Section 6 of PAJA?**

### R – Rules
**Legal Principles:**
- **Definition of Administrative Action**: Under PAJA, administrative actions are decisions of an administrative nature made by an organ of state that adversely affect rights and have direct legal effects ([21], [22]).
- **Rights and Legitimate Expectations**: PAJA protects rights that are materially and adversely affected by administrative actions ([29]). The determination of whether rights exist hinges on whether actions violate concrete rights or legitimate expectations grounded in established practices ([30]–[32]).
- **Rationality and Arbitrariness**: A decision must be rational, meaning it should be based on the reasons provided. Judicial scrutiny under PAJA includes examining the logical basis upon which decisions are made ([34]).

### A – Application
The Court, led by Nugent JA, examined the designation of the Minister’s decision as administrative action. The Court concluded that the decision to lease state land does indeed fall under administrative action, as the Minister exercised a public power that had legal consequences for Bluefin ([26]–[28]). 

However, the Court found that the appellants did not hold any proprietary rights in the leased property that would warrant claims of adverse effects. They did not have a legitimate expectation to continue using Lot 86 without interference or consultation, as the state had previously refused to guarantee that the land would remain undeveloped ([30]–[33]).

Regarding claims of irrationality, the Court highlighted that the Minister's decision was informed and not arbitrary. The Minister had considered the broader implications of the development and was not swayed merely by opposing views from Environmental Affairs. Moreover, the Minister had a legitimate interest in diversifying opportunities for historically disadvantaged communities, aligning with governmental policy ([34]–[35]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming that the Minister's decision constituted lawful administrative action under PAJA. The appellants failed to establish that their rights were adversely affected, nor could they demonstrate a legitimate expectation regarding consultation or use of the property ([36]).

### Implications
This case underscores the importance of demonstrating concrete rights or expectations when challenging administrative decisions related to state property. It illustrates the court's commitment to balancing governmental objectives with the rights of private parties and affirms the broader interpretative approach regarding administrative action as established in South African jurisprudence. The ruling reinforces the principle that the exercise of public power must still adhere to constitutional norms of legality, rationality, and procedural fairness but does not grant property rights beyond what is expressly conferred.
