FIRAC Case Note: Buffalo City Metropolitan Municipality v Asla Construction (Pty) Ltd [2019] ZACC 15

Constitutional Court 2019 [2019] ZACC 15 #### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Proce

FIRAC Case Note: Buffalo City Metropolitan Municipality v Asla Construction (Pty) Ltd [2019] ZACC 15

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–11])

The Buffalo City Metropolitan Municipality (the "Municipality") sought a review of its own decision to award a contract for the development of housing units in the Greater Duncan Village area to Asla Construction (Pty) Ltd (the "Respondent"). This decision was made following a public tender process initiated on 5 November 2013, whereby the Municipality aimed to appoint a contractor under a turnkey agreement to construct between 3,000 and 5,000 housing units.

A series of unsuccessful tenders led the Municipality to engage the Respondent based on a new contract related to the Reeston Phase 3 project, which was intertwined with their previous contracts initiated under the Turnkey contract. However, by August 2015, issues had arisen concerning the legality of the awarded Reeston contract, specifically that it had not undergone the necessary competitive procurement procedures mandated by section 217 of the South African Constitution. Despite recognizing potential irregularities, the Municipality asserted it was unaware of the illegality until an investigation prompted by the appointment of a new Chief Financial Officer.

The Municipality filed for judicial review in the High Court, which found that the Reeston contract was unlawfully awarded, but later the Supreme Court of Appeal ruled that the Municipality had not sufficiently justified its delay in bringing the review application, thus rendering the issues of merit unaddressed. The Municipality subsequently sought leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 12–16])

The primary legal questions arose from the following issues:

  1. Did the Municipality unreasonably delay the initiation of the review application in contravention of administrative justice principles?
  2. Should any delay incurred be overlooked in light of the violation of section 217 of the Constitution governing public procurement?
  3. What impact does the Gijima precedent have on self-review applications by state organs invoking the legality principle instead of PAJA?

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 17–38])

The Court referenced critical constitutional principles, specifically:

  • Section 217 of the Constitution, which mandates that procurement processes be fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective.
  • Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA), detailing the requirements for administrative actions.
  • The judgement in Gijima Holdings (Pty) Ltd v State Information Technology Agency SOC Limited [2017] ZACC 40, which established the precedent that organs of state must pursue self-review applications through the legality principle rather than PAJA.

The ruling from the High Court (Buffalo City Metropolitan Municipality v Asla Construction [2016] ZAECGHC 55) was also significant in the appellate context, highlighting issues of unlawful conduct in public procurement decisions.

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 39–107])

In its majority judgment, the Constitutional Court explored the complexity surrounding the Municipality’s claims of unlawfulness due to the failure to pursue a proper procurement process. The majority analyzed whether the delay in initiating the review was unreasonable and should be overlooked.

Theron J pointed out that while there was substantial delay, it could be explored whether it should affect the Municipality's claims of illegality. The Court drew upon established jurisprudence from Gijima, suggesting that despite the delay, the Court may have a constitutional duty to declare unlawful actions invalid under section 172(1)(a) of the Constitution. The majority found that the Reeston contract was indeed unlawful, being devoid of requisite competitive bidding, thus mandating a declaration of its invalidity.

The judgment also detailed the procedural shortcomings in the Municipality’s conduct and shed light on their failure to provide adequate explanations for the delay. The Court juxtaposed the conduct of municipal officials against the requirements of responsible public governance.

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 108–107])

The Constitutional Court held that the Municipality's decision to award the Reeston contract was constitutionally invalid due to non-compliance with procurement processes as dictated by section 217 of the Constitution. The appeal was upheld, reversing the Supreme Court of Appeal's finding regarding delay. The judgment emphasized that the unlawfulness had to be addressed regardless of the procedural shortcomings that emerged through the delay.

Furthermore, the judgment underscored that public entities carry a higher responsibility to adhere to the law, asserting that actions undermining these procedures could lead to institutional dysfunction and malfeasance. Importantly, while the Court recognized the existence of a lengthy delay, it also upheld the principle of legality, confirming that a court is required to evaluate unlawful conduct, thus reaffirming the necessity for organs of state to act within the constitutional framework even amidst procedural missteps.

Both Cameron J and Froneman J concurred with Theron J on various aspects but suggested the need to approach the matter with caution, pointing out that undue delay should generally preclude judicial intervention unless justified.

References

  • Buffalo City Metropolitan Municipality v Asla Construction (Pty) Ltd [2019] ZACC 15.
  • State Information Technology Agency SOC Limited v Gijima Holdings (Pty) Limited [2017] ZACC 40.
  • Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000.
  • Section 217 of the South African Constitution.

This case highlights the complexities of legal procedural requirements in public procurement and the responses of state organs to allegations of unlawfulness in administrative actions. The implications extend beyond individual contracts, emphasizing the elevated duties public officers hold in maintaining the integrity of governmental processes.
