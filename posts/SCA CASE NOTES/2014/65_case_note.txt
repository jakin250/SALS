Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: Road Accident Fund v Faria (567/13) [2014] ZASCA 65

## F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–4])

In this case, the appellant, the Road Accident Fund (RAF), appealed against the order of the South Gauteng High Court, which had awarded the respondent, Fonesca Rui Fernando Faria, general damages of R350,000. The respondent was a bicycle rider who sustained serious injuries in a collision with a motor vehicle on January 26, 2011, driven by Ms. J.M. Tladi. The RAF was liable to compensate the plaintiff under the provisions of the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996, as amended ([para 3]).

The injuries sustained by Faria included a head injury that rendered him comatose for four and a half days, multiple rib fractures, and extensive abrasions. Initially, Faria claimed R850,000 in damages for these injuries. The RAF disputed both the merits and quantification of the claim. However, it had no witnesses to contest the plaintiff's version, leading the high court to find the RAF liable for 100% of the actual damages, less an agreed amount for past medical expenses ([paras 5–6]).

The case later focused on the claim for general damages related to the serious injury assessment, which required assessment by medical practitioners in accordance with regulations established under the amended Act. There was conflicting medical evidence regarding whether Faria's injuries met the 'serious injury' threshold defined by the Road Accident Fund Regulations ([paras 7–11]).

## I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 17–20])

The central issue of the appeal was whether the high court had the legal authority to award general damages based on the medical assessments provided, particularly in light of the RAF's objection to its own expert’s evaluation of Faria's injuries ([paras 18–20]). 

The case raised important legal questions about:
1. The authority of the RAF to reject its own expert’s findings regarding a serious injury ([para 20]).
2. Whether the proper procedures stipulated in the regulations are binding, given the conflicting assessments from the medical practitioners ([paras 18–20]).

These questions were seen as crucial given the ongoing legal ambiguities surrounding the grant of general damages post-2008 amendments to the legislation governing the RAF.

## R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 14, 26–34])

The appeal primarily relied on the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996, as amended, and the accompanying regulations, which define the process for establishing claims for general damages ([paras 26–30]). Key provisions included:

- **Section 17(1)** stipulating that compensation for non-pecuniary loss can only be paid for ‘serious injuries’ as defined in Section 17(1A) ([para 26]).
- Regulations that require proper serious injury assessments to be submitted by medical practitioners and outline the RAF's options for disputes over these assessments ([paras 27–30]).

Precedent was also set by previous courts in notable cases like *Road Accident Fund v Duma* and *Road Accident Fund v Lebeko*, where it was established that the RAF is not bound by its experts' conclusions concerning the seriousness of injuries and can reject assessments it does not agree with ([paras 14, 33–34]).

## A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 35–36])

In examining the facts, the appellate court determined that the high court had erred in its assessment of the RAF’s obligations. The high court had essentially dismissed the regulatory framework that requires the RAF to be satisfied regarding the seriousness of an injury in order to permit an award for general damages. The trial judge had used a joint minute prepared by experts that was not in compliance with the procedural requirements set out by the regulations ([para 36]).

The appellate court emphasized the legislative shift which made the assessment of serious injuries an administrative rather than judicial determination ([para 34]). The RAF’s authority to call for further assessments or reject a serious injury report was underscored, thereby reinforcing that the RAF could not be compelled to pay damages merely on the basis of conflicting opinions from medical professionals.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 37])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the RAF's appeal, finding that the high court had overstepped by awarding general damages contrary to established regulations. The decision reaffirmed the importance of the administrative role of the RAF in determining claims for serious injury, a criterion the legislature intended to prioritize over judicial discretion ([para 37]). 

The ruling has significant implications for future claims against the RAF, emphasizing that plaintiffs must adhere strictly to procedural regulations regarding serious injury assessments. This case sets a precedent that reinforces the autonomy of the RAF in evaluating claims and the necessity for thorough compliance with legislative frameworks to ensure claims for general damages can be reasonably entertained.

In light of this, the court ordered that the high court’s decision to award R350,000 in general damages be set aside, solidifying the administrative control of the RAF in providing such compensations within defined legal parameters ([para 37]).

This case note provides a detailed analysis of the decision in *Road Accident Fund v Faria*, recognizing its significance within the framework of South African tort law and offering insights into the evolving landscape of injury compensation under legislative reforms.
