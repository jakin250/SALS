# FIRAC Case Note: Rohde v The State (1007/2019) [2019] ZASCA 193

## F – Facts

The case of **Rohde v The State** revolves around **Jason Thomas Rohde**, the appellant, who was convicted of the murder of his wife and of obstructing the administration of justice on **16 April 2019**. Following his conviction, which resulted in a 20-year sentence, the appellant sought bail pending his appeal against the conviction. The initial application for bail was denied by the Western Cape Division of the High Court under Judge Salie-Hlophe, primarily due to concerns that the appellant was a flight risk, as well as the serious nature of the crime for which he was convicted ([paras 1–2]).

After being denied bail, Rohde appealed to the **Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA)**, arguing that the granting of leave to appeal implied that his conviction had reasonable prospects of success. He contended that the factors weighing in his favor included a lack of flight risk, the potential detriment to his business interests, and his compliance with previous bail conditions ([para 3]). The court a quo found that he posed a flight risk and had not strictly complied with bail conditions previously ([para 4]).

## I – Issues

The central legal issues pertained to:

1. **Whether the appellant could demonstrate that it was in the interests of justice to be released on bail pending appeal** ([paras 4–6]).
2. **Whether there were reasonable prospects of success on the appeal against his conviction and sentence**. This relates to the presumption of innocence and the increased risk of abscondment upon conviction ([para 9]).
3. **Whether the appellant could be deemed a flight risk**, considering his personal circumstances and the nature of his charges ([paras 13–20]).

## R – Rules

The Supreme Court, in its analysis, relied on several laws and precedents, including:

1. **Section 60 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA)**, which stipulates the conditions under which bail may be granted or denied for Schedule 5 and Schedule 6 offences ([paras 4, 8]).
2. **The pertinent case law**, including **S v Scott-Crosley** and **Masoanganye v S**, which elucidates the considerations a court must evaluate when deciding bail applications, especially concerning flight risks and the seriousness of the charges ([para 7, 9]).
3. **Judicial Matters Amendment Act**, which reflects a stringent approach towards bail applications for serious offences and underscores the legislative intent to prioritize community safety ([para 7]).

## A – Application

The court analyzed the appellant's situation against the backdrop of the law. It acknowledged that while the granting of leave to appeal is a significant factor, it does not automatically grant bail ([para 12]). The analysis of whether the appellant presented a real flight risk was particularly comprehensive.

The court assessed:

- **Emotional and financial ties**: The appellant's claim of needing to provide emotional support to his children was considered tenuous, given that other family members were already fulfilling this role ([para 18]). 
- **History of compliance**: Previous stricter compliance with bail conditions was overshadowed by the court’s concern over the gravity of his present conviction. The fact that he had previously faced significant penalties and had an international background made him a potential flight risk ([para 14]).
- **Evidence of relocation tendencies**: The appellant held multiple passports and had a history of international travel. While there was reference to passport control, the court noted the challenges of enforcing such limitations effectively ([paras 14–15]).

Despite arguments from the appellant that his circumstances warranted bail, the court found that he did not meet the burden to demonstrate that his release was justified in terms of the interests of justice. Rohde's financial deterioration and the argument that it impeded his ability to post higher bail were deemed insufficient in persuading the court ([paras 20, 19]).

The dissenting judgment by **Van der Merwe JA** presented an alternative viewpoint, positing that the emotional toll and prospects of success on appeal should have warranted bail. He suggested that while the convictions were serious, this did not inherently mean the appellant would abscond ([paras 22–27]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court’s decision to refuse bail based on the perceived flight risk and the severity of the conviction ([paras 20, 21]). 

The judges emphasized the balancing act between the presumption of innocence until proven guilty and the realities of the legal framework surrounding bail in serious criminal cases. This case reinforces the strict interpretation and application of bail provisions for convicted individuals, particularly when substantial custodial sentences are at stake. The dissenting opinion highlighted the intricacies of judicial reasoning in cases of this nature and underscored the necessity for robust evidence to establish whether an accused is indeed a flight risk.

This case serves as a critical reference point for future bail applications in South Africa, illustrating the intersections of law, personal circumstance, and societal responsibilities in the prosecution of serious crimes.
