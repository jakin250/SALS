Detailed FIRAC Case Note: Afriforum v The Minister of Trade and Industry (469/2013) [2013] ZASCA 184

The case arises from an appeal by Afriforum (the Appellant) against the judgment and order of the North Gauteng High Court (Victor J), dated 12 March 2013 ([1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 184

F – Facts

The case arises from an appeal by Afriforum (the Appellant) against the judgment and order of the North Gauteng High Court (Victor J), dated 12 March 2013 ([1]). The Appellant sought relief for the setting aside of two Government Gazettes: Gazettes 221 and 898, which were published on 14 March 2011 and 31 October 2011, respectively ([1][2]). Following the ruling of Victor J, the court found that Government Notice 898 had been set aside, but ordered that the order would take effect within 30 days, and mandated the First and Second Respondents to publish a notice regarding specific municipalities as per the Consumer Protection Act 68 of 2008 ([2]).

On 17 September 2013, the First and Second Respondents served a notice of abandonment regarding paragraphs 2 and 3 of the order on Afriforum’s attorneys, which indicated their intention to not pursue the implementation of the deferment outlined in the notice of 14 March 2011 ([3]). In a letter dated 27 September 2013, they confirmed that they would not invoke the deferment ([4]). As a consequence, Afriforum decided to limit its appeal to contesting the order of costs from the court below and to seek an order reflecting the abandonment of the earlier notice ([4][5]). The underlying issue was whether the original Government Notice of 14 March 2011 continued to hold validity, given the subsequent proceedings.

I – Issues

The central issue in the case concerns the implications of the abandonment notice served by the Respondents and its effect on the Appellant's right to pursue the appeal against the costs order. Specifically:

  1. Did the abandonment of parts of the order negate the necessity or practical effect of Afriforum's appeal against the costs ruling?
  2. Should the court interfere with the costs order made by Victor J, based on the grounds of the appeal and the submissions regarding standing and the volume of documentation submitted for consideration?

These questions originate from the effective utility of the appellate procedure in light of the Respondents' withdrawal from certain aspects of the prior court’s judgment ([5][6]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal framework revolves around the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959, specifically section 21A(1), which permits courts to dismiss an application that is deemed to be academic and devoid of practical consequences ([5]). The principles governing the adjudication of costs are embodied in the discretion vested with the courts, as noted in Nedbank Ltd v. Bressler 2008 (1) SA 277 (SCA), which affirms that an appellate court will not interfere with a lower court's costs order absent a manifest misdirection or improper exercise of discretion ([6]).

Victor J’s prior ruling was based on her evaluation of the factual submissions, particularly noting the lack of detailed pleadings from Afriforum, the volume of unnecessary documentation presented, and the bona fide negotiation efforts which had broken down due to the parties’ conduct ([6]).

A – Application

In applying the law to the facts, the Supreme Court of Appeal (Malan JA) reasoned that the abandonment of the portions of the order rendered Afriforum’s appeal academic; the application had no practical effect as the Respondents were not pursuing any deferment under the Government Notice of 14 March 2011. Thus, any ruling concerning this notice would effectively have no real-world implication, aligning with the constraints laid out in section 21A of the Supreme Court Act ([5][6]).

Furthermore, regarding the costs ruling by Victor J, Malan JA acknowledged that the lower court had exercised its discretion judiciously. The reasons provided by Victor J for ordering each party to bear its own costs were well-founded, considering the conduct of the parties, the gravity of the matter raised, and the extensive documentation that was presented without clear relevance ([6]). The Court noted there was no basis for overturning the discretion exercised, particularly as both parties had succeeded on some issues while they failed on others, leading to a fair costs outcome.

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Afriforum's appeal with costs from the date of the notice of abandonment, reiterating that the appeal was rendered moot due to the lack of practical consequences stemming from the abandonment ([6][7]). The decision underscores judicial adherence to procedural integrity and the principle that courts will not entertain appeals devoid of substantive merit. The ratio decidendi of this case reinforces the understanding of academic versus actionable matters in appellate procedures and delineates the boundaries of costs orders, emphasizing the exercise of judicial discretion in such contexts ([7]).

This decision holds significant implications for future cases whereby parties must carefully evaluate the practical consequences of continued litigation when substantial changes occur post-trial, particularly regarding abandonment of claims and the courts’ interactions with the principles of efficiency and reasonableness in cost allocations.
