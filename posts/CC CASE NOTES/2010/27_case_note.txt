Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

# Case Note: The State v Kholekile Witness Thunzi and Siyabulela Mlonzi

### FIRAC Case Note

**Court**: Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Case Number**: CCT 81/09  
**Decision Date**: 2 December 2010  
**Judgment By**: Froneman J  

---

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–11])

The case originated from a referral to the Constitutional Court for confirmation of an order made by the Eastern Cape High Court in Mthatha, which declared the “applicability” of section 4 of the Dangerous Weapons Act (Transkei) (DWA (Tk)) unconstitutional due to unfair discrimination against crime perpetrators in the former Transkei, who were subjected to a harsher sentencing regime ([para 1]). 

The High Court's order did not fully declare the provisions themselves as unconstitutional but focused on their applicability under specific circumstances. Notably, the High Court limited its ruling to cases where accused individuals had not yet pleaded, thus inadvertently perpetuating injustices for those who had already pleaded under this Act ([para 2]). The Constitutional Court, exercising its inherent powers, sought to address this oversight and correct the order to prevent further injustice ([para 2]).

During the Court's review, it became evident that similar legislation existed in other former homelands (Venda, Bophuthatswana, and Ciskei), which did not impose such differential sentencing regimes ([para 3]). This pointed to a potential constitutional obligation for Parliament to harmonize the laws governing dangerous weapons across the nation, raising significant questions about the legislative intent behind sustaining such differential regimes long after the transition to a constitutional democracy ([paras 4–5]).

As the legislative framework stood, Parliament acknowledged its obligation to rationalize these laws, pledging to introduce necessary amendments within the 2011 legislative session ([para 8]). However, the existing laws were not operational, minimizing immediate legal impact, thus prompting the Court to postpone proceedings to ensure compliance with this commitment ([paras 9–11]).

---

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 4–6])

The principal constitutional issues before the Court were:

1. Whether the provisions of item 2 of Schedule 6 to the Constitution impose a constitutional obligation on Parliament to rationalize the laws governing the use of dangerous weapons in the territories of the former Transkei, Bophuthatswana, Venda, and Ciskei ([para 6]).
  
2. If such an obligation exists, was Parliament in breach of this responsibility for failing to establish uniform legislation across South Africa regarding dangerous weapon use? ([para 6]).

3. What appropriate relief, if any, should the Court provide if Parliament was found to be in breach of its obligation? ([para 6]).

4. Lastly, the Court contemplated whether the continued existence of multiple Dangerous Weapons Acts was unconstitutional, with a consideration to potentially invalidate these statutes ([para 6]).

---

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 2, 5, 9])

The Court relied significantly on the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, particularly Schedule 6, which addresses old order legislation and its compatibility with the new constitutional framework ([para 6]). This was complemented by a reference to inherent powers granted to courts under section 173 of the Constitution, which allows them to regulate their own processes and protect justice ([para 2]).

The Court looked to the precedent established in *Matatiele Municipality and Others v President of the Republic of South Africa and Others* [2006] ZACC 2; 2006 (5) SA 47 (CC), where it was noted that uncertainties surrounding the constitutionality of a law necessitate judicial inquiry, particularly for substantial constitutional questions ([para 5]).

Moreover, the case drew insight from *Executive Council, Western Cape Legislature and Others v President of Republic of South Africa* to emphasize that a proactive role is essential when unaddressed constitutional matters become apparent, reaffirming the Court's obligation to explore these areas ([para 5]).

---

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 3, 8, 9])

The Court recognized the importance of addressing the ongoing legislative discrepancies regarding dangerous weapon regulation, noting that the retention of outdated laws could undermine the principles of equality and justice as enshrined in the Constitution ([para 4]). By questioning the rationale and purpose behind maintaining such old legislation, the Court underscored the transitional provisions' limitations to merely facilitating legal continuity until a uniform system was established ([paras 4–5]).

In its assessment, the Court observed that Parliament accepted its obligation to rationalize these laws, thus aligning itself with constitutional mandates ([para 8]). The acknowledgment indicated a consensus that the status quo was untenable and that legislative action was urgently required. Given that the problematic provisions were not currently operational, the Court deemed it unnecessary to declare them immediately unconstitutional or impose judicial invalidation, as an ongoing commitment from Parliament was evident ([para 9]).

The Court ultimately decided that the most judicious course was to defer the matter until November 2011 to allow monitoring of Parliament's legislative actions, ensuring that the rights, equal protections, and constitutional mandates were fulfilled within an appropriate timeframe ([para 11]).

---

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 12])

The Court postponed its decision until 29 November 2011, compelling the Speaker of the National Assembly, the Chairperson of the National Council of Provinces, and the Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development to update the court on steps taken towards rationalization ([para 12]). 

The implications of this case extended beyond the immediate matter by highlighting the Constitutional Court's active role in safeguarding democratic governance and ensuring that legislative bodies honor their obligations under the Constitution. The engagement also underscored a commitment to justice, emphasizing the need for the law to evolve and reflect equitable treatment across all legislative domains ([para 11]). 

In conclusion, the Court not only acted to rectify an immediate injustice but also set a precedent that reinforces the expectation of legislative accountability within the South African constitutional framework, echoing the collective aspiration for a unified approach to legal issues stemming from historical inequalities.
