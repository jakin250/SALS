Supreme Court of Appeal

Basson v Hugo & Others (968/16) [2017] ZASCA 192

The present case arises from a disciplinary inquiry conducted by the Health Professions Council of South Africa (the Council) against Dr Wouter Basson, a cardiologist.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2017] ZASCA 192

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–7])

The present case arises from a disciplinary inquiry conducted by the Health Professions Council of South Africa (the Council) against Dr Wouter Basson, a cardiologist. This inquiry was initiated under the Health Professions Act 56 of 1974, primarily concerning Dr Basson's involvement in unethical conduct related to chemical and biological warfare research during his service in the South African Defence Force during the 1980s ([para 2]).

In December 2013, Dr Basson was found guilty of unprofessional conduct on multiple counts. During the ongoing proceedings in January 2015, a request was made to the inquiry's Committee for the recusal of Professors J F M Hugo and R E Mhlanga, based on a claim of bias, as both professors were associated with groups advocating for Dr Basson's removal from the Register of Medical Practitioners. The request for recusal was denied ([paras 2–4]).

As the inquiry advanced, the appellant successfully sought a high court order to prevent the inquiry from proceeding until the Committee disclosed the professors' affiliations with the organizations advocating for his removal ([para 6]). Later, the appellant formally applied for the recusal of both professors on grounds of interest and bias. However, this recusal application was again rejected ([para 7]). Subsequently, Dr Basson approached the court of first instance to review the Committee's decision to deny the recusal, but the court dismissed the application, insisting that he first exhaust internal remedies available under the Act.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 8–10])

The principal legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether Dr Basson was required to exhaust internal remedies under section 7(2) of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) prior to launching a judicial review of the Committee's decision denying the recusals. The court had to consider:

  • Whether the internal remedies were adequate and effective in light of the allegations of bias against members of the Committee ([para 8]).
  • Whether exceptional circumstances existed to exempt Dr Basson from this requirement ([para 10]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 11–12])

The relevant statutory framework is found in section 7(2) of PAJA, which states that:

  • No court should review administrative action unless any internal remedy provided by law has first been exhausted ([para 11]).
  • Courts may exempt applicants from exhausting such remedies in "exceptional circumstances" if deemed in the interests of justice ([para 11–12]).

Key case law referenced includes:

  • SA Veterinary Council v Veterinary Defence Force Association ([2003] ZASCA 27) affirming the definition of administrative action under PAJA.
  • Koyabe & Others v Minister for Home Affairs ([2009] ZASCA 23) which elaborated on what constitutes exceptional circumstances regarding the exhaustion of remedies ([para 12]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 13–28])

The court noted that the internal remedy constitutes an appeal to the ad hoc appeal committee, which could assess the merits of the recusal application ([paras 13–16]). However, it highlighted that while the Committee had the potential power to confirm or set aside its own findings, the implications of bias alleged against the Committee were critical.

The judgment critically considered the claim of bias, connecting it to the right to a fair trial. The court referenced prior findings, notably in Moch v Nedtravel Pty Ltd, which emphasized that the refusal to recuse constitutes a nullity that undermines the entire proceedings ([paras 17–19]). Thus, it reasoned that if the recusal should have been granted due to bias, then all subsequent decisions by the Committee would similarly be rendered void, and no appeal committee could vacation such jurisdictional defects.

Importantly, the court recognized the constitutional underpinnings of bias and the right to a fair hearing embedded within the Bill of Rights. It established that an effective internal remedy must be one that allows for the substantive rights to be upheld, which in this case, the appeal process under section 10(3) of the Act failed to provide ([paras 20–24]).

Considering Dr Basson's circumstances, the inquiry demonstrated a close inspection of whether the appeal committee could adequately resolve the bias claims, concluding it lacked the requisite jurisdiction to address such fundamental issues raised ([para 27–28]).

C – Conclusion: Court's Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 29–30])

The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the decision of the court a quo, deciding that Dr Basson was indeed exempted from the obligation to exhaust internal remedies due to the presence of exceptional circumstances ([para 29]). The court held that the internal remedy of appeal was ineffective and inadequate, given that it could not provide redress for the alleged bias entrenched within the administrative procedure ([paras 28–29]).

This ruling affirmatively recognized that allegations of bias warrant direct judicial scrutiny, highlighting a court's ability to intervene when internal remedies fail to uphold constitutional rights. In moving the case back to the Gauteng Division of the High Court for review of the recusal application, the court signaled a protective stance towards due process and fair administrative practice ([para 29]).

By emphasizing the impermissibility of bias and the ineptitude of mere procedural remedies, this decision enhances the procedural rights of individuals subjected to administrative action and strengthens the application of PAJA in protecting justice and fairness in administrative decision-making.
