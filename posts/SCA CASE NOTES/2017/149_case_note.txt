Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: Qhinga v State (1327/2016) [2017] ZASCA 149

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 2–8])
On 12 July 2006, a group of armed men carried out a series of violent acts at a community hall in Newlands, Eastern Cape. They robbed security personnel and bystanders, inflicting injuries and stealing firearms and money intended for social grant payments ([para 2]). Subsequently, seven men, including the appellant, M Qhinga, were indicted on numerous charges, including four counts of robbery with aggravating circumstances and two counts of attempted murder. At trial, the Eastern Cape Local Division convicted six of the seven men ([para 3]). The convictions resulted in lengthy sentences, with the appellant receiving 28 years in prison ([para 4]).

All six accused sought to appeal their convictions and sentences, initially denied by Dhlodhlo ADJP at trial. Following a petition to the Constitutional Court, the matter was remitted to the Supreme Court of Appeal, which granted leave for an appeal ([para 4]). Although the full court of the Eastern Cape High Court set aside the second attempted murder conviction, they had a split decision regarding the appellant's case, leading him to seek further appeal ([para 5]).

The evidence against the appellant was primarily based on a pointing-out (akin to a confession) he made during police interrogation and a subsequent confession given after the pointing-out. While the pointing-out was admitted by the trial court, the admissibility of the later confession was contested ([paras 6–7]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 9–10])
The key issues presented in this case were:

1. **Whether the content of the appellant’s pointing-out was sufficiently proved.** The court needed to determine if the evidence pertaining to the pointing-out was properly recorded and capable of being relied upon as evidence of the appellant’s guilt ([para 9]),
   
2. **Whether the pointing-out should have been admitted in the first place.** The focus was on whether the circumstances surrounding the appellant's pointing-out violated his fair trial rights, thereby rendering it inadmissible ([para 10]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 19])
The principles guiding the case included the obligation of the State to show that any confession or pointing-out was made freely and voluntarily. The relevant legal framework was established by the South African Constitution and the South African Police Service Act 68 of 1995, particularly emphasizing the fundamental rights of individuals under detention. Section 1 of the Constitution asserts the rule of law and the need for compliance with fundamental rights ([para 19]). 

The court also referenced the legal standards required for evidence to be admissible against an accused, focusing on the need for safeguarding against coercive police practices, which can lead to wrongful convictions and undermine the integrity of the justice system ([para 20]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 11–14])
Upon examination, the court found significant issues concerning the evidence related to the appellant’s pointing-out. During the trial-within-a-trial, although the police procedure was initially deemed legally acceptable, it emerged that the actual evidence confirming the contents of the pointing-out was either not led during the trial or had become lost and irretrievable ([para 11]). As such, the court noted that no admissible evidence existed to implicate the appellant in the robberies, leading to the conclusion that the pointing-out was substantively unsupported.

The absence of Superintendent Sonwabile Nkosiyana's evidence, who allegedly conducted the pointing-out, rendered the admissibility questionable. The parties conceded to the irretrievable nature of this evidence, resulting in a lack of proof against the appellant ([paras 12–14]). The majority opinion thus concluded that without admissible evidence, there could be no valid conviction for the crimes charged ([para 14]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 14–20])
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that the absence of reliable and admissible evidence against the appellant necessitated the setting aside of his convictions and sentences. The ruling underscored the judicial commitment to maintaining fair trial standards, as mandated by the Constitution. It also highlighted the critical importance of proper evidence handling and the ethical constraints on police conduct during investigations ([para 14]).

The decision further illuminated broader implications for law enforcement practices, stressing that any investigative conduct that pressures an accused or compromises their right to silence cannot be accepted in a constitutional democracy founded on human dignity and equal rights ([para 19]). This ruling serves as a stern reminder of the legal obligations of police forces and reiterates the necessity for strict adherence to procedural fairness, ensuring the integrity of the justice system is upheld ([para 20]).

This case illustrates the paramount importance of maintaining evidentiary integrity and the defence rights of the accused in criminal prosecutions, reinforcing the judiciary's role in safeguarding constitutional rights in the face of potential abuses by the state.
