### Case Note: National Director of Public Prosecutions v Moodley (263/08) [2008] ZASCA 137

#### FIRAC Method

**F – Facts**  

The case involves an appeal by the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDP) against a High Court decision. The three respondents, Sarojini Moodley, Shunmugam James Moodley, and Shawn Beharie, were accused of multiple drug-related charges, including racketeering, in the Pietermaritzburg Regional Court. The second and third respondents were arrested on 8 July 2003 on charges of dealing in and possessing mandrax. On 10 September 2003, the prosecutor postponed the case to investigate the possibility of racketeering charges. 

On 10 December 2003, the charge sheet was presented to the respondents' attorney, outlining 14 counts, three of which pertained to racketeering, contrary to s 2(1)(e) and (f) of the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (POCA). The authorisation for prosecution under s 2(4) POCA, which requires written approval from the National Director of Public Prosecutions before charges can be laid, was granted on 24 March 2004. However, the respondents contended that they had been 'charged' prior to this authorisation when the charge sheet was handed to their attorney in December 2003. 

The respondents sought to declare the racketeering counts unlawful and set them aside, claiming that the absence of prior written authority had rendered the charges invalid. The High Court agreed, finding the authorisation inadequate and overly broad. The NDP appealed against this decision.

**I – Issues**  

The primary legal issue was the interpretation of the term 'charged' as used in section 2(4) of POCA. The court needed to consider:
1. What constituted being 'charged' for the purposes of legal authorisation under s 2(4) of POCA.
2. Whether the prosecution was lawful despite the timeline of the authorisation relative to the 'charging' of the respondents.  

**R – Rules**  

The main legal rule at issue was the interpretation of section 2(4) of POCA, which states that a person shall only be charged with committing an offence under subsection (1) if a prosecution is authorised in writing by the NDP. Precedents from prior cases, particularly the definitions provided in *Sanderson v Attorney-General, Eastern Cape* 1998 (1) SACR 227 and *Du Preez v Attorney-General, Eastern Cape* 1997 (2) SACR 375, were referenced for the context of 'charged' in South African criminal law. 

Additionally, the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, particularly s 76(1), was relevant as it stipulates the process of lodging a charge sheet with the clerk of the court to commence proceedings.

**A – Application**  

The court's analysis centered on the differing interpretations of 'charged.' The respondents advanced a broad interpretation, arguing that receipt of the charge sheet by their attorney constituted being charged. Conversely, the NDP contended for a narrow interpretation, suggesting that the respondents were not 'charged' until the proces was formalized in court (i.e., when the charge sheet was lodged with the clerk). 

The court ultimately found merit in the NDP's argument that the written authorisation granted on 24 March 2004 made the subsequent prosecution lawful. It held that the lack of prior authorisation did not invalidate the prosecution since the respondents had not yet been asked to plead. The court noted that, following the granting of authorisation, the NDP was entitled to continue prosecution, irrespective of earlier steps in the process. 

Furthermore, despite the High Court's conclusions about the inadequacy of the authorisation, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) refrained from delving deeper into that aspect, given that the primary concern was whether the prosecution could proceed lawfully post-authorisation.

**C – Conclusion**  

The SCA upheld the appeal by the NDP, affirming that the prosecution was lawful following the written authorisation issue. The court dismissed the arguments that the prosecution was tainted or invalid due to the lack of prior authorisation, emphasizing the legal capacity of the NDP to charge the respondents once the requisite authorisation was granted. 

The order of the High Court was set aside, and the appeal was upheld alongside costs. The case is significant in clarifying the interpretation of 'charged' under POCA and reinforcing the legislative intent behind requiring authorisation for certain types of prosecutions, thereby upholding prosecutorial discretion while ensuring that procedural requirements are met. This decision underlines the importance of continuous legal clarity in the procedural aspects of criminal law, particularly concerning organised crime legislation.
