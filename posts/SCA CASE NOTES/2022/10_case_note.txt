Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: Eskom Holdings Soc Ltd v Lekwa Ratepayers Association and Others; Eskom Holdings Soc Ltd v Vaal River Development Association (Pty) Ltd and Others (870/20) [2022] ZASCA 10

## F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([1]–[20])

This case arises from the appeals involving Eskom Holdings SOC Ltd (Eskom), the primary electricity supplier in South Africa, and two local municipalities—Ngwathe and Lekwa—represented by their respective residents' associations. The core issue stems from Eskom's unilateral decisions to reduce electricity supply to these municipalities, which had defaulted on long-standing payment obligations under their respective electricity supply agreements. 

The Ngwathe municipality, specifically Parys and Vredefort, had their supply reduced to a maximum demand (NMD) of 21 MVA and 4.3 MVA, respectively, while the Lekwa municipality faced reductions to a supply threshold of 55 MVA across areas including Standerton, Sakhile, and Meyerville. These actions by Eskom were taken amid significant financial arrears from the municipalities—R1.26 billion for Ngwathe and R1.13 billion for Lekwa—which highlighted their dysfunctional operational status and inability to meet service obligations to residents ([2]–[14]).

The municipalities sought interim interdicts from the Gauteng Division of the High Court in Pretoria, arguing that the reductions would result in catastrophic social, environmental, and economic impacts, including the disruption of essential services like healthcare, water supply, and sanitation ([16]–[19]). The High Court initially ruled in favor of the municipalities, granting interdicts to restore electricity supply levels pending further legal review, but these orders were appealed by Eskom ([4]–[5]).

## I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([6]–[7])

The key legal issues presented before the court included:

1. **Whether the residents established a prima facie right for interim interdictory relief against Eskom's reductions in electricity supply.** This involves determining whether Eskom's unilateral decisions adhered to constitutional and statutory obligations—particularly those related to the provision of basic services and intergovernmental cooperation as mandated by sections 40 and 41 of the Intergovernmental Relations Framework Act 13 of 2005 (IRFA) and the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa.

2. **The appealability of interim interdicts**, in light of usual judicial reluctance to permit appeals against such orders due to their inherently provisional nature. The court needed to assess whether the exceptional circumstances warranted allowing the appeal to proceed ([6]–[7]).

## R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([8]–[30])

The court relied on various legal authorities and statutory provisions:

- **Constitutional Foundations**: Sections 40 and 41 of the Constitution provided a framework requiring all organs of state to engage in good-faith efforts to resolve intergovernmental disputes before resorting to judicial proceedings.

- **The Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: This Act mandates procedural fairness in administrative actions undertaken by state organs, including Eskom.

- **Precedents**: The court cited *Government of the Republic of South Africa v Grootboom and Others* [2001] (1) SA 46 (CC) to assert that the provision of basic services, including electricity, forms the cornerstone of realizing other constitutional rights. The court also referenced its own ruling in *Resilient Properties (Pty) Ltd v Eskom Holdings SOC Ltd and Others* [2020] ZASCA 185, which emphasized the constitutional obligations of state organs when providing essential services ([8]–[30]).

## A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([21]–[32])

In applying the legal framework, the court evaluated the prima facie right established by the residents. It established that the municipalities' failure to meet payment obligations does not override Eskom's responsibility to ensure the continuity of service delivery, especially concerning basic needs such as electricity. The court emphasized that Eskom's decisions, notably the drastic reduction in supply, could not be justified when considering the dire socio-economic consequences they triggered for residents and municipal functioning ([21]–[29]).

Furthermore, the court highlighted that Eskom had a constitutional obligation to exhaust intergovernmental dispute resolution mechanisms as specified in the IRFA, thus invalidating its unilateral approach to limiting supply, which created a scenario where municipalities could not fulfill their service obligations to residents. This breach could potentially infringe on citizens' rights to basic services and overall quality of life, thereby establishing a prima facie case for judicial review under PAJA ([30]–[32]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([33])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Eskom's appeals in both cases and upheld the High Court's orders, concluding that the residents had adequately established a prima facie right necessary for the issuance of interim interdictory relief. The court underscored the importance of adhering to constitutional and statutory frameworks governing intergovernmental relations and service delivery.

The court's decision implies a strong affirmation of the necessity for state organs, including Eskom, to cooperate and engage constructively in resolving disputes, particularly those affecting essential service delivery to citizens. This ruling reinforces the principle that financial disputes, while significant, cannot justify actions that jeopardize the fundamental rights of individuals, especially concerning basic services like electricity. It also highlights the relevance of the IRFA and constitutional imperatives in local governance contexts ([33]). 

---

This case establishes a precedent emphasizing the accountability of state-owned enterprises in their governance and operational decisions, urging compliance with constitutional frameworks to protect citizens' rights and welfare.
