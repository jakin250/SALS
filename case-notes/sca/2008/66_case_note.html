Supreme Court of Appeal

Kini Bay Village Association v Nelson Mandela Metropolitan Municipality (434/2007) [2008] ZASCA 66

The appellant initiated legal proceedings seeking to set aside the rezoning recommendation made by the local authority, which had disregarded the appellants' objections ([para 3]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2008 [2008] ZASCA 66

F – Facts

The appeal arises from a decision of the Port Elizabeth High Court (Pakade J) which ordered the Kini Bay Village Association (the appellant) to furnish security for costs for the second and third respondents (Chase Street Properties (Pty) Ltd and Pierre Kolesky, respectively) pursuant to section 13 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973 ([para 1]). The appellant represents the interests of residents opposed to a rezoning application by the respondents to convert residential property (Erf 78 Kini Bay) into a guesthouse and conference facility ([para 2]).

The appellant initiated legal proceedings seeking to set aside the rezoning recommendation made by the local authority, which had disregarded the appellants' objections ([para 3]). When the respondents requested the appellant’s financial statements to assess its ability to pay potential costs, the appellant refused to disclose this information, asserting its status as a voluntary association under section 38 of the Constitution ([para 4]).

The respondents subsequently applied for security for costs, which the High Court granted after examining the appellant's financial record, revealing its incapacity to bear such costs. The court held that the appellant’s claim directly impacted the respondents’ property rights, affirming their entitlement to oppose the application ([paras 5–7]).

I – Issues

The principal issue on appeal is whether the High Court erred in its exercise of discretion under section 13 of the Companies Act and whether the appellant's purported constitutional rights justified the refusal of the security for costs order ([para 8]). The appellant contends that imposing a security order would have a chilling effect on the ability of impecunious entities to pursue constitutional claims ([para 8]).

R – Rules

Section 13 of the Companies Act empowers the court to require security for costs if there is credible evidence suggesting that the company or entity will be unable to pay costs if unsuccessful ([para 9]). This provision aims to prevent vexatious claims by financially unstable plaintiffs ([para 10]). In determining whether to grant a security order under section 13, the court must balance the right of access to courts against the potential injustice to the opposing party ([paras 12-15]).

Legal precedents, such as Ferreira v Levin NO ([para 7]) and Affordable Medicines Trust v Minister of Health ([para 17]), elucidate principles regarding costs in constitutional litigation, emphasizing that while there is a general rule against awarding costs against unsuccessful litigants in constitutional matters, exceptions may apply based on the case's specific facts.

A – Application

The appellate court examined whether the High Court's exercise of discretion was flawed. It found that the respondents provided adequate credible testimony of the appellant’s inability to pay costs, supported by evidence from the appellant’s financial statements, which revealed negligible assets ([para 13]). The High Court correctly identified that the appellant's litigation efforts were financially supported by wealthy members, indicating that it wasn’t genuinely impecunious, which undercut its constitutional defense ([para 15]).

Regarding the appellant's constitutional claim, the court assessed the underlying dispute as primarily concerning property rights rather than solely constitutional issues. Therefore, while the constitutional aspect was acknowledged, it did not substantially alter the nature of the proceedings ([para 16]).

Lastly, the court emphasized that the possibility of a chilling effect, while significant, does not serve as an absolute bar against orders for security, given the context of the case ([para 12]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately concluded there was no basis for interfering with the High Court’s judgment, affirming that the security for costs order against the appellant stands. Thus, the appeal was dismissed with costs ([para 21]). The decision underscores the court's position that while access to justice is paramount, it must be balanced against the integrity of the legal process, whereby financially capable parties cannot shield themselves from costs liability via the facade of a corporate entity.

This judgment delineates the contours of constitutional claims vis-à-vis procedural financial obligations, signalling to potential litigants the importance of transparency regarding financial capacity when initiating claims in South African courts. The case illustrates judicial reluctance to allow entities to use a non-profit status as a shield against financial liabilities in legal proceedings, reinforcing adherence to established legal standards while accommodating the proper exercise of constitutional rights.
