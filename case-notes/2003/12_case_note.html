Abduraghman Thebus and Moegamat Adams v. The State (CCT 36/02)

Constitutional Court 2003 CCT 36/02

F - Facts

The case revolves around an appeal to the Constitutional Court of South Africa concerning the appellants, Abduraghman Thebus and Moegamat Adams, who were convicted in the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) for murder and attempted murder ([1].) The incident in question occurred on November 14, 1998, when a group of residents in Ocean View, Cape Town, conducted a protest against alleged drug dealers. During this protest, a confrontation ensued, resulting in gunfire exchanged between the protestors and one Grant Cronje, a drug dealer. Tragically, a seven-year-old girl named Crystal Abrahams was fatally shot, and two others were injured ([2]).

Following the incident, both appellants were arrested. The arresting officer, Sergeant McDonald, informed Thebus of his rights and that any statement made could be used against him. However, Thebus nonetheless gave a verbal account, albeit without making a written statement ([3]–[4]). Over the course of the trial, the state presented evidence placing both Thebus and Adams at the scene of the shooting, primarily from a witness named Gregory Edward Kiel ([5]). The trial court ultimately convicted both appellants based on the common purpose doctrine, which asserts that participants in a joint criminal enterprise can be liable for actions taken by their associates ([6]).

Both appellants received an eight-year suspended sentence initially, but after an appeal by the state, their sentences were increased to fifteen years by the SCA ([8]). The appellants subsequently sought special leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court on two main constitutional issues: the development and application of the common law doctrine of common purpose and the drawing of negative inferences from Thebus's failure to disclose an alibi before trial ([9]).

I - Issues

The critical legal questions before the Constitutional Court were:

  1. Did the SCA fail to comply with its obligation under section 39(2) of the South African Constitution to develop the common law of common purpose to align it with constitutional rights such as dignity, freedom, and the presumption of innocence? ([17])
  2. Did the SCA err in drawing a negative inference from Thebus's pre-trial silence regarding his alibi, consequently infringing upon his constitutional right to remain silent? ([17])

R - Rules

The legal framework heavily references the South African Constitution, particularly sections 10 (right to dignity), 12 (right to freedom and security), 35 (right to a fair trial), and the common law doctrine of common purpose as established in S v Mgedezi ([10], [18]). The doctrine of common purpose allows for liability when individuals act collectively with a shared intention to commit an unlawful act, requiring presence at the scene, awareness of the unlawful acts, and participation ([18]).

Moreover, section 39(2) mandates that all courts must interpret legislation and develop common law in alignment with the Constitution's Bill of Rights ([24]). The right to silence, as articulated in section 35(1)(a), safeguards accused persons from self-incrimination, emphasizing that they need not disclose an alibi before trial ([35]).

A - Application

The court examined whether the common law doctrine of common purpose remained consistent with constitutional values. It emphasized that since the Constitution's adoption, courts must ensure that the application of common law does not unjustifiably infringe upon the rights granted to individuals. The court found that neither the pre-constitutional requirements of common purpose nor its applications fell short of constitutional scrutiny, thus dismissing the appellants' claims for re-evaluation based on constitutional development ([26]–[40]).

Regarding the alleged infringement of Thebus's right to silence, the court acknowledged the SCA's approach, which inferred guilt based entirely on Thebus's failure to disclose his alibi. The Constitutional Court noted that while it is permissible to evaluate credibility in light of the failure to disclose an alibi, it is impermissible to draw a direct inference of guilt solely from this failure. The court highlighted that drawing such inferences undermines the fundamental right to a fair trial ([86], [90]). Consequently, it ruled that while an adverse inference drawn from silence during the trial constitutes an infringement on the right to silence, it was not sufficient to overturn the conviction given the overwhelming evidence against Thebus articulated by Kiel ([93]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the appeals from both Thebus and Adams, affirming the lower courts' rulings. The court determined that the common law doctrine of common purpose did not violate constitutional rights, as its application in the appellants' case subserved a societal need to control crime committed collectively. Although it recognized that Thebus's right to silence was compromised by his prior failure to disclose his alibi, the court concluded that sufficient evidence remained to uphold his conviction ([74]).

This judgment has significant implications for the application of common law in South Africa, reinforcing how traditional doctrines must be scrutinized within constitutional parameters while also affirming that accused persons should not be penalized for exercising their right to silence in a vacuum of thorough context. The balance struck between societal protection and individual rights exemplifies the challenges inherent in the framework of criminal justice ([96]–[106]).
