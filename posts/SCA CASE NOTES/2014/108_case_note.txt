Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# Case Note: W[...] v The Minister of Police (92/2012) [2014] ZASCA 108

## F - Facts

This case revolves around the arrest and subsequent unlawful detention of the appellant, V[...] J[…] W[...], by the South African Police Services (SAPS) following an armed robbery at a Nedbank branch in Port Elizabeth on 14 June 2007. Video evidence linked several individuals, including Mr. W[...], to the robbery, leading to his arrest by Inspector Kuhn on 20 November 2007 ([paras 1-2]).

Upon his initial appearance in court, the case was postponed multiple times to accommodate Mr. W[...] in securing legal representation and applying for bail. On 11 and 12 December 2007, Mr. W[...] and his co-accused applied for bail, which was opposed by the State due to the nature of the charge as a Schedule 6 offence under the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, requiring them to show exceptional circumstances for bail to be granted ([paras 2-3]). Inspector Kuhn, the only witness and the individual who had viewed the incriminating video prior to the bail hearing, gave an account leading the magistrate to conclude that there were no grounds for bail ([paras 4, 5]).

Following the denial of bail, Mr. W[...] remained in custody until 13 January 2009 when the charges were withdrawn due to the prosecution’s inability to identify him from the video evidence, leading him to pursue legal action against the Minister of Police alleging unlawful arrest, detention, and malicious prosecution ([paras 5-6]).

## I - Issues

The primary legal questions in this case include:

1. **Unlawful Arrest**: Did Inspector Kuhn have reasonable grounds to suspect Mr. W[...] of being involved in the robbery to justify his arrest under section 40(1)(b) of the Criminal Procedure Act?
  
2. **Unlawful Detention**: Was Mr. W[...]’s prolonged detention lawful considering the magistrate’s reliance on the information provided by Inspector Kuhn at the bail hearing?
  
3. **Malicious Prosecution**: Did Inspector Kuhn initiate a prosecution without reasonable and probable cause and with malice towards Mr. W[...]?

These issues are significant in examining the balance between law enforcement’s powers and individual rights under the South African Constitution ([paras 6-8]).

## R - Rules

The court mainly referenced the following legal principles and precedents:

1. **Reasonable Suspicion**: Based on *Duncan v Minister of Law and Order* 1986 (2) SA 805 (A), the test for arrest under section 40 is not merely whether the officer believes there is a reason for suspicion, but whether there are reasonable grounds for such suspicion ([para 8]).

2. **Bail and Exceptional Circumstances**: The court emphasized previous rulings including *S v Rudolph* 2010 (1) SACR 262 (SCA), where it was established that the accused bear the onus to demonstrate exceptional circumstances, especially concerning the State’s case strength ([para 3]).

3. **Constitutional Principles**: The right not to be deprived of freedom arbitrarily or without just cause under section 12(1)(a) of the South African Constitution is critical when addressing claims of unlawful arrest and detention. The courts have held that this right imposes a duty on the State to act in a manner that respects individual liberty ([paras 25-28]).

4. **Malicious Prosecution**: Criteria established in *Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development v Moleko* 2008 (3) All SA 47 (SCA) were used to evaluate claims of malicious prosecution, focusing on whether reasonable and probable cause existed for the prosecution and whether malice was present ([paras 33-35]).

## A - Application

In addressing the issues, the Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed the evidence and the actions of Inspector Kuhn. 

### Unlawful Arrest

The court found that Inspector Kuhn's evidence regarding the identification of Mr. W[...] was based predominantly on his subjective assessment of the video evidence, which in reality did not clearly depict Mr. W[...]. The video evidence was insufficient to ground reasonable suspicion, thus rendering the arrest unlawful ([paras 10-17]). 

### Unlawful Detention 

The magistrate’s decision to deny bail was heavily influenced by the erroneous belief in the strength of the State's case as presented by Inspector Kuhn, which constituted a breach of the latter's public law duty to present all relevant information, thus leading to Mr. W[...]’s unlawful detention ([paras 19-30]). The court clarified that the unlawful nature of the detention lay not only in the arrest's legality but also in how the prosecution proceeded based on flawed assertions, constituting a breach of Mr. W[...]’s constitutional rights ([paras 27-28]).

### Malicious Prosecution

On the malicious prosecution claim, the court assessed whether Inspector Kuhn acted without reasonable and probable cause and whether there was malice involved. The court concluded that Inspector Kuhn’s belief in the correctness of his actions, despite being negligent, indicated a lack of intentional malice — thus failing to meet the threshold necessary to support a claim for malicious prosecution ([paras 33-36]).

## C - Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Mr. W[...]’s appeal against the decision of the Eastern Cape High Court and ordered the Minister of Police to compensate Mr. W[...] for the unlawful detention, awarding R500,000 for the duration of 13 months from 12 December 2007 to 13 January 2009. The court concluded that the evidence presented at the bail hearing, coupled with the negligence demonstrated by Inspector Kuhn in handling the proceedings, directly led to Mr. W[...]’s unlawful detention ([paras 42, 40-41]). 

This case highlights the paramount importance of proper police conduct in respecting individual rights and underscores that constitutional protections against arbitrary detention must be upheld in South Africa’s legal framework.
