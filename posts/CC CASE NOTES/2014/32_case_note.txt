Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: Helen Suzman Foundation v President of the Republic of South Africa; Glenister v President of the Republic of South Africa [2014] ZACC 32

## F – Facts
The cases at hand involve two distinct applicants, the **Helen Suzman Foundation (HSF)** in CCT 07/14 and **Hugh Glenister** in CCT 09/14, challenging the constitutionality of sections within the **South African Police Service Amendment Act 10 of 2012** and the **South African Police Service Act 68 of 1995**. 

The HSF's application aimed to declare several provisions of these acts constitutionally invalid, focusing on the adequacy of independence of the Directorate for Priority Crime Investigation (DPCI) as a specific anti-corruption entity ([paras 6–7]). Glenister's challenge revolved around the entire legislative scheme of the DPCI and its location within the South African Police Service (SAPS), which he claimed undermined its independence ([para 15]).

The High Court had ruled partially in favor of the HSF, declaring certain amendments unconstitutional while denying Glenister's broader challenges. Glenister sought confirmation of the invalidity of the entire legislative scheme ([paras 6–8]). Both applications came before the Constitutional Court, which heard the matters on 19 August 2014.

## I – Issues
The case presents several legal and constitutional questions, including:

1. **Independence of the DPCI**: Does the legislative framework provide the DPCI with adequate structural and operational independence as mandated by the Constitution? ([paras 2–8])
2. **Non-joinder of Parliament**: Should the Speaker of the National Assembly and the Chairperson of the National Council of Provinces have been joined as parties to the proceedings? ([paras 11–14])
3. **Striking out of evidence**: Was the High Court correct in striking out certain additional evidence presented by Glenister? ([para 22])
4. **Constitutionality of specific sections**: Are specific provisions of the SAPS Act and its amendments consistent with constitutional requirements? ([paras 32–34], [108])

## R – Rules
The constitutional principles at play involve:

1. **Section 206(1) and 207(2) of the Constitution**: These sections dictate the framework under which police powers and policies are determined by the Minister and managed by the National Commissioner ([para 16]).
2. **Glenister II Criteria**: Prior judgments, particularly *Glenister v President of the Republic of South Africa* ([2011] ZACC 6), established that an adequately independent anti-corruption unit must meet certain standards of independence ([para 20]).
3. **Separation of Powers and Institutional Independence**: The need for political accountability must coexist with a certain degree of operational independence, as highlighted in *Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources and Others* ([2009] ZACC 14) ([para 37]).

## A – Application
In determining whether the DPCI was constitutionally sound, the Court addressed each of the challenged provisions ([paras 62–76]). Glenister's argument against the DPCI's independence, predicated on public perception of widespread corruption within the SAPS, stressed the impossibility for an anti-corruption agency to function effectively within a potentially corrupt institution ([para 8]).

The Court also examined the claims about the need for a more robust appointment process for the DPCI's National Head. Concerns over untrammeled ministerial discretion in appointing and retaining the Head significantly contributed to the assessment of whether existing provisions sufficiently safeguard against undue political interference.

On striking out evidence, the Court noted that such an application must be guided by relevance and potential prejudice. The majority found that many of the additional evidence claims were not scandalous or vexatious and should have been admitted for consideration ([paras 124–147]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court affirmed the High Court's declaration of invalidity regarding certain provisions of the SAPS Amendment Act and the original SAPS Act that were inconsistent with constitutional requirements. Specifically, the provisions allowing unchecked ministerial discretion regarding the budget and integrity testing measures were rejected as unconstitutional ([para 5]).

The findings underscored the necessity for the DPCI to operate with adequate autonomy, emphasizing the need for independence from political pressures. The Court's order placed a responsibility on Parliament to revise the relevant legislation, ensuring that it adhered to constitutional standards. The judgment raised critical implications about the integrity of anti-corruption mechanisms in addressing public distrust in governmental institutions amid allegations of systemic corruption ([paras 106–222]).

This decision reinforced the role of the judiciary in safeguarding constitutional mandates and provided a framework for evaluating institutional independence in South Africa's ongoing struggle against corruption.
