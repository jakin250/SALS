Constitutional Court

Gabriel Ntleli Swartbooi and Others v Lilian Ray Brink and Gerrit Niewoudt

Constitutional Court of South Africa CCT 27/02 Heard on: 12 November 2002 Decided on: 21 November 2002 Judgment by: Yacoob J

Constitutional Court 2002 CCT 27/02

Constitutional Court of South Africa
CCT 27/02
Heard on: 12 November 2002
Decided on: 21 November 2002
Judgment by: Yacoob J

F – Facts

The case arises from a review application concerning the Nala Local Municipal Council ([1]). The applicants, Gabriel Ntleli Swartbooi and seventeen other council members, sought leave to appeal a High Court decision that directed them to pay the costs personally on an attorney and own client scale due to their conduct in the council's affairs ([1]). Initially, the council itself had sought to appeal against the High Court's cost order, but when leave was denied, the applicants attempted to intervene on behalf of the council by appealing to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([2]).

This appeal was subsequently met with refusal, prompting the applicants to bring an application before the Constitutional Court, representing themselves while naming only the original two respondents, Lilian Ray Brink and Gerrit Niewoudt ([2]). The Chief Justice of the court issued directions for written argument regarding various critical questions, including the jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court in such matters and the appropriateness of the cost orders in question ([3]).

Notably, the Constitutional Court raised concerns over the lack of the municipal council as a party in the appeal, given its material interest in the outcome, leading to its inclusion in the proceedings ([4]).

I – Issues

The key legal issues presented to the court were:

  • Jurisdiction: Does the Constitutional Court have jurisdiction to determine the circumstances under which municipal council members can be ordered to pay costs personally (de bonis propriis) ([3]a)?
  • Cost Orders: In what circumstances should members of a municipal council be ordered to pay such costs, and were the High Court's orders appropriate in this case ([3]b)(i)?
  • Implications: What are the broader implications for the functioning of municipal councils and their members concerning the legal privileges and immunities as stipulated by section 28 of the Local Government: Municipal Structures Act 117 of 1998 ([5])?

R – Rules

In addressing these issues, the court relied on various legal precedents and statutory provisions:

  1. Local Government: Municipal Structures Act 117 of 1998 - Particularly section 28, which outlines the privileges and immunities of municipal councillors, became central to the discussion regarding the potential liabilities imposed on them in legal proceedings.
  2. Principles of Judicial Review - The court also considered the principles establishing the legal framework for reviewing decisions made by municipal councils and how these principles relate to the cost orders imposed upon council members.
  3. Separation of Powers Doctrine - Given the interplay between different branches of government, the court examined how the decisions of the High Court might interfere with the functions and autonomy of local municipalities ([5]).

A – Application

The court applied these rules to the circumstances of the case, taking into consideration the importance of establishing clear guidelines for municipal council members and the principle of accountability.

  • Jurisdiction: The court affirmed its jurisdiction over the matter, noting that the issues raised were pertinent to the operations of municipal councils across South Africa and that establishing clear jurisprudence on these issues would benefit local governance ([5]).
  • Cost Orders: In examining the appropriateness of the cost orders, the court weighed the conduct of the council members against the backdrop of their responsibilities and the potential implications for the local government structure. It emphasized the need for a measured approach to ensure that such orders are not imposed without just cause, particularly when they threaten to undermine democratic governance at the municipal level ([5]).

The court underscored the importance of having the Nala Municipal Council as a party to ensure that the implications of the court's ruling were fully considered, recognizing that any reversal of the High Court's cost order would shift the liability back to the council itself. This concern demonstrated the interconnectedness of municipal operations and judicial oversight.

C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Constitutional Court granted the applicants leave to appeal against the decision of the Free State High Court, indicating its willingness to engage with significant questions concerning local governance and the implications of judicial cost orders on council members ([6]). The court's decision to join the Nala Municipal Council as a party underlines the importance of involving all parties with a vested interest in the proceeding, ensuring a comprehensive judicial examination of the issues at hand.

The court's ruling not only served the immediate parties involved but also established a guiding framework for similar future cases, reinforcing the principles of accountability while safeguarding the functioning of municipalities. The detailed circumstances under which cost orders could be applied were left open for future determination, signifying an evolving interpretation of the roles and responsibilities of municipal members versus the overarching legal framework encapsulated in the Local Government Act.

This case provides essential legal precedents that underscore the balance between accountability and functional independence of local government, which will be pivotal for councils across South Africa in similar contexts.

Chaskalson CJ, Langa DCJ, Goldstone J, Kriegler J, Mokgoro J, Ngcobo J, O’Regan J, and Sachs J concurred with Yacoob J.

For the applicants: D. Marais instructed by Ramsurjoo & Du Plessis Inc, Johannesburg.
For the respondents: S.J. Reinders instructed by G.P. Niewoudt & Partners, Bothaville.
