Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: **Majope and Others v The Road Accident Fund** (663/2022) [2023] ZASCA 145

## F – Facts
The case revolves around an appeal against orders granted by the Mpumalanga High Court, which initially ruled in favor of the plaintiffs, **Tina Majope** and **Abednego Machabe**, both of whom sought damages from the **Road Accident Fund (RAF)** after sustaining injuries from separate vehicle accidents ([1–4]). Majope was injured in a collision on **19 December 2019**, while Machabe fell off a moving vehicle on **1 February 2019**. They engaged **Fortunate Nwa-Khosa Ngomana** to represent them in their claims against the RAF, signing attorney and own client fee agreements on **20 February 2019** and **3 March 2020** respectively ([3]).

The RAF did not appear to defend either claim, conceding liability for negligence but disputing the quantum of damages. Despite this concession, when the matters were heard on **22 March 2022**, the RAF was absent ([4]). During the hearing, the high court directed the plaintiffs’ attorneys to prepare draft orders, which highlighted the absence of a contingency fee agreement ([6–10]). 

On **26 May 2022**, the high court issued a judgment that included unsolicited orders affecting the legal representatives Ngomana and her associate, asserting that their fee agreement was invalid under the Contingency Fees Act because it had not complied with necessary statutory provisions ([10–11]). Notably, the orders made were not requested by any party and deprived Ngomana and her associate of their right to claim fees for their services ([11–12]).

## I – Issues
The case raises significant legal questions, including:
1. Whether the high court erred in issuing orders regarding the attorneys' fees that were not sought by any of the parties and not fully canvassed in court.
2. Whether the attorney-client fee agreements constituted valid contracts or improper contingency fee arrangements, in light of the Contingency Fees Act 66 of 1997 ([9–11]).

## R – Rules
The legal framework governing this case primarily stems from the Contingency Fees Act 66 of 1997, which regulates the conditions under which contingency fee agreements may be validly entered into, requiring judicial oversight for such agreements ([12]). The court relied heavily on prior judgments, including **Road Accident Fund v MKM obo KM** and **Another; Road Accident Fund v NM obo CM** ([12]), which clarify that a contingency fee agreement is a bilateral contract between the client and attorney, separate from any obligations to third parties, such as the RAF.

Moreover, the principle that a court may not make orders beyond those sought by the parties is a foundational aspect of judicial authority, closely aligned with procedural fairness and the right to be heard ([11]).

## A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) undertook a detailed examination of the high court's findings, emphasizing that it had misdirected itself by making orders that had not been requested, thereby infringing upon the principles of natural justice ([14]). The court highlighted the following critical points:
- The attorney-client fee agreement in question was based on mutual consent and should be judged under general contract law principles, not restricted by the conditions applicable to contingency fee agreements ([12]).
- Although the high court viewed the agreements as non-compliant with the Contingency Fees Act, the SCA pointed out that such arrangements do not, inherently, confer the court the authority to make unsolicited orders that impede attorneys' rights to payment ([14]).
- The court reiterated that the context of the fee agreement had no relation to the litigation, invalidating the justification for including it in the court order ([12–13]).

The high court's findings effectively deprived the attorneys of their rightful claim without providing them a forum to contest these assertions. Such orders were deemed not only procedurally flawed but also fundamentally unjust, as they affected parties not represented in the proceedings at all ([11]).

## C – Conclusion
The SCA ultimately held that the high court's orders were not valid, overturning them in their entirety. The claims were reinstated in favor of the appellants, entitling them to the damages sought while also reassessing the attorney-client fee agreements in light of contractual principles rather than invalidating them due to a perceived failure to meet the conditions of the Contingency Fees Act ([15]).

This judgment underscores the importance of procedural fairness in judicial proceedings, particularly regarding attorney-client agreements. It strengthens the autonomy of legal practitioners to negotiate fees without undue interference from judicial proceedings, thereby reaffirming the contractual principles underpinning attorney representation in South Africa's legal landscape.

The implications of the ruling highlight the necessity for legal practitioners to have their fee agreements respected while promoting the integrity of the judicial process, which must afford all affected parties an opportunity to be heard before any adverse judgments are rendered against them.
