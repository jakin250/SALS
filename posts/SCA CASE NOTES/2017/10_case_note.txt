Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note: Westminster Tobacco Co v Philip Morris Products SA (925/2015)

**Neutral Citation:** Westminster Tobacco Co v Philip Morris Products SA (925/2015) [2017] ZASCA 10 (16 March 2017)  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Coram:** Leach, Wallis, Petse, Zondi JJA and Mbatha AJA  
**Heard:** 23 February 2017  
**Delivered:** 16 March 2017  

## F – Facts
In this case, Westminster Tobacco Co (Cape Town and London) (Pty) Ltd ("Westminster") held two registrations for the trade mark "PARLIAMENT" associated with cigarettes (registration numbers 1952/00688 and 1997/17613). The first respondent, Philip Morris Products SA ("Philip Morris"), claimed that Westminster had not made bona fide use of said marks for the preceding five years as required under s 27(1)(b) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 ("the Act"). 

Philip Morris's application aimed to have Westminster's marks expunged from the register, arguing that such marks were in continual non-use. The High Court ruled in favor of Philip Morris, asserting Westminster's failure to establish genuine use, which prompted Westminster's appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 1–2]).

Westminster is a subsidiary of British American Tobacco South Africa (Pty) Ltd (BATSA), part of the larger British American Tobacco group, and Philip Morris operates within the Philip Morris International group. The crux of the dispute arose from Philip Morris’s inability to register the "PARLIAMENT" mark in South Africa due to Westminster's existing registrations which precluded its entry into the market ([paras 2–3]).

The facts reflect Westminster's activities prior to and during the relevant period. Although BATSA began production of PARLIAMENT cigarettes in 2007 and initiated a launch in early 2008, the marketing strategy placed the cigarettes primarily in less prominent retail outlets with seemingly inadequate follow-up measures. Subsequent evidence during litigation revealed that BATSA's use of the mark alongside problematic aspects of poor sales fuelled claims that the use was primarily an attempt to avoid cancellation of the trade marks ([paras 8–9], [10–16]).

## I – Issues
The primary issue on appeal was whether Westminster had made bona fide use of the "PARLIAMENT" trade mark during the statutory period of five years prior to 22 July 2008. Specifically, the court needed to determine what constitutes "bona fide use" as defined under the Trade Marks Act and whether Westminster’s activities had met this requirement, given the surrounding circumstances and intentions behind their use of the mark ([paras 1, 4, 40]).

## R – Rules
Under s 27(1)(b) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, a registered trade mark may be removed from the register if it has not been used bona fide for a continuous period of five years prior to the application for expungement. The term "bona fide use" has been interpreted by South African courts to mean actual use aimed at protecting, facilitating, and furthering trade in the goods for which it is registered. Courts have emphasized that the use must be genuine and not merely tokenistic. Relevant jurisprudence includes *A M Moolla Group v The Gap* and *Ansul BV v Ajax Brandbeveiliging BV*, which outline the requirement for a genuine, non-token use of a trade mark that serves its essential function of distinguishing goods ([paras 5–6], [38]).

## A – Application
The Supreme Court conducted a thorough examination of Westminster's evidence concerning its use of the "PARLIAMENT" mark. Although Westminster's marketing efforts were criticized as poorly executed and insufficiently robust, the court was tasked with determining whether the use was genuine, as outlined by Ms Heglund, the marketing manager of BATSA, who claimed the objective was not solely to protect the trade mark but also to assess market dynamics and disrupt competitors ([paras 26–32]).

Importantly, the court considered whether the motives behind the marketing activities could classify them as bona fide use. The evidence presented suggested that, while concurrent with an ulterior motive of protecting the marks, the launch of the "PARLIAMENT" cigarettes served commercial purposes aimed at market penetration and viability, especially given the insights gained from testing market dynamics in the low-price sector. This ultimately laid the foundation for the court's conclusion that, while there may have been substantial imperfections in the execution of the marketing strategy, those imperfections did not negate the bona fide nature of the endeavour ([paras 39–48]).

Moreover, the court noted that despite the shortcomings inherent in the launch strategy, significant sales of approximately 20 million cigarettes within the five-year timeframe suggested genuine engagement with the market under the mark ([para 44]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court held that Westminster had indeed demonstrated bona fide use of the "PARLIAMENT" trade mark as stipulated by the Act. The appeal was upheld, the High Court’s order was set aside, and Westminster’s registration of the marks was maintained. The decision highlighted that bona fide use could coexist with the trade mark owner's broader commercial objectives, including market defense against competitors. The court's ruling establishes critical precedents for assessing the substance of trade mark use, reaffirming the position that genuine trade activities, albeit occasionally flawed in execution, align with statutory requirements when they sufficiently fulfill the functions of a trade mark.

The court ordered Philip Morris to pay costs, including those associated with the employment of two counsel, reflecting the significance of the case's complexities and the implications of the ruling in the context of trade mark law in South Africa ([paras 49–50]).
