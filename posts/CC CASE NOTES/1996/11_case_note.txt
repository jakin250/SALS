Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1996

# FIRAC Case Note: Gardener v Whitaker, CCT 26/94

**F – Facts**  
[1] This case arises from an application for leave to appeal against a judgment delivered by Froneman J in the Eastern Cape Division of the Supreme Court on 11 November 1994 ([para 1]). The applicant, Nigel Maurice Rhett Gardener, was the town clerk of East London who sought damages for defamation from the respondent, city councillor Eric Whitaker, in relation to a statement made during a council meeting on 21 June 1993. During this meeting, the respondent asserted that a report, which included contributions from the plaintiff, contained a "lie" ([para 2]). Gardener contended that this statement was defamatory; Whitaker denied both the defamation claim and suggested defences based on truth, public benefit, and qualified privilege. The trial court, finding that the statement was indeed defamatory, granted absolution from the instance on the basis that it arose from a matter of public interest and was made in an environment demanding open discussion ([para 2]). The judge concluded there was no malice involved in Whitaker's statement ([para 2]).

**I – Issues**  
[4] The primary issues for consideration were whether the provisions of South Africa’s Constitution applied to litigation pending at the time the Constitution came into force, whether the rights in Chapter 3 could be applied horizontally between private individuals, and how these rights affected the common law of defamation ([para 4]). Additionally, the implications of these constitutional considerations on the merits of the defamatory claim were central to the appeal ([para 12]).

**R – Rules**  
[5] The legal framework governing this case is rooted in the South African Constitution, particularly Chapter 3, concerning fundamental rights. The court referenced several provisions, including sections 7 (rights to dignity and freedom), 33 (right to just administrative action), and 35 (rights of accused persons) ([para 6]). The case of De Waal v Ziervogel 1938 AD 112 was applied as a leading authority on qualified privilege in defamation ([para 2]). The court also relied on the general principles of law regarding the balancing of competing fundamental rights—namely, the right to free speech and the right to reputation, both considered essential ([para 7]).

**A – Application**  
[6] The court's analysis predominantly revolved around whether the common law of defamation needed reformulation in light of constitutional guarantees. Froneman J concluded that the rights to free speech (section 15) and to reputation (as part of the right to dignity) are of equal standing ([paras 7–11]). He posited that the burden of proving that the right to reputation took precedence over freedom of speech should lie with the plaintiff, shifting the traditional onus seen in defamation cases regarding freedom of expression ([para 10]). The judge articulated that for a plaintiff to succeed, they must not only prove the defamatory nature of the statement but also that it does not warrant protection under the framework of free speech ([para 11]).

Despite these findings, the overarching conclusion was that Gardener had not proven that Whitaker's statement should be deemed unworthy of protection as an expression of free speech. The public interest considerations and the context of the statement were deemed relevant in this assessment, leading to the court's support of the defendant's claim of qualified privilege ([paras 8–9]).

**C – Conclusion**  
[18] Ultimately, the Constitutional Court dismissed the application for leave to appeal. The judgment of Froneman J, with all its constitutional implications on the law of defamation, was upheld. The court affirmed that constitutional interpretations regarding rights balance must generally consider both parties' interests in the context of any litigation. The applicant may seek to appeal to the Appellate Division if advised, allowing for potential further examination under their jurisdiction ([para 18]).

This decision is significant as it underscores the evolving dynamic between constitutional rights and traditional common law principles, indicating that the courts would scrutinize common law in the light of constitutional values going forward.
