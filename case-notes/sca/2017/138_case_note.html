FIRAC Case Note: Maharaj & Others v Mandag Centre of Investigative Journalism NPC & Others (844/2016) [2017] ZASCA 138

F – Facts

The case revolves around the refusal of the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) to grant permission for the publication of evidence given by Mr. Sathyandranath Ragunanan Maharaj and his wife, Ms. Zarina Carrim Maharaj, during an in-camera investigation conducted under §28 of the National Prosecuting Authority Act 32 of 1998 (NPA Act) ([1]–[4]).

The appellants, both significant public figures in South Africa, testified before the Directorate of Special Operations (the Scorpions) on June 19 and 20, 2003, regarding matters linked to a national arms deal ([5]). Following reports of suppression by the Mail & Guardian (M&G)—where it claimed its story was censored due to legal threats—M&G sought to publish findings regarding the Maharajs’ testimony, claiming a clear public interest ([2], [6]).

M&G applied to the NDPP for permission to disclose the evidence on November 21, 2011, asserting public interest grounds and the alleged previous disclosures by the Maharajs themselves that placed the information in the public domain ([7], [8]). The NDPP, however, rejected this request on January 3, 2012, reiterating a policy of non-disclosure and invoking concerns regarding ongoing investigations ([10]).

The M&G ultimately sought a review of the NDPP's refusal in the North Gauteng High Court, which ruled in favor of M&G, allowing the publication of the testimonies ([12]). The appellants subsequently appealed against this order, leading to the present case ([13]).

I – Issues

The central legal question is whether the NDPP's exercise of discretion under §41(6) of the NPA Act in denying permission to publish the evidence was lawful, particularly in light of claims of public interest and the existing publication of related material ([12], [21]). Additional issues include:

  1. The balance between freedom of expression and the integrity of the criminal justice system.
  2. The proper interpretation and application of s 28 and s 41 of the NPA Act.
  3. Whether the NDPP's reasons for refusing publication reflected a proper consideration of the facts presented.

R – Rules

Key legal rules and regulations invoked in this case include:

  • National Prosecuting Authority Act 32 of 1998: §28 allows for the in-camera investigation by the DPP, while §41(6) prohibits disclosure without approval, underlining the need to protect sensitive information gathered during investigations ([4]).
  • Constitution of South Africa: Specifically, s 16, which protects freedom of expression, and the necessity of balancing such rights against the NDPP's mandate to maintain the integrity of public prosecutions ([21]).
  • Precedents:
  • Print Media South Africa v Minister of Home Affairs (2012) recognized the importance of balancing press freedom and legal limitations.
  • National Director of Public Prosecutions v Freedom Under Law (2014), stressing that the NDPP is expected to fulfill a duty of transparency and respect court processes ([24]).

A – Application

The SCA undertook a thorough examination of whether the NDPP had appropriately exercised her discretion under the Act. It concluded that:

  • The NDPP's rationale, particularly her claim of being only generally aware of the content of the s 28 evidence, demonstrated a lack of proper consideration of the specifics discussed during the investigation—effectively rendering her decision irrational ([25]).
  • The court assessed the NDPP’s failure to adequately weigh the public interest against her policy of non-disclosure, finding that the M&G's arguments about public accountability merited substantial consideration [[27],[29]].
  • The court emphasized the nature of the evidence and allegations, concluding that the public was entitled to be informed about actions and conduct of public officials, echoing constitutional commitments to transparency and accountability ([28],[39]).

Despite the NDPP's insistence on protecting third-party interests and ongoing investigations, the court noted these factors were irrelevant given the public knowledge about the allegations ([30],[31]). Ultimately, the SCA mandated the NDPP to exercise her discretion in a manner consistent with both legal expectations and the urgency of public disclosure, concluding that a mere adherence to policy without context undermined the purpose of the legal provisions at play ([25],[39]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA upheld the ruling of the North Gauteng High Court, affirming that the NDPP unlawfully denied the M&G’s request to publish testimony that significantly pertains to public interest and accountability in governance. The court emphasized that the NDPP’s exercise of discretion must involve a nuanced understanding of competing interests, prioritizing transparency and public interest, particularly in matters involving public officials. The appeals by both the Maharajs and the NDPP were dismissed with costs ([41]).

In this landmark decision, the court reaffirmed the role of the media as crucial to democratic oversight, signaling a pivotal stance on transparency in accountability, particularly regarding allegations of corruption against public figures. The judgment provides critical guidance on the importance of balancing statutory obligations with constitutional protections of freedom of expression, reinforcing the need for rigorous, context-sensitive decision-making by prosecutorial authorities.
