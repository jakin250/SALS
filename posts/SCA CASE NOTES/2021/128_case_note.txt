Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

### FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title:** MEC for Health and Social Development, Gauteng v MM on behalf of OM **(Case no 697/2020)** [2021] ZASCA 128 (30 September 2021)

---

**F – Facts:**

On **7 December 2010**, a baby boy, referred to as OM, was born to the respondent, **MM**. During her pregnancy, MM was diagnosed as **HIV positive** and was placed on **antiretroviral therapy** beginning at **18 weeks gestation** ([1]). On the day of delivery, MM experienced **ruptured membranes** followed by severe abdominal pain, leading to her admission to the **Laudium Clinic** at **06h00**. Due to complications, she was later transferred to **Kalafong Hospital**, a facility known to manage **500-600 births per month** ([1]).

Upon arrival at Kalafong, MM’s cervix was dilated to **7 cm** at **20h15**, and subsequently to **9 cm** by **21h15**. OM was born at **21h50**, suffering from a **hypoxic ischemic injury** that caused cerebral palsy. MM initiated legal proceedings against the MEC for Health and Social Development, alleging negligence on the part of hospital staff during the labor process ([1], [2]). The **Gauteng Division of the High Court** found the MEC liable for the agreed or proven damages, prompting an appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([2], [3]).

**I – Issues:**

The core legal issues before the court were: 

1. **Negligence**: Did the hospital staff act negligently during MM’s labour? 
2. **Causation**: If negligence was established, did it directly contribute to the injury sustained by OM? ([6]).

Both issues required a finding on the standard of care expected from medical professionals and the application of that standard to the facts presented ([6], [34]).

**R – Rules:**

The court referred to established principles of negligence, particularly focusing on the standard required of medical practitioners, which includes:

- **Standard of Care**: Medical practitioners are expected to act according to the general level of skill and diligence in their profession ([6]).
- **Causation**: The claimant must prove, on a **balance of probabilities**, that the alleged negligence caused the injury ([6], [34]).
- References to prominent precedents included **Van Wyk v Lewis** (1924) and **Maqubela v S** (2017), which emphasized the distinction between **scientific proof** and **judicial proof** in assessing liability ([6], [7]).

**A – Application:**

The court scrutinized the evidence presented regarding the **CTG tracings** (cardiotocography) during the critical period of labour, particularly between **20h30 and 21h50**. Expert witnesses for both MM and the MEC provided differing interpretations of these tracings:

1. **MM’s Experts**: They contended that the CTG recordings indicated signs of fetal distress, which should have prompted immediate medical intervention. For example, decelerations were noted at **20h28 and 20h58**, and a pathological tracing was confirmed at **21h32**, necessitating an urgent response ([15], [21]).
   
2. **MEC’s Expert (Prof Pattinson)**: He argued that the tracings were not sufficiently indicative of distress leading to a duty for intervention. His position was ultimately found to diverge from the consensus among other experts and lacked supporting evidence ([21], [32]).

The trial involved detailed examinations of when the **hospital staff** should have recognized the signs of distress, combined with the necessity for monitoring, intervention, and accurate recording of maternal conditions. The court held that the failure to reconnect the CTG after **21h05** exacerbated the situation, with all experts agreeing that monitoring should have continued ([21], [23]).

Ultimately, the MEC’s staff were found to have not met the expected standard of care during the critical period. The anticipated intervention following the identification of fetal distress was not undertaken ([32]).

**C – Conclusion:**

The **Supreme Court of Appeal** upheld the lower court’s findings that the MEC was liable for the injuries sustained by OM. The ruling confirmed that the appellant had failed to demonstrate adherence to the standard of care required in the circumstances of the case, leading to the injury caused by **hospital negligence** ([35]). 

**Implications**: This case emphasizes the stringent obligations upon healthcare providers, particularly in emergency settings, to adequately monitor and react to indications of fetal distress. The ruling also reiterates the importance of expert evidence in establishing negligence, underscoring that in healthcare, practitioners are expected to meet the prevailing standards within their profession. The costs awarded highlighted the court's recognition of the legal and evidential complexities involved in such cases.
