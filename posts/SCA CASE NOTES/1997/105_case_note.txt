# Case Note: Relier (Pty) Limited v Commissioner for Inland Revenue

### FIRAC Method

---

### F – Facts

The present appeal, case No: 256/96, concerns Relier (Pty) Limited (the Appellant) contesting an assessment by the Commissioner for Inland Revenue (the Respondent), concerning the inclusion of certain building costs in its gross income under the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962. The matter arose after Relier appealed against an assessment issued to it, which was initially heard and materially unsuccessful before the Transvaal Special Court for Income Tax Appeals ([para 1]).

To understand the context of this appeal, it is crucial to consider the transactions that took place on 29 March 1983, involving Holloway, the sole shareholder of Economic Data Processing (Pty) Ltd (EDP), and Mr. Anderson, the managing director of National Acceptances Ltd. Responding to tax efficiency concerns, Holloway entered into a series of transactions that involved the sale and lease of land, orchestrated to facilitate the construction of a building for EDP, with Relier as a key participant. Holloway sold the properties to Relier for R100,000 and subsequently leased those properties to a provident fund controlled by National Acceptances, under conditions that heavily restricted modification of the properties by the tenant. Notably, EDP was required to finance building costs of R820,000 [paras 2–8].

The transactions were constructed so that the Fund was perceived as the tenant of the property, while EDP was to erect a building at its expense, allowing Relier to benefit from capital gains without direct tax liability owing to its passive role [paras 7–8]. Subsequent assessments rendered the building costs as part of Relier’s gross income, leading to a diminishing return from the original claim [para 9]. The thrust of the appeal rested on whether Relier had any enforceable right under the lease agreements, particularly concerning improvements on the land.

---

### I – Issues

The primary legal question presented for determination was whether Relier had established, on a balance of probabilities, that it was not entitled to include the building costs as part of its gross income under the Income Tax Act. This inquiry hinged on whether a right had accrued to Relier, under an agreement relating to the grant of the right of use or occupation of the land by another person [paras 9–10].

Additionally, the court examined whether the structure of the transactions constituted a mere simulation, aimed at circumventing tax liability, which involved interpreting the intent behind the interrelated agreements [para 11].

---

### R – Rules

The case fundamentally engages with the provisions laid out in the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962, notably the definition of 'gross income' as stipulated in Section 1 and the allowances set forth in Section 11(g), which pertain to claims for capital allowances on improvements made to property [para 3].

Furthermore, the court referenced the precedent established in *Erf 3183/1 Ladysmith (Pty) Ltd and another v Commissioner for Inland Revenue* 1996 (3) SA 942 (A), wherein it was affirmed that the accrual of a right within the context of property improvements necessitates a consideration of substance over form, alongside the applicability of common-law principles to nullify tax-avoidance schemes [paras 10–11]. 

The determination of whether the parties entered into simulated transactions would be critically assessed against the backdrop of the legal requirement that all or any agreements must reflect the intent of the parties, rather than the mere structural form created for tax benefits [para 11].

---

### A – Application

Upon examining the evidence and the structure of the transactions, the court noted that the interconnectedness of these dealings appeared as a "package deal," one that was designed to achieve a specific tax outcome rather than substantive economic benefit [para 9]. The rental arrangements and the stipulation of obligations allocated to EDP revealed a lack of genuine intent to engage in substantive occupational leasing, suggesting a simulation in the construction of the agreements [para 12].

While the agreements ostensibly reflected an arrangement that adhered to legal formalities, a closer analysis pointed to a reality where the Fund and EDP did not intend to execute the obligations imposed under the agreements, particularly concerning the conditions around property improvements. The arrangement was found to confer no palpable benefit to the tenant (the Fund) while imposing burdens that suggested an alignment with the outcomes favorable to Relier and Holloway's interests [paras 12–14].

Harms JA critically assessed the evidence demonstrating that the parties involved, specifically Holloway, did not fully grasp the implications of the agreements, leading to a determination that the contracts did not reflect their true intentions. It was concluded that the interposition of the Fund was merely a simulated arrangement aimed at tax evasion [paras 14–15].

Consequently, the appeal was dismissed as the court found Robust reasoning from the Special Court supporting the notion that Relier maintained an enforceable right regarding the improvements and thus its gross income included the rents relating to that scheme. The findings aligned with prior jurisprudence indicated that indulgence in tax avoidance through simulated agreements was fundamentally at odds with the revenue statutes in question [paras 10–11, 15].

---

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal of Relier (Pty) Limited, affirming that the building costs constituted part of Relier's gross income, as it possessed an enforceable right under the structured agreements. The court elucidated the implications of their judgment, underscoring that tax avoidance schemes, even when sophisticated, would not prevail if found to be merely simulations lacking substance [paras 11–15].


This case serves as a significant reminder that tax arrangements must conform not only to formal requirements but also to the substantive realities of the arrangements. The ruling sends a clear message that courts will look beyond the documentation to comprehend the real intent of the parties, emphasizing that tax planning strategies must align with permissible legal frameworks and genuine business intents.
