Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

### FIRAC Case Note: Ausplow (Pty) Ltd v Northpark Trading 3 (Pty) Ltd and Others

**Neutral Citation**: Ausplow v Northpark Trading (742/10) [2011] ZASCA 123 (7 September 2011)

**Court**: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Coram**: Harms AP, Ponnan, Snyders, Malan JJA, Plasket AJA

---

#### F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([1–6])

The case revolves around an appeal involving Ausplow (Pty) Ltd ("the patentee") against a judgment from the Court of the Commissioner of Patents where the patentee's application to amend its patent ZA 95/0812, titled "Improvements in or relating to seeding machinery," was dismissed. The original patent was granted on 29 November 1995, claiming priority from an earlier Australian patent application ([1]).

Northpark Trading 3 (Pty) Ltd ("the first respondent") had counterclaimed for the revocation of the patent on grounds of lack of novelty and obviousness after the patentee alleged infringement. The Commissioner of Patents, Southwood J, initially found the patent valid, issuing a certificate of validity concerning claim 1 of the patent ([3]–[4]). However, this decision was appealed, and the Supreme Court of Appeal ("SCA") held that the invention claimed in that specific claim was obvious, thus revoking the patent provisionally while allowing for an amendment application within a month ([4]).

Subsequent disputes around procedural compliance and the nature of the amendments led to further litigation. The SCA noted that proper legal principles regarding amendments and the original finding of invalidity were not thoroughly understood or applied by the lower court ([5]–[6]).

---

#### I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([9–10], [24])

The primary legal issue in this case is whether the application to amend claims 1 and 13 of the patent should have been granted. This involves distinct inquiries including:

1. **Compliance with SCA's Order**: Did the patentee fulfill the requirements set by the SCA?
2. **Conflict with Patent Act**: Do the proposed amendments conflict with provisions outlined in sections 51(6) or (7) of the Patents Act 57 of 1978?
3. **Validity of Amended Claims**: Are the amended claims likely to be valid when considered under the tests for novelty and inventiveness?
4. **Discretionary Refusal**: Should the court's discretion be exercised to refuse an otherwise valid amendment based on factors like delayed application or alleged culpable conduct? ([9], [10]).

---

#### R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([6], [9], [24])

1. **Patents Act 57 of 1978**: Specifically sections 51 and 68, which govern amendments and revocation conditions respectively ([7], [9]).
2. **Case Law**: The court relied on precedents, notably *Ensign-Bickford (SA) v AECI Explosives and Chemicals* as it related to discretionary powers regarding amendments, and various decisions clarifying the standards for obviousness and novelty ([9], [24]).
3. The principles concerning obviousness were traced through previous rulings focusing on whether the invention was non-obvious to a person skilled in the art, aligning with statutory definitions of "inventive step" ([24]).

---

#### A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([11–48])

The court examined each inquiry systematically:

- **Compliance with SCA's Order**: The patentee was found to have properly applied for amendments respecting the timeline set by the SCA. The responses from respondents were seen as lacking in merit, particularly since they acknowledged compliance with legal procedural norms ([10], [11]).

- **Conflict with Patent Act**: The amendments did not conflict with the provisions stated in the Act per the respondents' own admissions, reinforcing the patentee's arguments against standing objections ([10]).

- **Validity of Amended Claims**: The court analyzed the inventive step required under the Act, concluding that the amendments would likely not suffer from obviousness due to the incorporation of new elements and combinations that met statutory standards ([26]). This assessment pointed towards the court's greater reliance on thorough evidence rather than conjectural arguments against validity that lacked evidentiary support ([41]–[44]).

- **Discretionary Refusal**: The court addressed concerns surrounding alleged culpable conduct. The respondents' claims that the patentee acted in bad faith were dismissed on the grounds that the patentee did not knowingly pursue an invalid patent; multiple court findings had previously confirmed the patent's validity ([46], [48]).

---

#### C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([49])

The SCA ruled in favor of the patentee, allowing the proposed amendments to claims 1 and 13. The court determined that the amendments would cure the identified invalidity while discontinuing the provisional order of revocation. It reiterated the importance of ensuring that the patent system remains accessible and fair for innovators, thus balancing public interest against proprietary rights in patent law.

In summary, the court's decision reinforces the necessity of thorough evidentiary support for claims of obviousness and the proper exercise of discretion regarding amendments to patent claims. The implication is that patent holders may now pursue amendments under clearer guidelines, provided they can substantiate the inventive merits of their claims. The ruling emphasizes a willingness of the SCA to provide a pathway for amendments that rectify issues of validity whilst adhering to statutory requirements and procedural fairness.
