Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: A B v Pridwin Preparatory School (1134/2017) [2018] ZASCA 150

## F – Facts
The case involves an appeal concerning the termination of contracts between Pridwin Preparatory School (the School) and the parents of two children, AB and CB, whose children were enrolled at the School, DB and EB. The contracts were governed by a termination clause (clause 9.3), allowing the School to terminate for any reason provided that it gives a full term’s notice.

The relationship deteriorated after several incidents of misconduct by AB, including aggressive confrontations with School staff over various issues, which created a toxic atmosphere. The School maintained that AB's conduct not only affected the learning environment but necessitated termination of the contracts in a manner preserving the children's interests ([1]–[9]).

After repeated breaches by the appellants, the principal, Mr. Marx, exercised the termination clause on June 30, 2016, granting notice until the end of the academic year. The appellants contested the termination, claiming it violated their right to fair procedures under the Constitution, specifically citing sections 28(2) (the best interests of the child) and 29(1) (the right to basic education) ([23]–[25], [38]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issues to be resolved by the court included:
1. Whether the School was obligated to provide a hearing before terminating the contracts, given the constitutional rights of the children involved ([2]–[3], [23]–[25]).
2. Whether the termination clause itself was contrary to public policy and unconstitutional due to its failure to mandate a fair process ([2]–[3], [27]–[29]).
3. The validity of applying sections 28(2) and 29(1) of the Constitution to a private contract ([25]–[40]).

## R – Rules
The judgment cited several legal authorities:
1. The concept of **pacta sunt servanda**, meaning agreements must be honored, as the framework for contractual obligations ([27]).
2. The application of public policy principles in contractual terms, where an obligation can be imposed if deemed contrary to constitutional values ([27]).
3. Constitutional interpretation principles emphasizing the **best interests of the child** and the obligation to consider children's rights in decisions affecting them ([28], [30], [38]).

Relevant case law included **Barkhuizen v Napier**, which elucidates the balance between contractual freedom and the constitutional imperative of fairness, and **S v M**, which contextualizes the application of the best interests principle ([27], [30], [68]).

## A – Application
The court first examined the appellants' claim for a fair hearing. It noted that while constitutional principles apply, there is no blanket right to a hearing in all contract terminations, particularly under private school contracts. The School's obligation to act in the children's best interests was executed in that they were provided with more than the contractual notice period, which was a reasonable exercise of discretion given the misconduct surrounding AB's behavior ([31]–[35]).

As for the claim that the termination clause violated public policy, the court found no grounds to declare the clause itself unconstitutional since it provides for notice and does not infringe on moral or public values. The appellants failed to demonstrate that the enforcement of the clause would cause substantial harm, as ample alternative schooling options existed ([77]–[80], [81]).

Thus, the School's conduct was deemed reasonable under the circumstances, considering its duty to the entire student body, not just the appellants' children ([27]–[30]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the High Court's ruling that the School acted within its contractual rights, and its decision to terminate the contracts was lawful ([81]–[82]). The court underscored the duality of rights: those of the appellants' children against the collective interests of the School community. This case reaffirms the contractual integrity while contextualizing it within constitutional frameworks, particularly regarding children's rights in educational contexts. 

The implications of this ruling stress the necessity for private educational institutions to act responsibly but also uphold the sanctity of signed agreements—an essential aspect of modern contract law in South Africa.
