Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of **South African Airways (Pty) Ltd v Aviation Union of South Africa** [2011] ZASCA 1 (11 January 2011)

### F – Facts

The case **South African Airways (Pty) Ltd v Aviation Union of South Africa** involves an appeal concerning the interpretation of **section 197** of the **Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995** (LRA). The appellant, South African Airways (SAA), was the former employer of employees whose contracts were transferred to LGM South Africa Facility Managers and Engineers (PTY) Ltd (LGM) following an outsourcing agreement effective from 1 April 2000 ([para 5]) and ending on 31 March 2010 ([para 6]). 

Due to a change in ownership of LGM, SAA terminated the outsourcing agreement effective 30 September 2007, notifying LGM and subsequently calling for tenders for the services previously provided by LGM ([paras 7–8]). The affective employees, represented by the Aviation Union of South Africa (AUSA), sought confirmation from SAA regarding their employment status post-termination of the outsourcing agreement ([paras 9–10]).

The Labour Court, in its initial ruling, concluded that there had not been a transfer of employees under section 197 LRA, indicating that the section only applies to the first transfer of a business as a going concern ([paras 12–14]). The Labour Appeal Court, however, found that a second-generation transfer could also fall under section 197's protections ([para 16]). SAA appealed this decision, claiming errors in statutory interpretation and factual inaccuracies surrounding the concept of a business transfer as a going concern.

### I – Issues

The central issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:

1. **Interpretation of Section 197**: Whether the Labour Appeal Court correctly interpreted section 197 to allow for "second-generation outsourcing" transfers and if so, under what circumstances it applies ([paras 20–21]).
   
2. **Existence of a Transfer**: Whether a transfer of business as a going concern had occurred, fulfilling the requirements of section 197 regarding transfer from the old employer to the new employer ([paras 20–22]).

3. **Role of the Courts**: The appropriateness of the application of a purposive interpretation of section 197 to protect employees in the context of second-generation outsourcing ([paras 32–33]).

### R – Rules

The legal framework that guided the court’s ruling primarily involved:

1. **Section 197 of the Labour Relations Act**: It outlines conditions regarding the automatic transfer of contracts of employment upon the transfer of a business as a going concern, including the rights and obligations of employees ([para 3]).

2. **Judicial Interpretive Principles**: The court emphasized the need to respect the literal language of legislation, as established in cases such as **South African Police Service v Public Servants Association** ([para 30]), and cautioned against "judicial activism" that undermines legislative intent ([para 29]).

3. **Case Law**: Reference was made to **Cosawu v Zikhethele Trade (Pty) Ltd**, which supported the argument for a broader interpretation of transfer to include second-generation outsourcing, though the majority struck a balance with a more literal interpretation ([paras 15, 18]).

### A – Application

In applying these rules to the facts, the court meticulously analyzed the evidence of the contractual relationships and transfers of business:

1. **First and Second Generation Outsourcing**: The court reiterated that for section 197 to apply, two elements must be satisfied—there must be a transfer of a business as a going concern, and that transfer must be executed by the old employer ([para 22]). Ultimately, the court rejected the Labour Appeal Court's sweeping interpretation, maintaining that only the first transfer can be operationalized under section 197 ([paras 19, 33]).

2. **Factual Basis**: The court identified a lack of sufficient evidence to demonstrate that a tangible business transfer occurred from LGM back to SAA or any subsequent contractor ([paras 34–41]). The presence of a contract or a handover plan was not established, hindering claims to continuity of employment rights under section 197.

3. **Purposive Interpretation Rejected**: The majority opinion highlighted that while safeguards for employees are essential, judicial interpretations must not alter unequivocal statutory language. By interpreting “by” to mean “from,” the Labour Appeal Court dangerously broadened the scope beyond legislative intent, potentially undermining the principle of separation of powers ([paras 25, 29]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld SAA’s appeal, concluding that the Labour Appeal Court erred in its interpretation of section 197 and the existence of a business transfer. The appeal was upheld with costs, and the Labour Appeal Court’s order was replaced, dismissing the unions' claims ([paras 42]).

This judgment affirms the necessity for clear evidence and strict adherence to contractual obligations for determining employment status in outsourcing contexts or transfers of business under South African law. The ruling underscores the boundaries of judicial interpretation in labor law, affirming the principle that legislative clarity must not be deceptively construed. The decision has significant implications for how future second-generation outsourcing arrangements are interpreted and the extent of employee protections therein.
