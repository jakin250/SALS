Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2001

# Case Note: Potgieter v. Member of the Executive Council: Health (CCT 26/01)

## FIRAC Method

### Facts
[1] The case arose from a High Court decision where Bertelsmann J declared section 68(4) of the Mental Health Act 18 of 1973 ("the Act") invalid. This section limited the time to initiate legal proceedings related to actions performed under the Act to three months, significantly shorter than the three-year limitation provided by the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 ([1]). 

[4] The applicant, Mr. Frans Barnard Potgieter, an employee of the Presidential Protection Unit, was detained under the Mental Health Act following allegations of family violence. Mr. Potgieter claimed that the actions taken against him were unlawful and sought to sue for damages. However, he needed access to his medical records to substantiate his claim. He applied to the High Court for access to these records in March 2001, arguing that the section 68(4) limitation violated his constitutional rights. The court agreed, granting him access to his records based on his right to information under section 32 of the Constitution ([5]).

### Issues
[5] The central issues before the Constitutional Court were:

1. Whether the three-month limitation period imposed by section 68(4) constituted a reasonable and justifiable limitation of the right of access to the courts under section 34 of the Constitution. 
2. If the limitation was unconstitutional, what should be the retrospective application of such a declaration of invalidity.

### Rules
[6] The relevant legal rules revolve around the interpretation of the Constitution of South Africa. Section 34 guarantees every individual the right to have any dispute resolved before a court of law. Additionally, the court referenced precedents, particularly *Mohlomi v Minister of Defence* and *Moise v Transitional Local Council of Greater Germiston*, where provisions restricting access to the courts were deemed unconstitutional ([7]).

[6-7] The Constitutional Court emphasized that while statutes often provide limitations on bringing claims, these limitations must allow for a "real and fair opportunity" for individuals to enforce their rights, which was not the case with section 68(4). The absence of justification from the respondents further delimited the validity of the contested limitation.

### Application
[8] In applying the constitutional framework to the facts, the Court found that section 68(4) created an "outrageous" and "drastic" limitation on the right to access to the courts, especially given that the affected individuals are often among the most vulnerable members of society, such as those detained under the Mental Health Act ([7]). 

[9] The High Court's decision to make the invalidity of section 68(4) effective retroactively to April 27, 1994, was upheld as being within the court's powers, and the language surrounding the order was re-evaluated to ensure clarity regarding its scope ([8-9]).

### Conclusion
[10] The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court's order declaring section 68(4) unconstitutional and invalid, with retrospective effect, applicable to all actions instituted before or since April 27, 1994, that had not been resolved by final judgment or valid settlement ([10]). The Court reinforced the principle that the right of access to courts is fundamental and should not be unduly limited by legislative provisions. Furthermore, the respondents were ordered to bear the costs of the application.

This ruling not only affirms the protection of constitutional rights within the mental health framework in South Africa but also underscores the essential role of the judiciary in safeguarding individual rights against legislative encroachments.
