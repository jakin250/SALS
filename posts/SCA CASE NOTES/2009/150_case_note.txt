Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2009

# FIRAC Case Note: SouthernEra Resources Ltd v Farndell (664/2008) [2009] ZASCA 150

**F - Facts**  
The case concerns the sale of mineral rights from the Estate of the late Marjorie Diana Dent ("the estate") to SouthernEra Resources Ltd ("the appellant"). The initial agreement was concluded on 30 September 1995, stipulating a cash purchase price of R1,792,269.64, contingent upon the registration of cession of the mineral rights ([para 2]). The appellant was required to furnish a bank guarantee as security for the purchase price within 30 days of signing the agreement ([para 2]). However, complications arose due to the heirship issues within the estate, delaying necessary consents from the Master of the High Court ([para 2]).

An addendum to the original agreement was executed on 14 December 2001, amending the guarantee provision linked to the Master’s consent, necessitating the appellant to furnish the bank guarantee within 14 days of receiving formal notification of that consent ([para 3]). The Master issued the required certificate on 21 April 2004, but the appellant did not respond adequately ([para 4]).

On 1 May 2004, a significant legal change occurred when section 3(1)(m) of the Deeds Registries Act was repealed, prohibiting the registration of cessions of mineral rights ([para 4]). Letters sent by the respondent's attorney on 22 November 2004 invoked the appellant's obligation to provide the bank guarantee, which went unheeded, prompting legal action from the respondent in the North Gauteng High Court ([para 5-6]).

The court a quo held that the sale had become perfecta before the impossibility of registration due to the repeal of the Act, entitling the seller to payment for the mineral rights ([para 7]).

**I - Issues**  
The core issue presented to the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the sale of the mineral rights became perfecta before the repeal of the statutory provision that rendered the registration of cession impossible ([para 1]). This inquiry involved determining if the requirement for the appellant to furnish a bank guarantee constituted a suspensive condition or a mere term of the contract ([para 1], [para 10]). 

### Sub-issues:
1. Was the sale completa by 1 May 2004?
2. Did the appellant's failure to provide the bank guarantee affect the validity of the contract?

**R - Rules**  
1. **Contract Law Principles**: A sale is considered perfect (perfecta) when there is an agreement on the subject (merx) and price (pretium), and all suspensive conditions have been fulfilled ([para 9]). 

2. **Conditions and Terms**: The distinction between suspensive conditions that affect the validity of a contract and enforceable contractual terms ([para 12]). A suspensive condition suspends the execution of the contract until fulfilled, while an enforceable term relates to the obligations within the existing contract after acceptance ([para 12-13]).

3. **Impossibility of Performance**: A party may be discharged from contractual obligations if supervening impossibility occurs after the contract becomes binding ([para 8]).

**A - Application**  
The court determined that the appellant’s obligation to furnish a bank guarantee was an enforceable term of the contract, not a suspensive condition. This conclusion was drawn on two main points: 
- First, once the Master issued consent, the appellant was legally bound to perform its contractual obligation to provide the guarantee ([para 14]).
- Second, the phrasing of the agreement made clear that registration depended on the guarantee, but did not prevent the sale from being perfecta once the Master’s consent was communicated ([para 14-18]).

The argument from the appellant that a clause pertaining to registration of cession constituted a suspensive condition was rejected. The court established that the guarantee's requirement primarily served to protect the respondent from delivering rights without securing payment, which did not negate the completion of the sales agreement ([para 10-17]).

Consequently, because the sale was held to have become perfecta by 1 May 2004, the risk associated with the mineral rights transferred to the appellant, thereby holding them liable for the purchase price, despite the impossibility of performance thereafter ([para 18-19]).

**C - Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, asserting that the sale had indeed become perfecta prior to the repeal of the legislative provision preventing cession ([para 19]). As a result, the risk of loss transferred to the appellant, implying they were liable to pay the purchase price as stipulated in the agreement. The ruling established that delays due to bureaucratic complications did not negate the contractual obligations established by mutual consent. The decision clarifies the nuances of contract law regarding suspensive conditions and the impact of legislative changes on existing agreements, reinforcing obligations that arise once contracts are deemed enforceable. The appellant was consequently ordered to bear the costs of the appeal, reflecting the court's view that the case lacked merit.
