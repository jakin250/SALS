Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# Case Note: Mbambisa and Others v Nelson Mandela Bay Metropolitan Municipality

**Citation**: Mbambisa and Others v Nelson Mandela Bay Metropolitan Municipality (Case no 272/2023) [2024] ZASCA 151 (8 November 2024)

## FIRAC

### F – Facts

The current appeal arises from the actions instituted by the Nelson Mandela Bay Metropolitan Municipality (the Municipality) against former senior officials, namely the first appellant (Mpilo Sakile Mbambisa), second appellant (Mhleli Mlungisi Tshamase), third appellant (Trevor Harper), fifth appellant (Walter Shaidi), and others. The genesis of the case lies in the Municipality’s appointment of Erastyle (Pty) Ltd (Erastyle) in February 2014 for consultancy services regarding the Integrated Public Transport System (IPTS), valued at approximately R6 million, which occurred without following the requisite public tender processes prescribed in the Municipality’s Supply Chain Management Policy (SCM Policy) ([para 1]).

Upon realizing the invalidity of the appointment, the Municipality commenced action on 11 February 2016 in the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, seeking restitution for payments made to Erastyle amounting to R5,263,179.89, R1,390,800, and R984,197.21, reasoning that these expenses constituted unauthorized, irregular, and fruitless expenditure under section 32 of the Local Government: Municipal Finance Management Act 56 of 2003 (MFMA) ([paras 3–4]). The Municipality contended that the officials acted negligently in facilitating these payments, thereby violating their statutory obligations. The first and second claims focused on the unlawfulness of the appointment and associated payments, while the third claim involved allegations of fraudulent misrepresentation ([para 6]).

Subsequently, the High Court ruled in favor of the Municipality on several counts, leading to this appeal by the defendants ([paras 27-29]).

### I – Issues

The principal legal questions presented in this appeal involve:

1. **Statutory Interpretation of Section 32 of the MFMA**: What constitutes the liability of municipal officials under this provision? Is liability contingent on the Municipality's demonstration of loss or damage?
2. **Exclusion of Liability under Section 176(1)**: Can municipal officials claim protection under this provision concerning their duties?
3. **Unreasonable Delay**: Was the Municipality precluded from pursuing its claims against the defendants due to an alleged unreasonable delay in instituting action?
4. **Duplication of Claims**: Were the orders made by the High Court properly formulated, particularly regarding the duplication of claims?

### R – Rules

The judiciary interpreted the following provisions central to the resolution of the case:

1. **Section 32 of the MFMA**: Provides the framework for liability concerning unauthorized, irregular, or fruitless and wasteful expenditure incurred by municipal officials. This section implies that a municipality is mandated to recover such expenditures from the liable individuals, independent of whether the Municipality suffered loss ([para 38]).

2. **Section 176(1) of the MFMA**: Offers immunity to officials from liability regarding loss or damage resulting from actions performed in good faith under the MFMA, which the appellants argued relates to their defense ([para 33]).

3. **Undue Delay in Administrative Action**: The principle governing the timeliness of challenges to administrative decisions was assessed, particularly the implications of such delays on the possibility of reviewing administrative actions ([para 57]).

4. **Legal Precedents**: Previous decisions on statutory interpretation, especially regarding the role and responsibility of municipal officials. The court mentioned Capitec Bank Holdings Ltd v Coral Lagoon Investments 194 (Pty) Ltd and Others regarding the interpretation of statutes using the broader context and purpose rather than mere lexicon ([para 40]).

### A – Application

The court effectively scrutinized the wording of section 32, asserting that it must be interpreted in its entirety. Notably, the court emphasized that the statutory framework does not require a showing of loss to establish liability: "Section 32 is not conditional upon a municipality sustaining loss or damage" ([para 56]). This interpretation aligns with parliamentary intent to ensure accountability within municipal financial management, reinforcing that officials can be held liable simply based on the incurrence of irregular expenditures ([para 52]).

The defendants contended that the existence of protection under section 176(1) negated their liability. However, the court clarified that this section does not mitigate the obligations under section 32. The court stressed that the liabilities arising under section 32 are distinct from those arising under section 176, effectively dismissing the argument that this provision could shield them from the claims of the Municipality ([para 54]).

On the matter of delay, the court concluded that any alleged unreasonable delay was irrelevant in this case, particularly in light of section 32's independent framework which does not necessitate prior judicial review of decisions to recover contested expenditures ([para 58]). 

### C – Conclusion

The appeal was upheld partly, modifying the High Court's findings with respect to the claims against certain individual defendants while maintaining liability for the unauthorized and irregular expenditures under section 32 of the MFMA. The court ultimately ordered the defendants to pay the stated amounts, aligning with the principle that public funds must be safeguarded against irregular expenditures ([para 63]).

This judgment underscores the stringent accountability measures officials hold under the MFMA and affirms the necessity for municipalities to adhere to lawful processes in public procurement and financial management. The implications of this case extend to other municipalities, serving as a precedent reinforcing the legislative intent behind the MFMA to promote good governance and financial accountability. 

In sum, the South African judiciary remains vigilant regarding adherence to procedural frameworks in municipal finance, safeguarded by statutory instruments aimed at preventing maladministration and misuse of public funds.
