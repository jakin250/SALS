Supreme Court of Appeal

Member of the Executive Council for the Department of Health, Eastern Cape v M[....] (213/2021) [2022] ZASCA 140 (24 October 2022)

The case arose from a civil claim for damages for medical negligence brought by the respondent, M[....] (the plaintiff), against the appellant, the Member of the Executive Council for the Department of Health, Eastern Cape (MEC) ([1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 140 (24 October 2022)

F - Facts

The case arose from a civil claim for damages for medical negligence brought by the respondent, M[....] (the plaintiff), against the appellant, the Member of the Executive Council for the Department of Health, Eastern Cape (MEC) ([1]). The plaintiff claimed damages in both personal and representative capacities on behalf of her minor child, who allegedly suffered harm during birth at Mthatha General Hospital, a facility under the authority of the MEC. The action commenced in July 2015, and by October 2019, Dawood J had found the MEC liable for the damages suffered by the respondent ([2]).

Subsequent court orders directed various settlement conferences and pre-trial arrangements, all aimed at resolving the quantum of damages. On 28 November 2019, Brooks J scheduled several points for resolution, culminating in a further postponement to allow for settlement discussions ([3]) and later meetings on 16 and 19 March 2020, facilitating the agreement of certain quantifiable damages ([4]-[6]).

However, when the case was brought before Griffiths J on 23 March 2020, although the parties’ legal representatives had reached an agreement on the damages to be paid, the MEC’s representatives indicated they did not have instructions to settle based on that quantum ([8]). To this end, a rule nisi was issued, compelling the Superintendent General of the Department of Health to show cause why an order should not be granted in the terms proposed by the parties' representatives ([8]-[9]).

On 30 June 2020, Brooks J confirmed the rule nisi, referencing the supposed binding nature of the agreement reached by the legal representatives ([10]). After the MEC was denied leave to appeal in the high court, further application resulted in leave being granted to the Supreme Court of Appeal to examine if the rule nisi was appropriately issued given the lack of authority to settle ([11]).

I - Issues

The primary issues before the court were:

  1. Whether it was competent for the high court to issue a rule nisi in a situation where the legal representatives of the MEC did not have proper authority to agree to settle the quantum of damages as per the pre-trial agreements ([11]-[12]).
  2. The implications of judicially directing a settlement without the litigant’s consent in an adversarial system ([25]-[26]).

R - Rules

In determining the validity of the rule nisi, the court examined the nature and procedural requirements of the rule nisi itself, which traditionally calls for an affected party to articulate why relief should not be granted ([12]). The court acknowledged that this process emerges from common law and is governed by the principles of fairness (audi alteram partem) and the necessity of a prima facie case being made ([13]-[15]).

Furthermore, there are specific limitations placed on the authority of legal representatives within the context of litigation, especially regarding the management of claims without explicit instructions from their principals ([19]-[20]). The ruling underscored that any agreement must reflect the will of the client for it to be binding, which in this case was not satisfied ([18]).

A - Application

The court critically assessed both the procedural conduct and the substantive implications of the rule nisi issued on 23 March 2020. The Court noted the absence of proper evidence to support the rule, as the matter had not proceeded to a trial where evidence could have been led ([17]-[24]).

The Court emphasized that the rule nisi had called upon the MEC to justify non-compliance with what was deemed a firm agreement, despite the lack of authority from the legal representatives to bind the MEC ([23]-[24]). The principles of litigant autonomy formed a significant counterpoint, asserting that parties must not be compelled to settle without their explicit instructions being followed, promoting the right to access impartial adjudication as mandated by the South African Constitution, Section 34 ([25]-[26]).

The Court also alluded to past critiques of the MEC’s conduct in similar cases as indicative of systemic failures in managing litigation effectively, which had precipitated the issuance of the rule nisi. Despite these concerns, the Court found that the manner in which the rule had been deployed was itself fundamentally flawed ([27]-[28]).

C - Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal discharged the rule nisi on grounds that it was procedurally and substantively incorrect to have been issued in the first place. The appeal was upheld, and new orders were set, replacing the high court's confidences with a clear directive that no costs would be incurred ([29]-[30]).

The ruling reiterates fundamental principles of litigation autonomy, specifically that parties must consent to terms negotiated on their behalf, emphasizing the limits of legal representatives’ authority in settlements. The implications of this ruling extend toward reinforcing judicial integrity in the face of systemic litigation challenges faced by entities like the Department of Health in South Africa, thus shaping future approaches in cases of medical negligence and state liability.

This case contributes to the ongoing discourse about the accountability of public officials and the mechanics of achieving just resolutions in civil litigation, safeguarding the rights of litigants to engage autonomously in their legal disputes whilst underlining the necessity of procedural propriety in judicial decisions.
