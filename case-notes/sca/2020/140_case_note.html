THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA - Afribusiness NPC v Minister of Finance

Before the regulations were finalized, the Minister had published Draft Procurement Regulations for public comment.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The case revolved around the validity of the Preferential Procurement Regulations, 2017 (the 2017 Regulations) promulgated by the Minister of Finance (the Minister) under Section 5 of the Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act 5 of 2000 (the Framework Act). The appellant, Afribusiness NPC (Afribusiness), an organization representing a significant number of business members, challenged these regulations after they were adopted on 20 January 2017 ([para 1–2]).

Before the regulations were finalized, the Minister had published Draft Procurement Regulations for public comment. Afribusiness raised concerns about the adequacy of the public comment period, which was later extended from 15 July 2016 to 23 September 2016 ([para 4–6]). Despite submitting comments within this framework, Afribusiness felt that the regulations were promulgated without sufficient opportunity for meaningful public participation. On 19 May 2017, Afribusiness sought legal redress, claiming the Minister acted ultra vires and failed to meet the requirements of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([para 5–7]).

The High Court dismissed Afribusiness' application and upheld the Minister’s defenses, including his assertion that the regulations’ application is discretionary and within his legal authority ([para 8]). Consequently, Afribusiness appealed the High Court's decision with the support of the South African Property Owners’ Association NPC (SAPOA) as amicus curiae ([para 9–10]).

I – Issues

The principal legal issues in this case are:

  1. Did the Minister exceed his powers under Section 5 of the Framework Act when promulgating the 2017 Regulations?
  2. Were the 2017 Regulations invalid due to failing to adhere to requirements of transparency and fairness as outlined in Section 217 of the South African Constitution?
  3. Is the procedure followed by the Minister in the creation of the regulations sufficient to satisfy PAJA's standard for public participation? ([para 14]).

R – Rules

The legal framework for this case is primarily derived from:

  1. Section 2 of the Framework Act, requiring a preference point system for procurement and stating that contracts must be awarded to those scoring the highest points unless objective criteria justify awarding to another ([para 19]).
  2. Sections 217(1) and (2) of the South African Constitution mandate a procurement system that is fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective, allowing for categories of preference to those historically disadvantaged.
  3. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA), which governs the procedural fairness requirements for administrative actions.
  4. Precedent established in cases such as Minister of Home Affairs and Another v Public Protector of the Republic of South Africa and Airports Company South Africa SOC Ltd v Imperial Group Ltd and Others that underline the importance of legality in public procurement ([para 15, 39]).

A – Application

The court first determined whether the regulations were within the Minister's legislative authority. It was found that the Minister's powers are not free from constraints; regulations must align with both the objectives of the Framework Act and the constitutional framework of procurement ([para 36]). It highlighted that the pre-qualification criteria introduced by the 2017 Regulations deviate from transparency and equity, as they allow for a pre-qualification process that could effectively disqualify tenderers without considering their overall tender ([para 38]).

The judgment emphasized that the Minister's discretion in implementing these regulations lacked the necessary framework to ensure fairness and equitable competition, a requirement under both the Framework Act and the Constitution ([para 40]). Furthermore, the court found that the manner in which the Minister promulgated the regulations failed the test of rationality under PAJA which necessitates that decisions must be based on relevant information, thereby leading to arbitrary regulations that risk being misused ([para 39–41]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal held that the 2017 Regulations were invalid as they did not conform to the Framework Act and the constitutional requirements of fairness and transparency. The court declared the regulations invalid but suspended this declaration for 12 months to allow the Minister to amend the regulations appropriately ([para 47]). This ruling underscores the principle of legality and the necessity for proper procedure in the exercise of public power, embodying core constitutional values in the context of public procurement. The case establishes a significant precedent for future procurement legislation, emphasizing the need for both adherence to the law and the importance of public participation in the legislative process ([para 46]).

This FIRAC case note can serve as a comprehensive legal reference for understanding the court's findings and implications regarding administrative discretion and public procurement in South Africa.
