# FIRAC Case Note: Cohen v. Cohen [2002] ZASCA 10

**F – Facts**  
The case involves an appeal before the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa concerning maintenance obligations following a divorce. The appellant, Roy Selwyn Cohen, and the respondent, Brenda Cohen (née Coleman), were divorced in February 1995. This divorce included a consent paper that specified maintenance payments from the appellant to the respondent until certain conditions were met, notably that she either remarried or lived with another man as if married for over six months in one year. The appellant subsequently sought modifications of these maintenance payments, citing that the respondent was now employed and requiring less support. 

Initially, in January 1998, the maintenance court reduced the payment from R 3,000 to R 1,500 per month. However, after a series of hearings and further applications from both parties, a magistrate, Mr. Venter, issued a new order in December 1998, increasing the maintenance to R 3,500. Importantly, this order did not reference the "dum casta" clause establishing conditions for terminating maintenance ([paras 1–8]).

In January 2000, the appellant sought a declaratory order from the High Court, arguing that the respondent had violated the "dum casta" condition and thus he was relieved of maintenance obligations. The respondent contended that the order from the maintenance court had effectively deleted this clause, thereby negating its enforceability. 

**I – Issues**  
The central legal question in this case is whether the maintenance court's order to vary the amount of maintenance payments also altered the enforceability of the "dum casta" clause embedded in the original Supreme Court order, which specified conditions under which the obligation to pay maintenance would cease ([paras 1–10]).

**R – Rules**  
The judgment examines several statutory provisions and case law relevant to maintenance orders, particularly the Maintenance Act 99 of 1998, especially sections 16(1)(b)(i) and 22. Section 16(1)(b)(i) empowers the maintenance court to make a maintenance order that substitutes a prior order, while section 22 mandates notification when any such orders are made or discharged ([para 15]). The principles of statutory interpretation are also invoked, particularly regarding whether a variation of one part of an order implies variation of other unrelated clauses. 

Precedential authority within the judgments discussed (e.g., *Purnell v Purnell* 1993 (2) SA 662 (A)) establishes that the variation of maintenance amounts does not inherently affect or nullify other specific terms if those are not explicitly amended in the new order. This reflects a general principle where orders should be interpreted restrictively concerning their intended effect and scope ([paras 12–19]).

**A – Application**  
The court examined the intent and scope of the magistrate's order in the maintenance court. It was determined that the order was strictly limited to varying the amount of maintenance payments without addressing the status of the "dum casta" condition. The magistrate's failure to mention or act upon this clause indicated to the court that the clause remained enforceable ([paras 12–14]). The rationale behind the ruling emphasized that unless explicitly revised, existing conditions within the consent paper continue to prevail. 

Arguments presented against the retention of the "dum casta" clause misapplied the principle of total replacement indicated in the Maintenance Act, as the variation order concerned only the monetary component and did not imply a wider alteration of the original consent ([paras 16–18]).

The court reasoned that if the maintenance court had intended to nullify the "dum casta" clause, it would have needed to either include it explicitly in its order or address it as part of the discussion. The absence of any reference indicated that the magistrate's focus was solely on current financial circumstances, not the fundamental terms that defined the maintenance obligations ([paras 12–19]).

**C – Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, asserting that the maintenance court's order did not vary or remove the "dum casta" clause. As a result, the appellant’s obligation to pay maintenance was reinstated under the conditions specified in the original consent paper. The court affirmed the principle that variations to maintenance orders must be interpreted with respect to their specific scope and that unaddressed terms remain intact ([paras 20]). Given the court's reasoning, parties seeking modifications to maintenance agreements must ensure that any terms they wish to alter are explicitly stated in the orders sought. 

The judgment has implications for how maintenance orders are framed and amended, emphasizing the importance of clarity in drafting and the need for comprehensive context when presenting maintenance applications.
