Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: Ntlemeza v Helen Suzman Foundation [2017] ZASCA 93

**FIRAC Method**

## F - Facts

In this case, the appellant Lieutenant-General Mthandazo Berning Ntlemeza (General Ntlemeza) appealed against a decision of the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, which had affirmed the appointment of a new leader for the Directorate for Priority Crime Investigations (DPCI), made by the Minister of Police. General Ntlemeza was initially appointed to the National Head position of DPCI on 10 September 2015, following a term as the acting head ([paras 1-2]).

The appointment was challenged by the first respondent, Helen Suzman Foundation (HSF), and the second respondent, Freedom Under Law (FUL), on grounds that it was irrational and did not fulfill the constitutional requirement for such an officer to be a "fit and proper" person ([paras 6, 13]). Central to the challenge were previous findings by Matojane J, who had made adverse comments regarding General Ntlemeza's integrity in a separate matter involving the suspension of Major General Shadrack Sibiya ([paras 8-9]).

Following the High Court's dismissal of General Ntlemeza's application for leave to appeal and the granting of an execution order allowing HSF and FUL to enforce the ruling to set aside his appointment, the appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal was lodged ([paras 16-17]). The legal question revolved around the interaction of these various proceedings and precisely when execution of a judgment could take place pending appeals ([paras 3, 17]).

## I - Issues

The core legal issues identified in the appeal were:

1. Whether a refusal of an application for leave to appeal negates the legal basis for an execution order ([paras 17, 27]).
2. Whether General Ntlemeza could demonstrate exceptional circumstances as required under section 18(1) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 which would allow for execution before finalization of the appeal process ([paras 35-39]).
3. Whether the respondents could prove on a balance of probabilities that they would suffer irreparable harm if the execution order were not granted ([paras 46-47]).

## R - Rules

The relevant legal framework encompassed the following rules and principles:

- **Section 18 of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013** outlines the circumstances under which an execution order may be granted pending appeals. Specifically, it requires a demonstration of exceptional circumstances for an execution order to be granted, and necessitates proof that the applicant will suffer irreparable harm unless the order is issued, while the opposite must be true for the respondent ([paras 22-24]).
- Previous case law, including *South Cape Corporation (Pty) Ltd v Engineering Management Services (Pty)* and *Incubeta Holdings & another v Ellis & another*, established that execution of a judgment is ordinarily stayed pending an appeal, and that applications for execution depend on the unique facts of each case ([paras 19-20, 36-39]).
- The principle set forth in *Democratic Alliance v President of the Republic of South Africa & others* was referenced regarding the requisite integrity and fitness for the role when appointing the head of significant state bodies ([paras 13-14]).

## A - Application

The court approached the application of the rules in light of the unique facts of General Ntlemeza's case. The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the dismissal of General Ntlemeza's earlier application for leave did not extinguish the possibility of further appeal processes. Section 18(1) of the Act was construed to mean that rules around suspension of judgment pending appeal remain in force even if a leave to appeal was refused ([para 27]).

Moreover, the necessity of demonstrating exceptional circumstances was explored in detail. The court emphasized that HSF and FUL had, indeed, articulated such circumstances due to the public interest at stake in having a person of integrity leading the DPCI, particularly in combating corruption ([paras 39-40, 45]). The court also determined that the assertions of irreparable harm raised by General Ntlemeza were outweighed by the potential harm to the public interest if he continued to hold office while under scrutiny ([para 47]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed General Ntlemeza's appeal on several grounds, noting particularly the significant public interest in ensuring that the DPCI head is unimpeachable in terms of integrity and fitness ([para 50]). The court reaffirmed the necessity of both exceptional circumstances and a balanced assessment of harm to evaluate applications for execution orders.

The implications of this decision were significant: it underscored the judiciary's role in maintaining integrity within institutions that uphold the rule of law and illustrated the strict standards applied to appointments within key governmental roles. Therefore, the ruling confirmed the framework established by the Superior Courts Act and reinforced the principle that public interest must prevail in cases involving the functionality of key state institutions. General Ntlemeza was ordered to personally bear the costs, which further reflected the weight of the legal proceedings initiated against him ([para 51]).
