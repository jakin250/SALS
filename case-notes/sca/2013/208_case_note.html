Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 208

F – Facts

The case involves Mr. Farouk Meyer, the appellant, who sought special leave to appeal a judgment from the Witwatersrand Local Division dismissing his appeal against convictions and sentences imposed by the trial court ([para 1]). The incidents leading to these convictions stemmed from a shooting at Club 12 Play in Roodepoort on 17 October 1999, where three individuals were killed and another was injured ([para 2]). Meyer faced multiple charges, including three counts of murder, one count of attempted murder, and one count of assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm. The trial court's sentences varied significantly, with two murder counts resulting in life imprisonment and other sentences ranging from six months to ten years for lesser offences.

After approximately 11 years post-conviction, Meyer filed an application for special leave to appeal, accompanied by requests for condonation for the lateness of the application and the introduction of new evidence ([paras 1–3]).

Procedural History

The trial court found Meyer guilty based on conflicting testimonies regarding the shooting. The State, represented by a series of eyewitnesses, argued Meyer had acted aggressively. Conversely, Meyer claimed self-defence amidst a larger conflict involving gang members ([paras 5–6]). The trial court's rejection of his version of events relied upon evidentiary support from independent witnesses, including the club manager and the head of security, who directly observed the shooting events ([paras 10–12]).

Meyer attempted to introduce new witnesses and evidence in his appeal regarding alleged mishandling of ballistic evidence by the police, which the court dismissed for lack of relevance, as it did not pertain to matters requiring fresh evidence ([para 13]).

I – Issues

The main legal issues needing adjudication pertained to:

  1. Whether the court should grant special leave to appeal against convictions based on the evidence presented.
  2. The merit of introducing new evidence at the appeal stage.
  3. The appropriateness of the life sentences imposed by the trial court, particularly in light of procedural misdirections regarding the minimum sentencing legislation ([paras 4, 13, 15]).

R – Rules

The applicable rules and precedents include:

  • S v Senkhane (2011), which outlines the standard for condonation applications in relation to non-compliance and prospects of success ([para 3]).
  • Smith v S (2012), regarding the determination of reasonable prospects of success on appeal, stipulating that the appellant must provide grounds demonstrating a real chance of a different conclusion than that reached by the trial court ([para 4]).
  • S v Legoa (2003) and S v Makatu (2006), which establish the prosecution's responsibility to notify an accused of any intention to apply minimum sentencing legislation, thereby safeguarding the constitutional right to a fair trial ([para 16]).

A – Application

Condonation and Leave to Appeal

The court evaluated Meyer’s application for condonation for the late filing of his appeal, considering the explanation for the delay that cited the need to gather proof of being framed ([para 3]). However, the court found this lackluster against the lengthy delay and the substantial evidence in support of the convictions ([para 4]). While he requested to appeal the convictions, the court found no reasonable prospects that would warrant a different conclusion from that reached by the trial court given corroborating eyewitness accounts ([para 12]).

New Evidence

Meyer's attempt to present new evidence also failed; the court dismissed proposed witnesses as irrelevant or unnecessary, particularly as the core allegations concerning ballistic evidence had already been addressed during the trial. The court noted that one of the proposed witnesses had recanted testimony that was integral to the earlier findings, rendering it inadmissible ([para 13]).

Sentencing

On the sentences, the court identified a significant procedural misdirection in the initial application of the minimum sentencing provisions. The prosecution had not informed Meyer of the intention to apply such provisions at trial, which would have altered defence strategies regarding the charges ([para 15]). Therefore, while acknowledging the severity of Meyer’s actions, the court opted to revise the sentences. It imposed a new sentence of 24 years for the murder counts and ensured that various sentences would run concurrently, acknowledging Meyer’s long incarceration period and the lack of prior convictions as mitigating factors ([paras 15–18]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded:

  1. Condonation was granted for the late filing of the application for special leave to appeal.
  2. The appeal against the convictions was dismissed, finding no reasonable prospects of success based on the evidence presented.
  3. The application to lead new evidence was also dismissed on similar grounds of irrelevance and lack of material change in circumstances.
  4. Special leave to appeal regarding the sentences was granted, resulting in the life sentences being reduced to 24 years’ imprisonment due to a significant procedural misdirection regarding minimum sentencing laws.

The ruling serves as a reminder of the prosecution's duty to inform the accused adequately of minimum sentencing implications, safeguarding the principles of fair trial rights in South African criminal law ([paras 15–18]).
