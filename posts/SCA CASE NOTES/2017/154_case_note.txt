# Case Note: Carneiro v The State (1040/2016) ZASCA 154

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts

Jose Pedro Morais Carneiro (the appellant) was convicted of murder by the Regional Court in Johannesburg on 12 September 2006. Following his conviction, he received a sentence of seven years’ imprisonment, which he subsequently appealed. The appeal against the conviction was dismissed by the Gauteng Local Division on 1 December 2010. Dissatisfied with the outcome, Carneiro filed an application for leave to appeal against the dismissal of his appeal on 8 December 2010. This application was heard by the same court on 29 April 2016 but was subsequently struck off the roll due to jurisdictional concerns, leading the appellant to seek special leave to appeal this decision ([1]–[3]).

The crux of the lower court’s reasoning for striking the application involved its interpretation of sections 16(1)(a) and (b) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 (the new Act), which suggested that the appellant needed special leave from the Supreme Court of Appeal to pursue his application ([4]). The Gauteng Local Division held that the new Act was regulatory in nature and, therefore, applied retrospectively to Carneiro's case, which had been pending since its initiation in 2006. The court relied incorrectly on precedents from previous cases, notably Nkabinde & Another v Judicial Service Commission & Others ([5]).

### I – Issues

The principal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was the determination of which legislative framework governed the appellant's application for leave to appeal: the old Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959 or the new Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which had come into effect while the application was still pending ([1]). Specifically, the court needed to resolve whether the appellant’s existing rights were adversely affected by the provisions of the new Act, necessitating its retrospective application, or if his case should be adjudicated according to the old Act, under which the threshold for appeal was comparatively less stringent.

### R – Rules

The Supreme Court of Appeal's decision heavily referenced provisions from both the old and new legislation. Of particular importance were:

1. **Section 16(1)(a) and (b) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013**: These sections mandate that appeals against decisions made by the division on appeal to it require special leave to be granted by the Supreme Court of Appeal ([4]).

2. **Section 20 of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959**: This section outlines procedures concerning applications for leave to appeal, emphasizing the established rights of a convicted person seeking appellate relief ([6]).

3. **Section 52 of the Superior Courts Act**: This section states that pending proceedings must continue under the governing law as it existed before the new Act came into effect, which the Gauteng Local Division failed to consider adequately ([6]).

4. **Section 12(2) of the Interpretation Act 33 of 1957**: It provides that the repeal of a law does not affect any rights accrued under that law unless a contrary intention is indicated ([6]).

### A – Application

In applying these rules, the Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed whether the interpretation of the new Act by the Gauteng Local Division was appropriate and whether it led to an erroneous situation regarding the appellant's rights. The court concluded that:

- The provisions of the new Act were not intended to apply retrospectively, primarily because the appellant's application for leave to appeal was still pending at the time the new Act came into force ([6]–[8]).
- The differentiation in the threshold for appeal established under the new Act implied that applying its stricter provisions could infringe upon the appellant's established rights. The court expressed that the appellant was entitled to have his application considered under the provisions of the old Act, which required only the demonstration of "reasonable prospects of success," rather than the "compelling reasons" necessitated by the new legislation ([7]–[10]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the lower court had erred in applying the provisions of the new Act retroactively, which improperly burdened Carneiro with a heightened threshold for his application for leave to appeal. The court thus allowed the appeal, setting aside the lower court's order that had struck the matter off the roll and directing that Carneiro’s application for leave to appeal be dealt with under the provisions of the old Act ([10]).

The implications of this decision affirm the principle that changes to procedural law generally do not adversely affect rights accrued under previous laws and underscore the importance of ensuring that established legal rights are not prejudiced by subsequent legislative changes. This case serves as a critical reminder of the necessity for careful interpretation of both procedural and substantive rights within the context of evolving legal frameworks in South Africa.
