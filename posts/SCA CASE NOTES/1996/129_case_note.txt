Case Note: HARRY BANNENG ZAKADE
Source Folder: 1996

# FIRAC Case Note: **Harry Banneng Zakade and Others v The State**

## F – Facts
The case of *Harry Banneng Zakade and Others v The State* (Case No 129/96) involves five appellants, all convicted and sentenced on various charges after trial in the Transvaal Provincial Division ([para 2]). Three co-accused were acquitted. The appellants’ only incriminating evidence was a series of written confessions, the admissibility of which was heavily contested. These confessions were recorded on 5 October 1993, following the arrest of the appellants nine days after the commission of the alleged offences, one of which included the killing of a policeman in Boipatong ([para 2]).

The appellants, during their intake on the day of confession, displayed various physical injuries, which they attributed to assaults by police during their arrest, thus challenging the voluntariness of their confessions on these grounds ([para 5]). The trial court admitted the confessions into evidence, ruling that there was no sufficient evidence to doubt their voluntariness and that the confessions were made freely and without undue influence ([para 4]).

## I – Issues
The core legal issue in this case is whether the trial court correctly admitted the confessions of the appellants. This encompasses a broader examination of whether the confessions were made voluntarily, amidst allegations of coercion and assault by police ([para 2], [para 4]). Secondary issues include the degree of evidentiary reliability concerning the confessions and the implications of assault allegations on their admissibility in light of constitutional protections against self-incrimination and torture or inhumane treatment ([para 14]).

## R – Rules
The rules pertinent to this case primarily involve the provisions of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 concerning confessions and their admissibility, particularly sections 217 and 219A ([para 4]). Section 217 stipulates requirements for the admissibility of confessions, necessitating that they be made freely and without undue influence, while section 219A details the requirements for admissions to be included in records.

Significant case law also informs the court’s reasoning, notably the precedents set in *S v Mkwanazi* 1966 (1) SA 763 (A) and *S v Radebe* 1968 (4) SA 470 (W), which counsel for greater scrutiny when confessions serve as the sole incriminating evidence ([para 18]).

## A – Application
The court meticulously analyzed the evidentiary products submitted by both the State and the appellants. The State's case relied heavily on the testimonies of Sergeant Michael Kolokoto and Constable Nkosi Ngubo, who denied any assaults on the appellants ([para 8]). However, the credibility of these claims was called into question given the demonstrable injuries the appellants presented at the time of confession ([para 6]).

The trial court’s rationale hinged on the assertion that the admissions made by the appellants regarding the voluntariness of their confessions constituted sufficient prima facie proof. This logic was flawed, as the evidentiary burden lay with the State to dispel reasonable doubt concerning the coerciveness of the confessions ([para 16]). The court found that significant doubt arose from the physical evidence of injuries consistent with police assault, thus leading to the conclusion that the admissions could not sufficiently counterbalance the dubious nature of the subsequent confessions ([para 18]).

The pivotal analysis from *Howie JA* highlighted that the injuries raised a reasonable inference that coercive practices may have been employed to elicit confessions. Additionally, the absence of credible explanations from the police witnesses regarding the injuries significantly undermined the State’s case ([para 17]).

## C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Appeal Court, guided by the principles of justice and evidentiary integrity, concluded that the confessions were inadmissible. The evidence produced by the appellants warranted a reasonable doubt regarding the voluntariness of their statements, as well as a concern about the methods employed in obtaining them. The trial court's ruling admitting the confessions was reversed, leading to the following orders: the appeals of all appellants were allowed, and all convictions and sentences were set aside ([para 19]).

The case underscores the critical importance of safeguarding confession rights under South African law, especially in light of constitutional protections against torture and inhuman treatment. The decision serves as a compelling reiteration of the justice system's obligation to rigorously ensure the voluntariness of confessions and the overarching need for accountability within law enforcement practices.
