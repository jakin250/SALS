Constitutional Court

Director of Public Prosecutions, Transvaal v Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development & Others [2009] ZACC 8

The High Court, on its accord, raised the constitutional validity of certain CPA provisions, particularly those concerning child complainants.

Constitutional Court 2009 [2009] ZACC 8

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–20])

The case revolves around the treatment of child complainants in sexual offence trials, highlighting their experiences in court settings where they testify. Historically, such complainants relived traumatic experiences in open court and faced severe emotional stress, with outdated legal provisions that insufficiently protected their rights. The central question arises from amendments made to the Criminal Procedure Act (CPA) through the Criminal Law (Sexual Offences and Related Matters) Amendment Act [Act 32 of 2007], designed to enhance protections for child witnesses.

The High Court, on its accord, raised the constitutional validity of certain CPA provisions, particularly those concerning child complainants. Sections 153(3) and (5), 158(5), 164(1), and 170A(1) and (7) were questioned for possibly violating the constitutional right of children, specifically under section 28(2) of the South African Constitution. This section mandates that a child's best interests be of paramount importance in all matters concerning them. The High Court ultimately found these provisions unconstitutional due to their failure to afford adequate protection to child witnesses, leading to criminal cases against accused persons Phaswane and Mokoena being consolidated for review.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 3, 4, 8, 31])

  1. Constitutionality of the CPA Provisions: Whether sections 153(3) and (5), 158(5), 164(1), 170A(1), and 170A(7) of the CPA are consistent with the constitutional requirement that the best interests of child complainants are paramount ([para 3]).
  2. Judicial Authority: Whether a court may raise constitutional issues of its own accord within the adversarial system and the implications of such powers for cases involving child witnesses who are not formal parties to the proceedings ([para 4]).
  3. Impact of Invalidated Provisions: The implications of the High Court’s declaration of invalidity and whether the orders of invalidity should be confirmed by the constitutional court, including the effects of these provisions on child complainants in future proceedings ([para 31]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 2, 22, 71])

  1. Section 28(2) of the Constitution: Stipulates that a child’s best interests are to be of paramount importance in all matters concerning the child. This foundational principle underpins the court’s analysis of legislative provisions impacting child complainants ([para 2]).
  2. Constitutional Court Precedents: Previous judgments regarding the judicial authority to raise constitutional issues (e.g., Carmichele v Minister of Safety and Security [2001] ZACC 22). This includes the duty of the judiciary to ensure laws are interpreted in a manner that promotes and protects the Bill of Rights ([para 22]).
  3. International Legal Instruments: Reference to the United Nations Convention on the Rights of the Child (CRC) and how its provisions align with the constitutional mandates for protecting children, particularly regarding matters of evidence in criminal proceedings ([para 71]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 70, 152, 164])

  1. Constitutionality of Provisions: The court found that various CPA sections require interpretation that aligns with section 28(2) of the Constitution. For instance, the phrasing of “undue mental stress” was held to be inadequately protective, as it contradicts the goal of minimizing stress for child complainants when giving evidence. The court asserted that the procedural framework must be adapted to provide children with optimal support, indicating a need for systemic reforms within the judicial process ([paras 70, 164]).
  2. Judicial Discretion and Implementation: The judgment discussed the discretion given to judicial officers regarding the appointment of intermediaries (section 170A(1)) and the necessity for consistent application to protect child witnesses. The court emphasized the crucial role of adequately trained intermediaries and the availability of facilities that facilitate child-friendly testimony. It rejected interpretations of the provisions that could lead to discriminatory outcomes regarding the treatment of child witnesses ([paras 152, 164]).
  3. Impact of Findings: The court highlighted the practical implications of its findings, advocating for proactive measures to ensure that the legislative intent — to provide a supportive environment for child witnesses — is fulfilled in practice. It called for enhanced training of judicial officers and prosecutors as well as a review of existing facilities to improve compliance with constitutional mandates ([paras 192–203]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 208–209])

The Constitutional Court concluded by not confirming the High Court’s declarations of invalidity of the CPA’s provisions, arguing that properly construed, these provisions do not conflict with the Constitution. The court asserted that the issues stemming from the improper interpretation and implementation of the CPA provisions pointed towards systemic deficiencies rather than inherent constitutional flaws. The ruling emphasized the need for judiciary and executive cooperation in remedying these deficits, particularly concerning the provision of intermediaries and court facilities for child witnesses ([paras 208–209]).

Moreover, it highlighted the necessity for a report from the Director-General for the Department of Justice and Constitutional Development regarding the availability of intermediaries and facilities for child complainants, thereby reinforcing the state’s constitutional obligations to safeguard the rights of vulnerable witnesses in the judicial process.

Ultimately, the case represents a critical step towards enforcing children’s rights in the South African legal system, signaling the ongoing need for reform in handling child testimony, which the legislature must prioritize to fulfill its constitutional obligations ([para 208]).
