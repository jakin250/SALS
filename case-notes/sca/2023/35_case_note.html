Supreme Court of Appeal

Solidariteit Helpende Hand NPC and Others v Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (104/2022)

This case arose amidst the COVID-19 pandemic, which prompted the South African government to declare a national state of disaster under the Disaster Management Act 57 of 2002 (DMA).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023

F – Facts

This case arose amidst the COVID-19 pandemic, which prompted the South African government to declare a national state of disaster under the Disaster Management Act 57 of 2002 (DMA). The Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs promulgated various regulations, including restrictions on religious gatherings. Initially, regulations banned all social and faith-based gatherings for two weeks, later extended to an indefinite period ([paras 2–8]).

In January 2021, the appellants—Solidariteit Helpende Hand NPC, the South African National Christian Forum (SANCF), and the Muslim Lawyers Association—launched a challenge against these regulations under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA), claiming that the restrictions infringed upon their constitutional rights to freedom of religion ([para 8]).

Notably, prior to the High Court hearing, these regulations were amended, allowing limited religious gatherings under certain protocols ([para 9]). The Gauteng Division of the High Court dismissed the application as moot, indicating that the lifted ban presented no live issue for adjudication ([para 10]). The appellants contested this determination on appeal, suggesting a need for substantive judicial interpretation of the regulations despite their repeal ([para 11]).

I – Issues

The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) centered on whether the matter was moot due to the repeal of the contested regulations. Specifically, could the court adjudicate the legality and constitutionality of the repealed regulations, despite the absence of active legal controversy? Additionally, the court was tasked with determining if it had jurisdiction to entertain an appeal that was effectively dismissed for mootness by the lower court ([paras 10–11]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal principles in this case relied heavily on the concept of mootness as defined in South African law. A matter is considered moot when a court's judgment would have no practical effect on the parties involved, thus rendering the dispute abstract and hypothetical ([para 12]). The Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 provides the judiciary the discretion to hear matters even if they are moot if the interests of justice require it ([para 18]).

Relevant precedent, including President of the Republic of South Africa v Democratic Alliance and Independent Electoral Commission v Langeberg Municipality, establishes that courts typically refrain from issuing advisory opinions unless specific conditions warrant the exercise of discretion ([paras 12–13]). This case also invoked Minister of Justice and Correctional Services v Estate Late Stransham-Ford which reinforced that lower courts lack similar discretion and can only rule on live controversies ([para 18]).

A – Application

In applying the law to the present matter, the SCA recognized that the impugned regulations had been lifted and were no longer enforceable, which fundamentally rendered the High Court’s findings on mootness sound and unassailable ([para 21]). The appellants argued that there remained significant issues of public interest and potential impacts on individuals arrested under the repealed regulations, seeking to establish a class action premise for the review of those regulations ([para 16]).

However, the Court underscored the importance of maintaining a boundary against issuing non-justiciable, advisory opinions on previously enforced legislation that no longer affected any parties significantly due to their repeal ([para 20]). The potential liabilities of those arrested under earlier regulations did not provide sufficient grounds for an appeal’s adjudication since the core questions revolved around the legality of regulations no longer in force, thus lacking an existing controversy ([para 17]).

Consequently, despite the appellants' calls for substantive judicial review, the SCA maintained that adjudicating these issues would effectively place the court in a position to render academic commentary rather than effectuate meaningful change or legal resolution ([paragraph 21]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the SCA dismissed the appeal, affirming the High Court's determination that the matter was moot and thus non-justiciable. The court concluded that, given the absence of current regulations, any declared opinion regarding their validity or justification would lack practical implications, relegating it to mere academic inquiry ([para 21]). Importantly, the decision underscored the judiciary's role in adjudicating live controversies and its reluctance to engage with outdated, repealed regulations. This ruling had implications for the future handling of similar cases, underscoring the need for timely judicial consideration in rapidly changing circumstances, such as public health emergencies.

In accordance with the Biowatch principle, the Court ordered that no costs be awarded against either party ([para 22]). This decision reinforces the judiciary's caution in dealing with moot cases and highlights the complexities involved when constitutional rights intersect with emergency legislation in times of national crisis.
