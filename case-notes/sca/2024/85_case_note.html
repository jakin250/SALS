The National Director of Public Prosecutions v Sijoyi Robert Mdhlovu (Case No. 194/2023) [2024] ZASCA 85

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 85

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–13])

In The National Director of Public Prosecutions v Sijoyi Robert Mdhlovu, the appellant, the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP), sought leave to appeal the judgment of the Mpumalanga Division of the High Court, which granted a claim of malicious prosecution against it. The respondent, Sijoyi Robert Mdhlovu, withdrew charges against several accused in criminal cases, citing inconsistencies in a ballistics report. The investigating officer, Sergeant Nkambule, who had consulted with the respondent regarding the cases, later complained that Mdhlovu had acted improperly by withdrawing the charges without proper authority or notice to the police.

Consequently, the Deputy Director of Public Prosecutions initiated a prosecution against Mdhlovu on charges of fraud and defeating the ends of justice. After the trial commenced, Mdhlovu was discharged at the close of the state’s case. Aggrieved by his prosecution, he launched a malicious prosecution claim in March 2018, asserting that the prosecution was motivated by malice and initiated without reasonable cause ([para 13]).

The High Court found in favor of Mdhlovu, leading to the NDPP's application for leave to appeal, questioning both the absence of reasonable cause for prosecution and the presence of animus iniuriandi ([paras 1–14]).

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 14–16])

The core issues on appeal were whether:

  1. The High Court erred in finding that Mdhlovu established a lack of reasonable and probable cause for the prosecution.
  2. The High Court improperly determined that the NDPP acted with animus iniuriandi, intending to cause harm to Mdhlovu ([paras 14–19]).

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 18–20])

A successful claim for malicious prosecution in South African law requires proof of four elements:

  1. The defendant instigated or instituted the legal proceedings.
  2. The defendant acted without reasonable and probable cause.
  3. The defendant was actuated by malice or animus iniuriandi.
  4. The prosecution failed ([para 18]).

The standard for determining reasonable and probable cause is an objective one, based on what a reasonable person would conclude given the available evidence at the time of initiation of the prosecution ([para 20]). Key precedents include Prinsloo and Another v Newman and Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development and Others v Moleko, which outline the necessary legal principles surrounding malicious prosecution ([paras 18, 20]).

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 21–38])

The Supreme Court of Appeal found the High Court had erred significantly in its analysis of the evidence regarding probable cause. The High Court had focused excessively on the concept of a “prima facie” case instead of considering whether there existed any reasonable grounds for suspicion against Mdhlovu at the time the prosecution was initiated ([para 22]). The NDPP presented evidence showing that various legal professionals had acknowledged substantial grounds for prosecuting Mdhlovu, as indicated by the investigations and statements provided by the investigating officer and others ([para 26]).

The Court emphasized that while Mdhlovu was ultimately discharged in the trial, the mere fact of a prosecution failing does not negate the existence of reasonable and probable cause at the time of filing. The information available to the Deputy Director of Public Prosecutions (DDPP) suggested legitimate grounds for suspicion that justifiably warranted further legal action against Mdhlovu, thereby establishing probable cause ([paras 29–30]).

Regarding animus iniuriandi, the Court held that the High Court had employed an overly narrow interpretation of the evidence. The DDPP's intent, as derived from her professional duty and absence of any personal ties to Mdhlovu, did not demonstrate a conscious intent to injure. Instead, the prosecution could be seen as motivated by the need to uphold prosecutorial accountability, given the serious nature of the allegations involving Mdhlovu’s conduct ([paras 33–36]). The Court underlined that for malicious prosecution to hold, clear evidence of malevolence must be presented, which the respondent failed to sufficiently demonstrate in this case ([paras 31–38]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 39–41])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately concluded that the respondent failed to prove the essential elements of his claim, particularly regarding the absence of reasonable and probable cause and the requisite animus iniuriandi. As such, the appeal was upheld, the order of the High Court was set aside, and the claim against the NDPP was dismissed with costs ([paras 39–41]).

The decision reaffirms the legal standards governing malicious prosecution and emphasizes the necessity for clear, demonstrable evidence of both a lack of probable cause and malicious intent to succeed in such claims. This ruling has implications for the independence of prosecutorial discretion, encouraging a balanced approach that allows prosecutors to act on evidence while safeguarding against unfounded claims of malice. The ruling echoes the importance of maintaining prosecutorial integrity while mitigating the risks of personal grievances affecting prosecutorial decisions.
