Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

### FIRAC Case Note: Ditsoane v ACWA Power Africa Holdings (Pty) Ltd [2023] ZACC 44

#### Facts
The applicant, Seipati Joyce Ditsoane, sought leave to appeal a judgment from the Labour Court that barred her from pursuing a claim against her employer, ACWA Power Africa Holdings (Pty) Ltd, for unfair dismissal. Ms. Ditsoane commenced her employment in November 2015 and was served with a retrenchment letter on 27 October 2016, effective 30 November 2016. She referred her dispute to the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation, and Arbitration (CCMA), which issued a certificate indicating that the dispute remained unresolved ([para 2]).

On 11 May 2017, Ms. Ditsoane filed her unfair dismissal claim in the Labour Court, represented by Mulima Denga Attorneys (MDA). During the proceedings, the respondent raised objections concerning the timing of her claim and the content of her statement of case. Ms. Ditsoane consulted a new law firm, Ndumiso Voyi Incorporated (NVI), on 24 October 2017, and requested that MDA file a notice of withdrawal without understanding that this action would terminate her claim ([paras 4-5]).

MDA misunderstood the instruction and filed the withdrawal notice the following day. NVI later sought to amend the claim and indicate their representation on 26 October, unaware that MDA had already filed a withdrawal notice. This led to confusion regarding the court proceedings ([paras 6-8]).

On 3 November 2017, a court order was issued, which postponed the condonation application sine die and stipulated that Ms. Ditsoane must explain her reasons for wanting her statement of case revived within ten days or face automatic dismissal ([para 8]). Ms. Ditsoane discovered the existence of the order only on 18 January 2018 and filed her application to revive her case thereafter.

The Labour Court dismissed this application and ruled against Ms. Ditsoane, asserting that the delay and the unreasonable conduct were attributable to her ([paras 12-16]). The Labour Appeal Court also denied her leave to appeal ([para 13]).

#### Issues
The primary legal issues in this case revolved around:

1. Whether the notice of withdrawal filed by MDA had any legal effect given that it was made without the applicant’s authority ([para 18(a)]).
2. If the filing did not effectively withdraw her application, did the applicant still need court permission to revive her case or set aside the notice of withdrawal? ([para 18(b)])

#### Rules
The legal principles applicable to this matter include the following:

1. The authority of an attorney to withdraw a case is contingent upon demonstrating either actual or implied authority from the client ([paras 26-29]). The applicant emphasized that her instruction to MDA was to represent her claim, not withdraw it, satisfying traditional legal standards regarding agency and authority.

2. The doctrine of estoppel may prevent a client from denying an attorney's actions if the client has behaved in such a way as to suggest the attorney had authority ([paras 22-23]). Previously decided cases like *Kruizenga* are relevant in defining the boundaries of an attorney's implied authority ([para 31]).

3. The interest of justice as articulated in sections 23(1) and 34 of the South African Constitution guarantees the right to fair labor practices and properties of judicial fairness ([para 24]).

#### Application
In evaluating the applicant's claims and the Labour Court's previous decisions, the Constitutional Court noted that:

- MDA's withdrawal notice was issued without Ms. Ditsoane's authority, as the uncontested evidence indicated that she only intended to switch attorneys and not to withdraw her claim altogether ([paras 26-28]). The miscommunication pointed to a misunderstanding; hence, the purported withdrawal did not bind the applicant.

- Applying the principles set forth in *Kruizenga*, the Court determined that MDA misjudged its role, as withdrawal of a case is not generally considered part of the usual attorney’s authority ([paras 29-34]). The Court further found that Ms. Ditsoane never ratified the actions of MDA as sufficient evidence did not support that she suffered undue delay or stress attributable to her actions ([para 27]).

- By focusing on the interests of justice and the prejudices experienced by each party, the Court highlighted that denying Ms. Ditsoane the ability to revive her claim would effectively undermine her right to a fair hearing ([para 40]). The procedural history and her proactive steps demonstrated a legitimate interest in pursuing her legal remedy.

#### Conclusion
The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal, allowing Ms. Ditsoane to proceed with her claim. The Court overturned the Labour Court’s ruling, declaring that the withdrawal notice was unauthorized and effectively set aside. Furthermore, the applicant's statement of case was revived without a necessary accompanying order for costs, allowing both parties to bear their own costs ([para 42]). The decision reiterated the necessity of clear authority in attorney-client relationships, emphasizing the equitable treatment of litigants in labor disputes and the significance of procedural fairness in judicial processes. The judgment provides a crucial precedent, highlighting the implications of miscommunication in legal representation and the systems designed to protect individual rights within the labor law context.
