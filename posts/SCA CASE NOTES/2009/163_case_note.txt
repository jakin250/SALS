# Case Note: Minister of Safety & Security v Slabbert (668/2008) [2009] ZASCA 163

## F - Facts

This case arising from the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa concerns an appeal against a judgment by Kroon J in the High Court, Grahamstown, where the appellant was ordered to pay damages to the respondent, Pieter Samuel Theo Slabbert, for unlawful detention ([paras 1–2]). The events leading to the appeal occurred on the night of February 5, 2005, when Constable Magoxo and his partner, while on patrol in Klipplaat, came across a parked vehicle obstructing Main Street. 

Upon encountering Slabbert, the officers noted that he appeared drunk and was attempting to drive the vehicle. Concerned for public safety, Magoxo arrested him for being drunk and disorderly, which led to a physical struggle requiring the use of pepper spray ([para 3]). After his arrest, Slabbert was taken to the local police station and detained overnight. His wife later requested his release, which Constable Magoxo declined based on departmental policy to allow detainees time to sober up ([para 4]). Slabbert was eventually released the following morning, but subsequent to the incident, he sought damages for wrongful arrest and detention, among other claims.

At trial, the court held that while the initial arrest was lawful, the period of further detention after the request from Slabbert's wife was unlawful, and it awarded Slabbert damages of R20,000 ([paras 4–5]). The appellant, Minister of Safety and Security, appealed against this finding.

## I - Issues

The primary issue on appeal was whether the High Court's determination that part of Slabbert's detention was unlawful addressed a matter fully covered in the pleadings ([paras 7–8]). Specifically, the court needed to decide if Slabbert's claim about the refusal to release him into his wife's care was a legal issue that had been properly tendered and addressed during the initial trial. The implications of this determination raised questions about the nature of pleadings and their role in defining legal issues for resolution.

## R - Rules

The legal framework governing this case primarily stems from common law principles of delict and constitutional protections afforded by the South African Constitution, particularly regarding the right to freedom and security of the person ([para 20]). The key sources relevant to the case include:

1. **Constitutional Law**: The right to dignity, freedom, and security of the person under sections 10 and 12 of the Constitution of South Africa.
2. **Criminal Procedure Act**: Section 40 permits arrests without a warrant under specific circumstances, which were acknowledged by both parties concerning the initial detention ([para 9]).
3. **Case Law Precedents**:
   - *Van Niekerk v Minister of Safety and Security*: Highlighting the need for police to properly justify continued detention once circumstances change, including the request for release by a relative ([para 5]).
   - *Nelson v Minister of Safety and Security*: This case examined the responsibility of authorities in assessing the immediate risk presented by a detainee at the time of release ([para 15]).

## A - Application

The court applied the legal standards articulated above to assess the appropriateness of the High Court's ruling. Upon review, it found that the pivotal argument regarding further detention had not been properly pleaded by Slabbert. The particulars of his claim focused on the legality of the arrest and denial of the grounds upon which he had been held, but did not specifically contend that the refusal to release him into his wife’s care rendered the detainment unlawful ([paras 10–11]).

Moreover, despite the High Court’s conclusion that Slabbert exhibited calmness and compliance, the Supreme Court noted that the evidence did not support the claim that he was indeed sober or non-dangerous at the time of his wife's request. The police practice of retaining individuals for a minimum of four hours for safety reasons was deemed both reasonable and justifiable under the circumstances ([para 16]). 

The lack of testimony from Slabbert’s wife further compounded the issue; without her account of the circumstances or guarantees regarding his behavior post-release, the Court could not accept the High Court's inferences as factually or legally sound ([paras 14–16]).

Finally, the Supreme Court underscored that assertions must be grounded in the pleadings and that the trial court had erred in considering issues not fully explored at trial. The court encapsulated its position by reaffirming the principle that issues not canvassed during trial cannot form the basis of a judgment ([paras 12–13], [22–24]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that the High Court erred in concluding that part of Slabbert's detention was unlawful. It overturned the previous decision, leading to the dismissal of Slabbert's claims with costs ([paras 17–18]). The judgment highlighted critical aspects of the legal process, particularly the importance of precise pleadings where a party's claims must align with the issues presented at trial. This ruling serves as a cautionary reminder regarding procedural diligence in asserting claims within the litigative structure.

The implications of this case reaffirm the necessity for judicial consistency and adherence to proper procedural conventions in South African law, particularly concerning the rights of individuals under arrest. The lack of evidence supporting the claim for wrongful detention, alongside an acknowledgement of police practices aimed at ensuring public safety, underscored the court’s commitment to balance individual rights with practical law enforcement considerations.
