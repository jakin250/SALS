Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: Levenstein and Others v Estate of the Late Sidney Lewis Frankel and Others

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The case arose out of the constitutional challenge to **Section 18** of the **Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA)**, which imposes a 20-year prescription period on the prosecution of sexual offences other than rape or compelled rape. The eight applicants alleged that they were sexually assaulted by the late **Sidney Lewis Frankel** between 1970 and 1989 when they were children ([1]–[5]). The alleged assaults resulted in severe emotional and psychological trauma, leading the applicants to later seek prosecution against Frankel only to be denied by the **Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP)** based on the 20-year prescription period contained in Section 18 ([3]–[8]).

The applicants approached the **Gauteng Local Division of the High Court**, seeking to declare Section 18 unconstitutional to the extent it barred prosecution for sexual offences, arguing that it unjustly differentiated between rape and other sexual crimes. They contended that all sexual offences should carry the same potential for prosecution without prescription limitations, citing the irrational and arbitrary nature of the distinction made by the law ([9]–[10]).

The High Court agreed, determining that there was no rational basis for the different treatment of sexual offences and declared Section 18 invalid, pending further legislative action to amend the law ([12]–[14]). The case was then taken to the **Constitutional Court** for confirmation as per **Section 172(2)** of the **Constitution** ([1]–[4], [8], [11]).

### I – Issues
The principal issues before the Constitutional Court were:
1. Whether **Section 18 of the CPA** was inconsistent with the Constitution.
2. If Section 18 was found inconsistent, what relief should be provided, particularly regarding the suspension of the declaration of invalidity, its retrospective effect, and the mechanism for interim relief ([4]–[5]).
3. The implications of Section 18's provisions on the applicants' and accused’s rights under the Constitution ([16]–[18]).

### R – Rules
The court's analysis relied upon several key constitutional principles:
- **Rationality:** Legislative measures must have a rational connection to a legitimate governmental objective. The irrationality of Section 18 in distinguishing between types of sexual offences was fundamentally scrutinized.
- **Equality and Dignity:** The rights enshrined in **Section 9** (equality) and **Section 10** (dignity) of the Constitution prohibit arbitrary differentiation between sexual offences ([9]–[12]).
- **Principle of Legality:** According to **Section 35(3)(l)** of the Constitution, an accused cannot be convicted for conduct that was not a crime when it was committed ([15]–[18]).
- **Remedial Measures:** The court has discretion under **Section 172(1)(b)** to suspend its declaration of invalidity and provide an interim reading-in to maintain legal certainty and allow legislative correction without immediate disruption ([66]–[74]).

### A – Application
In applying these rules, the court determined that:
- The rationale behind Section 18’s differentiation lacked a clear justification and served to prevent survivors of various sexual offences from seeking justice. This was deemed unconstitutional as it failed to rationally connect to any valid public policy ([41]–[60]).
- The court noted the extensive psychological impacts of sexual trauma, which could impede timely reporting of any sexual offences, not only rape. Thus, the arbitrary 20-year prescription unjustly penalized survivors and potentially allowed perpetrators to remain unpunished ([51]–[60]).
- The court affirmed the High Court's conclusion that the violation of the applicants' rights to dignity, equality, and access to justice rendered Section 18 invalid. The opinions of amici curiae supported the notion that all victims, regardless of the type of sexual offence, face similar barriers to reporting ([11]–[22]).
- Concerning the remedy, the court opted for a 24-month suspension of the declaration of invalidity, allowing Parliament time to enact corrective legislation. In the interim, Section 18(f) was to be read to include all other sexual offences, thus expanding the possibility for prosecution ([66]–[76]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court unanimously confirmed the High Court’s declaration of constitutional invalidity of Section 18 of the Criminal Procedure Act. It recognized that the distinction made was irrational and arbitrary, constituting a violation of survivors' rights under the Constitution. The court emphasized the need for legislation to reflect the seriousness of all sexual offences uniformly, aligning with constitutional tenets of dignity and equality ([89]).

The judgment reinforces the obligation of the State to ensure protective measures for all survivors of sexual violence and underscores the need for legislative reform that accommodates the realities of trauma relating to all forms of sexual assault. Effective legislative action must be taken within 24 months following this ruling, with a clear directive that failure to comply would lead to the interim reading-in becoming permanent ([14]–[78]). 

This case serves as a pivotal point for the intersection of constitutional rights and legislative measures, particularly in the context of addressing and responding to sexual offences in South Africa.
