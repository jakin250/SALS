Azwifaneli Rasirubu v The State (656/12) [2013] ZASCA 140

Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 140

F – Facts

Azwifaneli Rasirubu, a 19-year-old male, was charged with rape in the Venda High Court, Thohoyandou. He was accused of raping a 13-year-old girl on 25 June 2004 ([1]). Rasirubu pleaded guilty to the charge and provided a statement under Section 112 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977. In his statement, he admitted to having sexual intercourse with the complainant without her consent, acknowledging the unlawfulness of his actions ([1]).

To substantiate the complainant's age, the State submitted her birth certificate, alongside a medical report reflecting injuries consistent with sexual assault, including swelling and tearing in the genital area and superficial anal injuries ([2]). During sentencing, Rasirubu presented evidence in mitigation, expressing remorse, his status as a school pupil in grade 11, and his role as a financial supporter for his unemployed mother and younger siblings ([3]).

Despite the mitigating circumstances, the High Court imposed a life sentence. Rasirubu subsequently appealed, challenging only the severity of the sentence. Leave to appeal was granted by the High Court ([4]).

I – Issues

The critical legal question surrounding this appeal was whether the High Court properly exercised its judicial discretion in imposing a life sentence given the insufficient evidence regarding the circumstances of the offence, the appellant’s background, and whether substantial and compelling reasons existed to deviate from the prescribed minimum sentence under the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 ([5]).

Additionally, the appeal raised the issue of whether the trial court's failure to consider relevant factors and evidence constituted a misdirection sufficient to warrant the intervention of the Supreme Court of Appeal ([5], [6]).

R – Rules

The judgment hinged on various legal principles relevant to sentencing in rape cases. Under Section 51(1)(a) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act, certain minimum sentences apply for specified offences, including rape ([5]). In cases where a life sentence is a possibility, the court must identify whether substantial and compelling circumstances justify a lesser sentence, as emphasized in Rammoko v Director of Public Prosecutions 2003 (1) SACR 200 (SCA) and S v Siebert 1998 (1) SACR 554 (SCA) ([5], [6]).

The Supreme Court reiterated that it is the duty of the trial court to ensure that all relevant information to exercise its sentencing discretion correctly is available, irrespective of counsel's diligence ([6]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court critically assessed the trial court's actions, noting the lack of relevant evidence, including a pre-sentencing report, victim impact assessment, and a thorough consideration of Rasirubu’s circumstances ([5], [7]). The appellate court found that the trial court did not conduct a proper inquiry into whether substantial and compelling circumstances existed, resulting in a misdirection ([5]).

Despite this misdirection, the court acknowledged the significant time Rasirubu had already served in custody—approximately nine years since his original sentencing. Given this context, the court determined that remitting the matter to the trial court would not serve the interests of justice, as it would only prolong Rasirubu's incarceration without any benefit ([8]).

The Supreme Court emphasized that a broader inspection of the facts justified reducing the sentence. It took into account Rasirubu's young age, his guilty plea, absence of prior convictions, and the nature of the injuries inflicted on the complainant ([8]). State counsel's concession that the years already spent in custody would suffice as punishment corroborated the appellate court's reasoning.

C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Rasirubu's appeal, setting aside the life sentence imposed by the High Court. It replaced it with a sentence of 8 years and 10 months, backdating to 29 November 2009, thus recognizing the time already endured in custody ([9]). This ruling reflects the court's commitment to ensuring judicial discretion in sentencing is exercised judiciously, considering the specific facts and circumstances of the case while upholding the rights of the accused and the demands of justice.

The implications of this ruling underscore the necessity for a comprehensive understanding of each case's facts in sentencing and the need for courts to actively gather relevant evidence to safeguard against disproportionate sentencing outcomes.
