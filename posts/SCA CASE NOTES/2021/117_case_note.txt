Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# Case Note: Ombud for Financial Services Providers v CS Brokers CC and Others (Case No: 781/2020) [2021] ZASCA 117 

## F - Facts

On 19 November 2009, Mr J B Wallace invested R730,000 with Sharemax, deriving R600,000 from his mother, a UK-based pensioner, and the remainder from his own savings. Mr Wallace's initial advisor, Mr Marais, referred him to Mr Emile Storm, the second respondent, who operated as an authorized representative for the first respondent, CS Brokers CC, under the Financial Advisory and Intermediary Services Act 37 of 2002 (FAIS) ([1]). Wallace and Storm met multiple times, leading to the investment, but disputes arose regarding who advised Wallace regarding the Sharemax investments ([2]).

By September 2010, Wallace had not received any income from the investments and subsequently demanded the return of his capital from Storm. After failing to secure his funds, Wallace lodged a complaint with the Ombud for Financial Services Providers (the Ombud) on 10 December 2010. In 2011, CS Brokers applied to the Ombud under section 27(3) of FAIS, requesting a hearing or referral to a court due to the alleged need for oral evidence to resolve factual disputes. On 11 May 2011, the Ombud declined this request, stating that the Office does not hold hearings ([4]).

Five years later, the Ombud ordered CS Brokers to pay Wallace R730,000 plus interest, leading to CS Brokers appealing the Ombud’s decision. This appeal to the Appeal Board was ultimately dismissed, prompting CS Brokers to seek a review of the Ombud's decisions at the Gauteng Division of the High Court, culminating in a judgment which set aside multiple findings of the Ombud ([6]–[7]). The Ombud's application for leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal was granted ([8]).

## I - Issues

The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the Ombud correctly exercised her discretion in declining CS Brokers’ application under section 27(3) of FAIS for a hearing or referral to a court ([14]). Related questions included the legal standards governing the Ombud's discretion, and the extent to which her refusal to hold a hearing aligned with her statutory obligations under FAIS ([12]–[14]).

## R - Rules

The relevant legal framework is encapsulated in the Financial Advisory and Intermediary Services Act 37 of 2002, particularly in:
- **Section 20**: Establishes the office of the Ombud and sets its objectives of addressing complaints in a fair, speedy, and informal manner ([9]).
- **Section 27(3)(c)**: Grants the Ombud the authority to decline to entertain a complaint if it is deemed more appropriate for it to be dealt with in court ([11]).
- **Section 28**: Outlines the powers and procedures of the Ombud, defining her determinations as akin to a court’s civil judgment ([10]).

Case law interpreting the Ombud's discretion shows that while she holds considerable powers, her decisions must be exercised judiciously, taking into account the specifics of each case ([12]).

## A - Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal determined that the Ombud had failed to exercise her discretion correctly. The Ombud's response to CS Brokers' application for a hearing was based on a predetermined policy that did not consider the specifics of the request or the legitimacy of the factual disputes highlighted by the parties. Gorven JA noted that the Ombud indicated, “this Office does not hold hearings,” without appropriate justification or engagement with the matter's complexities ([15]–[16]).

The court emphasized the need for the Ombud to provide individualized responses commensurate with her statutory duties. The reasonableness of her refusal to allow a hearing was not demonstrated adequately, as she neglected to explain her reasoning in relation to the factual disputes critical to the case ([16]–[18]). A failure to engage in a discretionary process equated to a failure in her statutory obligations, leading the court to conclude that the Ombud’s decisions were reviewable on the grounds of not exercising discretion ([18]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, holding that the Ombud did not exercise her discretion at all regarding CS Brokers' application under section 27(3) of FAIS. The court's ruling underscored significant implications for the Ombud’s office, highlighting the necessity of a reasoned approach wherein discretionary powers must be exercised thoughtfully in line with statutory requirements ([19]). The implications suggest a need for procedural safeguards in the Ombud’s processes, ensuring that legitimate disputes, particularly those involving financial advice, are duly considered and resolved fairly either through hearings or alternative legal routes. This decision reinforces the legal standard that reviewable discretion entails not just a decision but a decision exercised properly, which is critical in ensuring accountability in such quasi-judicial roles.
