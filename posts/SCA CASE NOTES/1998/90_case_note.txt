Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFR
Source Folder: 1998

# Case Note: Santam Limited v Christians Gerdes [1998] ZASCA 43 (SCA)

## F – Facts
The case revolves around the tragic circumstances surrounding the death of Gerd Janssen Gerdes, who was killed in a motor vehicle accident in Durban on 10 September 1990 while temporarily assigned to work in South Africa. His widow, Christians Gerdes, the respondent, sought damages for loss of support from Santam Limited, the appellant, which had been appointed as the agent for claims under the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund Act 93 of 1989 ([para 2]).

At the commencement of the trial, it was established that the plaintiff had received payments from two German funds due to her husband’s death: (1) D M 41 278,88 from Seekasse, a pension insurance institution; and (2) D M 671 535,17 from Norddeutsche Metall-Berufsgenossenschaft, described in greater detail later in the judgment. The latter sum included a death grant, periodic payments, and a final settlement amount ([para 3]). 

The defendant accepted liability for any damages that the plaintiff could substantiate. However, two critical issues emerged for resolution by the court: 
(a) Whether the damages suffered by the plaintiff should be assessed under German law or South African law; and 
(b) Whether the payments received in Germany were to be deducted from her loss of support claim ([para 4]).

While the court ruled that South African law would apply, the appeal focused solely on whether the German payments qualified as deductible under South African law, specifically in relation to section 1 of the Assessment of Damages Act 9 of 1969 ([para 6]).

## I – Issues
The pivotal legal questions presented for the court's determination were:
1. Should the quantum of damages be assessed according to South African law as opposed to German law? ([para 4]) 
2. Are the payments received by the widow under German social security legislation to be deducted from the loss of support claim in accordance with South African law? ([para 6]) 

The crux of the discourse hinged on whether the payments from the German fund could appropriately be classified as either a "pension" or "insurance money" as defined under South African law ([para 8]).

## R – Rules
The key legal frameworks applied in the court's determination include:
1. **Assessment of Damages Act 9 of 1969**, specifically section 1(1), which stipulates that no “insurance money, pension or benefit” resulting from the death shall be taken into account when assessing damages for loss of support ([para 6]).

2. **Common Law Principles**: Prior to the enactment of the Assessment of Damages Act, South African common law principles permitted deductions from damages for support due to payments such as insurance or pension benefits received as a result of the deceased's death ([para 7]).

3. **Statutory Interpretation**: The court emphasized that terms like "pension" and "insurance money," while not defined in the Act, should be interpreted according to their ordinary grammatical meanings ([para 8]).

## A – Application
The court closely analyzed the nature of the payments made under the BG fund against the definitions and intent articulated in the Assessment of Damages Act. It itemized the provisions of the fund, revealing that:
- Contributions were made solely by employers, without direct contributions from the employees, which contradicted traditional notions of pension schemes ([paras 9–10]).
- The "survivor's pension" provided to the widow incorporated periodic payments and an option for a lump sum payment, which the appellant argued could negate its classification as a true pension ([para 10]).

The court countered these points with strong reasoning, noting that various statutory pension schemes exist that also do not require direct contributions from beneficiaries and that the B G payments met the functional definition of a pension as a recurring benefit paid after the death of a worker, irrespective of how contributions are structured ([para 12]).

Moreover, the court aligned its interpretation with the overarching purpose of the Assessment of Damages Act, aimed at safeguarding dependents’ financial positions post-accident. It emphasized that the nature of pensions varies, and the ordinary meaning of the term does not necessitate a monetary contribution from the dependent ([paras 15–16]).

In conclusion, the court held that the German payments to the widow constituted pension payments as contemplated by the Act and, thus, were exempt from deduction ([para 16]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa dismissed the appeal by Santam Limited, upholding the lower court's judgment that the German payments qualified as pension payments under the Assessment of Damages Act. The court concluded that these payments should not be deducted from the damages awarded to Christians Gerdes for loss of support. The ruling illustrates a significant affirmation of the principles underpinning the Act, emphasizing its objective to ensure that dependents are not prejudiced by payouts from social security schemes in the aftermath of a tragic loss ([para 16]).

Overall, the case underscores the importance of interpreting statutory provisions in a manner that aligns with their intended purpose in protecting vulnerable dependents, affirming the broader aim of ensuring equitable financial support in the wake of loss. The implications of this judgment reinforce the need for careful consideration of how foreign social securities interact with domestic law in claims for loss of support.
