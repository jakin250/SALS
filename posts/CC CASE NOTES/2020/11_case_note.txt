Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note: New Nation Movement NPC and Others v President of the Republic of South Africa and Others [2020] ZACC 11

## F - Facts
The case originated from an urgent application brought by the New Nation Movement NPC and other applicants on 18 April 2019. They sought leave to appeal directly to the Constitutional Court against a judgment of the High Court of South Africa, Western Cape Division, Cape Town, which had dismissed their challenge to the constitutionality of certain provisions of the Electoral Act 73 of 1998 ([1]–[5]).

The applicants contended that the Electoral Act was unconstitutional as it limited the right conferred by section 19(3)(b) of the Constitution, which grants every adult citizen the right to stand for public office and, if elected, to hold office, exclusively through membership of political parties. They also argued that this limitation infringed their right to freedom of association as guaranteed by section 18 of the Constitution ([4], [5],[6]).

The High Court dismissed the application primarily by arguing that section 19(3)(b) does not explicitly provide for candidates to stand as independents, thus implicitly requiring membership in a political party. The High Court further relied on the constitutional framework that endorses a multi-party system, asserting that Parliament has discretion in determining the electoral system ([5], [6]). The applicants' appeal was upheld by the Constitutional Court, which deemed the limitations imposed by the Electoral Act as unconstitutional and ordered Parliament to adapt the legislation within a specified timeframe ([25], [26]).

## I - Issues
The key legal and constitutional questions considered by the court were:
1. Does the Electoral Act unjustifiably limit the right to stand for public office as established under section 19(3)(b) of the Constitution, by mandating that such candidacy can only occur through political parties? ([4]–[5])
2. Does the limitation imposed by the Electoral Act infringe upon the right to freedom of association enshrined in section 18 of the Constitution? ([5])
3. What is the appropriate interpretation of section 19(3)(b) concerning the role of independent candidates within the electoral framework?[2]

## R - Rules
The court referenced several legal principles and constitutional provisions to guide its analysis, including:
- **Section 19(3)(b) of the Constitution**: This provision guarantees every adult citizen the right to stand for public office and to hold office if elected.
- **Section 18 of the Constitution**: This section ensures the right to freedom of association.
- **The multi-party system established by Section 1(d) of the Constitution**, which underscores the importance of a democratic process that includes various political parties ([1], [2], [3]).
- **Previous judgments** such as *Ramakatsa v Magashule* [2012] ZACC 31 and *My Vote Counts II* [2018] ZACC 17, which were invoked to elucidate interpretations related to political participation and the role of independent candidates ([4], [8], [10]).

## A - Application
The court applied the above rules by interpreting section 19(3)(b) in a manner that foregrounded the historical context of disenfranchisement and exclusion. It emphasized that the purpose of this provision is to ensure that no citizen, regardless of their choice to join or not join a political party, is denied the opportunity to seek public office ([21], [32],[38]).

The court found that requiring candidates to be affiliated with political parties infringed on the constitutional guarantee of individual political rights. It held that this requirement could not be sustained in light of the Constitution’s favoring of individual political choices and participation in representative democracy. Thus, the limitation imposed by the Electoral Act on candidates seeking to stand for election as independents was deemed unconstitutional as it obstructed the exercise of the rights enshrined in sections 18 and 19(3) ([25],[34]).

In justifying its decision, the court emphasized the importance of a democracy where citizens must have the option to be represented by their own elected officials, directly accountable to them, rather than through political party nominees, who may not reflect the will or interests of all constituents ([38],[40]).

## C - Conclusion
The Constitutional Court held that the provisions of the Electoral Act requiring individuals to stand for public office solely through political parties are unconstitutional. The court declared these provisions invalid and ordered that Parliament rectify the situation within twenty-four months of the ruling, while also mandating that the Minister of Home Affairs pays the applicants' costs, inclusive of two counsel fees ([5],[39]).

### Implications
This decision has significant ramifications for the electoral landscape in South Africa, reaffirming the principle of individual political rights, and the recognition of independent candidates as essential components of a robust representative democracy. By compelling legislative change within a strict timeline, this case emphasizes the dynamic nature of constitutional rights and the judiciary's role in ensuring legislative compliance with the foundational values of the Constitution. 

The court's interpretation underscores the need for an electoral framework that is fully inclusive, enhancing democratic accountability and encouraging greater political engagement among citizens across all spectrums of society.
