FIRAC Case Note: My Vote Counts NPC v Speaker of the National Assembly and Others [2015] ZACC 31

Constitutional Court 2015 [2015] ZACC 31 F – Facts: The case involves *My Vote Cou

FIRAC Case Note: My Vote Counts NPC v Speaker of the National Assembly and Others [2015] ZACC 31

F – Facts:
The case involves My Vote Counts NPC (the applicant) against various respondents, primarily the Speaker of the National Assembly and the Chairperson of the National Council of Provinces, regarding the alleged failure of Parliament to fulfill its constitutional obligation to enact legislation that requires political parties to disclose their private funding sources ([1]–[5]). The applicant, a non-profit organization advocating for transparency and accountability in politics, contends that access to information on private political funding is essential for the effective exercise of the right to vote as enshrined in section 19(3) of the South African Constitution ([2]). The respondents argue that Parliament has satisfied its constitutional duties through the Promotion of Access to Information Act (PAIA) and contest the applicant’s assertions ([3], [4]). The case was heard on February 10, 2015, and the judgment was delivered on September 30, 2015.

I – Issues:
The central legal questions include:

  1. Whether the lack of legislation compelling the disclosure of political parties' private funding constitutes a failure by Parliament to fulfill its constitutional obligations as per section 32(2) of the Constitution ([1]), [20]).
  2. Whether access to such financial information is reasonably required for the exercise of the right to vote under section 19(3) of the Constitution ([31]).
  3. Whether the principle of constitutional subsidiarity applies to the applicant's claims and, if so, how it impacts the applicant’s ability to seek relief through the Constitutional Court without first challenging the constitutionality of PAIA ([76], [160]).
  4. Whether the Constitutional Court has the jurisdiction under section 167(4)(e) to compel legislative action regarding political party funding transparency ([21]).

R – Rules:
The court relied on:

  • Section 167(4)(e) of the South African Constitution, which grants exclusive jurisdiction to the Constitutional Court to decide if Parliament has failed to fulfill a constitutional obligation ([1]).
  • Section 32 of the Constitution, which establishes the right of access to information held by the state and private bodies necessary for the exercise or protection of rights ([4], [32]).
  • Case law references, including Doctors for Life International v Speaker of the National Assembly, which outlines the obligations of Parliament to legislate in line with the Constitution ([6], [12]).

Various provisions of PAIA were discussed, emphasizing its limitations regarding the right to access political parties' funding information ([94], [102], [117]).

A – Application:
The court evaluated the applicant’s claim against the backdrop of established jurisprudence. The applicant asserted that without national legislation specifically compelling disclosure of political parties' private funding, there exists a failure on Parliament's part to comply with its constitutional obligations under section 32(2) ([5], [118]). The court found that access to funding information is essential for voters to make informed decisions and that the applicant seeks not just the fulfillment of a right, but actionable legislative changes ([2], [42]).

The discussion centered on whether PAIA effectively fulfills this need for transparency. The minority judgment highlighted PAIA's restrictions, suggesting that its reactive nature does not facilitate the proactive disclosure necessary for democratic participation ([94], [101], [117]). The majority judgment took the position that the principle of subsidiarity applied, indicating that clarity regarding PAIA's scope must be achieved through a formal legislative challenge, thus asserting Parliament's autonomy in law-making ([121], [150]).

C – Conclusion:
The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the application, concluding that while access to political parties' private funding information is indeed significant for exercising the right to vote, the applicant's challenge did not constitute a frontal attack on the validity of PAIA as required under the principle of subsidiarity ([195]). The majority's stance underscored the necessity for legislative entities to operate within their defined constitutional roles, thereby upholding the separation of powers ([130], [150], [175]). The judgment implies that the legislative framework for transparency in political party funding remains an essential aspect to be addressed by Parliament, leaving the door open for future challenges post-reform or inadequate legislative response concerning party financing ([94], [118]).

The order concluded with a dismissal of the application, suggesting that while the need for transparency is recognized, the means of achieving it falls under Parliament's jurisdiction and discretion, not the Court's mandate to direct legislative processes ([195]).
