Supreme Court of Appeal

D & H Piping Systems (Pty) Ltd v Trans Hex Group Ltd [2006] SCA 31 (RSA)

The dispute centered on the contractual relationship between the parties and whether the respondent was liable for the consequential losses resulting from the defects. The court a quo initially found in favor of the respondent, leading to the appellant’s appeal ([paras 1–3]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2006

F – Facts

The appellant, D & H Piping Systems (Pty) Ltd, is a manufacturer of concrete products, specifically concrete pipes. For over thirty years, it had sourced dolomitic aggregate and sand from the respondent, Trans Hex Group Ltd, which primarily manufactures lime products. In the latter half of 1998, the appellant purchased aggregate and sand from the respondent for the production of concrete sewerage pipes for a client. Following the sale, the appellant experienced significant failures in the pipes, due to alleged latent defects in the aggregate and sand, incurring liabilities exceeding R13 million. The appellant subsequently sought to recover this amount from the respondent.

The dispute centered on the contractual relationship between the parties and whether the respondent was liable for the consequential losses resulting from the defects. The court a quo initially found in favor of the respondent, leading to the appellant’s appeal ([paras 1–3]).

I – Issues

The appeal presented several legal issues:

  1. Contractual Obligation: Was it a term of the contract that the respondent would supply dolomitic aggregate and sand for concrete piping manufacturing?
  2. Liability of Manufacturing Seller: Was the respondent a manufacturing seller liable for consequential losses due to alleged latent defects, or were the respondent’s general terms and conditions applicable, which contained exclusion clauses?
  3. Tacit Agreement for Exclusion: Did the parties tacitly agree to exclude liability for consequential losses on the part of the respondent? ([para 8]).

R – Rules

The legal principles pertinent to this case included:

  • Contract Law Principles: The concept of "pactum de contrahendo" denotes an agreement to contract in the future, which existed between the parties ([para 7]). Additionally, the necessity of mutual assent for terms to be incorporated into a contract was central to the argument ([paras 12–13]).
  • Liability of Manufacturing Sellers: The court explored the definitions surrounding manufacturing sellers. Historically, a seller is liable for latent defects if they publicly profess expertise in the products sold, as articulated in landmark cases such as Holmdene Brickworks (Pty) Ltd v Roberts Construction Co Ltd ([paras 20-31]).
  • Exclusion Clauses: The validity and enforceability of general terms and conditions that sought to limit liability formed another vital legal point ([para 10]).

A – Application

Contractual Obligation

Initially, the court concluded that the respondent had indeed undertaken to supply the required materials, ruling that the appellant had sufficiently amended its claim to reflect this understanding without prejudice ([para 9]).

Liability of Manufacturing Seller

The issue of whether Trans Hex was a manufacturing seller was central. The court articulated that, regardless of the expertise or skill associated with the manufacturing process, liability for consequential damages from latent defects is attached to sellers of their manufactured goods ([paras 30-31]). They clarified that the respondent could be liable without needing to demonstrate special skill or expertise in the manufacture of the aggregate and sand ([paras 23-31]).

Exclusion Clauses

The court found that the respondent’s general terms and conditions, which purportedly limited liability, did not form part of the contractual arrangements. The court held that knowledge of these terms must be notified to authorized representatives for them to be validly incorporated into the contract. Since the appellant's authorized representative, Mr. Lombard, was unaware of these terms, they could not bind the appellant to the exclusion ([paras 10-14]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the appellant, establishing several important legal principles:

  1. The respondent was required to supply dolomitic aggregate and sand to the appellant, confirming the contract's terms.
  2. The respondent was determined to be a manufacturing seller, liable for consequential losses arising from latent defects in the goods sold, irrespective of any general terms and conditions that attempted to limit this liability and were found not applicable ([paras 39-40]).
  3. The court's judgment reaffirms the necessity for clarity in incorporating exclusion clauses to ensure they are binding.

The appeal was successful, and costs, including those of two counsel, were awarded to the appellant, but with an acknowledgment that the appellant still faced challenges in recovering the claimed amount in subsequent proceedings ([paras 40]).

This case underscores the need for clarity in contractual dealings and the rigorous standards required for the incorporation of exclusion clauses within commercial relationships, particularly concerning manufacturing defects.
