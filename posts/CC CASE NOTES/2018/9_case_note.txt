Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: Saidi and Others v Minister of Home Affairs and Others [2018] ZACC 9

## F – Facts
The case involved Saidi and 28 other applicants—foreign nationals seeking refugee status in South Africa—who applied for asylum at the Cape Town Refugee Reception Office (RRO) ([4]). They were issued temporary asylum permits as per section 22(1) of the Refugees Act 130 of 1998, which allowed them to reside lawfully in South Africa during the application process. However, their applications faced delays, leading to the expiration of these permits ([4]).

Previously, the RRO had followed a practice of extending permits automatically whenever applicants lodged judicial reviews under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) against negative decisions from the Refugee Status Determination Officer (RSDO) ([4]). This practice was halted by a new acting manager at the RRO, who denied extensions post-internal review, insisting that extensions could only occur via High Court order ([4]).

The asylum seekers filed urgent applications in the High Court to compel the RRO to renew their permits until their PAJA reviews were resolved. The High Court acknowledged that while the RRO had the power to extend permits, this power came with discretion, and thus remitted the matter back to the RRO for a decision on permit extensions ([4]).

Following adverse decisions obtained through internal reviews and appeals, the respondents (Minister of Home Affairs, Director-General of the Department of Home Affairs, and the Acting Manager of the Cape Town Refugee Facility) appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which ultimately largely upheld the High Court’s decision. The applicants then sought leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court ([4]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal questions were:
1. Does an RRO have the authority to extend temporary asylum permits pending the outcome of a judicial review based on PAJA? 
2. If such authority exists, is the extension automatic, or does it require the exercise of discretion by the RRO? ([1], [5], [14]).

## R – Rules
The Court primarily relied on the following statutes and legal principles:
- **Refugees Act 130 of 1998**, particularly sections 22(1) and 22(3), which deal with the issuance and extension of asylum permits ([5]).
- **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**, which provides the framework for challenging administrative actions ([5]).
- Constitutional principles regarding access to justice and just administrative action, as rooted in section 33 of the Constitution ([5]).

Significant precedents included:
- The purposive interpretation principle concerning the protection of rights in the Bill of Rights ([5]).
- The principle of **non-refoulement**, embedded within international law and section 2 of the Refugees Act, which prohibits the return of individuals to places where they would face persecution ([6]).

## A – Application
### Power to Extend Pending Judicial Review
The Court interpreted **section 22(1)** of the Refugees Act as requiring the issuance of a permit pending the resolution of an asylum application. The Court stated that failing to extend permits while judicial reviews are pending would violate the principle of non-refoulement and contravene the rights afforded to asylum seekers ([14]).

The Court concluded that:
- The power afforded to RROs under **section 22(3)** did not merely grant discretion but imposed an obligation to act in light of the circumstances surrounding each asylum applications and the existence of ongoing judicial reviews. Denying an RRO the authority to extend permits would threaten the applicants' rights to life and security while their status was being determined ([17]).

### Discretion vs. Obligation
The Court contended that while other interpretations of “may” might confer discretion, the interpretation promoting the protection of asylum seekers’ rights was more appropriate in this context. The majority further argued that the automatic extension of permits was essential to avoid exposing asylum seekers to the risk of unlawful deportation during the judicial review process. The Court rejected the interpretation that suggested RROs had unfettered discretion to deny extensions, stating that it was irrational and unconstitutional to allow for such powers when the potential for grave harm was present ([18], [20]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately:
1. Granted leave to appeal and cross-appeal.
2. Upheld the applicants' appeal while dismissing the cross-appeal.
3. Set aside orders from both lower courts, declaring that RROs possess the authority to extend asylum permits pending the outcomes of judicial reviews under PAJA. It concluded that extensions must occur automatically according to the relevant statutory provisions and regulations that govern such proceedings ([1], [14]).

The implications of this judgment extend to affirming the obligations of state officials to protect the rights of asylum seekers effectively and ensuring that administrative actions comply with constitutional guarantees of fairness and security ([1], [6]). The Court emphasized a holistic interpretation of statutory provisions aligned with international legal standards, particularly the principle of non-refoulement, underscoring its overarching importance in refugee law ([12], [14]).
