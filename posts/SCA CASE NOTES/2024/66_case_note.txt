# Case Note: National Student Financial Aid Scheme v Moloi and Others (574/2022 & 194/2022)

**Neutral Citation**: National Student Financial Aid Scheme v Moloi and Others (574/2022 & 194/2022) [2024] ZASCA 66 (03 May 2024)  
**Court**: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Coram**: Dambuza ADP and Hughes, Mabindla-Boqwana, Goosen, Molefe JJA  
**Heard**: 9 May 2023  
**Delivered**: 3 May 2024  

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The National Student Financial Aid Scheme (NSFAS) initiated this case with a challenge regarding its eligibility criteria for funding postgraduate qualifications under its bursary scheme, particularly relating to a Bachelor of Laws (LLB) degree ([4]–[11]).

1. **Background**: NSFAS, established under the National Student Financial Aid Scheme Act No. 56 of 1999, is responsible for providing financial aid to eligible students in South Africa. The criteria for funding are annually updated, involving collective consultations with the Minister of Higher Education and relevant stakeholders, including Universities South Africa ([4]).

2. **2021 Guidelines Context**: In March 2021, due to budget constraints exacerbated by the COVID-19 pandemic, the Minister announced modifications to the 2021 funding guidelines, excluding funding for new students seeking second or postgraduate qualifications ([6]–[9]). 

3. **Claimants**: Three respondents—Samantha Moloi, Linda Makhaza, and Keabetswe Motaung—were enrolled at Wits University and sought NSFAS funding for a postgraduate LLB degree. They argued that they had a legitimate expectation of funding based on previous guidelines ([10]–[13]).

4. **Legal Challenge**: The respondents filed an application at the Gauteng High Court, asserting that the Minister’s decision to exclude LLB funding was unfair, irrational, and in violation of their rights under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) and the principle of legality ([20]).

5. **High Court Decision**: The High Court ruled in favor of the respondents, stating that the exclusion of the LLB degree from funding was an administrative action that failed to meet procedural fairness requirements, particularly the lack of consultation with affected students ([20]).

### I – Issues
The case raised several significant legal and constitutional issues:

1. **Characterization of Action**: Was the decision to exclude postgraduate LLB funding characterized as an administrative action or an executive policy formulation? ([22]–[27]). 

2. **Procedural Fairness**: Did NSFAS and the Minister act with procedural fairness by not allowing affected students to make representations prior to the decision concerning funding for postgraduate qualifications? ([20]).

3. **Legitimate Expectation**: Did the respondents possess a legitimate expectation that their studies would be funded based on prior guidelines and practices established by NSFAS? ([48]).

4. **Rationality and Reasonableness**: Was the decision made by NSFAS and the Minister rationally connected to the objectives of the funding scheme, and did it adhere to the principles of reasonableness as outlined in the Constitution? ([38]–[47]).

### R – Rules
The court considered multiple statutory provisions and principles pertinent to the issues at hand:

1. **National Student Financial Aid Scheme Act**: Under Section 4(b), NSFAS functions in consultation with the Minister to develop funding eligibility criteria. This statutory framework underpinned the authority for determining funding eligibility ([4], [31]).

2. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act**: PAJA, particularly Section 6, stipulates that administrative actions must be procedurally fair, lawful, and rational. The requirements for procedural fairness stem from both PAJA and the constitutional right to lawful administrative action ([52]).

3. **Case Law**: The judgment referenced key case law, including *Masethla v The President of the Republic of South Africa* ([14]) and *Albutt v Centre for the Study of Violence and Reconciliation* (2010) to clarify the requirements of rationality and procedural fairness in executive decisions ([55], [59]).

4. **Constitutional Principles**: The principles within Sections 29(1) and 33 of the Constitution were pivotal in examining the respondents' rights to further education and the legality of executive actions impacting this right ([38], [46]).

### A – Application
In its analysis, the court pivoted on several critical points:

1. **Characterization of the Decision**: The court determined that the exclusion of the LLB from funding was an exercise of executive power, rejecting the notion of it being solely an administrative action. This assessment was based on the need for broader consultations and considerations inherent in budgetary and policy formulation processes ([27], [31]).

2. **Procedural Fairness Evaluation**: The court recognized that while the consultations with USAF and SAUS did not include all affected students, the urgency and context surrounding the budget shortfall justified the decision. Thus, the consultation conducted was deemed adequate under the circumstances ([62]).

3. **Legitimate Expectation**: While the respondents argued they had a legitimate expectation of funding based on historical practices, the court found insufficient evidence to support that claim. NSFAS did not establish a consistent practice of funding postgraduate LLB degrees, particularly with the updated guidelines highlighting a focus on first-time entry students ([50], [51]).

4. **Rationality of the Decision**: The appellants successfully argued that the decision to prioritize first-time undergraduate funding over postgraduate degrees was rational, given budget limitations and the broader aim of supporting more students. The court acknowledged that while the decision might be viewed as harsh, it was justifiable given the economic context ([42], [44], [47]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, setting aside the High Court's order that had initially favored the respondents. The court concluded that:

1. The exclusion of LLB funding was correctly characterized as an executive action, not an administrative one requiring PAJA compliance.
2. The procedural measures taken, despite being limited, were adequate given the urgent financial context.
3. The claim of legitimate expectation was not substantiated by a well-established practice of funding the postgraduate LLB within the NSFAS guidelines.
4. The decision to prioritise funding toward first-time undergraduate qualifications was rational, aimed at maximizing the number of beneficiaries under financial constraints.

The effective order replaced the High Court's ruling, dismissing the respondents' application with no costs awarded to either party ([63]). 

This decision underscores critical aspects of administrative law, highlighting the necessity of balancing procedural fairness with overarching public interests and fiscal realities in policy implementation. Further implications suggest a continuing reliance on principles of rationality and the limits of legitimate expectations within the realm of executive decision-making ([38]–[47]). 

For future cases, the delineation between executive and administrative actions, along with the sufficiency of consultation processes, remains a focal point for constitutional scrutiny.
