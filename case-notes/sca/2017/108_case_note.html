FIRAC Method for Mostert NO v The Registrar of Pension Funds (986/2016)

The case revolves around an appeal initiated by Antony Louis Mostert, serving as one of the joint liquidators of the Picbel Groepvoorsorgfonds (the "Fund"), against the dismissal of his review application in the Gauteng Local Division.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 Mostert NO v The Registrar of Pension Funds (986/2016) ZASCA 108 (15 September 2017)

Neutral Citation: Mostert NO v The Registrar of Pension Funds (986/2016) ZASCA 108 (15 September 2017)

F – Facts

The case revolves around an appeal initiated by Antony Louis Mostert, serving as one of the joint liquidators of the Picbel Groepvoorsorgfonds (the "Fund"), against the dismissal of his review application in the Gauteng Local Division. The review sought to declare Regulation 35(4), established under the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956 (the "PFA"), as ultra vires and unenforceable due to its alleged irrationality and unconstitutionality ([paras 1-2]).

The Fund, established in 1971, was closed to new members by 2001, and was ultimately dissolved in 2008 with Mostert appointed as liquidator ([paras 15-17]). The regulatory framework implicated in this appeal is crucially tied to the provisions under the PFA, especially section 15B concerning the apportionment of actuarial surplus among members ([paras 19-20]). Regulation 35(4) mandates that unclaimed benefits, when a member cannot be traced, be allocated to a contingency reserve account, potentially impacting around R41.8 million initially allocated for former members ([paras 22-23]).

The application to review was dismissed by Van der Linde J on the grounds of non-compliance with the 180-day limit prescribed by section 7(1) of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (the "PAJA"), a key statutory framework guiding administrative action in South Africa ([para 3]).

I – Issues

The primary issues determining the outcome of this appeal include:

  1. Whether the court a quo erred in dismissing the review application based on an alleged failure to comply with the 180-day period stipulated in section 7(1) of the PAJA.
  2. Whether the court had the authority to raise the issue of delay mero motu, particularly when such a point was not raised by the respondents in their initial responses ([paras 1-4, 31]).
  3. Determining the commencement date of the 180-day period concerning knowledge of the administrative action taken by the Minister concerning the regulation 35(4) ([paras 7, 40, 44]).

R – Rules

Key legal principles and statutory provisions governing this case include:

  • Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA): Particularly sections 6 and 7, which outline review processes and time frames for challenging administrative actions ([paras 12-14]).
  • Prescription Act 68 of 1969: Although not directly applicable, comparisons are drawn regarding the fixed time limits imposed for legal action ([para 37]).
  • Case law precedents: Such as Camps Bay Ratepayers’ and Residents’ Association v Harrison [2011] ZACC 2 and Opposition to Urban Tolling Alliance v South African National Roads Agency Ltd [2013] 4 All SA 639 (SCA) which contextualize the application of delay principles and administrative action recognition ([paras 26, 34, 44]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal, led by Ploos van Amstel AJA, analyzed the invocation of the 180-day period for review applications under section 7(1) of PAJA. This court articulated that the period itself is pre-determined as unreasonable if the action is instituted outside the specified timeframe unless extended under section 9. In this instance, arguments regarding the delay were first introduced in the appellant’s heads of argument, suggesting a retroactive acknowledgment of potential delays ([para 25]).

The court also contended with the notion of when the 180-day period should commence. It rejected the appellant’s argument that it should start only when the regulation directly impacted the Fund, emphasizing a broader interpretation aligning with established norms that known impacts from regulatory actions should trigger knowledge for all affected parties ([para 44, 48]).

The court further evaluated whether the lower court properly allowed the Minister to challenge jurisdiction on the basis of delay, affirming that the Minister could raise such an issue even if not explicitly pleaded, as the court has an authority to ensure adherence to statutory time limits ([paras 31-38]).

Ultimately, the court found the appeal delayed by nearly 12 years from the promulgation of Regulation 35(4), far outside the bounds of the legislated 180 days. Most importantly, it underscored the obligation of the appellant to demonstrate compliance with procedural timelines, which he failed to do. Thus, the dismissal of the application was upheld ([paras 52-53]).

C – Conclusion

The appeal in Mostert NO v The Registrar of Pension Funds was dismissed, with costs ordered against the appellant. The ratio decidendi establishes that failure to comply with the 180-day timeline stipulated under PAJA cannot be overlooked, thereby confirming the necessity for strict adherence to legislative provisions governing administrative reviews. This decision has significant implications for future administrative actions, emphasizing the responsibilities of applicants to remain vigilant regarding compliance with statutory timeframes and procedural requirements ([paras 54]).

In conclusion, the ruling reinforces the critical balance between the need for timely administrative justice and the necessity for administrative actions to withstand scrutiny within reasonable and well-defined parameters. The implications for liquidators and fund administrators in similar contexts underscore the importance of practical knowledge of administrative processes and timelines.
