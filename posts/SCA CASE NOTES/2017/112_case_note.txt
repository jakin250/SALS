# Case Note: FIRAC Method on *Reclamation Property Holdings (Pty) Ltd v ArcelorMittal South Africa Ltd* 

**Court**: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Neutral Citation**: *Reclamation Property Holdings (Pty) Ltd v ArcelorMittal South Africa Ltd (986/2016) ZASCA 112 (21 September 2017)*  
**Case No**: 1074/2016  
**Judges**: Ponnan, Mathopo JJA, Plasket, Ploos van Amstel, Rogers AJJA  
**Heard**: 5 September 2017  
**Delivered**: 21 September 2017  

---

## F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1-8])

This case involves the validity of a sale agreement related to land in a proposed township known as Benoni Extension 74. The appellant, Reclamation Property Holdings (Pty) Ltd, alongside the second and third respondents (collectively referred to as the Reclamation Group), is a major recycler of metals in South Africa. In 1999, the Reclamation Group purchased the business of National Metal (Pty) Ltd, previously a subsidiary of Iscor Ltd, which eventually became ArcelorMittal South Africa Ltd (the first respondent).

In the early 2000s, Iscor initiated investigations into the establishment of the Benoni Extension 74 township, where it owned land. The Reclamation Group expressed intent to purchase the leased land, which resulted in a sale agreement in March 2003. A critical aspect was that the sale was contingent upon the establishment and approval of the township as per the Town-Planning and Townships Ordinance 15 of 1986 ('the Ordinance'). However, as of January 2010, Iscor claimed the agreement had lapsed due to unmet suspensive conditions, which led to Reclamation Group alleging that the agreement remained valid and requesting a court declaration for its enforceability.

The Gauteng Local Division, presided over by Judge Wepener, ruled that the agreement was void per section 67 of the Ordinance, which prohibits the sale of an erf in proposed townships until they have been formally approved. The appeal challenged this ruling, entering the Supreme Court of Appeal.

## I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 9-24])

The principal legal issue at hand was whether the agreement of sale constituted a binding contract, given that it seemingly contravened section 67 of the Ordinance, which prohibits selling erven in a proposed township before it has received formal approval. Additionally, the court needed to ascertain if Portion 32 of the land fell within the definition of 'erf' according to section 1 of the Ordinance, which is integral to determining the breadth of the prohibition imposed by section 67.

## R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 9-11])

Section 67 of the Ordinance explicitly states that after steps have been taken to establish a township, no contracts for the sale, exchange, or alienation of an erf within that township may be entered into until it is declared an approved township. Key subsections include:

1. Contracts in conflict become devoid of force and effect (s 67(2)).
2. The definition of 'erf' encompasses not only approved erven but also land intended to be part of a township, regardless of formal recognition.

Case law referenced includes *Soja (Pty) Ltd v Tuckers Land & Development Corporation (Pty) Ltd* 1981 (3) SA 314 (A) and *Headermans (Vryburg) (Pty) Ltd v Ping Bai* 1997 (3) SA 1004 (SCA), which address analogous provisions in earlier ordinances and the rationale behind prohibiting transactions that could harm potential buyers.

## A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 12-23])

The court's primary focus was whether Portion 32 fell under the definition of 'erf,' impacting the validity of the sale agreement under section 67. The definition mentioned two components: land in an approved township and land laid out for a township, which might not yet be recognized. Evidence confirmed that the local authority approved the proposed township in 2009 while the sale agreement predated this approval.

The appellant contended that, since Portion 32 would lose its identity upon the establishment of the township (merging into erf 8740), it should not be classified as an 'erf' under the Ordinance. This argument was dismissed as it would impose unnecessary distinctions counter to the prohibition’s intent. The court emphasized that the legislation aimed to protect buyers from risks associated with unapproved properties—regardless of the land’s future designation within the township—and held that the prohibition applied uniformly to all particular portions of land laid out for township establishment.

## C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 24-26])

The Supreme Court upheld the decision of the court a quo, affirming that the sale agreement was void under section 67 of the Ordinance because the agreement involved an erf as defined in the legislation, rendering it of no force and effect. The court emphasized the uniformly applied prohibition for all portions of land involved in a proposed township, irrespective of their future designation. The ruling reinforces the protective intent of section 67 for buyers regarding properties in unapproved townships, maintaining a stringent interpretation of the Ordinance and minimizing financial risks for potential purchasers.

The appeal was dismissed with costs, including those arising from the employment of two counsel. Notably, the appeal's outcome clarifies the thresholds for establishing contracts involving land within proposed township frameworks and underlines the necessity for strict compliance with urban planning laws in South Africa.
