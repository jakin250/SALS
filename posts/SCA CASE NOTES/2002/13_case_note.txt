# Case Note: Katazile Mkangeli and 241 Others v Joshua Johannes Joubert and Others

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Case No:** 220/2001  
**Judgment Delivered:** 25 March 2002

---

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
[1] The case arises from a parcel of land known as 'Itsoseng', a 23-hectare property situated in a semi-rural area northwest of Johannesburg, registered in the name of 'The trustees for the time being of the Itsoseng Community Development Trust' ('the trust'). The 242 appellants, consisting of informal settlers moved to Itsoseng on 6 June 2000 with the trustees' consent, having received a donation to facilitate their relocation from another informal settlement owned by Fedsure Life Assurance Limited.

[3] Shortly after the move, property owners in the vicinity, represented by the respondents, sought an eviction order from the High Court. Initially, the appellants were not included in the application but were later added at the direction of the court. The application was based on two primary complaints: violation of a town-planning scheme which permitted only one dwelling per plot and allegations of nuisance caused by the informal settlement.

[5] The appellants admitted to some violations but denied responsibility for more serious allegations of criminal activity. Their main defense centered on the protection afforded by the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (Esta), asserting that the High Court lacked jurisdiction to order their eviction based on the provisions of this act.

### I – Issues
[6] The primary issue before the court was whether the protections under Esta applied to the appellants, particularly in relation to the respondents' status as non-owners seeking their eviction. This raises fundamental constitutional questions about the rights of informal settlers under legislation designed to secure tenure against eviction, and the conflicting rights of property owners.

### R – Rules
[8] Esta was enacted as a response to the constitutional imperative established in Sections 25(6) and (9) of the Constitution of South Africa, which guarantees secure tenure for individuals whose rights to land are insecure due to past discriminatory laws. The act defines 'occupiers' and establishes protections against eviction, particularly emphasizing the requirement for consent from the landowner for any termination of occupancy rights.

[11-13] The act specifies conditions under which an occupier's right to residence can only be terminated on lawful grounds and if it is deemed just and equitable to do so (sections 8 and 11). Importantly, the act delineates that eviction orders can only be issued under its provisions, and the jurisdiction to enforce these provisions typically lies with the magistrate's and Land Claims Courts, with limited exceptions for High Court jurisdiction.

### A – Application
[20] The court analyzed whether the provisions of Esta apply to situations where eviction is sought by non-owners. The respondents argued that since the legislation focuses on the owner-occupier relationship, it should not extend to non-owners. However, the court emphasized a literal interpretation of the act, noting that it seeks to afford robust protections to occupiers regardless of the applicant's status. 

[24] The Supreme Court of Appeal affirmed that the High Court erred in ordering the eviction. The court underscored that the appellants were neighbors of the respondents and thus their interests were significantly intertwined in the context of land rights. The requirement for the owner's consent implies that the actions of non-owners seeking eviction must align with Esta's provisions to ensure that the rights of occupiers are not arbitrarily terminated.

[27] The judgment outlined the risks of interpreting the act in a manner that protects only property owners while exposing occupiers to arbitrary evictions by third parties, one of the very scenarios the legislation sought to rectify.

### C – Conclusion
[27] The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of the appellants, emphasizing the importance of safeguarding the rights of those occupying land in vulnerable positions. The court's ruling not only reversed the eviction order but underscored the legislative intent behind Esta to provide comprehensive protections for occupiers. In doing so, the judgment reaffirms that the jurisdictional requirements under Esta must be adhered to, asserting that the High Court had no authority to issue the eviction order without full compliance with the statutory prerequisites.

---

This case serves as a critical precedent in the interpretation of land tenure rights in South Africa, highlighting the complexities of ownership, occupancy, and the socio-political context surrounding land use in post-apartheid South Africa. It reinforces constitutional protections aimed at providing security and equity for historically displaced communities.
