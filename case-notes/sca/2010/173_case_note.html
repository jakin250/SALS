Sandlundlu (Pty) Ltd v Shepstone and Wylie Inc (63/10) [2010] ZASCA 173

A lease agreement was drafted, reflecting a rental of R4,500, as opposed to the orally agreed R50,000 ([4–6]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2010 [2010] ZASCA 173

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The case concerns an appeal and a cross-appeal stemming from the Kwa-Zulu Natal High Court's decision. The appellant, Sandlundlu (Pty) Ltd, had successfully sued its former attorney, Shepstone and Wylie Inc., for damages resulting from a failure to include an orally agreed monthly rental in a written lease agreement ([1]). The appellant owned prime real estate housing the Estuary Hotel in Port Edward, which the Reardons intended to develop alongside financing from Dr. Kotter of Biz Afrika 987 (Pty) Ltd ([2–3]).

A lease agreement was drafted, reflecting a rental of R4,500, as opposed to the orally agreed R50,000 ([4–6]). Biz-Afrika took possession of the hotel in June 2000 but failed to pay any rental after November 2000. The appellant eventually had to seek eviction and saw Biz-Afrika liquidated in 2007 without recovering any rental ([7–9]). Following arbitration, the lease agreement was rectified to reflect the correct rental, but the appellant claimed damages from Breytenbach's negligence ([10–11]).

I – Issues

The legal questions posed in this case concern:

  1. Whether Breytenbach's negligence caused tangible loss to the appellant ([12–14]).
  2. Whether damages awarded for loss of rental income should encompass the entire period until the eviction of Biz-Afrika ([22–24]).
  3. The appropriate nature of costs awarded for the arbitration ([25]).

R – Rules

The court identified that to establish a breach of contract, the plaintiff must show, on a balance of probabilities, that the breach caused the loss incurred ([13]). In terms of causation, the "but-for" test is used to ascertain if the loss would have occurred but for the defendant's wrongful conduct ([14]). The "realistic possibility" test for damages emphasizes foreseeability both at the time of contract and breach ([21]).

The case of Holmdene Brickworks (Pty) Ltd v Roberts Construction Co Ltd established foundational principles for awarding damages, asserting that the aggrieved party should be restored to the position it would have occupied had the contract been performed as intended, without imposing undue hardship on the defaulting party ([20]).

A – Application

In applying these rules, the court examined factual causation, pointing out that the appellant would likely have pursued recovery of the rental much sooner had the lease agreement accurately reflected the parties' agreement ([16–19]). Conclusively, the negligence of Breytenbach was established as a probable cause of the appellant's financial loss.

However, while damages were warranted, the court limited the award of loss of rental income to that which could be considered foreseeable—focusing on the relationship dynamics and financial capacities of the parties involved at the time ([22–23]). Notably, beyond March 2002, no evidence supported that the appellant would successfully receive rental payments ([24]).

Regarding the costs incurred during arbitration, the court determined that such costs were indeed recoverable, aligning them with the reasonable foreseeability of executing a rectified contract reflecting the correct rental amount ([25]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed both the appeal and the cross-appeal. The judgment affirmed that while Breytenbach's negligence was a significant factor in causing the appellant's loss, damages would not extend beyond March 2002 due to lack of evidence for continued rental recovery. The appellant was entitled to its arbitration costs calculated as attorney and client fees, clarifying that this is an appropriate remedy considering the nature of the negligence involved ([26]).

This case underscores the importance of precise legal drafting and highlights the complexities involved in proving causation and damages in contractual disputes. The judgment reinforces the necessity for legal professionals to adhere strictly to their mandates to mitigate potential financial repercussions for their clients.
