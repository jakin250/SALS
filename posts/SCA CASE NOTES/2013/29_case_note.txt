Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

### FIRAC Case Note: AllPay Consolidated Investment Holdings (Pty) Ltd & Others v The Chief Executive Officer of the South African Social Security Agency & Others (678/12)

**Neutral Citation:** AllPay Consolidated Investment Holdings & others v The Chief Executive Officer of the South African Social Security Agency & others (678/12) [2013] ZASCA 29 (27 March 2013)

**Coram:** Nugent, Ponnan, Theron, Petse JJA and Southwood AJA

#### F – Facts

The case revolves around a public tender issued by the South African Social Security Agency (SASSA) for the payment of social grants, which was awarded to Cash Paymaster Services (Pty) Ltd (CPS). AllPay Consolidated Investment Holdings (Pty) Ltd, along with several associated companies, lodged an application with the North Gauteng High Court, contesting the validity of the tender award to CPS ([1]–[2]).

Initially, the High Court declared the tender process illegal and invalid but did not set aside the award to CPS. AllPay appealed against this non-set-aside decision, while CPS cross-appealed the declaration of illegality ([2]). Throughout the hearings, it became clear that AllPay's allegations were largely based on irregularities rather than outright accusations of corruption, which were later clarified as not constituting part of their case ([5]). The situation was further complicated by AllPay's attempt to introduce new evidence post-hearing, which was refused by the court ([6]–[18]).

The procurement process emphasized electronic payment solutions and robust biometric verification to reduce fraud and theft in grant disbursement ([23]–[39]). The tender submissions underwent a thorough evaluation process with both AllPay and CPS assessed against technical and financial criteria. CPS's solution was deemed superior in providing comprehensive biometric verification ([44]–[46]).

AllPay raised several complaints regarding the procurement process, citing failures to adhere to procedural requirements, the composition of evaluation committees, and issues regarding the assessment of BEE partners ([49]–[65]). The court ultimately dismissed all of AllPay’s concerns and upheld the decision to award the contract to CPS based on the merit of its proposal ([96]–[106]).

#### I – Issues

The central legal issues raised were:

1. **Validity of the Tender Process:** Whether the irregularities identified by AllPay were sufficient to invalidate the tender award to CPS.
2. **Procedural Fairness:** Whether AllPay was treated fairly regarding the evaluation and awarding of the tender based on the allegations of irregularities in the process.
3. **Authority to Introduce New Evidence:** The appropriateness of allowing AllPay to introduce new evidence after the conclusion of arguments.
4. **Public Interest Considerations:** The potential impact on public welfare of invalidating the contract for social grant payments.

#### R – Rules

The applicable legal framework includes:

1. **Public Finance Management Act 1 of 1999** and **Constitution Section 217:** These statutes mandate that public procurement must be conducted in a fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective manner ([20]).
2. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA):** This enactment enforces standards for administrative action in public procurement ([20]).
3. **Case Law Precedents:** Cited decisions include *S v N*, affirming that new evidence might be admitted in exceptional circumstances ([13]), and *Millenium Waste Management* and *Moseme Road Construction*, discussing complications arising from setting aside administrative acts ([98]–[99]).

#### A – Application

The court analyzed AllPay's complaints concerning various elements of the procurement process. Key complaints included:

- **CPS's Submission Format:** AllPay argued that CPS did not provide separate bids for each province as required. The court found that CPS's submission complied with the RFP's guidelines and did not require multiple documents for a unified solution ([50]–[53]).
  
- **Bid Evaluation Committee Composition:** AllPay contended that the Bid Evaluation Committee did not follow procedural norms as per an internal circular. The court clarified that the BEC was an advisory body and not statutorily constituted, therefore its composition did not invalidate the contract award ([54]–[59]).

- **Assessment of BEE partners:** It was argued that SASSA failed to assess the promised partnership with BEE entities. The court noted that SASSA had discretion in its evaluation and could manage risks through contractual provisions without needing to validate partner capabilities in advance ([65]).

- **New Evidence Admission:** When AllPay attempted to introduce new evidence post-argument, the court held that finality in litigation is crucial and that the evidence presented did not affect the original case positively, thus denying the request ([6]–[18]).

In its evaluation of these issues, the court emphasized the necessity of maintaining public trust in procurement processes and the imperative that insignificant procedural flaws should not outweigh public interest, especially in contexts that directly affect millions reliant on social grants ([21]).

#### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed AllPay's appeal and upheld the cross-appeal by CPS. The court ruled that there were no fatal irregularities in the tender process and that CPS’s proposal was superior concerning the RFP requirements. The court stated that to invalidate the contract over inconsequential flaws would be detrimental to public interest and the welfare of grant recipients ([96]–[107]).

This judgment reinforces the principle that while public procurement processes must adhere strictly to legal and procedural norms, a balance must be struck with considerations of public service and welfare, particularly in matters affecting vulnerable members of society.
