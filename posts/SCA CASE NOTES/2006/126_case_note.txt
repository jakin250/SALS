Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2006

# FIRAC Case Note: Shaik & Others v Director for Public Prosecutions, KwaZulu Natal [2006] SCA 154 (RSA)

## F – Facts

The appeal concerns four appellants (Rashid Shaik, Renesh Singh, Gerald Govender, and Sumugan Muthusamy Govender) who were involved in a series of violent crimes, including kidnapping, murder, attempted murder, and robbery. The events occurred on 13 June 1998, following an altercation at a shopping center involving the second appellant and the deceased. After the altercation, a group led by the appellants, allegedly under the instructions of a gang leader named Nithia Chinnasamy, sought to retaliate against the deceased.

The group initially abducted a friend of the deceased, Gabriel David, in an effort to locate him. Once the deceased was found, he was confronted, and a violent assault ensued, resulting in the deceased being murdered and another victim, Deepchund, severely injured ([paras 1-8]). 

At the first instance, under Judge Magid, the first and second appellants received sentences of life imprisonment for murder and ten years for attempted murder, alongside lesser concurrent sentences for kidnapping. The third and fourth appellants received similar life sentences and lesser sentences for attempted murder ([paras 2-3]).

The court granted leave to appeal solely regarding their sentences. The court of first instance later found substantial and compelling circumstances to reduce the life sentences of the first and second appellants to 20 years, and reduced the third appellant's sentence to 12 years. The fourth appellant's sentence was not altered due to his lack of a similar appeal ([paras 3, 12]).

## I – Issues

The critical legal issues in this case focused on the appropriateness of the sentences imposed considering the gravity of the offences, the culpability of the appellants, and the comparative roles they played during the commission of the crimes. Specifically, the court assessed:

1. Whether the sentences of life imprisonment were justified given the appellants’ involvement and personal circumstances, particularly in light of the court's discretion under the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 ([paras 10-14]).
2. The question of uniformity in sentencing among co-accused given differing degrees of involvement in the commission of the same offences and whether this warranted differentiation in their respective sentences ([para 12]).

## R – Rules

The relevant legal authorities and statutes guiding the court’s decision include:

1. **Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997**, particularly section 51(1) and section 51(3)(a), which govern mandatory sentences for certain crimes unless substantial and compelling circumstances exist ([para 10]).
2. Precedents such as *S v Malgas* 2001 (1) SACR 469 (SCA) and *S v Goldman* 1990 (1) SACR 1 (A) establishing the legal framework for evaluating the appropriateness of sentencing and the need for uniformity among co-accused who have participated equally in the same crime ([para 12]).

## A – Application

In applying the law to the facts, the court recognized the brutal nature of the offences and the substantial bodily harm inflicted on the victims. However, it also noted significant differences in the degree of participation and moral culpability among the appellants. The first and second appellants were deemed to have instigated the attack and played primary roles, leading to their recommended sentences of 20 years. In contrast, while the third appellant partook in the assault, his involvement was deemed less severe compared to the first two and was accordingly sentenced to 12 years. The fourth appellant, who raised no appeal, had received life imprisonment, but the court found that his actions closely related to those of the first and second appellants, leading to a recommendation for similar sentencing ([paras 11-15]).

The court acknowledged that while uniformity in sentencing is desirable, it should not come at the cost of justice and equity. The respective roles and actions of each appellant during the attack were crucial to differentiating their sentences, especially where one had engaged in acts of particular brutality, as was evident in the case of the third appellant ([paras 9-15]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the corresponding findings from the court a quo regarding the first, second, and third appellants. The sentences of the first and second appellants were appropriately reduced to 20 years, while the third appellant's sentence was reduced to 12 years, highlighting that their comparative culpability warranted distinct sentencing structures. However, the court found merit in protesting the life sentence of the fourth appellant, ultimately substituting it with a 20-year term, initializing a more balanced approach to sentencing within the context of their involvement ([paras 16-17]).

In summary, this case reaffirms the principle that while the desire for consistent sentencing among co-accused is important, the courts must also account for the specifics of each individual's role and moral blameworthiness in the offence when determining appropriate sentences.
