Vodacom (Pty) Ltd v Makate and Another ([2024] ZASCA 14)

The Gauteng Division of the High Court initially remitted the compensation determination back to the CEO, providing specific directives.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 14)

Case Title: Vodacom (Pty) Ltd v Makate and Another ([2024] ZASCA 14)

Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

Coram: Mocumie JA, Schippers JA, Mothle JA, Nhlangulela AJA, Kathree-Setiloane AJA

F - Facts:

The extensive dispute between Vodacom (Pty) Ltd (the Appellant) and Kenneth Nkosana Makate (the First Respondent) centers on the "Please Call Me" (PCM) service conceptualized by Makate in 2000 while employed as a trainee accountant at Vodacom. After lengthy negotiations following a 2016 Constitutional Court order that required Vodacom to negotiate in good faith over Makate’s compensation for his idea, Vodacom’s CEO, Shameel Joosub, determined in January 2019 that Makate would receive R47 million as compensation, which Makate contested as inadequate ([paras 2-3]).

The Gauteng Division of the High Court initially remitted the compensation determination back to the CEO, providing specific directives. This faced opposition from Vodacom, leading to an appeal against that order ([paras 4-6]). Vodacom had contested the approach taken by the First Respondent and stated that the CEO exercised reasonable discretion in determining the compensation, as per the synergy established in the prior legal frameworks ([paras 7-11]).

I - Issues:

The appeal brings forth several legal questions:

  1. Whether the High Court correctly interpreted the mandate of the CEO under the Constitutional Court’s order to determine reasonable compensation.
  2. Whether the instructions provided to the CEO by the High Court were appropriate.
  3. Whether the factual findings made by the CEO should be viewed under the standards established in Bekker v RSA Factors, particularly on whether the determination was patently inequitable ([paras 12-12]).

R - Rules:

The standard of judicial review concerning the CEO’s determination is derived primarily from the principles set out in Bekker v RSA Factors (1983) which mandates that a third person tasked with valuation or pricing must exercise reasonable judgment. Furthermore, the reviewability is not merely confined to administrative action grounds but also hinges on the fairness and reasonableness of the resultant decision ([paras 13-14]).

The ruling reiterated principles from Dean v Prince, mentioning that while the expertise of the CEO determines the compensation, that decision retains susceptibility to challenge if there is evidence of mala fides or unreasonableness. This formed the backdrop against which the High Court assessed both procedural and substantive fairness ([paras 15-17]).

A - Application:

The SCA assessed whether the High Court had correctly applied the second leg of the Bekker test, particularly focusing on the nature of the determination made by the CEO. While it acknowledged that Vodacom argued for the wide discretion of the CEO based on practical and historical considerations of how Vodacom structures contracts for new services, it ultimately found that the CEO deviated from established guidelines by subjectively determining revenue figures and not adhering closely to established contractual frameworks or the parties’ agreed percentages ([paras 22-23]).

The findings of the High Court, which mandated a review of the CEO’s assessment, were grounded in significant pricing discrepancies between what Vodacom proposed in justifications against Makate’s claims. Furthermore, the ambiguity concerning the duration of the contract (whether five years or the suggested eighteen) critically jeopardized Makate’s earnings based on Vodacom’s projections for PCM ([paras 24-27]).

Through rigorous examination of both documents and oral submissions, the SCA determined that discrepancies in the figures used by the CEO were too significant and thus warranted intervention. The CEO’s reliance on hypotheses without adequate empirical backing culminated in a decision that lacked the requisite rationality and should be deemed manifestly unfair ([paras 28-30]).

C - Conclusion:

The SCA ultimately concurred with the High Court’s assessment that the CEO’s decision was inequitable, resulting in the remittance of the matter for a recalculation, directing him to adopt better foundations reflecting an eighteen-year contract based upon the actual ongoing use and successful revenue generation from PCM. This conclusion was fortified by an extensive examination of the initial agreement and how Vodacom had benefited commercially from that innovation over an extended period ([paras 35-37]).

Consequently, the appeal was partially successful. The SCA ordered that the determination of reasonable compensation due to Makate for the PCM idea as determined by the CEO be reviewed, replacing the five-year projection with an eighteen-year duration while applying the time value of money. The court also held Vodacom accountable for costs, highlighting the prolonged nature of the litigation and Vodacom’s non-disclosure issues related to revenue figures ([paras 38-39], [203-207]).

Overall Implications: This case reiterates the necessity for corporate entities to uphold fair negotiation standards and transparently communicate with inventors whose ideas contribute significantly to their product offerings. Moreover, it emphasizes legal principles governing contract interpretation, particularly in cases divided by longstanding disputes involving intellectual property and corporate remuneration.
