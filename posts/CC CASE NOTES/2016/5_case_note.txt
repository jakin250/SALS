Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# Case Note: Pitje v Shibambo and Others [2016] ZACC 5

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The case centers around Isaac Rasepitle Pitje (Applicant), who claims to have a legitimate interest in a property located at erf 4157, Block M, Mamelodi Township, Pretoria. The property originally belonged to his late father and was registered in the name of his brother in 1992. Due to financial difficulties, Pitje's brother defaulted on a bond held by Nedcor Bank, prompting Mr. Pitje to negotiate a purchase agreement with his brother in 2001. Under this agreement, Mr. Pitje assumed the bond payments, intending to acquire the property eventually for R63,000, conditional on securing financing.

In 2010, however, Mr. and Mrs. Josiah Oupa Shibambo (First and Second Respondents) purchased the property from Pitje's brother for R380,000. The Shibambos were unaware of the prior sale to Mr. Pitje, as evidenced by the brother's transfer of the property to them. Shortly after this transfer, the Shibambos sought eviction of Mr. Pitje from the residence, leading to High Court proceedings where an eviction order was granted against him, primarily due to Mr. Pitje's failure to file opposing papers despite initially indicating his intention to do so ([paras 1–9]).

### I – Issues
The case raises several legal questions, particularly concerning:
1. Whether the High Court appropriately applied the **Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act** (PIE), especially regarding section 4's procedural requirements.
2. Whether the court's decision in allowing the eviction was just and equitable, taking into consideration Mr. Pitje's age, health, and lack of alternative accommodation.
3. The legality of the Shibambos' standing as bona fide purchasers and the implications surrounding the double sale of the property and prior notice requirements ([paras 8–15]).

### R – Rules
The relevant legal framework includes:
- The **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa**, particularly sections relating to the right to property (section 25), and the right to access adequate housing (section 26).
- The **Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998** (PIE), which establishes protocols for eviction proceedings, including:
  - Requirement for notice to the unlawful occupier at least 14 days prior to proceedings (§4(2)).
  - Consideration of relevant circumstances to evaluate the justness of eviction orders (§4(7)).

The court further relied on **Bowring v Vrededorp Properties CC** [2007] ZASCA 80; 2007 (5) SA 391 (SCA), where it was held that a first purchaser could claim rights over a property even when it had been sold again, assuming the second purchaser had knowledge of the first sale ([paras 11–18]).

### A – Application
In its analysis, the Constitutional Court found that:
- The High Court's reliance on Bowring was misplaced, as the latter case primarily dealt with issues of ownership transfer rather than eviction. The procedural fairness mandated by PIE had not been fully adhered to by the High Court. It overlooked the necessity to consider Mr. Pitje's health and potential homelessness upon eviction, indicating a lack of a comprehensive inquiry into the "just and equitable" standard laid out in PIE. The Court emphasized that the possibility of suitable alternative accommodation must be determined within the context of the vulnerable status of Mr. Pitje ([paras 19–21]).

Additionally, the court noted that the High Court had failed to allow Mr. Pitje's application to file a rejoinder, denying him an opportunity to present evidence regarding his health and the risk of homelessness, which further underscores the need for a thorough examination when dealing with eviction cases ([paras 18–23]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately upheld Mr. Pitje's appeal, concluding that the application of PIE by the High Court had been inadequate. It highlighted the importance of considering all relevant circumstances, including Pitje's vulnerability due to age and health, before issuing an eviction order. The case was remitted to the High Court for reconsideration with directions to apply PIE correctly and take into account Mr. Pitje's submissions regarding his right to adequate housing. The appeal underscored the necessity for courts to ensure that evictions do not violate constitutional protections and highlighted the critical balance between property rights and the rights of vulnerable occupants ([paras 22–24]).

This ruling serves as a significant affirmation of the protective provisions of PIE, reiterating that evictions must be fair and equitable, particularly for those in precarious situations.
