Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2008

### Case Note: Kruger v The President of the Republic of South Africa

**Court Reference:** Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Case Number:** CCT 57/07  
**Judgment Reference:** [2008] ZACC 17  
**Heard On:** 19 February 2008  
**Decided On:** 2 October 2008

---

### F - Facts
In this case, the applicant, Marius Kruger, an attorney, sought confirmation from the Constitutional Court regarding the Pretoria High Court’s decision that declared a proclamation issued by the President of South Africa on 11 July 2006 as “null and void and of no force and effect” ([1]). The President's proclamation was intended to bring certain sections of the Road Accident Fund Amendment Act 19 of 2005 into effect.

Concurrently, the Road Accident Fund applied for direct access to clarify the status of a second proclamation issued by the President on 28 July 2006, which allegedly lawfully commenced the provisions of the Amendment Act on 31 July 2006 ([2]). The High Court's referral stemmed from concerns regarding the validity of these proclamations, particularly that the first proclaimed sections incorrectly identified provisions of the Amendment Act ([8]).

Both proclamations displayed incompetence regarding the public powers involved, raising issues of legal interpretation concerning the effect of amendments and the procedural clarity applied by the executive ([14], [55]).

### I - Issues
The central legal and constitutional questions arising in this case include:
1. Should the Constitutional Court confirm the High Court’s declaration of invalidity regarding the First Proclamation, which referred to incorrect provisions of the Amendment Act? ([16](a)).
2. What is the status of the Second Proclamation purportedly intended as a remedial correction, and did it lawfully bring the correct provisions of the Amendment Act into effect? ([16](b)).
3. What are the consequences if either of the proclamations are found invalid? ([16](c)).

### R - Rules
This case is framed by several pertinent legal principles and authorities:
1. **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa:** Section 172 provides that only the Constitutional Court has the authority to confirm orders of invalidity made by lower courts, effectively making such declarations void until confirmed ([1], [2]).
2. **Interpretation Act 33 of 1957:** Section 13 allows for varying effective dates for different provisions of a single Act, subject to a proclamation from the President ([9]).
3. **Public Administration Principles:** Judicial review applies to the exercise of public power, necessitating lawful and rational decision-making by the President ([11]).
4. **Severability Doctrine:** Common law allows for invalid parts of legislation to be severed from valid provisions to preserve the latter, provided the valid parts can stand independently and fulfill legislative intent ([94]).

### A - Application
The court scrutinized the issuance of the First Proclamation, acknowledging that the President made an error by referencing sections 4, 6, 10, 11, and 12 instead of the intended sections 1 to 5 of the Amendment Act ([50], [55]). This error led to claims that the First Proclamation was fundamentally irrational due to its arbitrary nature, thereby rendering it invalid ab initio ([52]).

In discussions surrounding the Second Proclamation, while it was argued that this Proclamation served as a corrective action, the court determined that the President lacked the authority to amend a proclamation that was already void from inception ([62]). The lack of clarity in the wording of the second Proclamation rendered it inconsistent with legal standards underpinning effective legislation ([66]).

The court ultimately viewed the need for legal certainty and the administration of justice as paramount. As the consequences of invalidation were severe, including operational chaos within the Fund, the court recognized exceptional circumstances requiring that, despite the invalidity of both proclamations, measures be adopted to ensure continuity for affected parties ([70], [74]).

### C - Conclusion
The Constitutional Court upheld the High Court's ruling, declaring both proclamations invalid. However, the court coupled this with practical remedies, ordering the President to issue a new proclamation bringing the correct provisions of the legislation into force retroactively from 31 July 2006, thereby mitigating adverse impacts from the invalid proclamations ([80]). The court emphasized the need for clear communication in executive actions, reflecting the fundamental constitutional principles of legality, certainty, and respect for the rule of law.

While there were divergent opinions within the Court regarding the extent of the validity of the Second Proclamation and the President's powers, the dominant legal rationale espoused by Skweyiya J sought to affirm the role of the Constitutional Court as custodian of the Constitution, reflecting an unwavering commitment to uphold constitutional democracy in South Africa ([81]).

---

This case provides a critical insight into the relationship between legislative processes, executive authority, and the judiciary, emphasizing constitutional adherence in all governmental undertakings.
