# Case Note: Larbi-Odam & Others v Member of the Executive Council for Education

**Citation:**  
*Larbi-Odam v Member of the Executive Council for Education (North-West Province)* 1997 (12) BCLR 1612 (CC).

---

### FIRAC Analysis

#### F – Facts

The case concerns an appeal against the decision of Waddington J in the Bophuthatswana Provincial Division of the Supreme Court, which dismissively ruled on the validity of regulation 2(2) of the "Regulations regarding the Terms and Conditions of Employment of Educators" (the regulations). This regulation stipulated that no individual could be appointed as a permanent educator unless they were a South African citizen ([1][2]). 

The appellants, a group of eight foreign educators, had been employed temporarily in the North-West Province. Their qualifications were notable, with many holding postgraduate degrees. Some appellants were permanent residents of South Africa, having lived in the country for over a decade. They were earlier restricted in their employment opportunities by the Bophuthatswana National Education Act, which also required citizenship for permanent positions ([4][5]).

Following the introduction of regulation 2(2), the appellants faced the prospect of termination of their employment solely based on their non-citizenship status. This was particularly pertinent as the first respondent's ministry was engaging in the rationalization of educational posts, converting temporary positions to permanent ones, effectively excluding the appellants from permanency, which they argued was a form of unfair discrimination without justification according to section 8(2) of the interim Constitution ([3][6]).

The Supreme Court was asked to declare the regulation unconstitutional and ultra vires the Educators’ Employment Act ([1][4]).

#### I – Issues

1. **Constitutionality of Regulation 2(2):** The primary legal question was whether regulation 2(2) constituted unfair discrimination against the appellants due to their non-citizen status, thus violating section 8(2) of the interim Constitution.
   
2. **Justification of Discrimination:** An ancillary issue arose regarding the respondents' justification for the regulation under section 33 of the interim Constitution. This provided that limitations on rights could be permissible if justifiable in an open and democratic society.

3. **Interpretation of Regulations:** A further issue emerged regarding the interplay between regulation 2(2) and regulation 5(1), which seemed to allow for the appointment of any educator to vacant posts irrespective of citizenship ([11][14]).

#### R – Rules

1. **Section 8 of the Interim Constitution:** This section provided for equality before the law, prohibiting unfair discrimination based on various grounds, including citizenship, and establishing a presumption of unfairness when discrimination was found to occur on specified grounds ([15][18]).

2. **Section 33 of the Interim Constitution:** This section allows for limitations on rights if they are justifiable in an open and democratic society, which demands a nuanced consideration of the limitation's purpose and its proportionality ([26][27]).

3. **Judicial Precedents:** The court referenced the principles laid out in prior cases such as *Hugo*, *Prinsloo*, and *Harksen*, which delineated a framework for analyzing allegations of discrimination and assessing fairness ([16][17]).

#### A – Application

**A. Discrimination Analysis:**

The Court determined that the discrimination was established as a consequence of the citizenship requirement in regulation 2(2), which adversely impacted the appellants' fundamental dignity ([19][20]). The predominance of non-citizens represented a vulnerable group, lacking political power or longevity in the employment context. This facilitated a finding that the appellants were discriminated against unfairly, undermining their dignity and autonomy. 

**B. Justification Analysis:**

The Court questioned the transparently legitimate aim claimed by the respondents: to prioritize employment for South African citizens amidst high unemployment in the teaching sector. The Court while acknowledging the legitimacy of the goal, pointed out that it should not come at the direct expense of permanent residents or skilled foreign educators, asserting that quality education should take precedence over indiscriminate job allocation ([31][32]).

**C. Regulatory Interpretation:**

On interpreting regulation 5(1), the Court recognized the complexities in the relationship between the regulations. Regulation 5(1) ostensibly allowed for certain exceptions but also inherently perpetuated discrimination, as it primarily aided those who already held positions while regulating non-citizens' access to permanent roles ([12][13]). 

Ultimately, the Court found that regulation 2(2) could not be justified under section 33, given that the adverse impacts on the appellants were severe, and the aim of protecting the employment of citizens should extend to permanent residents as well ([35][40]).

#### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court, concluding that regulation 2(2) violated the interim Constitution, deemed it invalid. The decision underscored the obligation of educational authorities to ensure that all qualified individuals, particularly those with strong ties to South Africa, including permanent residents, should not face discrimination in employment due to their citizenship status. Consequently, the ruling emphasized a need for sensitive balancing of policy objectives like employment promotion for citizens and providing equitable opportunities for non-citizens, especially given their valuable contributions to the educational landscape. 

The appeal succeeded, with costs granted to the appellants, and the previous ruling of Waddington J was set aside ([48][49]). The Court's judgment represents a significant affirmation of the principles of equality and non-discrimination, reinforcing the imperative for all educational policies to comply with constitutional mandates.
