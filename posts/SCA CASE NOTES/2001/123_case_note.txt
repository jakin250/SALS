# FIRAC Case Note: Van Hulsteyns Attorneys v Government of the Republic of South Africa and Another

## F – Facts

The case concerns a stolen cheque drawn on 11 October 1994 by the Department of Customs and Excise, payable to OTK (Kooperatief) Beperk. The crossed cheque was marked “not negotiable” and was lost en route to its intended recipient. Subsequently, the cheque ended up in the trust bank account of Van Hulsteyns Attorneys (the appellant) on 28 October 1994 after being fraudulently inserted into the banking system of First National Bank of Southern Africa Ltd (the second respondent) ([1][2]). 

On becoming aware of the fraud, the Government of the Republic of South Africa (the first respondent) instituted action for recovery of the loss in the Transvaal Provincial Division of the High Court. The first respondent primarily claimed against the appellant under section 81 of the Bills of Exchange Act 34 of 1964, which allows the true owner of a stolen cheque to recover from any person who possessed the cheque after the theft ([3]).

The High Court ruled in favor of the first respondent, affirming that Van Hulsteyns Attorneys possessed the stolen cheque as per the relevant statutory provisions. The main question posed on appeal was whether the appellant had indeed possessed the cheque as defined in section 81 ([3][4]).

## I – Issues

The central legal issue in this case was whether Van Hulsteyns Attorneys possessed the stolen cheque as intended by section 81 of the Bills of Exchange Act ([4]). This raised specific legal questions including:

1. What constitutes "possession" under section 81 of the Bills of Exchange Act?
2. Does the irregularity surrounding the cheque's deposit negate the presence of possession?
3. What is the extent of liability for loss under the Act concerning possession of the cheque? 

## R – Rules

The statutory framework is found in section 81 of the Bills of Exchange Act, which states:

- A true owner may recover from any person who possesses a stolen cheque either for consideration or as a donee ([1]).

Relevant precedents, such as *Barlow Motors Investments Ltd v Smart* 1993 (1) SA 347 (W), emphasize the importance of common law's acceptance of various forms of possession, particularly mediate possession. The courts have clarified that a collecting bank does not possess a cheque for purposes of liability under section 81 unless it is held for a specific client ([6]).

Further, it is well established that possession entails both physical control and the intention to hold as owner, which leads to the concept of mediate possession in banking ([6]). 

## A – Application

The court examined whether Van Hulsteyns Attorneys had possessed the cheque under the stipulations of the Act. The appellant's defense hinged on the extraordinarily irregular scenario in which the cheque was introduced into the banking system, contending that such an irregularity precluded any notion of legitimate possession ([7][8]).

However, the court found that the circumstances did not negate the intention behind the deposit. The officials at the second respondent (the bank) acted on the belief that they were processing a legitimate transaction, as they had seen the deposit slip upon which the credit to the applicant’s account was based. Therefore, the intention to hold the cheque on behalf of the appellant remained valid ([8]).

The appellant's claim of lack of possession based on procedural irregularities was dismissed. The court concluded that since the cheque had been credited to the appellant’s account and was later presented for payment, such actions indicated that the intent to hold the cheque for the appellant’s account was present ([8]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the decision of the High Court, affirming that Van Hulsteyns Attorneys possessed the stolen cheque within the meaning of section 81 of the Bills of Exchange Act. The court dismissed the appeal, emphasizing that the appellant's possession was valid despite the procedural irregularities that led to the cheque's introduction into the banking system. Consequently, liability for the loss was affirmed, aligning with statutory provisions allowing the true owner to recover their loss from any possessor after theft. 

The appeal was dismissed with costs, including the costs associated with the employment of two counsel due to the case's significance ([9][10]). Moreover, the first respondent was ordered to pay specific costs related to applications for condonation and the second respondent's costs of appeal, reflecting the complexities surrounding administrative and procedural issues in the course of the proceedings ([10]).

This ruling reinforces the importance of the principles of possession in commercial transactions, particularly in the context of banking and the handling of stolen property. It establishes precedent for future interpretations of similar statutory provisions under the Bills of Exchange Act.
