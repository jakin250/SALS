Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Chairperson of the National Council of Provinces v Malema (535/2015) [2016] ZASCA 69

### F - Facts
This case involves a constitutional clash between parliamentary privilege and freedom of speech. The first respondent, Mr. Julius Malema, President of the Economic Freedom Fighters (the EFF), made statements during a parliamentary debate that were characterized as “unparliamentary” by the appellant, the Chairperson of the National Council of Provinces (NCOP) ([paras 1-4]). Malema’s remarks were made in the context of former President Jacob Zuma’s State of the Nation Address on June 17, 2014, during which he accused the ANC government of massacre during the Marikana protests. Subsequent to his refusal to retract his statements, he was ordered to leave the parliamentary session by the Chairperson, who ruled that Malema's statements imputed improper motives and accused the ANC government of murder ([paras 5-6]).

Malema, alongside the EFF, challenged the Chairperson’s ruling in the Western Cape Division of the High Court, seeking the review and setting aside of the Chairperson’s decisions concerning the characterization of his speech and the subsequent order for his removal ([paras 7-10]). The High Court ruled in favor of Malema, leading to an appeal by the Chairperson of the NCOP to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 10]).

### I - Issues
The primary legal questions presented in this case include:

1. **Does parliamentary privilege encompass the right to make inflammatory statements within the context of legislative debate?** In particular, does the characterization of Malema's statements as "unparliamentary" infringe on the constitutional right to freedom of speech in Parliament as enshrined in Section 58 of the Constitution?

2. **Was the Chairperson's ruling rational and lawful within the context of the parliamentary standing orders that regulate conduct in parliamentary debates?** Specifically, did the Chairperson overstep her authority by conflating criticism of the government with criticism of individual members of Parliament?

### R - Rules
1. **Constitutional Provisions**: The primary legal authority relevant to this case includes Section 58 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, which ensures that members of the National Assembly (NA) enjoy freedom of speech in parliamentary proceedings, subject to the rules and orders emanating from the provisions of Section 57 ([para 14]).

2. **Parliamentary Privilege**: The principles regarding parliamentary privilege, particularly concerning freedom of speech, are articulated in past cases such as *Speaker of the National Assembly v De Lille* [1999] ZASCA 50 and *Democratic Alliance v Speaker of the National Assembly* [2016] ZACC 8, which assert that such privileges protect political speech within the legislative framework ([paras 11, 14]). 

3. **Standing Orders**: The relevant standing order stipulates that members must not impute improper motives to other members, or reflect personally on their integrity, thereby guiding the decorum expected within parliamentary debates ([para 16]).

### A - Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal conducted a detailed examination of the Chairperson's assertions and the overall context of Malema's speech. The court emphasized that while parliamentary speech is not unbounded, it must allow for robust and emotive dialogue on issues of immense public interest ([paras 17-21]). 

The court found that Malema’s statements were directed predominantly at the ruling party as a political entity rather than at individual members of Parliament. As such, the remarks did not contravene the standing order since they did not target Parliament members specifically but instead emphasized broader governmental accountability. Furthermore, the Chairperson's logic - which equated the ANC government with individual ANC parliamentarians - was deemed constitutionally flawed because it infringed upon the protected right to criticize government conduct ([para 21]).

The ruling also noted that even if Malema's criticisms could be interpreted as targeting members of the ANC who are also MPs, such remarks would still be constitutionally protected as legitimate political speech essential for democracy ([paras 22-24]).

### C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the Chairperson’s appeal. The ruling reinforced the necessary balance between maintaining parliamentary decorum and safeguarding freedom of speech, underscoring that any expressions of political criticism, particularly on issues of public concern, fall within the ambit of protected speech under Section 58 of the Constitution ([para 28-30]). The case has significant implications for the operation of parliamentary debates and the extent to which elected representatives can freely discuss and criticize government actions without fear of censure or punishment. Furthermore, it affirms the judiciary's role in scrutinizing parliamentary conduct to ensure adherence to constitutional principles, thereby supporting an open and democratic society in South Africa. 

This ruling not only sheds light on the enforcement of parliamentary rules but also emphasizes the importance of political discourse in a constitutional democracy ([para 29]).
