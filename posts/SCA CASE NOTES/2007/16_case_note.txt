# Case Note: Van der Berg v General Council of the Bar of South Africa [2007] SCA 16 (RSA)

## FIRAC

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1-10])

This matter concerns the appellant, Johan van der Berg, a senior advocate with over 30 years of experience at the Cape Bar, whose name was struck from the roll of advocates by the Cape High Court. The application was initiated by the General Council of the Bar of South Africa (GCB), citing serious misconduct. The conduct in question stemmed from Van der Berg's association with one Jürgen Harksen, who had solicited large sums from investors under fraudulent pretenses, leading to criminal prosecution against him.

Harksen claimed to possess a fortune held in a trust fund (referred to as SCAN 1000) that later turned out to be non-existent. When one of Harksen's creditors sought to sequestrate him in 1995, he produced affidavits from purported trustees, later found to be forged. Van der Berg, who was among Harksen's legal representatives, traveled to Switzerland to investigate the legitimacy of these trustees. Upon discovering deceit by the purported trustee Siegwart, the appellant drafted new affidavits that suggested a facade of corporate legitimacy.

Subsequent charges against Van der Berg by the GCB received further support from subsequent actions in which he allegedly misled the court on Harksen's behalf. The High Court ultimately decided to remove his name from the roll, prompting this appeal.

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 11-20])

The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether Van der Berg’s conduct warranted removal from the roll of advocates, particularly concerning the ethical obligations associated with advocacy. Specific inquiries included:

1. Did Van der Berg know or suspect the veracity of the evidence he settled on behalf of Harksen?
2. Did his actions breach the ethical standards expected from an advocate, especially the duty to the court?
3. To what extent did the absence of fraudulent intent mitigate the findings of impropriety?

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 21-28])

The judgment traversed various legal precedents and established standards for the conduct of advocates, including:

- The necessity for advocates to uphold the integrity of the legal profession as stipulated in the Uniform Rules of Professional Conduct ([para 21]).
- Previous cases illustrating the obligations to the court versus obligations to the client, particularly in Rondel v Worsley [1967] 1 QB 443 (CA), reaffirming that advocates must avoid knowingly submitting false evidence ([para 24]).
- Advocacy as a profession RM which does not merely relay a client’s viewpoint but actively contributes to the administration of justice ([para 14]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 29-45])

The SCA carefully dissected the appellant’s conduct against the legal standards that govern advocacy. The court held that merely adhering to client instructions does not absolve an advocate from the duty to ensure the court is not misled. Van der Berg's actions were deemed wholly inadequate for acknowledging the sensitive nature of the evidence presented, despite his alleged lack of fraudulent intention.

The court highlighted a critical distinction: while an advocate may allow a client’s case to be presented even amidst personal suspicions of its truthfulness, knowledge of false evidence changes the ethical obligations significantly. The judgment reasoned that once Van der Berg became aware of the fraudulent nature of the affidavits, his failure to disclose this to the court constituted a serious breach of his professional duties ([paras 45-47]).

The GCB’s complaint that Van der Berg charged fees improperly, without proper instruction from an attorney, was also upheld, reinforcing the necessity for advocates to abide by the established procedural rules ([paras 22-28]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 49-53])

In conclusion, the SCA found that the cumulative misconduct of Van der Berg demonstrated a fundamental disregard for the ethical obligations incumbent upon advocates. His actions in permitting misleading evidence to be submitted to the court, coupled with his failure to maintain proper financial protocols within advocacy, rendered him unfit to continue as an advocate.

The court ultimately dismissed the appeal, upholding the High Court’s order to strike his name from the roll, thereby emphasizing the necessity of upholding the integrity of the legal profession. This decision serves as a stern reminder to all practitioners in the legal field regarding their dual obligations to their clients and to the administration of justice. The court reiterated that the protection of the public and the sanctity of the judicial process take precedence over previous standing within the profession ([para 52]).

This case highlights the intricate balance that advocates must maintain in advocating for their clients while adhering to ethical standards and duties to the court. It reinforces that ethical breaches, regardless of intent, can result in significant professional consequences.
