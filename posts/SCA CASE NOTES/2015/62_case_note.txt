Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note for *Shakawa Hunting & Game Lodge (Pty) Ltd v Askari Adventures CC* ([2015] ZASCA 62)

## F – Facts

The case revolves around the interpretation of a contract between *Shakawa Hunting & Game Lodge (Pty) Ltd* (the appellant) and *Askari Adventures CC* (the respondent) initially established on 1 March 2004 by their respective representatives, Mr. Peter Wilkinson and Mr. Paul Ferreira ([1]). The contract stipulated that Askari would receive not less than 10% of the "value" of Shakawa Hunting and Game Lodge upon its sale or in the event of Wilkinson's passing away. 

In January 2011, Wilkinson sold the assets of Shakawa for R32,500,000. Subsequently, Askari claimed that it was entitled to R3,230,600 — which it calculated to be 10% of the gross value of the sale minus specific deductions for legal fees and severance payments ([2]). Shakawa admitted to the contract but disputed the amount claimed, asserting it was entitled to calculate Askari's share on a net amount after deducting various liabilities, primarily Wilkinson’s loan account of R28,270,000 ([3],[6]). In February 2012, Askari secured a court order for the retention of R3,230,600 pending the outcome of the claim, which was litigated in the North Gauteng High Court ([1],[2]).

The trial court ruled against Shakawa, asserting that Askari was entitled to the full claimed amount based on the interpretation of "costs" in the original agreement ([4]). Shakawa subsequently appealed the decision on multiple grounds, focusing on the misinterpretation of the agreement and the calculation of value ([5]).

## I – Issues

The key legal issues identified in this case revolve around:

1. The correct interpretation of the term “value” as it appears in the contract regarding the determination of the amount owed to Askari upon the sale of Shakawa ([1],[5]).
2. Whether the loan account of Wilkinson, alongside other deductions, should be applied when calculating Askari’s entitlement ([6],[12]).
3. The nature of the counterclaim filed by Shakawa, which sought damages arising from the devaluation of the Rand against the British Pound Sterling based on the same contractual interpretation mistakes ([19]).

## R – Rules

The court relied on several jurisprudential principles concerning contract interpretation, including:

1. The *ejusdem generis* rule — stating that when general terms are used after specific examples, the general terms shall only include items of the same kind as those specifically mentioned ([14]). 
2. The definitions chronicled in previous cases such as *Durbach v Fairway Hotel Ltd* (1949) and *Odgers’ Principles of Pleading and Practice in Civil Actions* emphasized the importance of clarity in pleadings to prevent misleading legal arguments ([8]).
3. The foundational interpretation principles articulated in *Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality* (2012), namely, that the context and the language used in the contract should determine its meaning ([11]).

These rules underscore the focus not merely on the parties' subjective intentions but on the objective meaning of the wording in the contract.

## A – Application

The court’s application of these principles revealed a structural misinterpretation by the lower court regarding the agreements’ stipulations. In reviewing the trial court's findings, the Supreme Court of Appeal emphasized the necessity to first establish what the “value” of Shakawa entails before deductions for costs could be applied to ascertain Askari’s entitlement ([15],[17]). 

The initial court's conclusion that costs were strictly defined without owing consideration to the “value” definition established in clause 1 was deemed erroneous. Furthermore, it recognized the need to account for liabilities like Wilkinson’s loan when calculating the precise net value, reinforcing that on proper interpretation, the loan should indeed be considered a liability and thereby deducted from the selling price ([16],[18]).

The formulations for Askari’s claim and corresponding judicial interpretation were assessed under the broader principles of contracting law, specifically highlighting the static separation between gross and net values in claims arising under similar commercial contexts. Thus, after applying these deductions, the correct amount to be paid to Askari was confirmed to be R403,600 ([2],[18]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the appeal succeeded, thereby modifying the lower court's order to reflect the proper amount of R403,600 owed to Askari, including interest at 15.5% per annum from the date of the sale ([1],[21]). The court affirmed the importance of rigorous contract interpretation, emphasizing that parties must adhere to the agreed terms, and any claim must be grounded in their precise contractual obligations without ambiguity.

The implications of this decision reaffirm the precedent regarding contractual clarity and the necessity of distinguishing between different forms of value — particularly when percentages are claimed from sales proceeds that encompass distinct financial dimensions. Both parties were ordered to bear their respective costs of the appeal, balancing the interests of justice with the substantive outcome that arose from the original parties' contractual negotiations ([20],[21]). 

This ruling serves as a critical reference for future contractual disputes in South Africa, reiterating that courts will prioritize explicit, rigorous interpretations of commercial agreements to preserve the integrity of contractual relationships and the equitable treatment of all parties involved.
