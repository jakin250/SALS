Supreme Court of Appeal

King Sabata Dalindyebo Municipality v Landmark Mthatha (Pty) Ltd & Another (136/11) [2013] ZASCA 91

In 1999, the Municipality acquired property known as "The Remainder of Erf 912 Umtata." This property was donated by the National Government to the Provincial Government of the Eastern Cape, which in turn donated it to the Municipality.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 91

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The underlying dispute in this case arose from a contractual relationship between the King Sabata Dalindyebo Municipality (hereafter "the Municipality") and Landmark Mthatha (Pty) Ltd (hereafter "Landmark"). The litigation was initiated after Bulk Earthworks (Pty) Ltd, contracted by Landmark to undertake bulk earthworks for the Mthatha Retail Development, alleged that Landmark breached its payment obligations due to difficulties in obtaining financing related to the property subject to a land claim ([1]).

In 1999, the Municipality acquired property known as "The Remainder of Erf 912 Umtata." This property was donated by the National Government to the Provincial Government of the Eastern Cape, which in turn donated it to the Municipality. A 30-year lease agreement was signed on October 12, 2006, granting Landmark rights to a portion of the land ([3]). However, in 2007, it became apparent that the subject land was encumbered due to land claims lodged well prior to the lease agreement. The KwaLindile and AbaThembu communities lodged claims for restitution of land rights in 1998, with formal notice made public on May 25, 2007 ([7], [8]).

Landmark was unable to fulfill its financial obligations under the contract with Bulk Earthworks largely due to the impending land claims and the threat of interruption to development. The construction project was interdictively halted on October 2, 2007, pending negotiations, which eventually broke down. Subsequently, Bulk Earthworks brought claims against Landmark for unpaid amounts ([6], [12]).

The Municipality's defense asserted supervening impossibility of performance due to these external circumstances, claiming that it was incapable of granting vacant possession as stipulated in the lease agreement ([12], [20]).

I – Issues

A pivotal legal question emerged regarding whether the Municipality's plea of supervening impossibility of performance should have led to the dismissal of Landmark's claims. Additionally, questions relating to the calculation of interest on damages and the fundamentals of reasonable interest rates attached to loans were pivotal in this assessment ([2], [20], [30]).

Key issues included:

  1. Did the circumstances surrounding the land claims constitute supervening impossibility that would absolve the Municipality from performance of the contract?
  2. What were the implications for the assessment of damages and interest, particularly concerning the bridging finance obtained by Landmark at a reportedly high-interest rate?

R – Rules

The court primarily relied on principles from contract law concerning impossibility of performance; the key rule being that impossibility arising from vis major may excuse performance, provided the impossibility is not self-created ([24]). Relevant legislative frameworks included the Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994 ("the Act"), which governs land claims in South Africa and requires notification to the Land Claims Commissioner if land is developed. Specific section 11(7) of the Act prohibits development on land that is subject to a claim ([7], [8]).

Further, the court analyzed the requirements under section 2A of the Prescribed Rate of Interest Act 55 of 1975 concerning the implications of interest on debts arising from breaches of contract ([2], [37]).

A – Application

In adjudicating the case, the court found that the defense of supervening impossibility was unconvincing. Importantly, the court noted that while certain conditions surrounding the land claims delayed Landmark’s performance, the responsibility to inform and advise about the implications of these conditions lay with the Municipality, who was aware of the claims but failed to disclose them to Landmark ([14], [20]). The court reaffirmed that whether the Municipality was aware of the claims was irrelevant to the breach of contract, as fault does not preclude an action for breach ([19]).

The court also scrutinized the interest implications related to the bridging finance which Landmark secured. It opined that the burden of proving unreasonableness in the interest rate claimed lay with the Municipality, which failed to demonstrate that alternatives existed at lower rates ([35]). The court accepted the high-interest claims as part of the damages awarded to Landmark.

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal brought by the Municipality and allowed Landmark’s cross-appeal regarding the interest on the damages. The court reinforced the position that supervening impossibility could not be claimed where the inability to perform was a result of the contractor’s own actions or inactions. Consequently, the court ordered the Municipality to pay Landmark R141,781,201.85 in damages, which included calculated interest from the specified date ([1], [40]).

This case underlines the duties of transparency and notification in contractual obligations, especially within the context of governmental entities dealing with land that has potential claims, and establishes important precedents regarding contract performance amid unforeseen legal limitations grounded in public law. The implications for local authorities dealing with land developments and their responsibilities are substantial, particularly in matters involving community land claims.
