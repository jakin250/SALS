Supreme Court of Appeal

The Special Investigating Unit v Phomella Property Investments (Pty) Ltd and Another (Case no 1329/2021) [2023] ZASCA 45

This matter originates from the lease of the SALU building in Pretoria, meant for the Department of Justice and Correctional Services (DOJ).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 45

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

This matter originates from the lease of the SALU building in Pretoria, meant for the Department of Justice and Correctional Services (DOJ). The lease was executed between the Department of Public Works (DPW) and Phomella Property Investments (Pty) Ltd, the first respondent ([1]). Subsequently, the lease was transferred to Rebosis Property Fund Limited, the second respondent, and both companies were part of the same corporate group, under the leadership of one Mr. Ngebulana ([1]). The lease commenced on September 22, 2009, covering a term of 9 years and 11 months, and was established without an open bidding process but rather through a negotiated sale ([1]).

In February 2017, the Special Investigating Unit (SIU), acting as the appellant, filed an application with the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria ([2]). The initial goal was to have the lease declared void ab initio, but the duration of the lease had elapsed by that time; thus, the SIU shifted its focus to seeking a declaration that the lease was unlawful and to recover R103,880,357.65 as wasteful expenditure for purported excess leased space ([2]).

The SIU's claim hinged on two primary allegations: first, that the DPW did not employ an open bidding process; second, that even if a negotiated lease was permissive, a prior assessment of space needs by the DOJ was mandatory and unmet ([3]). The high court upheld the declaration of unlawfulness but dismissed the financial claim under section 172(1)(b) of the South African Constitution ([4]). Leave to appeal this latter decision was granted by the high court.

I – Issues

The core issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) is whether the high court’s decision not to issue an order under section 172(1)(b) of the Constitution is subject to judicial interference by this Court. The questions were whether (1) the high court exercised its discretion properly in refusing the order for payment and (2) any errors in interpretation or principles affected the judgment ([4]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal provisions include section 172(1) of the Constitution, which delineates a court's authority in constitutional matters to declare laws or conduct inconsistent with the Constitution as invalid ([5]). Specifically, subsection (a) obliges courts to make declaratory judgments where inconsistency is found, while subsection (b) permits courts to make orders that are "just and equitable," including those limiting the retroactive effects of any such declaration ([8]).

The SCA also referenced precedents concerning the nature of judicial discretion — particularly distinguishing between "true discretion," where no manifest errors must appear for appellate courts to interfere, and "loose discretion," where the appellate court can substitute its view based on the facts ([11]–[12]). Examples from you might include State Information Technology Agency SOC Ltd v Gijima Holdings (Pty) Ltd and Bengwenyama Minerals (Pty) Ltd v Genorah Resources (Pty) Ltd are used to illustrate this distinction in judicial review ([9]–[10]).

A – Application

In assessing the high court's decision, the SCA first affirmed that the lease was indeed unlawful, citing the insufficiency of the evidence to show a negotiated process was properly approved. This was based on the failure to conduct a required needs assessment prior to lease execution ([5]–[6]). However, the critical analysis stemmed from the high court’s invocation of section 172(1)(b) and whether the exercise of discretion was adversely influenced by incorrect legal principles or factual misinterpretations.

The SIU charged the high court with misapplying legal standards stemming from prior case law, particularly maintaining that the rationale from Mott MacDonald (regarding returns of payments under invalid contracts) was not duly applied ([14]). The SCA systematically dismantled the SIU's assertions, explaining that just because an unlawful contract exists, it does not inherently grant a right to a repayment or enforcement of collateral consequences—nor does the SIU's interpretation align with precedents established in the Allpay cases ([16]–[19]).

Additionally, the court examined whether there had been errors on material facts as claimed by the SIU; it found that the high court’s findings on the necessity of a needs assessment did not involve factual failings ([21]–[23]). The SIU's assertion about Mr. Ngebulana's alleged complicity in wrongful conduct was also dismissed as incoherent due to a lack of evidence that he was aware of any need for the open bidding process or irregularities ([24]–[26]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA concluded that the high court’s refusal to grant equitable relief under section 172(1)(b) did not warrant interference as there was no demonstration that the high court's discretion was exercised unjustly or incorrectly ([28]). The decision was fortified by the high court having correctly characterized the contextual application of discretion, ensuring that the outcome adhered to principles of justice and fairness without misapplying relevant precedents.

As a result, the appeal was dismissed with costs, affirming the high court’s original ruling while emphasizing the critical balance between legal formality and equitable resolution in administrative law cases ([4]).

This case note presents a succinct yet thorough analysis suitable for legal scholars, practitioners, and students focusing on administrative law within the context of South African jurisprudence. The issues of procurement irregularities and the concomitant balance between legality and justice remain salient points of examination in future cases of similar nature.
