South African Human Rights Commission on behalf of South African Jewish Board of Deputies v Masuku and Another [2022] ZACC 5

After the South African Jewish Board of Deputies (SAJBD) lodged complaints against Masuku's statements, the South African Human Rights Commission (HRC) perceived these remarks as hate speech and instituted proceedings in the Equality Court.

Constitutional Court 2022 [2022] ZACC 5

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–12])

At the center of this case lie issues of fundamental rights concerning equality, human dignity, and freedom of expression within the context of hate speech and its regulation under the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000 (Equality Act). The dispute arose from statements made by Bongani Masuku, a representative of the Congress of South African Trade Unions (COSATU), during a series of public gatherings in 2009, where he insinuated that support for Zionism was akin to a historical antagonism associated with Nazi Germany.

After the South African Jewish Board of Deputies (SAJBD) lodged complaints against Masuku's statements, the South African Human Rights Commission (HRC) perceived these remarks as hate speech and instituted proceedings in the Equality Court. The HRC argued that Masuku’s utterances explicitly incited hatred against the Jewish community, thereby contravening section 10(1) of the Equality Act.

The Equality Court ruled that the statements did indeed amount to hate speech, propagating hatred and inciting harm, which led to a directive for an unconditional apology from Masuku to the Jewish community. However, the Supreme Court of Appeal rejected this conclusion, contending that the statements did not constitute hate speech under section 16(2) of the Constitution, prompting the HRC to appeal to the Constitutional Court.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 96–97])

The major legal issues raised before the Constitutional Court revolved around the following:

  1. Whether the Supreme Court of Appeal erred by relying solely on section 16(2) of the Constitution and neglecting the provisions of section 10(1) of the Equality Act, in light of the principle of subsidiarity.
  2. What the correct interpretation of section 10(1) of the Equality Act is concerning the definition of hate speech.
  3. Whether the impugned statements by Masuku constituted hate speech as defined under section 10(1).
  4. The implications of the Supreme Court of Appeal’s cost order in the context of the HRC's claims.

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 18–28], [96–120])

In addressing the above issues, the Court primarily referenced the following legal frameworks and precedents:

  • Section 10 of the Equality Act articulates a prohibition against hate speech defined as words that could reasonably be understood to demonstrate an intention to be harmful, propagate hatred, or incite harm.
  • Section 16 of the Constitution, which enshrines the right to freedom of expression, while excluding forms of expression that represent propaganda for war, incitement of imminent violence, or advocacy of hatred based on specified grounds.
  • The concept of subsidiarity, which mandates that when legislation exists to give life to a constitutional right, courts should primarily interpret and adjudicate disputes within the confines of such legislation rather than directly appealing to the Constitution.
  • Precedent from the Constitutional Court in cases such as Qwelane v South African Human Rights Council and Pillay v MEC for Education, underscoring the importance of respecting enacted legislation that regulates constitutional rights.

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 121–171])

The Constitutional Court performed a rigorous analysis to ascertain the implications of the statements made by Masuku within the frameworks established by the Equality Act and the Constitution.

First, the Court held that the Supreme Court of Appeal had erred in bypassing the Equality Act and conducting its assessment based solely on the Constitution's provision, violating the principle of subsidiarity. The Court emphasized that the Equality Act was designed to provide a comprehensive statutory framework for assessing hate speech, and thus the context of the law must be maintained to ensure comity between the judicial and legislative branches.

Next, the Court confirmed that the test for hate speech requires an objective assessment of the meaning of the statements, the context in which they were made, and potential harm to the targets identified. The hearing referenced expert testimony while emphasizing that it should be used contextually to support understanding rather than defining the adjudicative outcome. The Court also highlighted the historical and societal context regarding offensive speech directed at Jewish people, asserting that words carry specific meanings shaped by past injustices.

Determining the first statement’s implications, the Court affirmed that it constituted hate speech as defined in section 10(1) of the Equality Act. The subsequent statements, made at a rally, though inflammatory, were deemed to have a political context targeting proponents of the Israeli state rather than the Jewish community broadly, leading to the conclusion that these latter statements did not meet the legal threshold for hate speech.

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 172–172])

In conclusion, the Constitutional Court dismissed the application for recusal against Chief Justice Mogoeng and granted leave to appeal. It upheld certain findings of the Equality Court while overturning aspects related to the latter statements which did not constitute hate speech. The Court reinstated the Equality Court's initial ruling concerning the first statement and mandated Masuku to issue an unconditional apology to the Jewish community.

This ruling not only clarifies the boundaries and operational frameworks of hate speech laws in South Africa but also underscores the necessity of thoughtful consideration of societal context when adjudicating speech, thereby contributing significantly to the evolving discourse on balancing the rights to free expression and protection from hate speech. The decision sets a precedent emphasizing the interrelatedness of rights safeguarded under the South African Constitution, ensuring nuanced protection for marginalized communities against hate speech.

Overall, this case has profound implications for the regulation of speech in a post-apartheid society, where the balance between freedom of expression and the need to protect against hate speech remains an ongoing challenge in the fabric of democratic governance.
