# FIRAC Case Note: Chief Avhatendi Ratshibvumo Rambuda and Others v Tshibvumo Royal Family and Others

**Court:** Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Case Number:** CCT 255/22  
**Neutral Citation:** Chief Avhatendi Ratshibvumo Rambuda and Others v Tshibvumo Royal Family and Others [2024] ZACC 15  
**Date Heard:** 30 November 2023  
**Date Decided:** 17 July 2024  
**Judges:** Maya DCJ, Chaskalson AJ, Dodson AJ, Kollapen J, Mathopo J, Mhlantla J, Rogers J, Schippers AJ, Tshiqi J  
**Judgment Delivered by:** Mathopo J (unanimous)

## F – Facts

The dispute arises from a succession contention concerning the headmanship of Tshibvumo Village in Limpopo, traditionally governed by the Rambuda Royal Family ([1]–[5]). Following the death of the previous headwoman, Ms. Nthambeleni Tshibvumo Singo, both the Rambuda Royal Family and the Tshibvumo Royal Family put forth candidates for the headmanship: Mr. Ndwambi Donald Rambuda (supported by the Rambuda family) and Mr. Mbulaheni Lucas Mavhungu (supported by the Tshibvumo family) ([6]–[8]).

The contest was initiated by the Tshibvumo Royal Family via an urgent application seeking recognition of their candidate. This culminated in a settlement requiring the Premier of Limpopo to review the respective claims, which subsequently led the Premier to recognize Mr. Rambuda as the headman ([17]–[20]). The Tshibvumo Royal Family, dissatisfied with this decision, pursued a review application in the High Court, which found that the Premier's decision failed to comply with statutory obligations concerning the identification of successors ([26]).

The High Court determined that the relevant statutory provisions required internal dispute resolution, as per section 12(2) of the Limpopo Traditional Leadership and Institutions Act 6 of 2005 (“Limpopo Traditional Leadership Act”), leading to the appeal presented in this case ([26], [62]).

## I – Issues

The following key legal issues were identified:

1. **Jurisdiction**: Does the Constitutional Court have jurisdiction to hear the appeal, and is it in the interests of justice to grant leave for appeal? ([36])
2. **Royal Family Authority**: Which royal family holds the authority to identify a suitable successor for the position of headman or headwoman? ([36])
3. **Existence of Royal Families**: Was the High Court correct in affirming the existence of two rival royal families in the context of Tshibvumo Village? ([36])
4. **Review of the Premier's Decision**: Was the High Court justified in reviewing and setting aside the Premier's decision recognizing Mr. Rambuda as headman? ([36])
5. **Remittal to Premier**: Should the High Court have remitted the matter back to the Premier for reconsideration? ([36])

## R – Rules

The relevant legal framework includes:

1. **Limpopo Traditional Leadership and Institutions Act 6 of 2005**:
   - Section 12(1) outlines the process for identifying a successor for traditional leadership, requiring the royal family to identify a candidate in accordance with customary law.
   - Section 12(2) stipulates that if there are allegations or evidence challenging this identification, the Premier may either refer the matter for recommendations or refuse recognition while returning the matter for reconsideration ([9]–[10]).

2. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**:
   - Section 6 provides grounds for reviewing administrative action, particularly focusing on whether the decision maker followed proper procedures and considered relevant information ([26]).

3. **Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act**:
   - Provides procedural guidelines for resolving disputes in traditional leadership contexts, emphasizing the importance of adhering to customary law ([36]).

## A – Application

The Court analyzed the acting authority under the Limpopo Traditional Leadership Act, affirming that the Rambuda Royal Family is the traditional ruling entity within the community's jurisdiction. Given this recognition, only they can rightfully nominate a new headman, as established by statutory definitions of a royal family ([44]–[50]).

The Court scrutinized the opposition's claim regarding dual royal family status and concluded that only one royal family exists within the framework of traditional authority in Tshibvumo Village, which is the Rambuda Royal Family. This interpretation maintains legal coherence with the provision that traditionally, the royal family oversees headmanship matters ([48]).

Critically, the Court concluded that the Premier's failure to adhere to the dispute resolution processes outlined in section 12(2) constituted a legal misstep. The Premier not only recognized Mr. Rambuda based on flawed premises but also neglected the required investigatory steps that the circumstances merited ([55]–[58]).

The decision to remit the matter back to the Premier with directives for further consultations underscored the necessity for adherence to the statutory frameworks governing traditional leadership, aiming conservatively to re-institute respect for customary practices ([62]).

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court found in favor of the applicants on the critical points while acknowledging certain flawed reasoning contained in the High Court's judgment. Specifically, the Court ordered the following:

1. **Condonation of Delayed Filing**: The Court found justifiable reasons for the late filing of the appeal.
2. **Leave to Appeal**: It was determined that it was in the interests of justice and awarded leave to appeal ([64]–[65]).
3. **Remittal Order**: The matter was remitted to the Premier with explicit directions to adhere to procedural and statutory obligations, particularly to refer to the provincial house of traditional leaders for proper evaluation and guidance, thereby rectifying the previous oversight of due process in leadership recognition ([65]).

In summary, the ruling serves to reaffirm customary law principles, reestablish the integrity of traditional leadership structures, and ensure future compliance with existing legal frameworks, particularly in succession matters pertinent to customary governance.
