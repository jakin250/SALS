Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: Van Wyk v The MEC: Department of Local Government and Housing of the Gauteng Provincial Government [2019] ZASCA 149

### F - Facts
The case centers around an appeal from a decision by the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, concerning a dispute over the transfer of two properties, Erf 302 and Erf 537 in Moreletapark, to appellant Mark Van Wyk. On June 15, 2000, Van Wyk purchased Erf 302 at a public auction for R570,000. He signed a "Conditions of Sale" document, mandating the State Attorney to facilitate the property registration under his name ([paras 1–2]).

Despite paying the required deposit and providing a bank guarantee for the remaining amount, Van Wyk faced difficulties in securing the transfer of the property. Over a period of five years, the department indicated that transfer was impeded due to the property being notarially tied to Erf 537. Subsequently, a second agreement was reached for Erf 537, but the department later claimed that neither property was vested in the Gauteng Provincial Government ([paras 3–4]).

In 2008, the Department of Land Affairs issued a certificate confirming the vesting of these properties in the Provincial Government, but by 2010, the department retracted its agreement, asserting that the sale was invalid, having lacked legal authority to transfer ownership. Van Wyk filed a summons against the department in November 2011 to compel the transfer of the properties, which was dismissed by the High Court, leading to this appeal ([paras 6–8]).

### I - Issues
The main legal questions in this case involve:
1. Whether the respondent (the Gauteng Provincial Government) had the authority to enter into the sale agreement if the properties were not vested in it at the time of the sale.
2. The validity of the Item 28(1) certificate issued by the Department of Land Affairs and its implications for the transfer of property ([paras 11–12]).

### R - Rules
Key legal principles established include:
1. **Contract Law**: It is not required that a seller must own the property for a sale agreement to be valid. A seller must only ensure that the buyer is placed in possession and warrants against eviction ([para 9]).
2. **Authority of Sale**: The authority to dispose of state land comes from legislation, specifically the State Land Disposal Act, and the Constitution, which outlines the framework for property vesting between national and provincial governments ([paras 10–11]).
3. **Item 28(1) of Schedule 6**: Provides that a registrar must endorse property transfers according to a certificate from a competent authority confirming vesting in a specific government ([para 15]).

### A - Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal addressed several points in its analysis:
1. The High Court mistakenly believed that the validity of the sale depended on the seller's ownership of the property, contravening established contract law. Therefore, Van Wyk’s rights under the sale were valid despite the seller’s lack of ownership ([para 9]).
2. The Item 28(1) certificate was deemed valid, as the Department of Land Affairs issued it after proper consideration and endorsement from the Gauteng Provincial State Land Disposal Committee. The court highlighted that the certificate could not be disregarded until it was judicially set aside ([paras 16–17]).
3. The court noted that the respondent's plea did not effectively challenge the basis of Van Wyk’s claims, as it primarily consisted of blanket denials without addressing specific legal points. This failure improperly shifted the burden of proof onto the appellant when it should have remained with the respondent to establish its defenses ([paras 11–12]).

### C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the High Court erred in its dismissal of Van Wyk’s claim by focusing on issues not properly raised in pleading. The court held that the appellant was entitled to have the sale agreement enforced and mandated the respondent to facilitate the transfer of the properties. The appeal was upheld with costs, and the matter was remitted with clear directives for the effective transfer ([paras 20]). This ruling reinforces the principle that procedural adherence and substantive rights must coalesce in property disposals, particularly within government constraints.
