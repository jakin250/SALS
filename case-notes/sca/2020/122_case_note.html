Supreme Court of Appeal

Altech Radio Holdings (Pty) Limited and Others v City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality (1104/2019) [2020] ZASCA 122

The third appellant, ABSA Bank Limited, along with the Development Bank of Southern Africa, financed the project, which aimed to construct a 1500 km fibre-optic network over 18 years.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 122

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–44])

The case revolves around a legality review regarding a municipal broadband network project initiated by the City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality ("the City"). In 2013, the City recognized that its existing 500 km communications network infrastructure was insufficient for developing a "smart city" aimed at improving service delivery and bridging the digital divide ([para 3]). The project was led by Mr. Jason Ngobeni and Mr. Dumisani Otumile, culminating in the issuance of a public tender on 27 May 2013, which was aborted. A subsequent tender process began in August 2014, leading to the award of the tender to Altech Radio Holdings (Pty) Ltd (“Altech”) on 9 June 2015. This included the establishment of a special purpose vehicle, Thobela Telecoms (RF) (Pty) Limited (“Thobela”), to execute the project.

The third appellant, ABSA Bank Limited, along with the Development Bank of Southern Africa, financed the project, which aimed to construct a 1500 km fibre-optic network over 18 years. By 2016, when the City transitioned to a new political leadership after elections, the outgoing African National Congress (ANC) administration was succeeded by the Democratic Alliance (DA). Subsequent to this transition, the new administration indicated intentions to review and potentially cancel the project ([paras 11–12]).

Approximately a year after taking office, on 22 August 2017, the City applied to the Gauteng Division of the High Court for an urgent interdict coupled with a review application regarding decisions made from September 2014 to April 2016, involving the tender and subsequent agreements ([para 12]). The application was struck off by Brenner AJ in September 2017 due to lack of urgency, and the City later pursued a special motion under Part B which was granted by Baqwa J on 16 July 2019, declaring the agreements invalid ([paras 14–15]). The appellants, Altech, Thobela, and ABSA, subsequently appealed.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 15–16])

The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal centered on the delay in the City’s review application and whether that delay was unreasonable enough to warrant its dismissal. The appellants contended that the City’s delay—over two years since the tender award—was egregiously unreasonable and unacceptable in a constitutional context, particularly given a municipality's duty to act without undue delay in such reviews. The secondary issue revolved around the principles and implications of legality review vis-a-vis the established norms under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 2000, and whether any found irregularities warranted the contracts' invalidation despite such delay ([paras 15–19]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 18–21])

The court highlighted the principles undergirding both legality reviews and the familiar standards by which undue delays are assessed. The test for assessing unreasonable delay consists of two parts: first, determining whether the delay was indeed unreasonable based on the matter's facts, and second, if found so, deciding whether the court's discretion ought to be exercised to overlook that delay ([para 19]).

Key cases underlined in this judgment include Valor IT v Premier, North West Province [2020] ZASCA 62 and Khumalo and Another v Member of Executive Council for Education: KwaZulu-Natal [2013] ZACC 49, among others. The court also noted that while conducting a legality review, the principles encapsulated under PAJA do not necessarily apply, thus giving greater discretion to review proceedings ([para 18]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 22–70])

The court's analysis began with the acknowledgment of the delay and the City's failure to provide a satisfactory explanation for it. The court scrutinized the timeline of events, identifying that significant knowledge of potential irregularities existed within the City long before the review application was made. The DA's leadership had insights into the procurement processes that justified urgent action but failed to act promptly ([paras 22–26]).

The court asserted that political change in the City’s governance did not excuse the delay. Key reports and findings regarding the project were already available in the public domain and under council debate, undermining the City’s rationale for the delay. Documents, such as a 288-page report that outlined issues with the project, were overlooked by the City when assessing its obligations ([paras 30–31]).

Upon assessing the merits of the appeal, the court examined the substance of the alleged contractual irregularities. It concluded that the flaws presented did not constitute material irregularities that would justify the contracts' invalidation. The provisions of the MFMA, specifically section 33—which governs the necessary processes for contracts extending beyond budgetary implications—were analyzed extensively, with the court finding compliance on part of the City. Earlier approvals from relevant bodies were duly obtained, and ambiguities alleged by the City concerning responsibilities of Altech and Thobela were unfounded ([paras 54–66]).

The court held that despite minor compliance issues, the projects had materialized and were already in action, which benefits the citizens they were designed to serve. To set aside the agreements would cause exorbitant financial distress and disrupt ongoing projects, aligning with previous judgments emphasizing the weight of operational impacts provided by legal participants ([paras 67–70]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 71–79])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal, emphasizing the unreasonableness of the City's delay coupled with its insufficient justification for initiating review proceedings so late. The court underscored the importance of accountability from public entities, stating that their failure to act decisively could not be overlooked.

Consequently, the court set aside the lower court's decision, thereby reinstating the validity of the BOT and Tripartite agreements, a move noted for its broader implications in maintaining the integrity of public procurement processes. The judgment also serves to clarify the stringent expectations regarding timely action by governmental bodies within judicial review contexts.

This case reiterates the necessity for public administrations to act expeditiously to uphold the rule of law, reinforcing existing jurisprudential standards while delineating the balance between public interest and contract enforcement ([paras 72–79]).
