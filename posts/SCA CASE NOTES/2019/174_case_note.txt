Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: Kgopana v Matlala (1081/2018) [2019] ZASCA 174 

## F – Facts

The case revolves around an appeal by Mr. Ntsieni Morris Kgopana (the appellant), following a ruling by the Limpopo Division of the High Court, which found in favor of Ms. Mohlaki Rosina Matlala (the respondent), regarding a WhatsApp message allegedly constituting an offer to enter into a contract ([1]–[2]). 

In July 2015, Kgopana won a significant sum of R20,814,582.20 in the National Lottery, which was deposited into his bank account on July 29 ([1]). Following some six months later, Kgopana sent a WhatsApp message to Matlala, the mother of one of his seven children, in which he alluded to his potential financial capacity to distribute R1 million to each of his children if he were to receive R20 million, implying he could retain R13 million ([1]). 

The relationship between Kgopana and Matlala had deteriorated since March or April 2003, although they had a child together born in August 2003 ([3]). Kgopana had adhered to a maintenance order to pay R1,000 monthly for their child but later expressed that he could no longer maintain that obligation due to ill health and a consequent reduction in earnings from his job at the South African Revenue Service ([4]). 

After Kgopana briefly acknowledged making an initial maintenance payment of R100,000 to Matlala in January 2016, further communications led Matlala to believe she was entitled to more, referencing the WhatsApp message sent in January 2016. In September 2016, Matlala issued a summons claiming R900,000 based on the implication of the WhatsApp message, after which the trial court ruled in her favor ([7]).

Kgopana disputed the claim, emphasizing a lack of intention to contract (animus contrahendi), asserting the message simply addressed hypothetical future distributions of wealth, and that Matlala had misinterpreted his intentions ([8]–[12]). The trial court concluded that Matlala reasonably regarded the message as an offer ([9]), contrasting Kgopana’s position that he intended merely to disengage from further direct obligations ([8]).

## I – Issues

The principal legal issue at stake is whether the WhatsApp message sent by Kgopana constituted a binding offer animo contrahendi, sufficiently clear and definite to allow for acceptance and the formation of a valid contract ([2]):

1. Did the WhatsApp message reflect an intention to create a legal obligation, thus making it an offer subject to acceptance?
2. Was there a manifestation of consensus ad idem between the parties regarding the contractual terms?

Additionally, this case raises questions regarding the application of legal principles related to contract formation in informal communication settings like messaging applications.

## R – Rules

The court relied upon foundational principles of contract law, especially concerning the offer and acceptance framework and the doctrine of quasi-mutual assent:

1. **Consensus ad Idem**: According to the principles articulated in *Christie’s Law of Contract in South Africa*, contractual liability hinges upon the parties reaching a true agreement, discernible through external manifestations of intention ([10]).
   
2. **Quasi-Mutual Assent**: This doctrine allows for contractual obligations to arise even in the absence of mutual agreement, contingent on reasonable reliance by one party on the representations of another ([10]).

3. **Offer and Intention**: The inquiry into whether a statement constitutes an offer requires examining the express terms and surrounding circumstances to establish whether the offeror intended to create a binding commitment ([11]).

4. Previous case law, such as *Spes Bona Bank Ltd v Portals Water Treatment South Africa (Pty) Ltd* and *Steyn v LSA SA Motors Ltd*, was noted. These cases highlighted the legal expectations of clarity in the expression of an offer and the acceptability of the offer under reasonable circumstances ([9]).

## A – Application

Within the judgment, the court critically analyzed the content and context of the WhatsApp message. The key argument against the existence of a contractual offer hinged on the lack of *animus contrahendi* from Kgopana. The court highlighted that:

1. Kgopana consistently denied winning the lottery, which undermined any interpretation that he intended to offer a sum from it ([12]).
   
2. The terms outlined in the message appeared hypothetical and speculative, rather than presenting any firm offer requiring acceptance. The language used suggested “what he might do” rather than an actionable commitment ([12]).

3. The absence of immediate response from Matlala after receiving the message implied an understanding that it was indeed not an offer to contract, thus failing to constitute true agreement ([12]).

The trial court’s findings, which inferred a clear contractual offer from the communication, did not align with the necessity for clear intent as required under South African contract law. The indication that Matlala's interpretation of the message as an offer was reasonable appeared to overshadow the essential subjective intent of Kgopana at the point of communication ([9]–[14]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Kgopana's appeal, concluding that the WhatsApp message did not constitute a binding offer capable of creating an enforceable contract. The court found that there was no true agreement resulting from the communication due to Kgopana's lack of intention to contract and the speculative nature of the message ([14]).

The ruling emphasizes the importance of clarity in proposals and the defining expectations of contractual obligations, especially in informal formats such as electronic messages. The court also decided on the costs, noting the morally complex nature of the situation concerning the welfare of the minor child, deeming it fair to make no costs order in both the lower court and on appeal ([14]).

This judgment contributes to contract law by reiterating the essential elements of offer and intention, particularly in informal settings. It serves as a significant precedent for future cases involving contract formation through electronic communications.
