Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# Case Note: Levenstein v The State (890/12) [2013] ZASCA 147

## F - Facts

The appellant, Jeffery Israel Levenstein, was tried in the South Gauteng High Court on multiple charges: six counts of fraud and two counts under the Companies Act 61 of 1973. Levenstein denied guilt but was ultimately convicted on all counts following a protracted trial that lasted several years ([paras 1–2]).

The charges stemmed from events surrounding the collapse of Regal Treasury Private Bank Limited, of which Levenstein was the CEO. After a tumultuous history marked by mismanagement and an eventual liquidity crisis in 2001, Regal was placed into final liquidation in 2004 ([paras 11–13]). The fraud allegations mainly revolved around misleading financial disclosures and misrepresentations made to auditors and investors regarding the bank's financial status, particularly relating to the valuation of branding income and the handling of share transactions in a financially precarious environment ([paras 15–31], [62–64], [68–85]).

Levenstein was sentenced to eight years’ imprisonment for the six counts of fraud and additional time for contraventions of the Companies Act. However, an appeal was lodged against both the convictions and the sentences imposed, resulting in a partial success on appeal ([paras 1–4], [16, 120]).

## I - Issues

The case presented several legal issues, including:

1. **Fraud Convictions**: Whether the trial court legally sustained the convictions for fraud based on the alleged misrepresentations in the financial statements, including the materially inflated earnings per share (EPS) and unapproved branding income valuation ([paras 32–36]).
  
2. **Companies Act Violations**: Whether the appellant was guilty of contraventions under the Companies Act, including improper financial assistance for share purchases, and whether he acted recklessly in managing Regal's affairs ([paras 97–105]).

3. **Sentencing Appropriateness**: The appropriateness of the sentences imposed in respect of each count, particularly given the flaws pointed out regarding the trial court's approach to the severity of the sentences based on the nature of fraud committed ([paras 120–129]).

## R - Rules

The legal framework supporting the prosecution's case drew primarily from the following:

1. **Criminal Law Principles**: Specifically, the definition of fraud as involving misrepresentation with intent to deceive and induce actions causing actual or potential prejudice to others. The prosecution relied on established case law emphasizing the necessity of intent and the actual effect of misleading statements ([paras 31, 35]).

2. **Companies Act 61 of 1973**: Sections pertaining to false financial reporting and prohibitions against financial assistance for share acquisitions (s 38), explicitly designed to protect creditors and maintain corporate integrity within public companies ([paras 97–105]).

3. **Sentencing Guidelines**: Guidance found in previous judgments which stress the importance of proportionality in sentencing alongside the unique circumstances of the offences and the offender. Courts are required to consider individual circumstances to determine the appropriate punishment ([paras 120–128]).

## A - Application

The court conducted a thorough analysis of the facts against the elements of fraud, ultimately ruling that while some misrepresentations did qualify as actions constituting fraud, others did not. For example, it was determined that although Levenstein knew the financial statements had not been fully audited, the claim regarding the EPS required careful consideration due to the lack of clearly established misrepresentation on that point ([paras 34–39]).

The court also highlighted erroneous procedural aspects surrounding the Companies Act violations. It noted that while Levenstein's actions had indeed been reckless and had violated statutory duties, the particulars of the charge failed to meet legal requirements of specificity, particularly concerning alleged financial assistance in share purchases that were ambiguously presented in the indictment ([paras 97–105]).

In terms of sentencing, the court identified significant discrepancies in how the trial court imposed uniform sentences across different counts without recognizing disparities in severity. The court found that a flat eight-year sentence for all counts was an error. The determination was made to consolidate sentences for similar fraud-related counts, yielding an overall reduced sentence of eight years to reflect concurrent serving ([paras 120–135]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately affirmed some of the convictions while overturning others. It upheld Levenstein’s convictions on counts 1, 3, 4, 5, and 8, but dismissed convictions for counts 2, 6, and 7. The court modified the sentences imposed, arriving at an effective term of eight years’ imprisonment based on the reinstated counts of fraud and Companies Act violations ([paras 1–5], [137]).

The case underscores the critical intersection of corporate governance and criminal liability, emphasizing stringent compliance requirements for directors in public companies. Furthermore, it reinforces the necessity for careful legal drafting in indictments and the expectation for transparency in corporate financial reporting, particularly in high-stakes financial environments such as banking.
