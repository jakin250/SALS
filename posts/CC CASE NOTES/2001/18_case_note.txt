Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2001

# FIRAC Case Note: Khalfan Khamis Mohamed v President of the Republic of South Africa & Ors

## F – Facts

In this case, the first applicant, **Khalfan Khamis Mohamed**, is a Tanzanian national who had been arrested in Cape Town on **5 October 1999** under allegations connected to terrorist activities related to the bombings of the U.S. embassies in Nairobi and Dar es Salaam on **7 August 1998** ([1]–[9]). Following his arrest, he was detained and interrogated by South African immigration officers and subsequently handed over to agents of the **Federal Bureau of Investigation (FBI)** for removal to the United States, where he faced federal charges that included murder and conspiracy, carrying the potential penalty of death ([2]–[4], [27]).

The applicants sought declaratory and mandatory relief against the South African government, arguing that Mohamed's arrest and extradition to the U.S. constituted an unlawful transfer, violating his constitutional rights to life, dignity, and protection against cruel and inhumane punishment as delineated in the **Constitution of South Africa** ([3]–[6]). They contended that such actions amounted to a disguised extradition in breach of the **Aliens Control Act** ([4], [6]).

The facts reveal that evidence was collected through a sequence of events that included cooperation between South African immigration and law enforcement with the FBI. Mohamed, having entered South Africa on a false passport under an assumed identity, had obtained temporary asylum status, and was later subject to a series of legal proceedings in South Africa aiming to evaluate the legality surrounding his arrest and removal to the U.S. ([3]–[11]).

## I – Issues

The critical legal issues before the Constitutional Court were:

1. **Was the removal of Khalfan Khamis Mohamed to the United States constituted illegal deportation or a disguised extradition?**
2. **Did the South African authorities breach Mohamed’s constitutional rights by failing to prevent the imposition of capital punishment?**
3. **Was Mohamed’s alleged consent to his removal legally valid, and did it waive his rights under the Constitution?**
4. **What remedies were appropriate given the findings of the Court?** ([28]).

## R – Rules

The Court's analysis involved several key legal rules and principles, including:

1. **Separation of Powers**: The principle that the judiciary should not interfere in executive functions, yet must ensure compliance with constitutional mandates ([71]).
  
2. **Extradition vs. Deportation**: A distinction was made between these two legal actions, citing **Shearer’s Extradition in International Law** and **South African case law**, which articulated strict requirements for extradition that were not applicable to deportation, unless specific conditions related to the death penalty were involved ([29]–[34]).
  
3. **Constitutional Rights**: The rights enshrined in sections 10 (dignity), 11 (life), and 12(1) (freedom from cruel, inhuman, or degrading treatment) of the South African Constitution ([10], [38]).

4. **Aliens Control Act**: Sections of this Act provided regulations governing the detention and deportation of foreign nationals in South Africa ([34]–[36]).

5. **International Norms and Treaties**: Concerns regarding the imposition of the death penalty were framed within the context of South Africa's commitments under international treaties such as the **European Convention on Human Rights** and the **Convention Against Torture** ([48]–[60]).

## A – Application

The Court reasoned that Mohamed's removal from South Africa constituted a disguised extradition rather than a lawful deportation, primarily for the following reasons:

1. **Legal Authority**: The Court determined that the South African authorities lacked the legal standing to deport Mohamed to the U.S. under the Act since the U.S. was not a permissible destination for deportation as outlined in **Regulation 23** of the Aliens Control Regulations ([36]).

2. **Capital Punishment Concerns**: It was found that the South African government had an obligation to protect Mohamed’s constitutional rights, which included securing an assurance from the U.S. that he would not face the death penalty if convicted ([38]–[49]). The Court underscored that this protection is an essential part of the Constitution's guarantees of the right to life and dignity.

3. **Validity of Consent**: The Court critiqued the nature of the consent allegedly given by Mohamed. They found that there were serious doubts as to whether he could appropriately consent to removal under circumstances where he was deprived of legal advice. The Court noted that his consent was questionable given the lack of information about his rights and the significance of the death sentence ([62]–[68]).

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ruled that the handover of Khalfan Khamis Mohamed from South Africa to the FBI was unlawful for multiple reasons: it infringed his constitutional rights to life and dignity due to the failure of South African authorities to secure an assurance against the imposition of the death penalty, and it also conflicted with the procedural requirements outlined in the Aliens Control Act ([74]). The Court concluded that the transfer constituted an unlawful act by the government of South Africa, and issued a declaratory order to this effect. 

The ruling emphasized the necessity for the state to uphold constitutional principles, particularly when dealing with the rights of individuals facing severe penalties in other jurisdictions. The implications of the ruling reinforced the commitment of South Africa to respect human rights, multi-national legal standards, and the rule of law ([69]–[74]). The Court determined appropriate orders to convey its findings to relevant judicial authorities in the U.S. and recognized the complexities involving executive action in matters of international criminal law ([74]).

---

This case not only underlines the significance of close examination of procedural legality in matters of extradition and deportation but also affirms the necessity of safeguarding fundamental human rights against foreign actions. The South African Constitutional framework serves as a robust protector of individual rights, demanding that the state remains vigilant and responsive to such standards even in cooperation with international law enforcement bodies.
