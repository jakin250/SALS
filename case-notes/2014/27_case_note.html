Nxumalo v President of the Republic of South Africa and Others [2014] ZACC 27

Although the Commission concluded its investigation and submitted its report to the President on January 21, 2010, it was established that the Commission itself was dissolved on January 31, 2010 ([5]–[6]).

Constitutional Court 2014 [2014] ZACC 27

F – Facts

The case revolves around the application by Mpinsane Eric Nxumalo, seeking the restoration of the kingship of the amaShangana traditional community and his recognition as its king. The Commission on Traditional Leadership Disputes and Claims (the Commission), established under the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003 (the Framework Act), conducted an investigation into Nxumalo's claim ([1]–[2]). Following public hearings in which testimonies were given regarding the historical kingship, the Commission concluded that the kingship had effectively been destroyed between 1894 and 1897, prior to the statutory cut-off date of September 1, 1927, and thus dismissed Nxumalo’s claim for lack of good grounds ([4]–[6]).

Although the Commission concluded its investigation and submitted its report to the President on January 21, 2010, it was established that the Commission itself was dissolved on January 31, 2010 ([5]–[6]). The President publicly endorsed the Commission’s report in July 2010 without recognizing Nxumalo's claim, which led him to seek a review of both the President's decision and the Commission's dismissal of his claim in the North Gauteng High Court ([8]–[9]). The application was denied, prompting Nxumalo to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal, which dismissed his appeal due to lack of reasonable prospects ([9]). Nxumalo subsequently sought leave to appeal in the Constitutional Court ([10]).

I – Issues

The central legal issues in this case include:

  1. Whether the Constitutional Court should follow its precedent in Sigcau v President of the Republic of South Africa and Others, which addressed the applicability of the Framework Act versus the later Amendment Act.
  2. The propriety of the President's decision to act without authority under the correct statutory framework, and whether both the President's decision and the Commission’s dismissal of Nxumalo’s claim were properly reviewed by the High Court ([11]–[13]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal authority stems from the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act and the Amendment Act, which established the Commission’s modus operandi regarding traditional leadership disputes. According to Section 25 of the Framework Act, the Commission had the authority to investigate disputes pertaining to traditional leadership claims, focused on customary law. The President was obliged to implement the Commission’s findings rather than acting independently, as affirmed by the Constitutional Court precedent in Sigcau that established bounds on the exercise of statutory powers ([2], [12]).

The principles from Minister of Education v Harris were also pertinent, clarifying that exercising power outside the conferred statutory provisions renders such action unlawful.

A – Application

The Constitutional Court underscored that the President erred by using the Amendment Act, asserting that the Commission’s jurisdiction had terminated at the date the report was produced. In Sigcau, the Court established that when an authority exercises a power under an inapplicable act, this action is null and void ([14], [15]). The Court emphasized that the President misinterpreted his authority under the new regime and incorrectly asserted a role in deciding on Nxumalo’s claim, which was to be determined by the Commission.

The Court found that the Commission had correctly determined the historical context and legal grounding of the amaShangana kingship, which played a crucial role in their dismissal of Nxumalo's claim. The High Court's deference to the Commission's findings was justified, as the Commission possessed specialized knowledge regarding traditional governance ([21]–[23]).

In distinguishing the present case from Sigcau, the Court concluded that the essence of the authority derived from the Framework Act was misapplied by the President, resulting in an unlawful decision not to recognize Nxumalo's kingship claims ([14], [16]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately upheld the appeal concerning the President's decision, rendering it void as he lacked the authority under the relevant Framework Act. The decision regarding the Commission’s dismissal of Nxumalo’s claim was not overturned, affirming the precedent that the assessment of such claims lies within the specialist jurisdiction of the Commission. The Court emphasized that while Nxumalo's appeal succeeded to the extent that the President’s decision was annulled, it did not result in substantial relief for Nxumalo since the foundational ruling of the Commission remained intact ([24]–[26]). The Court concluded that no order as to costs was merited, reflecting the complexities and the limited degree of success achieved by Nxumalo’s application ([26]).
