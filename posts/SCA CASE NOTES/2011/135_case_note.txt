Source Folder: 2011

# Case Note: Roux v Health Professions Council of South Africa (786/2010) [2011] ZASCA 135

## F – Facts
The case involves Lynette Mary Roux, a clinical psychologist who provided forensic work and a report to the Family Advocate concerning custody issues related to a minor child, Byron, whose parents included Oliver Michael Powell and Linda Petzer ([1], [2]). After treating Byron, allegations arose from Powell stating that Roux acted unprofessionally by maintaining dual roles and misdiagnosing him ([4]). Powell formalized his complaints to the Health Professions Council of South Africa (HPCSA), which led to an inquiry originally limited to the charge concerning “multiple relationships.” However, as pressure mounted, additional charges, including one for misdiagnosis, were added without proper authorization ([5], [7], [9]).

There were delays in the inquiry, prompting Roux to file an application in the North Gauteng High Court to declare the inquiry unlawful and seek various forms of relief ([8], [10]). The High Court judge ruled against Roux, arguing that procedural protocols were followed and that the pro forma complainant had acted within his authority ([12]). Roux appealed the decision, narrowing the dispute to whether the pro forma complainant could introduce unapproved charges against her ([13]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal question was whether the pro forma complainant possessed the authority to prefer additional charges against a health practitioner that had not been authorized by the committee of preliminary inquiry ([13], [14]). Specifically, the court considered the implications of the principle of legality in conjunction with the statutory provisions governing the HPCSA, particularly section 41 of the Health Professions Act 56 of 1974 ([14], [32]).

## R – Rules
The controlling statutes include the Health Professions Act 56 of 1974 and its associated regulations, particularly:

1. **Section 3(n)** - empowering the HPCSA to investigate complaints and ensure appropriate disciplinary action ([3]).
2. **Section 41** - granting the professional board authority to institute inquiries into charges of unprofessional conduct ([15]).
3. **Regulation 3(1)(b)** - outlining procedures for the registrar when receiving complaints, including the requirement to notify the accused ([18]).
4. **Regulation 3(4)** - specifying that if a preliminary inquiry committee decides that an inquiry should occur, the registrar must arrange it ([18]).

The court also referenced the **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)** relating to the definition of administrative action and the principle of legality, which asserts that all actions taken by public bodies must have a lawful basis ([29], [31]).

## A – Application
The court analyzed the statutory framework, noting that the committee's role is to vet complaints and establish grounds for formal charges. It held that allowing the pro forma complainant to introduce new charges without committee sanction undermined the statutory process ([26], [27]). 

The judgment articulated that the inquiry's legitimacy hinges on adherence to the procedural rights of the accused, which include being apprised of specific charges. Any broadened scope of inquiry by the pro forma complainant was deemed illegitimate, thus violating the principle of legality ([32]). Furthermore, the court dismissed the respondents' argument that the case was not ripe for review, asserting that a challenge to statutory compliance does not depend on the completion of the inquiry process ([33]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Roux's appeal, concluding that the pro forma complainant exceeded his authority by including the misdiagnosis charge without approval. The judgment underscored the importance of statutory compliance in disciplinary processes, noting that the HPCSA must act within the limits of its legal powers ([35]). 

The court ordered that:
1. The charge concerning misdiagnosis be set aside.
2. The HPCSA must conduct an inquiry solely on the charge of multiple relationships, ensuring expedited proceedings within two months.
3. Costs were awarded in favor of the appellant ([35]).

This case illustrates the critical nature of adhering to statutory mandates in regulatory processes, emphasizing that the principle of legality is foundational in safeguarding the interests of both practitioners and the public. It also reflects ongoing issues in the timeliness and efficacy of disciplinary inquiries within professional regulatory frameworks, highlighting systemic concerns that may impact public confidence in health professions oversight.
