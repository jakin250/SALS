Supreme Court of Appeal

Women in Capital Growth (Pty) Ltd and Another v Scott and Others (1193/2019) [2020] ZASCA 95

In this case, the appellants, Women in Capital Growth (Pty) Ltd and Akhona Trade & Investment (Pty) Ltd, were shareholders of the third respondent, African Legend Investment (Pty) Ltd (ALI).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 95

FIRAC Method

F – Facts ([paras 1–9])

In this case, the appellants, Women in Capital Growth (Pty) Ltd and Akhona Trade & Investment (Pty) Ltd, were shareholders of the third respondent, African Legend Investment (Pty) Ltd (ALI). On February 2019, they provided irrevocable undertakings to the first and second respondents, Mpho Innocent Scott and Abdoolrawoof Ahmed, who were directors of ALI. These undertakings mandated the appellants to vote their shares in favor of critical resolutions at any forthcoming shareholders' meeting ([para 1]).

The relevant undertakings involved a special resolution related to a transaction entailing the sale of shares in ALI to Glencore South Africa Oil Investments (Pty) Ltd (Glencore SA), as well as the removal of Mr. Mashudu Ramano from the board of directors ([para 2]). Concern arose among Scott and Ahmed about Mr. Ramano’s actions that could undermine their board control. In response, they sought assurances from the appellants to honor their undertakings, but correspondence revealed that the appellants did not consider these undertakings valid ([paras 3–4]).

Despite this contention, a meeting was convened for April 4, 2019, where the special resolution was to be proposed. The appellants were unwilling to vote in support of the removal of Mr. Ramano, leading Scott and Ahmed to file an urgent application for declaratory relief ([paras 5–6]). The Gauteng Division of the High Court ruled in favor of Scott and Ahmed, declaring the appellants bound by their undertakings and requiring them to vote according to the agreements made. The court dismissed the appellants' counter-application challenging the validity of their undertakings ([paras 6–8], [para 9]).

I – Issues ([paras 10–20])

The primary issues in this case revolved around whether the appellants' irrevocable undertakings were valid and enforceable. In particular, the court needed to determine:

  1. Whether the irrevocable undertakings contravened sections 58(8)(c) and 71(2)(b) of the Companies Act, which pertain to proxy voting and the rights of directors to be heard before removal ([paras 7–8]).
  2. Whether the appeal was moot due to the passage of time and the expiration of the undertakings before the decision could be resolved ([paras 16–20]).

An ancillary issue arose concerning whether the court should adjudicate on the validity of the undertakings given that the resolution outcomes had already been achieved, thus questioning if the decision would have any practical effect going forward ([paras 15–20]).

R – Rules ([paras 15–27])

The rules applicable to this case were grounded in the South African Companies Act, specifically:

  • Section 58(8)(c): Prohibits irrevocable proxy appointments when shareholders are invited to appoint persons named by the company as proxies.
  • Section 71(2)(b): Mandates that a director must be afforded an opportunity to be heard before a vote on their removal is taken.

The court also referenced established case law, such as Amoils v Fuel Transport (Pty) Ltd and Others and Desai and Others v Greyridge Investments (Pty) Ltd, to clarify the acceptability of agreements among shareholders regarding voting practices at general meetings ([para 8]).

Moreover, the Supreme Court of Appeal relied on principles from Independent Electoral Commission v Langeberg Municipality and JT Publishing (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Safety and Security concerning the management of moot cases, highlighting that judicial resources should not be expended on matters without practical effect ([paras 15, 20]).

A – Application ([paras 13–22])

The Supreme Court examined the mootness of the appeal and concluded that the key issues had become irrelevant due to the successful passing of the resolutions sought by the respondents. The sale of shares to Glencore and the removal of Mr. Ramano from his director position occurred independently of the appellants' accepted obligations ([paras 10, 17–20]).

While the first appellant argued that the undertakings' validity remained a live issue due to ongoing litigation regarding the share register rectification, the court found this line of reasoning speculative. It emphasized that if the rectification were achieved, the undertakings would have already expired, thereby rendering the need for reviewing their validity moot ([paras 14, 18, 19]).

The second appellant noted that evaluating the correctness of the high court's interpretation of the Companies Act provisions was in the interest of justice, yet the court found no compelling reason to engage substantively, as the issuesn were not pertinent to current or future litigation at that time ([paras 21–22]).

C – Conclusion ([paras 28–29])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, ruling that it had no practical effect due to the passage of time and the complete execution of the contested resolutions. The court also dismissed the considerations of exceptional costs as grounds for engagement, noting that such costs are inherent to commercially and legally significant litigation ([paras 26–27]). The outcome emphasizes the principle that judicial resources must focus on live disputes to ensure efficiency within the legal system ([para 15]).

The ratio decidendi underscores the importance of ensuring that appeals must serve a tangible purpose within the legal landscape, affirming that parties who choose to appeal when issues have been resolved bear the associated costs. Thus, responsibility for the legal fees incurred rests with the appellants, who were ordered to pay costs jointly and severally for pursuing a moot appeal ([paras 29]).
