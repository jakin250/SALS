Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# Case Note: N[…] E[…] NO v Estate Late B[…] C[…] B[…] [2014] ZASCA 27

## F – Facts
This case arose from the interpretation of two interrelated wills belonging to J[…] A[…] J[…] B[…] (the great-grandfather) and J[…] H[…] B[…] (the great-great-grandmother) of a minor child, referred to as J[…]. The legal dispute centered on whether J[…] could inherit from these wills following the death of her father, J[…] B[…], who predeceased the deceased, B[…] C[…] B[…], by several years.

The relevant factual background indicates that the deceased, who was the fiduciary of fideicommissa established under the wills of his father and mother, faced questions about the heirship of his granddaughter, J[…], due to the predeceased status of her father ([paras 1–3]). The appellant, N[…] E[…], as the mother and guardian of J[…], contested a ruling made by the North Gauteng High Court that stated J[…] could not inherit under the terms of the established fideicommissa because the inheritance occurred via a dead parent ([para 2]).

The executor of the estate, J[…] A[…] B[…] (the son of the deceased), initiated an ex parte application to declare the fideicommissa terminated upon the death of J[…] B[…]. Following procedural developments, including a counter-application by the appellant, the high court ultimately ruled that J[…] was not entitled to inherit ([paras 4–6, 15]).

## I – Issues
The primary issue in this case was whether a grandchild can inherit through a deceased parent under the specific terms of a fideicommissum. More specifically, the court needed to determine if J[…] could inherit her father's portion of the estate despite his predeceased status. The legal provisions surrounding the interpretation of wills and fideicommissa, particularly in light of the provisions of the Immovable Property (Removal or Modification of Restrictions) Act 94 of 1965, also came into focus ([paras 16–17]).

Another critical issue addressed was the implications of the wills’ wording regarding survivorship requirements and the absence of any provisions excluding grandchildren from inheritance in the case of a predeceased parent ([paras 19–20]).

## R – Rules
The court relied on several legal principles concerning inheritance and fideicommissa:

1. **Fideicommissum** - Defined as a testamentary disposition that requires a fiduciary to pass property to a designated fideicommissary, typically subject to certain conditions ([para 8]).
  
2. **Representation and Per Stirpes Inheritance** - The legal presumption in South African succession law is that descendants inherit per stirpes (by representation) unless a clear intention to the contrary exists within the will ([paras 21–23]). 

3. **Survivorship Requirement** - According to established jurisprudence, a condition of survivorship is generally implied in fideicommissa where the fiduciary is a living person. However, this implication does not universally apply in cases of fideicommissa multiplex ([paras 13, 19]).

4. **Intent of the Testator** - The court emphasized the principle that there is a strong presumption against the intention to disinherit descendants ([para 24]).

## A – Application
In addressing the case, the court thoroughly examined the terms of the wills in question, focusing particularly on the absence of any explicit clause barring grandchildren from inheritance due to their parent's previous death. The court interpreted the language of the wills, noting that both the first testator’s and second testatrix’s declarations did not differentiate among the grandchildren, thus indicating an intent to allow all grandchildren, including those of predeceased parents, to inherit ([paras 5–6, 25]).

The court also criticized the high court’s reliance on precedents that suggested an absolute condition of survivorship, noting that such interpretations must be consistent with the testator's expressed intent and the nature of a fideicommissum multiplex ([paras 19–20]). The implication from the relevant authority ([Corbett et al, The Law of Succession in South Africa] <sup>[1]</sup>) underlined the notion that the death of a sibling prior to the vesting of an inheritance does not negate the rights of other descendants.

The statutory framework—specifically the Immovable Property (Removal or Modification of Restrictions) Act—was reviewed, affirming that the testators intended to establish generational transfer of the property without limitation as to the lifespan of any singular fiduciary, thus allowing J[…] to inherit as the first fideicommissary ([paras 16–17]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal issued a ruling that upheld the appeal of the appellant, reversing the decision of the high court. The court concluded that J[…] had the right to inherit her father's portion of the estate per stirpes, reinforcing the principle that a grandchild is not disqualified from inheritance due to the predeceased status of a parent. The judgment specifies entitlements and makes it clear that the fideicommissa established in the relevant wills remains valid, allowing for J[…] to inherit unrestricted by future generational claims ([paras 25–26]).

By clarifying the interpretation of the wills and reaffirming the principle of representation in succession laws, this ruling extends the legal framework surrounding fideicommissa, enhancing the protections afforded to grandchildren in South African inheritance law.
