Source Folder: 2007

# FIRAC Case Note: Gary Walter Van Der Merwe v Inspector Taylor & Others

**Case Citation:** 
CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA Case CCT 45/06 [2007] ZACC 16 

---

## F - Facts:

The case centers around the seizure of €130,000 and US$21,249 from Mr. Gary Walter Van Der Merwe (the first applicant) by State officials at Cape Town International Airport on July 13, 2004, as he was set to travel abroad ([paras 1-2]). The currency was seized after a customs official questioned Mr. van der Merwe about contraventions of the Exchange Control Regulations ([para 4]). The customs officials initially allowed him to board the plane but later removed him for questioning when they suspected a violation of foreign currency regulations ([para 4]).

After Mr. van der Merwe explained the currency's ownership, the matter was escalated to the SAPS, leading to his overnight detention and subsequent release on bail ([paras 5-7]). The seized currency was handed over to the South African Revenue Service (SARS) following a notice under Section 99 of the Income Tax Act, which appointed Inspector Taylor as an agent for tax obligations, although no clear tax liabilities had been established against van der Merwe ([para 6]).

The applicants, after the denial of their request for currency return in the High Court and a subsequent failed appeal, sought legal recourse in the Constitutional Court, claiming ownership and demanding the return of the currency on constitutional grounds ([paras 8-13]).

---

## I - Issues:

The main legal issues before the court include:
1. Whether Mr. van der Merwe is the lawful owner of the seized currency and thus entitled to its return ([para 21]).
2. The legal basis upon which the currency was seized and whether the State had the authority to hold it pending criminal proceedings ([paras 12, 20]).
3. Whether the seizure of the currency constituted an arbitrary deprivation of property under Section 25(1) of the Constitution ([para 13]).
4. The implications of Regulation 3 of the Exchange Control Regulations concerning automatic forfeiture ([para 68]).

---

## R - Rules:

1. **Constitutional Provisions:**
   - Section 25(1) of the Constitution protects property rights and states that no one may be deprived of property except in terms of law and without arbitrary deprivation ([para 25]).
   - Section 195 of the Constitution mandates public officials to maintain professional ethical conduct and accountability ([paras 71-72]).

2. **Relevant Statutes:**
   - Exchange Control Regulations, specifically Regulation 3(1)(a), which prohibits the unauthorized exportation of foreign currency ([para 12]).
   - Section 20 of the Criminal Procedure Act (CPA) affords the State the authority to seize items concerned with the commission or suspected commission of an offense, and Section 31(1)(a) requires return of seized items if no proceedings are instituted or if they are not required for evidence ([paras 53, 60]).

3. **Common Law Principles:**
   - The rei vindicatio is the action for recovery of property that belongs to the claimant, requiring proof of ownership, existence, and detention by the defendant ([paras 22-23]).

---

## A - Application:

The court assessed ownership based on evidence provided by Mr. van der Merwe and the implications of the laws governing property rights and state seizure ([paras 20-47]). 

1. **Regarding Ownership**:
   - The court found that Mr. van der Merwe could not prove ownership of the €109,135 as he had claimed that he was carrying it for a group ([paras 119-131]). In contrast, it determined that he was the owner of at least €20,865, which he had purchased as his travel allowance. His explanation provided regarding the other amounts was not enough to rebut the presumption that he did not consider himself the owner ([para 47]).
   
2. **Legal Authority for Seizure**:
   - The court noted that the basis for the State's seizure was contentious, with reliance initially placed on Section 99 of the Income Tax Act and then shifting to Section 20 of the CPA, causing legal uncertainty. The court emphasized that when a legal basis is in dispute, it raises concerns over potential arbitrary deprivation, invoking constitutional protections under Section 25 ([paras 18, 20]).

3. **Justification for Continued Possession**:
   - The court rejected the notion that the government could retain possession of the currency pending criminal proceedings; it found no justification for this under the given circumstances. This included considering that upon Mr. van der Merwe's assertion of ownership, the burden shifted to the State to justify its continued possession ([para 59]).

---

## C - Conclusion:

The Constitutional Court held that Mr. van der Merwe had proven ownership of €20,865, which should be returned to him. The full court’s conclusion that ownership had not been established was reversed, given the evidence produced ([paras 47-55]). The State could not lawfully continue to hold the currency since it failed to justify its possession beyond the time of seizure ([para 68]). 

The ruling had broader implications for the conduct of state officials, underscoring the necessity for transparency and accountability when seizing private property, aligning with the principles of public administration enshrined in the Constitution ([paras 72-73]).

**Order Issued by the Court**:
1. Return of €130,000 to Mr. van der Merwe.
2. The currency should be converted to its Rand equivalent based on the rate applicable at the date of seizure.
3. Costs awarded against the respondents ([para 138]).
