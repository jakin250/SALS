Oosthuizen & Another v S (144/2018) [2018] ZASCA 92

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 92

F – Facts

In the matter of Oosthuizen & Another v S (144/2018), the applicants, Willem Jacobus Albertus Oosthuizen and Theo Martins Jackson, sought leave to appeal against the refusal of bail pending the outcome of their appeal against convictions for several serious crimes, including kidnapping, attempted murder, and assault with intent to cause grievous bodily harm. The events leading to their convictions occurred on 17 August 2016, involving the abduction of Victor Rethabile Mlotshwa, who was allegedly forced into a coffin, threatened with violence, and assaulted ([paras 3–10]).

The applicants were initially convicted on multiple counts and sentenced to significant periods of imprisonment: Oosthuizen received an effective sentence of 11 years, while Jackson received 14 years ([paras 12–13]). The trial judge, Mphahlele J, highlighted the severity of the crimes, which had garnered national attention due to their racial undertones, referring to the case as 'the coffin case' ([paras 1–2]). After their applications for leave to appeal against the convictions were denied, the applicants applied for bail pending their appeal ([paras 14–15]).

The trial court denied bail, asserting that there were no reasonable prospects of success in the appeal and indicating that the sentences imposed would likely be custodial ([para 15]). On 2 February 2018, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) granted leave to appeal on the convictions and sentences, which motivated the applicants to seek a review of the bail refusal ([para 16]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue in this case concerned whether the applicants could be granted bail pending their appeal, despite the trial court's refusal. Specifically, the SCA needed to consider:

  1. Whether the applicants had a reasonable prospect of success on appeal against their convictions and sentences.
  2. Whether there are other compelling reasons justifying the granting of bail despite the serious nature of the crimes ([paras 41–42]).

Additionally, the role of provocation in their defense, its potential impact on the charges of violence and kidnapping, and how racial connotations affected the case were significant secondary issues ([paras 38–42]).

R – Rules

The SCA referred to the principles established in its earlier decision in S v Masoanganye ([2012] ZASCA 119), which clarified that the mere grant of leave to appeal does not entitle an applicant to bail. Instead, there needs to be a real prospect of success concerning the appeal. This case emphasized the importance of assessing the nature of the offences and the likelihood of non-custodial sentences ([para 29]).

The court also alluded to the principles surrounding provocation as a defense in criminal law. Under South African law, provocation may mitigate a sentence, but it must be demonstrated that the reaction followed immediately upon the provocation and that the provocation was significant enough to justify the ensuing conduct ([paras 30–34]).

A – Application

In its application of law to the facts, the SCA noted that the likely outcomes of a successful appeal would involve custodial sentences that extend beyond the period already served by the applicants ([para 40]). The court recognized the severity of the charges laid against the applicants, which were aggravated by racial overtones that added weight to the moral culpability of their actions ([paras 39–41]).

The admissions made by the applicants played a pivotal role in the SCA’s decision. Both Oosthuizen and Jackson acknowledged being involved in the physical and psychological torment inflicted upon Mlotshwa. Despite their claims of provocation stemming from alleged threats made by Mlotshwa, the SCA suggested that the nature of their responses constituted vigilantism rather than justified self-defense ([paras 25–27, 36–38]).

The court differentiated their claims about provocation, indicating that even if accepted, the mitigating effect would be minimal in light of the egregious nature of their actions, further reinforcing the trial court's assessment that significant custodial sentences were probable upon appeal ([para 30]).

C – Conclusion

The court ultimately dismissed the applicants' applications for leave to appeal against the bail refusal. The judgment underscored the absence of reasonable prospects of success for their appeal due to the serious nature of their offences and the implications of the case's racial context. The SCA's conclusion reinforced that the public interest and justice warranted a firm stance against crimes rooted in racial intolerance, as articulated in the judgment ([paras 42–44]).

This case affirmed the judiciary's commitment to addressing racial violence and highlighted the significance of individual accountability in preserving the tenets of justice and equality enshrined in the South African Constitution. The refusal of bail exemplified the court's resolve to deter such acts and the recognition of the societal responsibility to combat racial hatred ([paras 41–43]).
