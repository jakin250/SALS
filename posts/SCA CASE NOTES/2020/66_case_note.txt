# FIRAC Case Note: P[...] N[...] obo E[...] N[...] v The Member of the Executive Council for Health of the Gauteng Provincial Government ([2019] ZASCA 66)

## F – Facts ([paras 1–10])

In June 2012, the appellant, P[...] N[...] (hereafter "the appellant"), initiated an action against the respondent, the Member of the Executive Council for Health of the Gauteng Provincial Government (hereafter "the MEC"), seeking delictual damages for injuries sustained by her minor son, E[...] N[...] (hereafter "the minor"), due to the alleged negligence of medical staff at Chris Hani Baragwanath Hospital. On 12 September 2006, the minor suffered perinatal asphyxia arising from prolonged labor and the failure to perform a timely Caesarean section, resulting in severe brain damage, cerebral palsy, mental retardation, and epilepsy ([para 3]).

Following the incident, on 23 August 2012, the MEC filed a plea that essentially denied liability without articulating any defenses related to the development of the common law concerning medical services ([para 4]). The case proceeded to trial regarding liability only, where the court, under Moshidi J, held the MEC liable in a judgment resulting in a draft order on 24 April 2017 to pay the appellant 100% of her proven damages ([para 5]).

Subsequently, while preparing for a quantum determination set for 4 February 2019, the MEC amended her plea to propose providing medical services at state facilities instead of making a lump-sum monetary payment ([para 6]). This amendment suggested that the common law ought to be developed to facilitate alternative forms of compensation, including the possibility of paying in installments ([para 7]).

In the court a quo, Van der Linde J separated issues regarding whether the prior order restricted the MEC from providing services instead of money and whether provisions in the Public Finance Management Act 1 of 1999 (PFMA) restricted such decisions ([paras 8–9]).

## I – Issues ([paras 8–10])

The main legal questions addressed in this case were:

1. **Interpretation of the Previous Order**: Did the prior order, which mandated the MEC to "pay" the plaintiff 100% of her damages, preclude the MEC from seeking to provide services and medical items in lieu of monetary compensation?
2. **Application of the PFMA**: Did sections of the PFMA or accompanying Treasury Regulations bar the court from authorising the MEC to provide services or pay in installments?

## R – Rules ([paras 1–22])

In addressing the above issues, the court considered various legal standards and precedents, including:

1. **Common Law Principles**: The traditional approach to delictual claims required a monetary lump sum payment for proven damages. The court drew from the "once and for all" rule regarding damages ([para 14]).
2. **Development of Common Law**: The court referenced the Constitutional Court case of *MEC for Health and Social Development, Gauteng v DZ obo WZ* ([2018] ZACC 1), which had opened the possibility of developing common law to permit structured payment schemes for future medical expenses ([para 15]). However, the appellant claimed that no such flexibility existed at the time the draft order was made ([paras 15–16]).
3. **Burden of Interpretation**: The phrase "shall pay" was scrutinised in the context of the parties' intentions, clarifying that any payment referenced was strictly monetary based on the parties' circumstances and agreement ([para 16]).

## A – Application ([paras 11–20])

In its analysis, the court found that the a quo had misinterpreted the draft order by not considering its context properly. The court emphasized that the prior order was explicit in its terms and that both parties had agreed on the understanding that payment would be monetary and made as a lump sum ([paras 11–14]). The absence of any plea concerning the development of common law within the initial pleadings further solidified this interpretation ([para 15]).

The court held that the MEC’s proposed defenses were raised opportunistically, primarily after the Constitutional Court's decision. Such a conduct suggested a lack of genuine intent to adhere to the parties' originally intended framework of obligations. Thus, it reinforced the interpretation that "to pay" meant the respondent owed the appellant a specific monetary liability specifically due to the unequivocal nature of the earlier legal agreement ([para 16]).

Moreover, the court noted that while there exists a legislative consideration (the State Liability Amendment Bill) for claims of this nature, it still stands that the current understanding and obligations set forth in the court require the respondent to fulfill compensation via monetary payment rather than service provisions ([paras 20–21]).

## C – Conclusion ([paras 21–22])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately resolved to uphold the appeal, reiterating and clarifying the terms of the original order made by Moshidi J. It established that the phrasing did indeed preclude the MEC from offering medical services instead of making a monetary payment ([para 21]). Consequently, the appeal was upheld with costs awarded to the appellant, including costs for employing two counsels. The court’s decision affirms the importance of clear and unambiguous legal drafting in setting obligations in claims of this nature ([para 22]). 

This ruling holds significant implications not just for the parties involved but for the interpretation of liability in similar medical negligence cases and the approach courts might take towards the provision of alternative arrangements for damages claims going forward.
