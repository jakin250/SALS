Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note

## Case Title: 
**Body Corporate of the Pinewood Park Scheme No 202 v Dellis (Pty) Ltd (498/11) [2012] ZASCA 105**

### F – Facts
The case involves an appeal before the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa concerning a dispute over the payment of levies owed by Dellis (Pty) Ltd (the Respondent) to the Body Corporate of the Pinewood Park Scheme No 202 (the Appellant). The respondent is the registered owner of Sectional Title Unit 7 within the scheme located in Pinetown, KwaZulu-Natal ([paras 1–2]).

On November 2, 2006, the body corporate issued legal summons against the owner for R123,101.00 in allegedly overdue levies. The body corporate also sought interest on this amount from August 1, 2006, and requested attorney-client costs ([paras 3–4]). The owner admitted to its obligation to pay levies as per the legislation and rules governing the scheme, but denied liability for the specified amount, claiming that such debts were subject to prescription and were improperly allocated by the body corporate ([paras 4]).

During a pretrial conference on February 23, 2010, the owner argued that the high court lacked jurisdiction to address the claim due to the precedent set in **Body Corporate of Greenacres v Greenacres Unit 17 CC** (2008), which established that disputes between body corporates and owners regarding levies should be resolved through arbitration ([para 5]). The trial court concurred with this sentiment, ruling in favor of the owner and dismissing the body corporate's claim, a decision subsequently upheld by the Full Court of the KwaZulu-Natal High Court ([paras 5–6]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether management rule 71, which requires arbitration for disputes regarding levies between body corporates and owners, operated to exclude the provisions of the Arbitration Act 42 of 1965 concerning the arbitration process. Furthermore, the court needed to determine whether a valid dispute existed that warranted arbitration as per management rule 71 ([paras 6–9]).

### R – Rules
The case engages with several statutory and case law sources:

1. **Section 44(1)(b) of the Sectional Titles Act 95 of 1986** obligates the owner to pay levies to the body corporate ([para 2]).
2. **Section 35 of the Sectional Titles Act** provides that management rules dictate the control and administration of the scheme, and that disputes may be resolved via those management rules ([paras 6–7]).
3. **Management Rule 71** specifically mandates that disputes should be resolved through arbitration, except in the case of urgent court relief. It states that if an aggrieved party notifies the other of a dispute that remains unresolved after 14 days, arbitration may be demanded ([para 7]).
4. The **Arbitration Act 42 of 1965**, particularly Section 6, stipulates procedures for staying proceedings in court pending arbitration ([paras 10–13]).

Several precedent cases were consulted to frame the court's approach, including the **Greenacres** case, which established that without a dispute, arbitration is not applicable, and **Telecall (Pty) Ltd v Logan** (2000), which interpreted the necessity of a dispute for arbitration to proceed ([paras 8–9], [12]).

### A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal evaluated whether legitimate disputes between the parties merited arbitration under management rule 71 and whether the arbitration was indeed compulsory. The court noted that the trial court dismissed the body corporate's claim based on the supposed existence of an arbitrable dispute, agreeing with the trial court's interpretation of prior case law ([paras 9–10]).

However, the court disagreed with the lower court's conclusion regarding the compulsory nature of arbitration in this instance, focusing on the consensual nature of the management rules. The judgment reasoned that, unlike other statutes such as the Labour Relations Act, which establish clear paths to arbitration, the Sectional Titles Act does not explicitly mandate such arbitration procedures, thereby allowing room for interpretation ([paras 18–21]).

The Court further clarified that while the management rule could involve arbitration, it did not preclude the body's access to court under certain circumstances, emphasizing the necessity of determining whether an actionable dispute existed. The Appellant's argument that the owner never formally demanded arbitration was significant, providing grounds for the court’s analysis of procedural propriety under the Arbitration Act ([paras 10–11], [20]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately found that there was merit in the body corporate's appeal, reasoning that the trial court erred in dismissing the claim outright. It ordered the point in limine submitted by the owner be dismissed with costs, enabling the matter to either continue in court or be referred to arbitration, contingent on the existence of a legitimate dispute ([paras 22–23]).

The Court's decision implies that while management rules may outline procedural matters for dispute resolution, the absence of a formal demand for arbitration and uncertainty regarding the dispute's nature allows parties to pursue claims in the High Court, thereby clarifying the interplay of statutory obligations and mandated procedures for sectional title schemes in South Africa.
