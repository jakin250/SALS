Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: PG Group & Others v NERSA [2018] ZASCA 56

### F: Facts 
The appellants, comprising PG Group (Pty) Ltd, The South African Breweries (Pty) Ltd, Consol Glass (Pty) Ltd, Nampak Limited, Mondi Limited, Distribution & Warehousing Network Ltd, and Illovo Sugar South Africa Ltd, collectively initiated a review of the National Energy Regulator of South Africa's (NERSA) decision made on 26 March 2013. NERSA had approved Sasol Gas Limited’s application for maximum gas prices, leading to significant price hikes for natural gas. 

Sasol Gas is a subsidiary of Sasol Limited and holds a monopoly over piped gas supply, an essential energy resource for various industries. Following a regulatory agreement with the South African government, Sasol was allowed to charge monopoly prices for natural gas for ten years until 25 March 2014. NERSA, established by the National Energy Regulator Act 40 of 2004, is responsible for the regulation of the piped gas industry. 

The appellants argued that NERSA's approval of the maximum price methodology was unreasonable and irrational, given the market dynamics and Sasol Gas’s monopolistic position ([1]–[3]).

### I: Issues 
The primary legal issues addressed were:
1. Whether the determination of the methodology for regulating gas prices constituted administrative action within the meaning of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([30]–[31]).
2. Whether NERSA’s determination of the maximum gas prices was rational and lawful, considering its obligation to ensure prices reflect a competitive market, given the monopolistic context ([40]).

### R: Rules
The applicable legal framework includes:
1. **PAJA**: Defines administrative action that adversely affects rights and has a direct external legal effect ([30]).
2. **Gas Act 48 of 2001**: Mandates that maximum prices must be regulated in the context of inadequate competition (Section 21(1)(p)) ([12]).
3. **Regulations under the Gas Act**: Require NERSA to employ a systematic methodology for evaluating price applications ([12], [17]).

Jurisprudence relevant to the case includes precedents on rationality in administrative decisions, such as *Bato Star Fishing v Minister of Environmental Affairs* 2004 (4) SA 490 (CC) and principles established by the Constitutional Court regarding the promotion of administrative justice ([40]).

### A: Application 
In examining the argument of administrative action, the court emphasized that the determination of the methodology did not constitute an independent administrative act but was embedded in the broader process leading to the approval of maximum prices. The court's analysis articulated that finality in administrative action only occurs upon the determination of maximum prices, and thus, the appellants were justified in reviewing this decision without concern for the alleged delay ([30]–[39]).

Subsequently, the rationality of NERSA's decision-making process was scrutinized. The court found that NERSA’s methodology was fundamentally flawed, as it relied on comparative prices of alternative fuels—higher-cost sources—not reflective of a competitive gas market. NERSA's decision to use such a methodology led to an undue increase in gas prices contrary to its mandate to provide fair and reasonable pricing for consumers ([46]–[49]).

The court's analysis culminated in a determination that the resultant maximum prices from the methodology allowed for unreasonably high charges and did not substantively address monopolistic pricing abuses identified by NERSA itself. This failure indicated a clear disconnect between NERSA's duties and its adopted regulatory practices ([46]–[52]).

### C: Conclusion 
The Supreme Court of Appeal found in favor of the appellants, concluding that NERSA's decisions regarding maximum gas prices were both irrational and unreasonable. The ruling emphasized the necessity for NERSA to reassess its methodology in determining maximum prices and mandated that any new pricing structures be applied retroactively from 26 March 2014, ensuring consumer protection against unwarranted charges resulting from regulatory failures. The appeal was successful, reflecting a critical examination of regulatory actions in the context of energy pricing, highlighting the court's commitment to upholding rational governance in administrative law ([59]).
