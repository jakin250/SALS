Supreme Court of Appeal 2008 [2008] ZASCA 152

I. Facts
The appeal arises from a tragic collision that occurred on 12 April 2001 involving a passenger bus operated by the first respondent, Trans Zambezi Express (Pty) Ltd, driven by the second respondent, Thulani Winston Sibeni, and a stationary Puma army vehicle belonging to the Zimbabwean Defence Force. At the time of the accident, the bus was transporting approximately 40 passengers, including the appellants, Hannah Flanders and Johannes Mathys Pretorius, south of Masvingo, Zimbabwe ([1]–[3]).

The collision occurred just after 02h00 on a straight, dark section of road, devoid of artificial lighting, and featured a tarred surface with clear lane markings. The Puma had broken down and was parked unattended, angled such that its rear protruded over the broken yellow line, creating an obstruction. Testimonies regarding the vehicle's angle and the extent of its protrusion were disputed ([3]–[5]). Mr. Sibeni claimed he maintained the legal speed limit of 80 km/h while noticing the headlights of an oncoming vehicle, which he argued obscured his view of the stationary Puma until the last moment, thus preventing any evasive action ([6]–[7]).

An independent witness, Mr. Chame, contradicted Sibeni's version regarding the oncoming vehicle's headlights, stating that they remained high-beamed. Subsequently, the trial judge found the second respondent liable for the accident. However, upon appeal to a full court, this decision was overturned, leading to the current appeal before the Supreme Court of Appeal ([2]–[12]).

II. Issues
The primary issues under consideration are:

  1. Was the second respondent, Mr. Sibeni, negligent in the operation of the bus at the time of the collision despite the presence of an obstruction?
  2. Did the full court err in its finding of absolution from the instance, particularly regarding the burden of proof on the appellants to demonstrate negligence and causation ([2]–[12])?

III. Rules
The legal framework governing negligence in South African law revolves around the standard of care owed by the defendant, as articulated in cases such as Seemane v AA Mutual Insurance Association Ltd and Manderson v Century Insurance Co Ltd. The standard is primarily based on what a reasonable person would do in similar circumstances ([13]).

  1. Negligence: Established through a failure to observe the requisite standard of care, which requires a consideration of the foreseeable risks in the specific context. In night-driving scenarios, the court recognizes the challenges presented by unobserved obstructions ([13]–[14]).
  2. Causation: The plaintiffs must not only establish that the defendant was negligent but also that such negligence caused the harm suffered. This was underscored by the evidentiary burden required to demonstrate the direct link between Sibeni's actions and the resultant injuries ([16]).

IV. Application
The court criticized the previous full court's findings, particularly around Sibeni's alleged lack of precautionary measures in response to the blinding lights of the oncoming vehicle and the stationary Puma. It recognized that reasonable foresight would dictate a reduction of speed when approaching potential hazards, such as unlit obstructions ([11]–[12]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal also highlighted that Sibeni's operational decisions, including not braking or slowing down promptly after noticing the obstruction, lacked prudence. Despite the driver's initial testimony that conditions were hazardous due to lights, the court concluded that these factors were foreseeable and that a professionally trained driver had an obligation to adjust accordingly ([15]–[17]).

The analysis also detailed how Sibeni's failure to act constituted a breach of his duty of care towards the passengers, as it was determined he had not exercised a reasonable standard of driving given the circumstances. The argument that the extraordinary situation of approaching headlights should negate responsibility did not find favor, as the court stated that a driver should anticipate such obstacles routinely encountered during night driving ([14]–[16]).

V. Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately concluded that the appellants successfully discharged their onus of proving that Sibeni's negligence was a direct cause of the collision and their injuries. The court upheld the appeal, reversed the full court's ruling, and ordered the respondents liable to compensate the appellants, thereby stressing the necessity for professional drivers to adjust their actions in light of foreseeable dangers on the road. The decision reinforces the standards of care required in road traffic accidents, particularly highlighting the responsibilities of drivers in anticipating potential hazards during night travel. The final outcome thus not only addresses the tragic consequences of the case but also sets a clear precedent for future negligence claims in similar scenarios ([18]–[19]).
