Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: Bool Smuts and Another v Herman Botha (887/20) [2022] ZASCA 3

## F – Facts
The case revolves around an incident that occurred on 23 September 2019, when a group of cyclists, during an adventure ride organized by Quantum Adventure, crossed the farm Varsfontein owned by Mr. Herman Botha ([para 1]). Cyclist Nicholas Louw observed two cages on the farm: one containing a dead baboon and the other a dead porcupine, and he suspected they had died from dehydration due to poor conditions. Upset by this, he took photographs and sent them to Mr. Bool Smuts, a wildlife conservationist affiliated with the Landmark Leopard and Predator Project ([para 1]).

Upon receiving the photographs, Mr. Smuts contacted Mr. Botha, who confirmed that he possessed valid permits for capturing and killing the animals in question ([para 2]). Subsequently, Mr. Smuts posted the photographs, along with Mr. Botha’s personal information (such as his home address, business location, and a photograph of him with his daughter) on social media, criticizing Mr. Botha's practices as unethical and detrimental to biodiversity ([para 2]). This resulted in significant public backlash, and Mr. Botha sought legal intervention, claiming that the posts violated his right to privacy and were defamatory ([para 3]).

The Eastern Cape High Court initially granted an interim order preventing Mr. Smuts and Landmark Leopard from further publishing related content, citing the infringement of Mr. Botha's right to privacy ([para 4]). The High Court ultimately confirmed this order despite acknowledging some public interest in the topic ([para 5]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issue centers on whether the published personal information of Mr. Botha is protected under the right to privacy, in the context of balancing this right against Mr. Smuts' right to freedom of expression regarding a matter of public interest ([para 6]). Additionally, the court needed to determine whether Mr. Smuts' disclosures were necessary to inform the public about animal trapping practices and whether they could have been communicated without revealing Mr. Botha’s personal details ([para 6],[para 7]).

Key questions include:
1. Does the publication of Mr. Botha’s personal information constitute a breach of his right to privacy?
2. Is there sufficient public interest in the issues of animal welfare and trapping that justifies Mr. Smuts’ disclosures?
3. Did the High Court adequately balance the rights to privacy and freedom of expression? 

## R – Rules
The case invokes several important legal principles.
1. **Right to Privacy**: The Constitution of South Africa guarantees the right to privacy, which includes the right to keep personal information out of the public domain ([para 8]).
2. **Right to Freedom of Expression**: The right to freedom of expression is considered fundamental in a democratic society and allows individuals to express opinions and disseminate information ([para 11]).
3. **Balancing Test**: When the rights to privacy and freedom of expression conflict, a proportionality test is often employed to determine the appropriate balance ([para 7],[para 19]).
4. **Public Domain Rule**: Information that has been voluntarily disclosed to the public may no longer enjoy the same protection under privacy laws, as established in *Bernstein v Bester NO* ([para 9],[para 31]).

## A – Application
The court extensively analyzed the nature of Mr. Botha’s actions in relation to the photographs posted by Mr. Smuts. It found that by openly admitting to using animal traps and discussing his farming activities, Mr. Botha had placed a substantial amount of his personal information in the public domain, diminishing his expectation of privacy ([para 19],[para 23]).

The court also noted that the public has a legitimate interest in knowing about animal welfare, suggesting that Mr. Smuts’ motivations to alert the public were rooted in wider societal concerns regarding animal cruelty ([para 24]). Moreover, the court rejected the High Court's stance that the posts infringed on Mr. Botha’s right to privacy, arguing that the posts merely highlighted practices already known to the public ([para 31]).

The court determined that while Mr. Botha has a right to privacy, that right was significantly weakened by his own disclosures and the public's interest in animal welfare and conservation. Furthermore, the court critiqued the High Court for focusing too heavily on the emotional responses elicited by the Facebook comments rather than the critical nature of the dissent being expressed by Mr. Smuts ([para 21],[para 22]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Mr. Smuts' right to express his views and publicize information concerning the treatment of animals on Mr. Botha’s farm, concluding that Mr. Botha’s personal information was not protected under the right to privacy as it was already in the public domain. The appeal was upheld with costs, thereby highlighting the tension between individual privacy rights and the broader rights of expression and public discourse ([para 32]).

This case sets a precedent emphasizing that the right to freedom of expression, especially concerning matters of public interest like animal welfare, outweighs individual privacy claims when the individuals involved have publicly presented their information. Legal practitioners must consider the implications of this judgment when dealing with cases that intersect privacy rights and freedom of expression in democratic society.
