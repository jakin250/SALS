# Case Note: Breda NO v The Master of the High Court, Kimberley [2015] ZASCA 166

## FIRAC Structure

### F – Facts

The appellant, Lanel Breda NO, acted as executrix of the insolvent estate of her late husband, Sarel Johannes Breda ([para 1]). She sought to challenge the decision of the second respondent, Nomzi Khumalo NO, in her capacity as the Assistant Master of the High Court, who had provisionally admitted a claim by the third respondent, Petrus Arnoldus Els, at the first meeting of creditors on 27 February 2013. The claim amounted to R3,569,678.97 and was based on loans made to the deceased between 6 December 2006 and 25 August 2008 ([para 1]).

The appellant advanced two primary arguments to contest this decision ([para 3]): (a) the claim was purportedly prescribed based on the ex facie documents; and (b) the affidavit submitted by the third respondent failed to comply with the stipulations set out in the regulations governing the commissioning of oaths, thereby rendering the admission of the claim invalid. The High Court (Kgomo JP and Williams J) dismissed the application, asserting that the claim had not prescribed due to an interruption caused by a payment made on 30 June 2009 ([para 4]).

Following the dismissal at the lower court, the appellant was granted leave to appeal on grounds not initially raised during the original proceedings: the alleged disqualification of the commissioner of oaths who commissioned the affidavit due to a conflicting interest, and the unusual nature of the documents submitted, particularly in light of prescription ([para 5]).

The historical context of prior distrust between the appellant and the third respondent highlighted the underlying motives, including the eventual removal of trustees from the estate's administration ([paras 6–11]).

### I – Issues

The legal issues before the court revolved around the following:

1. Did the second respondent err by admitting the claim, and should she have conducted an interrogation regarding the prescription status of the claim ([paras 14–18])?
2. Was the affidavit in support of the claim invalidated by the statutory restrictions on the commissioner of oaths based on a potential conflict of interest ([paras 25–29])?
3. If the claim was ex facie prescribed, was the presiding officer required to reject it outright, or could provisional admission of the claim occur pending further inquiries ([paras 19–22])?

### R – Rules

The legal framework primarily revolved around the Insolvency Act 24 of 1936, with a specific focus on sections 44(3), 44(4), 44(7), and 45. Section 44 outlines the requirements for admitting claims against an insolvent estate and the powers of the Master in evaluating such claims ([para 17]).

Furthermore, the Justices of the Peace and Commissioners of Oaths Act 16 of 1963, particularly regulations governing the administration of oaths, stipulated conditions under which commissioners may operate, including prohibitions regarding interests in the matters they officiate ([para 28]).

Several pertinent case precedents were cited, including **Marendaz v Smuts** 1966 (4) SA 66 (T), discussing the discretion of presiding officers, and **Aspeling & another v Hoffman’s Trustee** 1917 TPD 305, addressing the nature of proof required in admitting claims and the implications of prescription ([paras 15, 19]).

### A – Application

The court applied the legal principles to the facts at hand, considering the appellant’s arguments regarding prescription. It emphasized that while the documents presented could suggest that the debts had prescribed, the context of a payment made in 2009 complicated this assertion ([paras 24–25]). The High Court's finding that the running of prescription was interrupted was consequently upheld, supporting the second respondent's provisional admission of the claim ([para 24]).

The court also addressed the procedural aspects regarding the affidavit in question. It concluded that despite the potential interest of the commissioner, an exemption applied, given that the affidavit was considered to have been administered for record purposes under the supervision of a state officer ([para 41]). This aligned with the amended schedule to the regulations which recognized the role of the Master or presiding officers in relation to claims ([para 39]).

Overall, it was determined that both the procedural steps taken by the second respondent were valid and adhered to statutory requirements, reaffirming that the proper forum for determining the nuances of prescription and validity belonged with the appointed trustees of the estate, not the presiding officer at the first meeting of creditors ([para 24]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, citing a lack of evidence that the second respondent had committed any error in admitting the claim ([para 42]). The court highlighted the importance of trust in the trusteeship and reaffirmed that the decisions regarding claims against an insolvent estate should primarily rest on the examination and discretion of the appointed trustees, as they are tasked with the thorough evaluation of claims and the interests of creditors ([para 24]).

This case signifies critical implications for insolvency law in South Africa, reaffirming the need for due process in the admission of creditor claims, the procedural rights of debtors and creditors, and the significance of statutory compliance regarding administrative functions within courthouses. The ruling draws valuable distinctions between provisional admissions and final determinations, underscoring the importance of comprehensive involvements of trustees in managing insolvent estates.
