Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2007

# FIRAC Case Note: Lubbe NO v Millennium Style [2007] SCA 10 (RSA)

### F – Facts ([paras 1–12])

This case revolves around a trademark dispute whereby the appellants, a group of trustees, sought an interdict for alleged trademark infringement against the respondents, which included a competing shoe business and various individuals associated with it. The high court previously dismissed the appellants’ infringement application and ordered the expungement of six trademarks (TM 1998/14074; TM 1987/14074; TM 1987/9449; TM 1988/5584; TM 1987/9450; TM 1987/9451) registered under the Trade Marks Act 62 of 1963. It was determined that these registrations were not valid as the shapes and configurations they purported to protect were not permissible under the 1963 Act ([para 1]).

The Trust’s trademarks were admitted to be devoid of distinctiveness, with the court observing that the marks were registered to suppress competition rather than serve a legitimate trademark function. Notably, just days prior to the appeal hearing, the Trust acknowledged that two of its marks lacked distinguishability yet aimed to retain them on technical grounds. The court noted that the registration of shape as trademarks violated the statutes governing trademark registration ([paras 2–3]).

The case highlighted that the essence of the dispute was not solely about trademarks but also addressed competitive suppression tactics. The appellants claimed that a former employee, through the design of his competing shoes, infringed on their trademarks. However, their claims of unfair competition did not hold in the high court, and they did not attempt to reconstruct this argument on appeal ([paras 4–5]).

In the appeal, the two key issues revolved around whether the registered marks were valid and distinctive under the legal framework established by the 1963 Act and the later 1993 Act ([para 8]). The court analyzed whether the appellants had standing to oppose the expungement of TM 1998/14074, arguing that the appellants' lack of infringement claims barred the respondents from being considered ‘interested parties’ for rectification ([paras 9–10]).

### I – Issues ([paras 1–12])

1. **Validity of Trademark Registrations**: Were the trademarks of the appellants valid under the Trade Marks Act 62 of 1963, specifically concerning the registration of shapes and configurations?
  
2. **Distinctiveness in Trademark Sense**: Did the registered shapes serve a distinctive function as required under trademark law, particularly after the enactment of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993?

3. **Standing of Respondents**: Did the respondents have the necessary standing to seek the expungement of the trademark registrations, despite the appellants' claims?

### R – Rules ([paras 1–12])

The court applied several legal principles drawn from statutory frameworks and case law:

- **Trade Marks Act 62 of 1963**: Section 16 prohibited the registration of shapes as trademarks, emphasizing that only “marks” could be registered. 

- **Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993**: Allowed for the registration of shapes as trademarks but required that such shapes possess distinctive character capable of indicating the source of goods. This act acknowledges a shift in trademark applicability but maintains that distinctiveness is essential ([para 8]).

- **Case Law**: Previous case law including *Weber-Stephen Products Co v Registrar of Trade Marks* [1994] (3) SA 611 (T) and *Bergkelder Bpk v Vredendal Koöp Wynmakery* [2006] SCA 8 (RSA) provided context for trademark distinctiveness and valid registration. These cases underscored the necessity of a mark’s potential to distinguish products in the marketplace ([para 9]).

- **Interest in Rectification Claims**: In *Ritz Hotel Ltd v Charles of the Ritz Ltd* [1988] (3) SA 290 (A) and *Mars Inc v Candy World (Pty) Ltd* [1991] (1) SA 567 (A), it was established that a party operating in the relevant market has an interest in clearing the trademark register, which allows them to contest registrations that do not meet legal standards ([para 10]).

### A – Application ([paras 1–12])

The court conducted a thorough analysis of the registered trademarks, determining that registration of shapes was inherently flawed as the 1963 Act did not permit it. The Trust’s contention that the shapes could be classified as "devices" did not alter their inability to serve as trademarks. The court underscored that even post-1993 trade mark law demands distinctiveness as a critical factor in trademark validity.

The court's examination of the marks revealed that they were unlikely to be perceived by consumers as indicators of origin but rather as ornamental aspects of shoe design. The absence of substantial use of the trademarks over two decades compounded the invalidity determination, reinforcing the conclusion that the marks could not serve their intended trademark purpose ([para 9]).

Regarding the standing of the respondents, the court dismissed the appellants’ argument that a lack of active pursuit of the infringement claim eliminated any standing. It was made clear that parties interested in the trademark landscape are entitled to contest the validity of questionable registrations, aligning with the purpose of maintaining a clear register ([para 10]).

### C – Conclusion ([paras 1–12])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, agreeing with the high court’s findings that the trademark registrations were void due to non-compliance with existing legal standards. The court emphasized that the trademarks not only failed to demonstrate the requisite distinctiveness but were also deployed in a manner that attempted to stifle competition rather than protect legitimate commercial interests. This decision serves to clarify the principles governing trademark registration and reinforces the requirement that marks must bear distinctive characteristics capable of designating the source of goods.

The implications extend beyond this case, indicating a firm stance on upholding the integrity of trademark law and eradicating practices that misuse the register for anti-competitive ends. The court's ruling reaffirms the importance of ensuring that registered trademarks genuinely fulfill their purpose in the marketplace, thereby safeguarding competition and innovation ([para 12]).
