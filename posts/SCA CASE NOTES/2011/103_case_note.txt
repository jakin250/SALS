Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: Furlong v State (064/11) [2011] ZASCA 103

## F – Facts:
In the case of *Furlong v State*, the appellant, Sheldon Patrick Furlong, stood trial before the regional court in Musina charged under section 36 of the General Law Amendment Act 62 of 1955, which penalizes those found in possession of goods suspected of being stolen without a satisfactory explanation ([1–2]). On 24 October 2006, Furlong was apprehended while in possession of a stolen Audi Q7 valued at R750,000, which had been hijacked from its owner, Mr. Hlanigani Joseph Maluleke. Maluleke's testimony revealed the traumatic circumstances under which the vehicle was seized, including armed robbery and unlawful detention ([5–7]).

Having pleaded guilty, Furlong was convicted and sentenced to seven years of imprisonment ([4]). He later appealed this sentence at the Pretoria High Court, which was dismissed. Subsequently, he sought permission to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which was granted ([5]).

The appeal raised concerns regarding the appropriateness of the sentence, particularly considering that Furlong had expressed contrition by pleading guilty and was a first-time offender ([10–12]). In analyzing the original sentence, factors included the appellant's age (40 years), the duration he had already spent in custody, his prior lack of criminal record, and his precarious financial situation, supporting dependents ([16–18]).

## I – Issues:
The central issues for determination were:
1. Whether the trial court committed a material misdirection in imposing the seven-year sentence, particularly by equating Furlong’s offence with more serious ones such as robbery.
2. Whether the appellate court should reach a different conclusion as to the appropriate sentence considering the circumstances of the case ([11–12]).

## R – Rules:
The applicable legal principles revolve around:
- The discretion of trial courts in sentencing, which traditionally respects the principle that an appellate court should only interfere if the sentence is disturbingly inappropriate, grossly disproportionate, or the result of a misdirection ([9]).
- Comparisons made with precedents, such as *S v Mtungwa & 'n ander* 1990 (2) SACR 1 (A), which set out the grounds on which appellate interference is warranted, focusing on proportionality, moral responsibility, and the objectives of punishment ([9–10], [20]).
- The statute under which Furlong was charged, specifically the definition of possession under section 36, which requires that possession is connected to the unlawful act and a failure to provide a satisfactory explanation ([2]).

## A – Application:
The SCA found the trial court had, in its reasoning, misdirected itself by conflating the offence of unlawful possession with the more severe crimes of robbery and theft. The magistrate's acknowledgment of the seriousness of the hijacking and its implications influenced the sentence disproportionately, thus amounting to a material misdirection ([12–13]). The SCA determined that while the offence was indeed serious, the sentence did not appropriately reflect the specific charge against the appellant.

Moreover, the court reviewed the compelling factors presented, including Furlong's age, his status as a first-time offender, and his previous good conduct, alongside evidence of remorse illustrated by his guilty plea. It highlighted a tendency in the trial court's reasoning to impose a sentence meant to act as a deterrent, which detracted from fair sentencing reflective of the specific crime committed ([10–20]).

Ultimately, the SCA ruled that a sentence of three years and two days imprisonment—reflective of the time already served and the specifics of the charge—would serve justice better than the overly punitive seven-year sentence originally imposed. The court decided that there were no further aspects that necessitated continued imprisonment for the appellant ([21]).

## C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal allowed the appeal, demonstrating that the trial court's sentence was disproportionate relative to the offence and flawed due to a significant misdirection. It replaced the seven-year sentence with a lesser sentence of three years and two days imprisonment, effective from the date of the original sentencing. This outcome serves to reiterate the necessity for sentences to align closely with the offending actions and to take into account personal circumstances and moral culpability, thereby fostering the principles of justice and fairness within the South African legal context. The decision not only highlights the importance of addressing systemic issues around sentencing but also reinforces the need for proportional responses to crime ([21]).
