Technology Corporate Management (Pty) Ltd and Others v De Sousa and Another (Case No 613/2017) [2024] ZASCA 29

The case involves a dispute between shareholders of Technology Corporate Management (Pty) Ltd (TCM), a company initially founded by two friends, Luis de Sousa (Luis) and Andrea Cornelli (Andrea), under the premise of equal partnership.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 29

F – Facts

The case involves a dispute between shareholders of Technology Corporate Management (Pty) Ltd (TCM), a company initially founded by two friends, Luis de Sousa (Luis) and Andrea Cornelli (Andrea), under the premise of equal partnership. Over the years, TCM grew significantly, leading to the introduction of new shareholders, including Iqbal Hassim (Iqbal).

Luis and his colleagues, Jose and Tony, began to feel marginalized following the incorporation of a new major shareholder, Iqbal, who was introduced to enhance TCM's Black Economic Empowerment (BEE) profile. Luis alleged that from 2007, he was excluded from management and faced unfair treatment regarding his employment and compensation. His subsequent dismissal in 2009, upheld by the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA) as fair, led to the contention that the manner of the company's management was unfairly prejudicial, thus providing grounds for relief under Section 252 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973.

The High Court found in favor of Luis and Jose, ordering TCM to purchase their shares at a determined value and citing unfair prejudice in how the company was managed. The ruling prompted an appeal by TCM, represented by the remaining shareholders. The appeal focused on whether there was legitimate unfair prejudice and whether the High Court's orders regarding the purchase of shares were appropriate within the context of the Companies Act.

I – Issues

  1. Was the High Court correct in determining that the manner in which TCM’s affairs were conducted was unfairly prejudicial to minority shareholders?
  2. To what extent does the CCMA ruling on the fairness of Luis's dismissal impact his claim under Section 252?
  3. Did TCM's financial handling, particularly concerning Luis and Jose's exclusion and their shares, amount to unfair prejudice?
  4. Was the High Court justified in ordering TCM to purchase the plaintiffs’ shares?
  5. What constitutes a fair offer for the buyout of shares in a company?

R – Rules

The legal framework for this case primarily stems from:

  • Section 252 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973, which empowers the court to grant relief where a shareholder claims that certain actions or omissions are unfairly prejudicial, unjust, or inequitable.
  • Labor Relations Act 66 of 1995, particularly sections dealing with unfair dismissal, which provide a backdrop to the claims of unfair prejudice based on employment status.
  • The rule from Hollington v Hewthorn, which states that findings from disciplinary or criminal proceedings are often inadmissible in subsequent civil proceedings unless otherwise stipulated.

Relevant case law includes:

  • O’Neill v Phillips which underscores the need for fairness in managing shareholder relations.
  • McMillan NO v Pott concerning the implications of shareholder exclusion without a reasonable offer.

A – Application

The Court of Appeal scrutinized the findings of the High Court regarding the alleged unfair prejudice Luis and Jose suffered. Key arguments presented included:

  1. The Nature of Shareholder Relationships: The court acknowledged that although TCM started with a partnership-like relationship, the introduction of Iqbal and subsequent legal agreements shifted these dynamics significantly, negating claims of a quasi-partnership which would necessitate equal treatment in management decisions.
  2. Luis's Dismissal and CCMA Findings: The appellate opinion highlighted the CCMA’s determination that Luis's dismissal was fair, thus undermining his position in the exclusion claim since prejudicial claims must stem from unfair treatment. By reinforcing the CCMA’s findings, the appeal established that his claims on grounds of unfair management were not sufficient without demonstrating that the dismissal itself was unfair.
  3. Management Decisions and Financial Holdings: The court emphasized that for unfair prejudice to be established, the plaintiffs must effectively link their claims to specific actions that evidentially demonstrated misconduct or a failure in the standard of probity expected from the majority shareholders. The allegations regarding the mismanagement of the Supplies Division were found to lack merit after scrutinizing financial records, which indicated growth across the business.
  4. Obligations of Majority Shareholders: The ruling addressed the obligations of majority shareholders concerning navigating buyout offers. It noted that reasonable offers had been made by TCM to purchase the shares of Luis and Jose, and the absence of acceptance of these offers could not constitute unfair prejudice. The court interpreted the refusal to negotiate outside the confines of the shareholders’ agreement as an exercise of lawful rights rather than an act of unfair prejudice.

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the findings of the High Court regarding unfair prejudice and the obligation for TCM to purchase the shares were misdirected. The appeal was upheld, and the court iterated that the relationship dynamics within TCM had fundamentally altered following the incorporation of new shareholders and formal agreements that reframed shareholder expectations.

The court's determination urged clear financial communication and validity in operational agreements, establishing that the dynamics in small businesses evolve and shareholder rights must be assessed not simply through historical relationships but through contractual agreements executed during the lifetime of the company.

The implications of this case highlight the necessity for clarity in shareholder agreements and emphasize that claims of unfair prejudice must be substantiated with clear evidence of misconduct or mismanagement reflecting a departure from reasonable expectations grounded in those agreements.
