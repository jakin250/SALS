Hilda Van der Burg and Edward Van der Burg v National Director of Public Prosecutions and Centre for Child Law (CCT 75/11)

Constitutional Court 2012 CCT 75/11

FIRAC Analysis

F – Facts

The case involves an appeal by Hilda and Edward Van der Burg against a forfeiture order made under the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (POCA). The couple owned a residential property in Athlone, Cape Town, which they had purchased for R169,000 in 2000. By 2006, they were running an unlicensed liquor outlet, referred to as a shebeen, from their home, generating significant disturbance within the neighborhood and receiving substantial complaints from residents ([2]–[9]).

The applicants contended that the forfeiture of their property, which had been declared an instrumentality of crime due to its use in illegal activities, would be disproportionate and detrimental, particularly concerning their four children, three of whom are minors ([3]–[4], [10]–[11]). The National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) sought the forfeiture due to persistent violations of liquor laws that led to police interventions and legal sanctions ([12]–[15]).

Following an unsuccessful initial hearing, the Full Court of the Western Cape High Court dismissed the couple's appeal against the forfeiture order ([17]–[18]). The constitutional implications of the law enforcement efforts to combat organized crime and maintain the sanctity of property rights were significant features throughout the proceedings ([1]–[2]).

I – Issues

Three main questions emerged for consideration in this case:

  1. Whether the forfeiture provisions of POCA applied to the activities conducted on the property and the associated criminal conduct.
  2. Whether the consequences of the forfeiture order would be disproportionate to the offenses committed by the applicants.
  3. Whether the best interests of the applicants’ minor children, as required by the Constitution, warranted further action by the court to address their welfare ([4]).

R – Rules

The pertinent legal rules and constitutional provisions stem from:

  • Prevention of Organised Crime Act (POCA): Particularly §50(1)(a), which allows for the forfeiture of property deemed an instrumentality of an offense (§50(1)(a)) ([20]–[22]).
  • Constitution of South Africa: Specifically §25(1), which provides the right not to be arbitrarily deprived of property; §26, concerning the right to adequate housing; and §28(2), which emphasizes the best interests of children as paramount in all decisions affecting them ([25], [28]).
  • Judicial Precedents: Cases such as Mohunram v National Director of Public Prosecutions and others that provide the basis for proportionality assessments in forfeiture decisions ([26], [29]).

A – Application

The court began by addressing whether the forfeiture provisions were applicable to the Van der Burgs' case. It noted that the selling of liquor without a license, while not specifically categorized under organized crime, still fell within the ambit of POCA as an offense requiring civil forfeiture. The court upheld previous findings that the property constituted an instrumentality of an offense due to the consistent illegal activities conducted by the applicants ([31]–[41]).

On the proportionality of the forfeiture, the court examined the persistence of the illegal activities and the ineffectiveness of traditional law enforcement strategies, positing that the use of forfeiture was justified under POCA as a necessary step to curb ongoing criminality. The extensive evidence of community disturbance and law enforcement attempts reinforced the argument that the forfeiture was an appropriate remedy ([50]–[56]).

The best interests of children's provisions were critically assessed. The court conceded that while the forfeiture could potentially result in the family's homelessness, the applicants failed to convincingly demonstrate that their economic situation would leave them unable to find alternative accommodation ([58]–[60]). The court acknowledged the overlap between the interests of the parents and the children but underscored the necessity to study the children's environment beyond just the family context, considering the broader implications of prolonged exposure to illegal activities ([76]–[78]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the appeal, ruling that the forfeiture order did apply under POCA and was proportional given the nature of the offenses committed. Furthermore, the court recognized the pressing need to consider child welfare as a distinct and separate inquiry under the Children’s Act, ordering a social work investigation into whether the children were in need of care and protection ([80]–[83]).

This ruling has significant implications for similar cases where the intersection of property rights, criminal activity, and child welfare is at stake, reinforcing the court's commitment to uphold constitutional rights while prioritizing the safety and welfare of children.
