Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

**Case Note: Frank Mhlongo and Others v Tryphina Mokoena N O and Others (723/20) [2022] ZASCA 78**

---

### F – Facts

The appellants, Frank Mhlongo and others, are beneficiaries of the Nhlangwini Community Trust, purportedly representing 78 other beneficiaries ([2]). They filed an application in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria (the High Court), seeking an order compelling the trust's trustees and their attorneys to provide financial information regarding the trust, including bank statements and payment histories ([2]). The respondents included the trustees of the trust, the Master of the High Court, and various attorneys who had been involved with the trust ([2]).

The eighth respondent raised a point in limine, arguing that the High Court lacked jurisdiction because the matter concerned a property located in Mpumalanga province, thus necessitating the application to be heard in the Mbombela or Middelburg circuit courts, as dictated by a practice directive issued by the Judge President of Gauteng ([3]). The High Court upheld this point, ruling that it did not have jurisdiction to consider the appellants' application, and thus the merits were never evaluated ([4]).

Dissatisfied with this ruling, the appellants sought leave to appeal, which was initially denied but later granted by the Supreme Court of Appeal on a limited basis ([5]). The key issues before the court included the jurisdiction of the Gauteng High Court and the validity of the Judge President’s directive ([5]).

### I – Issues

The pertinent legal issues were:
1. Whether the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria had the jurisdiction to hear the appellants' application ([5]).
2. Whether the Judge President of the Gauteng Division possessed the authority, pursuant to section 7(1) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, to exclude the jurisdiction of the Gauteng Division regarding matters arising in areas under the jurisdiction of circuit courts defined by the practice directive, given that the Mpumalanga Division had not yet been formally established at the time ([5]).

### R – Rules

The legal framework pertinent to the case includes:
- **Section 21 of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013**: This statute outlines the jurisdictional reach of divisions of the High Court ([9]), detailing that a Division has jurisdiction over persons residing within its area and for causes arising therein ([8]).
- **Section 6(3)(a) of the Superior Courts Act**: This section mandates that the Minister of Justice, after consultation with the Judicial Service Commission, determines the geographical jurisdiction of the High Courts ([9]).
- **Section 7(1) of the same Act**: This allows the Judge President to delineate circuit districts for adjudication but does not extend to altering jurisdiction established by legislation ([12]).
- **Constitutional provisions**: Sections 34 and 173 of the Constitution were also referenced, albeit their applicability was minimized since the jurisdictional issue is fundamentally a matter of statutory interpretation rather than constitutional entitlement ([19]).

### A – Application

The court engaged in a rigorous interpretation of the relevant statutory provisions. Initially, it recognized that the High Court needed to retain its jurisdiction until a formal notice establishing the Mpumalanga Division was promulgated, which occurred on 1 May 2019 ([11]). The directive issued on 1 September 2017 could not validly strip the High Court of its jurisdiction as it was acting beyond its powers ([14]).

The court further clarified that the High Court and the newly defined circuit courts in Mpumalanga would have concurrent jurisdiction over the relevant matters, which meant that there was an overlap: both the High Court and circuit courts could hear matters arising in these areas ([13]). Importantly, the court articulated that practice directives cannot contradict existing legislative frameworks, affirming that jurisdiction must be assessed based on statutory law ([10]).

The court ultimately determined that the High Court erred in granting the eighth respondent's preliminary point regarding jurisdiction. It was established that jurisdiction is not discretionary and that the Judge President did not have the authority to exclude jurisdiction pertaining to the Gauteng Division in the absence of a clear legislative basis for doing so ([14], [20]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal and set aside the Gauteng High Court's decision ([16]). It ruled that:
1. The Gauteng High Court had jurisdiction to hear the appellants' application, as the statutory framework permitted.
2. The practice directive's clause asserting the cessation of the High Court’s jurisdiction was declared null and void ab initio ([16]).

The judgement reaffirmed the principle that practice directives cannot override statutory provisions governing jurisdiction. The decision strengthens the foundations of jurisdictional authority within South African law and clarifies the limits of a Judge President's power in delineating jurisdictional boundaries ([16], [17]).

In light of this ruling, litigants within the jurisdiction of the Gauteng Division can expect a reaffirmed access to the courts, and clarity is provided on the concurrency of jurisdiction between High Courts and circuit courts, ensuring fairness and consistency in adjudicating legal matters ([28]).
