# Case Note: **Ramakatsa and Others v African National Congress and Another**, Case No. 724/2019 [2021] ZASCA 31 (31 March 2021) 

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([para 1]–[para 6])

The dispute arises from an appeal concerning the legality of the Provincial Conference (PC) of the Free State branch of the African National Congress (ANC), which convened on 18 and 19 May 2018. The appellants, Matshepo Ramakatsa and others, sought a declaration that this PC was held in violation of a prior court order issued by the Free State High Court on 29 November 2017. This earlier order, known as the Van Zyl order, mandated that certain Branch General Meetings (BGMs) be conducted lawfully before any PC could take place ([para 2]). 

The Van Zyl order had declared irregularities in numerous BGMs and deemed their outcomes invalid, directing that no PC could occur until the requisite BGMs were lawfully re-run ([para 4]). Despite respondents convening some BGMs in December 2017, issues persisted, leading to the appellants' contention that the PC held in May 2018 was thus unlawful and unconstitutional ([para 5]).

Following these events, the appellants initiated legal proceedings, alleging that the respondents had acted in contempt of the Van Zyl order and failed to address numerous irregularities before the PC ([para 2]). On 21 February 2019, the Free State High Court dismissed their application. The appellants subsequently appealed this decision to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 7]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([para 8]–[para 12])

The primary legal issues before the court were:

1. Whether the Provincial Conference held on 18 and 19 May 2018 was lawful in light of the non-compliance with the Van Zyl order ([para 3]);
2. Whether the appellants could contest the validity of BGMs that had not been specifically challenged in the prior court order ([para 21]);
3. The applicability of the Plascon-Evans rule, which guides the resolution of disputes of fact in motion proceedings ([para 8]);
4. Whether inadequacies in the audit process and the conduct of BGMs rendered the PC invalid ([para 22]).

The issues incorporate not only procedural compliance with the ANC's internal regulations but also engagement with substantive issues regarding compliance with constitutional obligations under Section 19 of the South African Constitution regarding political participation ([para 12]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([para 13]–[para 24])

In addressing the issues, the court relied on a variety of legal provisions and precedents, including:

1. **The ANC Constitution**: Specifically, Rule 17, which outlines the procedure for convening a Provincial Conference, stressing compliance and the requirement of participation from members in good standing ([para 13]);
2. **The Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013**, particularly relating to the criteria for granting leave to appeal under Section 17, emphasizing reasonable prospects of success and compelling reasons ([para 10]);
3. **Plascon-Evans Paints (TVL) Ltd v Riebeck Paints (Pty) Ltd** [1984] 3 SA 623, which establishes that an applicant in motion proceedings is entitled to relief if the facts it alleges, together with the respondents' responses, justify such relief ([para 8]);
4. **Ramakatsa v Magashule**, a precedent that affirmed the right of ANC members to ensure their party acts in accordance with its Constitution and the overarching constitutional provisions ([para 11]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([para 26]–[para 28])

The Supreme Court of Appeal critically assessed the compliance of the respondents with the Van Zyl order. The court emphasized that both the ANC Constitution and the party's own guidelines required an audit of branches and mandatory, lawful BGMs prior to holding a Provincial Conference ([para 22], [para 24]). The court defined the violations of these rules as rendering the May 2018 PC unlawful.

In analyzing individual BGMs, the court raised concerns about the processes followed in various wards where significant irregularities—including inadequate notice, failure to use proper attendance registers, and the election of delegates from meetings that failed to meet quorum thresholds—were documented ([para 16]–[para 20]). The court concluded that not only had the respondents breached their obligations under the ANC Constitution, but the lack of good standing among many delegates raised serious doubts about the legitimacy of the PC ([para 27]).

On the issue of the Plascon-Evans rule, the court found that despite the respondents' claims of compliance, the appellants' evidence concerning the alleged irregularities remained substantially unrefuted and warranted a different conclusion than that reached by the High Court ([para 25]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([para 29])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, concluding that the Provincial Conference convened on 18 and 19 May 2018 was indeed unlawful and unconstitutional due to non-compliance with the prior court order regarding the need for valid BGMs. The court's order declared the PC and its resolutions void, thereby reiterating the importance of adherence to internal party rules and the Constitution ([para 29]).

The court's decision underscores the commitment to rule of law within political parties in South Africa, drawing a clear line that violations of internal constitutions can engage constitutional concerns, particularly in the context of member participation and legitimate governance practices. This ruling serves as a critical reminder to political parties about the necessity of lawful procedures, impacting future governance within the ANC and potentially influencing similar disputes across diverse political entities ([para 12], [para 29]). 

Overall, the decision reinforces the principle that political parties must operate within their constitutional frameworks to maintain their legitimacy and uphold democratic values in the political sphere.
