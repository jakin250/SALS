Sibongile Zungu v Premier of the Province of KwaZulu-Natal and Others [2018] ZACC 1

Constitutional Court 2018 [2018] ZACC 1

F – Facts

This case stems from an application for leave to appeal regarding an order from the Labour Appeal Court, which dismissed Dr. Sibongile Zungu's appeal with costs ([para 1]). The applicant had been employed as the Head of the Department of Health in KwaZulu-Natal under a five-year contract from December 1, 2009, to July 31, 2014. Following the conclusion of her contract, the Premier of KwaZulu-Natal, the first respondent, notified Dr. Zungu on June 26, 2014, that her contract would not be renewed despite a recommendation from the selection committee for her reappointment ([paras 1–2]).

During this period, her contract was extended multiple times due to allegations made against her by the National Education, Health and Allied Workers’ Union (NEHAWU), prompting an investigation ([paras 3–4]). On March 30, 2015, Dr. Zungu sought an urgent interdict from the Labour Court to prevent her replacement and compel her appointment based on the committee's recommendation ([para 5]). The Labour Court eventually dismissed her application, stating it lacked jurisdiction and that a legitimate expectation of contract renewal did not exist ([paras 10–11]).

Dr. Zungu's subsequent appeal before the Labour Appeal Court was similarly dismissed. The court held that the matter constituted a dispute over a dismissal as defined in section 186(1)(b) of the Labour Relations Act, which requires arbitration rather than judicial resolution ([para 15]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal refused leave to appeal to the merits but granted it regarding costs, leading to the current proceedings ([paras 16–20]).

I – Issues

The principal legal issues before the Constitutional Court include:

  1. Whether the Labour Appeal Court was correct in denying Dr. Zungu's appeal on the grounds that her dismissal fell within the definitions provided in the Labour Relations Act, requiring adherence to dispute resolution processes ([paras 12–15]).
  2. Whether the costs awarded against Dr. Zungu by the Labour Court and Labour Appeal Court were appropriate and in line with the principles governing costs in labour matters ([paras 22–26]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal authorities and rules applicable to the case include:

  1. Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995, particularly section 186(1)(b), which defines a dismissal as including a refusal to renew a fixed-term contract ([paras 12, 20]).
  2. Public Finance Management Act 1 of 1999, particularly sections regarding the appointment of accounting officers and the requirements of executive accountability ([paras 8, 21]).
  3. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA), which provides the framework for judicial review of administrative decisions ([para 12]).
  4. Labour Relations Act Section 162, which governs the procedural fairness in awarding costs in Labour Court matters, establishing that costs should align with the requirements of law and fairness rather than strict adherence to the ‘costs follow the event’ rule ([paras 23–25]).

A – Application

Jurisdiction and Dismissal

The Constitutional Court endorsed the Labour Appeal Court's ruling that Dr. Zungu's claims regarding her dismissal (or the non-renewal of her contract) were properly categorized under section 186(1)(b). This classification compels adherence to the arbitration process outlined in section 191 of the Labour Relations Act, thus affirming the jurisdictional limitations initially cited by the Labour Court ([paras 20]). The court explained that the issues raised by Dr. Zungu, centered around her expectation of reappointment and the Premier’s alleged disregard for the selection committee's recommendation, fell squarely within the parameters of a labour dispute, thus necessitating arbitration ([para 21]).

Costs Award

The examination of the costs awarded revealed significant deviation from established norms in labor matters. The Constitutional Court articulated that costs in labour disputes should not automatically follow the outcome of the case. Instead, costs should be determined based on fairness and justice, taking into account the parties' conduct and whether the matter was appropriately brought before the court ([para 24]). The lack of reasoning behind the costs awarded against Dr. Zungu indicated that the Labour Court and Labour Appeal Court had not exercised their discretion judiciously, potentially preferring adherence to traditional cost norms over fairness ([para 26]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court made several important rulings:

  1. Leave to appeal regarding the merits of the disputes was denied, affirming the Labour Appeal Court's decision that it lacked jurisdiction over Dr. Zungu’s claims ([para 27]).
  2. Leave to appeal concerning the costs orders was granted, with the Court upholding the appeal on costs and vacating the costs orders issued by both the Labour Court and Labour Appeal Court. Each party was ordered to bear their own costs ([paras 27–28]).

The implications of this ruling underscore the importance of adhering to principles of fairness and the nuances in legal standards applied to employment disputes, reflecting a judicial commitment to protecting workers' rights while clarifying the procedural avenues available for recourse in such matters.
