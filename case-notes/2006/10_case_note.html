Constitutional Court 2006 CCT 62/05

Case Note: David Dikoko v Thupi Zacharia Mokhatla (CCT 62/05)

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History
The case arises from statements made by David Dikoko, the Executive Mayor of the Southern District Municipality, during his appearance before the North West Provincial Public Accounts Standing Committee (the Standing Committee). These statements were made in response to inquiries about his overdue cell phone account which had reached R21,000, causing dissatisfaction among the Provincial Auditor-General (PAG) ([para 1]–[2]). Dikoko accused Thupi Mokhatla, the Municipal CEO, of manipulating accounting procedures to facilitate political attacks on him. As a result, Mokhatla brought a defamation claim against Dikoko for damages ([para 6]–[7]).

Dikoko's defense claimed the statements enjoyed privilege under section 28 of the Municipal Structures Act, arguing that the Standing Committee was a legitimate venue for such comments. The High Court, however, ruled that these comments were not protected by privilege as they were made in the context of a provincial legislature hearing, not a council meeting ([paras 19]–[20]).

After the High Court found in favor of Mokhatla, awarding him R110,000 in damages ([para 20]), Dikoko sought leave to appeal, asserting that the privilege extended to statements made in the Standing Committee, and he also challenged the amount of the damages awarded ([para 21]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions

  1. Interpretation of Privilege: Did the High Court's interpretation of the privileges afforded to municipal councillors under sections 28 of the Municipal Structures Act and section 161 of the Constitution fail to recognize that Dikoko was conducting council business during the Standing Committee meeting? ([para 31]).
  2. Quantum of Damages: Was the quantum of damages awarded by the High Court excessive and irrational in light of the context of the statements made, and did it infringe on the principles of freedom of expression? ([para 24]).
  3. Constitutional Matters: Does this appeal raise a constitutional issue concerning the balance between freedom of expression and the right to dignity? ([para 30]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon
The decision hinges on several constitutional and legislative provisions:

  1. Constitution of South Africa:
  • Section 161: Provides for privileges extended to municipal councils and their members.
  • Section 117(1): Offers privilege and immunity in provincial legislatures ([paras 9], [12]).
  1. Local Government: Municipal Structures Act:
  • Section 28(1): Grants councillors freedom of speech within council meetings and immunity from civil or criminal proceedings for statements made therein ([para 10]).
  1. Precedents:
  • The Court referenced "Swartbooi and Others v Brink" to elucidate the scope of privilege and "Poovalingam v Rajbansi" to explore the conditions under which parliamentary privilege is applicable ([paras 34], [36]).
  • Review of Canadian cases addressing the extension of parliamentary privilege outside legislative confines ([paras 35], [36]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law
The Court assessed whether Dikoko’s statements fit within the scope of municipal councillor privileges provided in the Constitution and the Municipal Structures Act. It concluded that the statements made during the Standing Committee did not pertain to the council's business, as they were primarily concerned with Dikoko's personal financial accountability, disqualifying them from immunity under section 28 ([paras 31], [40]).

On the issue of quantum, the Court engaged a two-pronged analysis addressing both the High Court's assessment methods and the thresholds required for defamation damages. Ultimately, it recognized that the High Court may have overlooked mitigating factors such as the nature and extent of publication, leading to an unreasonably high damage award ([pars 51–59]). The Court put significant weight on the need for damages to be proportionate to the impact of the defamation, balancing compensatory needs against discouragement of damaging speech ([paras 90]–[107]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications
The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the appeal regarding the defamation finding but partially upheld Dikoko's claim concerning the damages award. It emphasized that while democratic governance should encourage free speech, this must align with accountability norms, particularly for public officials ([para 82]–[83]).

The Court adjusted the award to R50,000, asserting that damages in defamation cases must reflect the core principles enshrined in the Constitution: dignity and freedom of expression—suggesting future focus on restorative justice through apologies and reconciliatory measures in defamation cases rather than solely on monetary compensation ([paras 63], [106]). The decision sets a significant precedent for clarifying the limits of governmental privilege while highlighting the balance between freedom of expression and protection against defamation in a constitutional democracy.
