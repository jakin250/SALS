Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA

# FIRAC Case Note: Quartermark Investments (Pty) Ltd v Mkhwanazi & Another (768/2012)

**Case Title:** Quartermark Investments (Pty) Ltd v Mkhwanazi & Another  
**Neutral Citation:** [2013] ZASCA 150  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Judges:** Maya, Bosielo, Theron, Pillay, and Petse JJA  
**Date Heard:** 20 August 2013  
**Date Delivered:** 1 November 2013  
**Order:** The appeal is dismissed with costs.

---

## F – Facts

The case involves an appeal from a ruling made by the South Gauteng High Court (Spilg J) concerning a sale of property induced by fraud ([1]). The first respondent, Pinky Mkhwanazi, contested the legitimacy of the sale and lease agreements she entered into with Quartermark Investments (Pty) Ltd, a property investment company. Mkhwanazi claimed that she was fraudulently induced into signing documents that she believed were loan agreements ([1]), without understanding they pertained to the sale and lease of her immovable property, Erf 1795 Klipfontein ([2]).

In 2004, Mkhwanazi purchased the property with a loan secured through Nedbank and subsequently defaulted, leading to a judicial attachment of her property ([2]). In 2007, seeking financial assistance, she approached Quartermark’s agent, George Mthebe, for a loan of R30,000 ([3]). Mthebe urged her to sign various documents under the pretense of addressing her financial troubles, without giving her the chance to read them. Mkhwanazi later discovered that she had signed a sale agreement, a lease agreement, and a power of attorney, effectively transferring ownership of her home to Quartermark ([6]).

Mkhwanazi's suspicion was raised upon receiving utility bills in Quartermark's name and being informed by Inspector Ngobeni and Mr. Mutayi that her transactions with Quartermark might involve fraud ([5]-[6]). The agreements were procedurally contested in the high court, where Mkhwanazi sought to have the transfer of property nullified and declared void along with the sale agreements ([1]). The court ruled in her favour, leading to Quartermark’s appeal.

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal were:
1. Whether Mkhwanazi established a case for fraudulent misrepresentation sufficient to annul the sale and lease agreements ([12]).
2. Whether the high court's directive to transfer the property back to Mkhwanazi was appropriate, particularly in light of her failure to tender restoration of the benefits derived from the agreements ([12]).

## R – Rules

The legal framework of the case primarily pertains to contract law and the doctrine of fraudulent misrepresentation. The essential elements required to establish a case of fraudulent misrepresentation include:
- The existence of a representation.
- The representation must concern a fact.
- It must be false and material.
- It must be intended to induce the party to enter into the contract ([14]).

Additionally, the court considered principles related to restitution, particularly the doctrine of *restitutio in integrum*, which aims to restore individuals to their original state before a contract was made ([18]). The rei vindicatio, which allows a party to reclaim property without the obligation to restore any benefits received, was also relevant ([26]).

## A – Application

The court began its analysis by affirmatively recognizing that Mkhwanazi presented sufficient evidence of fraudulent misrepresentation. The absence of contradicting evidence from Quartermark, particularly from Mr. Mthebe, who failed to testify, compounded Mkhwanazi’s claims ([16]). Also, it was established that Mkhwanazi was misled into believing the documentation was related to a loan, not a sale—which was a significant factor in establishing the fraudulent nature of the inducement ([17]).

With respect to the high court's remedial measures, the Supreme Court agreed that Mkhwanazi was entitled to *restitutio in integrum*, despite the lack of a tender from her to restore any alleged benefit ([18]-[19]). The rationale was that she did not voluntarily transfer ownership of the property; her consent was procured under fraudulent circumstances and, therefore, the ownership had not legally transferred ([25]). The application of the rei vindicatio was also relevant. The court noted that ownership under South African law does not necessarily pass with registration if fraud taints the underlying contractual agreement ([24]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the high court’s decision, affirming that Mkhwanazi was the rightful owner of the property due to the fraudulent circumstances surrounding the agreement. The appeal by Quartermark was dismissed, establishing that even in the absence of a tender to restore the benefits received, Mkhwanazi was entitled to reclaim her property on the grounds of the unjust nature of the agreements made under duress ([28]). This case highlights the court’s commitment to equity and justice in cases of contractual fraud, underscoring the importance of safeguarding property rights against fraudulent practices.

--- 

This case sets a vital precedent in South African law regarding the principles of fraudulent misrepresentation and the ownership of immovable property, providing significant legal clarity on how ownership can be reclaimed in similar circumstances.
