# Case Note: Association of Mineworkers and Construction Union and Others v Royal Bafokeng Platinum Limited and Others [2020] ZACC 1 (CCT 181/18)

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–56])

The case revolves around the legality of sections 23(1)(d) and 189(1) of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995, which addresses consultation obligations during retrenchments. The first applicant, the Association of Mineworkers and Construction Union (AMCU), along with individual miners as the second to further applicants, challenged the Royal Bafokeng Platinum Limited's (RBPlat) retrenchment process. AMCU argued that its members were denied the right to be consulted in the retrenchment process because the employer had previously entered a collective agreement that only recognized the National Union of Mineworkers (NUM) and UASA (the union representing some non-AMCU members) as parties to the consultation.

AMCU contended that this exclusion violated their constitutional rights, particularly the right to fair labour practices as described in section 23(1) of the Constitution. The Labour Appeal Court initially ruled against AMCU, prompting the current appeal to the Constitutional Court, which examined whether the statutory framework established an obligation for individual consultation beyond that provided in the collective agreement ([paras 1–10][11]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 11–15])

The primary legal issues addressed in the case include:

1. **Does section 189(1) of the Labour Relations Act adequately ensure the right to fair consultation for individual employees during retrenchments?** 
2. **Does the principle of majoritarianism as interpreted from previous case law apply in the context of retrenchments?**
3. **Are the rights enumerated in the applicants' challenges—namely the right to fair labour practices, equality, and freedom of association—unreasonably limited under the framework established by section 189(1) of the LRA?** ([paras 12-14])

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 16–40])

1. **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa** – Section 23(1), which grants everyone the right to fair labour practices, was central to the applicants' argument.

2. **Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995** – Specifically:
    - **Section 189** detailing employers' consultation obligations prior to dismissals due to operational requirements, indicating the hierarchical order of consultation and the absence of an explicit requirement for individual consultations beyond what is defined by agreements.
    - **Section 23(1)(d)**, which pertains to the extension of collective agreements to unrepresented workers.

3. **Case law** – Key precedents such as *AMCU v Chamber of Mines* ([2017] ZACC 3) established foundational interpretations of majoritarianism pertaining to membership exclusion and consultation processes. Other notable cases included *International Labour Organisation* (ILO) principles which guided the court's understanding of the principles of fair labour practice internationally ([paras 16–22][23]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 41–127])

In its analysis, the Constitutional Court examined the intricacies of the Labour Relations Act, particularly focusing on the nature and purpose of section 189(1) alongside the rightful expectations of fair labour practices embodied in the Constitution.

1. **Interpretation of Section 189(1)** – The court affirmed that, while section 189 did not impose a duty for individual consultation, it did require consultation with trade union representatives as a means to represent collective interests. This was viewed within the legislative framework aimed at achieving labour peace through collective agreements ([paras 41-45]).

2. **Majoritarian Principle** – The court identified that majoritarianism, which is core to collective bargaining processes, should not be conflated with individual rights to consultation. The court highlighted that compliance with the procedural framework established under section 189(1) does not guarantee outcomes consistent with fairness if the collective representative acts against the interests of non-members ([paras 46–56]).

3. **Procedural Fairness** – The court also emphasized that procedural fairness under section 189 consisted of meeting established criteria and obligations, and not merely satisfying subjective notions of individual consultation ([paras 57–70]). The obligation to ensure collective representation was articulated as essential to facilitating the negotiated outcomes of any retrenchment, minimizing the ambiguities that individual consultations could introduce.

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 128–132])

The Constitutional Court ultimately upheld the Labour Appeal Court's conclusion, ruling that the statutory framework under section 189(1) does not confer individual consultation rights during retrenchment, as the provision was designed to operate within a majoritarian framework ([para 128]). The court determined that such a limitation on rights under the Labour Relations Act is rationally connected to promoting collective workplace representation, thus maintaining that there were no unjustifiable infringements of the rights to fair labour practices, equality, or freedom of association as claimed by AMCU.

Consequently, the court dismissed the appeal, affirming that the existing legislative provisions were constitutionally valid, and underscoring the necessity of collective bargaining principles ([paras 131-132]). This ruling reinforces the interpretation of majoritarianism in South African labour relations law and delineates clear boundaries for individual rights in the context of collective agreements, contributing significantly to the landscape of labour law in South Africa.
