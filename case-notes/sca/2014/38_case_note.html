Supreme Court of Appeal

FIRAC Method for Dean Gillian Rees v Investec Bank Limited

Investec sought claims exceeding R34 million, based on agreements involving 12 principal debtors, primarily corporations, with the appellants acting as sureties.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2014

F – Facts

This appeal arises from the South Gauteng High Court's ruling delivered by Hutton AJ on 5 March 2013. The proceedings involved a combined summons issued by Investec Bank Limited (Investec), encompassing 14 claims primarily against the first appellant, Dean Gillian Rees, and the second appellant, Edward Christopher Jowitt, alongside Benjamin Henry Jowitt in his capacity as trustee of the Aljebami Trust ([1]–[2]).

Investec sought claims exceeding R34 million, based on agreements involving 12 principal debtors, primarily corporations, with the appellants acting as sureties. Following defaults and winding-up of several principal debtors, the appellants notified their intention to defend the action, leading Investec to institute summary judgment proceedings against them ([3]–[4]).

The key affidavit in support of the summary judgment was deposed by Ms. Mirielle Ackermann, a Recoveries Officer at Investec. She asserted personal knowledge sufficient to verify the claims based on her access to the bank’s records. In rebuttal, Mr. Rees countered that Ackermann's knowledge was exclusively derived from documents, lacking the necessary personal knowledge to substantiate the claims ([5]–[6]).

Hutton AJ eventually granted summary judgment for 13 of the 14 claims, emphasizing that the affiant, although not present during the creation of the surety agreements, possessed adequate knowledge by virtue of her role ([7]–[8]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions arising for consideration were:

  1. Whether Ms. Ackermann, as the deponent of the affidavit supporting the summary judgment, had sufficient personal knowledge to satisfy the requirements of Rule 32(2) of the Uniform Rules of Court.
  2. Whether the summary judgment was improperly granted given the assertions regarding the bona fides of the appellants' defenses ([4]–[8]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal framework is encapsulated in Rule 32(2) of the Uniform Rules, which requires that a party seeking summary judgment must file an affidavit made by the plaintiff or someone who can “swear positively to the facts” asserting the cause of action and the claimed amounts, as well as stating there is no bona fide defense ([9–10]).

Key case law considered included:

  • Maharaj v Barclays National Bank Ltd 1976 (1) SA 418 (A), establishing the requirement for personal knowledge in affidavits supporting summary judgments.
  • Barclays National Bank Ltd v Love 1975 (2) SA 514 (D) which indicates that a bank manager's positional authority may suggest a level of knowledge appropriate to support summary judgment.
  • Subsequent cases affirming that first-hand knowledge of all facts is not always necessary, particularly for corporate representatives acting within their duties ([10]–[12]).

A – Application

The court assessed whether Ackermann's role as Recoveries Officer endowed her with sufficient knowledge to affirm the claims made in Investec's particulars of claim. It reasoned that her access to records, correspondence, and prior interactions with the appellants' legal representatives substantiated her claims about their financial standing and the amounts claimed ([14]).

While Mr. Rees argued that Ackermann could not swear positively to the facts since she derived her understanding solely from documents, the court held that direct personal knowledge, in the sense of having witnessed every transaction, was not obligatory. Instead, obtained knowledge from engagement in routine duties qualified as sufficient under the Rule 32(2) standard ([13]–[15]).

Furthermore, the court scrutinized the appellants' challenges to the financial records shared by Investec and determined that Mr. Rees' assertions regarding their lack of a bona fide defense were unsubstantiated, as he failed to demonstrate the readiness to raise legitimate defenses once proper discovery was achieved ([16]–[22]).

The court also noted that the high threshold for granting summary judgment was justifiable when the evidence was consistent and pointed towards the appellants' defaults in the agreements ([23]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the South Gauteng High Court's imposition of summary judgment against the appellants was justified based on a thorough review of the evidence presented. The court upheld Hutton AJ’s ruling, noting that the affidavit of Ackermann met the legal requisites by virtue of her comprehensive understanding of the bank’s records in her capacity as Recoveries Officer ([24]).

The appeal was thus dismissed, with costs awarded in favor of Investec, reinforcing the principle that procedural rigors around summary judgments serve to balance creditors’ rights with defendants’ rights to contest claims ([25]).

This case underscores the evolving judicial interpretation of Rule 32(2) and the nuanced understanding of personal knowledge within corporate contexts, affirming that banks and corporate entities can rely on employees possessing relevant record access to substantiate claims in legal proceedings.
