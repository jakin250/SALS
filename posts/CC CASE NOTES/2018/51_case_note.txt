Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: Law Society of South Africa and Others v President of the Republic of South Africa and Others [2018] ZACC 51

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The case arises from an application initiated by the **Law Society of South Africa** and six landowners from Zimbabwe. They challenged the President's conduct regarding the suspension of the Southern African Development Community (SADC) Tribunal and the signing of the 2014 Protocol, which they argued curtailed the Tribunal’s jurisdiction over individual disputes with member states. The applicants, citing various constitutional violations, sought an order declaring the President’s actions unconstitutional, unlawful, and irrational ([1-7]).

The High Court ruled on March 1, 2018, declaring the President’s actions, including the suspension of the Tribunal and the signing of the Protocol, unconstitutional and invalid. The court directed that the President withdraw his signature from the Protocol ([1, 18-19]). The matter was subsequently brought before the **Constitutional Court of South Africa**, which confirmed the High Court's order and addressed the seriousness of the issues concerning access to justice for individuals facing human rights violations in Zimbabwe ([1, 41-45]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues raised in the case were:
1. Whether the President’s participation in the decision to suspend the operations of the SADC Tribunal was unconstitutional and unlawful.
2. Whether the signing of the 2014 Protocol by the President was irrational, unconstitutional, and violated South Africa’s obligations under international law, particularly concerning access to justice for individuals ([1, 17, 47-49, 60-61]).

The court also addressed the issue of "prematurity," questioning whether the application could be brought before the court before the Protocol was ratified by the necessary parliamentary processes ([21-23]).

### R – Rules
The Constitutional Court relied on several constitutional provisions, notably:
- **Sections 7(1)-(2)** and **8(1)** of the Constitution, which obligate the State to respect, protect, promote, and fulfill the rights in the Bill of Rights, particularly in relation to access to justice.
- **Section 231 of the Constitution**, which regards the negotiation and signing of international agreements and establishes the role of Parliament in ratifying treaties ([72-83]).
- **Articles 18 and 26 of the Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties**, which impose obligations on states to refrain from acts that would defeat the object and purpose of a treaty they have signed ([35-43]).

The court also addressed precedential authority, including the principles from related cases that speak to the legality of executive power, including the need for laws to be exercised rationally and with good faith ([47-48]).

### A – Application
In applying the law to the facts, the court emphasized the seriousness of the President’s signature on the Protocol, indicating that such an action had significant implications on the rights of individuals seeking justice in the SADC Tribunal. The majority opinion stated that the President’s conduct effectively stripped South Africans and other SADC citizens of their access to a crucial judicial mechanism for addressing human rights violations ([60-61, 72-81]).

The court considered the implications of the constitutional obligations under Section 7 and Section 8, emphasizing that the President's signature was not merely a procedural act but an expression of South Africa’s commitment to uphold international law and the principles enshrined in the Constitution ([76-83]). The court also highlighted the irrationality of the President’s actions, noting that the amendment procedures outlined in the original Treaty were not followed, making his actions ultra vires the powers afforded to him under the Constitution ([46-72]).

Regarding the prematurity argument, the court concluded that the potential for a violation of citizens' rights warranted immediate judicial intervention, as waiting for the full legislative process might allow irreversible harm to occur to the individuals affected ([21-34]).

### C – Conclusion
The **Constitutional Court of South Africa** ultimately reaffirmed the High Court's ruling, categorizing the President’s actions as unconstitutional, unlawful, and irrational. The court declared that the President was directed to withdraw his signature from the 2014 Protocol, thereby confirming the legal principle that executive actions must comply with constitutional provisions concerning the protection and fulfillment of citizens' rights. However, the court denied costs to the amici curiae who participated in the case, emphasizing the responsibilities of parties involved ([1-3, 93-96]).

This case is significant as it underscores the essential role of the judiciary in enforcing constitutional rights against executive overreach and affirms South Africa’s commitment to the rule of law and human rights in the regional context. It also reinforces the imperative that executive actions remain within the strict bounds of constitutional authority, linking the legitimacy of international agreements to adherence to domestically recognized rights ([97]).
