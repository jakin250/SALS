# Case Note: Thepanyega N O and Others v Letsoalo and Others ([2022] ZASCA 30)

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The case concerns an appeal before the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa involving the trustees of the Madibeng Letupi Community Trust (the Trust) as appellants against Herman Letsoalo and others as respondents ([1]). The Trust is the registered owner of Portion 6 of the Farm Kalkfontein in Limpopo Province ([2]). For several years, the respondents had grazed livestock on the Trust's property under a verbal agreement, which required payment of grazing fees. However, the respondents allegedly failed to pay the agreed fees ([2]).

In seeking enforcement of this agreement, the appellants initiated eviction proceedings against the respondents' livestock in the Magistrate's Court of Molemole. The magistrate initially granted the eviction and an interdict against further grazing on the property ([2]). Subsequently, the respondents appealed to the Limpopo Division of the High Court, which set aside the magistrate's order, ruling that the appellants had not demonstrably cancelled the grazing agreement prior to filing for eviction ([3]). This appeal followed, with the key factual backdrop being that the respondents had been allowed to graze since 2012 and that the appellants did not effectively terminate the respondents' rights before attempting to evict them ([4], [5]).

### I – Issues
The principal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was the legal question concerning whether the respondents had a legitimate right to graze their livestock on the Trust's property and whether that right had been unequivocally terminated by the appellants prior to the initiation of eviction proceedings ([4]).

Another consequential legal issue revolved around the necessity for a clear termination of rights in possession claims, particularly whether an owner must prove that any prior contractual claim by another party had been cancelled before seeking possession through eviction or interdict ([6]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework governing this case includes established principles of contract law and possession claims in the context of property law. The High Court previously recognized the existence of a verbal agreement granting the respondents grazing rights, which must be unequivocally cancelled before any eviction or interdict can be enforced ([3], [6]).

The case draws on precedents, notably *Morkel v Thornhill* and *Chetty v Naidoo*, which underscore the requirement that the termination of any prior possessory rights must be communicated clearly and unequivocally for an eviction claim to succeed ([9]). In *Chetty v Naidoo*, it was established that if a defendant denies the existence of a rightful claim but does not assert a direct right, the burden of proving the termination of that right lies with the plaintiff ([9]). 

### A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal applied the aforementioned legal principles to evaluate the facts of the case. It emphasized that, at the time of the eviction proceedings, there was no evidence presented by the appellants demonstrating the unilateral cancellation of the grazing agreement ([6],[7]). The court noted that the appellants' claim of having a valid agreement was undermined by their inaction over the previous four years during which they failed to seek payment for grazing fees or to formally cancel the agreement ([7],[8]).

Also crucial to the Court's analysis was the finding that a tacit agreement allowing the respondents to graze their livestock continued to exist, which meant their usage of the land was not unlawful ([8]). 

The court rejected the proposition advanced by the appellants that the respondents’ agreement was void on the grounds of not contracting with the trustees, as it was evident that the trustees had recognized the agreement by attempting to enforce payment of grazing fees ([10]).

### C – Conclusion
In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming the High Court's ruling. The court held that the appellants could not prove the necessary cancellation of the grazing rights before seeking eviction, thus validating the respondents' claim to graze their livestock under the existing agreement ([12]). 

This judgment reinforces key principles in property law regarding the necessity of clear communication when terminating contractual rights and emphasizes the legal rights of parties arising from agreements, highlighting the importance of due process in eviction proceedings. The implications of this case may influence how contractual agreements are managed and enforced in similar contexts, ensuring clarity in the termination of rights and the necessity for property owners to take prompt action to protect their interests. 

The case serves as a reminder of the judicial expectation that claims must be substantiated with a clear evidentiary basis, as well as the importance of recognizing tacit agreements in property law.
