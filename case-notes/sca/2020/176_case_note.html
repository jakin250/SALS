Thobejane and Others v Premier of Limpopo Province and Another (Case No: 1108/2019) [2020] ZASCA 176

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 176

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–14])

This case arises from an application for judicial review filed by the first to fifth appellants, who sought to challenge the decision of the first and second respondents (the Premier of Limpopo and the MEC for Traditional Affairs of Limpopo, respectively) not to recognize them as traditional leaders of the Tjatje Community in Limpopo. The appellants' application included a request for an order compelling recognition as traditional leaders, with the first appellant claiming the title of Khoshigadi (Chieftainess), while the second to fourth appellants aspired to be recognized as headmen, and the fifth as a headwoman ([1]).

In response, the respondents asserted a preliminary point of non-joinder, arguing that the appellants had failed to join two parties with a direct and substantial interest in the proceedings: the Commission on Traditional Leadership Disputes and Claims of the Limpopo Provincial Committee (the Commission), which investigated traditional leadership disputes in the community, and the Marota-Mohlaletsi Traditional Council ([1]–[2]).

The Limpopo High Court initially dismissed the respondents' preliminary point regarding non-joinder ([3]). However, in a subsequent judgment delivered on 17 May 2019, the same judge revisited this issue without explanation, ultimately ruling in favor of the respondents on the non-joinder question. Thereafter, she struck the appellants' application from the roll and granted leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ([4]).

The SCA had to determine whether the high court's decision of 24 April 2019 (dismissal of the preliminary point) was final and whether the high court possessed the authority to revisit that order when it issued the May judgment ([5]–[6]). Ultimately, the SCA identified that the high court erred in revisiting the previous ruling and thus deemed its May order a nullity ([7]).

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 5–10])

The central issues are twofold:

  1. Whether the high court's ruling on the preliminary point on 24 April 2019 was final in nature and whether it could subsequently be altered or revisited by the same court ([5]–[6]). This question encompasses whether the high court acted competently in determining that its previous order was subject to alteration, which inherently ties to the principles of finality in legal proceedings.
  2. Whether the Supreme Court of Appeal possessed jurisdiction to assess the merits of the case given that the high court had not yet rendered a judgment on the substantive issues ([8]–[9]). This issue addresses whether judicial review requires intrinsic substantive findings before an appellate court can act on it.

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 5–9])

The key legal principles and precedents cited in the judgment include:

  1. Final Orders: The SCA reiterated the three attributes of a final order as established in *Zweni v Minister of Law and Order*—the order must (1) be final in effect, (2) be definitive of the parties' rights, and (3) dispose of a substantial portion of the relief claimed ([5]). The application of this standard shows that the high court's dismissal of the preliminary point met these criteria.
  2. Functus Officio Doctrine: Invoking the concept of functus officio, the court highlighted that a court cannot alter its own final order, underscoring the essential principle of finality of litigation ([6]).
  3. Jurisdictional Limitations: The SCA referenced prior rulings to affirm that it cannot assume jurisdiction over substantive matters if the lower court has not ruled on them, thereby maintaining judicial hierarchy and respect for the trial court's role ([9]).
  4. Substitution Orders: The court relied on Trencon Construction (Pty) Limited v Industrial Development Corporation of South Africa Limited and other related jurisprudence to underscore the stringent criteria for substitution orders. It noted that such orders must only be made in exceptional circumstances and after thorough examination of all factors ([10]).

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 10–12])

The SCA applied the legal rules to the case at hand with careful consideration. It confirmed that the high court's ruling on 24 April 2019 constituted a final order, thereby asserting that the high court was enigmatic in its authority to revisit it without clear justification ([6]). The SCA reiterated the paramount nature of finality in judicial decisions.

Moreover, when addressing the jurisdictional aspect, the SCA thoroughly examined whether it was appropriate to take on the merits of the case. It concluded that since the high court had not made substantive findings, the SCA could not step into the role of the high court without breaching procedural propriety ([9]). The appeal hence was upheld primarily on the basis that the respondents had failed in their legal duty to support the high court's ruling, thereby establishing the need for the matter to be remitted for a proper hearing on the merits ([12]).

In its discussions surrounding the possibility of a substitution order, the SCA found it lacking in the context of exceptional circumstances as required by law. The potential implications of substitution on existing community leadership were considered significant enough to necessitate further inquiry in the lower court ([11]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 12–15])

In conclusion, the SCA upheld the appeal and set aside the order of the Limpopo High Court dated 17 May 2019, ruling that the matter should be remitted to the high court for a determination of the merits of the review application ([15]). The ratio decidendi centered on the principles of finality in judicial determinations and procedural propriety, reaffirming the necessity for a reasoned approach to judicial review rather than unsubstantiated revisitations.

The implications of this ruling are noteworthy. The SCA has reaffirmed the importance of adhering to procedural norms in judicial reviews, ensuring the integrity of settlements of rights arising from traditional leadership disputes. This decision may serve as precedent for future disputes involving traditional leadership recognition, emphasizing the need for comprehensive procedural adherence before adjudication of substantive matters. Moreover, it highlights the delicate balance between promoting local governance through traditional structures and ensuring that such processes are undertaken in alignment with statutory frameworks and judicial oversight.
