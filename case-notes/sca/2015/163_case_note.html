Supreme Court of Appeal

The South African Dental Association v Minister of Health (20556/2014) [2015] ZASCA 163

Many dental assistants are disproportionately composed of individuals from previously disadvantaged backgrounds, and since 1995, the Dental Assistants Association of South Africa (DAASA) has advocated for their recognition and regulation ([5]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 [2015] ZASCA 163

F – Facts

The appellant, the South African Dental Association (SADA), a significant representative body of dentists in South Africa, sought to contest regulations enacted by the first respondent, the Minister of Health. These regulations recognized dental assistants as health professionals, outlined qualifications for their registration, and defined the scope of their duties as professionals ([1]–[2]).

Many dental assistants are disproportionately composed of individuals from previously disadvantaged backgrounds, and since 1995, the Dental Assistants Association of South Africa (DAASA) has advocated for their recognition and regulation ([5]). SADA contended that these regulations could lead to a workforce shortage, as a significant number of current dental assistants would lose their ability to practice due to the new qualification requirements, a view that SADA asserted undermined the welfare of both dental assistants and the public ([2], [12]).

The High Court dismissed SADA's application to set aside the regulations, leading to the present appeal ([4]). Important procedural aspects included the court's assertion that SADA's challenge was largely time-barred under section 7(1) of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA) 3 of 2000, which imposes a strict 180-day limit on judicial review claims ([38]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues presented were:

  1. Whether SADA's application to challenge the regulations was time-barred as per the time limits stipulated in PAJA ([38]).
  2. Whether the Minister acted beyond his statutory powers in enacting the regulations ([12], [38]).
  3. The validity of the regulations concerning their compliance with the principle of legality and procedural fairness ([39]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal framework includes:

  1. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) - Section 7(1) mandates the judicial review application be instituted within 180 days after the applicant became aware of the administrative action or the reasons for it.
  2. Health Professions Act 56 of 1974 - Sections 15(1) and 33 outline the Minister's authority to establish professional boards and define the scope of regulated health professions ([47],[51]).
  3. Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996 - The right to administrative action that is lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair (s 33).

Court precedents such as Minister of Health & another NO v New Clicks SA (Pty) Ltd & others drew parallels between ministerial actions regarding regulations and administrative actions under PAJA, emphasizing the necessity for compliance with PAJA when governmental ministers enact regulations ([41]).

A – Application

In its analysis, the court underscored that SADA’s challenge to the regulations, except for those defining the scope of dental assistants, was time-barred as it was not filed within the statutory period prescribed by PAJA ([38],[40]). The court did consider SADA's arguments around the legality of the regulations, particularly focusing on whether the Minister had acted ultra vires.

The court found that SADA’s position that the establishment of a register for dental assistants was a precondition for defining their scope of work was flawed. It reasoned that registers and boards could be established concurrently, and the Act does not prescribe a strict sequential process ([53],[54]). Moreover, the dissent regarding consultations and the efficacy of the Minister's decision failed to demonstrate any substantial neglect of consultation duties ([57]).

The court also addressed the grave implications for dentist-dental assistant relationships, agreeing that while concerns about employment conditions and potential criminal liabilities were valid, adequate measures were taken by the Minister and the Health Professions Council to mitigate these risks ([58]).

Thus, the court concluded that there was no substantial ground for challenging the legality of the Scope Regulations after addressing the necessary steps taken by the Minister to engage with stakeholders ([57]–[58]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed SADA's appeal, emphasizing the necessity of adhering to procedural constraints outlined in PAJA ([60]). Furthermore, it highlighted the importance of legislative and regulatory frameworks in the evolving landscape of healthcare professions, advocating for a structured and fair recognition of all stakeholders involved ([60]). The decision underscored the regulatory authority vested in the Minister, aligning with broader public health goals as articulated within the statutes governing health professions.

The ruling also serves as a reminder for professional associations of the importance of timely and coherent engagement in regulatory processes to protect their interests effectively. The acknowledgment of the various roles played by dental assistants in the healthcare ecosystem was deemed vital, reiterating the need for inclusive dialogue in the formulation of health regulations. The case elucidates the complexities of transitioning from informal to regulated professions while balancing the interests of existing practitioners and vulnerable workforce segments.
