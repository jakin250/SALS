Supreme Court of Appeal

Minister of Agriculture, Land Reform and Rural Development and Others v Ndumo (obo Emdwebu Community)

The appeal concerns a land restitution claim under the Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994 (the "Act").

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 Minister of Agriculture, Land Reform and Rural Development and Others v Ndumo (obo Emdwebu Community) (Case No 577/2022) [2023] ZASCA 136 (19 October 2023)

Neutral Citation: Minister of Agriculture, Land Reform and Rural Development and Others v Ndumo (obo Emdwebu Community) (Case No 577/2022) [2023] ZASCA 136 (19 October 2023)

F – Facts

The appeal concerns a land restitution claim under the Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994 (the "Act"). Mr. Bongani Cyprian Ndumo, chairperson of the Emdwebu Community, lodged a claim for restitution on 31 December 1998 regarding land in KwaZulu-Natal, previously owned by his father, Nokhenke Ndumo. The claim was submitted to the Regional Land Claims Commissioner (RLCC) and noted as a family claim.

In 2017, the RLCC conducted an investigation, leading to a Rule 5 report classifying the claim as a community claim. This classification was later formalized when the RLCC published a notice in the Government Gazette on 14 February 2020 stating the claim was on behalf of the Emdwebu Community.

Members of the community signed a settlement offer for monetary compensation; however, the RLCC later reversed its earlier stance, stating it lacked the authority to convert the claim from a family to a community claim, particularly due to the absence of explicit indication in the original claim form that it was lodged on behalf of the community.

The Land Claims Court (LCC) initially ruled in favor of Ndumo, granting him a mandamus to compel the Commission to proceed with the settlement. The appellants appealed this ruling, particularly contesting whether the RLCC acted properly in condoning the conversion of the claim from a family to a community claim ([1]–[30]).

I – Issues

The primary issue on appeal is whether the RLCC can lawfully convert a claim for restitution of rights in land, lodged by an individual or family, into a community claim. Specifically, the appellate court sought to clarify if the actions of the second and fourth respondents (the Chief Land Claims Commissioner and the Regional Land Claims Commissioner, respectively) amounted to an implicit granting of condonation for such conversion without appropriate legal standing ([3], [8], [23]).

An additional consideration raised was whether the transformation of the claim aligns with the jurisdictional requirements established under sections 2, 10, and 11 of the Act, which dictate how claims should be lodged and processed ([15], [18]–[20]).

R – Rules

Three key provisions from the Restitution of Land Rights Act are central to this case:

  1. Section 2: Outlines the entitlement to restitution, requiring claims to relate to dispossession occurring after 19 June 1913 and to be lodged by a specified cut-off date ([15]).
  2. Section 10: Mandates that claimants must specify whether a claim is made on behalf of an individual, family, community, or trust, and requires supporting documentation to accompany claims lodged on behalf of a community ([16]).
  3. Section 11: Describes the procedure following lodge claims, where the RLCC must confirm that the claim meets specified jurisdictional criteria before publishing it in the Gazette ([17]).

The court also referenced existing case law, specifically Minaar N.O. v Regional Land Claims Commissioner: Mpumalanga, which set precedent regarding the limitations of a RLCC's authority to alter the nature of claims post-lodgement ([23]).

A – Application

The court meticulously analyzed the procedural history surrounding the claim. It determined that the original claim form submitted by Ndumo explicitly denoted the claim as a family claim, lacking any reference to a community. The supposed discretionary powers of the RLCC under section 11(2) to condone procedural anomalies were found insufficient to permit a modification from a family to a community claim, especially given that essential elements of the original claim remained unchanged after an extensive delay of 22 years ([4], [27]).

Moreover, the court articulated that Ndumo's later attempts to present his claim as a community claim were inherently contradictory. The representations made in a letter and an affidavit long after the submission of the original claim indicated a shift in intent rather than the clarity of purpose required at the time of lodging the claim. This belated attempt highlighted the absence of a clear legal basis to justify the RLCC's actions ([4], [27]). The RLCC’s gazetting of the claim under the incorrect designation was ruled deficient as these actions failed to comply with the procedural mandates of the Act as delineated in prior case law ([25]).

The appellate court concluded that the conversion of a family claim into a community claim did not meet the statutory requirements or validate its standing under the law, ultimately ruling against Ndumo’s position ([29]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the RLCC unlawfully converted the Ndumo family claim into a community claim, as the original claim form did not support such a classification. The conversion was not permissible under the provisions of the Restitution of Land Rights Act, particularly sections 2, 10, and 11, as there was no clear legal basis to condone the change.

Consequently, the appeal succeeded, overturning the initial LCC decision and substituting it with a dismissal of Ndumo's application with no order as to costs. This case reinforces the necessity of adherence to statutory procedures in the processing of land claims, and clarifies the limits of the RLCC's authority regarding claim classification ([30]).
