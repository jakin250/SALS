# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis 

### Constitutional Court of South Africa 
**Case Title:** Print Media South Africa and South African National Editors’ Forum v Minister of Home Affairs and Film and Publication Board (CCT 113/11) [2012] ZACC 22  
**Decision Date:** September 28, 2012  
**Judge:** Skweyiya J (Mogoeng CJ, Cameron J, Jafta J, Khampepe J, Nkabinde J, Maya AJ, and Zondo AJ concurring)  

## F – Facts 
The matter came before the Constitutional Court for confirmation of an order declared by the South Gauteng High Court, which found that specific provisions of the Films and Publications Act 65 of 1996 (the Act), as amended by the Films and Publications Amendment Act 3 of 2009, were constitutionally invalid ([1]). 

The applicants were Print Media South Africa (first applicant) and the South African National Editors’ Forum (second applicant), both representing diverse sectors of the print media industry ([2]–[3]). The first respondent was the Minister of Home Affairs, and the second respondent was the Film and Publication Board. The High Court ordered a declaration of unconstitutionality primarily focused on provisions that “require prior classification of publications depicting certain types of sexual conduct” ([17], [19]). 

The High Court determined that the provisions of the Act limited the right to freedom of expression and equality unconstitutionally ([17], [19]). The declarations concerned sections 16(1), 16(2)(a), and 24A(2)(a), fundamentally arguing the vagueness and overbreadth of the criteria within the Act ([18], [19]).

## I – Issues 
The main legal issues before the Constitutional Court included:
1. **Constitutionality of Administrative Prior Classification:** Whether the legislative provisions that mandate prior classification of publications unjustifiably limited the right to freedom of expression [17]–[19].
2. **Inequality in Regulatory Framework:** Whether the exclusion of magazines from the exemption afforded to newspapers created unconstitutional differentiation [25]–[26].
3. **Drafting Errors and Causal Absurdities:** Whether drafting errors within the provisions warranted a declaration of unconstitutionality [17]–[19].

## R – Rules 
In assessing the constitutionality of the contested provisions, significant legal rules and precedents were invoked:
1. **Freedom of Expression as a Core Constitutional Right:** Section 16 of the Constitution of South Africa recognizes the right to freedom of expression, which includes the press and media ([47]). Any limitation on this right must be justified as per section 36 of the Constitution ([58])—the limitations clause. 
2. **Prior Restraint Doctrine:** The doctrine of prior restraint mandates that expressions should not be suppressed before they have been published unless there is substantial justification for such restraint ([44]).
3. **Equality before the Law:** The Constitution's equality clause (section 9(1)) prohibits discrimination, establishing that any arbitrary differentiation in treatment (such as between newspapers and magazines) must be justified [79].

## A – Application 
### Constitutionality of Administrative Prior Classification
The Court examined whether the scheme for administrative prior classification under section 16(2)(a) of the Act unjustifiably infringed the freedom of expression. The provisions were criticized for being vague and overbroad, making it difficult for publishers to discern what constitutes a violation of the law ([42]–[46]). The implications of criminalizing the failure to submit a publication for classification, along with the lack of timely processing and clear criteria, imposed excessive burdens on freedom of expression ([60]).

### Inequality in the Regulatory Framework
The Court held that excluding magazines from protections given to newspapers was irrational, given the lack of significant differentiation between the two forms of media ([80]). This created an unconstitutional disparity of treatment that served no legitimate legislative purpose, violating the principle of equality before the law.

### Drafting Errors
The Court found that section 24A(2)(a), which criminalizes the distribution of unclassified publications, contained a drafting error referring erroneously to section 16(1) instead of section 16(2). The respondent’s concession regarding this error validated the applicants' claim ([85]).

## C – Conclusion 
The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court’s declarations of unconstitutional invalidity regarding:
- **Section 16(2)(a)**: Severed from the Act due to unjustifiable limitation on freedom of expression.
- **Sections 16(1), 16(2), and 24A(2)(a)**: Declared invalid for their exclusion of magazines from protections offered to newspapers, with instructions for "reading-in" provisions to correct unjust discrimination. 
- **Section 24A(2)**: The entirety of this section was rendered invalid due to its connection to the flawed section 16 [90].

The Court also provided for costs, emphasizing the applicants' success in a fundamental matter of constitutional rights [89]. The outcome reinforces the South African constitutional commitment to freedom of expression while holding that legislative attempts to regulate must meet strict constitutional scrutiny to avoid infringing this fundamental right. 

This judgment not only reaffirms the right to freedom of expression but also underscores the importance of legislative clarity, equality, and the necessity to protect against prior restraints on media, aligning with the historical context of censorship in South Africa.
