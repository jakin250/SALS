# Case Note: Government of the Republic of Zimbabwe v Fick & Others (657/11)

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The case arose from the ongoing dispute between the Government of the Republic of Zimbabwe (the appellant) and two former farmers, Louis Karel Fick and others (the respondents), who sought enforcement of a decision from the Tribunal of the Southern African Development Community (SADC) ([1]–[4]). The Tribunal had previously ordered Zimbabwe to pay the legal costs incurred by the respondents due to the confiscation of their farms under Zimbabwe's land reform policy, articulated in Section 16B of the Zimbabwe Constitution ([6]–[7]). Zimbabwe, however, failed to comply with this order, prompting the respondents to seek recognition and enforcement of the Tribunal's order at the North Gauteng High Court. 

The lower court, presided over by Rabie J, granted a default judgment in favor of the respondents after Zimbabwe declined to participate in the proceedings ([14]). Zimbabwe later contested this judgment, claiming sovereign immunity and arguing that the court lacked jurisdiction, leading to a series of applications for rescission ([3]–[5]).

### I – Issues
The key issues in this case were:
1. **Sovereign Immunity**: Did Zimbabwe enjoy sovereign immunity from civil proceedings in South Africa ([18])?
2. **Jurisdiction**: Was the SADC Tribunal competent to issue orders against Zimbabwe, considering its claims that the Tribunal's Protocol was not binding upon it ([30])?
3. **Enforcement of Foreign Judgments**: Could a South African court recognize and enforce a ruling from a foreign tribunal under South African law and the Foreign States Immunities Act 87 of 1981 ([22], [28])?
4. **Service of Process**: Was service of the application for enforcement properly conducted in accordance with the relevant statutory requirements ([23])?

### R – Rules
The rules applicable to this case included:
- **Foreign States Immunities Act 87 of 1981**: This Act governs the immunity from jurisdiction granted to foreign states in South Africa and includes provisions on the waiver of immunity ([20]).
- **Common Law Principles**: Under South African common law, foreign judgments can generally be recognized and enforced, provided that certain criteria are met, such as jurisdiction, finality, and compliance with public policy ([28]).
- **Treaty and Protocol on the Tribunal**: The SADC Treaty and the Protocol establishing the Tribunal lay out its jurisdiction and binding nature of decisions, including provisions for their enforcement ([43]).

### A – Application
The court found that Zimbabwe had waived its sovereign immunity by participating in the SADC proceedings and consequently was subject to the Tribunal's jurisdiction ([20], [30]). The court dismissed claims of improper service, asserting that the procedures followed aligned with both South African law and the obligations established by the SADC Treaty ([24]). 

Furthermore, the court applied common law principles regarding the recognition and enforcement of foreign judgments, confirming that the Tribunal's orders met the necessary legal criteria for enforcement in South African courts ([28], [29]). On the issue of jurisdiction, the court emphasized that Zimbabwe had previously accepted the Tribunal's authority by participating in the proceedings and nominating judges to the Tribunal, thus solidifying its commitment to the Tribunal's jurisdiction, despite its subsequent claims to the contrary ([31]), ([36]).

### C – Conclusion
The court ultimately dismissed Zimbabwe's appeal and upheld the lower court's order recognizing the SADC Tribunal's ruling as enforceable. The decision reiterated the binding nature of international treaty obligations upon the member states of SADC, notably that Zimbabwe's previous conduct constituted a waiver of any claims to immunity and validated the Tribunal's jurisdiction. The implications of this case affirm the precedence of regional integration initiatives and their enforcement in national legal systems, particularly under the auspices of international treaties ([49]). 

The court's ruling, thereby, serves as a critical affirmation of the effectiveness of regional tribunals and their orders in a member state, despite sovereignty claims, ultimately emphasizing compliance with international law and commitments ([50]).
