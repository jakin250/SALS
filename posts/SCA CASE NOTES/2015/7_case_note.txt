Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: Mkhize v Department of Correctional Services [2015] ZASCA 7

## F – Facts

The appellant, **Mr. Sithembiso Siphelele Mkhize**, was employed as a warder by the **Department of Correctional Services** (the Department). In January 2008, following a disciplinary inquiry—most of which Mr. Mkhize refused to attend—he was dismissed for allegedly bringing dagga (cannabis) into the Johannesburg Correctional Centre ([1]). The subsequent arbitration held by the **General Public Services Sectoral Bargaining Council** deemed the dismissal both procedurally and substantively fair ([1]).

Mr. Mkhize challenged this decision in the Labour Court, where Judge **Rabkin-Naicker** dismissed his review application on June 21, 2012 ([1]). The **Labour Appeal Court** subsequently denied leave to appeal on February 28, 2013 ([1]). This current appeal, heard on March 6, 2015, seeks to readdress arguments previously raised and introduces new evidence in the form of an affidavit from **Gilbert Sibiya**, another warder ([2]).

Mr. Sibiya’s affidavit admitted that he had lied during the inquiry due to personal animosity toward Mr. Mkhize. He alleged that a prisoner named **Zola** had planted the dagga in Mr. Mkhize’s bag, which had resulted in the investigation leading to Mr. Mkhize's suspension and dismissal ([2]).

Apart from introducing this new evidence, Mr. Mkhize's appeal also aimed to revisit prior arguments regarding the alleged procedural time bar and the arbitrator’s findings of fairness regarding his dismissal ([3]).

## I – Issues

The primary issue in this case revolves around whether Mr. Mkhize can introduce new evidence (the Sibiya affidavit) at the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) stage. Specifically, the court must consider the following legal questions:

1. **Whether the affidavit from Gilbert Sibiya should be admitted in light of its potential impact on the fairness of the original arbitration ([4])**.

2. **Whether the SCA has the authority to remit the case back to the General Public Services Sectoral Bargaining Council on the basis that the new evidence could indicate a miscarriage of justice ([5])**.

3. **Whether the merits of Mr. Mkhize’s arguments regarding procedural time bars and the determination of fairness in his dismissal warrant re-examination ([3])**.

## R – Rules

The legal framework governing this case includes provisions of the **Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA)**, focusing on unfair dismissal and arbitration processes. The SCA has established precedents encapsulated in cases such as *National Union of Mineworkers v Samancor Ltd* and *Herholdt v Nedbank Ltd* that guide the review of arbitration awards and the evidence admissibility ([3], footnote 1).

Section **22 of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959**, under which the SCA operated in this instance, was relevant to the powers of the court in addressing matters of appeal and remittal ([5]).

Additionally, guidelines about the admissibility of recanting witness testimony, as addressed in the cases of *R v Van Heerden* and *S v N*, are applicable, stressing the need for caution in reliance on such potentially unreliable evidence ([4]).

## A – Application

In assessing Mr. Sibiya's affidavit and its implications, the SCA acknowledged that, should the content of the affidavit prove credible, it could have materially affected the arbitration’s outcome ([4]). However, the court also emphasized that mere admission of the affidavit was insufficient; it required rigorous testing, including potential cross-examination of Sibiya and other witnesses ([5]).

The crucial factor was the principle that new evidence may necessitate a fresh determination, particularly when it raises the specter of a miscarriage of justice ([4]). The court's decision to remit the case back to the General Public Services Sectoral Bargaining Council reflects an understanding that the tribunal must reassess whether Mr. Mkhize’s dismissal was substantively unfair, taking into account the newly surfaced evidence.

It was further noted that the SCA could not entertain the procedural arguments regarding time limits, which had been previously canvassed and failed to meet the threshold for special consideration ([6]). The focus on substantive fairness took precedence as the court sought to ensure that justice was served based on all material facts.

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld Mr. Mkhize's appeal, setting aside the orders of the Labour Court and the Labour Appeal Court ([10]). The court remitted the issue of substantive unfairness back to the General Public Services Sectoral Bargaining Council to reassess in light of Sibiya's affidavit and the need for further evidence ([10]). 

The decision emphasizes the limited scope for judicial intervention in labour disputes but maintains that significant new evidence warrants reconsideration of previous judgments. The SCA's reluctance to issue costs orders against the Department showcased a broader commitment to fairness, seeing the potential for Mr. Mkhize's case not simply as legal transactions but humanitarian elements deserving scrutiny and deliberation on their merits ([9]). 

The implications of this ruling extend beyond Mkhize himself, serving as a precedent for future cases dealing with similar issues of evidentiary admission and the substantive evaluation of fairness in dismissal cases within the framework of South African labor law. 

This case underlines the judiciary's role in ensuring that justice is served, even when procedural norms might otherwise curtail the examination of contentious issues arising from prior decisions.
