# Case Note: Steenkamp v Edcon Limited [2019] ZACC 17

## F – Facts
The case before the Constitutional Court of South Africa involved an appeal by Karin Steenkamp and 1817 other applicants against Edcon Limited concerning their retrenchment proceedings governed by the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA). The applicants claimed that their dismissals were procedurally unfair and sought compensation under section 189A(13) of the LRA. The applicants' retrenchments occurred during a restructuring initiated by Edcon from 2013 to 2015, affecting approximately 3,000 employees across various categories ([1]–[5]). 

Initially, the Labour Court granted the applicants condonation for their late application, which was subsequently overturned by the Labour Appeal Court. Edcon argued that the condonation was improperly justified, as the delay ranged from 10 months to two and a half years, primarily attributing it to a previously pursued, but ultimately unsuccessful, legal strategy based on earlier judgments that had since been invalidated ([6]–[10], [12]–[20]).

## I – Issues
Several legal questions emerged from the proceedings:

1. Whether the Labour Appeal Court correctly overturned the Labour Court's decision to grant condonation for the late application under section 189A(13).
2. Whether compensation for procedural unfairness can be claimed as a self-standing remedy in the context of large-scale retrenchments as per section 189A(13)(d) of the LRA ([21]).

## R – Rules
The following legal authorities and previously established principles shaped the court’s understanding:

1. **Section 189A of the LRA**: Provides the framework for large-scale retrenchments and sets specific requirements for procedural fairness and consultation prior to dismissals ([6]–[9]).
2. **Condonation Principles**: The LRA allows for the Labour Court to grant condonation on good cause shown for any failure to comply with time limits set under section 189A(17). The courts have established that granting or refusing condonation is a judicial discretion that aims to uphold justice while considering the circumstances of each case ([26]–[30]).
3. **Remedial Hierarchy**: Section 189A(13) introduces a strict hierarchy of remedies, concluding that paragraph (d) compensation is not a stand-alone remedy but rather applicable only when the other forms of relief under paragraphs (a)-(c) are inappropriate ([60]–[64]).

## A – Application
In a unanimous judgment authored by Basson AJ, the Constitutional Court examined the reasoning behind the Labour Court's condonation grant and the Labour Appeal Court's overturning of that decision. The Constitutionality of access to justice, especially in relation to the right to fair labor practices encapsulated in section 23 of the Constitution, was pivotal ([22]-[23]).

The Constitutional Court found that while the Labour Court held valid concerns about the applicants' reliance on previous legal strategies, this did not constitute an adequate explanation for the lengthy delays in filing their application. The court reinforced that the purpose of section 189A(13) is to ensure the expedient resolution of disputes arising from retrenchments and cautioned against prolonged delays, which undermine the overarching objectives of the LRA ([25]–[56]).

The court rejected the argument that compensation under section 189A(13)(d) could be sought independently, emphasizing that it is contingent on the prior examination of the other remedies. Thus, the immediate intake and consideration of procedural issues must occur as close as possible to the retrenchment process, ensuring that the consultation's integrity is preserved ([58]–[76]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately upheld the Labour Appeal Court's decision, dismissing the appeal and reaffirming that the Labour Court had erred in its discretion when granting the late condonation application. The ruling illuminated critical aspects of labor law regarding procedural fairness and the strict, obligatory timelines necessitated by the LRA. No order as to costs was made, reflecting the court's recognition of the significant legal questions raised by the matter ([78]–[79]).

This decision underscores the importance of timely recourse to statutory mechanisms and reinforces the central goal of the LRA: to facilitate the prompt, constructive resolution of labor disputes in South Africa.
