Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

### FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title:** Minister of Justice and Correctional Services and Others v Wilhelm Pretorius and Others (Case no 440/2022) [2023] ZASCA 155 (17 November 2023)

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Coram:** Dambuza, Meyer, Matojane, Goosen JJA, and Unterhalter AJA

**Heard:** 13 September 2023 

**Delivered:** 17 November 2023

---

### F – Facts
The appellants in this case are the Minister of Justice and Correctional Services, the National Commissioner of the Department of Correctional Services, and the Head of the Zonderwater Correctional Centre ([1]). The respondents, Wilhelm Pretorius and his family members, were long-term prisoners sentenced to 20 to 30 years at the Zonderwater Correctional Centre in Gauteng ([2]). They had access to communal computers from 7:00 AM to 2:00 PM but were denied the ability to use personal laptops in their cells. This limitation on their educational pursuits led the respondents to challenge the Department's Formal Education Policy in the Gauteng Division of the High Court ([2]).

On 14 May 2018, the High Court ruled in favor of the respondents, declaring that the policy constituted unfair discrimination under the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000 ([3]). The appellants appealed to the full court, which upheld the original ruling ([4]). However, by the time the appeal reached the Supreme Court of Appeal, the respondents had been released on parole, prompting the question of mootness regarding the appeal ([5]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issue before the Supreme Court was whether the appeal should be dismissed on the grounds of mootness ([6]). Specifically, the court sought to determine if—despite the mootness—the interests of justice warranted a decision on the merits of the appeal ([7]). A secondary issue involved the authority of the High Court under the Equality Act, particularly whether the presiding judge had been properly designated to handle cases under this Act ([8]).

### R – Rules
The pertinent legal authorities include the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, particularly § 16(2)(a), which addresses the dismissal of appeals that have no practical effect ([6]). Additionally, the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000 governs the claims of unfair discrimination made by the respondents ([3], [8]). The court also referred to the precedent set in *Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development and Others v Ntuli*, which clarified the requirements for a High Court judge to preside over matters arising under the Equality Act ([9]).

### A – Application
The Supreme Court noted that the appeal is moot since the respondents had been released on parole, meaning any decision rendered would lack practical effect ([7]). However, the court considered whether it should exercise its discretion to hear the appeal despite this mootness. It emphasized the necessity for judicial contribution to the understanding of legal principles, particularly in cases with potential implications for similarly situated individuals ([7]).

The court relied heavily on its previous judgment in *Ntuli*, which similarly raised questions about the jurisdiction of the High Court under the Equality Act. It found that the judge did not possess the requisite designation to handle issues of unfair discrimination as per the statutory requirements ([10]). This conclusion led the court to declare the High Court's order unconstitutional and thereby invalid, despite the mootness of the current appeal ([10], [12]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal, therefore, dismissed the appeal with costs, including those of counsel, ruling that the issues raised need not be corrected in light of the recent changes in the respondents' status ([14]). The court's decision affirmed the rights of inmates to further their education and established the requisite procedural protections under the Equality Act, extending the implications of its ruling beyond the immediate parties ([12]). The judgment underscores the importance of formal designations in adjudicating matters related to equality and discrimination, emphasizing that procedural integrity is vital to uphold the rule of law in correctional settings ([10], [12]). 

The ultimate conclusion posits a broader commitment to educational access and the rights of incarcerated individuals, even as related cases may present moot issues arising from changes in the status of the parties involved ([14]).
