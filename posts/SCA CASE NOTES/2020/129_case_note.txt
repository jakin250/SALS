# FIRAC Case Note: Transnet National Ports Authority v Reit Investments (Pty) Limited and Another

**Neutral Citation**: Transnet National Ports Authority v Reit Investments (Pty) Limited and Another (Case no 1159/2019) [2020] ZASCA 129 (13 October 2020)

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–34])

This appeal concerns the rental determination for properties leased from Transnet National Ports Authority (TNPA) by Reit Investments (Pty) Ltd (Reit) under five long-term notarial agreements executed in the 1960s. The core issues arose when disputes regarding the appropriate rental amount emerged following a valuation conducted by M C Seota, appointed as an expert valuer to resolve differing appraisals between the parties.

Transnet initially sought to reassess rental rates citing the provisions of section 67 of the National Ports Act 12 of 2005 (NPA), stating that existing long-term leases were skewed favorably for lessees and would undermine its operational viability. By October 2008, Transnet identified Reit’s leases as needing review, leading to negotiations culminating in the 2009 "Declarations of Rental" that purportedly varied the rental basis from a percentage of market value excluding improvements, to a market-related rental value.

Despite executing these declarations, disagreements persisted regarding the new rental amounts, leading Reit to instigate review proceedings in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which ruled in favor of Reit, setting aside Seota’s rental determination.

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–36])

The appeal presented two interrelated issues:

1. **Review Standards for Expert Determinations**: When, if at all, can the determination made by an expert valuer, appointed jointly by parties under a long-term agreement, be set aside by a court?
   
2. **Variation of Lease Terms**: Did the 2009 Declarations of Rental effectively amend the basis for calculating rental for the duration of the leases, or did they pertain solely to the 2009–2014 period?

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 12–68])

The primary legal framework governing this case includes:

- **National Ports Act 12 of 2005**: Particularly §67, which provides guidance on adjusting rental terms for long-term leases that may be deemed prejudicial to port operations.
  
- **Contract Law Principles**: Established jurisprudence that distinguishes between expert valuers and arbitrators; valuers exercise fact-finding authority while arbitrators act in a quasi-judicial capacity. This distinction is crucial as it delineates the scope of judicial review regarding valuation findings (see *Estate Milne v Donohoe Investments*).

- **Non-Variation Clauses**: Clause 18 of the notarial leases, stipulating that any amendments must be in writing and executed by both parties, is central to the discussion of whether the declarations constituted a valid alteration to existing leases (see *Shifren*).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 35–70])

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) scrutinized the High Court's decision through several lenses:

1. **Expert Valuation Review**: The SCA emphasized that Mr. Seota’s determination should not have been easily overturned without evidence of a clear failure to follow the terms of the mandate derived from the 2009 Declarations and applicable legal standards.

2. **Nature of the Lease Variations**: The court found that the historical context and the negotiations leading to the 2009 Declarations indicated a tacit agreement to change the rental determination method, consistent with the objectives of the NPA. It dismissed Reit’s argument asserting that these changes had only temporary validity (between 2009 and 2014), underscoring how the clauses made significant changes to the rental scheme indicating a shift towards market-related valuation.

3. **Interpretation of Contractual Intent**: The SCA applied established principles of contractual interpretation, indicating that the intent and practical implications of the 2009 Declarations should prevail over rigid literal interpretations. The declarations were viewed as part of a broader negotiation process aimed at remedying previously untenable leasing conditions.

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 70–71])

The Court upheld the appeal and overturned the judgment of the High Court, emphasizing that Reit failed to establish sufficient grounds for reviewing Mr. Seota’s determination. The ratio decidendi reinforced the principle that expert valuations, absent evidence of misconduct or clear deviation from agreed mandates, should not be invalidated lightly. 

Overall, the ruling clarified how variations in leasing contracts, as evidenced by clear negotiations and the invocation of statutory provisions, can pave the way for different rental assessment methodologies, thereby enhancing the operational viability of public entities like TNPA. The decision also sets a precedent regarding the limitations of judicial intervention in expert valuations and the comprehensive analysis required when determining the enforceability of contractual amendments under statutory legislation.
