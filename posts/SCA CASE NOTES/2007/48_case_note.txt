# Case Note: Registrar of Pension Funds v Brian Angus NO [2007] SCA 48 (RSA)

### F – Facts
The case revolves around the interpretation of the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956 (PFA) concerning the exemptions outlined in section 2(1). The dispute arose between the **Registrar of Pension Funds**, the **National Union of Metal Workers of South Africa (NUMSA)**, and several respondents representing the **Engineering Industries Pension Fund (EIPF)** and the **Metal Industries Provident Fund (MIPF)** ([paras 1–2]).

The **EIPF** was established through a constitution adopted by representatives of the metal industries on August 28, 1957. The declared intent was to create an industry pension fund, and a relevant agreement to collect contributions was published on July 19, 1957 ([para 10]). A similar process occurred for the **MIPF**, adopted on March 22, 1991, with an agreement published on April 19, 1991. The question arose whether both funds were “established in terms of” agreements published under **section 48 of the Industrial Conciliation Act** 28 of 1956, thus exempt from the provisions of the PFA ([paras 29–41]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal question was whether the EIPF and MIPF were established exclusively under the provisions of the Industrial Conciliation Act, allowing them to fall under the exemption detailed in section 2(1) of the PFA. Specific issues included:

1. The interpretation of the phrase “in terms of”, specifically whether it encompasses only funds made directly under such agreements or also those indirectly established pursuant to them.
2. Whether or not a fund created without explicit publication under section 48 of the Industrial Conciliation Act is still eligible for exemption ([paras 27–48]).

### R – Rules
The core legal rule stems from the interpretation of **section 2(1) of the PFA**, which stipulates that pension funds established “in terms of” a published agreement are exempt from the provisions of the act ([para 28]). The terminology “in terms of” can be understood in both narrow (direct establishment) and broad (pursuant to or in accordance with an agreement) senses ([paras 3–4]). 

Additionally, relevant case law was cited, notably **Oosthuizen v Standard Credit Corporation Ltd** and **Slims (Pty) Ltd v Morris NO**, which shed light on the semantics of “in terms of” and its implications in statutory constructs ([para 3]). The PFA and the Industrial Conciliation Act were created with the understanding of collective bargaining rights under the Constitution ([para 6]).

### A – Application
The court analyzed the legislative history and context of the PFA alongside the agreements that established the EIPF and MIPF. It concluded that:

1. The use of "in terms of" in section 2(1) should be interpreted broadly to include funds established consistent with a gazetted agreement. Thus, the key factor determining the applicability of the PFA should be whether the funds served the interests of the specific industries, regardless of the technicalities surrounding their establishment.
  
2. The established principles from previous case law suggested that funds arising from collective agreements, even if indirectly, should retain exemptions provided they adhered to the agreements’ terms ([paras 19–20], [42–43]).

The court found that both the agreements created a clear intent to establish the EIPF and MIPF in line with their respective industries' needs and that the absence of more detailed statutory publication did not disqualify them from exemption. Indeed, both funds were seen as maintaining a connection to the agreements governing them ([para 24]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the EIPF and MIPF were established pursuant to and thus “in terms of” the published agreements. Therefore, the funds were exempt from the provisions of the PFA ([paras 25–26]). 

In its ruling, the court reaffirmed the interpretation that legislative history and collective bargaining frameworks are essential to understanding statutory contexts. This prevents disconnects in oversight between industrial councils and pension fund regulations, fostering legal coherence in how funds are administered within the industrial sector. Consequently, the appeal was dismissed with costs awarded to the first appellant, reflecting the broader legislative and public interests at stake ([paras 25–26]).

The judgment emphasized the critical role of collective bargaining in the governance of pension funds and highlighted the necessity for clear legislative frameworks that acknowledge industry-specific structures, ultimately protecting both the funds' integrity and the employees' rights within those frameworks.
