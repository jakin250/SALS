National African Federated Chamber of Commerce and Industry v Mkhize (805/13) [2014] ZASCA 177

Supreme Court of Appeal 2014 [2014] ZASCA 177

F - Facts

The case arises from a dispute within the National African Federated Chamber of Commerce and Industry (NAFCOC), a voluntary association established in 1964. The organization has been embroiled in internal conflict, leading to divergent factions represented by the appellants (the NAFCOC leadership) and the respondents (a rival faction).

On 6 November 2012, notice was sent for a Council meeting scheduled for 6 December 2012, aimed at electing a new President to replace Mr. Lawrence Bhekinkosi Mavundla and to remove various executive committee members (3rd to 8th appellants). The appellants contended that this meeting was unlawfully convened, citing issues with the constitution's interpretation. Specifically, they sought judicial declaration that the meeting and resolutions passed therein lacked validity due to improper convening ([1]–[3]).

Procedurally, the appellants initiated urgent relief in the South Gauteng High Court, affirming the need for a declaratory order on the legality of the meeting, following an interim court order on 4 December 2012 that suspended the resolutions until the application could be heard ([8]–[9]). Despite this arrangement, the high court ruled against the appellants, invoking various interpretations of NAFCOC's constitution ([10]).

I - Issues

The principal issue in this case revolved around the lawfulness of the 6 December 2012 Council meeting and its resolutions. Specifically, two sub-issues arose:

  1. Was the December meeting lawfully convened according to NAFCOC's constitution?
  2. What constitutional provisions governed the powers to call such a meeting?

Further, the court needed to determine the status of Mr. Mavundla as NAFCOC's President and whether he or his deputy could legally convene the meeting ([4]–[5]).

R - Rules

The interpretation of the NAFCOC constitution was vital in resolving the issues. The following constitutional provisions were particularly important:

  • Clause 28.4.4: Stipulates that the Council must convene no less than four times each calendar year as determined by the President.
  • Clause 17.1: Allows for requisitioned meetings, but this is generally interpreted to pertain to general NAFCOC membership meetings rather than Council meetings.
  • Common law principles relating to voluntary associations, including those established in significant cases like Jockey Club of South Africa v Feldman ([10]).

Additionally, the court relied on the fundamental principle that the constitution of a voluntary association is to be interpreted like any other contract, giving preference to sensible interpretations that further the purpose of the document ([21]).

A - Application

In its analysis, the Supreme Court of Appeal examined the procedural handling and constitutional interpretation by the lower court. It concluded that the December 2012 meeting was not convened in accordance with the governing rules set out in the 2008 constitution. The lower court's misinterpretation of the constitution, particularly regarding the authority to convene council meetings, was central to this finding.

The Supreme Court noted:

  1. There was no recognized position of "Chairperson of the Council" as claimed in the meeting's convening notice. Hence, the assertion that the meeting was validly convened under this title was unfounded ([16]–[20]).
  2. The President, not any other member, had the exclusive authority to convene council meetings in line with clause 28.4.4.
  3. The court dismissed the argument that the irregularities did not affect the appellants' civil rights, affirming the direct implications of the disputed meeting on governance and the organisation's operations ([36]).

Finally, the court refuted the respondents' reliance on common law in this matter, emphasizing that the constitution expressly outlined processes for convening meetings, negating any need to adhere to common law interpretations in this context ([23]–[30]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, declaring the meeting on 6 December 2012 unlawfully convened. Consequently, all resolutions passed at that meeting were deemed invalid and of no effect. The court's ruling reinforced the importance of adhering to constitutional norms within voluntary associations, sending a clear message regarding the necessity of proper governance structures.

The implications of this judgment extend beyond the current dispute, reaffirming the legal sanctity of organizational constitutions and the protocol for internal governance. Ultimately, NAFCOC's struggle illustrates the complications arising from internal disputes and emphasizes the need for coherence and structural integrity in such associations ([37]–[38]).
