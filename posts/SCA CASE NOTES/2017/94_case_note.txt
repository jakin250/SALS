Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note: NMB Bank Limited v Capsopoulos (505/2016) [2017] ZASCA 94

## F – Facts

The appellant, NMB Bank Limited, engaged in banking business in Zimbabwe, brought a claim against the respondents, David Capsopoulos and Lindsay Joan Dent Capsopoulos, seeking recovery of over $6.2 million. This sum was alleged to have been misappropriated through a fraudulent scheme involving the respondents between 2005 and 2007 ([1]). At the time of the events in question, the respondents were living in Zimbabwe and operated a business importing kitchen equipment and furniture through a company known as Haus (Pty) Ltd ([2]).

The respondents also established Cardinal Finance LLC in Wyoming, USA, which became the central entity for their transactions with the appellant bank ([3]). The background of the case is set against the backdrop of hyperinflation in Zimbabwe, making it near impossible for businesses to obtain foreign currency lawfully ([6]–[14]). To acquire the necessary US dollars, the respondents engaged in transactions in the black market, purchasing these dollars from Mr. Edward Tome, who proposed a scheme involving falsified transactions ([15]–[16]).

The transactions facilitated payments of fictitious exports purportedly to have been made by Haus in exchange for US dollars credited to Cardinal's Swiss bank account, thus circumventing Zimbabwe’s Exchange Control Act ([20]–[22]). The trial court initially dismissed the appellant's claim, concluding that the respondents had not been complicit in the fraud as their involvement did not amount to knowledge of the fraudulent misrepresentations made to the bank ([1]).

## I – Issues

1. **Legal Complicity**: Did the respondents have knowledge of the fraudulent misrepresentations made by Mr. Tome to the appellant bank?
   
2. **Liability for Illegal Payments**: Were the respondents liable to repay the amounts received from the appellant bank under an unlawful scheme?

3. **Restitution and Enrichment**: Can the appellant claim restitution of amounts paid under a misrepresentation, regardless of the illegality of the payments?

## R – Rules

### Legal Authorities

1. **Complicity in Fraud**: The court relied on established principles from **First National Bank of Southern Africa Ltd v Perry NO & others** 2001 (3) SA 960 (SCA) and **Nissan South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Marnitz NO & others** 2005 (1) SA 441 (SCA) regarding knowledge and participation in fraudulent schemes ([32]–[33]).

2. **Zim Dollar Valuation and Foreign Exchange Regulations**: The court acknowledged the extreme devaluation of the Zimbabwean dollar and outlined the legislative framework governing foreign exchange within Zimbabwe ([6]–[14]).

3. **Restitution Principles**: The doctrine of unjust enrichment as applied in cases where false representations lead to a party being induced to pay money was pivotal in determining the right to restitution ([32]–[33]).

## A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal assessed the trial court's findings, concluding that the respondents could not have been oblivious to the unlawful nature of their dealings. Evidence indicated they were aware that Mr. Tome’s payment mechanism, which involved using systematically forgery and luxury codes to bypass regulations, reflected a conscious decision to proceed despite potential illegality ([20]–[22]).

The court found that the respondents’ actions were inconsistent with their claims of ignorance about the fraudulent nature of the transactions, especially as their correspondence with Baobab involved coded language to avoid detection, demonstrating an awareness of the sensitive and illegal nature of their dealings ([20]). Additionally, the respondents’ testimony, particularly from the first respondent, painted a picture of a businessman who had navigated the complexities of international finance while being acutely aware of his surroundings ([19], [25]).

Furthermore, the court held that the illegal nature of the transactions did not absolve the respondents of liability. This adhered to the precedent established by **Nissan**, where courts indicated that payments made under coercive terms or misinformation are recoverable regardless of the illegality of the payments themselves ([31]–[33]). The loss to the appellant was clearly demonstrable through the transactions that took place, further supported by expert testimony regarding the state of Zimbabwe’s fiscal crisis at the time ([7]–[10]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the respondents were complicit in the fraud perpetrated against NMB Bank and were thus obliged to repay the amounts received through unauthorized foreign exchange transactions. Ultimately, the trial court's ruling was set aside, and the respondents were ordered to jointly and severally refund $6,230,329.01 to NMB Bank, alongside interest on the amounts from the date of payment ([38]).

This decision underscores the strong judicial stance against complicity in fraudulent schemes while affirming the entitlement of parties to reclaim sums where payments have been induced through misrepresentation. The implications of the ruling extend to future cases underlining the need for strict adherence to statutory obligations in foreign exchange dealings.
