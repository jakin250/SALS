Case Note: SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# Case Note: The State v Nkunkuma & Others [2013] ZASCA 122

## F – Facts:
The incident that led to the appeal occurred on April 20, 2011, when TB, a 38-year-old woman living with her mother, BB, and 3-year-old grandson on a smallholding near Beacon Bay, East London, was targeted by three intruders ([para 1]). At approximately 10 PM, BB woke up to find three armed men in their home. The intruders demanded money while threatening BB and her grandson, which included a specific threat to electrify the child if BB did not comply ([para 1]). Soon after TB returned home and, upon confronting the assailants, they fled. However, they soon returned to assault TB, pack stolen goods, and subsequently raped her ([paras 2-3]).

The perpetrators, identified as Phakamani Allan Nkunkuma, Bulelani Makaleni, and Avuyile Maseti, were arrested two to three days later, possessing items stolen from their victims ([para 5]). They faced multiple charges, including housebreaking with intent to rob, robbery with aggravating circumstances, and rape, with DNA evidence linking Nkunkuma and Maseti to the assault ([para 5]). The second respondent, Makaleni, was not charged with rape but was implicated in the other offences ([para 5]).

During proceedings in the High Court, the respondents were convicted but received lenient sentences that significantly departed from the minimum sentences mandated by the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 ([paras 6-7]). The State subsequently sought to appeal these sentences, claiming that the lower court failed to properly apply the principles regarding minimum sentencing as stipulated by legislation ([para 7]).

## I – Issues:
The primary legal issues in this case revolve around whether the trial court properly applied the legal framework concerning minimum sentences under the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, particularly:

1. Did the trial court correctly find "substantial and compelling circumstances" justifying a departure from the prescribed minimum sentences for robbery and rape? ([paras 9-18])
2. How should youth and prior convictions of the respondents impact their sentencing in the context of the seriousness of the crimes committed? ([paras 19-20])

## R – Rules:
The applicable legal framework consists of sections of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, specifically:

- **Section 51:** This section prescribes minimum sentences for specified offences, including life imprisonment for certain serious offences, with provisions allowing for lesser sentences if "substantial and compelling" circumstances justify such a departure ([para 8]).
  
- **S v Malgas:** This case establishes the interpretation of "substantial and compelling circumstances," emphasizing that the standard prescribed by the legislature must not be disregarded for flimsy reasons ([para 9]).

Furthermore, the court referenced previous cases that underline the necessity of stringent adherence to prescribed minimum sentences in the absence of compelling reasons ([para 9]).

## A – Application:
Upon reviewing the sentencing in the lower court, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that the trial court had misapplied the law. It inferred that the circumstances considered for departing from minimum sentences were neither substantial nor compelling when juxtaposed against the seriousness of the crimes ([paras 18-20]).

1. **Seriousness of the crimes:** The court highlighted that both rape and robbery with aggravating circumstances are deemed severe societal crimes, necessitating stern punishment ([para 17]). The shocking statistics on the prevalence of these crimes were noted, underlining the need for the judicial system to respond decisively ([para 16]).

2. **Youth and character of the respondents:** The court scrutinized the backgrounds of the respondents, noting that while the second respondent's comparatively lesser role and mature age were believed worthy of consideration ([para 19]), the first and third respondents did not exhibit substantial mitigating factors. It was ruled that their youthfulness did not sufficiently diminish their moral blameworthiness for such grave offenses ([para 14]).

3. **Lack of remorse:** The absence of genuine remorse was emphasized, indicating that a mere denial of guilt does not equate to innocence or show genuine contrition, which is crucial in sentencing considerations ([para 14]).

The court ultimately found that the trial court had improperly evaluated the "substantial and compelling circumstances" leading to an inadequate sentencing response to the grave actions committed by the respondents ([para 20]).

## C – Conclusion:
In a comprehensive review, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the State's appeal, concluding that the sentences imposed by the lower court were unduly lenient. The appellate court reimposed the minimum sentences, emphasizing the legislative intent behind minimum sentencing laws, which aims for a uniform, severe response to serious crimes that damages societal fabric ([paras 21-22]).

The ruling reiterated the judiciary's role in ensuring proper application of legislative provisions regarding sentencing, emphasizing that the courts must adhere to established norms unless truly compelling circumstances warrant departure. The implications of this case extend beyond the individual offenders, addressing broader societal issues regarding crime and punishment, and reaffirming the need for effective deterrents against serious offenses like rape and robbery.
