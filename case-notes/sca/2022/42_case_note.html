Supreme Court of Appeal

Minister of Police v Mzingeli and Others (115/2021) [2022] ZASCA 42

The trial initially scheduled for 15 October 2019 was marked by unsuccessful attempts to resolve the quantum of damages, leading the court a quo to separate the issues of liability and quantum as per Rule 33(4) of the Uniform Rules of Court.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 42

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

In the case of Minister of Police v Mzingeli and Others, the respondents—Xolile Mzingeli, Luthando Ndayi, and Mpumezo Xabadiya—initiated a delictual action against the Minister of Police, claiming damages for alleged unlawful arrest, detention, and malicious prosecution ([para 3]). The events that led to the claims occurred on 13 September 2009, when the respondents were arrested for housebreaking, theft, and murder. Following their detention, on 17 September 2009, the first and third respondents were convicted of theft, receiving a 12-month prison sentence, while the murder charge was still under investigation. After serving their sentences, they remained incarcerated until the murder charges were withdrawn on 24 July 2014, leading the respondents to seek damages for their unlawful detention ([para 4]).

The trial initially scheduled for 15 October 2019 was marked by unsuccessful attempts to resolve the quantum of damages, leading the court a quo to separate the issues of liability and quantum as per Rule 33(4) of the Uniform Rules of Court. The court found the Minister liable for unlawful detention, leaving quantum for resolution on 16 October 2019 ([paras 5–6]). During this hearing, the quantum was determined through a stated case but without oral evidence. The court subsequently awarded damages of R3,000,000 to the first and third respondents, and R4,000,000 to the second respondent but acknowledged that the stated case lacked sufficient evidentiary support ([para 6]).

I – Issues

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) identified three critical issues for determination:

  1. Whether the court a quo had the authority to decide the quantum of unliquidated damages without hearing oral evidence, especially in light of EFF and Others v Manuel ([2021] ZASCA 172).
  2. Whether the stated case was properly formulated in accordance with relevant court rules and whether it met the required standards to allow a determination of the quantum of damages ([para 1]).
  3. The appropriateness of the quantum of damages awarded by the court a quo ([para 1]).

R – Rules

The key legal principles applied in this case reference Rule 33 of the Uniform Rules of Court, which allows the parties to present a written statement of agreed facts for the adjudication of the court ([para 7]). Specific rules stipulate that such a statement must include facts agreed upon, disputes of law, and supporting documentation. Background precedents highlighted include Minister of Police v Mboweni ([2014] ZASCA 107), underscoring that a special case should contain agreed facts, not assumptions, for the court's consideration ([para 8]).

A – Application

The SCA found that the court a quo adopted an inappropriate approach in determining the quantum of damages based solely on the inadequately framed stated case, which did not provide sufficient factual basis for its conclusions. Several critical details regarding the respondents’ alleged experiences during detention—the extent of assaults, claims of torture, and degrading conditions—were either absent or inadequately substantiated in the stated case ([paras 9–12]). Therefore, as recognized by the court a quo itself, the failure to adduce evidence to substantiate the claims significantly undermined the legitimacy of its damage awards ([para 10]).

The court noted that while a judge can draw inferences from adequately stated facts within a stated case, any such inferences must arise from verified and substantiated facts. Given that the quantum of unliquidated damages claimed was heavily disputed, the absence of substantive evidence or a fully drafted statement rendered the decision invalid. Ultimately, the SCA concurred with the parties that proceeding via a stated case was inappropriate, and no consensus on relevant facts necessary for quantum resolution was established ([paras 13–14]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA concluded by overturning the court a quo's order on the grounds that the quantum was improperly decided without requisite evidence. It ordered that:

  1. The appeal was upheld with no order as to costs given the unopposed nature of the appeal.
  2. The court a quo's order was set aside, and the matter was remitted for appropriate assessment of quantum, ensuring future determinations are based on robust evidentiary foundations ([paras 16–17]).

This case illustrates the paramount importance of evidential accuracy and the appropriate formulation of stated cases in the determination of damages, underscoring the judiciary’s commitment to ensuring justice and the correct application of procedural rules in South African law.
