Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: LA Health Medical Scheme v Horn (385/13) [2014] ZASCA 72

## F – Facts

The appellant, **LA Health Medical Scheme** (LA Health), operates a medical scheme for local authorities in the Western Cape. The respondents, **Johannes Petrus Louw Horn** and seven others, were previously employed by LA Health and were members of the **Cape Joint Retirement Fund** (the Fund) as a condition of their employment ([1]). 

On **1 January 2005**, the administration of claims against LA Health transitioned to **Discovery Health (Pty) Ltd** (Discovery), under the stipulations of section **197(2)(a)** of the **Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995**, resulting in the automatic transfer of the respondents to Discovery ([1]). The respondents asserted their entitlement to redundancy or retrenchment benefits as per the rules of the Fund. This claim was upheld by the **Western Cape High Court** (Erasmus J), and subsequently affirmed by a Full Court ([1]).

The Fund was established under the **Local Authorities (Pension Funds) Ordinance 23 of 1969**, aiming to serve local authority employees. Notably, LA Health was not designated as a local authority; hence, the respondents' membership was considered anomalous ([2]). Nevertheless, LA Health effectively operated as an employer under the rules of the Fund until the transfer to Discovery occurred.

The crux of the dispute rested on Rule **7.1A(1)** of the Fund, which outlines eligibility for redundancy or retrenchment benefits ([3]). Following the termination of their employment with LA Health, the respondents contended that they met the criteria for these benefits as their positions were abolished. However, LA Health argued that such benefits were only applicable to employees whose employment contracts explicitly provided for them ([4]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issue under consideration was whether the respondents were entitled to the redundancy or retrenchment benefits specified in Rule **7.1A(1)** upon their transfer to Discovery, particularly in light of their anomalous membership in the Fund as employees of a non-local authority entity ([5]). 

Additionally, the court assessed whether the applicable definitions and conditions outlined in the Fund's rules extend to employers like LA Health, who were not involved in the collective bargaining process that established these benefits ([16]).

## R – Rules

The key legal rules pertained to the interpretation of the rules governing the Fund, particularly Rule **7.1A(1)**, which stipulates that benefits in cases of redundancy or retrenchment are provided by the **LOCAL AUTHORITY** ([3]). The stipulation further references the nature of employment contracts and the conditions that entitle employees to redundancy benefits.

The court was guided by principles of statutory interpretation found in **Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality** 2012 (4) SA 593 (SCA), emphasizing the importance of contextual interpretation of rules and agreements within pension law ([7]).

## A – Application

The court’s analysis began by scrutinizing the language of Rule **7.1A(1)** and its surrounding provisions ([5]). The introductory wording, which suggests that both redundancy and retrenchment benefits must arise from the **conditions of service** delineated in employment contracts, was central to LA Health's argument. They maintained that since such contracts did not explicitly provide for these benefits, the respondents had no claim.

Respondents contended that the introductory words of the rule merely recorded a factual scenario and did not constitute a prerequisite for entitlement to the benefits ([6]). The high court had endorsed this interpretation, leading to the finding that the respondents were indeed entitled to the benefits under Rule **7.1A(b)**.

The court also examined historical context, noting that Rule **7.1A** was introduced amid significant restructuring within local authorities following South Africa’s transition to democracy. The provision aimed to address redundancies resulting from these organizational changes, predominantly affecting local authority employees, thus reinforcing the notion that benefits governed by this rule intended to operate within the confines of traditional local authority employment contexts ([10]).

The court determined that although LA Health's participation in the Fund implied it was subject to the rules, it did not create obligations under Rule **7.1A(1)** that were intended for traditional local authority employees. Moreover, the prerequisites stipulated in the rule for benefits were not met by the circumstances surrounding the respondents' employment and the specific nature of their contracts ([19]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately concluded that **LA Health** was not liable to pay the redundancy or retrenchment benefits to the respondents as they were not employees of a local authority as intended by the rule. The court's rationale highlighted the importance of interpreting the rules in the context of their historical genesis and the specific employment circumstance of the respondents ([20]).

The court’s order revised the previous court’s decision, upholding LA Health’s appeal with costs and altering the order of the lower court to one that dismissed the application with costs ([21]). This decision clarified the limitations of the Fund's rules concerning entitlements linked to redundancy or retrenchment, particularly in relation to the anomalous membership of non-local authority entities like LA Health, and affirmed the necessity for explicit provisions when establishing employer obligations under pension fund rules.
