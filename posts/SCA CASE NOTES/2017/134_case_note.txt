Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: Cipla Agrimed (Pty) Ltd v Merck Sharp Dohme Corporation (972/16)

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts 
The appellant, Cipla Agrimed (Pty) Ltd, is the proprietor of South African Patent 92/7457 (the 1992 patent), while the first and second respondents, Merck Sharp Dohme Corporation and Merial Limited (Merial LLC), are joint proprietors of South African Patent 98/10975 (the 1998 patent). Merial South Africa (Pty) Ltd serves as a registered licensee of the 1998 patent. The parties have been engaged in continuous litigation, with the infringement action pending since 2011, in which Merck sought a final interdict against Cipla for alleged infringement of their patent rights ([1]).

On 4 March 2016, the Court of the Commissioner of Patents (CCP), presided over by Louw J, granted Merck an interim interdict, restraining Cipla from infringing claims of the 1998 patent prior to the final determination of the infringement action. This interim interdict included a condition that it would lapse if the infringement action was not determined by the expiry of the 1998 patent on 3 December 2018 ([2], [4]). The matter being appealed involved the question of whether this interim order was appealable ([18]).

### I – Issues 
The principal issue for consideration was whether the interim interdict granted by the CCP was appealable. Cipla contended that, despite being titled an "interim" interdict, it was effectively final, asserting that the impending expiration of the patent rendered any delay in final resolution moot. The respondents argued against this appealability, maintaining that the order was inherently provisional and did not dispose of the underlying issues ([17], [19], [21]).

### R – Rules 
The court's determination of appealability relied on established principles, particularly those stemming from the jurisprudence surrounding interdicts. It reiterated that an interlocutory order must satisfy three criteria to be considered final: (i) it must be final in effect, (ii) it must definitively resolve the rights of the parties, and (iii) it must dispose of substantial claims in the main proceedings ([18], [39]).

Previous cases, such as **Zweni v Minister of Law and Order** and **Cronshaw v Fidelity Guards Holdings (Pty) Ltd**, emphasized that an interim interdict usually does not meet the threshold for appealability because it does not finally resolve the underlying issues ([19], [34]). The court referenced the statutory context provided by section 16(1) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which allows for appeals against decisions characterized as final ([18]).

### A – Application 
In applying the established legal principles to the case, the court underscored that the nature of the order as an interim interdict rendered it inherently provisional. Drawing on precedent, it asserted that the fact that the interdict was subject to conditions (i.e., lapsing at the expiry of the patent) illustrated its interlocutory nature. The risk of prejudice or inconvenience to Cipla due to the timing of the litigation did not suffice to confer appealability on what remained an interim interdict ([21], [25], [49]).

Furthermore, it was determined that there remained an opportunity to resolve the res judicata point which Cipla had raised in its submissions. The court highlighted that if the validity of the patent could be determined favorably to Cipla, it could subsequently discharge the interdict based on changed circumstances ([25], [27]).

### C – Conclusion 
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the interim interdict was both interim in form and substance, and thus, the appeal was not justiciable. It concluded that the conditions surrounding the interdict did not convert its nature into final relief, and therefore, Cipla's appeal was struck from the roll with costs ([32]). The case reaffirms the principle that interim interdicts are generally not appealable unless they effectively decide substantive rights within the larger context of the ongoing litigation. 

In light of the ruling, this case reinforces the necessity for parties engaged in patent disputes to understand the implications of interim orders and the limitations placed on appellate review before final adjudications of related claims occur.
