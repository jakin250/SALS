# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title**: Global & Local Investments Advisors (Pty) Ltd v Nickolaus Ludick Fouché (71/2019) [2019] ZASCA 08 (18 March 2020)  
**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Coram**: Navsa, Saldulker, Makgoka and Nicholls JJA and Mojapelo AJA  
**Heard**: 24 February 2020  
**Delivered**: 18 March 2020  
**Citations**: [paras 1–17]

---

## F - Facts 

The case concerns an appeal by Global & Local Investments Advisors (Pty) Ltd ("Global"), a financial services provider, against a ruling by the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg ([para 1]). The dispute arose from instructions sent via email which were fraudulently generated by a third party who had hacked the Gmail account of Mr. Nickolaus Ludick Fouché ("Fouché"), the respondent. Fouché had initially provided Global with a written mandate on 23 November 2015 to manage and invest his money, explicitly stating that all instructions must be sent via fax or email accompanied by his signature ([paras 2–3]).

In August 2016, the fraudsters used Fouché's email credentials to send three unauthorized email instructions to Global, resulting in the transfer of R804,000 from Fouché's Corporate Cash Manager account to accounts of third parties ([para 3]). Upon discovering the fraudulent activity, Fouché sought reimbursement from Global, asserting that the transfers violated the written mandate since they lacked his handwritten signature. Global contended that the typewritten name "Nick" at the end of the emails constituted an electronic signature as stipulated by section 13(3) of the Electronic Communications and Transactions Act 25 of 2002 ([paras 4–6]).

The High Court found in favor of Fouché, ruling that Global had fundamentally breached the client’s mandate by executing the transfers based on elements that did not meet the signature requirements under the mandate ([paras 7–8]).

---

## I - Issues 

The primary issue before the Supreme Court was whether Global breached the mandate authorized by Fouché by responding to fraudulent email instructions ([para 1]). The central legal question was whether the email with the typewritten name constituted a valid signature under the mandate in light of the criteria established in section 13(3) of the ECT Act. Further, the appeal raised questions about the proper interpretation of the written mandate and the nature of electronic signatures in this context ([paras 9–12]).

---

## R - Rules 

The court relied on various legal precedents and statutory provisions:

1. **Signature Requirements**: According to the definitions provided in various legal sources, a signature serves as a mode of commitment or authorization regarding a document's contents ([para 10]). The formal requirement under the mandate was for a signature, clarified in common law as requiring authenticated verification ([para 12]).

2. **Electronic Communications and Transactions Act (ECT Act)**: Section 13(3) stipulates the conditions under which electronic signatures are deemed valid when parties have not agreed on a type of electronic signature. It necessitates that:
   - A method must identify the person and indicate approval of the communication; and
   - The method should be reliable considering the context ([para 5]).

3. **Case Law**: Previous cases, including Goodman v J Eban Ltd and Spring Forest Trading 599 CC v Wilberry (Pty) Ltd, provided context regarding signature validation and the implications of electronic communications ([paras 11–16]).

---

## A - Application 

In applying the law to the facts, the court noted that the emailed instructions did not satisfy the signature requirement as dictated by the mandate. It rigorously interpreted the mandate's term "signature" to imply a handwritten or otherwise authenticated form of signature, necessitating a higher standard than what was presented ([paras 7–9]).

The court emphasized that electronic signatures require clear prior agreement between parties, lacking in this case. It rejected Global's argument that merely appending a typewritten name could fulfill the mandate, asserting that this was insufficient under common commercial understanding of a signature's role ([para 14]). Moreover, the fraudulent nature of the communications mitigated any presumption of reliability in the electronic signatures purportedly made ([para 16]).

In finding for Fouché, the High Court had properly discerned the intent of the parties in requiring a distinctive means of authentication—a requirement that was not satisfied by the fraudulent emails ([para 14]).

---

## C - Conclusion 

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court's ruling, dismissing the appeal with costs. It affirmed the principle that mandates regarding financial instructions must be adhered to with strict compliance to signature requirements to protect clients from fraud ([para 17]). The ruling reaffirms the necessity for clear, unambiguous authentication methods in electronic communications related to financial services, underscoring the importance of safeguarding client interests against fraudulent activities. As such, this case sets a precedent for the interpretation of electronic signatures and mandates in financial transactions, prompting firms to enforce stringent verification measures in their operational protocols ([para 17]).

---

This case note details a significant interpretation of electronic signatures, emphasizing adherence to established legal standards to mitigate the risks of fraudulent transactions. It serves as a critical reference point for both legal practitioners and financial service providers navigating the evolving requirements of electronic communications and client mandates.
