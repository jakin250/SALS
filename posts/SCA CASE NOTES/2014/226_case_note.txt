Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# Case Note - FIRAC Method

**Title**: Bengwenyama-ya-Maswazi Community v Genorah Resources (Pty) Ltd (784/2013) [2014] ZASCA 140 (26 September 2014)

## F - Facts

[1] This case arises from competing applications for preferent community prospecting rights over the farm Eerstegeluk in the Sekhukhuneland area of Limpopo Province, South Africa. The appellants comprise the Bengwenyama-ya-Maswazi Community (BYMC), the Bengwenyama-ya-Maswazi Tribal Council (the Tribal Council), and Miracle Upon Miracle Investments (Pty) Ltd (MUM), while the respondents are Genorah Resources (Pty) Ltd and two entities affiliated with the Roka Phasha Community. 

[2] The appellants allege that they have historical ties to the land and submitted an application for prospecting rights that the Minister for Mineral Resources (the Minister) initially overlooked in favor of the Roka Phasha Community’s application. The BYMC claims that they were not duly consulted and that their rights were not properly considered, especially in light of the Constitutional Court's earlier ruling in *Bengwenyama Minerals (Pty) Ltd v Genorah Resources (Pty) Ltd* ([2011] 4 SA 113 (CC)), which emphasized the need for consultation and the community's right to representation.

[3] The factual dispute in this appeal revolves around the legitimacy and authority of the Tribal Council, the historical claims to the land, and whether MUM can act on behalf of the BYMC. 

## I - Issues

[4] The primary legal questions presented for determination by the court in this case include:

1. Can a corporate vehicle, such as MUM, apply for and be awarded a preferent community prospecting right in the context of section 104 of the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA)?
  
2. Does the absence of registered title to the land affect the entitlement of a community to the prospecting rights?

3. What is the legal standing of the Tribal Council and does it represent the community competently in these proceedings?

[5] An additional concern is whether the Minister should have granted the preferent prospecting rights to the Roka Phasha Community, particularly in light of the lack of consultation with the BYMC.

## R - Rules

[6] The legal framework primarily involves the MPRDA and associated legislation, particularly section 104, which establishes the criteria for communities seeking prospecting rights. This provision stipulates that any community wishing to obtain such rights must be the registered owner or occupier of the land in question.

[7] The Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003 (TLGFA) provides the context for the existence and authority of traditional councils, stipulating their roles in relation to traditional communities. Section 211 of the Constitution also recognizes the role of traditional leadership and customary law.

[8] The court is guided by the Constitutional Court's prior decision in the Bengwenyama case, which underscored the importance of consultation and the hearers' rights of communities concerning land and mineral rights.

## A - Application

[9] The Supreme Court of Appeal examined the legitimacy of the Tribal Council and its authority to represent the BYMC. The court acknowledged the historical claims of the BYMC to the land and the continuity of its community structure as a traditional authority. The court noted that the Tribal Council had a significant role in advancing the community’s application through MUM and adequately met the statutory criteria outlined in the MPRDA.

[10] The court also considered the issue of registered title. It held that while a lack of registered title could typically impede a community's rights under section 104, the fact that the BYMC had a legitimate land claim under the Restitution of Land Rights Act indicated a substantial probability of future registration. Thus, the court deemed the absence of title as insufficient grounds to deny rights to the BYMC.

[11] On the question of whether MUM could be seen as representative of the community's interests, the court evaluated the shareholders' agreements and proposed amendments to ensure a minimum community shareholding that would protect community interests. The court found that the proposed adjustments sufficiently addressed concerns about community control and allowed for MUM's role as a valid vehicle.

## C - Conclusion

[12] The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the Minister's initial refusal to grant the prospecting rights to the BYMC was procedurally flawed, as it failed to adhere to the Constitutional Court’s guidance on consultation and community representation.

[13] The appeal was upheld, and the cross-appeal was dismissed. The court set aside the Minister's decisions regarding the prospecting rights and awarded exclusive rights to MUM, subject to the amendment of the shareholders' agreement to ensure community control. 

[14] The case has significant implications for the interpretation of community rights under the MPRDA and highlights the importance of legislative compliance and community consultation in matters of land and mineral rights. The ruling reinforces the recognition of traditional leadership within the legal framework and upholds the rights of historically disadvantaged communities in South Africa, ensuring their voices are adequately recognized in resource management decisions.
