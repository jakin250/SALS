Lion Match Company (Pty) Ltd v Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (301/2017) [2018] ZASCA 36

In the case of Lion Match Company (Pty) Ltd v Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service, the appellant, Lion Match Company (Pty) Ltd (hereafter "the taxpayer"), disposed of its entire shareholding in the Kimberly Clark Group during the 2008 tax year ([para 2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 36

F – Facts

In the case of Lion Match Company (Pty) Ltd v Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service, the appellant, Lion Match Company (Pty) Ltd (hereafter "the taxpayer"), disposed of its entire shareholding in the Kimberly Clark Group during the 2008 tax year ([para 2]). For tax purposes, the taxpayer adopted the shares' market value as of 1 October 2001 as their base cost. However, on 30 April 2013, the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (SARS) issued an additional assessment adjusting this base cost, which resulted in an increase in the taxpayer's taxable capital gain ([para 2]).

Subsequently, the taxpayer objected to SARS’s assessment on 12 July 2013. This objection was disallowed, leading to the taxpayer's appeal to the Tax Court in Durban ([para 3]). On 14 August 2015, SARS submitted its statement of grounds of assessment and opposition, conforming to Rule 31 of the Tax Administration Act 28 of 2011 (TAA). Rather than respond with their statement as permitted by Rule 32, the taxpayer filed an application claiming that SARS included grounds that altered the factual or legal basis of the assessment, seeking to invalidate SARS's statement ([para 3]).

The Tax Court, presided over by Moodley J, dismissed the taxpayer’s application but granted leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 4]).

I – Issues

The core legal issue pertains to the appealability of the Tax Court's decision dismissing the taxpayer's application. Specifically, the question is whether the Tax Court's order can be appealed independently of the main proceedings regarding the assessment at issue. The inquiry centers on the interpretation of the relevant sections of the TAA, particularly regarding the nature of decisions that can be appealed under sections 129 and 104 of the Act ([para 5], [para 8]).

R – Rules

The legal framework revolves around the TAA and its provisions concerning taxpayer appeals. Specifically:

  1. Section 117 provides the scope of the Tax Court's jurisdiction over appeals lodged under section 107. Section 107(1) allows a taxpayer to appeal against an assessment or decision ([para 6]).
  2. Section 133 of the TAA outlines the conditions under which appeals against Tax Court decisions are permissible. Appeals are limited to those decisions defined under section 104(2), which does not include decisions of a procedural nature like the dismissal of an application ([para 8]).
  3. It is established legal precedent that the dismissal of an exception (a jurisdictional challenge) is not appealable unless it specifically pertains to the court's jurisdiction ([para 9]). The court in Beinash v Wixley emphasized that only jurisdiction-related exceptions have the potential for appealable outcomes ([para 9]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal conducted a legal analysis to determine the appealability of the Tax Court’s order. It established that the order in question was not appealable because it did not fall under the specific categories mentioned in section 104(2) of the TAA. Thus, the matter at hand could not be reviewed separately from the main appeal ([para 10]).

The court further noted the procedural nature of the taxpayer's application, likening the appeal to a dismissal of an exception, which is not independently appealable ([para 9]). Recognizing that the taxpayer did not pursue their objection through formal channels prescribed by the TAA (e.g. by raising a jurisdictional challenge through exception or special plea), it was concluded that the Tax Court did not expressly pronounce on jurisdiction. Therefore, the Supreme Court found that the taxpayer had not adequately substantiated a basis for appeal ([para 10]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the Tax Court's decision, finding that the appeal was not competent and consequently struck it from the roll with costs. The court’s rationale focused on the limitations set by the TAA regarding the appealability of decisions, underscoring the procedural framework governing South African tax law ([para 10]).

The implications of this ruling reiterate the importance for taxpayers to adhere closely to procedural mandates when challenging assessments or decisions made by SARS. This case serves as a precedent that procedural dismissals, particularly those concerning jurisdiction, cannot be readily appealed. It emphasizes the need for taxpayers to fully engage with the prescribed procedural steps if they wish to challenge an assessment or assertion made by SARS, thereby upholding the statutory integrity of the TAA.

In summary, the case establishes clear boundaries regarding which Tax Court decisions are appealable and stresses the importance of correct procedural compliance in tax disputes ([paras 8–10]).
