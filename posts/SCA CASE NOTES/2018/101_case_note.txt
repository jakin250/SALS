Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA

# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of The Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service v The South African Breweries (Pty) Ltd (442/2017) [2018] ZASCA 101

### F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–4])

This case concerns an appeal brought by the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (the Commissioner) against a ruling of the Gauteng Division of the High Court wherein the court upheld an appeal by The South African Breweries (Pty) Ltd (SAB) regarding the classification of certain flavoured alcoholic beverages (FABs) for the purpose of excise duty under the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 (the Act). 

On 30 October 2013, the Commissioner classified the FABs under tariff heading TH2208.90.22, making them taxable under tariff item 104.23.26. In contrast, SAB contended that the correct classification should be under TH2206.00.85, leading SAB to appeal the determination. The High Court sided with SAB, categorizing the FABs as fermented beverages under TH2206.00.85 and giving an order that replaced the Commissioner’s tariff determination with one classified under TH2206.00.85 until 27 February 2013, transitioning to TH2206.00.90 thereafter.

The Commissioner aimed to challenge the High Court ruling, claiming a conflict with prior jurisprudence that required resolution by the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 1–4]).

### I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 3, 16, 30])

The core issue in this case revolves around the appropriate classification of FABs for excise duty under the applicable legal regime. Specifically, the questions for determination include:

1. Whether the FABs are correctly classifiable under TH2206.00.85, as SAB contends, or if they should be classified under TH2208.90.22, as per the Commissioner’s initial determination.
  
2. The interpretation of definitions related to alcoholic beverages, particularly regarding fermentation and distillation processes, and their implications for classification ([para 3]).

3. The correctness of the High Court's conclusion and the conflict with existing case law regarding tariff interpretation ([paras 4, 16, 30]).

### R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 5–9, 21, 29])

The legal framework relevant to this case is primarily established by section 47 of the Customs and Excise Act, which governs the levying of excise duties on goods. The interpretation of tariff headings and subheadings is guided by:

1. The General Rules for the Interpretation of the Customs Tariff Schedule, particularly GRI 1, which stipulates that classification is primarily determined by the language of the headings and any relevant section or chapter notes.
  
2. The application of the International Convention on the Harmonized Commodity Description and Coding System, and its accompanying explanatory notes, albeit these are secondary to the statutory text ([paras 5–7]).

3. The precedent set in cases such as *Secretary for Customs and Excise v Thomas Barlow and Sons Ltd* and *Distell Ltd and Another v Commissioner of South African Revenue Service*, which have elucidated principles surrounding the classification of goods under the tariff headings.

The principle articulated in *Distell* further emphasizes that where the character of a product corresponds with a clear definition within the tariff headings, it must prevail over general classifications ([para 21, 29]).

### A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 16–30])

In evaluating the classification of the FABs, the Court noted that the processes of fermentation and distillation are critical to determining the appropriate tariff heading. The evidence presented showed that SAB’s FABs were primarily produced through fermentation, which is consistent with the characteristics outlined in TH22.06. 

The Commissioner argued that the FABs could be classified under TH22.08 due to their similarities to “alcoholic lemonade,” but the Court found that this classification would misinterpret the requirements of TH22.08, which relates to spirituous beverages predominantly produced via distillation. The distinction between fermentation and distillation was underscored as fundamental, and the absence of distillation in the production of the FABs supported the argument for classification under TH2206.00.85 ([paras 16–25]).

Additionally, the court considered the application of GRI, concluding that the FABs in question were effectively mixtures of non-alcoholic and fermented alcoholic beverages, thereby falling within the characteristics of TH2206.00.85 prior to the amendment and transitioning to TH2206.00.90 thereafter ([paras 30–31]).

### C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 1, 2, 33])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the Commissioner’s appeal, upholding the High Court's finding that the FABs were correctly classified under TH2206.00.90, with the reasoning that the characteristics of these products aligned with the definitions and statutory requirements of fermentation rather than distillation. 

The ruling also set aside Paragraph 3 of the High Court's order, replacing it with new tariff determinations that better reflected the characteristics of the FABs from the date of the respondent’s determination ([para 2, 33]).

The implications of this ruling extend beyond the immediate financial considerations; it reinforces the importance of precise classification within customs duties and demonstrates the court's commitment to upholding statutory principles in the face of competing interpretations. The ruling serves as a significant reference point for future cases involving product classification and the interplay of statutory interpretation and administrative discretion under the Customs and Excise Act ([paras 1, 4]).
