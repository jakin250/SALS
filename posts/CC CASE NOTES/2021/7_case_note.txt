Source Folder: 2021

# FIRAC Case Note: **Sithole and Another v Sithole and Another [2021] ZACC 7**

## F – Facts ([paras 1–28])

The case before the Constitutional Court of South Africa deals with the constitutional challenge to the validity of section 21(2)(a) of the Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984 (the MPA), primarily initiated by Mrs. Agnes Sithole, the first applicant, and the Commission for Gender Equality, the second applicant. Mrs. Sithole is a 72-year-old housewife from KwaZulu-Natal, who, alongside her husband Gideon Sithole, was married on December 16, 1972, under the provisions of the Black Administration Act 38 of 1927 (the BAA), specifically section 22(6), which stipulated that such marriages were automatically out of community of property.

Despite significant socio-economic contributions, like running a home-based business and funding her children’s education, Mrs. Sithole found herself in a precarious position when her husband threatened to sell their jointly owned house, registered solely in his name. Unaware of her legal standing and without access to sufficient resources to contest the matter through divorce, she sought injunctive relief to prevent the sale of their home, which set the juridical backdrop for her challenge against section 21(2)(a).

The High Court of KwaZulu-Natal recognized that the impugned provisions perpetuated unfair discrimination against Black women and declared them unconstitutional, leading to the matter being escalated to the Constitutional Court.

## I – Issues ([paras 29–40])

The Constitutional Court grappled with several pivotal legal issues:

1. **Constitutionality of the MPA Provisions**: Whether section 21(2)(a) of the MPA, which maintained a discriminatory default position for marriages concluded under the BAA, was constitutional.
   
2. **Discrimination Grounds**: Whether the provision constituted unfair discrimination on the basis of race, gender, and socio-economic status, undermining the equality guarantees enshrined in section 9 of the South African Constitution.

3. **Justification of Discrimination**: If discrimination was established, could it be justified under the limitations clause contained within section 36 of the Constitution?

4. **Remedial Orders**: What remedies should the Court impose, given the findings related to the unfair discrimination attributable to the legislative provisions?

## R – Rules ([paras 41–65])

The Court's judgment was anchored in various legal principles and precedents:

1. **Equality and Non-Discrimination**: Section 9 of the South African Constitution provides that no one may be discriminated against on the grounds listed therein, which includes race and gender ([para 44]). The Court emphasized a two-stage enquiry to assess whether a differentiation constitutes unfair discrimination as articulated in *Harksen v Lane N.O.* [1998] (1) SA 300 (CC).

2. **Intersectionality**: The Court acknowledged that laws can have intersecting impacts, particularly on marginalized groups, including Black women, who suffer compounded forms of disadvantage based on multiple intersecting identities ([paras 50–55]).

3. **Remedial Equality**: Citing rulings such as *National Coalition for Gay and Lesbian Equality v Minister of Justice* [1999] ZACC 17 and *Mahlangu v Minister of Labour* [2020] ZACC 24, the Court noted that substantive equality does not merely demand formal equality but also the removal of barriers that hinder equal enjoyment of rights ([paras 58–63]).

4. **Historical Context of Discrimination**: The Court recognized the legislative history of systemic discrimination under apartheid, which continued to impact marginalized groups and aligned its findings with the Constitution’s overarching commitment to human dignity and equality.

## A – Application ([paras 66–134])

In applying these rules to the case at hand, the Court concluded that:

1. **Discriminatory Nature of the Provision**: The Court established that section 21(2)(a) perpetuated the same discriminatory conditions set out in section 22(6) of the BAA, disadvantaging Black couples and particularly women who remained uninformed or incapable of changing their matrimonial property regimes ([paras 73–80]).

2. **Failure to Justify Discrimination**: The government, tasked with justifying any alleged discrimination, failed to provide compelling reasons demonstrating that the differentiation based on race and gender was warranted or beneficial ([paras 82–85]).

3. **Intersectional Discrimination**: The Court found that the legislative framework disproportionately affected Black women by reinforcing socio-economic vulnerabilities, leading to inadequacies in property ownership rights during marriage, thereby infringing their rights to dignity, property, and equality ([paras 95–112]).

4. **Remedial Orders and Retrospective Effect**: Subsequently, the Court ruled the provisions unconstitutional and declared that marriages previously categorized as out of community of property under the BAA should henceforth be classified as marriages in community of property, thus providing equitable remedies for Black couples while incorporating mechanisms for opting out ([paras 114–131]).

## C – Conclusion ([paras 135–141])

The Constitutional Court, in a unanimous decision penned by Justice Tshiqi, concluded that section 21(2)(a) of the MPA was indeed unconstitutional as it preserved the remnants of racial discrimination from the apartheid era, hence denying substantive equality to Black couples. 

The Court confirmed the High Court’s order declaring section 21(2)(a) invalid and mandated that all applicable marriages should be converted to community of property unless couples expressly opted otherwise. The ruling not only serves as a significant rectification of historical injustices but reinforces the guiding constitutional tenets of dignity, equality, and the right to property for all South Africans.

This decision contributes to the ongoing discourse on intersectional discrimination and the imperative for legislative reform, underscoring the judiciary's role in dismantling systemic injustices entrenched in the legal fabric of South Africa post-apartheid. The immediate implications affect hundreds of thousands of Black women who had endured the inequitable ramifications of the historical legislative framework. 

The order further stressed the need for the Department of Justice to take proactive measures to inform affected parties about their rights, advocating for greater awareness and legal protections moving forward.
