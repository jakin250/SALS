Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note for President of the Republic of South Africa and Others v South African Dental Association and Another [2015] ZACC 2

## F – Facts

The case stems from an application for direct access to the Constitutional Court under section 167(6)(a) of the Constitution. This section allows a person to bring a matter directly to the Constitutional Court when it is in the interests of justice. The application arose following the President's Proclamation (No. 21 of 2014), which sought to bring sections 36 to 40 of the National Health Act 61 of 2003 into operation on 1 April 2014, a month after its issuance on 21 March 2014 ([1]–[3]). The sections prohibiting the provision of health services without a 'certificate of need' penalised health service providers, rendering their current operations unlawful as the required regulations for obtaining such certificates were still pending ([4]–[5]).

The President, along with various ministers and their Director-Generals, claimed that the Proclamation was issued in error due to mistaken advice and sought an order declaring it invalid based on its irrationality ([2]–[6]). The South African Dental Association (SADA) and the Hospital Association of South Africa (HASA) supported this claim, arguing that the Proclamation compromised the ability of health service providers to operate legally ([8]).

## I – Issues

The central constitutional issue concerned whether the President’s decision to issue the Proclamation was rational and legally valid given that it commenced crucial legislative provisions absent necessary regulatory frameworks ([11]–[12]). Specifically, the Court had to evaluate:
1. **Was the Proclamation issued in accordance with the rule of law?**
2. **Did the exercise of public power by the President have a rational basis?** 
3. **Was it in the interests of justice for the Court to grant direct access and rule on this matter?** ([10]).

## R – Rules

The court relied primarily on the constitutional principles enshrined in sections 1 (supremacy of the Constitution), 7 (rights protection), and 172 (court's power to declare laws invalid) ([7]–[8]). 

1. **Legality and Rationality**: The exercise of public power must not be arbitrary and must be rationally connected to the purpose for which that power was granted, as established in Pharmaceutical Manufacturers Association of South Africa and Others v President of the Republic of South Africa ([13]–[14]).
2. **Direct Access**: The factors determining whether direct access is in the interests of justice include the urgency of the ruling, whether factual disputes exist that may complicate proceedings, and whether the matter could be resolved in lower courts or requires the Constitutional Court's intervention ([9]).

## A – Application

The court noted that the Proclamation’s premature nature led to a situation where healthcare providers were inadvertently criminalised, as they could not comply with provisions that necessitated regulations not yet promulgated ([11]). The President's explanation of the Proclamation being issued in error did not absolve the legal ramifications of the act. The inability to withdraw the Proclamation further compounded the issues, necessitating judicial review ([12]).

The court examined whether the President's decision to bring the law into effect was rationally related to the intended orderly implementation of health services regulations. It concluded that issuing the Proclamation without the regulatory framework directly undermined its purpose, thus failing the rationalitytest established in previous case law ([15]–[16]). The court reaffirmed that while the intent might have been good, the outcome created a legal void, rendering health service provision illegal. This irrationality justified the court's intervention in declaring the Proclamation invalid ([17]).

Moreover, the considerations for granting direct access were convincingly met, given the urgency of the matter—delaying the proceedings could have had dire consequences for health service provision and regulatory compliance in South Africa. The absence of factual disputes further supported the appropriateness of the Court hearing the matter directly ([10]).

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court granted direct access and declared Proclamation 21 of 2014 invalid, effectively setting it aside ([18]). The ruling emphasized the importance of ensuring that legislative provisions aligning with public health regulations must not operate absent a reasonable regulatory framework. In doing so, the Court reaffirmed its role in safeguarding the rule of law and ensuring that public power is exercised rationally, which resonates with the constitutional mandate to uphold the rights to health and safety for the South African populace.

This case underscores the significance of compliance with parliamentary procedures and the implications of executive decisions in the governance of public health policy. The ruling also reinforces the necessity for clear regulatory guidelines preceding the implementation of laws to prevent inadvertent violations of legal norms impacting health service delivery. The judgment serves as a crucial precedent for future cases involving the intersection of public administration, health law, and constitutional validity.
