Constitutional Court 2018 [2018] ZACC 49

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The South African Veterinary Association (SAVA), a body representing over a thousand veterinarians, sought direct access to the Constitutional Court to challenge the validity of the Medicines and Related Substances Amendment Act of 2015, specifically concerning the insertion of the word “veterinarian” in section 22C(1)(a) of the Medicines and Related Substances Act 101 of 1965 ([1]–[2]). SAVA argued that Parliament did not meet its constitutional obligation to facilitate public participation during the legislative process leading to this amendment, violating sections 59(1)(a), 72(1)(a), and 118(1)(a) of the Constitution ([2]-[3]).

Prior to the Amendment Act’s introduction, veterinarians were not required to possess a license to compound and dispense medicines, a key shift arising from the inclusion of the term after public hearings had already been conducted, thereby denying prior opportunity for public engagement ([4]–[7]). Public hearings were held in different provinces, but importantly, there was a lack of consultation with veterinarian stakeholders following the amendment that affected their professional operations ([13]–[14]). Consequently, SAVA contended that the amendment process was procedurally improper due to insufficient public participation, and SAVA sought a remedy declaring the amendment constitutionally invalid ([47]).

I – Issues

The case presented several legal and constitutional questions:

  1. Should the Constitutional Court grant direct access to SAVA based on the alleged failure of Parliament to fulfill its constitutional obligations regarding public participation?
  2. Did Parliament breach its constitutional duty to facilitate public involvement in the law-making process concerning the insertion of “veterinarian” into the Medicines and Related Substances Act?
  3. What appropriate remedy should be granted if the Court finds that Parliament failed to comply with its constitutional obligations? ([5]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal rules and authorities cited by the Court include:

  • Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996: Sections 59(1)(a) (obligation of the National Assembly to facilitate public involvement), 72(1)(a) (obligation of the National Council of Provinces to facilitate public involvement), and 118(1)(a) (provincial legislatures’ obligation for public involvement) ([2]–[3]);
  • Case law:
  • Doctors for Life International v Speaker of the National Assembly (2006) ZACC 11 established a justiciable duty to facilitate public participation ([19]);
  • Democratic Alliance v Masondo N.O. emphasized that the Constitution envisions a pluralistic democracy requiring the inclusion of diverse voices in legislative processes ([22]).

Together, these rules underscored the principle that public participation is an essential component of South Africa's constitutional democracy, significantly relevant to scrutinizing parliamentary conduct in the law-making process.

A – Application

The Court first determined that direct access was appropriate and exercised its exclusive jurisdiction under section 167(4)(e) of the Constitution, given that Parliament's obligations are fundamental ([15]–[17]). It agreed with SAVA’s assertion that the amendment process leading to the insertion of the word “veterinarian” lacked adequate public participation. Notably, critical discussions regarding this amendment did not occur in any of the public hearings preceding the amendment, and the absence of consultation specifically targeting veterinarians constituted a failure of the constitutional duty ([24]–[32]).

The Court highlighted that the nature of the amendment—introducing a licensing requirement for veterinarians—had far-reaching implications, qualifying it as a substantive change rather than a mere technical amendment ([27]). In reviewing the conduct of the National Assembly (NA) and the National Council of Provinces (NCOP), the Court noted that no reasonable opportunity was provided for public participation in light of the significant amendment that was introduced after official public hearings had concluded ([24]–[46]).

Further, the court examined procedural compliance with parliamentary rules, ultimately finding the absence of due process in the legislative procedure concerning the veterinary profession's inclusion ([26]–[30]). It also emphasized the necessity of reasonableness and consultation with directly affected stakeholders, and ruled that both the NA and the NCOP failed to meet these requirements ([34]–[46]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court unanimously concluded that the NA's and the NCOP's failure to facilitate adequate public participation regarding the amendment was unconstitutional. Therefore, the Court ordered that the word “veterinarian” be severed from section 22C(1)(a) of the Medicines and Related Substances Act, declaring this insertion invalid under section 172(1)(a) of the Constitution ([52]).

The judgment specified that while the insertion was void, the rest of the legislative provision would remain intact, allowing for the continued functioning of provisions applicable to other health professionals. The Court imposed costs against the NA and the NCOP, recognizing their shared responsibility for the flawed process ([54]–[55]). This case reinforces the fundamental role of public participation in South Africa's constitutional framework, yielding significant implications for both future legislative processes and the regulation of professions.
