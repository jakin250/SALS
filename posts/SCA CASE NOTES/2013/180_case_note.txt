Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# Case Note: I O Tech Manufacturing (Pty) Ltd v Gallagher Group Ltd [2013] ZASCA 180

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–2])

The case revolves around a patent infringement action initiated by Gallagher Group Ltd and its exclusive licensee, Gallagher Power Fence (SA) (Pty) Ltd (collectively referred to as "the respondents"), against I O Tech Manufacturing (Pty) Ltd (first appellant), Nemtek (Pty) Ltd (second appellant), and Nemtek Contracting (Pty) Ltd (third appellant, collectively "the appellants"). The respondents hold South African Patent No. 96/6799, titled "Safety Operation for a Security Device," aimed at ensuring safety regulations in electric fencing technology by coordinating energy pulse emissions from multiple energisers ([para 1]).

The appellants manufacture and sell energisers designed for electric fences and contested the infringement claims brought against them. A counterclaim for revocation of the patent was postponed, leading to the central focus on alleged direct and indirect infringement of the patent's claims, particularly claims 1 and 2, during the trial in the Court of the Commissioner of Patents ([para 2]).

The initial ruling granted the respondents interdictory relief against the appellants and ordered the delivery of certain products, without detailed findings on damages, but was contested on appeal ([para 2]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 3–4])

The primary legal question addressed in this case is whether the respondents successfully proved that the appellants engaged in direct infringement of the patent in question. This inquiry involves the interpretation and application of the essential elements or integers defined in the patent claims. Specifically, s 45(1) of the Patents Act 57 of 1978 outlines the rights granted to patentee to exclude others from using their inventions, raising the legal standard for establishing infringement ([para 3]).

Additionally, the court needed to determine whether the evidence presented by the respondents met the necessary legal threshold to assert that all essential integers of the patent claims were present in the appellants' products, thereby constituting infringement ([para 4]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 4–6])

To evaluate the infringement claim, the court applied the two-stage inquiry established in *Johnson & Johnson (Proprietary) Limited v Kimberly-Clark Corporation* [1985] BP 126 (A), which mandates the proper construction of the claims followed by a rigorous assessment of whether the accused products embody each essential integer of those claims ([para 4]).

The standard of proof regarding patent infringement, as cited by the court, requires that all elements of the claim must be satisfied. In *Rodi & Wienenberger A G v Henry Showell Ltd* [1966] RPC 441 (CA), it is emphasized that infringement occurs only if each integer of the patent claims is present in the offending product, thereby defining the scope of the patentee's rights ([para 4]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 8–16])

The Supreme Court of Appeal undertook a critical analysis of the evidence presented. The respondents relied heavily on the testimony of Mr J T Raubenheimer, who purported to prove infringement through testing of the Merlin energisers, which were at the center of the dispute ([para 8]).

The court found significant shortcomings in Raubenheimer's evidence, particularly regarding his expertise—despite being an electrical engineer, his limited qualifications and experience in conducting relevant tests undermined the probative value of his opinion ([para 12]). His tests were conducted using an oscilloscope without a valid calibration certificate, further questioning the reliability of his findings and indicating that critical integers of the claims were not established, notably:

1. The coordination of energisers in restricting pulse magnitude within predetermined parameters ([para 13]); 
2. The assertion that energisers could operate independently until reset by a synchronisation signal ([para 15]).

Consequently, the court determined that no infringement was proven because not all essential integers of claims 1 and 2 were satisfied, leading to the conclusion that the court of first instance erred in its interpretation and application of the patent law ([para 16]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 17–18])

The Supreme Court of Appeal's holding was that the appeal was upheld, and the prior judgment was set aside, with the respondents' claim dismissed. The court emphasized the importance of rigorous evidentiary standards in patent infringement cases, asserting that failure to substantiate claims with credible evidence leads to dismissal ([para 18]).

The decision serves as a critical reminder that patent holders must ensure rigorous proof of infringement allegations, reinforcing the legal precedent governing the requisite elements of patent claims. Furthermore, the judgment imposes a necessity for precise and substantiated evidence in patent litigations, asserting the need for clear connections between the alleged infringing products and the claimed patent integers, thus shaping future cases in South African patent law.

This case illustrates the balance of protecting patent rights while simultaneously safeguarding against unfounded infringement claims, maintaining the integrity of intellectual property law in South Africa.
