Source Folder: 1999

# Case Note: Molefe Pius Morebudi v The State (Case No. 547/98)

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The present appeal arises from a drug offence case in which Molefe Pius Morebudi (the appellant) was convicted of dealing in dagga, in contravention of section 5(b) of the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 140 of 1992, alongside a co-accused. The conviction took place in the Regional Court of Pretoria, where the appellant received a sentence of 14 years’ imprisonment, and the co-accused received 7 years. Following this, both parties appealed to the Transvaal Provincial Division of the High Court, which dismissed their appeal against conviction and sentenced. The appellant's further appeal, limited to the sentence, was heard before the Supreme Court of Appeal on 16 November 1999 and judgment was delivered on 26 November 1999 ([paras 1–2]).

The facts of the case established that on 28 June 1995, the South African Narcotics Bureau (SANAB) stopped a vehicle owned by the appellant, a mechanical horse and trailer, and discovered 1433 kg of dagga hidden in a secret compartment within the trailer. The vehicle was under the control of Kgosana, the driver, who absconded shortly after the incident. The appellant claimed to be operating a legitimate business selling fruit and vegetables in Lesotho and Transkei, asserting ignorance concerning the dagga hidden in his vehicle. However, the magistrate rejected this defense, inferring that the business was merely a façade for the drug operation given the covert nature of the trailer’s modifications. The previous conviction of the appellant for a similar offence dating back approximately 20 years also played a role in the magistrate's sentencing decisions ([paras 2–6]).

### I – Issues
The central issue of this appeal is whether the sentence of 14 years’ imprisonment imposed on the appellant was appropriate, given the circumstances of the case, including his previous conviction and the quantity and nature of the drug involved. Additionally, the appeal examines whether the magistrate misdirected himself when considering the seriousness of the offence and the appellant's personal circumstances in the sentencing process ([paras 6–15]).

### R – Rules
The court referred to multiple legal principles and precedents in its judgment. 

1. **Previous Convictions**: Section 271A of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 provides for the lapse of certain previous convictions after 10 years; however, the appellant’s conviction did not fall under this category and thus did not lapse ([para 7]).

2. **Sentencing Guidelines**: The court relied on the principles outlined in *S v Nkombini* 1990 (2) SACR 465 (Tk) regarding the seriousness of offences involving substantial quantities of drugs. The sentence of 10 to 15 years is typically reserved for serious offenders who organize large-scale drug operations, as clarified by earlier cases including *S v Smith en Andere* 1978 (3) SA 749 (A) ([paras 8–11]).

3. **Proportionality in Sentencing**: The appellate court indicated that for it to interfere with a sentence, it must induce a sense of shock or represent a striking disparity compared to what it would have imposed ([para 15]).

### A – Application
In considering the appeal, the court rigorously analyzed the reasons provided by the magistrate for imposing the 14-year sentence. The appellant contended that the magistrate had erred by overemphasizing the previous conviction, treating him as a major dealer or "Mafia-type organiser," and not adequately considering personal circumstances, including his age, family status, and financial situation.

1. **Previous Conviction**: The court agreed with the magistrate's evaluation that while the previous conviction was old, it was relevant for assessing the appellant's awareness of the consequences of his actions. The magistrate correctly found that the appellant had not heedfully changed his behaviour since the earlier conviction ([paras 6–7]).

2. **Seriousness of the Offence**: The court compared the facts of this case to those in *S v Nkombini* and *S v Smith en Andere*. The detailed and meticulous planning evident in the vehicle modifications suggested that this was not a simple act of dealing, but part of organized drug trafficking. The sheer quantity of dagga also indicated the seriousness of the offence ([paras 8–11]).

3. **Personal Circumstances**: While the judge acknowledged the appellant’s personal circumstances, including his age and familial responsibilities, it was emphasized that previous behaviour demonstrated a disregard for the law. There was no indication that the appellant had reformed or intended to alter his conduct in light of the previous conviction. The sentence served not only as a penalty but also as a deterrent for similar future offences ([paras 12–15]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appellant's appeal against the sentence, confirming the 14 years’ imprisonment imposed by the magistrate. The court concluded that there were no misdirections in the sentencing process significant enough to warrant intervention. The operation was classified as serious, and the sentence was deemed appropriate given the nature of the crime, the substantial quantity of drugs involved, and the appellant's history of prior offences. The decision underscores the judiciary's commitment to addressing narcotics trafficking rigorously, particularly when previous offences and the degree of planning are evident ([paras 15–15]).

This case highlights the judiciary's approach to sentences in drug-related offences in South Africa, aligning with legislative frameworks aimed at curbing drug trafficking while balancing considerations of previous behaviours and personal circumstances within sentencing discretion.
