Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

## FIRAC Case Note: Naidoo v Sunker (126/11) [2011] ZASCA 216

### F – Facts

The case **Naidoo v Sunker** involves a dispute over the unlawful occupation of property, specifically the Remainder of Portion 1 of the farm Umkomanzi Drift No 1357 in KwaZulu-Natal. The appellants, Rajagopaul Veneketas Naidoo (First Appellant) and Mrs. Naidoo (Second Appellant), have resided on the property for over 40 years. They claim to have a lifelong right of tenancy based on an oral agreement with the previous owner, C.S. Naidoo, who permitted them to live on the property at a nominal rental fee ([paras 1-4]).

The respondents, Rabind Sunker, Muthu Pillay, Rukmany Pillay, and Surima Investments CC, purchased the property from Naidoo's heirs in February 1998. They were aware of the Naidoos' occupation during the transaction but claimed they were notified that the appellants were informal tenants without a formal lease ([paras 7-8]).

Initially, the appellants were ordered to vacate the property by October 15, 2009, through a ruling from the KwaZulu-Natal High Court, which was later upheld by the Full Court ([paras 3-4]). Following the failure of the Naidoos to vacate, the respondents initiated further proceedings in 2008, leading to an application for eviction ([paras 10-15]).

### I – Issues

The principal legal issues are:

1. Whether the appellants are entitled to the protections afforded by the **Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997** (ESTA) and the **Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998** (PIE) given their long-term occupation.
2. Whether the first appellant had any form of valid lease or occupancy agreement that would bind the respondents in their claims for eviction ([paras 17-20]).

### R – Rules

The court relied upon several legal statutes and precedents, including:

1. **Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA)**: This Act protects unlawful occupiers, requiring a procedural framework to be followed before eviction can occur. It defines an "occupier" and outlines the conditions under which eviction is permissible ([para 18]).

2. **Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE)**: Similar to ESTA, PIE lays down procedures safeguarding against unlawful eviction from residential property by requiring that all relevant circumstances are considered by the court prior to eviction ([para 26]).

3. **Case Precedents**: The court referred to *Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd* (1984) which establishes the presumptive truth of a respondent's affidavits in motion proceedings, and *Wightman t/a J W Construction v Headfour (Pty) Ltd* (2008), discussing the requirements for raising special defences ([paras 22-23]).

### A – Application

The court assessed the appellants' arguments concerning the applicability of ESTA and PIE. The appellants claimed they held rights under ESTA but did not explicitly invoke it in their pleadings, which the court deemed critical. The absence of a written agreement and their failure to follow established procedures meant that they could not establish a credible defence under ESTA ([paras 18-20]).

On the issue of the respondents’ knowledge of the appellants’ claim to a long-term lease, their lack of substantiated detail weakened their position. The appellants merely asserted the existence of such knowledge without providing sufficient evidentiary support ([paras 22-24]).

The court also pointed out that while socially protective legislation must be interpreted fairly, the appellants had not presented any compelling evidence to rehabilitate their claim. This critique was critical because the protections expected under these legislative frameworks require proactive engagement and proper legal recognition ([para 21]).

In final evaluation, the court noted that the respondents had complied with all requisite legal procedures for eviction under PIE, emphasizing the necessity for consideration of the financial and social dimensions of the appellants' situation but ultimately endorsing the eviction due to their failed defences ([para 26]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the order of the court a quo for the appellants to vacate the property. The appellants were ordered to leave by February 29, 2012. The court concluded that the appellants had not provided a bona fide defence concerning their occupancy rights, nor had they sufficiently demonstrated compliance with the statutory obligations imposed by ESTA and PIE.

The ruling has significant implications for the protection of long-term occupiers, underscoring the requirement for clear documentation and procedural adherence within the frameworks of property law in South Africa ([paras 26-27]). The case serves as a cautionary tale for individuals claiming rights to property based on informal agreements, highlighting the importance of formalising such arrangements to secure legal protection.
