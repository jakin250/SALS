Supreme Court of Appeal

Pappalardo v Hau (63/08) [2009] ZASCA 160

After the respondent noticed rainwater accumulation in December 2003, discussions between the parties regarding modification of the boundary wall to facilitate drainage took place but ended unsuccessfully, leading to the respondent's legal action in August 2004.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009 [2009] ZASCA 160

F – Facts

The dispute in Pappalardo v Hau arose in the context of drainage and the rights of property owners in an urban setting. The parties, Gianfelice Pappalardo (Appellant) and Gary Hau (Respondent), own adjoining erven in Waterford Estate, an upscale township near Sandton, where the properties slope from south to north ([para 1-2]). The appellant, as owner of Erf 944, constructed a boundary wall that the respondent claimed is obstructing rainwater from draining from his property, Erf 945, leading to flooding ([para 1-3]).

After the respondent noticed rainwater accumulation in December 2003, discussions between the parties regarding modification of the boundary wall to facilitate drainage took place but ended unsuccessfully, leading to the respondent's legal action in August 2004. Hau sought a declaratory order allowing the insertion of drainage pipes into the boundary wall to direct water from his property onto that of the Appellant. The respondent’s claim was based on the principle that as the owner of the lower lying land, the appellant was obliged to accept the natural flow of rainwater ([para 4]). The South Gauteng High Court ruled in favor of Hau, prompting Pappalardo's appeal ([para 1, 6]).

I – Issues

The main legal issues the Supreme Court of Appeal had to address were:

  1. Whether the lower property owner is obliged to accept rainwater flowing onto their property from a higher-lying neighbour.
  2. The definitional scope of "natural flow" in the context of urban development, specifically whether the appellant could be compelled to accept water that does not flow naturally due to alterations made during construction.
  3. The application of the Sandton Town Planning Scheme (§13(2)) pertaining to drainage practices and the obligations it imposes on property owners ([paras 1, 20-21]).

R – Rules

The primary legal principles derived from Roman-Dutch law regarding the flow of water between properties were integral to the court's reasoning. It is well entrenched that a lower property owner must accept naturally flowing water from a higher property owner, provided that flow has not been significantly altered by construction or other artificial means ([para 6]).

Key precedents included:

  • Williams v Harris 1998 (3) SA 970 (SCA), which affirmed that while the right to discharge water exists, the upper owner must prove what constitutes the "natural flow" ([para 14]).
  • Bishop v Humphries 1919 WLD 13 and Green v Borstel 1940 (2) PH M 89, which addressed the limitations of that right in urban settings and emphasized the obligation of the higher owner to avoid artificially concentrating runoff ([paras 8-11]).

Additionally, the provisions of §13(2) in the Sandton Town Planning Scheme indicated scenarios under which water must be drained to the street where practical ([para 20]).

A – Application

In reviewing the lower court's findings, the Supreme Court highlighted several inaccuracies in how the law was applied. The court noted that the lower court accepted Hau's claim that water would naturally flow to the appellant's property without investigating the actual historical flow patterns prior to development, particularly in an urban context ([paras 9-10]).

The Supreme Court concluded that the respondent failed to prove the existence of a clear "natural flow" of water from his property to that of the appellant, a critical element underlined in earlier cases. Moreover, the court examined the provisions of the Sandton Town Planning Scheme, indicating that if it is practicable to drain stormwater directly to the street, that method must be preferred ([para 20]). They noted that evidence suggested alternative drainage solutions were feasible without encumbering the appellant ([para 20]).

The court also observed that the duties imposed by the national building regulations concerning drainage might not have been considered adequately in the lower court’s judgment, leading to a potentially erroneous conclusion regarding the liabilities of the appellant ([para 21]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the decision of the South Gauteng High Court, ruling that the respondent had not established his entitlement to impose an artificial drainage system on the appellant’s property without clear evidence of the historical natural flow of water. The court held that the order for the lower court’s declaratory relief was improperly granted, and therefore, the appeal was upheld with costs. The implications of this ruling reaffirm the principles of neighbour law, particularly in urban contexts, stipulating that property owners must substantiate their claims regarding water flow and drainage, considering the complexities implemented by urban development ([paras 2, 22]). The judgment reiterates the necessity for precise legal evidence in matters of property law involving water rights and drains, ensuring both parties understand their rights and obligations under both common law and local statutes.

This FIRAC case note interprets the court's reasoning and effectively encapsulates the legal principles pertaining to neighbour law relevant to drainage disputes in urban environments, providing a comprehensive analysis suitable for academic and professional use.
