Source Folder: 2005

# FIRAC Case Note: Republic of South Africa (Distcor Export Partners and Another v Director-General of the Department of Trade and Industry)

## F – Facts

In the case of **Distcor Export Partners and Another v Director-General of the Department of Trade and Industry**, Case No. 521/03 ([1]), the appellants, Distcor Export Partners and Distcor Yacht Exporters, faced litigation from the Director-General of the Department of Trade and Industry (the "Department"). This litigation arose from claims for delictual damages or unjustified enrichment resulting from payments made by the Department to the appellants under the General Export Incentive Scheme (GEIS) in 1992 and 1994 ([1]). The appellants contended that the Director-General lacked *locus standi in iudicio* to institute claims against them, arguing that the State, as a legal entity, could only sue in its own name ([1]-[2]).

The procedural history indicates that the appellants raised a special plea regarding the Director-General's standing before the court, which was initially dismissed by McLaren J in the lower court. The case then proceeded as an appeal against this dismissal, leading to a hearing on 5 November 2004 ([1]). 

## I – Issues

The primary issue in this appeal was whether the Director-General, as nominal plaintiff, had the requisite authority to bring action on behalf of the State against the appellants for alleged damages ([1], [7]). The appellants argued that the legal procedure required the State to litigate in its own name and not through one of its officials ([2]). Further, the appellants questioned whether the Director-General represented the State adequately and whether a nominal plaintiff could sue without a sufficient interest in the matter, as is typically required for *locus standi* ([6])-([9]).

## R – Rules

The judgment hinged on several legal principles regarding the authority of State officials and the State's capacity to sue. The court referred to previous cases such as *Die Spoorbond and Another v South African Railways* (1946 AD 999) to establish that while the State possesses a juristic personality, it does not operate like a corporation ([3]). The court also noted the State Liability Act 20 of 1957, specifically section 2, which allows for a Department’s Minister to be cited as a nominal defendant ([5]). 

These legal principles suggest a flexible approach concerning the authority of State officials to bring litigation in their capacity as representatives of their respective departments, a practice accepted in various cases where heads of specialized units did not require express statutory authorization to institute proceedings ([8]).

## A – Application

In its analysis, the court examined whether the Director-General acted within his authority in suing as a nominal plaintiff. The judgment emphasized that litigation conducted *nomine officii* does not attribute personal agency to the official; rather, the official embodies the department ([4]), with the understanding that the authority to sue derives from their role and responsibility within the department ([12]). The court noted that such a structure was particularly relevant in cases like this one, where the Director-General had the ultimate discretion and responsibility over the GEIS [10].

Moreover, the court indicated that questions of internal departmental authority were secondary to the legal authority vested in the Director-General; as long as a claim could demonstrate that the Director-General had been given explicit or implicit authority through departmental guidelines, the standing issue could be resolved in favor of the nominal plaintiff ([7]). This flexibility underlines that the reasoning behind adopting the special procedure was not unprecedented, particularly when a departmental head had overall control and ended the decision-making process concerning the case ([10]-[11]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately affirmed the lower court’s decision, establishing that the Director-General did possess the requisite standing to sue the appellants in this instance ([1]). The court's ruling confirmed the capacity of State departments to litigate through appointed officials without undermining the principles of legal personality attributed to the State. Furthermore, this decision has significant implications for how State entities might pursue claims in similar circumstances, confirming the flexibility in the interpretation of *locus standi* and reinforcing the legal authority of departmental heads to act on behalf of their departments in litigation ([12]). The appeal was dismissed, with costs awarded to the respondent, further illustrating the legal efficacy of administrative officials acting on behalf of State entities in litigation ([1]).
