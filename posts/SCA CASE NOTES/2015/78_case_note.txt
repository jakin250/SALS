# Case Note: Etraction (Pty) Ltd v Tyrecor (Pty) Ltd (20185/2014) 2015 ZASCA 78

### FIRAC Case Note

#### F – Facts
The appellant, Etraction (Pty) Ltd ("Etraction"), operates in the business sector dealing with wheels, tyres, and related products and is the registered proprietor of the trade mark **INFINITY** under the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, with registration number 2008/08612, effective from 15 April 2008 ([1]). Etraction claimed it had consistently used the mark since **1995**, primarily in relation to rims ([4]), but did not provide evidence of its use on tyres.

The respondent, Tyrecor (Pty) Ltd ("Tyrecor"), began importing and selling tyres under the name **Infinity** as early as **2006**, initially through its predecessor, Falck Trading (Pty) Ltd ([2], [9]). Tyrecor became the primary seller of these tyres from **early 2009** onwards ([2]). Etraction sought to interdict Tyrecor from using the **Infinity** mark, asserting trademark infringement ([1]).

In the initial hearing, the High Court dismissed Etraction’s application for an interdict and granted Tyrecor's counter-application to expunge the reference to **“tyres”** from Etraction’s mark ([1]). Etraction then appealed this decision ([1]).

#### I – Issues
The core issues in this case revolved around:
1. Whether Tyrecor's use of **Infinity** constituted an infringement of Etraction’s registered trademark.
2. Whether Tyrecor could successfully claim a defense under section 36(1) of the Trade Marks Act for prior bona fide usage.
3. The legitimacy of Etraction's intention to use the trademark in relation to tyres as required under section 27(1) of the Act ([3], [26]).

#### R – Rules
The legal framework applicable to the case primarily stems from the **Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993**, which governs trademark registration, infringement, and expungement handles the following:

- **Section 34(1)(a)**: A registered mark is infringed by unauthorized use of an identical mark in the course of trade regarding goods the mark protects ([3]).
- **Section 36(1)**: This section protects against claims of infringement if a person or their predecessor has made continuous bona fide use of a mark prior to the registration of the contested mark ([11])[16]).
- **Section 27(1)(a)**: A registered trademark can be expunged if there was no bona fide intention to use it in relation to registered goods ([26]).

The court also referenced precedents that define bona fide or genuine use and provide insights into establishing reputational rights through prior usage in cases of perceived infringement ([32]).

#### A – Application
The court applied the facts to the relevant legal standards as follows:

1. **Infringement Analysis**: Given that Tyrecor began using the **Infinity** mark for tyres prior to Etraction’s claim of usage in relation to the same goods, the infringement claim based on unauthorized use was substantiated ([4], [10]). Etraction's own evidence did not supporting its usage of the mark for tyres, leaving Tyrecor's prior use, particularly under Falck, as a valid counter to Etraction’s claims ([8]).

2. **Bona Fide Use**: The court found that Tyrecor sufficiently demonstrated continuous and bona fide use of the mark since 2006. Tyrecor’s established sales and advertising figures quantified the extensive market presence of **Infinity** tyres, overshadowing Etraction's claims ([10], [21]). 

3. **Intent to Use**: Etraction's application for the registration of the **INFINITY** trade mark for tyres was deemed strategic and not genuine. The court highlighted instances suggesting Etraction acted with ulterior motives, especially due to the timing of its registration request shortly after becoming aware of Tyrecor’s operations ([31], [32]). The court concluded that Etraction's conduct indicated an intention to disrupt Tyrecor's legitimate business rather than a good faith intention to trade in tyres under the mark, which is fundamental for maintaining a trademark ([32]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Etraction's appeal against the High Court's ruling that referred to Tyrecor’s claims of prior usage and the expungement order for the term **“tyres”** within Etraction’s trademark registration ([24], [38]). The court affirmed that Tyrecor was, through its predecessor, Falck, the rightful user of the **Infinity** mark in relation to tyres, which negated Etraction's infringement claim. 

The decision illustrates the significance of establishing genuine intention and continuous use in trademark disputes. Additionally, it solidifies judicial precedent regarding the interpretation of bona fide use under the Trade Marks Act, thereby having implications for future cases concerning trademark rights, defenses, and registrations.
