Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: Moor v Tongaat-Hulett Pension Fund (518/17)

## F – Facts:
The case **Moor v Tongaat-Hulett Pension Fund** ([2018] ZASCA 83) emerged from a dispute initiated by appellants Bruce St Clair Moor and Willem Jan Hazewindus, both former members and trustees of the Tongaat-Hulett Pension Fund ([1]). This Fund was established following the unbundling of Tongaat-Hulett Limited and Hulamin Limited, with a regulatory approval by the Registrar of Pension Funds on 13 August 2013, which validated its restructuring and scheme ([2]-[3]). The appellants challenged the Fund's allocation of surplus assets amounting to R363.2 million to the Employer Surplus Account (ESA) in 2012, arguing that the allocation contravened section 15C of the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956 (PFA) ([1]-[4]). 

The core of the appellants’ complaint was that the distribution failed to adequately account for the interests of all stakeholders, especially retired members. The allocation to the ESA was based on a rule (Rule 11.5.4.1(b)) which allowed for the transfer of excess assets, but the appellants argued that this should have been governed by the stricter requirements set forth in section 15C of the PFA, which they contended emphasized the need for a lawful apportionment of actuarial surpluses ([7]-[9]).

The Kwazulu-Natal Local Division dismissed their application, leading to the subsequent appeal ([1]). The key issues revolved around the legality of the allocation made under a rule intended for distributing 'excess assets' rather than specifically addressing 'actuarial surplus' as outlined in section 15C ([4]-[5]).

## I – Issues:
The primary legal questions presented in this case were:

1. Did the Tongaat-Hulett Defined Benefit Pension Fund contravene the provisions of section 15C of the PFA when allocating R363.2 million to the ESA in 2012? ([1]-[7])
2. Was the allocation to the ESA based on 'excess assets' as opposed to 'actuarial surplus' compliant with the legislative framework established by the PFA? ([10]-[19])
3. Did the Board of Trustees act with bias or conflict of interest when approving the surplus allocation? ([33])

## R – Rules:
The relevant legal rules established by the court include:

1. **Section 15C of the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956**: This section mandates that any actuarial surplus must be apportioned fairly and in recognition of the interests of all stakeholders, and provides guidelines for how surpluses can be allocated within pension funds ([9]-[12]).
   
2. **Rule 11.5 of the Fund**: This rule allows for the allocation of 'excess assets' and specifies a distribution method, contributing 20% to the ESA. This nomenclature became significant during the court's assessment of whether this mode of distribution served as a sufficient basis for the actions taken by the Board ([9]-[19]).

3. **Common law principles concerning bias and conflict of interest**: The case also references how a Board's composition and decisions must not exhibit bias, which is paramount when deliberating on matters that may benefit one stakeholder group over another ([33]).

## A – Application:
The Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized whether the R363.2 million allocated to the ESA constituted actuarial surplus in accordance with section 15C, interpreting rules and definitions from the PFA. The court acknowledged that the appellants did not successfully dispute that the amount was actuarial surplus; however, they focused instead on the legitimacy of the method by which it was distributed ([14]-[22]).

Rule 11.5, although termed to deal with 'excess assets', effectively facilitated the allocation of actuarial surplus, according to the definitions and interpretations applied by the court. The court reasoned that a rigid interpretation that separates 'excess assets' from actuarial surplus could undermine the legislative intent of the PFA ([25]-[29]). Importantly, it was established that the Board had performed necessary actuarial valuations and followed proper procedural methods to allocate the surplus, thus validating their decision ([20]-[22]).

In evaluating the bias claims, the court found no evidence of improper conflict of interest among Board members. The Board was composed of an equal number of employer and employee representatives, and allegations of bias lacked substantive legal grounding ([33]-[34]).

## C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the allocation of R363.2 million to the ESA was lawful under the provisions of the Pension Funds Act and Rule 11.5 of the Fund. The court concluded that the Board acted within its rights, that the allocations complied with section 15C, and that the appellants failed to demonstrate significant bias or interest conflicts amongst the Board members. Thus, both the application to lead further evidence and the appeal were dismissed with costs, affirming the regulatory outcome and the legitimacy of the pension fund's actions ([41]). This case thus reinforces the interpretation of surplus allocation mechanisms within pension funds, emphasizing the importance of procedural integrity and stakeholder rights in such distributions.
