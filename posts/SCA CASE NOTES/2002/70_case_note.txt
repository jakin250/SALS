# FIRAC Case Note: **S v Ndhlovu**

## F – Facts

The case involves four appellants (Vusi Vincent Ndhlovu, Bafana Godfrey Mthethwa, Bongani Masinga, and Jabu Sweetdreams Nkosi) indicted for the murder of Johannes Jochemus Jansen van Rensburg in Katlehong on 17 January 1999 [1]. The victim was attacked while working with a plumbing team; several assailants approached his vehicle, and he was shot. The attackers fled with his cellphone, and Jansen van Rensburg succumbed to his injuries shortly after. 

Fifteen months post-incident, the appellants faced charges of murder and robbery in the Johannesburg High Court, where they were found guilty largely based on the testimonies and confessions of accused 3 and 4, who implicated themselves and co-accused during police questioning but later denied making such statements during trial [3]. The trial court convicted all accused, applying minimum sentencing provisions effective from 1 May 1998 and incorporating restitution elements for firearm possession [4].

Goldstein J presided over the initial trial and admitted hearsay evidence under section 3 of the Law of Evidence Amendment Act 45 of 1988, which regulates the use of hearsay evidence in South African law [10]. The essential question before the court was the admissibility of hearsay statements made by accused 3 and 4 against co-accused 1 and 2 [6]. 

## I – Issues

The primary legal issue is whether out-of-court statements made by accused 3 and 4, incriminating themselves and their co-accused, are admissible against the latter despite their subsequent disavowal during the trial [1]. Additionally, the case raises constitutional concerns regarding the hearsay provisions of the 1988 Act and their compliance with the right to a fair trial enshrined in the Bill of Rights [26]. 

## R – Rules

The relevant legal provisions concerning the case include:

1. **Hearsay Evidence Admission Rules (Act 45 of 1988)**:
   - Section 3 stipulates that hearsay evidence is inadmissible unless:
     - Both parties agree to its admission.
     - The declarant testifies in court.
     - The court, based on various factors—such as the nature of proceedings and the probative value—determines that admitting it serves the interests of justice [11].

2. **Constitutional Guarantees**:
   - Section 35(3) of the Constitution of South Africa guarantees the right to a fair trial, including the right to adduce and challenge evidence [24].

3. **Precedential Cases**:
   - **S v Ramavhale** emphasized the reluctance of courts to admit hearsay evidence unless there are compelling justifications [24][40].
   - **S v Ndlovu** clarified the importance of allowing the opportunity for cross-examination of hearsay declarants [19].

## A – Application

Cameron JA examined the admissibility of the hearsay evidence by applying the statutory framework laid out in section 3 of the 1988 Act, emphasizing the necessity of aligning it with constitutional values [13]. The court determined that while hearsay evidence typically does not hold judicial weight, under certain conditions—including the presence of compelling evidence or reliability of the hearsay—such evidence may be admissible.

1. **Discussion of Hearsay Evidence**:
   - The court found the initial statements by accused 3 and 4 credible due to their spontaneous and immediate nature, recorded soon after the incidents [40]. The admissions included specific details that correlated with the physical evidence, such as the firearm used in the murder and the cellphone transaction [46].

2. **Underlying Judicial Philosophy**:
   - Emphasizing caution regarding hearsay evidences, especially in criminal proceedings, the court underscored that the admission of hearsay cannot introduce undue prejudice against the accused [49]. It was highlighted that the core inquiry must focus on whether the withdrawal of the hearsay statement upon testifying diminishes its reliability.

3. **Evaluation Against Co-accused**:
   - The court posited that the hearsay statements of accused 3 and 4 held significant probative weight in corroboration of the prosecution's case against accused 1 and 2, especially considering the circumstantial evidence leading to their conviction [37]. The nature of the collective action and shared understanding among the accused suggested a culpable association that could not be dismissed, even amid disclaimed statements during the trial.

4. **WEIGHS Interests of Justice**: 
   - The court, following the operational mandate of the hearsay provisions, ultimately ruled that the interests of justice warranted the admission of the hearsay evidence against all appellants, providing enough grounds to affirm their convictions [34][50].

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the hearsay statements made by accused 3 and 4 were admissible under the provisions of the 1988 Act despite their disavowals at trial, as their initial statements met the standard for reliability [56]. They maintained that:

1. The statements provided critical corroborative evidence that aligned with other circumstantial evidences against the appellants.
2. The respective actions were inherently linked, meaning that the admissions significantly contributed to establishing the murder and robbery charges against the appellants.

Thus, the appeals against both conviction and sentence were dismissed in their entirety, with the court reaffirming the suitable application of hearsay evidence rules alongside constitutional fairness principles [56]. The adjustment of sentences reflected considerations of youth and the nature of intent, signifying a nuanced approach to sentencing in criminal law [54].

The implications of this case extend to the continued interpretation of hearsay evidence admissibility in conjunction with the necessity for due process and fair trial rights within the constitutional framework of South Africa. This decision reaffirms the judicial commitment to balancing evidential integrity and the accused's rights within the ambit of criminal proceedings.
