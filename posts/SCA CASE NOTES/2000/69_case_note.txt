# Case Note: REPUBLIC OF SOUTH AFRICA v LYNETTE NONQABA HLOBO

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts ([paras 1–15])

On 13 November 1994, Lynette Nonqaba Hlobos’s minor daughter, Tembisa, was injured in a motor vehicle collision on the old Berlin road in Eastern Cape. The Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accident Fund (the respondent) became liable to compensate Tembisa for her injuries. Appellant Hlobos issued summons for compensation in July 1996, and litigation proceeded with only the quantum in dispute ([para 2]).

Initially set for trial on 4 March 1997 and later on 20 August 1997, the case was settled two days prior to the hearing. An agreement was reached between Hlobos’s attorney, Mr. B A Lowe, and the respondent’s attorney, Mr. D H De la Harpe. The terms included a cash payment of R2,148,807.60 for future medical expenses, along with an undertaking to cover certain agreed future expenses in terms of Article 43 of the applicable statute ([para 3]).

On 7 September 1997, the respondent sought to set aside the settlement agreement, asserting that the intention had only been to provide an undertaking, not a cash payment. The court a quo ultimately set aside the agreement on three grounds: first, that a Mr. V Short, a claims handler for the respondent, lacked "contractual capacity" to finalize the agreement; second, that the agreement was vitiated by error on Short's part; and third, that the respondent (considered equivalent to the State) suffered prejudice by being compelled to make a cash payment instead of providing an undertaking ([paras 4–5]).

### I – Issues ([paras 5–8])

The core legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:

1. Whether Mr. Short had the requisite authority and capacity to bind the respondent through the settlement agreement.
2. The implications of a claimed error on the part of Mr. Short for the validity of the agreement.
3. The legal standing of a state entity in repudiating contractual obligations based on alleged misunderstandings or errors made by its employees ([paras 5–8]).

### R – Rules ([paras 9–12])

The court relied upon principles of contract law, including the common law doctrine of apparent authority, where an individual's authority within a corporate or institutional setting may bind the entity they represent. The court referenced the precedents, notably *George v Fairmead (Pty) Ltd* [1958 (2) SA 465 (A)], which emphasizes the need for courts to consider the reasonableness of a party's reliance on the apparent authority of another in a contractual context ([paras 10–12]).

Additionally, the court examined the backdrop of endorsement and authority in legal representation, incorporating rules from *R v Matonsi* [1958 (2) SA 450 (A)] and *Benjamin v Surewitz* [1973 (1) SA 418 (A)], which affirm that attorneys generally possess the authority to settle claims unless contradictory instructions are given. The court also noted the inapplicability of doctrines preventing parties from evading contracts on the grounds of unilateral mistake, especially where no misrepresentation had occurred ([para 12]).

### A – Application ([paras 10–14])

In addressing the first issue, the court found that Mr. Short’s alleged lack of capacity was irrelevant, as the settlement was negotiated and concluded between Mr. Lowe and Mr. De la Harpe, the latter of whom had clear authority to settle the matter. It dismissed the court a quo's conclusion that Short’s authority affected the validity of the agreement ([paras 8–9]).

On the second ground, concerning the alleged error, the court emphasized the importance of examining the entirety of the circumstances. It indicated that the respondent could not escape the implications of a binding contract simply based on Mr. Short's purported misunderstanding. Short had acted as the claims-handler entrusted with the case and had a professional obligation to forthrightly engage with the situation. The court reiterated that the existence of a unilateral mistake does not typically permit retrial of agreements absent a basis of deception or culpable disregard ([paras 10–12]).

Lastly, the court addressed the third issue regarding the state’s status. It determined that notwithstanding the respondent's claim to be equivalent to the state, the law requires that parties must adhere to legislative obligations when engaging in contracts. The respondent could not repudiate a lawful settlement without just cause ([para 14]).

### C – Conclusion ([paras 15])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, ruling that the initial court’s decision to set the settlement aside was unfounded. The agreement between the parties was valid and binding due to the apparent and actual authority bestowed upon the respondent’s attorney. As a result, the respondent was ordered to comply with the settlement terms, including paying legal costs for both the original application and the appeal.

The ruling underscores the importance of maintaining the integrity of settlements and reinforces the principles of contractual obligations within the context of state entities. By clarifying the bounds of authority and the implications of misunderstandings, the court set a critical precedent in the realm of contract law relevant to state-related entities ([paras 15]).

---

This case demonstrates the courts' commitment to uphold contractual agreements while considering the legal authority of representatives within organizations, specifically in cases with substantial public implications such as those involving the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accident Fund.
