National Sorghum Breweries (Pty) Limited t/a Vivo Africa Breweries v International Liquor Distributors (Pty) Limited

Case Number: 72/99 Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa Date of Hearing: 9 November 2000 Date of Delivery: 28 November 2000

Supreme Court of Appeal 2000

Case Number: 72/99
Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Date of Hearing: 9 November 2000
Date of Delivery: 28 November 2000

F – Facts

[1] The appellant, National Sorghum Breweries (Pty) Limited (trading as Vivo Africa Breweries), was engaged in litigation against the respondent, International Liquor Distributors (Pty) Limited, concerning claims arising from three written agreements executed in December 1994. Under these agreements, the appellant sold rights for distribution of products in specified regions to the respondent for R50,000 per agreement, summing to a total payment of R150,000 by the respondent ([paras 1–2]).

[2] Subsequently, the respondent initiated action in the Pretoria Magistrate’s Court, seeking restitution of the R150,000 based on allegations of material breach of contract by the appellant, asserting it had withdrawn from the agreements ([para 2]).

[3] The appellant did not contest this action, leading to a default judgment in favor of the respondent, which was subsequently satisfied with a payment of R150,000 ([paras 2–3]).

[4] On 26 April 1996, the respondent filed a further action in the Transvaal Provincial Division, asserting similar claims but now alleging damages amounting to approximately R4 million due to the appellant's breach of contract. The damages were claimed for expenses incurred under the agreements and loss of income subsequent to the breach ([para 4]).

[5] The appellant filed a special plea, arguing that the prior judgment acted as res judicata concerning any claims arising from the alleged breach of contract, thus rendering the second claim inadmissible. The plea contended that the respondent should be estopped from asserting claims in the second action that were substantially the same as those in the first ([para 5]).

[6] The court of first instance dismissed the special plea, leading to the appellant's appeal on grounds related to the availability of defenses based on res judicata and the "once and for all" rule ([para 7]).

I – Issues

[7] The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal concerned:

  1. Whether the appellant could successfully invoke the exceptio rei judicatae (defense of res judicata) to bar the respondent's second claim.
  2. Whether the "once and for all" rule precluded the respondent from claiming damages after having sought restitution in the prior action ([paras 7–11]).

R – Rules

[8] The doctrine of res judicata requires:

  • Identical parties (idem actor and idem reus).
  • The same issue or cause of action (eadem res and eadem causa petendi) ([para 2]).

[9] The legal precedent suggests that these elements must demonstrate substantial identity in claims for the exception to apply. Relevant case law includes African Farms and Townships Ltd v Cape Town Municipality 1963 (2) SA 555 (A) and Custom Credit Corporation (Pty) Ltd v Shembe 1972 (3) SA 462 (A) ([paras 2–4]).

[10] The "once and for all" rule, rooted in English law, mandates that any claims generated from the same cause of action should be brought in a singular legal proceeding, thus preventing multiple actions on the same basis ([paras 10–11]).

A – Application

[11] The court analyzed whether the second action's claims substantially overlapped with the first. In its evaluation, the court emphasized the distinctions between the nature of the claims: restitution in the first action versus damage claims in the second ([paras 4–6]).

[12] The appellant’s argument relied heavily on Custom Credit Corporation (Pty) Ltd v Shembe, suggesting that the inability to recover on both penalty and damages constitutes a barrier to subsequent actions. However, the court found that the claims in the current matter arose from distinctly different legal bases—restitution versus damages. Thus, they could not be viewed as fundamentally the same ([paras 6–9]).

[13] The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the necessary elements for the exceptio rei judicatae were absent. The claims did not arise from the same cause of action, and significant differences existed in the proof requirements for each claim ([paras 10–12]).

[14] Moreover, the analysis of the “once and for all” rule affirmed that it does not mandate the combination of contractual claims and damages within a single action, allowing for separate but related actions to proceed ([para 10]).

C – Conclusion

[15] The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, affirming that neither the exceptio rei judicatae nor the "once and for all" rule barred the respondent's claim for damages based on the contractual breach. The court highlighted the distinctions between the nature of claims for restitution and damages, allowing for the respondent to seek relief in the second action ([paras 11–12]).

[16] This decision clarifies the boundaries of res judicata and the "once and for all" rule within civil litigation, underscoring the need for parties to differentiate between the types of relief sought in contractual disputes. The ruling carries significant implications for future cases concerning similar contractual relationships and the defenses available to parties in breach of contract disputes ([para 12]).

References

  • African Farms and Townships Ltd v Cape Town Municipality 1963 (2) SA 555 (A).
  • Custom Credit Corporation (Pty) Ltd v Shembe 1972 (3) SA 462 (A).
