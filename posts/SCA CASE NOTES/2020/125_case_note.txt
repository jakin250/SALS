# Case Note: Mawonga and Another v Walter Sisulu Municipality and Others

**Citation:** Mawonga and Another v Walter Sisulu Municipality and Others (Case no 574/19) [2020] ZASCA 125 (7 October 2020)  

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Coram:** Petse DP, Mbha, Nicholls JJA, Eksteen, and Unterhalter AJJA.

---

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–10, 12–13])
Mr. Thembinkosi Mawonga, the first appellant, served as the municipal manager of the Walter Sisulu Local Municipality, having previously managed the Gariep Local Municipality from August 1, 2007, under an employment contract governed by the then-applicable Local Government: Municipal Structures Act 117 of 1998. Mawonga's initial five-year contract was set to automatically terminate on July 31, 2012, without any legitimate expectation of renewal or extension.

Prior to the termination of this initial contract, the Gariep Local Municipality resolved to extend Mawonga's tenure for another five years, allowing for the possibility of further renewal. After a municipal merger, Mawonga's contract transferred to the newly formed Walter Sisulu Local Municipality.

On July 20, 2017, he was granted a second renewal of his contract extending his employment to July 31, 2021, which contained an option for an additional year. However, on January 8, 2018, the council decided to rescind Mawonga's contract citing non-compliance with statutory requirements governing the appointment of municipal managers, leading to Mawonga's subsequent application in the High Court to set aside this decision.

The High Court dismissed Mawonga's application, declaring his contract null and void based on his non-compliance with the Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 (MSA) and its amendments.

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 4, 5, 16, 20])
The case presents three primary legal questions regarding the renewal of Mawonga's contract:
1. Does a municipal manager’s contract become vacant after five years even if renewed under certain terms specified in the contract?
2. What constitutes "valid renewal" under section 57(6) of the MSA?
3. Was Mawonga’s contract validly renewed according to statutory stipulations?

These questions necessitated an examination of sections 57(6)(c) and 54A of the MSA and its amendments, especially in light of recent court interpretations regarding the competitive hiring process for municipal managers.

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 13–15, 21, 28])
The ruling centers on interpreting several statutory provisions:
- **Section 57(6)(a)** of the MSA mandates a maximum contract length of five years for municipal managers, which cannot be extended indefinitely.
- **Section 54A** requires advertised vacancies to attract a qualified candidate when a position becomes vacant, emphasizing national competency standards and compliance.
- Previous case law, notably **Xuma v Engcobo Local Municipality**, states that any extension or renewal must adhere to statutory provisions; failure to do so renders such actions null and void.

The court also referenced the **Constitutional Court**’s pronouncement on the Amendment Act aimed at improving municipal governance by ensuring only qualified individuals are appointed to managerial positions.

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 25–28, 29–31])
The Supreme Court of Appeal affirmed the High Court's conclusion that the attempt to renew Mawonga's contract before the expiration of the five-year term violated section 54A(4) of the MSA. The court highlighted two crucial points:
1. The post of municipal manager must be advertised nationally upon expiration of the five-year term, irrespective of any terms stipulated in a renewal contract.
2. A renewal constituted an extension rather than a new appointment, thus failing to address the statutory requirements surrounding the position's vacancy.

In considering Mawonga's situation, the Court ruled that as his initial contract stretched beyond the maximum limit allowed, both the first and second renewals were invalid ab initio due to failure to comply with the requirements of both sections 54A and 57(6). The decision emphasized the importance of adhering strictly to governance rules to prevent incompetently qualified individuals from holding municipal management positions indefinitely.

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 32])
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Mawonga's appeal with costs, affirming the High Court's ruling regarding the nullity of his employment contract. This judgment reinforces existing legislative frameworks necessitating compliance in municipal appointments and confirms the imperative of a national competitive process in filling such positions. The decision underlines the need for stringent adherence to legislative protocols designed to enhance governance and accountability within local municipalities.

The implications of this ruling extend beyond Mawonga’s case; it sets a significant precedent that reaffirms the importance of compliance with statutory regulations in the appointment of municipal managers, ensuring that individuals in these positions are adequately qualified to address the complexities of local government management.
