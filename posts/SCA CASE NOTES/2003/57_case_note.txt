Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2003

# FIRAC Case Note: Shoprite Checkers (Pty) Ltd v Bumpers Schwarmas CC

## F – Facts
The case arises from an appeal by **Shoprite Checkers (Pty) Ltd** (the appellant) against the judgment of the court below, where it granted rectification to **Bumpers Schwarmas CC** and its members, **Rudolf Jacobus Klopper** and **Madelaine Johanna Klopper** (the respondents). The factual background is extensively detailed in the court below’s judgment but can be summarized as follows ([1-2]).

The dispute centers around a written agreement where the appellant sold a business undertaking as a going concern to the first respondent. The key contention is the obligation of the appellant regarding a lease for the premises where the business would operate. The respondents sought rectification of the sale agreement to include a term requiring the appellant to negotiate a lease with the property owner that mirrored the previous lease with the former tenant, Palmer, except for the duration and rental terms.

The court below concluded that the central question was determining which party bore the responsibility to arrange for the conclusion of the lease ([3]). The respondents presented evidence supporting their claim for rectification, while the appellant provided contradictory testimony regarding the terms that were understood during negotiations, most notably concerning a "development clause" that such arrangements would necessitate ([4-10]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal question pertains to whether the court below was correct in granting the respondents' application for rectification of the sale agreement ([1-2]). More specifically, the issues include:
1. **Existence of a mutual intention**: Was there a common understanding between the parties regarding the lease terms?
2. **Valid grounds for rectification**: Did the respondents meet the burden of proof necessary to justify a rectification of the agreement?
3. **Weight of evidence**: How should the evidence concerning the negotiations be assessed in light of the contradictory testimonies provided by the appellant's representatives and the respondents?

## R – Rules
The legal principles surrounding contract rectification under South African law provide that for a party to succeed in a claim for rectification, they must demonstrate a common intention that was not captured in the written agreement ([3, 12]). Important rules and precedents relevant to this case include:

- The need for clear and convincing evidence indicating a discrepancy between the parties' initial intentions and the final agreement.
- The principle established in prior judgments regarding the weight and credibility of testimonial evidence.
- Rectification can be granted if a party can show that the document failed to reflect what both parties intended due to a mistake ([12]).

## A – Application
In its analysis, the court closely examined the evidence presented by both sides, recognizing the testimonies of **Hirschfield** and **Fine**, representatives of the property owner, as pivotal. This testimony indicated that **Hirschfield** clearly communicated the necessary "development clause" that would obligate the tenant to vacate during renovations. Furthermore, discussions about the lease arrangements strongly indicated that there would be substantial deviations from the previous lease with Palmer, contradicting the respondents’ assertion of a common understanding ([4-10]).

The court determined that the evidence put forth by the appellant, particularly the testimony by Hirschfield, undermined the claim for rectification. It noted that it would be improbable for the second respondent and **Van Tonder** (representative of the appellant) to have shared a common intention that contradicted the known requirements of the landlord concerning contractual obligations ([12-13]). This conclusion emphasized that the respondents failed to meet the necessary burden of proof, as there was no mutual intention regarding the terms reflected in the lease claimed by the respondents.

Additionally, the court found little relevance in the respondents’ argument that the appellant’s representatives acted to secure a lease, which could suggest an obligation. The conduct was equally explicable as actions aligned with the appellant's own interests ([14]).

## C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal by Shoprite Checkers, concluding that the court below had erred in granting rectification of the sale agreement. The court ruled that the respondents had not established that their understanding of the lease terms was shared and accepted by the appellant ([14-15]). The order of the lower court was set aside, with the court substituting it with a dismissal of the respondents’ prayer for rectification and ordering them to pay the costs incurred by the appellant ([16-19]). This decision crystallizes the importance of clear mutual intentions and the credibility of evidence in contract law, serving as a precedent for similar future cases where rectification is sought. 

**Implications**: The ruling has further emphasized the necessity for precise documentation and clear communication between contracting parties to avoid disputes regarding intentions, particularly in business transactions where significant financial stakes are involved.
