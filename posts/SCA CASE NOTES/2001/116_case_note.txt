Source Folder: 2001

# Case Note: REPUBLIEK VAN SUID-AFRIKA v WILLEM SWARTZ [2001] ZASCA 31

**FIRAC Analysis**

## F – Facts
[1] On Saturday, February 19, 1994, at approximately 20:15, a collision occurred between a Datsun motor vehicle driven by the appellant, Willem Swartz, and a brown Hereford bull owned by the respondent, Jannie Delport. The accident took place on the R43 national road between Hermanus and Botrivier in the Western Cape, resulting in serious injuries to the appellant, significant damage to his vehicle, and the immediate death of the bull.

[2] The appellant initiated a claim in the Cape Provincial Division against the respondent, seeking damages for his injuries and the fair market value of his vehicle. He alleged that the accident was solely caused by the negligence of the respondent and/or his employees, detailing multiple grounds of negligence: (a) failure to exercise any or adequate control over the bull; (b) failure to ensure that the bull remained in a pen; (c) failure to take any or adequate precautions to prevent the bull from accessing the road, and (d) failure to secure a gate on the respondent's farm.

[3] At the commencement of the trial, the presiding judge issued an order to separate the merits and the quantum of damages in line with Rule 33(4) of the Uniform Rules of Court. Consequently, only the issue of merits was heard, focusing on whether there was negligence on the part of the respondent and whether the appellant contributed to his own negligence. The trial court found for the appellant on both issues, but this decision was overturned by the Full Court on appeal, leading to the present final appeal by the appellant.

[4] The respondent was a farmer at Rooistrand, and his property bordered both sides of the R43.

## I – Issues
[5] The primary legal question before the court was whether the respondent and/or his employees were negligent concerning the collision. If negligence was established, the court needed to determine whether the appellant had contributed to the accident through his own negligence and to what degree.

## R – Rules
[6] In adjudicating negligence claims, the courts apply the reasonable person standard, considering whether a defendant acted as a reasonable person would have in similar circumstances (see **Carmichele v Minister of Safety and Security** 2004 (3) SA 20 (CC)). The plaintiff bears the burden of proving negligence on a balance of probabilities, which includes demonstrating both a duty of care and a breach of that duty. Additionally, once a prima facie case of negligence is established, the burden may shift to the defendant to explain the circumstances that may negate liability (see **Arthur v Bezuidenhout and Mieny** 1962 (2) SA 566 (A)).

## A – Application
[7] The court rigorously analyzed the facts, particularly the state of the fence that the respondent was supposed to maintain. Evidence presented by the appellant sought to establish that some cattle were seen by him earlier in the day grazing near the fence, suggesting that the fence lacked sufficient integrity to prevent the bull from escaping onto the road. However, the presiding judge found this testimony inconclusive and dismissed it as irrelevant due to conflicting evidence regarding the actual status of the fences at the time of the accident.

[8] The respondent maintained that no cattle were present in the adjacent pen at the time of the accident, and this was supported by the testimony of a police officer who inspected the site post-incident. The respondent explained that the bull was kept in a different pen approximately 800 meters from the site of the collision and specified that the fences were regularly maintained.

[9] The court was particularly concerned with the lack of direct evidence demonstrating that the fence was not maintained properly at the time of the accident. Despite context suggesting the possibility of negligence, such as the general obligation to prevent livestock from endangering road users, the absence of definitive proof regarding the condition of the fence led to a ruling against the appellant.

[10] Moreover, the appellant's claim that once the bull escaped, it indicated that there was negligence on part of the respondent (the bull going through what was presumed to be an inadequate fence) was deemed speculative. The ruling emphasized that the plaintiff bears the onus to prove negligence, which was not accomplished in this case.

## C – Conclusion
[11] Consequently, the court ruled that the appellant failed to satisfy the evidentiary burden regarding the respondent's negligence in maintaining the fence and ensuring that the bull did not access the roadway. It affirmed the trial court's findings concerning the absence of direct evidence linking the state of the fence to negligence.

[12] Thus, the appeal was dismissed with costs, underscoring the principle that claims arising from negligence will necessitate a clear demonstration of breach and causation, especially in cases involving animals straying onto public highways ([paras 17–18]).

This case elucidates the complexities involved in negligence claims relating to livestock and emphasizes the need for plaintiffs to substantiate allegations with clear and compelling evidence as a fundamental prerequisite for relief. The ruling reflects ongoing judicial commitment to promoting road safety policies and responsible animal husbandry practices within the agricultural sector.
