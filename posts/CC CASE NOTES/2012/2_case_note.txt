Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# Case Note: Maphango and Others v Aengus Lifestyle Properties (Pty) Ltd 

**Citation**: [2012] ZACC 2, Case CCT 57/11  

## FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts
The applicants in this case are tenants of the Lowliebenhof apartments in Johannesburg, occupying units based on various leases ([2]–[3]). The respondent, Aengus Lifestyle Properties (Pty) Ltd, purchased the building, conducted upgrades, and sought to impose significant rent increases that exceed the terms of existing leases. To accomplish this, it invoked a termination clause in the leases, which allowed either party to terminate with written notice. The tenants contested the legitimacy of this termination, arguing that it was meant to circumvent statutory protections and rental control provisions under the Rental Housing Act 50 of 1999 (the Act) ([3], [22]).

The litigative journey saw the tenants initially losing in the South Gauteng High Court and subsequently in the Supreme Court of Appeal ([2]–[3], [15]). They argued that the termination constituted an unjust infringement of their right to adequate housing as provided under section 26 of the South African Constitution. Moreover, they claimed that the landlord should have sought a court order to increase the rent as mandated by the Act and challenged the fairness of the eviction process ([4], [5]).

The landlord maintained that they had acted within their legal rights by terminating the leases and that the escalation clauses that governed rent increases were not applicable post-termination ([22], [29]). 

### I – Issues
The legal questions primarily revolve around the enforceability of a lease termination for the purpose of achieving higher rents and whether this constitutes an unfair practice under the Act. Central to the case are issues regarding:

1. The applicability of the Rental Housing Act to the lease agreements between the tenants and the landlord.
2. The legitimacy of the eviction process initiated following the lease termination. 
3. Whether the actions of the landlord violated the constitutional right of tenants to adequate housing ([6], [12]).

### R – Rules
The **Rental Housing Act 50 of 1999** governs the relationship between landlords and tenants, aiming to protect tenants against unfair practices and ensuring access to adequate housing ([28], [38]). Specifically, the Act stipulates that:

- No tenant may be evicted without a court order and considering all relevant circumstances (s 26(3) of the Constitution; s 13(9) of the Act) ([1], [41]).
- The landlord’s rights include termination of leases only on specified grounds and those that do not constitute an unfair practice ([26], [39]).
- An unfair practice is defined as any act by a landlord that contravenes the Act or adversely impacts a tenant unreasonably ([40], [42]).

Judicial precedents from **Barkhuizen v Napier** (2007) underscored the importance of fairness in the enforcement of contractual rights and recognized that public policy should guide the interpretation and application of contractual obligations ([22], [123]).

### A – Application
The Court held that the termination of the leases should be subjected to scrutiny under the **Rental Housing Act**. It asserted that the provisions of the Act provided a clear mechanism for resolving conflicts between landlords and tenants, particularly regarding rental increases ([4], [30]). The majority judgment emphasized that the mere act of terminating the lease could still fall within the scope of being an unfair practice under the Act, especially when motivated solely by a desire to bypass regulated rent increases ([30], [59]).

While the Supreme Court of Appeal had earlier concluded that the landlord’s actions did not violate any terms due to an interpretation of "practice," the Constitutional Court countered that fairness must consider the circumstances of the termination, including the potential for undue hardship on tenants ([4], [55]). The Court indicated that failure to consider the context of a landlord’s motivation for termination could ultimately undermine the protective measures envisaged by the Act. 

The decision also highlighted that the landlord should not invoke termination to implement rent increases outside the agreed escalation clauses. Therefore, even if termination clauses are legally valid, their abuse for purposes contrary to the intentions of the Act could constitute an unfair practice ([26], [52], [60]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court granted the tenants leave to appeal, underscoring the necessity for the Tribunal to adjudicate the claimed unfair practices under the Act, which had not been adequately addressed in previous instances ([68], [70]). The Court specifically called for a review of the termination's context, allowing tenants recourse through the Tribunal to seek a remedy if the termination was deemed unlawful. If found to be an unfair practice, the termination could be set aside, effectively safeguarding the tenants' rights ([68], [70]).

This judgment clarifies that leases must be enforced not only according to their terms but also in harmony with broader legislative frameworks intended to protect tenants. The ruling highlights the importance of balancing landlord rights with tenants' constitutional entitlements, reinforcing that termination clauses cannot be wielded arbitrarily in the pursuit of profit. 

#### Implications
This ruling reaffirms the primacy of the **Rental Housing Act** in landlord-tenant relations and encapsulates significant judicial acknowledgment of social justice principles under the Constitution, further ensuring the protection of vulnerable tenants against exploitative practices in South Africa's housing sector.
