Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# Case Note: Director of Public Prosecutions, Eastern Cape v Coko (2024) ZASCA 59

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts

In *Director of Public Prosecutions, Eastern Cape v Coko*, the respondent, Loyiso Coko, was appealing against a conviction for rape handed down by a regional court. The incidents leading to the conviction unfolded on the night of 1 July 2018, when Coko and the complainant, TS, who was 21 years old at the time, engaged in an intimate relationship that had begun just weeks earlier. TS had explicitly communicated her wish to remain a virgin and her readiness to engage in penetrative sex on this occasion had not been established before their meet ([paras 11-15]). 

On that night, after a series of mutual solicitations and comforting reassurances from Coko that no sexual intercourse would occur, the parties engaged in kissing and oral sex ([paras 16-19]). Coko then proceeded to penetrate TS without her consent, despite her pleas and objections for him to stop ([paras 19-20]). The regional court ultimately found Coko guilty of contravening Section 3 of the Sexual Offences Act, which defines rape as the unlawful and intentional act of sexual penetration without consent ([paras 24-25]).

Upon appeal to the Eastern Cape High Court, the court upheld Coko's appeal, stating the regional court had fundamentally misdirected itself on issues concerning consent ([paras 28-30]). The decision led to further proceedings resulting in a State appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal which discussed the legal interpretations surrounding the nature of consent in this case ([paras 1-2]).

### I – Issues

The key legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal were:

1. **Nature of Consent**: Whether the High Court erroneously interpreted the nature and requirement of consent as established by the parties' previous interactions and intentions ([paras 41-44]).
2. **Sufficient Evidence**: Whether the State successfully proved beyond a reasonable doubt that TS had not consented to the act of sexual penetration ([paras 11, 25, 27]).
3. **Factual Findings**: Whether interference by the High Court with the regional court's findings was warranted given the factual evidence presented during the trial ([paras 28-30]).

### R – Rules

The relevant legal frameworks and principles include:

1. **Sexual Offences Act (2007)**: This Act abolished common law definitions of rape and established a comprehensive statutory definition including a stringent requirement for the voluntariness and explicit nature of consent ([paras 42-44]). 
2. **Constitutional Principles**: The interpretation of laws, including the Sexual Offences Act, must promote the spirit and objects of the Bill of Rights, focusing particularly on the rights to dignity and bodily integrity ([paras 52-54]).
3. **Standard of Proof in Criminal Law**: The State must prove the guilt of the accused beyond a reasonable doubt, and this includes demonstrating that consent was absent ([paras 34-37]).

### A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal, led by Deputy President Petse, reviewed the High Court's reasoning critically. The court emphasized that while TS’s participation in kissing and oral sex was noted, it did not equate to consent for penetrative sexual intercourse. It rejected the notion that lack of resistance equaled consent, affirming that consent must be explicit and must apply distinctly to each sexual act ([paras 55-67]).

The court held that the regional court was correct in finding that Coko did not have reasonable grounds to believe that TS was consenting to penetrative sex given her explicit statements against engaging in such acts and her immediate recourse to pushing him away and asking him to stop ([paras 71-79]). Moreover, TS’s messages following the incident corroborated her objections and indicated that her apparent submission during the act was a response to confusion and intimidation, not genuine consent ([paras 81-84]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the High Court's ruling, reinstating the conviction of Coko. It concluded that the evidence clearly demonstrated beyond a reasonable doubt that TS did not consent to the sexual penetration, and that Coko’s misapprehension of the situation did not absolve him from criminal liability. Furthermore, the court laid down significant implications for understandings of consent, particularly within intimate relationships, emphasizing that previous agreements or mixed signals do not authorize an assumption of consent ([paras 99]). 

The case underscores judicial expectations surrounding consent in sexual offences, affirming the necessity for unequivocal agreement to each distinct sexual act while highlighting the ongoing societal issues surrounding sexual violence against women, particularly in intimate contexts. 

This decision reinforces the legal position that consent in sexual relations must be informed, free, and ongoing, with any ambiguity falling to the side of the complainant's rights and dignity.
