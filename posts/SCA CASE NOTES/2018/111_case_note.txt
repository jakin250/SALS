Case Note: SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

### FIRAC Case Note: Jiyane v The State (943/2017) [2018] ZASCA 111

#### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–6])

The appellant, Ishmael Jiyane, was charged with robbery under section 51 of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 at the Daveyton Magistrate's Court on 20 April 2015. The charges stemmed from an incident on 24 August 2014, where the appellant was alleged to have unlawfully assaulted the complainant, Mr. John Malinga, and forcibly taken his cellphone and R1000. Following a conviction and a sentence of three years’ imprisonment, which also included a declaration of unfitness to possess a firearm, the appellant was granted leave to appeal. The appeal was initially dismissed by the Gauteng Division of the High Court, leading to a further appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 2]). 

Key facts of the case include that on the day of the incident, the complainant, intending to deliver money to a tenant of the appellant, was confronted by Jiyane, who emerged from his house wielding a sjambok. The appellant chased and assaulted the complainant, ultimately taking his cellphone and money, inflicting serious injuries that required the complainant to be hospitalized for ten weeks ([paras 3–5]).

#### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 8–12])

The primary legal issues addressed in this appeal were:

1. Whether the conviction for robbery should stand given the particulars of the assault and theft.
2. Whether the appellant received a fair trial, specifically regarding the magistrate's conduct during the trial.
3. Whether the proper verdict should have been that of assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm rather than robbery.
4. The appropriateness of the sentence imposed upon conviction ([paras 9–12]).

#### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 10–13])

The legal principles considered in this case included:

- The standard of proof required in criminal cases, specifically the necessity of proving the intentions behind the conduct to establish the crime of robbery.
- The definition of robbery, which necessitates a demonstration that violence was used with the intent to deprive the complainant of their belongings (R v Dhlumayo 1948 (2) SA 677 (A); S v Pistorius 2014 (2) SACR 314 (SCA) para 30) ([para 13]).
- The competent verdict rule under section 260 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, allowing conviction for lesser charges where evidence supports such a finding.

#### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 8–14])

In analyzing the evidence, the court was unable to find that Jiyane's actions constituted robbery as defined legally. The primary question was whether the appellant had the requisite intention to deprive the complainant of his property at the time of the assault. The court noted, based on the complainant’s testimony, that there was uncertainty as to the sequence of events related to the taking of the property ([para 13]).

The court found that the essential elements of robbery—specifically, that the violence was used to compel submission to theft—were not established. The injuries sustained by the complainant were acknowledged, leading the court to conclude that while assault was proven, the robbery conviction was flawed due to the lack of evidence supporting the intent behind the appellant’s violent actions ([para 14]).

Regarding the trial proceedings, the court assessed the conduct of the magistrate. Despite submissions suggesting undue influence in questioning, the court upheld the magistrate’s decisions and found no substantial deviation from required standards ([para 10]).

Lastly, the court noted that a reconsideration of sentencing was warranted due to the improper conviction. The appellant was deemed unsuitable for a custodial sentence in light of the circumstances, resulting in a wholly suspended sentence of three years ([paras 15–17]).

#### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 17])

The Court ultimately upheld the appeal, setting aside both the conviction for robbery and its accompanying sentence, substituting it with a conviction for assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm. The sentence was modified to three years' imprisonment, wholly suspended for five years, contingent on the accused avoiding further violent offenses ([para 17]). 

The implications of this judgment reinforce the necessity for courts to thoroughly consider the intentions behind assaults in determining appropriate charges, alongside ensuring fair trial standards are consistently upheld, diminishing the potential for wrongful convictions. This case also exemplifies the appellate court's role in correcting overreach in charges where the evidence does not definitively support such claims.
