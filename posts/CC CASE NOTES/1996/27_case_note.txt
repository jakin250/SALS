Case Note: IN THE CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1996

# FIRAC Case Note: Ferreira v Levin NO and Others; Vryenhoek and Others v Powell NO and Others [1996] 1 BCLR 1 (CC)

### F – Facts

On December 6, 1995, the Constitutional Court declared section 417(2)(b) of the Companies Act invalid, specifically regarding its coercive nature that forced individuals to answer self-incriminating questions during inquiries ([1]). The initial applications arose from decisions made by the Witwatersrand Local Division of the Supreme Court, where the applicants sought interdicts against answering questions they believed would infringe their right against self-incrimination. Van Schalkwyk J dismissed these applications, leading the applicants to appeal, which the Full Bench upheld with costs ([2]).

The key fact driving the applicants’ objections was the perception that section 417(2)(b) of the Companies Act not only compelled individuals to answer questions but also allowed self-incriminating evidence to be used in future criminal proceedings. Ultimately, five pivotal issues were raised in the referral to the Constitutional Court, primarily concerning the constitutionality of section 417(2)(b) and the admissibility of self-incriminating evidence ([2]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal question was the constitutionality of section 417(2)(b) of the Companies Act, particularly whether it violated the applicants' rights against self-incrimination ([2]). The secondary issues pertained to the procedures followed at inquiries held under this provision and the implications of evidence obtained during such inquiries ([2]). The court addressed whether the obligation for the applicants to answer self-incriminating questions remained intact, despite the invalidation of the provision as it pertains to usage in subsequent criminal proceedings ([5]).

### R – Rules

The legal framework relevant to this case includes the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa Act 200 of 1993, particularly sections 100(2) and 102(1) on direct access and referrals, respectively. The court referenced previous jurisprudence, including **Kruger Bros. and Wasserman v Ruskin** and **Fripp v Gibbon & Co**, which outline the discretionary nature of cost orders ([3]). The court also discussed relevant precedents concerning the treatment of self-incriminating evidence such as the implications of the right against self-incrimination articulated in constitutional law ([3]).

### A – Application

The court applied the legal principles by evaluating the coercive nature of section 417(2)(b). Despite striking down the provision concerning the admissibility of self-incriminating evidence, the court reiterated that the applicants remained bound to respond to inquiries under section 417. The court emphasized that the invalidation did not undermine the legality of the inquiry process itself nor change the obligations of the applicants to furnish evidence ([5]).

Furthermore, in assessing the issue of costs, the court observed the general principle that the successful party should typically recover costs, but this is also substantially governed by the context and conduct of both parties. Notably, the court concluded that the applicants had not achieved significant success against the respondents despite the partial victory in challenging the constitutionality of the provision. As such, they were not entitled to costs for what was considered a technical success ([6]-[8]).

The court declined to equate this matter with other jurisdictions, such as Canada, where different contextual factors applied, emphasizing that the nature of the litigation between private parties, as opposed to state actions, necessitated a distinct approach to costs ([10]). The court ultimately decided that each party would bear their own costs, acknowledging that the applicants’ need to approach the court was intrinsic to the constitutional challenge raised, irrespective of opposition from the respondents ([11]).

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court, therefore, dismissed the applicants' claim for costs, holding that while they had partially succeeded in challenging the constitutionality of section 417(2)(b), they had not sufficiently defeated the central issue or achieved substantial relief. The ratio decidendi centered on the lack of a decisive victory in the context of invoking constitutional rights against a legislative framework. The court's decision reinforced the nuances in cost allocation in constitutional litigation while establishing a precedent about the obligation to respond to self-incriminating inquiries, despite a declaration of invalidity concerning the use of that testimony in criminal contexts ([1]-[11]). This ruling carries significant implications for future interactions between individuals and statutory inquiry processes, informing advocates and legislators about the balance of rights in constitutional litigation.
