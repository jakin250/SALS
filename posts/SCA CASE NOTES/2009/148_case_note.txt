Source Folder: 2009

# Case Note: Umgeni Water v Mshengu [2009] ZASCA 148

## FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts ([paras 1–3], [6])
In this case, the respondent, Bonginkosi Vincent Mshengu, was employed by Umgeni Water, a statutory water utility established under the Water Services Act 108 of 1997. Following an unsuccessful job application during restructuring in 2002, Mshengu faced disciplinary proceedings and was ultimately dismissed for misconduct on November 27, 2002. Prior to his dismissal, he had notified Umgeni Water of his intention to take early retirement effective April 1, 2003, which would have entitled him to both a withdrawal benefit and the employer's contribution to his retirement fund managed by the second appellant, the principal officer of the Umgeni Water Retirement Fund.

Mshengu's claim revolved around the assertion of his entitlement to these retirement benefits, amounting to R1,917,181.00. However, the first appellant contended that the claim for the benefits was prescribed since it arose on April 1, 2003, and the summons was issued over four years later, on June 29, 2007. In an agreed special case, the legal question revolved around whether Mshengu's claim had prescribed before the issuance of the summons.

### I – Issues ([paras 4–5])
The primary legal issue before the court was whether Mshengu's claim for his retirement benefits had become prescribed under the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 before the summons was served on June 29, 2007. Specifically, the court needed to determine when the cause of action arose, particularly in light of Mshengu's dismissal being in force, which could have impeded his ability to assert such a claim.

### R – Rules ([paras 5–6])
The Prescription Act provides that a debt becomes prescribed after the lapse of specific periods (s 10). For Mshengu's claim, the applicable period was outlined in s 11(d) of the Act, which stipulates a three-year prescription period. Section 12(1) further clarifies that prescription commences when the debt is due. The court emphasized that for a debt to be "due," the creditor must possess a complete cause of action, which encompasses all necessary facts to support a legal claim.

The core definition of a "cause of action" has been previously articulated to encompass every fact a plaintiff must prove to secure a judgment, highlighting that it does not arise until all elements are satisfied ([6]).

### A – Application ([para 7])
The court applied the principles established in the Prescription Act, examining the timeline of events and the implications of Mshengu's dismissal on his claim. The court noted that, as long as the dismissal was operative, Mshengu could not assert his right to the retirement contribution because he could not prove the necessary fact of his retirement. Therefore, the court reasoned that Mshengu's ability to assert a complete cause of action was only realized when the CCMA determined the dismissal to be both substantively and procedurally unfair.

The court asserted that had Mshengu attempted to initiate action on April 1, 2003, he would have been unable to substantiate his claim regarding his retirement due to the ongoing effect of his dismissal. The ruling emphasized that the last necessary fact—that he had been unfairly dismissed—only emerged once the CCMA issued its finding, making the commencement of prescription begin at that moment. Hence, the court affirmed that Mshengu’s claim was not prescribed as of the issuance of the summons ([7]).

### C – Conclusion ([para 8])
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded by dismissing the appeal, thereby upholding the ruling of the Pietermaritzburg High Court, which had determined that Mshengu’s claim had not prescribed. The court's holding clarified that a dismissal must not serve as an impediment to claim the retirement benefits until such dismissal is established as unfair. Consequently, the ruling reaffirmed the principle that a complete cause of action must exist for prescription to commence. The judgment highlights the importance of procedural rights and substantive justice in employment law, emphasizing how the legal implications of dismissal can influence the assertion of claims for retirement benefits.

---

This case reflects the nuanced interplay between employment law, retirement fund claims, and the application of the Prescription Act, underlining the intention of South African law to safeguard employees' rights in complex situations involving dismissal and financial benefits.
