Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: MEC Local Government and Traditional Affairs v Botha NO

**Citation**: MEC Local Government and Traditional Affairs v Botha NO  (887/13) [2014] ZASCA 211 (1 December 2014)

**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Coram**: Navsa ADP, Shongwe, Theron, and Swain JJA, and Fourie AJA

---

## F – Facts

[1] This appeal centers on the valuation of immovable property for municipal rates, specifically whether a late objection to a municipal valuation roll may be accepted under Section 80 of the Local Government: Municipal Property Rates Act 6 of 2004 (MPRA). The case involves properties situated in the jurisdiction of the Municipality of Ethekwini.

[2] The property in question, Erf 324, Suburb Assagay, was valued at R23 million in the municipality's valuation roll in 2008 when it was owned by Universal Retail Portfolio (Pty) Limited (URP). 

[3] Following URP's liquidation order on 31 March 2010, the liquidators sold the property in September 2011 to a third party for R4.35 million. However, they discovered that URP owed a substantial amount of R2,708,900 for rates based on the outdated municipal valuation.

[4] The liquidators contended that this 2008 valuation was clearly inflated and requested the municipality to reconsider it based on an alternate valuation that indicated a fair market value not exceeding R4.5 million. The municipality maintained that the valuation would not be adjusted since no prior objections had been lodged.

[5] Upon failing to get a response from the MEC for Local Government concerning a late objection, the liquidators sought the High Court's intervention, requesting to set aside the 2008 valuation or alternatively obtain a condonation for a late objection pursuant to Section 80 of the MPRA.

[6] The High Court dismissed the request to set aside the valuation but ordered that the application for condonation be referred back to the MEC for consideration, leading to the current appeal and cross-appeal.

---

## I – Issues

[7] The central legal issue is the applicability of Section 80 of the MPRA, which pertains to the condonation of late objections to valuation rolls. Specifically, whether the provisions can be invoked by liquidators representing URP, who had failed to lodge an objection in a timely manner.

[8] A secondary issue was whether the liquidators, having succeeded to URP's rights, could assert any rights under Section 80 regarding a late objection against the 2008 municipal valuation.

---

## R – Rules

[9] The MPRA, particularly Sections 55 and 80, governs the procedure for valuation and objections related to valuation rolls. According to Section 80 of the MPRA:

- The MEC for Local Government can condone non-compliance with any timeframes specified in the Act for the lodging of objections, provided sufficient cause is demonstrated (s 80(1)).
- Notably, non-compliance with certain provisions (s 21, 31, or 32) may not be condoned (s 80(2)), indicating limits on the MEC's power to grant relief.

[10] Additionally, the principles from the Companies Act dictate that liquidators may only act within the rights that the company possessed prior to liquidation, and they cannot enforce rights or claims that the insolvent company did not have.

---

## A – Application

[11] In applying the law, the court emphasized that the absence of an objection from URP against the 2008 valuation meant that the liquidators were precluded from seeking relief under Section 55 or pursuing a supplementary valuation under Section 78, as these would require preceding actions that URP failed to take.

[12] The court outlined that Section 80 is not meant to offer a blanket remedy for all parties. The provisions were designed to close the valuation disputes within specific timeframes to maintain order and predictability in property tax levying.

[13] The judge noted that the 2008 valuation was clearly inflated, being significantly higher than any market assessments since then. However, despite the liquidators' legitimate grievances about valuation accuracy, the ability to lodge late objections under Section 80 does not compensate for past failures to meet statutory obligations.

[14] Moreover, the court ruled that because the liquidators stand in the shoes of URP, they could not claim any rights to assert against the municipality that URP itself could not have successfully invoked. This interpretation aligned with the principles of the Companies Act, enforcing that the liquidators’ abilities to act are strictly bound by the company's previous rights and obligations.

[15] The court also rejected arguments from the liquidators regarding the breadth of Section 80; it concluded that while the MEC could have the discretion to allow late objections, the liquidators' claim for such a remedy based on inflated valuation could not be legally sustained since it stemmed from the company's prior fraudulent misrepresentation of the property’s value.

---

## C – Conclusion

[16] The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the MEC's appeal, dismissing the cross-appeal from the liquidators. The court reasoned that no remedy was available under the MPRA for the liquidators to lodge a late objection against the valuation originally assigned to URP.

[17] The ruling underscores the importance of adherence to procedural deadlines as set by the MPRA, discouraging any retroactive claims by parties that had failed to act promptly within the established frameworks. The implications of this ruling reinforce the principle that parties cannot benefit from their own negligent or fraudulent conduct concerning statutory obligations, particularly in the context of property taxation.

[18] Thus, the order of the High Court was set aside, and the application was dismissed, concluding that procedural compliance is essential for the protection of municipal interests and proper revenue management.

[19] The decision sets a firm precedent about the boundaries within which liquidators operate and highlights the firm legislative framework governing property valuations and objections within South African municipal law.
