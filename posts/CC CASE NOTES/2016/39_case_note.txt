Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Department of Transport and Others v Tasima (Pty) Limited [2016] ZACC 39

**F – Facts**  
The case revolves around a disagreement about the extension of a five-year contract between the Department of Transport and Tasima (Pty) Limited for the provision of services related to the electronic National Traffic Information System (eNaTIS). Originally awarded in July 2001 for R355 million, the contract was set to expire on May 31, 2007. On the eve of this expiration, Tasima requested a five-year extension, which was rejected by the then Director-General of the Department. This led to a temporary month-to-month arrangement after the contract's expiry, which continued from June 2007 until April 2010. 

In April 2010, the new Director-General approved a five-year extension without following proper procurement processes as mandated by section 217 of the Constitution and the Public Finance Management Act (PFMA). This extension was subsequently contested in court, resulting in a series of orders, including findings of contempt against Department officials for failing to comply with court directives about the contract's performance and payment obligations ([1]–[30]).

The litigation culminated in the Supreme Court of Appeal upholding a finding of contempt against certain officials, but dismissing the Department's counter-application seeking to review the extension of the contract due to a perceived delay in raising the challenge ([56]–[60]).

**I – Issues**  
Several pivotal issues were raised in this case:
1. Whether the Department's officials and the Road Traffic Management Corporation were in contempt of court concerning earlier orders.
2. Whether the Supreme Court of Appeal should have condoned the delay in the Department's review application against the extension.
3. Whether the Department could raise a collateral challenge to the extension of the contract.
4. The broader question of judicial authority: whether courts may decline to decided counter-applications for the review of administrative actions where there are existing court orders enforcing said actions ([61]).

**R – Rules**  
Key legal principles underpinning the judgment include:
1. **Rule of Law**: Administrative actions must align with constitutional mandates, particularly sections 217 and 38 of the PFMA, which emphasize fairness, transparency, and accountability in public procurement.
2. **Judicial Authority**: Courts are obligated by section 172(1)(a) of the Constitution to declare any conduct inconsistent with the Constitution as invalid.
3. **Contempt of Court**: Established definitions of contempt require proof of willful defiance against court orders, emphasizing the importance of obeying court directives to maintain judicial authority and public confidence ([4]–[12]).
4. **Jurisdictional Basis**: The principle established in Oudekraal confirms that illegal administrative actions remain effective until set aside but should not be allowed to reinforce unlawful conduct ([82]–[88]).

**A – Application**  
The court analyzed the application of these rules, particularly the reaction to the unlawful extension of the contract between the Department and Tasima. The majority opinion held that:
- The Department was justified in bringing a reactive challenge against the extension despite the lapse of time due to the executive’s failure to conduct itself lawfully.
- The Supreme Court of Appeal misapplied the concept of contempt, suggesting that the decision not to comply with an illegal order would not necessarily be contempt—thereby undermining the basis of the integrity of judicial processes.
- The court emphasized that this situation resulted in significant financial inefficiencies and unjust enrichment of Tasima at the expense of public funds, highlighting the need for accountability ([97]–[109], [131]–[199]).

Notably, the defined actions of the Department after the extension had expired were seen as steps taken in preparation for the transition of services, rather than contempt of court ([114]).

**C – Conclusion**  
The Constitutional Court ultimately ruled in favor of the Department's counter-application, declaring the extension of the contract void ab initio due to its unconstitutional foundations in procurement law. The court mandated the immediate transition of eNaTIS services to the Road Traffic Management Corporation. The decision not only clarified the law concerning judicial compliance with court orders but reinstated the necessity of lawful procurement processes in public service contracts. Each party was directed to bear its own costs, mitigating further public expenditure on legal actions against previously held contempt findings ([204]–[208]).

This case serves critical insights into administrative law, the administrative actions of state entities, and the power of the judiciary to ensure compliance with constitutional mandates, further entrenching the principle of the rule of law within the South African legal framework.
