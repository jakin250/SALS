Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# Legal Case Note: NMZ obo SFZ v MEC for Health and Social Development of the Mpumalanga Provincial Government

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–2])

On 3 October 2012, NMZ gave birth to her child, SFZ, at Piet Retief Hospital in South Africa. The child was diagnosed with cerebral palsy due to asphyxia during delivery, which the appellant attributed to the alleged negligent conduct of the hospital's staff ([para 1]). Subsequently, NMZ filed an action against the MEC for Health and Social Development (the respondent) for damages related to medical negligence ([para 1]). 

On 24 October 2019, NMZ sought an order from the Mpumalanga Division of the High Court to condone her late service of a notice of intention to institute legal proceedings as stipulated under section 3(4) of the Institutions of Legal Proceedings Against Certain Organs of State Act 40 of 2002 (the Act) ([para 1]). The MEC opposed this application, leading to a dismissal by the High Court, which held that the delay in notice was unreasonable and the claim lacked prospects of success ([paras 1–2]). The matter was thereafter appealed, and the Supreme Court of Appeal heard the case on 17 November 2021 ([para 2]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 2–2])

The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the High Court's decision to dismiss NMZ's application for condonation due to her failure to provide timely notice under section 3 of the Act was correct ([para 2]). Specific points of contention included whether the appellant had established “good cause” for her delay and whether the MEC was unreasonably prejudiced as a result of that delay ([para 2]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 14–15])

The Court relied on the statutory framework established in sections 3(2) and 3(4) of the Act, which require that notice be given within six months of the debt becoming due and outline the conditions for obtaining condonation for late service of notice. Section 3(4)(b) specifies that a court may grant condonation if it finds that: (i) the debt has not been extinguished by prescription, (ii) good cause has been shown for the delay, and (iii) the organ of state has not suffered unreasonable prejudice as a result of the delay ([paras 14–15]).

The Court reiterated several precedents, including **Mothupi v Member of the Executive Council**, which emphasized that timely notification enables the state to investigate claims swiftly to prepare its defense adequately ([para 15]). This principle was crucial in assessing both the procedural and substantive issues of the case.

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 22–42])

1. **Prescription**: The Court agreed that the first requirement regarding prescription was established; NMZ's claim was on behalf of her minor child and had not yet prescribed ([para 22]).

2. **Good Cause**: The Court scrutinized various components surrounding the appellant's delay in servicing the notice. The analysis revealed that while NMZ was informed of her child's diagnosis shortly after birth, she did not obtain the relevant medical records until June 2016, two years later ([para 26]). The High Court had incorrectly determined that the appellant should have initiated her claim sooner, overlooking the reality of circumstances that delayed her access to medical documentation.

3. **Prospects of Success**: The Court engaged deeply with the merits of the appellant's case, noting that the High Court had erred in concluding that her claim lacked merit. The Court pointed out that the hospital's failure to adhere to prescribed monitoring protocols might establish a prima facie case of negligence ([paras 29-31]). The discrepancies in medical records suggested that there were valid grounds for a claim, necessitating further examination of evidence in a trial setting ([para 37]).

4. **Unreasonable Prejudice**: The respondent’s assertion that it would suffer undue prejudice due to the delay was deemed opportunistic, especially given that NMZ had diligently attempted to obtain her medical records via her attorneys and hospital staff ([para 39]). The Court noted that the respondent's interest in procedural finality could not overshadow the pursuit of justice for a minor child.

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 42–43])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld NMZ’s appeal, reversing the High Court's dismissal of her condonation application ([para 42]). The judgment confirmed that NMZ had established “good cause” pursuant to section 3(4) of the Act, setting a precedent for how courts interpret the complexities around cases involving medical negligence, particularly where access to information may be compromised.

The implications of this case extend to the greater field of administrative litigation in South Africa, emphasizing the importance of judicial discretion in considering matters that affect vulnerable individuals, particularly minors. The Court’s decision asserts that procedural technicalities should not thwart substantive justice when reasonable explanations for delays exist.
