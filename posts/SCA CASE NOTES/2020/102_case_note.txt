# FIRAC Case Note: **Mbatha v The State** [2020] ZASCA 102

---

## F - Facts

In this case, **ZAKHELE DERRICK MBATHA** (the applicant) was originally charged in the **Magistrate’s Court** with several serious offenses, including robbery with aggravating circumstances, possession of an unlicensed firearm, assault with intent to cause grievous bodily harm, and attempted murder ([1]). The applicant was acquitted on one of the robbery counts but convicted on the rest on **30 January 2013** ([1]). He received an effective sentence of **30 years imprisonment**, comprising 15 years each for the robbery counts and 10 years for attempted murder, with certain sentences ordered to run concurrently ([3]).

After the guilty verdicts, the applicant sought leave to appeal against both his conviction and sentence, but the applications were denied due to insufficient prospects of success ([4]). He subsequently filed petitions for reconsideration with the **Gauteng High Court** and later with the **Supreme Court of Appeal** (SCA), which were also dismissed ([4]). Ultimately, the applicant applied for reconsideration under **s 17(2)(f)** of the *Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013* ([4]).

## I - Issues

The SCA had to address several legal questions in this reconsideration:

1. **Whether the conviction for possession of an unlicensed firearm (count 3) was justified** given the lack of evidence that the applicant ever handled a firearm.
2. **Whether the sentencing process took adequate account of the time the applicant spent in custody awaiting trial**.
3. **Whether the various sentences imposed on the applicant should run concurrently**, considering the nature of the offenses and existing legal principles ([10]).

## R - Rules

The applicable legal principles considered by the court included:

- **Joint Possession**: The court evaluated the standard for joint possession of firearms as established in *S v Nkosi*, which requires evidence that the group intended to exercise possession collectively and that the actual possessor held the firearm on behalf of the group ([20]).
  
- **Exceptional Circumstances**: Under s 17(2)(f), the President of the SCA has discretion to refer a decision denying appeal for reconsideration if exceptional circumstances are demonstrated, which may include situations where a grave injustice could ensue ([12]).

- **Cumulative Sentencing**: The court acknowledged the principles from *S v Vilakazi* regarding the accounting for time spent awaiting trial in an effective sentence and the need for proportional sentences that do not result in an unfair cumulative effect ([23]).

## A - Application

In assessing the application, the SCA noted that:

1. The conviction for possession of the firearm was premised on the theory of joint possession, which was contested by the applicant. He argued that mere knowledge of another’s possession of a firearm does not suffice for joint possession unless it can be shown that there was a common intention to act collectively regarding the firearm ([18]–[20]).

2. The magistrate’s conclusions regarding joint possession were scrutinized, and the applicant's legal arguments were credible, suggesting that the evidence did not unequivocally establish his possession beyond reasonable doubt ([22]).

3. The SCA also highlighted that the sentencing framework applied may not have properly accounted for the applicant’s pre-trial incarceration and that the imposition of consecutive sentences resulted in an excessive effective sentence ([23]).

4. Ultimately, the court concluded that the state conceded the improper application of principles regarding the cumulative effect of sentences and the absence of evidence supporting the possession conviction, leading to reasonable prospects of success on appeal ([6]).

## C - Conclusion

The SCA granted the application for reconsideration, allowing the applicant to appeal against his conviction for unlawfully possessing a firearm and the sentences for counts 1, 2, 4, and 5. The court found that exceptional circumstances existed warranting the reconsideration based on the identification of potential legal errors and the risk of grave injustice should the appeal rights remain closed ([26]). 

In summary, this case underscores the importance of rigorous legal standards regarding possession convictions and the careful application of sentencing principles to avoid disproportionate outcomes. The court’s reconsideration served to highlight procedural safeguards within the criminal justice system aimed at achieving just outcomes in potentially flawed convictions.
