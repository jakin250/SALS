Supreme Court of Appeal

Road Accident Fund v Samuel Feli Thugwana

This case revolves around an appeal from a ruling by the Court a quo concerning the interpretation of a regulation made under the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 (the "Act").

Supreme Court of Appeal 2003

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

This case revolves around an appeal from a ruling by the Court a quo concerning the interpretation of a regulation made under the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 (the "Act"). Specifically, the issue at hand is Regulation 2(1)(c) from Government Gazette 17939, which mandates that a person injured in a hit-and-run accident must provide an affidavit to the police within a stipulated timeframe.

On November 17, 1998, Samuel Feli Thugwana, the respondent, was hit by an unidentified vehicle while walking on the Springs/Kwa Thema Road. Following the accident, he was hospitalized for several weeks. During this time, he verbally reported the incident to a police officer. However, no formal report was made until he sought clarification at the Springs police station in February 1999 and then proceeded to complete the required affidavit on multiple occasions, albeit not submitting one to the police until August 29, 2002, well beyond the mandated timeframe.

Upon filing a claim for compensation against the Road Accident Fund ("the Fund"), the Fund’s special plea contended that Thugwana failed to comply with the affidavit submission requirement as stated in Regulation 2(1)(c). The respondent argued that he had either complied or substantially complied with the regulation, or that it was ultra vires the Act. The Court a quo dismissed the special plea, leading to the current appeal ([paras 1–5]).

I – Issues

The Supreme Court of Appeal was tasked with adjudicating three principal issues:

  1. The interpretation of Regulation 2(1)(c) under the Road Accident Fund Act.
  2. Whether the regulation in question is to be treated as peremptory, implying that compliance is compulsory for a claim to proceed.
  3. If deemed peremptory, whether the regulation is ultra vires the enabling statute, thus lacking legal validity ([paras 1–2]).

R – Rules

The legal framework governing the case consists of relevant provisions from the Road Accident Fund Act and its accompanying regulations:

  • Section 17(1) of the Act provides for the Fund's liability in compensation claims arising from vehicle accidents where the identity of neither the owner nor the driver can be established, subject to regulations made under Section 26.
  • Section 26(1) empowers the Minister to make regulations deemed necessary to achieve the objectives of the Act.
  • Regulation 2(1)(c) delineates the procedural requirement for claimants in hit-and-run cases to submit an affidavit to the police within 14 days, if reasonably possible, after being in a position to do so ([paras 2–4]).

Precedent cases cited include Ncisimane & Others v Santam Insurance Co Ltd and Mbatha v Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund, which help clarify the peremptory nature of such regulations ([paras 11–14]).

A – Application

The court interpreted Regulation 2(1)(c) as imposing a clear obligation on claimants, emphasizing its peremptory character due to the consequence of non-compliance—namely, that the Fund would incur no liability. The ruling considered that the requisite affidavit must be submitted to the police, and any document not physically handed over does not meet this requirement ([paras 10–11]).

In evaluating Thugwana's circumstances, the court concluded that he was indeed in a position to submit the required affidavit soon after the accident but failed to do so within the established timeline. Regarding his argument of substantial compliance, the court found that merely swearing to an affidavit or providing it to his attorney did not equate to submission to the police as mandated by the regulation.

On the aspect of whether the regulation was ultra vires, the court determined that it was not excessive in its authority under the Act. Various judicial interpretations affirmed that regulations setting procedural requirements to guard against fraud aligned with legislative intent, thereby falling within the Minister's regulatory powers ([paras 11–15]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appeal from the Court a quo should be upheld, thereby reversing its decision. Thugwana’s claim would be dismissed due to his non-compliance with Regulation 2(1)(c), notwithstanding the potential injustice that might arise from the strict interpretation of procedural norms. The ruling allowed for the possibility of amendment allowing Thugwana to invoke Section 24(5) of the Act, which could deem his claim valid despite its initial dismissal.

The implications of the judgment stress the necessity for future claimants to be aware of and adhere to specific procedural requirements established within their respective legislative frameworks, thereby underscoring the tension between procedural precision and equitable claims ([paras 18]).
