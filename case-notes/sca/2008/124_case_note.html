Supreme Court of Appeal

Angelina Procopos v National Director of Public Prosecutions (401/07) [2008] ZASCA 124

The appeal in this case concerns a restraint order issued against Angelina Procopos under the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (the Act) concerning her purported ownership of certain assets alleged to be linked to criminal proceeds ([paras 1–2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2008 [2008] ZASCA 124

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The appeal in this case concerns a restraint order issued against Angelina Procopos under the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (the Act) concerning her purported ownership of certain assets alleged to be linked to criminal proceeds ([paras 1–2]). On 24 February 2006, Rabie J of the Pretoria High Court issued a provisional order prohibiting Procopos from dealing with specified assets under section 26 of the Act, relating to a total value of R9 700 000, described as the value of a "known gift" ([para 1]).

On 24 May 2007, Claassen J confirmed this order in relation to Procopos’s interests in two close corporations and her bank account at Absa Bank, whereas other assets were released from restraint ([para 1]). Procopos appealed the confirmation of the order, specifically pertaining to her interests in the close corporations, as her counsel conceded that the appeal regarding the bank account could not succeed ([para 2]).

The restraint order was sought by the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) following allegations that Procopos’s daughter was involved in a grand fraud scheme amounting to R26 500 000 and had appropriated funds into the accounts of family members, including Procopos ([para 13]). It was claimed that Procopos received an "affected gift" from her daughter, classified as a sum of R9 761 607, directly correlated with the fraudulent activities ([paras 14–15]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the Court were:

  1. Whether the NDPP had to prove on a balance of probabilities that an affected gift was made to Procopos or whether it sufficed to show reasonable grounds for such a belief ([para 34(a)]).
  2. If a gift had to be proven, whether the NDPP met this burden based on available evidence ([para 34(b)]).
  3. The applicability of section 16(1) of the Act, which deems certain transfers of property to be gifts under specific conditions ([para 34(c)]).
  4. Whether any of the alternative arguments presented by the NDPP could stand if it were determined that Procopos had not received an affected gift ([para 34(d)]).

R – Rules

The relevant provisions of the Prevention of Organised Crime Act included:

  • Section 12(1): Defines an “affected gift” as any gift made by the defendant not more than seven years prior to the prosecution date ([para 8]).
  • Section 16(1): Provides that a transfer of property is deemed a gift if the consideration received is significantly less than that provided by the defendant ([para 11]).
  • Sections 18 and 26: Deal with the processes of confiscation orders and restraint orders, establishing the NDPP's role in seeking restraints on property alleged to be linked to crime ([para 4 and 12]).

A – Application

On analysis, the Court noted several important factors in relation to the NDPP’s burden of proof regarding the "affected gift." It concluded that proving the existence of an affected gift was a crucial element to justify the restraint order against Procopos ([para 35]). The Court maintained that the standard required was on the balance of probabilities, and that merely having reasonable grounds for suspicion was insufficient ([para 35]).

The Court scrutinized the evidence presented by both the NDPP and Procopos. It found that the NDPP's assertion that Procopos acted merely as a conduit through which funds were channeled did not automatically permit the conclusion that these funds constituted an affected gift ([paras 36–37]). The absence of factual support for the conclusion drawn under section 16(1) led the Court to hold that substantive evidence demonstrating an intent or agreement to make a gift was missing.

Moreover, the Court ruled that the lack of any meaningful consideration supplied by Procopos further invalidated the NDPP's claims, as requisite criteria for the deeming provision under section 16(1) were not satisfactorily met ([para 38]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal allowed Procopos's appeal, primarily on the grounds that the NDPP had failed to demonstrate the existence of an affected gift as legally defined under the Act ([para 42]). Consequently, the restraint order was set aside in respect to all of Procopos's assets except for her bank account, which was not contested. The demand for cross-examination was mandated, implying that the factual disputes regarding inter-family transactions require thorough adjudication at an evidentiary hearing.

This case underscores the evidential burdens and theoretical complexities surrounding property transfers deemed to be criminal proceeds and the application of statutory provisions meant to protect against the proceeds of crime. The decision also emphasizes the importance of clear factual support when asserting claims against third parties under the auspices of organised crime statutes. The implications extend beyond the immediate parties, fostering a precedent for future restraint applications where the nuances of "gift" and "consideration" significantly influence the outcomes of cases involving alleged interconnections with crime ([paras 40–41]).
