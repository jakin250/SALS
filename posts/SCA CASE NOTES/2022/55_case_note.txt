Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: David Papiki Komane v The State (51/2019) [2022] ZASCA 55

### F – Facts
The appellant, David Papiki Komane, was convicted alongside three co-accused of robbery with aggravating circumstances by the Limpopo Division of the High Court, Polokwane, on 26 January 2018, and was sentenced to 18 years’ imprisonment ([1]). The high court did not find substantial and compelling circumstances to warrant a lesser sentence as prescribed by the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 ([1]).

The robbery, which amounted to over R11 million, occurred on 9 December 2015 at a cash depot operated by G4S in Marble Hall. A group of armed men, disguised in female clothing, invaded and robbed the facility ([4]). After the incident, Komane, employed as a police constable, was arrested and led authorities to where a large sum of money had been hidden. He later confessed to Colonel Serfontein, which included details of the robbery ([5], [12]).

Key testimony came from state witnesses, Messrs Papo and Segoapa, who were involved in the criminal conspiracy and had received indemnity ([6]). Their evidence, along with DNA profiling and circumstantial evidence, constituted the basis of the conviction against Komane ([12], [18]).

### I – Issues
The primary issue on appeal was whether there were reasonable prospects of success regarding the appeal against both the conviction and sentence ([4]). Specifically, the court had to determine:
1. The adequacy of circumstantial evidence, particularly DNA evidence, to support the conviction ([13]).
2. The admissibility of Komane’s confession made to Colonel Serfontein and whether it was obtained involuntarily ([21]).
3. The propriety of the pointing out conducted by the police and whether proper procedures were followed ([27], [29]).

### R – Rules
The rules governing this case arise from various legislative and judicial principles:

1. **Sufficiency of Circumstantial Evidence**: The trial court must establish if evidence confirms the accused’s guilt beyond a reasonable doubt, as reiterated in *S v Ntsele* 1998 (2) SACR 178 (SCA) ([37]).
2. **DNA Evidence**: The relevance of DNA evidence is established through the principles that it must form part of the collective impression that establishes guilt beyond a reasonable doubt ([36]).
3. **Confession Admissibility**: Section 35(5) of the Constitution mandates that evidence obtained in violation of rights must be excluded if its admission compromises the fairness of the trial. The state bears the onus to prove confessions were made voluntarily ([21]).
4. **Pointing Out Procedures**: According to the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, Section 217(1) mandates that a confession must be made voluntarily, and a pointing out should be conducted properly by a non-investigating officer to ensure fairness ([29]).

### A – Application
The Court assessed the weight of the circumstantial evidence, notably the DNA findings linking Komane to the crime scene. The DNA analysis, conducted by forensic analyst Ms. Jenny Cooks, indicated that Komane's DNA was found on items connected to the robbery ([13], [18]). The evidence was not merely coincidental, as it consistently indicated his presence with co-accused individuals involved in the planning and execution of the crime, leading to an overwhelming inference of guilt ([19]).

In terms of the confession, the court found that Komane had been detained prior to the confession but demonstrated that his statement was made voluntarily. The judge noted that Komane had explicitly referred to his legal rights, contradicting claims of duress or coercion ([21], [24]). The evidence about the pointing out procedure revealed discrepancies among police officers, with the Court identifying a need for an independent facilitator per Section 217(1) to avoid compromising the process ([27], [29]).

Despite procedural anomalies, the totality of the evidence presented by the State (DNA, confession, and circumstantial elements) was deemed sufficient to uphold the conviction. The Court differentiated between recognizing procedural deficiencies and the necessity of the State to prove guilt beyond reasonable doubt.

### C – Conclusion
The appeal was dismissed, confirming the trial court’s conviction as sound due to the sufficiency of circumstantial evidence, the admissibility of the confession made by the appellant, and procedural shortcomings in the pointing out process having been remedied by the overwhelming evidential context ([1], [34]). The decision underscores the principle that although processes must adhere strictly to legal norms, the substantive outcomes should reflect the compelling weight of evidence pointing towards guilt ([38]).

This case illustrates the intricate balance between procedural fairness and substantive justice within the South African criminal justice framework, further emphasizing the judiciary's role in ensuring both elements are preserved in criminal proceedings.
