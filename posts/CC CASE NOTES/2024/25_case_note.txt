Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

**Case Note: Govan Mbeki Local Municipality v Glencore Operations South Africa (Pty) Ltd and Others; Emalahleni Local Municipality v Glencore Operations South Africa (Pty) Ltd and Others [2024] ZACC 25** 

### FIRAC Case Summary

---

#### F – Facts [1–12]

The Govan Mbeki Local Municipality ("Govan Mbeki") and the Emalahleni Local Municipality ("Emalahleni") both enacted municipal planning by-laws, specifically Sections 76 and 86, which imposed transfer embargoes on properties. These embargoes required property owners to obtain certificates confirming compliance with various municipal planning requirements before being allowed to register transfers of their properties. The by-laws' provisions aimed at enforcing municipal planning compliance were challenged by several mining and property companies, including Glencore Operations South Africa (Pty) Ltd. The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) upheld a High Court decision declaring these transfer embargo provisions unconstitutional, leading to the municipalities' appeal in the Constitutional Court.

---

#### I – Issues [13–38]

1. Whether the municipalities had the authority to enact transfer embargoes under their legislative powers.
2. Whether the transfer embargoes constituted an arbitrary deprivation of property under Sections 25 and 156 of the Constitution.
3. Whether the by-laws were inconsistent with national legislation, particularly Section 118 of the Municipal Systems Act.
4. If the provisions were invalid, what remedies should be granted?

---

#### R – Rules [39–60]

The applicable legal principles stem from the South African Constitution, particularly:

- **Section 25**: Protects against arbitrary deprivation of property.
- **Section 156**: Outlines the executive and legislative authority of municipalities, allowing by-laws for matters within their jurisdiction.
- **Sections 118(1)** of the Municipal Systems Act: Mandates the issuing of rates clearance certificates as a precondition for property transfers while limiting the timeframe for liabilities to two years.

Legal precedents include *Mkontwana v Nelson Mandela Metropolitan Municipality* and *DVB Behuising*, which underscore the necessity for any by-law to fall within the legislative competency of the municipalities and not encroach upon national competencies.

---

#### A – Application [45–94]

The Constitutional Court held that the municipalities lacked the legislative authority under Sections 156(2) and 32(1) of SPLUMA to impose the transfer embargoes as they effectively regulated property registration, a matter of national competence. 

1. **Legislative Authority**: The Court determined that the transfer embargoes did not fall within municipal planning but encroached on deeds registration, an exclusive national function.
2. **Arbitrary Deprivation**: The judgment focused on whether the transfer embargo was justified. The Court found that, while certain provisions aimed to enforce compliance with planning regulations (Sections 76(2)(b) and (c)), the provisions regarding compliance with the actions of previous owners were arbitrary and unconstitutional.
3. **Validity of Provisions**: The Court declared Sections 76(2)(a), (d), (e), (f) of the Govan Mbeki By-Law and Sections 86(2)(a), (d), and (e) of the Emalahleni By-Law unconstitutional due to their expansive and arbitrary implications.
4. **Remedy**: The Court indicated that unconstitutional by-laws must be declared invalid entirely, not limited to specific provisions, affirming the necessity for a uniform application of constitutional standards.

---

#### C – Conclusion [95–98]

The Constitutional Court confirmed the decisions of the High Court and SCA, declaring the transfer embargo provisions unconstitutional and invalid due to lack of legislative authority and the risk of arbitrary deprivation of property. The Court emphasized that while municipalities can enforce planning regulations, the methods employed must align with constitutional mandates and not extend to functions reserved for national legislation. The order issued by the Constitutional Court set aside the prior decisions, upholding the invalidity of specific provisions while safeguarding the broader integrity of municipal and national governance frameworks.

--- 

**This case illustrates the delicate balance between municipal powers and the overarching legislative framework set by national law, emphasizing the need for clarity in the delegations of authority within South Africa's constitutional context.**
