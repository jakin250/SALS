Supreme Court of Appeal

XO Africa Safaris CC v The Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (395/15) [2016] ZASCA 160

SARS issued a letter of assessment on March 4, 2011, for VAT related to services provided in various tax periods ([2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 160

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

XO Africa Safaris CC (the appellant, XO) is a registered VAT vendor in South Africa, engaged in assembling package tours for foreign tour operators (FTOs), which include accommodation, travel, and various recreational activities ([1]–[4]). The services provided are primarily localized, catering to tourists in South Africa. During an audit, the South African Revenue Services (SARS) contested XO's application of a zero VAT rate under section 11(2)(l) of the Value Added Tax Act 89 of 1991 (the Act), asserting that the services provided to FTOs and their customers during their stay in South Africa were subject to the standard VAT rate of 14% ([1]).

SARS issued a letter of assessment on March 4, 2011, for VAT related to services provided in various tax periods ([2]). XO objected, and while SARS upheld the objection regarding penalties, it dismissed the VAT assessment. Subsequently, XO's appeal to the Western Cape Tax Court was also dismissed, affirming that XO directly supplied local services and thus VAT was payable at the standard rate ([3]).

The evidence presented indicated a contractual framework in which XO arranged services through local suppliers without a direct contractual relationship with the customers of FTOs ([5]–[7]). Despite the lack of direct contracts with customers, XO claimed that the local services provided were not subject to VAT under section 11(2)(l) of the Act ([9]).

I – Issues

The main legal issue before the court was whether the services supplied by XO to the FTOs and their customers were zero-rated under section 11(2)(l) of the Act, or whether they were subject to the standard VAT rate as determined by SARS. Specifically, the questions were:

  1. What services did XO supply?
  2. To whom were these services supplied?
  3. Were the parties receiving these services residents of South Africa or present in the Republic at the time of service provision ([25])?

R – Rules

  1. Section 7(1)(a) of the Value Added Tax Act imposes VAT on the supply of goods and services by a vendor engaged in a business in South Africa ([21]).
  2. Section 11(2)(l) provides for a zero VAT rating on services supplied to non-residents, with specific exceptions including services connected to land or movable property located inside South Africa at the time the services are rendered, or if the services are supplied to persons present in South Africa when rendered ([22]–[24]).
  3. The interpretation of tax statutes, especially concerning jurisdiction and application, follows principles set out in Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality as well as in Commissioner, South African Revenue Service v Bosch on the legislative intent ([20]).

A – Application

The court's analysis focused on the nature of the services supplied by XO. It reasoned that while XO claimed to only provide organizational services, the documentation and evidence showed that XO indeed provided local services (such as accommodation and meals) directly to the tourists ([28]). The court emphasized that the absence of direct contractual relationships between XO and the end-users (the tourists) did not negate the direct supply of services. Rather, the nature of the service (its consumption and enjoyment by the tourists in South Africa) was pivotal in determining VAT applicability ([29]).

Moreover, the court addressed XO's argument for zero rating based on a misinterpretation of the legislative intent underlying section 11(2)(l). The court noted that allowing such an argument would undermine the fiscal objectives of the VAT system by permitting XO to circumvent output tax while claiming input VAT on local services consumed within South Africa ([31]). Following precedents, the court concluded that the correct VAT treatment was the standard rate on services consumed in the Republic ([31]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed XO's appeal, affirming SARS's assessment that the supplied services attracted VAT at the standard rate. The court's rationale underscored the need for a comprehensive understanding of the service provisions and their direct connection to consumption within South Africa. This decision clarified the interpretation of zero-rated services in the context of making services available in South Africa and confirmed that the enjoyment of those services on South African soil obliges providers to fulfill VAT liability at the standard rate ([32]). The implications of this ruling are significant for businesses organizing services for foreign clients, emphasizing the importance of contractual arrangements and the need to comply with tax obligations under South African law.
