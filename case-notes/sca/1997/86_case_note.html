Supreme Court of Appeal

Republic of South Africa Case No 298/94

The evidence presented by the State centered around two shooting incidents involving the deceased, Sibusiso Nkosinathi Chonco and Fisher Makhoba.

Supreme Court of Appeal 1997

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case in question involves four appellants, Mbongiseni Hadebe, Kwenza Khuzwayo, Majaji M. A. Dondo, and Dumisane M. T. Hethwa, who were convicted by the Witwatersrand Local Division of one count of murder, one count of attempted murder, unlawful possession of four firearms, and unlawful possession of ammunition, resulting in death sentences for the murder charge and imprisonment for the other counts ([paras 2–3]).

The evidence presented by the State centered around two shooting incidents involving the deceased, Sibusiso Nkosinathi Chonco and Fisher Makhoba. Chonco was killed on November 6, 1992, while in his vehicle, and Makhoba was killed on November 7, 1992, in a separate incident where another individual, Jeffrey Flower Njikelani, was wounded. In both instances, various spent cartridges were found, and a red Nissan Sentra, occupied by the appellants, was pulled over by police shortly after the second shooting, leading to the discovery of firearms in the vehicle ([paras 4–6]).

The appellants denied involvement, asserting conspiracy and highlighting inconsistencies in the police evidence. They claimed the firearms found were not in their possession and advanced an assertion of police misconduct throughout the investigation ([paras 8–10]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the court centered on:

  1. Whether the circumstantial evidence was sufficient to support a conviction for murder and related charges.
  2. Whether there was reasonable doubt regarding the conspiracy allegations made by the appellants against the police.
  3. The appropriateness of the sentences imposed, particularly in light of evolving constitutional standards following a relevant Constitutional Court decision ([paras 9–11, 28–29]).

R – Rules

The court applied several legal principles of criminal law, particularly relating to circumstantial evidence and the meaning of “beyond a reasonable doubt.” The approach outlined in R v Blom ([1939] AD 88) was referenced, which stipulates the necessity for sufficient evidence that meets this standard. The court also reaffirmed the burden placed on defendants under section 40(1) of the Arms and Ammunition Act 75 of 1969, which requires individuals to substantiate their claim of lack of possession of firearms ([paras 26, 28]).

Moreover, the judgment acknowledged the precedent established in S v Makwanyane and Another ([1995] (3) SA 391), where the Constitutional Court abolished the death penalty, making the imposition of such sentences no longer valid in current contexts ([para 29]).

A – Application

The court found that the circumstantial evidence presented constituted a strong case against the appellants. Ballistic evidence connected the seized firearms to the shootings related to both deceased individuals. No credible evidence substantiating the appellants’ conspiracy theory was accepted, and their assertions were perceived as implausible and lacking substantive support ([paras 8–10, 18, 24]).

The appellate court emphasized that it would only interfere with factual determinations of the trial court if a clear misdirection was apparent, which it found not to be the case. The testimony of the police officers was given appropriate weight, and inconsistencies noted by the appellants did not correlate to a conspiracy but were minor in nature and related to peripheral issues ([paras 12–16, 22]).

C – Conclusion

The court ultimately dismissed the appeals against the convictions, affirming the findings of the lower court and concluding that the evidence sufficiently demonstrated the appellants’ guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. However, it ruled that the death sentences must be set aside, in accordance with constitutional developments regarding capital punishment, and remitted the case for resentencing ([paras 28–29]).

The implications of this ruling span not only the immediate responsibilities of the justice system to uphold substantive justice but also the constitutional directives that underscore contemporary sentencing practices. The case serves as a pivotal reference point in South African jurisprudence regarding the intersections of evidence, state conduct, and the protection of rights under evolving legal standards.
