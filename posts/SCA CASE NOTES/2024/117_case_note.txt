# FIRAC Case Note: De Kock v Du Plessis and Others (284/2023)

## F – Facts

The case revolves around an eviction dispute involving Leon de Kock (the Appellant) and Wanda Luus du Plessis and Andre du Plessis (the First and Second Respondents, respectively). The factual background of this case, as detailed in the judgment, can be summarized as follows:

1. **History of Relationships and Property Sale**: The Appellant was previously married to the daughter of the Respondents, Nicquelette, until her passing in 2018. In 2015, discussions began between the Appellant and Respondents regarding the sale of their residence at erf 4629, 9 Muscadel Street, Wellington (the property), necessitated by alleged financial difficulties faced by the Respondents ([paras 1–5]).

2. **Property Transaction**: In 2016, a deed of sale was executed that transferred ownership of the property from the First Respondent to the Appellant for R4,500,000. The Appellant financed this purchase through a mortgage from ABSA Bank, while the First Respondent received R3,500,000, of which R1,000,000 was used to discharge an existing mortgage ([paras 6–7]).

3. **Post-Transaction Occupation**: Following the transfer, the Respondents continued to reside in the property without paying rent, reportedly under an agreement that they could live there rent-free until the loan of R2,500,000 was repaid. Tensions escalated in mid-2019, primarily over family disagreements pertaining to the late Nicquelette’s estate and access to the minor children ([paras 8–9]).

4. **Eviction Proceedings**: After refusing to vacate the property despite multiple notices from the Appellant, the Respondents’ continued occupation prompted the Appellant to file for eviction under the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (the PIE Act) in June 2020 ([paras 11–12]).

5. **Composite Agreement Claim**: The Respondents claimed a composite agreement—a single oral arrangement—permitting their continuous occupation until the full amount owed was repaid. The Appellant countered, asserting that the loan agreement was unlawful under the National Credit Act 34 of 2005 ([paras 14–15]).

6. **Lower Court Decisions**: Initially, the application for eviction was dismissed by the High Court, which accepted the Respondents’ version of the events and found the agreements to be inseverable and void ab initio. This decision was subsequently upheld by the full court of the Western Cape Division, leading the Appellant to seek leave to appeal ([paras 18–20]).

## I – Issues

The issues before the court were:

1. **Admission of Evidence**: Whether the High Court erred in refusing to admit a supplementary replying affidavit that the Appellant sought to present, which contained critical information regarding the Respondent's cancellation of the alleged agreement in subsequent action proceedings ([paras 23–24]).

2. **Effect of Cancellation on Occupation Rights**: If the affidavit had been admitted, what implications would that have on the Respondents' assertion of a right to occupy the property, given that they had allegedly cancelled the agreement with the Appellant in their particulars of claim? ([paras 31–33]).

3. **Just and Equitable Consideration**: Should the court grant the eviction order under the PIE Act, considering the circumstances surrounding the Respondents’ health, financial standing, and the nature of their relationship with the Appellant? ([paras 42–45]).

## R – Rules

The court’s judgment relied on several key legal principles and statutory provisions:

1. **Procedural Law**: The court recognized its discretion to admit further affidavits based on previous case law, emphasizing the importance of adjudicating matters based on all relevant facts ([paras 24–26]). 

2. **Contractual Law Principle**: The court highlighted principles of election in contract law—that once a party exercises a right to cancel a contract, they can neither approbate nor reprobate it, thus extinguishing their previous contractual rights ([paras 34–36]).

3. **Protection of Occupiers**: The PIE Act is designed to prevent unlawful evictions and underscores the need for a just and equitable assessment in eviction proceedings, which includes considerations related to health, age, and the socioeconomic circumstances of the occupiers ([paras 42–43]).

4. **Balancing Rights**: The ruling in Grobler v Phillips illustrated the balancing of rights in determining just and equitable outcomes in eviction cases ([para 51]).

## A – Application

Upon examining the facts against the relevant legal framework, the court made several determinations:

1. **Admission of the Affidavit**: The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the High Court should have admitted the supplementary affidavit as it contributed significantly to the argument regarding the Respondents' right to remain on the property. The court determined that the affidavit did not introduce new causes of action but instead clarified ongoing proceedings concerning the alleged cancellation of the earlier agreement ([paras 27–30]).

2. **Cancellation and Its Effects**: With the admitted evidence, the court found that the Respondents had indeed cancelled their agreement with the Appellant. The consequence of such cancellation was the loss of their right to occupy the property, thus classifying them as unlawful occupiers under the PIE Act ([paras 37–41]).

3. **Just and Equitable Evaluation**: Regarding the question of whether the order for eviction would be just and equitable, the court acknowledged the Respondents' vulnerable state due to age and health. However, it ultimately determined that the Appellant's continuous liability towards the mortgage and his efforts to find alternative accommodation for the Respondents shifted the balance in favor of eviction. The Appellant had proposed suitable alternative living arrangements which were considered adequate given the circumstances ([paras 46–52]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal, setting aside the lower courts' orders and allowing the eviction of the Respondents from the property. It ruled that the supplementary affidavit should have been admitted, which clarified the Respondents' cancellation of the agreement impacted their right to occupy the property. 

Though the Respondents' vulnerable condition was acknowledged, the court ruled the eviction would be just and equitable due to the ongoing financial burdens on the Appellant and the reasonable accommodations offered to the Respondents. The conditions for the eviction included provisions for alternative accommodation and financial support for relocation costs, ensuring that the Respondents would not be left homeless ([paras 53]).

This case underscores the court's commitment to balancing property rights with protections against arbitrary evictions, particularly in sensitive familial relationships, whilst also affirming the enforceability of contractual decisions made by the parties involved.
