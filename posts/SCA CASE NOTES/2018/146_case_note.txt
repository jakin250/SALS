# FIRAC Case Note on **Gesiena Maria Botha N.O. & another v The National Director of Public Prosecutions 920/2017** [2018] ZASCA 146

## F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([1–8])

The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa decided on an appeal against a judgment from the Northern Cape Division of the High Court concerning the forfeiture of property and shares owned by the late Yolanda Rachel Botha, under the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (POCA) ([1]). The appellants were Gesiena Maria Botha, executrix of the estate of the deceased, and Angelique Botha, a trustee of the Jyba Beleggings Trust ([2]). The State sought forfeiture on the grounds that the property and shares were proceeds of unlawful activities stemming from alleged corruption ([4]).

Yolanda Rachel Botha was the Head of the Northern Cape Department of Social Services and Population Development from January 2001 until April 2009 ([3]). During her tenure, she was implicated in corrupt practices involving the awarding of tenders to Trifecta, a company associated with a close associate who had died prior to the trial. The allegations included a violation of procurement processes to facilitate six lease agreements totaling approximately R81 million, in exchange for renovations valued at R1.2 million and a 10% shareholding in Trifecta ([4],[5]). Following Parliament’s investigation into her conduct, Botha passed away prior to the conclusion of her criminal trial ([8]).

The High Court had previously declared the property and shares forfeited under section 50 of POCA after finding that they constituted the proceeds of corruption ([1]). The central issues raised in the appeal concerned the legality of the forfeiture of the entire property and whether it was constitutionally justified ([6]).

## I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([2–3])

The principal issues for determination were:

1. **Whether the trial court correctly determined that the property and shares constituted the proceeds of unlawful activities, as defined under section 50(1)(b) of POCA** ([6]).
2. **Whether the forfeiture of the entire property to the State was justified or whether it constituted an arbitrary deprivation of property under section 25(1) of the Constitution of South Africa** ([40]).

These issues arose from the established facts that the deceased had allegedly received unlawful benefits in exchange for facilitating corrupt contracts ([5]).

## R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([9–10],[11])

The legal framework pertinent to this case includes:

1. **Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (POCA)**:
   - Section 48 empowers the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) to apply for a forfeiture order.
   - Section 50 details the criteria for declaring property as the proceeds of unlawful activities, stipulating that the court must determine this on a balance of probabilities ([10]).

2. **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa**:
   - Section 25(1) prohibits arbitrary deprivation of property ([40]).
   - Section 217 mandates that state procurement processes must be fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective ([11]).

3. **Case Law**:
   - Reference was made to precedents, including **Mohunram v NDPP** and **Prophet v NDPP**, which outline the need for a proportionality inquiry when evaluating forfeiture orders and emphasize that forfeiture serves to remove the incentives for crime rather than to punish offenders ([40]).

## A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([39–46])

In considering whether the property and shares constituted proceeds of unlawful activities, the court concurred with the findings of the High Court that the deceased had induced the renovations and received shares from Trifecta as gratification for her role in the procurement process ([39]). The evidence revealed significant irregularities and a deliberate breach of procurement regulations by the deceased to favor Trifecta, thus satisfying the threshold for unlawful activity under POCA ([22]).

The court then addressed the proportionality of the forfeiture order. It noted that while the property was indeed linked to illicit acts, the deprivation of the first appellant’s property must be weighed against her established rights. Here, the Supreme Court questioned the conclusion reached by the High Court on the reasonableness of seizing the entire property, as the NDPP had only intended to claim the value commensurate with the unlawful benefits received ([43]).

The court emphasized that forfeiture is not a punitive measure but one aimed at achieving a broader societal goal of deterring crime ([44]). Given the circumstances, the forfeiture of the entire property was deemed disproportionate, leading to a modification of the High Court’s ruling. The court found that the amount of R758,014.83 represented the actual benefit received and was the correct figure for the forfeiture ([45]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([46])

The appeal was upheld in part, altering the High Court’s decision by limiting the forfeiture order to the determined amount rather than the entire property. The first appellant was ordered to pay an amount of R758,014.83 to the state within a specific timeframe ([46]). Additionally, it reinforced the principle that property forfeitures must align with the constitutional safeguards against arbitrary deprivation.

The implications of this judgment stress the necessity for law enforcement to adhere to fair processes in forfeiture proceedings. The court’s decision illustrates the balancing act between eradicating corruption and ensuring that individuals are not subjected to disproportionate penalties for their involvement in unlawful acts. Moreover, it highlights the need for clear communication from the prosecuting authority regarding the extent of property sought for forfeiture. Overall, the judgment serves as a critical reference point in future cases involving property rights under POCA.
