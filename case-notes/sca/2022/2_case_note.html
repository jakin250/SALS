Supreme Court of Appeal

Eastern Cape Rural Development Agency and Another v Agribee Beef Fund (Pty) Ltd and Others

This case addresses a tripartite agreement concluded on 16 July 2018 involving the Department of Rural Development and Agrarian Reform of the Eastern Cape provincial government, the Eastern Cape Rural Development Agency (the Agency), and Agribee Beef Fund (Pty) Ltd (the ECBF).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 2 (6 January 2022) Coram: Saldulker ADP, Da

Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Case No: 827/2020
Neutral Citation: [2022] ZASCA 2 (6 January 2022)
Coram: Saldulker ADP, Dambuza, Mathopo, Mocumie, and Plasket JJA
Heard: 22 November 2021
Delivered: 6 January 2022

FIRAC Method Case Analysis

F – Facts

This case addresses a tripartite agreement concluded on 16 July 2018 involving the Department of Rural Development and Agrarian Reform of the Eastern Cape provincial government, the Eastern Cape Rural Development Agency (the Agency), and Agribee Beef Fund (Pty) Ltd (the ECBF). This agreement aimed to support the transition of black smallholder beef farmers into commercial producers, funded by a budget of approximately R67.5 million over three years, that was to be administered by the Agency ([1]–[7]).

Following the signing of the agreement, on 19 March 2019, the Agency and the Member of the Executive Council for Rural Development and Agrarian Reform sought to challenge its validity in the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court. Initially, their application was dismissed by Brooks J, who ordered them to pay costs. However, leave to appeal was granted ([1]–[2]).

The core issue presented before the Supreme Court was whether the agreement in question constituted a procurement for goods and services, which would necessitate compliance with the constitutional requirements set out in section 217(1) of the South African Constitution. This section mandates that any trade by an organ of state must follow a fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective procurement system ([2]–[3]).

I – Issues

The pivotal issue in this case is whether the agreement between the state departments and Agribee qualified as one for the procurement of goods or services under section 217(1) of the Constitution. Should it be determined that it did, the absence of a corresponding procurement process would render the agreement invalid. The respondents argued that the agreement was effectively a transfer of funds rather than a procurement for goods or services, while the appellants contended that it clearly fell within the scope of section 217(1) ([2]–[2], [30]–[34]).

R – Rules

The legal framework governing this case includes:

  1. Constitution of South Africa - Section 217(1) which states:

> "When an organ of state … contracts for goods or services, it must do so in accordance with a system that is fair, equitable, transparent, competitive and cost-effective."

  1. Relevant case law:
  • Airports Company South Africa SOC Ltd v Imperial Group Ltd and Others ([2020] ZASCA 2) wherein the court interpreted the application of section 217(1) to include various forms of service delivery agreements involving state entities.
  • Auditor-General of SA v MEC for Economic Opportunities, Western Cape and Another ([2021] ZASCA 133) discussed accounting standards but did not deal directly with procurement rules, which Agribee was keen to leverage in its defense ([31]–[32]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal, led by Plasket JA, emphasized the necessity for an evaluative approach centered on the actual obligations undertaken by the parties rather than mere nomenclature regarding the nature of the agreement ([25]–[37]).

In evaluating the agreement, the court found that it involved the delivery of tangible goods (beef weaners, veterinary kits, feed supplements) and associated services (training and mentorship) to smallholder farmers. The significant involvement of public funds (over R67.5 million) positioned the agreement squarely within the statutory framework of procurement laws as intended by section 217(1) ([12], [36]).

The court distinguished this case from Auditor-General of SA, explaining that the latter was concerned with accounting practices rather than direct procurement issues ([32]–[34]). It further found that the absence of a proper procurement process made the agreement invalid as it did not comply with constitutional and legislative mandates. It reiterated the importance of transparency and fairness in public procurement, echoing prior judgments regarding the interpretation of contracts involving state functions ([35]–[37]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the agreement between the Department, the Agency, and Agribee was indeed one that involved procurement for goods and services and therefore was subject to compliance with section 217(1). The court declared the agreement invalid due to the absence of a required public procurement process ([37]).

Order of the Court:

  1. The appeal was upheld with costs.
  2. The previous court’s ruling was set aside, replacing it with a declaration that the agreement was invalid. The first respondent (Agribee) was ordered to pay the applicants' costs.

This judgment reinforces the imperative of adhering to constitutional procurement stipulations by public entities, thereby upholding the principles of transparency and accountability in the utilization of public funds ([38]).
