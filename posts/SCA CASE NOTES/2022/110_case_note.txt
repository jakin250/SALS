Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# Case Note: Imbuko Wines (Pty) Ltd v Reference Audio CC (405/2021)

## F - Facts

The case revolves around a dispute between Imbuko Wines (Pty) Ltd (Appellant) and Reference Audio CC (Respondent) concerning a claimed cession of debt from Dipole CC (Dipole) to Imbuko. The initial agreement between Dipole and Reference Audio, established in December 2012, involved Dipole supplying audio equipment to Reference Audio, with an obligation for the latter to pay within 30 days of receiving an invoice from Dipole ([para 2]). 

During the relevant period from January to April 2013, Imbuko alleged that Dipole had ceded its rights to claim payment from Reference Audio to Imbuko. Imbuko submitted nine invoices to Reference Audio, which listed transactions between Dipole and Reference Audio as evidence of their claims. Imbuko's claim against Reference Audio was for a total of R602,866.22, which included a detailed statement of account demonstrating Imbuko as the creditor and Reference Audio as the debtor ([paras 3–4]).

In its defense, Reference Audio denied any knowledge of the alleged cession and argued that it had fulfilled its payment obligations to Dipole. The matter first went to trial in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, where the judge ruled in favor of Imbuko, determining that a valid cession had occurred. However, the full court, on appeal, reversed this finding, leading Imbuko to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal for a final resolution ([paras 1–3, 13]).

## I - Issues

The primary legal issues for determination were:

1. Whether an oral cession of debt had been established between Dipole and Imbuko to allow Imbuko to pursue Reference Audio for payment.
2. Whether Reference Audio had sufficiently demonstrated that it had settled its debts to Dipole, as alleged ([paras 14]).

## R - Rules

The court relied upon key principles of cession in South African law, primarily:

- Cession is defined as a bilateral juristic act that involves the transfer of rights through an agreement between a cedent and a cessionary ([para 15]).
- The burden of proof lies on the cessionary (Imbuko) to demonstrate the existence of the cession on a balance of probabilities. The mere act of cession does not require confirmation from the debtor ([paras 15–17]).
- Knowledge of the cession by the debtor is not a condition for the validity of the cession; however, it is relevant in determining the rights of a creditor once payments are made ([para 20]).

Relevant precedents included:
- **LTA Engineering Co Ltd v Seacat Investments (Pty) Ltd** stipulated that cessions can occur without debtor concurrence ([para 15]).
- **McWilliams v First Consolidated Holdings (Pty) Ltd** highlighted that a party’s silence in the presence of an assertion can constitute an admission ([para 26]).

## A - Application

The court critically analyzed the testimonies presented by both parties, particularly the evidence of Mr. Sudhir Manmohan Singh from Imbuko and Mr. Michael Hoffman from Reference Audio. The court found Mr. Singh's account regarding the cession credible and consistent with the operational context of the agreement between Dipole and Reference Audio. The court noted the long-standing relationship among the parties and acknowledged that the frustrations regarding invoicing and payment may have prompted the cession arrangement ([paras 16–17]).

The Supreme Court of Appeal determined that the high court had correctly concluded that Imbuko had established cession on a balance of probabilities. The failure to call Dr. Singh to corroborate the alleged cession was not considered fatal; the cession could be established through the conduct of the parties ([para 17]). Additionally, Reference Audio’s claim of ignorance about the cession lacked substantiation, especially given their proactive engagement in payments to Imbuko ([paras 21–27]).

On the issue of payment obligations, the court found that Reference Audio had not presented credible evidence to refute the claims of Imbuko or to confirm its own payments to Dipole during the relevant period ([para 28]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that Imbuko had established the cession of debt from Dipole, thus entitling it to pursue the claim against Reference Audio for the unpaid invoices. The court reversed the full court's decision, reinstating the High Court’s judgment in favor of Imbuko, thereby underscoring the evidentiary threshold required to establish cession and the importance of credible engagement in business transactions ([paras 29–30]). 

The implications of this ruling reinforce the principles surrounding the cession of debts, emphasizing that while debtor awareness is significant in terms of operational acknowledgment, it does not obstruct the validity of the cession itself, affirming the position of creditors in ensuring compliance from debtors through proper financial documentation and communication.
