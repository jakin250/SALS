# FIRAC Case Note on Dlepu v The State [2007] SCA 81 (RSA)

## F - Facts

In the case of **Dlepu v The State** [2007] SCA 81 (RSA), the appellant, Norman Dlepu, was convicted alongside two others for robbery with aggravating circumstances and unlawful possession of a firearm and ammunition ([1]). The Port Elizabeth Regional Court sentenced them to 15 years imprisonment for robbery, with Dlepu receiving an additional 18 months for possession of a firearm and a fine for possession of ammunition, all sentences to run concurrently ([1]).

Following their conviction, Dlepu and his co-accused appealed to the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, which partially upheld their appeals by dismissing the possession charges but affirming the robbery conviction ([2]). Dlepu's attempt to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was eventually granted ([3]).

The incident in question occurred on 1 March 1999 when the victim, Albert Henry Collin Moorcroft, was robbed of his vehicle and belongings by two individuals shortly after leaving a bank ([4]). Shortly thereafter, police officers received an alert to be on the lookout for the vehicle. After spotting a suspicious gold Audi, the officers followed and conducted a search upon stopping it. They found items linking the Audi to the robbery, including Moorcroft's briefcase and identification ([5], [6]).

Dlepu was identified as a passenger in the Audi, having not participated directly in the robbery itself ([8]). The trial court’s judgment rested heavily on circumstantial evidence and inconsistencies in the testimonies of the accused ([11]–[12]). 

Underpinning the conviction was the conclusion that all occupants of the Audi were at least complicit in the robbery due to their combination of narratives indicating a common purpose ([10], [13]). Importantly, no direct identification by Moorcroft was made against any of the accused during the identification parade ([9]).

## I - Issues

The primary issue before the SCA was whether the circumstantial evidence presented at trial warranted a rejection of Dlepu's version of events and justified the conclusion that he was part of a common purpose with the actual robbers ([18]). In particular, the Court contemplated whether Dlepu’s version was "reasonably possibly true" in the context of the evidence before them, particularly regarding claims that his guilty principle was established beyond a reasonable doubt ([17], [30]–[31]).

## R - Rules

The court’s analysis was grounded in established legal principles regarding circumstantial evidence and the burden of proof in criminal cases. The law stipulates that for a conviction based on circumstantial evidence to be valid, all proven facts must exclude every reasonable inference save the one sought to be drawn ([15]).

A vital precedent referenced was **S v Reddy** 1996 (2) SACR 1 (A), which emphasized the requirement for all facts to collectively lead to a single inference of guilt, while also outlining that an accused's version must be considered and cannot simply be rejected on the basis of improbability alone ([16]). Similarly, in **S v Shackwell** 2001 (2) SACR 185 (SCA), the court cautioned against dismissing an accused’s version merely because it is improbable; it could only be rejected if proven to be "so improbable that it cannot reasonably possibly be true" ([31]).

## A - Application

In its deliberation, the SCA found that the regional court had indeed applied the incorrect test by treating the versions of Dlepu and his co-accused as unified when they should have been considered individually ([21], [23]). The SCA recognized Dlepu's account—that he was using the Audi innocently in connection with his business—was not inherently contradictory and had not been effectively rebutted by the state ([25], [27]). 

The circumstantial evidence presented did not directly implicate him in the planning or execution of the robbery. Notably, Dlepu was neither the driver of the Audi nor had he been identified as fleeing the scene ([28])—his account of being a mere passenger waiting for another individual to conclude a transaction was deemed reasonable. Evidence supporting the state's conclusions about the Audi being a "pick-up" car for the robbers did not definitively establish Dlepu’s involvement in the robbery plot ([29]–[30]).

Consequently, the SCA held that the assumptions made by the regional court about the implications of the circumstantial evidence were overstated and misdirected. The SCA articulated that the regional court failed to appropriately explore whether Dlepu's version was reasonably possibly true ([24], [31]).

## C - Conclusion

The SCA ultimately concluded that Dlepu's appeal should be upheld, recognizing that his version of events was reasonably possibly true and should have been accepted. The earlier conviction and sentence were set aside, with the court ruling Dlepu not guilty of all charges against him ([32]). 

The ruling carries critical implications for future criminal cases involving circumstantial evidence, reinforcing the necessity for courts to individually consider each accused’s narrative without presuming a unified defense based solely on discrepancies among co-accused's testimonies. Moreover, it underscores the principle that the prosecution must discharge the burden of proof beyond reasonable doubt, especially when determining complicity based on circumstantial evidence and the common purpose doctrine.
