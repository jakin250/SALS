Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

### Case Note: Road Accident Fund v Masindi (586/2017) [2018] ZASCA 94

#### FIRAC Method

**F – Facts:**
The core issue in Road Accident Fund v Masindi ([1]) revolves around the calculation of the five-year prescription period relevant to a claim for compensation lodged by Khathutshelo Gladys Masindi. The appellant, the Road Accident Fund (RAF), contended that the claim prescribed on June 16, 2014, the last day of the five-year period, which was also a public holiday. Masindi's claim stemmed from an accident on June 17, 2009, where she and her minor child were severely injured due to the negligence of the driver of an insured vehicle. The claim was formally instituted in the High Court in June 2014, just before the expiration of the statutory period prescribed by Section 23(3) of the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 (the RAF Act).

The appellant contended that the summons needed to be served before the expiry of the five-year period to interrupt prescription, asserting that the last day in consideration was June 16, 2014. The High Court initially found in favor of the respondent on this issue, dismissing the special plea of prescription ([3]). The High Court reasoned that a strict interpretation that led to the expiration of the right to claim on a holiday was unjust, thus allowing for the next working day to be counted for the service of summons ([2], [4]).

**I – Issues:**
The primary legal questions considered by the court were:
1. How should the five-year prescription period under Section 23(3) of the RAF Act be calculated, particularly when the last day falls on a public holiday ([1], [4])?
2. Should a rigid interpretation of statutory provisions limit a claimant’s right to access to justice as outlined in Section 34 of the Constitution of South Africa ([12])?

**R – Rules:**
The court primarily relied on the interpretation of the following legal provisions:
- **Section 23(3) of the RAF Act** specifies that no claim under Section 17(1) will prescribe before a period of five years from the cause of action has elapsed.
- **Section 4 of the Interpretation Act 33 of 1957**, which provides guidelines on how to compute periods expressed in days, stating that the last day is not included if it falls on a public holiday ([8]).
- **Regulation 1 under the RAF Act**, defining what constitutes 'a day' for the purposes of the Act ([9]).
- **Sections 34 and 39 of the South African Constitution**, which protect the right to access to courts and require courts to consider international and foreign law, respectively ([12]).

The court also considered precedents, notably the case of *Nedcor Bank Ltd v The Master of the High Court* ([5]), which provides insight into statutory interpretation. 

**A – Application:**
The court found that despite the applicant's arguments for a strict interpretation of the timeline, there was significant merit in a more purposive interpretation to reconcile the statutory provisions with the Constitution's guarantees ([11], [20]). The majority posited that the strict application resulting in the deprivation of a right to claim on a public holiday would essentially discriminate against the claimant and negate the intent behind the RAF Act, which is designed to protect individuals against the negligence of others ([12]).

The court analyzed the impossibility of service due to the holiday and drew parallels with English law (specifically *Pritam Kaur v S Russel & Sons* and *Nottingham City Council v Calverton Parish Council*), supporting the notion that statutory time limits should accommodate circumstances beyond the claimant's control ([16], [17]). Therefore, the High Court's reasoning that the five-year period should extend to the next working day, as Monday was a holiday, was upheld as it enriches justice rather than diminishes it ([14], [20]).

**C – Conclusion:**
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal and affirmed the High Court's decision to allow the claim to proceed, thus ruling that the five-year prescription period in situations where the expiry date falls on a public holiday should be extended to the next working day ([22]). The decision underscored the importance of balancing statutory interpretation with constitutional rights, emphasizing that the legislature likely did not intend for such strict rigidity that would impede justice ([20]). The court’s approach illustrates an ongoing commitment to ensuring access to justice, aligning legislative interpretation with the welfare of individuals as prescribed by social legislation like the RAF Act.

The implications of this ruling highlight the need for a nuanced approach in statutory interpretation, particularly in social justice contexts, and reinforce the significance of constitutional protections in judicial decision-making. The court’s dismissal of the appeal with costs signifies a clear endorsement of the respondent's right to access the legal system without undue barriers imposed by technicalities within the law.
