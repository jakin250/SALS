# Case Note: Eskom Holdings SOC Ltd v Vaal River Development Association (Pty) Ltd and Others [2022] ZACC 44

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])
This case involves Eskom Holdings SOC Limited (Eskom), a state-owned enterprise responsible for electricity supply in South Africa, which sought to challenge an interim interdict granted by the High Court. The interdict prohibited Eskom from reducing the bulk electricity supply to two municipalities—Ngwathe and Lekwa—until the matter was adjudicated in a forthcoming review application. 

Eskom generates bulk electricity, supplying it to municipal entities under contract, including a Notified Maximum Demand (NMD) established in prior agreements with each municipality. By July 2020, due to significant non-payment issues and operational deficiencies, Eskom initiated "rotational load reduction,” effectively restricting supply to contractually agreed levels. This restriction had dire implications for residents of the municipalities who relied on consistent electricity for basic services, including water supply and sewage processing ([paras 6–9]). 

The residents, through the Vaal River Development Association and Lekwa Ratepayers Association, raised urgent applications before the High Court for interim relief to restore electricity supplies, arguing that Eskom's decisions infringed upon rights protected under the Bill of Rights ([paras 8–10]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 11–20])
Key issues before the court included:
1. Whether the residents established a prima facie right to the supply of electricity pending a review of Eskom’s decision, despite the lack of a contractual relationship with Eskom.
2. Whether Eskom, as an organ of state, owed any constitutional or statutory duties to the residents and if those duties were breached by the supply reduction.
3. The implications of procedural fairness under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA) regarding Eskom’s decisions ([paras 19–20]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 21–32])
The court based its analyses on several legal principles, including:
- The concept of administrative action and its binding nature under section 33 of the Constitution, which mandates just administrative action and is applicable to both typical and exceptional circumstances.
- Precedents from South African case law, particularly *Government of the Republic of South Africa v Grootboom* [2000] ZACC 19, where basic services like electricity are intertwined with the enjoyment of various fundamental rights (such as dignity and life).
- Provisions under the Electricity Regulation Act (ERA), specifically section 21(5), outlining Eskom's rights and obligations regarding electricity supply.
- The Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA), emphasizing the necessity for procedural fairness in administrative actions affecting public rights ([paras 21–24]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 35–45])
The court noted that:
- The rights to a sustainable electricity supply are inextricably linked to other constitutional rights, even if not explicitly mentioned in the Bill of Rights. The residents' claims were thus evaluated against the backdrop of these broader rights ([paras 22–26]).
- Eskom failed to appropriately engage the residents in the decision-making process surrounding supply reductions, which is inconsistent with principles of procedural fairness as outlined in PAJA and general administrative law ([paras 35–40]).
- On balancing harms, the court found that the detriment to the residents outweighed the potential operational difficulties Eskom cited, indicating that the social and health crises resulting from inadequate electricity would have catastrophic effects on the residents’ wellbeing ([paras 41–45]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 46–60])
The Constitutional Court upheld the urgent need for an interim interdict, asserting that:
1. The residents had established a prima facie case for the review, demonstrating several rights violations.
2. Eskom, by reducing the electricity supply without prior notice or proper engagement with affected stakeholders, acted unreasonably and failed to uphold constitutional norms of procedural fairness.
3. The necessity of maintaining the integrity of public rights and needs justified the interim restoration of electricity as a critical measure to avert further humanitarian crises.

Eskom's appeal was thus dismissed with costs, including the costs for two counsel. The decision reflects a broader constitutional commitment to safeguarding citizens' rights while maintaining checks on state powers and underscores the importance of procedural fairness in administrative actions ([paras 56–60]). 

---

This case establishes significant precedents related to the rights of individuals concerning public service entities and their obligations under constitutional law, showcasing the intersection of administrative and rights law in South Africa.
