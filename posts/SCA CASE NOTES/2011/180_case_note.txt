Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: Votani Majola v Nitro Securitisation 1 (PTY) LTD

## F – Facts
The case concerns an appeal brought by Votani Majola (the appellant) against a summary judgment granted by the South Gauteng High Court, which ordered him to return a Jaguar S Type 3.0 SE vehicle to Nitro Securitisation 1 (Pty) Ltd (the respondent) and to pay costs on an attorney and client scale. The summary judgment was issued on November 20, 2009, following Majola's failure to make payments under an installment sale agreement with Firstrand Bank Limited (trading as Wesbank), which had ceded its rights to Nitro.

Initially, leave to appeal was granted on July 29, 2010. The first hearing of the appeal took place on May 10, 2011, but neither Majola nor a representative attended, resulting in the dismissal of the appeal for non-prosecution and imposition of costs on an attorney-client scale. After deliberation, Nitro requested that the matter be set down again, waiving its right to be paid costs prior to the hearing. The appeal was set down for September 19, 2011. Majola again failed to appear, claiming the set down was irregular due to non-compliance with the court's prior directives. The court was informed on both occasions of his phone call to his attorney indicating he would not attend.

Notably, Majola, despite being an attorney himself and thus an officer of the court, displayed a lack of professionalism in his absences and failed to provide explanations or apologies regarding his non-appearances ([1]–[12]).

## I – Issues
The core legal questions in this case revolve around:

1. **Whether the appellate court has discretion to dismiss an appeal for non-appearance under Rule 13(3) of the Supreme Court of Appeal Rules** ([13]).
2. **Whether sufficient grounds exist to justify an alternative order, such as striking the appeal from the roll or granting a postponement** ([14]).
3. **What factors ought to be considered in exercising that discretion, particularly given the appellant's repeated non-appearance and the consequences for the respondent** ([15]).

## R – Rules
The legal framework applicable to the appeal includes:

1. **Rule 13(3) of the Supreme Court of Appeal Rules**, which stipulates that failure to appear results in dismissal for non-prosecution unless the court directs otherwise ([13]).
2. **Precedents concerning dismissal for non-appearance**, notably **Gumede v Protea Assurance Co Ltd**, where the court provided guidance on the default position being dismissal unless circumstances warrant a less drastic order ([14]).
3. **The circumstances surrounding non-appearance**: Relevant factors include the reasons for absence, implications for the respondent, and the prospects of success in the appeal ([15]).

## A – Application
Upon examining the circumstances surrounding Majola's non-appearance, the court found no justifiable reason to postpone the matter or strike it off the roll. Significant factors included:

1. **Repeated Absence**: Majola failed to appear on two separate occasions, with an absence lacking any substantive explanation or apology ([16]).
2. **Professional Conduct**: As an attorney, Majola was expected to maintain a higher standard of professionalism, and his actions showed a discourteous attitude towards the court ([16]).
3. **Prejudice to the Respondent**: Nitro had already suffered prejudice owing to the prolonged possession of the vehicle despite having received judgment nearly two years prior. Continued delays would perpetuate this situation and undermine the finality intended by the court ([17]).
4. **Meritorious Arguments**: Majola's points challenging the summary judgment were ultimately found to lack merit. The court assessed each argument raised and found no reasonable prospects of success on appeal ([18]–[24]). For instance, the issues regarding the section 129 notice were not sufficiently substantiated, the authority of the respondent's representative was validated, and it was determined that Wesbank's cession of rights did not require Majola's consent.

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Majola’s appeal with costs on an attorney and client scale. The court reiterated the necessity of adherence to procedural rules and recognized the implications of an attorney's conduct in relation to court expectations. This case serves as a cautionary tale highlighting the importance of profesionalism and compliance within legal proceedings. The judgment emphasizes that a failure to respect procedural requirements and court orders can lead to adverse outcomes, establishing a robust precedent regarding dismissal for non-appearance in appellate matters ([25]–[27]).
