Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: Ruth Eunice Sechoaro v Patience Kgwadi (896/2021) [2023] ZASCA 46 (4 April 2023)

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–12])

The case revolves around **Ruth Eunice Sechoaro** (the applicant) and **Patience Kgwadi** (the respondent), concerning a dispute over property rights following the death of Ms. Kgwadi's ex-husband, Mr. Israel Kgwadi. The critical events unfolded as follows:

1. **Marital History**: Ms. Kgwadi married Mr. Kgwadi in community of property on **14 May 1987**. Their marriage was dissolved on **25 October 1991**, during which a settlement agreement was established but did not include the division of a jointly owned property in Boksburg ([paras 1–3]).

2. **Post-Divorce Arrangements**: Although the settlement agreement left the property undivided, there was a verbal agreement that each party would receive half of its value, with Mr. Kgwadi promising to pay Ms. Kgwadi her share, which he failed to do. The respondent later married the applicant on **25 September 2010**, just before Mr. Kgwadi's death on **29 September 2014**.

3. **The Contested Agreement**: While Ms. Kgwadi was hospitalized due to severe injuries sustained in a car accident, a messenger from a law firm presented her with a "variation agreement" on **18 July 2012**. She signed it, believing it reaffirmed the original understanding about the property; however, it instead transferred sole ownership to Mr. Kgwadi without compensation ([paras 5–7]).

4. **Claims Arising**: Upon attempts to sell the property, Ms. Kgwadi discovered that the new agreement negated her claims to 50% of the property’s sale value, leading her to challenge its enforceability ([para 8]).

5. **High Court Ruling**: The Gauteng Division of the High Court found the 2012 agreement unenforceable, upholding Ms. Kgwadi’s claim based on her mistaken belief and the circumstances of her signing the agreement ([para 11]).

6. **Application for Leave to Appeal**: The applicant, Ms. Sechoaro, sought leave to appeal the high court’s decision, which was initially denied ([paras 12, 13]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 13–15])

The principal legal issues at hand include:

1. **Nature of the Agreement**: Determining whether the 2012 agreement was indeed a variation of the prior settlement agreement, or if it constituted an unenforceable sham that did not reflect the true understanding between the parties regarding property rights ([para 13]).

2. **Unilateral Mistake**: Evaluating whether Ms. Kgwadi's unilateral mistake in signing the agreement was reasonable and excusable under the circumstances, allowing her to repudiate the contract ([para 16]).

3. **Assessment of Evidence**: Deciding whether the high court adequately considered the subjective elements of Ms. Kgwadi’s mental state and intention at the time of signing the agreement and whether these aspects constituted a legitimate dispute of fact ([para 14]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 16–22])

The court relied on several principles and precedents regarding contract law and unilateral mistake:

1. **Unilateral Mistake**: The test for unilateral mistake, referenced in *George v Fairmead (Pty) Ltd* and *National and Overseas Distributors Corporation (Pty) Ltd v Potato Board*, states that a party may escape contractual obligations if they can prove the mistake was reasonable (justus) and not attributable to their conduct ([paras 16–17]).

2. **Intent and Misrepresentation**: It is established that if a party had no reasonable grounds to believe they were binding themselves, and if the other party had not misrepresented facts, the grounds for claiming unilateral mistake are limited ([para 19]).

3. **Duty of Care in Agreements**: The court emphasized the responsibility of the party presenting an agreement to ensure that the other party is aware of the significant implications of what they are signing, particularly in contexts where the signing party is under duress or compromised mental capacity ([para 20]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 21–22])

1. **Evaluation of the 2012 Agreement**: The court invalidated the notion that the 2012 agreement was merely a variation of the earlier settlement agreement. Instead, it recognized that the latter agreement dispossessed Ms. Kgwadi of her property rights without appropriate consideration, leading to clear implications of deceit on the part of Mr. Kgwadi ([para 18]).

2. **Assessment of Mistake**: In considering the evidence, the court deemed Ms. Kgwadi's unilateral mistake— arising from her hospitalization and lack of mental acuity—comprised grounds for invalidating the contract, as she could not have reasonably understood the implications of signing away her rights to the property ([para 22]).

3. **Judgment on Appeal**: The court found no merit in the applicant's arguments concerning Ms. Kgwadi's alleged awareness and intention to bind herself contractually, reaffirming that her unchallenged evidence of her physical and mental condition at the signing was compelling enough to support her claims ([para 21]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 22–23])

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal held that:

1. Ms. Kgwadi's unilateral mistake was reasonable and excusable, affirming the high court’s decision to invalidate the 2012 agreement as unenforceable ([para 22]).

2. The court emphasized the lack of prospects for the applicant’s appeal, taking a firm stance on the importance of consent and understanding in contractual obligations.

3. The ruling highlighted significant implications for contract law in South Africa, particularly concerning the enforcement of agreements signed under duress or severe personal distress, asserting the rights of individuals who may be misled or not fully informed of their contractual obligations ([paras 22–23]).

4. The judgment reaffirmed the need for legal practitioners to uphold their duty in ensuring that clients understand the nature of agreements they are signing, particularly in vulnerable circumstances.

The application for leave to appeal was hence dismissed, with costs awarded against the applicant, reinforcing the court’s message of accountability and informed consent in contractual dealings ([para 23]).
