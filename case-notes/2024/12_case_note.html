FIRAC Case Note: Mphephu-Ramabulana Royal Family v Premier of the Limpopo Province and Others [2024] ZACC 12

Constitutional Court 2024 [2024] ZACC 12 --- #### F – Facts The *Mphephu-Ramabulana R

FIRAC Case Note: Mphephu-Ramabulana Royal Family v Premier of the Limpopo Province and Others [2024] ZACC 12

F – Facts

The Mphephu-Ramabulana Royal Family (applicant) sought to challenge the decision made by the Premier of Limpopo Province (first respondent) regarding the recognition of an acting King of the Vhavenda. This case stems from a protracted history of disputes regarding the leadership of the Vhavenda community, particularly the recognition of Mr. Toni Peter Mphephu-Ramabulana as King.

Initially, on 14 August 2010, Ms. Mphephu-Ramabulana was identified as the King, with the formal recognition granted by the President on 21 September 2012. However, a review application was initiated by Ms. Masindi Clementine Mphephu in December 2012, contesting this recognition. The High Court dismissed the application; however, upon appeal, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) found the earlier decisions unlawful and invalid, emphasizing the necessity for resolution of disputes regarding traditional leadership before any successor could be appointed ([paras 2–6]).

In February 2022, the MEC and Minister informed the Royal Family they would implement the SCA's ruling to withdraw recognition from Mr. Mphephu-Ramabulana. Consequently, the Royal Family recognized Mr. Mavhungu David Mphephu as the acting King on 1 March 2022 and requested formal recognition from the Premier nearly eight months later. The Premier's failure to act prompted the Royal Family to approach the High Court for review on 29 November 2022 ([paras 4–8]).

I – Issues

The primary issues before the Constitutional Court were:

  1. Whether this matter engaged the Court’s jurisdiction and warranted direct leave to appeal.
  2. Whether the appointment of an acting King was, in fact, premature given the circumstances surrounding the previous rulings.
  3. Whether the Premier's failure to recognize Mr. Mavhungu David Mphephu as the acting King constituted a breach of duty.
  4. If so, what appropriate remedies could be imposed? ([paras 9–10]).

R – Rules

The Court's jurisdiction was invoked under constitutional grounds, specifically concerning the review of public power exercised by officials. The principles outlined in relevant cases, such as Union Refugee Women v Director, Private Security Industry Regulatory Authority and Freedom of Religion South Africa v Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development, are instrumental in determining whether direct appeals should be admitted ([paras 11–13]).

The Court reiterated that typically, bypassing the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) is discouraged unless significant constitutional issues are at stake, which could justify such an approach ([para 13]).

A – Application

The Court found that the applicant failed to establish sufficient grounds for direct appeal to the Constitutional Court, primarily because it had not presented adequate reasons why lower courts should be bypassed. The applicant argued that the issue was pressing due to a perceived void in traditional leadership. However, the Court noted that this concern did not automatically merit direct engagement. The SCA’s previous findings had not addressed the specifics of whether the Premier unjustifiably failed to act, hence indicating that issues remained that required further judicial interpretation ([paras 9–16]).

Regarding the claim of premature appointment, the Court held that the application’s timing was indeed premature as it was contingent on unresolved matters outlined by the SCA. Similarly, the relief sought by the Royal Family was deemed incompetent; even though the High Court had initially struck the matter from the roll due to lack of urgency, it had nevertheless dealt briefly with the merits and dismissed the application, emphasizing the finality of its judgment ([paras 17–21]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed the application for direct leave to appeal, affirming the High Court's ruling. The Court underscored the operational difficulties surrounding the recognition of traditional leaders but reaffirmed that procedural integrity dictated the refusal of the requested leave due to the lack of compelling justification for bypassing the SCA. The Court asserted that the Royal Family could still pursue their appeal within the appropriate court, highlighting that the lacuna in leadership necessitates prompt and serious attention ([paras 22–25]).

The judgment emphasizes the need to adhere to procedural processes while navigating complexities surrounding traditional leadership, showcasing the interplay between statutory framework and customary law in South African jurisprudence. The Court also decided that no order as to costs was appropriate, reflecting the complexities and sensitivities involved in this case ([order]).

This case note is prepared for legal professionals and scholars interested in constitutional and traditional leadership law in South Africa.
