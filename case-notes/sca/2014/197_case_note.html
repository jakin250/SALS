Delport v The State (861/13) [2014] ZASCA 197

Supreme Court of Appeal 2014 [2014] ZASCA 197

F – Facts

The appellants, Hendrik Frederick Delport and others (collectively referred to as "the appellants"), were convicted of serious offences, including fraud and racketeering, before a regional magistrate after a lengthy trial that commenced on 12 July 2004 and spanned several years ([paras 2–5]). The appellants were accused of defrauding the South African Revenue Service (SARS) of approximately R264 million during the period from May 1998 to March 2002 ([para 2]).

The prosecution was led by Mr. P.A. van Wyk SC and Ms. T. Kannemeyer, whose previous affiliations with SARS raised questions about their authority to conduct the prosecution. During the trial, the appellants claimed that the prosecutors had not been properly appointed pursuant to the National Prosecuting Authority Act 32 of 1998 (NPA Act), leading to allegations of a lack of fair trial rights ([paras 3–4]).

A critical point arose when the magistrate ruled in 2009 that the prosecutors’ appointments were improper based on the criteria established in Bonugli v Deputy National Director of Public Prosecutions, which held that a reasonable perception of bias could infringe on the right to a fair trial ([paras 6–8]). The high court subsequently remitted the matter for trial continuation without addressing the merits ([para 12]). This remittance sparked prolonged litigation, eventually leading the appellants to seek leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) regarding specific questions of law surrounding the prosecutors’ titles to prosecute ([paras 13–14]).

The SCA ultimately addressed whether the remittal order was itself appealable, concluding that the high court's decision was not appealable due to its nature ([para 1]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions arising from this case were as follows:

  1. Appealability of the High Court's Remittal Order: Is the order of the high court that remitted the matter for the trial's continuation appealable, particularly given the procedural history and the questions of law identified? ([paras 1, 15])
  2. Authority of Prosecutors: Were the appointments of the prosecutors valid under the provisions of the NPA Act, and did the irregular appointment implicate the appellants' right to a fair trial? ([paras 3–4, 29])
  3. Jurisdictional Issues: Did the SCA have jurisdiction to hear the appeal given that the high court limited the grounds of appeal upon granting leave? ([para 40–43])

R – Rules

Several statutory provisions and established legal principles were crucial to the court’s reasoning:

  1. NPA Act: Sections 38(1) and (3) set forth the conditions under which prosecutors may be appointed, requiring agreements in writing, and Section 20 outlines the authorisation needed to institute prosecutions ([para 29]).
  2. Criminal Procedure Act: Section 106(1)(h) defines grounds upon which an accused can challenge the authority of the prosecution ([para 33]).
  3. Principles of Appealability: The court referenced a long-standing practice where uncompleted criminal proceedings are generally not subject to appeal, emphasizing that appeals are typically entertained only if the order is final, definitive of the parties' rights, and effectively disposes of a significant portion of the relief sought ([paras 20–24]).
  4. Constitutional Imperative: The right to a fair trial, as enshrined in Section 35(3)(d) of the Constitution, mandates that trials must commence and conclude without unreasonable delay, which influences the measure of judge’s discretion in determining appealability ([paras 22–23]).

A – Application

In its analysis, the SCA applied the rules regarding appealability flexibly, acknowledging that the remittal order did not meet the criteria for a final order suitable for appeal. The court reiterated its historical skepticism regarding piecemeal appeals during ongoing criminal proceedings, expressing concerns about disrupting the continuity of trials and the resultant implications for justice ([paras 19–27]).

The court entertained the possibility that unusual circumstances might justify an appeal; however, it assessed the appellants' claims against the backdrop of the trial's protracted timeline and concluded that no exceptional circumstances were present that warranted such a departure from the established procedure ([paras 39]). The court pointed out that the prosecution's title challenge arose well into the trial, leading to significant delays and compounding the inconvenience to all parties involved ([paras 36–38]).

Moreover, considering the high court's previous findings of substantial compliance with the NPA Act's requirements, the court ultimately determined that a jurisdictional void in the prosecutors' title was not evident nor sufficiently substantial to merit an appeal ([paras 41–43]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA struck the appeal from the roll, affirming the high court's order that remitted the case for continuation of the trial. It held that the remittal was not appealable as it did not constitute a final or definitive order concerning the rights of the parties involved, nor did it trigger the exceptional circumstances necessary to entertain a premature appeal in an unfinished trial ([paras 1, 40]).

The ruling underscores the importance of maintaining trial integrity and finality in the criminal process, reiterating that appeals should generally be reserved for completed proceedings to prevent undue delays and ensure the swift administration of justice ([paras 20–24, 27]). The implications of this decision highlight the judiciary's commitment to upholding procedural order, especially in complex cases that span many years while also protecting the constitutional rights of individuals facing serious criminal charges.
