Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# Case Note: BSB International Link CC v Readam South Africa (Pty) Ltd (279/2015) [2016] ZASCA 58

## FIRAC Case Notes

### F – Facts
This appeal originated from the Gauteng Local Division of the High Court, Johannesburg, concerning a demolition order issued against a building owned by BSB International Link CC (BSB) in Parkmore Township. The first respondent, Readam South Africa (Pty) Ltd (Readam), contended that BSB constructed its building unlawfully without the requisite approval under section 7 of the National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act 103 of 1977 (the NBSA) ([para 1]). The Gauteng Local Division ruled in favor of Readam, declaring the building's erection unlawful due to violations of the Sandton Town Planning Scheme and directed that BSB partially demolish the structure to ensure compliance ([paras 1-2]).

BSB contended that it had received valid approvals for its building plans and sought to challenge the High Court’s dismissal of its counter-application for further discovery from the municipality regarding the approvals ([paras 4-6]). Notably, the municipality did not actively participate in these proceedings, which complicated the court's determination of the matter ([paras 2-3]).

### I – Issues
The principal legal issues before the court included:
1. Whether BSB had standing at common law to challenge the legality of the building approval granted to it by the municipality.
2. The applicable standard of discretion for demolition orders under the NBSA versus common law regarding unlawful encroachments ([para 20]).
3. Whether BSB had indeed breached provisions of the Sandton Town Planning Scheme concerning building coverage and parking requirements ([paras 8, 10, 16]).
4. The validity of the temporary certificate of occupancy issued to BSB ([para 30]).
5. The necessity and scope of a partial demolition order versus total demolition under the competing claims of statutory and common law ([para 27]).

### R – Rules
The court primarily relied on:
- **National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act 103 of 1977 (NBSA)**, particularly sections 7 and 21 ([para 21]).
- **Sandton Town Planning Scheme**, particularly clauses regarding building coverage and parking requirements ([paras 9, 15]).
- Relevant case law, particularly *Lester v Ndlambe Municipality* ([para 22]) indicating that a magistrate lacks discretion to avoid total demolition orders when jurisdictional facts are established.
- Judicial principles from prior decisions, particularly on common law rights concerning neighbour encroachment and applicable remedies ([paras 24-26]).

### A – Application
The court analyzed the evidence surrounding BSB’s compliance with building regulations. Notably, Readam provided expert evidence establishing that BSB's building coverage exceeded the permissible 60% limit established by the scheme, and BSB failed to produce contrary evidence ([paras 10-14]). The court found that BSB's denials lacked sufficient factual support, failing to create a bona fide dispute of fact. The ordering of partial demolition was scrutinized in light of the statutory requirements under the NBSA and common law standards ([paras 20-27]).

The court noted that, under common law, if a building infringes upon the rights of a neighbor, a remedy may be granted at the court's discretion. However, section 21 of the NBSA sets a stricter framework with less judicial flexibility once unlawful erection is established ([para 27]). The court ultimately concluded that while common law affords a broader discretion, statutory law imposes non-negotiable mandates regarding unlawful structures.

Significantly, it clarified that BSB's actions of continuing construction despite the known illegality were detrimental, and thus, the court was justified in enforcing the demolition order to preserve compliance with lawful development frameworks ([para 26]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that the High Court's order requiring partial demolition must be amended to clarify that any demolition is contingent upon certification from a qualified engineer regarding the structure's safety ([para 33]). The court found merit in Readam’s arguments advocating for compliance with building regulations and found BSB's counter-application to compel further discovery lacking in substance.

The appeal was granted in part, affirming the need for compliance with statutory and local planning requirements while emphasizing the importance of procedural integrity in municipal approvals. This case underscores the tension between statutory strictures and common law remedies, revealing implications for future disputes involving building compliance and municipal authority over local developments ([paras 1, 17, 22, and 27]).
