Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: Vesagie NO & Others v Erwee NO & Another [2014] ZASCA 121

### F – Facts
The case revolves around a dispute concerning the sale of shares and loan accounts between the appellants, trustees of the BEN Trust, and the respondents, trustees of the ACE Trust ([para 2]). The third appellant concluded an agreement with the respondents on behalf of the BEN Trust, which stipulated both deferred payments of the purchase price (R15 million) and interest liabilities ([paras 2-3]). The appellants made an initial payment of R750,000, after which no further payments were made, prompting the ACE Trust to seek to recover the outstanding balance of R14.25 million, plus interest and costs ([paras 2-3]).

The primary legal contention during the trial was whether the agreement constituted a "credit transaction" under section 8(4)(f) of the National Credit Act 34 of 2005, which requires that any party extending credit must be registered as a credit provider per section 40 of the Act ([paras 3-4]). If the court determined that the agreement was indeed a credit transaction due to the stipulation of interest on deferred payments, the agreement would be declared void ab initio ([paras 1, 4]). The trial court ruled that the agreement did not require the payment of interest, allowing the respondents' claim to proceed ([para 5]).

### I – Issues
The central issues for determination by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) were:
1. Whether the sale agreement stipulated that interest was payable on the deferred purchase price, thus qualifying it as a credit transaction under section 8(4)(f) of the National Credit Act.
2. If the agreement was a credit transaction and the ACE Trust was not registered as a credit provider, whether the agreement could be deemed void ab initio ([paras 1, 3]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework applies mainly due to the National Credit Act 34 of 2005. Specifically, section 8(4)(f) defines a credit transaction in the following manner: any agreement that involves the deferral of payments with a charge, fee, or interest being payable constitutes a credit transaction ([para 4]). In interpreting such contractual obligations, the court must consider both the text of the agreement and its context, adhering to regular principles of contract interpretation ([para 9]). The court has also consistently held that valid contracts should not be rendered invalid unless absolutely necessary ([para 10]). The implications of section 89(5) of the Act dictate that any unlawful agreement must be declared void ex tunc, meaning it is null from the outset ([paras 22-23]).

### A – Application
In its analysis, the SCA carefully scrutinized the terms of the sale agreement, particularly paragraph 2, which outlines the payment structure and interest provision ([para 6]). The court emphasized that paragraph 2.2 clearly states that "interest will be charged at prime minus 1%" without any restrictive conditions ([para 11]). The court opined that this provision must be interpreted as entailing interest on the total deferred balance from the outset and not merely upon default, as determined by the trial court's interpretation, which found it to be "meaningless" ([paras 8-12]).

The SCA further posited that the original claim made by the respondents in their particulars of claim, which included interest from the date of the agreement's execution, substantiated the notion that both parties understood the agreement to call for interest on deferred payments ([paras 17-19]). The misinterpretation by the court a quo, where it considered potential business consequences of declaring the contract void, was rejected by the SCA as it did not align with standard business practices ([paras 20-21]).

In concluding that the agreement constituted a credit transaction as defined in the National Credit Act, the court noted that the conclusion drawn warranted a declaration of the agreement as void ab initio under section 89(5) ([paras 22-23]) due to the ACE Trust’s non-registration as a credit provider ([para 22]).

### C – Conclusion
The SCA upheld the appeal, emphasizing that the trial court erred in its interpretation of the contract, specifically regarding the stipulation of interest on deferred payments. It ultimately declared the sales agreement null and void ab initio under the provisions of the National Credit Act, reaffirming that the parties must return any performances rendered under the invalidated contract ([paras 22-23]). This decision sets a crucial precedent regarding the enforceability of agreements that qualify as credit transactions, highlighting the mandatory requirement for credit provider registration which aligns with promoting consumer protection under South African law. The implications extend to ensuring compliance with statutory requirements, effectively safeguarding against unregistered lending practices in credit transactions.
