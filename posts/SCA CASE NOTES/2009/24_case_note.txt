Source Folder: 2009

# FIRAC Case Note: *Shoprite Checkers (Pty) Ltd v Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration and Others* [2008] ZASCA 24

### F – Facts
This case concerns the appeal of Shoprite Checkers (Pty) Ltd ("Shoprite") against a judgment of the Labour Appeal Court ("LAC") related to the reinstatement of Mr. Jan Maake, a long-time employee. Maake, employed since 1972, faced disciplinary action in late 2000 for consuming food without authorization, which violated Shoprite's workplace policies ([paras 1–3]). After disciplinary proceedings, he was dismissed on 2 December 2000([para 4]).

Maake's union, the Retail and Allied Workers Union (RAWU), referred the matter to the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA) under the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 ("LRA") for conciliation and later arbitration ([paras 8–9]). Initially, the CCMA ruled in Maake's favor, ordering reinstatement effective from the date of dismissal; however, Shoprite sought review in the Labour Court, claiming the arbitrator made a gross error ([para 10]). The Labour Court eventually referred the matter back to CCMA for another arbitration before a different commissioner, where Maake received a final warning instead of reinstatement ([paras 11–12]).

Following another round of arbitration that also faced delays, the LAC ruled that the second commissioner's findings were reasonable but that the failure to grant retrospective reinstatement was unjustified. The LAC ordered the reinstatement to operate retrospectively ([paras 18–22]). It is this order that Shoprite subsequently appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal ("SCA") ([paras 23–24]).

### I – Issues
The primary issue before the court was whether the LAC correctly substituted the award of the second CCMA commissioner by ordering retrospective reinstatement for Maake ([para 23]). This raised questions about the appropriate limits of judicial intervention in CCMA decisions under the LRA, particularly the standards of reasonableness applying to reinstatement orders ([paras 26–27]).

### R – Rules
The relevant legislative framework is encapsulated in the Labour Relations Act, particularly section 145, which outlines the grounds for reviewing CCMA arbitration awards. The foundational principle from *Sidumo v Rustenburg Platinum Mines Ltd* 2008 (2) SA 24 (CC) establishes that a commissioner’s decision should be evaluated against a standard of reasonableness, focusing on whether a reasonable decision-maker could reach the same conclusion ([para 27]).

The principles governing dismissal reinforce employees' rights to fair labour practices (s 23(1) of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996) and emphasize the importance of securing employment, which the LRA aims to facilitate ([paras 24–25]). Moreover, where a commissioner finds a dismissal to be unfair, according to section 193(1)(a), they may order reinstatement but have the discretion to determine whether such reinstatement should be retrospective or not ([para 32]).

### A – Application
The SCA highlighted that the LAC exceeded its jurisdictional authority by substituting its own decision for that of the commissioner. In doing so, it effectively bypassed the standard of review outlined in section 145, venturing into the realm of factual determinations (e.g., appropriateness of the sanction) that were within the commissioner's discretion ([paras 29–30]).

The court discussed that while the issue of retrospective reinstatement could be addressed by the commissioner, the decision to grant such reinstatement was left to their discretion. The LAC's deviation from the standard established in *Sidumo* amounted to substituting judicial discretion for that of the arbitrator, which was not permissible ([paras 31–33]). The SCA acknowledged systemic failures that had caused significant delays in the resolution of this dispute, yet asserted that the reasoning of Commissioner Mbha remained sound and consistent with the law ([paras 34]).

### C – Conclusion
The SCA concluded that the LAC acted beyond its powers by substituting its discretion for that of the arbitration commissioner, resulting in an incorrect decision regarding the retrospective reinstatement of Maake. Consequently, the appeal by Shoprite was upheld, and the order of the LAC was set aside ([paras 35]). There was no order as to costs, reflecting the court's recognition of the systemic issues impacting this case ([paras 35]).

This ruling reinforces the importance of adhering to established standards of review in labour disputes, ensuring that the discretionary powers of commissioners are respected in line with the principles outlined in the LRA and reinforced by prior case law.
