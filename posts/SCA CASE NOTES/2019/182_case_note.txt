Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: Oosthuizen & Another v The State (180/2018) [2019] ZASCA 182

## I. Facts
In an incident that garnered significant media attention, Willem Jakobus Albertus Oosthuizen (the first appellant) and Theo Martinus Jackson (the second appellant) were implicated in violent acts against two individuals, Delton Sithole and Victor Rethabile Mlotshwa, on 17 August 2016 ([1]–[3]). The appellants, initially charged with multiple offences, including unlawful possession of a firearm, assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm, kidnapping, attempted murder, intimidation, and defeating the ends of justice, were later convicted of several serious charges while being acquitted of unlawful possession of a firearm ([2]).

Pre-trial videos documented the brutal treatment of Mlotshwa, which included being forced into a coffin, leading to a nationwide uproar and calls for justice ([1], [10], [11]). During the trial at the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, the appellants pleaded not guilty, while making significant admissions about their conduct. They were ultimately sentenced to lengthy prison terms, with Oosthuizen receiving 16 years (11 years effective) and Jackson 19 years (14 years effective) for their roles in the offenses ([3], [4]).

Following their convictions, the appellants applied for leave to appeal, which was initially denied by the trial court but later granted by the Supreme Court of Appeal ([4]).

## II. Issues
The core issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:

1. **Credibility of Evidence**: Whether the trial court applied the necessary caution when evaluating the testimony of the complainants, both single witnesses, in light of discrepancies and contradictions ([14], [15]).
   
2. **Sufficiency of Evidence**: Did the evidence presented at the trial meet the standard required for a conviction beyond a reasonable doubt, particularly in regards to the elements of assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm and kidnapping? ([13], [24]).

3. **Appropriateness of the Sentences**: Whether the sentences imposed were appropriate given the circumstances of the case and the personal circumstances of the appellants ([25], [28]).

## III. Rules
The decision relied upon various legal principles, notably:

1. **Standard of Proof in Criminal Cases**: The State bears the onus to prove the guilt of the accused beyond a reasonable doubt ([13]). The court must find the accused's version improbable before rejecting it ([14]).
   
2. **Cautionary Rule for Single Witnesses**: The court must consider the cautionary principle when assessing the evidence from single witnesses, with reference to cases such as *R v Mokoena* and *S v Sauls* ([14]).

3. **Elements of Assault and Intent**: Under South African law, for assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm, it is requisite that the act results in impairment of another’s bodily integrity or represents a credible threat of such impairment ([22]).

4. **Sentencing Principles**: The courts traditionally consider factors such as deterrence, retribution, rehabilitation, and the need to uphold societal values in determining appropriate sentences ([31], [32]).

## IV. Application
The Supreme Court evaluated whether the trial court erred in its treatment of the evidence, especially the testimonies of Sithole and Mlotshwa. The court found that the trial court had indeed failed to apply the cautionary rule adequately, as it heavily relied on the testimonies despite significant contradictions and improbabilities ([16], [19]). The appellants had admitted to significant elements of the offenses, including the unlawful detention of Mlotshwa and the physical threats against him ([12]).

The court noted that while some charges must be set aside, the appellants were still culpable of kidnapping and assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm based on the severity of the threats implied in their actions ([20], [24]). Notably, the psychological impact and the nature of the threats, including references to the potential use of petrol and a snake, evidenced the intention to instill fear and potential harm ([21]).

Regarding sentencing, the court indicated that the trial court did not sufficiently address the aggravated nature of the offenses, given the appellants’ apparent lack of remorse and the public outcry the actions had generated ([28], [30]). It called attention to the personal backgrounds of the appellants but indicated that these factors did not outweigh the seriousness of their deeds or the implications for the broader society ([26], [27], [30]).

## V. Conclusion
In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal held that the trial court had erred in its assessment and application of key legal principles governing the credibility of witness testimonies and the elements of the charged offenses. The convictions for assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm and kidnapping were upheld, while those for attempted murder, intimidation, and unlawful possession of a firearm were set aside ([35]).

The Court also concluded that the sentences needed to reflect not only the personal circumstances of the offenders but also the community's expectations of justice and deterrent effect. Consequently, the sentences were revised to five years’ imprisonment for assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm and one year for kidnapping, with all sentences running concurrently, resulting in a total effective sentence of five years ([35]). 

This case highlights the judiciary's critical role in responding to violent acts that have broader societal implications and the necessity of upholding the rule of law in a manner that reflects the values of justice and humanity within a constitutional framework.
