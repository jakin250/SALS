Supreme Court of Appeal

Director of Public Prosecutions v Booysen (580/2017) [2018] ZASCA 07

The respondent, Winston Booysen, was convicted in the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, Grahamstown, on two counts of murder and one count of robbery with aggravating circumstances.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 07

F – Facts

The respondent, Winston Booysen, was convicted in the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, Grahamstown, on two counts of murder and one count of robbery with aggravating circumstances. The crimes occurred in a context marked by personal relationship complexities and violence. On the first count, Booysen was convicted for the murder of his girlfriend, Ms Brenda Finnis, who wished to terminate their relationship. On the day of the incident, after an evening of drinking with friends, the respondent forcibly attempted to take Brenda away. In full view of the police, he stabbed her in the chest, leading to her immediate death from significant blood loss due to a stab wound that penetrated vital organs ([paras 1–4]).

Five days later, Booysen murdered Mr George Featherstone at his farm, which was located approximately 60 km from where the first murder took place. He entered Featherstone’s home, armed, and shot the elderly man multiple times before stealing food and Featherstone’s delivery vehicle. A substantial amount of the evidence against him was based on his own confessions made during a trial-within-a-trial, including the actions he took in both murders ([paras 5–6]). The trial court, not finding substantial or compelling circumstances to deviate from the mandatory minimum sentences prescribed by the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, imposed minimum terms: 15 years for the first count, but wrongly sentenced him to concurrent terms of 15 years for the second count (murder) and 8 years for the robbery ([paras 8–10]).

I – Issues

The central issue on appeal was whether the trial court erred in its sentencing approach by failing to impose the prescribed minimum sentences for counts 2 and 3, given its finding that no substantial or compelling circumstances existed to warrant a lesser sentence. The appeal also examined if the sentence imposed by the trial judge was "shockingly inappropriate," thereby justifying appellate interference ([paras 9–11]).

R – Rules

The legal framework for sentencing in this case is primarily governed by the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, which specifies minimum sentences for various serious crimes, including murder and robbery with aggravating circumstances. In particular, s 51(1) mandates life imprisonment for planned murders or those resulting in death during a robbery where aggravating circumstances exist. S 51(2)(a)(i) prescribes a minimum sentence of 15 years for first-time offenders convicted of robbery involving aggravating circumstances ([paras 8–9]).

Furthermore, appellate courts can modify sentences when the trial judge has misdirected themselves or when the imposition of the sentence is deemed to be unjust under the circumstances. The landmark ruling in S v Malgas [2001] (1) SACR 469 (SCA), reiterated that sentences prescribed by the legislature are generally to be imposed unless a compelling justification for deviation is presented ([paras 11–12]).

A – Application

In this case, the SCA noted that the trial court had failed to apply the law correctly regarding the sentencing for count 2 (murder of Featherstone) and count 3 (robbery with aggravating circumstances). Though the trial court articulated reasons for its decision based on the absence of substantial and compelling circumstances, it nonetheless imposed sentences inconsistent with the mandatory minima dictated by law.

The SCA agreed that the trial court erred in failing to impose life imprisonment for the second count of murder, as the circumstances met the requirements for such a sentence. For the robbery charge, the requirement of the law was also clear; the circumstances—namely, the use of a firearm and the serious injury inflicted on Featherstone—necessitated a 15-year sentence. The appellate court concluded that the lower court's failure to impose the correct minimum sentences represented a significant misdirection, warranting modification of the sentences imposed ([paras 12–13]).

C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the SCA found the appeal from the DPP to be justified. The sentences imposed by the trial court for counts 2 and 3 were set aside, and the appellate court substituted them with the mandatory minimum sentences. The respondent was duly sentenced to life imprisonment for the second murder and 15 years for the robbery. This case underscores the judiciary's duty to adhere strictly to the minimum sentencing regime for serious crimes and highlights the repercussions of failing to recognize aggravating circumstances properly. The ratio decidendi reaffirms the necessity of imposing prescribed minimum sentences unless a strong case for deviation is clearly established, integral for the uniform application of justice in South Africa's criminal law ([paras 14]).

References

  2. Director of Public Prosecutions, KwaZulu-Natal v P [2006] (1) SACR 243 (SCA).
  3. S v Dodo [2001] (3) SA 382 (CC).
