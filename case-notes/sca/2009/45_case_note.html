S A Post Office v De Lacy (19/08) [2009] ZASCA 45

The application of earlier case law emphasized that mere irregularities or a preference for one bid over another, without dishonesty in the process, do not constitute grounds for recovery of damages ([paras 14, 118]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009 [2009] ZASCA 45

F – Facts

The case concerns a dispute following the awarding of a tender for a biometric payment system by the South African Post Office (SAPO) to a consortium named Kumo. The respondents, Brian Patrick De Lacy and Barry Jack Beadon, were associated with Cornastone, an unsuccessful tenderer. They alleged that the tender process was marred by dishonesty and corruption, particularly citing the actions of SAPO employee Andrew Topper, who was accused of soliciting bribes and favouring Kumo’s bid. The trial court initially ruled in favour of the respondents on their primary claim (Claim A), awarding them R60 million, while dismissing their secondary claims (Claims B and C) related to the anticipated profits from future contracts and unjust enrichment. SAPO appealed the decision.

I – Issues

  1. Whether SAPO's employees, particularly Topper, engaged in dishonest conduct during the tender evaluation process ([paras 1-6]).
  2. The legal relationship and obligations arising from tender processes, particularly regarding the claims for quasi-delictual damages by unsuccessful tenderers ([paras 3-4, 14]).
  3. Whether the verdict of the trial court was consistent with the principles established in previous cases concerning tender processes ([paras 2-6]).

R – Rules

  1. Legal Principles of Tender Evaluation: Previous cases like Olitzki Property Holdings v State Tender Board and Steenkamp NO v Provincial Tender Board outline that unsuccessful tenderers cannot claim damages unless there is proof of fraud or dishonesty on the part of public officials ([paras 2-3, 14]).
  2. Vicarious Liability for Fraud or Dishonesty: Minister of Finance v Gore NO established that the state (or its entities) may be held liable for the dishonest conduct of its employees if such conduct directly impacts the tender award process ([para 5]).
  3. Standards of Proof: The onus rests on the claimant to prove, on a balance of probabilities, that the contract award was influenced by dishonest conduct ([para 14]).

A – Application

The court meticulously analyzed the evidence presented against the established legal principles, particularly focusing on whether the respondents could substantiate their claims of dishonesty. It concluded:

  • There was insufficient evidence to support the assertions of misconduct on the part of Topper or other SAPO officials. The allegations, while compelling, were not supported with demonstrable proof of fraud or a conspiracy ([paras 15-30, 31-39]).
  • The trial court had erred in its reliance on unfounded inferences to conclude that dishonesty had influenced the outcome of the tender ([paras 36-39]).
  • The detailed evaluation process adhered to SAPO’s stated criteria, with the Evaluation Committee providing reasonable explanations for its recommendations, which the Tender Board upheld as valid ([paras 40-60, 108-111]).

The application of earlier case law emphasized that mere irregularities or a preference for one bid over another, without dishonesty in the process, do not constitute grounds for recovery of damages ([paras 14, 118]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) found in favour of SAPO, overturning the trial court's decision. The court dismissed the respondents’ claims and upheld the principle that claims for lost profits based on allegations of misconduct have to be supported by concrete evidence of dishonesty ([paras 122]). The SCA's ruling clarified the legal standards regarding the obligation to demonstrate deceit in public tender processes and reinforced the notion that mere speculation regarding dishonesty cannot suffice to establish a claim for damages. This case underscores the challenges faced by unsuccessful tenderers in seeking redress in the absence of clear evidence of dishonesty or corruption among public officials.

The implications of the ruling extend beyond the immediate parties, establishing clearer boundaries and evidentiary requirements for future cases involving public tenders and claims of wrongful conduct ([paras 4, 118-122]).
