Walus v Minister of Justice and Correctional Services and Others

Constitutional Court 2022 [2022] ZACC 39 Court: Constitutional Court of South Afr

Neutral Citation: [2022] ZACC 39
Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa
Coram: Zondo CJ, Madlanga J, Majiedt J, Mhlantla J, Mlambo AJ, Theron J, Tshiqi J, Unterhalter AJ

F – Facts

The applicant, Janusz Jakub Walus, sought leave to appeal against the decision of the Gauteng Division of the High Court that dismissed his application for parole following a life imprisonment for the 1993 assassination of Thembisile "Chris" Hani, a prominent anti-apartheid leader ([1]–[2]). Walus conspired with Clive Derby-Lewis to execute this politically motivated assassination, which nearly led to civil war in South Africa ([4]–[6]).

Since 2011, Walus had repeatedly applied for parole, with all applications being denied by the Ministers of Correctional Services for various reasons, including the nature of the crime and sentencing remarks ([7]–[10]). In 2015, a High Court ruling deemed a ministerial refusal irrational, eventually leading to a cycle of reconsiderations that continually upheld the rejection despite recommendations for parole from various boards ([10]–[21]).

The Minister's refusal in March 2020 cited the serious nature of the crime and the broader implications of his actions. Despite the applicant’s clean disciplinary record and evidence pointing to low recidivism risk, the Minister ultimately judged the negative connotations of the crime and the original sentencing remarks as outweighing the positive aspects ([17]–[20], [38]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions posed in this case include:

  1. Whether the Minister’s refusal to grant Walus parole constituted a rational exercise of discretion as required by law under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act ([2], [60]).
  2. Whether the weighing of positive vs. negative factors in accordance with legislative parameters was appropriately conducted ([60]).

R – Rules

The court’s review relied on several legal principles and statutory provisions, including:

  1. The Correctional Services Act 111 of 1998, particularly section 36, emphasizing rehabilitation and correction of prisoners ([3]).
  2. The Correctional Services Act 8 of 1959, as it governed Walus’s parole eligibility, allowing consideration for parole after serving a minimum period given certain conditions ([40]–[43]).
  3. The principles established in Pharmaceutical Manufacturers Association v President of the Republic of South Africa regarding administrative decisional rationality. According to this ruling, public power must be exercised in a manner that is rationally connected to the legislative purpose ([62]).

A – Application

In analyzing the law's application, the court determined that the Minister's assessment was largely flawed due to two significant factors:

  1. The Minister conflated sentencing remarks and the nature of the crime with the evaluation criteria for considering parole. The court noted that these remarks are not meant to overshadow statutory obligations to assess other rehabilitative indicators, such as good behavior and evidence of rehabilitation ([37], [60], [78]).
  2. The court concluded that given Walus's long imprisonment, compliance with all rehabilitation requirements, and other favorable factors, a refusal based solely on immutable aspects of the past constituted a failure to exercise discretion judiciously. The court found that references to the nature of the crime and its serious implications failed to justify ongoing parole denial in light of Walus's circumstances after nearly 30 years of imprisonment ([81], [82], [95]).

The applicability of principles of equality under the law, enshrined in section 9 of the Constitution, further underscored this decision, stressing that all persons, regardless of their past, are entitled to fair consideration of their rights ([90]).

C – Conclusion

The court concluded that the Minister's decision was not rational within the legal framework established by the CSA and PAJA. It pronounced that Walus should be placed on parole, ordering his immediate release, while also emphasizing the need for the Minister to follow through with the operational steps to facilitate his parole within ten days ([96], [98]).

Thus, the judgment underlined a critical engagement with fundamental principles of justice and rehabilitative purpose, reinforcing that while the nature of a crime holds weight in parole decisions, the evolving dynamics of rehabilitation and personal accountability must equally substantiate administrative powers ([96]).

References

South Africa: Correctional Services Act 111 of 1998, Correctional Services Act 8 of 1959; Pharmaceutical Manufacturers Association v President of the Republic of South Africa [2000] ZACC 1; 2000 (2) SA 674 (CC).
