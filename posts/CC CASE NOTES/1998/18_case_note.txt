# Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA: Jooste v Score Supermarket Trading (Pty) Ltd

### FIRAC Method

## F – Facts [Paras 1-5]
On 27 December 1995, Susara Elizabeth Magdalena Jooste (the applicant) sustained injuries while working as a cashier at Score Supermarket Trading (Pty) Ltd (the respondent) due to an alleged negligence of the respondent's employees. Following this incident, on 29 April 1997, the applicant initiated legal proceedings in the Eastern Cape High Court, seeking general damages for her injuries under the grounds of negligence.

The respondent raised a special plea asserting that the applicant's claim was barred by section 35(1) of the Compensation for Occupational Injuries and Diseases Act 130 of 1993 (the “Compensation Act”), which prohibits employees from claiming damages for occupational injuries against their employers. The applicant countered that this section was unconstitutional as it violated her rights under the interim Constitution and later the 1996 Constitution, specifically regarding equality before the law, fair labour practices, and access to the courts. This led to a referral to the Constitutional Court for confirmation of the High Court's ruling that section 35(1) was unconstitutional.

## I – Issues [Paras 6-10]
The key issues confronting the Court included:
1. Whether section 35(1) of the Compensation Act, which denies employees the right to claim damages from their employers for occupational injuries, contradicts the provisions of the interim Constitution and the 1996 Constitution concerning equality, discrimination, and access to justice.
2. The appropriateness of determining the constitutionality of an Act of Parliament without allowing the relevant organ of state, specifically the Minister of Labour, to participate in the proceedings.

## R – Rules [Paras 4, 6, 8, 10]
The Court analyzed the relevant constitutional provisions, notably:
- **Section 8(1) and (2)** of the interim Constitution, which provide for equality and the right not to be unfairly discriminated against.
- **Section 9(1) and (3)** of the 1996 Constitution, which emulates the equality rights in the interim Constitution.
- **Section 172(2)** of the 1996 Constitution, which allows the High Court and Constitutional Court to declare legislation unconstitutional.
- The court rules regarding joinder, requiring relevant government entities to be notified in matters affecting the constitutionality of laws.

Notably, previous case law established that legislative differentiation must hold a rational connection to a legitimate government purpose to avoid a finding of unfair discrimination [see Prinsloo v Van der Linde, [1997] 3 SA 1012 (CC)].

## A – Application [Paras 10-18]
Upon consideration of the applicability of section 35(1), the Court found that the differentiation created by this provision did indeed inhibit employees’ common law rights but did so within a structured compensation system designed to expedite and simplify claims for occupational injuries. 

The Court underscored that the purpose of the Compensation Act was to provide a quicker resolution for employees suffering workplace injuries without the need to prove negligence, thereby facilitating compensation directly related to economic loss. Jooste's argument that employees were at a disadvantage compared to non-employees was considered; however, it was determined that the aims of facilitating benefits to employees through the Compensation system outweighed individual claims for damages. 

The Court further opined that the equality challenge failed the rationality test since section 35(1) logically aligned with the government’s aim of ensuring effective compensation absent the complexities and resource burdens that traditional litigation entails. Overall, the applicant’s claim of unfair discrimination was found to lack evidential basis and thus was dismissed.

## C – Conclusion [Paras 23-25]
The Constitutional Court ultimately ruled that the order of the High Court declaring section 35(1) unconstitutional was not to be confirmed. Consequently, the order of the respondent’s special plea was upheld, dismissing Jooste's claim for damages without any order for costs.

The Court’s decision carried broader implications, reinforcing the legitimacy of structured compensation systems over erstwhile common law rights and highlighting the precedence of legislative intent in labor law. The Court clarified that policy decisions about worker compensation and rights were to remain within the legislative domain, demonstrating a reluctance to judicially intervene in favor of legislated frameworks.

This case illustrates the balance between compensation processes for employees and the legal instruments that govern these relationships, emphasizing the legitimacy of limiting common law claims under constitutional scrutiny as long as the limitations are justified within a rational legislative framework.
