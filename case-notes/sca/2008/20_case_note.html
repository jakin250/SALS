Supreme Court of Appeal

Macru Farming CC v The Standard Bank of South Africa Ltd (64/2007) [2008] ZASCA 20

This matter concerns an appeal brought by Macru Farming CC (hereafter "the appellant") against The Standard Bank of South Africa Ltd (hereafter "the respondent") regarding the confirmation of a winding-up order issued by the Mmabatho High Court ([paras 1–2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2008 [2008] ZASCA 20

F – Facts

This matter concerns an appeal brought by Macru Farming CC (hereafter "the appellant") against The Standard Bank of South Africa Ltd (hereafter "the respondent") regarding the confirmation of a winding-up order issued by the Mmabatho High Court ([paras 1–2]). The respondent filed an urgent application on 24 October 2005, asserting the appellant's inability to pay its debts under section 68(c) of the Close Corporations Act 69 of 1984 (the CC Act) and subsequently obtained a provisional winding-up order from Zwiegelaar AJ on 31 October 2005 ([para 2]).

During proceedings before Landman J on 15 December 2005, the appellant conceded its commercial insolvency but sought to postpone the confirmation of the winding-up order to allow it time to liquidate its assets. The opposition from major creditors, including the respondent and Agri Feed Operations Limited, led to the court's refusal of this request ([para 3]). The final order was granted on 23 January 2006, primarily due to the complexities involved in asset realization and the absence of prospects for saving the close corporation ([para 4]).

Leave to appeal was granted on the basis that the appellant alleged the court had failed to adequately consider circumstances suggesting improper inducement on the part of the respondent in securing the winding-up order ([paras 5–6]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions in this case revolve around:

  1. Whether the High Court improperly exercised its discretion when confirming the winding-up order by not investigating the surrounding circumstances leading to the application.
  2. Whether the respondent improperly induced the appellant into liquidation, and if so, whether this should have impacted the confirmation of the winding-up order ([paras 5–6]).

R – Rules

The appeal was adjudicated based on several statutory provisions and precedents:

  • Section 347(1) of the Companies Act 61 of 1973, which allows the court discretion to grant or dismiss winding-up applications ([para 4]).
  • Sections 68(c) and 69(1)(c) of the Close Corporations Act 69 of 1984, defining the basis for winding-up proceedings due to inability to pay debts ([para 2]).
  • Code of Banking Practice, outlining the duties of banks to assist clients in financial difficulties ([para 9]).
  • Precedent cases that emphasize the need for courts to consider relevant circumstances and motivations surrounding applications for winding-up ([para 12]).

A – Application

The court, led by Cachalia JA, critically evaluated the appellant's claims of improper inducement. Two key arguments were considered:

  1. Disclosure of Information: The appellant claimed improper conduct by its former attorney, who allegedly shared privileged information with a liquidator, resulting in the respondent calling up the overdraft facility. The court noted that this did not have any substantiated link to the respondent because there was no evidence the bank had procured the information ([para 8]).
  2. Failure to Follow Banking Code: The appellant also contended that the bank had violated its own Code of Banking Practice by failing to develop a financial assistance plan before initiating liquidation. However, the court found that the respondent had previously acknowledged the appellant's insolvency and held discussions regarding the financial situation, undermining the appellant's reliance on the Code as a defense ([para 10]).

The court underscored that the urgency cited by the respondent in bringing winding-up proceedings was justified by the risk of asset sales that would have adversely affected the bank's security and the interests of the broader creditor body ([paras 11–12]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, holding that the appellant failed to show that the High Court had improperly exercised its discretion. The rationale was supported by the absence of credible evidence of inducement, the justified urgency on the part of the respondent, and the acknowledged commercial insolvency of the appellant ([para 13]). This case affirms the ability of creditors to initiate winding-up proceedings under the CC Act when proper grounds are established, reinforcing the principles related to the responsibilities of financial institutions in their dealings with insolvent entities. The ruling further clarifies the parameters within which courts must assess alleged improprieties in such proceedings, ensuring alignment between statutory provisions and practical undertakings in insolvency cases.
