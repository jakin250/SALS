# FIRAC Case Note: Joubert Scholtz Inc v Elandsfontein Beverage Marketing (307/11 & 765/11)

**Court:** The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Neutral Citation:** Joubert Scholtz Inc v Elandsfontein Beverage Marketing (307/11 & 765/11) [2012] ZASCA 6 (9 March 2012)  
**Coram:** Brand, Heher, Mhlantla, Malan, and Majiedt JJA  
**Heard:** 21 February 2012  
**Delivered:** 9 March 2012  

---

### F - Facts

The case arose from a dispute surrounding the repayment of surplus funds held in a trust account managed by the appellant law firm, Joubert Scholtz Inc ([1]). The respondent, Elandsfontein Beverage Marketing (Pty) Ltd, had entered into an agreement to acquire a business from Goosen and associated corporations, under which it was responsible for settling liabilities up to R12 million ([6]). 

The respondent claimed that it authorized Joubert Scholtz to manage payments to certain creditors, specifically First National Bank (FNB) and Standard Bank, through a trust account ([8]). However, it alleged that instead of following these instructions, Joubert Scholtz misappropriated funds by paying them to other creditors, which resulted in unjust enrichment for those creditors at the respondent’s expense ([2]). 

The trial court dismissed the respondent's claims while finding in favor of Goosen in a counter-claim for the statement and debatement of his capital loan account with the respondent ([3]). The Full Court overturned this decision, awarding monetary judgments against the appellants ([4]).

### I - Issues

The central issues for appeal were primarily identified as follows: 
1. Whether Joubert Scholtz acted within the scope of its mandate in distributing the funds in the trust account and whether it owed a duty to account for surplus funds to the respondent ([11]).
2. The existence and extent of Goosen's authority to instruct Joubert Scholtz on behalf of the respondent in relation to the payments ([11]).
3. The legal basis for unjust enrichment claims against the defendants ([11]).

### R - Rules

The legal principles governing this case include the authority of an agent, particularly in attorney-client relationships, as well as the concepts of unjust enrichment. In relation to the former, the court refers to the fundamental rules concerning mandates in agency law, particularly the conditions under which an attorney can be held liable for failing to act per the terms of a mandate ([11],[96]). 

The concept of **condictio sine causa** was also applicable, suggesting that unjust enrichment arises when a benefit has been conferred without a lawful basis ([96]). The legal standards govern the examination of whether funds received have resulted in unjust enrichment and whether the plaintiff has suffered impoverishment ([98]).

### A - Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal conducted a thorough analysis of the evidence presented, particularly around the instructions conveyed to Joubert Scholtz by both the plaintiff and Goosen. The court emphasized the need to ascertain whether the instructions given were indeed limited to paying only the debts of FNB and Standard or included broader authority to pay other creditors ([11],[73]). 

Witness credibility and the weight of evidence were critical, particularly the reliability of testimony provided by Abdoola from the respondent against that of Joubert and Goosen. The court identified the lack of corroborating evidence on the respondent’s side, making the case for an explicit authority to limit payments less compelling ([13], [92]). 

Ultimately, it was found that no clear limitation had been imposed on Goosen's authority by the board of the respondent. Therefore, the actions of Joubert in disbursing the funds as directed by Goosen fell within the lawful bounds of their engagement, thereby not constituting a breach of their mandate ([93]).

For the unjust enrichment claims, the court reiterated that to succeed, the plaintiff must demonstrate both enrichment of the defendant and corresponding impoverishment of the plaintiff. In this respect, the court found no evidence of impoverishment on the part of the respondent since Goosen's loan account had been credited with payments made out of the trust ([98],[99]).

### C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appellants were not liable to account for the surplus funds, as Joubert had acted within the limits of its mandate based on the authority granted, even if that authority was implicitly held by Goosen. The court reaffirmed the dismissal of the respondent’s claims on the basis that they failed to establish either a breach of duty by Joubert Scholtz or unjust enrichment ([99]). 

The order of the Full Court was set aside, and the original trial court's decision was restored, concluding the appeals of the appellants with costs ([99]). This ruling highlights critical aspects of agency authority and the strict requirements necessary to substantiate claims of unjust enrichment in South African law.
