Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# Case Note: City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality v Blue Moonlight Properties 39 (Pty) Ltd & Others

**Neutral Citation:** City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality v Blue Moonlight Properties 39 (Pty) Ltd & Others (338/10) [2011] ZASCA 47 (30 March 2011).

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Judges:** Navsa, Tshiqi, Theron JJA, Plasket and Petse AJJA

**Heard:** 18 February 2011

**Delivered:** 30 March 2011

---

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The appeal involved the City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality (the City) contesting an order made by the South Gauteng High Court that mandated the eviction of the occupiers residing at the property on Saratoga Avenue, Johannesburg, and imposed a constitutional obligation on the City to provide temporary emergency accommodation for the evicted occupants ([paras 1-3]). The occupiers, many of whom had resided unlawfully in the building since the late 1970s, were in dire financial straits, with an average household income of R790 per month ([para 16]). The property was a converted commercial building with unsafe living conditions and was operated initially by a company known as Kernel Carpets before being acquired by Blue Moonlight Properties ([paras 9-11]).

The high court's ruling had determined that the City's housing policy, which provided emergency accommodation only to individuals evicted from properties deemed unsafe by the City itself, was unconstitutional ([paras 4-6]). Following continuous neglect of the premises, the City had been implicated in upholding the occupiers' rights but contended that it was unable to assist due to limited resources and statutory constraints ([para 20]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues before the court centered on whether the City had an affirmative constitutional duty under section 26 of the Constitution to provide emergency shelter to evictees from privately-owned property, despite its housing policy suggesting otherwise. Furthermore, the case raised questions about the constitutionality of the City's housing policy, which purportedly discriminated against individuals evicted by private landlords compared to those evicted by the City itself ([paras 2, 21, 56]).

### R – Rules
The relevant constitutional provision was Section 26 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, which grants individuals the right of access to adequate housing and mandates the state to take reasonable legislative and other measures to achieve the progressive realisation of this right ([para 26]). The case also referenced the Constitutional Court’s precedent in *Government of the Republic of South Africa & Others v Grootboom & Others*, as well as statutory frameworks, including the Housing Act 107 of 1997 and the National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act 103 of 1977, which underpinned municipal obligations regarding housing ([paras 28-40]).

### A – Application
The court engaged in a substantial examination of the obligations imposed on the City under existing socio-economic rights jurisprudence. It found that the City had not exercised its powers in a manner that aligned with its constitutional obligations to assist vulnerable communities. The court emphasized that the local government has direct responsibilities to ensure access to housing and cannot simply defer its duties to national or provincial structures ([paras 42-49]).

The court also declared that the discriminatory nature of the City’s housing policy undermined the principles of equality and dignity as enshrined in the Constitution ([paras 56-60, 66]). This led to the conclusion that the City's failure to provide housing assistance to occupiers evicted by private landlords constituted a failure to comply with its constitutional obligations, thereby justifying the order for emergency accommodation to be provided to the affected parties ([para 54]).

### C – Conclusion
In its verdict, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the City’s appeal and upheld the high court’s ruling that mandated the provision of temporary emergency accommodation. The court also ordered the eviction, while specifying a time frame that ensured the City would secure housing for the occupants prior to their forced removal, highlighting the delicate balance between private property rights and constitutional obligations ([paras 72-75]).

This judgment underscores the judiciary's role in holding local governments accountable for their constitutional duties, particularly in contexts involving socio-economic rights and evictions, thus reinforcing the importance of maintaining human dignity and ensuring that vulnerable individuals are not left homeless as a consequence of state or private actions. The ruling is significant in shaping municipal policies that dictate housing development and emergency accommodation provision ([para 73]).
