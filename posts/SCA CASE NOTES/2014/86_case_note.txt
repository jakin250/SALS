# Case Note: Butters v Mncora (419/13) [2014] ZASCA 86

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The appellant, Andrew Kinloch Butters, and the respondent, Nomsa Virginia Mncora, had been in a long-term relationship from 1988, during which they had two children and cohabited as partners. The circumstances of their relationship changed dramatically when Butters married another woman on November 15, 2007, without Mncora's knowledge, effectively ending their relationship ([paras 1–2]).

In 2010, the Eastern Cape High Court, under Chetty J, granted an order declaring that a universal partnership existed between Butters and Mncora, pertaining to all assets acquired from 1998 until the termination of their relationship. However, this order was appealed unsuccessfully by Butters, with subsequent attempts to appeal to the Constitutional Court also failing ([paras 1–4]).

Following this, Mncora applied to the High Court for a variation of the original order, citing a typographical error regarding the commencement year of the partnership, requesting it to be amended from 1998 to 1988. This amendment was critical to include assets acquired prior to 1998—specifically, those aligning with the commencement of their relationship. The High Court granted Mncora’s application, prompting an appeal from Butters ([paras 4–6]).

### I – Issues
The primary issues for determination included:

1. Whether the High Court had the authority to vary its previous judgment under Rule 42(1) of the Uniform Rules of Court.
2. Whether the alleged error in the original order was indeed a patent error attributable to the court, or if it stemmed from the respondent's legal representation ([paras 8–9]).

### R – Rules
The pertinent legal provisions considered in this case were:

1. **Uniform Rule 42(1)**, which enables a court to rescind or vary an order where there is an ambiguity, a patent error, or a mistake common to the parties:

   - (a) **Erroneously granted orders** in the absence of affected parties.
   - (b) **Orders with ambiguities or patent errors**.
   - (c) **Orders resulting from mistakes common to parties** ([para 8]).

2. The principles surrounding universal partnerships, where the law recognizes:

   - The requirement of a tacit agreement capable of arising from the conduct of the parties.
   - Contributions to a partnership need not strictly be financial or commercial ([para 11]).

The court also referenced notable precedents, including *Firestone South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Genticuro AG* 1977 (4) SA 298 (A) and *Mostert NO v Old Mutual Life Assurance Co (SA) Ltd* 2002 (1) SA 82 (SCA), to substantiate the authority to vary judgments and the nature of universal partnerships ([paras 14–15]).

### A – Application
In analyzing the case, the court needed to determine whether the High Court's amendment of the original judgment was justified. It was argued by the appellant that the 1998 date was crucial, and that it encapsulated an agreement between the parties with significant implications concerning asset distribution ([para 9]).

However, the court noted:

1. The High Court correctly identified the 1998 date as a **patent typographical error**, as established facts confirmed that the relationship and potential partnership commenced in 1988.
2. The amendment served to preserve the substance of the judgment without altering its meaning, a principle upheld in previous rulings that a court can correct typographical errors without losing its original intent ([paras 7–10]).

The court examined whether Butters' opposition was predicated upon a legitimate concern regarding the partnership's existence and relevance of the commencement date. However, it was concluded that the real matter at hand was a refusal on Butters' part to acknowledge a partnership, rather than the factual basis of its commencement ([paras 12–13]).

### C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the High Court acted within its authority under Rule 42(1) to correct the judgment, correctly identifying the 1998 date as an erroneous entry. The amendment to reflect the year 1988 as the commencement date of the universal partnership reinforced the original intent of the order, aligning it with the established historical context of the relationship ([paras 17–18]).

The final order was varied to amend the commencement date while dismissing the balance of Butters' appeal. The court also ordered Butters to pay the respondent's costs, including that of two counsel where employed, affirming the importance of equitable distribution in partnerships ([paras 1–3]).

This judgment has significant implications for the recognition of cohabitating partners' contributions in a universal partnership context, particularly in cases where non-financial contributions are crucial. It underscores the judiciary's role in rectifying errors to achieve justice and equitable outcomes in partnership disputes.
