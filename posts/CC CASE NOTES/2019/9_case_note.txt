Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: Buffalo City Metropolitan Municipality v Metgovis (Pty) Limited [2019] ZACC 9

## F - Facts ([paras 1–11])

The case revolves around an appeal from a decision of the High Court of South Africa, Eastern Cape Division, wherein Buffallo City Metropolitan Municipality (the Municipality) was obligated to pay Metgovis (Pty) Limited (Metgovis) for services rendered under a tacit agreement regarding a software licensing agreement. Initially, on February 4, 2008, the Municipality and Metgovis had entered into a software license agreement for the use of Metgovis' Metval property valuation management system, which was essential for the Municipality's property valuation functions ([para 2]). 

The agreement, which had been set to expire on June 30, 2011, provided for automatic renewal unless expressly terminated three months prior to the end of its term. The Municipality failed to terminate the agreement, leading Metgovis to assume that it had been renewed. Despite the Municipality's outstanding payments, Metgovis continued to offer services until June 2013, even after the agreement's alleged termination ([paras 3–4]). 

In a pivotal meeting on October 17, 2012, it was decided that the Municipality would issue a new tender for these services, but Metgovis continued providing support due to the lack of alternative arrangements ([para 6]). On July 4, 2013, Metgovis wrote to the Municipality for clarification on its service and billing procedures, but received no response. Following this, it requested a continued provision of services based on the existing agreement ([paras 7–8]). 

The situation culminated with the Municipality disputing the existence of an agreement for the subsequent months, while Metgovis argued there was a tacit month-to-month agreement ([para 12]). The High Court found in favor of Metgovis, a decision the Municipality subsequently appealed, claiming no lawful agreement was formed as per public procurement legislation ([paras 12–14]).

## I - Issues ([paras 17–30])

The critical legal question was whether the High Court misapplied the law concerning tacit contracts, particularly with respect to the necessary authority for public functionaries to enter contractual obligations ([para 17]). The Municipality contended that the existence of a tacit agreement was unlawful and void due to non-compliance with public procurement requirements as set forth in section 217 of the Constitution ([para 30]). 

Additionally, the matter raised questions about the development of common law regarding tacit contracts and whether the High Court's ruling established a substantial precedent that may affect how municipalities contract for services in compliance with constitutional obligations ([para 37]).

## R - Rules ([paras 15–29])

The Court analyzed the principles governing tacit agreements, which require unequivocal conduct supporting the existence of a contract ([para 15]). The High Court utilized the "preponderance of probabilities" test established in *Ocean Commodities*, which mandates that a party alleging the existence of a tacit contract must demonstrate conduct capable of no other reasonable interpretation than that the parties intended to contract ([para 15]). 

Furthermore, the application of Section 217 of the Constitution dictates that any contractual engagement by state organs be conducted transparently, equitably, and in a manner that ensures value for public funds ([para 21]). The Court noted that a tacit contract that violates public procurement principles is void ab initio and highlighted that the Municipality failed to demonstrate how the common law needed alteration to align with constitutional requirements ([para 30]).

## A - Application ([paras 31–38])

In reviewing the case, the Constitutional Court concluded that the High Court had adequately established factual grounds for its finding of a tacit agreement, grounded on the assessment of conduct and circumstances surrounding the ongoing relationship and service provision ([paras 31–36]). The distinction made between legal interpretation and factual determination was crucial; the mere misapplication of legal principles did not elevate the matter to a constitutional issue ([para 31]).

While the Municipality argued for a more stringent testimonial burden in proving tacit agreements, the Court found no evidence within its submissions that suggested the current common law was inconsistent to an extent warranting high court review ([para 29]). Importantly, it highlighted that the practical need for service continuity did not justify an unlawful contract's existence. Consequently, since the case was rooted in factual disputes rather than constitutional violations, the Court held it lacked jurisdiction to entertain the appeal ([paras 36–37]). 

## C - Conclusion ([paras 38–41])

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court dismissed the application for leave to appeal based on the absence of jurisdiction, leading to the conclusion that the High Court's findings do not raise any constitutional issues deserving of this Court's review ([para 38]). The implications of this ruling suggest a reaffirmation of existing legal standards regarding tacit agreements, particularly with public functionaries, while simultaneously highlighting the necessity of adhering to public procurement legislation. The ruling underscores that disputes centered primarily on factual interpretations do not satisfy the threshold necessary for constitutional engagement. Costs were awarded in favor of the respondent, Metgovis ([para 41]).
