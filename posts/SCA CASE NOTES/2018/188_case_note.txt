Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note  
**Case Title:** Colchester Zoo SA Properties (Pty) Ltd v Mdakane & others (1108/17) [2018] ZASCA 188  

## F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–8])
The case involves an appeal regarding the status of Mr. Sifiso Maduna (the second respondent) as an "occupier" under the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA) following a counter-claim by Colchester Zoo SA Properties (Pty) Ltd (the appellant) for his eviction from a homestead on their farm Geluk in KwaZulu-Natal. The appellant became the registered owner of the farm on 7 April 2015. On 24 August 2015, Mdakane (the first respondent) and Maduna initiated proceedings in the Land Claims Court to be recognized as labour tenants under the Land Reform (Labour Tenants) Act 3 of 1996 (LTLRA), which the appellant disputed.

The Land Claims Court found that Maduna was an occupier under ESTA, notwithstanding that he had not sought this designation and disputed it. The court based its decision on Maduna's longstanding connection to the farm, noting that he had lived there intermittently since childhood due to his father's employment with one of the farm's former owners. The court ruled on various aspects of the counter-claim, eventually declaring that Maduna could only be evicted under ESTA’s provisions.

## I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–4, 8–14])
The principal legal issues raised in this appeal are:
1. Whether Mr. Sifiso Maduna qualifies as an "occupier" in terms of ESTA, thus having protections against eviction.
2. If not, whether the absence of this status entitles the appellant to an eviction order, and what legislative compliance would dictate regarding eviction procedures.

The appeal also engages with the broader context of land reform and the rights of individuals historically associated with land that has changed ownership.

## R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 6–12])
The relevant legal principles derive primarily from:
- **Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA)**: The definition of "occupier," which entails a person residing on land owned by another with consent or a legal right (s 1(1)).
- **Land Reform (Labour Tenants) Act 3 of 1996 (LTLRA)**: Specifies the conditions under which labour tenants can claim rights over land.
- **Recent Case Law**: The court refers to *Mathebula v Harry* as a precedent, particularly around the interpretation of "reside" and the implications of physical presence interrupted by economic activities.
- Historical considerations from cases involving the legal status of individuals on land for burial rights and the socio-economic realities of South Africa influencing the interpretation of residency.

The court also cites the case *Kiepersol Poultry Farm Pty Ltd v Phasiya*, emphasizing that established presence should be understood in the socio-legal context, not merely numerically quantified.

## A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 9–15])
Applying the rule of law to the factual matrix, the SCA critically examined the definition of "occupier" under ESTA, particularly focusing on the need for a sustained physical presence and the intention to return to the homestead on a permanent basis. The court recognized that while Maduna had historical ties to the land, the evidence of his actual physical presence was insufficient. His lengthy absences due to economic necessities—in particular, work opportunities outside the farm—did not align with the definition of being an occupier since his presence was not continuous nor did it signify a permanent home.

The court further highlighted that the conclusion drawn by the Land Claims Court relied heavily on a sympathetic interpretation of Maduna's connection to the land; however, it ultimately found that this sentiment could not substitute for the legal definitions applicable under ESTA.

Despite denying Maduna's occupant status, the court clarified that this does not automatically entitle the appellant to an eviction order under the assumption that an engagement with legislative compliance is necessary. It emphasized that the current circumstances of Maduna—whether he resided on the farm at that time—needed consideration before concluding any eviction order could be justly implemented. Thus, it decided to refer the eviction claim back to the Land Claims Court for further determination of the circumstances surrounding Maduna's status on the land.

## C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([para 16])
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal by partially setting aside the previous orders made by the Land Claims Court involving the declaration of Maduna’s occupancy status. The court concluded that Mr. Maduna was not an "occupier" under ESTA. However, it refrained from issuing an eviction order, deferring that decision to the Land Claims Court for a thorough examination of the current circumstances surrounding Maduna's presence on the farm.

The implications of this judgment underscore the judiciary's sensitivity to socio-economic factors in land reform cases while ensuring that legislative processes are observed. The ruling reinforces the principle that an individual's social ties to land must be complemented by a demonstrable legal status to mandate protection against eviction. This case is pivotal in shaping the landscape of land reform jurisprudence in South Africa, particularly concerning individuals historically linked to agricultural land amidst the dynamics of ownership changes.
