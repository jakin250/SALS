eThekwini Municipality and Another v Independent Schools Association of Southern Africa and Others (960/2019) [2021] ZASCA 155

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 155

F – Facts ([paras 1–4])

This case involves an appeal by eThekwini Municipality (First Appellant) and Stellenbosch Municipality (Second Appellant) against a judgment from the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court, which mandated them to comply with the Local Government: Municipal Property Rates Act (MPRA), 6 of 2004, as amended in 2010. The Independent Schools Association of Southern Africa (ISASA) and two National Ministers participated as respondents in the suit.

The genesis of this dispute dates back to March 2010 when the Minister for Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (Minister for CoGTA) promulgated regulations that limited municipal rates on properties belonging to public benefit organisations (PBO property) to a maximum of 25% of the rate levied on residential property. This regulation aimed to support PBOs, particularly educational institutions represented by ISASA.

Following the promulgation of these amended regulations, ISASA filed an application in the High Court, seeking an order to prevent eThekwini from imposing rates exceeding this cap. eThekwini, in response, launched a counter-application challenging the validity of these regulations and the constitutionality of section 19 of the MPRA. The High Court concluded in favour of ISASA, thereby allowing its application and thereby denying eThekwini’s counter-application. Subsequent to this judgment, eThekwini and Stellenbosch sought to appeal the High Court's order.

I – Issues ([paras 4, 21–27])

The core issues presented before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) were:

  1. Applicability of the 2010 Regulations: Whether the 2010 amended regulations applied to eThekwini and whether the municipality could evade the prescribed limitations by excluding PBO properties from its rates policy.
  2. Constitutionality of Section 19(1)(b): Whether this provision, which limits a municipality's power to levy rates on non-residential properties, breaches the constitutional principle of municipal autonomy as outlined in the South African Constitution.
  3. Validity of Regulations: The municipalities contested the validity of the 2010 amendments, arguing, specifically, that proper consultation as required by the MPRA had not occurred.

R – Rules ([paras 16–27])

The SCA emphasized several key legal frameworks:

  1. Local Government: Municipal Property Rates Act (MPRA), 2004: Sections 19 and 83 provide the legal basis for regulating municipal property rates and stipulate the Minister's power to promulgate regulations. Section 19 prohibits municipalities from levying different rates for residential and specified non-residential properties beyond prescribed limits.
  2. South African Constitution, Sections 40, 151, and 229: These provisions establish the autonomy of municipalities while also permitting regulation of municipal powers by national and provincial legislation. This regulatory oversight ensures that local governments operate within a national economic framework.
  3. Judicial Precedent (City of Tshwane v Marius Blom): The court referred to this case to support the autonomy granted to municipalities in managing their rates policies, whilst recognizing that such autonomy exists only subject to national legislation, highlighting the balance between local discretion and national oversight.

A – Application ([paras 16–35])

In interpreting the applicability of the 2010 regulations, the SCA determined that the municipalities could not claim discretion in defying national regulations. eThekwini’s argument—that its powers allowed it to omit PBO properties from its rates policy—was rejected outright. The court emphasized that exclusion from a rates policy does not negate the obligations imposed by the prescribed ratio in the regulations, consistent with sections 19 and 229 of the Constitution.

The SCA addressed the constitutional challenge presented by eThekwini, affirming that section 19(1)(b) does not violate the principle of municipal autonomy. Instead, it functions as a necessary framework for regulating municipal taxation powers, thereby preventing potential harm to the national economic policies outlined within the Constitution. The crucial point made was that municipal autonomy does not equate to absolute independence; local governments operate within a structured regulatory environment accepted by the Constitution.

Moreover, regarding the validity challenge, the court noted that eThekwini and Stellenbosch had previously participated in consultations concerning the regulation revisions back in 2007 and did not raise formal objections when the draft amendments were issued. The regulations could not be deemed invalid on the grounds of the alleged lack of proper consultation.

C – Conclusion ([paras 39–41])

The SCA ultimately dismissed the appeals from eThekwini and Stellenbosch, upholding the High Court's decision. The court ruled that the municipalities were mandated to comply with the amended rates regulations, reinforcing the intended protections for PBO properties under the MPRA as well as ensuring equitable treatment in municipal taxation. The appellants were held liable for the costs incurred by the respondents.

This judgement underscores the principle of cooperative governance and regulatory oversight within South African law, affirming that municipal policies must align with national legislation. The outcome not only sets a precedent for future enforcement of the MPRA but also clarifies the relationship between national regulations and municipal autonomy, promoting consistency in local government operations across South Africa.
