Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: Mafokate v The Law Society of the Northern Provinces [2013] ZASCA 125

**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Citation**: Mafokate v The Law Society of the Northern Provinces (786/12) [2013] ZASCA 125 (23 September 2013)  
**Coram**: Mthiyane AP, Lewis, Shongwe and Wallis JJA and Zondi AJA  
**Heard**: 20 August 2013  
**Delivered**: 23 September 2013  

## F – Facts

The case revolves around the appeal by Johannes Tlhonela Mafokate, a sole practitioner, against the decision of the North Gauteng High Court that ordered his striking off the roll of attorneys due to professional misconduct ([1]). Mafokate, who had been practising since 1989, was accused of failing to properly account for funds received from two clients related to claims against the Road Accident Fund (RAF) ([2]).

The allegations against him arose from complaints filed by clients Samuel Muchanga (recorded initially as Malesela Piet Mabitsela) and Busang. Muchanga claimed that after settling his claim, Mafokate did not account for the R392,240 received on his behalf, asserting that he had not been given a statement of account or payment ([4], [7]). Although Mafokate advanced explanations for his failure to provide timely accounts, the evidence presented, including inconsistencies in his records and the absence of corroborating documentation, greatly undermined his defense and clarity regarding payments made to Muchanga ([10]-[12]).

In another instance involving Busang, he similarly claimed that Mafokate failed to account for the R60,000 settled in his case against the RAF. Although Mafokate argued that he had lent Busang R40,000, he could not produce adequate evidence supporting this claim ([16], [18]).

## I – Issues

The central legal issues in this case include:

1. **Professional Conduct**: Was Mafokate guilty of unprofessional, dishonourable, and unworthy conduct sufficient to justify his removal from the roll of attorneys?  
2. **Fitness to Practice**: Is Mafokate a fit and proper person to continue practising law, considering the allegations against him and his past conduct?  
3. **Legal Standards for Sanction**: Should the court's approach regarding sanctions assume striking off as a default consequence of misconduct, or should it consider lesser sanctions based on the circumstances? ([20]-[22]).

## R – Rules

The court relied upon several legal principles and precedents in adjudicating this matter:

- **Attorneys Act 53 of 1979**: Under Section 22(1)(d), a court has the discretion to strike an attorney's name from the roll if the attorney is found unfit to practice ([2]). This determination involves a three-stage inquiry as outlined in *Law Society, Northern Provinces v Mogami* ([3]).
  
- **Standard of Proof**: The applicable standard in cases concerning professional conduct is based on a preponderance of probabilities ([3]). In this instance, the burden was on the applicant (the Law Society) to establish its allegations against Mafokate.

- **Judgment Standards**: Prior case law, such as *Malan & another v Law Society, Northern Provinces* and *Jasat v Natal Law Society*, suggests that striking off is not a matter of course but requires assessing the nature of the misconduct and its implications for the attorney's character and the protection of the public ([21]).

## A – Application

Upon careful scrutiny, the court determined that Mafokate's explanations for his failure to account were implausible and riddled with contradictions ([15], [20]). The court assessed each complaint against evidence provided and concluded that the explanations offered were insufficient to exonerate him. Despite his assertion about the alleged loans to Busang and payments to Muchanga, the absence of contemporary documentation and the inconsistencies in testimonies effectively negated his defense.

For instance, even assuming that payments were made, the amounts involved lacked adequate proof and resulted in residual fund discrepancies ([11]-[14]). The court emphasized the importance of transparency and the attorney's fiduciary duty towards clients, holding that Mafokate’s dealings were contrary to these principles ([22]).

Despite the severity of the measures associated with striking off, the court found that the evidence overwhelmingly supported a finding of unprofessional conduct, thus deeming him unfit to practice ([20]). The lack of exceptional circumstances further precluded the court from considering suspension as an alternative to removal ([23]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded by dismissing Mafokate’s appeal and confirming the High Court's order striking his name from the roll of attorneys with costs on an attorney-and-client scale ([24]). The court's determination underscores the stringent professional standards expected of attorneys and affirms the necessity of integrity and accountability in legal practice.

The decision serves as a clear reminder that failing to maintain professional standards not only undermines the legal profession but also places public protection at risk. The judgment reaffirms that while the consequences of a striking off are severe, they are justified in circumstances reflecting a repeated failure to uphold fiduciary responsibilities, thereby upholding the public’s trust in the legal profession.
