Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: Collett v Firstrand Bank Ltd

**Court**: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Neutral Citation**: Collett v Firstrand Bank (766/2010) [2011] ZASCA 78 (27 May 2011)

**Coram**: Mpati P, Brand, Maya, Malan, and Tshiqi JJA

---

### F – Facts

This case revolves around the appellant, Sally Ann Collett, who had defaulted on her mortgage bond repayment with the respondent, Firstrand Bank Ltd. Following her default, Collett applied for debt review in terms of **Section 86(1)** of the **National Credit Act 34 of 2005** (NCA) on 4 January 2010 ([para 2]). The debt review process was initiated by her debt counsellor, Gerhard Stoltz, who notified the bank of the application on the same day. 

On 15 February 2010, Stoltz informed Firstrand Bank that Collett's application was successful, and efforts to restructure her debt obligations were underway. However, the bank did not accept the proposed restructuring ([para 2]). On 29 March 2010, Stoltz referred Collett's case to the East London Magistrate’s Court under **Section 86(8)** of the NCA, seeking an order to declare Collett over-indebted and facilitate a rearrangement of her debt ([para 3]).

More than 60 days after her application, specifically on 7 April 2010, Firstrand Bank invoked **Section 86(10)** of the NCA to terminate the debt review process relevant to the mortgage bond ([para 3]). The Magistrate’s Court hearing, aimed at addressing the proposed debt arrangements, was postponed, and Firstrand subsequently served summons against Collett seeking a summary judgment for R677,254.92 ([para 1]). Collett's appeal initially focused on whether Firstrand was entitled to terminate the review under **Section 86(10)** given the pending hearing ([para 5]).

### I – Issues

The pivotal issue at hand was whether a credit provider, like Firstrand Bank, is entitled to terminate a debt review initiated under **Section 86** of the NCA when the debt counsellor has referred the matter to the Magistrate's Court for an ordered relief under **Section 87**, and while the hearing is still pending ([para 6]). This question brought into focus interpretations of the relevant sections within the broader framework of the NCA, particularly the implications of **Sections 86(10)** and **86(11)** ([paras 6-7]).

### R – Rules

The case primarily revolved around these statutory provisions of the NCA:

1. **Section 86(10)** allows credit providers to terminate the debt review after the consumer has defaulted, provided that 60 business days have passed since the debt review application was submitted.

2. **Section 86(11)** provides that if a credit provider, having terminated a debt review, proceeds to enforce the credit agreement, the Magistrate's Court may order the resumption of the debt review on terms it finds appropriate.

3. **Section 87** describes the functions of the Magistrate’s Court in rearranging a consumer's debt obligations ([para 4]).

Also relevant were various interpretations from previous cases, highlighting the conflicting judicial approach towards the rights of credit providers during and after a debt review process ([paras 6-7]).

### A – Application

The court examined the legislative intent of the NCA, focusing on its goals to protect over-indebted consumers and promote responsible lending ([paras 10-11]). It was outlined that while Section 86(10) permits termination by credit providers, this right must be exercised judiciously and cannot undermine the overarching structure that aims to facilitate debt restructuring through debt reviews.

The court emphasized the importance of Section 86(11), which limits the credit provider's ability to interrupt the debt review process initiated by the consumer and enforced by the debt counsellor, preventing unilateral termination that could derail the restructuring process ([para 15]).

The context of the legislation indicated that the termination rights of credit providers, as regulated by **Section 86(10)**, coexist with obligations to participate in negotiations in good faith during the debt restructuring process ([para 14]). The court rejected the argument that the referral to the Magistrate's Court automatically precluded the termination of the debt review, asserting that the right to terminate persists until a definitive order is made by the court under Section 87 ([para 11]).

Moreover, the court noted that the debt counsellor's role is not merely an isolative application process but is integral to achieving an ordered and effective resolution of debts, consistent with the framework laid out in the NCA ([para 11]).

### C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the ruling of the Eastern Cape High Court, determining that (1) Firstrand Bank was entitled to terminate the debt review process under **Section 86(10)** despite the ongoing court proceedings, and (2) the court did not recognize the appellant's claim that such termination precluded Firstrand from proceeding with their enforcement action in court ([paras 20]). The court's ruling clarifies the relationship between Section 86(10) and 86(11), emphasizing that while credit providers retain termination rights, they must also comply with obligations aimed at promoting responsible debt restructuring.

This decision signals a cautious approach to consumer protection versus creditors' rights within the framework of the NCA, highlighting the intricacies involved in navigating debt reviews, and firmly establishing the interplay between judicial enforcement and legislative intent ([para 19]). 

---

**Key Implications**: This case reinforces the balancing act legislated by the NCA and clarifies the obligations of both consumers and creditors in debt reviews, ensuring that credit providers cannot disrupt the intended process without just cause. The ruling may affect how credit providers engage in debt reviews moving forward, emphasizing compliance and the necessity for judicious participation in ongoing negotiations.
