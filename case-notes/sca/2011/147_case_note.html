Supreme Court of Appeal

Pick ‘n Pay Retailers (Pty) Ltd v Eayrs & Others NNO

This case concerns a dispute over a right of pre-emption in the context of a franchise agreement.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2011

F – Facts

This case concerns a dispute over a right of pre-emption in the context of a franchise agreement. The parties involved included Pick ‘n Pay Retailers (Pty) Ltd, the franchisor (First Appellant), and various trusts and companies acting as sellers and purchasers of shares in the franchisee company, specifically the Holdstock Family Trust and Carter Trading (Pty) Ltd ([1]).

On March 1, 2004, Pick ‘n Pay Retailers entered into a franchise agreement with the Holdstock Family Trust and others, who owned all issued shares in Carter Trading, a company operating a Pick ‘n Pay franchise in Port Elizabeth ([1]). On April 22, 2010, the Holdstock Family Trust sold 50 percent of the shares to Daku Trust (First Respondent), with the intent of transitioning from a Pick ‘n Pay franchise to a Superspar franchise ([2]).

Under the franchise agreement, Pick ‘n Pay was afforded a right of first refusal regarding sales of shares or business interests ([4]). The franchise agreement included a clause that mandated the seller to notify Pick ‘n Pay in writing if they intended to sell shares, offering them the first opportunity to purchase the shares at a specified price before any sale could occur ([4]).

On April 30, 2010, the franchisee provided written notice of termination of the franchise agreement, effective June 1, 2010 ([3]). However, complications arose due to communications between the seller, purchaser, and franchisor concerning the sale and the contractual obligations under the franchise agreement ([6]–[10]).

The crux of the appeal is rooted in the events following the termination of the franchise agreement and the subsequent discussions between the parties concerning the exercise of the franchisor's right of pre-emption, leading to a legal dispute over the enforceability of these rights and obligations ([14]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue in this appeal pertains to the enforceability of the franchisor's right of pre-emption following the sale of shares agreement and the implications of the addendum made to the franchise agreement ([15]). Specifically, the court was tasked with determining whether the extended period of pre-emption negotiated through the addendum took precedence over the initial 30-day pre-emption right established in the franchise agreement, and whether the seller's unilateral actions constituted a breach of contract ([15], [16]).

Additionally, the court considered whether the principles of qui prior est tempore potior est iure (the one who is prior in time is stronger in right) could be applied to favor the interests of the purchaser over those of the franchisor under these circumstances ([23]).

R – Rules

The legal rules relevant to this case include the principles governing rights of pre-emption, as established in preceding case law, particularly emphasizing the need for a written offer to be made by the seller to the holder of the pre-emption right before the sale can proceed to a third party ([19]).

The principle qui prior est tempore potior est iure indicates that rights derived from an earlier contract have precedence over subsequent agreements or claims, which was substantially referenced by Eksteen J in the court below ([16]). Moreover, the enforcement of any variations to the contractual rights mandated that they be recorded in writing as specified by clause 43.3 of the franchise agreement ([20]). This aspect highlighted that any amendment must be formally agreed upon to be valid, thus affecting the exercise of rights under the franchise agreement.

Another rule considered was that a contractual party is entitled to enforce their rights. However, competing claims present a more complex scenario, where specific performance must be resolved according to principles established in case law (e.g., Farmer’s Co-operative Society (Reg) v Berry and others) ([17]).

A – Application

The court analyzed the correspondence and actions of both parties in light of the established rules. It noted that the franchisor, Pick ‘n Pay, did not actively exercise their right of pre-emption within the 30-day timeframe initially allocated by the franchise agreement, and instead engaged in negotiations that culminated in an addendum extending their period of pre-emption ([11], [13]).

The court emphasized that while the franchisor was entitled to a pre-emption right, its failure to act on that right within the stipulated time frame significantly weakened its position. The addendum was viewed as creating a new contractual obligation that established an extended period for exercising the pre-emption rights, borne out of mutual agreement ([22]).

However, the purchaser’s position was strengthened by the fact that their rights under the sale of shares agreement were valid and unassailable upon the franchisor's failure to exercise its right within the original 30-day period ([21]). The principle of qui prior est tempore potior est iure was thus applied to the facts, solidifying the purchaser's claims to specific performance in the face of the franchisor's delay and inability to enforce rights effectively ([22]).

The court reaffirmed that allowing the franchisor to exercise its pre-emption rights post-deadline would undermine the buyer’s rights, reinforcing the validity of the transaction conducted with the seller ([22]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the decision of the court below, which mandated the seller to transfer 50 percent of the shares in the franchisee company to the purchaser against payment of the purchase price ([14], [23]). The court's holding underscored the importance of adhering to the agreed contractual timelines and the ramifications of modifying such rights without proper agreement.

This judgment serves as a significant pronouncement on the complexities involved in franchise agreements, pre-emption rights, and the necessity of explicit written agreements for any variations to such rights. The ruling illustrates the balance of power in contractual agreements and the weight of prior established rights, setting a key precedent for similar disputes in franchising and commercial law within South Africa.
