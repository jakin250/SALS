Supreme Court of Appeal

The Director of Public Prosecutions: Gauteng Division, Pretoria v Mbonani (1198/2019) [2020] ZASCA 115

Mbonani entered a plea of not guilty on kidnapping and rape but pleaded guilty to murder as per the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 115

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The case revolves around Tokologo Mbonani, who was arraigned in the Gauteng High Court on charges including kidnapping, murder, robbery, and rape. The prosecution alleged that, on 20 March 2017, Mbonani misled a lady friend regarding a job opportunity, which led to the deceased, a relative of the friend, visiting him. The deceased subsequently disappeared, leading her family to suspect Mbonani, who was later arrested upon the discovery of the deceased's cellphone in his possession. On 28 March 2017, Mbonani pointed out the body of the deceased, which had been burned and showed signs of a violent death, with carbon monoxide poisoning as the cause ([paras 2–4]).

Mbonani entered a plea of not guilty on kidnapping and rape but pleaded guilty to murder as per the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977. Nevertheless, the state rejected this plea. The trial court, led by AJ Phahlane, acquitted Mbonani on all counts, citing a lack of admissible evidence ([paras 5–9]).

The applicant, the Director of Public Prosecutions, sought to reserve questions of law regarding the trial court's refusal to accept certain legal points post-trial. This led to the present appeal, wherein the core questions hinged on whether the trial court correctly dismissed certain applications for the reservation of legal questions under section 319 of the Criminal Procedure Act ([paras 10–12]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues in this case include:

  1. Whether the trial court properly exercised its discretion in disregarding the contents of the section 112(2) plea statement as evidential material after the state rejected it ([paras 14–19]).
  2. Whether the trial court erred at law by declaring a verbal confession made to a justice of the peace inadmissible solely due to its lack of a written record ([paras 27–30]).
  3. Whether the questions concerning the application of circumstantial evidence and the overall assessment of the trial court constituted valid legal questions that could be reserved under section 319, or whether they were simply inquiries about the factual determinations made by the trial court ([paras 31–34]).

R – Rules

Applicable legal rules include:

  1. Section 319 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977: This statute allows a trial court to reserve questions of law during proceedings, contingent upon accurate framing and clarity of the legal issue ([paras 12–13]).
  2. Section 112(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act: This provision governs the acceptance of guilty pleas and states that if such a plea is rejected by the state, it cannot be used against the accused ([para 16]).
  3. Section 217 of the Criminal Procedure Act: This statute specifies that confessions made to a magistrate or justice of the peace need not be written down to be considered admissible ([para 28]).
  4. Relevant case law includes S v Hendricks (1995) regarding the use of guilty pleas in establishing factual contexts and Director of Public Prosecutions, Western Cape v Schoeman and Another (2019), which elucidates the standards for reviewing the findings of trial courts ([para 19], [para 33]).

A – Application

The court analyzed the first question regarding the admissibility of the section 112(2) plea statement, concluding that the trial court erred in disregarding it. The judge noted that substantial admissions in Mbonani's plea statement, taken in conjunction with autopsy findings, indicated that the state had enough evidence to establish his involvement in the murder ([paras 19–22]).

On the second question about the verbal confession, the Acting Judge of Appeal, Matojane AJA, agreed with the argument that such confessions to a justice of the peace are permissible without a requirement for them to be recorded, thereby establishing the confession's admissibility ([paras 27–30]).

Regarding the third and fourth questions, the judgment maintained that these did not constitute genuine points of law. The court clarified that they required an evaluation of the factual circumstances and thus did not meet the criteria for issues that could be reserved under section 319 ([paras 31–34]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded as follows:

  1. The application for leave to appeal was upheld regarding the first two questions, supporting the view that the trial court erred in its assessments.
  2. The application concerning questions three and four was dismissed, indicating these were matters of fact rather than law.
  3. The appeal against Mbonani’s acquittal for alternative counts of murder and theft was accepted, while his acquittal on the counts of premeditated murder and robbery was dismissed.
  4. The case was thus referred back to the trial court for sentencing on the findings of murder and theft ([paras 34]).

This judgment underscores the critical standards for evaluating claims of evidential admissibility within legal proceedings and clarifies the boundaries for appeals based on legal versus factual determinations, thereby reinforcing the procedural rigor required in criminal cases.
