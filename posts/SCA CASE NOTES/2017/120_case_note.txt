Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note: **State Information Technology Agency SOC Limited v ELCB Information Services (Pty) Ltd & another** ([2017] ZASCA 120)

### F – Facts

This appeal arises from a judgment of the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria (Mabuse J), which dismissed a review application filed by the appellant, State Information Technology Agency SOC Ltd (SITA), against ELCB Information Services (Pty) Ltd (first respondent) and Leon Dicker NO (second respondent). The High Court had also granted a counter-application from the first respondent that the arbitrator’s award on 31 March 2014 be made an order of court in terms of s 31(1) of the Arbitration Act 45 of 1965 ([1]).

The appellant and respondent had entered into two agreements: the first on 12 March 2006 for the development of an information management system for the South African Social Security Agency (SASSA), and a second purported agreement for a records management system for the Eastern Cape Department of Health, based on an unsigned letter ([3]). The first agreement contained a clause mandating arbitration for unresolved disputes ([4]).

Substantial payments were made under both agreements; however, disputes arose, leading the respondents to demand payment through letters and subsequently to file for arbitration ([5]-[6]). The appellant contested the existence and validity of the second agreement. On 31 March 2014, an arbitration award was made against the appellant after it withdrew from the proceedings when the arbitrator dismissed its application for the agreements' invalidity ([8]-[9]).

Following the dismissal of the review application and the granting of the counter-application by the High Court, the appellant sought leave to appeal, contending various grounds, including alleging the arbitrator’s gross irregularities and violations of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 ([2]).

### I – Issues

The key legal issues in this case revolved around whether the arbitrator committed gross irregularities warranting the review and setting aside of the arbitration award. Specifically, the appellant contended the following:

1. Whether the appellant was effectively excluded from participating in the arbitration proceedings.
2. Whether the lack of a signed second agreement invalidated the arbitration proceedings.
3. Whether the arbitrator exceeded his authority in awarding interest without proper demand.
4. Whether the arbitrator failed to adequately consider evidence.
5. Whether the failure to provide written reasons constituted a gross irregularity according to the provisions of s 33(1)(b) of the Arbitration Act ([12]-[19]).

### R – Rules

The legal framework governing the case is rooted in the Arbitration Act 42 of 1965, which provides for the review of arbitration awards on grounds of misconduct or gross irregularity ([19]). The relevant provisions include:

- **Section 33(1)** indicates that a court may set aside an arbitration award if a tribunal has committed gross irregularities in conducting the proceedings or has exceeded its powers.
  
Further, established jurisprudence, such as *Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd* ([1984] 3 SA 623 (A)) and *Telcordia Technologies Inc v Telkom SA Ltd* ([2007] 3 SA 266 (SCA)), guide the understanding of procedural compliance and the nature of arbitration reviews ([12]).

### A – Application

**Appellant's Contentions:**

1. **Exclusion from Proceedings:** The court found that the appellant chose to withdraw from the arbitration and failed to cure its default despite multiple opportunities, leading to a meritless claim of exclusion ([13]-[14]).
   
2. **Unsigned Second Agreement:** The court highlighted that the unsigned second agreement did not preclude the arbitrator from rendering an award because the parties had historically acted upon it. The appellant's claim of its non-existence was effectively dismissed due to its conduct and compliance with obligations under the agreements ([15]).

3. **Interest Award:** Contrary to the appellant's assertion, there were ample documented demands for payment before the interest award, dismissing any claims of exceeding authority in this regard ([16]).

4. **Failure to Consider Evidence:** The court clarified that the lack of an appellant statement of defense contributed to the arbitrator not needing to consider unproven assertions, affirming the standard for reviews does not entail a re-evaluation of merits ([17]).

5. **Written Reasons:** The timeline and circumstances surrounding the arbitrator's provision of written reasons were deemed reasonable and not amounting to gross irregularities, particularly given that parties were informed of the delays due to the arbitrator's health ([18]-[20]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court's ruling, establishing that the appellant failed to prove any gross irregularities. The decision underlined the importance of adherence to arbitration procedures, emphasizing that the appellant’s inaction led to its default and the need for deference to arbitrators' awards under the Arbitration Act. The court also criticized the manner in which the appellant pursued its litigation, hinting at the need for accountability in public procurement ([22]).

The implications of this decision emphasize the integrity and finality of arbitration awards, particularly for state entities, reinforcing the necessity of operational diligence in dispute resolution and adherence to contractual obligations in public sector engagements.
