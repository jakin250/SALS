Supreme Court of Appeal

Mabaso v The State (200/2015) [2016] ZASCA 29

On July 14, 2005, after his arrest, Mabaso indicated involvement in the murder to Captain Dladla en route to the police station, prompting a questioning session.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 29

F – Facts

The appellant, Thembani Mabaso, was charged with murder in the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court, Pietermaritzburg ([1]). During the trial, he claimed his confession, which linked him to the murder, was obtained through coercion while being interrogated by police officers, alleging assault and that he made a formal pointing-out under duress ([1]). The prosecutor characterized the sole evidence against the appellant as the pointing-out made to Captain van Rensburg during a trial-within-a-trial, which aimed to determine the admission of the pointing-out evidence ([2], [3]).

On July 14, 2005, after his arrest, Mabaso indicated involvement in the murder to Captain Dladla en route to the police station, prompting a questioning session. However, critical procedural irregularities were identified, such as the police officers not documenting Dladla’s alleged confession, and not offering Mabaso the opportunity to make a formal confession ([5], [6]). Furthermore, despite the appellant's request for legal representation, he was subjected to the pointing-out as Van Rensburg continued without providing an opportunity to contact his sister-in-law, who was a police officer ([10], [11]). Ultimately, the trial court relied solely on the pointing-out evidence to convict him, which led to a life sentence, but his application for leave to appeal was initially denied ([4]).

I – Issues

The core legal issues in this case included:

  1. Whether the pointing-out made by the appellant was admissible as evidence given the circumstances surrounding its procurement, specifically with regard to his constitutional rights to legal representation and a fair trial as founded in section 35 of the Constitution ([14], [17]).
  2. Whether the confession elicited during the pointing-out could be separated from the pointing-out itself and admitted in evidence ([12], [13]).
  3. The reliability of the handwritten notes made by Van Rensburg, particularly regarding the failure to confirm the notes with the appellant ([15], [16]).

R – Rules

The court relied on various legal principles and authorities in rendering its judgment:

  • Section 218 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 permits the prosecution to adduce evidence of a pointing-out even if it is part of an inadmissible confession. However, as established in S v Mbele (1981) and S v Magwaza (1985), a confession cannot be disguised within the pointing-out ([12]).
  • Section 35 of the Constitution guarantees the right to a fair trial, including the right to legal representation ([14]). This right is critical when considering the circumstances under which the pointing-out was made.
  • Precedent establishes that if the right to legal representation is denied in a significant manner, any statements or confessions made can be deemed inadmissible, as shown in cases such as S v Mthembu ([14]).

A – Application

In assessing the admissibility of the evidence, the court emphasized the failure of the police officers, particularly Van Rensburg, to afford Mabaso the opportunity to contact his sister-in-law for legal advice before proceeding with the pointing-out. The court expressed concern about Van Rensburg's disregard for the appellant's request and noted the implications for the appellant’s constitutional rights ([10], [14]). Furthermore, it was found that the interrogation methods employed violated fundamental principles that safeguard against involuntary confessions, leading to serious doubts about both the pointing-out and the confession's admissibility.

The court also scrutinized the reliability of the handwritten notes made by Van Rensburg. The evidence revealed discrepancies, notably with Zondo, the interpreter, denying that he read back the notes to the appellant. This negated any claim that the appellant had approved the incriminatory statements recorded ([15], [16]). As such, the notes were deemed inadmissible hearsay, undermining the weight of the prosecution's case against Mabaso.

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the pointing-out and confession were improperly obtained, primarily due to the flagrant disregard of Mabaso's constitutional rights. The trial court’s reliance on this evidence was inadequate to justify a conviction for murder, leading to the appellate court’s decision to uphold the appeal ([18]). Consequently, the court set aside the conviction and sentence imposed upon Mabaso, reinforcing the critical importance of adhering to constitutional rights within criminal procedures. This judgment has broader implications for the necessity of guaranteeing legal representation during police interrogations, ensuring adherence to due process in criminal trials.

Thus, the court's decision in Mabaso v The State underscores the fundamental legal principle that confessions obtained in violation of inherent rights will not withstand judicial scrutiny, aligning with constitutional safeguards in South African law.
