Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note on **City of Cape Town v Strümpher** (104/2011) [2012] ZASCA 54

### F – Facts:
The case at hand involves an appeal from the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa concerning the City of Cape Town (the Appellant) and Marcel Mouzakis Strümpher (the Respondent). For 37 years, the Respondent operated a caravan park on a property supplied with water by the City ([1]). In May 2007, the City notified the Respondent of pending water disconnection due to arrears amounting to R182,000. The Respondent raised a dispute regarding the water account after discovering that a defective water meter contributed to inflated charges ([2]–[3]). Despite this dispute, the City disconnected the water supply on 17 August 2007 without addressing the Respondent’s claims ([4]–[5]).

The Full Bench of the Western Cape High Court initially ruled in favor of the Respondent, affirming a spoliation order that required the City to reconnect the water supply ([1]). The City contended that the water supply was solely based on a contractual agreement and therefore not subject to spoliation remedies. Upon receiving leave to appeal, the City maintained its argument that the disconnection was lawful and justified under its by-laws ([1]–[6]).

### I – Issues:
The primary legal issue presented in the appeal is whether the City had the authority to cut off the Respondent's water supply due to the non-payment of arrears, especially given that the Respondent disputed the amount owed ([6]). Two specific questions arise:
1. Is the Respondent's right to water merely a contract-based personal right, or is it reinforced by constitutional and statutory mandates?
2. Was the City's disconnection of the water service lawful under applicable laws and its own bylaws, thereby justifying the City's actions?

### R – Rules:
The case engages multiple statutory and constitutional provisions:
1. **Water Services Act 108 of 1997**: Sections 3(1), 11(1), and 27(2) establish the constitutional right to water as a public good and impose a duty on municipal authorities to provide access to sufficient water ([9]–[10]).
2. **City of Cape Town Water By-law and Debt Collection By-law**: Sections 18, 19, and 30 of the City’s bylaws provide frameworks for establishing contractual relationships for water provision and outline the conditions under which water services may be restricted due to non-payment ([7]–[13]).
3. **Judicial Precedents**: The case referenced **Telkom SA Ltd v Xsinet (Pty) Ltd** and **Impala Water Users Association v Lourens NO**, which discuss whether rights to services can be purely contractual or if they carry more substantial public law implications ([8]–[11]).

### A – Application:
In addressing the City’s argument, the court distinguished the nature of the Respondent's water supply rights from mere contractual obligations. The court asserted that while a water supply contract exists, it does not diminish the constitutional entitlement to access water ([9]). The court referred to the Water Services Act, emphasizing the importance of ensuring access to water services ([10]).

Regarding the City's justification for the disconnection, the court found shortcomings in the City’s adherence to procedural requirements of fairness outlined in the Water Services Act. The City’s by-laws mandate clear dispute resolution processes when payments are disputed, which were not followed in this instance. The court pointed out that the City did not acknowledge the Respondent’s declared dispute, thereby violating its own procedural guidelines ([12]–[16]).

Additionally, reliance on precedent from **Rademan v Moqhaka Municipality** was deemed inappropriate, primarily due to significant factual distinctions. The circumstances in Rademan involved deliberate non-payment, contrasting the Respondent’s proactive dispute regarding billing accuracy ([17]).

### C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the lower court's decision by rejecting the City’s appeal, concluding that the Respondent had a right, not just under contract but reinforced by wider constitutional and statutory protections, to access adequate water supplies. The court affirmed the application of the mandament van spolie, emphasizing the principle that no one should take the law into their own hands, thereby validating the Respondent's claim for reconnection based on deprivation of service. The appeal was dismissed with costs, underscoring the court's stance that municipalities must adhere strictly to legal and procedural frameworks to avoid unjust disconnection of essential services ([20]).

In summary, this ruling holds critical implications for municipal authorities in South Africa, reminding them of their public law duties to ensure equitable access to necessary services, highlighting the intertwining of private contractual relations and public rights in the provision of essential services such as water.
