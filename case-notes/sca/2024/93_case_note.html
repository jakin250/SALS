Ubisi and Another v Road Accident Fund (711/2023) [2024] ZASCA 93

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 93

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–4])

The case originates from a claim made by Mr. Matedewuja Kenneth Ubisi (the first appellant) against the Road Accident Fund (the RAF) due to injuries sustained from a motor vehicle accident. Ubisi filed his claim for R9,500,000 on 15 September 2017, seeking compensation for various damages including past and future medical expenses and loss of earnings. The RAF disputed liability but later settled the merits of the case, agreeing to compensate Ubisi for 100% of his proven or agreed damages by 5 June 2019 ([paras 3–4]).

On 25 November 2021, the RAF offered a settlement regarding the quantum of damages, which was formally accepted by Ubisi's legal representatives, Nel, van der Merwe & Smalman Incorporated (Smalman Inc), on 16 February 2022. The settlement was subsequently consented to by the RAF on 6 May 2022 ([para 6]).

However, after hearing arguments related to the settlement on 5 June 2022, the Gauteng Division of the High Court, led by Justice Mbongwe, set aside the settlement agreement. The court ordered Ubisi’s attorneys to bear the costs of the action, including those incurred by Ubisi’s experts ([paras 2–5, 7]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 8–12])

The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the high court had the authority to set aside a settlement agreement between the parties which had not been challenged by either side. Specific legal questions included:

  1. Did the high court err in setting aside the settlement agreement, given that the agreement was not unconscionable or illegal, nor objected to by either party?
  2. Was the high court justified in making adverse findings against Ubisi’s legal representatives without providing them the opportunity for a fair hearing? ([paras 8–17])

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 12–15])

The Supreme Court ground its decision on established legal principles, especially from earlier case laws such as Eke v Parsons ([2015] ZACC 30), which establishes that:

  • Courts must respect and enforce contractual agreements that have been concluded freely and voluntarily.
  • A settlement agreement should only be set aside if its terms are unconscionable, illegal, or violate public policy ([paras 12–14]).

The court also relied on Mafisa v Road Accident Fund ([2024] ZACC 4) and Road Accident Fund v Taylor ([2023] ZASCA 64), which reiterated that once a settlement has been made, it effectively concludes the litigation between the parties, rendering intervening judicial review unwarranted unless terms of the agreement are significantly problematic ([para 15]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 16–18])

In reviewing the decision of the high court, the Supreme Court noted that the high court lacked jurisdiction to set aside the settlement agreement. The appellants had presented an indisputable legal proposition that once a settlement was reached—especially one not contested by either party—the court's jurisdiction over the matter had concluded. The issues of the settlement’s validity were to be decided by the parties alone, and this was reinforced by the precedent set in Mafisa where the court emphasized the limited grounds upon which judicial interference would be permitted ([paras 16–17]).

Furthermore, the Supreme Court criticized the high court’s findings of dishonesty and impropriety against Ubisi’s attorneys, arguing that such conclusions were made without factual support and without affording them an opportunity for defense. The absence of clear evidence for claims of fraud rendered the high court’s findings not only inappropriate but also unjust ([para 18]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 20–22])

The Supreme Court ultimately upheld the appeal, asserting that the high court's decision to set aside the settlement agreement was incorrect and lacked jurisdiction. The agreement reached by the parties was reinstated and made an order of court. The court also underscored the principle that the integrity and autonomy of legal practitioners must be protected from unsubstantiated claims of misconduct ([paras 20–21]).

This holding reinforces the importance of upholding settlement agreements in civil litigation, showcasing the judiciary's restrained role in contractual matters among litigating parties. The court concluded that there would be no order as to costs due to the RAF's non-opposition and recognizing that the matter should have never escalated to an appeal if proper legal principles had been adhered to ([para 22]).

In not ordering costs, the court avoided further burdening the RAF while underscoring the issue of judicial overreach. The findings in this case importantly reaffirm the principles of contractual freedom and the legal profession's ethos in South African law.

References:

  1. Eke v Parsons [2015] ZACC 30; 2015 (11) BCLR 1319 (CC).
  2. Mafisa v Road Accident Fund and Another [2024] ZACC 4.
  3. Road Accident Fund v Taylor [2023] ZASCA 64.
