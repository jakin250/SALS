Constitutional Court

Minister of Defence v Andries Diphapang Potsane; Legal Soldier (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Defence

The Constitutional Court of South Africa decided on two consolidated cases, Minister of Defence v Andries Diphapang Potsane (CCT 14/01) and Legal Soldier (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Defence (CCT 29/01), heard on 23 August 2001 and decided on 5 October 2001.

Constitutional Court 2001 CCT 14/01

F – Facts

The Constitutional Court of South Africa decided on two consolidated cases, Minister of Defence v Andries Diphapang Potsane (CCT 14/01) and Legal Soldier (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Defence (CCT 29/01), heard on 23 August 2001 and decided on 5 October 2001. The Potsane case involved an appeal against a decision by the Free State High Court, which affirmed a constitutional challenge to certain disciplinary proceedings in military courts, asserting their inconsistency with the constitutional provisions governing prosecution. The Legal Soldier case, on the other hand, originated in the High Court in Pretoria, where the applicants challenged the constitutionality of the Military Discipline Supplementary Measures Act 16 of 1999 (the Act) in its entirety.

Both cases questioned whether military prosecutors possess authority to institute and conduct prosecutions in military courts, potentially infringing upon the exclusive prosecutorial authority of the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) as conferred by section 179 of the Constitution ([1]–[2]). The applicants argued that the structure created by the Act, allowing military prosecutors authority independent from the NDPP, was unconstitutional. Notably, the Free State High Court ruled that specific sections of the Act were unconstitutional because they violated the authority of the NDPP ([5]) and these sections allowed military prosecutions for civilian offenses ([2]).

I – Issues

The central issues presented before the Court were:

  1. Prosecutorial Authority: Whether the provisions of the Military Discipline Supplementary Measures Act 16 of 1999, which empower military prosecutors to conduct prosecutions independent of the NDPP, are unconstitutional under section 179 of the Constitution, which establishes the NDPP as the sole authority for prosecutions ([2]).
  2. Equality Rights: Whether the provisions of the Act infringe upon the equality rights guaranteed under section 9 of the Constitution. This issue was less prominent in the applications and not directly addressed in Legal Soldier, but it presented an additional consideration in the decision-making process ([3]).

R – Rules

The constitutional framework primarily revolves around section 179 of the Constitution, which establishes:

  • A single national prosecuting authority headed by the NDPP ([14]);
  • The authority to institute criminal proceedings on behalf of the state, along with the power to formulate prosecution policy ([14]).

The provisions under scrutiny include specific sections of the Military Discipline Supplementary Measures Act, notably sections 13, 14, and 22, which govern the assignment of duties and powers to military prosecutors ([5], [11]). The principles of equality under section 9 of the Constitution were also invoked, hinting at potential discriminatory implications of a bifurcated prosecutorial authority in military contexts.

A – Application

1. Prosecutorial Authority: The Court scrutinized the interpretation of section 179, emphasizing its imperative language, which suggests a singular prosecutorial authority vested in the NDPP. The learned judge from the Free State High Court interpreted this provision to mean that a separate military prosecution framework was constitutionally impermissible ([12], [26]). The Constitutional Court agreed with this interpretation, concluding that the Act's provisions infringing on the NDPP's exclusive authority were unconstitutional ([22], [42]).

The military’s argument hinged on historical context, asserting that section 179 aimed to streamline prosecutorial functions across jurisdictions but did not intend to exclude military prosecutions altogether ([17], [20]). The Court ultimately rejected this argument, indicating that a bifurcated system risks introducing conflicts and undermines the oversight needed for civilian prosecuting functions ([25]).

2. Equality Rights: The Court examined whether the different prosecutorial policies for military versus civilian contexts amounted to unfair discrimination under section 9 of the Constitution. The judge noted that differentiation between soldiers and civilians was rationally connected to the need for maintaining military discipline, hence not constituting unfair discrimination. Legal soldier applicants also faced difficulties since their arguments did not demonstrate that the treatment under the military justice system was prejudicial against their rights ([43]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded that the appeal by the Minister of Defence must be upheld. The relevant sections of the Military Discipline Supplementary Measures Act 16 of 1999 were struck down for their inconsistency with section 179 of the Constitution, affirming the NDPP’s exclusive authority over prosecutions ([46]). The judgment underscored the necessity for a unified prosecutorial authority to uphold the rule of law, reflecting a key principle of constitutional governance ([42], [44]).

The implications of the judgment thus reaffirmed the supremacy of constitutional dictates over legislative provisions that may inadvertently complicate or disrupt fundamental rights, affirming the need for civilian oversight in prosecutorial matters and addressing the equality concerns raised in the application ([46]). The Court’s decision further clarified the constitutional boundaries within which the military justice system operates, thereby ensuring that the integrity of the prosecutorial framework is upheld across jurisdictions.
