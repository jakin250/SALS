Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note on *Venator Africa (Pty) Ltd v Watts and Another* ([2024] ZASCA 60)

## F – Facts
The case arises from actions taken by Venator Africa (Pty) Ltd, the appellant, against two directors of Siyazi Logistics and Trading (Pty) Ltd (Siyazi), namely Lloyd Mason Watts (first respondent) and Martin Bekker (second respondent) ([para 3]). Venator contracted with Siyazi for clearing and forwarding services beginning around 2016, which included the issuance of disbursement accounts to reflect amounts owed to the South African Revenue Service (SARS) ([paras 7.1–7.4]). 

Between 2018 and 2019, disbursement accounts totaling R66,395,006.27 were issued by Siyazi to Venator, all of which were paid ([para 8]). However, it was claimed that Siyazi only remitted R31,353,697.27 to SARS, resulting in a shortfall of R34,612,576.19. The difference led to further assessments by SARS, which included VAT, penalties, and interest totaling R41,407,220.00 in damages for Venator ([paras 13–14]).

Venator alleged that the directors engaged in reckless or grossly negligent conduct as defined under sections 218(2) and 22(1) of the Companies Act 71 of 2008 ([paras 16–18]). Specifically, it was argued that the first and second defendants controlled Siyazi’s activities in a way that led to the fraudulent misrepresentation concerning payments to SARS ([para 19]).

After the particulars of claim were filed, Watts filed exceptions arguing that under section 22(1), liability is imposed on the company, not its directors, and that neither section 22 nor section 218(2) provided a basis for the claims against him as a director ([para 5]). The high court upheld this exception, prompting the appeal from Venator ([paras 12–13]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal revolved around:
1. Whether directors of a company can be personally held liable for reckless trading under section 22(1) as read with section 218(2) of the Companies Act 71 of 2008 ([para 12]).
2. Whether the exceptions raised by the first respondent hold merit, particularly regarding the interpretation of directors' responsibilities and potential liabilities under the Companies Act ([paras 5–6]).

## R – Rules
The key legal rules identified in the case include:
- **Section 218(2)** states that any person who contravenes a provision of the Act may be liable to another for loss or damage incurred ([para 18]).
- **Section 22(1)** prohibits a company from conducting its business recklessly or fraudulently ([paras 16–17]).
- **Section 76(3)** outlines the fiduciary duties of directors, emphasizing that these duties are primarily owed to the company ([para 31]).
- **Section 77(3)(b)** stipulates that directors are liable for losses incurred by the company if they acquiesce in the company's actions that contravene section 22 ([para 29]).

Notably, the principles from prior cases such as *Rabinowitz v Van Graan* where it was held that a person guilty of an offence under the Act might be found to have contravened its provisions, was referenced, though the high court ultimately did not agree with this interpretation ([para 7]), leading to divergences in judicial views.

## A – Application
In applying these rules, the Supreme Court of Appeal took a closer interpretive lens on the statutes involved. The court noted that while section 218(2) appears to provide for personal liability, it does so conditionally based on contraventions of other specific provisions of the Act ([paras 26–27]). The court reinforced the interpretation that section 22 explicitly imposes obligations upon the company itself and does not extend to actions or omissions directly attributable to its directors ([para 28]). 

The court critiqued the high court's acceptance of the exceptions, emphasizing that permitting personal liability for directors requires clear statutory language to differentiate such liability from that owed to the company ([paras 31-33]). This interpretation precludes a broad personal liability paradigm and aligns liability strictly with the framework established within the Act for directorial responsibilities.

Consequently, the court upheld the high court's decision that Venator's particulars of claim did not sufficiently establish a viable cause of action against the directors, as the alleged reckless trading under section 22(1) did not translate into personal liability under section 218(2) for directors ([para 35]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Venator's appeal, thus confirming the high court's ruling that directors of a company could not be held personally liable for reckless trading in circumstances such as those presented by Venator ([para 36]). This judgment implies a critical stance reinforcing the separation between corporate liability and personal liability of directors under the framework of the Companies Act, potentially limiting creditors' avenues for redress against directors. It substantiates the premise that claims against directors must be tethered to specific breaches defined by the Act, emphasizing a clear directive from the legislature regarding the nature of corporate governance responsibilities and the delineation of liabilities ([para 34–35]). 

This case underscores the importance of clearly defined statutory provisions in company law, emphasizing that personal liability of directors cannot be broadly inferred without explicit legislative intention, thereby sustaining the integrity of the separate corporate personality doctrine fundamental to company law in South Africa.
