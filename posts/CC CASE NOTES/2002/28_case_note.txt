Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2002

# Case Note: The Democratic Alliance and Michael Sean Moriarty v. Amos Masondo N.O. & Minister of Provincial and Local Government (CCT 29/02)

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The case arose from an appeal to the Constitutional Court of South Africa concerning the representation of minority political parties on the mayoral committee within a municipal council. The first appellant, the Democratic Alliance, serves as the main opposition party in the Johannesburg metropolitan council. The second appellant, Michael Sean Moriarty, is the leader of the Democratic Alliance's caucus in that council. The first respondent, Amos Masondo, is the executive mayor of the council, while the second respondent is the national minister responsible for the Structures Act. 

The appellants argued that the current mayoral committee appointed by Mayor Masondo violated constitutional rights by excluding minority party representation, as only the African National Congress (ANC), the majority party, was included in the appointments. The High Court of Johannesburg dismissed the appellants' application, holding that neither the Structures Act nor the Constitution mandated minority representation on the mayoral committee. This dismissal led to the appeal to the Constitutional Court ([1]-[3]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal question presented in this case was whether minority political parties within a municipal council are entitled to representation on a mayoral committee established under section 60 of the Local Government: Municipal Structures Act 117 of 1998 (the Structures Act) and section 160(8) of the Constitution. The appellants asserted that the failure to include minority representation was unconstitutional and contrary to the principles of democracy encapsulated in the Constitution ([1]-[5]).

### R – Rules
The legal framework underlying the appellants' claims consists of:
1. **The Constitution of South Africa**: Specifically, section 160(8) which asserts that members of a municipal council are entitled to participate in its proceedings and committees in a manner that ensures fair representation of parties and interests.
2. **The Local Government: Municipal Structures Act 117 of 1998**: Particularly section 60, which provides for the appointment of a mayoral committee by the executive mayor, specifying the conditions under which such appointments must be made and powers that can be delegated ([4]-[6]).

### A – Application
The Court evaluated the statutory interpretation of both the Constitution and the Structures Act. It examined whether the mayoral committee constituted a "committee of the municipal council" as defined by section 160(8) of the Constitution. The majority held that the specific role of the mayoral committee was to assist the mayor in the exercise of executive authority, focusing on effective and efficient governance rather than deliberative representation ([19]-[33]).

The Court noted that while the democratic principles reflected in the Constitution necessitate the consideration of minority voices, a distinction was drawn between the functions of committees involved in deliberative processes versus those designated for executive roles, like the mayoral committee ([18]-[24]). The lack of explicit language in the Structures Act requiring proportional representation in the mayoral committee further supported the Court's conclusion that minority representation was not mandated ([24]-[34]).

The dissenting judgments offered compelling arguments for the necessity of fair representation in executive decision-making bodies, emphasizing that the systemic context and historical injustices warranted a more inclusive interpretation of representation at the local government level ([37]-[82]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately upheld the decision of the High Court, concluding that the mayoral committee is not a committee of the municipal council for the purposes of section 160(8) of the Constitution; therefore, the requirement of minority party representation did not apply. The Court maintained that section 60(1)(a) of the Structures Act does not conflict with the Constitution, and as such, the appeal was dismissed. Each party was ordered to bear its own costs, reflecting the serious nature of the constitutional questions involved ([34]-[36]).

The case reaffirmed the delicate balance between ensuring effective governance through strong executive authority and the principle of inclusivity in democratic practices, highlighting ongoing debates in South African local governance regarding representation and accountability.
