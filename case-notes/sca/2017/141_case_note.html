Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 141

FIRAC Method Case Note

F - Facts

The appeals in Bondev Midrand (Pty) Ltd v Puling and Bondev Midrand (Pty) Ltd v Ramokgopa revolve around the claim of the appellant, Bondev Midrand (Pty) Ltd, a property developer, against the respondents for the re-transfer of immovable property due to non-compliance with registered conditions in their title deeds. [1]

In case 802/2016, Mr. and Mrs. Puling purchased property from Bondev, which included a condition requiring the construction of a dwelling within 18 months. The specified period elapsed without any construction, leading Bondev to claim re-transfer of the property more than four years later, on March 2014. The respondents contended that Bondev's claim had prescribed as more than three years had passed since the claim became due. [3-4]

In case 803/2016, Mr. Ramokgopa faced a similar situation, where he failed to comply with the building condition, leading to Bondev instituting action in January 2014, again claiming that the right to re-transfer had prescribed. [2-4] The Gauteng Division dismissed the claims based on the applicability of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, determining that the claims constituted debts that had indeed prescribed. [5-6]

I - Issues

The primary legal issue is whether Bondev’s claim for the re-transfer of property is a ‘debt’ subject to the Prescription Act or whether it constitutes a real right that does not prescribe within the three-year period stipulated in section 11(d) of the Act. [6] The secondary issue concerns any additional defences raised by the respondents concerning the enforceability of the conditions based on constitutional grounds, including rights to adequate housing. [5-7]

R - Rules

The core legal authority involves the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, particularly its provisions regarding the prescription period for debts. Section 11(d) stipulates a three-year period for debts, unless they fall under the exceptions outlined in sections 11(a), (b), or (c), which are not applicable in this case. [6-7]

The differentiation between personal rights and real rights is pivotal, as established through relevant case law. The analysis of rights within the framework of property law also invokes principles from Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937, which states that personal rights do not qualify for registration against title deeds, thus cannot be enforced against third parties. [15]

A - Application

The court applied the legal tests to determine whether the conditions in the title deeds gave rise to real rights or personal rights. It concluded that the first clause—imposing an obligation on the transferee to construct a dwelling—creates a real right encumbering the property. However, the second clause, allowing Bondev to claim re-transfer, was interpreted as a personal right akin to an option to repurchase, which does not encumber the property in a way that would prevent automatic prescription. [12-20]

Notably, the court looked at comparative precedent, particularly Cape Explosive Works Ltd v Denel (Pty) Ltd, where similar intricacies of property rights were examined. [16-19] It determined that while the obligation to build was binding, Bondev's right to demand re-transfer could stand independently and thus prescribed after the elapsed three years, leading to the dismissal of the claims. In addressing the respondents’ constitutional arguments citing inadequate housing, the court emphasized that such issues were extraneous to the primary question of rights and obligations arising from the title conditions. [7-8, 22]

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed both appeals, affirming that Bondev's claims had prescribed, concluding that the right to enforce the re-transfer constituted a personal right and not a real right capable of surviving the limitations prescribed. The court affirmed the lower court's ruling emphasizing the rationale behind the statutory prescription timelines, which serves to provide legal certainty and protect parties from antiquated claims. Consequently, the court upheld the notion that while the obligation to build imparts a real right, enforcement through claims to re-transfer is limited by statutory timelines under the Prescription Act. [24] This case reflects distinctly the balance between property rights and the statutory framework designed to ensure that claims remain actionable only within reasonable periods, reaffirming constitutional integrity over the procedural rigidity of title deed stipulations.
