# Case Note: Cochrane Steel Products (Pty) Ltd v M-Systems Group (1272/2016) [2017] ZASCA 189

### F - Facts ([paras 1–5])
The case involves an appeal by Cochrane Steel Products (Pty) Ltd ("Cochrane") against a ruling from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria. Cochrane had applied for the registration of the trademark "CLEARVU," which was intended to cover products in Class 6 (non-electric cables and wires of common metal; metal fences; metal mesh; pipes and tubes of metal) and Class 37 (building, construction, repair, and installation services). M-Systems Group Pty Ltd ("M-Systems"), a competitor, opposed this registration on two grounds: (i) the mark was not capable of distinguishing the goods or services, and (ii) it was descriptive, violating sections 9(1) and 10(2)(a) and (b) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 (the Act).

The High Court eventually ordered the registration of "CLEARVU" but included specific disclaimers: it gave no exclusive right to the standalone words "clear" and "view," and directed that Cochrane must not prevent others from using these descriptive terms in trade ([paras 2–4]). The appeal by Cochrane, with leave from the High Court, focused primarily on the admission and disclaimer regarding the words “clear” and “view” ([para 5]).

### I - Issues ([paras 4–5, 12])
The legal questions at the core of this appeal involved whether:
1. The registration of the trademark "CLEARVU" should allow Cochrane exclusive rights over the components "clear" and "view."
2. The High Court's inclusion of a disclaimer against preventing bona fide descriptive use of the words "clear" and "view" in trade was appropriate and justified under the Act.

### R - Rules ([paras 9–12, 14, 22])
The court relied on established principles of trademark law, emphasizing that the core purpose of trademark protection is to prevent misleading commercial speech and uphold competitive integrity ([para 9]). Section 15 of the Act outlines the registry's authority to impose disclaimers when a trademark includes terms that do not function as distinguishing features, which aims to prevent monopolization of descriptive language ([para 12]).

The principle from *Cadbury (Pty) Ltd v Beacon Sweets & Chocolates (Pty) Ltd* emphasizes that courts have discretion in applying disclaimers, as these are fundamentally meant to clarify the rights associated with a trademark, particularly in a manner that does not inhibit lawful competition ([paras 17–19]).

### A - Application ([paras 20–22])
The court scrutinized Cochrane's argument surrounding the uniqueness of the term "CLEARVU," particularly focusing on the deliberate misspelling of "view." It concluded that this misspelling does not change the descriptive character of the words "clear" and "view," which are commonly used in the fencing industry. The court determined that allowing Cochrane exclusive rights would unduly restrict competition, as these terms are widely utilized to describe fencing products in the market ([paras 21–22]).

The court emphasized that trademark law is designed to support fair competition rather than to allow monopolistic claims over common language. Thus, they affirmed the necessity of disclaimers, reinforcing that trademarks should not preclude others from being able to describe their products accurately ([paras 21–22]). In amending paragraph 1.2 of the High Court's order, the court appropriately aligned its ruling with established legal principles without negating Cochrane's rights entirely.

### C - Conclusion ([paras 22–23])
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Cochrane's appeal, upholding the High Court's order with the modification of removing the phrase "the trademark registrant admits that" from paragraph 1.2. This case illustrates crucial interpretations of trademark law where descriptive terms are involved—highlighting the balance between protecting trademarks and fostering a competitive marketplace. The decision effectively prevents Cochrane from establishing a monopoly over commonly used terms in the fencing industry, setting a precedent concerning the limitations of trademark registration regarding descriptive language ([paras 22–23]).

The implications extend to trademark registration practices in South Africa, stressing the importance of disclaimers and the potential that misused trademarks can lead to unfair restrictions on market competition. This ruling underscores the judiciary's commitment to ensuring that trademark law does not overshadow the fundamental principles of fair trading and the free use of the English language, particularly in the commercial sphere.
