# Case Note: Solidarity obo Barnard v South African Police Service [2013] ZASCA 177

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The case **Solidarity obo Barnard v South African Police Service** revolves around the unsuccessful promotion application of Renate Mariette Barnard, a police captain within the South African Police Service (SAPS). Despite being identified as the best candidate for the role of Superintendent in a specialized unit, Barnard was denied the position based on her race, a decision that she claimed constituted unfair discrimination under the **Employment Equity Act 55 of 1998** (EEA) ([1]–[2]). Barnard had initially applied for the position of Superintendent in 2005, receiving the highest evaluation score (86.67%) among all candidates. However, the post was ultimately declared unfilled for reasons related to equity and representivity, a decision later met with skepticism due to the later appointment of a white male to fill the vacancy, which was subsequently advertised again ([4]–[10], [28]–[30]).

Throughout the proceedings, it became clear that Barnard’s applications were mired in debates about representivity in the SAPS and adherence to the EEA. The SAPS maintained that appointing a white female would adversely affect representivity, while Barnard posited that her exclusion was purely based on race ([31]–[47]). Barnard pursued her grievance through the SAPS grievance procedure, then the **Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA)**, before ultimately contesting the decisions in the Labour Court, Labour Appeal Court, and finally, the Supreme Court of Appeal ([40]–[46]).

### I – Issues
The core legal issue in this case is whether Barnard was subjected to unfair discrimination by the SAPS when she was denied promotion to the position of Superintendent because of her race. More specifically, the court was tasked with assessing whether the SAPS justified its decision against the backdrop of affirmative action and the Employment Equity Plan (EEP) ([47]–[50], [56]).

Additionally, the case raised subsidiary issues surrounding the interpretation and application of the EEA regarding the fairness of discrimination claims and the obligations of employers in the context of affirmative action ([9]–[11], [12]). The Labour Court’s findings were challenged on appeal by the SAPS, with the Labour Appeal Court concluding that no discrimination occurred since there was no appointment made ([46]–[47]).

### R – Rules
The principal statutory provisions applicable to this case include:

1. **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996**: Specifically, section 9(1) addresses equality before the law, while section 9(2) allows for measures aimed at promoting equality for disadvantaged groups. Section 9(5) clarifies that discrimination is only deemed fair under specific conditions ([9], [50]).
   
2. **Employment Equity Act 55 of 1998**: Sections pertinent to the case include:
   - **Section 5**: Responsibilities of employers to create fair employment opportunities.
   - **Section 6**: Prohibiting unfair discrimination, with particular emphasis on race, gender, and other grounds listed ([12]–[13]).
   - **Section 11**: Obligation of the employer to show that any alleged discrimination is fair ([14]).
   - **Section 20**: Obligations for designated employers to prepare an employment equity plan aimed at achieving representivity ([17]–[18]).

3. **National Instruction (1/2004) of SAPS**: Provides guidance on how promotion processes should align with the commitments to equity and service delivery.

Precedent from cases including **Minister of Finance & another v Van Heerden** [2004] and **Harksen v Lane NO** [1998], particularly the principles concerning affirmative action and equality, were also heavily relied upon ([11], [55]).

### A – Application
The application of these rules involved a detailed scrutiny of the facts surrounding Barnard's claims of discrimination. The Supreme Court of Appeal rejected the Labour Appeal Court’s notion that no discrimination could occur in the absence of an appointment, reiterating that discriminatory practices could be implicit in negative treatment based on race, regardless of whether a position was ultimately filled ([49]–[50]).

In examining the SAPS' justification for not appointing Barnard, the court evaluated the adequacy and reasonableness of the National Commissioner’s rationale, which cited the need for representivity and alleged non-‘critical’ status of the post ([28], [39], [66]). The court found that the SAPS had not adequately discharged its onus of proof in demonstrating that its refusal to appoint Barnard was socially and legally justifiable under the EEA ([54]–[79]). Furthermore, the reluctance of the SAPS to consider the inputs from the interview panel and Divisional Commissioner suggested a disregard for proper procedure in reaching an equitable decision ([57], [60], [66]).

The court also emphasized that the employment equity goals should not be pursued at the cost of fairness to individuals, and that the rigid application of numerical targets without a contextual evaluation risks entrenching injustices ([23]–[26], [52]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Barnard’s appeal, concluding that unfair discrimination had been established due to her race, and that the SAPS failed to prove that such discrimination was fair within the meaning of the EEA. Significantly, the court emphasized the need for a nuanced approach to employment equity that avoids mechanical application of demographic quotas ([80]–[81]). 

The court ordered the SAPS to compensate Barnard for the income difference, limited to a two-year period, acknowledging the detrimental impacts of her double rejection on her career and morale within the organization ([81]). This case is pivotal in its reinforcement of not only legal standards for fair treatment in employment processes but also for the broader conversation on equity and transformation within South African society.
