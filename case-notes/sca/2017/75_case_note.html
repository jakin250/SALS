Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2016] ZASCA 75

FIRAC Case Notes

F – Facts

The appellants, Khetani Mbuisé Nkabinde, Orapeleng Lawrence Mogoje, Aubrey Mmushi Dikobe, and Jabulane Elliot Papi Makhené, appealed against convictions of multiple serious crimes, including murder, attempted murder, robbery with aggravating circumstances, and unlawful possession of explosives, firearms, and ammunition, following a cash-in-transit heist that occurred on August 8, 2008, near Petrusburg, Free State Province ([1]).

On that day, a Fidelity Security Services armoured vehicle transporting R2.5 million was ambushed. The vehicle, driven by four security guards, was pursued by at least five vehicles and subjected to gunfire. The attack was characterized by extreme violence and tactical execution, resulting in the murder of Mr. De La Rey, an innocent motorist, and multiple attempted murders of others caught in the vicinity ([2]-[8]). The robbers attempted to access the cash by forcibly obtaining keys and using explosives, although they ultimately fled without the money ([3]-[5]).

The police, involved in a mock exercise nearby, pursued the robbers after receiving reports of the heist. Following a high-speed chase, the police intercepted the Audi vehicle used by the appellants, discovering multiple incriminating items, including firearms tied to the robbery ([9]-[13]). The appellants were arrested at the scene, where evidence indicated their active involvement in the robbery, including possession of stolen firearms and explosives ([14]-[20]).

In the trial court, the state established a case against the appellants. Despite their defense claiming innocence and alleging various procedural irregularities during the trial, the court found them guilty of all charges, leading to life sentences and additional lengthy imprisonment terms ([20]-[49]).

I – Issues

The central legal issues before the court included:

  1. Whether the appellants were correctly convicted of murder, robbery, and related charges under the doctrine of common purpose.
  2. Whether procedural irregularities alleged by the appellants justified their appeal against the convictions and sentences ([21]-[32]).
  3. Whether the trial court properly granted the appellants' requests for special entries under section 317(1) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([21], [26]-[32]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal principles included:

  1. Common Purpose Doctrine: This principle enables liability for crimes committed by co-conspirators if one or more members act in furtherance of the common plan (S v Mgedezi & others 1989 (1) SA 687 (A)). The doctrine posits that if a group agrees to commit an unlawful act and one member acts in furtherance of this plan, all may be held liable for actions taken in execution of that plan.
  2. Procedural Fairness: Under section 317 of the Criminal Procedure Act, an accused may apply for special entries regarding claimed irregularities during trial proceedings. The purpose of such entries is to document irregularities that do not seem evident on the trial record ([26]).
  3. Principles of Sentencing: The court emphasized that sentencing is largely at the discretion of the trial court, and minimum sentences prescribed by the legislature should be imposed unless substantial and compelling reasons justify deviation (Malgas 2001 (2) SA 1222 (SCA)) ([49], [54]).

A – Application

The court applied the rules of common purpose to the established facts within the context of the violent robbery. The evidence demonstrated that all appellants participated in the robbery and were aware that their actions could lead to potential harm or death. Hence, the conviction for murder was upheld on the basis that they foresaw the possibility of violent outcomes stemming from their conduct during the robbery ([40]-[42]).

Regarding the procedural complaints, the majority of the appellants’ claims did not meet the threshold for irregularities to warrant a special entry. The court determined that the so-called procedural issues raised did not materially affect the fairness of the trial, as there was no credible evidence of bias or substantial procedural defects, nor did they undermine the safety of the verdict ([32]-[39]).

In terms of sentencing, the trial court’s imposition of minimum sentences aligned with statutory requirements and reflected the severity of the crimes committed, further confirmed by the lack of substantial and compelling circumstances that would merit leniency ([49]-[54]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the convictions and sentences of the appellants. The court concluded that the evidence established their participation in a violent crime spree that resulted in murder and numerous attempts on innocent lives. The findings supported the legal principles surrounding common purpose, highlighting that the appellants acted collectively in a planned and violent robbery. The procedural irregularities alleged by the appellants were found to lack merit and did not influence the trial's fairness.

Consequently, the court reaffirmed the trial court’s discretion in sentencing, noting that the circumstances of the case warranted the severe sentences imposed. The court’s ruling further elucidated the application of the common purpose doctrine in armed robbery contexts, affirming the legal consequences of group criminal intent ([55]).
