MEC, Health and Social Development, Gauteng v DZ obo WZ [2017] ZACC 37

Facts At the heart of this case is the birth of WZ, who suffers from cerebral palsy as a result of alleged medical negligence at the Chris Hani Baragwanath Hospital, Johannesburg, on 19 November 2009 ([1]).

Constitutional Court 2017 [2017] ZACC 37

Facts

At the heart of this case is the birth of WZ, who suffers from cerebral palsy as a result of alleged medical negligence at the Chris Hani Baragwanath Hospital, Johannesburg, on 19 November 2009 ([1]). The respondent, DZ, brought action in the Gauteng Local Division of the High Court against the Member of the Executive Council for Health and Social Development, Gauteng (Gauteng MEC), on behalf of her son, seeking damages for medical expenses. The Gauteng MEC admitted negligence, thereby accepting vicarious liability. The matter of contention was the quantum of damages, specifically future medical expenses amounting to R19,970,631 of a total claim of R23,272,303 ([2]).

The MEC contested the format of the future medical expenses, arguing that she was entitled, under common law, to pay these expenses directly to service providers rather than in a lump sum. This contention was reiterated in her amended plea ([2]).

The High Court and later the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed her plea, affirming that the common law "once and for all" rule prohibited the proposed method of payment since the claim should encompass all damages in one action. The appeal was subsequently brought before the Constitutional Court to clarify these key legal issues ([3]).

Issues

The primary legal questions before the Constitutional Court were:

  1. Whether the common law permitted the Gauteng MEC to pay future medical expenses to service providers directly rather than as a lump sum ([3]);
  2. Whether the "once and for all" rule should be reconsidered or modified to allow for alternative payment structures in medical negligence claims against public healthcare entities ([3]).
  3. The implication of such a decision on the ability of public healthcare entities to fulfill their constitutional obligations to provide healthcare services ([3]).

Rules

The legal frameworks implicated in this case included:

  1. The “Once and For All” Rule: Established in the common law, it stipulates that a claimant must assert all recoverable damages flowing from one cause of action in a single legal action. This principle is associated with the avoidance of multiplicity in lawsuits and is intended to ensure the resolution of disputes in a definitive manner ([15]).
  2. Section 39(2) of the Constitution of South Africa: This provision mandates that courts must promote the spirit, purport, and objects of the Bill of Rights when developing common law ([37]).
  3. Section 173 of the Constitution: Grants the courts inherent powers to develop the common law while considering the interests of justice ([37]).
  4. Delictual Laws: As articulated in previous cases such as Ngubane v South African Transport Services and Evins v Shield Insurance Co Ltd, these reinforce the principle that damages are generally assessed in monetary terms, but also provide for the possibility of considering the adequacy of alternative compensation mechanisms ([26], [38]).

Application

The court analyzed whether the existing common law framework could accommodate the realities of public healthcare funding and access to care. It noted the Gauteng MEC's failure to present sufficient evidence to justify deviating from the lump-sum payment model, rendering her arguments ineffective.

On the broader context, the court acknowledged that while the common law had a longstanding preference for lump sum awards, there existed a necessity for incremental development of the law to better serve the constitutional obligations towards victims of medical negligence, particularly vulnerable individuals such as WZ. The court stressed that while the existing rules must not lead to unjust outcomes, any development should not detract from the essential nature of personal injury claims ([39]).

Furthermore, the court emphasized that the Gauteng MEC's argument hinged on a misunderstanding of the "once and for all" rule. It clarified that the rule relates to the assertion of claims, not to the subsequent enforcement of payment, which could indeed allow for arrangements such as periodic payments if justified ([39]).

The court highlighted the potential implications of recognizing alternatives to the lump-sum payment model, including concerns regarding the sustainability of public healthcare in light of rising costs and continued healthcare access ([39]).

Conclusion

The Constitutional Court dismissed the Gauteng MEC's appeal, affirming that under the established common legal principles, future medical expenses must be awarded in a lump sum. The court reiterated that the existing common law was inadequate for adjusting to changing societal needs, especially in light of constitutional imperatives surrounding healthcare access. It reiterated the needs for further development within the legal framework to balance opportunities for fair compensation to victims while ensuring that public healthcare systems remain viable and functional ([40]).

In effect, the judgment served as a reminder that while the existing legal structures must be respected, they must also evolve to meet justice’s demands as enshrined in the Constitution ([39]–[41]). The ruling has significant implications for how future medical expenses will be managed in negligence claims against public healthcare entities. While the court remained open to further development of the common law, it found no basis for the Gauteng MEC's specific request and thus reinforced the inherent authority of courts to regulate their processes in delivering justice.

This case highlights the complex interplay between the preservation of established legal principles and the necessity for adaptation to fulfill constitutional obligations in healthcare. It leaves open the prospect for legislative action to clarify and develop the law in this important area.
