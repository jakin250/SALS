Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: Mchunu v The State (825/2012) ZASCA 126

## F – Facts

The appellants, Vamile Michael Mchunu and Cecilmigida, were involved in a criminal case originating from the KwaZulu-Natal High Court (Levinsohn J) where they faced multiple charges: two counts of murder and one count of attempted robbery with aggravating circumstances, as defined in section 1(1)(b) of the *Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977* (the Act) ([1]). The crimes took place on 8 August 1997 at Sanjon Chemicals, a family-owned business, culminating in the tragic murder of the owners, Mr. and Mrs. Hayes, in the presence of their two daughters, aiming to steal weekly wages ([1]–[4]).

The trial commenced on 7 May 1999, and on 25 May 1999, both appellants were convicted alongside a co-accused, Mduduzi Mkhize. Their sentences totaled 55 years for the first appellant and 50 years for the second, with no indication of concurrency in the sentences ([1]). The High Court denied their application for leave to appeal against both conviction and sentence, leading to a petition for leave to appeal against sentence only, which was granted by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ([2]).

On 10 February 2012, the full court increased the murder sentences to life imprisonment and fixed a compulsory minimum period of 20 years' imprisonment before any consideration for parole could be granted. This decision was based on the later-enacted section 276B of the Act, which came into force on 1 October 2004, although the crimes occurred before its enactment ([3]). The appellants then sought further leave to appeal the non-parole period fixed by the full court.

## I – Issues

The primary legal issue was whether the sentencing court was correct in fixing a non-parole period of 20 years for the appellants, given that the crimes occurred before the enactment of section 276B of the *Criminal Procedure Act* ([3]). This raised questions about the retrospective application of new sentencing laws and principles regarding the increase of penalties post-offence commission ([4]).

## R – Rules

The court relied on established legal principles, specifically the common law precedent that penalties are assigned at the time of the crime, not at the time of conviction or sentencing. This principle has been emphasized in cases such as *R v Mazibuko* and *S v Mpetha*, which reinforce that a subsequent increase in penalty—such as the fixing of a non-parole period—should not apply retrospectively if it imposes an additional burden that did not exist at the time the offence occurred ([5]). The core rules of law regulating the non-retrospective application of sentencing principles are especially pertinent in this case.

## A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal applied the established principles by emphasizing that the liability for a penalty arises at the time the crime is committed. The non-parole period imposed by the full court was regarded as an increase in penalty that should not apply retrospectively due to the timing of the crimes, which occurred before the effective date of section 276B ([5]). The court noted the absence of any "special circumstances" that could justify a departure from the general rule against retroactivity. Thus, the inclusion of a non-parole period for crimes committed prior to the relevant legislative amendment was deemed incorrect, leading to the conclusion that the appeal had merit.

## C – Conclusion

The appeals were upheld, and the Supreme Court of Appeal set aside the order requiring a non-parole period of 20 years for the appellants ([7]). The ruling underscored the importance of respecting the temporal alignment between the commission of a crime and the implementation of penal provisions, safeguarding the principles of fairness and justice in the legal system. The case clarifies that new legislative provisions cannot be applied to past offences in a way that retroactively imposes harsher penalties, thus benefiting the appellants and upholding foundational legal principles within the South African criminal justice framework. 

The implication of this ruling is significant, not only in protecting the rights of individuals against retroactive sentencing but also in reinforcing the integrity of the legislative process in establishing and respecting the bounds of the criminal law.
