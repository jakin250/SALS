Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 National Union of Metal Workers of South Africa and Others v Dunlop Mixing and Technical Services (Pty) Ltd and Others (Case no 6/2020) [2020] ZASCA 161 (7 December 2020)

Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Neutral Citation: National Union of Metal Workers of South Africa and Others v Dunlop Mixing and Technical Services (Pty) Ltd and Others (Case no 6/2020) [2020] ZASCA 161 (7 December 2020)
Judges: Goosen AJA, Wallis JJA, Molemela JJA, Schippers JJA, Dlodlo JJA.

F - Facts

The case involved a dispute between the National Union of Metal Workers of South Africa (NUMSA) and three companies predominantly involved in manufacturing rubber products (Dunlop Mixing and Technical Services (Pty) Ltd, Dunlop Belting Products (Pty) Ltd, and Dunlop Industrial Hose (Pty) Ltd) regarding the application of the Regulation of Gatherings Act 205 of 1993 (the Gatherings Act) to a picket authorized under the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA).

On 4 July 2012, NUMSA referred a labour dispute to the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation, and Arbitration (CCMA) which remained unresolved, prompting the union to announce a strike action. In support of the strike, NUMSA authorized a picket on 22 August 2012 outside the employer's premises. Unfortunately, this picket escalated into violence, leading to property damage and prompting an interdict from the Labour Court against the appellants.

Following this incident, the respondents (the Dunlop companies) issued summons against NUMSA for damages relating to what was alleged as "riot damage" pursuant to s 11 of the Gatherings Act ([para 4]). NUMSA's defense argued that since the picket was authorized under s 69 of the LRA, the Gatherings Act did not apply, and any claims should be adjudicated in the Labour Court. The high court held that the picket was indeed a 'gathering' as defined under the Gatherings Act, leading to this appeal ([paras 1–6]).

I - Issues

The central issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether a picket organized by a trade union in support of a protected strike was classified as a 'gathering' that fell under the provisions of the Gatherings Act—particularly s 11, which addresses liability for riot damage ([para 1]).

The court was also to consider whether the appellants were entitled to immunity from civil claims as posited by the provisions of the LRA, particularly concerning their actions during an authorized picket ([paras 5–6]).

R - Rules

  1. Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA):
  • Section 67(2): Provides immunity from civil claims to persons participating in a protected strike or lawful conduct in furtherance thereof.
  • Section 68: Outlines the Labour Court's exclusive jurisdiction over unlawful strikes and related conduct, enabling claims for just and equitable compensation ([paras 7, 10]).
  1. Regulation of Gatherings Act 205 of 1993:
  • Section 11: Establishes liability for riot damage arising from a gathering, with a broad definition of 'gathering' including assemblies of more than 15 persons ([para 10]).
  • Definitions: The Gatherings Act defines 'gathering', 'demonstration', and 'riot damage', ensuring statutory clarity regarding gatherings ([paras 12–18]).

A - Application

The court's analysis hinged on interpreting the relationship between the LRA and the Gatherings Act. The high court's finding suggested that the provisions of both acts were reconcilable and that participation in a strike could be looked at under the lens of both legal frameworks, potentially exposing trade unions to civil liability depending on the nature of the conduct during the picket ([para 19]).

However, the Supreme Court of Appeal highlighted that the LRA provides a specialized framework catering specifically to strike actions and associated conduct. It was emphasized that the right to picket and the associated protections under the LRA specifically aim to safeguard these activities from civil liability, provided the conduct remains lawful ([paras 29–36]). The acts and outcomes of the picket should thus be evaluated exclusively under the provisions of the LRA, which specifically addresses such labor disputes ([paras 32–46]).

The court found that even if a picket could conventionally qualify as a gathering under the Gatherings Act, the LRA's explicit mechanisms and protections rendered the provisions of the Gatherings Act inapplicable to strikes and related picketing actions. Thus, liability under s 11 of the Gatherings Act could not be applied to actions taken within the parameters of a lawful picket authorized by the LRA ([paras 46]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, ruling that an authorized picket under s 69 of the LRA does not constitute a gathering to which s 11 of the Gatherings Act applies. Consequently, the liability ascribed in the Gatherings Act for riot damage did not extend to the picketing actions of NUMSA during the strike ([para 49]).

The decision clarified the legal distinction between the LRA and the Gatherings Act, reinforcing that pickets authorized under labor legislation are distinct from general gatherings, thereby providing necessary protections to union members engaging in lawful industrial action. Furthermore, the court underscored procedural delays within the judicial system, reflecting on the unnecessary four-year delay of the high court in concluding the matter ([para 47]).

The ruling serves to reinforce the nuanced understanding of labor rights under South African law, particularly elucidating the relationship between legislative frameworks concerning collective labor action and public demonstrations.
