Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# FIRAC Case Note 

**Case Title:** Ingquza Hill Local Municipality & Another v Jongintaba Mdingi (Case No 1110/2019) [2021] ZASCA 75 (10 June 2021)

**Coram:** Petse DP and Zondi and Dlodlo JJA and Ledwaba and Mabindla-Boqwana AJJA 

### F – Facts

The case at hand involves the removal of Mr. Jongintaba Mdingi from the position of mayor of Ingquza Hill Local Municipality by a resolution adopted by the municipal council on 23 January 2019. Mr. Mdingi was initially elected mayor on 3 August 2016. The resolution for his removal was ostensibly made under section 53(1) of the Local Government: Municipal Structures Act 117 of 1998 (the Act) ([1]).

The council meeting that led to Mr. Mdingi’s removal followed an earlier meeting on 14 December 2018, which addressed an investigation report prepared by the Department of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs (COGTA) regarding alleged misconduct by the municipal manager. The report had recommended the manager's precautionary suspension ([15]). During this meeting, a motion for Mr. Mdingi's removal was introduced but was not formally acted upon, and instead, it was agreed that the matter would be discussed at a later council meeting ([16]).

Subsequent to this, the council held an emergency meeting on 23 January 2019. Here, the motion to remove Mr. Mdingi was discussed without prior notice to him or to other council members, leading to a vote that resulted in his removal ([20]). The council then elected Mr. Bambezakhe Goya as the new mayor. Mr. Mdingi challenged this action in the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, arguing that the municipality failed to provide adequate notice or an opportunity for him to respond to the alleged grounds for his removal ([23]).

The High Court, presided over by Brooks J, ruled in favor of Mr. Mdingi, holding that the council's process lacked adequate adherence to procedural fairness, specifically the audi alteram partem principle ([24]). The court found that the motion for removal was not properly adopted and that there was a complete oversight regarding prior notice ([24]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal question is whether the council's resolution to remove Mr. Mdingi complied with the procedural requirements set forth in section 53(1) of the Local Government: Municipal Structures Act and whether the High Court erred in its decision to set aside the resolution and subsequent election following the alleged removal ([24]).

Several sub-issues arise from this main question:
1. Did the council provide adequate prior notice of the motion for Mr. Mdingi's removal to all council members?
2. Was the motion properly characterized as a removal from office as a member of the executive committee, in adherence to section 53(1)?
3. Did the council's failure to comply with procedural requirements frustrate Mr. Mdingi's rights to a fair hearing and due process under the law? ([19], [25], [30]).

### R – Rules

The applicable legal framework includes:
1. **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996**, specifically sections 160(1) and 160(8), which mandate that municipal councils must operate in a manner that facilitates democratic participation, especially in decision-making processes ([2]).
2. **Local Government: Municipal Structures Act 117 of 1998**: 
   - Section 53(1) allows for the removal of executive committee members by council resolution, provided that prior notice of intent to remove is given ([5]).
   - Section 48 governs the election of mayors, indicating that a mayor may only vacate office under certain conditions including removal as an executive committee member ([6]).
   - The principles outlined in **audi alteram partem**, which dictate that parties must be given a chance to be heard when decisions may affect them, are also applicable ([24]).

Judicial precedents, such as **Democratic Alliance v Matika** ([11]) and **Makume and Another v Northern Free State District Municipality** ([12]), emphasize the importance of adequate notice and procedural participation for council members in the context of council decisions, reiterating the necessity for transparency and fair conduct.

### A – Application

Upon review, the court analyzed whether the notice provided prior to the motion for Mr. Mdingi’s removal complied with the legislative requirements as stipulated in section 53(1). The court highlighted that, although Mr. Mdingi was made aware of the motion by Mr. Mtshazo during the council's emergency meeting, formal notice was not provided to him or to the rest of the council in accordance with the law ([[29]). 

The court evaluated the high court’s determination that the motion was improperly characterized. The motion had been referenced in vague terms that did not clearly identify Mr. Mdingi’s removal as a member of the executive committee, thereby violating the explicit requirements of section 53(1) concerning the necessary conditions for removal. By framing the motion within the context of Mr. Mdingi as mayor and not as an executive committee member, the respondents effectively undermined the statutory provisions meant to protect the rights of council members to participate in decisions impacting their positions ([30]).

Moreover, the court examined whether the procedural deficiencies negated the integrity of the council’s decision-making process. Key testimonies indicated that many members of the council were uninformed about the reasons for Mr. Mdingi’s removal, thereby illustrating the lack of proper engagement and debate, which contravenes the democratic principles enshrined in the Constitution ([28], [29]). 

Furthermore, the omission of proper procedural safeguards followed by the council led the court to confirm the necessity for comprehensive adherence to the legislative framework governing council conduct, rejecting arguments positing that informal notice sufficed ([29]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court's ruling to set aside Mr. Mdingi's removal, affirming that procedural fairness was essential and that the absence of proper notice resulted in a miscarriage of justice ([31]). The court’s decision reinforces the significance of statutory compliance within municipal governance and the necessity for inclusive participation in decision-making processes to uphold democratic ideals.

The implications of this ruling resonate throughout municipal law, establishing a stronger precedent for ensuring that local governance operates transparently and fairly, thereby safeguarding the rights of elected officials and constituents alike ([33]). Given the complexities of municipal dynamics, this case underscores the critical balance between statutory adherence and the execution of democratic governance.

The appeal was ultimately dismissed with costs, including those incurred by the employment of two counsel, thus setting a powerful precedent of accountability within the framework of local governance in South Africa.
