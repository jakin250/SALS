Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: FIRAC Method on Graham Robert Herbert N.O. and Others v Senqu Municipality and Others [2019] ZACC 31

### F – Facts 
The case concerns the constitutional validity of specific provisions in South African legislation that govern land tenure rights. The applicants, comprising trustees of the Teba Property Trust, sought a declaration from the Constitutional Court of South Africa to confirm a previous High Court order which deemed certain legislative exclusions unconstitutional. Initially, under the Upgrading of Land Tenure Rights Act 112 of 1991 (the Upgrading Act), the applicants argued that their longstanding rights to land—under a ‘permission to occupy’ granted on 1 September 1949—were unjustly affected by amendments limiting access to secure land tenure rights, specifically the exclusion of section 3 of the Upgrading Act in geographic applications limited to the former TBVC states ([1]–[4]).

The impetus for this litigation was the municipality's opposition to the Trust's claim to convert its permission to occupy into ownership. The High Court in the Eastern Cape determined that excluding section 3 breached the constitutional rights to equality and property as enshrined in sections 9 and 25 of the Constitution ([12]–[20]). The High Court's order confirmed that sections from the Land Affairs General Amendment Act 61 of 1998 and the Upgrading Act were inconsistent with the Constitution, necessitating confirmation from the Constitutional Court ([20]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues addressed by the Constitutional Court were:

1. **Whether the exclusion of section 3 from the applicability of the Upgrading Act contravenes the rights enshrined in the Constitution, particularly sections 9 (equality before the law) and 25 (property rights).**

2. **The validity of the legislative change under the Amendment Act that restricts the conversion of land tenure rights, prompting a constitutional challenge from the applicants regarding unequal treatment between land tenure rights holders in the former ‘homelands’ and those in the old South Africa ([15]–[20]).**

### R – Rules
The Court applied constitutional principles, focusing particularly on:

1. **Section 9 of the Constitution**, which guarantees equality before the law and equal protection and benefit of the law to everyone. The overriding question was whether the difference in treatment constituted arbitrary discrimination.

2. **Section 25 of the Constitution**, concerning property rights, which necessitates just compensation and secure land tenure for those dispossessed by discriminatory laws.

3. The precedents set in *Harksen v Lane NO* [1998] ZACC 12, establishing a two-stage test for determining unconstitutional discrimination, and *DVB Behuising v North West Government* [2000] ZACC 2, affirming the purpose of the Upgrading Act to convert insecure land rights into legally secure tenure ([29]–[35]).

### A – Application
The Court analyzed the implications of section 25A of the Upgrading Act, which excluded certain sections from its nationwide application. Section 25A narrowly imposed restrictions that perpetuated existing disparities by denying the conversion right of land tenure holders in the erstwhile TBVC states. The majority opinion stressed that the differentiation between land rights holders based on geographic location lacked a rational connection to any legitimate governmental purpose, rendering it irrational and discriminatory. 

By extending section 25A's provisions to exclude section 3 outright and thereby denying affected persons their right to convert their land tenure rights into ownership, the legislature effectively maintained a historically oppressive land tenure regime, perpetuating inequality in violation of constitutional mandates. This persistent legislative remediation was viewed as contravening the foundational principles of dignity and equality intended by the Constitution ([26]–[47]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court's order that sections 1 of the Amendment Act and 25A of the Upgrading Act be declared inconsistent with the Constitution. It mandated that the applicability of section 3 be extended throughout the Republic. Furthermore, the judgment emphasized the importance of rectifying discriminatory provisions inherited from apartheid legislation. Thus, the Court's ruling not only rectified the immediate statutory inconsistencies but also bolstered the rights of land tenure holders in formerly marginalised regions, asserting the necessity of equitable access to land rights as a fundamental constitutional right ([49]–[50]). 

The implications of this ruling are significant, signaling the need for the legislature to urgently amend existing laws to ensure constitutional compliance and redress historical injustices associated with land tenure in South Africa.
