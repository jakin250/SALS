Damons v City of Cape Town [2022] ZACC 13

The majority ruling stressed that fulfilling employer obligations towards reasonably accommodating employees with disabilities is essential to mitigate systemic disadvantages, thus promoting equality and fairness in workplaces ([144–149]).

Constitutional Court 2022 [2022] ZACC 13

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

Mr. Adam Damons, the applicant, was employed as a firefighter by the City of Cape Town, the respondent, since February 2001. Following an injury sustained during a fire drill due to the respondent's negligence, he became permanently disabled, rendering him incapable of performing strenuous physical tasks necessary for firefighting ([1–2]). Though he retained his job title and salary, a subsequent employment Policy introduced on April 1, 2009, stipulated physical fitness as an inherent requirement for promotion to senior firefighter. Damons requested a waiver of this requirement to enable his advancement, which the respondent refused. Following failed conciliation at the CCMA and subsequent arbitration, the dispute escalated through the Labour Court to the Labour Appeal Court, ultimately reaching the Constitutional Court ([3–7]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue centered on whether the respondent's refusal to waive the physical fitness requirement constituted unfair discrimination against Damons due to his disability, particularly in light of the Employment Equity Act (EEA), sections 5 and 6. The case further required assessment of the intersection between the inherent requirements of the job and reasonable accommodation for persons with disabilities ([8–10]).

R – Rules

The case relies on several legal authorities, particularly the EEA, sections 5 and 6. The EEA prohibits unfair discrimination, including on grounds of disability. Section 6(2)(b) provides a defense for inherent job requirements, asserting it is not unfair discrimination to discriminate based on an inherent job requirement that is genuinely necessary for the role ([43–46]). Furthermore, the Code of Good Practice accompanying the EEA guides employers on reasonable accommodations for disabled employees, emphasizing the importance of allowing individuals the opportunity to fulfill their job functions ([62]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court, through Majiedt J (majority), acknowledged that while physical fitness is indeed an inherent requirement of the position, the application of the requisite policy to Damons raised questions about the fairness of advancement opportunities offered to him in non-operational roles. Although the Labour Appeal Court maintained the validity of the inherent requirement, it was contended that this did not negate the necessity for reasonable accommodations to be considered ([27–30],[140–144]). The case underscored the interplay of individual rights against organizational policies, with dissenting opinions highlighting the necessity of reevaluating the respondent’s obligations considering the circumstances of the injury sustained by Damons ([100–107], [140]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court affirmed the Labour Appeal Court's decision, concluding that the refusal of the City to adapt its policies and promote Damons, consequently excluding him from advancements solely on the grounds of his disability, amounted to unfair discrimination as envisaged by section 6(1) of the EEA. The case set a notable precedent regarding the balance between inherent job requirements and the duty of employers to afford reasonable accommodations to disabled employees, emphasizing the transformative intent of the EEA and the Code of Good Practice ([109],[148]).

The majority ruling stressed that fulfilling employer obligations towards reasonably accommodating employees with disabilities is essential to mitigate systemic disadvantages, thus promoting equality and fairness in workplaces ([144–149]).
