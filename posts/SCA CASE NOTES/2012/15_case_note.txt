Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note: Democratic Alliance v The Acting National Director of Public Prosecutions (288/11)

## F – Facts
The case revolves around the decision made by the Acting National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP), Mr Mokotedi Mpshe, on April 6, 2009, to discontinue the prosecution against Jacob Zuma, then the President of South Africa, in a corruption case ([1]). The Democratic Alliance (DA), a registered political party and the official opposition, sought to review and set aside this decision on constitutional grounds, claiming the decision was inconsistent with the South African Constitution. The DA requested the record of the decision from the NDPP, which included confidential representations made by Zuma for terminating the prosecution. The NDPP refused to produce the record, citing confidentiality and the argument that a political party lacked locus standi to challenge prosecutorial decisions. Consequently, the DA approached the North Gauteng High Court for a review ([2]-[3]).

Two other appellants, Richard Young and CCII Systems (Pty Ltd), sought to intervene, claiming a direct interest due to their involvement in the arms procurement process, and alleged that they had been unfairly excluded from the tender ([5]-[6]). The accompanying high court ruling, which dismissed both the DA's review application and the intervention application, led to the appeal filed by the DA and the other two appellants ([21]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal questions before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) in this appeal were:
1. Whether the decision to discontinue prosecution was subject to review ([22]).
2. Whether the DA had the standing to bring the application for review ([12]-[13]).
3. Whether Young and CCII had the standing to intervene in the application ([19],[47]).

## R – Rules
The key legal principles and statutes relevant to this case included:
- **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: Defines administrative action and provides for reviewable grounds ([10]).
- **Section 38 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa**: Specifies who has the right to approach a court regarding perceived infringements of the Bill of Rights ([12]).
- **The doctrine of legality**: As outlined in previous case law, including the Constitutional Court's interpretations in *Affordable Medicines Trust* and *Pharmaceutical Manufacturers Association*, emphasizing that the exercise of public power must comply with constitutional norms and values ([28], [29]).

## A – Application
The SCA began its application of the law by addressing the reviewability of the NDPP's decision, noting that while prosecutorial decisions are generally excluded from review under PAJA, the NDPP conceded that the decision to discontinue prosecution could be reviewed under the doctrine of legality ([27]). This was a significant concession that allowed the court to assess whether the NDPP's exercise of power complied with constitutional standards.

Regarding the DA's standing, the SCA concluded that as a political party representing constituents, it had a genuine interest in the rule of law and the integrity of the prosecutorial process, thus fulfilling the criteria for standing under section 38 of the Constitution ([45]). The DA's argument hinged on public interest, emphasizing that all citizens have a substantive interest in ensuring that influential figures do not evade legal accountability due to their status ([44]).

Conversely, the intervention applications by Young and CCII were denied. The SCA found that neither had demonstrated a substantial private interest justifying their interference in the review application, mainly due to the lack of direct assertions of rights being affected by the prosecutorial decision ([46]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal held in favor of the DA on the issues regarding its standing to challenge the NDPP's decision to discontinue the prosecution of Zuma, declaring the decision itself reviewable ([28]). The court ordered the NDPP to provide a record of the decision, reflecting a commitment to transparency and accountability in the prosecutorial process, crucial for upholding the rule of law in South Africa ([51]). However, the appeals from Young and CCII were dismissed, affirming that mere complaints or interests in procedural fairness were insufficient without a direct and substantial interest in the decisions being reviewed.

This case elucidates the evolving jurisprudence around standing in public interest litigation and the scope of judicial review concerning prosecutorial decisions. It reinforces the notion that political parties, as representatives of the electorate, can play a pivotal role in ensuring adherence to constitutional values and the rule of law in South Africa's democratic framework.
