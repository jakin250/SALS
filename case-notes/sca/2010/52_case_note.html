Supreme Court of Appeal

Matlou v The State

The case involves Shiko Phineas Matlou and William Sekwati Serumula (the appellants), who were charged with murder, robbery with aggravating circumstances, and unlawful possession of a firearm in the North Gauteng High Court, Pretoria ([paras 2-4]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2010

F – Facts

The case involves Shiko Phineas Matlou and William Sekwati Serumula (the appellants), who were charged with murder, robbery with aggravating circumstances, and unlawful possession of a firearm in the North Gauteng High Court, Pretoria ([paras 2-4]). The charges arose after the murder of a victim who had gone missing between 13 and 15 March 2002. The deceased's body was not found until the appellants led police to the location several days later. Notable evidence during the trial included statements made by the first appellant, who claimed he acted with others in the crime, and assertions about the recovery of the deceased’s property, including a vehicle, welding machine, and an angle grinder ([paras 6-10]).

The trial judge initially convicted the first appellant of murder and robbery, sentencing him to life imprisonment and additional years for the robbery charge ([para 4]). The second appellant received similar sentences. The appellants challenged their convictions, arguing that their incriminating statements and pointings-out made during police custody were inadmissibly obtained due to alleged assault by police ([para 5]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue revolved around the admissibility of evidence related to the pointings-out of the deceased's body and the firearm made by the first appellant, given the circumstances of his arrest and alleged police coercion. Specifically, the court needed to determine whether these statements were the product of undue influence or assault, therefore violating the appellants' constitutional rights to a fair trial ([paras 15-16]). Another significant issue was whether the evidence of possession of the deceased's property was sufficient for conviction in light of the exclusion of the pointings-out evidence ([para 31]).

R – Rules

The court relied on several legal principles. First, Section 35(1) of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, Act 108 of 1996, guarantees the right to remain silent and to be free from compelled confessions ([para 20]). Moreover, Section 35(5) dictates that any evidence obtained in a manner violating rights enshrined in the Bill of Rights must be excluded if it renders the trial unfair or detrimental to the administration of justice ([para 22]).

Another statutory reference was to Section 218(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which generally allows the admission of pointings-out irrespective of the admissibility of associated confessions, leading to tension between statutory provisions and constitutional protections ([para 21]). The court also cited relevant case law, including R v Samhando and S v Sheehama, which affirm the necessity for voluntary confessions and statements free from coercion ([para 23]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the trial court had erred in admitting the pointings-out evidence. The first appellant's claims of police assault and the resultant injuries were substantiated, suggesting that his statements were not made voluntarily ([para 20]). The court emphasized that the alleged beatings were likely intended to coerce cooperation from the appellant in locating the deceased's body and firearm. Recognizing the primary purpose of the assault to extract confessions, the majority ruled that admitting such evidence would compromise the trial's fairness ([paras 30-31]).

Consequently, the court determined that, without the pointings-out, the remaining evidence, consisting of possession of the deceased's property soon after his death, permitted only a conviction for theft and not for murder or robbery ([paras 32-33]). The court noted the appellants did not provide a satisfactory explanation for their possession of the deceased’s belongings, ultimately leading to the conclusion that theft was the only reasonable conviction ([para 32]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal regarding the murder conviction and the charge of robbery with aggravating circumstances. The convictions for these counts were set aside, reflecting the court's finding that the evidence supporting them was inadmissibly tainted due to procedural violations and police misconduct. Instead, the appellants were convicted of theft regarding the deceased’s property, specifically the vehicle and other items, and were sentenced to six years, taking into account their status as first offenders and the period already served in custody ([paras 34-35]). This case underscores the delicate balance between effective law enforcement and protecting constitutional rights, highlighting the judicial system’s commitment to fairness even amidst serious allegations of crime.
