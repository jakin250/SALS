Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

### Case Note: Jazz Spirit 12 (Pty) Ltd & others v The Regional Land Claims Commissioner: Western Cape & others  
Neutral citation: Jazz Spirit 12 (Pty) Ltd & others v The Regional Land Claims Commissioner: Western Cape & others (704/2013) [2014] ZASCA 127 (22 September 2014)  

**FIRAC Case Note**

#### F - Facts  
The present case arises from a protracted and complex legal dispute concerning a land claim lodged by the second and third respondents regarding a property known as Erf 2274 Constantia (also referred to as Sillery Farm) ([para 4]). The appellants opposed this claim and, following unsuccessful mediation and negotiation attempts, the matter was referred to the Land Claims Court under the Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994 ([para 4]). After a trial that extended over 21 days, the judgment was delivered on 7 December 2012, wherein the court acknowledged that the claimants had succeeded against the State ([para 1]). However, it opted not to award costs, stating that the claimants were funded by the State, leading to the subsequent appeal against this costs order ([paras 2-3]).

An important aspect of the case involves the subsequent variation of the court’s order concerning the allocation of alternative state land to the respondents ([para 6]). This variation prompted an application from the South African Riding for the Disabled Association, which maintained an established lease on a portion of the land and sought to intervene in the appeal proceedings claiming it was adversely affected by the judgment ([para 8]). The intervening party argued that it was entitled to just and equitable compensation as they had occupied and improved the property without any compensation ([paras 8-9]).

#### I - Issues  
The appeal primarily concerns whether the costs order made by the Land Claims Court was justified and whether the intervening party had the requisite standing to seek rescission of the earlier order under Section 35(11) of the Restitution Act ([paras 4-5]).

1. **Was the costs order made by the Land Claims Court appealable?**  
2. **What constitutes ‘exceptional circumstances’ under Section 21A(3) of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959?**  
3. **Did the intervening party possess a direct and substantial interest in the subject matter of the appeal?**  

#### R - Rules  
The legal framework governing this case primarily hinges on the following statutes and precedents:

- **Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994**: This Act facilitates land restitution claims on behalf of those dispossessed of their land due to racially discriminatory laws ([para 10]).
  
- **Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959**: The relevant provisions are Section 21A(1) and (3), which address the appealability of costs orders and establish criteria for determining whether an appeal may proceed when it might have no practical effect ([para 15]).

- **Key Case Law**:  
  - *MV AIS MAMAS Seatrons Maritime v Owners, MV AIS MAMAS & another* 2002 (6) SA 150 (C): Established criteria for assessing ‘exceptional circumstances’.
  - *Oudebaaskraal (Edms) Bpk v Jansen van Vuuren* 2001 (2) SA 806 (SCA): Illustrated circumstances warranting consideration of costs in cases of public or social interest.
  
#### A - Application  
The appeal court, upon reviewing the arguments, was cognizant of the reluctance to interfere with the discretion of lower courts in matters related to costs ([para 15]). The legal representatives for the appellants posited the argument that the case presented 'exceptional circumstances' due to the drawn-out nature of the trial and the conduct of the respondents which allegedly contributed to excessive legal costs ([para 16]).

The court scrutinized whether these claims qualified as ‘exceptional circumstances’ as defined legally ([para 19]). The court concluded that the duration of the trial and the general discontent with the proceedings did not rise to the level of exceptional or unusual circumstances necessary for an appeal under Section 21A(3) ([paras 24-25]).

Regarding the intervening party's application for rescission, the court noted that, due to their lack of participation in the original proceedings and the appeal's limited focus on costs, the intervening party failed to demonstrate a direct and substantial interest in the matter ([paras 13-14]). The statutory requirement that the appeal be on an order directly affecting the intervening party was found unmet, leading to the dismissal of both the application to intervene and the rescission ([para 14]).

#### C - Conclusion  
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, holding that the costs order made by the Land Claims Court was not appealable under the circumstances, and the claims made did not meet the threshold of exceptional circumstances required by Section 21A(3) of the Supreme Court Act. Additionally, the application by the intervening party for rescission was dismissed due to lack of standing, affirming the notion that procedural compliance is fundamental in litigating land claims under social justice statutes ([paras 25, 28]).  

The judgment underscores the courts' commitment to uphold the principles of fairness and social justice, particularly in land restitution matters, aligning with the overarching objectives of the Restitution Act without imposing adverse cost orders in the absence of special circumstances ([para 26]).
