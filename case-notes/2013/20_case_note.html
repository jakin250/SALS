Constitutional Court 2013

F – Facts

The case concerns an application by Hugh Glenister for a variation of a previous costs order made by the Constitutional Court in Glenister II ([2]). The procedural history originates from challenges made by the applicant regarding the constitutional validity of the National Prosecuting Authority Amendment Act and the South African Police Service Amendment Act ([3]). In Glenister II, the applicant was successful, resulting in a costs order that included the costs of two counsel but did not specifically address the costs associated with an expert witness. The applicant's attorney contended that an oversight had occurred in regard to this omission and sought to correct it ([4]). The applicant had previously included the request for expert witness costs in his Notice of Application and Heads of Argument but neither the majority nor minority judgment referenced this issue ([5]). The matter was brought under Rule 42(1) of the Uniform Rules of Court, which allows for the variation of judgments that contain errors or omissions ([6]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions addressed in this case were:

  1. Did the court err in not including the costs of the expert witness in the original costs order?
  2. Did Glenister’s expert provide relevant assistance that would necessitate the payment of their fees as part of the costs awarded? ([6]).

R – Rules

The court cited Rule 42(1) of the Uniform Rules of Court, allowing for variations in cases where judgments are deemed to have ambiguity, a patent error, or omissions ([6]). It referenced precedents establishing that costs should generally follow the event, particularly where services rendered are deemed necessary ([5]). The court also highlighted the requirement for expert witnesses to provide “appreciable help” to the court and cited Gentiruco A.G. v Firestone S.A. (Pty.) Ltd. for distinguishing between admissible expert testimony and that which merely expresses an opinion without substantial relevance ([7]).

A – Application

The court evaluated the role of expert witnesses in legal proceedings, emphasizing that their opinions are only relevant if they adequately assist the court in understanding matters beyond its own expertise ([7]). In this case, the court determined that Glenister's expert did not provide “appreciable help” as the issues at hand were well within the competence of the court to decide ([8]). The court observed that the applicant’s expert had not been qualified and lacked specialized knowledge relevant to the constitutionality of the impugned statutes ([9]). Consequently, the court ruled that the expert’s testimony was not essential for the court to exercise its duties and determine the constitutional issues presented.

Thus, the court found that the applicant had failed to meet the requirements of Rule 42(1) because there was no legitimate basis to vary the original costs order ([9]).

C – Conclusion

The application to amend the costs order in Glenister II was ultimately refused. The court clarified that absent applicable expert testimony that genuinely aided the court, costs associated with such services would not follow the event in the same manner as those of legal counsel. The apex court concluded that there was no ambiguity or error in the original judgment that warranted a variation, reiterating the criteria necessary for a successful application under Rule 42(1) ([10]). The decision underscored the importance of ensuring that expert testimony is both relevant and necessary for judicial determinations in constitutional matters, raising the bar for what qualifies for reimbursement as part of costs orders in future cases.

The understanding of expert witness relevance and the procedural propriety following prior rulings are vital takeaways from this case.
