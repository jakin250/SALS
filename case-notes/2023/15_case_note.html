Makana People’s Centre v Minister of Health and Others [2023] ZACC 15

The High Court had earlier found that the statutory scheme for involuntary detention violated sections 10 (right to dignity), 12(1)(a) (right to freedom and security of the person), and 34 (right to a fair hearing) of the Bill of Rights.

Constitutional Court 2023 [2023] ZACC 15

F – Facts

The case before the Constitutional Court of South Africa was initiated by the Makana People’s Centre (the Applicant), a non-profit organization focused on improving the lives of marginalized South Africans. The challenge arose from provisions in the Mental Health Care Act 17 of 2002 (the Act), which governed the involuntary admission and treatment of mental health care users. The applicant sought to have sections 33 and 34 of the Act declared constitutionally invalid, asserting that they lacked necessary procedural safeguards to comply with the Constitution ([5]–[6]).

The High Court had earlier found that the statutory scheme for involuntary detention violated sections 10 (right to dignity), 12(1)(a) (right to freedom and security of the person), and 34 (right to a fair hearing) of the Bill of Rights. Consequently, the High Court declared invalid the provisions that failed to ensure automatic independent review of involuntary detentions prior to or immediately following the initial detention ([7]–[9]). The respondents included the Minister of Health and various Members of the Executive Council for Health in the provinces ([6]).

During the proceedings, evidence was introduced regarding systemic failures in mental health care, including the infamous Life Esidimeni tragedy which highlighted the consequences of poorly structured mental health laws. Makana contended that the existing legal framework failed to protect the rights of users adequately ([44]).

I – Issues

The central constitutional issues addressed in the case were:

  1. Do sections 33 and 34 of the Mental Health Care Act limit the rights of involuntary users guaranteed in sections 10, 12(1), and 34 of the Bill of Rights?
  2. If so, are the limitations justified according to section 36 of the Bill of Rights?
  3. Is Chapter IV of the Mental Health Care Act constitutionally invalid for failing to provide adequate independence for the Mental Health Review Boards?
  4. What remedies are appropriate if the declarations of invalidity and other claims are upheld? ([79]).

R – Rules

The Court considered multiple constitutional provisions and precedents in its analysis:

  1. Section 10 (Dignity): Everyone has the right to have their dignity respected and protected.
  2. Section 12(1)(a) (Freedom and Security): Prohibiting arbitrary deprivation of freedom.
  3. Section 34 (Fair Hearing): Providing the right to a fair public hearing by an independent tribunal.
  4. Section 36 (Limitation of Rights): Allowing for the limitation of rights under certain conditions.
  5. International Instruments: The UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities (CRPD) and other relevant international laws were taken into consideration to assess the constitutionality of the Act ([102]–[132]).

A – Application

In its decision, the Court emphasized that involuntary inpatient treatment constituted a deprivation of liberty under section 12(1)(a) and required adherence to fair procedural safeguards ([113]–[114]). However, it held that despite the absence of an automatic judicial review prior to admission, the Act included substantial protections that ensured a fair process was followed through assessments by qualified practitioners and subsequent reviews by appointed Mental Health Review Boards ([128]–[138]).

The Court found that the terms of the Act did not infringe on the users' rights as it administered multiple layers of review through healthcare practitioners, the Review Boards, and the High Court in the case of further detainment ([188]–[192]). It also found that the Review Boards were sufficiently independent to uphold their role in the review process ([151]–[172]). The assertions that the subsidization of Review Boards could compromise their independence was countered by the lack of evidence for systemic bias influencing their decisions ([159]–[160]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court, therefore, did not confirm the High Court's declarations of constitutional invalidity. The judgments held that the statutory scheme under sections 33 and 34 of the Mental Health Care Act, particularly regarding involuntary detention and treatment, aligned with the constitutional framework established by the Bill of Rights. Consequently, the Court reaffirmed the operational viability of the existing legislation and asserted it did not infringe upon the rights of mental health care users as articulated by Makana. The Court also established that the costs from the High Court should be borne by the respective parties, setting aside the previous costs orders ([202]–[203]).

The outcome signifies that while enhanced scrutiny of mental health laws is justified, the current legislative framework is compliant with constitutional norms, thereby indicating a support for the status quo of legal protections in South Africa regarding mental health care.
