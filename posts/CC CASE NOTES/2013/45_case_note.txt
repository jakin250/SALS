# Case Note: Minister of Mineral Resources v Sishen Iron Ore Company (PTY) Limited

## FIRAC Method

### F - Facts
This case arises from a dispute concerning the interpretation and application of the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA) in relation to the rights over iron ore and quartzite during a statutory transitional period ([1]–[1]). The parties include the Minister of Mineral Resources and various officials from the Department of Mineral Resources as applicants, and Sishen Iron Ore Company (PTY) Limited (Sishen) and ArcelorMittal South Africa Limited (AMSA) as respondents.

Prior to the MPRDA's enactment on May 1, 2004, Sishen and AMSA were joint holders of a mining right under the Minerals Act of 1991, holding undivided shares (78.6% and 21.4%, respectively) of the iron ore rights on properties near Kuruman in the Northern Cape ([18]–[22]). Following the implementation of the MPRDA, old order mining rights were meant to be converted into new rights under the MPRDA within five years; failure to do so led to the cessation of those rights ([23]–[24]).

While Sishen successfully converted its right before the cut-off date, AMSA did not apply for conversion within the designated five-year window, leading to it losing its old order mining right by midnight on April 30, 2009 ([25]–[26]). Following AMSA's loss of its right, Sishen sought a mining right concerning the previously held AMSA shares ([23]–[25]). Imperial Crown Trading 289 (PTY) Limited concurrently applied for a prospecting right on the same properties, ultimately being granted such right despite Sishen’s objections ([26]).

The North Gauteng High Court ruled in favor of Sishen, stating that Sishen had acquired the entire mining right following conversion. The matter subsequently elevated to the Supreme Court of Appeal, which held that Sishen did obtain conversion of its own old order rights but not AMSA’s ([34]–[34]). The Supreme Court of Appeal noted that AMSA’s unconverted right ceased to exist, placing Sishen as the exclusive holder of the mining right ([36]).

### I - Issues
The primary legal issues in this case hinge on several key questions ([38]–[38]):
1. **Conversion Scope**: Whether Sishen applied for and was granted conversion of both its own and AMSA’s old order mining rights.
2. **Legal Basis**: What legal basis exists for attributing AMSA’s right to Sishen upon conversion.
3. **Legal Consequences**: Whether Sishen’s conversion decision, considering the Oudekraal principle, affects third-party rights.
4. **AMSA’s Rights**: What happened to AMSA’s old order mining right after the five-year period expired without conversion.

### R - Rules
The MPRDA aims to transition from a regime that recognized private mineral rights to a system in which mineral resources are considered state property and made equitable ([10]–[10]). Central to this transition is the enforcement of transitional provisions, specifically Item 7 of Schedule II, which governs how old order rights would continue post-MPRDA enactment ([48]–[48]). 

Key components include:
- An old order mining right remains valid for no longer than five years conditional on conversion ([7(1)])).
- The Minister must convert rights when the applicant complies with specific provisions ([7(3)]).
- Rights lapse if not converted, reverting to the state if not pursued ([7(8)]).

The statutory framework mandates that shifting from common law to the regulatory framework requires the state’s custodianship to advance equitable access ([10]–[10]). Judicial precedents reinforce that, without conversion, old order rights cease to exist, preventing any claims beyond what was converted ([68]).

### A - Application
The court applied these statutes to address CAMSA's failure to convert its old order rights. Sishen’s application for conversion was properly granted before the expiration of the five-year limit, thus it fulfilled statutory requirements. Upon analysis, the court rejected claims that Sishen's conversion implicitly included AMSA’s lapsed rights. Item 7 of Schedule II clarified that conversion was only applicable to the right in possession of the applicant ([66]–[68]).

Judicial focus was centered on the interpretation that Sishen’s conversion encapsulated only its undivided share rights, reinforcing that AMSA's rights expired as they did not comply with the activate procedures to convert ([66]–[66]). The implications of Oudekraal were deemed irrelevant in recognizing the transitional authority and the lapse of AMSA’s rights paved the way for statutory reallocation to Sishen.

### C - Conclusion
In conclusion, the Constitutional Court overturned previous findings from lower courts confirming that Sishen did not acquire AMSA’s lapsed claims upon conversion. The court declared that AMSA’s right to the mineral had ceased to exist, reverting to state authority for reallocation under MPRDA provisions ([125]).

The ruling illustrates important principles of mineral rights management in South Africa, emphasizing the necessity of adherence to the regulatory framework while ensuring equitable access to resources. The implications of this decision reinforce the overarching governance objectives of the MPRDA, portraying a significant step toward transformation in the mining sector, while resolving the intricate liabilities resulting from the transitional provisions outlined in the legislation. 

The case underscores the need for diligence in the formalities concerning claims over mineral rights, with failure to comply resulting in forfeiture to the state, emphasizing the statute’s integrated approach to reparative justice post-apartheid ([123]–[123]).
