# FIRAC Case Note: Tongoane and Others v Minister for Agriculture and Land Affairs and Others

**Case Title:** Tongoane and Others v Minister for Agriculture and Land Affairs and Others  
**Court:** Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Case Number:** CCT 100/09  
**Judgment Date:** 11 May 2010  
**Citation:** [2010] ZACC 10  

---

## F – Facts
The case arose out of a constitutional challenge against the **Communal Land Rights Act (CLARA)**, enacted in 2004. The applicants—representatives of four communities—argued that CLARA undermined their security of tenure over communal land, which was fundamentally governed by indigenous law ([1]–[4]). The communities—Kalkfontein, Makuleke, Makgobistad, and Dixie—sought to protect their land interests against legislation they claimed was improperly enacted: without following the required parliamentary procedures as delineated in the Constitution, particularly sections 76 and 75, which concern how Bills should be tagged based on their potential impact on provinces ([6]–[9]).

The initiation of this challenge included both procedural and substantive elements: first, the argument that Parliament misclassified CLARA as a section 75 Bill (ordinary Bills not affecting provinces), when it should have been classified as a section 76 Bill (ordinary Bills affecting provinces) ([4]–[5]). The High Court partially agreed, declaring some provisions of CLARA invalid, yet declined to declare the entire act unconstitutional, reasoning that the legislature acted in good faith ([37]).

## I – Issues
The case presented several legal and constitutional questions ([3]–[4], [43]):
1. Did Parliament follow the correct procedure in enacting CLARA?
2. Did Parliament comply with its constitutional obligation to facilitate public involvement in the legislative process?
3. Should the order of invalidity issued by the High Court be confirmed?
4. What relief were the applicant communities entitled to?

## R – Rules
The essential legal rules derived from the Constitution include:
- **Section 25(6)**: It affirms that people whose land tenure is insecure due to past discrimination should have access to legally secure tenure or comparable redress ([2]).
- **Section 76**: Outlines the procedure for enacting Bills that affect provinces, necessitating greater involvement from provincial legislatures.
- **Section 75**: Governs the process for ordinary Bills that do not affect provinces and establishes a simpler procedure compared to section 76 ([4]).

The courts have established the "substantial measure" test for determining whether a Bill should be classified under section 76, emphasizing the importance of public participation in the legislative process ([61]–[70]).

## A – Application
The Constitutional Court found that CLARA should have been classified under **section 76** because it substantially affected indigenous law and traditional leadership—both functional areas listed in Schedule 4 of the Constitution ([97], [99]). In its analysis, the Court clarified that:
- CLARA aimed to alter existing systems of land administration deeply rooted in indigenous law, through the establishment of land administration committees and other mechanisms that would fundamentally alter community governance structures ([78]–[89]).
- The High Court's conclusion that there was no substantial breach of the **audi alteram partem** rule was misplaced, considering the procedural misclassification meant that the provinces did not have a proper opportunity to participate ([98]–[109]).

The Court emphasized that legislative failures to adhere to constitutional procedures render the legislation unconstitutional, as per the principle of legality and the need for robust public involvement in governance ([110]–[124]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court found CLARA unconstitutional in its entirety due to the failure to comply with the procedural requirements set out in section 76 of the Constitution ([111], [132]). The order from the High Court was upheld in part but modified to declare CLARA invalid outright. The ruling reinforced the necessity for legislative processes that respect and protect the rights and interests of communities effectively impacted by such laws. This case not only stresses the significance of procedural correctness but also highlights the ongoing challenges related to land rights in South Africa post-apartheid.
  
### Implications
This judgment has significant implications for future legislation affecting land tenure and communal land rights in South Africa, mandating that any new laws must not only comply with constitutional standards but actively involve affected communities in the legislative process to uphold their rights [133]–[135].
