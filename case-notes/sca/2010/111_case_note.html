Supreme Court of Appeal

The Member of the KwaZulu-Natal Executive Council for Local Government, Housing and Traditional Affairs v Amajuba District Municipality [2010] ZASCA 111

The case arises from a political conflict within the Amajuba District Municipality concerning the removal of two councillors—DB Mabuyakhulu and JCN Khumalo—from the municipality's executive committee.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2010 [2010] ZASCA 111

F – Facts

The case arises from a political conflict within the Amajuba District Municipality concerning the removal of two councillors—DB Mabuyakhulu and JCN Khumalo—from the municipality's executive committee. Following their removal, the council refused to re-elect these councillors, which prompted the then MEC for Local Government, Housing and Traditional Affairs, Mr. Mabuyakhulu (the brother of one of the removed councillors), to seek a declaratory order in the High Court. The MEC sought to compel the council to reinstate the two councillors, arguing that the council's refusal was unlawful.

The council of the Amajuba District Municipality consists of 25 councillors, primarily from the Inkatha Freedom Party (IFP) and the African National Congress (ANC), and operates under a collective executive system as mandated by the Local Government: Municipal Structures Act 117 of 1998 (the Act). Initially, the ANC, in a coalition with a minority party, had a majority. However, that minority party later allied with the IFP, altering the power dynamics.

The council convened meetings where motions to remove the ANC councillors were passed, leading to an investigation into the opposition funded by the municipal funds. The council subsequently removed the two councillors under Section 53 of the Act. The ANC attempted to fill their vacancies by nominating the same councillors, which the council refused to accept, resulting in a deadlock.

Subsequently, the MEC sought intervention from the provincial executive council, suggesting the appointment of an administrator to oversee the election of new executive committee members. This proposal was accepted, leading to a significant legal dispute regarding the MEC's authority to intervene and the council's obligations regarding representation.

I – Issues

The primary legal questions in this appeal were:

  1. Did the council have an obligation to elect the ANC’s nominated councillors to the executive committee as per Section 43 of the Act?
  2. Was the council's refusal to elect the ANC's nominations lawful under the Act and the Constitution?
  3. How should the term "elect" in the context of appointing executive committee members be interpreted under the legislation?

R – Rules

  1. Local Government: Municipal Structures Act 117 of 1998:
  • *Section 43(1)*: A municipal council must elect a necessary number of councillors for effective governance, with proportional representation stipulated under Section 43(2).
  • *Section 53(1)*: Provides the mechanism for the removal of members from the executive committee and subsequent filling of vacancies.
  1. Constitution of the Republic of South Africa:
  • *Section 152*: Outlines the objectives of local government.
  • *Section 160*: Deals with the election procedures for municipal councils and mandates that decisions are taken by a majority vote. Particularly Section 160(3)(c) specifies that all questions before a council, including elections, must be decided by a majority of votes cast.
  1. Case Law:
  • The case of Democratic Alliance & another v Masondo NO & another serves as a precedent regarding the interpretation of minority party representation in municipal governance.

A – Application

The court examined the interplay between the legislative framework established by the Act and the constitutional mandates regarding local governance. The appellant argued that the term "elect" should be broadly interpreted to mean "select," implying that the council was obliged to accept nominees from minority parties without the necessity of a majority vote against them.

However, the court emphasized that Section 160(1)(c) of the Constitution, which requires the council to "elect" its executive committee subject to national legislation (the Act), mandates that decisions concerning appointments must reflect a majority sense. The justices pointed out that a majority’s will is an essential characteristic of democratic governance, aligning with constitutional values that advocate for fair representation while also ensuring decision-making authority remains with the council.

Participants in the proceedings recognized that while it is important to uphold the rights of minority parties, the ultimate authority over executive committee appointments and elections rest with the council, which is bound to act democratically in accordance with its majority. The court stressed that granting minority parties unqualified veto power over nominations could undermine the democratic framework outlined in both the Constitution and the Act.

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the ruling of the High Court. The court clarified the interpretation of "elect" in Section 43(1) of the Act, concluding that it involves a decision made by the majority of the council, rather than a simple confirmation of nominations by minority parties.

The judgment underscored the importance of democratic processes in local governance and reaffirmed that while minority representation must be considered, it should not supersede the decisional authority of the majority elected council. This case has critical implications for the interpretation of local government laws and the functioning of municipal councils, setting a significant precedent for future disputes regarding governance and representation in South African local government.

The appeal was dismissed with costs, ensuring a clear indication of the legal principles at stake and the importance of adherence to legislative and constitutional provisions surrounding local governance.
