Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note

## Case Title: De Vasconcelos & others v Business Partners Ltd (637/2018) [2019] ZASCA 80 (31 May 2019)

### F – Facts
In 2009, Mr. Roberto Carlos De Freitos De Vasconcelos (first appellant) sought a loan on behalf of Eastprop Property Trust to finance working capital for three close corporations, using a property it owned in Wadeville, Johannesburg, as security. The property was originally bonded to Absa Bank for R25 million, later reduced to R18 million, prompting the Trust to seek additional financing ([paras 1–2]).

Business Partners Limited (respondent), specializing in financing small to medium enterprises, ultimately agreed to lend R8 million to the Trust after extensive negotiations. The loan agreement had an interest rate one percentage point above the prime lending rate, repayable in 84 installments over seven years ([para 2]).

In addition to the loan agreement, a 'royalty agreement' was signed, requiring the Trust to pay a royalty calculated at the higher of R12 896 964 or 24% of the future market value of the property, with payments due on 1 August 2019, subject to acceleration if repayment obligations were not met. The Trust fell into arrears, leading the respondent to issue summons for the outstanding loan balance and the royalty amount ([paras 3–4]).

The appellants, acting as sureties, defended against the claim citing unlawful and usurious nature of the royal payment which they argued disguised excessive interest on the loan, rendering the agreement contra bonos mores ([paras 5–6]).

### I – Issues
The principal legal questions revolve around the nature of the royalty agreement: 
1. Is the royalty payment essentially disguised interest?
2. Does the royalty agreement contravene public policy (contra bonos mores)?
3. Does the in duplum rule apply to limit the interest claimed by the respondent? ([paras 9–26]).

### R – Rules
The relevant legal frameworks entail:
1. The common law against usurious agreements, which renders a contract void if evidence demonstrates it is tainted by oppression or fraud. An agreement may be deemed unenforceable if it is excessively oppressive or extortionate ([para 12]).
2. The National Credit Act 34 of 2005 (NCA), which does not apply to loans exceeding R250,000 made to juristic persons ([para 10]).
3. In duplum rule, which precludes claims for interest on a debt exceeding double the capital amount owed ([para 26]).

### A – Application
The court analyzed the relationship between the loan and royalty agreements, concluding they were interconnected yet distinct obligations stemming from the same transaction. The appellant's claim that the royalty payment was merely additional interest was examined against the respondent's business model aimed at compensating for the risk involved in lending ([paras 11–19]).

The court stressed that the appellants needed to prove that the royalty agreement was illegitimate due to its alleged hidden nature and extortionate basis, which they failed to do. Testimony from the respondent clarified that the first appellant was aware of the full extent of obligations under both agreements, thereby negating claims of deception or breach of duty to disclose ([paras 10–25]). 

As to the in duplum rule, the court differentiated between the obligations arising from the loan and royalty agreements. It concluded that the royalty could not be combined with the loan's interest for the purposes of calculating any entitlement under the in duplum rule. Thus, the aim of the royalty to mitigate risk for the respondent was legitimate and enforceable ([paras 26–29]).

### C – Conclusion
The appeal was dismissed with costs, reaffirming that the royalty agreement's construction did not contravene public policy, and the operation of the in duplum rule was inapplicable to the case at hand. The ratio decidendi emphasizes the court's unwillingness to permit parties to escape contractual obligations under the guise of public policy when such obligations were entered into knowingly and voluntarily. The decision highlights the complexities of commercial transactions and the necessity for parties to thoroughly consider their agreements in business arrangements ([paras 29–30]). 

In conclusion, the judgment clarifies the standing of contracts resembling usury, the role of full disclosure in commercial litigation, and the boundaries of legal protections afforded to business transactions in South Africa.
