# Case Note: Public Protector v Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service and Others [2020] ZACC 28

## F - Facts

The case involves the Public Protector of South Africa (the Applicant), who sought to compel the South African Revenue Service (SARS) to disclose taxpayer information regarding former President Jacob Gedleyihlekisa Zuma (the Second Respondent). The dispute arises from allegations made in a journalist's book claiming that Zuma received payments from a security company, Royal Security CC (the Fourth Respondent), without paying taxes. In response to a complaint from Mmusi Maimane, the leader of the opposition, the Public Protector initiated an investigation and issued a subpoena to SARS for the pertinent taxpayer information.

SARS, represented by its Commissioner (the First Respondent), refused to comply, citing prohibitions against disclosing taxpayer information under the Tax Administration Act 28 of 2011. Subsequent to unsuccessful negotiations, the Commissioner sought a declaratory order from the High Court that allowed the withholding of taxpayer information under the secrecy provisions of the Tax Administration Act, which were deemed to provide just cause under section 11(3) of the Public Protector Act 23 of 1994 ([paras 1-9]).

The High Court found in favor of the Commissioner, concluding that the Public Protector's subpoena powers did not extend to confidential taxpayer information, thereby allowing SARS to withhold such information. The Public Protector sought leave to appeal, raising issues of constitutional jurisdiction related to the power of subpoena and the implications for the taxpayer’s right to privacy([paras 10-12]).

## I - Issues

The central issues before the court were:

1. **Does the Public Protector's power to subpoena take precedence over the prohibition on disclosing taxpayer information contained in the Tax Administration Act?** [para 14]
  
2. **Is the interpretation of section 69(1) of the Tax Administration Act consistent with the rights enshrined in the Constitution, particularly the right to privacy?** [para 15]

3. **What are the implications of a personal costs order against the Public Protector in her capacity within a constitutional framework?** [para 29-31].

4. **Were there any procedural irregularities and claims of mala fides that justified the High Court’s order regarding costs?** [para 34].

## R - Rules

The decision rests on key legislative frameworks and case law, including:

1. **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa** - section 182 allows the Public Protector to investigate misconduct in state affairs and empowers her to subpoena relevant information.
 
2. **Public Protector Act 23 of 1994** - section 7(4) grants the Public Protector the authority to direct individuals to provide information during investigations, while section 11(3) criminalizes non-compliance without just cause ([para 6-7]).

3. **Tax Administration Act 28 of 2011** - section 69(1) stipulates that SARS officials must maintain the secrecy of taxpayer information and can only disclose it under specific conditions, including a court order ([para 8-9]).

4. **Relevant case law** - The judgment referenced _Economic Freedom Fighters v Speaker of the National Assembly_ relating the powers of the Public Protector to investigate without being hampered by statutory limitations that infringe constitutional mandates ([paras 19-20]).

## A - Application

The court analyzed the interplay between the Public Protector's constitutional mandate and the provisions of the Tax Administration Act. It noted that the Public Protector's subpoena powers are designed to enhance her efficiency in conducting investigations, yet these powers cannot override explicit statutory prohibitions designed to protect taxpayer information ([para 24]).

The court found that the case did not engage novel constitutional principles because it did not challenge the constitutionality of section 69(1) of the Tax Administration Act; thus, it was not sufficient merely to assert that the Public Protector was entitled to the information by virtue of her constitutional mandate. The court held that the Public Protector should have pursued available legal alternatives, including seeking the taxpayer’s consent or obtaining a specific High Court order for the release of the information ([paras 18-19]).

The just cause interpretation, as articulated by the High Court, which asserts that the prohibition on disclosure promotes taxpayer privacy and encourages tax compliance, was deemed reasonable. The court found no compelling argument that justified overriding these privacy protections for the sake of the Public Protector’s investigation ([paras 12-14]).

In terms of the costs order, the court stated that the High Court’s use of certain terms indicated a punitive approach towards the Public Protector's conduct. Notably, the High Court's expectations of a "high degree of perfection" in the execution of her duties were not aligned with existing legal standards regarding public officials ([paras 35-40]).

## C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ruled as follows:

1. **Leave to appeal regarding the declarator by the High Court was denied, meaning that SARS may withhold taxpayer information based on statutory provisions.**
   
2. **The appeal against the dismissal of the Public Protector’s counter-application was also denied on the basis that it did not raise a significant constitutional issue.**
   
3. **The court granted leave to appeal on the costs order, setting aside the personal costs order against the Public Protector, emphasizing the importance of protecting her constitutional role against undue punitive measures.**

4. **The court ordered that each party bears its own costs in the appeal to mitigate the chilling effect personal costs orders could have on the performance of constitutional duties by public officials ([paras 51-52]).**

This decision illustrates the delicate balance between the investigation powers of constitutional bodies and the statutory frameworks designed to protect sensitive information, reinforcing the need for public officials to operate within legally defined boundaries to maintain the integrity of their offices and the laws that govern them.
