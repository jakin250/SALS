Constitutional Court 1996 [1996] ZACC 2

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The case of Rudolph and Another v Commissioner for Inland Revenue and Others [1996] ZACC 2 arises from the actions of the Commissioner for Inland Revenue, who granted search and seizure authorizations under section 74(3) of the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962. These authorizations were issued to Mr. CT Prinsloo, allowing officials to search the home of the first appellant, G. Rudolph, and the business premises of the second appellant, Glynn Rudolph & Co (Pty) Ltd, in October 1993. On 22 April 1994, further searches were conducted under the same authorizations, leading to the seizure of multiple documents which the officials secured in a locked store room. The appellants contended that this action was unlawful under the new constitutional framework initiated by the interim Constitution, which came into effect on 27 April 1994.

Following these searches, the appellants unsuccessfully sought interim relief from the Witwatersrand Local Division of the Supreme Court, prompting the Appellate Division of the Supreme Court to refer specific questions to the Constitutional Court regarding the validity of section 74(3) of the Act and the jurisdictional boundaries between the Appellate Division and the Constitutional Court [1]–[4].

I – Issues

The Constitutional Court was tasked with addressing several key issues, notably:

  1. Whether section 74(3) of the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962 is contrary to Chapter 3 of the interim Constitution and therefore invalid [1(2)(a)].
  2. If the Appellate Division could adjudicate common law grounds of invalidity given potential conflicts with constitutional provisions particularly section 24 [1(2)(b)].
  3. If the Appellate Division could adjudicate, what directions should be provided for the disposal of the appeal [1(2)(c)].
  4. If the common law grounds of invalidity fell within the exclusive jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court, whether such grounds were well founded [1(2)(d)].

R – Rules

The primary statute in question was section 74(3) of the Income Tax Act, which authorizes the Commissioner and designated officials to conduct searches and seize documents believed to be material for tax assessments [2]. The relevant constitutional provision is section 13 of the interim Constitution, which guarantees protection against unreasonable search and seizure [6]. Additionally, the principles derived from precedents like Du Plessis v De Klerk [1996] ZACC 4 and others were critical in understanding the retroactive applicability of constitutional rights [8]–[9].

A – Application

In assessing the legality of the actions taken under section 74(3), the Court noted that all actions associated with the search and seizure occurred before the interim Constitution came into effect. Consequently, any challenges based on violations of constitutional rights under the interim Constitution were deemed irrelevant. The seizure was completed on 22 April 1994, prior to the new constitutional framework, thus aligning with the principles established in Key v The Attorney General regarding the non-retroactive nature of constitutional protections [15].

Regarding the jurisdictional questions, the Court ruled that the Appellate Division retained competence to hear the common law grounds of invalidity because the actions in question preceded the constitutional changes. It clarified that the common law grounds did not fall under the exclusive jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court, thus affirming the Appellate Division's ability to determine the appeal [16]–[18].

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded that:

  1. The question of whether section 74(3) contravenes the interim Constitution was irrelevant as the actions inquired about transpired before its enactment.
  2. The Appellate Division indeed possessed the authority to adjudicate upon the common law validity of the authorizations in question.
  3. The Court refrained from making any orders concerning costs due to the complexities of the case and the misunderstandings surrounding the applicability of the Constitution to the events that transpired [23].

The implications of this ruling highlight the importance of the timing of events in relation to the applicability of constitutional rights and constraints. Furthermore, it delineates the jurisdictional lines between general common law and constitutional oversight, particularly within the scope of administrative actions.
