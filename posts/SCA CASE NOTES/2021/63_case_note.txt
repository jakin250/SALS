Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# Case Note: Engen Petroleum Limited v Rissik Street One Stop CC and Another (Case no 209/2020) [2021] ZASCA 63

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts

The case arises from a dispute regarding a lease agreement and subsequent eviction proceedings initiated by the appellant, Engen Petroleum Limited (Engen), against the respondents, Rissik Street One Stop CC (Rissik Street Engen) and Mr. Willem Johannes Knoesen. Engen is a licensed retailer and distributor of petroleum products, leasing premises from the property owner since 1998. The lease agreement, which was set to expire on 30 June 2018, had been periodically renewed, with the last renewal in March 2015. This lease contained provisions that permitted the dealer, Mr. Knoesen, to propose a successor to Engen for the continuation of operations, subject to Engen's approval.

As the expiration approached, the parties entered negotiations for the lease's renewal. However, they failed to reach an agreement on new terms, primarily due to Engen's demand for an upfront payment of R3 million, which Rissik Street Engen objected to as it had never been required in the past. Following this impasse, on 2 October 2017, Engen notified the respondents of its decision not to renew the lease, which the respondents contested, claiming the demand for the payment was unjust and unreasonable ([paras 1–11]).

Subsequently, on 25 July 2018, Mr. Knoesen submitted a request to the Controller of Petroleum Products for arbitration under section 12B of the Petroleum Products Act 120 of 1977, citing unfair contractual practices, particularly related to Engen's refusal to allow the sale of the business to proposed buyers ([paras 15–18]). Engen responded by initiating eviction proceedings on 14 March 2019 after Rissik Street Engen and Knoesen remained in possession of the premises despite the lease's expiry. Rissik Street Engen filed a counter-application, seeking a stay of proceedings pending the outcome of the arbitration ([paras 20–22]).

On 12 February 2020, the Limpopo Division of the High Court granted a stay of eviction proceedings and interdicting Engen from taking adverse actions against Rissik Street Engen's operations, leading to this appeal by Engen ([paras 23–24]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the court a quo correctly ordered a stay of eviction proceedings in light of the ongoing arbitration under section 12B of the Petroleum Products Act. Specifically, the court needed to determine:

1. **The competency of the stay** - Could the High Court properly grant a stay of eviction proceedings based on the arbitration referral?
2. **Rights arising from the expired lease** - Did the respondents possess any rights to occupy the premises beyond the expiry of the lease under the circumstances presented?
3. **Application of section 12B** - How does section 12B of the Petroleum Products Act interact with the respondents’ rights to continue operations post-lease expiry? ([paras 1–2, 25–28]).

### R – Rules

The court's analysis relied heavily on the provisions of the Petroleum Products Act, particularly section 12B, which allows for arbitration concerning unfair or unreasonable contractual practices between retailers and wholesalers of petroleum. The court referred to the following key provisions:

- **Section 12B(1)**: Grants authority to the Controller to refer disputes to arbitration.
- **Section 12B(4)(a)**: Empowers the arbitrator to correct unfair contractual practices through remedies ([paras 2–3, 29–34]).

The court also analyzed previous case law, including *Business Zone 1010 CC v Engen Petroleum Limited*, which established the concept that arbitration procedures, under certain circumstances, can suspend court litigation ([para 28]).

### A – Application

The Supreme Court evaluated whether the court a quo had appropriately granted a stay based on the factors of the case. It concluded that the refusal to extend the lease agreement constituted the termination of the respondents' contractual rights, and thus they did not retain a right to occupy the premises. The court emphasized that:

1. **Expired Lease**: By the time of eviction proceedings, the lease had expired, and the parties had not successfully renegotiated its terms, nullifying any rights of the respondents to remain on the premises ([paras 30–37]).
  
2. **Arbitration Scope**: Mr. Knoesen's request for arbitration did not include claims about the non-renewal of the lease as constituting an unfair practice; thus, no basis was provided for the arbitrator to determine any retained rights of occupancy ([paras 32–34]).

3. **Discretionary Powers**: The underlying purpose of section 12B is to facilitate quicker resolutions of disputes and does not provide a blanket authority for parties to remain in premises when contractual obligations lapse ([para 39]).

Overall, the court found that the interdict granted by the court a quo was misaligned with the legislative intent and provisions of the Act, thus justifying the appeal ([para 38]).

### C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal ruled in favor of Engen, overturning the lower court’s orders. It concluded that granting a stay and interdict against eviction was an incorrect exercise of judicial discretion. The court allowed Engen to proceed with the eviction of Rissik Street Engen and Mr. Knoesen, ordering their vacating of the premises within 30 days, along with the costs of the appeal. The court underscored that the decision reinforces the contractual boundaries established by lease agreements and the importance of abiding by the stipulated terms regarding renewals and sales ([paras 40–41]). 

This ruling also holds broader implications for the rights of retailers under the Petroleum Products Act, clarifying the limitations on the applicability of arbitration procedures in relation to expired contractual relationships and highlighting the importance of adhering to the established legal frameworks governing lease agreements.
