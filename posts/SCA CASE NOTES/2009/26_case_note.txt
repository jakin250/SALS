## FIRAC Case Note: Checkers Supermarket v Lindsay [2009] ZASCA 26

### F – Facts ([1–2])

On 16 September 2005, Esme Lindsay, the respondent, suffered personal injuries after slipping on an oily substance while shopping at Checkers Supermarket in Pine Town, KwaZulu-Natal. Following the incident, she initiated legal proceedings against Checkers Supermarket (the appellant) in the Pietermaritzburg High Court. The court, presided over by Van der Reyden J, resolved to first address the issue of liability, having agreed to stay the quantum of damages. This approach led to a trial focusing solely on the issue of negligence. The High Court ultimately ruled in favor of Lindsay, concluding that her fall was attributable to the appellant's negligence due to an inadequate cleaning system. The appellant appealed this ruling with the leave of the High Court.

### I – Issues ([4–6])

The central legal issue in this case was whether Checkers Supermarket had implemented a sufficient system for identifying and responding to hazardous spillages in its store, specifically in relation to the slippery conditions present at the time of the incident. More specifically, the court needed to determine:

1. Whether the appellant’s system for managing spillages was reasonably adequate and efficient, thereby meeting the duty of care owed to customers.
2. If the hazardous condition that led to the respondent's fall was reasonably preventable, which would establish a breach of the duty of care.

### R – Rules ([5–9])

The principles governing negligence within the South African context were laid out, establishing that liability arises when there is a reasonable possibility of harm to a third party and a failure to take reasonable steps to prevent such harm. The seminal case of **Kruger v Coetzee** 1966 (2) SA 428 (A) was referenced to affirm that:

1. A duty of care exists, which requires a reasonable standard of care for the safety of customers in commercial establishments.
2. The applicable standard involves not merely the avoidance of harm or spillage but necessitates prompt and effective action to mitigate risks arising from known hazards.

In terms of cleaning procedures, the court cited **Probst v Pick ‘n Pay Retailers** [1998] 2 All SA 186 (W) and **Brauns v Shoprite Checkers** 2004 (6) SA 211 (E), emphasizing that a retailer must maintain a system capable of swiftly addressing hazardous conditions, particularly in high-risk zones such as the fruit and vegetable section of a supermarket.

### A – Application ([7–9])

In its analysis, the appellate court affirmed the High Court's findings regarding the inadequacy of Checkers’ cleaning system. The evidence presented demonstrated that:

- The supermarket employed a cleaning service that was responsible for cleaning the premises only to a limited extent during operational hours, which was insufficient given the size of the store (2971.72 square meters) and the known risk factors associated with spilled items in the fruit and vegetable section.
- The court noted that although there was a procedure for reporting spillages to cleaning personnel, the system failed to ensure there were adequate staff present to respond to potential hazards promptly.
- Importantly, the High Court's conclusion that the lack of a dedicated cleaner in high-risk areas significantly contributed to the unfortunate incident was upheld. The respondent’s experience of slipping on a spillage that had remained unattended for an extended period directly supported the claim of negligence.

In light of these considerations, the court concluded that the appellant had failed to demonstrate that a reasonable system for managing hazardous conditions was in place. This finding made the specific application of the res ipsa loquitur doctrine unnecessary, as the facts clearly indicated negligence without needing to invoke this principle.

### C – Conclusion ([10])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Checkers Supermarket’s appeal, confirming that the High Court had correctly identified and adjudicated the relevant issues surrounding the appellant’s negligence. The judgment underscored the responsibility of retail establishments to maintain safe environments for patrons, particularly in areas prone to spillages. The court’s ruling emphasized the critical need for an effective cleaning system that ensures prompt attention to hazards, further establishing precedent for similar cases concerning negligence in commercial settings. The dismissal of the appeal with costs highlighted the court's endorsement of the lower court's reasoning and findings.

This case serves as a silent reminder to all businesses in the retail sector about their duty of care to customers, particularly regarding hazardous conditions in high-traffic areas. The implications of this decision stress the importance of adequate staffing and operational procedures in managing potential risks effectively.
