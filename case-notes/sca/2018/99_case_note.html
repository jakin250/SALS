Supreme Court of Appeal

Nndwambi v S (168/2018) [2018] ZASCA 99

This appeal originates from a decision rendered by the Limpopo Division of the High Court in Thohoyandou, presided over by Makgoba AJ, on 22 August 2005.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 99

F – Facts

This appeal originates from a decision rendered by the Limpopo Division of the High Court in Thohoyandou, presided over by Makgoba AJ, on 22 August 2005. The appellant, Ntsavhaveni Samuel Nndwambi, was convicted alongside three co-accused for the offenses of murder and robbery with aggravating circumstances. Notably, the first accused, who predominantly facilitated the convictions, made an extrajudicial admission that largely exculpated himself while simultaneously incriminating the other three co-accused, including the appellant ([1]).

The trial court based its decision predominantly on this sole extrajudicial statement provided by the first accused in reaching its convictions. Consequently, the appellant was sentenced to life imprisonment for murder and 20 years for robbery. His subsequent application for leave to appeal against this conviction was denied on 16 March 2007. After over ten years of incarceration, the appellant sought to reinstate his appeal, which had lapsed due to various procedural delays, predominantly attributed to systemic court delays and the illness of the State's counsel ([2]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue before the court was whether the extrajudicial admission made by the first accused was admissible against the other co-accused, particularly the appellant. The court had to determine if the reliance on such an admission compromised the constitutional right to a fair trial for the appellant. Additionally, the matter raised procedural considerations regarding the restoration of the appeal after a lapse and the appropriate standard of proof required in criminal proceedings ([3]–[4]).

R – Rules

The court referenced established legal principles concerning the admissibility of evidence in criminal trials. Specifically, it noted the implications outlined in section 3(1)(c) of the Law of Evidence Amendment Act, which permits hearsay evidence in limited circumstances. However, precedents such as S v Litako [2014] ZASCA 54, as cited in the current case, reinforced the common law principle that extrajudicial admissions by one accused cannot be utilized against co-accused individuals. The rationale behind this principle is rooted in the protection of the right to a fair trial, as outlined in the Bill of Rights, which emphasizes the necessity of proving guilt beyond a reasonable doubt ([4]).

A – Application

In applying these legal principles, the Supreme Court of Appeal acknowledged the State's concession that the extrajudicial admission relied upon by the trial court was insufficient to meet the burden of proof requisite for a criminal conviction. The admission lacked corroborative evidence, leading the court to determine that the trial court had erred in relying heavily on this evidence to convict the appellant alongside his co-accused ([3]).

Furthermore, in referencing Mulaudzi v S [2016] ZASCA 70, the court demonstrated consistent judicial reasoning wherein the absence of adequate evidence led to the stratification of judicial fairness. As such, the SCA upheld that the convictions against the appellant were legally untenable due to the sole reliance on extrajudicial statements made by another accused without supporting evidence ([5]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the convictions against the appellant for murder and robbery with aggravating circumstances were to be set aside due to the legal inadmissibility of the co-accused's extrajudicial admission. The court underscored the importance of ensuring that the integrity of the judicial process is upheld, thus reinforcing the principles of fair trial rights enshrined in the Constitution ([6]).

The court also recognized the inefficiencies in the delay of proceedings which, while not directly attributable to the appellant, highlighted the necessity for prompt judicial action in criminal matters. Consequently, the appeal was reinstated, leading to the appellant's release from custody based on findings that no guilt had been established against him beyond a reasonable doubt ([6]).

In summary, the court underscored its commitment to upholding the rule of law, the right to a fair trial, and the principles of justice by setting aside the prior convictions and declaring the appellant not guilty of the charges against him.
