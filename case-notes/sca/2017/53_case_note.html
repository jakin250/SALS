Supreme Court of Appeal

Khumalo v The State (62/17) [2017] ZASCA 53 (18 May 2017)

The appellant, Thulani Ncube Khumalo, faced charges of robbery with aggravating circumstances in the regional court of Alexandra ([1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 53 (18 May 2017)

F – Facts

The appellant, Thulani Ncube Khumalo, faced charges of robbery with aggravating circumstances in the regional court of Alexandra ([1]). During the incident on September 2, 2012, the appellant, accompanied by two accomplices, entered a restaurant in Paulshof, Rivonia, brandishing firearms and demanding money from the restaurant manager, Mr Happy Molonga. The robbers successfully obtained R5000 and an additional R3000 from the safe, then ordered patrons to lie down before fleeing the scene ([1]).

After the robbery, Mr Molonga and others pursued the robbers. The appellant ran into a cul-de-sac and was cornered by the crowd, attempting to dispose of his firearm before being apprehended by the police ([5]). Critical evidence came from Mr Molonga, Mr Nkanyese Letsoaliso (a security guard), and Constable Andile Thlame, all of whom testified against the appellant. Mr Molonga was particularly emphatic that he recognized the appellant throughout the incident, even noting the reflective vest he was wearing, which is characteristic of security personnel ([6]–[11]). The trial court found the appellant guilty of robbery on count one but acquitted him on count two due to insufficient evidence ([1]).

The initial application for leave to appeal was denied by the regional court and the South Gauteng High Court, which led to the appellant subsequently receiving special leave from the Supreme Court of Appeal to contest the refusal ([2]).

I – Issues

The principal issue raised in this appeal was whether the refusal of leave to appeal by the courts below was justified, specifically relating to whether the appellant had demonstrated “reasonable prospects of success” on appeal ([2]). This necessitated a thorough examination of the evidence presented at trial, particularly the identification and credibility of witnesses.

Furthermore, the case examined whether the sentence imposed upon the appellant—15 years of imprisonment under section 51 of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997—was appropriate given the circumstances of the crime and the appellant's background ([15]).

R – Rules

The legal framework surrounding the case primarily stems from the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, particularly section 309C, which governs appeals against convictions and sentences ([1]). Additionally, the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 contains provisions for minimum sentences for serious crimes, such as robbery ([1]).

In the landmark case of S v Malgas, the Supreme Court of Appeal emphasized that the minimum sentences outlined in the Act should ordinarily be applied unless there are substantial and compelling circumstances justifying a departure from these guidelines ([16]). Legal principles regarding the credibility and reliability of witness testimony were also pertinent, particularly in instances of identification evidence ([12]).

A – Application

In its analysis, the court scrutinized the evidence presented during the trial. The testimony of Mr Molonga was pivotal; he provided a clear account of the sequence of events and identified the appellant multiple times during both the robbery and the subsequent chase ([11]). The court found that the conditions for identification were conducive, given the clear visibility provided by streetlamps, emphasizing the improbability that Mr Molonga would falsely identify the appellant under these circumstances.

The appellant's defense hinged on denying involvement in the robbery and suggesting an alibi, claiming he was at a garage waiting for his uncle ([10]). However, the court determined that this alibi lacked credibility, given the inconsistencies in the appellant's accounts and the strong correlate of testimony from both Mr Molonga and Mr Letsoaliso ([13]).

When assessing the sentence, the court reiterated the seriousness of armed robbery and the psychological trauma inflicted on the victims. It acknowledged the trial court’s consideration of the violent nature of the crime and the community’s expectation for courts to impose substantial sentences in such serious offences ([18]).

The appellant's claims that there were substantial and compelling circumstances justifying a lesser sentence were also dismissed. The trial court had taken into account mitigating factors such as the appellant’s age and clean record but found these insufficient to warrant a sentence below the statutory minimum ([20]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that the appellant did not possess reasonable prospects of success on appeal, firmly dismissing the appeal against both conviction and sentence ([21]). The court underscored the credibility of the witnesses and the compelling nature of the evidence suggesting the appellant's guilt, which was corroborated and consistent across various testimonies.

The decision reinforces the stern stance of the judiciary against violent crimes and underscores the importance of accurately assessing the credibility of witness testimonies in criminal proceedings. The implications of this judgment extend beyond the appellant, reflecting the court's commitment to upholding public safety and the integrity of the criminal justice system in South Africa. The case illustrates the judicial system’s challenge of balancing individual rights against the need for societal protection from violent crimes.
