Occupiers of Erven 87 & 88 Berea v Christiaan Frederick De Wet N.O. [2017] ZACC 18

Constitutional Court 2017 [2017] ZACC 18

F – Facts

The case arose from eviction proceedings concerning 184 residents (the "Occupiers") of a block of flats in Berea, Johannesburg, who had purportedly consented to their eviction. On 10 September 2013, the High Court approved an eviction order against the Occupiers, initially granted on the basis of an alleged settlement agreement between the parties ([paras 1–2], [3]). The occupants had varied occupancy histories, with some residing on the property for as long as 26 years and largely consisting of low-income individuals or families ([pars 3–4]).

The land in question was owned by M L Rocchi Investments CC, which went into liquidation, leading to the first and second respondents, the liquidators, facilitating the sale of the property to a property developer, Mr. Calvin Maseko ([para 4]). The eviction proceedings commenced following a series of notices provided to the Occupiers, including a section 4(2) notice as stipulated under the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE) ([paras 6–8]).

Upon realizing the eviction was granted without proper representation or understanding, the Occupiers sought legal assistance from the Socio-Economic Rights Institute of South Africa (SERI). They then lodged an application for rescission of the eviction order on the basis of lacking informed consent, among other grounds ([para 10]). The High Court eventually dismissed these applications, leading the Occupiers to appeal at the Constitutional Court, emphasizing the need for a comprehensive review of their rights and circumstances ([para 12]).

I – Issues

The Constitutional Court was tasked with addressing several legal questions, specifically:

  1. Whether the court is absolved from its duties to consider all relevant circumstances when an eviction order is purportedly consented to by the unlawful occupier ([para 1]).
  2. Whether an eviction order can be rescinded if the consent to the eviction is deemed invalid or uninformed ([para 18]).
  3. Whether the local authority should have been joined to the eviction proceedings, considering potential homelessness ([para 18(g)]).
  4. The assessment of the concept of "valid defence" under the provisions of PIE ([para 62]).

R – Rules

The issue at hand is governed by both constitutional principles and specific statutory provisions. Notably, Section 26(3) of the South African Constitution stipulates that no person may be evicted from their home without a court order, after considering all relevant circumstances. This principle is operationalized via PIE, which mandates that any eviction must be "just and equitable" ([para 40]).

Further, Rule 42(1)(a) of the Uniform Rules of Court allows for a rescission of an order that was “erroneously sought or granted in absence of the affected party”, which ties into common law principles around just error (iustus error) and the requirements for valid consent in legal agreements ([paras 22–23]).

The ruling in Changing Tides [2012] ZASCA 116 established that courts must actively engage with eviction matters, and it is incumbent upon them to evaluate alternative accommodations for unlawful occupiers, particularly in cases where eviction might lead to homelessness ([para 44]).

A – Application

In applying these legal rules, the court found that the High Court erred in assuming consent was a sufficient basis for granting the eviction order. The absence of legal representation for the Occupiers raised concerns about whether their consent could be legally valid ([para 56]). The court elaborated that any purported consent must be informed and voluntary, thereby highlighting the lack of understanding of the Occupiers regarding their legal rights and the implications of the eviction ([para 33]).

The court emphasized that the High Court's duties under PIE extend beyond merely acknowledging consent; it must investigate the circumstances surrounding the eviction and gather pertinent information to meet the "just and equitable" standard required by law ([para 54]).

Regarding the joining of the City of Johannesburg, the court found that there was a duty for the municipality to provide temporary accommodation, thus necessitating their involvement in the proceedings to ensure that the interests of the Occupiers were taken into account ([paras 61–62]).

The court found that the High Court’s refusal to consider the absence of a defense to eviction given the circumstances constituted an error. Specifically, it failed to appreciate the extent of homelessness risk the Occupiers faced ([para 56]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court ruled in favor of the Occupiers, stating that the eviction order was rescinded based on the lack of informed consent and the procedural faults of the High Court ([para 84]). The matter was remitted to the High Court for expedited consideration, specifically including the City of Johannesburg as a respondent to address potential homelessness ([paras 84]).

This judgment reiterates the importance of judicial oversight in eviction cases, particularly regarding the rights of vulnerable populations and ensuring that proper procedures are followed to uphold constitutional guarantees against arbitrary eviction. The ruling enforces a clear precedent that eviction orders should not solely rely on consent but must encompass a thorough consideration of all relevant circumstances surrounding the eviction.
