Supreme Court of Appeal

Guardrisk Insurance Company Limited v Café Chameleon CC

[2] The case originated in the Western Cape High Court, where Café Chameleon sought a declaration of liability from Guardrisk for losses incurred since the lockdown began on 27 March 2020.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 173 (17 December 2020) Coram: CACHALIA, SAL

Neutral Citation: Guardrisk Insurance Company Limited v Café Chameleon CC (Case no 632/20) [2020] ZASCA 173 (17 December 2020)
Coram: CACHALIA, SALDULKER, MBHA JJA, LEDWABA, EKSTEEN AJJA
Heard: 23 November 2020
Delivered: 17 December 2020

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

[1] Café Chameleon CC (hereinafter referred to as "Café Chameleon") operates a restaurant in Cape Town. Following the outbreak of the Covid-19 pandemic, the restaurant experienced significant and unquantified loss of income due to a national lockdown instituted by the South African government. Café Chameleon claimed indemnity from Guardrisk Insurance Company Limited (hereinafter referred to as "Guardrisk") under an insurance policy designed to cover loss of income due to business interruption caused by notifiable diseases. Covid-19 is classified as a notifiable disease under South African regulations.

[2] The case originated in the Western Cape High Court, where Café Chameleon sought a declaration of liability from Guardrisk for losses incurred since the lockdown began on 27 March 2020. The High Court ruled in favor of Café Chameleon, allowing for relief, but Guardrisk was granted leave to appeal on specific procedural grounds.

[3] The critical dispute revolved around the interpretation of the insurance policy's infectious diseases clause, which indemnifies against losses resulting from interruptions caused by notifiable diseases occurring within a 50 km radius of the restaurant premises. Café Chameleon maintains that the lockdown measures are part of the insured peril, while Guardrisk contends that their insurance coverage should not extend to government responses that were not localized.

I – Issues

[4] The central legal questions in this case include:

  • Whether the Covid-19 pandemic is covered under the infectious diseases clause of the insurance policy.
  • Whether the government-imposed national lockdown can be considered a part of the insured peril.
  • Whether Café Chameleon has established a causal link between the occurrence of Covid-19 and the business interruption experienced due to the lockdown.

R – Rules

[5] The applicable rules for this case derive from the construction of insurance contracts, which have been clarified in several precedential cases such as Centriq Insurance Company Ltd v Oosthuizen and Ma-Afrika Hotels and Another v Santam Limited. The courts have held that:

  • Insurance contracts must be interpreted in a manner that is reasonable and fair, aligning with the contracting parties' intentions.
  • Terms must be construed liberally in favor of the insured to avoid defeating their claim without clear necessity.
  • The existence of causal links in insurance claims must be interpreted with common sense and practicality, recognizing that multiple causes may contribute to a single outcome.

A – Application

[6] The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) analyzed the facts against the insurance policy’s wording and relevant legislation. The court noted that Guardrisk accepted that Covid-19 was a notifiable disease that had indeed occurred within the stipulated radius and that therefore the conditions for coverage were met.

[7] Applying the principles of insurance interpretation, the SCA reasoned that coverage inherently involves a reasonable expectation of indemnity for losses caused by government responses to an outbreak. The analogy drawn between a public health response to an infectious disease and actions taken by firefighters to mitigate the effects of a fire was compelling, reinforcing the argument that government actions in response to a notifiable disease form part of the insured peril.

[8] The SCA found it crucial that the lockdown, although a national decree, was a necessary reaction to the rapid transmission of Covid-19, which was predominantly affecting the Cape Town area at the time. Thus, the national lockdown should be considered as sufficiently linked to the local outbreak to fall within the scope of the infectious diseases clause.

[9] On the issue of causation, the SCA endorsed the High Court's assertion that the nexus between the local outbreak of Covid-19 and the lockdown was sufficiently clear. The court emphasized the concept of multiple causation and that both the local Covid-19 infections and the government’s response were fundamental in determining business interruption.

[10] Additionally, Guardrisk's arguments—specifically regarding the localized nature of coverage, the idea that the pandemic was an unanticipated event, and the invocation of trends clauses—were systematically dismantled. The SCA rejected the idea that any such operational clauses negated liability, stating that they pertained more to the quantification of loss than to the underlying issue of liability.

C – Conclusion

[11] The SCA concluded that the governmental response to the Covid-19 pandemic, manifested in the form of the national lockdown, is encompassed within the insured peril of the infectious diseases clause of Café Chameleon’s insurance policy. Thus, Café Chameleon is entitled to indemnity for its business interruption losses.

[12] The final order by the SCA set aside specific paragraphs from the High Court ruling, affirming the core outcome that Guardrisk must indemnify Café Chameleon for the losses incurred due to the national lockdown's impact on its business. The ruling affirms important principles concerning the interpretation of insurance contracts, particularly in the context of notifiable diseases and government responses to public health crises.

[13] The implications of this decision are profound, especially in the realm of insurance law, highlighting the necessity for insurance providers to account for unpredictable pandemics and the broader impacts of governmental actions in their coverage provisions.
