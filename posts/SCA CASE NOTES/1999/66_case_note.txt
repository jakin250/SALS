# Case Note: IN DIE HOOGSTE HOF VAN APPÈL VAN SUID AFRIKA - LIBERTY LIFE ASSOCIATION OF AFRICA LIMITED v P J de WAAL NO AND J G ROUX NO

**Citation**: Liberty Life Association of Africa Limited v P J de Waal NO and J G Roux NO [1999] ZASCA 57; 1999 (4) SA 273 (A).

**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Judges**: Van Heerden AHR, Vivier, Harms, Marais, Scott AR

**Hearing Date**: 9 September 1999

**Delivered**: 21 September 1999

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
On **2 July 1991**, the appellant, Liberty Life Association of Africa Limited, issued a life insurance policy valued at R200,000 to the deceased, C J Roux, based on an application form completed by him ([1]). Following Roux's death, Liberty Life refused to pay the benefits claimed by his estate, leading the respondents, acting as the estate's executors, to initiate legal proceedings against the appellant for the payment of the policy benefits. The appellant raised several defenses, notably claiming that the deceased provided materially false information in the application, which was the basis for its refusal to pay ([1][2]).

The confusion revolved around purported misrepresentations made in the application regarding the deceased's prior insurance history and medical condition. Specifically, the deceased fraudulently answered questions about having previously obtained a life policy after providing a full medical declaration and the nature of his health status ([3][4]). Both the trial court and the Supreme Court had to consider whether the incorrect representations materially affected the risk assessment by the insurer ([5]).

### I – Issues
The central issues for determination were:
1. Did the deceased's misrepresentations in the application form exonerate the insurer from liability under the policy?
2. Did these misrepresentations materially influence the insurer's decision to undertake the risk associated with the policy in terms of **Section 63(3) of the Insurance Act 27 of 1943** ([5])?
3. Did the trial court err in its approach to determining whether the insurer successfully discharged its burden of proof regarding materiality?

### R – Rules
The relevant legal provisions include **Section 63(3) of the Insurance Act**, which states that an insurer cannot repudiate a policy based on misrepresentation unless it can prove that the misrepresentation materially affected the underwriting risk at the time of issuing the policy ([5]). Additionally, the court referred to precedents such as *President Insurance Co Ltd v Trust Bank of Africa Ltd* 1989 (1) SA 208 (A) and *Qilingele v South African Mutual Life Assurance Society* 1993 (1) SA 69 (A), which articulate the principles surrounding the proof of materiality and the questions regarding the nature of the representations.

### A – Application
The court analyzed the trial court's decision, particularly Beckley R's conclusion that Liberty Life had failed to discharge its burden of proof regarding whether the misrepresentations were material. The SCA noted that the trial court adopted an erroneous analytical framework by requiring that the insurer demonstrate that the misrepresentation significantly influenced the assessment of the risk instead of simply determining whether the correct responses would have affected a reasonable insurer’s acceptance of the risk ([6]).

The SCA emphasized that the relevant inquiry should be whether a prudent insurer, with knowledge of the true state of health and prior insurance history, might have taken a different risk acceptance decision. The court elaborated that the representations in question related to the deceased's medical history and previous insurance applications were essential for evaluating the true risks posed ([7]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that Liberty Life had indeed established that the misrepresentations significantly influenced the insurer's risk assessment and were therefore material. Consequently, the SCA set aside the trial court's judgment in favor of the respondents, dismissing their claim with costs besides the expenses incurred due to specific inclusions in the appeal ([10]). 

This decision underlines the criticality of accurate representations in insurance applications and clarifies the burden of proof on insurers regarding claims of misrepresentation, thus reinforcing the principles set out in the Insurance Act. The judgment also serves as important precedent for how courts assess the materiality of misrepresentations in insurance law, offering insights into the obligations of both insurers and insured parties in contract formation.
