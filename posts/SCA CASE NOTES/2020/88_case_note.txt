Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note: National Commissioner of Police and Another v Gun Owners of South Africa (561/2019) [2020] ZASCA 88

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–7])

The case revolves around an urgent interim interdict issued by the Gauteng Division of the High Court in Pretoria, preventing the South African Police Service (SAPS) from enforcing the Firearms Control Act 60 of 2000 (the Act). The respondent, Gun Owners of South Africa (GOSA), sought this interdict to prohibit SAPS from demanding the surrender of firearms from license-holders whose licenses had expired. GOSA, a voluntary association with about 40,000 members, aims to protect the rights of lawful firearm owners in South Africa and promotes the responsible ownership of firearms. 

GOSA filed its urgent application in July 2018, contending that an interim interdict should be granted pending a main application that would eventually challenge provisions of the Act concerning expired firearm licenses. The application was opposed by the National Commissioner of Police and the Minister of Police, who argued that GOSA sought extraordinary relief that undermined the separation of powers, and that granting the interdict would violate legislative mandates regarding firearm ownership.

The high court ultimately granted the interim interdict, which prompted the appellants to appeal this decision.

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 8–10])

The primary issues for determination were:

1. **Appealability of the Interim Interdict**: Whether the interim interdict issued by the high court was appealable despite being intended as temporary relief.
  
2. **Separation of Powers**: Whether the interdict constituted an impermissible intrusion upon executive authority, and whether it was consistent with constitutional and statutory mandates.

3. **Requisites for Interim Interdict**: Whether GOSA met the requisite criteria for the granting of an interim interdict, including the existence of a prima facie right, apprehension of irreparable harm, balance of convenience, and absence of alternative remedies.

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 11–31])

The court referenced several legal principles and precedents in assessing the appeal against the interim interdict:

1. **Separation of Powers Doctrine**: Citing the Constitutional Court's ruling in *Minister of Safety and Security v South African Hunters and Game Conservation Association* [2018] ZACC 14, the court noted that the legislative framework regarding firearm ownership is meant to be followed strictly, thereby reinforcing the necessity of maintaining the separation of powers.

2. **Requirements for an Interim Interdict**: Established in *Eriksen Motors (Welkom) Ltd v Protea Motors* [1973] 3 SA 685 (A), the requisites for an interim interdict include:
   - A prima facie right.
   - A well-grounded apprehension of irreparable harm.
   - A balance of convenience favoring the relief.
   - Absence of available alternative remedies.

3. **Legitimacy of Expectations**: The court examined the legitimacy of GOSA's legitimate expectations as explored in *South African Veterinary Council v Szymanski* [2003] 4 SA 42 (SCA), whereby expectations must be reasonable and based on clear representations made by the relevant authority.

4. **Legislative Framework of the Act**: The Act establishes a structured licensing regime requiring timely renewal of firearm licenses and prescribes obligations and consequences for non-compliance. 

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 32–55])

In analyzing the issues, the court highlighted several points:

1. **Appealability**: The court found the appealable nature of the interim order given its national implications and finality in effect. The interdict imposed significant restrictions on SAPS’s capacity to enforce firearm legislation, which demanded clarity on appeal for public safety and legislative integrity.

2. **Separation of Powers**: The court underscored that GOSA's application sought to alter statutory provisions without challenging their constitutionality. Thus, the interim interdict disrupted the functioning of legislative powers, failing to respect the legislative framework of the Act. The implications of not enforcing the licensing system could lead to unlawful possession of firearms, posing risks to the public.

3. **Requisites for the Interim Interdict**:
   - **Prima Facie Right**: GOSA’s claims of a prima facie right based on legitimate expectations were found untenable. The court noted that no reasonable expectation could justifiably be derived from the authorities’ previous acknowledgments of incapacity. The established legal framework categorically prohibited extensions of expired licenses.
   - **Irreparable Harm and Balance of Convenience**: GOSA had not substantiated claims of irreparable harm. The potential public safety risks associated with extending responsibilities on the SAPS were emphasized. The majority of firearm owners had complied with licensing requirements, which undercut claims that immediate intervention was necessary.
   - **Alternative Remedies**: The court ruled that alternatives were available. GOSA could have opted to comply with the renewal processes established under the Act, thus demonstrating a fundamental misunderstanding of legislative expectations.

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 56–58])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, concluding that the high court's decision to grant an interim interdict was not justified. The court's ratio decidendi underscored that GOSA had not satisfied the necessary criteria for obtaining such a drastic form of relief and reaffirmed the importance of maintaining the legislative framework guiding firearm ownership and control.

The implications of this judgment are significant, as it reinforces the separation of powers and the importance of strict adherence to statutory frameworks. The ruling serves to clarify the limitations of judicial interdicts in matters involving statutory authority, particularly where public safety and legislative integrity are concerned. The decision reflects a commitment to upholding lawful governance processes over individual claims that do not substantiate a clear legal right, thereby ensuring the continued efficacy of the regulatory environment pertaining to firearms in South Africa.

The ordered dismissal with costs, which included costs for two counsels, further emphasised the court's stance that GOSA's application lacked merit and that public interest considerations should prevail in adjudication of this nature.
