# Case Note: Lourens NO v Impala Water Users Association [2006] SCA 82 (RSA)

## F: Facts

This appeal arises from the actions of Impala Water Users Association, a water management institution established under Chapter 8 of the National Water Act 36 of 1998. The appellants, who are irrigation farmers and members of the association, contested an order authorising the association to restrict their water supply due to alleged non-payment of water use charges. The central facts of the case are laid out as follows:

1. On 3 June 2002, the association informed members about the imposition of water use charges for the 2002-2003 year, primarily for the maintenance of the Paris-Bivane Dam, with charges payable by December 2002 ([para 4]).
   
2. By February 2003, the association believed that several farmers were in arrears and attempted to restrict water supply under s 59(3) of the Act. After an unsuccessful spoliation application prompted by the farmers, the association resolved to limit water flow again, this time adhering to the procedural requirements imposed by the Act ([paras 5-6]).

3. The present application initiated on 3 November 2003, sought judicial sanction for the restriction of water supply. After various legal proceedings, the court a quo ruled in favour of the association, which prompted the current appeal ([paras 8-10]).

4. Key legal questions arose concerning the proper interpretation and application of ss 57 and 59 of the National Water Act in the context of determining liability for the water charges and the procedures for restricting supply ([paras 10-11]).

## I: Issues

1. The primary legal issue concerns whether the association had the proper authority under the National Water Act to impose and collect charges from the farmers and to restrict their water supply for non-payment. This includes whether the debt owed was “unquestionably due” when the procedures outlined in s 59(3) were invoked ([para 33]).
   
2. Additional issues arose regarding procedural compliance with ss 59(3) and (4) in the context of proving the debt before initiating restrictions on water supply. Specifically, whether the association satisfied the requirement that the debt be established or admitted before such restrictions were considered lawful ([paras 43-45]).

3. There was also a question concerning the interpretation of sections defining the roles and responsibilities of water management institutions and whether a water user association could be considered a relevant authority to implement water use charges ([paras 21-28]).

## R: Rules

1. The **National Water Act 36 of 1998**, particularly sections 57 and 59, governs water use charges and the authority of water management institutions to collect these charges and restrict water supply for non-payment.

2. **Section 57** outlines the power to establish water use charges within water management areas, granting authority to the “relevant water management institution.” The appellants argued that such authority was vested solely in a catchment management agency, not in a water user association ([para 16]).

3. **Section 59(3)** provides that if a water use charge is unpaid, the water supply may be restricted, provided certain procedural safeguards are met, including giving high regard to the opportunity for the debtor to make representations regarding the suspension ([para 19]).

4. Interpretations from previous cases, such as *Impala Water Users Association v Lourens NO and Others [2004]* (referred to as "Impala 1"), serve as precedents and emphasize the need for proof of the debt before invoking supply restrictions ([paras 32-38]).

## A: Application

In its judgment, the court analyzed:

1. Whether the association acted within its authority under the National Water Act. The court found that despite the appellants’ arguments, the statutory framework allowed for the association to charge for water use based on the pricing strategy outlined in the Act. The legislature’s intent was interpreted as granting local water user associations the ability to collect such charges if established correctly ([paras 30-31]).
   
2. The requirement that the association demonstrate that the charges were "unquestionably due" at the time of invoking sections 59(3) and (4) was critically discussed. The court held that the association did not prove that the debts claimed were legally owed by the farmers at the relevant times, particularly since the question of liability was concurrently being contested in other legal proceedings. This failure demonstrated non-compliance with the statutory requirements for making such restrictions ([paras 42-44]).

3. The procedural compliance by the association in notifying farmers and affording them an opportunity to contest the debts was also scrutinized. The court noted that the requisite procedural steps were not fully adhered to and found the association's reliance on these sections to be premature due to the ongoing disputes regarding the authenticity of the debt ([para 45]).

## C: Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the Impala Water Users Association failed to establish the lawful basis for restricting water supply to the farmers. The appeal was upheld, reversing the order of the court a quo and finding that the application to restrict the water supply was improperly founded. The ruling emphasized the necessity of strictly adhering to procedural and substantive requirements under the National Water Act before levying water use charges and enforcing restrictions on supply. The case reinforces the importance of clarity and compliance within statutory frameworks designed to manage essential natural resources such as water ([paras 48-49]).

*Lourens NO v Impala Water Users Association [2006] SCA 82 (RSA) serves as a precedent illustrating the legal intricacies surrounding water rights and debt enforcement under South African law.*
