Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# Case Note on *Farjas (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Agriculture and Land Affairs for RSA* (753/11) [2012] ZASCA 173

## F - Facts
The appellants, Farjas (Pty) Ltd and Rainy Days Farms (Pty) Ltd, both owned by Mr F Jasat, were owners of immovable properties situated on Farm Whispers, Pietermaritzburg. These properties were acquired for township development and were expropriated on 24 June 1991 under the Expropriation Act 63 of 1975 by the Minister of Housing. As compensation, Farjas received R260,000 and Rainy Days received R280,000. Although they were promised an additional R10,000 each as solatia, this payment was never fulfilled ([1]–[2]).

Dissatisfied with the compensation, the appellants began legal proceedings in the Natal Provincial Division but switched to lodging claims with the Regional Land Claims Commissioner under the Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994 (the Restitution Act) following its enactment. Their claims for restoration were initially rejected but later overturned by the Land Claims Court ([2]).

After abandoning their claims for restoration, the appellants sought financial compensation, stating they were under-compensated by a sum of R656,000 according to a valuation provided by expert Nicholas Maritz. While they agreed on the compensation amounts, the respondents did not accept additional recommendations regarding the calculation methods, particularly concerning lousy payment of solatia and interest for non-payment over 19 years ([3]–[4]). 

## I - Issues
The main legal issues on appeal included:

1. Whether the Land Claims Court erred in its application of the Consumer Price Index (CPI) instead of other indices for determining compensation changes due to inflation ([14]), 
2. Whether the appellants were entitled to solatia payments as they were promised ([14]), and 
3. The appropriateness of costs awarded in relation to the parties' conduct throughout the proceedings ([29]).

## R - Rules
The court's analysis invokes various statutory provisions and precedents:

- **Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994**: Governed the procedural framework under which claims for land restitution were made ([4]). 
- **Section 25(7) of the Constitution**: This stipulates the rights of those dispossessed of property due to past racially discriminatory laws or practices and entitles them to restitution or equitable redress ([4]).
- **Section 33 of the Restitution Act**: Specifies factors guiding the Land Claims Court in decisions regarding compensation: the desire for restitution, human rights considerations, economic viability, and changes over time in the value of money ([8]).
- **Judicial precedents** including *Alexkor Ltd v The Richtersveld Community* and *Goedgelegen Tropical Fruits*, which address the purpose and interpretation of the Restitution Act ([5]–[6]).

## A - Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal assessed whether the discretion exercised by the Land Claims Court regarding the application of the CPI was judicious. The Land Claims Court had reasoned that the CPI was an appropriate measure for adjusting compensation over time. This position was reinforced through evidence presented by Mr Ramballi, who attested to its prior acceptance in similar claims ([12]).

The appellants’ claim for alternative indices, including the ABSA House Price Index and compound interest, was addressed. The court emphasized that the CPI is a governmental and recognized index, appropriate for assessing monetary value changes over time, countering appellants’ assertion of it being inadequate due to their expected returns exceeding CPI ([24]). The Supreme Court ultimately agreed with the Land Claims Court’s methodology ([23]).

Regarding the claim for solatia, the court concluded that the appellants failed to substantiate their claims with requisite evidence of hardship or entitlement under the Restitution Act. Furthermore, the prior promises made under the Expropriation Act could not be directly claimed under the new legal framework ([26]).

With regards to costs, although there was some incompetence attributed to the Commission for delays, both parties shared the responsibility for those delays. The initial ruling that awarded costs on a party-and-party scale, excluding the costs incurred for producing an international report on restitution, was amended to include costs associated with this report, recognizing its necessity ([30]).

## C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, thus affirming the application of the CPI as a means of adjusting compensation under the Restitution Act. The court found that the appellants had not demonstrated entitlements to solatia or an alternate compensation calculation. The appellants were ordered to pay the costs of the appeal, while the previous costs order was modified to include additional expenses related to expert involvement for the requested report ([31]). 

This case underscores the importance of adapting compensation calculations to equitable principles while adhering to legislated frameworks, reflecting the tension between commercial interests and redress for historical injustices.
