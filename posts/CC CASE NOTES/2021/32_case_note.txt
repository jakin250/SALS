# FIRAC Case Note: Chairperson of the Council of UNISA v AfriForum NPC [2021] ZACC 32

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 25–28])

The case concerns the University of South Africa (UNISA), the largest distance-learning institution in South Africa, which adopted a new language policy in 2016 aimed at phasing out Afrikaans as a language of instruction while promoting indigenous African languages. The revision was motivated by declining demand for Afrikaans, resource allocation issues, and a commitment to equity in language policy. AfriForum NPC challenged this policy, asserting it violated section 29(2) of the South African Constitution, which provides for the right to receive education in one’s language of choice where reasonably practicable. 

AfriForum initiated proceedings in the High Court to review the language policy on grounds of procedural irregularity and unconstitutionality, particularly claiming that it did not meet the educational needs of Afrikaans-speaking students. The High Court dismissed the application, leading AfriForum to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which found in favor of AfriForum and set aside UNISA's language policy.

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 34–36])

The primary legal issues before the Constitutional Court were: 

1. Does UNISA's new language policy, which effectively limits the provision of Afrikaans as a medium of instruction, comply with the requirements set forth in section 29(2) of the Constitution? 
2. Was the process of adopting the new language policy rational and satisfying procedural fairness?
3. If the new policy did not comply with constitutional requirements, what should be the appropriate remedy, considering the practicalities of reintroducing Afrikaans as a medium of instruction?

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 46–48])

The court applied the principles set out in section 29(2) of the South African Constitution, which states that everyone has the right to receive an education in the official language(s) of their choice in public educational institutions where such education is reasonably practicable. The judgment also draws on prior case law, including the rulings in **Ermelo**, referring to the government’s obligation to provide education in a chosen language without unjustifiably limiting that right. 

The court emphasized the necessity of equity, practicability, and addressing past injustices as part of assessing reasonable practicability (as established in **University of the Free State**). The Constitutional Court also acknowledged that language policy decisions carry significant weight in the context of the country’s historical challenges and social dynamics.

## A – Application: Analysis of how the Court Applied the Law ([paras 52–66])

The court scrutinized UNISA's decision by evaluating whether it had adequately considered the required factors in section 29(2) during the formulation of its language policy. The court noted that UNISA had failed to provide sufficient evidence that it took into account the needs of Afrikaans-speaking students and the demand for Afrikaans tuition. The court also found that there was no justification for foregoing Afrikaans without establishing that maintaining it would impede efforts to promote other official languages.

In assessing whether the policy represented reasonable justification for limiting language choice, the court emphasized that past experiences of marginalization within educational settings must not be repeated. The court reiterated that when students were already benefitting from instruction in a language of their choice, the state holds a negative duty to avoid diminishing that right without justifiable reasons.

Furthermore, the court concluded that UNISA's procedural shortcomings severely undermined the legitimacy of its policy change. The absence of due regard for the statutory requirements laid down in internal regulations, including the failure to conduct a proper vote in the Senate, indicated a disregard for procedural fidelity, ultimately leading to the conclusion that UNISA's actions were unconstitutional.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 79–88])

The Constitutional Court held that UNISA's revised language policy contravened the provisions of section 29(2) of the Constitution, declaring it unconstitutional and invalid. The order issued by the SCA was maintained, mandating UNISA to reinstate Afrikaans as a medium of instruction.

The court concluded that the appeal by UNISA was to be dismissed, emphasizing that institutions of higher learning must navigate the complexities of language policy while respecting constitutional imperatives. The ruling underlines the importance of equitable access to education in one's language of choice and establishes a precedent that mandates careful consideration of language rights within educational frameworks, reflecting South Africa's commitment to rectifying the injustices of its past.

This judgment serves as a crucial reminder for public educational institutions to balance linguistic diversity and inclusivity while ensuring adherence to constitutional mandates.
