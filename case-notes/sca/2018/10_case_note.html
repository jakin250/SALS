Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 010

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case involves Tengimpilo Maqebhula (the Applicant), a lieutenant-colonel in the South African Police Service (SAPS), who was convicted on 6 February 2014 by the Port Elizabeth Regional Court of two counts of fraud, following which he was sentenced to a fine of R20,000 or two years’ imprisonment. The applicant sought leave to appeal both the conviction and the sentence, but the magistrate dismissed his application. A subsequent petition to the High Court also failed ([paras 1–2]).

The fraud charges stemmed from two unauthorized trips made using police vehicles. The first trip took place from 24-25 June 2010, ostensibly to attend court in Cradock, for which he had applied for vehicle use, claiming he was subpoenaed as a witness. However, evidence revealed that he did not conduct any official business that day; rather, he traveled extensively within and outside of the official route ([paras 3–10]). The second trip occurred on 12 February 2011, for which he failed to obtain permission altogether. The vehicle’s tracking showed deviations from official business, raising suspicions of misuse ([paras 22–23]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) were:

  1. Whether the applicant had reasonable prospects of success in appealing the dismissal of his petition for leave to appeal against the conviction and sentence.
  2. Whether the evidence presented during trial supported a conviction for fraud under the circumstances ([paras 1–2, 30–35]).

R – Rules

The court applied several key legal principles in assessing the case:

  1. The standard for granting leave to appeal requires the applicant to demonstrate reasonable prospects of success (not just a likelihood of succeeding on appeal) ([para 2]).
  2. Fraud under South African law entails a misrepresentation, made with intent to defraud, that results in damage or potential harm to another ([paras 4–5]).
  3. The application of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA) necessitates that misrepresentation or acts of non-disclosure lead to establishing fraud ([paras 34–35]).

A – Application

The SCA, in its analysis, examined the credibility of the applicant's defense and the sufficiency of the evidence against him:

  1. First Count of Fraud:

The court found that the applicant had misrepresented his official duty to travel for a subpoenaed appearance by fabricating a false subpoena, which was crucial for his application to use the police vehicle. Despite his testimony claiming misunderstandings, the core accountability rested on whether he had sought and received necessary limited permissions for his travel, which the magistrate found he had not ([paras 10–19]).

The discrepancies in the applicant's account—specifically regarding the distances traveled and the purposes of his trips—led the court to conclude that there were no reasonable prospects of another court finding his narratives credible ([paras 15, 21]).

  1. Second Count of Fraud:

The court noted that no authorization was sought for the second trip. The applicant provided contradictory explanations that were not just unconvincing but absurd in light of tracking evidence indicating a personal rather than an official journey. This count demonstrated clear intent to deceive, exposing weaknesses in his defense ([paras 25–31, 34–35]).

Overall, the SCA underscored the inconsistencies in the applicant's narrative, finding his explanations not only implausible but indicative of a calculated attempt to misappropriate police resources ([paras 27–29]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA concluded that the applicant failed to establish reasonable prospects of success for his appeal. It found that the trial court had appropriately assessed the evidence, and there was no basis for interfering with the conviction or sentence ([paras 39–40]). The court ultimately dismissed the application for leave to appeal, reinforcing the principle that upholding integrity and accountability within public service necessitates strict adherence to procedural norms ([para 40]).

This decision serves as a precedent emphasizing the judicial system's intolerance for fraud, particularly by those in positions of authority, and reiterates the significance of maintaining public trust in law enforcement agencies. The ruling effectively underscores the necessity for police personnel to observe regulations diligently regarding the use of official resources, thereby maintaining discipline and accountability within the SAPS.
