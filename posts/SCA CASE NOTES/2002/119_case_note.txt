Source Folder: 2002

# Case Note: Precismeca Limited v Melco Mining Supplies (Pty) Limited

### FIRAC Method Case Note

#### F – Facts

This appeal arose from the High Court's decision related to the jurisdiction over a licensing agreement concerning patent rights ([1]). The appellant, Precismeca Limited, had granted the respondent, Melco Mining Supplies (Pty) Limited, the right to manufacture and sell products defined in the licensing agreement as “rollers and idlers” ([2]). In exchange, Melco was obligated to pay royalties and account for the number of items produced under this agreement. The agreement explicitly referred to patents applying to the products, which had not been formally granted, nor had any trademark agreement been concluded ([4]).

The licensing agreement was made in 1975, and both parties had performed their obligations under it for over twenty years ([13]). However, it was acknowledged that the patents mentioned had expired, and no additional patents were pursued or granted ([5]). Upon being sued for the payment of royalties and for disclosing accounts on manufactured items, Melco raised a special plea claiming the High Court lacked jurisdiction, arguing that resolution of the dispute involved patent rights that could only be addressed by the Commissioner of Patents under section 18(1) of the Patents Act 57 of 1978 ([1], [5]).

The court a quo agreed with Melco, framing the dispute as one solely within the jurisdiction of the Commissioner due to its dependence on patent rights ([1], [6]). Precismeca, contesting this jurisdictional challenge, maintained that its claim stemmed from common law and that the mere mention of patents did not preclude the High Court's jurisdiction to hear the case ([8]).

#### I – Issues

The primary issue for determination in this appeal was whether the jurisdiction of the High Court to adjudicate on matters relating to a licensing agreement was precluded by section 18(1) of the Patents Act ([1]). Specifically, it necessitated a decision on whether the claim for royalties and an accounting were intrinsically tied to the validity of the patents mentioned within the licensing agreement.

Further, the court was required to assess whether the licensing agreement in question should appropriately be classified as a patent licensing agreement under the Patents Act or a common law licensing agreement ([12]).

#### R – Rules

The legal framework central to this case included:
- **Section 18(1) of the Patents Act 57 of 1978**, which restricts jurisdiction to the Commissioner of Patents for resolving matters directly relating to patents ([1]).
- **Common law principles** applicable to contractual agreements, particularly in relation to claims for payment and accounting ([8]).
  
Precedential cases cited included:
- **Buckingham v Doyle & others 1961 (3) SA 384 (T)**, in which the court ruled that actions based on partnership rights did not automatically fall under the jurisdiction of the Commissioner, provided they did not seek to resolve the ownership of a patent ([9]).
- **Helios Ltd v Letraset Graphic Art Products (Pty) Ltd 1973 (4) SA 81 (T)**, where the court affirmed its jurisdiction to grant relief without determining the validity of a patent directly as the core of the claim sought remedies based on common law principles ([10]).

These precedents collectively illustrated that common law actions could be pursued in superior courts, even when incidental issues of patent validity arose.

#### A – Application

In applying these legal principles, the court focused on interpreting the nature of the agreement between Precismeca and Melco ([13]). The court determined that the licensing agreement was not a patent licensing agreement as defined by the Patents Act, given that:
1. The patents referenced had either expired or were never granted, meaning they had no legal effect enhancing or terminating the agreement.
2. The nature of obligations under the agreement did not solely derive from the existence of patents but instead were contractual obligations enforceable under common law.

The significant scrutiny fell on clause 3.5 of the agreement, which stated that until patents were granted, the agreement would act as a license under the applied-for patents. The court concluded this clause indicated that the parties did not intend for the existence of patents to be a prerequisite to the licensing agreement's validity, thereby reinforcing its common law basis ([14]). 

The court’s analysis also highlighted the absence of a list of valid patents at the time of signing, asserting this supported the claim that no intention existed to fashion a restrictive patent licensing agreement ([13]).

#### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal found in favor of Precismeca, asserting that the High Court possessed jurisdiction to adjudicate the claims regarding royalties and accounting under the common law ([16]). The initial ruling of the High Court upholding Melco's special plea was overturned, establishing the court's jurisdictional authority to enforce the contractual rights dictated by the licensing agreement. 

The implications of this ruling indicate a notable precedent affirming that common law rights can exist and be pursued independently of patent rights under the statutory regime, thereby preserving the role of higher courts in matters involving contractual obligations and the enforcement of commercial agreements.

The court ruled that the claim did not fall within the ambit of section 18(1) of the Patents Act, thus allowing Precismeca to proceed with its claims in the High Court. This decision emphasized the distinction between contractual obligations and the substantive laws surrounding patents, potentially guiding future cases involving similar disputes. 

Overall, the outcome reinforced the judiciary's stance on maintaining accessible legal avenues for parties engaged in commercial contracts without direct dependence on the complexities of patent law. The appeal was upheld with costs awarded to Precismeca, including those of two counsel ([16]).
