**FIRAC Case Note: Road Accident Fund v C K (1024/2017) [2018] ZASCA 151**

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–4])**

On 28 November 2009, the respondent, Mr C K, sustained severe bodily injuries in a motor vehicle accident near Blue Horizon Bay, Port Elizabeth. Following the accident, Mr K instituted a claim for damages against the Road Accident Fund (the Fund), which admitted liability for 100% of the damages he would prove. Subsequently, the trial proceeded to determine the quantum of damages, specifically general damages and past and future loss of income or income-earning capacity, as well as costs.

In a trial before the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, the court awarded Mr K R700,000 for general damages and R4,562,306 for past and future loss of earnings, alongside interest and costs, including expert witness fees. The Fund appealed this decision, particularly contesting the quantum regarding loss of earnings. The appeal was initially dismissed by the Full Bench of the High Court, leading to the current appeal before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA).

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 4, 22–24])**

The principal issues before the SCA included:

1. Whether Mr K had sufficiently proven his loss of income and loss of earning capacity due to the injuries sustained in the accident.
2. Whether the approach taken regarding the quantum of the loss (calculated based on the income aspirationally expected from Mr K's competency as a diesel mechanic) was justified given his previous earnings from R-Tec Motorsport.
3. The appropriateness of the contingency deductions applied to the damages awarded, particularly in light of Mr K’s age and career prospects.

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 25–32, 41])**

The court's determination was guided by several legal principles, including:

- The necessity for concrete proof of actual loss in income before awarding damages (Rudman v Road Accident Fund 2003 (2) SA 234 (SCA) at para 11).
- The precedent established in **Herman v Shapiro** (1926 TPD 367) regarding the assessment of damages where loss is proved but its quantum cannot be precisely calculated.
- The principle that courts may use the best available evidence when assessing damages, as reaffirmed in **Esso Standards SA (Pty) Ltd v Katz** (1981 (1) SA 964 A).
- Issues surrounding contingency deductions were discussed, with reference to cases like **Bee v Road Accident Fund** (2018 (4) SA 366 SCA), where younger claimants are subject to higher contingencies due to the increased uncertainties in their career trajectories.

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 27–34, 39–56])**

The SCA analyzed Mr K’s situation, confirming that while he had sustained significant injuries that affected his earning capacity, the proof of loss was complex. Notably, the court emphasized:

- The critical nature of Mr K’s previous income from R-Tec Motorsport and the implications of failing to produce verifiable financial statements to substantiate his claim for loss of future income.
- The trial court had correctly opined that despite the absence of precise income figures, Mr K’s loss of earning capacity due to the injuries was evident. The court affirmed the trial court's approach in calculating both past and future losses based on the best available evidence.
- The contention surrounding contingencies was significant. While the Fund argued for higher deductions due to Mr K’s non-disclosure of financial information, the court maintained that the trial court’s chosen rate of 15% for future loss was reasonable based on the evidential context presented.

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 58])**

In its judgment, the SCA upheld the appeal to the extent of reducing the awarded amount for losses to R3,038,137, indicating that:

1. The court supported the necessity for Mr K to demonstrate actual income loss, yet it acknowledged that the trial court's factual findings regarding his future earnings capacity remained largely uncontested and valid.
2. The decision underscored the importance of substantial evidence in quantifying damages and the challenges inherent within such evaluations, especially roles played by expert testimonies, which require rigorous judicial scrutiny.
3. The ruling also reaffirms that in personal injury cases, even in the absence of definitive income proof, courts are duty-bound to assess damages with the available evidence without unduly penalizing claimants for gaps in evidence, as long as the overall loss is demonstrably established.

The outcome serves to clarify not only the burden of proof in loss of income claims within tort law but also addresses the nuances pertaining to the reliance on expert assessments and the judiciary's discretion in applying contingency deductions consistent with the realities of a claimant’s life circumstances.

The implications of this judgment extend to future personal injury cases, reiterating that courts must carefully evaluate the nuances of income loss claims while balancing the rights of claimants against the evidential requirements demanding precision and reliability in judicial determinations.
