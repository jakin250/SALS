Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: Netshimbupfe and Another v Mulaudzi and Others (563/17) [2018] ZASCA 98

## F – Facts
The dispute in *Netshimbupfe and Another v Mulaudzi and Others* arose from conflicting claims to the position of the senior traditional leader of the Tshimbupfe Traditional Community in Limpopo. Netshimubpfef Fundzani Ernest (the first appellant) and members of the Netshimbupfe royal family (the second appellant) were involved in a succession dispute against Mulaudzi Vhangani Carthcart (the first respondent) and the Premier of Limpopo, among others. 

The crux of the issue concerned the competing claims from two cousins after the death of the previous leader, Munyadziwa, in June 2013. Netshimbupfe Fundzani Ernest was identified for the role by a royal family meeting, while the fifth respondent was identified by the royal council. Following their respective identifications, both parties sought recognition from the Premier, who had yet to resolve the matter. The appellants initiated a review application to contest the identification of the fifth respondent. The High Court dismissed their application, concluding that the identification did not adhere to customary law due to the absence of specific family members crucial to the identification process.

The appellants subsequently appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) ([paras 1–6]).

## I – Issues
The principal legal issue was whether the appellants’ review application was premature, as the Premier, as mandated by the Limpopo Traditional Leadership and Institutions Act 6 of 2005, had not yet made a determination on the recognition status of either claimant ([paras 3–4]). Another significant aspect was whether the High Court and full court erred in failing to declare the identification of the fifth respondent unlawful and invalid, considering that both courts acknowledged a lack of compliance with customary law; specifically, whether principles of customary law were applied correctly in deciding the case and what that would imply for the respondents' actions under section 12(2) of the Limpopo Act ([paras 12–13], [31]).

## R – Rules
The legal framework governing this dispute includes the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996, particularly sections 211 and 212, which recognize traditional leadership based on customary law ([para 7]). The Limpopo Traditional Leadership and Institutions Act 6 of 2005 provides the structure for traditional leadership identification and recognition, particularly section 12, which outlines both the identification process by the royal family and the Premier's role in recognizing traditional leaders ([paras 8–9]).

The relevant statutory provisions entail:
- **Section 12** of the Limpopo Act, which stipulates that identification by the royal family must precede the recognition by the Premier. If any allegations arise that the identification didn't comply with custom, the Premier has the discretion to refer the matter for further recommendations or consideration ([paras 8–9]).

The precedential case *Allpay Consolidated Investment Holdings (Pty) Ltd & others v Chief Executive Officer, South African Social Security Agency & others* was cited to emphasize the necessity for courts to declare decisions invalid when they are found to be unlawful ([para 13]).

## A – Application
In assessing the application of law to the facts, the SCA underscored the importance of adhering to statutory procedures for resolving customary law disputes. It asserted that since both the appellants and fifth respondent had approached the Premier for recognition, the issue of identification had not reached a final resolution; hence, their review was premature. The court cautioned against encroaching upon the Premier’s authority in the executive function as entrenched under the Limpopo Act ([paras 23–24]).

The court also examined prior decisions, including *Emmanuel Segwagwa Mamogale v Premier, Northwest Province and others*, which clarified the authority and restrictions concerning royal family disputes in traditional leadership matters. The necessity for customary law as applicable in the identification process was discussed, highlighting that statutory mediation was a prerequisite before seeking judicial intervention ([paras 19, 20]).

The SCA found that the absence of key family members during the identification meetings raised significant questions about the validity of the succession claims, but ultimately, these issues were best settled internally or through the statutory mechanisms before being subject to court review ([para 21]).

## C – Conclusion
The SCA dismissed the appeal, agreeing with the full court that the review application was indeed premature. The court determined that the appellants should await the Premier's decision, emphasizing the need for adherence to customary processes and reinforcing the principle of separation of powers. This judgment implies that customary law disputes must prioritize internal resolution methods, and courts are to refrain from interference until statutory remedies are exhausted ([paras 25–26]). Thus, the appeal was dismissed with costs, maintaining that the dispute be referred back to the Premier for resolution in accordance with the Limpopo Act ([para 26]).
