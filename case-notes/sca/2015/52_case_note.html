Van Willing & Another v The State (109/2014) [2015] ZASCA 52

This case arises from a tragic incident on 27 June 2012, where Lance Harrison (the deceased) was shot and subsequently died from his injuries shortly thereafter ([1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 [2015] ZASCA 52

Facts

This case arises from a tragic incident on 27 June 2012, where Lance Harrison (the deceased) was shot and subsequently died from his injuries shortly thereafter ([1]). Antonio Van Willing (first appellant) and Fareez Mohamed (second appellant) were charged with murder, as well as unlawful possession of a firearm and ammunition under sections 3 and 90 of the Firearms Control Act 60 of 2000, respectively. Both appellants were found guilty of murder, receiving life sentences and additional sentences for firearm-related convictions against the second appellant. They were granted leave to appeal the conviction and sentence by the court below ([1]).

The convictions rested significantly on the testimony of a key identifying witness, Ms. Erica Petersen, who testified about the events leading to the shooting. She placed both appellants at the crime scene, linking them to the deceased just before the shooting occurred. Moreover, statements made by the deceased to Ms. Petersen and Constable Ncedo Ndyamara, identifying the appellants, played a crucial role in the prosecution's case ([2]).

The critical evidence was challenged by the appellants on several fronts: the reliability of the single identifying witness, the admissibility of the deceased's hearsay statements, and the efficacy of their alibi versus the prosecution's assertions ([3]).

Issues

The appeal raised several key legal issues, centered around the following:

  1. Was the evidence of the identifying witness, Ms. Petersen, credible and reliable? ([3])
  2. Should the hearsay evidence given by the deceased to both Ms. Petersen and Const. Ndyamara be deemed admissible? ([3])
  3. Did the state prove that Antonio Van Willing made common cause with the shooter? ([3])
  4. Did the trial court err in rejecting the appellants' alibis? ([3])

Rules

The court utilized various legal principles and precedents to address the issues:

  1. Credibility and Reliability of Witnesses: The assessment of witness credibility, particularly for single-identifying witnesses, is governed by cautionary principles as established in S v Sauls and reaffirmed in later decisions ([19]).
  2. Hearsay Evidence: The rules governing hearsay evidence are encapsulated in section 3 of the Law of Evidence Amendment Act 45 of 1988. Hearsay is generally inadmissible unless all parties consent or it fits the interests of justice through specific considerations outlined in the statute ([22]-[23]).
  3. Common Purpose Doctrine: The prerequisites for establishing a common purpose, as articulated in S v Mgedezi, require proof of presence, intent to participate, and a manifestation of that intention through acts associating oneself with the conduct of others ([36]).
  4. Failure to Testify: Silence from the accused party, while not indicative of guilt, allows the court to draw conclusions based on the prosecution's evidence if unchallenged, as discussed in S v Boesak ([39]).

Application

In reviewing the evidence presented, the court concluded that Ms. Petersen's testimony, while subject to scrutiny as she was an identifying witness, was sufficient to establish a credible account of events. She identified both appellants and described their actions leading up to the shooting in detail, asserting that they called for the deceased and engaged him prior to the shots being fired. The court noted that her evidence was corroborated by Const. Ndyamara’s account of the deceased's subsequent identification of the shooters ([18]-[20]).

Regarding the hearsay evidence from the deceased, the court found that the admission of such statements met the criteria under section 3(1)(c) of the Law of Evidence Amendment Act, notably as they corroborated the identifying witness and were admitted in the interests of justice. Despite the deceased's own identification being challenged on hearsay grounds, it played a significant role in corroborating the eyewitness testimony that identified both appellants as being present ([22]-[34]).

The common purpose was effectively established; the first appellant's presence at the scene, his immediate involvement in calling the deceased out, and his failure to act during or after the shooting indicated a shared intent with the actual shooter. The appellants' narrative of their alibi was undermined by their decision not to testify, thereby leaving the prosecution's case intact and uncontested ([36]-[39]).

Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the convictions and sentences of both appellants, dismissing the appeal on all grounds. The ruling reaffirmed the trial court's findings on witness credibility, the admissibility of hearsay, and the establishment of common purpose between the appellants. The court emphasized that the factual findings of the trial court deserved deference in the absence of significant misdirection ([47]). Consequently, the appeal was denied, establishing a clear precedent regarding the implications of hearsay evidence and the criticality of corroborative witness accounts in murder convictions. The decision also underscored the legal tenets associated with the evaluation of evidence and the obligations of defendants in presenting their case.
