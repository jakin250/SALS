Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

# Case Note: Nokeng Tsa Taemane Local Municipality v Dinokeng Property Owners Association (518/09) [2010] ZASCA 128

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

The case of **Nokeng Tsa Taemane Local Municipality v Dinokeng Property Owners Association** addresses the authority of a local municipality to impose property rates. The appeal revolves around the municipality's operating and capital budgets for the financial years 2003/2004 and 2004/2005, which included assessment rates dedicated to properties within its jurisdiction. The municipality’s actions were evaluated against the **Local Government Transition Act 209 of 1993 (LGTA)** and relevant constitutional provisions ([1]).

The appellant, Nokeng Tsa Taemane Local Municipality, was established according to the **Municipal Structures Act 117 of 1998**, serving various communities within its boundaries, which included urban, rural, and traditionally Black townships ([2]). The first respondent, the Dinokeng Property Owners Association, was formed in 2005 and represented various landowners within the municipality ([3]). Despite opposition from the association regarding the tariffs’ implementation, the municipality proceeded to implement the rates ([4]).

The association sought legal intervention 29 months after the 2003/2004 tariffs were assessed and 17 months post the 2004/2005 tariffs, claiming that the types of notifications adhered to did not comply with the necessary legal frameworks ([4]). Initially, the North Gauteng High Court ruled in favor of the association, declaring the tariffs null and void ([5]).

### I – Issues

Critical to this case were several legal questions:

1. Did the Nokeng Tsa Taemane Local Municipality have the authority to impose the assessment rates as per the stipulations in the LGTA?
2. Did the procedures followed by the municipality comply with the requirements of s 10G(7) of the LGTA regarding public notification and the consideration of objections to the tariff resolutions? 
3. Should the delay in challenging the approval of the tariffs affect the validity of the rates imposed by the municipality? 
4. To what extent is the court’s intervention justified given the obligations defined by the Constitution concerning local governance and the economic implications of any declaration of invalidity? ([6]-[8]).

### R – Rules

The court's analysis was grounded in several legal principles derived from the Constitution, including:

- **Section 229(1)(a)**, which authorizes municipalities to impose rates on property and surcharges on service fees. The power to levy rates cannot be delegated to other entities as per **Section 160(2)** ([6]).
- **Section 229(2)(a)** mandates that this power must not materially and unreasonably prejudice national economic policies ([8]).
- The LGTA’s provisions, particularly **s 10G(7)**, outline the required process for approving property rates, which includes public notification and the duty to consider objections lodged by the public ([11],[12],[13]).
- The obligation for municipalities under **s 152(1)** of the Constitution to provide services effectively and efficiently accords them a constitutional mandate that must be respected in judicial reviews of their conduct ([15]).

### A – Application

In analyzing the municipality’s compliance, the court found significant procedural adherence to the statutory requirements. The notice issued by the municipality on 28 May 2003 was deemed to satisfy the statutory requirements for public notification, including clarity on the resolutions’ general purport ([18],[22]). The court concluded that the phrase "surrounding properties" did not render the notice void due to vagueness, as understanding could be attained from the context of the municipality’s jurisdiction ([17]).

Regarding the objections to the property rates, the association claimed that the municipality failed to consider objections submitted, thus violating **s 10G(7)(d)** of the LGTA. The High Court initially found fault with the municipality for “noting” objections without adequate consideration; however, on appeal, the Supreme Court reviewed whether objections were lodged within the requisite 14-day period and determined that many of the submissions did not equate to formal objections as stipulated in the statute ([29],[30]).

Moreover, issues surrounding the timing of objections elicited significant analysis. The Supreme Court concluded that the late emergence of some arguments during litigation reflected poorly on the association’s procedural adherence, indicating challenges with timeliness and relevance ([31]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal from the North Gauteng High Court, overturning the earlier declaration of the tariffs as null and void. The Court recognized the procedural compliance of the municipality in applying the rates and acknowledged that the delays by the association in raising objections undermined its claims ([33]).

This decision reinforces the autonomy of municipalities to govern local affairs and provides guidance on the necessity of prompt legal action to challenge local government decisions. Furthermore, the ruling implies that delays in raising objections may diminish the validity of subsequent claims regarding procedural impropriety, fostering a culture of timely response from stakeholders within municipal jurisdictions. The order was made with costs, emphasizing the court's stance against frivolous claims that yield unnecessary protraction in municipal law matters ([33]).

In summary, this case exemplifies the dynamic interactions between local governance, statutory compliance, and the responsibilities of public stakeholders in South Africa.
