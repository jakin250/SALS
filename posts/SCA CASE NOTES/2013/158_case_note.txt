# Case Note: Orange Brand Services Ltd v Account Works Software (Pty) Ltd [2013] ZASCA 158

### FIRAC Method

---

## F – Facts

[1] **Background**: The respondent, Account Works Software (Pty) Ltd, submitted an application to the Registrar of Trade Marks for the registration of the mark **ORANGEWORKS** in Class 9, designated for ‘computer software and software programmes relating to accounting,’ while disclaiming exclusive use of the word or colour "orange" apart from the mark itself. 

[2] **Procedural History**: Prior to this, **Orange Personal Communications Services Ltd**, a UK-based entity part of the wider Orange Group, had sought registration for the mark **ORANGE** in the same class of goods. This application encompassed a wide range of goods related mainly to telecommunications, including ‘computer programmes’ and ‘computer software’. The opposition to Account Works' application was initiated by Orange Personal Communications, but the Registrar dismissed this opposition. Following this, Orange Brand Services (the successor in interest) appealed to the North Gauteng High Court, but the appeal was also denied—prompting the current appeal.

[3] **Statutory Framework**: The relevant legal provision is found in Section 10(15) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, which prohibits registration of a mark likely to cause confusion or deception with an earlier mark concerning goods or services that are similar.

[4] **The Controversy**: The core issue the court was tasked with determining was whether the registration of **ORANGEWORKS** would likely deceive or cause confusion regarding its association with **ORANGE**, specifically concerning computer software and accounting applications.

---

## I – Issues

[4] The primary legal issue is whether the use of the mark **ORANGEWORKS**, in connection with software goods that overlap with the goods covered by the **ORANGE** registration, would create a likelihood of confusion among consumers—considering the different classes of business they represent.

---

## R – Rules

[3] **Legal Provisions**: Section 10(15) of the Trade Marks Act articulates the conditions under which registration may be refused, particularly focused on the likelihood of confusion arising from similar marks in relation to the same or closely related goods.

[6] **Judicial Precedents**: The court referenced several landmark cases:
  - **Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd**: In opposition proceedings, the relevant question is how both marks may be used if registered ([7]).
  - **Cowbell AG v ICS Holdings Ltd**: Provided a comprehensive framework for assessing potential confusion, asserting that a “value judgment” guides the court’s analysis, focusing on the overall impression created by the marks involved ([9]).

[10] Importantly, the assessment of confusion is fact-dependent, which limits the applicability of precedent unless they establish clear principles. Per **Kerly’s Law of Trade Marks and Trade Names**, the variations of case specifics render each judgment contextually unique but not beyond general principles ([10]).

---

## A – Application

[5] **Evaluation of Marks**: The court critiqued the lower courts for overly emphasizing the different industries the parties operated within. The error lie in reasoning that because **ORANGE** was tied to telecommunications, it would not logically lead to confusion with **ORANGEWORKS** positioned in accounting ([6]). This approach failed to address how the marks would function if both were registered, which is the foundational inquiry in opposition proceedings.

[7] **Assessment Methodology**: The court employed a holistic view in its comparative judgement of the marks, emphasizing that the public's visual, aural, and conceptual implications of **ORANGEWORKS** could indeed lead to initial confusion. Critically, the dominant perceptual element was the word **ORANGE**, which was argued to overshadow the suffix **WORKS** ([15]).

[12] Concerning public perception, while the respondent attempted to argue that the public may discern the marks with additional identifiers due to experiences and usage patterns, the court maintained that such distinctions were moot when analyzing initial reactions to the marks in the marketplace ([12]).

[13] **Consumer Acumen**: The court acknowledged the sophisticated nature of software purchasers, asserting that confusion, even if momentary, could render a mark unfit for registration. The standard for determining confusion is not confined to lasting impressions; transient confusion suffices ([13]).

[14] Ultimately, the court concluded that the substantial characteristic of the "ORANGE" mark, devoid of explicit associations with accounting software, remained distinctive and likely to mislead consumers regarding the origin of goods when viewed alongside **ORANGEWORKS** ([16]).

---

## C – Conclusion

[17] The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, reversing the decisions of both the Registrar and the North Gauteng High Court. The court determined that **ORANGEWORKS** was likely to cause confusion with the established mark **ORANGE**, particularly in common consumer perceptions—even considering the varied contexts of usage in different sectors.

The court's holding underscores the emphasis on protecting registered trade marks from confusingly similar marks, reflecting broader principles of consumer protection and market fairness within trademarks law. The implications of this judgement elucidate the stringent standard necessary to satisfy the regulatory requirements for registration under South African trade mark law, spotlighting the overall consumer experience as paramount in assessing potential confusion.
