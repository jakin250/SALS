Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: FIRAC – De Almeida v State (728/2018) [2019] ZASCA 84

## F – Facts

The case arises from an appeal against the decision of the Gauteng Local Division, Johannesburg, which denied leave to appeal a sentence of 8 years’ imprisonment imposed on Daniël da Silva Marques de Almeida for attempted murder ([1]). Initially, the appellant was tried in the regional court and convicted despite pleading not guilty ([1]). Following his conviction, Almeida sought leave to appeal the sentence; however, the regional court and subsequently the high court rejected his application ([1]). The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) granted special leave to appeal to evaluate the reasons for denial of leave by the high court ([1]).

At the time of sentencing, Almeida was 55 years old, divorced, and a primary caregiver of his six-year-old son, who was also left motherless ([6]). He had no previous convictions for similar offences ([6]). The incident that led to the charge occurred in a pub, where he fired at an individual who had assaulted him earlier, thereby endangering the lives of the other patrons ([6]). The regional court's decision did not account for the recommendations made in three pre-sentence reports, which suggested alternatives to direct imprisonment, such as correctional supervision ([7]). 

Counsel for the appellant argued that the severity of the sentence was disproportionate to the circumstances surrounding the offence. The state, while offering a defence of the seriousness of attempted murder, recognized a misdirection in the regional court’s disregard for the pre-sentence reports ([8]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issue presented to the SCA was whether the high court erred in refusing leave to appeal against the sentence of imprisonment ([1]). A secondary issue pertains to whether there were reasonable prospects of success on appeal concerning the appropriateness of the sentence based on the appellant's personal circumstances and the nature of the offence ([5]).

## R – Rules

The SCA based its analysis on the provisions of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, particularly section 309C, which allows for leave to appeal from the high court in cases where the regional court has refused to grant such leave ([1]). The test governing whether leave to appeal should be granted is whether the appellant has demonstrated a reasonable prospect of success on appeal ([5]).

The court referenced precedents, including *S v Khoasasa* and *Dipholo v The State*, which established that appeals from magistrates' courts must first be heard by the high courts ([2]), highlighting the delineation of jurisdiction within the South African judicial hierarchy ([2], [3]).

## A – Application

In assessing the merits of Almeida’s case, the court noted that while attempted murder is a serious offence, the circumstances surrounding Almeida's actions indicated potential mitigating factors. Counsel emphasized that Almeida's sentence did not take into account the three pre-sentence reports, which recommended correctional supervision over incarceration. This oversight constituted a material misdirection potentially warranting an appeal ([7], [8]).

Moreover, the court addressed the argument that correctional supervision could be suited to Almeida, given his personal circumstances, including his role as a single father and his lack of previous convictions for violence. This argument aligns with the judicial reasoning in *S v De Villiers*, where it was noted that rehabilitative considerations should be factored into sentencing decisions ([7]).

The admissions made by the state that the regional court’s decision was misdirected reinforced the SCA's conclusion that the appellant had reasonable prospects of success in a subsequent appeal against the imposing of the 8-year sentence ([8]).

## C – Conclusion

The SCA held that there was a reasonable prospect of success regarding the appeal against Almeida's sentence and thus overturned the high court's decision to deny leave ([10]). The court emphasized that the grant of leave to appeal does not signify agreement with the appellant’s arguments but rather an acknowledgment of the justiciability of his grievance and its need for further examination at the high court level ([9]).

Consequently, the SCA ordered that the high court’s refusal to grant leave be set aside, permitting Almeida to pursue his appeal against the sentence imposed by the regional court ([10]). This case highlights ongoing issues within the sentencing regime in South Africa, particularly the appropriate recognition of mitigating circumstances and the need for adherence to procedural correctness when examining appeals in criminal matters.
