Supreme Court of Appeal

Randfontein Municipality v Grobler and Others (543/08)

Facts The appellant, Randfontein Municipality, sought to evict the first and third respondents (the occupiers) from property identified as Portion 74 of the Farm Elandsvlei 249 IQ, Randfontein.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009 1997 (ESTA) rather than the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19

Facts

The appellant, Randfontein Municipality, sought to evict the first and third respondents (the occupiers) from property identified as Portion 74 of the Farm Elandsvlei 249 IQ, Randfontein. Mr. Grobler, the owner of the property since February 2005, applied for the eviction on the basis that the occupiers had no legal right to remain on the land. The occupiers contested this, asserting that they resided there with consent, thereby invoking the protections afforded under the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA) rather than the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE). The High Court, however, ruled in favor of Grobler, dismissing the alleged consents and concluding that no key factual dispute existed to necessitate oral evidence ([paras 1-4]).

The occupiers had settled on the land since 1959 and constituted a community approximately 2,000 strong, residing in various forms of housing. The municipality, in its capacity as a state functionary, was also involved in the legal proceedings, orchestrating the obligations arising under Sections 26 and 27 of the South African Constitution regarding housing rights ([paras 2-3]).

Issues

The central legal question addressed by the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the High Court had the jurisdiction to order the eviction of the occupiers under PIE, particularly in light of the potential applicability of ESTA due to the claim of consent for land occupation ([para 1]).

Additionally, the court was tasked with discerning:

  1. Whether any of the individuals residing on the property qualified as an ‘occupier’ under ESTA.
  2. What constitutes 'consent'—express or tacit—in these circumstances.
  3. If proven, whether the respective provisions of ESTA or PIE would govern the eviction process ([para 21]).

Rules

The decision engaged primarily with ESTA and PIE, both statutes aimed at regulating evictions and addressing the security of tenure for land occupiers, aligning with the constitutional rights outlined in Sections 26 and 27 ([para 4]).

  • ESTA governs land occupation with consent, defining “consent” as either express or tacit. It offers protections specifically in rural contexts, and occupancy with consent renders individuals non-unlawful in terms of PIE's definitions.
  • PIE applies where occupation is without the owner's consent and emphasizes a responsibilities-based framework for landlords seeking eviction.
  • The statute employs a presumption of consent for individuals residing continuously for over three years, as per Section 3 of ESTA ([para 8]).

The distinction between the two statutes is pivotal, notably that occupiers recognized under ESTA cannot be classified as ‘unlawful occupiers’ under PIE ([para 4]).

Application

In the present case, the court scrutinized the nature of the consent the occupiers claimed to have had concerning their lengthy occupation of the land. The Court notably reasoned that the prolonged and established community living on the property, coupled with the assertion of tacit consent, created legitimate grounds for a dispute regarding the factual circumstance of occupation ([para 7]).

The court illuminated how Grobler's affidavit lacked substantive information addressing whether the relevant consent had existed prior to his purchase. It underscored that for the court to act under PIE, Grobler bore the onus to convincingly prove that PIE was applicable—a burden he did not fulfill, as many factors pointed toward the potential existence of consent ([paras 11-16]).

Moreover, judgements from cases such as Rademeyer v Western Districts Council and Residents of Joe Slovo Community v Thubelisha Homes were instrumental in contextualizing the legal backdrop of consent and highlighting the need for a factual inquiry via oral evidence ([paras 9-11]).

Ultimately, the appeal court concluded that given the serious nature of the disputed facts—concerning consent and the overall right to occupy—the matter warranted further examination through oral evidence, contradicting the High Court's prior dismissal on the grounds of lack of a genuine dispute ([paras 20-21]).

Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, thus vacating the initial eviction order and positioning the matter for further adjudication regarding the factual determinations of consent under ESTA. The implications of this decision are significant: it illustrates the necessity of thorough scrutiny in eviction processes and underscores the legal protections afforded to long-term occupiers under ESTA. The court's ruling affirms that matters relating to consent in eviction proceedings necessitate a robust examination of evidence, especially in cases involving established communities ([paras 21]).

This ruling thus sets a precedent on how courts must approach cases involving land rights and the regulation of occupancy, affirming the intertwined rights enshrined within the South African Constitution.
