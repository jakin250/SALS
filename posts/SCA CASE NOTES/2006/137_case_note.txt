# Case Note: Farocean Marine (Pty) Ltd v The Minister of Trade and Industry of the Republic of South Africa 2006 SCA 165 (RSA)

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts 

This case revolves around an appeal by Farocean Marine (Pty) Ltd (the Appellant) against the judgment of Wagley J, who dismissed the Appellant's special pleas regarding the Minister of Trade and Industry's (the Respondent) claims for the recovery of benefits that had been unduly paid under the General Export Incentive Scheme (GEIS) ([1]–[2]). 

The Appellant constructed and exported a yacht on 28 February 1996, subsequently claiming benefits amounting to R 1,723,861 under the GEIS, which were paid on 5 July 1996 ([2]). The GEIS was established to incentivize exports by providing financial benefits to qualifying exporters, but there were stipulations that such payments were subject to verification ([3]).

Following an investigation from November 1996 to January 1998, the Director-General of the Department of Trade and Industry concluded that the yacht did not meet the eligibility criteria under GEIS. Specifically, it was determined that the export did not produce inflows of foreign currency within the stipulated period, leading to the disallowance of the Appellant’s claim ([4]). The Minister proceeded with legal action to recover the payment, prompting the Appellant to raise two special pleas regarding the Minister's standing to sue and the matter of prescription ([6]–[9]).

### I – Issues 

The primary legal issues presented in this case were:

1. **Locus Standi of the Minister**: Whether the Minister of Trade and Industry had the authority to claim back the benefits paid under the GEIS or whether only the Director-General had that authority as stipulated in the guidelines ([6]).
   
2. **Prescription**: Whether the Respondent's claim had become prescribed under the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, specifically whether knowledge of the debt by the Respondent precluded the claim from being actionable after a three-year period ([9]).

### R – Rules

1. **Locus Standi**: It is established that in proceedings brought by the State, actions can be initiated either in the name of the State or by a nominal plaintiff, typically the Minister responsible for the department ([8]). The legal basis for the Minister's standing relies on the argument that the actions taken must be on behalf of the Department ([8]).

2. **Prescription**: According to section 11 of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, the period for prescription is typically three years, but certain debts owed to the state may be treated differently under section 11(b), which provides for a fifteen-year prescription if the debt is considered an advance or loan from the state ([10]). Section 12(3) asserts that a debt is not considered "due" until the creditor has sufficient knowledge of the debtor's identity and the facts from which the debt arises ([15]).

### A – Application 

#### Locus Standi

In addressing the first issue of locus standi, the court interpreted the guidelines within the broader context of the GEIS. The court asserted that while the Director-General had the authority for the operational functions of the scheme, the Minister could initiate legal action on behalf of the Department ([8]). The court rejected the Appellant’s assertion that only the Director-General had the jurisdiction for recovery, determining that the Minister was indeed acting within his official capacity to represent the interests of the Department ([8]–[9]).

#### Prescription

On the second issue, the court analyzed whether the Respondent’s claim had prescribed. The Appellant argued that the claim arose when the benefits were paid on 5 July 1996, implying that the Respondent should have acted well within three years of this date, as they had knowledge by September 1996 of the circumstances surrounding the claim ([9]–[10]). The Respondent countered this by invoking a fifteen-year prescription period, citing section 11(b) of the Prescription Act. 

The court ultimately held that the nature of the payments under the GEIS could not be classified as an advance or loan. It concluded that the payment made was not a contractual obligation and thus did not fall under the extended prescription provisions. As a consequence, the debt only became due after the Director-General had investigated and determined that the obligations under GEIS had not been met ([10]–[14]). 

The court emphasized that the investigation into the claim was necessary for the Respondent to ascertain the facts from which the debt arose. Therefore, since the investigation established that the Director-General could only disallow the claim based on specific criteria, the court determined that the Respondent did not have the requisite knowledge to establish the debt until the investigation concluded ([26]).

### C – Conclusion 

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming that both special pleas raised by the Appellant were rightly dismissed by the court a quo ([26]). The court elucidated that the Minister of Trade and Industry had the authority to recover the benefits paid out under GEIS, thus upholding the Minister's locus standi. 

Furthermore, the court clarified that the nature of the payments under the GEIS and the conditions under which they were payable fundamentally influence the recognition of debts and their classification under the Prescription Act ([10]–[15]). The implications extend beyond this case, contributing to the understanding of the relationship between administrative authority and the exercise of fiscal responsibility in state matters, particularly in public finance and the conditions under which claims may arise and be reclaimed. 

The precedent set in this case serves as a significant authority on issues relating to prescription and administrative law within the context of state financial claims.
