Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note: Resultant Finance (Pty) Ltd v Head of Department for the Department of Health, KwaZulu-Natal and Another (2020) ZASCA 87

## F – Facts
The case revolves around Resultant Finance (Pty) Ltd (the appellant), which submitted a bid to the KwaZulu-Natal Provincial Department of Health (the Health Department) in response to a Request for Proposal (RFP) on 2 March 2015 ([2]). The tender for managing the leasing of medical and non-medical equipment was awarded to Resultant Finance on 8 April 2015. The contract was formally signed on 22 April 2015 and was set to last for five years, potentially valued at hundreds of millions of rand ([2]).

From August 2015 onwards, the Health Department began issuing orders for equipment. However, in January 2016, the Health Department raised concerns about the appellant's performance, culminating in an order to show cause regarding the potential cancellation of the contract due to alleged non-performance ([4]). By June 2017, the contract was cancelled, citing several reasons for the appellant's alleged failures, including non-compliance with bid requirements and a lack of secured funding ([5]).

In response, Resultant Finance filed an application in the KwaZulu-Natal High Court to compel the Health Department to comply with its contractual obligations ([4]). Concurrently, an application was filed by the MEC for Finance in September 2017, reviewing the award of the tender to Resultant Finance, arguing that it was invalid due to non-compliance with the RFP ([6]). 

The High Court ruled that the Health Department lacked authority to unilaterally cancel the contract without court involvement ([7]). However, the court ultimately found that the appellant's bid was irregular, leading to the award being set aside ([12]). 

Dissatisfied with the High Court’s ruling, Resultant Finance appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([13]).

## I – Issues
The key issues for the Supreme Court of Appeal to consider included:
1. The validity and enforceability of the contract resulting from the tender award ([14]).
2. Whether the cancellation of the contract constituted an unlawful act on the part of the Health Department and the MEC for Finance ([14]).
3. The propriety of the condonation granted for the MEC for Finance's delayed application for review ([14]).
4. Whether the appeal should be dismissed due to mootness, given that the contract period had lapsed by the time of the appeal ([26]).

## R – Rules
The legal framework surrounding this case involved several statutes and legal principles:
- **Section 217 of the Constitution**: This section mandates that all procurement processes by state organs must be fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective ([21]).
- **Public Finance Management Act (PFMA)**: Pertinent parts of this act were cited concerning the responsibilities of public officials in managing procurement ([10]).
- **Principle of legality**: The principle dictates that governmental actions must comply with the law and can be reviewed if found unlawful ([21]).
- **Section 16(2)(a)(i) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013**: This provision allows for an appeal to be dismissed if the issues involved have become moot and will not have a practical effect ([26]).

## A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal relied heavily on the principle of mootness. The judges noted that since the contract had expired, the issues raised no longer constituted a live controversy ([26]). The appellant argued that being denied an appeal would impede their access to the courts for potential damages claims stemming from the Health Department's actions, yet the court found that the appellant could pursue such remedies independently of the appeal’s outcome ([27]).

The court re-emphasized that the issues of lawful procurement and compliance with tender requirements were critical but had been overtaken by events. Although the judicial examination of procurement propriety is serious and can be made even after a contract has lapsed, the specifics of this case did not present any substantial grounds that warranted such examination ([21], [37]). 

Given the rights at play, the court evaluated the nature and complexity of the dispute, concluding that while the appellant's grievances were not trivial, they were ultimately personal grievances of a commercial nature that did not engage broader public-interest considerations ([37]). 

In addressing the importance of the factual context, the court reiterated that there was no evidence of unconscionable conduct by the respondents, distinguishing the instant matter from other cases where public interest had justified the continuation of a moot dispute ([36]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeals on the grounds of mootness, emphasizing that the issues had been rendered irrelevant due to the expiration of the contract ([42]). The court held that even though the tender had been awarded irregularly, there was no legal basis to challenge the verdict on mootness for the circumstances of this case.

Thus, while the court acknowledged the serious implications of procurement irregularities, it clarified that the judicial resources ought not to be used for merely academic disputes when no practical outcome could result from the appeal. Overall, the appeals were dismissed, and the appellant was ordered to pay costs, reinforcing the upholding of the lower court's findings concerning the irregularities uncovered ([42]).
