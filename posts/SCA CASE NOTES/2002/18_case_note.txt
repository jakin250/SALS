# Case Note: Republic of South Africa, **Merak S** ([2000] 1 Lloyd's Rep 619)

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The appeal arises from a judgment delivered by Niles-Dunér J in the Durban and Coast Local Division of the High Court, where the appellant's request for a reduction in a bank guarantee, previously provided to secure the release of the vessel *Merak S* from arrest, was dismissed ([1]). The vessel had been arrested under section 5(3) of the *Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983* (hereinafter "the Act"). This action was taken to provide security for future claims the respondent, Bulktrans (Europe) Corporation, intended to advance against the appellant, who had chartered the vessel to the respondent ([2]). 

After Niles-Dunér J granted leave to appeal, developments indicated the respondent would not pursue the claims in arbitration, leading the appellant to seek an order for the return of the guarantee ([3]). Despite the practical implications of the appeal being moot, the Supreme Court of Appeal exercised its discretion to hear the case due to the legal questions involved ([4]). 

### I – Issues
The primary legal issue in this case hinges on whether the bank guarantee provided by the appellant constitutes "security" under section 5(2)(d) of the Act. This concern is linked to whether the court possesses the jurisdiction to reduce the amount of the guarantee and to order counter-security from the respondent for an anticipated claim ([6]). Further, the court examined if the guarantees given under existing maritime practice fell within the definition of "security" intended by the legislature when enacting the Act ([9]).

### R – Rules
The determination of what constitutes "security" in the context of maritime law and the relevant sections of the Act is central to resolving these issues. Sections particularly relevant include:

- **Section 3(10)(a)**: Defines the conditions under which property is deemed to be arrested, establishing a framework for understanding when guarantees, undertakings, or securities may apply ([6]).
- **Section 5(2)(b)-(d)**: Grants courts authority to require security for costs or claims and provides discretion in the conditions under which arrests or attachments are made ([6]).
- **Section 5(3)**: Allows the arrest of property to secure claims that are potentially subject to arbitration—paralleling the broader legal context established by English maritime law ([7], [8]).

Existing case law, including *The Christiansborg* and *The Tjaskemolen*, underlines the conventions in interpreting guarantees and their customary practice as "security" in both South Africa and England ([8]).

### A – Application
The court critically reviewed the interpretation of the term "security" as applied by Niles-Dunér J, finding her position—that the bank guarantee did not meet the criteria for security under section 5(2)(d)—to be erroneous. Farlam JA underscored that the language of the Act does not explicitly exclude guarantees from the definition of security and argued convincingly that practical maritime practice, wherein guarantees are routinely accepted as equivalent to cash deposits or bail, ought to inform the interpretation ([7], [9]).

Upon exploring the implications of the arrest being deemed continuous as supported by prior jurisprudence (*mv the Alam Tenggiri*), the court affirmed that the appellant was entitled to seek counter-security. This holds significant weight in understanding the court's jurisdictional powers regarding securing rights for claims that have not yet been formally pursued by the respondent ([12], [15]).

### C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal allowed the appeal, thus confirming the bank guarantee constituted legitimate security under the Act and affirmatively ruling that the court retains the power to reduce the amount of security ([14]). As a result, the original order of Niles-Dunér J was set aside and replaced with a declaratory order affirming the appellant's entitlements regarding the guarantee and the potential to require counter-security from the respondent for pending claims ([15]).

The decision elucidates critical aspects of admiralty law within South Africa, particularly regarding the interpretation of security and the court's discretion to ensure equitable resolutions in maritime disputes. It reaffirms the judiciary's role in enhancing commercial practices by aligning legal interpretations with established industry standards, highlighting an ongoing relevance in the adjudication of maritime law cases.
