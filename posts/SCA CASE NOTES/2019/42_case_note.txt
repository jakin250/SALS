Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: MEPF v SAMWU PF (1412/2018) [2019] ZASCA 42

## F – Facts
The case revolves around the interpretation of particular rules of the South African Municipal Workers’ Union National Provident Fund (the Fund), specifically rules 3.2.1 and 11.11, concerning the membership of 124 employees of Ntabankulu Local Municipality (the Municipality) ([1]–[2]). These employees were initially members of the Fund by virtue of their employment with the Municipality, which was a participating employer in the Fund. 

Beginning on 1 September 2013, with the Municipality’s consent, these employees sought to switch their pension benefits to the Municipal Employees Pension Fund (MEPF), leading to the Municipality ceasing its contributions to the Fund ([2]–[3]). The Fund initiated proceedings in the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court to compel the Municipality to pay arrear contributions, asserting that the employees had not validly terminated their membership while remaining in service with the Municipality ([3]).
 
The main argument from the MEPF and its administrator, Akani Retirement Fund Administrators (Pty) Ltd, was that the relevant Fund rules were unconstitutional as they limited employees' rights to freedom of association and trade ([3]–[5]). The court a quo found against the MEPF and Akani, interpreting the Fund's rules to prohibit employees from terminating their membership while still employed by the Municipality. The MEPF and Akani subsequently appealed ([5]–[6]).

## I – Issues
The case presented several legal issues for determination:
1. The proper interpretation of rules 3.2.1 and 11.11 of the Fund regarding their prohibition on in-service cessation of membership ([7]).
2. Whether the provisions in rule 3.2 infringe the constitutional rights to freedom of association of the employees and freedom of trade of the MEPF ([7]).
3. The legality of the rules being reviewed and set aside on grounds of unreasonableness and lack of proper justification in their adoption ([7]).

## R – Rules
In interpreting the relevant rules, the court referred to earlier precedents regarding the obligations of trustees and the governance of pension funds, which stipulate that trustees must act in accordance with the fund’s rules, which are treated as the fund’s constitution ([13]–[14]).

Specifically, rule 3.2 states:
- **3.2.1**: A member may not withdraw from the Fund while remaining in service.
- **3.2.2**: Membership ceases upon cessation of service ([8]).

Section 13A(5) of the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956 (PFA) outlines conditions under which members can leave a fund and transfer benefits. Additionally, section 14 delineates the requirements for the amalgamation or transfer of business involving funds ([18], [25]). The Fund contended that these statutory provisions were vital in guiding the rights of members.

## A – Application
The court began its analysis by performing a textual interpretation of rule 3.2.1, determining that the prohibition against withdrawal from the Fund while in service was clear and could not be misconstrued as permission for an elective transfer of membership ([16]–[17]). 

Further, the court concluded that the MEPF and Akani’s interpretations of sections 13A(5) and 14 did not align with the rules of the Fund or their intended legal effect. The court posited that a member cannot transfer their benefits unless their membership has been duly terminated under the Fund’s rules ([23]).

On the constitutional challenges, the court held that the stipulation of membership to a particular fund does not infringe upon the employees' freedom of association, as they were free to choose to join another fund at employment commencement and during their membership ([53]–[61]). The Fund's rules were seen as provisions ensuring member retention, which directly correlates with the fund's stability and longevity, thereby serving both the interest of the members and the regulations under the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962 ([33]–[38]).

Regarding the grounds for reviewing the Fund’s rules, the court found no actionable basis presented by the appellants to substantiate claims of unreasonableness or lack of justification ([65]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled that the interpretation imposed by the court a quo was correct, affirming that the employees of the Municipality had not validly terminated their memberships in the Fund while remaining in service. Therefore, the appeal was dismissed with costs, including the costs of two counsel ([66]). 

The court clarified that while employees have freedom of association and trade, these rights do not extend to the unilateral termination of pension fund membership while in active service, thereby upholding the integrity of the Fund’s governance processes. The implications of this decision are significant for pension fund rules and member rights within similar organizational contexts in South Africa, reinforcing the stringency of regulations against in-service membership withdrawal.
