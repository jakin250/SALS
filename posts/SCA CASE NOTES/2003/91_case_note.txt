Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2003

# Case Note: Metro Projects CC v Klerksdorp Local Municipality [2003] ZASCA 99

## F – Facts
The case revolves around a tender dispute involving Metro Projects CC and African Unity Developers CC (the appellants) against Klerksdorp Local Municipality and eleven other parties (the respondents). The appellants submitted a tender for the development of 1,333 stands in Jouberton Extension 10, a township within the first respondent's jurisdiction, following their invitation for tenders related to local government housing development ([1]).

The first respondent received a grant of R18,400 per stand from the Provincial Government of the North West Province ([2]). The tender included requirements for providing basic services as well as constructing and handing over houses for each stand. Each tenderer was required to provide a breakdown of prices and a description of services, including the proposed floor area of the houses ([2]).

Despite offering a significantly smaller house (30.2 sq m), the ninth respondent's tender was recommended for acceptance by the city civil engineer, Mr. Nicolas Els. His recommendation was marred by inaccuracies. He misleadingly indicated that the size and layout of the house would be discussed with the community, despite the tender explicitly detailing the proposed dimensions ([4]).

The mayoral committee requested clarification regarding the floor area and the track record of the ninth respondent before making a tender decision. Els, however, misrepresented information by providing a different plan (34.3 sq m) to the mayoral committee without revealing that it was not part of the original offer ([8]–[9]). The flawed process drew scrutiny, especially concerning whether the tender procedures followed were fair as mandated by the Local Government Transition Act and the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act ([11]–[12]).

## I – Issues
The central legal issue in this case revolves around whether the tender process followed by the Klerksdorp Local Municipality was fair and compliant with statutory obligations under the Local Government Transition Act and the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act ([12]).

Additionally, the issue pertains to whether the actions of the city civil engineer, particularly the manipulation and misrepresentation of the tender information to the decision-making bodies, amounted to a breach of the principles of transparency, fairness, and competitiveness mandated in public procurement ([14]).

## R – Rules
The legal framework governing the tender process in South Africa includes:

1. **Local Government Transition Act 209 of 1993, section 10G(5)(a)**: Requires the tendering process to be fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective.
  
2. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000, section 3(2)(a)**: Mandates that administrative processes be lawful, procedurally fair, and justifiable.
  
3. **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, section 217(1)**: States that when an organ of state contracts for goods or services, it must do so in accordance with a system that is fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective.
  
4. **Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act 5 of 2000**: Emphasizes the requirement for compliance with tender conditions, defining an 'acceptable tender' as one that fully meets the specifications and conditions set within the tender documents ([11]).

**Key Precedent**: In *Logbro Properties CC v Bedderson NO and Others* 2003 (2) SA 460 (SCA), Cameron JA highlighted the expectation of fairness and clarity in tender processes, pointing out that ambiguity should be directed toward fair evaluation ([12]).

## A – Application
The court analyzed the actions of Mr. Els and the tender processes that ensued. It concluded that the engineer's representation of the ninth respondent's tender to the mayoral committee was erroneous and fundamentally misleading. Despite the clear rejection of a smaller house size by the other bids, Els presented an inflated plan amid a concealing of critical information. 

His failure to provide honest communication about the ninth respondent's proposal stripped the tender process of fairness, particularly concerning competitive equality, leading to potential prejudice against the appellants and other tenderers ([14]). 

The court underscored that the tender evaluation must be based on the information provided during the bidding process and that any amendments post-tender submission need to be transparently communicated to preserve the integrity of the process. The court concluded that allowing a post-tender augmentation presented a significant deviation from the established codes of fairness and transparency ([15]).

## C – Conclusion
The appeal by Metro Projects and African Unity Developers succeeded, leading the court to set aside the award of the tender to the ninth respondent due to the serious procedural irregularities in the tender process that violated the principles of fairness dictated by applicable statutory provisions. The court highlighted that the misrepresentation of the tender permits and failure to provide essential information to the decision-making committee fundamentally undermined the equal evaluation of bids.

As a result, the implications of this case for public procurement in South Africa are substantial. It reinforces the necessity of adhering to fairness and transparency in the tender process and sets a precedent for the stringent application of both statutory law and principles of administrative justice in public contracting ([15]). The court ordered the first respondent to pay the costs of the application, reinforcing accountability in local government procurement decisions ([15]).

This case serves as a pivotal reminder of the crucial role that transparent and fair processes play in upholding not only legal standards but also the public trust in governmental procurement mechanisms.
