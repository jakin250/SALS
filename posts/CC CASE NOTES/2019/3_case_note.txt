# Case Note: Stokwe v Member of the Executive Council: Department of Education, Eastern Cape and Others [2018] ZACC 3

## FIRAC Method Analysis

### F - Facts
The case of **Stokwe v Member of the Executive Council: Department of Education, Eastern Cape and Others** ([1]) revolves around Thandiwe Cynthia Stokwe, the applicant, who was employed by the Eastern Cape Department of Education as a Deputy Chief Education Specialist. In 2009, during an emergency situation, she awarded a temporary service contract to her husband’s unregistered transport company without the necessary approval from the Head of Department. Subsequently, she was charged with misconduct and dismissed in June 2011, more than five years after the alleged misconduct.

After her dismissal, Stokwe submitted an internal appeal, which was delayed for years. In 2014, her appeal was ultimately dismissed. She then referred her case to the Education Labour Relations Council (ELRC), contesting the fairness of her dismissal. The ELRC arbitrator found her dismissal substantively fair but did not address the procedural fairness issue adequately. The Labour Court dismissed her review application, prompting Stokwe to seek leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court.

### I - Issues
The key issues raised in this case include:
1. **Procedural Fairness**: Was the dismissal procedurally unfair due to the excessive delay in concluding the disciplinary proceedings? ([24])
2. **Substantive Fairness**: Was the finding of substantive fairness in her dismissal reasonable, particularly regarding the misapplication of relevant guidelines? ([28])
3. **Delays in Proceedings**: Did the inordinate delay in handling the disciplinary process, and the failure to provide timely reasons for her dismissal, amount to a procedural unfairness that warranted review? ([64–66])
4. **Waiver of Right to Discipline**: Did the Department effectively waive its right to discipline the applicant through its dilatory conduct? ([77])

### R - Rules
The legal framework guiding this case includes:
- **Employment of Educators Act (EEA)** ([3]):
   - Section 18, which outlines educator misconduct.
   - Schedule 2, which provides guidelines for disciplinary procedures, emphasizing prompt and fair action ([36]).
- **Labour Relations Act (LRA)**, particularly sections 185 and 188, which protect employees' rights against unfair dismissal ([46]).
- Relevant case law, including **Sidumo v Rustenburg Platinum Mines** and **Bato Star Fishing**, which establish standards for substantive and procedural fairness in dismissal ([6], [56]).

### A - Application
In the present case, the Constitutional Court found that while the arbitrator determined the substance of the dismissal to be fair based on the applicant’s failure to obtain permission for her actions, it failed to adequately address procedural fairness. The Court noted that the disciplinary process took excessively long, undermining the principles outlined in the EEA that disciplinary matters should be resolved expeditiously ([68]).

The Court highlighted the lack of clear communication from the Department regarding the status of her disciplinary proceedings and the appeal process, indicating a failure to adhere to the guidelines set out in Schedule 2 of the EEA. The Court differentiated between the less stringent requirement of timely resolution and the need for procedural justice, asserting that the excessive delay amounted to a breach of fair process principles ([74], [76]).

In addition, the Court noted that while the Department argued that the applicant's continued employment indicated a breakdown in the trust relationship, the excessive and unexplained delays hampered the employment relationship's integrity, raising questions about motivation for the dismissal ([78]).

### C - Conclusion
The Constitutional Court held unanimously that the dismissal of Stokwe was procedurally unfair due to the significant delays in the disciplinary process and the lack of timely communication from her employer. The Court emphasized the essential nature of procedural fairness as central to maintaining the dignity of employees and the integrity of the disciplinary process. As a result, the Court upheld Stokwe's appeal, categorizing her dismissal as procedurally unfair, and remitted the matter back to the Labour Court to determine an appropriate remedy ([87]).

This judgment serves as a critical reminder of the importance of adhering to disciplinary procedures and ensuring that delays do not jeopardize employees’ rights to fair treatment. The ruling clarifies that any employer must act promptly and transparently when managing disciplinary processes, or risk undermining the legal foundations of employment relationships in the public sector. The Court’s decision to remand the matter for further remedy underscores the ongoing need for the Labour Court’s scrutiny in ensuring proper procedural outcomes in employment disputes.
