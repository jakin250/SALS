Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note: Motor Industry Staff Association and Another v Great South Autobody CC; Solidarity obo Strydom and Others v State Information Technology Agency SOC Limited [2024] ZACC 29

## F – Facts

This case involves two separate appeals to the Constitutional Court of South Africa regarding the dismissal of employees on the grounds of age, specifically focusing on the interpretation of section 187(2)(b) of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 ([paras 1–6]). 

### The Landman Case (CCT 298/22)
The first applicant, the Motor Industry Staff Association (MISA), represented Willem Frederick Landman (Mr. Landman), who had been employed as a procurement officer by Great South Autobody CC since November 2007 ([paras 13–14]). The employment contract stipulated a retirement age of 60. Mr. Landman reached this age on March 15, 2018, but continued to work until he was notified of his termination on February 12, 2019, asserting that his dismissal was based on age ([paras 14–19]). 

The employer claimed that their departure from the agreed retirement age was justified under section 187(2)(b), which allows for fair dismissal based on age once the agreed retirement age has been reached ([para 16.1]).

### The Solidarity Case (CCT 346/22)
Solidarity represented six applicants, including Gerhardus Viljoen Strydom, all dismissed by the State Information Technology Agency (SITA) for reaching the retirement age of 60 ([para 79]). The employees challenged their dismissals on the basis that they had been allowed to work beyond their retirement age and argued that SITA should not rely on section 187(2)(b) as a justification for their dismissals ([paras 86–104]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal questions addressed by the Court include:

1. Whether a dismissal based on age is automatically unfair if the employee has been allowed to work beyond the agreed or normal retirement age in contravention of section 187(1)(f) of the Labour Relations Act ([paras 1–6]).
2. The interpretation and implications of section 187(2)(b) regarding the timing of dismissals in relation to age ([paras 2–6]).

## R – Rules

The Court's analysis centers around the interpretation of the Labour Relations Act, specifically:

### Section 187(1)(f)
This section declares that a dismissal is automatically unfair if the reason for the dismissal is based on unfair discrimination against an employee on arbitrary grounds, including age.

### Section 187(2)(b)
This section provides an exception to section 187(1)(f), allowing for a fair dismissal if it occurs after the employee has reached their normal or agreed retirement age ([paras 7–8]).

The courts must also consider the constitutional framework emphasizing human dignity, equality, and the need to prevent age discrimination as established in section 9 of the Constitution of South Africa ([para 26]).

## A – Application

### In the Landman Case
The Court found that since Mr. Landman was dismissed approximately nine months after reaching the agreed retirement age, and given that he had continued to work without any objection from the employer, his dismissal could not be justified under section 187(2)(b) ([paras 73, 74]). The interpretation forces a binary option on employers to act on the retirement age immediately or forfeit that right subsequently. 

The judgment evaluated the historic misinterpretation stemming from the Waco case, which allowed for dismissals even after exceeding the retirement age and deemed them fair without taking the employee's continuous service into consideration ([paras 69–72]). This led to the conclusion that Mr. Landman's dismissal was automatically unfair ([para 74]).

### In the Solidarity Case
The Court applied similar reasoning, holding that dismissals of the individual applicants were automatically unfair because the dismissals occurred long after reaching the normal retirement age and were not preceded by a fair procedure ([para 110]). Moreover, the argument that employees had an implied right to work until 67 based on the employer's consent was upheld, which further complicated the employer's position when dismissing employees who had continued working past the retirement age ([para 120]).

### Dissenting View (Rogers J)
Rogers J noted that allowing dismissals based on age should not necessitate a dismissal occurring exactly on the retirement age date, arguing this is impractical ([paras 183–202]). He maintained that the law should permit employers to make decisions regarding dismissals based on age flexibly, without the strict requirement of timing posed by the majority's conclusions. 

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded that:

1. In the **Landman case**, the dismissal of Mr. Landman was deemed automatically unfair, leading to a restoration of his compensation for 24 months ([para 135.1]).
2. In the **Solidarity case**, the dismissals of the individual applicants were also automatically unfair, and they were awarded similar compensation as well as costs in all courts ([para 135.2]).

The judgments uphold the necessity for employers to approach dismissals based on age with care and consideration to adherence to procedural fairness and timing, aiming to curb potential abuse while recognizing the rights and dignity of older employees ([paras 73–74, 110–120]). This case reinforces judicial protection against age discrimination in the employment sector and seeks to balance the complex dynamics of employer-employee contracts within the context of an evolving labor market.
