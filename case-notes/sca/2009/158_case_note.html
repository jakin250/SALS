Supreme Court of Appeal

Turbek Trading CC v A & D Spitz Limited (565/08)

Citation: Turbek Trading v A & D Spitz (565/08) [2009] ZASCA 158 (27 November 2009).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009 [2009] ZASCA 158 (27 November 2009). Coram: Harms DP, Lew

Citation: Turbek Trading v A & D Spitz (565/08) [2009] ZASCA 158 (27 November 2009).

Coram: Harms DP, Lewis, Ponnan, Mhlantla JJA, and Hurt AJA.

F – Facts

The appellant, Turbek Trading CC ("Turbek"), is the proprietor of two registered trademarks: TM 2000/03828 (class 25 for clothing, footwear, and headgear) and TM 2000/13267 (a service mark in class 35), both consisting of the initials "KG". The initials are associated with Mr. Kenneth Gordon, a shoe designer, who has utilized them in commerce ([1]).

Turbek filed for registration on 7 March 2000, initially applying for "KG Italia". However, due to limitations suggested by the Registrar of Trade Marks regarding origin, Turbek amended its application to "KG" only. Following proper procedures, the trademarks were accepted without opposition and registered on 29 February 2005 ([2]).

The first respondent, A & D Spitz Limited ("Spitz"), claims prior use of the "KG" mark, asserting it held rights based on its earlier registrations. Spitz has been using the trademark "Kurt Geiger," which shares the same initials, since 17 December 1990, primarily in association with footwear ([3]). It initiated proceedings to expunge Turbek’s registrations, claiming trade mark infringement and passing-off ([4]).

In the lower court, Spitz's claims were upheld, leading to this appeal ([4]).

I – Issues

The legal issues presented include:

  1. Did Spitz have an existing common-law right to the "KG" mark prior to Turbek’s application in accordance with section 10(16) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993?
  2. Was the use of the registered trade marks by Spitz sufficient to establish a claim for trade mark infringement and passing-off?
  3. Was there unjustifiable delay on Spitz's part that could preclude its claims against Turbek?
  4. Did the amendment of Turbek's trademark application from "KG Italia" to "KG" violate any provisions of the Trade Marks Act ([6]–[12])?

R – Rules

The applicable legal rules include:

  1. Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, particularly:
  • Section 10(16): A mark may not be registered if it is contrary to the existing rights of any person who filed an application for the same or similar mark.
  • Section 34(1)(b): Trade mark infringement occurs if the use of a trademark leads to a likelihood of deception or confusion.
  • Section 34(2)(g): Registration provides no defence against the use of an identical or confusingly similar registered trade mark.
  1. Passing-off principles: As discussed in Kellogg Co v Bokomo Co-operative Ltd 1997 (2) SA 725 (C) and Glenton & Mitchell v Ceylon Tea Company 1918 WLD 118, a registered trade mark does not serve as an absolute defence to passing-off claims if the claimant can show prior rights ([8]–[12]).
  2. Acquiescence: This equitable doctrine suggests that a delay by a party in asserting rights can preclude them from relief ([15]).
  3. Evidential burden: Spitz needed to establish an existing reputation in "KG" for its passing-off claim, aligning with the standards set in Moorgate Tobacco Co Ltd v Philip Morris Inc and Adcock-Ingram Products Ltd v Beecham SA (Pty) Ltd ([17]).

A – Application

The Court assessed Spitz's claim to prior rights by scrutinizing the evidence of its use of "KG". It found that Spitz could not demonstrate significant use or reputation attached to the mark "KG" independently. While Spitz marketed its products under the name "Kurt Geiger", which may colloquially have been referred to as "KG", this did not substantiate a separate and distinct reputation for "KG" itself.

Significantly, Spitz's evidence consisted mostly of inadequate proof after the critical date of 7 March 2000, which did not establish its reputation at that time ([19],[20]).

The court refuted Spitz's arguments of dominant use of "KG" as part of its combination mark, emphasizing that the full name "Kurt Geiger" remained prominent in marketing and packaging. Moreover, the timing and nature of Spitz's applications and threats suggested an expectation to maintain its "Kurt Geiger" brand rather than independently brand "KG" ([21]).

Regarding the claims of trade mark infringement and passing-off, the Court concluded that since Spitz did not establish rights in "KG", the claims failed ([23]).

On the issue of the amendment of Turbek’s application from "KG Italia" to "KG", the Court held that any irregularity in the amendment process, which was not properly contested through review as required, could not be used to undermine Turbek’s trademark ([22]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal found in favour of Turbek, thus setting aside the lower court's ruling which had upheld Spitz's application to expunge Turbek's trademarks. The primary holdings can be summarized as:

  1. Spitz failed to establish existing rights in the "KG" trademark at the time of Turbek’s application to warrant expungement under section 10(16).
  2. The lack of significant use and reputation for "KG" precluded claims of infringement and passing-off.
  3. The procedural failure of Spitz to pursue a proper review weakened its position regarding the amendment of Turbek’s trademark application.

The appeal was thus upheld with costs, affirming that Turbek’s registrations should remain intact ([24]).

This ruling highlights the principles surrounding trade mark rights and the importance of establishing concrete evidence of use and reputation in support of claims, particularly in the competitive landscape of trademark law.
