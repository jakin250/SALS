Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# Case Note: Merifon (Pty) Limited v Greater Letaba Municipality and Another [2022] ZACC 25

## F: Facts
This case centers around an application for leave to appeal against a decision made by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which had dismissed an appeal from the High Court ([paras 1–2]). The petitioner, Merifon (Pty) Limited, sought payment of R52 million from the Greater Letaba Municipality (the Municipality). The heart of the dispute was an agreement between Merifon and the Municipality concerning the acquisition of land designated for human settlement development. 

The Municipality found itself in dire need of land to facilitate a public housing programme under the Reconstruction and Development Programme, which had been impeded by its inability to procure suitable land. As a result, the Municipality’s mayor initiated communication with the Provincial Department of Local Government for assistance ([paras 3–4]).

The negotiations led to the identification and eventual sale of the Farm Mooiplaats by City Blox (Pty) Limited to Merifon on 18 April 2012 for R14.5 million. The Municipality later sought to acquire this property, expressing intent through letters from the Head of Department of CoGHSTA and various communications between the parties ([paras 5–8]). 

The crux of this legal dispute arises from the contention that the agreement was never valid due to non-compliance with Section 19 of the Local Government Municipal Finance Management Act 56 of 2003 (MFMA). The Municipality claimed that the agreement was illegal and unenforceable for lack of mandated statutory procedure ([paras 20–21]).

## I: Issues
The fundamental legal issues here include:
1. Whether the contract was legally enforceable given potential non-compliance with statutory requirements stipulated by the MFMA, particularly Section 19 ([para 24]).
2. The applicability of the doctrine of estoppel and the Turquand rule in the context of this case, specifically whether these principles can override statutory limitations imposed on municipal financial obligations ([paras 20–22]).

## R: Rules
The constitutional and statutory rules pertinent to this case include:
1. **Doctrine of Legality** - Public officials must operate within the constraints set by law; failure to comply renders actions ultra vires and thus void ([paras 1–2]).
2. **Section 19 of the MFMA** - This provision stipulates that municipalities may only spend on capital projects if stipulated conditions regarding budget allocation and council approval are met. This section is imperative and relates directly to financial management within local government ([para 18]).
3. **Doctrine of Estoppel** - Prevents parties from departing from previous conduct to the detriment of another party that relied on such conduct ([para 21]).
4. **Turquand Rule** - Affirms that outsiders dealing with a company or entity may assume that internal procedures are complied with unless they have knowledge to the contrary ([para 22]).

## A: Application
The court thoroughly examined whether the agreement constituted a enforceable contract, emphasizing that compliance with the MFMA is a prerequisite for the legality of such municipal agreements ([paras 19–24]). The SCA had upheld the findings of the High Court, affirming that the high hurdles stipulated in Section 19 were not met—the Municipality had neither appropriated the requisite funds nor received necessary approvals, rendering the agreement void from inception ([paras 16–19]).

The argument from Merifon hinged on the claim that it should not be responsible for ensuring compliance with internal processes of the Municipality and that it relied on representations made by the Municipality to enter the contract. However, the courts underscored that estoppel cannot validate actions which contravene statutory provisions; thus, the principle of legality prevailed ([paras 21–22, 43]).

The court also analyzed Merifon’s assertion that they were an innocent third party, positing that the Turquand rule should protect them. However, the court clarified that void acts cannot be validated through the application of this rule, particularly where legality has never been established ([paras 22, 43]).

## C: Conclusion
The Constitutional Court concluded that Merifon had not shown any reasonable prospect of success in its appeal against the SCA's decision. It thus refused leave to appeal and ordered Merifon to pay the costs of the Municipality, which included costs for two counsel. The judgment reinforced the primacy of statutory requirements in municipal law, reiterating that compliance with Section 19 of the MFMA is mandatory for the enforceability of municipal agreements. This ruling has significant implications, emphasizing that municipal entities must adhere strictly to legislative requirements to uphold legality and protect public resources ([paras 1–2, 45–48]). 

The court's unanimous decision also highlights the necessity for clear communication and adherence to statutory processes within municipal governance to prevent financial mismanagement and protect the integrity of public contracting activities.
