Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: TM obo MM v Member of the Executive Council for Health and Social Development, Gauteng [2022] ZACC 18

## F – Facts

The dispute in this matter arises from a claim instituted by Ms. TM in her capacity as the mother and legal guardian of her minor child, MM, who suffered cerebral palsy due to birthing complications. This case involved the failure of medical staff at Charlotte Maxeke Academic Hospital and was initiated after allegations of negligence surfaced ([para 1]).

On 8 July 2010, at a routine antenatal visit, Ms. TM was informed her pregnancy was normal. However, several appointments were disrupted due to a strike by nursing staff, which delayed her access to care ([para 2]). Eventually, she presented at the hospital in active labor on 28 August 2010. Immediate complications arose, leading to an urgent decision for a caesarean section at 16h00. Due to theater availability issues, her procedure did not occur until after 18h15, resulting in MM sustaining a hypoxic ischemic injury during birth, leading to permanent brain damage ([paras 3–4]).

The High Court initially held the respondent responsible for negligence, citing a failure to manage resources adequately, resulting in a delay in treatment ([para 42]). However, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) later overturned this decision, asserting that the applicant failed to prove negligence ([para 54]).

## I – Issues

The primary questions for consideration pertained to:
1. Whether the case raised a constitutional issue of substantial public interest, thus allowing the Constitutional Court to accept jurisdiction ([para 15]).
2. Whether the Supreme Court of Appeal erred in its finding that the respondent was not negligent and consequently not liable for the damages suffered by MM ([para 11]).

## R – Rules

The crucial legal framework involved included:
- Sections 7(2), 27, and 28(2) of the South African Constitution, which guarantee rights to health care and the best interests of children ([para 12]).
- Principles of negligence in tort law: duty of care, breach of that duty, and causation (i.e., the “but for” test) ([para 13]).
- Precedents from similar birth injury cases, particularly the implications of wrongful conduct by public hospitals and the accountability mechanisms therein ([para 20–22]).

## A – Application

The Constitutional Court, led by Justice Mathopo, examined both the factual findings of the SCA and the pleadings from the High Court to determine jurisdiction. It noted that while the SCA acknowledged a duty of care owed to TM and MM, the applicant had not sufficiently established that hospital staff's actions constituted negligence, as many issues such as resource management were not adequately pleaded ([para 44]).

The judgment criticized both the High Court's and the SCA's conclusions on negligence, emphasizing the need for a clearer link between hospital staff's conduct, causation of injury, and adherence to the standard of care expected in such high-stakes medical scenarios. The majority opinion from the SCA provided an insight into how resource limitations affected treatment, which was misapprehended in Ms. TM’s arguments regarding the timelines and operational efficiency of the maternity unit ([paras 69–77]).

Moreover, the court asserted that Ms. TM's proposed constitutional arguments were inadequately established in the pleadings and lacked engagement with the deeper systemic issues that could give rise to a public policy matter ([para 18]). 

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately refused to grant leave to appeal, asserting that the matters presented did not raise significant constitutional issues and that they were fundamentally rooted in factual disputes concerning negligence, which were already decided by the SCA. The court emphasized the principle established in prior cases: challenges rooted solely in factual disparities among lower court decisions do not engage their constitutional jurisdiction ([para 23]). 

The implications of this ruling reaffirm the limitations on public hospital accountability in circumstances where resource management errors do not breach the expected standard of care, particularly within the constraints of the constitution and the intersection of law and public policy. The court also noted that, in the absence of exceptional circumstances, the parties involved would each bear their own costs ([paras 22, 23]). This case reflects a judicial reluctance to extend liability under public health conditions that have systemic resource challenges, requiring claimants assert a more robust case rooted in constitutional principles to invoke court consideration effectively.
