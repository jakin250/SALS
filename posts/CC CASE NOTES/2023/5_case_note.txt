# Case Note: R[…] v R[…] [2023] ZACC 5

## Factual Background

### F – Facts
This case revolves around an appeal regarding contempt of court proceedings and the variation of a divorce decree from the High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Division. The parties, Mr. R[…] (Applicant) and Mrs. R[…] (Respondent), were previously involved in divorce proceedings where various assets and maintenance duties were addressed ([1]–[5]).

The central issue in the contempt application was Mr. R[…] being accused of failing to comply with specific orders from the divorce ruling made on the 23rd of February 2018. This included his alleged failure to remit rental income from jointly-owned property in Mozambique and transferring 40% of his shareholding in his businesses to Mrs. R[…] ([16]–[18]. 

The High Court had originally determined that the parties would share ownership of the property in Mozambique and split any rental income generated therein. Following the contempt proceedings initiated by Mrs. R[…], the High Court held Mr. R[…] in contempt for failure to comply with these orders and unilaterally varied prior orders regarding the transfer of shares and the distribution of assets ([2]–[12], [35]).

### Procedural History
Mr. R[…] initiated an appeal against the High Court’s contempt ruling and its variations, claiming improper procedures and a breach of his constitutional rights ([30]–[31]).

## I – Issues
Several legal questions were brought forth for determination:

1. Was Mr. R[…] in contempt of court based on his failure to comply with the High Court's orders regarding the rental income and shares?
2. Could the High Court unilaterally vary its original divorce order, specifically paragraphs concerning the transfer of shares?
3. Did the actions of the High Court infringe upon Mr. R[…]’s rights under sections 12 and 34 of the Constitution, especially in relation to his right to freedom and security, and the right to a fair trial? ([30]–[42], [71]).

## R – Rules
The relevant legal authorities include:

1. **Matrimonial Causes Act of Zimbabwe, Act No. 33 of 1985**: Section 7 grants judicial discretion to redistribute matrimonial property in divorce proceedings ([8]).
2. **Uniform Rules of Court**: Rule 42(1)(c) allows the court to vary or rescind an earlier order if it was made as a result of a common mistake ([49]).
3. **Constitution of South Africa**: Sections 12 and 34 ensure the rights to freedom and security, and the right to justiciability in judicial proceedings ([30], [35]).

### Case Law
- **Coetzee v Government of the Republic of South Africa**: Established that imprisonment for failure to pay a debt is unconstitutional ([44]).
- **Jayiya v Member of the Executive Council for Welfare, Eastern Cape**: Reaffirmed that money judgments are not typically enforced through contempt proceedings, except in maintenance cases ([45]).

## A – Application
In analyzing the contempt proceedings, the Constitutional Court found the following:

1. **Claim of Contempt**: The Court held that the High Court’s order in paragraph 2.3 was declaratory, which did not impose a legal obligation on Mr. R[…] to pay Mrs. R[…] the rental income, as it merely affirmed their co-ownership status of the property. Therefore, his failure to remit the rent did not constitute contempt ([37]–[40]).

2. **Imposition of Imprisonment**: The Court determined that the conditional sentence of imprisonment imposed by the High Court was unconstitutional. It echoed principles established in *Coetzee*, stating that civil debts should not result in imprisonment without a clear and direct legal obligation to comply with a court order ([44]–[45]).

3. **Variation of the Divorce Order**: The High Court's decision to unilaterally amend the divorce decree was deemed inappropriate. The Constitutional Court found that the variation made by the High Court lacked procedural fairness. The Court emphasized that no mistake had been established under Rule 42(1)(c), and the High Court, therefore, acted outside its competency ([49]–[60]).

4. **Failure to Provide Notice**: The High Court's unilateral decisions were criticized for not providing the parties the opportunity to address proposed changes, thereby breaching procedural fairness requirements outlined in Rule 42(3) ([69]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court upheld the appeal, concluding that:

1. Mr. R[…] was not in contempt of the High Court’s orders as they did not impose a direct obligation to pay Mrs. R[…] the rental income.
2. The High Court lacked the jurisdiction to amend the divorce order without proper procedural safeguards.
3. The decision rendered by the High Court was inconsistent with established constitutional principles, especially regarding fair trial rights. 

Accordingly, the Court set aside the High Court's orders related to contempt and variations, substituting them with an order stating that the application for contempt was dismissed and each party would bear their own legal costs ([74]–[75]). The ruling underscores the importance of procedural fairness and adherence to constitutional principles in judicial determinations.
