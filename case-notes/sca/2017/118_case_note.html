Supreme Court of Appeal

Drift Supersand (Pty) Ltd v Mogale City Local Municipality (1185/2016) [2017] ZASCA 118

A lengthy procedural history illustrates that the township application was submitted by the second respondent, Greenville Gardens CC, in 2006.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 118

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case arises from an administrative dispute regarding the approval of a township establishment application by Mogale City Local Municipality (the Municipality), which was approved on 28 August 2012. The appellant, Drift Supersand (Pty) Ltd, owns several properties adjacent to the proposed township site, known as Portion 33 of the farm Roodekrans 183 IQ ([1]). The appellant contended that it was an interested party entitled to an opportunity to oppose the application based on its proximity to the proposed development, which could adversely affect its quarrying operations ([2]).

A lengthy procedural history illustrates that the township application was submitted by the second respondent, Greenville Gardens CC, in 2006. The appellant first became aware of the township proposal during a public participation process conducted under the National Environmental Management Act on 18 May 2012 ([4]). Following this, the appellant raised objections, stating that the approval of the township was irrational and procedurally unfair, primarily due to the Municipality's failure to notify them adequately and inadequately consider their objections ([5], [6]).

The Gauteng Local Division dismissed the appellant's application to have the approval reviewed, prompting an appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([1]). This appeal concerned standing to challenge the township approval, procedural fairness, and the Municipality's obligations under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([7], [20]).

I – Issues

The key issues in this case pertained to:

  1. The standing of the appellant to challenge the decision made by the Municipality regarding the proposed township ([8], [20]).
  2. The procedural fairness of the approval process, specifically whether the appellant was entitled to a hearing due to its status as an interested party ([21], [27]).
  3. The relevance of the appellant’s alleged failure to exhaust internal remedies before seeking judicial review as stipulated in PAJA ([44], [46]).

R – Rules

The legal framework governing this case includes:

  • The Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) which mandates that administrative action must be lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair ([7]).
  • The Town Planning and Townships Ordinance 15 of 1986, specifically Section 69, which delineates the procedures for township applications, including requirements for notifications and participation ([5], [22]).
  • Relevant case law establishing principles around procedural fairness, legitimate expectation to a hearing, and standing, including Joseph & others v City of Johannesburg and JDJ Properties CC v Umngeni Local Municipality ([8], [20], [28]).

A – Application

The court first determined standing under PAJA, establishing that the appellant, as the owner of nearby properties, had sufficient interest to challenge the township approval. The court rejected the respondents' argument that only the appellant’s subsidiary, which held the mining rights, could claim standing ([9], [20]). Thus, the court held that the appellant had the right to safeguard its property interests against the potential detrimental impact of the township development on its quarrying activities ([11], [12]).

The court also examined the procedural fairness of the decision-making process. The appellant argued that it had a legitimate expectation to be heard based on assurances given by a municipal official. The court accepted this assertion and found that the Municipality’s failure to invite the appellant to a tribunal hearing constituted a breach of procedural fairness ([35], [43]). The court highlighted that the Municipality instead ignored relevant objections by suggesting the application was unopposed, despite having received the appellant's objections ([38], [42]).

The final issue addressed was whether the appellant was obliged to pursue internal remedies before seeking judicial review. The court determined that the appellant had been excluded from the decision-making process and was not necessitated to appeal under section 104 of the Ordinance since it was not afforded the opportunity to participate meaningfully in the process ([46], [48]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the appeal by Drift Supersand (Pty) Ltd was justified, setting aside the approval of the township application and emphasizing the Municipality's failure to adhere to procedural fairness requirements mandated by PAJA and the Ordinance. The court's judgment underscored the importance of the public's participation in local governance decisions that could significantly affect property owners and operators in proximity to such developments ([55]). The implications of this case highlight the necessity for municipalities to ensure compliance with procedural fairness obligations, thus fostering trust and accountability in public decision-making processes.

This case illustrates a reinforcing precedent in administrative law regarding standing, procedural fairness, and the legitimate expectation to be heard, ensuring that administrative bodies remain accountable to affected parties under the rule of law.
