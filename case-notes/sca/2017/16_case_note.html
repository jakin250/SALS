Supreme Court of Appeal

Sentrachem Limited v Terreblanche (2017)

The central dispute in this case involved a pension fund's erroneous double payment to Mr.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 16 (22 March 2017) Coram: Leach, Majiedt, Ma

Neutral Citation: Sentrachem v Terreblanche (237/2016) [2017] ZASCA 16 (22 March 2017)

Coram: Leach, Majiedt, Mathopo and Mocumie JJA and Schippers AJA

F – Facts ([paras 1-4])

The central dispute in this case involved a pension fund's erroneous double payment to Mr. A.L. Terreblanche. Mr. Terreblanche was a member of the Sentrachem Group Pension Fund, which, during implementation of a surplus apportionment scheme, mistakenly paid him two sums on October 6, 2010: R94,614.99, which was not contested, and R453,872.31, which became the subject of legal action. The pension fund claimed under the condictio indebiti, asserting that the second payment was made in error believing it was due to him.

On December 13, 2011, the fund was approved for voluntary dissolution, leading to Mr. Jeremy Andrew being appointed as liquidator. After a cession agreement between Mr. Andrew and Sentrachem on September 27, 2013, Sentrachem sought to substitute MR Andrew as the plaintiff, which was formally executed on December 3, 2013. Terreblanche raised a special plea of prescription, arguing that the claim had become prescribed under section 11(d) of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 because more than three years had elapsed since the debt became due.

I – Issues ([paras 1, 5, 7])

The primary legal issue to be determined was whether the substitution of Sentrachem as the plaintiff after litis contestatio resulted in a valid prescription plea, considering the claim initiated by the original creditor (the pension fund) had not been pursued to final judgment prior to the substitution. Furthermore, the court analyzed the implications of whether the cession, occurring after litis contestatio, introduced a new creditor pursuing a claim after the three-year prescription period.

R – Rules ([paras 8-9, 12-16])

The legal basis for the court's analysis stemmed from the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, particularly sections 11(d), which presents a three-year prescription period for claims of this nature, and section 15 concerning judicial interruption of prescription, which emphasizes that service on the debtor of a process claiming the debt interrupts the prescription period.

The court referred to Van Rensburg v Condoprops 42 (Pty) Ltd 2009 (6) SA 539 (E) and Fisher v Natal Rubber Compounders (Pty) Ltd [2016] ZASCA 33 as foundational cases establishing that the identity of the creditor does not affect the underlying debt prescribed, provided that the procedural aspects of the claim remain uninterrupted.

A – Application ([paras 14-17])

Upon application of the rules to the circumstances of the case, the court determined that Sentrachem's substitution as plaintiff did not constitute the commencement of new proceedings. The claim remained continuous and unaltered, despite the creditor change from the pension fund to Mr. Andrew and finally to Sentrachem. The court highlighted that prescription had been effectively interrupted by the initial summons served upon Terreblanche and thus maintained validity throughout the subsequent procedural changes.

Furthermore, the court rejected Terreblanche's argument regarding impoverishment, establishing that the property right and obligation introduced by the cession continued as predicate, pointing out that Sentrachem acquired the right to enforce a claim where the original creditor had been impoverished due to the erroneous payment.

C – Conclusion ([paras 18])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Sentrachem’s appeal, concluding that the special plea of prescription was dismissible. The court ruled that the substitution of the plaintiff did not introduce a new claim and therefore did not affect the continuation of proceeding against the debt, which had remained unaltered. Moreover, the argument suggesting that Sentrachem itself was not impoverished was dismissed. The implications of this ruling reaffirm the legal principle that a valid cession of a claim operates to confer all rights and obligations of the original creditor onto the cessionary, preserving the legal efficacy of the original claim during the entire duration of litigation.

Order:

  1. The appeal is upheld with costs.
  2. The court a quo's order is set aside and replaced with a judgment against Terreblanche for R453,972.31 with interest at 15.5% per annum from July 11, 2011, until payment in full.

This case affirms the continuity of legal standing and procedural integrity prior to and after the substitution of parties in a creditor-debtor context, highlighting the crucial role of the original service process in interrupting prescription.
