Khantsho Llale v The State (663/2016) [2017] ZASCA 049

During the proceedings in the Regional Court of Gauteng, Johannesburg, the co-accused were acquitted of the wheel cap theft charge at the close of the State's case ([2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 049

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The appellant, Mr. Khantsho Llale, was arrested on August 17, 2013, at FNB Stadium alongside his co-accused. Following this, they were charged with two counts: one for the attempted theft of a white Toyota Hilux and another for stealing three wheel caps from a different vehicle ([1]).

During the proceedings in the Regional Court of Gauteng, Johannesburg, the co-accused were acquitted of the wheel cap theft charge at the close of the State's case ([2]). However, on May 12, 2014, all three were convicted of the attempted theft of the vehicle, receiving three years' imprisonment each. They subsequently applied for leave to appeal against their conviction and sentence, which was denied ([2]).

The appellant's application for leave was separated and rejected on May 27, 2015, even though his co-accused's appeals were successful ([3]). Consequently, on June 13, 2016, Mr. Llale sought special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) against the dismissal of his petition ([3]).

I – Issues

The primary legal question was whether there were reasonable prospects of success in Mr. Llale's appeal against his conviction, and whether special circumstances existed that warranted the granting of special leave to appeal to the high court ([4]). The fact that his co-accused succeeded in their appeal added an additional layer to the assessment of special circumstances ([4]).

R – Rules

The court relied on several legal principles, including the requirements for leave to appeal as outlined in section 309C of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977. This section allows an accused to petition the Judge President for leave to appeal if a lower court refuses such a request ([3],[4]).

Moreover, the judgment referenced precedents such as S v Van Wyk & Another and the unreported decision Zulu v S, underscoring the need for a thorough examination of the sufficiency of evidence against the standard of conviction ([5]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal evaluated the credibility of the witness, Mr. Scheepers, whose testimony had been pivotal in the trial court's conviction. The court found that the trial court had not sufficiently considered the inconsistencies between the witness's written statements and his oral testimony ([4]). Given the reliance on a single witness where the evidence was deemed not compelling, the court identified reasonable prospects of success on appeal for Mr. Llale ([4]).

In determining special circumstances, the outcome of his co-accused’s appeal played a crucial role, as the fact that their convictions were set aside implied potential flaws in the case against Mr. Llale ([4]). The court concluded that these aspects justified the granting of special leave to appeal, as they indicated that Mr. Llale's situation was similarly precarious.

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Mr. Llale's appeal and set aside the order of the court a quo, substituting it with an order granting his petition for leave to appeal in terms of section 309C of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977. The court emphasized the relevance of the single witness's unreliable testimony and the recent success of the co-accused's appeals ([6]).

The case illustrates the importance of rigorous judicial scrutiny concerning witness credibility and the fair administration of justice, particularly in criminal convictions based on potentially flawed or singular evidence. The implications of this judgment extend beyond Mr. Llale's case, highlighting the judicial system's obligation to rectify miscarriages of justice where substantial doubt exists.

Thus, the SCA's decision not only enables Mr. Llale to challenge his conviction but also reinforces the principle that the integrity of the trial process must be upheld, ensuring that any conviction is firmly grounded in reliable evidence.
