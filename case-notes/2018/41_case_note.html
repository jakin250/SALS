Maledu and Others v Itereleng Bakgatla Mineral Resources (Pty) Limited and Another [2018] ZACC 41

The first and second respondents, Itereleng Bakgatla Mineral Resources (Pty) Limited and Pilanesberg Platinum Mines (Pty) Limited, held mining rights over the land.

Constitutional Court 2018 [2018] ZACC 41

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

In Maledu and Others v Itereleng Bakgatla Mineral Resources (Pty) Limited, the applicants, including Grace Masele Mpane Maledu and thirty-seven others, represented informal rights holders of land in the North West Province of South Africa. They alleged historical ownership of a farm, known as Wilgespruit 2 J.Q., asserting it was rightfully purchased by their ancestors in 1919. Due to past racially discriminatory laws, the land was registered in the name of the Minister of Rural Development and Land Reform, holding it in trust for the Bakgatla-Ba-Kgafela community.

The first and second respondents, Itereleng Bakgatla Mineral Resources (Pty) Limited and Pilanesberg Platinum Mines (Pty) Limited, held mining rights over the land. They sought to evict the applicants from the farm citing the exercise of their mining rights, which they claimed was legally condoned under the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA).

The High Court initially granted the respondents’ eviction application, ruling that the applicants had no rightful claim to the property. The applicants contested this decision, arguing they were not only occupiers but rightful owners, and that the respondents had failed to consult them as required by both the MPRDA and the Interim Protection of Informal Land Rights Act 31 of 1996 (IPILRA).

I – Issues

The key legal issues before the Constitutional Court included:

  1. Whether the applicants, as informal land rights holders, had to be consulted before the grant of the mining rights and the subsequent eviction.
  2. Whether the respondents were required to exhaust internal processes under section 54 of the MPRDA before seeking eviction.
  3. Whether the eviction constituted a deprivation of the applicants' informal rights in terms of IPILRA.
  4. The implications of the community’s decision to allow mining operations on communal land according to customary law.

R – Rules

The matter implicates several statutory and constitutional provisions:

  • Section 25(6) of the Constitution: It provides that a person or community whose tenure of land is insecure due to past racially discriminatory practices is entitled to legally secure tenure or comparable redress.
  • Section 54 of the MPRDA: This legislation stipulates that mining rights cannot be exercised without notifying the Regional Manager if access to the land is obstructed. It implies a need for consultation and fairness in engaging with land rights holders.
  • Section 2 of IPILRA: It protects informal rights holders against deprivation without consent and requires adherence to community customs when such rights are disposed of.
  • Customary Law: Under section 211 of the Constitution, recognizing the role of traditional leadership and customary law systems in land tenure, while ensuring such systems are consistent with constitutional norms.

A – Application

The Constitutional Court, addressing the issues, analyzed whether the respondents had complied with the statutory obligations to consult with the applicants. It reaffirmed that formal property rights do not extinguish underlying informal rights without due process. The Court highlighted the need for the respondents to utilize internal remedies outlined in section 54 of the MPRDA before proceeding with court actions regarding eviction.

In reviewing the consultative process, the Court found that the High Court's reliance on the assertion that the applicants were consulted was insufficient to meet the standards of meaningful engagement prescribed by both the MPRDA and IPILRA. The idea of "free, prior and informed consent" under international law, particularly concerning consultation with affected communities, was emphasized, illustrating the necessity for community participation in decisions that impact their land rights.

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court upheld the appeal by the applicants, setting aside the High Court's eviction order and determining that the respondents had not validly extinguished the applicants' informal land rights. The Court concluded that the respondents were indeed required to exhaust internal processes under section 54 of the MPRDA prior to evicting the applicants. It reaffirmed the importance of consultation as a fundamental right for informal land holders and emphasized the need for a balanced approach in protecting both the rights of mining operations and the tenure security of historically disadvantaged communities.

The ruling has broader implications for land rights in South Africa, emphasizing the need for regulations that equitably address the interests of both mining companies and local communities. It sets a precedent for the application of customary law in land rights, ensuring that the voices of affected communities cannot be overlooked in favor of economic advancement.
