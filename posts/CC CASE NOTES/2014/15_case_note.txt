Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: S v Molaudzi [2014] ZACC 15

**F – Facts**

The case of *S v Molaudzi* ([1]) concerns the applicant, Thembekile Molaudzi, who was convicted alongside several co-accused for the murder and robbery of an off-duty policeman and for illegal possession of a firearm and ammunition. The North West High Court in Mahikeng sentenced the applicant to life imprisonment for murder and an additional 21 years for the other charges. Following the conviction, the applicant sought and was granted leave to appeal to the Full Court, but this appeal was subsequently dismissed. The Supreme Court of Appeal also refused further leave to appeal. 

The applicant subsequently submitted an application to the Constitutional Court seeking leave to appeal on the basis that he was wrongfully convicted. However, the court noted that the application mostly revolved around disputing the factual findings of the trial, which does not constitute a constitutional issue needed for review by the Constitutional Court ([2]). Additional details revealed that there was a significant delay in obtaining the trial record, which the applicant claimed impeded his ability to mount an effective appeal. The trial record was not signed until October 2008, and the complete record was only received in 2012—eight years post-conviction ([4]).

**I – Issues**

The primary legal issue presented in this case is whether the Constitutional Court should grant leave to appeal in light of the applicant's claims of wrongful conviction based on an alleged factual dispute. Secondary issues include the implications of the unacceptable delay in providing the trial record and whether such delays infringe upon the applicant’s fair trial rights as encapsulated in Section 35(3)(d) of the South African Constitution, which guarantees the right to a fair trial without unreasonable delay ([5]).

**R – Rules**

The Constitutional Court primarily relied on existing legal precedents and statutory provisions regarding fair trial rights under the Constitution. Notably, the court referred to *Mbatha v University of Zululand* [2013] ZACC 43, *Phoebus Apollo Aviation CC v Minister of Safety and Security* [2002] ZACC 26, and *S v Boesak* [2000] ZACC 25 to underscore its point that disputes regarding factual findings in lower courts do not typically constitute constitutional matters appropriate for the Constitutional Court's jurisdiction ([2]). 

Furthermore, the court invoked Section 35(3)(d) of the Constitution, which emphasizes the right to a fair trial, including the necessity for trials to conclude without unreasonable delay, as underscored in *Sanderson v Attorney-General, Eastern Cape* [1997] ZACC 18 ([5]).

**A – Application**

Upon applying the legal principles to the facts, the court emphasized that the applicant's appeal was untenable because it fundamentally rested on a challenge to the factual determinations made by the trial court, which does not meet the threshold for a constitutional issue ([2]). The court expressed concern over the lengthy period taken to process the appeal and obtain the trial record, recognizing that such delays could compromise the fairness of the trial process; however, it concluded that the record was ultimately completed and available in a form suitable for appeal ([5]).

The court further acknowledged the egregious nature of the delay, highlighting the duty of judicial officers to prevent such occurrences. The mention of intervention by legal aid organizations, like the Mahikeng Justice Centre and Lawyers for Human Rights, illustrated the procedural obstacles faced by defendants in obtaining essential documents necessary for a fair appeal ([4]).

**C – Conclusion**

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court dismissed the application for leave to appeal, confirming that the applicant’s claims did not raise a constitutional issue warranting their intervention ([6]). The court's ruling reflected its stance that while judicial delays are a serious concern that may undermine trial fairness, in this instance, the eventual provision of a complete trial record allowed for a fair appellate review. The court also directed the Registrar to inform relevant legal bodies of their ruling, emphasizing the obligation of judicial authorities to uphold the integrity of the trial process and mitigate delays in the future. The result indicates a reaffirmation of the separation between factual disputes and constitutional issues, with significant implications for both future cases and the treatment of procedural justice within the South African legal system.
