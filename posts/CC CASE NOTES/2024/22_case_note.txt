# Case Note: Botha v Smuts and Another [2024] ZACC 22

## F – Facts
The case arises from a dispute between Herman Botha (the Applicant) and Bool Smuts along with Landmark Leopard and Predator Project – South Africa (the First and Second Respondents respectively). Mr. Botha is an insurance broker and farmer based in Gqeberha, Eastern Cape, who owns the farm Varsfontein in Alicedale. Mr. Smuts is a conservationist and the founder of the Second Respondent, which focuses on human-wildlife conflict management. The Campaign for Free Expression NPC participated as amicus curiae to emphasize free expression rights.

The central incident occurred when Nicolaas Louw, while cycling through Botha’s farm on 23 September 2019, encountered trapped animals. Outraged, he shared photographs and information with Mr. Smuts, who then contacted Mr. Botha demanding clarification on his trapping permits. After receiving unsatisfactory responses, Mr. Smuts published a post on social media on 9 October 2019, which included photographs of the trapped animals and comments about Botha's trapping practices, alongside Mr. Botha’s home and office address.

Following this publication, Mr. Botha sought urgent relief from the High Court, claiming that the information disclosed was defamatory, intended to undermine his reputation, and posed a risk to his family’s safety. The High Court issued a rule nisi prohibiting the Respondents from further publishing references to Botha, as well as ordering the removal of the original post.

On appeal, the Supreme Court of Appeal reversed this ruling, stating that Mr. Botha's right to privacy had not been infringed, primarily because the information he contested was already in the public domain.

## I – Issues
The primary legal issue in this case pertains to the tension between the right to privacy (section 14 of the Constitution) and freedom of expression (section 16). The Court needed to decide:
1. Did Mr. Botha hold a reasonable expectation of privacy regarding his home and business address in light of their public availability and his previous disclosures?
2. Did the right to freedom of expression supersede any privacy concerns Mr. Botha may have had?

## R – Rules
The relevant constitutional provisions frame the rights in dispute:
1. **Right to Privacy**: Section 14 ensures the right to privacy, which includes control over personal information and protection against unwanted intrusions.
2. **Freedom of Expression**: Section 16 protects the right to freedom of expression, encompassing the dissemination of information in public interest, particularly about matters such as animal welfare and environmental protection.

The Court relied on the dual expectation test established in Bernstein v Bester [1996] ZACC 2, which combines subjective and objective analyses of privacy expectations. Furthermore, international standards regarding privacy, digital information dissemination, and the implications of social media activities were acknowledged as relevant.

## A – Application
The Constitutional Court examined both Mr. Botha’s expectations of privacy in light of the facts presented. In analyzing the ownership of his farm and the practice of trapping, the Court concluded that:

1. **Ownership and Control of Farm**: There was no subjectively reasonable expectation of privacy regarding Mr. Botha’s ownership and control over the farm due to the public nature of his activities and the legal permissions he held for trapping. This was bolstered by fact that the name and location of the farm were recorded in public registries.
   
2. **Insurance Brokerage Address**: Pertaining to Mr. Botha’s business address, the Court acknowledged that he had intentionally published this information through several online platforms. Thus, he could not maintain a reasonable expectation of privacy regarding this information, as it was voluntarily shared to promote his business.

3. **Personal Home Address**: The Court found that Mr. Botha did hold an expectation of privacy over his home address, especially as it served as a place where he lived with his family. The ongoing publication of this information, coupled with the aggressive public responses that the post elicited, created a reasonable apprehension of potential harassment and intrusion.

4. **Balancing Rights**: The Court balanced Mr. Botha’s right to privacy against the Respondents’ right to freedom of expression. The Court concluded that while freedom of expression is paramount, it does not justify the invasion of intimate privacy, particularly as related to one’s home. The publication of Mr. Botha’s home address was deemed an unreasonable intrusion on privacy rights, and the potential for harm to Mr. Botha and his family was significant.

## C – Conclusion
The Court ultimately ruled in a split decision, granting leave to appeal and partially upholding Mr. Botha's appeal. The Supreme Court of Appeal's order was set aside, with the provisions that:
1. The respondents were directed to delete any reference to Mr. Botha’s home address from the Facebook post.
2. The respondents were prohibited from publishing any further references to Mr. Botha's home address.
3. The parties were ordered to bear their own costs throughout all stages of litigation.

This decision underscores the delicate balance between privacy rights and freedom of expression, particularly in the age of digital information and social media, paving the way for future jurisprudence in similar cases. The judgments reflect a growing recognition of privacy even when information is made public, particularly concerning deeply personal contexts.
