Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: Kgoro Consortium (Pty) Ltd and Another v Cedar Park Properties 39 (Pty) Ltd and Others (935/2020) [2022] ZASCA 65

## F – Facts: Background and Procedural History

[1] On June 30, 2020, the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg, dismissed an application for placing Cedar Park Properties 39 (Pty) Ltd (Cedar Park) under supervision to commence business rescue proceedings. The application was initiated by Kgoro Consortium (Pty) Ltd (Kgoro), with Regiments Capital (Pty) Ltd (in liquidation) (Regiments) supporting the application. Vantage Mezzanine Fund II Partnership (Vantage) opposed the application. The High Court ordered Smit Sewgoolam Incorporated (Smit Sewgoolam), attorneys for the appellants, to pay Vantage’s costs de bonis propriis on the attorney and client scale due to alleged misconduct.

[2] Cedar Park, a wholly-owned subsidiary of Kgoro, was intended as a vehicle for property acquisition and development in Sandton, Gauteng, valued at R280 million. On June 5, 2013, Cedar Park entered a loan facility agreement with Vantage for R150 million, which matured on June 30, 2018, but Cedar Park defaulted. Following this, Vantage initiated liquidation proceedings against Cedar Park due to outstanding debt exceeding R300 million. Kgoro preemptively filed for Cedar Park's business rescue on February 15, 2019. Vantage challenged this move, asserting that Kgoro lacked locus standi, as they had ceded shares in Cedar Park to Vantage as collateral.

[3] The appellants aimed to justify business rescue under Section 131(4)(a) of the Companies Act 71 of 2008, arguing Cedar Park was financially distressed yet still had reasonable prospects for recovery. They presented the project as valuable, estimating market value and indicating potential interest from buyers. Conversely, Vantage contended that no reasonable prospects for rescue existed, emphasizing the appellants’ delay in proceedings and the clear financial distress faced by Cedar Park.

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions

[4] The core issue in the Kgoro appeal was whether Kgoro had adequately established that Cedar Park was capable of being rescued as per the criteria outlined in Section 131(4)(a) of the Companies Act. Specifically, this included determining whether Cedar Park could be shown to have reasonable prospects for recovery and whether Kgoro had the necessary standing to initiate the business rescue proceedings.

[5] In the Smit Sewgoolam appeal, the issue revolved around whether the High Court was justified in ordering Smit Sewgoolam to pay costs de bonis propriis and refer their conduct to the Gauteng Legal Practice Council based on alleged negligence for failing to disclose relevant prior court orders.

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents

[6] Section 131(4) of the Companies Act provides that the court may place a company under supervision and commence business rescue proceedings if certain conditions are satisfied, including financial distress and the presence of reasonable prospects for rescue. Relevant precedents addressing the interpretation of reasonable prospect, including *Oakdene Square Properties (Pty) Ltd v Farm Bothasfontein (Kyalami) (Pty) Ltd* [2013] ZASCA 68 and *Newcity Group (Pty) Ltd v Pellow NO* [2014] ZASCA 162, illustrate the threshold required to demonstrate reasonable prospects—higher than a mere prima facie case yet lower than reasonable probability.

[7] The courts have previously emphasized the necessity for applicants seeking business rescue to present substantial evidence outlining a feasible plan for recovery, as vagueness and speculation are insufficient to meet the statutory requirements (*Southern Palace Investments 265 (Pty) Ltd v Midnight Storm Investments 386 (Pty) Ltd* 2012 (2) SA 423 (WCC)).

## A – Application: Analysis of the Court's Reasoning

[8] The appellate court analyzed whether Kgoro had succeeded in demonstrating the requisite reasonable prospect of Cedar Park being rescued. Despite acknowledging Cedar Park's financial distress, the court found that Kgoro's application failed to establish how business rescue would lead to a solvent continuation or better returns for creditors compared to immediate liquidation.

[9] The court also discussed Kgoro's reliance on property valuation and potential sales, clarifying that mere potential interest in selling the development did not satisfy the burden of proof needed under Section 128(1)(b). Kgoro had not provided concrete details on financing the proposed development or maintaining Cedar Park's status as a solvent entity, leading the court to conclude that they failed to lay a material foundation for the claim of reasonable prospects.

[10] The court further dismissed Vantage's application to introduce supplementary arguments regarding alleged misconduct associated with Smit Sewgoolam, affirming that the court could not rely on unverified external reports.

[11] In the Smit Sewgoolam appeal, the court decided that the punitive costs order and referral for misconduct lacked justification. The order against Smit Sewgoolam for costs de bonis propriis did not permit them the opportunity to respond adequately to the allegations, violating the principles of fair hearing, thus leading to the overturning of that specific order.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications

[12] The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the Kgoro appeal, concluding that Kgoro had not met the necessary legal threshold to place Cedar Park in business rescue, highlighting the importance of providing concrete evidence of reasonable prospects for recovery.

[13] Conversely, the court upheld the appeal by Smit Sewgoolam, set aside the costs order against them, and reinforced the necessity for fairness in judicial processes, specifically regarding the opportunity for legal representation and response.

[14] The implications of this judgment underline the stringent requirements for initiating business rescue proceedings in South Africa and the necessity for attorneys and parties involved to act with transparency and diligence, as established procedural safeguards play a critical role in maintaining judicial integrity.
