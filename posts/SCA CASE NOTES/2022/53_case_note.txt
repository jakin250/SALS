Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

### Case Note: Loskop Landgoed Boerdery (Pty) Ltd and Others v Petrus Moeleso and Others (390/2021) [2022] ZASCA 53

**FIRAC Method**

---

#### F - Facts [Paras 1–9]

The case concerns an appeal from the Land Claims Court (LCC) regarding the reduction of grazing camps allocated to the respondents, Petrus Moeleso and others, on the farm Barnea 231 in Bethlehem, Free State. The owner of the farm, Willem Adriaan Pieters, along with Loskop Boerdery (Pty) Ltd, represented by his son Riaan Pieters, argued that the grazing areas allotted to the respondents were significantly overgrazed, necessitating their reduction from three camps to one. 

The respondents alleged they had initially inherited livestock and were historically permitted to utilize three grazing camps. However, following an ecological assessment indicating overgrazing, the appellants instructed the respondents to remove their cattle. When the respondents refused, the appellants unilaterally moved the cattle to another camp without a court order, resulting in the contention that the respondents were effectively evicted, albeit indirectly. Hence, they sought the LCC's intervention for restoration of grazing rights and for a declaration of the appellants' conduct as unlawful.

In the LCC, the court found in favour of the respondents, ordering the restoration of their rights to the grazing camps and deeming the appellants' actions unlawful. Leave to appeal regarding the first, second, and costs order was granted.

---

#### I - Issues [Paras 9–10]

The primary legal issue concerns whether the appellants' reduction of the grazing area constituted an illegal eviction under the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA) and whether the appellants had the right to unilaterally remove cattle due to overgrazing under the Conservation of Agricultural Resources Act 43 of 1983 (CARA). A secondary issue arose regarding the correctness of the LCC's order, given that it had not been subjected to proper legal grounds as per the pleadings from either party.

---

#### R - Rules [Paras 11–18]

The legal framework relevant to this case involves provisions under the Extension of Security of Tenure Act (ESTA) and the Conservation of Agricultural Resources Act (CARA). ESTA provides protections for "occupiers," delineating their rights to reside on land and use it for specific purposes, including grazing under certain conditions [para 15].

The court in **Adendorffs Boerderye v Shabalala** elucidated that an occupier's rights to graze livestock are derivative and contingent upon consent from the landowner. The actions taken against the respondents were questioned under the lens of possession rights and whether the appellants' acts amounted to spoliation, thus invoking the **mandament van spolie** principles established in **Nino Bonino v De Lange** and **Bon Quelle (Edms) Bpk v Munisipaliteit van Otavi**.

Further, the jurisprudence established in **Minister of Rural Development and Land Reform v Normandien Farms** elucidated the circumstances under which eviction can occur and the requisite legal framework involved when removing livestock from grazing land, specifically concerning obligations for rehabilitation of farmland and the rights of occupancy.

---

#### A - Application [Paras 10–22]

The appeal court critically examined the LCC's determination that the appellants' actions constituted eviction without a court order, thereby implicating ESTA. The court underscored that respondents were not evicted in the traditional sense; rather, their cattle were relocated to another grazing area on the same property. The court clarified that the legal definition of “eviction” under ESTA implied deprivation of residence or linked access to land or resources, not merely the relocation of livestock.

The court expressly disagreed with the LCC's conclusion regarding unlawfulness and considered the appellants' obligation under CARA to manage land sustainably. It articulated that both the landowners and occupiers bear responsibilities concerning land use that extends beyond mere occupation, emphasizing that any removal conducted must adhere to legal protocols like obtaining necessary court orders.

Furthermore, the court criticized the LCC for granting remedies beyond those submitted in pleadings, asserting that the scope of a court’s order should not exceed the parameters set by the dispute’s framing. It emphasized the principle of ambiguity and vagueness undermining the legitimacy of the judgment granted by the LCC.

Lastly, it was established that the actions of the appellants, characterized as spoliation, warranted the restoration of the respondents' grazing rights—an order aligning with the **mandament van spolie** as established in precedent cases.

---

#### C - Conclusion [Paras 23–24]

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld parts of the LCC's order while amending others, confirming that the respondents had been wrongfully stripped of their grazing rights without due process. The appeal partially succeeded: the court reaffirmed the invalidity of the appellants' unilateral actions regarding livestock removal and directed the restoration of the original terms of possession concerning both grazing camps. 

Each party was ordered to bear its own costs, countering the LCC’s initial decision, reinforcing the principle that costs in LCC proceedings should be exceptional. Importantly, the judgments served to clarify the delineations of rights under ESTA in relation to grazing permissions and affirmed the necessity of adhering to statutory frameworks governing land management. The implications highlight the need for landowners to operate within the legal bounds when addressing agricultural resource issues, reiterating the importance of judicial remedies in maintaining tenure security for occupiers.

--- 

This case encapsulates significant legal principles surrounding land tenure, possession, and the statutory obligations inherent in managing agricultural resources, thus serving as an important precedent in South African property law and tenure rights.
