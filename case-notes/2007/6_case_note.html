NM and Others v Smith and Others [2007] ZACC 6

In March 2002, "Patricia de Lille," a biography authored by Charlene Smith and published by New Africa Books (Pty) Ltd, included the identities of three women, NM, SM, and LH, who are HIV positive.

Constitutional Court 2007 [2007] ZACC 6

FIRAC Method

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([1]–[20])

In March 2002, "Patricia de Lille," a biography authored by Charlene Smith and published by New Africa Books (Pty) Ltd, included the identities of three women, NM, SM, and LH, who are HIV positive. The inclusion of their names was made without their consent, leading the women to claim that their rights to privacy, dignity, and psychological integrity were violated. Consequently, they initiated legal proceedings against Smith and De Lille, which were dismissed by the Johannesburg High Court on 13 May 2005. The High Court ordered the publisher to pay R15,000 in damages and mandated the removal of the applicants' names from unsold copies of the book. Neither the applicants nor the respondents contested several aspects of the judgment, including the dismissal of claims against Smith and De Lille ([1]–[6]).

Following the High Court’s ruling, the applicants sought leave to appeal, which was denied twice — first by the High Court and later by the Supreme Court of Appeal without providing reasons ([10]–[20]).

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([21]–[30])

The Constitutional Court was faced with several key issues:

  1. Whether the legal issues raised constituted constitutional matters warranting adjudication.
  2. Whether the disclosure of the applicants’ identities and HIV status constituted the publication of private facts.
  3. Whether the disclosure was wrongful and done with intent to harm the applicants.
  4. Whether the common law should evolve to impose liability on those who negligently publish confidential information.
  5. The appropriate quantum of damages should liability be established ([21]).

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([21]–[30])

The court referenced relevant constitutional provisions, particularly Section 14, which guarantees the right to privacy. The court also discussed the common-law action of injuria, which protects privacy, dignity, and psychological integrity, especially concerning the publication of private facts. Notably, it drew upon Bernstein and Others v Bester NNO and Others to explore the contours of privacy rights. The principles established in National Media Ltd and Another v Jooste were critical to understanding the legal boundaries regarding the publication of private facts. The court had the authority to develop common law per Section 39(2) of the Constitution ([29]).

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([31]–[60])

The court examined whether the applicants’ HIV statuses were privately held secrets. It determined that such personal medical information warranted protection due to its sensitive nature. The respondents’ claim that the information was public knowledge through the Strauss Report was dismissed, as the court emphasized that attending meetings with the media did not equate to consent for publication in a book.

Ultimately, the court found that the first respondent, Smith, acted negligently in assuming consent existed due to a flawed understanding of the Strauss Report, and she could have used pseudonyms in the publication to avoid endangering the privacy of the applicants. The disclosure constituted a wrongful infringement of the applicants' rights ([32]–[60]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([61]–[90])

The Constitutional Court held that the disclosure of the applicants' HIV statuses was wrongful, concluding that the respondents had acted with intention to harm or, at a minimum, with negligence. The Court determined that the actio injuriarum should evolve to encompass negligence in cases of privacy violations, subjecting media defendants, such as Smith and New Africa Books, to heightened standards of care concerning the disclosure of sensitive information. The Court ultimately ordered the respondents to pay damages of R35,000 to each applicant and upheld the necessity of sensitivity surrounding the disclosure of HIV statuses. Crucially, the decision underlined the need for clear standards for consent and the potential for community impact, especially regarding vulnerable individuals living with HIV ([61]–[90]).
