Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2021

# Case Note: Tramore Property Group (Pty) Ltd v Vosloorus Square CC [2021] ZASCA 41

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

The underlying facts of this case revolve around the **Agreement of Exchange of Land by Leasehold** (exchange agreement) concluded on **17 September 1991** between the **City Council of Vosloorus** and **Permprop (Pty) Ltd**, which later became **Tramore Property Group (Pty) Ltd** (the appellant) ([para 1]). The exchange was structured so that properties owned by Permprop (the Tramore properties) would be exchanged with council properties owned by the city council. The agreement stipulated that the transfers should occur simultaneously, with no monetary exchange involved. In subsequent years, the **Ekhuruleni Metropolitan Municipality** succeeded the council and the title of the properties was altered from leasehold to outright ownership ([paras 2-4]).

On **7 April 2000**, Tramore entered into two **Agreement of Sale** contracts with **Vosloorus Square CC** (the respondent), one concerning the aforementioned council properties ([para 3]). Following this, a township proclamation that included the council properties was issued on **19 September 2012**, imposing specific development conditions ([para 4]).

Upon Vosloorus Square's request for the transfer of the council properties, Tramore refused, leading Vosloorus Square to apply to the **Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria** for specific performance of the sale ([para 5]). Tramore opposed the application, claiming that the sale agreement had been cancelled due to alleged breaches by Vosloorus Square regarding guarantees for internal services ([paras 8-9]).

Initially dismissed by the court of first instance for lack of locus standi, Vosloorus Square's appeal was later upheld by the Full Court, which ordered the transfer of properties as per the exchange agreement and confirmed Vosloorus Square's rights to develop the properties ([paras 6-7]).

### I – Issues

The central legal issues in this case include:

1. **Locus Standi:** Whether Vosloorus Square had the requisite standing to enforce the transfer of the council properties according to the exchange agreement.
2. **Cancelation of Contract:** Whether Tramore's assertion that it had cancelled the sale agreement on grounds of breach was valid.
3. **Enforcement of Rights:** The extent to which Vosloorus Square could enforce rights stemming from the exchange agreement, particularly in light of the alleged lack of privity between it and the municipality ([paras 20-22], [30]).

### R – Rules

Several legal principles were examined, specifically regarding the enforceability of contracts and locus standi. The court relied on established contract law principles, particularly the concept of **privity of contract**, as outlined in case law such as **Theron and Du Plessis v Schoombie** ([para 22]). The judgment also cited statutory guidelines concerning property development and the primary obligations of sellers in property transactions.

A pivotal point in the judgment hinged on whether guarantees required by a local authority had indeed been called upon, as stipulated in the sale agreement, and whether amendments to agreements can occur without mutual consent from all parties ([paras 12-18]). 

### A – Application

In addressing the locus standi argument, the court determined that it was necessary to examine the substantive rights involved in the exchange agreement. Tramore had effectively transferred its rights concerning the council properties to Vosloorus Square, thus empowering the latter to enforce these rights, even though there was no direct privity of contract with the municipality ([para 28]).

Regarding the alleged cancellation of the contract, the court extensively analysed Tramore's claims concerning breaches. It established that the necessary conditions for the enforcement of the guarantees were not present because the local authority had not formally requested them (clause 10.3 of the sale agreement) prior to Tramore's purported cancellation. This conclusion was reinforced by the finding that there was no evidence to support that the municipality had mandated any guarantees at that stage ([paras 13-18]).

Moreover, it was determined that the conditions outlined in the original sale and exchange agreements indeed implied that Vosloorus Square was entitled to the rights previously held by Tramore. Subsequently, Tramore's obligations to execute the transfer of properties remained enforceable ([paras 22-28]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Tramore's appeal with costs, affirming the Full Court's holding that Vosloorus Square had the right to enforce the terms of the sale agreement and seek specific performance regarding the transfer of properties. The implications of this decision clarify the legal standing of parties in property transactions, particularly in cases involving complex agreements like exchanges and sales, reinforcing that effective delegation of rights can occur without direct amendments as long as the underlying agreements support such transfers ([paras 30-31]).

In conclusion, this case highlights the nuances of property law as it intersects with contractual obligations, making clear that obligations can persist and be enforced despite the absence of direct privity, provided that the underlying intent and agreements support such a conclusion.
