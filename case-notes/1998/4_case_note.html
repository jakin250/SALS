Die Oranje Vrystaatse Vereniging vir Staatsondersteunde Skole v Die Premier van die Provinsie Vrystaat (CCT 12/96)

Upon the first hearing, Lombard J granted the declaratory relief but refused the consequential orders the applicants sought.

Constitutional Court 1998 CCT 12/96

F – Facts

The case revolves around the decision made in October 1995 by the Orange Free State Provincial Administration to terminate bursaries and transport subsidies for students attending state-aided schools ([1]). The applicants, Die Oranje Vrystaatse Vereniging vir Staatsondersteunde Skole, and Dimakatso Anna Nkiane, sought to have this decision declared unconstitutional under the interim Constitution of South Africa ([1]). They sought not only a declaratory order but also consequential relief, which included reinstating bursaries and transport subsidies and preventing the termination of these funds without adherence to mandates set out in Section 247 of the interim Constitution and the White Paper on Education issued on March 15, 1995 ([1]).

Upon the first hearing, Lombard J granted the declaratory relief but refused the consequential orders the applicants sought. Consequently, the applicants appealed to the Constitutional Court ([2]). However, after initially filing for leave to appeal, they withdrew the application in December 1996 without paying the respondents' costs, citing the South African Schools Act 84 of 1996, which abolished state-aided schools effective January 1, 1997, rendering their case moot ([3]).

Despite this withdrawal, negotiations regarding costs ensued between the parties but were unsuccessful, leading the respondents to seek a determination from the Court regarding costs incurred from the leave to appeal proceedings ([3]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions in this case include:

  1. Whether the withdrawal of the appeal affects the Court's discretion to award costs ([4]).
  2. Whether the actions of the applicants in pursuing the appeal and subsequently withdrawing it were frivolous or vexatious ([4]).
  3. The implications of the mootness of the case on the determination of costs ([4]).

R – Rules

To evaluate the matter of costs, the Court drew upon established principles from previous case law, notably that litigants should not be deterred from pursuing constitutional rights for fear of incurring costs, especially when the complaints presented have substantive merit ([4]). The Court referenced several cases:

  • Ex Parte Gauteng Provincial Legislature concerning constitutional rights enforcement (1996) 3 SA 165 (CC).
  • Motsepe v Commissioner for Inland Revenue, which also reflects on the treatment of costs in cases involving constitutional matters ([4]).

Further, the Court acknowledged the principle that it is not in the interests of justice to hear cases on issues that have become moot ([5]).

A – Application

In its analysis, the Court considered both the nature of the applicants' withdrawal and the merits of their initial claims. It acknowledged that the reasons provided by the applicants for withdrawing their appeal were credible, as they were based on substantial legislative changes that affected their claims ([4]). The Court emphasized that the applicants had not acted with improper motives and reinforced the importance of protecting litigants who pursue constitutional claims from the financial burden of adverse costs when their claims are of significant public concern ([4]).

The mootness of the case further contributed to the reasoning, as the Court deemed it inappropriate to extend proceedings towards issues devoid of practical relevance, which could lead to unnecessary costs beyond what had already been incurred in relation to the leave to appeal ([5]).

C – Conclusion

The Court ultimately ruled that with regard to the application for leave to appeal, there would be no order as to costs ([6]). This decision underscored the Court's commitment to protecting litigants' constitutional rights. By refraining from imposing costs, the Court signaled its understanding of the complexities involved in cases concerning constitutional violations and the impact of legislative changes. Importantly, the ruling reflected the balance between the need to encourage citizens to assert their rights and the recognition that some issues, once rendered moot, should not occupy judicial resources.

The implications of this decision extend beyond this case, reinforcing a principle that acts as a deterrent against discouraging legitimate claims by making the financial repercussions of litigation unduly burdensome for the parties involved.
