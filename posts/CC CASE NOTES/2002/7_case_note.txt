Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2002

# Case Note: Rethea Bierman v The State (CCT 52/01)

## F – Facts
The case revolves around an application for special leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court following the applicant, Rethea Bierman, being convicted of murder and two additional offences related to unlawful possession of a firearm and ammunition. The High Court in Pretoria sentenced Bierman to life imprisonment. Subsequent requests for leave to appeal were denied by both the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal ([1]).

Bierman's application to the Constitutional Court argued that her constitutional rights were violated, specifically citing the admission of evidence from Rev. G. Bothma, which she contended breached her right to privacy under section 14 and her right to a fair trial under section 35 of the South African Constitution. The crux of her argument was that had the testimony of Rev. Bothma been excluded, the conviction would not have stood ([2]).

Rev. Bothma had claimed that Bierman confessed to her guilt in a private conversation during prison visits, and he believed these communications lacked privilege based on legal advice. The High Court ultimately permitted this testimony, despite objections from Bierman’s legal counsel. Other evidences, including confessions to a friend and circumstantial evidence from various witnesses, contributed to her conviction ([3]).

The State contested Bierman’s application for special leave on two grounds: first, challenging her right to raise constitutional claims since these were not presented in her application to the Supreme Court of Appeal; and second, asserting that the conviction did not solely hinge on the admission of Rev. Bothma’s evidence, thus leaving no reasonable prospects for success ([4]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal questions addressed in this case include:

1. Did the High Court's admission of Rev. Bothma’s evidence infringe Bierman’s constitutional rights, specifically her right to privacy and fair trial?
2. Was Bierman’s failure to raise these constitutional issues in her application for leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal a bar to her current application before the Constitutional Court?
3. Do the circumstances of the case provide a reasonable prospect of success for Bierman's appeal, and is granting leave to appeal in the interests of justice? ([2], [4], [9]).

## R – Rules
The applicable legal authorities and principles discussed in the case include:

- Section 14 of the Constitution of South Africa, which guarantees the right to privacy.
- Section 35 of the Constitution, which provides for the rights of accused persons in criminal matters, including the right to a fair trial.
- The decision in *Smit v Van Niekerk NO en ’n ander* 1976 (4) SA 293 (A) regarding the lack of public policy protecting confessions to clergymen as privileged, which the High Court referenced to justify the admission of Rev. Bothma’s testimony ([5]).
- The principles of common law regarding the admissibility of evidence, specifically that issues regarding these principles should first be addressed by the Supreme Court of Appeal before being escalated to the Constitutional Court ([7], [8]).

## A – Application
The Court examined the argument that the admission of Rev. Bothma's evidence violated Bierman’s constitutional rights. It noted the lack of discussion on these constitutional considerations in her application to the Supreme Court of Appeal, suggesting a procedural barrier to her current claims. The Court held that the admission of this evidence did not inhibit her fundamental rights in such a manner that would substantially impact her case ([6], [9]).

Furthermore, considering the multiple sources of evidence that led to her conviction, the Court concluded that even if Rev. Bothma's testimony were deemed inadmissible, it would not sufficiently alter the conviction to warrant granting special leave to appeal. The Court underscored that success in the appeal process required a clear identification of issues that had not been judiciously addressed at prior levels ([9]).

The Court also reiterated a broader point: issues relating to the intersection of common law rules and constitutional principles merit preliminary review by the Supreme Court of Appeal, indicating the necessity of exhaustively navigating through established legal channels before approaching higher courts for the potential reevaluation of long-held doctrines ([7], [8]).

## C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Constitutional Court dismissed Bierman's application for special leave to appeal. The Court asserted that the lack of raised constitutional issues in previous appeals constituted a significant barrier to proceeding with her claims in this context. Additionally, the assessment revealed that the conviction did not hinge exclusively on the perceived inadmissibility of Rev. Bothma’s testimony, thus there was no reasonable prospect that a decision in her favor would lead to the overturning of her conviction ([10]).

This decision highlights procedural discipline within the South African legal framework, emphasizing the need for adherence to procedure and established legal principles when contesting decisions involving the intersection of common law and constitutional rights. The ruling also leaves open the potential for future exploration of whether common law principles regarding clergy communications align with constitutional imperatives, suggesting an unresolved tension ripe for discussion in forthcoming cases.
