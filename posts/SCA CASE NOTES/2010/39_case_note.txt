# FIRAC Case Note for Smith v Parsons (187/07) [2010] ZASCA 39 

### F – Facts

This appeal arises from a decision of the Durban and Coast Local Division of the Kwazulu-Natal High Court concerning the estate of Walter Percival Smith, who tragically committed suicide on 25 February 2007 ([1]). The appellant, Heather Wendy Smith, sought a court order directing the Master of the High Court to accept a suicide note as an amendment to the deceased’s last will in accordance with the Wills Act 7 of 1953 ([2]). 

The deceased’s will, which was secured in a safe, did not include any provisions for the appellant. The respondents, who served as the executors, did not oppose the application; however, Jeremy Smith, the deceased's son, did contest it, leading to the latter’s conditional counterclaim ([2]-[3]).

The court found that while the note was indeed written by the deceased, it did not unequivocally reflect the intention to amend his will. The appellant appealed this finding ([4]). 

### I – Issues

The central issue is whether the suicide note constituted an amendment to the deceased's will under section 2(3) of the Wills Act ([5]). The interpretation of the deceased's intentions as articulated in the note is critical for the court's decision ([6]).

### R – Rules

Section 2(3) of the Wills Act states:

*“If a Court is satisfied that a document or the amendment of a document drafted or executed by a person who has died since the drafting or execution thereof, was intended to be his will or an amendment of his will, the court shall order the Master to accept that document, or that document as amended, for the purposes of the Administration of Estates Act, 1965 (Act 66 of 1965), as a will, although it does not comply with all the formalities for the execution or amendment of wills referred to in subsection (1)”* ([6]).

The court cited **Van Wetten & another v Bosch & others** and **Harlow v Becker NO & others** in explaining the necessary inquiries into the deceased's intent based on the wording of the document and circumstantial evidence ([7]).

### A – Application

Upon close examination of the suicide note, the court identified several explicit intentions regarding the disposition of his estate. The deceased clearly expressed his wishes concerning the distribution of his assets, such as indicating that the appellant could sell the house and provided specific monetary instructions to her regarding a bank account ([12]-[17]).

Additionally, although the note lacked a formal signature—merely signing off as “Wally”—the court rejected the argument that this indicated a desire to draft formal amendments later ([18]). Notably, the note's placement under a crucifix suggested a deliberate action by the deceased to give it prominence, reinforcing the assertion that the note was intended to be acted upon upon his death ([19]).

The court considered the argument from Jeremy Smith's counsel that the note’s language indicated a *donatio mortis causa*, but it concluded that the deceased intended to regulate the disposition of his estate and not to make a gift conditional upon acceptance by the appellant ([21]-[23]). 

### C – Conclusion

The court ultimately found that the suicide note was intended by the deceased as an amendment to his will under section 2(3) of the Wills Act. Therefore, the appeal by Heather Wendy Smith was upheld, and the prior ruling was set aside, requiring the Master of the High Court to accept the note as an amendment for the purposes of estate administration ([24]).

The decision highlights the court's willingness to interpret the intentions of the deceased flexibly, aiming to honor what the court viewed as a clear testamentary wish, despite the absence of traditional formalities typically adhered to in will creation ([16], [17], [24]). 

This case signifies a judicial approach prioritizing substantive intent over procedural rigidity in testamentary matters, aligning with broader efforts to ensure fairness and recognition of individual wishes in estate planning ([5], [8]).
