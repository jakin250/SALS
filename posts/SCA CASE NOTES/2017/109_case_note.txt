Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note: PepsiCo v Atlantic Industries (983/16) [2017] ZASCA 109

## F – Facts
The matter before the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa involved PepsiCo Inc (the appellant), the registered proprietor of various trademarks incorporating the words PEPSI and PEPSI-COLA, and Atlantic Industries (the respondent), a subsidiary of The Coca-Cola Company, which owned trademarks including TWIST, LEMON TWIST, and DIET TWIST ([1]). Both parties' trademarks fell under class 32, which pertains to non-alcoholic beverages ([1]).

PepsiCo sought to register a mark PEPSI TWIST in 2006, which Atlantic opposed, arguing that the term was too similar to its registered marks. Subsequently, PepsiCo applied for the expungement of Atlantic's trademarks, asserting they were not capable of distinguishing the products per sections 10(2)(a) and 10(2)(b) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 ([4]). The Gauteng High Court initially dismissed PepsiCo's expungement application but granted registration for PEPSI TWIST ([3]). Both parties appealed, but the full court eventually upheld Atlantic's appeal and dismissed PepsiCo's cross-appeal, prompting PepsiCo to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([3]).

## I – Issues
1. Did the High Court err in dismissing the expungement application under sections 10(2)(a) and 10(2)(b) of the Trade Marks Act, which pertained to the distinctiveness of Atlantic's TWIST trademarks?
2. Was the proposed trademark PEPSI TWIST likely to deceive or cause confusion due to its similarity to Atlantic's marks, violating section 10(14) of the Trade Marks Act? ([16]).

## R – Rules
The court relied on several statutory provisions and principles of trademark law, notably:

1. **Section 10(2)(a)** of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 prohibits registration of marks that are not capable of distinguishing goods ([4]).
2. **Section 10(2)(b)** outlines that marks consisting exclusively of signs or indications that serve to describe the characteristics of goods or services cannot be registered ([4]).
3. **Section 10(14)** prohibits registration of trademarks that are identical or similar to registered trademarks if there is a likelihood of consumer confusion ([15]).
4. The "other traders" test established in *W & G Du Cros* and applied in various case law, including *Clark Equipment Co v Registrar of Trade Marks* and *Pepkor Retail*, helps determine the likelihood of confusion based on consumers' perceptions of the trademarks ([8], [10], [12]).

## A – Application
### Expungement of Atlantic's Marks
The court concluded that the term 'TWIST' was not merely descriptive as argued by PepsiCo. The analysis indicated that while 'twist' might have some associations, it did not carry a universal ordinary meaning within the South African market that would render it purely descriptive. The judgment highlighted that the registration of TWIST had been sufficiently established through prior use and advertising, qualifying it as inherently capable of distinguishing Atlantic's products ([11], [14]). 

### Registration of PepsiCo's Marks
In examining the likelihood of consumer confusion regarding PEPSI TWIST, the court emphasized that both marks were used for identical goods—i.e., soft drinks. The test for confusion favored Atlantic's position, given that PepsiCo intended to use 'TWIST' prominently within PEPSI TWIST. The court noted that consumers might associate 'TWIST' with either brand and indicated that introducing 'PEPSI' did not necessarily mitigate the confusion, especially as consumers might drop 'PEPSI' when making an order, leading to potential mix-ups ([30]). The court underscored that even a substantial portion of consumers could be confused by the marks ([30], [32]).

The court drew parallels to important EU case law, particularly *Medion AG v Thomson*, noting that while one element of a composite sign could be dominant, secondary elements could still play an independent distinguishing role that could mislead consumers ([33]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed PepsiCo's appeal, reaffirming the full court's ruling that Atlantic's trademarks were valid and distinct. This highlighted the importance of consumer perception in trademark law and reinforced the notion that the incorporation of a distinguishable component in a trademark does not offer sufficient protection against consumer confusion when such a component is shared with a competing mark ([36]).

The implication of this ruling is significant for trademark owners, emphasizing diligent protection of distinctive marks to avoid confusion and potential legal challenges based on the perceived association of goods within consumer markets. The decision serves as a notable precedent in the ever-related scope of trademark law, particularly in the non-alcoholic beverage sector.
