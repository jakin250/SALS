Supreme Court of Appeal

Mystic River Investments 45 (Pty) Ltd & Another v Zayeed Paruk Incorporated & Others [2023] ZASCA 54

The second appellant, Mawji, is the sole director of Mystic River but is a peregrinus (a foreign litigant without assets in the Republic) with a temporary presence outside of South Africa, having moved to Portugal in 2018 ([1]–[2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 54

F – Facts

In this matter, the first appellant, Mystic River Investments 45 (Pty) Ltd (hereinafter "Mystic River"), and the second appellant, Mr. Karim Issa Mawji, initiated an application in the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court, Pietermaritzburg, against several respondents, including Zayeed Paruk Incorporated and others (the "Moosa brothers"). The appellants alleged that the respondents had unlawfully taken control of Mystic River and diverted its funds, leading to calls for the return of said funds, comprehensive accounting, and prohibition from unauthorized actions on behalf of Mystic River.

The second appellant, Mawji, is the sole director of Mystic River but is a peregrinus (a foreign litigant without assets in the Republic) with a temporary presence outside of South Africa, having moved to Portugal in 2018 ([1]–[2]). Facing a demand for security for costs from the respondents based on his status, the high court ordered Mawji to provide such security, leading to his appeal against this decision.

I – Issues

The central issue is whether the high court correctly exercised its discretion in requiring the second appellant to furnish security for costs under Rule 47 of the Uniform Rules of Court, considering his status as a peregrinus ([6]–[7]). A secondary consideration pertains to the costs awarded for the applications regarding security for costs ([6]).

R – Rules

The legal principles relevant to the granting of security for costs are nuanced and arise from established jurisprudence. Security for costs is discretionary and considered where there’s reasonable apprehension that a plaintiff (in this case, a peregrinus) may not be able to satisfy a costs order should their claim fail ([7]). The leading case, Magida v Minister of Police, establishes that security is not automatically required from peregrinus claimants; it must reflect equitable considerations ([7]).

Further, Shepstone & Wylie & Others v Geyser NO clarifies that courts should not fetter their discretion and must consider all circumstances without a predisposition for or against granting security, striking a balance between the plaintiff's right to pursue a claim and the defendant's need for protection in case of failure ([8]). The discretion to grant security was found to be classified under strict and broad categories, where strict discretion allows for limited appellate interference ([10]–[11]).

A – Application

Upon reviewing the high court’s exercise of discretion, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that the latter had misapplied the established principles by adopting a predisposition that a peregrinus must furnish security when asked, which contradicts the guidance from Shepstone & Wylie ([13]). The high court, despite quoting relevant case law, failed to appreciate the specifics of each case as required by *Blastrite (Pty) Ltd v Genpaco Ltd*—that each case should be examined on its unique factual matrix, not through a blanket rule ([14]).

Critically, the second appellant’s arguments that Mystic River, an incola, could cover any adverse costs failed to hold up. The court highlighted that Mawji's involvement, with claims to a share of profits from Mystic River's assets, despite his peregrinus status, necessitated an order for security to prevent potential injustices to the respondents ([16]–[20]).

Additionally, the court pointed out the impracticalities of enforcing any costs order against Mawji abroad, reinforcing the necessity for him to provide security to ease the burden on the respondents in the event of a costs order ([20]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the high court had erred in its direction to order security for costs and awarded costs against the second appellant exclusively, emphasizing that the procedural fairness and equity necessitate such a requirement in this situation ([22]). The appeal was partially dismissed, with the court asserting the need for a reassessment of the application for security on correct legal principles.

This case highlights the nuanced handling of costs related to peregrinus litigants and emphasizes the balance required to ensure fairness in litigation while safeguarding the interests of all parties involved, showcasing judicial prudence in upholding equitable remedies within South African law.
