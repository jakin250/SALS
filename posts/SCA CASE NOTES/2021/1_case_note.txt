# Case Note: FIRAC Method

## Haitas v Froneman and Others (1158/2019) [2021] ZASCA 01 (06 January 2021)

### F - Facts
In the context of a family feud erupting after the death of wealthy businessman Evangelos Haitas on 21 October 2018, the present appeal arises concerning the management of the Kam Trust and its related entities. The appellant, Konstantinos Haitas, is the deceased’s son and sole heir, also acting as the only income and capital beneficiary of the inter vivos trust established in 2007. Following the deceased's death, tension escalated between the appellant and the two surviving trustees, Froneman and Haitas, the latter being the appellant's aunt. These trustees, previously appointed by the deceased, faced allegations of mismanagement and impropriety, particularly regarding financial transparency and alleged unauthorized payments made to Ms Sofia Batista, the deceased's romantic partner ([paras 1-10]).

The appellant lodged an application with the Gauteng Division of the High Court, seeking the removal of the trustees, who he accused of failing to disclose financial information regarding the trust and its companies, as well as failing to provide for his maintenance despite his position as a beneficiary ([paras 3-4, 23-25]). The High Court dismissed the application, leading to the appeal now under consideration ([paras 3, 4]).

### I - Issues
The primary legal questions before the Supreme Court of Appeal are:
1. Whether the conduct of the trustees, particularly regarding financial management and communication with the beneficiary, justifies their removal under Section 20(1) of the Trust Property Control Act 57 of 1988.
2. Alternatively, whether the breakdown in the relationship between the beneficiary and the trustees necessitates their removal under common law principles ([paras 29-30]).

### R - Rules
The framework for determining the removal of trustees is established under Section 20(1) of the Trust Property Control Act, which permits such removal if it is in the interests of the trust and its beneficiaries. This is further reinforced by common law principles requiring a court to exercise this power only when the continued office of a trustee endangers the trust’s proper administration or the interests of its beneficiaries. Key precedents cited include:

- *Gowar and Another v Gowar and Others*, which emphasizes that mere disharmony is insufficient for removal unless it threatens trust assets ([paras 30-31]).
- *Kidbrooke Place Management Association and Another v Walton and Others*, which allows for removal in cases of conflict of interest, although this was not substantiated in the current appeal ([paras 32-33]).
- The central tenet elucidated in *Volkwyn NO v Clarke and Damant* that removal should be exercised with circumspection and is not warranted by mere accusations of mismanagement without material detriment to the trust’s assets ([paras 30, 35]).

### A - Application
In analyzing the conduct of the trustees, the Court emphasized that, while communication and financial management had room for improvement, the actions in question did not rise to a level warranting removal. The Appellant's claim that the trustees had not disclosed financial documents was acknowledged; however, the Court noted that the trustees eventually provided the relevant records after the appellant’s appointment as a trustee, thus suggesting a lack of proactive engagement on the appellant's part ([para 36]).

The Court further considered claims surrounding the payments made to Ms Batista. It was concluded that, while possibly ill-advised, such payments were made under the directive of the deceased and did not amount to personal gain by the trustees. Hence, they did not justify removal ([paras 37-39]). Crucially, the breakdown in communication between the trustees and the appellant was recognized, but the Court concluded it did not pose an imminent threat to the trust’s viability ([paras 38-40]).

### C - Conclusion
The appeal was ultimately dismissed, reinforcing the High Court's ruling. The Court held that while the trustees had shown shortcomings, they had not engaged in misconduct that imperiled the trust or favored their interests over those of the beneficiary. The decision affirms the principle that mere dissatisfaction or hostility does not suffice for removing trustees. The ruling underscores the necessity for maintaining the stability of trust management and the importance of allowing trustees the discretion to operate without undue interference unless clear misconduct or harm to the trust's assets can be demonstrated ([paras 41-43]).

---

This case serves as a significant reference for the interpretation of trustee conduct under South African trust law, reiterating the importance of fiduciary duties while balancing them against the operational necessities of trust management.
