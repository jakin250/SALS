# Case Note: Nkandla Local Municipality v MEC for the Department of Co-operative Governance and Traditional Affairs 

**FIRAC Case Notes**

**Facts**  
[1] This case encompasses two appeals regarding the appointments of municipal managers in the Nkandla and Mthonjaneni municipalities, challenged by the Member of the Executive Council (MEC) for the Department of Co-operative Governance and Traditional Affairs (the MEC). The first appellant, the Nkandla Local Municipality, appointed Mr Langelihle Jili as its Municipal Manager after passing an interview and competency assessment ([2]). Similarly, the Mthonjaneni Local Municipality appointed Mr Philani Sibiya ([5]). Both appointments, however, were later deemed invalid by the MEC based on their failure to meet the minimum experience requirements set forth in the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 (the Systems Act) and its Regulations ([1]). The MEC initially attempted informal remedial actions before filing for judicial review, which included questioning the municipalities’ compliance with the Act and demanding remedial steps to rectify the appointments ([3]-[4], [6]). 

**Issues**  
[9] The Supreme Court of Appeal had to determine several key legal issues:  
1. Whether the High Court had jurisdiction to consider the MEC’s review application, or if it fell under the exclusive jurisdiction of the Labour Court due to the nature of the appointments.
2. The relevance of a prior constitutional ruling regarding the invalidity of similar provisions in the SAMWU case.
3. Whether the MEC's delay in filing for review was unreasonable and if it should affect the case’s merits.
4. Whether Jili and Sibiya met the required minimum experience for their positions as municipal managers.

**Rules**  
[12] The legal framework for this case is provided fundamentally by the Systems Act, particularly Section 54A, which stipulates the qualifications and experience required for the appointment of municipal managers ([11]). Additionally, the court referred to the definitions provided in the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) and explored principles of legality concerning administrative actions ([32]-[35]). The court’s analysis also referenced the inappropriate use of delay as a defense against potential claims of illegality because of the necessity for legally compliant governance ([41]-[42]). 

**Application**  
[20] The Court affirmed that the MEC had appropriately brought the actions seeking to enforce lawful compliance, and that this dispute did not exclusively reside within the realm of employment law and, therefore, the High Court retained jurisdiction ([19]). Noting the judicial standards surrounding the timing of review applications, the Supreme Court scrutinized the MEC’s delay, highlighting periods of inaction which spanned appreciably beyond the legally stipulated 14-day window for responses, demanding a detailed explanation of the delay ([22]-[31]). The justices emphasized the legislative purpose underlying the rapid enforcement of administrative oversight and brought the municipal appointments’ legitimacy into question given the unequal experiences claimed by Jili and Sibiya ([46]-[50]). The inquiry concerning whether the managers met the experience requirements mainly relied on the evidence presented by the municipalities, but it was revealed that Jili’s qualifications were imperfectly assessed ([46]-[50]).

**Conclusion**  
[54] Ultimately, the Court concluded that the High Court had erred in its ruling regarding the legality of the appointments and in its assessment of the MEC’s delay as not unreasonable. However, it held that given the unjust consequences of invalidating the managers’ appointments retroactively, the appeals by the municipalities were upheld with no order as to costs. The ordering included the continuance of the Municipal Managers in their positions until the expiration of their respective contracts, thus preserving stability in municipal governance ([54]-[102]). 

In this case, the Supreme Court recognized the critical need for swift adherence to the legal standards enshrined in the Systems Act, while balancing the operational realities facing public service governance, reflecting a nuanced approach to the doctrine of legality therein intertwined with principles of cooperative governance ([60]-[61]).
