Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note: Micaren Exel Petroleum Wholesaler (Pty) Ltd v Stella Quick Shop (Pty) Ltd and Another [2020] ZASCA 61

### F – Facts
The case arises from a dispute regarding a fuel dealership agreement concluded on 1 July 2014 between Micaren Exel Petroleum Wholesaler (Pty) Ltd (hereinafter "Micaren") and Stella Quick Shop (Pty) Ltd (hereinafter "Stella") ([1][2]). Under this agreement, Micaren was responsible for supplying petrol and diesel fuel exclusively to Stella, who was to sell the fuel from its predetermined premises in Stella, North West Province. The agreement expressly prohibited Stella from purchasing fuel from any distributor other than Micaren, stipulating that only fuel purchased from Micaren could be stored in the tanks provided by Micaren ([2][3]).

Between November 2016 and January 2017, a series of events led to escalating tensions between the two parties. Micaren ceased fuel deliveries to Stella, and by January 2017, Stella had begun purchasing fuel from a competitor, Elegant Fuel (Pty) Ltd, thereby directly contravening the terms of their agreement ([4][5]). Subsequent to a formal demand for payment from Micaren regarding outstanding debts, Stella initiated a cancellation of the dealer agreement, asserting that Micaren had repudiated the contract by ceasing deliveries and imposing additional costs ([5][6]). On 28 January 2017, Micaren sought a court interdict to prevent Stella from purchasing fuel from any other dealer ([5][6]).

The High Court of the North West, however, dismissed Micaren's application for an interdict, ruling that Micaren's conduct constituted a repudiation of the dealership agreement, thereby allowing Stella to cancel the contract ([1][9]). Following this ruling, Micaren appealed with permission from the High Court ([1]).

### I – Issues
The central legal issue in this appeal is whether Micaren repudiated the fuel dealership agreement and whether Stella's response to this alleged repudiation was legally justified, allowing for the cancellation of the contract ([1][2]). Specifically, the court needed to determine whether Micaren's failure to deliver fuel constituted a repudiation of the agreement, thereby providing Stella grounds for cancellation and subsequent purchase of fuel from third parties, in violation of their contract ([4][9]).

### R – Rules
The principal authority guiding the court's evaluation of repudiation originates from contract law principles, as articulated in cases such as *Nash v Golden Pumps (Pty) Ltd* and *Datacolor International (Pty) Ltd v Intamarket (Pty) Ltd*. These authorities elucidate that a party is only considered to have repudiated a contract when there is an unequivocal intention not to be bound by it, judged through the lens of objective reasonableness ([11][12][13]).

Furthermore, the inquiry into repudiation considers not only the actions of the purported repudiator but also the overall context of the parties' conduct. The court must analyze whether a reasonable person, in the aggrieved party's position, would interpret the actions of the repudiator as a clear intention to repudiate the contract ([11][13]).

### A – Application
In its judgment, the court noted that the High Court's assessment was flawed as it predominantly relied on Stella's assertion regarding Micaren's failure to deliver fuel without adequately considering the context of the totality of interactions between the two parties ([13][14]). Micaren successfully argued that its cessation of deliveries was a direct consequence of Stella's failure to settle outstanding debts, thereby not indicating an intention to repudiate the contract but rather adhering to its terms regarding payment ([10][14]). 

The court underscored that the totality of conduct over the preceding months indicated that Stella, having incurred debts and acknowledged its obligations, should not have reasonably concluded that Micaren was repudiating the agreement by ceasing deliveries due to non-payment ([14][13]). Furthermore, the court found that the imposition of RAS levies, which Stella claimed to be unlawful, did not factor into the alleged repudiation since they were purposed for capital investment, not arbitrary costs ([4][5]).

Thus, the court concluded that Stella's actions of purchasing fuel from a competitor constituted a breach of the original agreement, which remained in force ([14][15]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Micaren's appeal, establishing that there had been no repudiation of the dealership agreement by Micaren. Consequently, the High Court's prior ruling that permitted Stella to cancel the agreement was found to be erroneous ([15]). The Court ordered that Stella be interdictively restrained from purchasing or storing fuel acquired from other distributors unless sourced from Micaren, reaffirming the contractual obligations stipulated in their agreement ([15]).

This ruling has significant implications, reaffirming the necessity for both contract parties to adhere strictly to their contractual terms and emphasizes that allegations of repudiation must be substantiated with comprehensive contextual analysis. Micaren was awarded costs, reflecting the Court's recognition of the appeals' merit and failure of the initial ruling by the High Court ([15]).
