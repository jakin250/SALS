Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: The National Credit Regulator v Lewis Stores (Pty) Ltd

### F – Facts

This case involves the National Credit Regulator (the "Regulator") appealing against Lewis Stores (Pty) Ltd ("Lewis") regarding allegations of violations of the National Credit Act 34 of 2005 (the "NCA"). The Regulator had previously investigated Lewis for purported contraventions, particularly concerning the sale of extended warranties and club fees to consumers ([paras 1–4]). 

Initially, the Regulator sought a declaratory order from the National Consumer Tribunal (the "Tribunal") claiming that Lewis repeatedly contravened several sections of the NCA, specifically sections 90, 91, 100, 101(1)(a), and 102(1). The Tribunal dismissed the Regulator's application, and a subsequent appeal to the High Court was also unsuccessful. Consequently, the Regulator obtained special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 1]).

The Regulator's primary contention was twofold: (1) Lewis's extended warranties, which covered goods for an additional two years after the expiry of supplier warranties, constituted unlawful charges under the NCA; and (2) the subscription fees for membership in the Lewis Family Club were also categorized as illegal costs of credit ([para 2]).

### I – Issues

The case presents critical issues of statutory interpretation concerning the following:

1. Whether the charges levied for the extended warranties violated sections 100, 101(1)(a), and 102(1) of the NCA, thereby constituting unlawful 'costs of credit.'
2. Whether club fees charged to consumers who entered into credit agreements were unlawful under the same sections of the NCA.
3. Jurisdictional concerns regarding the authority of the Tribunal to adjudicate claims concerning sections 90 and 91 of the NCA ([paras 2–4, 10]).

### R – Rules

The primary legal authorities and statutes at issue include:

1. **National Credit Act, 34 of 2005**: Aimed at promoting a fair, transparent, and accessible credit market while protecting consumers ([para 3]).
    - **Section 90**: Concerns unlawful provisions in credit agreements.
    - **Section 91**: Prohibits credit providers from circumventing section 90.
    - **Section 100**: Prohibits charging consumers amounts in violation of the act.
    - **Section 101**: Defines permissible charges in credit agreements.
    - **Section 102**: Authorizes the inclusion of certain fees and charges in the principal debt.

2. **Jurisdictional Principles**: The Tribunal's powers, as outlined in section 27 of the NCA, include adjudicating applications relating to prohibited conduct ([para 5]).

3. **Commercial and Consumer Law Principles**: The importance of written agreements and parol evidence in establishing the terms of a contract ([paras 18–19]).

### A – Application

**Extended Warranties**: The court critically analyzed the Regulator's assertions that the extended warranties were unlawful due to documentation inaccuracies and alleged consumer misunderstanding. The court emphasized that the real essence of the agreements mattered more than the written documentation ([paras 11–20]). 

Lewis maintained that they consistently informed consumers of the existence of warranties and advertised them clearly. The lack of evidence proving that consumers did not receive the benefits of the extended warranties undermined the Regulator's position ([paras 11–14]). 

The court ruled that the incomplete documents did not automatically invalidate the warranties, as consumers were, in fact, receiving the benefits as per the agreements. Therefore, the court concluded that Lewis did not contravene sections 100, 101(1)(a), or 102(1) regarding the extended warranties ([paras 21–22]).

**Club Fees**: The Regulator's additional claims regarding club fees faced similar scrutiny. Evidence showed that membership was open to the general public, and consumers had the option to join independently of their credit agreements. Moreover, the fees for club membership did not constitute 'costs of credit' under the NCA ([paras 24–27]).

Lewis argued that membership fees were collections separate from credit liabilities, thereby not subject to the prohibitions under the NCA. The court ultimately agreed that the membership should not be categorized under the definitions of prohibited charges in sections 100, 101, and 102 of the NCA ([paras 30–37]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the initial findings of the Tribunal and the Gauteng High Court, dismissing the Regulator's appeal with costs ([para 58]). The court reaffirmed the importance of contractual integrity and the necessity for detailed evidence to substantiate allegations of unlawful conduct under the NCA. 

This case illustrates the nuances of consumer credit law, especially concerning what constitutes lawful charges and the responsibilities of credit providers. It also reaffirms the necessity for clear, substantiated claims in regulatory enforcement while clarifying the jurisdictional limits of the Tribunal in adjudicating issues concerning credit agreements ([paras 38–57]).

The case emphasizes a proactive approach in protecting consumers while balancing the commercialization of credit provisions, affirming that regulatory actions must align closely with established legal parameters.
