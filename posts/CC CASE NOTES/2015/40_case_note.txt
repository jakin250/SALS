# Case Note: Toyota SA Motors (Pty) Ltd v CCMA and Others [2015] ZACC 40

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–36])

The case involves Toyota SA Motors (Pty) Ltd ("Toyota") seeking leave to appeal a decision from the Labour Court that dismissed its application for the review of an arbitration award regarding the unfair dismissal of its employee, Makoma Makhotla ("Makhotla"). Toyota employed Makhotla in 2006, but he was dismissed on March 24, 2011, for being absent without leave for four days ([para 4]). Prior to his dismissal, he had submitted a notice of resignation, which Toyota's management did not accept ([para 5]). Makhotla then referred the matter to the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA) on April 21, 2011. His claim for unfair dismissal was subsequently upheld by the CCMA, which found his dismissal to be substantively unfair, ordering reinstatement and compensation of six months’ salary ([para 11]).

Toyota filed a review application under section 145 of the Labour Relations Act (LRA) concerning the arbitration award. However, a significant delay occurred in prosecuting this application, attributed largely to issues in reconstructing the arbitration record, which the CCMA failed to provide in full ([paras 14–16]). The Labour Court ultimately dismissed the review application based on this excessive delay and the inadequacy of Toyota's record submission, instigated by a motion from Makhotla and the Retail and Allied Workers Union ([para 19]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 19–30])

The central questions before the Constitutional Court were:

1. **Delay and Condonation**: Whether the Labour Court erred in dismissing Toyota’s application for review due to what it characterized as an "inordinate delay" in prosecuting the review.
2. **Right to a Fair Hearing**: Whether the dismissal of Toyota's review application undermined its rights to fair labour practices and access to courts as enshrined in the Constitution, specifically in light of the incomplete arbitration record.
3. **Arbitrator’s Conduct**: Whether the CCMA arbitrator's failure to allow adequate cross-examination of Makhotla constituted a gross irregularity, thereby affecting the validity of the arbitration award.

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 30–42])

The court relied on several key statutes and precedents, including:

- **Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA)**: Specifically, section 145 concerning the review of arbitration awards and section 158 concerning the Labour Court's authority to make arbitration awards orders of court ([paras 12, 16, 27]).
- **Constitution of South Africa**: Section 23, which provides for the right to fair labor practices, section 33 concerning the right to just administrative action, and section 34 regarding the right to have disputes resolved by impartial bodies ([para 20]).
- Relevant case law: The judgment addressed the objectives outlined in cases such as *CUSA v Tao Ying Metal Industries* [2008] ZACC 15 and *Sidumo v Rustenburg Platinum Mines* [2007] ZACC 22, which highlight the need for arbitral awards to be final and the standard of review for such decisions ([paras 34–46]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 42–52])

The Constitutional Court underscored the importance of timely resolution of labor disputes, viewing unnecessary delays as detrimental to both employees and employers. The majority judgment, delivered by Nkabinde J, affirmed the principle that a dismissal can be based on delay if one party neglects their duty to progress the case ([para 34]).

The Court scrutinized intensive procedural lapses by Toyota concerning reconstructing the arbitration record, finding that despite several attempts and communication with the CCMA, there were lengthy periods where Toyota took no action ([para 36]). The Court observed that the delays compounded the initial issue of the incomplete arbitration record, concluding that such procedural inefficiencies warranted the Labour Court's decision to dismiss the review application for being inequitable and detrimental to the expeditious resolution of disputes.

In a notable discussion on the merits of the case, the Court examined the role of the arbitrator, especially regarding the critical issue of Makhotla's explanation for his absence. Ruling that the arbitrator's insufficient probing into conflicting accounts of Makhotla constituted a gross irregularity, the Court opined that Toyota would have had a reasonable chance of success had the matter proceeded to a substantive hearing ([paras 45–46]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 52–53])

The Constitutional Court dismissed Toyota's application for leave to appeal based primarily on the significant and unjustified delay in prosecuting the review and found no basis to contravene the Labour Court's ruling. The implications are profound; the decision reaffirms the importance of adhering to procedural timelines in labor disputes while also upholding the need for fair administrative justice. The end result affirmed the upholding of Makhotla's rights to a fair hearing alongside reinforcing the CCMA's responsibility to ensure complete and accurate recordings of arbitration proceedings under their purview ([paras 49–53]).

In dissent, Zondo J highlighted concerns regarding the impact of resignation on the reinstatement and emphasized that the CCMA and arbitrator’s failure to facilitate a complete record should have led to more favorable consideration of Toyota's position. Furthermore, the dissenting opinion addressed potential issues about the nature of Makhotla's resignation in relation to its effect on reinstatement rights, illustrating the complex interplay between procedural integrity and substantive justice in labor relations law. 

Overall, the case represents a critical juncture in South African labor law concerning procedural timelines, the importance of complete records in arbitration, and the nuanced implications of resignation on dismissal and reinstatement remedies.
