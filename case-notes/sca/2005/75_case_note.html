Supreme Court of Appeal

City of Cape Town v W D Bourbon-Leftley No & M M Bourbon-Leftley No (Case No: 415/04)

A servitude agreement, signed in 1952, granted the riparian owners, including the trust’s predecessor, access to a specified maximum allocation of water from the dam—400 million gallons annually, sectioned into free water and water sold at a reduced rate ([3]–[4]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2005

F – Facts

The dispute arises from water rights related to the Wemmershoek Dam in South Africa. The appellant, the City of Cape Town (CMC Administration), owns the dam and the pipelines connected to it. The respondents, W D Bourbon-Leftley No and M M Bourbon-Leftley No, are trustees of the Bourbon-Leftley family trust which operates the Môrelig fruit farm in the Wemmershoek Valley ([1]–[2]).

A servitude agreement, signed in 1952, granted the riparian owners, including the trust’s predecessor, access to a specified maximum allocation of water from the dam—400 million gallons annually, sectioned into free water and water sold at a reduced rate ([3]–[4]). The trust subsequently inherited the servitude in 1992 when it took over Môrelig, with annual water allocations of 151,536 kilolitres ([6]).

From 1999 to 2001, the trust exceeded its water allocation substantially without notification from the appellant due to errors in metering ([10]–[12]). The trust was later billed R1,696,758.58 for excess water consumed during this period. The municipality claimed the trust was liable to pay for exceeding its allocation based on a tacit term in the servitude agreement or, alternatively, on delictual grounds, alleging unlawful misappropriation of water ([13], [15]–[16]).

The Cape High Court rejected these claims, leading to the current appeal by the City of Cape Town ([18]).

I – Issues

The central legal questions presented in this case are:

  1. Does the servitude agreement contain a tacit term obligating the trust to pay for excess water drawn beyond its allocation?
  2. Alternatively, is the trust liable in delict for the excess water consumed?

R – Rules

The decision relies heavily on principles concerning tacit terms in contracts, notably established in Wilkins NO v Voges 1994 (3) SA 130 (A) and further reiterated in Botha v Coopers & Lybrand 2002 (5) SA 347 (SCA) and Consol Ltd v Twee Jonge Gezellen (Pty) Ltd [2004] 1 All SA 1 (SCA) ([19]–[20]). These cases elucidate that a tacit term can only be inferred if it is evident both parties would necessarily have included it had it been discussed, as per the "officious bystander" test.

On the delictual claim, the rules surrounding the unlawful taking of property—specifically the requirements of intention or negligence—are grounded in established precedents, including Hefer v Van Greuning 1979 (4) SA 952 (A) ([27]).

A – Application

The court scrutinised the servitude agreement’s explicit terms and the circumstances under which it was entered ([21] – [25]). It highlighted that both parties did not foresee excess consumption, and thus, they could not have tacitly agreed on charging for excess usage at a going rate. The appellant argued that as no provision was made in the agreement for excessive use, the logical inference was that parties would agree to charge at market rates should such a case arise. The appellate court refuted this, asserting that the likely response would focus on compliance with agreed allocations rather than compensation for excess usage.

Regarding the delictual claim, the court observed that although the appellant argued that misappropriation occurred, the lower court found the evidence insufficient to prove intentionality or negligence. Moreover, it deemed the existence of damages unclear, given that evidence indicated the dam's capacity was never fully depleted, meaning the claim of loss lacked substantiation ([28]–[29]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the City's appeal, affirming the lower court’s ruling that no tacit term existed in the servitude agreement obligating the trust to compensate for excess usage of water. Furthermore, the delictual claim was also dismissed due to lack of evidence of intentional misappropriation and unproven damages. The judgment underscores the importance of clear contractual language in servitude agreements related to resource usage. The court’s findings also serve as cautionary notes on the necessity for monitoring and communication regarding resource allocations to prevent misunderstandings and unauthorized consumption ([30]).

Overall, this case emphasizes the complexities inherent in resource management agreements and the legal frameworks that govern them, illustrating the challenges faced by municipalities in protecting their interests while ensuring equitable resource allocation among users.
