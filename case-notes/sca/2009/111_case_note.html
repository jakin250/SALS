Supreme Court of Appeal

Riba v State (74/2009) [2009] ZASCA 111

Facts The appellant, Harry Nkwane Riba, a police inspector, and a colleague, a reserve constable, were involved in a case concerning charges of corruption under the now-repealed Corruption Act 94 of 1992 and theft ([para 1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009 [2009] ZASCA 111

FIRAC Method

Facts

The appellant, Harry Nkwane Riba, a police inspector, and a colleague, a reserve constable, were involved in a case concerning charges of corruption under the now-repealed Corruption Act 94 of 1992 and theft ([para 1]). They were originally convicted and sentenced to 15 years’ imprisonment by a regional court in Mamelodi. However, upon appeal to the High Court, their conviction for corruption was set aside but the conviction for theft was upheld, resulting in a reduced sentence of 4 years’ imprisonment ([para 2]).

On 8 September 2001, Riba and his colleague, while on patrol in Mamelodi, seized a mini hi-fi set and 80 CDs from a complainant, Mr. Johannes Mapoba, who was unable to produce receipts for the goods. The CDs were not his property but belonged to another individual, Mr. Neville Shirinda, but the police took them under the pretext of suspicion that they had been stolen ([paras 3-4]).

Upon taking the complainant into custody, Riba and his colleague allegedly demanded R400 from him to release the goods. After securing the money from the complainant's uncle, they kept the seized items at a satellite police station, rather than taking them to the main police station where they would be properly logged in the SAP 13 register ([para 5]). On 10 September, they attempted to return the goods to the main station after learning that investigating officer Captain Morudi was looking for them ([para 6]).

Issues

The core legal question was whether the appellant and his colleague had the intention to steal the goods ([para 7]). This necessitated an assessment of their actions, statements, and the context of the seizure ([para 8]).

Rules

The principles of theft in South African law are well-established. Theft constitutes the unlawful and intentional appropriation of property belonging to another with the intent to deprive the owner permanently of their property ([para 18]). The elements of theft include:

  1. An intentional appropriation of the property.
  2. The intent to deprive the owner permanently.
  3. The knowledge that the property is capable of being unlawfully appropriated ([para 18]).

The court cited previous case law, specifically S v Visagie and S v Boesak, to outline the required elements of intent and knowledge relevant to establishing theft ([para 18]).

Application

The court analyzed the actions taken by Riba and his colleague after seizing the goods. Notably, the failure to log the items in the SAP 13 register and the absence of a valid explanation as to why they were deposited at a satellite police station raised suspicion of their intentions ([para 9]).

The explanations offered by the appellant regarding being "too busy" to document the seizure, and confusion about the ownership, were deemed inadequate ([para 11]). The testimony from Inspector Tlobatla, who indicated that had he known the goods were seized from a suspect, he would not have accepted them, further undermined the appellant's claims ([para 10]).

The timing of their eventual attempt to register the goods just prior to being contacted by Captain Morudi suggested a covering up of their actions, leading to the conclusion that they intended to appropriate the goods, hence establishing mens rea for theft ([paras 15-18]).

Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal against conviction and upheld the sentence of 4 years’ imprisonment, which was antedated to account for Riba’s pre-appeal incarceration of ten and a half months ([paras 21-22]). The court reiterated the importance of integrity in police conduct, especially in matters involving the handling of seized evidence. This case highlights the legal principles governing theft, especially by public officials, and serves as a cautionary tale regarding the ramifications of corrupt practices in law enforcement.

Overall, the court found clear indicators that intended appropriation existed, resulting in affirming the lower court's decision ([para 18]). The implications stress the necessity for law enforcement officers to adhere strictly to procedural requirements when handling property seized during investigations.
