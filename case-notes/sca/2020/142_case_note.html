Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 142

F – Facts

The appeal arises from the Gauteng Division of the High Court of South Africa, where the Southern Sun Group Retirement Fund (the Fund) sought to have regulation 35(4) of the Pension Fund Regulations, promulgated by the Minister of Finance, declared invalid on the grounds that it exceeded the Minister's powers as prescribed by the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956 (the Act) ([paras 1–2]). The Fund, initially established as a defined benefit fund on March 1, 1994, later converted to a defined contribution fund but retained certain guaranteed benefits ([para 6]).

In January 2015, the Fund's actuarial valuation report was rejected by the Registrar of Pension Funds, who asserted that the Fund could not release unclaimed surplus benefits as proposed ([paras 7–8]). Regulation 35(4) necessitated the allocation of enhancements due to untraceable former members into a contingency reserve from which no funds could be released, except for payments to those members or payments into the Guardian’s Fund ([para 58]). The Fund contended that this regulation was contradictory to its rights under the PFA, claiming that the board of trustees should have discretion in establishing contingency reserve accounts ([paras 10, 24]).

The Registrar rejected the Fund’s arguments and affirmed the validity of the regulation, leading to the initial dismissal by the High Court, which upheld the regulation’s validity as an exercise of the Minister’s regulatory powers ([paras 34–37]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue for determination by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether regulation 35(4) was ultra vires, meaning beyond the powers granted to the Minister under the PFA. Specifically, the query was whether the Minister had overstepped the legal boundaries set by the Act when enacting the regulation, thereby violating the principle of legality ([paras 2, 4]).

Secondary issues arose, including whether the court below appropriately exercised its discretion regarding condonation for the delay in bringing the application and the implications of the regulation on the Fund’s ability to manage surplus funds ([paras 30, 34]).

R – Rules

The ultimate rulings were based on various statutory provisions within the Pension Funds Act, particularly sections 15B and 36, regarding the apportionment of actuarial surpluses and the power to enact regulations concerning those surpluses ([paras 57, 58]). The definition of "contingency reserve account," as well as the legal obligations and rights recognized in the PFA, were critical in interpreting the regulation's validity ([paras 44–49]).

Additionally, the principle of legality, an essential doctrine within administrative law, mandates that any exercise of public power must be grounded in law ([paras 60, 61]).

A – Application

The SCA found that regulation 35(4) imposed restrictions on the Fund’s inherent rights as outlined in the PFA, specifically undermining the board’s discretion to determine the appropriateness of setting up a contingency reserve account ([paras 58, 60]). The regulation was deemed contradictory as it forced the board to place disputed surplus funds into a reserve account that could not be accessed for the benefit of the fund or its members except in limited circumstances ([para 59]).

The interpretation of the relevant sections of the PFA revealed that the Minister lacked the authority to demand specific actions from the Fund's board regarding surplus management, thus rendering the regulation ultra vires. The court emphasized that such discretionary powers belong firmly within the scope of the board, as established by the intent of the pension legislation, which was remedial in nature ([paras 61–65]).

In terms of the condonation for the delay, the SCA concurred with the lower court's decision and asserted that the interests of justice warranted an extension of time given the significance of the legal issues involved ([paras 36, 40]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA upheld the appeal, declaring that regulation 35(4) was invalid and unenforceable as it exceeded the Minister’s powers under the PFA. The ruling underscored the importance of statutory compliance in the governance of pension funds and reaffirmed the principle that regulatory powers should not encroach upon the discretionary authority of the pension fund boards ([paras 67]).

This judgment carries profound implications for pension fund management in South Africa, establishing clear boundaries between legislative authority and regulatory power, thereby ensuring that the administration of pension funds remains both legal and fair to all stakeholders involved. In the words of the court, the regulation "sterilized" funds which could otherwise be utilized efficiently, exposing the regulatory overreach that undermined the legislative intent of the PFA ([para 60]).

The SCA's decision reinforces the essential legal framework governing pension funds and enhances the accountability of regulatory authorities, ensuring that pension funds operate within the boundaries set out by law, ultimately serving the best interests of their members.
