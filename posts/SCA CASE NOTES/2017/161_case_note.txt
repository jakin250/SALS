Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# Case Note: FIRAC Method – IEC v Cape Party and Khai-Ma Onafhanklike Kandidate Koalisie

**Court**: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Neutral Citation**: IEC v Cape Party (1233/17); IEC v Khai-Ma Onafhanklike Kandidate Koalisie (1268/17) [2017] ZASCA 161 (27 November 2017)  
**Coram**: Bosielo and Willis JJA and Plasket, Meyer and Makgoka AJJA  
**Heard**: 16 November 2017  
**Delivered**: 27 November 2017  

---

## F – Facts

The appeals arose from two separate matters involving the **Electoral Commission of South Africa (IEC)** as the appellant against two respondents: the **Cape Party** and the **Khai-Ma Onafhanklike Kandidate Koalisie (KOKO)**. The cases stemmed from events surrounding the municipal elections conducted on 3 August 2016. 

### Cape Party Matter

In the Cape Party matter, the Electoral Court had, on 14 September 2016, ordered a recount and verification of votes for specific wards in the Western Cape. The Cape Party alleged irregularities in the vote counts, providing three primary complaints:
1. A claim that three voters from a retirement village reported having voted for the Cape Party, yet no votes were recorded.
2. A discrepancy between vote counts from the IEC's offices in Swartland: five votes were recorded initially, but a later count showed four.
3. In Bergrivier, a reported three votes led to a final tally of zero votes.

The IEC dismissed these complaints as immaterial and unlikely to affect the overall election results, prompting the Cape Party to seek judicial review ([8–10]).

### Khai-Ma Onafhanklike Kandidate Matter

The Khai-Ma Onafhanklike Kandidate matter involved a dispute over the IEC’s refusal to accept KOKO's candidate nominations for the Namakwa District municipal elections. KOKO’s leader, Mr. Nicolaas Jano, submitted paperwork for the elections and paid requisite deposits but was informed that Mr. Strauss, KOKO's candidate, was not included on the ballot for Namakwa because the submission did not adhere to the correct procedures. Mr. Jano alleged that he had been misadvised by IEC officials regarding the nomination process ([11–13]).

In both cases, the respondents did not oppose the IEC's appeals, and the court invited amici curiae to provide additional perspectives ([5–6]).

---

## I – Issues

The central legal issues before the court were:

1. **Appealability**: Whether the orders of the Electoral Court could be appealed, considering the provisions of the **Electoral Act 73 of 1998** and the **Municipal Electoral Act 27 of 2000** ([20]).
2. **Merits of the Appeals**: Assessing whether the Electoral Court erred in its determinations regarding the appeals brought forth by the IEC concerning the Cape Party and KOKO ([24], [31]).

---

## R – Rules

### Legislative Framework

1. **Section 96(1) of the Electoral Act 73 of 1998**: Asserts that decisions of the Electoral Court in electoral disputes are final and not subject to appeal or review.
2. **Section 17(2)(d) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013**: Provides the criteria under which the Supreme Court of Appeal may grant leave to appeal ([21]).
3. **Materiality Requirement in Section 65 of the Municipal Electoral Act 27 of 2000**: States that election irregularities must materially affect the results to warrant setting aside an election ([24]).

### Precedents

1. **African Christian Democratic Party v Electoral Commission**: Clarifies the court's jurisdiction regarding appeals stemming from local government elections, suggesting that the prohibition in section 96 does not apply to disputes addressed under the Municipal Electoral Act ([21]).
2. **Plascon-Evans Rule**: Pertains to the handling of factual disputes in motion proceedings, guiding how courts should evaluate evidence presented in such contexts ([29]).

---

## A – Application

**Appealability and Jurisdiction**  
The court found that the restriction against appeals under section 96 of the Electoral Act does not extend to matters under the Municipal Electoral Act. Thus, it concluded that it had jurisdiction to hear the appeals ([21–23]).

**Merits Analysis**  
1. **Cape Party Matter**: The court dismissed issues based on hearsay and irrelevant discrepancies in vote counts, adhering to the materiality standard established under section 65. The court reiterated that the minor discrepancies cited by the Cape Party could not have affected the election outcome ([24]).

2. **Khai-Ma Onafhanklike Kandidate Matter**: The court highlighted the failure of KOKO to adhere to established nomination procedures. It found that KOKO's allegations of misadvice by IEC officials did not hold up against the available evidence, especially given that KOKO did not follow the correct procedural channels. The IEC's stance was supported by the amici, emphasizing a lack of a substantive basis for KOKO's claims ([26–30]).

---

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal granted the IEC leave to appeal in both matters, ultimately upholding the appeals and setting aside the orders of the Electoral Court. The court dismissed the applications of the Cape Party and KOKO, emphasizing the importance of strict adherence to electoral procedures and materiality in electoral disputes. It refrained from imposing costs, possibly aiming for finality and efficiency in electoral matters ([32]).

The case underscores the South African judiciary's commitment to upholding the integrity of electoral processes while ensuring that legal standards related to procedural compliance are rigorously applied.
