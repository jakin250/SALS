Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

### FIRAC Case Note: Bokolo v S (483/12) [2013] ZASCA 115

#### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–5])
On the night of October 30, 2004, the appellant, Sandile Bokolo, was accused of the horrific rape and murder of his four-year-old daughter. The child lived in Khayelitsha with Bokolo and her mother. On October 30, the mother observed no injuries on her child during a bath, and the child was seen playing happily before accompanying her mother to church. During the mother’s absence, the child disappeared. After a search, her body was discovered the next morning, revealing signs of sexual assault and asphyxiation ([paras 1–5]).

Bokolo, who was absent during the child’s disappearance due to being intoxicated at a shebeen, was charged along with another accused person (Accused 2), who was convicted of both murder and rape. Bokolo was convicted only of rape and sentenced to 15 years in prison. He subsequently sought leave to appeal his conviction, which was granted ([paras 1]).

#### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 6–8])
The primary legal question presented in this appeal concerned the reliability of DNA evidence used in the conviction. Specifically, the issues revolved around: 

1. Whether the DNA evidence was sufficient to establish Bokolo’s guilt beyond a reasonable doubt.
2. The interpretation of DNA profiles and the weight attached to their matches with crime scene samples, particularly in regard to the presence and absence of specific alleles.

These issues necessitated an examination of the technical aspects of DNA profiling and the probability of such evidence supporting a conviction ([paras 6–8]).

#### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 9–21])
The court relied on established principles of DNA profiling and the procedural integrity of forensic evidence. The rules guiding the assessment of DNA evidence included:

1. **Chain of custody**: Evidence must demonstrate that samples were properly collected and analyzed without contamination ([para 18(i)]).
2. **Interpretation of profiles**: The probative value of a match between an accused’s DNA and crime scene evidence depends on factors including the possibility of a match in the general population and the quality of the analysis ([paras 18–21]). 

Legal precedents, such as *Michael & another v Linksfield Park Clinic (Pty) Ltd* and *Buthelezi v Ndaba*, emphasized that the weight of expert evidence must rest on logical reasoning ([para 19]).

#### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 24–33])
In its analysis, the court dissected the DNA evidence linking Bokolo to the crime scene. Two samples from the sanitary pad placed on the victim showed a mixture of DNA indicative of at least three males, including alleles matching Bokolo’s profile, except for allele 22 at locus FGA, which was missing from the samples ([paras 24–26]).

Colonel Otto’s expert testimony suggested that although the allele was not clearly marked, it could still be inferred from the electropherogram data. Conversely, Dr. Oosthuizen argued that the absence of the allele indicated that it could not be read into the mixture, given its absence in enriched samples ([paras 27–31]).

Ultimately, the court placed more weight on Dr. Oosthuizen's testimony. The court found that reasonable doubt existed regarding whether Bokolo's DNA profile could be definitively included in the samples taken at the crime scene. The court also considered the implausibility of the prosecution’s timeline, which posited a sequence of events that appeared highly improbable given the testimony and circumstances presented ([paras 30–33]).

#### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 32–34])
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that Bokolo should not have been convicted of rape due to insufficient evidence that convincingly linked him to the crime. The appeal was upheld, and his conviction and sentence were set aside ([para 34]).

The case highlights critical issues related to the reliability of DNA evidence in criminal proceedings, particularly emphasizing the necessity of statistical backing to support claims of probability and the importance of expert testimony. In this instance, the findings reiterate the burden of proof resting firmly on the prosecution, underlining the principle that no individual can be convicted without incontrovertible evidence of guilt beyond a reasonable doubt.
