Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH
Source Folder: 2016

# Case Note: Gwababa v S (1290/16) [2016] ZASCA 200

## FIRAC Structure

### F – Facts
This case concerns an application for leave to appeal submitted by the applicant, Lungisa Gwababa, who was one of nine accused in a criminal trial for murder and related offenses ([1]). The trial took place in the North Gauteng High Court, where Gwababa and eight others were convicted on 25 August 2015. Each accused, including Gwababa, was sentenced to 15 years' imprisonment on 11 November 2015, with the ninth co-accused being found not guilty ([1]).

The events leading to these convictions involved the death of the deceased, Mr. Silvesta Jossefa Marcia, who was a taxi driver. It was alleged that on 26 February 2013, Marcia was apprehended by police officers (the accused) following a traffic violation. Reports stated that he was handcuffed and subsequently dragged behind a police vehicle, resulting in injuries that led to his death in police custody ([6]–[10]). The trial court found that the accused, operating under a common purpose, acted unlawfully in their treatment of Marcia ([12]).

On 11 December 2015, Gwababa’s application for leave to appeal was dismissed ([1]), but on 24 May 2016, another accused, Bonginkosi Mdluli, was granted such leave based on similar facts, prompting Gwababa to seek a referral of his own case for reconsideration ([2]–[4]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal question presented to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether exceptional circumstances existed to warrant reconsideration of the earlier dismissal of Gwababa's application for leave to appeal ([15]). Specifically, the court had to ascertain whether the previous decisions regarding Mdluli and other co-accused provided a sufficient basis for overturning the trial court's judgment against Gwababa ([4]–[15]).

### R – Rules
The applicable legal rule is found within Section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, which permits a decision to be referred back to the court for reconsideration if a grave injustice could result ([15]). This provision forms the basis for the consideration of whether there are exceptional circumstances that justify such a referral. The concept of "grave injustice" denotes a substantial risk that the legal process has not adequately served justice in a manner that respects the rights of the accused ([15]).

### A – Application
In applying the aforementioned rules, the court highlighted Gwababa's reliance on the successful appeals of his co-accused and the evidence suggesting a lack of clarity in the trial court's ruling ([4]–[15]). The court noted that even though the success of the co-accused does not automatically grant Gwababa similar relief, it raised concerns about the application of the doctrine of common purpose by the trial court, as well as how intent (mens rea) was assessed in his case ([12]–[14]).

The court emphasized the trial court's failure to clarify the reasoning behind its conclusions, particularly regarding the determination of whether Gwababa was aware of the dragging incident and the potential consequences of his actions in the chaotic environment surrounding the arrest ([10]–[14]). The court expressed skepticism about the trial court's handling of evidence and the overall justification for the conviction ([14]–[15]).

Given the potential for a grave injustice to arise from a dismissal of the appeal on these grounds, the SCA deemed it necessary to refer the case back for reconsideration, thus recognizing the existence of exceptional circumstances that merited a fresh evaluation of the merits of Gwababa's application for leave to appeal ([15]).

### C – Conclusion
The SCA ultimately granted Gwababa's application for condonation for the late filing and referred the dismissal of his application for special leave to appeal back to the court for reconsideration ([16]). This decision underscores the importance of ensuring that legal processes are just and fair, particularly in cases involving serious charges such as murder. The ruling raises significant implications for the assessment of co-accused in similar cases and highlights the necessity for clarity and thoroughness in judicial reasoning ([16]).

In summary, the judgment serves as a precedent reinforcing the principle that exceptional circumstances can prompt the high court to re-evaluate prior decisions, thereby ensuring that justice is not only done but is also seen to be done effectively in the appellate context. This case will be pivotal in understanding the balance of judicial discretion and procedural fairness in the South African legal landscape.
