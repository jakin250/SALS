Matatiele Municipality and Others v President of the Republic of South Africa and Others [CCT 73/05]

A coalition of applicants, which included various community organizations, associations, and businesses from the Matatiele area, contest the constitutionality of these legislative changes, asserting that the amendments were enacted without fulfilling the consultation obligations...

Constitutional Court 2006 CCT 73/05

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

This case arose from the constitutional challenge against the Constitution Twelfth Amendment Act of 2005 (“the Twelfth Amendment”) and the Cross-boundary Municipalities Laws Repeal and Related Matters Act 23 of 2005 (“the Repeal Act”). The amendment altered the basis for determining provincial boundaries in South Africa, shifting the criterion from magisterial districts to municipal areas. Consequently, the local municipality of Matatiele was transferred from KwaZulu-Natal to the Eastern Cape Province.

A coalition of applicants, which included various community organizations, associations, and businesses from the Matatiele area, contest the constitutionality of these legislative changes, asserting that the amendments were enacted without fulfilling the consultation obligations outlined in the Constitution. Specifically, they argued that the amendments usurped the authority of the Municipal Demarcation Board, which oversees municipal boundary adjustments as mandated by sections 155(3)(b) of the Constitution ([paras 1-3]).

The respondents included the President of the Republic of South Africa and various ministers. The court received the matter as urgent due to impending municipal elections, during which the validity of the Twelfth Amendment was a crucial issue ([paras 4-5]). Ultimately, the Court expressed additional concerns regarding the procedures followed in adopting the Twelfth Amendment and directed for further submissions on relevant constitutional questions ([paras 8-10]).

I – Issues

The core constitutional questions presented in this case were:

  1. Did the Twelfth Amendment comply with section 74(8) of the Constitution, which requires provincial approval when amending boundaries affecting specific provinces ([para 13])?
  2. Were the applicants entitled to direct access to the Constitutional Court regarding the Repeal Act, and if so, was the Repeal Act constitutionally valid ([para 15])?
  3. Relatedly, what obligations did the KwaZulu-Natal Legislature have under section 118(1)(a) concerning public consultation during the boundary alteration process ([para 86])?

R – Rules

The legal framework governing this case primarily involved the following constitutional provisions:

  • Section 74: Governing the amendment of the Constitution, particularly subsections (3) and (8), which delineate the voting requirements for amendments affecting provincial boundaries.
  • Section 118(1)(a): Mandates that provincial legislatures must facilitate public involvement in legislative processes.

The judgment emphasized the importance of public participation and consultation, aligning with the participatory democratic principles embedded in the Constitution ([paras 37-41]).

Moreover, reference was made to the ruling in Doctors for Life International v The Speaker of the National Assembly regarding public involvement and the necessary conditions for legislative validity involving constitutional amendments ([paras 50-68]).

A – Application

In determining whether the Twelfth Amendment was adopted in compliance with section 74(8), the Court found that the Twelfth Amendment indeed changed boundaries that specifically affected KwaZulu-Natal. The application of section 74(8) was engaged, necessitating approval from the provincial legislature ([paras 21-26]).

The applicants contended that the KwaZulu-Natal Legislature breached its duty by failing to facilitate public consultation as mandated by section 118(1)(a). The court concurred, noting that no public hearings were held in KwaZulu-Natal to gauge community sentiments concerning the significant alteration of boundaries, which affected the identity, rights, and administrative services to the citizens of Matatiele ([paras 78-85]). Consequently, the Court found that the failure to engage with the public constituted a clear violation of the constitutional requirements.

Regarding the Repeal Act, which sought to implement changes resulting from the Twelfth Amendment, the Court concluded that since part of the Twelfth Amendment was invalidated, the corresponding sections of the Repeal Act impacting Matatiele would suffer the same fate. However, since the applicants had the right to approach the Court directly concerning the Repeal Act, their application for direct access was granted ([paras 106-112]).

C – Conclusion

The Court ultimately held that the flawed legislative process of the KwaZulu-Natal Legislature under section 118(1)(a) rendered both the relevant part of the Twelfth Amendment and the applicable sections of the Repeal Act inconsistent with the Constitution and thus invalid. However, the declaration of invalidity was suspended for 18 months to allow for parliamentary correction of the constitutional defect.

This decision underlines the necessity for public engagement in legislative processes, especially regarding constitutional amendments that materially affect the territorial integrity and governance of specific provincial populations. The judgment also has broader implications for the relationship between national and provincial legislative processes and enriches the discourse surrounding participatory democracy in South Africa ([paras 90-97], [102]).

All respondents were ordered to bear the costs of the proceedings, with the exception of related procedural costs from earlier stages, reflecting a carefully balanced approach toward the accountability of government entities involved ([paras 112-114]).

This case emphasizes the critical importance of adherence to constitutional provisions and principles concerning public participation when legislating changes that affect communities directly, thereby reinforcing constitutional democracy.
