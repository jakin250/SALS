Title: Van Heerden & Another v NDPP & Others
Neutral Citation: [2017] ZASCA 105
Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Judgment Date: 11 September 2017
Appeal No: 145/2017
Coram: Navsa ADP, Bosielo JA, Lamont, Molemela, and Fourie AJJA

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The appellants, Pienaar and Anthea Lynette Van Heerden, were previously employed by British American Tobacco South Africa (BATSA) until their dismissals in March 2010. They faced allegations of theft involving substantial quantities of cigarettes, reportedly valued initially at R5 million but later reduced to R3.47 million ([paras 4-8]). In August 2011, the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) sought and obtained a restraint order under the Prevention of Organized Crime Act 121 of 1998 (POCA), which restricted the Van Heerdens from dealing with their assets ([paras 7-8]).

The court proceedings were marked by numerous postponements, largely attributed to delays on the part of the prosecuting authority and the State's failure to finalize the charge in a timely manner ([paras 10-22, 25-26]). The prosecution was eventually struck from the roll multiple times due to these delays, with substantial extensions of time for further investigations requested by the State and the absence of a finalized charge sheet ([paras 23-60]).

In 2015, the appellants initiated a high court application for permanent stay of prosecution, claiming their constitutional right to a fair trial within a reasonable time was infringed due to the extensive delays, which had led them to suffer significant personal and financial hardship ([paras 25-34, 61-70]). The lower court dismissed their application ([para 36]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions before the Supreme Court were as follows:

  1. Were the appellants entitled to a permanent stay of prosecution due to undue delays and infringements of their constitutional rights under section 35(3) of the Constitution, guaranteeing the right to a fair trial without unreasonable delay?
  2. Was the restraint order imposed on their assets justifiable given the context and the delay in prosecution?

R – Rules

The court relied predominantly on constitutional provisions and principles relating to the right to a fair trial:

  • Section 35(3) of the Constitution of South Africa, which provides that every accused person has the right to a fair trial, including the right to have trial begin and conclude without unreasonable delay.
  • Precedents such as Sanderson v Attorney General, Eastern Cape [1997] ZACC 18, which emphasized the need for time sensitivity in prosecuting criminal cases, and the criteria established in Wild & Another v Hoffert NO & Others [1998] ZACC 5 regarding the extraordinary nature of a permanent stay of prosecution.

A – Application

The Court noted the extensive and unacceptable delays, primarily attributed to the State, which resulted in the prosecution not progressing for periods extending over several years ([paras 57-68]). It emphasized that while there were inherent complexities, systemic negligence on the part of the NDPP contributed to the unreasonable delay in prosecution.

The Supreme Court extensively reviewed the actions and inactions of the prosecution, which were characterized as dishonest and lacking urgency, especially concerning the undertaking made to the lower court ([paras 66-69]). The court found that significant prejudice was suffered by the appellants, not only due to prolonged restraint on their assets but also due to the erosion of their ability to adequately prepare a defense alongside the societal stigma attached to prolonged criminal allegations ([paras 61-64]).

The Court ultimately ruled that the infringement of the right to a fair trial was sufficiently grave, necessitating a permanent stay of prosecution ([paras 69-70]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal and granted the following orders:

  1. The prosecution against the appellants was permanently stayed.
  2. The restraint orders related to their assets were set aside.
  3. The NDPP was ordered to pay the appellants' costs, including those of two counsel ([para 71]).

The ruling underscores the importance of diligent prosecution and the protection of defendants' rights to a timely trial. The decision signifies an affirmation of constitutional rights against the backdrop of systemic issues within the prosecutorial framework, reiterating the need for accountability and efficiency in criminal proceedings.

The implications of the judgment highlight the judicial system's commitment to upholding constitutional rights against undue state delay, affirming the balance between prosecutorial discretion and an individual's right to a fair trial.
