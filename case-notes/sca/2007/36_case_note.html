Supreme Court of Appeal

Wypkema v Lubbe [2007] SCA 36 (RSA)

This case involves a dispute between Frederick Willem Wypkema (appellant) and Tobias Johannes Lubbe (respondent) concerning a loan agreement and a dishonoured cheque drawn on the respondent’s trust account.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2007

F – Facts

This case involves a dispute between Frederick Willem Wypkema (appellant) and Tobias Johannes Lubbe (respondent) concerning a loan agreement and a dishonoured cheque drawn on the respondent’s trust account.

On 13 September 2004, the respondent, a practising attorney, sought a bridging loan of R1,850,000 (with a fee of R350,000) on behalf of his client, Rooihak Eiendomme (Edms) Bpk (Rooihak). The appellant agreed to provide the loan under the condition that the respondent would furnish a cheque for R2,200,000, which included the loan amount and the fee. The respondent delivered a cheque drawn on his firm’s trust account, dated 14 September 2004, to the appellant. The cheque was presented for payment by the appellant on 30 September 2004, but it was dishonoured due to insufficient funds marked ‘effects not cleared’ ([paras 1–3]).

The Pretoria High Court, presided over by Van Rooyen AJ, found in favour of the respondent, concluding that the respondent issued the cheque in a representative capacity, thus not personally liable. The court relied on provisions of the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979, particularly sections 78(1) and 78(7), asserting that the funds in a trust account are meant for clients and do not form part of the attorney's own assets ([paras 4–5]).

I – Issues

The primary issues for resolution by the Supreme Court of Appeal were as follows:

  1. Was the respondent personally liable for the cheque drawn from his trust account, or was he acting as an agent for Rooihak, thus exempting him from personal liability?
  2. Did the agreement underlying the cheque contain suspensive conditions, particularly regarding the registration of a mortgage bond over the concerned property?
  3. Could the agreement or the act of issuing the trust account cheque be deemed unlawful under the principle of contra bonos mores? ([paras 8–18]).

R – Rules

The court relied on several key legal authorities and statutory provisions:

  1. Attorneys Act 53 of 1979: Section 78(1) mandates attorneys to keep a separate trust account for clients’ funds. Subsection 78(7) establishes that funds in such an account are not part of the attorney's assets, effectively creating a fiduciary responsibility ([para 4]).
  2. Bills of Exchange Act 34 of 1964: Section 24(1) provides that a drawer who signs a bill (or cheque) without indicating they act in a representative capacity assumes personal liability ([para 8]).
  3. Case Law Precedents:
  • Fuhri v Geyser NO 1979 (1) SA 747 (N): Established the principles determining the relationship between trust account funds and the attorney’s liability.
  • De Villiers NO v Kaplan 1960 (4) SA 476 (C): Affirmed the attorney's authority to manage trust account funds but differentiated ownership from the right of control ([paras 6–7]).
  • Archbolds (Freightage) Ltd v S Spanglett Ltd [1961] 1 All ER 417 (CA): Discussed the implications of illegality in contractual agreements.

A – Application

In overturning the lower court's ruling, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that the court a quo erred in determining that the cheque was issued on behalf of Rooihak, rather than personally by the respondent. The absence of qualifying language on the cheque indicated that the respondent was acting in his personal capacity and therefore liable for the amount ([para 8]).

Additionally, regarding the alleged suspensive condition tied to the mortgage bond's registration, the court concluded that the evidence did not support any claims of such conditions being in place. The communication accompanying the cheque primarily stipulated that it would not be presented for payment before 29 September 2004, rather than stipulating any suspensive condition requiring prior registration ([paras 10–11]).

The court addressed concerns surrounding the legality of the cheque transaction, particularly in terms of whether the cheque was issued with an unlawful purpose. It established that there was insufficient evidence to conclude that either party intended to execute the agreement in an unlawful manner. The respondent failed to demonstrate any awareness of unlawfulness at the time of making the loan agreement ([paras 12–20]).

Finally, the court highlighted that only the dishonoured cheque and unfulfilled promises suggested irregularities in the respondent's conduct, warranting further investigation. However, this did not negate the enforceability of the cheque itself under conditions that did not amount to a violation of the law ([paras 21–22]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, thereby concluding that:

  • The respondent was personally liable for the dishonoured cheque as he acted as a principal and not in a representative capacity.
  • The argument for a suspensive condition related to the mortgage bond lacks support and thus did not render the agreement or the cheque invalid.
  • No unlawful intent was established that would preclude the enforcement of the loan agreement.

Consequently, the court ordered provisional sentence in favour of the appellant for the amount due, including interest and costs. The decision emphasizes the responsibilities of attorneys concerning trust accounts and reaffirms the principle that personal liability may arise from actions taken in a professional capacity when trust account funds are improperly used ([paras 22]).

The court also advised the Law Society of the Northern Provinces to review the conduct of the respondent for possible disciplinary measures, reflecting the judiciary's commitment to maintaining ethical standards in the legal profession.
