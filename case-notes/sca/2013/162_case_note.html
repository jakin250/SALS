Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 162

F (Facts)

This case involves an appeal by the Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality (the first appellant) and the Gauteng Department of Housing (the second appellant) against the decision of the South Gauteng High Court which had dismissed their application for the eviction of various unlawful occupiers from the housing development at Eden Park Extension 5 (Ext 5). The project for subsidized housing was initiated to address a backlog in housing needs in the community, particularly targeting applicants who were homeless or inadequately housed ([1]–[2]).

Starting in 2000, the Eden Park housing project faced numerous challenges including donor funding withdrawal, leading to a takeover by the municipal and provincial authorities ([4]). The allocation process for the housing units became contentious, particularly after a series of developments that saw only a small number of houses allocated to local residents despite significant applications from the Eden Park community ([3]). Tensions escalated with community protests and petitions leading to mass occupations of 651 incomplete houses by the respondents starting on 9 October 2008 ([8]–[10]).

Given the background of failed promises regarding housing allocations, the appellants' approach to eviction was deemed unjust and inequitable, as confirmed by the findings of the High Court which cited uncertainty over the identity of the occupiers and the integrity of the housing allocation process challenges ([10]–[11]).

I (Issues)

The central legal issue involves the applicability and interpretation of the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE) in conjunction with constitutional rights to housing as stipulated in Sections 26 and 28 of the South African Constitution. Key questions included:

  1. What constitutes "just and equitable" circumstances for eviction as required by PIE?
  2. How does the state's obligation under the Constitution impact the eviction proceedings initiated by an organ of state?
  3. Did the appellants meet the required burden of demonstrating that eviction was justified and in compliance with the dictates of PIE ([10]–[14])?

R (Rules)

The ruling was significantly influenced by the constitutional provisions encapsulated in Section 26, which mandates access to adequate housing and requires that evictions should not be arbitrary ([11]). Additionally, Section 28(1)(c) asserts the rights of children to basic shelter. The relevant legislative framework governing eviction is PIE, particularly:

  • Section 4, addressing the process of eviction and conditions under which a court can grant eviction orders, thus requiring an assessment of justice and equity ([15]).
  • Intersectional considerations highlighted in previous case law, such as Grootboom and the standard practices expected of state organs in making housing allocations, formed the basis of the court’s reasons for denying eviction ([12]).

A (Application)

The court applied constitutional reasoning to assess the justness of the eviction through a detailed inquiry into several factors, including:

  1. Reasonableness of State Actions: The court emphasized that state actions related to housing must reflect a commitment to human dignity and equitable treatment ([12]–[13]). The appellants were criticized for the unclear criteria used in the waiting list and housing allocation process that led to arbitrary outcomes ([26]).
  2. Unlawful Conduct vs. System Failures: Although the occupation was itself unlawful, the court established that the roots of this conduct were tied to systemic failings in housing policy implementation and community engagement, resulting in the perception that eviction would further marginalize already disadvantaged individuals ([20]–[24]).
  3. Balancing of Individual Rights: The court elaborated on the necessity of balancing individual rights and public interest, underscoring that the eviction order would further exacerbate the impoverished conditions of the respondents ([25]–[28]).
  4. Community Promises and Expectations: Testimonies indicated that promises made by municipal officials to prioritize local beneficiaries were not honored, which further compounded the community's grievances and justified their desperate actions to occupy the housing units ([27]).

C (Conclusion)

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court's decision, affirming that an eviction would not be "just and equitable" under the prevailing circumstances. The court underscored the obligations imposed on the state under the Constitution to ensure fair access to housing, highlighting significant procedural failings and the lack of clarity in the implementation of housing policies ([29]).

The case illustrates the complexities surrounding unlawful occupations, state obligations, and individual housing rights while reaffirming the need for housing policy that is transparent, equitable, and sensitive to the historical context and rights of affected communities. This ruling has significant implications for future eviction cases initiated by state organs, emphasizing that the right to housing should be prioritized even in instances where occupants have settled unlawfully.

The appeal was thus dismissed with costs, reflecting the court's endorsement of a careful, rights-sensitive approach to eviction proceedings ([29]).
