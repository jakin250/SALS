Ndabeni v Municipal Manager: OR Tambo District Municipality and Another [2021] ZASCA 08

On December 13, 2016, the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court (Mjali J) declared Ndabeni a permanent employee through the aforementioned resolution and required the municipality to pay her salary and benefits retrospectively from the date of cessation ([para 2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 08

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case of Ndabeni v Municipal Manager: OR Tambo District Municipality revolves around the appellant, Nosipho Portia Ndabeni, who was employed under a one-year contract beginning on July 1, 2005, as the Aids Training Information and Counselling Centre Manager ([para 1]). Her contract was renewed multiple times, but she was notably excluded from a December 2010 resolution by the OR Tambo District Municipality (second respondent) that converted temporary positions to permanent ones, known as resolution 10/11 ([paras 1–2]).

On December 13, 2016, the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court (Mjali J) declared Ndabeni a permanent employee through the aforementioned resolution and required the municipality to pay her salary and benefits retrospectively from the date of cessation ([para 2]). The respondents failed to comply, prompting Ndabeni to launch contempt proceedings in February 2019, alleging the municipality's unlawful non-compliance with the court order ([paras 4–5]).

The first respondent, Owen Ngubende Hlazo, the municipal manager, and the municipality contended that complying with the court's order would contravene section 66 of the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000 ([paras 5–6]). The high court dismissed Ndabeni's contempt application, finding Mjali J's order to be a nullity since it unlawfully required employment contrary to legal provisions regarding municipal staff establishments ([paras 6–7]). Ndabeni appealed this decision ([paras 7–8]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues presented to the Supreme Court of Appeal were:

  1. Whether the High Court's order of December 13, 2016, was a nullity because implementing it would contravene the provisions of the Municipal Systems Act ([para 1]).
  2. Whether the respondents’ failure to comply with Mjali J’s order constituted contempt of court ([para 6]).

R – Rules

The rules governing this case included:

  1. The binding nature of court orders as per section 165(5) of the Constitution of South Africa, which mandates compliance from all persons and state organs ([para 8]).
  2. The necessary standards of proof in contempt proceedings, which require the applicant to demonstrate non-compliance on a balance of probabilities ([para 5]).
  3. The requirements under section 66 of the Municipal Systems Act, which stipulate that appointments to municipal positions must conform to an approved staff establishment, making non-compliant appointments null and void ([para 11]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the high court erred in concluding that Mjali J’s order was a nullity. The Court emphasized that Mjali J’s order did not directly appoint Ndabeni but merely declared her rights under resolution 10/11 ([paras 15–16]). Furthermore, the Court rejected the argument that complying with the order would breach section 66 of the Municipal Systems Act, affirming that the respondents had failed to discharge their evidentiary burden to prove that their non-compliance was neither wilful nor with mala fides ([paras 21–24]).

The respondents’ assertion that salaries would divert funds from essential services and their fears regarding personal liability were found not to absolve them of their responsibilities to comply with a court order ([paras 20–23]). They had not presented sufficient evidence justifying their disregard for the order, thus failing to demonstrate a factual basis for their claims ([para 24]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, ruling that the municipality's failure to comply with Mjali J's order was unlawful and constituted contempt of court. The order of the high court was set aside, and the Court issued a new order declaring the respondents in contempt, instructing them to comply with the original order while recognizing that certain provisions were overbroad and thus limiting the extent of Mjali J’s declarations ([paras 27–28]).

The case serves as a critical acknowledgment of the binding nature of court orders and reinforces the necessity for state institutions to adhere to the rule of law while passing judgments regarding the employment relationship within local government structures. The Court emphasized that public functionaries must rectify unlawfulness in their operations and respect duly constituted court orders ([para 25]).
