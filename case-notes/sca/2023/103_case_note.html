Supreme Court of Appeal

Freedom Under Law v Judicial Service Commission and Another

A Judicial Conduct Tribunal (JCT) was formed to investigate; it concluded that Motata’s conduct amounted to gross misconduct and recommended to the Judicial Service Commission (JSC) that proceedings be initiated for his removal under section 177(1)(a) of the Constitution...

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 103 (22 June 2023) ## FIRAC Case Note ###

Case no: 550/2022
[2023] ZASCA 103 (22 June 2023)

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The case arose from the actions of Judge Nkola John Motata, who, in the early hours of 6 January 2007, was involved in a car incident where he collided with a boundary wall while allegedly under the influence of alcohol. Following this incident, Motata engaged in a verbal altercation with property owner Mr. Richard Baird, which involved racist remarks and refusals to cooperate with police officers ([1]–[4]). Judge Motata was convicted of driving under the influence and ultimately acquitted of resisting arrest ([5]–[9]). Multiple complaints were lodged against him, most notably from the Catholic Commission for Justice and Peace, AfriForum, and advocate Gerrit Pretorius regarding his egregious conduct ([8]–[10]).

A Judicial Conduct Tribunal (JCT) was formed to investigate; it concluded that Motata’s conduct amounted to gross misconduct and recommended to the Judicial Service Commission (JSC) that proceedings be initiated for his removal under section 177(1)(a) of the Constitution ([17]–[19]). However, the JSC later rejected these findings following internal deliberations wherein it deemed that while Motata's conduct was unbecoming, it did not constitute gross misconduct ([20]–[21]).

Freedom Under Law (FUL), the appellant, sought a review of the JSC's decision in the Gauteng High Court, arguing that Motata's actions warranted his removal ([22]–[23]). The High Court dismissed FUL's application against the AfriForum complaint but remitted the Pretorius SC complaint for further consideration ([23]). FUL appealed the dismissal of the review, prompting the JSC to cross-appeal the remittal order ([24]–[27]).

I – Issues

The principal legal questions arose from the JSC's interpretation and application of its statutory obligations under section 20 of the Judicial Service Commission Act 9 of 1994. Key issues included:

  1. Whether the JSC's decision to reject the findings of the Tribunal was justified, considering the established misconduct on part of Judge Motata.
  2. Did the JSC comply with its statutory obligations to consider the Tribunal's report and findings, particularly regarding the alleged misconduct and the procedural integrity of the complaint process ([29]–[30])?
  3. Whether the allegations of racial and derogatory remarks and the overall conduct of Judge Motata warranted a finding of gross misconduct ([1], [2], [78])?

R – Rules

The Court primarily applied the Constitutional provisions found in section 177 regarding the removal of judges and the operational framework set out in the JSC Act. Relevant legal standards from case law were cited, including:

  • The principle that a judge must maintain the highest standards of conduct, as elucidated in Re Chief Justice of Gibraltar and echoed in various judicial precedents stating that removal can only be justified in cases of egregious conduct undermining public confidence in the judiciary ([1]–[2], [22], [39]).
  • The statutory requirements under sections 20(1) to (5) of the JSC Act, which outlines the procedural obligations of the JSC to consider Tribunal reports and make necessary determinations on alleged misconduct ([104]–[112]).
  • Judicial precedent indicating the imperative of protecting public confidence in the judiciary, as highlighted in the Freedom Under Law v Acting Chairperson: Judicial Service Commission ([11]–[12]).

A – Application

In applying these legal standards, the Supreme Court of Appeal evaluated the substantive and procedural matters before the JSC. The Court expressed concern over the validity and rationale of the JSC's decision to reject the Tribunal's findings. It noted that while the JSC has discretion, it is not at liberty to disregard findings without substantial justification ([20], [20]–[34]).

Critical to the Court's decision was the premise that the JSC failed to appropriately consider the impact of Judge Motata’s racist remarks and overall conduct on public confidence in the judiciary, which constituted a breach of both ethical standards and constitutional mandate ([80]–[81]). The majority opinion held that the failure of the JSC to substantiate its rejection of the Tribunal's findings resulted in a decision that deserved to be set aside ([90]–[92]).

Ultimately, the Court found that the gross misconduct reflected in his conduct was sufficiently proven, and an order for his removal from judicial office was mandated due to the nature of his actions ([23], [94]–[96]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld FUL's appeal and dismissed the JSC's cross-appeal, emphasizing the severity of Judge Motata's misconduct. The Court ruled that the JSC must reconvene to address the case within the framework of section 20(4) of the JSC Act and facilitate appropriate actions consistent with the findings of gross misconduct. This decision was pivotal in reinstating public confidence in the integrity of the judiciary by reaffirming that judges must adhere to the highest standards of conduct ([1], [95], [96]).

The case highlights essential standards for judicial conduct and the responsibilities of oversight bodies in addressing misconduct, ultimately reinforcing constitutional principles aimed at preserving the integrity of the judiciary.
