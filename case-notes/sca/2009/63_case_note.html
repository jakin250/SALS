Supreme Court of Appeal

SARS v Moresport (PTY) LTD

The case arises from an appeal regarding an ex parte warrant issued by Magistrate Samuel Maku NO allowing for the seizure of counterfeit goods ([para 3]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2009 SARS v Moresport (360/08) [2009] ZASCA 63 Delivered 29 May 2009

Neutral Citation: SARS v Moresport (360/08) [2009] ZASCA 63
Coram: Nugent, Ponnan, Mlambo, Mhlantla JJA, Tshiqi AJA
Heard: 18 May 2009
Delivered: 29 May 2009

F – Facts

The case arises from an appeal regarding an ex parte warrant issued by Magistrate Samuel Maku NO allowing for the seizure of counterfeit goods ([para 3]). The appellant, the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (SARS), along with Crocs Inc, which owns the copyright to the Crocs footwear design, sought the seizure of 5,015 pairs of sandals alleged to be counterfeit ([para 3]).

On 20 June 2006, Crocs Inc submitted an application to SARS for the seizure of goods infringing its copyright, which was subsequently approved ([para 5]). The relevant footwear shipment had been imported by the first respondent, Moresport (PTY) LTD, which purchased the sandals from a supplier in Vancouver ([para 6]).

The goods arrived in Cape Town in July 2006, and upon inspection, a customs officer confirmed that the sandals appeared counterfeit. The customs officer advised Crocs Inc of the seizure ([para 6]). On 4 September 2006, Moresport disputed the seizure in correspondence, stating that it had a defense against Crocs Inc's claims ([para 7]). However, this defense was not disclosed to the magistrate when the warrant was sought on 14 September 2006 ([para 8]).

Moresport later applied to the High Court to set aside the warrant, which was granted by Judge Seriti, who held that the failure to disclose the defense constituted a failure of utmost good faith, thus rendering the warrant invalid ([para 8]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the non-disclosure of a defense by Moresport in the ex parte application for a seizure warrant rendered the warrant invalid due to lack of good faith ([para 8]). The case also raised the question of what constitutes material facts in the context of ex parte applications under the Counterfeit Goods Act 37 of 1997 ([para 9]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal framework is provided under the Counterfeit Goods Act 37 of 1997, particularly Sections 15(1), 15(3), and 6(1) concerning the seizure of counterfeit goods ([para 1]). According to Section 15(3), the Commissioner must grant an application for seizure if there are reasonable grounds to believe that the goods are counterfeit.

The procedural rules for ex parte applications require the utmost good faith and full disclosure of material facts. Previous case law, including National Director of Public Prosecutions v Basson and Schlesinger v Schlesinger, emphasizes a litigant's duty to disclose any material facts that may influence the court's decision ([para 8]).

A – Application

In its analysis, the Supreme Court of Appeal highlighted the relevant legal principles governing ex parte applications, particularly the emphasis on good faith and full disclosure ([para 9]). The court recognized past decisions that demonstrated an expectation for applicants to reveal facts that might materially affect the outcome, but distinguished this from the disclosure of defenses that may be raised later in proceedings ([para 9]).

The court determined that the magistrate's role when issuing a warrant was to ascertain whether there were reasonable grounds for believing that an act of dealing in counterfeit goods had occurred, rather than to adjudicate on the actual merits of the case ([para 9]). As such, the failure to disclose Moresport's defense did not automatically taint the application for the warrant.

Ultimately, the Court concluded that the material facts relevant to the issuance of the warrant were adequately disclosed, aligning with the statutory requirements of the Counterfeit Goods Act, and the Customs officer merely needed to establish a prima facie case for the warrant to be issued, devoid of a formal burden of proof ([para 9]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal against the High Court's ruling, concluding that the failure to disclose Moresport's defense did not render the ex parte application invalid. The Court held that all necessary facts had been disclosed satisfactorily, permitting the magistrate to issue the warrant based on the prima facie evidence of probable counterfeit infringement ([para 10]). The Court emphasized that while ethical considerations guide the disclosure obligations, these do not extend to requiring the disclosure of defenses for an ex parte application of this nature.

The ruling not only reinstated the seizure warrant but also reaffirmed the procedural integrity of ex parte applications under the Counterfeit Goods Act, thereby ensuring that the authorities remain empowered to act decisively against counterfeit goods entering the South African market ([para 10]). This decision has broader implications for the enforcement of intellectual property rights and the effectiveness of regulatory measures against counterfeit goods, reinforcing the significance of statutory compliance and efficient customs operations in safeguarding intellectual property.
