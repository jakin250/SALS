Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

### Case Note: City of Cape Town v The South African Human Rights Commission and Others (1337/2022; 368/2023) [2024] ZASCA 110

#### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–5])

In this case, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) examined an appeal brought by the City of Cape Town (the City) concerning its response to unlawful occupation of its land by homeless individuals. Between April and July 2020, the City’s Anti-Land Invasion Unit (ALIU), acting on behalf of the municipality, removed several individuals from its unoccupied land without a court order. This conduct included the demolition of informal structures—commonly referred to as shacks—constructed by these individuals, which involved both demolition and destruction of personal belongings. During this process, allegations arose regarding the use of excessive force, leading to accusations of inhumane treatment ([paras 2–3]).

On July 8, 2020, the South African Human Rights Commission (the Commission) filed an urgent application in the Western Cape High Court seeking interlocutory relief on behalf of the affected individuals, particularly focusing on two main parts: (i) a request for an interdict to prevent further removals and (ii) a declaratory judgment on the City’s conduct. The High Court ruled that the City’s actions were unlawful and unconstitutional, determining that their reliance on counter-spoliation—a common law defense allowing a party to reclaim possession of property—was incorrectly applied ([paras 4–5]).

#### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 8–9])

The primary legal issue at stake was whether the City was justified in its counter-spoliation actions—the practice of reclaiming possession of land after unlawful dispossession—without first seeking a court order as mandated by the applicable laws, especially in light of the constitutional rights to dignity and housing. Specifically, the SCA had to determine whether the High Court correctly concluded that the City misapplied the principles governing counter-spoliation, particularly regarding the timing of its actions (i.e., whether it acted "instanter," meaning immediately) ([para 8]). Additionally, the adequacy of supervision in such cases where municipalities may act against unlawful occupations was debated ([para 1]).

#### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 10–16])

The ruling referenced seminal legal principles concerning counter-spoliation as articulated in previous cases, notably *Tswelopele Non-Profit Organisation v City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality* and *Yeko v Qana*, which shaped the contemporary understanding of self-help remedies in property law ([paras 10–13]). According to the case law, counter-spoliation is a time-sensitive remedy only applicable where possession has not been perfected. It requires that the property be in a state of being unlawfully occupied without significant construction or occupation that would indicate a claim of peaceful possession ([paras 11–12]).

Additionally, the principles highlighted in the *Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act* (PIE) play a significant role in governing how municipalities manage land invasions, stressing the obligation of local authorities to act within the constitutional framework when dealing with housing and evictions ([para 4]). The fundamental rights enshrined in sections 10, 14(c), and 26(3) of the Constitution were instrumental in guiding the court's reasoning regarding the legality and humanity of the City's actions and the broader implications for society’s vulnerable populations ([para 36]).

#### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 30–38])

In applying these principles, the SCA identified that the City did not meet the "instanter" requirement of counter-spoliation. Evidence indicated that structures on the land were already developed to a state where they could be deemed inhabited, which meant that the City should have sought relief through a mandament van spolie or similar legal framework rather than resorting to self-help measures ([paras 30–32]). 

The Court further criticized the City’s handling of the situation for failing to provide adequate criteria for distinguishing between occupied and unoccupied structures, exposing a reliance on subjective judgment rather than objective standards or legal guidelines ([para 32]). A notable point was the testimony from ALIU personnel that many structures were equipped with furniture and other belongings, contradicting assertions that they were unoccupied ([paras 30, 33]).

The court stressed the need for municipalities to maintain a balance between law enforcement and constitutional obligations, particularly emphasizing the dignity and rights of residents engaging in land occupations, which are often driven by socio-economic imperatives ([para 37]). By necessitating judicial oversight in eviction scenarios, the court aimed to guard against arbitrary municipal actions that could otherwise infringe on citizens' rights. 

#### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 38–43])

In conclusion, the SCA dismissed the appeal, affirming the High Court's ruling that the City acted unlawfully and unconstitutionally in its evictions and demolitions. The court underscored that, while municipalities can invoke counter-spoliation under specific circumstances, such actions must be immediate and cannot infringe on the rights guaranteed by the Constitution ([para 43]). The ruling established critical precedents for how municipalities should approach unlawful occupations, emphasizing the necessity of judicial oversight in their operations.

This case serves as an essential reference point for municipal authorities, civil society, and legal practitioners regarding the complex interplay between property rights, human rights, and the ethical obligations of local governments. The decision reinforces the principle that local governments must operate within the constitutional framework and fosters a commitment to human dignity and respect for the rule of law, particularly in matters affecting the most vulnerable segments of society.
