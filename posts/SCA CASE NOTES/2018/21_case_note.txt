Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: DPP Eastern Cape v Yoyo (581/17) [2018] ZASCA 21

**F – Facts**  
This case involves an appeal by the Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP) against a sentence imposed by the High Court of the Eastern Cape Division on the respondent, Willie Themba Yoyo. Yoyo was charged with attempted rape, having pleaded guilty under section 3 of the Criminal Law (Sexual Offences and Related Matters) Amendment Act 32 of 2007, as well as section 261 of the Criminal Procedure Act, 51 of 1977 ([para 1–2]). During his guilty plea, Yoyo admitted to attempting to engage in sexual intercourse with a four-year-old child, who was the daughter of his girlfriend. The incident occurred on 23 December 2015 while the child was at Yoyo's residence, where he attempted to penetrate her after removing her panties ([para 4–5]). The court imposed a sentence of six years' imprisonment, with half suspended for three years on ambiguous conditions ([para 3]). Medical evidence presented during the trial indicated non-conclusive findings concerning sexual assault, noting only minimal physical injury ([para 6]).

The High Court noted the severity of the crime, yet Yoyo's plea of guilty and the absence of serious injuries were considered mitigating factors ([para 8]). Consequently, the court imposed a sentence it deemed appropriate for the circumstances, although the DPP contended that the sentence did not reflect the gravity of the offence amidst growing concerns regarding child sexual abuse in South Africa ([para 9]).

**I – Issues**  
1. Was the sentence of six years' imprisonment, with half suspended, unduly lenient and disproportionate to the seriousness of the offence?
2. Did the High Court misdirect itself in the imposition of the sentence such that it warranted intervention by the Supreme Court of Appeal?

**R – Rules**  
The foundational legal principles applicable to sentencing include the necessity for the sentence to adequately reflect the seriousness of the offence, particularly in cases involving sexual violence against minors. The court emphasized the need for a deterrent effect, especially in light of the prevalence of similar offences in South Africa ([para 9]). Relevant case law, including *S v Kgosimore* and *S v Ncanywa*, elucidates that an appellate court may interfere with a trial court’s sentence if there is a misdirection in the exercise of discretion ([para 12]). The standard for interference is whether the trial court failed to exercise its discretion reasonably and properly ([para 12]).

**A – Application**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the High Court's choice to impose a six-year sentence with suspension was not sufficiently justified, particularly considering the nature of the offence and the psychological impact on the complainant ([para 10–11]). The fact that Yoyo stood in a position of trust and abused that position was viewed as an aggravating factor ([para 10]). Moreover, the Court noted that the High Court did not adequately explain the rationale behind such a lenient sentence given the context and severity of the crime. This was exacerbated by the observed trend of leniency in sentences for similar offences, which undermines the effect of deterrence ([para 9]). The Supreme Court highlighted specific precedents wherein far harsher sentences had been implemented for comparable offences ([para 11]), thus reinforcing that the sentence in the present case lacked proportionality to the crime committed. Importantly, counsel for the respondent acknowledged in argument that a ten-year sentence would indeed be appropriate given the circumstances, further affirming the appellate court's stance([para 13]).

**C – Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court upheld the appeal and substituted the original six-year sentence with a ten-year term of imprisonment, antedated to 18 May 2017 ([para 14]). This decision underscores the court's commitment to ensuring that sentences reflect the seriousness of sexual offences against children, particularly given the societal imperative to deter such crimes. The implications of this judgment stress the importance of adhering to principles of justice and proportionality in sentencing, particularly for offences that traumatize vulnerable individuals. The ruling not only serves to correct the leniency exhibited by the lower court but also reinforces the paramount importance of safeguarding children within the legal framework against sexual exploitation.
