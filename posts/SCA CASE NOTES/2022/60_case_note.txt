Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# Case Note: Heidi Nicole Koch N O and Another v The Ad hoc Central Authority for the Republic of South Africa and Another (188/2021) [2022] ZASCA 60

## F – Facts
The case involves a four-and-a-half-year-old girl (the child) caught in a dispute between her parents after the mother, diagnosed with advanced colorectal cancer, brought her from the UK to South Africa for medical treatment. Initially, the child’s father agreed to this arrangement with the understanding that they would return to the UK after the mother’s recovery or in case of her death ([1–2], [6]). After the mother realized her condition was terminal, she expressed a desire for the child to remain in South Africa with her maternal aunt in the event of her death ([4]).

Despite the father’s initial consent for the temporary stay, he later sought the child’s return to the UK, asserting wrongful retention under the Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction, 1980 ([5]). The mother opposed this on two grounds: first, that the father had consented to the child’s prolonged stay, and second, that returning the child to the UK would expose her to grave psychological harm due to the father's history of mental health issues and alcohol misuse ([6], [45]).

The High Court dismissed the mother’s defences and ordered the child’s return, a decision which the aunt (acting as a co-appellant) challenged on appeal ([7], [18]). The mother passed away shortly before the High Court's decision ([7]).

## I – Issues
Two primary legal issues were addressed by the Supreme Court of Appeal:

1. **Consent and Acquiescence:** Whether the High Court erred in rejecting the mother’s defence under Article 13(a) of the Convention, which requires consent or acquiescence for a wrongful retention claim to fail.
  
2. **Risk of Harm:** Whether the mother established a valid defence under Article 13(b) of the Convention by demonstrating that returning the child to the UK would expose her to grave risk of psychological or physical harm ([8], [32]).

A subsidiary issue also arose regarding the admission of further evidence related to the child’s psychological state following the mother's death, which was deemed pertinent to the assessment of risk ([20]).

## R – Rules
The legal framework principally governing this case includes the following provisions of the Hague Convention, which seeks to protect children from wrongful removal or retention:

- **Article 3:** Outlines wrongful removal or retention. It stipulates that a child is wrongfully retained if the conditions of lawful custody rights are violated.
  
- **Article 12:** Establishes that a child wrongfully retained must be returned promptly unless they have settled into their new environment.

- **Article 13:** Excludes the immediate return of a child if the opposing party demonstrates either (a) that the person with custody did not exercise rights of custody at the time of retention, or (b) that there is a grave risk of harm ([30]).

Furthermore, South African case law indicates that the burden of proof for defences under Article 13 lies with the party opposing return ([32]).

## A – Application
### Consent and Acquiescence
The court assessed whether the father consented to the child remaining in South Africa beyond the scope of the mother’s treatment. Evidence presented by the mother confirmed that the initial consent was tied explicitly to her treatment ([35]). The court found that this consent did not extend to a permanent arrangement, given the mother's shift in intent following her prognosis ([44]).

### Risk of Harm
In evaluating the claim under Article 13(b), the court considered expert testimonies regarding the potential psychological impact of returning the child. Reports from psychologists suggested that the child’s attachment to her aunt was critical for her emotional stability, and uprooting her from this environment would pose a grave risk of harm ([46]–[52]).

The court further clarified that the psychological harm must be substantial and severe, considering the unique context of this case, which included the child having lost her mother shortly before the appeal ([74]). Both the High Court and the Supreme Court noted concerns about the adequacy of proposed protective measures in the UK, particularly considering the father's background and the unpredictable nature of care the child might receive ([63]).

### Admission of Further Evidence
The Supreme Court granted the application to introduce new evidence relating to the child’s psychological state post the mother’s death. This was deemed relevant as it could substantiate the claim regarding grave risk under Article 13(b), aligning with the interests of justice ([20], [25]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the High Court erred in its findings. It held that the mother had not established that the father’s consent extended to a permanent arrangement, thereby affirming the wrongful retention principle. More critically, it determined that the risk factors associated with returning the child to the UK—especially in light of the psychological assessments presented—were compelling enough to warrant refusing the return order.

The appeal was upheld, with the order from the High Court regarding the child’s return set aside ([78]). This decision reinforces the principles surrounding the Hague Convention while simultaneously highlighting the necessity of considering the specific social and emotional contexts impacting children in international custody disputes. The implications of this ruling stress the importance of ensuring protective measures tailored to the child’s needs in cases of abduction or wrongful retention.
