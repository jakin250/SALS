Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

# FIRAC Case Note: Falk v NDPP (689/09) [2010] ZASCA 117

## F – Facts 

The appeal in question revolves around the legal effects of a foreign restraint order registered in South Africa under the *International Co-operation in Criminal Matters Act 75 of 1996* (ICCMA) and the *Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998* (POCA). The appellants, Alexander Gerhard Falk and Falk Real Estate SA (Pty) Ltd, contested the dismissal by Louw J of their application to set aside a foreign restraint order and interdicts issued by the NDPP ([1]).

Falk was arrested in Germany on 6 June 2003 on multiple charges, which included allegations of share price manipulation. A German court issued an order on 25 August 2004 for the attachment of assets valued at over €31 million to secure potential forfeiture. Following a request from German authorities for assistance in enforcing this order, a certified copy was lodged with the Western Cape High Court on 13 September 2004, and it was registered pursuant to s 24 of the ICCMA ([2]). 

The NDPP sought interdicts on 16 August 2006 against Falk and his company, preventing them from dealing with significant assets pending the legal proceedings ([4]). This included a prohibition against the disposal of shares and funds amounting to €5.22 million ([4]).

Upon appeal, the court noted that the interdicts and registration of the foreign order remained in effect, leading to the current appeal ([1], [10]). The underlying criminal proceedings in Germany were ongoing, with Falk ultimately convicted in 2008, but without a forfeiture order being issued at that stage ([7]).

## I – Issues 

The central legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal include:

1. Whether the foreign restraint order was correctly registered under the ICCMA.
2. If the interdicts imposed by the NDPP were validly issued, particularly regarding the application of ancillary orders under POCA.
3. The interpretation of provisions within the ICCMA and POCA regarding the jurisdiction of South African courts over foreign restraint orders and their implications on the interests of justice ([10], [11], [15]).

## R – Rules 

The relevant statutory provisions include:

- **International Co-operation in Criminal Matters Act**:
  - **Section 24**: Details the procedure for registering foreign restraint orders and the conditions for such registration.
  - **Section 25**: States that a registered foreign restraint order has the same effect as a South African order.
  - **Section 26**: Outlines the grounds on which the registration of a foreign restraint order can be set aside ([3]).

- **Prevention of Organised Crime Act**:
  - **Section 26(8)**: Allows for ancillary orders to be granted alongside a restraint order.
  - **Section 26(10)**: Mandates the rescission of a restraint order when criminal proceedings conclude without a confiscation order ([11]).

The court also cited the principles surrounding appeal procedures and the jurisdiction of South African High Courts over foreign decisions, highlighting the limitations inherent in enforcing foreign civil judgments ([10], [14]).

## A – Application 

The court examined the arguments provided by the appellants concerning the jurisdictional and procedural validity of the foreign restraint order. They claimed that the registration should have been set aside under s 26 of the ICCMA on the grounds of lacking jurisdiction of the German court and the non-issuance of a confiscation order ([11], [12]).

Cloete JA dismissed these claims, emphasizing that a foreign order retains its character irrespective of South African procedural frameworks, thus limiting the applicability of domestic laws to the registered foreign order. The judgement highlighted that the South African court's ability to intervene is constrained to the conditions laid out under the ICCMA, which do not convert foreign orders into local ones ([12], [14]).

Furthermore, on the issue concerning the interdicts, the court affirmed the legitimacy of these orders as they were sought to preserve the assets and prevent dissipation pending resolution of the foreign proceedings ([5]). The court reiterated that the NDPP had a sound basis for interdicts, primarily due to Falk's ownership structure of FRS ([6]).

The application of the “interests of justice” standard was also critically assessed. The court clarified that the inquiry transcends a mere comparison of South African statutory provisions, requiring a broader interpretation of justice that considers the ramifications of potentially allowing asset dissipation before any forfeiture order could be settled ([15], [16]).

## C – Conclusion 

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appeal was to be dismissed, emphasizing the valid registration of the foreign restraint order and the legality of the interdicts imposed under POCA. The ruling upheld the principle that the enforcement of foreign orders in South African jurisdictions must align with specific legislative frameworks governing such matters, namely the ICCMA and POCA ([1], [18]). 

This case highlights significant implications for how South African courts engage with foreign criminal orders and mandate a careful analysis of both domestic and international legal principles concerning asset preservation in complex cross-border criminal matters. The appeal's dismissal further underscores the judicial commitment to maintaining integrity and stability in the interfacing of national and international legal frameworks in matters of organised crime and financial misconduct.
