Supreme Court of Appeal

City of Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality v Takubiza Trading & Projects CC and Others [2022] ZASCA 82

The City of Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality (the Municipality), the appellant, published an invitation to tender (Tender PS-F07-2020) in March 2020 for the appointment of finance meter management consultants.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 82

F – Facts

The City of Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality (the Municipality), the appellant, published an invitation to tender (Tender PS-F07-2020) in March 2020 for the appointment of finance meter management consultants. The tender was to manage the Municipality's electricity and water meter readings and credit control processes, over a period of 36 months, with a total value of approximately R117 million ([1]).

Originally set to close on 24 April 2020, the tender's closing date was extended to 11 June 2020 due to the COVID-19 pandemic. The bid validity period of 120 days was thus set to expire on 9 October 2020. On the last day of this period, Ms. Sanjuka Makhan, the ICT Acquisition Specialist, sent an email to all bidders requesting confirmation to extend the validity until 31 December 2020. However, the email's delivery failed for some bidders, including Aurecon, which necessitated a re-sending of the extension request ([2]-[3]).

By 19 November 2020, the Bid Evaluation Committee (BEC) recommended that Zutari (Pty) Ltd (formerly Aurecon) and Ntiyiso Consulting (Pty) Ltd be awarded the contracts for the respective areas of the tender. The Bid Adjudication Committee (BAC) subsequently approved these awards on 24 November 2020, followed by formal notification on 17 December 2020 ([4]).

The first respondent, Takubiza Trading & Projects CC, learned of its unsuccessful bid on 11 January 2021 and subsequently filed an urgent review application in the Gauteng Division of the High Court. The high court ruled on 14 June 2021 that the tender awards were invalid due to the tender's validity period expiring prior to the contract awards, thus suspending the declaration of invalidity for 150 days to allow the Municipality to commence a new tender process ([5]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the tender process could be deemed valid despite the awards being made after the expiration of the tender's validity period. The underlying question was whether the Municipality had acted within the constitutional and statutory framework governing public procurement, specifically Section 217 of the Constitution, which mandates a transparent, equitable, and competitive procurement process ([6]-[10]).

Additionally, the court considered whether the Municipality's actions could be justified under the premise of having reached out to bidders before the expiration of the validity period, given the delivery issues encountered with some emails ([10]-[12]).

R – Rules

The relevant rules and principles laid out in prior case law elaborated upon the concept of tender validity. As established in Telkom SA Ltd v Merid Training (Pty) Ltd, when a tender's validity period expires without an award, the process is concluded albeit unsuccessfully. Thus, no negotiations or further evaluations can occur unless all bidders agree to extend the validity period before it lapses ([7]).

In Joubert Galpin Searle Inc & others v Road Accident Fund, the court reiterated that once the validity period of proposals has lapsed, any tender process is complete, and an organ of state must recommence the tender process to ensure fairness and transparency ([8]-[9]).

The ruling in Defensor Electronic Security (Pty) Ltd v Centlec SOC Ltd added that any tender awards made after the expiration of the validity period without proper extensions are invalid. Importantly, the need for clarity and certainty was underscored: tenders cannot remain open-ended ([13]-[14]).

A – Application

The court recognized that the Municipality's attempts to seek confirmation of the extension after the expiration of the validity period did not hold legal merit. Ms. Makhan's email, sent at the last moment, did not elicit the requisite confirmations from all bidders by the due date. The court emphasized that the absence of responses from some bidders before the expiration signified that the tender process had effectively ended ([11]-[12]).

It further clarified that the mere act of sending a confirmation request did not equate to an extension if the consent was not received within the stipulated timeframe. The concessions made by three other bidders post-deadline could not rectify the invalidity caused by the lack of timely approval from all parties involved ([12]-[14]).

Citing the judgments from Telkom SA and related cases, the Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the Municipality had no authority to award the tender after the validity period expired—an act deemed as failing to adhere to the necessary transparent and equitable procedures guaranteed by the Constitution. Such an award could not be legitimized merely on the grounds that attempts were made to solicit an extension at the last minute ([15]-[16]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming the high court's ruling that held the tender awards to be invalid due to violation of procedural rules surrounding the validity period. The court's judgment reiterated the importance of adhering to strict timelines in public procurement processes to maintain transparency and competitiveness as mandated by South African law ([20]).

This case underpins the critical nature of timeframes and procedural compliance in public tender processes, reinforcing judicial principles aimed at ensuring fairness and predictability in governmental dealings. The implications extend to all future tender-related actions, setting a clear precedent regarding the necessity for strict adherence to prescribed timelines and communication protocols in governmental tenders.
