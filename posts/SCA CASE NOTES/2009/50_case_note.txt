# FIRAC Case Note: *Manong and Associates (Pty) Ltd v Eastern Cape Department of Roads and Transport & Others* 

**Court:** The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA)  
**Case No:** 369/08  
**Neutral Citation:** *Manong v Eastern Cape Department of Roads and Transport & Others* (369/08) [2009] ZASCA 50 (25 May 2009)  
**Coram:** Navsa, Brand, Jafta, Ponnan JJA and Bosielo AJA  
**Heard:** 5 May 2009  
**Delivered:** 25 May 2009  
---

### F – Facts
The case involved an appeal from the High Court in Bhisho, where the appellant, Manong and Associates (Pty) Ltd (hereafter "Manong"), contested its disqualification from a tender bid issued by the Eastern Cape Department of Roads and Transport (ECDRT) for the design and construction of provincial roads ([para 2]). 

In July 2007, the ECDRT invited tenders, and Manong submitted its bid in response ([para 3]). However, in December 2007, the company was disqualified for not meeting the required functionality points during the evaluation process. Manong sought urgent relief in February 2008, asserting that it was unlawfully disqualified, and filed its application in the Equality Court ([paras 3–4]). 

Froneman J of the High Court determined that the proceedings could be resolved without a temporary interdict, allowing the final determination of relief on the merits of the application instead ([para 6]). The application was founded on allegations of unfair discrimination as per the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000 (the Equality Act), claiming indirect discrimination based on its black ownership and limited experience ([paras 11–12]).

The ECDRT and the National Treasury opposed the relief sought, primarily on jurisdictional grounds, arguing that the Equality Court could not grant the requested administrative review ([para 13]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues revolved around:
1. Whether the Equality Court had jurisdiction and the legal authority to provide the relief sought by Manong, specifically whether it could conduct a review of administrative decision-making ([paras 14–15]).
2. Whether the established procedures for the Equality Court, as per the Equality Act, were adhered to by Manong and the High Court ([paras 74–80]).
3. The validity of the ECDRT’s tender evaluation process and whether it constituted unfair discrimination against previously disadvantaged individuals ([paras 11–12, 22–24]).

### R – Rules
The legal rules relied upon included:
- **Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000**: This Act provides the basis for the establishment of Equality Courts and delineates their jurisdiction ([paras 16–22]).
- **Constitution of South Africa, 1996**: Section 9 outlines the right to equality and the prohibition of unfair discrimination ([para 27–28]).
- **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: This Act governs administrative action and its review ([para 15]).
- Relevant jurisprudence from the Supreme Court of Appeal concerning the characterisation of the Equality Court's functions and its relationship with the High Court ([para 60]).

### A – Application
The SCA rigorously examined the nature and authority of the Equality Court. It notably concluded that while the Equality Court is indeed enabled by the Equality Act to deal with specific forms of discrimination under its jurisdiction, it is not a High Court with the broader jurisdictional reach as suggested by the court below ([para 56]).

The judge emphasized the need for the High Court to adhere to the procedural requirements of the Equality Act, noting that the lack of adherence may have compromised the fair trial rights of the parties involved ([paras 76–82]). This included proper directions hearings and considering the burden of proof established under Section 13 of the Equality Act. 

The court found that the merits of Manong's claims regarding indirect discrimination and the alleged flaws in the tender process, such as a lack of transparency and improper motivations, warranted further exploration, particularly in light of the procedural missteps that occurred in the lower court ([paras 81–92]).

### C – Conclusion
The SCA held that the appeal should be upheld and remitted the matter back to the Equality Court for proper adjudication in line with the statutory framework of the Equality Act, indicating that the High Court had erred in its characterisation of the Equality Court's jurisdiction ([paras 94–95]). The court also ruled that no order for costs would be made at that stage, allowing the parties to seek an order on costs following the resolution of the issues raised in the remitted proceedings ([para 94]).

This judgment underlined the importance of adhering to procedural rules established for the Equality Court and clarified its function in the broader ascertainment of legality regarding administrative actions within the context of anti-discrimination legislation.
