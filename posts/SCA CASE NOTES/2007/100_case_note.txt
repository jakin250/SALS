Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2007

# FIRAC Case Note: CSARS v The Baking Tin (Pty) Ltd [2007] SCA 100 (RSA)

### F – Facts

The case under consideration involves a tariff classification dispute centering on aluminium containers imported by The Baking Tin (Pty) Ltd from Hong Kong. The containers were described by the respondent as "catering consumables," primarily utilized for the preparation and packaging of pies and pre-cooked foods. The Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service classified these items under tariff heading 76.15, which pertains to "Hollowware for table or kitchen use," imposing a customs duty of 30% and anti-dumping duties ([para 1]). The respondent contended that the containers were not dutiable as they were categorized as consumables. An initial appeal to the Commissioner was unsuccessful; however, the Cape High Court overturned the classification per s 47(9)(e) of the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 ([paras 2–3]).

The specific classification issue arose under tariff heading 76.15, which describes "Table, kitchen or other household articles and parts thereof, of aluminium" ([para 3]). The appellant argued that the items met the classification criteria, while the respondent asserted that they were not durable and thus could not be classified as kitchen household articles. The Cape High Court sided with The Baking Tin, concluding that the containers did not represent items of a permanent or semi-permanent nature, and subsequently classified them under sub-heading 76.16.99.90—indicating "Other articles of aluminium" without duties ([paras 8–9]).

### I – Issues

The central legal issues presented in this case are:

1. Whether the aluminium containers imported by The Baking Tin fall under the classification of "table, kitchen or other household articles" as defined under tariff heading 76.15 of the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 and, if so, whether they constitute "hollowware" for table or kitchen use ([para 1]).

2. Whether the intention of the importer or manufacturer regarding the use of the containers is relevant for the classification of the goods in question ([paras 12–13]).

### R – Rules

The court relied upon several legal rules and precedents in addressing the classification issue:

1. The classification of goods under the Act follows a three-stage process involving (i) interpretation of the headings, (ii) assessment of the nature and characteristics of the goods, and (iii) selection of the most appropriate heading ([para 5]).

2. The General Rules for the Interpretation of the Harmonized System, specifically Rule 1, stipulates that classification must adhere to the terms of headings and relevant section notes ([para 5]).

3. Explanatory notes must not be construed as rigid rules; they serve primarily as guidance and may lack the precision typically expected from statutory provisions ([para 6]).

4. The intention of the manufacturer or importer does not influence the classification; the objective characteristics of the goods at the time of customs clearance are deemed critical ([paras 12–13]).

### A – Application

In analyzing the application of the rules to the facts, the court emphasized that the character of the aluminium containers is paramount in determining their classification. The Commissioner’s position, which classified the containers under heading 76.15, was based on the notion that they are indeed kitchen items, irrespective of their durability. The argument of The Baking Tin relied heavily on the disposability of the containers, which the appellant contended should not dictate their classification ([para 11]). 

The court also observed that although the high court found nuances in the comparison with other kitchen utensils, it failed to recognize that durability was not a prerequisite for classification under heading 76.15 ([para 14]). With regards to the definition of "hollowware," the court noted that the term encompasses a range of items that are not inherently rigid or long-lasting ([para 16]). The containers were identified as hollowware due to their design, which distinguishes them from flatware, and thus the classification under tariff heading 76.15 was deemed appropriate ([para 16]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the Commissioner’s classification of the aluminium containers under tariff heading 76.15, confirming the imposition of a 30% customs duty and anti-dumping duty. The previous ruling of the Cape High Court was set aside, reinforcing the principles surrounding the objective characteristics of imported goods, and reaffirming that the subjective intention of manufacturers and importers is secondary in customs classifications ([paras 17]). This case underscores the importance of adhering to established principles in tariff classifications within South African customs law and the implications for importers facing similar circumstances in determining the proper duty assessments.
