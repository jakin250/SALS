Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: National Scrap Metal v Murray & Roberts (809/2011) [2012] ZASCA 47

## F – Facts

The appellants, National Scrap Metal (Cape Town) (Pty) Ltd (NSM) and Murec Crushing and Milling (Pty) Ltd (Murec), appealed against an eviction order granted by the Western Cape High Court, which evicted them from property owned by the first respondent, Murray & Roberts Ltd (M&R Ltd). The dispute primarily revolved around the existence of an alleged oral lease agreement purportedly concluded in 2008, which would allow the appellants to remain on the property for an extended period after the expiry of a written lease that had terminated on 31 October 2010 ([paras 1–2]).

Both NSM and Murec contended they had a legal right to occupy the property based on this oral agreement. In counter, the respondents—M&R Ltd and its subsidiaries—denied the existence of any binding agreement beyond the written lease. The high court dismissed the appellants’ claims without allowing oral evidence, leading to the appeal ([paras 1–2], [11]).

Throughout the relevant years, the appellants had engaged in extensive capital investment in the premises, including significant improvements, which factored into their arguments for continued occupancy ([paras 6–9], [30]). Despite the continuance of business dealings between the parties, including supply agreements historically reliant on each other’s operations, the appellants eventually ceased compliance with eviction requests from the respondents ([paras 11–12]).

## I – Issues

The central legal question arose from the appellants' assertion of an oral lease agreement and whether the high court was entitled to deny its existence solely based on documentary evidence provided by the respondents without considering oral testimony. Specifically, the issues included:

1. Whether the allegations regarding the oral lease agreement were capable of being rejected on the papers alone given the disputed facts ([paras 1, 20]).
2. The validity of the respondents' claims that the alleged oral lease lacked authority and thus could not constitute a binding agreement ([paras 15–19]).

## R – Rules

The legal standards governing this case included:

1. **Test for Genuine Dispute of Facts**: Citing *Wightman v Headfour (Pty) Ltd*, a genuine dispute must be demonstrably evident in affidavits presented. The court stressed that the requirements for rejecting a version of facts based solely on documents must be stringently applied, meaning allegations cannot be dismissed unless they are deemed implausible or untenable ([para 17]).
2. **Authority of Agents**: According to general agency principles, the authority vested in representatives (e.g., Noonan) must be clear and established. The respondents argued that Noonan lacked the authority to bind M&R to the alleged oral lease ([para 15]).
3. **Bona Fide vs. Fabricated Claims**: Courts consider if claims appear genuine or fabricated based on the context and through analysis of the evidence presented. The assessment must take into account the past relationship and prior agreements between the parties ([paras 33–34]).

## A – Application

In the application of these rules, the court critically assessed whether the high court had incorrectly dismissed the appellants' claims. It highlighted that:

1. The high court had failed to recognize the implications of the alleged oral agreement’s context, where NSM’s significant investment and the close relationship with M&R suggested that a less formal oral agreement was plausible ([paras 20–24]).
2. The court acknowledged that the uncertainty surrounding the binding effects of contracts based on oral agreements is commonly encountered in business dealings, thus allowing for a degree of leniency when assessing the truthfulness of the appellants’ claims ([paras 22, 23]).
3. The court found that the respondents’ evidence, particularly the curious lack of formal documentation for such significant agreements, posed questions of credibility that could not dismiss the possibility of an oral agreement being accepted as factual ([paras 26–30]).

Ultimately, the court's analysis indicated that the appellants provided sufficient material to demonstrate a genuine dispute existed. The conclusion of the high court to dismiss the case without evidence was viewed as an error ([paras 28–30]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal reversed the lower court's decision, acknowledging that the high court's approach in denying the credibility of the oral lease allegations was premature and unwarranted ([para 34]). As a result, it dismissed the respondents' eviction application, ruling in favor of the appellants while addressing costs associated with the appeal and earlier interlocutory applications.

This case underscored the importance of allowing oral testimonies in disputes where factual allegations are contested, especially in complex business arrangements, thereby reaffirming the legal principle that findings based on mere documents must carefully consider the context and nature of the relationships involved ([paras 31–34]).
