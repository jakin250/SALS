Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

### Case Note: Radzilane v S (127/15) [2016] ZASCA 64

#### F – Facts ([paras 1–4])

In the case of **Radzilane v S**, the applicant, Lufuno Radzilane, sought special leave to appeal against the Gauteng Division of the High Court's refusal to grant leave for an appeal concerning a sentence imposed after a suspended sentence was enforced. The applicant had previously pleaded guilty to theft, having unlawfully withdrawn R560,000 from various clients while employed by ABSA Bank, leading to a conviction and originally a seven-year prison sentence under section 297 of the *Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977* (CPA). This sentence was wholly suspended for five years, conditioned upon the repayment of the stolen amount.

Radzilane initially paid R210,031.53, followed by a nominal payment of R4,000, before ceasing further repayments. The State moved to enforce the suspended sentence, but instead, the trial court imposed a new, lesser sentence of three years' imprisonment, which Radzilane completed. However, the respondent, The State, contested this new sentence. Upon appeal, the Gauteng High Court set aside the new sentence and remitted the case back to the trial court to consider implementing the original suspended sentence.

The application for leave to appeal that decision was subsequently rejected by the court a quo, leading to Radzilane's appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal.

#### I – Issues ([paras 5–10])

The primary legal questions before the court were:

1. Whether the court a quo had the competency to uphold the respondent's appeal against the trial court’s imposition of a new sentence.
2. Whether the correction of the trial court's error in imposing a new sentence warranted enforcement of the original suspended sentence.
3. The implications of the applicant having already served a period of incarceration under the lesser sentence, and whether a lesser sentence or conditions could be imposed despite the provisions of the CPA.

#### R – Rules ([paras 5–10])

The legal authorities considered in this case included:

- **Section 297 of the CPA**: Specifically subsections (7) and (9), which dictate that when the conditions of a suspended sentence are not met, the court has limited powers; it can enforce the original suspended sentence or suspend it further, but cannot impose a new sentence.
- **Case law precedent, notably Van Wyk v S and Galela v S**: These cases underscored the stringent requirements for granting special leave to appeal, emphasizing that beyond a reasonable prospect of success, special circumstances must exist to warrant further appeal.

The court examined how the CPA governs the enforcement and imposition of sentences, clarifying the boundaries of judicial authority in such proceedings.

#### A – Application ([paras 7–12])

In its analysis, the Supreme Court of Appeal noted that the a quo court's assertion regarding the trial court's lack of competency to impose a new sentence pursuant to an application for enforcement of a suspended sentence was legally sound. The trial court had consequently erred in imposing the lesser sentence, thus legitimizing the respondent's appellate action.

The court acknowledged the applicant's challenge surrounding the fairness of serving additional time for the offense, especially given that he had already served time under the lesser sentence. Although Radzilane argued that imposing a new sentence without considering the delayed proceedings and his inability to repay further damages was unjust, the court emphasized its reluctance to interfere with the trial court's discretion while suggesting that the trial court ought to consider extending the enforcement of the suspended sentence under adjusted conditions.

Despite Radzilane’s served sentence, the court recognized the substantial impact of the protracted litigation, expressing it would be unjust for Radzilane to face an additional seven years' imprisonment, especially when he expressed inability to repay the complainant further.

#### C – Conclusion ([para 12])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately concluded that special leave was warranted due to the potential for manifest injustice if Radzilane were subjected to another substantial period of imprisonment following the completion of his initial lesser sentence. The court dismissed the appeal but recommended that the trial court reassess the enforcement of the suspended sentence, considering both the elapsed time and Radzilane’s financial inability to fulfill the court's original conditions. 

This decision underscores the court’s commitment to ensuring justice through thoughtful consideration of the implications of past sentences and current realities faced by defendants, reinforcing the need for a judicious exercise of judicial discretion in cases involving procedural nuances in sentencing.
