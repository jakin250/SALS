Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development v Masingili & Others [2013] ZACC 41

Following their convictions, the respondents appealed to the Western Cape High Court, which upheld the convictions of the third and fourth respondents but questioned the culpability of Masingili and Volo.

Constitutional Court 2013 [2013] ZACC 41

F – Facts

In this case, the respondents, Nontombi Masingili (First Respondent), Siyabulela Volo (Second Respondent), Mzonke Mlindalazwe (Third Respondent), and Sithembile Govuza (Fourth Respondent), were charged and convicted of robbery with aggravating circumstances in the Cape Town Regional Magistrate’s Court. The events occurred in October 2009 when the third and fourth respondents entered a shop and robbed the owner by threatening her with a knife. The first respondent scouted the shop beforehand and waited outside in a car during the robbery, while the second respondent acted as the getaway driver ([paras 6–8]).

Following their convictions, the respondents appealed to the Western Cape High Court, which upheld the convictions of the third and fourth respondents but questioned the culpability of Masingili and Volo. The court expressed concern whether they could be found guilty of robbery with aggravating circumstances without intention regarding the aggravating factors, concluding that their actions did not necessitate the foresight of the use of a knife ([paras 7–9]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue was whether section 1(1)(b) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (“CPA”) created strict liability for accomplices to robbery with aggravating circumstances, contrary to the constitutional rights of fair trial and personal freedom ([paras 4, 5, 12]).

Specific questions included:

  1. What is the meaning of section 1(1)(b) of the CPA?
  2. Is robbery with aggravating circumstances distinct from mere robbery?
  3. Must intention regarding the aggravating circumstances be proven for conviction?
  4. Do the provisions of section 12(1)(a) and section 35(3) of the Constitution necessitate proof of intention in this context? ([paras 5(b)–(d)]).

R – Rules

The relevant rules stem from the CPA, particularly section 1(1)(b), which defines aggravating circumstances in relation to robbery. This defines robbery with aggravating circumstances as the unlawful taking of property involving the use of a dangerous weapon or infliction of grievous bodily harm ([para 15]).

The constitutional framework includes:

  • Section 12(1)(a): Protects against arbitrary deprivation of liberty and guarantees freedom and security of the person ([para 2]).
  • Section 35(3): Ensures the right to a fair trial, including the presumption of innocence ([para 2]).

Precedents such as S v Dhlamini and S v Legoa clarify the nature of culpability and accomplice liability in South African criminal law, establishing that a person's liability must traditionally align with the established fault principle (dolus) ([paras 8, 29, 34]).

A – Application

The Court analyzed the High Court's declaration of invalidity concerning section 1(1)(b) of the CPA. It clarified that the phrase “or an accomplice” extends liability but does not negate the requirement of mens rea concerning robberies involving aggravating circumstances. The Court emphasized that an accomplice could be liable if they intentionally furthered the commission of a robbery, which includes the use of violence, but it does not mean they forfeit their rights under the Constitution ([paras 24–30]).

Examining the distinction between mere robbery and robbery with aggravating circumstances, the Court reiterated that the former always carries implications for the degree of culpability required and that the intention behind the aggravating circumstances is not a standalone element of the offence but affects sentencing ([paras 30–34]).

Regarding sections 12 and 35 of the Constitution, the court held that requiring intent does not necessarily fit the constitutional framework regarding the definitions of culpability present in both the common law and statutory provisions. The necessity of proving intent was wholly addressed by establishing the culpability associated with the underlying crime of robbery, thus maintaining alignments with legislative goals and practical enforcement ([paras 36–54]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court did not confirm the Western Cape High Court's declaration of invalidity regarding section 1(1)(b) of the CPA. It concluded that:

  • Robbery with aggravating circumstances is not a separate crime but a more serious form of robbery requiring established culpability for the robbery itself, not the aggravating circumstances ([paras 59]).
  • The state must prove the essential elements of robbery, including intention, to satisfy the requirements of sections 12(1)(a) and 35(3) of the Constitution, thus affirming the compatibility of the CPA with constitutional provisions ([paras 60, 61]).

The appeal succeeded, allowing the matter to be returned to the High Court for finalization concerning the respondents' original convictions ([paras 61]). This ruling reiterates the necessity of intention in upholding constitutional rights while ensuring the legal framework for prosecution remains effective and just.
