Source Folder: 2013

# Case Note: NUMSA v Abancedisi Labour Services (857/12) [2013] ZASCA 143

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts

The case concerned the National Union of Metalworkers of South Africa (NUMSA) and several of its members, who were employed by Abancedisi Labour Services (Abancedisi), a temporary employment service provider. The employees, initially employed by Kitsanker (Pty) Ltd, were transferred to Abancedisi after being retrenched and subsequently re-hired under limited duration contracts. The employment arrangement stipulated that the contract would end when Kitsanker no longer required the employees' services ([1], [4]).

In July 2001, the employees participated in a short work stoppage to protest workplace conditions, including demanding the dismissal of a supervisor. Following this, Kitsanker required the employees to sign a new code of conduct by a fixed deadline. Those who refused to sign were barred from entering the premises and were subsequently replaced by new workers ([6], [7]). Abancedisi maintained the position that the employees remained employed and therefore had not been dismissed, even though they were neither assigned tasks nor paid wages ([8], [10]).

On 23 July 2001, NUMSA referred a dispute to the Bargaining Council, claiming unfair dismissal under the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995. The referral was followed by conciliation efforts that failed, leading to the employees taking their case to the Labour Court, which found the dismissal claim unproven ([8], [10], [11]). The Labour Appeal Court (LAC) partially reversed the costs order against the employees but ultimately ruled that the case was prematurely referred and that the employees could have contested their dismissal as an unfair labor practice ([10]).

### I – Issues

The central question was whether the employees were unfairly dismissed by Abancedisi when they were critical members of their assignment team but not assigned work following their exclusion from Kitsanker ([2], [11]). The issues included:
1. Whether the refusal to allow the employees to work constituted a dismissal under the Labour Relations Act.
2. The implications of the employment contract clauses related to the assignment and termination of employment in the interpretation of their dismissal ([17]).

### R – Rules

The relevant provisions of the Labour Relations Act (LRA) included:
- Section 198: Definitions relating to temporary employment services.
- Section 186: Defines dismissal to include situations where an employer terminates the contract of employment.
- Section 188: Specifies that a dismissal is unfair unless the employer can prove that it was for a fair reason related to conduct, capacity, or operational requirements, and was effected with a fair procedure ([17]).
- Section 191: Details the requirement for employees to refer disputes regarding unfair dismissal within 30 days of the dismissal ([12], [16]).

Judgments concerning the interpretation of employment contracts and unfair dismissals were also referenced, including the cases of *Council for Scientific & Industrial Research v Fijen* and *Myers v Abramson*, which discuss the definitions and implications of wrongful termination ([15], [17]).

### A – Application

The court examined the employment contracts' provisions, particularly the assignment clauses, to evaluate if the employees had indeed been dismissed. The court highlighted that while the employees technically remained on Abancedisi’s payroll post-exclusion from Kitsanker, the company did not offer them any alternative work or opportunities ([13], [14]). The court rejected the argument that the employees remained in a state of indefinite suspension and reiterated that an employee's engagement ceases when not allowed to perform their work, constituting a breach of contract by Abancedisi ([15], [17]).

It further argued that Abancedisi’s assertion that they were not dismissed could not withstand scrutiny in light of the circumstances: the lack of work assignments and payment. By failing to provide alternate employment or adhering to fair procedural standards, Abancedisi materially breached the employment agreements ([15], [19]).

The LAC’s earlier reasoning that the referral to the Bargaining Council was premature was also dismissed, as the employees acted within the statutory time frames laid out by the LRA, thereby fulfilling their obligations ([16], [17]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the employees were indeed unfairly dismissed as defined under the LRA. It emphasized that the contract's termination was effectively enacted by Kitsanker’s rejection of the employees and Abancedisi's inaction to reassign work to them. Consequently, the court overturned the Labour Appeal Court's ruling, reinstating the Labour Court's decision to award the employees 12 months' remuneration as compensation ([20]).

In summary, this case underscores the court’s commitment to upholding employees’ rights against exploitative practices often associated with temporary employment services. It emphasizes that employers must adhere to established procedural fairness when terminating employment, in line with both statutory requirements and broader constitutional protections against unfair dismissal. This ruling serves as a reminder for labour brokers and employers about their obligations under the Labour Relations Act, particularly concerning the unfair dismissal provisions ([19]).
