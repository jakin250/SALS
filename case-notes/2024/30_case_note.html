FIRAC Analysis of O’Brien N.O. v Minister of Defence and Military Veterans and Others 2024 ZACC 30

Constitutional Court 2024

F – Facts

In the case O’Brien N.O. v Minister of Defence and Military Veterans and Others, the applicant, Lieutenant Colonel K.B. O’Brien, a former military judge, sought to challenge the constitutionality of various provisions of the Defence Act 42 of 2002 and the Military Discipline Supplementary Measures Act 16 of 1999 (the MDSMA) ([1–2]). Specifically, O’Brien contested the legality of sections 101 and 102 of the Defence Act, and sections 15 and 17 of the MDSMA, which he claimed compromised the independence of military judges due to their assignments and removals being controlled wholesale by the Executive ([4]).

The judicial history of the case is essential. O’Brien presided over military trials and expressed concerns regarding the constitutional validity of the very provisions defining the assignment and removal of military judges ([16–23]). His apprehensions were initially treated as disruptive, leading to an inquiry against him that ultimately compromised his role as a military judge ([23]). After failing at the High Court and Supreme Court of Appeal levels, O’Brien applied for leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court, which focused solely on his counter-application ([36]).

I – Issues

The main constitutional questions before the Court revolved around:

  1. Whether sections 101 and 102 of the Defence Act, which allow for Executive-initiated boards of inquiry into the conduct of military judges, comply with the independence of the judiciary as guaranteed by the Constitution ([4]).
  2. Whether section 15 of the MDSMA's provision for renewable terms of military judges undermines judicial independence ([4]).
  3. Whether section 17 of the MDSMA, enabling the Minister to remove military judges upon a recommendation from the Adjutant General without independent oversight, violates the principle of judicial independence ([4]).

R – Rules

The Constitution of South Africa guarantees the independence of the judiciary under sections 165(2) and 174(7). These provisions state that courts must apply the law impartially, free from external influence ([6–8]). The Court has previously held that military courts are "courts" within the meaning of section 166(e) of the Constitution and are thus entitled to the same protections regarding independence ([57]).

The Court drew upon previous case law, such as Van Rooyen and Potsane, which elucidate that the essence of judicial independence requires structural autonomy and the absence of Executive influence ([57–62]). Furthermore, jurisprudential guidelines established in international law instruments, including the ICCPR and the African Charter on Human and People's Rights, reinforce the necessity of independence for all judicial bodies, including military courts ([85]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court found that the provisions allowing Executive control over military judges' conduct (sections 101 and 102 of the Defence Act) are unconstitutional. The Court noted that such provisions do not only infringe on the independence of military judges but also create the potential for abuse and undermine public trust in military justice ([107–109]).

On the issues of renewable term assignments under section 15 of the MDSMA, the Court ruled that these terms were inherently inconsistent with historical and widely accepted principles of judicial independence, particularly the requirement for non-renewability to promote objective permanence and independence ([111–115]).

Regarding the removal of military judges without independent oversight (section 17 of the MDSMA), the Court declared this provision as unconstitutional due to its failure to provide safeguards against arbitrary removal, aligning with the precedents in South African jurisprudence on judicial independence ([120–122]).

C – Conclusion

The Court unanimously found the impugned provisions unconstitutional and suspended the declarations of invalidity for 24 months to allow the legislature time to amend the relevant statutes ([125]). Specifically, it ruled:

  • Sections 101 and 102 of the Defence Act permit unconstitutional Executive interference in judicial matters.
  • The renewable terms of military judges are a breach of judicial independence, requiring amendments to ensure non-renewability.
  • The Executive's unchecked power to remove military judges invalidates section 17 of the MDSMA and calls for an independent process for evaluating military judges’ fitness ([125]).

This case represents a significant affirmation of judicial independence in South Africa, especially concerning military judicial structures, and sets a precedent for future governance regarding military judiciary appointments and accountability mechanisms.
