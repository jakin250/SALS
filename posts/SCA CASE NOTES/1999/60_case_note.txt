Source Folder: 1999

# FIRAC Case Note on NBS Boland Bank v One Berg River Drive and Others

**Title:** NBS Boland Bank v One Berg River Drive and Others  
**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Case No:** 291/98  
**Judges:** Mahomed CJ, Van Heerden DCJ, Olivier JA, Melunsky AJA, Mpati AJA  
**Heard:** 16 August 1999  
**Delivered:** 10 September 1999  
**Summary:** Authority of a mortgagee to unilaterally increase interest rates.

---

## F – Facts

In the Supreme Court of Appeal, three distinct appeals questioned the validity of a clause in mortgage agreements allowing the mortgagee to adjust the interest rate unilaterally. The appeals arose from prior decisions rendered by Southwood J in the Witwatersrand Local Division ([para 2]) and Thirion J in the Natal Provincial Division ([para 3]). Southwood J declared the clause invalid, reasoning that it conferred an unfettered power to the mortgagee that breached the principle of certainty in contractual obligations. Contrarily, Thirion J held that the clause included an implied condition where the mortgagee could only adjust interest rates in line with market trends, conditioned by the Usury Act ([para 4]).

In a third appeal involving Gihwala AJ's ruling in the Cape Provincial Division, the interpretation that the mortgagee’s power to change interest rates was not entirely unfettered was upheld, assuming compliance with prevailing banking standards ([para 5]). A historical context was provided in [para 6], referencing earlier cases that had tackled similar contractual clauses, highlighting a divergence in judicial interpretation about the enforceability of such provisions.

## I – Issues

The central issue across all three appeals revolved around the legality and enforceability of a mortgage contract clause permitting unilateral alterations of the interest rate by the mortgagee ([para 1]). Specifically, the court needed to assess whether such a clause, allowing one party to unilaterally determine a key contract term, was valid due to perceived vagueness and uncertainty inherent in the contract, a notion disputed in previous judicial interpretations ([para 9]).

## R – Rules

The court primarily considered established legal principles and prior case law. Notably, the jurisprudence around whether a contract term granting one party unilateral discretion over an essential term (in this case, the interest rate) could stand the scrutiny of contractual certainty ([para 9], [20]). Reference was also made to the principle articulated by Hoexter JA in Murray and Roberts Construction Ltd v Finat Properties (Pty) Ltd, where contracts leaving significant terms to the will of one party are considered void for vagueness ([para 19]).

From comparative perspectives, the court drew upon precedential rulings in cases such as Boland Bank v Steele ([para 6]), which upheld that mortgagee terms must be interpreted reasonably, and NBS Bank Ltd v Badenhorst-Schnetler Bedryfsdienste BK, which favored stricter adherence to elements solidifying clarity in contractual obligations ([para 6]). International legal frameworks were considered, specifically the implications of English common law and legislative standards in terms of contract enforcement ([para 11], [13], and [19]).

## A – Application

The court conducted a detailed analysis of the implications of allowing a mortgagee to unilaterally determine interest rate adjustments. The Deputy Chief Justice, Van Heerden, emphasized that contractual discretion is often permissible but must be arbitrarily exercised in good faith and according to a reasonable standard ([para 25]). 

The argument pivoted on the premise that the power to adjust the interest rate did not constitute an "essentialia" of a loan agreement, ensuring that the broader framework of the contract remained enforceable. The court concluded that the validity of such a clause does not inherently violate established doctrines of contract law provided the discretion exercised does not infringe upon public policy or goodwill ([para 30]).

By juxtaposing historical rulings and current legal standards, the court underscored that while unilateral powers may indeed create grounds for disputes, the initial premise of a valid mortgage agreement is not undermined by such provisions, unless exercised in a manifestly unreasonable or capricious manner ([para 28]).  

## C – Conclusion

The court ultimately ruled in favor of the appellant in appeal (1), validating the clause that enabled the mortgagee to adjust interest rates as they deemed fit, thus reversing the lower court's ruling declaring it invalid ([para 36]). Conversely, appeals (2) and (3) were dismissed. The decision established that unilateral clauses must be subject to reasonable exercise of power and established good faith, affirming the enforceability of contracts that allow for some measure of discretionary adjustments while safeguarding against arbitrary execution ([paras 31-36]).

This judgment has significant implications for future contracts in the banking and finance sector in South Africa, reinforcing the critical balance between contractual freedom and protection against potential misuse of unilateral powers by property financiers.
