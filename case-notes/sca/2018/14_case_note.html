Supreme Court of Appeal

National Police Commissioner v Ngobeni (327/2017)

In response to her suspension and the inquiry, Ngobeni sought judicial review in the KwaZulu-Natal High Court, seeking to declare the board of inquiry and her suspension unlawful.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018

F - Facts

The facts of the case presented before the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa involved Lieutenant General Betty Mmamonnye Ngobeni, who served as the Provincial Commissioner for KwaZulu-Natal. Appointed in 2009 and reappointed in 2014 by the then National Police Commissioner General Phiyega, Ngobeni faced a board of inquiry established by the acting National Commissioner Lieutenant General J.K. Phahlane on 18 March 2016, addressing issues of alleged misconduct and her fitness for office ([1]). Subsequently, on the same day, Ngobeni received a notice of intended suspension, which was executed on 20 May 2016 ([1]).

In response to her suspension and the inquiry, Ngobeni sought judicial review in the KwaZulu-Natal High Court, seeking to declare the board of inquiry and her suspension unlawful. The High Court, under Chili J, ruled in favor of Ngobeni, leading to the appeal by the National Police Commissioner and the Minister of Police ([1]).

The High Court did not grant leave to appeal, but Ngobeni’s appeal to this Court was permitted via petition ([1]).

I - Issues

The primary legal issue was whether the acting National Commissioner, Lieutenant General Phahlane, had the legal authority to establish a board of inquiry against Ngobeni and to suspend her based on alleged misconduct and unfitness for office ([4]). This question necessitated a close examination of the interplay between sections 205 to 207 of the South African Constitution and sections 8 and 9 of the South African Police Service Act 68 of 1995 (the Act) ([4]).

Should it be determined that Phahlane had the requisite authority under these provisions, the secondary issue revolved around whether his actions fell foul of conventional review standards ([4]).

R - Rules

The rules concerning the matter were rooted in constitutional and statutory frameworks. Section 207(1) of the Constitution vested the President with the appointment powers for the National Commissioner of Police, while subsection (3) mandated the National Commissioner, with the provincial Executive Council’s concurrence, to appoint Provincial Commissioners ([5]).

The key provisions from the Act included:

  • Section 8: This section outlines procedures when the National or Provincial Commissioner loses confidence in their position, providing the ability to establish a board of inquiry to investigate the circumstances surrounding such loss of confidence ([7]).
  • Section 9: This section relates to inquiries concerning misconduct, establishing that similar procedures under section 8 apply mutatis mutandis in cases of alleged misconduct or incapacity of the National or Provincial Commissioner ([8]).

The application of these rules was bolstered by precedents, including Masetlha v President of the Republic of South Africa ([12]) and Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality ([10]), guiding the interpretation of statutory provisions.

A - Application

The Court undertook a comprehensive analysis of the legislative framework to ascertain Phahlane's authority. It determined that sections 8 and 9 set out two specific contexts—loss of confidence and misconduct—under which inquiries could be initiated. Notably, section 8 provides a route for an inquiry following a loss of confidence, necessitating a report from the provincial Executive Council about such loss, which was not applicable in Ngobeni’s case, since Phahlane acted independently as the National Commissioner ([11]).

The Court concluded that while the Provincial Executive Council has a role in initiating inquiries under section 8(2), this does not extend to scenarios under section 8(8), where the National Commissioner’s discretion is emphasized, allowing him to act independently based on loss of confidence ([18]). Thus, the absence of the Executive Council's involvement was deemed appropriate and necessary for sound police management.

Furthermore, the Court distinguished that the inquiry process highlighted in section 9 reinforces the notion of autonomy for the National Commissioner regarding inquiries into Provincial Commissioners. The procedural integrity of the inquiry was upheld, and the lack of preconditions from the Executive Council for the inquiry was validated ([21]).

C - Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal held that the acting National Commissioner had the lawful authority to establish the board of inquiry against Lieutenant General Ngobeni and to suspend her from office. The High Court's initial ruling was overturned, with the Court holding that Ngobeni’s appeal was dismissed and costs were awarded against her ([23]).

The implications of this judgment reaffirm the National Commissioner’s discretion and autonomy in overseeing provincial police affairs, particularly concerning inquiries into misconduct and fitness for office. This case illustrates the balance of powers within the police regulatory framework and underscores the need for clear interpretations of statutory provisions governing police governance in South Africa.

This decision has critical ramifications for future cases involving police discipline and oversight, shaping the dynamics of power relations between the national and provincial layers of law enforcement management in South Africa.
