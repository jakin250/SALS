# Case Note: Republic of South Africa, Supreme Court of Appeal

### FIRAC Method Case Note

**F – Facts**  
This case originates from an appeal against the judgment of Hugo J in *Shepstone & Wylie and Others v Geyser NO 1998(1) S A 354 (N)*, which dismissed an application for security for costs as per Section 13 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973 ([para 1]). The appellants, consisting of an attorneys' firm and its partners, were involved in litigation against the respondent, who was the liquidator of Shepway Management Company (Proprietary) Limited. The respondent claimed amounts recoverable by the company which were alleged to have been lost due to mismanagement by the appellants, who were former directors of the company ([paras 2-3]).

The respondents sought security for costs, arguing that there was credible evidence suggesting that the appellants would not be able to cover the costs if the litigation failed ([para 3]). The trial court found against the necessity for security, leading to this appeal ([paras 2-3]).

**I – Issues**  
The primary legal issue concerns the appealability of the dismissal for security for costs under Section 13 of the Companies Act, and whether Hugo J exercised his discretion correctly in favor of the respondent ([para 2], [para 18]). Additionally, the court had to examine whether Section 13 applied to the statutory claims that the respondent sought to pursue ([paras 5-6]).

**R – Rules**  
Section 13 allows a court to order security in legal proceedings where a company is the plaintiff and there is credible evidence it may not be able to pay costs if unsuccessful. The Act provides discretion to the Court, which must be exercised considering the facts, and such discretion is reviewable on appeal under limited circumstances, particularly if the trial court relied on incorrect principles ([paras 1, 12]). 

Relevant authorities include:
1. *Ecker v Dean 1937 SWA 3*, which discussed the finality of the issue when security is sought ([para 4]).
2. *Petz Products (Pty) Ltd v Commercial Electrical Contractors (Pty) Ltd 1990(4) S A 196 (C)*, affirming that an order regarding security is not appealable under certain conditions ([para 4]).
3. *Keary Developments Ltd v Tarmac Construction Ltd and Another [1995] 3 All E R 534 (CA)*, which emphasizes a balancing exercise between the plaintiff's and defendant's interests when considering security for costs ([para 14]).

**A – Application**  
In reviewing the appeal, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) extensively considered the framework within which Hugo J's discretion was exercised. It underscored that Hugo J had misapplied the principles by not incorporating statutory claims within the ambit of Section 13 ([paras 5-6]).

The appeal court further highlighted that the grounds for dismissing the application, especially the possible prejudice of halting litigation in the public interest, lacked sufficient merit. While the public interest was acknowledged, it did not outweigh the justice owed to the appellants regarding the financial risks posed by continued litigation without security ([paras 19-20]). 

The Court also noted that the former directors' alleged negligence and mismanagement did not present sufficient reason to deny security, as the claims rested in part on fiduciary duties and responsibilities that directly influenced and facilitated financial misconduct ([paras 19]). The SCA ultimately concluded that an order for security must be granted in light of the provided evidence and the general rules governing such applications ([paras 20]).

**C – Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the order of the lower court, holding that the refusal to grant security was incorrect. The appeal was upheld, and the lower court's decision was replaced with an order for security for costs in favor of the appellants. This ruling emphasizes the importance of adhering to statutory requirements under Section 13 concerning security for costs—reaffirming a balanced approach to both parties' rights under similar circumstances. The implications of this judgment demonstrate the judiciary's intent to ensure that litigation does not proceed at the expense of a party's financial solvency without the adequate provision of security ([paras 20]).

**Judgment Citation**: Republic of South Africa, *Shepstone & Wylie and Others v Geyser NO*, Supreme Court of Appeal, Case No: 364/97 (28 May 1998) [Hefer JA].
