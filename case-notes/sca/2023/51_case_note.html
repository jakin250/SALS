Supreme Court of Appeal

Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development and Others v Pennington and Another (162/2022)

[2] The appellants countered by raising a special plea of prescription, asserting that the claims had prescribed.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

[1] Mr Franklin Pennington and his wife, Mrs Gail Jackson Pennington, initiated a civil action in the Gauteng Division of the High Court against the Ministers of Justice, Police, and Home Affairs of South Africa. The basis of their claims was underpinned by damages arising from wrongful arrest, malicious prosecution, and egregious delays hindering the appeal process associated with the first respondent's past criminal trials.

[2] The appellants countered by raising a special plea of prescription, asserting that the claims had prescribed. They also included a plea of non-joinder, arguing that the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA) and the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) should have been joined as defendants, given their role in the alleged malicious prosecution of the first respondent.

[3] The facts outlined indicate that the first and second respondents endured significant legal troubles throughout the 1990s. Mr. Pennington was first arrested in 1991 on allegations of fraud, forgery, and uttering, subsequently acquitted in 1992. Following another arrest in 1994, the first respondent faced a prolonged legal battle culminating in a wrongful conviction. The convictions were ultimately set aside in 2015, prompting the issuance of summons for damages on 15 June 2016.

[4] The respondents' claims articulated various grievances linked to the appellants' alleged failures—including unreasonable delays in the appeal process, wrongful actions leading to arrest, and the imposition of conditions that effectively barred Mr. Pennington from employment during the trial and appeal process.

I – Issues

[10] The two principal issues for determination were:

  1. Did the claims against the appellants prescribe under the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, specifically considering the timeline associated with the events leading to the claims?
  2. Could the appellants be liable for malicious prosecution given the non-joinder of relevant authorities?

R – Rules

[20] The court's analysis hinged on the provisions of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, particularly Sections 11(d) and 12. Section 11(d) stipulates that claims prescribe three years after the event giving rise to the claim. Section 12(3) emphasizes that a debt is not considered due until the creditor possesses sufficient knowledge of the identity of the debtor and the underlying facts establishing the debt, a criterion referred to as knowledge for prescription to initiate.

[21] The foundational case law underscored the “complete cause of action” principle, establishing that a claim becomes actionable only when the claimant can articulate and substantiate their allegations with a complete set of facts.

A – Application

[13] The High Court initially dismissed the appellants' special plea of prescription, positing a continuous chain of events stretching from the 1994 arrest to the successful appeal in 2015. This reasoning implied that the onset of prescription only commenced post-appeal conclusion, reflecting an insufficient connection between the respondents' injuries and the alleged wrongful acts based on the timeline.

[24] However, the Supreme Court of Appeal found merit in the appellants' argument. The court determined that the respondents had sufficient knowledge of their claims by the end of 1998 when it became apparent that the appellants could not compile an adequate record for appeal. The court further recognized that an application made in 2012 aimed at addressing the DPP’s failure to act reflected the respondents' full awareness of the situation, effectively starting the prescription clock on that date.

[20] The findings illustrated a legal expectation that the respondents could not indefinitely postpone the initiation of their claims, especially as they were aware of the procedural mishaps long before the summons was filed in June 2016.

C – Conclusion

[25] The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, concluding that the respondents’ claims had indeed prescribed, with reasonable knowledge of the underlying facts existing well before the summons issuance. The implications of this ruling reinforce the legal principles surrounding timely action within the judicial system, particularly emphasizing the necessity for claimants to assert their rights without undue delay after acquiring sufficient knowledge of their grievances.

The decision underscores the judiciary's commitment to managing the balance between the right to seek redress and the importance of finality and legal certainty within the civil justice framework. The dismissal of the non-joinder plea further clarifies the implications of procedural adherence in articulating claims against government entities within the context of alleged misconduct.
