# Case Note: Saayman v Road Accident Fund (329/09) [2010] ZASCA 123

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts:
In the *Saayman v Road Accident Fund* ([1]) case, the appellant, A T Saayman, sought an appeal against a damage award for future loss of earnings stemming from injuries sustained in a motor vehicle accident on 13 June 2005. Before the accident, the appellant was a highly skilled Market Risk Analyst employed by Standard Bank, earning R339,166 per annum ([2]). He had been earmarked for a Career Development Programme indicative of a promising career trajectory, with expectations of a promotion to Head of Market Risk by 1 January 2014, at a projected salary of R2.25 million ([3], [4]).

The accident resulted in severe brain injuries, significantly impairing his cognitive abilities and emotional resilience, which hindered his career advancement ([5]–[7]). After the accident, despite making efforts to return to work, the appellant remained in a diminished capacity, which rendered him no longer a viable candidate for advancement as previously anticipated, culminating in a claim for future income loss of R54,310,264 ([8]). The South Gauteng High Court (Maluleke J) awarded R4,295,290, which the appellant contended was inaccurate due to various alleged misdirections ([9]–[10]).

### I – Issues:
The pivotal legal questions involved:
1. Whether the trial judge had erred in instructing actuaries to calculate future income loss based on a 2009 rate rather than the agreed 2007 rate ([9]).
2. The appropriateness of the contingency deduction rates applied both pre- and post-accident, specifically whether a 50% contingency deduction was justified ([10], [11]).
3. The calculation of future income based on projected salary increases and the correctness of interest and inflation rates as employed in the trial judgment ([12]–[13]).

### R – Rules:
The court relied on established legal principles regarding the assessment of damages for loss of earnings, including how future income loss should be calculated with appropriate contingencies reflecting uncertainties in career progression. The principles established in cases like *Road Accident Fund v Guedes* 2006 (5) SA 583 (SCA) were central, particularly regarding the courts’ discretion to interfere when awards exhibit significant disparities due to judicial errors ([14]).

### A – Application:
In concluding that the trial judge erred ([14]), the SCA reviewed the evidence surrounding the appellant's career prospects carefully. Despite the judge’s conclusion that there were usual hazards affecting the appellant's chances for promotion, the cumulative expert testimony strongly suggested that, barring the accident, the appellant was almost certain to achieve the head position, justifying a lower contingency of 25% rather than the 50% applied ([10], [11]). The court found the post-accident labor market scenario to be riskier than previously asserted, warranting a higher contingency of 30% for the period after the accident due to the appellant's impaired cognitive functions that diminished his competitiveness and employability in a high-pressure environment ([11], [39]).

Regarding the financial assessment, the court ruled that the award should be recalculated reflecting future income at R2.25 million based on the 2007 rate with a 30% contingency deduction, considering inflation at 6% per annum and interest at 2.5% ([16]). The recalculations conducted by the appointed actuary, Mr. Ivan Kramer, supported the appellant’s position, ultimately leading to a total recalculated amount of R13,572,649 to be awarded ([18]).

### C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal ([18]) held that the initial award of R4,295,290 was insufficient, directing that it be increased to R13,572,649 to accurately reflect the future loss of income due to the accident. The court emphasized the need for precision in calculating damages for loss of earnings, reaffirming that judicial mistakes concerning actuaries’ instructions and contingency evaluations could warrant significant adjustments to awards in personal injury claims ([14], [18]). The implications of this case underscore the ongoing challenges in accurately predicting future earnings within the context of personal injury, particularly regarding the weight of expert testimony in establishing career trajectories.
