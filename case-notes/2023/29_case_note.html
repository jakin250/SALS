Mncwabe v President of the Republic of South Africa and Others; Mathenjwa v President of the Republic of South Africa and Others [2023] ZACC 29

The cases of Mncwabe v President of the Republic of South Africa and Mathenjwa v President of the Republic of South Africa were heard together by the Constitutional Court of South Africa.

Constitutional Court 2023 [2023] ZACC 29

F – Facts

The cases of Mncwabe v President of the Republic of South Africa and Mathenjwa v President of the Republic of South Africa were heard together by the Constitutional Court of South Africa. The applicants, Ron Simphiwe Mncwabe and Khulekani Raymond Mathenjwa, both advocates, were initially appointed as Directors of Public Prosecutions (DPPs) by former President Jacob Zuma, effective 1 February 2018, through Presidential Minutes 18 and 10, respectively ([1]–[4]).

Upon President Zuma's resignation on 14 February 2018, President Cyril Ramaphosa, who succeeded him, reviewed the appointments after an internal legal opinion indicated that these appointments had not been finalised due to the absence of public announcements ([5]–[8]). On 11 March 2019, Ramaphosa revoked these appointments through Presidential Minutes 67 and 69, prompting the applicants to approach the High Court for review and to set aside the revocations ([6]).

The central issue in the High Court was whether Zuma's decisions were final upon the applicants' notification, and thus whether Ramaphosa's revocation was lawful. The High Court concluded that without a public announcement, the appointments were not final, and thus Ramaphosa could revoke them ([9]–[10]). The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld this decision when the applicants sought leave to appeal ([11]).

I – Issues

  1. Legal Status of Appointments: Did the absence of public announcement prevent the appointments from being final, and did this justify the revocation by the President?
  2. Functus Officio Principle: Could President Ramaphosa claim that President Zuma was not functus officio regarding the purported decisions to appoint the applicants?
  3. Procedural Fairness: Were the revocation decisions made without putting the applicants on notice, thereby infringing upon their rights under the audi alteram partem principle?
  4. Authority of Notification: Did Mr. Abrahams, as the then-National Director of Public Prosecutions, have the requisite authority to notify the applicants of their appointments?

R – Rules

  1. Functus Officio Doctrine: This legal principle asserts that once an official has made a final decision, he is barred from revisiting that decision ([9]).
  2. Requirement of Notification: Section 13(1)(a) of the National Prosecuting Authority Act 32 of 1998 does not expressly mandate public announcement as a prerequisite for the effectiveness of a DPP appointment, although implications can be drawn on the necessity for formal notification as a part of procedural mechanism ([12]).
  3. Constitutional Framework: Sections 83, 84, and 179 of the South African Constitution prescribe the roles and powers of the President, particularly in relation to the appointment of DPPs. They emphasize the role of public power being exercised lawfully and within the ambit of constitutional provisions ([12]).
  4. Legal Precedents: The case of SARFU III provided contexts for understanding the finality of appointments and the necessary public announcement as part of that finality ([7]).

A – Application

Functus Officio and Finality of Appointments

The Court analyzed whether President Zuma was functus officio at the time of Ramaphosa's revocations. The High Court concluded that since there was no public announcement of the appointments, the decisions were not final. This view was scrutinized in light of the personal notifications made by Mr. Abrahams to the applicants. Majiedt J contended that notification to the affected individuals was sufficient for those appointments to take legal effect, thus rendering the former President functus officio ([13]).

Authority and Notification

The applicants argued that Mr. Abrahams was acting within the scope of his authority as the head of the National Prosecuting Authority when he informed them of their appointments. The Court established that a proper procedure surrounding Presidential Minutes indicated that once signed, the responsibility to communicate lay with the appointed authority. It was reaffirmed that Mr. Abrahams's notification rendered the appointments effective despite the lack of a formal public announcement ([14]).

Rationality and Procedural Fairness

Under the principles of legality, the President's decision to revoke the appointments was subjected to scrutiny for rationality. The lack of a public announcement was held not to negate the appointments but questioned the manner of their communication. The Court explored whether procedural fairness was upheld in Ramaphosa’s actions, determining that the lack of notice to the applicants constituted a violation of their rights as the decisions impacted their professional status ([15]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ruled that:

  1. The President unlawfully revoked the appointments since they were not rendered void due to the absence of public announcements; personal notifications by Mr. Abrahams were sufficient.
  2. The Court overturned the High Court’s and the Supreme Court of Appeal’s decisions and declared the revocations invalid.
  3. The applicants were entitled to resume their positions as DPPs and the appointments of Mr. Sakata and Ms. Kanyane were also rendered invalid as they were filled while the litigation was ongoing ([16]–[17]).

This case clarifies the significance of notification in executive appointments, emphasizing the importance of procedural adherence in governmental functions. It also highlights the balance necessary between executive authority and individual rights in appointments crucial to maintaining the integrity of the National Prosecuting Authority and the broader judicial system in South Africa ([12], [14]).
