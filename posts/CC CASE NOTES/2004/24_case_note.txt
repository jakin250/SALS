Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2004

# FIRAC Case Note: Radio Pretoria v Chairperson of the Independent Communications Authority of South Africa (CCT 38/04)

## F – Facts
Radio Pretoria, a community broadcaster established to serve the “Boere-Afrikaner” community, operated under a series of temporary broadcasting licenses granted by the Independent Broadcasting Authority (IBA) and, subsequently, the Independent Communications Authority of South Africa (ICASA), since its inception in 1993 ([1]–[2]). The applicant sought a permanent community broadcasting license in March 1998, but due to resource constraints faced by IBA and ICASA, its application was delayed, and annual temporary licenses were issued instead ([4]). 

In February 2000, Radio Pretoria applied for its sixth such temporary license ([5]). ICASA neither granted nor dismissed this application before the effective date. The authority eventually refused the application on 28 February 2001, necessitating the termination of its broadcasting service within 30 days ([6]). Subsequently, Radio Pretoria initiated review proceedings in the High Court, alleging that ICASA's refusal was unlawful for several reasons, including irrationality and lack of proper hearing. However, the High Court dismissed the application ([7]).

Radio Pretoria appealed the High Court's decision to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which declined to hear the appeal on 2 September 2004, on the grounds that the issues were moot, as the temporary license period had expired, and no practical effect would arise from the appeal ([14]–[15]). Radio Pretoria sought leave from the Constitutional Court to appeal this decision, claiming that its fundamental rights had been violated ([17]).

## I – Issues
The key legal issues before the Constitutional Court included:
1. **Constitutional Violations**: Whether Radio Pretoria's rights under sections 9(1) (equality), 16 (freedom of expression), 34 (access to courts), 36 (limitations on rights), and 38 (enforcement of rights) of the Constitution were infringed due to the decision by ICASA and the subsequent dismissal of its appeal by the SCA ([17]).
2. **Mootness**: Whether the SCA’s conclusion that the appeal was moot and without practical effect was justified, particularly in the context of ongoing broadcasting under interim court orders ([14]–[15]).
3. **Public Interest**: The extent to which a ruling on the temporary license could serve public interest or advance legal clarity for other broadcasters, given the constitutional considerations regarding broadcasting rights ([22]).

## R – Rules
The court relied on a number of constitutional and statutory frameworks:
1. **Section 192 of the Constitution**: Establishes the need for an independent body to regulate broadcasting, which conceivably encompasses licensing powers of ICASA ([20]).
2. **Section 16 of the Constitution**: Provides the right to freedom of expression, which is particularly significant in the context of broadcasting ([20]).
3. **Section 34 of the Constitution**: Ensures that all individuals have the right to have disputes resolved in a fair hearing, relevant to the procedural elements of Radio Pretoria's application ([17]).
4. **Section 36 of the Constitution**: This section allows for the limitation of rights provided that it is reasonable and justifiable in an open democratic society ([18]).
5. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA)**: Addresses the review of decisions made by public bodies, relevant to ICASA’s licensing decisions ([18]).

Additionally, precedence was cited from cases such as *National Coalition for Gay and Lesbian Equality v Minister of Home Affairs* regarding justiciability and mootness of cases ([15]).

## A – Application
Upon evaluating the legal issues in the context of the outlined rules, the constitutional significance of the matter was recognized. The Court noted that the refusal of the temporary broadcasting license raised serious questions regarding the balance between regulatory authority and constitutional freedoms, particularly under the framework established by sections 192, 16, and 33 of the Constitution ([20]). 

However, the Court faced a critical issue regarding the existence of a live controversy: the application challenging the decision on the four-year permanent license had not been initiated, meaning the facts pertinent to the review were absent in the current proceedings ([21]). 

The SCA had found that a ruling in favor of Radio Pretoria regarding the temporary license would not affect its situation, as it had continued broadcasting under a court order ([22]). The Constitutional Court noted that this rendered any decision on the temporary license moot, as Radio Pretoria's ongoing broadcasting was predicated on a separate basis and there was no alternative outcome that could ultimately resolve the underlying disputes regarding the four-year license ([21]).

The Court emphasized that while the application raised significant constitutional issues that could benefit the broader public interest, it lacked sufficient factual or legal grounding since Radio Pretoria had not pursued its review proceedings against the denial of the four-year license ([22]). 

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately dismissed Radio Pretoria’s application for leave to appeal, concluding that the issues raised no longer presented a living controversy and that addressing them would not serve a justifiable purpose. The court did not find compelling reasons to adjudicate the constitutional matters in the absence of an ongoing dispute relevant to the immediate application ([24]). 

The implications of this ruling underscore the importance of procedural avenues for broadcasters in asserting their rights and the necessity for addressing appeals in contexts where they possess ongoing justiciable claims. The decision reinforces the principles of mootness and justiciability within South African constitutional law, prompting broadcasters to ensure that legal challenges are firmly grounded in active disputes to be considered by the courts. 

In summary, the Constitutional Court’s refusal to grant leave highlights the challenges faced by entities like Radio Pretoria in navigating the regulatory landscape and the judicial mechanisms available for challenging administrative decisions pertaining to broadcasting rights.
