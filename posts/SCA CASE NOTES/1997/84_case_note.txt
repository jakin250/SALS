Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 1997

### Case Note: GOVERNMENT OF THE PROVINCE OF THE EASTERN CAPE v FRONTIER SAFARIS (PTY) LIMITED

#### F – Facts
The case revolves around a dispute regarding the validity of a lease agreement made in 1989 between the Government of the Province of the Eastern Cape (hereinafter referred to as "the government") and Frontier Safaris (Pty) Ltd (hereinafter referred to as "the respondent"). The appellant is a successor to the government of the former Republic of the Ciskei, which had enacted the Nature Conservation Act 10 of 1987 ("the Act") to regulate the protection and management of national nature reserves within its jurisdiction ([para 2]).

In November 1989, the government entered into a lease agreement with a nominee company purportedly to lease three national nature reserves for 40 years ([para 3]). Development of these reserves was critical to the government, who had also negotiated a loan for infrastructure improvements ([para 4]). 

In 1993, the Minister of Finance, representing the government, asserted that the lease was void and attempted to cancel it, which was perceived by the respondent as a repudiation of the contract. Subsequently, the respondent filed a claim for damages against the government, including substantial claims for future profits ([paras 4–5]). 

The government raised a special plea asserting that the lease was ultra vires the Act, as it allegedly divested the Department of Agriculture, Forestry and Rural Development of its responsibilities regarding the management of the reserves, thus making the contract unenforceable ([para 6–8]).

#### I – Issues
The central issues are:
1. Whether the lease agreement entered into by the government purported to abdicate its statutory responsibilities as per the Nature Conservation Act, and if so, whether such abdication rendered the contract void and unenforceable ([paras 7–8]).
2. Whether the government had the authority to delegate the management responsibilities to the respondent under the provisions of the Act ([paras 17–18]).

#### R – Rules
The crux of the government's argument lies within the Act itself. In particular:
- Section 25(1) states the control, maintenance, development, and management of a national nature reserve are vested in the Department ([para 10]) and distinctly defines the powers that the Department may exercise under subsection 25(2).
- The principle that public administrative bodies cannot delegate their statutory powers unless expressly or necessarily implied within the empowering legislation ([para 14–15]). 

Judicial precedent supports the notion that where a public authority has been given specific powers, the authority must itself exercise those powers and cannot transfer them to another body, adhering to the maxim **delegatus non potest delegare** ([para 4]).

#### A – Application
The court sought to determine whether the contractual arrangement constituted an abdication of the government's responsibilities. In examining the provisions of the lease agreement, it was apparent that while the respondent was assigned many administrative tasks, the overall responsibility remained theoretically with the government due to clauses that retained various powers and oversight mechanisms ([paras 19–25]).

The judgment highlights that the Act does not expressly forbid the government from entering into contracts that involve the outsourcing of certain functions, provided that those contracts do not undermine its statutory duties. The agreement included clauses stipulating that all actions must comply with the provisions of the Act and any other applicable laws, suggesting that the government preserved its authority ([para 26]).

### C – Conclusion
The court ultimately concluded that the lease agreement did not divest the government of its powers nor constituted a violation of the Nature Conservation Act. The appeal was dismissed, affirming that the government retained sufficient control and oversight to ensure that public interests were observed ([para 26]).

The decision clarifies the boundaries of administrative delegation within public governance, emphasizing the need for compliance with statutory frameworks while also allowing for practical management strategies through contractual arrangements. The implications extend to how governmental bodies may engage in public-private partnerships without relinquishing their statutory duties, fostering an environment where partnerships can exist within a regulated context.
