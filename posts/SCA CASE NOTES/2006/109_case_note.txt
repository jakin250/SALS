# Case Note: Hyperception Properties 572 CC v Waterhouse Properties CC [2006] SCA 137 (RSA)

## FIRAC Structure

### F – Facts ([paras 1–2])
This case involves a dispute between **Hyperception Properties 572 CC** (the appellant) and **Waterhouse Properties CC** (the first respondent), with **Jean-Marie Denis Maurice Moore** and **Juanita Catherine Moore** serving as the second and third respondents respectively. The issue arose from a prior order of the Bloemfontein High Court that mandated the demolition of a structure erected by the appellant on its property, which the respondents alleged had been unlawfully constructed. The appellant sought condonation for the late lodging of an appeal record against this order. However, during the proceedings, the appellant's counsel informed the court of the client's decision to withdraw the application for condonation, conceding the merits of the case. As a result, the order for demolition by the High Court remained in effect. The appellant tendered costs on a party and party scale, while the respondents sought an exceptional costs order on an attorney and client scale, alleging the appellant had behaved recklessly throughout the litigation process.

### I – Issues ([paras 3–4])
The primary legal issue raised was whether the respondents were entitled to an exceptional costs order against the appellant due to the alleged reckless and unreasonable behavior of the appellant throughout the litigation. This issue arises in the context of the procedural history surrounding the late lodging of the appeal record, as well as the appellant's withdrawal of its application for condonation.

### R – Rules ([paras 3–5])
The court considered the principles surrounding costs orders as articulated in previous case law. According to standard practice, costs are generally awarded on a party and party scale unless there are compelling reasons to grant an exceptional order on an attorney and client scale. The factors that typically justify such exceptional costs include conduct that demonstrates extreme recklessness, bad faith, or a significant failure to adhere to procedural rules as established in cases like *Masiya v Director of Public Prosecutions* [2007] ZASCA 132.

### A – Application ([paras 4–6])
In applying these rules to the present case, the court noted that although the appellant had indeed delayed in the prosecution of the appeal—having obtained leave to appeal on 10 June 2005, lodged a notice of appeal on 11 July 2005, and only submitted the record of appeal on 2 March 2006—the conduct leading to the request for costs on a higher scale lacked sufficient justification. The respondents argued that the appellant's conduct during the preceding interactions was high-handed and that the appeal was prosecuted carelessly. However, the court found that the appellant had resisted the respondents on the basis of legal advice, indicating that there was no clear recklessness or unreasonableness warranting an exceptional costs award. Consequently, the respondents were constrained to accept the appellant's costs tender but were not awarded the attorney and client costs they sought.

### C – Conclusion ([paras 6])
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the withdrawal of the application for condonation led to the affirming of the Bloemfontein High Court's demolition order. The court concluded that while the appellant's conduct was not excusable and demonstrated a degree of carelessness, it did not rise to the level necessary to justify an award of costs on an attorney and client scale. Thus, the court ultimately made no order regarding costs beyond accepting the tender made by the appellant. The judgment emphasizes the significance of procedural adherence while also highlighting the judiciary's reluctance to impose punitive costs unless clear misconduct is demonstrated. 

In summary, the judgment serves as a reminder of the importance of diligent legal practice and the cautious use of the court's discretion in awarding costs, particularly against parties resisting litigation based on legal counsel and procedural grounds.
