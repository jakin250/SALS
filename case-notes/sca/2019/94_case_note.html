NPGS Protection and Security Services CC & Another v FirstRand Bank Ltd (314/2018) [2019] ZASCA 94

In May 2017, FirstRand issued summons against the appellants for R649,197.39, alleging default on the loan.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 94

FIRAC Method Case Note

F – Facts

This case arises from the appeal by NPGS Protection and Security Services CC (NPGS) and Llewellyn Rwaxa against a judgment by the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg (Judge Wepener) that granted summary judgment in favor of FirstRand Bank Ltd ([1]–[4]). NPGS, a close corporation engaged in security services with Rwaxa as its sole member, entered into a credit facility agreement with FirstRand on 6 May 2009, receiving R250,000 as working capital, secured by a mortgage bond over Rwaxa's immovable property ([2]–[3]).

In May 2017, FirstRand issued summons against the appellants for R649,197.39, alleging default on the loan. The appellants formally acknowledged their intention to defend but later sought to establish a bona fide defense against the summary judgment application, primarily arguing about the absence of a certificate of balance, which they claimed was necessary to contest the claimed debt ([4]–[6]). The High Court dismissed these defenses and granted summary judgment, declaring Rwaxa's property especially executable ([8]).

I – Issues

The central legal issues in this case are twofold. First, whether the court a quo correctly granted summary judgment against the appellants based on their failure to show a bona fide defense to the claim ([1]–[15]). Second, whether it was appropriate for the court to declare Rwaxa's immovable property specially executable without sufficient evidence indicating how the execution would affect a right to housing under section 26 of the Constitution ([16]–[20]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal principles address the requirements for summary judgment under Rule 32 of the Uniform Rules of Court, necessitating that a defendant present a bona fide defense that discloses the nature and grounds of such defense ([11]). Precedent indicates that an opponent cannot rely solely on vague assertions but must specify facts, such as disputes over the amounts claimed and previous payments ([12]). Furthermore, judicial oversight requirements regarding the execution against a debtor's primary residence are governed by the National Credit Act 34 of 2005 and constitutional protections outlined in sections 26(1) and 26(3) ([26]–[30]).

In Jaftha v Schoeman, the Constitutional Court established that courts must conduct inquiries into the circumstances surrounding an eviction or execution of a home to protect Section 26 rights ([26]). The subsequent amendments to Rule 46 and its replacement with Rule 46A formally establish this judicial oversight ([50]–[55]).

A – Application

The High Court rejected the appellants' defenses, categorizing them as “sham defenses” largely based on the absence of specific factual evidence about the alleged debt and the failure to indicate any irregularity concerning the mortgage bond ([13]–[14]). The court emphasized that if NPGS had not received the funds as claimed, it would have been straightforward to make that clear. The appellants did not contest the fact of the loan nor express dissatisfaction with statements sent by the bank.

In relation to the property execution, while the appellants mentioned that Rwaxa’s property was his primary residence, this was raised only from the bar during oral argument and had not been substantiated by any affidavit or evidence prior to the court's decision. The absence of a full inquiry into the execution order led to a misdirection by the court. The appellate judges expressed that judicial oversight must occur regardless of the appellant's legal representation status, especially considering that Rwaxa's home rights were implicated ([16]–[20],[36]–[39]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) affirmed the summary judgment against the appellants, recognizing that the defenses raised were insufficient to establish a bona fide dispute ([15]–[19]). However, it held that the court a quo erred by not conducting a proper inquiry into whether the execution of Rwaxa's primary residence would infringe upon his constitutional right to housing ([20]–[40]).

Consequently, while the appeal against the summary judgment was dismissed with costs, the execution order regarding the property was remitted back to the court a quo for reconsideration under proper oversight, ensuring that Rwaxa’s rights under section 26 of the Constitution were duly considered. This case reaffirms the necessity for courts to maintain vigilant oversight in execution matters, especially in safeguarding vulnerable debtors' housing rights against unjust infringements ([43]–[69]).
