Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1997

## Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of The African National Congress & Another v Lombo [1997] ZASCA 36 

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([2]–[5])**

In this case, the respondent, Siphiwe Bethuel Lombo, initiated an action for damages against the African National Congress (ANC) and the South African Communist Party (SACP), both of which are political organizations capable of being sued in their own names. Lombo, a former member of the ANC, alleged that he was abducted by the appellants while en route to Botswana for military training. He claimed that he endured severe maltreatment, including unlawful imprisonment, assault, and torture. His detention spanned several years across various locations, primarily in Zambia and Angola, where he faced isolation, inadequate living conditions, and physical and mental abuse ([2]–[4]). 

Following the initiation of the suit, the appellants raised multiple special pleas, including a significant fifth special plea asserting that they, as 'banned' organizations under South African law from May 1986 to February 1990, had no legal existence and thus could not be held liable for the alleged delicts. The trial court dismissed this special plea, leading to an appeal by the appellants ([5]).

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([6]–[8])**

The core legal questions in this case revolve around:

1. Whether the ANC and SACP, having been declared unlawful organizations, retained any legal personality during the period in question (May 1986 to February 1990) and could, therefore, be held liable for the alleged actions that constituted the delicts against the respondent.
2. The implications of the legislative framework (specifically the Suppression of Communism Act 44 of 1950 and the Unlawful Organizations Act 34 of 1960) on the legal status of the appellants ([6]).

These issues raise significant questions regarding the balance between political activism and the law's recognition of organizations engaged in anti-establishment actions, particularly during apartheid-era South Africa.

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([9]–[12])**

The court examined the legal framework surrounding the status of unlawful organizations as delineated in various legislative acts, notably:

- **Suppression of Communism Act 44 of 1950**: This Act declared the Communist Party an unlawful organization and gave the government the authority to restrict activities and dissolve organizations deemed unlawful. 
- **Unlawful Organizations Act 34 of 1960**: This statute empowered the Governor-General to declare organizations unlawful, with similar provisions for their dissolution and the prohibition of their activities.

The court drew upon precedent, specifically *South African Flour Millers' Mutual Association v Rutowitz Flour Mills Ltd* (1938), where it was established that an organization declared illegal loses its legal personality and cannot engage in legal transactions or be held accountable for actions taken during its illegal status. The court also referenced outcomes from other cases that touched upon the implications of being deemed an illegal association ([9]–[11]).

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([13]–[32])**

In addressing the fifth special plea, the court analyzed whether the ANC and SACP had indeed lost their legal personality due to the declarations made under the aforementioned acts. It was established that while unlawful organizations are subject to various restrictions, there is no explicit legislative provision that extinguishes their legal personality outright.

The court noted that other legislative clauses implied that being declared unlawful did not negate the possibility of legal agency. For example, sections that allowed for individuals to resign from these organizations after being declared unlawful indicated that the organizations maintained some form of existence under the law ([22]–[25]). 

Moreover, the court rejected the appellants' argument that their actions during the unlawful period could not be subject to legal liability. It underscored that legal personality, while contested, was not so severed as to preclude any legal actions or responsibilities.

Ultimately, the court concluded that the provisions of Act 74 of 1982, which continued to treat the ANC and SACP as entities with legal existence post their respective "unbannings" in 1990, fortified this argument. Additional to this, the court critically assessed the appellants' non-compliance with procedural rules, indicating that their failure to adhere to the rules of the Appellate Division further diminished their appeal's viability ([30]–[35]).

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([34]–[35])**

The Supreme Court of South Africa dismissed the appeal against the trial court's ruling, affirming that the ANC and SACP retained legal personality despite their classification as unlawful organizations during the period in question. The court's ruling reinforced the principle that organizations, even when deemed unlawful, do not automatically cease to exist in a legal sense, thereby allowing for accountability under South African law.

The implications of this decision extend beyond the immediate parties involved; it underscored the nuanced relationship between law and political activism in South Africa, particularly regarding organizations that sought to challenge oppressive regimes. The ruling affirmed the potential for damages to be claimed against political organizations for wrongful acts, setting a vital precedent in the intersection of political action and legal accountability against the backdrop of apartheid [34]–[35]. 

The court's refusal of the condonation application further illustrated the importance of procedural propriety in appellate practice, ensuring that parties adhere to established legal standards and reinforcing judicial integrity in the pursuit of justice.
