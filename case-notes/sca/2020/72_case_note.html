Supreme Court of Appeal

s: The Road Accident Fund v Mbele (555/19) [2020] ZASCA 72

The issue of whether the Reach Stacker fell within the purview of “motor vehicle” was adjudicated in the Western Cape Division of the High Court under Rule 33(4) of the Uniform Rules of Court.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 72

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The case of The Road Accident Fund v Mbele arises from a tragic incident involving Mr. Simphiwe Robert Makutoana, who was killed on 20 February 2010 after being struck by a Reach Stacker at the Multipurpose Terminal in Cape Town Harbour, where he worked as a stevedore. The deceased’s common-law wife, Thandiswa Linah Mbele, initiated legal proceedings against the Road Accident Fund (RAF) for damages related to the loss of support for herself and her four minor children, asserting her claim under the RAF Act 56 of 1996. The RAF disputed the claim, primarily contending that the Reach Stacker was not a "motor vehicle" as defined by the RAF Act, thereby rendering Mbele's claim incompetent.

The issue of whether the Reach Stacker fell within the purview of “motor vehicle” was adjudicated in the Western Cape Division of the High Court under Rule 33(4) of the Uniform Rules of Court. The initial judgment by Desai J concluded that the Reach Stacker did not qualify as a motor vehicle; however, this decision was reversed by a full bench composed of Judges Gamble, Le Grange, and Sievers, which found that the Reach Stacker was indeed a motor vehicle. The RAF subsequently appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), who decided the matter without oral hearing ([paras 1–4]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue hinged on whether the Reach Stacker constituted a "motor vehicle" as defined in Section 1 of the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996. Specifically, this question encompassed the interpretation of the vehicle's design and application, assessing if its intended purpose aligned with the characteristics of a motor vehicle ("designed or adapted for propulsion or haulage on a road") as outlined in the statutory framework ([paras 5–9]).

R – Rules

The legal framework governing this determination is encapsulated in the definition of a "motor vehicle" as provided in Section 1 of the RAF Act, which includes vehicles designed for propulsion or haulage on roads. Key judicial interpretations relevant to the definition include:

  1. The test established in Chauke v Santam Limited (1997) regarding the application of an objective standard to assess if a vehicle poses a danger to other road users.
  2. Judicial precedents from cases such as Road Accident Fund v Mbendera (2004), Mutual and Federal Insurance Co Ltd v Day (2001), and Road Accident Fund v Vogel (2004), which provide interpretative guidance on assessing the suitability of vehicles for road use, focusing on design and intended purpose.

These precedents emphasize a dual assessment involving both subjective and objective elements to determine if a vehicle, despite its primary design purpose, can nonetheless be classified as a motor vehicle if it is equipped for safe road use ([paras 10–12]).

A – Application

In evaluating the applicability of the legal definitions to the specifics of the case, the SCA undertook a detailed analysis of the Reach Stacker's design features, capabilities, and intended use. The vehicle was described as primarily engineered for lifting, maneuvering, and stacking containers within port operations, yet it was equipped with mechanisms allowing for safe operation on public roads:

  • Equipped with standard traffic features such as headlights, indicators, and a maximum speed of 24.5 km/h, the Reach Stacker demonstrated characteristics conducive to road use.
  • Evidence revealed that it was capable of transporting cargo within the port and registered for operation on public roads, as required under the National Road Traffic Act. The court noted that, although escort vehicles were needed in some circumstances due to its size, these requirements did not negate its qualification as a motor vehicle.

The SCA emphasized the need for an objective determination of whether a reasonable person would perceive the Reach Stacker's use on roads as presenting exceptional hazards. Citing the full bench’s findings, the SCA concurred that the design included features ensuring safety during operation, and that any challenges in road use were mitigated by the provisions for escorting the vehicle ([paras 20–29]).

C – Conclusion

The SCA ultimately ruled that the Reach Stacker qualified as a motor vehicle under the RAF Act, dismissing the appeal of the Road Accident Fund with costs. The decision underscored a broader interpretation of what constitutes a motor vehicle, affirming that vehicles designed for specific operational environments could still meet the statutory definition if they possess necessary road safety features and are utilized according to safe operational practices. This ruling not only advances clarity on the scope of the RAF Act but also reinforces the principles of statutory interpretation within the context of vehicle classifications in South African law. The outcome has significant implications for similar cases where the classification of vehicles under the RAF Act may be contested ([paras 29]).
