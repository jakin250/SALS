Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: FIRAC on Parktown High School for Girls v Hishaam & another

### F - Facts
The appeal arises from an incident at Parktown High School for Girls during a fashion show organized by the Representative Council of Learners (RCL), which occurred on the school premises. The event had two sessions, with Naqeeb Emeran attending the evening show. The respondents allege that a failure in safety led to Naqeeb being injured when he leaned on a loose table top that fell, crushing his right hand. Mr. Hishaam Emeran, Naqeeb's father, seeks damages for medical expenses and loss of earnings.

The school, in defense, filed a special plea, contending that the plaintiffs should have sued the Member of the Executive Council (MEC) rather than the school itself, as the incident fell under the provisions of Section 60 of the South African Schools Act 84 of 1996 (the Act). The Gauteng Division of the High Court dismissed this special plea, allowing the school to appeal ([1]–[2]).

### I - Issues
Two primary legal questions arise from this case:

1. Is the injured party obligated to sue the State (represented by the MEC) rather than the school itself in light of the provisions of Section 60 of the Act?
2. Was the fashion show organized by the RCL considered a "business or enterprise" under Section 60(4) of the Act, thereby exempting State liability for the incident ([7]–[8], [10]).

### R - Rules
The key statutory provisions relevant to the dispute include:

- **Section 60(1)** states that the State is liable for any damages stemming from acts or omissions in school activities, except where otherwise indicated.
- **Section 60(3)** mandates that claims under Section 60(1) must be brought against the MEC.
- **Section 60(4)** excludes State liability for negligent conduct in connection with "an enterprise or business" operated under the authority of a public school for the purpose of supplementing the school's resources, as established in Section 36 of the Act ([5]–[6], [8]).

Notably, the term "school activity" encompasses various educational, cultural, and recreational undertakings by the school and its students. However, the terms "enterprise" and "business" are not defined in the Act and remain central to the case ([1], [11]).

### A - Application
The court undertook a detailed examination of whether the fashion show could be classified as a “business or enterprise.” While the respondents argued that the fundraising effort represented an enterprise aimed at supplementing school resources, the ruling emphasized that a dictionary definition of "enterprise" suggested a commercial initiative, rather than a school activity designed primarily for entertainment or fundraising without formal business structures ([11]–[12]).

The court ultimately concluded that the fashion show was not a business or enterprise in the statutory sense since it lacked the characteristics typically associated with commercial activity and did not receive requisite authorization from the school governing body ([12]–[16]).

Additionally, it highlighted that the funds collected did not directly supplement the school's resources in a manner contemplated by the law, as the proceeds were not administered through the school governing body's authority or funding policies. Instead, there was no evidence indicating that the RCL, through the fashion show's proceeds, acted under the needed governance framework established by Section 36 ([13], [15]).

### C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the special plea raised by the school, concluding that the respondents instituted proceedings against the incorrect party by failing to name the MEC as the defendant. The court dismissed the respondents' claims against Parktown High School for Girls, establishing that the fashion show was not a business or enterprise under the Act's provisions, thus exempting the state from liability. The ruling reinforces the necessity for claimants to accurately identify the proper defendants in cases involving public school-related injuries and delineates the boundaries of State liability under the South African Schools Act ([21]–[22]).

This case has significant implications for future claims against public schools, particularly concerning liability frameworks established in statutory law and reinforces the interpretation of what constitutes a "business or enterprise" in the context of educational activities.
