# FIRAC Case Note: Samsudin v De Villiers Berrange NO [2006] SCA 79

## F – Facts

This appeal involves Dato’ Samsudin bin Abu Hassan and Datin Mellaney Venessa Samsudin (the appellants) contesting a final order of sequestration granted by the Pietermaritzburg High Court ([para 1]). The case originated from an application for provisional sequestration initiated by P De Villiers Berrange, who was appointed the provisional liquidator of NRB Holdings Limited (in provisional liquidation) ([paras 2, 1]). The appellants contested the application on several grounds, including the lack of notice of the provisional order and alleged material non-disclosures by the respondent.

The facts indicate that in 1998, New Republic Bank Limited advanced approximately R32 million to NRB Holdings to acquire shares in Mitrajaya Holdings Berhad, listed on the Malaysian stock exchange. By mid-2000, NRB Holdings held a significant amount of shares, with a purported market value exceeding R124 million ([paras 3, 4]). The first appellant, in particular, was involved with Khidmas Capital, a company linked to him, during negotiations to dispose of these shares. However, in contravention of corporate governance, shares were allegedly misappropriated for personal gain without proper authorization from the company's board ([paras 5-30]).

In December 2003, proceedings initiated in Malaysia by the respondent to prevent the disposal of certain shares coincided with accusations of misappropriation, leading to allegations of insolvency against the appellants ([para 10]). The final sequestration order was thus sought on the basis that NRB Holdings had a liquidated debt against the first appellant exceeding R50 as required under s 9(1) of the Insolvency Act 24 of 1936 ([para 24]).

## I – Issues

The main issues for consideration included:
1. Whether the respondent established the necessary legal requirements under s 12(1) of the Insolvency Act for the final sequestration order, particularly proving that the appellants were insolvent based upon liquidated debts ([para 1]).
2. Whether the respondent's failure to provide notice to the appellants affected the validity of the provisional sequestration order ([paras 12, 50]).
3. The implications of alleged non-disclosures by the respondent during the ex parte application for the order ([para 14]).
4. The applicability of the acts of insolvency under s 8(a) and (d) of the Insolvency Act, particularly in light of any potential preferences among creditors ([para 36]).

## R – Rules

The judgment relied upon several statutes and case law:
1. **Insolvency Act 24 of 1936**: This Act governs the conditions for the sequestration of estates, particularly s 12(1), which allows for a final order of sequestration if certain statutory conditions are met ([para 24]).
2. **Acts of Insolvency**: Sections 8(a) and 8(d) delineate acts of insolvency that may include evading creditor liabilities and acts prejudicing creditor recovery ([para 36]).
3. **Case Law**:
   - *Ex Parte Neethling* [1951] 4 SA 331 (A), which supports the discretion of the court in dispensing with notice in urgent applications ([para 13]).
   - *Kleynhans v Van der Westhuizen NO*, which highlights that liquidated claims must have a fixed amount to qualify for sequestration ([para 35]).

## A – Application

In examining the requirements under s 12(1) of the Insolvency Act, the court analyzed the respondent's claims regarding the liquidated debts owed by the first appellant. It was determined that these debts stemmed from the alleged misappropriation of the 22,400,000 MITRAJAYA shares and unauthorized funds taken from NRB Holdings' accounts. The court found that the values related to the claims were definitively established, exceeding R50 and thus satisfying the liquidated debt requirement of s 9(1) ([paras 25-35]).

With regard to the lack of notice, the court concluded that the urgency of the matter justified the ex parte application. It highlighted prior conduct by the first appellant, suggesting an intention to dispose of assets unilaterally, which justified exclusion from notice ([paras 12, 13]). The court upheld that sufficient disclosure had been made regarding potential acts of insolvency including the improper transfer of company funds and assets, demonstrating an intention to prejudice creditors ([paras 38-46]).

The first appellant's defenses, including claims of consent from the board of directors regarding asset management and disposals, were deemed inadequate given the absence of documented authorizations and clear breaches of company law ([paras 31-34]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded by affirming the final sequestration order against the appellants, ruling that the respondent had effectively established both the requisite liquidated claims and acts of insolvency ([para 49]). The court emphasized the damage to NRB Holdings and the implications for creditor recovery, validating the respondent's decision to pursue sequestration as being in the interest of creditors. Concurring judgments reiterated the judicial discretion exercised in favor of maintaining the finality of the sequestration order, dismissing the appeal with costs ([paras 53-54]).

This case underscores the complexities surrounding insolvency proceedings, particularly evidentiary requirements, procedural disclosures, and the judicial discretion exercised in urgent financial disputes. The Court's approach not only reinforces the statutory framework of the Insolvency Act but also calls attention to the necessity for ethical management of corporate assets by fiduciaries.
