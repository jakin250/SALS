Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2009

### Case Note: National Tertiary Retirement Fund v Registrar of Pension Funds [2009] ZASCA 41

#### FIRAC Structure

---

#### F – Facts

The case involves the National Tertiary Retirement Fund (appellant), which applied to the Registrar of Pension Funds (respondent) for approval and registration of an amendment to its rules concerning benefits payable to members upon retirement. This application was predicated on a need to adjust benefits due to a substantial deficit that arose from members transferring from previous funds, the Associated Institutions Fund (AIPF) and the Temporary Employees Pension Fund (TEPF) ([paras 1–2]).

On 31 December 2003, a substantial number of members qualified for guaranteed benefits that exceeded their shares in the fund, leading to a guaranteed benefit deficit. Following an amendment introduced by the Pension Funds Second Amendment Act 39 of 2001, concerns arose regarding the minimum benefits payable to members who ceased to be part of the fund before retirement ([paras 4–7]). This situation compelled the appellant to seek to amend its rules so that the guaranteed benefits would remain payable upon retirement only if the respective employer would fund the portion of the guaranteed benefit. The financial instability rendered it impossible for the fund to independently sustain these guaranteed benefits, thus necessitating the rule amendment ([paras 8–9]).

The respondent refused the application on several grounds, including concerns that it reduced minimum benefits and placed members at the mercy of their employers ([para 10]). An appeal from this refusal was made to the Board of Appeal, which dismissed the appeal by the appellant, asserting that the amendment was inconsistent with the Pension Funds Act ([paras 11–14]). The appellant then sought a review of the Board’s decision at the High Court, which was also dismissed, prompting the appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 15–16]).

---

#### I – Issues

The central legal issues presented in this case include:

1. Whether the amendment to the pension fund rules was inconsistent with the provisions of the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956, particularly sections 12, 37A, and 14A.
2. Whether the registrar had the discretionary power to refuse the registration of the amendment based on concerns about the financial soundness and the protection of members ([paras 10, 14, 16–20]).

---

#### R – Rules

The following legal rules and precedents were pertinent to the Court's analysis:

1. **Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956**:
   - **Section 12(1)** permits a registered fund to amend any rule, provided it does not affect the rights of creditors, which must then be approved by the registrar and registered as per subsection (4).
   - **Section 37A** prohibits any reduction of benefits provided for in the rules of a registered fund unless consistent with the Act.
   - **Section 14A** mandates minimum benefits payable to members who cease to be part of the fund before retirement, ensuring these are not less than the minimum individual reserve ([paras 9, 21–22]).

2. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000**: This Act provides the principles of lawful administrative action, including a right of review if action is materially influenced by an error of law ([para 26]).

There were also judicial precedents considered, particularly the case of *Nichol and Another v Registrar of Pension Funds*, which stipulated that Boards of Appeal have substantial power to determine appeals regarding decisions made by the registrar ([para 13]).

---

#### A – Application

The Court analyzed the legislative framework and the application of the Pension Funds Act provisions to ascertain if the amendment proposed by the appellant was consistent with these statutes. 

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the registrar's decision was based on a misunderstanding of the powers conferred by section 12 of the Act. It elucidated that while the registrar possesses a duty to assess the financial soundness of amendments, the refusal to register must be consistent and justified under section 12(4), specifically evaluating whether such alterations contravened the Act ([paras 15–20]).

The Court determined that while the Left Big Hurdles amendment’s creation of a surplus was viewed as insufficient justification for financial soundness, it acknowledged that having a surplus does not inherently contravene the Act. Furthermore, the claim that the proposed changes would reduce guaranteed benefits was seen as misconstrued since a reduction of the benefit is only actionable by the rules’ dynamics and cannot merely arise from the contractual obligations of employers to fund those benefits ([paras 21–26]).

Also, the Court clarified that the amendment required clear legislative intention to prescribe prohibitions against rule amendments affecting benefits. It noted that the Act did not expressly prevent reductions via rule amendments, instead, it highlighted conditions wherein such amendments would be acceptable ([para 22]). 

Ultimately, the Court underscored that the Board of Appeal made errors in law that materially influenced its decision ([para 26]).

---

#### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the decisions of the lower courts and the Board of Appeal, concluding that the rule amendment was not inconsistent with the Pension Funds Act. The Court directed the registrar to register the amendment, emphasizing that the fund’s proposed amendments were within its rights under existing law, ensuring compliance with financial soundness criteria while balancing protections for its members. 

The implications of this ruling reaffirmed the legal standing of pension funds in their operational authority to amend rules, subject to defined legislative constraints, thereby clarifying the standards for legal interpretations concerning financial commitments of pension funds and employer responsibilities in relation to guaranteed benefits ([para 27]).
