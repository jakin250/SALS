Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note

## Case Title:   
Public Servants Association obo Olufunmilayi Itunu Ubogu v Head of Department of Health, Gauteng and Others [2017] ZACC 45

### F – Facts: 
The case arises from a dispute involving Olufunmilayi Itunu Ubogu, a Clinical Manager at the Charlotte Maxeke Johannesburg Academic Hospital, who was subject to a unilateral salary deduction by her employer, the Gauteng Department of Health, for payments allegedly made in error ([1]-[10]). Ubogu was appointed in 2006 and, following a transfer in 2010, she was mistakenly classified and overpaid according to the salary scale of a Clinical Manager: Medical instead of that of Clinical Manager: Allied. When informed of the overpayment, the Department unilaterally deducted portions of her salary without prior agreement or judicial intervention, claiming this was justified under section 38(2)(b)(i) of the Public Service Act 103 of 1994 ([4]-[8]). 

The Public Servants Association (PSA), representing Ubogu, contended that these deductions violated her constitutional right to fair administrative action and challenged the constitutionality of the provision allowing such deductions, citing both legal and equitable remedies. The Labour Court declared the section unconstitutional but initially introduced an interpretative remedy rather than a straightforward declaration of invalidity ([16]-[18]). The matter was subsequently escalated to the Constitutional Court for confirmation and resolution of the appeal ([29]).

### I – Issues:
The principal issues before the court were: 
1. Whether the Labour Court had the jurisdiction to declare section 38(2)(b)(i) of the Public Service Act unconstitutional, and whether that order required confirmation by the Constitutional Court ([29]).
2. Whether the deductions imposed by the Department constituted unlawful self-help, contravening the protections embedded in the Constitution, particularly sections 9 (equality), 23(1) (fair labor practices), and 34 (access to courts) ([29]-[30]).
3. If the provision was unconstitutional, what remedy would be appropriate ([29]).

### R – Rules:
Key legal authorities and rules considered include:
- Section 38(2)(b)(i) of the Public Service Act, which outlines the state's authority to recover overpayments without recourse to a court or agreement with the employee ([3]-[4]).
- Section 34 of the Constitution ensuring the right to have disputes resolved in a fair and public hearing ([62]).
- The principle of legality inherent in the rule of law, which mandates that individuals should not engage in self-help but rather seek judicial remedies ([66]).

The Court also elucidated the relevance of previous judgments regarding judicial interpretation and constitutional validity, specifically referencing *Khumalo v MEC for Education, KwaZulu-Natal* ([20]), *Chirwa v Transnet Ltd* ([20]), and authority on the limits of self-help in administrative law ([66]).

### A – Application:
The Constitutional Court analyzed whether the Labour Court rightly asserted jurisdiction over the declaration of invalidity. It ultimately found that the Labour Court did possess the authority to declare legislative provisions unconstitutional since it functions as a court of similar status to the High Court by virtue of the Labour Relations Act ([31]-[34]).

The Court scrutinized the application of section 38(2)(b)(i) and deemed its residual powers for unilateral deductions as an infringement of Ubogu's constitutional rights. The provision was considered to embody self-help, granting excessive discretion to the employer and allowing salary deductions without ensuring procedural fairness or legal recourse for the employee ([62]-[68]). Therefore, the Court concluded that section 38(2)(b)(i) constitutes an unjustifiable limitation on a range of constitutional rights.

The remedy fashioned by the Court emphasized not only confirming the unconstitutionality of the provision but also remitting the case back to the Labour Court to resolve substantive disputes regarding Ubogu's alleged indebtedness ([77]-[78]).

### C – Conclusion:
The Constitutional Court held that section 38(2)(b)(i) of the Public Service Act was unconstitutional, affirming procedural protections and reinforcing the principle of legality essential to maintaining due process and fairness in employer-employee relations ([80]). The order of the Labour Court was upheld, and the case was remitted for further proceedings to resolve outstanding disputes between the parties regarding the alleged overpayments. The Minister for Public Service and Administration was directed to pay the costs of the application ([80]).

This decision marked a significant affirmation of employee rights within public service employment, clarifying the protections against unilateral actions by state employers and underscoring the necessity of judicial oversight in matters of salary deductions.
