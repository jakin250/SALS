Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title: Stay At South Point Properties (Pty) Ltd v Mqulwana and Others (UCT intervening as amicus curiae) (1335/2021) [2023] ZASCA 108 (3 July 2023)**

## F – Facts

This case arises from the appeal by Stay At South Point Properties (Pty) Ltd against the decision of the Western Cape Division of the High Court, which discharged a rule nisi and dismissed the appellant’s application to evict the respondents from student accommodation managed by the appellant ([1]). The accommodation provided was associated with the Cape Peninsula University of Technology (CPUT) for students enrolled in the 2020 academic year. The respondents were students allocated to reside at New Market Junction but refused to vacate post their examination period despite notice from CPUT ([2]–[3]).

The circumstances leading to the appeal involved the appellant notifying the respondents to vacate the premises after CPUT indicated that all students must leave by a specific date post-examinations. Some students had been allowed to stay for an additional year; others were expected to move to alternative housing provided by the appellant for maintenance purposes ([3]). When the respondents resisted eviction efforts by private security guards, the appellant sought the court’s intervention on January 15, 2021 ([4]). This resulted in the appellant invoking the **rei vindicatio** to assert possession rights over the property.

The primary contention from the respondents was that the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE) applied to their occupation, rendering the eviction application procedurally defective. Conversely, the appellant argued that PIE did not apply since the residence was not a home and evicting the respondents would not render them homeless ([4]).

Despite the respondents vacating the premises shortly before the hearing, the appeal proceeded given its significance, focusing on students' rights concerning housing and the implications of PIE ([5]).

## I – Issues

The crux of the appeal revolved around the following legal issues:

1. Whether the provisions of PIE applied to the unlawful occupation of student accommodation by the respondents ([4]–[6]).
2. Whether the student accommodation provided by CPUT constituted a "home," thus attracting the protection of PIE ([11]).

The determination of these questions hinged upon interpretations of constitutional protections against evictions and the nature of temporary student housing within South Africa’s legislative framework concerning land and housing rights ([6]).

## R – Rules

The relevant legal framework includes:

1. **Section 26(3) of the Constitution**: No one may be evicted from their home without a court order that considers all relevant circumstances ([6]).
  
2. **Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE)**: The Act implements the constitutional provision regarding unlawful evictions and details the conditions under which evictions may be granted. Key provisions include:
   - **Section 2**: The Act applies to all land throughout the Republic.
   - **Section 4(7)**: Provides guidelines for the circumstances under which an unlawful occupier may be evicted ([8]).

3. **Judicial Precedents**: The court referred to **Lester v Ndlambe Municipality** and **Barnett and Others v Minister of Land Affairs**, which emphasize that PIE protects individuals from eviction only if they can establish that they would be rendered homeless ([9]–[10]).

## A – Application

The court analyzed whether the accommodation provided to the respondents amounted to a "home" within the context of PIE. It highlighted several critical points:

1. **Nature of Accommodation**: The court noted that the students came from their own homes to the residence for educational purposes and that the accommodation was characterized by its temporary nature. Hence, the respondents did not displace their pre-existing homes ([12]).

2. **Temporal Aspect**: The accommodation was intended for a limited period according to the academic calendar, indicating its temporary and transitional nature ([13]).

3. **Legislative Context**: The court underscored the policy frameworks governing student accommodation as detailed in the **Higher Education Act 101 of 1997** and related housing policies, which further confirmed that student housing should be understood as an ancillary right to education rather than an independent right to a home ([14]–[16]).

Through this reasoning, the court determined that PIE did not extend to the respondents' situation, since their occupation did not equate to occupying a permanent residence. Consequently, the appellant was entitled to use **rei vindicatio** to reclaim possession of the property ([18]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the high court erred in its refusal to grant the eviction order, declaring that PIE did not cover the respondents’ situation regarding their student accommodation. The court upheld the appeal and confirmed the appellant's right to secure the eviction based on the nature of the habitation provided ([19]). Importantly, the decision not to impose costs indicated a reluctance to burden the respondents amidst the broader educational and housing implications at stake ([20]).

This ruling elucidates the scope of PIE and reinforces the legal interpretation that temporary residential arrangements for students do not confer the same protection against evictions as a home would, thus clarifying the legal framework governing student housing within South Africa’s higher education sector. Such distinctions have significant implications for future cases involving similar circumstances.
