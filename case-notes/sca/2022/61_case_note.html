Frannero Property Investments 202 (Pty) Ltd v Clement Phuti Selapa and Others [2022] ZASCA 61

The case revolves around Frannero Property Investments 202 (Pty) Ltd (Frannero), the applicant, and a community of approximately 300 individuals (the respondents) occupying property known as Portion 35 of the Farm Waterval 306 in Rustenburg, North West Province.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 61

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The case revolves around Frannero Property Investments 202 (Pty) Ltd (Frannero), the applicant, and a community of approximately 300 individuals (the respondents) occupying property known as Portion 35 of the Farm Waterval 306 in Rustenburg, North West Province. The factual background indicates that the property was owned by Rustenburg Platinum Mines before changing hands multiple times, ultimately being purchased by Frannero’s predecessor in title and later by the applicant ([paras 1-4]).

In 2014, Frannero provided notice to the respondents, indicating the cancellation of their verbal lease agreements due to non-payment of rent and the intent to develop the land into an industrial park. The respondents challenged this cancellation, asserting their long-standing occupation of nearly 30 years and contending the applicant had no right to unilaterally terminate their leases without court intervention ([paras 4-10]).

In subsequent proceedings, the respondents were identified as protected occupiers under the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA), necessitating a lawful termination of their occupancy rights. The initial court found it lacked jurisdiction because the respondents were deemed "occupiers" under ESTA, and thus the eviction application failed ([paras 12-18]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:

  1. Whether the respondents were considered "occupiers" under ESTA.
  2. Whether the applicant had lawfully terminated the respondents’ rights to occupy the property, and thus, if the eviction based on the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE Act) should be granted ([paras 1, 19]).

R – Rules

The judicial determination rested on several legal principles and statutory provisions:

  1. The definition of "occupier" under Section 1(1)(x) of ESTA, determining lawful occupancy rights, focusing on consent, income thresholds, and the characterization of the land.
  2. Procedural obligations imposed by both ESTA and the PIE Act concerning evictions, emphasizing that evictions must consider fairness and justice ([paras 21-24]).
  3. The allocation of the burden of proof: the party invoking ESTA carries the burden to demonstrate eligibility under the Act, including absence of disqualifications such as exceeding income limits or utilizing the property for commercial purposes ([paras 24-30]).

A – Application

The court analyzed the substantial facts regarding the respondents' long-standing occupation, previously established consent by prior owners, and the commercial intent of Schreiber. Importantly, it was found that the respondents failed to satisfactorily prove their income did not exceed the prescribed threshold under ESTA. The court emphasized the legal principle that the burden of proof lay with the respondents, revealing insufficient evidence of their financial status to benefit from ESTA protections ([paras 30-33]).

Moreover, the appeal court refrained from adjudicating on the merits of the PIE Act application due to insufficient engagement in prior judgments. The court noted that neither lower court explored the practical implications of eviction or the circumstances surrounding the respondents excessively ([paras 32-34]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld part of Frannero's eviction application under the PIE Act while dismissing the appeal against certain respondents identified in Schedule A. The court determined that while the lower courts had erred in their holistic dismissal of the application, it recognized the necessity of re-evaluating conditions regarding the PIE appeal in the High Court. The decision underscores the importance of juristic clarity on ESTA applicability, burden allocation, and procedural fairness in eviction cases ([paras 35]).

The ruling reinforced the complexity of land tenure reforms in South Africa, illustrating the balance between property rights and the security of tenure for vulnerable communities, while providing a roadmap for similar cases in terms of evidentiary requirements and jurisdictional mandates. Consequently, the matter was referred back to the High Court for a comprehensive review of the PIE Act aspects, highlighting an ongoing dialogue around rights and responsibilities concerning land occupation ([paras 2-3, 35]).
