Supreme Court of Appeal

Bergkelder Bpk v Vredendal Koöp Wynmakery [2006] SCA 8 (RSA)

The first respondent, Vredendal Koöp Wynmakery, and the other respondents began using a similar Bocksbeutel bottle, prominently embossed with a crayfish on one side, which Bergkelder claimed infringed its registered trademark (TM 1977/00647).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2006

F – Facts

This case concerns a trademark dispute over the shape of a wine bottle known as the Bocksbeutel. The appellant, Die Bergkelder Beperk (hereinafter "Bergkelder"), had successfully marketed its Grünberger line of wines in such bottles since the early 1950s, particularly from the 1970 registration of its trademark. The label had substantial local market presence, reportedly selling around 2 million liters worth approximately R25 million during 2002.

The first respondent, Vredendal Koöp Wynmakery, and the other respondents began using a similar Bocksbeutel bottle, prominently embossed with a crayfish on one side, which Bergkelder claimed infringed its registered trademark (TM 1977/00647).

Initially, Bergkelder sought an interdict to prevent this alleged infringement, while the respondents countered by requesting the expungement of the trademark on the ground that it lacked the required distinctiveness. The Cape High Court, presided over by Wagley AJ, ordered the mark's expungement without addressing the infringement claim, leading to this appeal ([1]–[4]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions presented before the Supreme Court of Appeal involved:

  1. Whether the Bocksbeutel container mark possessed the requisite distinctiveness to qualify for trademark protection under the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 ([4], [14]); and
  2. Whether the use of a Bocksbeutel by the respondents constituted trademark infringement under section 34(1)(a) of the same Act, which addresses likely deception or confusion in the marketplace ([4], [14]).

R – Rules

The legal framework applicable to the case is governed primarily by the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, along with certain relevant aspects of the earlier Trade Marks Act 62 of 1963. Key provisions include:

  • Distinctiveness Requirement: According to section 16(1) of the 1963 Act, trademarks that are likely to deceive or cause confusion are not registrable. The 1993 Act similarly mandates that a mark must demonstrate capability to distinguish goods from those of other producers ([13], [14]).
  • Expungement Grounds: A registered trademark may be expunged if, under section 10(2) of the 1993 Act, it is not capable of distinguishing in the trademark sense unless it has gained this capability through usage ([14], [15]).
  • Container Marks: The evolving recognition of container shapes as potential trademarks is highlighted, wherein, despite visual distinctiveness, they must function as a badge of origin to avoid merely reflecting functional or design characteristics ([6], [8], [9]).

A – Application

In applying the relevant laws to the facts of the case, the court reasoned that:

  1. The Bocksbeutel, while having historical significance in certain wine markets (e.g., in Germany, Portugal), was not inherently distinctive enough to act as a badge of origin for Bergkelder's wines alone in the South African context. The court concluded that the general public would not interpret the Bocksbeutel as a unique identifier of Bergkelder’s wines, especially given the prevalence of similar bottles in the market ([15]).
  2. The argument posited by Bergkelder—that its exclusive use in South Africa since registration established distinctiveness—was dismissed. The court held that mere market presence does not translate into trademark distinctiveness necessary under legal guidelines. Moreover, the court noted that the Bocksbeutel did not distinguish Bergkelder’s products when compared to similar offerings from competitors ([16], [18]).
  3. The respondents' embossed crayfish did not constitute a significant visual differentiation that would avoid confusion or indicate a separate source of production among consumers ([4], [14]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court's decision, confirming that Bergkelder's trademark lacked the necessary distinctiveness to qualify for protection as a trade mark. Consequentially, the appeal was dismissed with costs due to the inadequacy of the Bocksbeutel as a mark capable of distinguishing the origin of the wine ([19]). This case illustrates the challenges faced in establishing distinctiveness for container marks in the trade mark law landscape of South Africa and emphasizes the necessity for such marks to transcend mere functional attributes in order to signify source origin effectively.

The implications of this judgment play a critical role in shaping future cases regarding the registration and enforcement of container shapes and design marks, clarifying the need for tangible consumer recognition to support claims of trademark protection.
