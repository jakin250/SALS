Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note: Primedia (Pty) Ltd t/a Primedia Instore v Radio Retail (Pty) Ltd & Others 

**Neutral Citation:** Primedia v Radio Retail (354/2011) [2012] ZASCA 32 (29 March 2012)

**Coram:** Mthiyane DP, Cloete, Cachalia, Malan, Leach JJA

**Heard:** 15 March 2012  
**Delivered:** 29 March 2012

---

## F – Facts ([paras 1–30])

This case arises from a dispute between Primedia (Pty) Ltd and three respondents—Radio Retail (Pty) Ltd, Radio Retail for Spar (Pty) Ltd, and ZaPOP (Pty) Ltd. The core issue stems from alleged unlawful competition regarding in-store media services marketing within the Spar Group franchise network.

The respondents had established exclusive marketing agreements with 330 Spar franchisees as early as 2009 ([para 3]). These agreements allowed the respondents to provide in-store promotions for media types. The conflict ignited when Mr. Riaan Labuschagne of ZaPOP accused Primedia of unlawfully interfering with these relationships by promoting its own media products within the franchise stores already associated with the respondents. Upon discovering this, Labuschagne requested Primedia to cease their activities and retract misleading communications sent to suppliers ([paras 4–6]).

Primedia, in response, asserted its standing based on prior agreements with 110 of those franchisees, contending that its media types were distinct from those offered by the respondents ([paras 12, 16]). The respondents filed for an interdict, arguing that Primedia’s actions were causing confusion, suggesting that Spar franchisees were obliged to contract with Primedia ([paras 10, 20]).

The Western Cape High Court ultimately sided with the respondents, finding that Primedia's actions did constitute unlawful competition and interference with contractual rights ([paras 14, 15]). In response, Primedia appealed the decision.

---

## I – Issues ([paras 1–30])

The primary legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether Primedia's actions constituted unlawful competition by interfering with the respondents' contractual agreements with the Spar franchisees. Specifically, the court needed to address:

1. Whether the respondents could establish a clear right and the reasonable apprehension of harm resulting from Primedia's interference with their agreements.
2. Whether Primedia had unlawfully induced any contractual breaches by promoting its products in stores already under contract with the respondents.
3. Whether the undertakings provided by Primedia to cease any false representations about the respondents obviated the need for further injunctive relief ([paras 19, 26]).

---

## R – Rules ([paras 1–30])

The court relied on several legal principles and precedents regarding competition and contractual interference:

1. **Unlawful Competition Rule**: According to *Schultz v Butt* 1986 (3) SA 667 (A), competition must remain lawful and cannot involve wrongful interference with another's rights, which constitutes an injuria that is actionable ([para 17]).
   
2. **Requirements for Interdict**: For an interdict to be granted, a party must establish: (a) a clear right; (b) a reasonable apprehension of irreparable harm; and (c) the absence of an adequate alternative remedy ([para 13]).
   
3. **Interference with Contractual Relationships**: The law allows for an interdict against someone who intentionally induces or procures another to breach a contract without justification, as established in *V & A Waterfront Properties v Helicopter & Marine Services* [2006] (1) SA 252 (SCA) ([para 18]).
   
4. **False Representations**: Past breaches cannot support an interdict unless there is a reasonable likely apprehension of future wrongful acts; as reiterated in *National Council of SPCA v Openshaw* 2008 (5) SA 339 (SCA) ([para 26]).

---

## A – Application ([paras 1–30])

Upon reviewing the case, the Supreme Court of Appeal discerned that the High Court had not adequately considered whether Primedia's actions amounted to unlawful competition, particularly regarding the assertion that it unlawfully induced breaches of the respondents' contracts.

1. **Clear Right and Imminent Harm**: The Appellate Court found that while the respondents claimed they had undergone confusion and potential harm, they failed to show actual breaches occurring as a consequence of Primedia’s actions, which undermined their assertion of a clear right ([paras 13, 19]). 

2. **Interference with Contracts**: The appellants argued Primedia’s conduct was lawful competition based on valid pre-existing agreements with 110 franchisees, thus not inducing any breaches. The Court noted that many franchisees were uncomfortable, yet discomfort alone does not predicate unlawful interference ([paras 22, 24]).

3. **False Representations**: After Primedia's promise not to disseminate false descriptions of respondents, the court concluded that any fear of future false communications was unfounded, negating the basis for an interdict ([para 26]).

The Court scrutinized the evidence provided for claims of unlawful interference and found significant gaps, particularly regarding the inconsistency in the respondents' assertions of how Primedia's actions had negatively impacted their agreements.

---

## C – Conclusion ([paras 1–30])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal set aside the High Court's order in favor of the respondents, concluding that there was insufficient evidence to demonstrate unlawful competition by Primedia. The provided evidence did not meet the necessary requirements to affirm that Primedia's actions constituted unlawful interference with the respondents' rights.

In summarizing the decision, the Court concluded:

- The respondents did not prove the requisite unlawfulness claimed against Primedia, as Primedia's conduct fell within the bounds of lawful competition by exercising rights under its prior agreements.
  
- The mandatory elements to justify an interdict were not satisfactorily established, particularly the absence of an adequate evidence base for claims of interference or false representation affecting future conduct.

Thus, the appeal by Primedia was upheld, and the respondents were ordered to bear the costs of the appeal, while Primedia would cover the costs arising from the counter-application ([para 30]). This ruling reiterates the balance between competitive practices and the protection of contractual relationships in South African law, emphasizing that mere apprehension of discomfort or confusion among parties does not equate to actionable interference in business practices.
