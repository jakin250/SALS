Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# Case Note: Mettenheimer v Zonquasdrif Vineyards CC (965/12) [2013] ZASCA 152

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts ([paras 1–24])

This case pertains to a legal dispute concerning the use of trade marks and the naming of corporations in the context of the South African wine industry. The first appellant, Mr. Alexander Anatole Theodor Mettenheimer, is the registered proprietor of the trade mark "Zonquasdrift" in class 33, which pertains to alcoholic beverages, notably wine, but not wine grapes. The second appellant is Zonquasdrift Estates (Pty) Ltd, a private company owned by Mettenheimer and his wife, which operates a farm called Zonquasdrift, located between Malmesbury and Riebeek Kasteel in the Western Cape ([para 1]).

The first respondent, Zonquasdrif Vineyards CC, was established on 4 August 2008 and produces wine grapes, which it sells to a corporate wine cellar. The respondent’s name references the historical site of a natural crossing known as "Zonquasdrift," situated approximately one kilometre away from the appellants’ farm ([para 2]). The appellants initiated proceedings in the Western Cape High Court, seeking two forms of relief: an interdict to prevent the respondent from infringing on their trade mark by using the name "Zonquasdrif" in commerce and a declaration that the respondent's name was likely to cause damage to their goodwill, demanding a name change under section 20(2)(b) of the Close Corporations Act 69 of 1984 ([para 3]).

The respondent opposed the application, asserting that it had no intention of infringing upon the appellants' mark and countered with a request for the removal of the appellants' trade mark from the register on the basis that it served merely to designate a geographical area of goods ([para 4]). The matter was adjudicated by Davis J in the court a quo, who dismissed the appellants' application and, consequently, the appeal was taken to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 3]).

### I – Issues ([paras 1, 10, 23])

The central issues in this case are:

1. **Trade Mark Infringement**: Whether the appellants established that the respondent's use of the similar mark "Zonquasdrif" in the context of wine grapes is likely to cause deception or confusion as stipulated in section 34(1)(b) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 ([paras 10, 12]).
  
2. **Name Change**: Whether the use of the corporate name Zonquasdrif Vineyards CC is calculated to cause damage to the appellants under section 20(2)(b) of the Close Corporations Act 69 of 1984, particularly given the appellants' claim that the name infringes upon their registered trade mark ([para 23]).

### R – Rules ([paras 10, 11, 20])

1. **Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993**: Section 34(1)(b) specifies that infringement occurs through the unauthorized use of a mark in trade, resulting in a likelihood of confusion or deception between the registered trade mark and the offending mark ([para 10]).

2. **Close Corporations Act 69 of 1984**: Section 20(2)(b) allows for a name change if the name is deemed to cause harm to another party as a result of the registration of a trade mark that may infringe upon that party's rights ([para 23]). This section underlines the importance of protecting established trade marks from misleading use by similar sounding business names.

3. **Common Law Principles**: The court also referred to precedents establishing that trade marks should not only denote the origin of goods but must not create confusion regarding their source, drawing from decisions such as *Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd (1984)* and *Danco Clothing (Pty) Ltd v Nu-Care Marketing Sales and Promotions (Pty) Ltd (1991)* ([paras 12, 20]).

### A – Application ([paras 11–20, 23–24])

In its examination of the case, the court undertook a thorough analysis to determine the likelihood of confusion between the two marks. The court noted that while the names "Zonquasdrift" and "Zonquasdrif" are similar, the significant difference lies in their respective goods—wine (registered under the appellants’ mark) and wine grapes (associated with the respondent) ([paras 11–12]).

The court identified that the application of section 34(1)(b) involves evaluating both the similarity between the marks and the goods or services associated with them ([para 11]). The court highlighted that the respective products target fundamentally different markets and that consumers of wine grapes (primarily wineries and co-operatives) would not confuse them with wine sold under the appellants’ mark as they are sold through different channels ([para 14]). 

Despite the appellants' argument that wine grapes are a raw material for wine and thus are closely linked, the court found that this argument conflated distinct consumer bases—those purchasing grapes versus those purchasing wine ([paras 15–16]). Furthermore, the court noted that the exclusivity of the channels of trade significantly diminishes the likelihood of confusion for the average consumer as wine grapes are not sold in retail settings alongside wine, leading to the conclusion that confusion was improbable ([para 14]).

In regard to the appellants' claim under section 20(2)(b) of the Close Corporations Act, the court concluded that since the appellants presented no substantive evidence of trade mark infringement, the argument that the respondent's name caused injury based on that premise was untenable ([para 23]).

### C – Conclusion ([para 24])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court's ruling and maintaining that the appellants had failed to establish a case of trade mark infringement, leading to the dismissal of their request for an interdict and a name change of the respondent's corporation. Furthermore, regarding the costs associated with the counter-application, the court found no grounds to alter the lower court's decision to refrain from awarding costs against the unsuccessful respondent ([paras 24]).

The implications of this judgment highlight the rigorous standards for proving trade mark infringement in cases where the involved goods or services are not only distinct but are likely marketed through different channels. This ruling underscores the significance of establishing a clear connection between marks and consumer perception, particularly in industries characterized by specialized product distribution like that of wine and associated agricultural products.
