Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: Van Deventer v Ivory Sun Trading 77 (Pty) Ltd (593/13)

### F – Facts

The appeal revolves around the right of pre-emption created by a testamentary disposition regarding the farm Dartmouth, bequeathed to Johannes, with a condition granting his brother, Christoffel Wessel Jacobus Van Deventer (the appellant), the first option to purchase the property upon its sale, at Land Bank valuation ([paras 1–4]). The respondent, Ivory Sun Trading 77 (Pty) Ltd, sought a declaratory order regarding a valid sale agreement between it and Johannes, asserting that the appellant's right of pre-emption had prescribed, a claim upheld by the lower court ([paras 2–4]).

The pertinent events include Johannes granting an option to one Dr. Cronjé to purchase the farm in September 2003, which led to a series of communications regarding the appellant’s right of pre-emption. The appellant indicated his intent to exercise this right and rejected the offer from Johannes to purchase the farm at a higher price than the option granted to Cronjé. Johannes later claimed through judicial proceedings that the condition to offer the farm at Land Bank valuation was invalid ([paras 5–11]).

In March 2007, the respondent executed the option to purchase the property as Cronjé’s nominee, notifying the appellant only in May 2008, well after the alleged transaction. This delay prompted subsequent legal discourse leading to the present appeal ([paras 10–11]).

### I – Issues

The case presents several issues for judicial determination:

1. **Right of Pre-emption**: The court needed to clarify the meaning and applicability of the right of pre-emption registered against the title deed of the property, determining when the right could be exercised and whether it had prescribed ([paras 12–14]).
   
2. **Prescriptive Period**: The primary legal question is whether the obligation of Johannes to offer the farm constituted a debt that is susceptible to prescription under the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, specifically regarding when the debt became due and whether the appellant’s right had prescribed ([paras 12–19]).

3. **Waiver and Estoppel**: The appellant's alleged waiver of his right and the claim of estoppel raised by the respondent were also contentious points requiring examination ([paras 40–47]).

### R – Rules

The court relied upon several legal principles and statutory provisions to guide its analysis:

1. **Prescription Act 68 of 1969**: The application of sections 11(d) and 12, which govern when a debt is deemed “due” and thus susceptible to prescription ([para 18]).

2. **Interpretation of Contracts**: Following the principles elucidated in *Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality*, the court emphasized a contextual reading of documents, focusing on the ordinary meaning of terms and the intent behind the agreements ([para 14]).

3. **Personal vs. Real Rights**: The judgment noted that while a right of pre-emption is considered a personal right, its registration against the title deed provides significant practical implications, influencing third-party awareness regarding the right ([paras 15–16]).

4. **Judicial Interruption of Prescription**: This involves understanding when and how the running of prescription may be interrupted by legal actions initiated by the creditor ([para 24]).

### A – Application

In assessing whether the appellant's right of pre-emption had prescribed, the court analyzed the nature of this right and when it became actionable. The interpretation favoured the view that an obligation to formally offer the property to the appellant was the trigger for the prescription period to commence; consequently, the right could not simply be asserted unilaterally by the appellant without a written offer from Johannes to buy the property ([paras 22–23], [37]).

The ruling pointed out that, without a triggering event such as a formal offer, the appellant could not have a complete cause of action, as outlined in previous case law such as *Evins v Shield Insurance Co Ltd* ([para 21]). Thus, while the appellant was informed of the option granted to Cronjé, this did not equate to a valid offer to him regarding the sale of the property, preventing the start of the prescriptive period ([paras 21–39]).

Regarding waiver, the court found that the respondent failed to demonstrate that the appellant had abandoned his pre-emption right, despite several communications asserting the necessity for a written offer. Evidence showed that the appellant maintained his stance, thus refuting the idea of waiver on his part ([paras 40–47]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the right of pre-emption had not prescribed, and the respondent failed to establish that a waiver or estoppel had occurred regarding the appellant's rights. The court rejected the lower court's findings and set aside the prior orders, reinforcing the necessity for operational legal processes to establish the right of pre-emption effectively ([paras 48]). 

The implications of this case underscore the importance of clear communication and formal processes in the exercise of pre-emptive rights, particularly how such rights interact with statutory frameworks governing prescription and the necessity of maintaining formal documentation in property transactions. The judgment establishes a precedent for future cases regarding the treatment of time-sensitive rights enshrined in testamentary dispositions and contractual arrangements.
