Constitutional Court

FIRAC - Minister of Health and Others v Treatment Action Campaign and Others

The judgment concerns applications for admission as amicus curiae lodged by Professor Mhlongo and Cotlands Baby Sanctuary in an appeal context involving the Minister of Health and others as appellants against the Treatment Action Campaign (TAC) and others as respondents.

Constitutional Court 2002 CCT 8/02

Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa
Case Number: CCT 8/02
Heard on: 2 May 2002
Decided on: 2 May 2002
Reasons Delivered on: 5 July 2002

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])

The judgment concerns applications for admission as amicus curiae lodged by Professor Mhlongo and Cotlands Baby Sanctuary in an appeal context involving the Minister of Health and others as appellants against the Treatment Action Campaign (TAC) and others as respondents. These applications arose from orders made by the High Court regarding the use of nevirapine, a drug intended to prevent mother-to-child transmission of HIV ([para 1]).

On 2 May 2002, Professor Mhlongo, the head of a medical department, sought admission to present new evidence related to the licensing withdrawal of nevirapine in the United States and to challenge the clinical trial's scientific integrity that led to nevirapine's approval ([paras 2–3]). The applicant sought amicus status based on the contention that his contributions would assist the Court by introducing new contentions regarding the efficacy and safety of nevirapine. Cotlands Baby Sanctuary, admitted as an amicus, aimed to present further evidence related to the conditions faced by HIV-positive children ([paras 7–10]).

The applications from both Mhlongo and Cotlands raised several procedural matters, particularly concerning the timing and discretion permitted in granting amicus status, as outlined in the Constitutional Court Rules and the judgment in Fose v Minister of Safety and Security ([paras 3–4]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 4–10])

  1. Timeliness and Relevance of Amicus Applications: The central issue was whether the applications for amicus curiae status brought by Professor Mhlongo and Cotlands satisfied the legal requirements related to timing and relevance. Specifically, did they comply with the provisions of Rule 9 regarding the application deadlines and the necessity of providing submissions that raised new and relevant contentions? ([paras 4–7])
  2. Admission of New Evidence: A related issue was whether Cotlands, having been admitted as an amicus, could present additional evidence concerning the living conditions of HIV-positive children in a manner that complied with Rule 30, particularly regarding whether the proposed evidence was incontrovertible and beneficial for the Court's understanding of the case ([paras 8–11]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 3–8])

The judgment referenced specific rules governing the admission of amicus curiae as set out in the Constitutional Court's procedural rules:

  • Rule 9(1) and Rule 9(4): These rules govern the admission of amicus curiae, stating that a person may be admitted based on written consent from all parties or by an application made to the Chief Justice, focusing on the relevance and helpfulness of the submissions proposed by the amicus ([para 3]).
  • Fose v Minister of Safety and Security: This precedent elucidated the principles governing amicus admissions, emphasizing that submissions must raise new contentions of value and relevance to the proceedings. The application must also be made in a timely manner ([para 3]).
  • Rule 30: This rule allows an amicus to introduce specific factual material relevant to the case, provided such facts are incontrovertible or of a nature capable of verification ([para 8]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 2–11])

The Court systematically evaluated the applications against the procedural requirements stipulated in the Rules.

  1. Professor Mhlongo's Application: The Court determined that Professor Mhlongo's request was made significantly late in the proceedings, coming on the first day of a protracted appeal. His application could not only introduce new evidence—raising an entirely new issue regarding the clinical trials of nevirapine—but could also disrupt the proceedings, necessitating postponements that would contradict the urgent need for resolution in health matters. The Court found that the introduction of such evidence was inadmissible, as it would not foster the efficient resolution of the urgent public health issue at stake ([paras 4–7]).
  2. Cotlands Baby Sanctuary's Application: Although Cotlands was already admitted as an amicus curiae, its attempts to introduce additional evidence regarding the living conditions of HIV-positive children were also scrutinized. The Court noted that while the new evidence could provide insight into the context of the pandemic, it was ultimately unnecessary for the Court's understanding of the key legal issue, which revolved around the constitutional right to health care. The existing evidence before the Court was deemed sufficient to address the urgent matters, and the potential for dispute arising from the late introduction of new evidence weighed heavily against its admissibility ([paras 8–11]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 1–11])

The Constitutional Court refused both applications for amicus curiae status on the basis of timeliness, relevance, and the disruptive potential of introducing new and contestable evidence at such a late stage in the proceedings. The refusal underscores the strict adherence to procedural rules governing the admission of amicus curiae and highlights the court's commitment to maintaining the integrity and efficiency of judicial processes, particularly in matters of urgent public health like the transmittance of HIV from mother to child ([paras 2–11]).

The judgment clarifies important standards for the admission of amicus curiae in South African constitutional law, emphasizing the need for timely applications and the relevance of the submissions proposed by amici. The ruling reinforces the principle that while the Court welcomes contributions from interested parties, these must enhance, not hinder, the judicial process in matters critical to public welfare.
