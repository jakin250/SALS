Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

## Case Note: Wakefields Real Estate (Pty) Ltd v Attree (666/10) [2011] ZASCA 160

### FIRAC Method

**F – Facts**  
The case involves Wakefields Real Estate (Pty) Ltd (the appellant), which is an estate agency claiming commission from Gavin Wayne Attree and Tracey Attree (the first and second respondents) following the sale of their property at 37 Monteith Place, Durban North, to Fiona Isobel Howard (the third respondent) ([1]). The Attrees sold their house through various estate agents but ultimately paid commission to Pam Golding Properties and Remax Estate Agents, as Remax held the sole mandate at the conclusion of the sale ([1], [12]). The core of the dispute was whether Wakefields was the "effective cause" of the sale and hence entitled to commission ([2], [12]).

Wakefields had previously assisted the Attrees in purchasing Monteith Place in 2001 and had advertised the property when the Attrees initially expressed a desire to sell ([4]). Evidence introduced by Wakefields suggested that the agency had sent a standard letter to the Attrees confirming an oral mandate to sell the house ([5]). However, the Attrees rejected this claim, and Wakefields could not produce the letter due to a system failure ([5]). Various agents, including Wakefields' agent, Phoulla Walker, had brought potential buyers to view the property before the sale took place ([6]).

In April 2005, while the Attrees were under the impression that they had the sole mandate with Remax, another estate agent, Mrs D de Marigny from Pam Golding Properties, fortuitously encountered the Howards, who had previously shown interest in Monteith Place ([10], [11]). Ultimately, De Marigny facilitated the sale by further negotiating the price and arranging for offers ([12], [18]).

The High Court ruled against Wakefields, deciding that they lacked an effective mandate and were not the effective cause of the sale, thus denying them commission ([2], [13]). The court acknowledged the high level of inter-agency competition and the complexity in attributing causation to one agent over another in property sales ([14]).

**I – Issues**  
The primary legal issue is whether Wakefields can be deemed the effective cause of the property sale to Howard, despite not concluding the sale directly ([3], [12]). The related considerations include:
- The existence of an oral or written mandate from the Attrees to Wakefields ([5], [12]).
- The nature of causation in real estate transactions and whether multiple agents can be entitled to commission from the same sale ([14]).
  
The court must address the applicability of previous case law defining the "effective cause" in real estate transactions, notably referencing Aida Real Estate Ltd v Lipschitz 1971 (3) SA 871 (W) and Basil Elk Estates (Pty) Ltd v Curzon 1990 (2) SA 1 (T) ([17], [18]).

**R – Rules**  
The legal principles governing agency and entitlement to commission hinge primarily on the concept of "effective cause." In determining this, courts often look to:
- **Causation** standards articulated in cases such as Aida, where the agent's actions must significantly contribute to the sale ([18]).
- Legal precedents from Webranchek v L K Jacobs & Co Ltd 1948 (4) SA 671 (A), where the court discusses the possibility of multiple agents contributing to a sale, thus possibly entitling more than one agent to commission ([14]).
- The distinction between "causa sine qua non" (the necessary cause) and "causa causans" (the cause that directly produced a result), emphasizing the importance of common sense in assessing causation in the context of the specifics of each case ([14]).

**A – Application**  
In its analysis, the Appeals Court carefully scrutinized whether Walker's actions in introducing Howard to Monteith Place constituted an effective cause of the eventual purchase ([19]). The court was particularly concerned with the significance of Walker’s initial connection compared to the later contributions made by other agents, particularly De Marigny, who executed the sale ([20]).

The court emphasized that Walker's introduction laid the groundwork for the purchase, even as Howard's initial financial constraints seemed to block immediate interest ([19]). Following Walker's efforts, De Marigny capitalized on the opportunity to negotiate the purchase directly with Howard and assisted in adjusting the sale price, thereby arguably playing a crucial role in facilitating the transaction ([21], [22]). However, the court noted that the sequence of events, leading to De Marigny's actions, was predicated on the introduction by Walker—pointing towards the latter's work as being materially significant in drawing Howard's attention to Monteith Place ([24]).

In contrast, the High Court's original ruling prioritized the role played by De Marigny, concluding that her action was indispensable in moving from interest to a finalized sale ([13], [16]). Nonetheless, the Appeals Court leaned towards recognizing Walker's role as the vital catalyst that initiated the eventual sale process, echoing sentiments from case law emphasizing the merit of original introducers in similar contexts ([18]).

**C – Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately found that Wakefields, through Walker's initial introduction of Howard to the property, was indeed the effective cause of the sale to Howard ([23]). The Appeals Court overturned the earlier ruling, stating that the Attrees owed commission, calculated at six percent of the sale price, to Wakefields. They reinforced that the intertwining efforts of various agents highlight a seller's responsibility in establishing clear mandates and agreements as a means to mitigate liability towards multiple agents ([23]).

In light of the findings, Wakefields' right to commission serves as a notable example of how courts navigate complex inter-agent dynamics in real estate transactions, ensuring fair compensation for agents whose efforts materially influence real estate deals. The ruling also underscores the necessity for clarity in establishing agent mandates to avoid disputes and multiplicity of claims in future property sales. The cross-appeal from Howard regarding costs was dismissed due to her unsuccessful merit on the appeal ([24]). Thus, the court adjusted the order accordingly, mandating the Attrees to pay Wakefields the owed commission with interest and full costs of suit ([25]).
