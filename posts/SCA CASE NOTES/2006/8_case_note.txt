Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2006

# FIRAC Case Note: Sackstein NO v Proudfoot SA (Pty) Limited [2006] SCA 9 (RSA)

## F – Facts
The appellant, L N Sackstein NO, serves as the liquidator of Tsumeb Corporation Limited, a firm incorporated in Namibia and registered as an external company in South Africa under section 322 of the Companies Act, 61 of 1973 ([1]). The company entered provisional liquidation in both Namibia on 29 April 1998 and South Africa on 29 July 1998, with the official insolvency proceedings becoming final in both jurisdictions ([1], [2]).

Following a scheme of arrangement sanctioned by the High Court of Namibia on 10 March 2000, the company was discharged from liquidation under Namibian law ([2]). Subsequently, on 14 April 2000, Sackstein commenced an action against Proudfoot SA (Pty) Limited to reclaim payments made to the respondent under a consultancy contract from 1 December 1997 to 29 April 1998 ([3]).

The appellant's claims comprised two statutory claims under the Insolvency Act of 1936: one for R2,637,927.00 under section 29(1) related to payments made within four months preceding the provisional liquidation, and another of the same amount under section 30(1) concerning payments made from 1 December 1997 until 28 January 1998. The appellant alternatively sought contractual relief for the total amount of R5,275,854.00 paid during the consultancy period ([4]).

The court of first instance, presided by Blieden J, dismissed all claims, leading to this appeal ([5]).

## I – Issues
The principal issues arose from the statutory claims related to the insolvency context. The crucial legal question was when a company must be considered unable to pay its debts under section 340(1) of the Companies Act for the purpose of impeaching transactions made prior to liquidation. Specifically, could the liquidator initiate proceedings based on the company's earlier insolvency, or must the inability exist at the time the action was instituted? Additionally, issues concerning the nature of the contractual claims and the requirements for restitution in such contexts were examined ([6], [7], [10]).

## R – Rules
Section 340(1) of the Companies Act stipulates: "Every disposition by a company of its property... may, if made by a company, be set aside in the event of the company being wound up and unable to pay all its debts" ([6]). This law parallels certain provisions of the Insolvency Act, especially sections 29 and 30 ([4], [6]).

The decision in *Taylor and Steyn NNO v Koekemoer* serves as a crucial precedent, holding that the inability to pay debts must be present at the time the court action is invoked, affirming its ordinary meaning and interpreting similar sections consistently within the Companies Act ([8]).

Moreover, the principles guiding contractual claims mentioned in *Willis Faber Enthoven (Pty) Limited v Receiver of Revenue* and *Extel Industrial (Pty) Limited v Crown Mills (Pty) Limited* underline the necessity of tendering restitution for unjust enrichment claims ([10], [11]).

## A – Application
In applying these rules to the case, the Supreme Court of Appeal examined the pivotal issue regarding the timing of the company’s inability to pay debts. The court agreed with the respondent's contention that the inability to pay must exist at the time the impeachment proceedings are brought. The appellant’s inability to prove the company’s insolvency at the action's institution precluded the statutory claims. The court ultimately found that the appellant conceded this point, leading to the dismissal of these statutory claims with an order for absolution from the instance ([9]).

Turning to the contractual claims, the court found that the primary claim for breach of contract failed as it did not substantiate that payments made to the respondent were due to an erroneous understanding or that the respondent had been unjustly enriched. Furthermore, no tender of restitution was made, which is a critical requirement for claims grounded in restitution following a cancellation ([10], [11]). The evidence presented suggested the consultancy yielded positive results for the company, thereby undermining the basis for claims of breach ([11]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled against the appellant on all fronts, dismissing both the statutory and contractual claims ([12]). The court ordered absolution from the instance regarding the statutory claims, highlighting the importance of proving a company's inability to pay at the time of instituting proceedings. The dismissal of claims related to contractual obligations underscored the necessity of clear evidential support for claims of breach and restitution.

This decision clarifies the interpretation of insolvency provisions in relation to the timing of claims, reinforcing the obligations placed upon liquidators in substantiating their claims under the Insolvency Act and Companies Act. The court's ruling highlighted the broader implications for liquidators in handling cross-border insolvency issues, particularly in how insolvency is interpreted across different jurisdictions ([6], [10]).
