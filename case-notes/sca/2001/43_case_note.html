Supreme Court of Appeal 2001

Case Note: RAPPORTEERBAAR

Case Number: 79 / 99
Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Date of Hearing: 19 February 2001
Date of Judgment: 26 March 2001
Judges: Olivier AR, Zulman AR, Mthiyane Wnd AR

F – Facts

The appellants in this case, Phodi Simon Khambule, Samuel Lehlohonolo Khoza, Sipho Steven Ngubane, Kenneth Vusumizi Mabizela, Thembile Mahamule, and Eric Thozamile Mhlanli, were charged with multiple offenses after a premeditated robbery at a mining facility in Welkom, South Africa. The offenses included theft of vehicles, armed robbery, attempts on life, murder, and illegal possession of firearms ([paras 1–4]).

Initially, the proceedings occurred in the Free State Provincial Division, where the appellants were found guilty on various counts, including the theft of a Volkswagen microbus and a Toyota Hilux, robbery with aggravating circumstances, attempted robbery involving a significant amount of money, the murder of K. Mqike, and several counts of attempted murder against individuals involved in the security operation ([para 1]). The trial court imposed significant prison sentences, with the first to fourth and sixth appellants receiving a cumulative sentence of 35 years and the fifth appellant (Mabizela) receiving 40 years ([paras 3–3.2]).

During the appeal, counsel for the appellants conceded that the factual basis for the convictions was not in dispute. This appeal was focused on specific legal issues surrounding the convictions and the sentences imposed ([para 4]).

I – Issues

  1. Was the appellants' conviction for the robbery of the Ruger firearm justified based on common purpose?

This inquiry centers around whether the appellants had foreseen and accepted the possibility of taking possession of the firearm and whether that possession was integral to their collective criminal plan ([paras 5, 6, 12]).

  1. Did the court adequately establish the mens rea regarding illegal possession of the firearm and ammunition based on the principle of common purpose?

Specifically, whether an intention to possess the firearm existed among the group in the context of their criminal objectives ([paras 7, 8, 9]).

  1. Was there sufficient evidence to support the convictions for attempted murder of the police officers in terms of foreseeable consequences?

This involved evaluating whether the appellants could foresee that violence towards law enforcement would likely ensue during their escape ([paras 14–20]).

R – Rules

The principles of common purpose and mens rea are pivotal in determining criminal liability in collective actions. Key legal precedents include:

  • S v Madlala [1969]: Establishes that a shared objective does not automatically encompass all actions taken unless the group had foreseen those actions and accepted them ([para 6]).
  • S v Nkosi [1998] and S v Fibi [1990]: Address the requirement that, for a group offense, all parties must possess an intention to control the firearms used, whether or not they were all armed ([para 9]).
  • S v Malinga [1963]: A case emphasizing the imputation of intention based on the shared criminal goals of the defendants, particularly in violent offenses ([para 19]).

A – Application

  1. Robbery of the Ruger Firearm:

The court concluded that the appellants' collective actions demonstrated a premeditated plan that included taking firearms from their victims, with particular regard to the Ruger used in the robbery, as their intention was to negate any resistance posed by armed security personnel ([paras 5–7]). Testimonies indicated that the use of weapons was integral to their strategy and hence the conviction was upheld.

  1. Illegal Possession of Firearms:

The court determined that the appellants' acknowledgment of the firearm’s capacity to aid in the robbery established a shared intent regarding possession. The judgment reiterated that awareness of the risks associated with their actions conferred liability for possession, highlighting that such weapons were fundamental to their modus operandi ([paras 8–12]).

  1. Attempted Murder Charges:

The appeal against the attempted murder convictions was dismissed based on evidence that the appellants had discharged weapons at pursuing police officers during their flight, implying a clear intention to threaten fatalities. The court noted that a reasonable person in their position would foresee the potential for lethal outcomes, thus justifying the charges ([paras 14–20]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the convictions and sentences of all appellants based on the robust application of the principles governing common purpose and the evidentiary link to their collective intent to commit armed robbery. The court found no basis to overturn the trial court's findings, confirming that the severity of the offenses warranted substantial imprisonment, particularly in the context of South Africa's rising violent crime rates ([paras 21–24]). The appeal of the fifth appellant was partially successful in that certain sentences were adjusted, but overall, the court reaffirmed the need for stringent penalties to deter such violent crimes ([paras 23, 26]).

This case not only illustrates the application of common purpose in group criminal activity but also emphasizes the necessity of rigorous accountability among co-defendants involved in violent offenses, reinforcing the integrity of South African criminal law.
