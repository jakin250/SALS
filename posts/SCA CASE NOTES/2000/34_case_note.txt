# Case Note: Commissioner for South African Revenue Services v Hulett Aluminium (Pty) Ltd

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Case No:** 337/98  
**Coram:** Hefer ADCJ, Nienaber, Howie, Olivier JJA, Farlam AJA  
**Date of Hearing:** 21 August 2000  
**Date of Delivery:** 1 September 2000  
**Citation:** [2000] ZASCA 112  

### F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1-3])

The appellant, the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Services (the Commissioner), issued additional assessments against Hulett Aluminium (Pty) Ltd (Hulett) regarding its income tax obligations for the years from 1983 to 1988. Hulett had claimed deductions for scientific research expenditure as regular operating costs under section 11(a) of the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962 (the Act). The Commissioner initially allowed these deductions but later contested their validity following amendments to the Act, particularly including a new section 23B inserted by the 1991 Amendment Act, which disallowed double deductions on expenditures qualifying for allowances under multiple provisions of the Act ([para 2]).

Following the 1993 amendments, the Commissioner issued additional assessments declaring that the previous allowances had been improperly granted and sought to recapture taxes ([paras 3-5]). Hulett's refusal to accept these assessments led to an appeal that initially succeeded in the Natal Income Tax Special Court but was subsequently appealed by the Commissioner to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([para 3]).

### I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 4-5])

The core issue was whether the additional assessments could be justified under section 79(1) of the Act, which mandates the Commissioner to issue additional assessments when a taxpayer has not been assessed on taxable amounts. A significant aspect of the case concerned the interpretation of the third proviso of section 79(1), which stipulates that additional assessments cannot be raised if the amounts were not assessed due to prevailing practices at the time of the initial assessment ([para 5]).

Hulett contended that, based on the prevailing practice established by an earlier Special Court ruling and subsequent administrative circulars, their deductions were permissible, therefore, precluding the Commissioner from retrospectively denying them ([para 6]).

### R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 5-6])

The Court considered the provisions of the Income Tax Act, particularly section 79(1) and the third proviso, alongside the interpretation of “prevailing practice” as outlined in *Commissioner for Inland Revenue v SA Mutual Unit Trust Management Co Ltd* 1990(4) SA 529 (A). The court underscored that the burden of proving the existence of a general practice lies with the taxpayer ([para 5]).

Additionally, significant consideration was given to the 1987 judgment of the Cape Income Tax Special Court that allowed double deductions for scientific research expenditure, which the Commissioner acknowledged influenced subsequent administrative practice ([paras 10-12]).

### A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 6-13])

The Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed the historical context provided by Mr Coetzee, the Commissioner’s director, who testified regarding the bureaucratic decision-making process after the 1987 judgment affirming double deductions. The court found that the administrative circular sent post-judgment effectively communicated that such deductions would be allowed pending legislative amendments ([paras 10-12]).

The court held that the subsequent administration’s acknowledgment of this practice, coupled with the absence of immediate legislative correction until 1993, constituted a prevailing practice that Hulett relied upon in their assessments ([para 13]). Consequently, even if double deductions were ultimately deemed incorrect due to retroactivity from the 1991 amendments, they had been granted in accordance with prevailing practices, thereby nullifying the application of section 79(1) for additional assessments ([para 14]).

### C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 7-14])

The court dismissed the Commissioner’s appeal, concluding that the section 79(1) additional assessments could not stand given that the deductions had been valid according to prevailing practices at the time they were claimed ([para 14]). The Court's decision emphasized the importance of administrative consistency and taxpayer reliance on established practice. 

As a result, this case reinforces the principles surrounding the interpretation of tax law and the significance of administrative circulars and decisions in determining how tax regulations are applied operationally. This ruling not only has implications for similar cases involving retrospective tax assessments but also sets a precedent concerning the seriousness with which taxpayers' reliance on administrative guidance is to be treated in assessing their tax obligations ([para 14]).

In summary, the comprehensive ruling of the Supreme Court of Appeal underscored that an established administrative practice must be honored in tax assessments and highlighted the critical role of interpretive consistency in tax law.
