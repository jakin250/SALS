# Case Note: S Smith v Road Accident Fund [2006] SCA 12 (RSA) – FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The case at hand involves the appellant, Sias Smith, who was sued by Mary Pedro for damages amounting to R2,563,728.20. Pedro sustained injuries while being a fare-paying passenger in a vehicle negligently driven by Smith ([1]–[2]). In his defense, Smith claimed that the accident was caused, at least in part, by two unidentified vehicles, a Sentra and a Mazda, whose details he did not know ([2]). 

Subsequently, Smith served a third-party notice to the Road Accident Fund (RAF) under Rule 13(1), alleging that the RAF was obliged to compensate Pedro for her damages as per section 17(1) of the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 ([3]). Smith sought to establish that both he and the RAF were joint wrongdoers, aiming to limit his liability to the first R25,000 of damages as defined by the Act. This premise was contested by the RAF, leading to a preliminary hearing in which the High Court of the Eastern Cape dismissed Smith's claims against the RAF, siding with the Fund that he could not join it as a joint wrongdoer in this matter ([4]).

The appellant appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal, challenging the lower court's ruling ([4]).

### I – Issues
The principal legal questions before the Supreme Court of Appeal were:
1. Whether a negligent driver, who is being sued by a third party, can classify the Road Accident Fund as a joint wrongdoer under the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 and the Apportionment of Damages Act 34 of 1956 ([5]).
2. Whether the Fund is liable to indemnify the negligent driver in cases involving unidentified vehicles ([7]–[10]).

### R – Rules
The Court primarily relied upon the following statutory provisions:
1. **Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996**, particularly sections 17(1)(b) and 18(1)(b), which cover the Fund's obligations to compensate victims of road accidents where the responsible party's identity is unknown ([1], [5]).
2. **Apportionment of Damages Act 34 of 1956**, specifically section 2(1), which outlines the rights of defendants to claim apportionment of damages when multiple parties are liable for the same injury ([9]).

The ruling emphasized that the Fund functions as a substitute defendant for compensation of victims, and its liability only arises when strict compliance with applicable regulations is demonstrated ([5]).

### A – Application
The Supreme Court dissected the appellant's arguments asserting a right to claim indemnification against the RAF. The Court highlighted that Smith was not a victim of the vehicle’s negligence but rather the tortfeasor himself, which disqualified him from claiming benefits from the Fund ([9], [10]). The Court also made clear that the provisions of the Road Accident Fund Act and the Apportionment of Damages Act were strictly delineated, noting that the Fund is not categorically a joint wrongdoer if the owner or driver is unidentified ([9]).

Despite counsel's efforts to argue implications supporting Smith's claim for indemnification, the Court found these arguments contrived and unsupported by legislative intent. The judgment underscored that the legislative framework aims to protect the interests of accident victims rather than negligent per se drivers ([9], [10]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Smith's appeal, affirming that the Road Accident Fund is not liable to indemnify a negligent driver against claims made by a third party ([11]). The ruling reiterates the legislative purpose of providing compensation solely for victims and clarifies that the Appellant’s request to classify the RAF as a joint wrongdoer does not align with the statutory framework. The judgment reinforces the principle that negligent drivers lack a moral claim for indemnity against a Fund which exists primarily to cover losses incurred by victims of road accidents. Thus, the appeal was dismissed with costs, establishing a significant precedent regarding the limitations of recovery against the RAF in scenarios involving negligent drivers ([11]).
