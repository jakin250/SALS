Supreme Court of Appeal

Heppell v The Law Society of the Northern Provinces (1096/16) [2017] ZASCA 119 (22 September 2017)

The appellant, Warrick Lesley Visser Heppell, is an attorney who was suspended by the Gauteng Division of the High Court for six months due to serious professional conduct issues following his voluntary surrender of his estate.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 119 (22 September 2017)

F – Facts

The appellant, Warrick Lesley Visser Heppell, is an attorney who was suspended by the Gauteng Division of the High Court for six months due to serious professional conduct issues following his voluntary surrender of his estate. He had previously been admitted as an attorney in 1991 and ceased practicing in 2007, later attempting to resume practice in 2010. However, his financial difficulties led him to seek voluntary surrender of his estate, which was granted on 11 January 2012 ([1]–[3]).

Following his sequestration, the Law Society of the Northern Provinces initiated an investigation into his fitness to practice, based on the non-disclosure of material facts during his application for sequestration. An inquiry conducted by the Investigative Committee revealed that Heppell had grossly misrepresented his financial situation, failing to accurately disclose liabilities that totaled around R20 million, while claiming under oath that they were only R146,000. Moreover, he failed to mention his status as a practicing attorney and misrepresented his income and marital status ([4]–[8]).

The High Court found that Heppell's conduct amounted to gross negligence and deemed him unfit to practice without sanction. The disciplinary processes culminated in the Law Society's application to have him suspended or struck off the roll of attorneys ([8]–[9]). Throughout the proceedings, Heppell maintained that he was not an unfit attorney, attributing his missteps to reliance on the advice of his attorney ([9]–[10]).

I – Issues

The principal legal questions before the court included:

  1. Whether the High Court was correct in finding that Heppell was not a fit and proper person to continue practicing as an attorney due to his conduct surrounding the voluntary surrender of his estate.
  2. If so, whether the sanction of a six-month suspension was appropriate given the nature of his misconduct ([11]).

R – Rules

The legal framework applicable to this case is primarily drawn from the Attorneys Act 53 of 1979, particularly section 22(1)(e), which stipulates that an attorney's name may be removed or they may be suspended if they cannot demonstrate they are fit to practice following the sequestration of their estate ([5]). The case law elaborates that the court must assess three key considerations:

  1. Establishing if the alleged conduct occurred based on the balance of probabilities.
  2. Determining the fitness of the attorney to continue practicing as per a value judgment standard.
  3. Assessing the appropriate sanction, whether it be suspension or striking from the roll, balancing the need for public protection and the rehabilitative interests of the attorney ([11]).

Key precedents cited included Jasat v Natal Law Society (2000) and Malan v The Law Society of the Northern Provinces (2009), which emphasize procedural standards in evaluating attorney conduct and sanctions ([11], [12]).

A – Application

The court's first inquiry was whether the conduct alleged against Heppell was established, noting that his non-disclosure of critical financial liabilities significantly impacted the integrity of the voluntary surrender process. The court found that Heppell was indeed grossly negligent, and his actions represented a clear deviation from the expected professional conduct of an attorney, particularly regarding the obligation to disclose all relevant material facts to a court ([11]–[12]).

The next stage of the inquiry required the court to consider whether Heppell was a fit and proper person. Given the serious misrepresentations and failures to disclose pertinent information, the court concluded he could not satisfy the threshold of being a fit attorney. This raised the question of public protection, which the court emphasized was of paramount importance ([13], [16]).

While acknowledging mitigating factors such as Heppell's previous professional record and claims of reliance on legal advice, the judgment underscored that these did not absolve him of accountability. It was communicated that being dishonest, particularly in the context of legal obligations, raised concerns about his future conduct as an attorney ([22], [24]). The period of suspension was deemed appropriate as it provided Heppell with an opportunity for rehabilitation, allowing him time to reflect on his ethical obligations ([16]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Heppell's appeal, affirming the finding that he was not a fit and proper person to practice law. The court held that the lower court did not exercise its discretion capriciously and that the six-month suspension was a fitting response to his conduct, necessary for public protection and professional accountability ([18], [24–25]). The judgment illustrates the serious implications of professional misconduct in the legal field and reiterates the courts' commitment to uphold the integrity of legal practitioners. The case reaffirmed the stringent standards expected from attorneys, particularly concerning transparency and honesty in all dealings related to their practice.
