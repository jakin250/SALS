# Case Note: The Baphiring Community v Tshwaranani Projects CC [2013] ZASCA 99

## FIRAC Method Case Note

### F – Facts

The case concerns the appeal filed by the Baphiring Community and associated appellants against Tshwaranani Projects CC and several other respondents regarding a land restitution claim under the **Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994**. The land in question, colloquially known as ‘old Mabaalstad’, was expropriated by racially discriminatory laws in 1971 from the Baphiring Community, who were subsequently relocated to the compensatory land known as ‘new Mabaalstad’ ([paras 1–2]).

The claim for restoration of portions of the land was lodged in 1998 and has a complex history, involving multiple decisions from the Land Claims Court (LCC). The LCC previously upheld the competence of the communal property association to lodge the claim, confirmed that the dispossessed rights included ownership and mineral rights, and found that the compensation received was inadequate ([paras 3–5]). The LCC ultimately issued a non-restoration order, claiming that it was not feasible to restore the land to the Baphiring Community due to financial considerations ([para 5]).

The appeal was supported by the Regional Land Claims Commissioner and the Minister for Rural Development and Land Reform, highlighting a significant state interest in the restoration of land to the Baphiring Community ([paras 6–8]).

### I – Issues

The main legal question was whether the LCC erroneously issued a non-restoration order in the absence of sufficient material evidence concerning the feasibility of restoring the land to the Baphiring Community. Specifically, the court needed to assess the state's duty to provide adequate evidence regarding the economic and social viability of land restoration, and whether the LCC's failure to consider such evidence constituted a material irregularity ([paras 9–10]).

### R – Rules

The applicable legal framework is primarily derived from the **Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994**, particularly the provisions regarding the feasibility and nature of land restoration. Established precedents, including the determination of what constitutes "just and equitable" compensation under section 2(2) of the Act, played a crucial role in the case ([paras 4, 10]). The court considered several factors outlined by previous cases, including the nature of the land, changes in the environment, land use planning measures, and the financial implications of restoration ([paras 11–12]).

The courts have emphasized the importance of restoration as a primary remedy in land restitution claims unless impracticalities arise due to public interest concerns. The **Constitutional Court** has stated that non-restoration orders must be substantiated by factual evidence presented by the public body ([paras 15–16]).

### A – Application

In its analysis, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that the LCC's decision lacked adequate evidence regarding the financial demands and practical considerations of restoring the land to the claimants. The LCC had improperly weighed financial constraints as a basis for determining the feasibility of restoration without conducting a comprehensive feasibility study, leading to a presumption against restoration due to presumed costs ([paras 14–19]).

The appellate court noted that the responsibility for leading evidence lay with the state and that the LCC had erred in concluding non-restoration without robust material evidence from the state about the feasibility of land restoration. Such absence of evidence was a material irregularity that undermined the legitimacy of the non-restoration order. Furthermore, the court identified essential factors that needed consideration, such as the current occupants of the land, potential for social disruption, and the implications of compensation for landowners if restoration were to occur ([paras 18–22]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the appeal should be upheld, setting aside the LCC’s order dated 19 January 2010, and remitting the matter to the LCC to reevaluate the feasibility of restoration in accordance with the evidence that the state is required to provide. The court emphasized that the analysis of feasibility must encompass a comprehensive assessment of all relevant factors that could influence the restoration and resettlement process, ensuring that the state fulfills its obligation to lead the necessary evidence to support its position ([para 4]).

The ruling establishes critical implications for future land restitution claims, reinforcing the need for public bodies, particularly the state, to actively engage in presenting evidence that justifies non-restoration decisions. The case reinforces the principle that financial considerations cannot solely dictate the feasibility of land restitution without thorough examination and evidence ([paras 19–22]). This decision marks an important step in the enhancement of the restorative justice framework concerning land restitution in South Africa.
