Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: Integrity Forensic Solutions CC v Amajuba District Municipality (662/2022) [2023] ZASCA 124

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

The appellate case of *Integrity Forensic Solutions CC v Amajuba District Municipality* revolves around a disputed contractual agreement for litigation support services in response to a forensic audit identifying procurement irregularities in a municipal context ([1]–[2]). On 10 August 2015, Amajuba District Municipality (Amajuba) initially engaged Integrity Forensic Solutions CC (IFS) to conduct a forensic investigation into its procurement processes after Thabani Zulu Incorporated (Thabani Zulu), its auditors, subcontracted the audit to IFS due to serious irregularities identified in a preliminary report ([2]–[3]).

After completing the forensic investigation, IFS presented an audit report outlining the irregularities in August 2016. Following this, on 17 May 2018, IFS initiated a summons against Amajuba for two claims: the first claim, related to the forensic investigation agreement, sought R276,297.51, while the second, stemming from an alleged second agreement for litigation support services in connection with the National Prosecuting Authority (NPA) and the Directorate of Priority Crimes (DPC), totaled R754,557.15 ([4]–[5]). Amajuba contended that no binding agreement had been concluded under various legal grounds, including the authority of Amajuba's municipal manager, Mr Afrika, to finalize the alleged agreement ([5]).

The trial court dismissed IFS's claim, establishing that no valid agreement existed which could be enforced, a finding that was upheld by the full court of the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court ([7]). The matter reached the Supreme Court of Appeal following a granted petition for special leave to appeal ([8]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issue was whether IFS successfully proved that a valid and enforceable agreement for the provision of litigation support services was concluded between the parties ([6]–[7]). Additionally, the case implicated questions about the jurisdictional requirements necessary for the Supreme Court of Appeal to hear the case, particularly regarding the “special circumstances” needed to grant special leave to appeal as outlined in section 16(1)(b) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 ([8]).

### R – Rules

The relevant legal standard is derived from the principles underlying the formation of contracts within South African contractual law, particularly focusing on consensus between parties, the authority of representatives, and the requisite certainty in contract terms. According to the *Absa Bank v Snyman* ruling, the SCA can only hear appeals if special leave has been granted by a high court or the SCA itself under specific statutory provisions ([8]).

In assessing whether a contract was concluded, the court referred to the necessity for clear terms outlining the scope, duration, and obligations of each party, drawing on precedents such as *Shepherd Real Estate Investments (Pty) Ltd v Roux Le Roux Motors CC* ([14]–[15]). The court also relied on constitutional provisions, particularly Section 217 of the Constitution, concerning procurement processes in public entities ([15]).

### A – Application

The court undertook a thorough examination of the evidence and testimonies presented during the trial. Mr Afrika, representing Amajuba, testified that while there was a desire for further discussions regarding litigation support, no definitive contract was formed, resulting in an “unenforceable agreement to agree” ([12]–[14]). IFS’s claims of an established tripartite agreement were also weakened when counsel acknowledged that the DPC had no obligations under the terms stipulated and that the letter of mandate outlined work without concrete agreements on necessary specifics like costs or duration ([11]–[12]).

Ultimately, the court confirmed the factual findings of the lower courts, reaffirming that no valid, binding agreement had been established due to indeterminate terms and lack of requisite authorizations ([15]). The failure to prove a contract translated into an inability to pursue equitable relief under Section 172 of the Constitution, as IFS's argument hinged on the existence of an enforceable contract to set aside ([15]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appeal should be struck off the roll with costs, including the costs of two counsel, as IFS did not meet the threshold for special leave to appeal with reasonable prospects of success or demonstrate special circumstances requiring further judicial interpretation of the law ([17]–[18]). This ruling underscores the critical importance of ensuring clear consensus and adherence to statutory procurement processes in public contracts, highlighting the legal framework's emphasis on enforceability and accountability in public service procurements. 

Thus, the case not only delineates the contours of enforceable agreements but also reinforces the judicial scrutiny required in matters involving public procurement and administrative law, establishing a precedent for future cases concerning municipal contracts and the necessity for explicit consensus in contractual obligations.
