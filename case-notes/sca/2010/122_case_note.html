Gamede v The State (161/10) [2010] ZASCA 122

Supreme Court of Appeal 2010 [2010] ZASCA 122

F – Facts

The appellants, Nomvula Gamede and Tanjane Justice Makunga, were convicted by the regional court in Durban for violations of section 5(b) of the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 1992 (Act 140 of 1992). The conviction stemmed from an incident in June 2004, where they were found to be involved in the manufacture and dealing of methaqualone, commonly known as mandrax, amounting to 556 kilograms with a street value estimated at R50 million ([1]).

Following their conviction, both appellants received sentences of 20 years imprisonment. They subsequently appealed to the KwaZulu-Natal High Court, which dismissed the appeal regarding conviction but permitted them to appeal the sentences ([2]).

Evidence presented at trial included the discovery of large quantities of a powdered substance resembling mandrax, various chemical supplies, and illicit manufacturing equipment at two farms. Appellants were linked to these locations through a series of police observations. The second appellant, Makunga, was arrested at Spitskop Farm with anthranilic acid, a substance used in the manufacture of mandrax. Meanwhile, Gamede was apprehended after leading police on a chase following her suspicious activity at Mange Farm, where police discovered incriminating items ([4]-[10]).

I – Issues

The primary issues presented in the appeal were:

  1. Whether the respondent proved beyond a reasonable doubt that mandrax was being manufactured at the accused's premises and whether the appellants were dealing in the substance as defined in the Act ([3]).
  2. The appropriateness of the sentences imposed on the appellants, particularly the alleged excessiveness of the sentences ([15]).

R – Rules

The key legal provisions relevant to this case include:

  • Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 1992: Section 1 provides a comprehensive definition of “dealing” in drugs, encompassing activities such as manufacture, supply, and distribution of controlled substances ([12]).
  • Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997: This Act outlines the minimum sentences for different drug-related offenses, particularly emphasizing mandatory minimum sentences for serious drug offenses ([15]).
  • Precedent set in S v Scott-Crossley 2008 (1) SACR 223 SCA, which discusses the principles guiding sentencing for drug-related offenses ([15]).

A – Application

The court affirmed the lower court's factual findings regarding the operations at the farms. The evidence indicated that both appellants were actively involved in illegal drug manufacturing. The first appellant’s actions during her arrest suggested she was complicit in obstructing justice by attempting to dispose of incriminating evidence. The court noted that her knowledge of the operation could be inferred from the scale of the manufacturing process, thus justifying her conviction ([13]).

For the second appellant, the evidence against him was compelling, with anthranilic acid found in his vehicle and testimony linking him to activities at the manufacturing sites. His subsequent actions did not exculpate him from culpability, reinforcing the conclusion that he was knowingly participating in drug offenses ([14]).

Regarding sentencing, the court acknowledged that while the first appellant’s role was limited to assisting in the disposal of evidence, the 20-year sentence was excessive for such involvement. It was decided that a lesser sentence of 5 years imprisonment would be appropriate for her contribution to the crime ([15]). In contrast, the second appellant was deemed to have directly engaged in drug operations, warranting a sentence aligned with the minimum encased in existing statutory frameworks, thus reducing his sentence to 15 years, reflective of both his culpability and clean prior record ([15]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the convictions of both appellants, affirming that the respondent successfully proved the elements of the crime charged, namely the dealing in and manufacture of mandrax. However, the court overturned their original sentences. The first appellant's sentence was revised to 5 years, while the second appellant’s was reduced to 15 years imprisonment, reflecting a more equitable approach to sentencing given their respective roles in the offenses ([16]).

This case underscores the court’s dedication to ensuring that sentences reflect both the gravity of the crime and the specific involvement of the accused, in line with established statutory mandates. The decision also reinforces the principle that convictions must be firmly grounded in evidence that convincingly establishes guilt beyond reasonable doubt.
