Source Folder: 2016

# Case Note: Tshivhulana Royal Family v Netshivhulana [2016] ZACC 47

### FIRAC Analysis

#### F – Facts

The dispute stems from the succession of leadership in the Tshivhulana community following the death of Mr Rasilingwani Piet Netshivhulana in 1976, who was succeeded by his son, Mr Mugoidwa Mutheiwana Wilson Netshivhulana, until his passing in 1992 ([paras 2–3]). Upon Mr Mugoidwa's death, the Tshivhulana Royal Family appointed Mr Davhana Elias Mulaudzi as the acting headman, arguing that the deceased left no direct heir due to his lack of a “dzekiso” wife, a requisite for succession ([paras 3–4]).

However, Mr Nditsheni Norman Netshivhulana, the deceased's firstborn son, contended that he was the rightful successor and claimed that he was deemed a minor because he was unmarried at the time of the succession meeting. The Tshivhulana Royal Family disputed this, asserting his illegitimacy based on maternal lineage ([paras 4–5]). By 2014, disagreements continued, with the Premier intervening by officially recognizing Mr Netshivhulana as headman, prompting the Tshivhulana Royal Family to seek a review of this decision in the High Court ([paras 7–9]).

Procedural history began with the High Court finding that the family had failed to exhaust internal remedies prescribed by section 21 of the Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act (Framework Act) before seeking judicial review, which led to an appeal to the Constitutional Court after the High Court denied the family leave to appeal ([paras 11–15]).

#### I – Issues

This case presents several legal issues ([para 17]):
1. Whether the late filing of the application should be condoned.
2. Whether leave to appeal should be granted.
3. Whether section 21 of the Framework Act is applicable to the dispute.
4. If applicable, whether exceptional circumstances exist to exempt the Tshivhulana Royal Family from exhausting internal remedies.
5. Costs related to the matter.

#### R – Rules

The relevant legislative framework includes:
- **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: This Act mandates the exhaustion of internal remedies prior to judicial review, unless exceptional circumstances apply ([paras 19–20]).
- **Traditional Leadership and Governance Framework Act 41 of 2003**: Specifically, section 21 provides for resolving disputes within traditional communities before approaching the courts ([para 20]).
- **Limpopo Traditional Leadership and Institutions Act 6 of 2005**: Governs the recognition and removal of headmen ([para 18]).

Precedent cases, such as *Koyabe v Minister for Home Affairs* and *Mamogale v Premier, North West*, highlight the importance of exhausting internal remedies within traditional frameworks before escalation to a higher court ([para 14]).

#### A – Application

The Constitutional Court determined that the High Court’s reading of section 21 was erroneous, as the real dispute was between the Tshivhulana Royal Family and the Premier—rather than among the traditional communities themselves. The Premier’s decisions constitute administrative actions subject to review under PAJA and do not classify as internal disputes as envisioned by section 21 of the Framework Act, which seeks to regulate resolutions among traditional communities ([paras 28–38]).

Moreover, the court noted that Section 12 of the Framework Act gives the Premier exclusive powers of recognition but does not provide for a review of his own decisions, indicating that there would be no further internal recourse available after a decision has been made. Consequently, there was no obligation for the Tshivhulana Royal Family to exhaust remedy processes under section 21 ([paras 40–44]).

The judgment's analytical approach emphasized that the nature of a dispute must align with statutory definitions and intentions, leading to the conclusion that the High Court should have dismissed the exhaustion of remedies objection and proceeded to address the merits of the case ([paras 48]).

#### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court upheld the appeal, ruling that the exhaustion of internal remedies is not a requisite in a dispute challenging the recognition of a headman by the Premier under PAJA. The order of the High Court was set aside, with the matter remitted for consideration on its merits, ensuring judicial clarity in succession disputes within traditional leadership structures ([paras 49–50]). 

The implications of this ruling reinforce the autonomy of traditional leadership disputes from general administrative review processes and clarify the scope of internal remedy requirements relating specifically to traditional governance ([para 27]). This judgment is significant for similar disputes in South Africa, drawing a clear line between internal community matters and formal administrative actions by state executives.
