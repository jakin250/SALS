Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# Case Note: Summermania Eleven (Pty) Ltd v Hattingh N O (316/2022) [2024] ZASCA 42

## F - Facts
The case arose from a contractual dispute related to a sale agreement executed between Summermania Eleven (Pty) Ltd (the appellant) and the Billy Hattingh Trust (the respondent) on February 3, 2015, regarding a game farm in Graaff-Reinet, Eastern Cape [2]. According to the agreement, the Trust warranted that there would be no material change in the composition and numbers of game from December 2014 until the transfer of property [2]. The transfer was registered on July 10, 2015, much later than initially planned, with the appellant taking occupation on July 15, 2015 [3].

A year later, on July 11, 2016, Summermania initiated legal proceedings alleging that the Trust had breached its warranty regarding the state of the game on the farm, particularly noting a significant shortfall in the number of Kudus reported in the sale agreement [4]. Summermania claimed a reduction of 150 Kudus, among other game species. The Trust denied these allegations, and the dispute focused largely on the expert evidence of Mr. Benjamin Van Niekerk, who conducted a game count that became a pivotal point in the litigation [7].

The trial court admitted Van Niekerk's affidavit into evidence since he was unable to testify orally due to health issues. Based on this evidence, the trial court found in favor of Summermania, declaring that the Trust had breached the sale agreement [7]. However, on appeal, the Eastern Cape Division's full court overturned this decision, arguing that the trial court had failed to properly evaluate the expert evidence and that the affidavit of Van Niekerk was inadequate [7-8].

## I - Issues
The primary legal issues for determination were:
1. Whether the trial court properly evaluated the expert evidence concerning the breach of warranty by the Trust [7-8].
2. Whether the appellant discharged the burden of proof concerning the alleged deficiencies in game numbers as stipulated in the warranty [8-10].
3. The admissibility and probative value of the affidavit provided by Mr. Van Niekerk, considering his inability to undergo cross-examination due to health constraints [15-17].

## R - Rules
The court relied on several legal principles and case law concerning evidence and the evaluation of expert testimony in civil trials:
1. The obligation to prove a breach of warranty lies with the party alleging the breach. In this case, Summermania was required to demonstrate that the Trust failed to fulfill its obligations concerning the number of game on the property [10].
2. Uniform Court Rule 38(2) dictates that evidence in trials is to be given orally, but allows for the submission of affidavits under specific conditions [15]. The court emphasized that even if affidavits were admitted, their reliability and credibility must still be scrutinized against other pieces of evidence [16].
3. Previous case law highlighted the importance of cross-examination in challenging the reliability of expert testimony, particularly regarding whether the evidence presented could sufficiently support the assertions made by the parties [17].

## A - Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal carefully analyzed the lower court's evaluation of evidence, particularly regarding Mr. Van Niekerk's affidavit. While his expertise in game counting was established, the nature of his health at the time of the trial and the challenges related to his affidavit rather outweighed his testimony's probative value. The court noted:
- Van Niekerk conducted the count under conditions that raised questions about the accuracy and reliability of his findings, particularly when the methodology employed departed from recognized practices in game counting [26].
- The Trust's testimony, particularly from Mr. William Henry Hattingh, countered Van Niekerk's findings and raised credible doubts about the efficacy of the aerial counting method [23-24].
- The issues surrounding Van Niekerk's affidavit were compounded by concerns over potential plagiarism. The court found that unacknowledged borrowing from academic sources misleadingly bolstered the credibility of his assertions, thus diminishing the weight such evidence carried [29-30].

Ultimately, the court concluded that the evidence presented by Summermania, largely hinging on Van Niekerk's findings, failed to adequately discharge the burden of proof necessary to establish that the Trust was in breach of the warranty [31].

## C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, acknowledging the full court's earlier judgment was correct in its analysis of the evidence [32]. The court’s decision reinforced the principle of requiring reliable and credible evidence to satisfy the burden of proof in breach of contract claims, particularly within the context of warranties. The implications of this case emphasize the necessity for parties to meticulously validate and prepare expert evidence while securing effective means of presenting it in court, especially when evaluating claims involving substantial monetary losses due to alleged breaches of contractual obligations. The judgment serves as a precedent in both contract and civil procedure law regarding the admissibility and evaluation of expert affidavit evidence in judicial proceedings.
