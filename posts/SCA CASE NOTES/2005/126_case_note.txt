# Case Note: David Leibowitz t/a Lee Finance v A T Mhlana and Others

**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Case No**: 422/2004  
**Judgment Delivered**: 01 December 2005  
**Coram**: Mpati DP, Streicher, Lewis, Van Heerden, Jafta JJA  

---

**FIRAC Case Note**

### F – Facts

The case revolves around a jurisdictional dispute arising from an application initiated by the first to eighth respondents (hereinafter “the respondents”) against the appellant, David Leibowitz t/a Lee Finance, and several insurance companies in the Transkei High Court ([1]). The respondents, teachers residing in the Transkei, applied for loans in Durban from the appellant, who was engaged in a moneylending business there, and provided insurance policies as security for these loans ([2]). 

In January 2001, the respondents sought an urgent interdict against the appellant and the insurance companies, aiming to prevent the cession or utilisation of certain policies, which they claimed had never been ceded to the appellant ([3]). The appellant objected to the court's jurisdiction, arguing that he had no legal connection to the Transkei ([4]). The initial ruling by Maya J indicated a lack of jurisdiction, leading to a dismissal of the application with costs ([1]). 

However, upon appeal, the full court reversed this decision, determining that jurisdiction did exist, and referred the matter back to the court of first instance for merit consideration ([1]). The appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal was subsequently lodged by the appellant against this latter order ([1]).

### I – Issues

The core issue in this case pertains to the jurisdiction of the Transkei High Court, specifically the interpretation of sections 19(1)(a) and (b) of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959 ([6]). The court considered:

1. Whether the Transkei High Court had jurisdiction over the appellant, given that he was based in Durban and the transactions occurred there.
2. Whether the insurance companies constituted sufficient grounds for jurisdiction based on their operational presence ([5]).

### R – Rules

The legal framework involves an interpretation of section 19(1)(a) and (b) of the Supreme Court Act, which delineates the jurisdictional authority of provincial or local divisions concerning actions arising within their respective areas. Specifically:

- **Section 19(1)(a)** affirms jurisdiction over persons or causes arising within the local jurisdiction.
- **Section 19(1)(b)** extends jurisdiction over individuals outside the jurisdiction if they are joined in a matter that falls under the court’s authority ([6]).

The interpretation of the term "cause" as posited in *Gulf Oil Corporation v Rembrandt Fabrikante en Handelaars (Edms) Bpk* [1963] serves as a foundational authority explicitly stating that “a cause” refers to legal proceedings ([7]). 

Additionally, the court examined precedents like *Bisonboard Ltd v K Braun Woodworking Machinery (Pty) Ltd* [1991] and *Federated Insurance Co Ltd v Malawana* [1986] to contextualize jurisdictional principles, notably that the principal place of business establishes jurisdictional links for companies ([8]).

### A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal undertook a meticulous analysis of the jurisdiction issue. It noted that the respondents' arguments for jurisdiction were inadequately substantiated. They claimed jurisdiction on the basis that loan repayments were to be collected in the Transkei; however, the appellant contended that all pertinent actions, including the contracts and transactions, were executed in Durban ([4]). 

The court reiterated that for the jurisdiction to apply, there must be a nexus established via consent or statutory provisions. It determined that neither consent nor substantial connections sufficed in this instance, signifying that the application lacked grounding under the statutory framework ([5]). 

Notably, the court concluded that the mere non-opposition of proceedings by some insurance companies could not equate to submission to jurisdiction ([8]). It was emphasized that jurisdiction does not extend to cases merely because they are against parties engaged in other legal matters within the court's jurisdiction, underscoring a significant degree of precision required in legal submissions around jurisdiction ([9]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal found the appeal meritorious, ruling that the Transkei High Court lacked the requisite jurisdiction over the appellant and the insurance companies. The previous order of the court a quo was set aside, and the appeal was upheld with specific costs adjustments concerning the preparation of the record ([14]). 

Ultimately, the judgment refined the understanding of jurisdiction within South African law, providing clear parameters regarding the necessary connections for a court to exercise its authority over external parties.

The case underscores the critical interplay between jurisdiction and substantive law, clarifying factors that influence the legal competence of courts in adjudicating matters involving parties from different geographical or administrative domains.
