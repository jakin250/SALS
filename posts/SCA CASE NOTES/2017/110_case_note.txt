Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note on *Big Five Duty Free (Pty) Ltd v Airports Company South Africa Ltd* [2017] ZASCA 110

### F – Facts

The case arises from a tender process conducted by Airports Company South Africa Ltd (ACSA) for the operation of duty-free shops at its international airports ([1]). On 26 August 2009, ACSA awarded a ten-year contract to operate these shops to Big Five Duty Free (Pty) Ltd (Big Five) after inviting tenders ([1]). However, an unsuccessful bidder, DSF Flemingo SA (Pty) Ltd (Flemingo), sought a review of this award, claiming it was made unlawfully by ACSA. The North Gauteng High Court granted an urgent interdict that prevented ACSA from implementing the award and later upheld Flemingo's review, deeming the award unlawful due to the non-fair and non-transparent process ([1]).

After the judgment from Phatudi J, Big Five and Flemingo negotiated a settlement and subsequently reached an agreement under which Flemingo abandoned the court order that set aside Big Five’s award and withdrew its review application ([2], [7]). They requested the full court to make this settlement an order, which it did ([2])—however, ACSA neither participated in the settlement nor in the appeal proceedings, choosing instead to abide by the outcome ([2]).

In the months following the settlement, ACSA indicated its intent to disregard the full court's order, citing a need to restart the tender process due to the purported ongoing validity of Phatudi J's judgment ([9], [10]). This prompted Big Five to return to court seeking compliance from ACSA with the original tender award ([9]).

Hughes J of the Gauteng Division dismissed Big Five’s application, concluding that the Phatudi J order was a public remedy that could not simply be abandoned through private agreement ([4]). Big Five then appealed this decision ([4]).

### I – Issues

The central legal issues are as follows:

1. **Whether ACSA is bound by the settlement agreement made an order of court, despite not being a party to the agreement.**
2. **To what extent does a judgment rendered in public law (like that of Phatudi J) restrict parties from settling a dispute through private agreement?**
3. **What is the effect of a full court, through its order, on a previous judgment in rem when the earlier judgment is abandoned by a party to the litigation?** ([2], [12]).

### R – Rules

The following legal principles and precedents are relevant to this case:

1. **Judgment in Rem**: A judgment that affects the rights of the public at large and cannot be simply abandoned by the parties involved; it is valid until overturned by a superior court ([12]–[14]).
2. **Settlement Agreements**: When a court makes a settlement agreement an order of court, it must ensure that the terms of the agreement comply with the law and public policy ([16]–[20]).
3. **Doctrine of Res Judicata**: Mandates that a final judgment on a matter cannot be re-litigated ([28]).
4. **Public Law and Administrative Action**: The courts retain a supervisory authority over public bodies to ensure legality in administrative actions. A private party cannot unilaterally affect the legality of administrative actions ([13]–[14]).

### A – Application

The court examined the validity of the argument that ACSA, which claimed to not be a party to the settlement, was nevertheless bound by the full court’s endorsement of the agreement. By abiding the outcome of the appeal, ACSA effectively accepted the court’s ruling. According to Big Five, the settlement directly addressed the core legal issues from Phatudi J’s judgment, effectively sidelining its public character ([5], [11]).

ACSA contended that it was not restrained by the settlement agreement due to its non-participation in the negotiation and that the abandonment of the Phatudi J judgment was ineffective concerning non-parties ([10], [12]); it argued that the public interest embedded in such judgments cannot be waived through private settlements. However, the court noted that the abandonment of a court order by a party to the litigation could do precisely that—effectively nullifying prior rulings relevant to the settled dispute ([23], [27]).

The court further reasoned that interpreting the settlement with respect to its surrounding circumstances led to the conclusion that the purpose was to invalidate Phatudi J's judgment and allow the implementation of the award to Big Five ([25], [27]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld Big Five's appeal, asserting that ACSA is indeed bound by the previous judgment once the settlement agreement was made an order of court ([29]). The court found that ACSA could not simply disregard the binding order made following the parties' settlement, and the assertion that Phatudi J's order remained valid despite the abandonment was rejected. The ruling concluded with ACSA being ordered to implement the original tender award and sign the lease agreement with Big Five within 30 days and pay legal costs ([29]). 

This ruling emphasizes the enforceability of settlement agreements as court orders in public law contexts, reinforcing the precedent that entities cannot unilaterally evade obligations arising from judicial endorsements of private agreements, thereby upholding the rule of law.
