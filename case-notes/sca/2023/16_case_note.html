Case Title G Phadziri & Sons (Pty) Ltd v Do Light Transport (Pty) Ltd and Another (765/2021) [2023] ZASCA 16 (20 February 2023)

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 16 (20 February 2023) ### F – Facts The presen

FIRAC Case Note

Case Title

G Phadziri & Sons (Pty) Ltd v Do Light Transport (Pty) Ltd and Another (765/2021) [2023] ZASCA 16 (20 February 2023)

F – Facts

The present case arises out of a dispute involving a written agreement amongst G Phadziri & Sons (Pty) Ltd (Phadziri), Do Light Transport (Pty) Ltd (Do Light), and the Department of Transport for the Limpopo Province (the Department). The agreement was initiated on 15 September 2010, wherein Phadziri, facing operational challenges, enlisted Do Light as a subcontractor to provide public transport services on identified routes due to Phadziri’s inability to do so.

On 23 September 2010, the parties further formalized their relationship through a tripartite agreement, which remained effective until integrated public transport services were to be introduced in the Vhembe district. The tripartite agreement allowed Phadziri to transfer operational licenses to Do Light and contained obligations for both parties, including the operation of services following an agreed timetable.

For nearly eight years following its execution, the tripartite agreement was carried out without problems. However, in September 2018, Phadziri asserted unilateral termination of the agreement, leading to a dispute when it began operations on the affected routes without Do Light’s consent.

The High Court upheld the validity of the tripartite agreement against Phadziri's claim that it was void due to vagueness, as two annexures were not attached to the agreement. The High Court also rejected Phadziri's assertion for a tacit term to be incorporated regarding the termination of the contract, which led to the present appeal ([paras 1–7]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions presented in this case are:

  1. Does the absence of annexures in the tripartite agreement render the contract void for vagueness?
  2. Should a tacit term indicating the agreement’s duration and the possibility of termination on reasonable notice be read into the contract? ([paras 1, 22, 23]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal principles are firmly rooted in contract law, particularly regarding the enforceability and interpretation of agreements:

  1. A contract remains valid unless it is void for vagueness, where terms must not leave essential obligations unexpressed. Relevant case law includes Hoffmann and Carvalho v Minister of Agriculture and Soteriou v Retco Poyntons (Pty) Ltd.
  2. The importance of interpreting contractual terms within the entirety of the agreement as well as within the factual matrix surrounding its formation was reiterated in University of Johannesburg v Auckland Park Theological Seminary and Germiston Municipal Retirement Fund.
  3. A tacit term should not contradict express provisions laid out in the contract, as established in Transnet Ltd v Rubenstein. Contracts are often interpreted to give effect to the parties' intent when they engaged in the agreement ([paras 12–19, 24, 32]).

A – Application

In justifying the enforceability of the tripartite agreement, the Supreme Court of Appeal assessed whether the omission of the annexures made its implementation impossible. The Court concluded that the definitions and obligations concerning the routes were adequately described, indicating that both parties had a common understanding of their commitments. The absence of the annexures did not hinder the practical execution of the agreement since the routes were sufficiently identified.

Furthermore, the Court recognized the principle that contractual agreements are designed to be implemented and emphasized that when granting relief, courts should strive to fulfill the parties' evident intentions, rather than voiding contracts based on technicalities. The decision rested on the overall goal of maintaining public transportation services in the region ([paras 11–20]).

As for the argument advocating for the implication of a tacit term of reasonable notice for termination, the Court found that there was insufficient evidence to support this claim. The arguments presented, which referenced statutory frameworks and government resolutions, did not demonstrate that the parties intended the duration of the tripartite agreement to extend beyond the clear terms articulated in the contract. The approach mirrored that of *Transnet Ltd v Rubenstein, reinforcing the need not to impose amendments that contravene established provisions ([paras 23–32]).

C – Conclusion

The appeal by G Phadziri & Sons (Pty) Ltd was dismissed with costs on 20 February 2023, affirming the conclusion that the tripartite agreement under consideration remains valid and enforceable. The Court held that the absence of attached annexures did not render the agreement void due to vagueness, and the quest to read a tacit term into the agreement concerning its duration contradicted the express provisions already set forth.

Consequently, the judgment reinforced a critical perspective in contract law regarding the enforceability of agreements, emphasizing the necessity of preserving contractual intentions and clarifying that reasonable business practices and the prevailing context should guide interpretations of contractual provisions. This case has implications for future negotiations, particularly in how parties document their agreements and the necessity of including all relevant annexures ([paras 33, 34]).

End of Case Note
