Case Note: SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# Case Note: DDP Valuers (Pty) Ltd v Madibeng Local Municipality (233/2015) [2015] ZASCA 146

### F – Facts
This case arises from a dispute regarding the awarding of a municipal tender by the Madibeng Local Municipality to Dijalo Property Valuers (the second respondent) for the compilation of a new General and Supplementary Valuation Roll for the period 2014 to 2018 ([para 1]). The DDP Valuers (Pty) Ltd (the appellant), which had previously served as the municipal valuer, lodged an objection after it was informed of the decision. The appellant argued that the successful tender from the second respondent was disproportionate when compared to its own bid, which was the second lowest ([para 2]).

Upon directing an objection in writing to the Municipality in accordance with regulation 49 of the Municipal Supply Chain Management Regulations, the appellant sought pertinent information regarding the award process and requested the appointment of a competent person to resolve the dispute ([paras 3-4]). The Municipality, however, refused engagement, citing a separate ongoing litigation regarding unpaid invoices ([paras 5-6]).

Subsequently, the appellant sought judicial review in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, challenging the tender award on multiple grounds that included improper evaluation criteria and failure to adhere to the Preferential Procurement Regulations ([paras 7-8]). The Municipality and second respondent raised a point in limine, contending that the appellant had failed to exhaust internal remedies as per section 7(2) of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) before resorting to the courts ([para 9]).

### I – Issues
The initial issue for the court's determination was whether the dispute resolution mechanism established under regulation 50 of the Municipal Supply Chain Management Regulations constituted an internal remedy as required by section 7(2) of the PAJA ([para 10]). The discretion under section 7(2)(c), permitting courts to exempt parties from exhausting internal remedies when deemed necessary in the interests of justice, was also pivotal ([para 12]).

### R – Rules
The court considered primarily the provisions of section 7(2) of the PAJA, stipulating that no court may review an administrative action unless any internal remedies provided for in other laws have been exhausted ([para 12]). The definition of "internal remedy," provided by Plasket J in **Reed and Others v The Master of the High Court** ([2005] ZAECHC 5, [2005] 2 All SA 429 (E)) was also referenced, wherein it was characterized as an administrative appeal or internal review ([para 13]).

In contrast, the relevant regulation 50 emphasized the lack of authority given to the appointed independent person to set aside decisions, thereby failing to establish an internal remedy as required by section 7(2) ([paras 16-19]). The evaluation of whether remedies were adequate or illusory was also addressed, considering the established principles that courts can condone non-exhaustion of seemingly ineffective remedies ([para 14]).

### A – Application
The court, through Mhlantla JA, undertook a comprehensive examination of regulation 50's provisions. It noted that the regulation requires an independent mediator without decision-making authority, which fundamentally contradicts the concept of an internal remedy as envisaged by the PAJA ([para 16]). The inquiry found that the procedure outlined in regulation 50 lacked specificity concerning how objections would be dealt with or the grounds upon which decisions could be contested, undermining the hierarchical and specialized structure anticipated for internal remedies ([paras 16-19]).

Building upon precedents, including **ESDA Properties (Pty) Ltd v Amathole District Municipality** ([2014] ZAECGHC 76), the court concluded that regulation 50 does not enable a party to challenge an administrative decision meaningfully, instead offering a non-binding resolution framework ([para 21]). As such, there was no requirement for the appellant to pursue this mechanism before applying to the court for judicial review ([para 22]).

Moreover, the court indicated that alternatives such as section 62 of the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act do provide for genuine internal remedies, as they encompass decision-making powers that can affirm or overturn prior decisions, thus were not available once the contract was awarded to the second respondent ([paras 23-25]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that regulation 50 does not constitute an internal remedy as required by section 7(2) of the PAJA, allowing the appellant to proceed with its application for judicial review without first exhausting internal mechanisms ([para 28]). It overruled the decision of the High Court, dismissed the point in limine, and remitted the matter for a determination on its merits, thus reaffirming the importance of judicial review in situations where administrative remedies are lacking ([paras 30-31]).

This case underscores the necessity for municipalities to ensure that their dispute resolution mechanisms comply with established principles governing administrative justice to avoid being rendered futile and ineffective in the review of public decisions. The ruling fortifies the judicial branch's role in monitoring adherence to the law, ensuring that all actions by authorities remain answerable to the standards required by the PAJA.
