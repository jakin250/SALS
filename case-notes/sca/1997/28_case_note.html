28 Case Note

The appellant, Pippa Skotnes, is an artist and a lecturer involved in the publishing of fine prints and handmade books through her press, Axeage Private Press.

Supreme Court of Appeal 1997 1982 ("the Act"). In 1991

FIRAC Case Note: Skotnes v South African Library [1997]

F - Facts: [paras 2-4]

The appellant, Pippa Skotnes, is an artist and a lecturer involved in the publishing of fine prints and handmade books through her press, Axeage Private Press. The respondent, the South African Library, functions as a legal deposit library in accordance with the Legal Deposit of Publications Act 17 of 1982 ("the Act"). In 1991, the appellant produced a work titled Sound from the Thinking Strings, which consisted of sixty-five specimens (hereinafter referred to as "the work"). The respondent demanded that a free copy of the work be provided under section 2(1) of the Act, which mandates that publishers supply legal deposit libraries with copies of their works if intended for public sale. The appellant's refusal led the respondent to seek a court order compelling compliance.

Initially, the action was dismissed in the Magistrate’s Court, but the Cape Provincial Division, upon appeal, ruled in favor of the respondent, ordering the appellant to furnish a copy of the work and pay costs. The appellant subsequently sought leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal.

I - Issues: [paras 4-6]

Three key legal issues arose for determination:

  1. Whether the work constituted a "printed book" as defined in the Act.
  2. Whether "copies" of the work, as described in section 2(1)(b) of the Act, had come into existence.
  3. The intended sales status of the works within the Republic, which was conceded by the appellant’s counsel.

The resolution of these issues hinged on statutory interpretation and the application of previous judicial interpretations regarding the definitions outlined in the Act.

R - Rules: [paras 4-14]

The applicable rules stem from the Legal Deposit of Publications Act 17 of 1982.

  • Section 2(1)(b) states that one copy of every publication must be supplied free of charge to legal deposit libraries if the publication is intended for sale to the public in South Africa.
  • Section 2(2)(a) specifies that such a copy must be supplied within 30 days of the commencement of distribution.
  • Section 1 defines "publication" broadly, including "a printed book, newspaper, magazine, [...] or any other similar printed matter." Notably, there is no statutory definition provided for the term "copy."

Judicial precedence, such as Tillmans and Co v S S Knutsford Limited [1908] 2 KB 385, was cited regarding the application of the eiusdem generis rule, which seeks to limit the interpretation of statutory terms based on similar items mentioned within a list. Additional cases, such as Rennie N O v Gordon Other N N O 1988 (1) SA 1, were discussed to highlight how ambiguity in legislative language affects interpretations.

A - Application: [paras 8-19]

In addressing the first issue—whether the work was a "printed book"—the court emphasized that the work met the essential characteristics of a printed book under the Act. The appellant’s argument that the definition of "printed book" should be restricted due to the mass-production nature of similar items was rejected. The court highlighted that the broad legislative intent was to encompass a variety of publications, including unique artistic works which might not follow the mass-production model.

In discussing the second issue regarding the existence of "copies," the court considered the appellant’s assertion that the etchings produced were original works of art rather than mere reproductions. However, the court reasoned that the Act does not differentiate between artistic merit and the physical existence of printed works; hence, the numerous etchings served as individual copies of the publication, which conformed with the statutory requirements.

On the matter of the appellant's constitutional rights, since the trial predated the enactment of the South African Constitution of 1996, the court opted not to analyze the statute's compatibility with constitutional provisions. Counsel for the appellant ultimately did not pursue this line of defense in the appeal.

Lastly, the court responded to the request for a special order regarding costs. The appellant’s suggestion that the respondent should have utilized the less adversarial procedure defined in section 5 of the Act was countered with the argument that such litigation was inevitable given the appellant's initial refusal to comply with the Act's provisions.

C - Conclusion: [paras 18-19]

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming that the work constituted a printed book under the provisions of the Act and that copies indeed came into existence as legally defined. The dismissal also included a costs order against the appellant, which was deemed appropriate due to the unavoidable nature of the litigation that arose from her refusal to comply with the legislative mandate.

The case underscores the significance of statutory interpretation in determining the scope of rights and obligations under the Legal Deposit of Publications Act, particularly in artistic contexts where definitions may intersect with creative considerations. The ruling asserts that the legislative intent is to foster a comprehensive national collection of publications, thereby enhancing the cultural and educational landscape of South Africa.
