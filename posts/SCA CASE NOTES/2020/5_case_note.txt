# FIRAC Case Note: The Director of Public Prosecutions, Grahamstown v T M (131/2019) [2020] ZASCA 05

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–7])

In the matter of **The Director of Public Prosecutions, Grahamstown v T M**, the respondent was convicted in the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, Grahamstown, on three counts of rape, as defined in section 3 of the Criminal Law (Sexual Offences and Related Matters) Amendment Act 32 of 2007. The victim, a nine-year-old girl, lived with her aunt and the respondent, who was her aunt's boyfriend, in a cramped two-roomed house ([para 1]). The abuse occurred over consecutive nights in April 2017 when the respondent, after returning home intoxicated with the aunt, unlawfully engaged in sexual acts with the complainant ([paras 3–4]).

The High Court, upon sentencing, imposed an effective term of 22 years' imprisonment. However, this was subject to appeal by the State, which argued that the High Court had erred by not imposing the mandatory life sentence prescribed by the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, unless substantial and compelling circumstances justified a lesser sentence ([para 1]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1, 8–12])

The primary issue for determination was whether the High Court was correct in departing from the prescribed minimum sentence of life imprisonment under the statutory framework provided by the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997. The central sub-issue involved whether the court correctly identified and articulated substantial and compelling circumstances that would justify a departure from the mandatory life sentence ([paras 1, 8]). 

Further issues arose concerning the nature of the facts considered by the High Court in relation to mitigation and whether the consideration of alcohol use and absence of physical injury were appropriate bases for mitigating the severity of the sentence ([paras 9-14]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 2, 8, 10, 12, 14])

The legal framework for this case revolves around the **Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997**, particularly section 51, which prescribes life imprisonment for certain sexual offences against minors, including rape of a child under 16 ([para 2]). The Act stipulates that a court may impose a lesser sentence only if it finds substantial and compelling circumstances, which must be documented in the record of proceedings ([para 8]).

Case law relevant to this ruling includes **S v S** 1995 (1) SACR 267 (A), which emphasized that factors such as alcohol use, while relevant, do not warrant leniency in severe cases of sexual violence. Furthermore, **Malgas v S** 2001 (2) SA 1222 (SCA) warned against allowing ordinary mitigating factors, including first-offender status and speculative assessments of circumstances, to constitute substantial and compelling factors sufficient to deviate from mandated sentencing ([paras 10, 12]). 

Additionally, the judgment highlights systemic issues regarding the prevalence of sexual violence in South Africa and the expectations of courts in addressing these issues through appropriate sentencing ([para 15]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 9–14])

The Supreme Court of Appeal critically examined the High Court's rationale for deviating from the mandatory life sentence. It found that the High Court misapplied the statutory requirements by failing to clearly articulate substantial and compelling circumstances. Despite acknowledging the prescribed minimum sentence of life imprisonment, the court relied on factors such as the respondent's first-offender status, his employment record, and the role of alcohol as mitigating factors ([para 11]). 

The Supreme Court ruled that these elements did not meet the standard required under section 51(3)(a) of the Act. Specifically, the Court noted that the High Court’s consideration of the absence of "gratuitous violence" against the complainant could not adequately justify a lesser sentence, as the psychological trauma inflicted upon the victim was severe, with long-term consequences that extended beyond immediate physical injury ([para 13]).

Moreover, the Supreme Court underscored the dangerous precedent set by allowing leniency based on the ordinary circumstances of the offender or the absence of physical injury, reminding that the trauma and societal impact of rape, particularly against minors, must dominate sentencing decisions ([para 14]). The judgment articulated a strong stance against leniency in cases of child sexual exploitation, asserting the need for a deterrent sentence in light of the societal context of persistent sexual violence ([para 15]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 16])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal from the State, concluding that the High Court had misdirected itself in its assessment of what constituted substantial and compelling circumstances. The sentence of 22 years’ imprisonment was set aside and replaced with a life sentence for the respondent. The court emphasized that the law mandates life imprisonment for such egregious acts against vulnerable children, thereby reaffirming the seriousness of such offences ([para 16]).

The ruling establishes a firm precedent reinforcing the statutory requirement for life imprisonment in cases involving the rape of minors unless exceptionally compelling reasons are present. It additionally reiterates the court's role in addressing the epidemic of sexual violence in South Africa and signals a judicial commitment to impose severe penalties as a deterrent against such crimes ([para 15]), thereby reinforcing societal norms that protect vulnerable individuals.
