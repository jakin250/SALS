Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

**FIRAC Case Note: East Rand Member District of Chartered Accountants and Another v Independent Regulatory Board for Auditors and Others (113/2022) [2023] ZASCA 81**

**F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–20])**

The case concerns an appeal by the East Rand Member District of Chartered Accountants and its chairman, Jaroslav Cerny (together referred to as "the applicants") against the Independent Regulatory Board for Auditors (IRBA) and its officials (collectively "the respondents"). The applicants sought to appeal a dismissal by the Gauteng Division of the High Court regarding their application to review and set aside the Mandatory Audit Firm Rotation Rule (MAFR), which was promulgated by the IRBA on 5 June 2017.

The first applicant, a voluntary association of chartered accountants, includes a significant number of registered auditors, while the IRBA is a statutory regulatory body established under the Auditing Professions Act 26 of 2005. The purpose of the MAFR is to enhance auditor independence by limiting the tenure of audit firms for public interest entities to ten consecutive years, with a mandatory cooling-off period of five years before reappointment.

The applicants argued against this rule, asserting their rights under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA). Upon review, the high court concluded that the applicants had delayed unreasonably in bringing their challenge. It ruled that the review was instituted outside the prescribed period and did not adequately consider the merits or the potential ultra vires nature of the IRBA’s actions. The applicants contended that the dismissal was based on a misinterpretation of statutory provisions and the implications of their own delay.

**I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 3, 8, 12, 18])**

1. Whether the high court properly concluded that the applicants' delay in instituting the review application was unreasonable and barred them from pursuing their review under PAJA.
2. Whether the promulgation of the MAFR by the IRBA was ultra vires its enabling legislation, specifically the Auditing Professions Act.
3. The implications of the delay and how it interplays with the merits of the review, specifically whether the IRBA acted within its granted authority.

**R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 6, 10, 16, 18])**

The court's analysis relied on various legal principles and statutory interpretations, notably:

- **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**, specifically sections 5 and 7 concerning requests for reasons and timelines for lodging judicial reviews.
- The interpretation of statutory authority under the **Auditing Professions Act 26 of 2005**, especially sections 4 and 10, which delineate the powers of the IRBA.
- Precedent set by **Esau and Others v Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs and Others** which clarified when administrative actions are ripe for review, emphasizing the significance of finality in policy decisions.

**A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 9, 14, 16-19])**

Upon considering the merits, the court found that the high court had erred in determining that the review was instituted outside the prescribed period. The delay in seeking to institute the review was seen as reasonable, primarily because the MAFR would take effect only years after its promulgation. The court determined that the 180-day period for review under PAJA commenced only after the applicants received reasons for the IRBA’s decision.

In terms of statutory interpretation, the court examined whether the MAFR was within the scope of the powers conferred on the IRBA. The IRBA only has the authority to prescribe rules that relate to standards of competence or ethics. The court concluded that the MAFR imposed restrictions that effectively limited the freedom of choice for entities in appointing auditors and thus exceeded the authority granted by the Act. It highlighted that the primary concern regarding the MAFR was not one of professional standards but regulatory overreach.

The court also acknowledged the public interest involved given the implications of auditor independence and integrity, deciding to uphold the appeal in light of both the procedural and substantive issues at stake.

**C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 20-21])**

The Supreme Court of Appeal granted leave to appeal and upheld the appeal against the high court's decision. It set aside the high court's order and declared the MAFR as promulgated by the IRBA to be ultra vires the Auditing Professions Act. The decision underscored the need for regulatory bodies to operate within their legally defined parameters and reaffirmed the principles underpinning administrative justice as articulated in PAJA.

The implications of this ruling extend beyond the immediate parties involved; it serves as a precedent reinforcing the limits of regulatory authority and ensuring that any impositions on professional practices must align with the enabling legislation. This case affirms the judicial oversight of administrative actions and the importance of maintaining clarity in statutory interpretations, particularly in regulatory frameworks that impact public and corporate interests.
