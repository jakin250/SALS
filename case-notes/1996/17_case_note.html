Constitutional Court of South Africa - CCT 15/96

Facts In accordance with section 160(1) of the interim Constitution of South Africa, the provincial legislature is entitled to adopt a constitution via a two-thirds majority resolution.

Constitutional Court 1996 CCT 15/96

FIRAC Case Note

Facts

In accordance with section 160(1) of the interim Constitution of South Africa, the provincial legislature is entitled to adopt a constitution via a two-thirds majority resolution. This was enacted when, on 15 March 1996, the KwaZulu-Natal (KZN) Legislature adopted its provincial constitution, reflecting extensive political negotiation ([1]–[2]). Following the adoption, the Speaker of the KZN Legislature submitted the provincial constitution for certification under section 160(4), asserting it was passed with the requisite majority. The certification was contested, primarily by the African National Congress (ANC) and the Government of National Unity (GNU), who submitted objections suggesting the provincial constitution contained inconsistencies with the interim Constitution ([3]).

The Speaker and the KZN Premier defended the constitution, arguing for its consistency. However, the King’s Council of KZN presented objections, which this Court indicated did not pertain to the certification process ([2]–[4]). Central to the objections was the argument that various provisions purportedly usurped national powers, violating the constitutional framework established by the interim Constitution ([13]). Noteworthy provisions identified as problematic included limitations on the powers and structure of the provincial legislature, the bill of rights included in the constitution, and provisions relating to its constitutional court ([14]–[37]).

Issues

The primary legal issues can be encapsulated as follows:

  1. Are the provisions of the KZN provincial constitution inconsistent with the interim Constitution and the Constitutional Principles defined therein?
  2. Do the provisions usurp national legislative powers, thereby breaching section 160(3) of the interim Constitution?
  3. Can provisions that are contingent or suspended be deemed certifiable under the requirements of the interim Constitution, particularly section 160(4) ([6]–[48])?

Rules

The court grounded its analysis in the interim Constitution, particularly section 160, which outlines the power of provincial legislatures to adopt constitutions. Key rules applicable to this case included:

  • Section 160(3): A provincial constitution must not be inconsistent with the interim Constitution, including the Constitutional Principles in Schedule 4.
  • Section 160(4): No provision of a provincial constitution has force unless certified by the Constitutional Court as being consistent.
  • Principles of Legislative Competence: The provincial legislature cannot surpass the powers defined in section 126 of the interim Constitution, and any attempt to assert greater powers would be constitutionally impermissible ([1]–[8]).
  • Certification Process Integrity: The necessity for the provisions' compatibility with national legislative authority, ensuring no usurpation of powers established by the interim Constitution ([10]–[42]).

Application

The Court systematically analyzed the provisions of the KZN provincial constitution against the backdrop of the interim Constitution's limitations.

  1. Usurpation of National Powers: The Court highlighted several provisions that appeared to assert powers beyond that granted to the KZN Legislature, notably concerning local government and the purported autonomy of the provincial constitution within the republic’s framework ([14]–[34]). The assertion of KZN as a "self-governing province" was deemed beyond the powers vested in the provincial legislature ([14]).
  2. Inconsistencies in the Bill of Rights: Provisions in the KZN constitution that detailed rights and remedies were also questioned regarding their competence. The Bill of Rights setting out rights governing matters outside provincial legislative jurisdiction was contested ([22]–[30]). The Court considered the existence of a provincial Bill of Rights alongside the interim Constitution's Chapter 3, emphasizing collaboratively adhering to legislative limitations in this context ([27]–[34]).
  3. Incompatibility of Consistency Clauses: The Court found that consistency clauses included in the provincial constitution sought to immunize specific provisions from being challenged under the certification process, thereby contravening section 160([36]–[38]). The argumentative basis that they would have no effect if found inconsistent was seen as an attempt to circumvent the necessary legal scrutiny and certification.
  4. Suspensive Conditions: The Court addressed provisions that mandated validation of certain rights and structures only contingent upon future developments, underscoring that these conditions do not conform to the immediate evaluative criteria required for certification as stipulated in section 160. It determined that such provisions illustrate a lack of finality, rendering the draft constitution inchoate and improper for certification ([39]–[46]).

Conclusion

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court held that the text of the Constitution of the Province of KwaZulu-Natal, adopted on 15 March 1996, is fundamentally flawed and cannot be certified under the provisions of section 160(4) of the interim Constitution. The Court’s critique focused on the identified usurpation of national powers, inconsistencies in the Bill of Rights, and the inefficacy of suspended conditional provisions. Consequently, the challenged constitution must undergo significant redrafting for any future certification to be considered ([47]–[48]).

The implications of this decision not only underscore the vital role of the Constitutional Court in certifying provincial constitutions but also highlight the importance of clearly delineated authority and the adherence to established constitutional standards.
