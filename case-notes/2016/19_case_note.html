City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality v Afriforum and Another [2016] ZACC 19

Constitutional Court 2016 [2016] ZACC 19

F – Facts

On 21 July 2016, the Constitutional Court of South Africa delivered a judgment in the case of City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality v Afriforum and Another (CCT 157/15). This matter arose from the City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality's decision to replace certain street names considered to be emblematic of South Africa's colonial and apartheid past. The names set to be changed included those of historical figures such as Dr. Hendrik Verwoerd and Louis Botha, to names that more accurately represented the nation's diverse history, including Nelson Mandela and Steve Biko.

The respondents, Afriforum and Evert van Dyk, sought to interdict the municipality’s actions, arguing that the names subjected to change were integral to their cultural heritage, thereby infringing their rights under section 31 of the Constitution, which safeguards cultural rights for communities.

The High Court initially granted an interim interdict preventing the removal of the old street names and mandating the municipality to restore any names that had already been replaced. The municipality sought leave to appeal against this order, claiming that it was overly broad and undue interference with its executive powers, which were constitutionally protected. After a series of appeals and reviews, the case came before the Constitutional Court for final resolution.

I – Issues

The primary legal issues addressed by the Constitutional Court included:

  1. Appealability of Interim Orders: Whether the interim interdict granted by the High Court, which restrained municipal actions regarding street names, was appealable.
  2. Requirements for Interim Interdicts: Whether the respondents successfully established the necessary elements for the grant of an interim interdict, including:
  • A prima facie right.
  • Reasonable apprehension of irreparable harm.
  • Balance of convenience favoring the grant of the interdict.
  • Absence of any alternative remedy.
  1. Separation of Powers: Whether the High Court's order unduly interfered with the municipality's executive powers and governance responsibilities.

R – Rules

The court relied on several key legal authorities and principles:

  • Setlogelo v Setlogelo: Established the criteria for granting an interim interdict, specifically the need for a prima facie right, apprehended harm, balance of convenience, and lack of an alternative remedy.
  • Zweni v Minister of Law and Order: Provided foundational criteria for determining the appealability of orders, specifically focusing on whether an order disposes of a substantial part of the issues in the main case.
  • Section 31 of the Constitution: Protects the cultural rights of communities, outlining that members of cultural communities have a right to enjoy their culture, practice their religion, and use their language while not infringing on others’ rights.
  • Separation of Powers Doctrine: Emphasizes that courts should be cautious in intervening in the internal functioning of the executive without compelling justification, ensuring that such interventions do not undermine the governance responsibilities of elected bodies.

A – Application

The Constitutional Court found that:

  1. Appealability: The orders by the High Court regarding the interim interdict profoundly impacted the municipality's ability to execute its governance responsibilities, thus meriting appealability per the interests of justice standard articulated in the context of interim orders.
  2. Prima Facie Right: While Afriforum asserted a prima facie right based on section 31, the majority noted that the right must not prevail excessively over the overall constitutional project of transformation and inclusivity which governs South African society. The respondents did not sufficiently articulate how their asserted rights outweigh the municipality's mandate to promote an inclusive identity.
  3. Irreparable Harm: The Court examined claims of emotional and cultural harm to Afriforum from the removal of the street names and found these claims to lack legal substance. The Court reasoned that while emotional connections to names are valid, they do not constitute irreparable harm if temporary name changes might be reversed pending the final resolution.
  4. Balance of Convenience: The balance of convenience favored the municipality, given the legal and financial implications tied to its governance functions. The financial burden of restoring street names after removal also weighed against Afriforum’s campaign.
  5. Separation of Powers: The court held that the interdict unduly limited the municipality’s ability to manage its own administrative affairs, thus encroaching upon the sphere of local governance that should be respected.

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court, thus, concluded that:

  1. Leave to appeal was granted, upholding the appeal based on the grounds that the interim order was inappropriate and did not meet the legal criteria for such relief.
  2. The orders of the Gauteng Division of the High Court were set aside, dismissing the application for the interim interdict.
  3. The costs order awarded against the municipality by the Supreme Court of Appeal was also set aside, with each party bearing its own costs.

The judgment underscores the importance of respecting the process of inclusivity and transformation inherent in South Africa's constitutional framework, while also delineating the limitations of historical cultural claims in the face of ongoing societal transformation efforts.
