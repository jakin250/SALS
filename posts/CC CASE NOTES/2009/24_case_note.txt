Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2009

# FIRAC Case Note for Reflect-All 1025 CC and Others v MEC for Public Transport, Roads and Works, Gauteng Provincial Government

**Case Citation:**
Reflect-All 1025 CC and Others v Member of the Executive Council for Public Transport, Roads and Works, Gauteng Provincial Government and Another [2009] ZACC 24, Case CCT 110/08.

---

### F – Facts

The case concerns the constitutional validity of sections 10(1) and 10(3) of the Gauteng Transport Infrastructure Act 8 of 2001 (the "Infrastructure Act"). The applicants, who are registered owners of various properties in Gauteng, claim that these sections arbitrarily deprive them of property rights in violation of section 25(1) of the Constitution and amount to expropriation without just and equitable compensation, contrary to sections 25(2) and (3) ([1–3]).

There are numerous properties in question, most of which were purchased before the act's commencement. The first applicant intends to construct a shopping centre, the second a township of luxury houses, while the other applicants have various proposed developments impeded by designated provincial roads. Letters and notices published under sections 10(1) and 10(3) issued by the MEC legally restrict the land use of the applicants' properties, effectively preventing development and use ([6–9]).

The High Court had earlier declared section 10(3) unconstitutional and invalid, taking issue with the arbitrary nature of deprivation posed by that section, while upholding section 10(1). The High Court did not declare section 10(1) unconstitutional, citing adequate past consultations as evidence of procedural fairness ([8]). The applicants appealed against this decision, while the respondents filed a cross-appeal regarding costs ([7–9]).

### I – Issues

The following constitutional issues arose in the case ([26]):
1. Do sections 10(1) and 10(3) constitute deprivation of property under Section 25(1) of the Constitution? If so:
   - Is such deprivation procedurally arbitrary?
   - Is such deprivation substantively arbitrary, particularly concerning section 10(3)?
   
2. Does section 10(3) amount to expropriation without just and equitable compensation in violation of sections 25(2) and (3)?
3. Does section 10(3) contravene the cooperative governance principles enshrined in section 41 of the Constitution?
4. Is the promulgation of Provincial Notices 2625 and 2626 considered administrative action under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA)?

### R – Rules

The court considered the following rules and precedents when evaluating the constitutionality of the provisions in question:
- **Section 25 of the Constitution**: This section provides protection against deprivation of property without compensation and articulates both substantive and procedural fairness requirements regarding property rights.
- **PAJA**: Provides for procedural fairness in administrative actions that materially and adversely affect rights ([10–12]).
- **First National Bank of South Africa Ltd v Commissioner, South African Revenue Service**: Established that deprivation would be deemed arbitrary if the law does not provide sufficient reason for the deprivation or is procedurally unfair ([29]).
- **Mkontwana v Nelson Mandela Metropolitan Municipality**: The court elaborated on the nature of property rights and the concept of deprivation, emphasizing that substantial limitations on property use may constitute deprivation ([34–36]).

### A – Application

In its judgment, the Constitutional Court affirmed the finding of the High Court that sections 10(1) and 10(3) do deprive property owners of certain rights regarding their property. The court found that this deprivation, under section 25(1), must be examined for procedural and substantive fairness ([38-39]).

1. **Procedural Fairness**: The MEC’s actions under section 10(1) were deemed not procedurally arbitrary. The court upheld the High Court's conclusion that historical consultations, although dated, provided a sufficiently fair procedural framework ([42-44]).
   
2. **Substantive Fairness**: Section 10(3) was inspected for substantive arbitrariness and its purpose in safeguarding long-term infrastructure planning. The court concluded that while the restrictions were significant, the MEC's actions pursued a clearly beneficial public purpose concerning road network development. The court found that the restrictions were not disproportionately invasive ([52-60]).
   
3. **Co-operative Governance**: The court ruled that the provincial government's authority over road planning was constitutionally valid, thus aligning with co-operative governance requirements under section 41. The procedures in the Infrastructure Act did not infringe on municipal authority ([75-77]).

4. **Administrative Action**: No administrative action was found in the publication of Notices 2625 and 2626, as these notices were designed to facilitate transitional measures rather than administrative governance. The court aligned this reasoning with established precedents ([84-83]).

### C – Conclusion

The court concluded that:
1. **Section 10(1)**: The appeal regarding its constitutional validity was dismissed; the applicants had no grounds for asserting arbitrary deprivation ([86]).
2. **Section 10(3)**: The applicants' appeal against the ruling declaring this section unconstitutional was also dismissed, as the court held the section did not comprise arbitrary deprivation under section 25(1) or expropriation requiring compensation under sections 25(2) and (3).
3. **Costs**: As both parties had not achieved substantial success in their appeals, each bore their own costs in both the High Court and the Constitutional Court ([88-90]).

In light of the ruling, this case underscores the balance between property rights and the need for regulatory frameworks supporting public infrastructure in a rapidly developing urban context. The outcomes provide clarity regarding procedural requirements for property owners within the context of provincial planning legislation, offering insight into the strengths and limitations of the existing legal framework governing property rights in South Africa.
