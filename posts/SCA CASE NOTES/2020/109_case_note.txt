Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

### Case Note: Phakula v Minister of Safety and Security (Case No 454/19) [2020] ZASCA 109

#### FIRAC Method

---

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–9])**

The appellant, Mr. Thabang Phakula, appealed against the judgment of Tuchten J from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, which dismissed his claims for damages due to his unlawful shooting, arrest, detention, and assault by members of the South African Police Services (SAPS) (the respondent) ([paras 1–2]). On 26 October 2010, Phakula was shot by a special task force of police officers while allegedly fleeing from a robbery scene. The police claimed he was part of an armed gang believed to be engaged in robbery at Mr. Mahomed Ameen Bhamjee's residence in Die Heuwel, Witbank, and the shooting occurred after they asserted he attempted to escape ([para 2]). 

Phakula was arrested and held in custody for approximately 11 months before being acquitted of all charges. He was left with serious injuries, including multiple gunshot wounds, disfigurement, and consequent loss of employment as an army officer ([para 3]). In his particulars of claim, he alleged the unlawful nature of the arrest without a warrant and failure to comply with constitutional rights, specifically sections 12 and 35 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa 1996 ([paras 4–5]).

The trial commenced with a separation of issues, where the court intended first to determine the legality of the shooting before addressing the other claims related to his arrest and detention ([paras 7–8]). The High Court ruled in favor of the respondent, concluding that the police officers acted within their rights under section 49(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, justifying the use of force to prevent Phakula's alleged escape ([para 8]).

---

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 12–13])**

The issues for determination before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:  
(a) Whether the shooting of Phakula was necessary and reasonable under section 49(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act, as amended;  
(b) The validity of the trial court's conclusion regarding Phakula's alleged injuries during the shooting;  
(c) The credibility of testimonies regarding the involvement of other police officers;  
(d) The burden of proof concerning the injuries sustained;  
(e) The justification for the shooting by Warrant Officer (W/O) De Klerk and the consequent implications for legality of the arrest and subsequent detention ([paras 12–13]).

---

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 18–19])**

The High Court relied significantly on section 49(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act, which provides for the lawfulness of using force during an attempt to arrest a suspect who is fleeing or resisting arrest ([para 18]). Furthermore, established precedents emphasize that while separation of issues can be beneficial, it must be exercised with caution to avoid inefficient and unproductive trials, as demonstrated in *Nature’s Choice Farms (Pty) Ltd v Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality* and *First Rand Bank Ltd v Clear Creek Trading 12 (Pty) Ltd* ([paras 19–20]). 

The rulings from these cases stress the need for clear articulation of issues in order to facilitate the efficient administration of justice. The Supreme Court underscored that issues being inextricably linked should ideally be addressed concurrently, rather than in isolation, which may lead to inconsistent findings ([para 20]).

---

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 21–22])**

In its judgment, the Supreme Court of Appeal critiqued the lower court's decision to separate the issues related to the shooting from those concerning arrest and detention, asserting that these elements were fundamentally interconnected ([para 22]). The court emphasized that a proper and comprehensive examination of all claims should have been conducted to ascertain the legitimacy of the actions taken by the police and the circumstances surrounding the arrest. 

The Court pointed out that had the issues not been separated, the evidence required to determine the lawfulness of the arrest and the associated elements of assault and detention would have been examined in a more coherent manner. Consequently, the Court held that the high court should not have isolated the determination of the shooting from other claims, which ultimately led to factual findings being incomplete and somewhat premature ([para 22]).

---

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 22–23])**

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Phakula’s appeal, emphasizing that the high court erred in its approach to separating the issues of the case. The judgment was set aside, and the matter was remitted to the high court for a retrial, thereby allowing for all claims—specifically those concerning the alleged unlawful shooting, arrest, and detention—to be reconsidered holistically by a different judge ([para 23]).

The implications of this case reiterate the necessity for courts to avoid unnecessary separations of issues that could hinder the合理性 of justice. It suggests a judicial commitment to ensuring that litigants receive an equitable hearing where the interconnectedness of claims is recognized and respected. The court’s decision serves as a reminder to practitioners of the need for clarity and coherence in both pleadings and judicial directions to prevent procedural ambiguities and inefficiencies ([para 23]).

--- 

This case underscores the continual evolution of legal principles surrounding police conduct, the use of force, and the fundamental rights enshrined in the Constitution, thus reaffirming the essential balance between law enforcement powers and individual rights.
