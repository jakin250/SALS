Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: Ngobeni v S (741/13) [2014] ZASCA 59

**F – Facts**  
This appeal stems from a conviction in the regional court of Pretoria, where Bigboy Cyril Ngobeni (the appellant) was found guilty of attempted murder and sentenced to four years’ imprisonment on 11 June 2004 under section 276(1)(i) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([1]). The appellant’s conviction was upheld by the North Gauteng High Court on 29 January 2007, although the appellant was granted leave to appeal on 12 September 2007.

The incident in question occurred during a road rage confrontation between the appellant and the complainant as they drove from Pretoria City Centre towards Atteridgeville. The two vehicles engaged in reckless behavior, including overtaking each other multiple times. After both vehicles stopped at an Engen petrol station, the complainant approached the appellant’s vehicle to confront him about his driving, allegedly stating “[my son] ek sal jou skiet” ("I will shoot you"). The complainant then claimed he was shot from behind as he began to walk away, sustaining gunshot wounds to his back ([2]–[3]).

In contrast, the appellant alleged that he was attacked by the complainant and two passengers, resulting in a physical assault that left him bleeding. He contended that he fired in self-defense, believing his life was in danger ([3]). Two state witnesses corroborated the complainant’s narrative to a significant degree, confirming the location and nature of the shooting, while a medical report noting the trajectory of the bullet was admitted into evidence ([3]–[4]).

However, the medical report bore ambiguity, with question marks under the section addressing clinical findings regarding the gunshot wounds, raising doubts about the entry and exit wounds ([5]). The trial court did not call the doctor to explain this uncertainty, which formed a central part of the appellant's challenge against the state’s case. The appellant asserted that the failure to call the doctor could not be overlooked, as it was critical to proving the necessary intent for the conviction under the requisite legal standards ([5]).

**I – Issues**  
The core legal issues presented in this case included:

1. **Self-Defense**: Whether the appellant acted in self-defense in response to the alleged unlawful attack by the complainant and his companions ([10]).
2. **Medical Evidence**: The implications of the medical report's ambiguity, particularly whether the failure to call the examining doctor constituted a fatal flaw in the State's case ([5]).
3. **Credibility of Witnesses**: Determining the weight of contradictory evidence offered by the complainant and the appellant ([7], [30]).
4. **Burden of Proof**: The standard of proof necessary for the conviction, specifically whether the State proved the appellant's guilt beyond a reasonable doubt ([10], [11]).

**R – Rules**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) relied on several legal principles in deciding this case:

1. **Self-Defense Doctrine**: For self-defense to be valid, the accused must demonstrate that he faced an unlawful and imminent threat, and the defensive response must not exceed what is reasonably necessary ([10]).
2. **Burden of Proof**: The State bears the onus of establishing the accused’s guilt beyond a reasonable doubt, as articulated in decisions such as *S v Difford* 1937 AD 370 and *S v V* 2000 (1) SACR 453 (SCA) ([11]).
3. **Credibility Assessment**: The court is tasked with evaluating witness credibility, accounting for contradictions and inconsistencies in testimony without immediately dismissing a witness’s testimony as unreliable due to minor discrepancies ([7], [30]).

**A – Application**  
The court's analysis began with the credible testimony that showed the appellant was physically assaulted before he used his firearm. The court emphasized the importance of the context of the confrontation and the appellant’s response during a highly charged situation ([8]).

In regards to the medical evidence, although it contained some ambiguity, the court determined that the direct evidence from the complainant and corroborative witnesses provided sufficient basis for the conviction. However, dissenting Judge Bosielo highlighted that the medical report's incongruence should have prompted the trial court to call the doctor for clarification, arguing it constituted a significant oversight in ensuring justice ([5], [34]). 

The court found that the contradictions in the complainant's account raised serious doubt about his credibility. Notably, the issue surrounding why the complainant delayed reporting the incident and various inconsistencies in his statements were crucial in assessing the reliability of his testimony. The Supreme Court of Appeal reiterated that a reasonable doubt cast upon the State's version due to these contradictions should favor the appellant ([5], [30]). 

Judge Bosielo noted that the trial court appeared to accept the complainant's testimony without fully addressing the apparent contradictions, particularly regarding the nature of the confrontation at the Engen garage ([20], [30]). 

Ultimately, the SCA found that the trial court's reliance on the ambiguous medical report, coupled with the lack of explanation from the attending physician, undermined the conviction. Thus, the dissenting opinion underscored the necessity to ensure that all relevant evidence, particularly from expert witnesses, is acted upon to uphold the integrity of the judicial process ([40]).

**C – Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal, by majority, upheld the appeal and set aside the appellant's conviction, determining that the State had failed to meet its burden of proof beyond a reasonable doubt due to reliance on contradictory and inconclusive evidence ([12]). The court also noted critical procedural aspects concerning trial delays, questioning the adequacy of judicial processes which allowed for an extended period before the final resolution of the case ([14]). 

In acknowledging dissent, Judge Bosielo emphasized the need for proactive judicial oversight in matters where medical evidence is pivotal to determining self-defence claims. The dissent ultimately called for clearer procedural adherence to the proper engagement of expert medical testimony, reaffirming the principle that a fair trial is predicated on the comprehensive and diligent pursuit of evidence by the judiciary ([40]–[43]). 

This case underlines an important precedent regarding the nuances of self-defence, the implications of medical evidence in violent crime cases, and the judicial responsibility to ensure a balanced and fair evaluation of testimony.
