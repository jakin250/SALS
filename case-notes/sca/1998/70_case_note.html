REPUBLIEK VAN SUID-AFRIKA evdw/ Saaknommer: 569/96 NKA

Willem Andreas Ferdinand Strydom (Appellant) initiated proceedings in the High Court of South Africa's Northern Cape Division seeking a court order regarding the sale of three properties from an estate, where Mev J W Duvenhage NO (First Respondent) acted as the executor.

Supreme Court of Appeal 1998

F – Facts

Willem Andreas Ferdinand Strydom (Appellant) initiated proceedings in the High Court of South Africa's Northern Cape Division seeking a court order regarding the sale of three properties from an estate, where Mev J W Duvenhage NO (First Respondent) acted as the executor. The Appellant contested that the purchase price, amounting to R432 496.06, included value-added tax (VAT), which he claimed should have been specified as a part of his purchase obligations. The Appellant's primary requests were for the court to declare that the purchase price included VAT, to compel the First Respondent to issue a tax invoice pursuant to section 20 of the Value-Added Tax Act 89 of 1991 ("VAT Act"), and to demand costs of the application.

The First Respondent opposed this application and sought a counter-order, specifically requesting that the Appellant pay a VAT amount of R43 249.60, which constituted 10% of the purchase price of the properties. She also sought rectification of clauses in the purchase agreement, suggesting that the terms should explicitly clarify the obligations regarding VAT and transfer duties.

Initially, the High Court dismissed the Appellant's application, ruling in favor of the First Respondent's counter-application, asserting that a tacit term should be inferred into the contract. This term would obligate the Appellant to pay the VAT due on the purchase. [paras 2-11].

I – Issues

The key legal questions arising from the case were:

  1. Whether the purchase agreement’s provisions were sufficiently clear regarding the inclusion of VAT in the purchase price.
  2. Whether a tacit term could be implied into the agreement obliging the Appellant to pay VAT, given the parties' apparent misunderstanding regarding tax liabilities.
  3. Whether the High Court was correct in affirming that such a tacit term fitted the parties' contractual intentions. [paras 2-11].

R – Rules

The legal framework relevant to the case includes the following:

  1. Value-Added Tax Act of 1991: This Act establishes that VAT is applicable on taxable supplies made by registered vendors and explains the conditions under which VAT is to be included in the price of goods and services. Notably, section 7(1)(a) of the VAT Act establishes the obligation of a vendor to charge VAT on sales. Under section 64(1), a price agreed upon by an entrepreneur is presumed to include VAT, irrespective of whether this is explicitly stated in the contract.
  2. Contract Law Principles: The doctrine of common intention within contract law dictates that contracts should reflect the actual and implied intentions of the parties. Courts have the right to imply terms necessary for the business efficacy of the contract, as highlighted in Alfred McAlpine & Son (Pty) Ltd v Transvaal Provincial Administration 1974 (3) SA 506 (A).
  3. Interpretation of Tacit Terms: The ability of courts to infer tacit terms is limited; they can only do so if the evidence indicates that the parties must have intended the term, particularly when such a term is necessary for the commercial efficacy of the agreement. This principle is recognized in several judicial precedents. [paras 9-12].

A – Application

In examining the evidence, the court recognized that neither party explicitly referred to VAT within the terms of the original purchase agreement. The First Respondent’s attempt to infer the necessity for VAT payment based on the supposed common intention between the parties was contentious.

The court dismissed the view that VAT was inherently included in the contract price simply because it is a transaction between registered vendors. It evaluated the applicability of section 64(1) of the VAT Act, acknowledging the context of how the parties perceived their responsibilities at the time of contract negotiation. The court expressed caution regarding the interpretation of obligations that did not form part of the original agreement, primarily underlining that the inference of a tacit term must be substantiated by clear evidence of mutual intent, which was not sufficiently established in this case.

Basson R, the presiding judge, opined that the reasoning from the lower court regarding the need for an inferred term served to create obligations not explicitly agreed upon by the parties. Ultimately, there was no conclusive evidence that both parties would have secured an avenue for VAT payment had they been aware of its application. Thus, the decision of the High Court to impose this implicit obligation was deemed unjustifiable. [paras 11-14].

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the High Court's decision affirming the appeal made by Strydom. The court declared that the Appellant was within his rights to assert that VAT was not part of his contractual obligations based on the established agreement. Consequently, the court ruled that the First Respondent’s counter-application was dismissed, with costs awarded in favor of the Appellant. The ruling emphasizes the importance of clarity in contract terms concerning tax obligations and exhibits a judicial reluctance to impose obligations not explicitly excised from the parties' expressed intentions. Thus, the judgement reinforces that both parties must articulate their tax liabilities clearly in contractual agreements to mitigate future disputes. [paras 15].
