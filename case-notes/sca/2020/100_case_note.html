Van Meyeren v Cloete (636/2019) [2020] ZASCA 100

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 100

F - Facts

The case of Van Meyeren v Cloete revolves around an incident that occurred on 18 February 2017, when the respondent, Mr. Cloete, was attacked by three dogs owned by the appellant, Mr. Van Meyeren, while he was lawfully walking down R Street in Port Elizabeth. Mr. Cloete suffered severe injuries, including the amputation of his left arm, as a consequence of the attack ([1]–[2]).

Mr. Cloete's claim was based primarily on the actio de pauperie, with an alternative claim for negligence. The dog attack occurred while Mr. Cloete was pulling a trolley after completing his gardening job. He had not provoked the dogs, nor did he have any specific interactions with them immediately before the incident ([3]–[4]). Eyewitness accounts revealed that the dogs were decidedly aggressive, overwhelming Mr. Cloete and even attacking a passer-by who intervened ([5]–[6]).

In the proceedings, Mr. Van Meyeren attempted to argue that an unknown intruder had tampered with the gate through which the dogs escaped, therefore absolving him of liability under the strict liability principle associated with the actio de pauperie. There were contradictory testimonies regarding the security of the gate, alleged tampering, and how the dogs gained access to the street ([8]–[10]).

The trial court, presided over by Judge Lowe in the Eastern Cape Division, rejected the appellant's claims, affirming Mr. Cloete's case based on the actio de pauperie and awarded him a declaratory order along with costs ([2]).

I - Issues

The primary legal issue at stake in this case is whether the defense of third-party negligence that led to the dogs escaping, as posited by Mr. Van Meyeren, can provide a valid basis for exemption from strict liability under the actio de pauperie. Specifically, the court needed to determine if the existence of such a defense could justify extending the principles established in Lever v Purdy ([20]–[22]).

Moreover, the court was tasked with interpreting whether the strictly held principles of animal liability should be extended to include incidental negligence of third-party individuals not directly in control of the animal ([21]–[24]).

R - Rules

The foundational principle in South African law regarding the liability of animal owners is encapsulated in the actio de pauperie. As established in O'Callaghan NO v Chaplin, the doctrine imposes strict liability on an animal's owner for harm caused by the animal when the injured party was lawfully present and without provocation ([15]).

In the case of Lever v Purdy, two categories were discussed regarding a third party's potential to absolve an owner from liability: (1) when the third party's positive conduct leads to the animal's behavior that causes harm, and (2) when a third party’s negligence allows the animal to cause harm. Mr. Van Meyeren's argument sought an extension of this rule under the assumption that negligence of a third party not in the animal's control should also absolve him from liability if their actions enabled the harm to occur ([22]–[26]).

A - Application

The court analyzed Mr. Van Meyeren’s contentions concerning the alleged intruder and the supposed tampering of the security gate. The evidence presented was deemed speculative, with significant inconsistencies regarding the lock's condition and the gate's status at the time of the incident ([10]–[11]).

The court highlighted that the judge's initial acceptance of the intruder explanation was flawed, given that no evidence robustly supported the proposition that such an intrusion had occurred. The burden of proof lay with Mr. Van Meyeren to demonstrate that a third party's actions were indeed responsible for the dogs escaping and that this should exempt him from liability under the actio de pauperie ([12]–[14]).

Moreover, the statutory principles governing strict liability were reaffirmed; the mere absence of fault on the owner's part does not negate liability once the prerequisites of the actio de pauperie are met ([34]–[36]). The court reiterated that the rationale behind strict liability is to ensure that the owner of the animal bears financial responsibility for its behavior towards innocent third parties, especially when such parties are without fault ([42]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Mr. Van Meyeren's appeal, upholding the decision made by the trial court. The court held that the principles of liability under the actio de pauperie were not subject to extension based on the alleged negligence of third parties not in control of the animal. The ruling reinforces the strict liability doctrine in cases involving domesticated animals and underscores the inherent responsibility that accompanies animal ownership.

The judgment serves as a crucial reference point in the context of animal liability, reiterating that owners are held liable regardless of whether they were negligent, thereby prioritizing the victim's need for recompense over the owner's potential defenses ([43]). This ruling has significant implications for future cases involving dog attacks and contributes to the ongoing discourse about the balance between property rights, personal safety, and public liability in South Africa's legal framework.
