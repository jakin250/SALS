# FIRAC Case Note: B[…] v B[…] (259/2023) [2024] ZASCA 116

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–5])

The case at hand involves an appeal against a decision of the North West Division of the High Court regarding the variation of a settlement agreement following a divorce between the appellant, M[….] B[…], and the respondent, R[…] B[...]. The appellant and respondent were married on November 4, 2000, out of community of property with the accrual system. Following the initiation of divorce proceedings in the Regional Court at Klerksdorp, a final decree was issued on March 9, 2021, which incorporated a settlement agreement signed by both parties.

Subsequently, the respondent sought to vary the settlement agreement on July 1, 2021, claiming that the accrual amount had been miscalculated due to a common mistake made by both parties. Particularly, the respondent contested clauses 3.2 and 3.5 of the agreement, with clause 3.2 stipulating a payment of R2,650,000 to the appellant in settlement of the accrual claim and clause 3.5 affirming ownership of specific assets. The Regional Court granted the variation without oral argument, allowing for the payment amount to be recalibrated based on the input of a liquidator, a stipulation not sought by either party.

The appellant subsequently appealed this decision in the High Court, which dismissed the appeal on grounds that it was interlocutory and not definitively resolving the parties' rights, as the appointment of a liquidator had not yet been concluded. This ruling prompted the appellant to seek special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA), which categorized the appeal as worthy of oral argument under Section 17(2)(d) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013.

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 2–6])

The primary issues debated in this appeal revolve around:

1. **The Existence of a Common Mistake:** Was there a common mistake between the parties when they signed the settlement agreement, specifically related to the clauses that the respondent sought to vary?
   
2. **Appealability of the Regional Court's Order:** Was the order of the Regional Court, which varied the settlement agreement by appointing a liquidator, appealable under the established criteria for appealable orders in South Africa?

3. **Legitimacy of Variation Claims:** Did the respondent have sufficient grounds to justify the variation of the settlement agreement based on alleged miscalculations attributed to his legal representation?

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 9–13])

The Court's ruling rested on several legal principles and precedents:

1. **Common Mistake Doctrine:** A party seeking to rescind an agreement on the basis of a common mistake must demonstrate that the mistake was fundamental to the parties' consent. The Court referenced **George v Fairmead (Pty) Ltd** ([11]), which illustrates the necessity of proving that a reasonable belief in a mutual mistake existed.

2. **Criteria for Appealability:** The definition of an appealable order was clarified in **Zweni v Minister of Law and Order** ([14]), which asserts that an appealable order must be final, definitive of the parties' rights, and substantive in resolving a portion of the relief claimed.

3. **Principles Governing Settlement Agreements:** The Court reaffirmed that settlement agreements serve to ensure final resolution of disputes, conferring res judicata on the issues resolved through compromise ([9]).

4. **Justus Error Requirements:** Drawing on **Sonap Petroleum (SA) (Pty) Ltd v Pappadogianis** ([12]), the test for justus error includes considerations of misrepresentation and the ensuing effects on both parties, necessitating analysis of whether either party misled the other.

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 13–15])

In adjudicating whether a common mistake existed, the Court found no misrepresentation by the appellant, emphasizing that lengthy negotiations had led to the voluntarily signed settlement agreement. The respondent’s subsequent change of heart—claiming processing errors by his attorneys—did not qualify as a common mistake; rather, it represented a unilateral error stemming from the respondent's own decision-making processes ([13]).

Regarding the appealability of the Regional Court's initial decision, the Court concluded that the decision was appealable given its final effect, contrary to the High Court's determination that it was interlocutory. The appointment of a liquidator—unrequested by either party—was deemed without justifiable basis, thus necessitating a review of the appeal ([15]).

The Court, thus, returned to the foundational principles governing settlement agreements, concluding that these agreements extinguish prior duties and obligations, underscoring the finality essential to avoid the protraction of litigation, as stated in **Road Accident Fund v Taylor** ([9]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 16–16])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appellant’s appeal, concluding that the High Court's dismissal was erroneous. The Court found no grounds for the variation based on common mistake, determining that the calculations the respondent questioned did not substantiate a claim for variation. The order of the High Court was thus set aside, and the original settlement agreement was reinstated in its entirety.

This case underscores critical themes in contract law, particularly relating to the sanctity of settlement agreements in divorce proceedings, the rigid standards required to prove common mistakes, and the necessity for clarity in legal representation involving substantial financial and personal stakes. The ruling reinforces the principle that parties must bear the consequences of decisions made on the advice of competent legal counsel, thereby enhancing the legal framework surrounding settlement negotiations and agreements in South Africa.
