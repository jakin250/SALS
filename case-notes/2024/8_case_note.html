Regenesys Management (Pty) Ltd t/a Regenesys v Ilunga and Others [2024] ZACC 8

By June 24, 2015, after rejecting the majority of employees’ applications, Regenesys communicated their retrenchments, which took effect on July 31, 2015 ([12]–[13]).

Constitutional Court 2024 [2024] ZACC 8

F – Facts

This matter concerns an appeal and a cross-appeal involving Regenesys Management (Pty) Ltd (the Applicant) and five former employees (the First to Fifth Respondents), specifically Sibongile Charlotte Ilunga, Maria Antonia Oliveira dos Santos, Mapaseka Patience Nkodi, Nompumelelo Mahlangu, and others. The backdrop of the case dates back to June 2015 when Regenesys expressed concerns about its financial viability, indicating the need for retrenchments due to a wage bill comprising 43% of its expenses. On June 17, 2015, an initial meeting was held to inform employees about potential retrenchments ([7]–[8]). Subsequently, the affected employees were informed of a new organisational structure and were invited to apply for their positions in the new structure within a very limited timeframe ([11]).

By June 24, 2015, after rejecting the majority of employees’ applications, Regenesys communicated their retrenchments, which took effect on July 31, 2015 ([12]–[13]). The employees contended that their dismissals were both procedurally and substantively unfair, prompting them to refer the dispute to the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA) on August 13, 2015, although this conciliation process was unsuccessful ([15]).

The subsequent Labour Court ruling in February 2020 found that all dismissals were procedurally unfair, with some also being substantively unfair. It ordered reinstatements for the procedurally unfair dismissals and compensation for others ([19]–[20]). Regenesys applied to the Labour Appeal Court, challenging the Labour Court’s findings, particularly regarding the jurisdiction to adjudicate procedural fairness claims based on operational requirements per section 189A(18) of the Labour Relations Act (LRA) ([23]).

I – Issues

The core legal issues pertain to:

  1. Whether the Labour Appeal Court erred in concluding that the Labour Court lacked jurisdiction to adjudicate disputes regarding procedural fairness in the context of dismissals based on operational requirements, as dictated by section 189A(18) of the LRA ([29], [43]).
  2. The meaning and applicability of section 189A(13) concerning the request for compensation as a standalone remedy for procedural unfairness amid ongoing disputes about the substantive fairness of the dismissals ([54]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal frameworks involve:

  1. Labour Relations Act, 1995 (LRA): Sections pertinent to this case include:
  • Section 189A: which outlines the procedures for retrenchment and stipulates that an employer must engage in a fair consultation process when dismissing a large number of employees due to operational requirements. Specifically, subsection (18) bars the Labour Court from adjudicating procedural unfairness in dismissals referred under section 191(5)(b)(ii) without complying with fair procedures ([4], [146]).
  • Section 189A(13): allows parties to approach the Labour Court for orders if an employer does not comply with a fair procedure ([66], [92]).
  1. Constitutional Principles: The right to fair labour practices as enshrined in Section 23 of the South African Constitution, which plays a fundamental role in interpreting these statutory provisions ([31]).

A – Application

The Court assessed whether the Labour Appeal Court misinterpreted section 189A(18), which purportedly ousts the Labour Court's jurisdiction over the procedural fairness claims, as argued by Regenesys. The Constitutional Court found that section 189A(18) pertains only to cases referred to it under section 191(5)(b)(ii) and does not exclude other procedures, like those presented under section 189A(13).

Additionally, the Labour Court’s finding of procedural unfairness stemmed from the rushed and inadequate consultations conducted by Regenesys. It further found that the claims for compensation, as articulated in section 189A(13)(d), could function as standalone remedies as established in prior cases (e.g., SA Five Engineering) and clarified their role as appropriate when the conditions for the orders outlined in (a)-(c) are inappropriate ([245]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court's holding was multifaceted:

  1. The appeal by Regenesys was dismissed, reaffirming the Labour Court's conclusion of procedural unfairness and ordering that compensation is warranted under section 189A(13)(d) as a legitimate response to procedural deficiencies ([242]).
  2. The cross-appeal by the employees was upheld, confirming the Labour Court's jurisdiction to adjudicate procedural fairness claims through a robust understanding of their rights as embodied in section 23 of the Constitution ([242]).

This judgment draws crucial attention to the urgency and ongoing nature of fair procedures in operational retrenchment cases, affirming employee protections against procedural shortcomings in the dismissal process while ensuring that employees retain recourse to the courts for adjudication ([142], [219]). The implications of this judgment resonate throughout the labour relations landscape, highlighting the necessity for compliance with fair procedures to uphold the rights of workers amidst operational challenges faced by employers.
