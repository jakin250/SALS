Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# Case Note: Group Five Construction (Pty) Limited v Minister of Water Affairs and Forestry

**FIRAC Method**

**F – Facts**  
The appellant, Group Five Construction (Pty) Limited (“Group Five”), initiated a claim against the respondent, the Minister of Water Affairs and Forestry (“Minister”), concerning unpaid moneys related to a contract for the construction of the Injaka Dam and associated works under the Sabie River Government Water Scheme. Specifically, Group Five sought moneys purportedly owed under clause 51 of the contract, which stipulates the conditions under which a contractor can claim additional payments or compensation. The appellant's claim consisted of four specific claims, while a fifth claim, referred to as claim E, was not part of the judicial review ([1]).

The Minister interposed a special plea of prescription, which warranted the lower court to hear this issue separately. To assist in adjudicating this matter, the parties agreed upon a list of facts and presented evidence. A critical aspect of the case revolved around the interpretation of the contract, particularly in light of two amendments made between the parties, which altered clause 61 to introduce a new dispute resolution mechanism. This mechanism required disputes to be submitted to a dispute review board that aimed to resolve conflicts arising from the engineer's rejection of claims submitted by the contractor ([2]).

The essence of the amendment dictated that while recommendations made by the dispute review board would be binding upon acceptance, any unresolved matters could subsequently be escalated to court provided that a written notice was given within 60 days by the party wishing to proceed to court. Importantly, if no notice was issued, the engineer's decision would become final ([3]).

The principal question to address was whether the appellant’s claims were “due” upon the dispatch of the written notice or only after the completion of the contract works, as argued by Group Five. Clarifying this issue was imperative as prescription, per section 12(1) of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, stipulates that the period for the commencement of prescription begins as soon as the debt becomes due ([4]).

The court of first instance, presided over by Southwood J, determined that the appellant's claims were indeed prescribed, a decision that Group Five contested on appeal ([5]).

**I – Issues**  
The legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal were primarily twofold:  
1. When did the appellant’s claims become due for the purposes of prescription under section 12(1) of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969?  
2. Did the interpretation of the contract and the accompanying dispute resolution mechanism alter the timeline from which prescription would commence? ([4])

**R – Rules**  
The relevant legal principle is anchored in section 12(1) of the Prescription Act, which articulates that the prescription period begins to run once the debt is due. The interpretation of the contract, particularly the amendments concerning dispute resolution, held significant weight in determining the procedural timelines for claims ([4]). The court's reliance on precedent set by other cases involving similar interpretations of contractual obligations further underscored the importance of clear definitions within agreements ([5]).

**A – Application**  
The court analyzed whether the claims advanced by Group Five were indeed “due” at the point when the written notice was given or if they only became due post-completion of the contract works. The appellant contended that their claims represented estimates rather than obligations due, implying that they were still in a negotiation phase. In contrast, the court found that the claims were complete and ripe for cession as soon as the written notice was initiated. The court upheld that the validity of Southwood J’s judgment should be sustained, emphasizing that the earlier judgments recognized the rigidity of the contract’s terms concerning the engineer's decisions ([5]).

The court dissected the relationship between the contractual amendments and their implications on the obligation's enforceability. It was determined that as per the contract, unless an assertion of intent to escalate to court was manifested within the prescribed timeframe, the claims would not survive the test of prescription. Southwood J’s interpretation of the contract was reaffirmed as satisfactory and justified ([5]).

**C – Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Group Five's appeal, affirming the decision of the North Gauteng High Court, which had found the claims to be prescribed. This dismissal confirms the necessity for clarity in contract terms and the perils associated with neglecting prescribed timelines dictated by contract clauses. The ruling serves as a critical precedent concerning the interpretation of contractual obligations and the interplay between contractual dispute resolution mechanisms and statutory prescription periods ([6]).

This case reinforces the essential duty for parties engaged in complex contractual arrangements to ensure clarity and understanding of the implications of dispute resolution processes in relation to their rights to claim and the timing thereof.
