Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 155 ### F – Facts In the matter of Gwiba v St

Case Note: Gwiba v State (117/2019) [2019] ZASCA 155

F – Facts

In the matter of Gwiba v State, the appellant, Joseph Bongani Gwiba, was convicted in the Regional Court at Bothaville of three counts of robbery with aggravating circumstances, despite his plea of not guilty ([para 1]). He was sentenced to 15 years imprisonment, and two co-accused were also convicted, although their cases do not pertain to this appeal ([para 1]). Following his conviction, Gwiba sought leave to appeal his sentence and convictions under section 309B of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA) ([para 2]). The regional magistrate denied this application, prompting Gwiba to petition the Free State Division of the High Court for leave, which was also refused ([para 2]).

Subsequently, Gwiba approached the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) for special leave to appeal the high court's refusal, and on 13 December 2018, the SCA granted this special leave effective for the purpose of allowing Gwiba to contest the refusal ([para 2]). The procedural history outlined shows an emphasis on rectifying the initial refusals of leave to appeal by the lower courts. The SCA's role was limited to determining if the high court erred in denying leave without delving into the merits of the appeal itself ([para 7]).

I – Issues

The principal legal issue before the SCA was whether the high court had correctly refused to grant leave to appeal against the convictions and sentence imposed by the regional court ([para 7]). This involved the interpretation of the legal standard of "reasonable prospects of success," which determines whether a higher court ought to grant an appeal ([para 9]). The appeal does not consider the substantive merits of the underlying case, but rather the procedural question of the legitimacy of the high court's refusal to allow an appeal ([para 7], [para 10]).

R – Rules

The governing statutes for this case include:

  1. Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (CPA):
  • Section 309B: Requires an accused to file an application for leave to appeal from a lower court to the regional magistrate.
  • Section 309C: Allows for a petition to the High Court if a lower court refuses an appeal ([para 3]).
  1. Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013:
  • The high court's refusal to grant leave constitutes a decision appealable only with special leave from the SCA, in accordance with Section 16(1)(b), which pertains to appeals from decisions rendered on appeal by a division of the High Court ([para 6]).

The interpretation of these sections indicated that the SCA had jurisdiction to review the high court’s decision due to the procedural restrictions that existed following the enactment of the Superior Courts Act and the repeal of the previous Supreme Court Act ([para 4], [para 5]).

A – Application

In their analysis, the SCA examined whether the high court erred in refusing leave to appeal based on the existence of "reasonable prospects of success" ([para 9]). The court undertook a review of the key evidence presented during the original trial, noting significant deficiencies in the identification of Gwiba as the perpetrator. Crucially, it was observed that there was no eyewitness testimony confirming his presence at the scene of the crime, and crucial items purportedly linked to the robbery were not definitively found in his vehicle as claimed by the prosecution ([para 10]).

The SCA highlighted that the magistrate's mindset and the high court’s assessment did not adequately reflect a robust evaluation of these evidential concerns. They inferred that the previously denied leave to appeal could be reconsidered because reasonable prospects for success were indeed present, related to the contested nature of key evidence against Gwiba ([para 11]).

Both the appellant's representatives and the state's counsel acknowledged that there were prospects of success on appeal, which reaffirmed that the original high court ruling lacked sufficient justification for its refusal ([para 12]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal against the refusal of the high court to grant leave to appeal. The court found that the high court should have acknowledged the reasonable prospects of success on Gwiba's appeal regarding his convictions and sentence ([para 13]). As a result, the SCA set aside the high court's order and substituted it with a directive to grant Gwiba leave to appeal to the Free State Division of the High Court against his convictions and sentence ([para 13]).

The implications of this ruling underscore the SCA's commitment to ensuring that all accused individuals have a fair opportunity to challenge convictions, particularly when there exist substantive doubts about the strength of the evidence against them. This case serves as a reminder of the judiciary's role in safeguarding the rights of defendants within the legal architecture of South Africa, reiterating the procedural safeguards established by legislative frameworks like the CPA and the Superior Courts Act.
