Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 26 F – Facts The appellant, Khumbulani Col

FIRAC Case Note: Ndlovu v S (925/2016) [2017] ZASCA 26

F – Facts
The appellant, Khumbulani Collen Ndlovu, was involved in a planned robbery on 14 August 2008 in Glencoe, KwaZulu-Natal, during which a security guard was murdered ([para 2]). Although he did not participate in the robbery itself, he had planned it, was aware of the potential for violence, and did not withdraw from the plan. Ndlovu was charged with two counts of robbery with aggravating circumstances, one count of murder, and one count of attempted murder, to which he pled guilty under section 112(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (the CPA) to robbery and murder, leading to the withdrawal of the remaining charges ([paras 2–3]).

The trial court considered Ndlovu's personal circumstances, noting he was a 37-year-old first offender, a widower, and a father of three, before imposing a combined sentence of 20 years’ imprisonment for murder and robbery, with a non-parole period of 13 years ([paras 4–5]). After the trial court's decision, Ndlovu appealed against the sentence, specifically contesting the imposition of the non-parole period without proper submissions from either party. The appeal was first dismissed by the KwaZulu-Natal Division and subsequently led to a further appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 6–7]).

I – Issues
The primary legal issue was whether the trial court correctly exercised its discretion under section 276B of the CPA by imposing a non-parole period without inviting submissions from the parties ([para 1]). Additionally, it was necessary to determine if there were exceptional circumstances justifying the non-parole period and whether the imposition of the non-parole period as outlined in section 276B(2) was adhered to appropriately ([paras 11–12]).

R – Rules
Section 276B(1) of the CPA allows a court, when sentencing a person to imprisonment for two years or longer, to fix a non-parole period (the "non-parole period"). This period must not exceed two-thirds of the term of imprisonment or 25 years, whichever is shorter ([para 7]). It is reiterated in case law, specifically S v Pauls, that such decisions should be made with caution, and a court must provide both the State and defense an opportunity to address whether exceptional circumstances exist that would warrant such an order ([para 10]).

The court referenced the authority of Jimmale v S, which emphasizes the need for a proper evidential basis when deciding on a non-parole period, as it impacts future release decisions and should thus be approached with deliberate care ([para 10]). Additionally, it is noted that failure to allow a convicted individual the opportunity to address the court on the imposition of a non-parole period can amount to a misdirection ([para 11]).

A – Application
In deciding the case, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that the trial court misdirected itself by imposing a non-parole period without properly informing either party, which contravened the requirements established in prior case law ([para 12]). It was also noted that the trial court improperly applied section 276B by imposing the non-parole period only concerning the murder sentence rather than the effective term of imprisonment ([para 12]).

Counsel for the State conceded that there was a misdirection regarding the non-parole period. Ultimately, the Supreme Court highlighted the lack of clear reasoning from the trial court regarding the imposition of the non-parole period and pointed to the necessity of explicit factors relating to any exceptional circumstances that may have justified such an order ([para 12]).

C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, specifically concerning the imposition of the non-parole period of 13 years, which was set aside. However, the appeal against the overall sentence was dismissed ([para 14]). The court's decision emphasized the importance of proper judicial processes in sentencing, particularly regarding non-parole periods, reinforcing that such determinations require input from both the prosecution and defense. The case clarifies procedural expectations in South African sentencing law and underscores the discretion a court exercises in matters of parole eligibility. As a result, the appellant remains sentenced to 20 years’ imprisonment, with the non-parole issue rectified ([paras 14–14]).

The implications of this ruling extend to ensuring adherence to fair judicial practices in sentencing, upholding both the rights of the accused and the integrity of the legal process in South Africa.
