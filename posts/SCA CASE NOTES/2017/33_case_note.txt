Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

### FIRAC Case Note: **Media 24 Limited v Du Plessis (127/2016) [2017] ZASCA 33**

#### F – Facts:

The case arises from an appeal against a defamation ruling by the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, where Bekker du Plessis (the respondent) initiated proceedings against Media 24 Limited, trading as Daily Sun, and its editor Themba Khumalo (the appellants). Du Plessis sought R500,000 in damages, alleging that a published article containing false claims about his conduct was defamatory ([paras 1–2]).

On 29 October 2010, the Daily Sun published an article detailing an incident where Orel Khoza, accused of stealing onions, was allegedly locked in a cold storage room at du Plessis's direction. The article’s sensational headline read: "Frozen for an onion," with a subheading claiming that Khoza was in the cold room for two hours ([paras 4–6]). The article suggested that the treatment Khoza received implied du Plessis was not only cruel but also racially insensitive, as it portrayed him as someone placing the value of an onion above a person's well-being ([paras 5–6]).

The appellants admitted to the publication but denied the article's defamatory nature, contending its truth and public interest ([paras 7–10]). In defense, they claimed the reporting was aligned with journalistic privilege and, therefore, justified ([paras 20–21]).

The High Court ultimately found in favor of du Plessis, awarding him R80,000 for damages, which led to this appeal due to disagreements over the facts and the reasonableness of the publication ([paras 8–9]).

#### I – Issues:

1. **Defamation**: Did the article published by the Daily Sun constitute defamation against Bekker du Plessis, and were the allegations substantially true and published in the public interest? ([paras 1–3])
2. **Reasonableness**: Was the publication of the article reasonable given the circumstances surrounding its creation and dissemination? ([paras 1–2])
3. **Damages**: Was the R80,000 award for damages excessive, necessitating judicial intervention? ([paras 1, 32–36]) 

#### R – Rules:

1. **Defamation Law**: Under South African law, a plaintiff must establish that a statement was defamatory, that it was published, that it referred to the plaintiff, and that it was false ([paras 20–21]). The defendant bears the onus of proving defenses such as truth, public benefit, and reasonableness of publication ([paras 22, 28]). 
2. **Test of Reasonable Reader**: The meaning attributed to statements is derived from the perspective of a reasonable reader of ordinary intelligence and not someone with a "morbid or suspicious mind" ([paras 15–16]). 
3. **Assessment of Damages**: The trial court has broad discretion in awarding damages; interference by an appellate court is only warranted under specific conditions such as misdirection on the law or substantial differences in damage assessment ([para 32]). 

#### A – Application:

The Supreme Court's analysis began by affirming that the contested statements were indeed defamatory and that du Plessis was clearly referenced within the article ([paras 1, 15]). The court examined the profound inaccuracies in the article, concluding that the appellants failed to substantiate their claims that the article was substantially true. Key inaccuracies included the false assertion that Khoza had endured two hours of freezing conditions, was handcuffed, suffered from a nosebleed, and had his hair frozen. Such fabrications contributed significantly to the article's defamatory sting ([paras 22–23]).

On assessing the defense of publication in the public interest, the court referenced cases establishing that defamation cannot be justified if the statements are found to be substantially untrue ([para 22]). Concerning the reasonableness of publication, the appellants attempted to pivot responsibility by arguing that journalistic deadlines and the inherent nature of their readership limited their investigative capabilities. However, the court found this unconvincing, highlighting that the appellants did not take adequate steps to verify the claims made, which was a significant breach of their duty to ensure accurate reporting ([paras 29–31]).

In considering damages, the court noted that although the publication indeed caused harm, the R80,000 award was excessive, particularly given the context of du Plessis’s own involvement in the incident and the flawed nature of the claims made against him ([paras 32–36]). The justices concluded that a more appropriate amount would be R40,000, justifying a reduction in damages.

#### C – Conclusion:

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the appeal was upheld, resulting in the set aside of the High Court’s ruling in favor of du Plessis, replacing the damages award with R40,000. The court found that the publication of the defamatory statement was neither substantially true nor reasonably executed, thereby infringing du Plessis's reputation in a serious manner. The ruling sends a clear message about the necessity for media professionals to adhere to factual precision and uphold ethical standards when reporting, particularly regarding sensationalized claims that can cause significant reputational harm. This case serves as a critical reference point in South African defamation law, emphasizing both the importance of truthful reporting and the careful balance that must be struck between freedom of the press and individual reputation ([paras 38]).
