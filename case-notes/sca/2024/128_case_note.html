Supreme Court of Appeal

Die Nederduitsch Hervormde Kerk van Afrika v. Die Wilge Hervormde Gemeente and Others (1089/2022)

In 2010 and 2011, some congregation members expressed dissatisfaction with the NHKA's stance on apartheid.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024

F - Facts

The case revolves around a protracted legal dispute involving the Nederduitsch Hervormde Kerk van Afrika (NHKA) and several of its former congregations (the respondents) regarding property rights linked to immovable properties registered under original congregations but later transferred to newly formed entities by dissatisfied members of those congregations. The respondents sought declaratory relief to confirm their authority to transfer these properties to new owners not affiliated with the NHKA ([1–7]).

In 2010 and 2011, some congregation members expressed dissatisfaction with the NHKA's stance on apartheid. Subsequently, a majority vote allowed these members, referred to as dissatisfied members, to donate and transfer properties from the NHKA congregations to newly formed entities outside the NHKA ([5]).

The Gauteng Division of the High Court ruled favorably on the respondents' claims, but raised a new issue of privity of contract, asserting that the NHKA lacked standing to challenge the validity of the transfers due to not being a party to the donation agreements. This led to the high court declaring the dispute moot ([10–14]).

Following this decision, a group of original congregations sought to intervene in the appeal, asserting their rights in the properties ([2], [19, 20]).

I - Issues

The main legal questions before the court were:

  1. Should the intervening parties (the four original congregations) be allowed to join the application for leave to appeal?
  2. Did the high court misdirect itself by introducing a new issue of privity of contract that was neither raised in the pleadings nor agreed upon by the parties?
  3. Did the NHKA have standing to assert its claims regarding the authority of congregations to transfer properties, given the high court's ruling ([1, 10–14], [21, 26])?

R - Rules

The court's decision hinged upon several important legal principles:

  1. Standing and Interest: The principle that parties seeking to intervene must demonstrate a direct and substantial interest that could be prejudiced by the court's decision ([17, 20]).
  2. Privity of Contract Doctrine: Under common law, only parties directly involved in a contract can sue or be sued on that contract. The trial judge's finding regarding privity of contract raised questions about the NHKA's ability to challenge the transfers, as it was not a signatory to any donation agreements ([11, 13]).
  3. Court's Authority to Raise Issues: The court is not permitted to introduce new questions not defined in the pleadings. A court can suggest new lines of argument but is bound to the issues framed by the parties ([21, 22]).
  4. Civil Litigation Overview: As established in Fischer and Another v Ramahlele and Others, it is the responsibility of the parties, not the court, to define their disputes ([22, 26]).

A - Application

In reviewing the appeal, the Supreme Court of Appeal held that:

  1. The original congregations, as titleholders to the properties, demonstrated a direct interest in the case; thus, they were entitled to intervene ([20, 21]).
  2. The introduction of the privity of contract issue by the high court was an inappropriate judicial intervention that prejudiced the NHKA. The original dispute was clearly defined in terms of whether the dissatisfied congregations had the authority, under NHKA's constitutional framework, to transfer the properties ([26, 27]).
  3. Since none of the original parties had raised the privity of contract issue in their pleadings, the high court misinterpreted the substantive foundation of the dispute. It failed to address the critical question of authority under NHKA's governing documents ([25, 28]).

Ultimately, the court ruled that the NHKA did have standing to challenge the transfers and ordered that the high court's decision be set aside and that the case return for proper adjudication ([29]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal granted the application for leave to intervene by the original congregations and upheld the appeal against the high court's ruling. The case was remitted for re-hearing at the high court by a different judge, and costs were awarded against the respondents for the intervention application, application for leave to appeal, and appeal ([4, 5, 29]).

This decision highlights essential principles concerning the roles of courts in determining disputes, standing, and the necessity of adhering to procedural frameworks established by the parties involved. It underscores the importance of correctly identifying the issues of law and fact that form the basis of litigation and serves as a significant precedent in the realm of ecclesiastical property rights within South African law.

In summary, the case reinforces fundamental doctrines of standing and the limitations on judicial discretion in raising issues not agreed upon by the litigants, ensuring that parties receive a fair opportunity to present their cases without undue interference. The implication of this judgment speaks to the integrity of the judicial process and the need for clarity in disputes involving complex ecclesiastical and property rights.
