Supreme Court of Appeal

De Leeuw v MEC Local Government and Housing, Free State [2006] SCA 88 (RSA)

This appeal arises from an application under the Abolition of Restrictions Act 84 of 1967 (‘the Act’) for amendments to the town planning scheme of Bloemfontein ([1]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2006

F - Facts

This appeal arises from an application under the Abolition of Restrictions Act 84 of 1967 (‘the Act’) for amendments to the town planning scheme of Bloemfontein ([1]). The application was directed to the first respondent, the Member of the Executive Council (‘MEC’) for local government and housing, Free State. The MEC’s decision to reject the application led to a review application in the High Court of Bloemfontein, which was dismissed by Van der Merwe R ([1]). The current appeal was brought with leave from this court.

The property in question is a business premises in Westdene, Bloemfontein, known as the Shoprite Checkers property, which is owned by a trust with the second, third, and fourth appellants serving as trustees. The first appellant is a consultant town and regional planner who prepared the application for amendment on behalf of the trustees. His involvement in the litigation subsequently raised questions about his standing, but this was not contested by the respondents ([2]).

As per the requirements set forth by the Act, notice of the application was duly published and submitted to the local authority, specifically the Mangaung Municipality. Initially, only the third respondent, the owner of the adjacent BMW building, objected to the application. However, the municipality later joined in, proposing ten conditions for the approval of the requested amendments ([3]).

In compliance with the Act, the Free State Development Board held a hearing that included a site inspection. During this hearing, only the first appellant testified, and counsel for the appellants and the third respondent presented their arguments. The third respondent raised concerns regarding the impact of the proposed development on traffic flow in the area ([4]).

In response, the appellants offered to submit an impact study conducted for a prior development, known as the Waterfront development, which related to a significantly larger area. This offer was rejected by the Development Board ([5]). The Development Board subsequently recommended to the MEC that the appellants' application for amendments should be approved. However, the MEC rejected this recommendation, citing the absence of a traffic impact study as a critical concern ([6]).

On appeal, the appellants’ grounds for review were articulated based on Section 6 of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (‘PAJA’) and were characterized as twofold. Firstly, they contended that the MEC's decision was unreasonable and lacked a rational connection to the information available ([7]). Secondly, they asserted that the procedures followed by the MEC were unjust ([7]).

The substantive ground of review involved arguing that the decision was irrational, whereas the procedural ground questioned whether the appellants should have been provided an opportunity to respond to the MEC’s concerns before his final decision ([8]).

I - Issues

The primary legal questions arising from this case include:

  1. Whether the MEC’s decision to refuse the application for amendments to the town planning scheme is reviewable on the grounds of irrationality and a lack of procedural fairness ([7]).
  2. The assessment of the merits concerning the need for a traffic impact study in relation to the application and the extent to which the appellants were afforded procedural justice ([11]).

R - Rules

The relevant legal rules encompass:

  1. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA): Particularly sections 6(2)(f)(ii)(cc) concerning the rationality requirement of decisions and section 6(2)(c) pertaining to the right to a fair administrative procedure ([7]).
  2. Abolition of Restrictions Act 84 of 1967: This Act governs applications for amendments to town planning schemes and outlines necessary procedures for such applications ([1]).

Precedent case law also informs this decision, emphasizing that substantive unreasonableness alone does not suffice for a review, unless the decision made by the administrative functionary was one that could not have reasonably been made based on the available information ([9]).

A - Application

The court began its analysis by considering the appellants’ substantive claim that the MEC's decision was irrational. The judgment emphasized that the MEC's concerns regarding the absence of a traffic impact study were logically connected to his final decision ([10]). The appellants argued that the previously conducted impact study sufficed; however, the court rejected this line of reasoning, asserting that the prior study did not specifically address the proposed development and therefore did not meet the MEC's requirements.

Ultimately, the court found that the MEC’s decision to reject the application could not be deemed irrational or unreasonable. The core dissent from the appellants rested on the belief that the MEC should have referred the application back to the Development Board. However, the court maintained that the ultimate decision was within the MEC’s purview and that there was no merit in suggesting that he acted irrationally by choosing not to refer the matter back ([10]).

On the procedural front, the appellants contended that the MEC should have provided them with his reasons before concluding his decision, allowing for an opportunity to address those concerns. The court noted that, while procedural fairness is crucial, the critical shortcoming—the absence of a traffic impact study—was a clear and evident issue that should have been apparent to an experienced town planner ([11]). The refusal to insist on a traffic impact study by the Development Board could not be attributed to the MEC's actions, thus absolving him from procedural unfairness claims based on that contention.

C - Conclusion

The appeal was ultimately dismissed, with the court affirming the MEC’s decision and holding that both the substantive and procedural grounds of review presented by the appellants lacked merit ([12]). The court underscored the importance of adequate information, particularly concerning traffic impacts, in considering town planning applications. The implications of this decision reinforce the need for appellants in town planning matters to present comprehensive, relevant data when seeking amendments. Moreover, the ruling highlights the administrative authority's discretion in deciding on such applications and the expectations of procedural fairness in administrative decision-making under PAJA.

The outcome demonstrates the delicate balance between administrative authority and the rights of applicants within planning contexts, emphasizing the administrative body's duty to ensure sound governance aligned with public interest concerns.
