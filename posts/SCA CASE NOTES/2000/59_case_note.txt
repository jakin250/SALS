# FIRAC Case Note

## Case Title: ABSA Bank Limited v Bond Equipment (Pretoria) (Pty) Limited
**Case No:** 580/98  
**Coram:** Smalberger, Harms, Schutz, Zulman JJA and Mpati AJA  
**Heard:** 8 September 2000  
**Delivered:** September 2000  
**Subject:** Vicarious responsibility of plaintiff for theft by employee

---

### F – Facts

The respondent, Bond Equipment (Pretoria) (Pty) Limited (the "plaintiff"), initiated a legal action against Absa Bank Limited (the "defendant") for damages related to the theft of thirteen cheques by its employee, A.J. Steyn ([para 1]). The particulars of the claim revealed that these cheques, which were crossed and endorsed as "not transferable" or "not negotiable," were never endorsed or processed by the plaintiff ([para 3.3]). Steyn unlawfully deposited these cheques into an account he controlled under the name of Bond Equipment (Pretoria) with the defendant ([para 4]). The bank, acting in its capacity as a collecting bank, was alleged to have negligently collected payments on these cheques without appropriate verification ([para 6]).

The parties submitted an agreed statement of facts, which delineated the circumstances surrounding the theft and raised several legal questions regarding liability and causation ([para 2]).

### I – Issues

The appeal raised several interrelated legal issues:

1. Is the plaintiff in law vicariously liable for the actions of Steyn, its employee, who stole the cheques?
2. Is the defendant liable for its conduct based on the negligence of its employees in light of Steyn’s actions?
3. Does the plaintiff's conduct in permitting the theft constitute a proximate cause of its loss? ([para 4]).

### R – Rules

The foundational legal principles at issue involved the doctrine of vicarious liability, defined as the responsibility of an employer for the negligent acts of an employee when those acts occur within the course and scope of employment. This principle was elucidated in *Mkize v Martens* 1914 AD 382, where it was stated that a master is only liable for the torts of a servant committed in the course of employment ([para 6]). The test for vicarious liability includes both a subjective and objective assessment of the employee’s intention and the nature of their actions vis-à-vis their official duties ([para 9]).

Previous cases were referenced to establish legal precedence, including:

- *Morris v C W Martin & Sons Ltd* [1966] 1 QB 716, where the court differentiated between the “frolic of his own” and professional duties on behalf of an employer.
- *Columbus Joint Venture v Absa Bank Ltd* 2000 (2) SA 491, which reiterated that a theft by an employee constitutes a complete severance of the employer-employee relationship at the time of the act ([para 8]).

### A – Application

The court analyzed the essential facts to determine whether Steyn had acted within the course and scope of his employment when he committed the theft ([para 9]). The findings indicated that:

- Steyn was expressly authorized to handle delivery of the cheques but utilized this authority to commit theft, which was against the interests of the plaintiff ([para 6]).
- The court ruled out vicarious liability, emphasizing that Steyn's actions were purely for his benefit and not aligned with the duties of his position ([para 9]). The fact that the employee misused his position to commit a crime negated any connection with the employer’s affairs.

The court further noted that the defendant's liability arose from a negligent act in collecting the cheques, but this could not be attributed to the plaintiff since Steyn's actions were criminal in nature and outside the scope of employment ([para 10]).

The final conclusion was that the theft did not introduce a clear causal connection to the plaintiff's loss—Steyn had completely severed his employment obligations when he unlawfully appropriated the cheques ([para 11]).

### C – Conclusion

The appeal was dismissed, affirming the lower court’s ruling that neither the plaintiff could be held vicariously liable for Steyn’s conduct nor could the defendant bank be held liable for negligence in light of the circumstances ([para 12]). The judgment emphasizes the critical distinction between a servant's authorized duties and actions performed wholly outside those duties for personal gain. This outcome underscores the importance of the intent and authority of employees in cases involving theft and vicarious liability, reinforcing that theft is distinctly outside the boundaries of employment responsibilities. The implications of this decision signal a cautious approach to claims of vicarious liability, especially where dishonesty is involved.

---

This case serves as a focal point for understanding the limits of employer liability regarding employee misconduct, clarifying that an employer is not automatically held liable for acts of theft committed by employees when such actions are entirely self-serving and unauthorized.
