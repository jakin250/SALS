Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: Madibeng Local Municipality v Public Investment Corporation Ltd (603/2017) [2018] ZASCA 93

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts

In the late 1980s and early 1990s, the Brits Town Council (the predecessor of the Madibeng Local Municipality) sought to raise short-term loans from various institutions, intending to invest the borrowed funds in capital markets [2]. However, by 1993, Brits faced severe financial difficulties that called for urgent remedial action due to a fiscal crisis [3]. To alleviate this crisis, Brits borrowed a significant sum from the Public Investment Corporation Ltd (the PIC) in January 1994, issuing zero coupon stock certificates and pledging insurance policies as security [4].

When Madibeng assumed the responsibilities from Brits, it failed to repay the PIC, prompting the latter to exercise its rights under the pledged policies and subsequently sue Madibeng based on the zero coupon stock certificates [5]. The PIC claimed outstanding amounts from three specific certificates: BR25 (R29,306,987.70), BR20 (R10,219,836.60), and BR26 (R26,072,786.40) [6].

Madibeng responded with a special plea citing the defence of prescription, along with an argument that the loans were unenforceable as Brits lacked the requisite authority under the Local Government Ordinance 17 of 1939 (the Ordinance) to issue the certificates [10–11]. This contention centered on whether the loans were made with the prior written consent of the Administrator, as stipulated in the Ordinance [12–13].

Two days prior to the trial, Madibeng requested the separation of issues relating to the enforceability of the loans, leading to an affidavit-based hearing on the enforceability point alone [15–16]. The High Court, presided over by Jansen J, ultimately found that Madibeng's defence lacked merit and imposed a punitive costs order against it [18].

### I – Issues

The appeal primarily concerned three issues:

1. **Enforceability of Loans**: Were the loans raised by Brits enforceable given the alleged lack of authority?
2. **Irregular Misconduct**: Was the procedure adopted by the High Court, which involved the use of affidavits instead of oral testimony, irregular?
3. **Costs Order**: Was the punitive costs order imposed on Madibeng justified? [19].

### R – Rules

The court primarily examined the provisions of Section 52 of the Local Government Ordinance 17 of 1939, which governs the raising of loans by municipalities. Section 52(1) delineates permissible reasons for raising loans, while Section 52(2) requires prior written consent from the Administrator where applicable [22]. The interpretation of these provisions was critical to determining the enforceability of the loans.

Further, the court referenced Rule 38(2) of the Uniform Rules of Court, allowing for evidence by affidavit in proceedings, which played a significant role in addressing Madibeng's objections regarding procedural irregularities [25]. The principle of unconscionable conduct by a state organ also informed the discussion surrounding the costs order [30].

### A – Application

The court's analysis began with the assumption that the Ordinance applied, yet it swiftly concluded that the loans taken by Brits were lawful under Section 52(2) since they pertained to repaying existing debts and did not require prior written consent from the Administrator [23–24]. Therefore, Jansen J's original findings were upheld upon appeal.

Regarding the procedural objection, the court determined that affirmatively agreed upon affidavit-based evidence was appropriately permissible under Rule 38(2). The court highlighted that the nature of the respective claims did not involve disputed facts, justifying the procedure adopted by the High Court [27].

With respect to the punitive costs order, the court expressed concerns regarding Madibeng’s conduct as a state organ, emphasizing that such entities ought to act ethically and transparently in their dealings. Madibeng’s actions of seeking delay and raising the unenforceability argument after initial acceptance of liability were deemed unconscionable and thus warranted the imposition of a punitive costs order against it [30–32].

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal set aside the High Court’s order and issued a declaratory order affirming that the loans raised by Madibeng were enforceable, as there was no requirement for prior consent from the Administrator [33]. The court also upheld a punitive costs order against Madibeng, underscoring the necessity for ethical conduct from public entities. Despite the appeal being dismissed, the court adjusted the initial terms, affirmatively clarifying the legal standing of the loans [33].

The implications of this ruling extend beyond the specific case at hand; they underscore the vigilant requirement for municipalities and other public entities to adhere to legal frameworks and act responsibly in financial dealings, promoting accountability within public administration.
