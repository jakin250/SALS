# Case Note: *Commercial Auto Glass (Pty) Ltd v Bayerische Motoren Werke AG* [2007] SCA 96 RSA

### FIRAC Method

## F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–6])

The appellant, *Commercial Auto Glass (Pty) Ltd*, sought leave to appeal against a decision by Prinsloo J in the High Court of Pretoria, which found that the appellant infringed certain trade marks owned by *Bayerische Motoren Werke AG* (hereafter "BMW"). The case centers around the statutory framework provided in Section 34(1)(a) of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 (the "Act"), which prohibits unauthorized use of registered trade marks in the course of trade. The appellant predominantly supplied and fitted windscreens for cars and was found to utilize BMW's registered marks – specifically, the identifiers “BMW”, “BM”, and “3 Series” – in advertising and selling windscreens purportedly compatible with various BMW car models.

The appellant did not contest the validity of BMW’s trade mark registrations but argued that its use of the marks served to inform potential customers of the products' compatibility with BMW vehicles, thus asserting its use was not misleading and constituted bona fide non-infringing use under Section 34(2)(c) of the Act. BMW contended that the appellant's use of its marks was likely to cause confusion, constituting trade mark infringement.

## I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 2–8])

The primary legal questions in this case included:

1. **Trade Mark Infringement**: Did the appellant's use of BMW’s trade marks amount to unauthorized use in the course of trade in relation to goods for which the marks were registered, and did such use mislead consumers in a way that constituted infringement under Section 34(1)(a)?

2. **Bona Fide Use**: Was the appellant’s use consistent with fair practice as contemplated by Section 34(2)(c)? 

3. **Descriptive Use Defense**: Did the appellant make a valid argument that its use of the trade marks was descriptive and therefore outside the purview of infringement? 

4. **Constitutional Implications**: Were there broader constitutional implications stemming from the appellant's assertion of its right to free commercial speech?

## R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 3–18])

The court referred to several key provisions and legal principles regarding trade marks:

1. **Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, Section 34(1)(a)**: This section outlines conditions under which trade mark rights are infringed, specifically unauthorized use of a mark in a way likely to deceive or cause confusion.

2. **Section 34(2)(c)**: Provides a statutory exception for bona fide use of a trade mark in certain contexts, including indicating the intended purpose of spare parts, provided such use is consistent with fair practice.

3. **Haley v. British Telecommunications plc [2001] EWCA Civ 1123**: Cited as a precedent illustrating the interpretation of trade mark use with respect to consumer perceptions.

4. **Verimark (Pty) Ltd v BMW AG [2007] SCA 53 (RSA) para 5**: The court reiterated that trade mark infringement requires proof of a likelihood that consumers interpret the mark as indicating the origin of goods.

5. **Bayerische Motoren Werke AG (BMW) and BMW Nederland BV v Deenik Case C-63/97**: A relevant ECJ ruling that addresses the nuance of advertising and its potential to mislead consumers concerning trade mark ownership and association.

6. **Gillette Company and Gillette Group Finland Oy v LA-Laboratories Ltd Oy [2005] FSR 37**: This case served to outline the factors affecting the bona fide use assessment and the overall presentation of goods involving trade marks.

## A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 9–15])

The court analyzed the appellant's case, focusing first on whether the use of BMW's trade marks constituted 'trade mark use,' emphasizing consumer perceptions. The court noted that the appellant's advertisements made specific references to the “BMW” marks in conjunction with prices for windscreens, which could lead consumers to believe that these products originated from or were sanctioned by BMW. The court found that the likelihood of confusion was pertinent, particularly as the appellant did not question BMW's claims regarding public deception.

Next, the judgment examined whether the appellant satisfied the requirements of Section 34(2)(c) for bona fide use. It determined that the use was neither clear nor sufficient to inform consumers that these were unauthorized parts. The court highlighted that honest use must unequivocally inform consumers of the nature of the goods.

The court reasoned that the appellant’s marketing strategies and terminology lacked clarity that would constitute fair practice. The failure to effectively communicate that the windscreens were unauthorized directly countered the claim for bona fide descriptive use. Consequently, the court found no errors in the lower court’s ruling, affirming that the appellant's use of the marks was misleading and constituted trade mark infringement.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 17–19])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded by granting leave to appeal but upheld the lower court's decision regarding trade mark infringement. The court determined that:

1. The use of BMW's trademarks by the appellant constituted an infringement under Section 34(1)(a), as it created a misleading impression about the origin of the goods.

2. The appellant did not successfully establish that its use fell under the exception of Section 34(2)(c) as bona fide and fair practice.

3. The misapplication of the law regarding the orders issued in the lower court necessitated a correction without substantial prejudice against the appellant regarding the costs.

Ultimately, this judgment reinforced the principles surrounding trade mark protection in South Africa, highlighting the delicate balance between commercial speech and consumer protection. The ruling also emphasized the importance of clear communication in trade practices to avoid misleading advertising, thereby facilitating fair competition in the market. The decision underlines the importance of adhering to both statutory obligations and consumer perception in the realm of trade mark law.
