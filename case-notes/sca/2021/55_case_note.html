Director of Public Prosecutions: Gauteng Division, Pretoria v Pooe (348/2019) [2021] ZASCA 55

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 55

F – Facts

In this matter, the Director of Public Prosecutions, Gauteng Division, Pretoria (the "State") applied for leave to appeal against a judgment rendered by the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, wherein Mr. Rethabile Amogelang Pooe (the "Respondent") was acquitted on five counts, including murder and robbery ([para 1]). The Respondent was only 16 years old at the time of the incident. He had been charged alongside a co-accused, Keorapetse Shabalala (Chabi), who pleaded guilty and testified against him.

On 22 November 2016, after writing exams, the Respondent and Chabi did not return home as expected. Instead, they waited at school, where Chabi disclosed to the Respondent that he possessed a firearm ([paras 7–8]). Eventually, they encountered their victim, a teacher, and the Respondent requested a lift, which she granted. Shortly thereafter, Chabi pointed a firearm at her and demanded she drive to an industrial area where she was subsequently tied up and shot, resulting in her death ([paras 8–10]).

The Respondent maintained throughout the trial that he was coerced by Chabi and had not planned the crime, arguing that he merely complied out of fear. The trial court, after assessing various inconsistencies in the testimonies, ruled that the State had failed to prove its case beyond a reasonable doubt and acquitted the Respondent on all charges ([paras 12–14]).

Following the acquittal, the State sought to reserve four questions of law for the appellate court's consideration in terms of Section 319 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (the "CPA"). The trial court, however, refused to reserve the questions ([para 15]), prompting the State to appeal that decision in line with Section 17(2)(b) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013.

I – Issues

The central legal issues presented in this appeal are:

  1. Whether the procedure followed by the State in applying for leave to appeal was correct, given that the trial court had refused to reserve the questions of law as requested ([para 4]).
  2. Whether the trial court erred in its evaluation of the evidence, specifically in relation to the existence of a prior agreement between the Respondent and Chabi to commit the crimes ([paras 15–16]).

R – Rules

The key legal frameworks governing this case are:

  1. Section 319 of the CPA - This section permits a trial court to reserve questions of law upon the request of either the prosecutor or the accused. However, strict adherence to the rules regarding the reservation of questions of law is required, which includes accurately framing the question and setting out the relevant facts upon which the question is based ([paras 17–19]).
  2. Section 17(2)(b) of the Superior Courts Act - Governs how the State may seek leave for an appeal and necessitates compliance with procedural requirements when a trial court has refused to reserve questions of law ([para 1]).

A – Application

The court applied the outlined statutes and prior case law to ascertain whether the trial court had met the requirements for reserving questions of law. The judgments referenced, such as Director of Public Prosecutions: Limpopo v Molope and Director of Public Prosecutions: Western Cape v Schoeman, emphasized the stringent requirements of Section 319, necessitating a clear exposition of both legal and factual elements of the case ([para 18]).

The court found that the questions posed by the State were, in essence, not questions of law but rather questions of fact. The trial court had rightly concluded that the inconsistencies in the testimonies did not rise to the level warranting legal error ([paras 29–30]). The appellate court reaffirmed the principle that it could not substitute its own factual determinations for those of the trial court without clear evidence of errors in law.

Moreover, despite the State's grievances about the trial judge's evaluation of the evidence, the court noted that merely failing to arrive at a particular conclusion or potentially misjudging evidence does not constitute a legal error as defined under the CPA. Cases deciding what constitutes a "question of law" further supported the court's conclusion that while the trial court may have erred in factual evaluations, this does not permit the State to utilize Section 319 for appeal ([paras 36–39]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the State's application for leave to appeal, upholding the trial court’s decision of acquittal. The ruling clarified that the questions raised were not questions of law, as they were rooted in factual determinations over which the appellate court had no jurisdiction to interfere ([para 43]). The court underscored the principle that the onus rests on the State to prove guilt beyond a reasonable doubt, and failing to do so results in acquittal. The judgment serves to emphasize the importance of adhering strictly to procedural rules when reserving questions of law, thus reaffirming the protections afforded to defendants in criminal proceedings ([para 41]).

There were no costs awarded against the State, recognizing the serious nature of the charges involved and the tragic circumstances surrounding the case ([para 41]).
