Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2001

# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title**: *The National Director of Public Prosecutions v Rebuzzi*, Case No: 94/2000, Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa.

### F – Facts

The instant case arises from a provisional restraint order granted under section 26 of the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (as amended). The appellant, The National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP), sought this order against the respondent, Charlotte Elizabeth Rebuzzi, who was a former employee of PG Bison Ltd. Rebuzzi was alleged to have misappropriated at least R897,066 from the company between 1 April 1997 and 30 July 1999 by concealing cash sales through improper use of rebate cheques issued by the company ([1]–[10]).

The NDPP, at the request of PG Bison Ltd, applied ex parte to the High Court for a provisional restraint order. The High Court initially granted the order which prohibited Rebuzzi from dealing with her property, subsequently appointing a curator bonis. However, the respondent opposed the confirmation of this order at a later date. The High Court, presided over by Goldstein J, eventually set aside the provisional order, reasoning that it would be unjust to issue a confiscation order where there was a direct claim by the identifiable victim, PG Bison Ltd, for recovery of misappropriated funds ([11]–[13]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal question before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether a court, in making a restraint order under the Prevention of Organised Crime Act, could do so despite the presence of a legitimate claim by the victim of the alleged crime against the defendant. Specifically, the court needed to determine if the issuance of a restraint order would necessarily conflict with the victim's right to pursue recovery of their losses ([2]–[14]).

### R – Rules

The court primarily referenced sections 18, 25, and 26 of the Prevention of Organised Crime Act. Section 18 outlines the conditions under which a confiscation order may be made against a convicted individual, focusing on the benefits derived from criminal conduct. Sections 25 and 26 describe the powers of a High Court to issue restraint orders in anticipation of confiscation orders, detailing the circumstances under which such orders may be issued (§ 25(1)). 

The court also relied on its interpretative authority to assess legislative intent, particularly the purpose of confiscation orders which is to prevent a convicted individual from profiting from crime, rather than to enrich the State ([2]–[4], [17]).

### A – Application

In its analysis, the Supreme Court of Appeal disagreed with the reasoning of the High Court. It found that the existence of a claim by a victim does not preclude the issuance of a restraint or confiscation order. The court asserted that a confiscation order serves primarily to prevent the defendant from profiting from their crime. It noted that provisions within sections 30 and 31 of the Act allow for both confiscation orders and claims from victims to coexist, and that the court may ensure victims are compensated after the proceeds of the confiscation order are realized ([12]–[19]).

Moreover, the court clarified that a court is not required to establish the defendant's guilt prior to granting a restraint order; rather, there must be reasonable grounds to believe that a conviction could be secured based on the evidence presented. They affirmed that the existence of reasonable grounds in this case warranted the appeal and ultimately the confirmation of the restraint order, albeit with specific modifications to correct the monetary amount and the removal of problematic disclosure provisions ([20]–[26]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal by the NDPP, thereby reinstating the provisional restraint order originally issued, but with modifications to correct inaccuracies regarding the amount that was subject to restraint. The court emphasized that a confiscation order does not preclude a victim's concurrent claim for recovery, highlighting legislative facilities that allow victim claims to be addressed without preventing the enforcement of public interest confiscation orders. The judgment indicates significant implications for the interplay between victim rights and the processes surrounding the confiscation of proceeds from crime, reinforcing the notion that the law seeks to deprive offenders of ill-gotten gains without undermining the rights of victims ([25]–[26]).
