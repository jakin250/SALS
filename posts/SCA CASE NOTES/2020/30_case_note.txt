Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# Case Note: Bergh and Others v The Agricultural Research Council [2020] ZASCA 30

## FIRAC Analysis

### F – Facts
The appeal arose from a 2018 order by the Western Cape Division of the High Court, which ruled in favor of the Agricultural Research Council (ARC), establishing jurisdiction to restrict the respondents’ use of a computer software program named BeefPro. The ARC claimed that the software, developed for cattle management, was its intellectual property, having been commissioned under its auspices. The dispute involved five appellants, former employees of the ARC and their consulting entity, Bull's Eye Consulting, as well as a close corporation named BenguelaSoft, which was involved in providing similar services to farmers.

The ARC argued that the appellants engaged in unlawful competition by misappropriating its intellectual property, specifically alleging that the appellants reproduced and capitalized on BeefPro through a rebranded product called BenguFarm. The complaint was not limited to copyright infringement but included claims of unfair competition and misrepresentation that undermined ARC's statutory duties under the Agricultural Research Act 86 of 1990 and the Animal Improvement Act 62 of 1998 ([paras 1–12], [30], [57]).

The court’s initial ruling was given without reasoning, leading to an extensive procedural delay, lasting nearly two years after the arguments were presented. The appellants challenged the validity of the claim by asserting that the ARC failed to demonstrate ownership and control over the software in question ([paras 3, 68]).

### I – Issues
The key legal issues in this case included:
1. Whether the ARC could substantiate its claim of copyright ownership over BeefPro, specifically whether it had established that the software was developed under its control as per the requirements of the Copyright Act 98 of 1978, especially regarding the definition of an author ([paras 5, 66]).
2. The implications of Section 5(2) of the Copyright Act, which asserts that copyright of work created under the direction of the state vests in the state, and whether this applied to the ARC as a juristic entity ([para 66]).

### R – Rules
The court relied on various legal rules and principles, particularly:
1. The definition of "author" as per the Copyright Act 98 of 1978, which vests copyright with the individual or entity that exercised control over the creation of a work ([para 23]).
2. Precedents including *Haupt t/a Soft Copy v Brewers Marketing Intelligence (Pty) Ltd* [2006] ZASCA 40, which established standards for determining "control" over software development ([para 59]).
3. The principles governing intellectual property rights in relation to commissioned work and the obligations arising from employment contracts under Section 21 of the Copyright Act, which delineates ownership in cases of employment versus independent contract agreements ([para 23]).

### A – Application
In assessing the evidence presented, the court found that the ARC failed to carry its burden of proof regarding the ownership of BeefPro. The appellants demonstrated that the software was developed by Mr. Tim Pauw, an independent contractor, who negotiated terms with the ARC that expressly retained ownership of the copyright within his consulting company, BenguelaSoft. 

The evidence indicated that Pauw operated independently, implementing his expertise without the ARC’s supervision and that the ARC’s subsequent claims regarding control were not substantiated by relevant material evidence. The court noted that the arrangements between Pauw and the ARC indicated that the nature of their agreement was more akin to a commission for his services rather than an employer-employee relationship, which would typically dictate copyright ownership ([paras 43–63]).

Furthermore, the court criticized the indefensible delays and lack of reasoning in the High Court's original decision, emphasizing the need for judicial transparency and accountability to uphold public confidence in the justice system ([para 67–70]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, setting aside the order of the lower court and substituting it with a dismissal of the ARC’s initial application. The court ruled that the ARC had not successfully demonstrated its claim to copyright ownership over BeefPro, as it failed to prove that the appellants acted under its control in the development of the software. The court’s decision reinforced the necessity for clear evidence in intellectual property disputes, especially regarding claims of control and ownership in commissioned works ([para 70]).

The implications of this ruling not only vindicated the appellants but also highlighted the essential standards of procedural justice and reasoned judgments in the South African legal system. The verdict serves to protect the rights of developers and entities involved in contractual agreements regarding intellectual property, emphasizing the importance of clearly defined ownership and control arrangements.
