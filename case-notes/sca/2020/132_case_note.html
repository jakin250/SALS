Smit and Others v Origize 166 Strand Real Estate (Pty) Ltd and Others

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 132 (19 October 2020) Coram: Petse DP, Makg Delivered 19 October 2020

Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Citation: Smit and Others v Origize 166 Strand Real Estate (Pty) Ltd and Others (Case no 710/19) [2020] ZASCA 132 (19 October 2020)
Coram: Petse DP, Makgoka and Nicholls JJA, Ledwaba and Eksteen AJJA
Heard: 25 August 2020
Delivered: 19 October 2020

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The dispute in this appeal revolves around the interpretation and enforcement of two powers of attorney granted to the first appellant, Mr. Sybrand Smit, by Origize 166 Strand Real Estate (Pty) Ltd (Origize), represented solely by the second respondent, Mr. O’Neil Jacobs ([1]). The powers were reportedly given as security for a debt related to the purchase of 33 sectional title units in a property scheme named Ocean View Villas, purchased from the liquidators of CLA Projects (Pty) Ltd in 2016 for R4.1 million ([2]).

Mr. Jacobs initially secured the property but struggled to provide the necessary guarantees for the purchase price, leading him to seek Mr. Smit's assistance ([3]). As part of their agreement, Mr. Smit advanced R1 million to prevent the contract from being canceled and was entitled to an equal share of the profits from any sale of the units. The importance of this transaction necessitated the execution of the powers of attorney by Jacobs, allegedly as a means to secure Mr. Smit's financial input ([4]-[5]).

Mr. Smit received a power of attorney from Origize, explicitly marked as irrevocable, on 21 July 2016, which would enable him to act on behalf of the company to manage and sell the property ([8]). However, after a series of unsuccessful attempts to sell the units at a profitable price and a further deterioration in their state leading to vandalism, Jacob's issued a resolution on 15 March 2018 purportedly revoking the authority ([11]-[12]).

In response, the appellants sought a court order for the enforcement of the powers of attorney, asserting that they were irrevocable due to their nature as security for the advanced funds ([13]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions that arose from the case were:

  1. Whether the powers of attorney granted to Mr. Smit could be revoked, given they were purportedly issued as security for a debt ([15]-[16]).
  2. Whether the appellants were justified in seeking the relief outlined in their application, specifically if Mr. Smit had the authority to accept offers on behalf of Origize ([30]).

R – Rules

The legal framework underpinning the resolution revolved around the Law of Agency in South Africa, particularly the principles regarding the revocability of power of attorney. According to traditional Roman-Dutch law, a power of attorney can generally be revoked unless it is granted to secure an interest for the grantee ([15]-[16]).

South African jurisprudence has recognized an exception to the general rule allowing irrevocable powers when they are coupled with an interest related to securing a debt, as evidenced in cases such as Koch v Mair (1894) and Natal Bank Ltd v Natorp and Registrar of Deeds (1908) ([20]-[24]). The case law illustrates that authority given for the purpose of securing a debt remains effective as long as the financial obligation persists ([28]).

A – Application

The court critically analyzed the initial determination by the High Court that a power of attorney could not be irrevocable, relying heavily on the South African Law of Agency. However, the appellate court scrutinized the resolution from 21 July 2016, emphasizing the intention behind granting the authority and the lack of a credible justification for the revocation ([26]-[30]).

The court concluded that the history of the transaction indicated the power of attorney was intended as security for Mr. Smit's advances and thus should not be revocable as long as the debt remained unpaid. Notably, the extensive powers described in the attorney resolutions allowed Mr. Smit to engage in various transactions and undertake actions on behalf of Origize that directly aligned with the interests of both parties ([31]).

On the grounds that Mr. Smit was effectively a partner sharing in profits and had undertaken substantial financial risk, the court found that he possessed the authority to accept offers and sign necessary documents for the sale of the units ([30]-[32]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal thus overturned the High Court's ruling, declaring that the power of attorney granted to Mr. Smit on 21 July 2016 was irrevocable. This ruling was critical in reaffirming the legal principles that support irrevocable powers of attorney when given as security for a debt, thereby validating the appellants' original request ([33]).

The court ordered that Mr. Smit had the authority to accept offers on behalf of Origize and that the respondents were liable for costs. Importantly, this case reaffirms the entrenched legal understanding of powers of attorney in South African law, emphasizing the importance of the context in which these powers are conferred. The implications suggest heightened scrutiny in future contracts and real estate transactions, particularly where financial securities are concerned [{28}], thus potentially reshaping practices within the realm of corporate governance and agency law.
