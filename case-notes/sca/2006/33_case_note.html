Case Note: MEC for Roads and Public Works v Intertrade Two (Pty) Ltd
Supreme Court of Appeal
Neutral Citation: MEC for Roads & Public Works v Intertrade Two (Pty) Ltd [2006] SCA 34 (RSA)
Coram: Maya AJA, Howie P, Farlam, Heher, and Van Heerden JJA
Heard: 23 February 2006
Delivered: 27 March 2006

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The case concerns Intertrade Two (Pty) Ltd (“Intertrade”), a mechanical and electrical plant supplier and repairer, which challenged the actions of the MEC for Roads and Public Works and the Provincial Tender Board of the Eastern Cape (the "appellants") through review proceedings brought before the Bhisho High Court ([1], [2]).

Intertrade was an unsuccessful tenderer in a series of tender processes for contracts related to the maintenance and repair of equipment at provincial hospitals. Following repeated invitations to tender, and subsequent failures to award contracts despite recommendations in their favor, Intertrade became increasingly concerned about the irregularities in the tendering process. Despite being the sole tenderer multiple times, their bids were not awarded, prompting an investigation by the Premier’s office which criticized the handling of the tenders ([3], [4]).

In 2004, after a prolonged period of ambiguity regarding the awarding of contracts, Intertrade formally requested documentation related to the tender process, identifying a perceived unfairness that had deprived it of the contracts ([5], [6]). Despite this, the appellants contested the validity of Intertrade’s requests for documents, arguing that requests under Section 7 of the Promotion of Access to Information Act (PAIA) could not be made concurrent with review proceedings established under Rule 53 ([2], [9]).

I – Issues

The principal legal issue revolved around whether Intertrade could seek access to information related to the tender adjudication process under PAIA before exhausting remedies under the Uniform Rules of Court (specifically, Rule 53). The specific legal questions addressed by the court were:

  1. Was Intertrade precluded from seeking additional documentation based on the limitations of Rule 53 proceedings?
  2. Did the requests made prior to the formal institution of review proceedings fall within the scope of access guaranteed under Section 7(1) of PAIA? ([2], [11]).

R – Rules

The key statutory and case law provisions relevant to the Supreme Court of Appeal’s decision included:

  1. Promotion of Access to Information Act 2 of 2000 (PAIA): Particularly Section 7(1), which restricts requests for records related to civil or criminal proceedings if made after the commencement of those proceedings ([10], [12],[19]).
  2. Uniform Rules of Court: Specifically, Rule 53, which outlines the process for bringing review applications of administrative decisions ([2], [8]).
  3. Constitution of South Africa, Section 32: This provision explicitly enshrines the right of access to information held by the state ([8]).

The court also referenced prior case law that interpreted both PAIA and the rules governing administrative review, notably Johannesburg City Council v The Administrator, Transvaal and Bato Star Fishing (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Environmental Affairs & Others, highlighting the accessibility of information for the protection of constitutional rights ([11], [15]).

A – Application

In applying the law to the facts, Maya AJA emphasized the interpretative obligation of courts under Section 39(2) of the Constitution, which requires that legislation, including PAIA, must promote the spirit and object of the Bill of Rights ([11], [18]). The court found that Intertrade's request for documents prior to the initiation of review proceedings did not trigger the limitations imposed by Section 7(1) since the request was informal and not made as part of the formal litigation process. The appellants’ interpretation that only the formal request initiated by the notice of motion counted was rejected for lack of precedent ([13], [14]).

The court further clarified that the documents requested were integral to ensuring fairness and transparency in the administrative process, consistent with the duty of public authorities to avoid obfuscation when rights are at stake ([20], [21]). Furthermore, it noted that the appellants had delayed in responding to Intertrade's requests, potentially compromising the ability of the latter to effectively challenge the tender decisions ([20]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the ruling of the Bhisho High Court, concluding that Intertrade was rightfully entitled to access the documents it sought and that the appellants' limitation arguments based on Section 7(1) of PAIA were misplaced ([22]). The decision signified an affirmation of the principles of transparency and accountability in public procurement processes. The implications of this holding highlight the judiciary's role in upholding constitutional rights against bureaucratic reticence, reinforcing the notion that public bodies must engage openly with the public regarding their activities. The appeal was dismissed with costs, indicating the court's disapproval of the appellants' conduct throughout the proceedings ([22]).

This case serves as a pivotal example of the interplay between access to information, public accountability, and administrative law in South Africa, asserting that constitutional rights must be honored in public governance.
