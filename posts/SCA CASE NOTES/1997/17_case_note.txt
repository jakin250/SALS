Source Folder: 1997

# Case Note on **Saak nr 168/94**

## F – Facts

The appellant, Petrus Kgwadi (hereinafter referred to as “the appellant”), was convicted in the Rustenburg Regional Court along with two other co-defendants (co-defendants 2 and 3) on charges including murder (charge 1), armed robbery (charge 2), and illegal possession of a firearm (charge 3) ([para 1]). The court found co-defendant 3 not guilty on all charges, while the appellant received a death sentence for charge 1 and a fifteen-year sentence for charge 2 ([paras 1-2]). Co-defendant 2, a minor at the time of the crimes, was referred to a reform school as per section 290(1)(d) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([para 2]).

The events in question took place on 26 May 1992, when the victim, an elderly farmer named Bernhard Hartkorn, was attacked in his home. Following a disturbance heard by the farm laborer James Matshona, who did not initially suspect foul play, the police were alerted after Matshona discovered Hartkorn’s body ([paras 3-4]). Despite being alive when the police arrived, Hartkorn succumbed to his injuries on 3 July 1992 ([para 5]). 

The three accused were arrested on the early morning of 20 June 1992 ([para 5]). The appellant provided a written statement and participated in the pointing-out of the crime scene ([para 5]). In subsequent legal proceedings, the appellant contested the admissibility of his statement and pointing-out, arguing that these were not made freely and voluntarily ([para 6]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues at hand pertained to the admissibility of the statements given by the appellant, specifically whether these were obtained under duress. Additionally, issues surrounding the sufficiency of evidence to support a conviction, particularly the basis for convictions under the doctrine of common purpose and the presence of “dolus eventualis,” were raised ([paras 6-8]).

## R – Rules

The court relied on precedent regarding the admissibility of evidence acquired from accused individuals, particularly ensuring that such evidence is procured voluntarily without coercion ([para 10]). The court referenced the necessity for proper procedures to be followed during interrogations, thereby underscoring the principles of fair trial safeguards enshrined within both statutory laws and constitutional provisions ([para 10-11]).

South African case law, notably *S v Mdululi* 1972 (2) SA 839 and *S v Magwaza* 1985 (3) SA 29, was cited, establishing the unacceptability of evidence extracted through improper means, although recognizing that procedural irregularities do not automatically nullify admissibility if the rights are sufficiently preserved ([para 10]).

The Constitutional implications that arise from the imposition of a death penalty were also significant, particularly following the decision in *S v Makwanyane and Another* 1995 (6) BCLR 665 (CC) which invalidated the death penalty's legality and necessitated reconsideration of any existing sentences under these principles ([para 25]).

## A – Application

The court meticulously examined the contention regarding the appellant's confession and pointing-out, highlighting the potential coercive circumstances under which these were obtained. Testimonies indicated that police officers allegedly urged the appellant to provide specific details regarding the crime, questioning both the voluntary nature of the confession and the truthfulness of his statements ([para 6-12]).

Subsequently, witness testimonies from co-defendants were assessed in light of their credibility and the extent of their involvement in the crime. The court reflected upon the contradictions presented in their statements, especially regarding who directed the violence towards the victim ([paras 21-24]). Ultimately, the court determined that while the appellant did not directly carry out the fatal assault, he possessed the requisite intent (dolus eventualis) necessary for a murder conviction, being aware that his actions could lead to serious injury or death for the victim ([paras 24-25]).

The inability to substantiate a leadership role in the criminal enterprise between the accused was a significant consideration, ultimately leading the court to affirm the appellant's conviction on the basis of principal participation.

## C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the court upheld the conviction of the appellant for murder, affirming the finding of dolus eventualis due to the foreseeability of death from the actions taken during the robbery ([para 25]). However, it invalidated the death sentence, aligning with the principles established in the *Makwanyane* case, necessitating a reinstitution of sentencing under the new constitutional framework ([paras 25-26]). The appellant’s appeal against the guilt finding was dismissed, while the appeal against the death sentence was upheld, necessitating the referral back to a lower court for resentencing consistent with constitutional norms. 

This case underscores the evolving nature of criminal liability under South African law, particularly with respect to constitutional protections and procedural integrity in the context of serious indictable offenses.
