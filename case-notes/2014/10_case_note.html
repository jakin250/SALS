FIRAC Case Note: National Union of Public Service & Allied Workers v National Lotteries Board [2014] ZACC 10

F – Facts

The case revolves around the dismissals of employees from the National Lotteries Board ("the Board") following their participation in union activities regarding their Chief Executive Officer ("CEO"). On March 20, 2008, the National Union of Public Service & Allied Workers (NUPSAW) sent letters to the Board expressing dissatisfaction with the CEO's performance and seeking to obtain a copy of his employment contract. Following the Board's refusal to engage, NUPSAW escalated the matter to the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA) for formal dispute resolution ([paras 13–20]).

During the conciliation process, the union drafted a critical letter on May 23, 2008, outlining numerous complaints against the CEO, which ultimately was leaked to a newspaper ([para 15–16]). Subsequently, on June 3, 2008, 41 employees signed a petition urging the dismissal of the CEO, stating they could no longer work with him. This led to disciplinary proceedings where the employees were found guilty of insubordination and subsequently dismissed. Their appeals through the Labour Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal were unsuccessful, prompting the union to seek redress from the Constitutional Court for what they argued was an automatically unfair dismissal under sections 187 and 188 of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 ([paras 28, 37]).

I – Issues

  1. Did the conduct of the employees amount to insubordination or bringing the employer into disrepute? ([para 56]).
  2. Was the dismissal automatically unfair under section 187 of the Labour Relations Act due to participation in lawful union activities? ([paras 37(c), 66]).
  3. Was the dismissal nevertheless unfair under section 188 of the Labour Relations Act? ([para 37(d)]).
  4. Were the allegations of misconduct, including insubordination, substantiated? ([paras 219-228]).

R – Rules

  1. Labour Relations Act, 1995:
  • Section 187(1): Automatically unfair dismissals, particularly for conduct in support of lawful activities of a trade union.
  • Section 188: Defines unfair dismissal where the employer fails to prove that the dismissal was for a fair reason related to conduct.
  • Section 4(2)(a): Employees have the right to participate in lawful activities of their union ([paras 29–30]).
  1. Constitutional Provisions:
  • Section 16: Right to freedom of expression.
  • Section 23: Protects the rights of workers to engage in trade union activities and collective bargaining ([para 28]).

A – Application

The court undertook a three-tier analysis to determine the nature of the employees' conduct during the grievance process. Firstly, it identified that the demand for the CEO’s dismissal was made outside of the structured processes outlined in the Labour Relations Act, thereby constituting an unlawful demand. The court emphasized that dismissals based on demands for wrongful termination, even under the guise of union activity, cannot be legitimized as lawful activities of the union ([paras 7, 66, 70]).

In its ruling, the court asserted that while the union was entitled to express dissatisfaction with the CEO's performance, the actions taken by employees crossed the line into insubordination when they demanded termination without due process. The court concluded that the participation by the employees in their union's activities did not afford them protection under the Act because their conduct undermined prescribed dispute-resolution mechanisms and publicized complaints that brought the employer and CEO into disrepute ([paras 31–33, 67]).

The court further noted that while the publication of the union's letter could be viewed as a breach of internal protocols, it did not necessarily equal insubordination unless it was proven that such actions were intended to obstruct workplace authority. Ultimately, the court found a lack of fair process in their dismissal, as the disciplinary actions taken did not adequately reflect consideration of the employees' constitutional rights to freedom of expression and union involvement ([paras 87, 90, 202]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court held that the dismissals of the employees were automatically unfair. The court ruled that the conduct of the employees, including their demands and participation in union activities, fell within the ambit of their rights protected under the Labour Relations Act. In doing so, the court determined that the employer’s failure to engage meaningfully with the union at various stages, as well as the lack of substantive justification for the dismissals, rendered them automatically unfair. As a remedy, the court ordered the reinstatement of the employees with full retrospective effect, alongside costs for the applicants ([paras 88, 207]). This case highlights the importance of adhering to fair processes and the recognition of employees' rights to participate in collective actions without fear of retaliation, ensuring that the integrity of the workplace relations framework is upheld.
