Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: Monyetla Property Holdings v IMM Graduate School of Marketing (20023/2014) [2015] ZASCA 32

**F – Facts**  
This case involves a dispute between Monyetla Property Holdings (Pty) Limited (the "Appellant") and the IMM Graduate School of Marketing (Pty) Limited and Nigel Colin Tatersall (together, the "Respondents") regarding a lease agreement and subsequent cancellation. The relevant facts are as follows:

1. The Appellant was the registered owner of immovable property known as Richmond Forum and had leased office space and parking bays situated on this property to the First Respondent under a lease agreement that began on 31 July 2007 and was set to expire on 30 September 2010 ([paras 3–4]).
   
2. The Second Respondent acted as a surety and co-principal debtor for the obligations of the First Respondent under this lease ([para 3]).

3. The First Respondent had met its financial obligations until 31 October 2007 but fell into arrears, accumulating a debt exceeding R2 million by 1 March 2009. Consequently, on 6 March 2009, the Appellant exercised its right under the lease to cancel it due to the breach ([paras 5–6]).

4. The First Respondent contested the validity of this cancellation and refused to vacate the premises. The Appellant then sought eviction of the First Respondent through motion proceedings initiated on 6 October 2009, along with a claim for amounts owed ([paras 6–8]).

5. A court ruling on 24 March 2010 favored the Appellant, confirming the cancellation of the lease and granting the requested relief ([para 7]).

6. Despite this ruling, the Respondents remained in occupation until late April 2010 and pursued further appeals, all of which were dismissed ([para 8]).

7. Subsequently, on 16 March 2012, the Appellant instituted a claim for damages totaling R1,192,493.81 for losses incurred due to the breach and unlawful occupation of premises after the lease was cancelled, contending that damages arose post-eviction ([paras 11–12]).

**I – Issues**  
The main legal questions at issue in this case include:

1. Whether the Appellant’s claim for damages had prescribed, given that it was instituted after the expiration of the three-year period stipulated by the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 ([para 13]).
  
2. Whether the period of prescription commenced at the point of cancellation of the lease on 6 March 2009 or post-vacation of the premises on 30 April 2010 ([para 13]).

3. The relevance of clause 26.2 of the lease, which required the lessee to continue paying rent while in occupation even amid disputes over cancellation ([paras 4, 14]).

**R – Rules**  
The legal principles applied in this case include:

1. **Prescription Period Under the Prescription Act**: Section 11 delineates the three-year prescription period for recovery of damages, running from the date the debt arises and ‘becomes due’, as clarified in section 12 ([para 13]).

2. **Definition of ‘Debt’**: A debt is considered ‘due’ once all factual elements that entitle a creditor to claim the amount are present ([para 15]).

3. **Contractual Breach and Damages**: According to common law, a lessor can claim damages post-breach that equate to the amount it would have received had the contract been fulfilled, which is the loss of income due to the breach of lease provisions ([para 16]).

4. **Mitigation of Loss**: A lessor is obliged to mitigate its losses by attempting to re-let the property, and any amounts received thereafter must be accounted for in calculating damages ([para 17]).

**A – Application**  
The court analyzed the issue of prescription, citing that:

1. The Appellant's claim for damages stemmed from the breach of the lease at the time of its cancellation and therefore should have been instituted within three years from the cancellation date, 6 March 2009 ([para 19]).

2. It rejected the Appellant’s assertion that the claim for damages only emerged when the First Respondent vacated the premises on 30 April 2010, emphasizing that the loss had occurred at cancellation. Thus, providing the Appellant with a right to sue upon that breach ([paras 18–20]).

3. The court highlighted that the continued occupation of the premises after cancellation does not negate the actual loss suffered by the lessor ([para 19]).

4. The court also noted that any payments made by the Respondents under clause 26.2 would be considered under damages calculation, thereby affirming that while the Respondents’ actions were not to be perceived as mitigation, they did not invalidate the claim's origination ([para 20]).

**C – Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the lower court's finding that the Appellant's damages claim had prescribed, as action was initiated more than three years after the debt became due on 6 March 2009. Consequently, the appeal was dismissed with costs ([paras 22–23]). The implications of this ruling underscore the critical importance of timely action in breach of contract cases, particularly in lease agreements, where subsequent claims for damages must be carefully considered in relation to statutory prescription periods. 

Overall, the case reaffirms legal standards relating to the timing of damage claims arising from lease cancellations and the requirements for lessors in claiming losses post-breach.
