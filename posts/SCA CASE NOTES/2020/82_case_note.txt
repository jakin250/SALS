Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# Case Note on **The School Governing Body Grey College, Bloemfontein v Scheepers and Others** (Case no 506/19) [2020] ZASCA 82

### FIRAC Methodology

---

## F – Facts

The central facts of this case are established within the context of a dispute involving the First Respondent, Mr. Deon Scheepers, who served as the principal of Grey College, Bloemfontein, and the Appellant, the School Governing Body (SGB) of the same institution. Mr. Scheepers was appointed principal in 2012, having been recommended by the SGB to the Head of the Free State Department of Education ([para 5]).

On May 3, 2018, Mr. Scheepers received an email inviting him to a special meeting scheduled for May 15, 2018, with the sole agenda item reportedly concerning the withdrawal of 'rights' and 'duties' delegated to him by the SGB ([para 6]). This purported withdrawal took place following complaints about his management, which were raised without prior notice or a formal investigation, leading to a breakdown of trust between Mr. Scheepers and the SGB ([paras 10–12]). At the meeting, Mr. Scheepers requested clarification about the rights being withdrawn, but was met with vague responses and a refusal to postpone the meeting for him to prepare ([paras 8, 10]).

Ultimately, 14 out of 17 members present voted in favor of a resolution that removed many of the functions previously delegated to Mr. Scheepers, appointing Mr. Jurie Geldenhuys as an interim school manager to take over these roles ([paras 14, 15]). The SGB's decision sparked legal action from Mr. Scheepers, leading to a review of the SGB's authority and processes.

The Free State Division of the High Court found in favor of Mr. Scheepers, determining that the SGB had acted beyond its statutory powers by stripping him of his delegated functions without proper procedures, prompting the SGB to appeal the decision ([para 4]).

## I – Issues

The principal legal issue at hand revolves around whether the SGB acted within its statutory authority when it withdrew delegated powers from Mr. Scheepers and appointed an interim school manager ([para 2]). Additionally, the matter raises concerns about procedural fairness. Specifically, it questions whether the SGB's actions constituted administrative action and if they complied with the provisions of the **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act** (PAJA) and the **South African Schools Act** ([paras 3, 21]).

## R – Rules

The court assessed the applicable statutory framework encapsulated in the **South African Schools Act 84 of 1996** (the Act) and various pieces of legislation, including the **Employment of Educators Act 76 of 1998** (EEA), and the **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000** (PAJA).

1. **South African Schools Act 84 of 1996**: Sections 15, 16, 20, and 23 were pivotal, establishing that public schools are juristic entities with specific powers vested in their governing bodies, which can delegate certain functions but not all ([para 32]).

2. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act**: Section 6(2)(a)(i) considers the requirements for procedural fairness in administrative decisions, emphasizing the necessity of a fair process before any action that affects individuals’ rights ([para 3]).

3. **Constitutional Precedents**: The court cited previous decisions by the Constitutional Court, particularly in the case of **Head of Department, Department of Education, Free State Province v Welkom High School and Others**; emphasizing the importance of balancing governance roles between the SGB and the principal to foster cooperative educational environments ([para 30]).

## A – Application

The court's analysis focused on the SGB's justification for its actions. Referring back to the legislative framework, the court reiterated that although the SGB possesses significant governance rights, it cannot unilaterally revoke functions that a principal executes under statutory authority without due process ([para 33]).

The court scrutinized the procedural fairness of the SGB's decision-making process. It highlighted the lack of notice given to Mr. Scheepers, the nature of the complaints against him, and the absence of a formal inquiry before the SGB reached its resolution ([paras 16, 20]). Moreover, the SGB’s assertion that it acted within its prerogative to delegate and revoke powers was dismissed; it was underscored that its actions amounted to an abdication of responsibilities rather than a legitimate withdrawal of delegated powers. This was contrary to the framework established by the Act and PAJA.

The judgment from the court below was upheld, maintaining that Mr. Scheepers’ statutory authority as principal encompassed a broader range of responsibilities than the management powers that the SGB sought to retract. The court concluded that the resolution adopted by the SGB lacked legal foundation, and failed to recognize the independent authority the principal holds under the relevant statutes ([paras 70, 72]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the SGB's appeal with costs, including those of two counsel. The decision substantiated the lower court's findings that the SGB acted beyond its statutory authority, failing to uphold the necessary standards of procedural fairness, and effectively denied Mr. Scheepers his legal rights afforded under the applicable education laws ([paras 78]). 

This case reinforces the delineation of powers between school governing bodies and school principals, illustrating that proper procedural safeguards must be followed to ensure that administrative decisions are lawful and fair. The ruling has significant implications for the governance of public schools in South Africa, particularly about managing principals' roles and the powers exercised by school governing bodies.
