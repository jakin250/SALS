# Case Note: Madzhadzhi v President of the RSA (160/2009) [2010] ZASCA 57

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The case of Madzhadzhi v President of the RSA ([1]–[4]) revolves around a dispute regarding the remuneration of traditional leaders, specifically headmen and headwomen, in Limpopo Province. The four appellants—N N Madzhadzhi (1st), N M Mudalahothe (2nd), R R Sumbana (3rd), and R A Ramugondo (4th)—sought declaratory relief after the provincial government terminated their salaries based on section 5(2) of the Remuneration of Public Office Bearers Act 20 of 1998 (REPOB).

The appellants had differing backgrounds concerning their employment status. Notably, the first appellant, while serving as a headman, was also employed as a public servant. The termination of their salaries on 30 September 2002 prompted the appellants to challenge the legality of this decision, asserting it was unlawfully enacted without proper consideration of their rights as traditional leaders ([5]–[9]). 

### I – Issues
The primary legal issue at hand was the interpretation of section 5(2) of REPOB, which prohibits traditional leaders from receiving dual remuneration from two public offices. The appellants contended that the section did not prohibit them from receiving a salary as traditional leaders while also holding public service roles ([11]–[12]). Chief questions include:
1. Does section 5(2) of REPOB apply to headmen who are also public service employees?
2. Were the actions of the provincial government in terminating salaries under this section lawful?

### R – Rules
The relevant legal authority includes the following:
1. **Section 5(2) of the REPOB** clarifies that traditional leaders who hold multiple public offices are entitled to the highest salary, with dual remuneration being explicitly prohibited.
2. **Section 30 of the Public Service Act 1994 (PSA)** governs public servants and stipulates that employees must gain permission from the relevant authority to perform remunerative work outside their public service role ([22]–[23]).

The court also considered previous legislative frameworks governing traditional leadership remuneration, including the Venda Traditional Leaders Proclamation and the dual authority of national and provincial statutes governing this aspect of traditional leadership ([14]–[17]).

### A – Application
In the application of these rules, the Supreme Court of Appeal found that the provincial government’s interpretation of section 5(2) was overly broad. The court reasoned that while the section aimed to prevent traditional leaders from receiving dual pay, it did not inherently prevent public service employees from also receiving remuneration as traditional leaders ([25]–[26]). The court specifically noted that the distinction between "public office holders" in REPOB did not include public servants under the PSA. Therefore, the provincial government was not justified in terminating the appellants' salaries based on this provision.

The court underscored that even if the appellants could have sought relief via the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA), this did not invalidate their application for declaratory relief under section 19(1)(a)(iii) of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959 ([27]). However, the court concluded that merely granting declaratory relief would not rectify the termination of salaries that had already occurred, thus complicating the appellants’ path to financial redress.

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, confirming the failure of the high court to appropriately interpret the applicable statutes. The court upheld the provincial government's salary determination due to the legislative framework underlying traditional leadership remuneration. Specifically, it found that the decision to terminate the salaries was indeed unlawful under section 5(2) of REPOB, but this was considered insufficient grounds for granting the declaratory order or reinstatement of salaries ([38]).

The implications of this decision illustrate the complexities inherent in the dual authority governing traditional leadership remuneration and the necessity for clear legislative frameworks that delineate the boundaries of public office holding and remuneration rights in South Africa.
