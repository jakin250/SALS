Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 129

F - Facts

[1] The first appellant, the N’Wandlamharhi Communal Property Association, is the registered owner of properties constituting the MalaMala Private Game Reserve (MalaMala) in Mpumalanga, which includes Charleston South and Charleston North. The second appellant, MalaMala Game Reserve (Pty) Ltd, manages MalaMala under a lease agreement. The respondents, Helen Lynne Westcott and Caroline Clare Cormack (sisters), and Rodrick Anton Beaumont and Michael Hemingford Beaumont (brothers) are descendants of previous owners of the Charleston properties.

[2] The properties originated from a farm owned by Mr. Frans Unger, the maternal grandfather of the respondents, who subdivided the land in 1958. By 1964, the two properties were registered in the names of Ms. Unger and Ms. Beaumont, respectively. The properties soon became part of the Sabi Sand Wildtuin reserve, eventually transitioning to a private eco-tourism venture as MalaMala.

[3] The litigation arose after the second appellant ceased recognizing the respondents' occupation and viewing rights pertaining to the Charleston properties, which prompted the respondents to seek enforcement of their rights through the courts. The case’s complexity was rooted in historical share agreements and the nature of the rights described therein.

[4] The shareholders agreements, particularly clauses defining "Viewing Rights" and other obligations, became focal points of contention. There was disagreement over whether the agreements created legally enforceable servitudes against the land or merely personal rights.

I - Issues

[5] Central to the appeal was the determination of whether the respondents possessed enforceable rights of access and occupation of the Charleston properties, specifically each property's implications regarding servitude registration against the land title.

[6] Additional questions included:

  • Did the shareholders' agreements reflect an intention to bind successors in title for servitude rights?
  • Were the respondents’ rights mere personal rights, and if so, could they be enforced under the doctrine of notice?
  • Does the Subdivision of Agricultural Land Act have any bearing on the validity of the respondents’ rights?

R - Rules

[7] A servitude is defined as a real right to utilize another’s property and must be registered to be enforceable against successors in title ([25]). For such rights to exist, two conditions must be satisfied:

  1. The creator of the right must intend to bind future owners of the property.
  2. The right must entail a subtraction of rights from the dominium of the property in question ([26]).

[8] The doctrine of notice allows personal rights to be enforced against individuals who have knowledge of the rights ([34]). In addition, the applicable statutory framework, namely the Subdivision of Agricultural Land Act 70 of 1970, sets out requirements for dealing with agricultural land ([22]).

[9] Precedents such as Ethekwini Municipality v Mounthaven (Pty) Ltd ([26]) provide interpretative guidance on the creation of servitudes, focusing on the intentions of the parties involved in the original agreements.

A - Application

[10] The Court analyzed the shareholders’ agreements and their implications regarding the successors’ rights. The agreements mentioned "successors in title" but did not manifest a clear intention to bind future owners or successors to the occupation and viewing rights, as established by previous cases ([29]-[30]).

[11] The respondents’ primary argument rested on interpreting clauses in the agreements which they claimed created rights that should be registered. However, the Court found that while the rights denoted a subtraction from the landowner's rights, this did not suffice in demonstrating the necessary intent for servitude creation ([30]-[33]).

[12] With respect to the doctrine of notice, the Court noted that since the rights had effectively terminated when the Charleston companies disposed of the properties, there existed no actionable right that could be enforced against the appellants or recognized under this doctrine ([35]). The appeal court also posited that since no valid servitudes were established, the provisions of the Subdivision of Agricultural Land Act were rendered irrelevant ([36]).

C - Conclusion

[13] The Supreme Court of Appeal effectively upheld the appeal, reinstating the original ruling that the respondents did not possess rights enforceable against the appellants to occupy or access the Charleston properties. The Court concluded that from the interpretation of the shareholders' agreements, the agreements did not manifest an intention to create binding servitudes for successors—a critical failure in the respondents’ argument ([37]).

[14] The implications of this ruling reaffirm the strict adherence expected in property rights and the ramifications of shareholders' agreements in determining real versus personal rights. Furthermore, the decision clarified the limits imposed by statutory provisions when considering the allocation and registration of real rights in land, particularly regarding agricultural land and its subdivision.
