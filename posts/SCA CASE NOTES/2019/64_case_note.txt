Case Note: SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: Ledwaba v The State, [2018] ZASCA 64

## F – Facts

Elijah Percy Sehlano Ledwaba (the appellant) was involved in a criminal case stemming from an incident on March 13, 2011, where he shot and killed Hendrick Jansen van Rensburg (the deceased) ([para 1]). The appellant, a police constable, was charged with murder and pointing a firearm, leading to a conviction for culpable homicide and an additional conviction for the unlawful pointing of a firearm ([para 1]). He was sentenced to five years' imprisonment for culpable homicide and two years for the firearm charge, to run concurrently ([para 1]).

Following his conviction, the appellant appealed to the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which upheld his culpable homicide conviction but set aside his conviction regarding the firearm ([para 2]). The high court confirmed the five-year sentence for culpable homicide and declared the appellant unfit to possess a firearm ([para 2]).

The appellant sought special leave to appeal the sentence imposed by the high court, contending that the five-year imprisonment was disproportionately harsh given the circumstances ([para 2]).

## I – Issues

The central legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) were:
1. Whether the sentence of five years' imprisonment for culpable homicide was appropriate given the circumstances of the case ([para 2]).
2. Whether the trial court had adequately conducted an enquiry into the appellant's fitness to possess a firearm as required under section 103(2) of the Firearms Control Act 60 of 2000 ([para 13]).

## R – Rules

The SCA frames its analysis within established legal principles regarding sentencing discretion:
1. Sentencing is fundamentally a matter of judicial discretion, and an appellate court should exercise caution when intervening with a sentence unless a substantial misdirection occurs ([para 9]).
2. In considering an appropriate sentence, courts must look at various factors, including the factual circumstances of the case, the offender's background, and the nature of the offense ([para 10]).
3. Section 103(2) of the Firearms Control Act stipulates that a court must hold an enquiry to determine whether an individual convicted of specific offenses is unfit to possess a firearm ([para 13]).

The SCA also discussed the case of *S v Malik* ([para 11]), where the sentence imposed was found to be disproportionate in comparison, emphasizing the necessity of proportionality in sentencing.

## A – Application

The SCA examined the trial court’s reasoning in imposing a five-year sentence. While recognizing that the deceased was the aggressor in the confrontation, the trial court maintained that the appellant, as a police officer with years of firearm training, should have known better than to use his firearm in that context ([para 10]). However, this reasoning was deemed lacking, as the SCA found that the appellant was indeed under attack and had no other reasonable means of self-defense ([para 8]).

The SCA also critiqued the trial court’s reliance on *S v Malik*, indicating a misunderstanding of its implications; the case derived a balance in sentencing that was not strictly applicable to the current matters ([para 11]). Rather than reinforcing the heavier sentence imposed, the SCA found that the appellant's personal circumstances, including the substantial loss of employment stemming from his conviction, warranted a reconsideration of the imposed sentence ([para 12]). The SCA concluded that the five-year imprisonment was excessively severe, contrasting it against the time already served by the appellant, which had amounted to seven months ([para 12]).

Regarding the firearm possession declaration, the SCA emphasized the explicit requirement for an enquiry under section 103(2) of the Firearms Control Act. The trial court had failed to conduct this requisite determination, which was an oversight recognized by both defense and prosecution ([para 14]). Thus, the declaration of unfitness to possess a firearm could not be upheld.

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal regarding the sentence, substituting the five-year imprisonment with a sentence of seven months' imprisonment, which was antedated to reflect the time already served ([para 2]). The SCA also set aside the order declaring the appellant unfit to possess a firearm due to the lack of proper judicial enquiry, underscoring the importance of due process in such decisions ([para 14]).

The implications of this judgment not only restore a more proportionate sentence for the appellant but also underscore the necessity for adherence to statutory requirements in determining fitness to possess firearms, thereby reinforcing the rule of law in the judicial system.
