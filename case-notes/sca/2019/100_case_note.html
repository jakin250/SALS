Sandvliet Boerdery (Pty) Ltd v Maria Mampies & Another (107/2018) [2019] ZASCA 100

Historically, the farms of Montina, which encompass both Onder-Plaas and Middel-Plaas, allowed for unfettered movement and access to burial sites for the occupants, fostering a sense of community and cultural continuity.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 100

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The case revolves around a dispute over the right to bury a deceased family member in a graveyard situated on land owned by Sandvliet Boerdery (Pty) Ltd, the appellant. The respondents, Maria and Hendrik Mampies, are a married couple who have familial ties to the deceased, Magdalene de Wee. Both respondents reside on a portion of land known as Onder-Plaas, having lived there for extended periods. The deceased had been an occupant of the same area prior to her death on February 22, 2017. The graveyard in question, situated on another parcel of land, Middel-Plaas, has been utilized historically by the respondents and their ancestors for burial purposes, with permission granted by former landowners.

Historically, the farms of Montina, which encompass both Onder-Plaas and Middel-Plaas, allowed for unfettered movement and access to burial sites for the occupants, fostering a sense of community and cultural continuity. However, upon changes in ownership in 2014, new restrictions were imposed. These included limiting the rights of the occupiers to their burial practices, prompting the respondents to initiate litigation to secure the right to bury the deceased in alignment with their cultural practices and beliefs ([paras 1–8]).

I – Issues

The central issue for determination was whether the respondents had the legal right to bury the deceased on the appellant’s property under section 6(2)(dA) of the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA). This legislative provision aims to secure the rights of occupiers and includes specific guidelines regarding burial rights. The appeal questioned whether the deceased's burial right could be claimed against the appellant, despite the deceased and the respondents residing on different but adjoining parcels of land ([paras 1, 11, 18]).

R – Rules

The legal frameworks applicable in this case included:

  1. Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 (ESTA) - Specifically, section 6(2)(dA) which defines the rights of occupiers to bury deceased family members in accordance with religious and cultural beliefs, provided there is an established practice on the land used ([para 16]).
  2. Constitutional Provisions - The provisions of section 25(6) of the Constitution, ensuring that individuals or communities whose land tenure is insecure due to historical injustices are entitled to secure tenure or comparable redress ([para 14]).
  3. Judicial Precedents - The court relied upon previous adjudications, including the case Dlamini & Another v Joosten & Others 2006 (3) SA 342 (SCA) that provided context on how burial rights under ESTA are aligned with residence and ownership ([para 12]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal applied a purposive interpretation to the term ‘reside’ as it pertains to ESTA, acknowledging that it was crucial to consider the facts of the case and the established practices surrounding burial rights. The court found that both the respondents and the deceased had sufficiently strong connections to the land in question, including the historical use of the Middel-Plaas graveyard.

The court contended that interpreting 'reside' strictly in relation to cadastral boundaries would undermine the intention behind ESTA, particularly as the legislation aims to protect the rights of historically marginalized groups. This interpretation was influenced by the concept that ‘reside’ should not be constrained to conventional definitions that exclude the cultural significance of ancestral burial sites ([paras 24–30]).

The court recognized that depriving the respondents of the right to mourn and conduct burial practices would infringe upon their dignity and religious rights, thereby failing to acknowledge the long-established practices that had developed on the land. The existence of an established practice of burying family members on Middel-Plaas, irrespective of ownership issues, reinforced the respondents’ claim ([paras 23, 28, 32]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appellant's appeal, affirming the ruling of the Land Claims Court, which had granted the right for the respondents to bury the deceased in the contested graveyard. The court articulated that any other interpretation would render the burial rights provided by ESTA ineffective, thereby contradicting the legislative intent. Notably, the decision underscored the need for a sensitive approach to interpreting legislative provisions in light of cultural practices and historical injustices.

The court’s ruling not only affirmed the rights of the Mampies family but also established a precedent for other occupiers and landowners in similar disputes. It highlighted the importance of understanding ‘residency’ in broader terms that encompass cultural identity and practices tied to the land ([paras 34–35]).

The judgment ultimately seeks to bolster the rights of vulnerable occupiers, ensuring that they are afforded dignity and security, necessitating that their established practices are respected and preserved, particularly in the context of ancestral rights ([paras 29, 34]).
