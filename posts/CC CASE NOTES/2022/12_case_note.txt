# Case Note: Ayres and Another v Minister of Justice and Correctional Services and Another [2021] ZACC 12

## FIRAC Structure

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–4], [5–8])

In **Ayres and Another v Minister of Justice and Correctional Services and Another**, the Constitutional Court of South Africa addressed the applicants' challenge against the constitutionality of section 63 of the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 140 of 1992 (the Drugs Act). The applicants, Gregory Craig Ayres and Valeri Lazanov Nikolov, were arrested in November 2014 for allegedly possessing methylenedioxymetamfetamine (MDMA), commonly known as ecstasy. The charge was based on their contravention of the Drugs Act ([para 2]). 

Prior to entering a plea, the applicants initiated proceedings in the KwaZulu-Natal Local Division of the High Court. They sought a declaration that section 63 of the Drugs Act and the reference to MDMA in Schedule 2 of the Act were unconstitutional and thus invalid. Section 63 empowers the Minister of Justice to amend schedules of the act, potentially conferring plenary legislative power to the executive, which they argued violated the principle of separation of powers ([para 3]).

The High Court dismissed the applicants' challenge, concluding that the legislature could delegate the power to amend the schedules to the Minister and found no evidence of abuse of discretion. Consequently, the court issued an order with costs against the applicants ([paras 5–6]). The applicants then sought permission to appeal this decision directly to the Constitutional Court, arguing that it conflicted with a prior judgment of this Court in **Smit v Minister of Justice and Correctional Services** ([paras 7–8]).

---

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 9–12], [14–16])

The primary issues presented before the Constitutional Court centered on:

1. **Separation of Powers**: Whether the delegation of legislative power to the executive through section 63 of the Drugs Act contravenes the separation of powers principle as provided by the South African Constitution.
2. **Stare Decisis and Legal Precedent**: Whether the High Court had acted inappropriately by not adhering to the binding precedent set by the Constitutional Court in **Smit** pertaining to the constitutionality of section 63 ([para 11]).
3. **Merits of the Appeal**: Specifically, whether leave to appeal should be granted in light of the conflict with the established precedent and the constitutional implications therein ([paras 9–12]).

---

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 6–10], [14–17])

The court relied on several key legal principles and precedents:

- **Separation of Powers**: The principle that legislative powers must reside with the legislature, as outlined in the South African Constitution.
- **Plenary Legislative Power**: Defined in **Smit**, the court explained that such a power denotes the full authority to enact laws, which cannot be improperly delegated to the executive ([para 5]).
- **Stare Decisis**: The necessity for courts to honor prior judgments unless there are compelling reasons to depart from established case law, as emphasized in the decisions from **Camps Bay Ratepayers’ and Residents’ Association** and **Ruta** ([paras 16–17]).
- **Legal Certainty and Judicial Integrity**: The court acknowledged that following precedent is central to maintaining predictability and coherence in the law.

---

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 14–19])

Upon review, the Constitutional Court determined that the High Court’s dismissal of the applicants' challenge was fundamentally flawed due to its failure to consider the implications of the **Smit** ruling. The **Smit** case had declared the same section 63 unconstitutional, making any contrary findings by the High Court inconsistent with binding precedent. The applicants had aptly argued that the High Court misplaced its reliance on the legality of the Minister's delegated powers without accounting for the explicit unconstitutionality determined in **Smit** ([paras 15–18]).

The court emphasized that the doctrine of precedent is integral to the rule of law, reinforcing that the High Court should have recognized the implications of the **Smit** decision. Given that the High Court’s reasoning failed to engage with the precedent, the Constitutional Court found that their ruling was untenable ([paras 16–18]).

Furthermore, regarding the costs order, the court recognized that the High Court’s decision had adverse implications for the applicants’ rights and interests. It was perceived as unfair for the applicants to bear costs when they had attempted to ensure that the **Smit** decision was part of the judicial consideration ([para 19]).

---

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([para 20–20])

The Constitutional Court, thus, concluded that:

1. **Leave to appeal on the merits was denied** due to the High Court’s order being correct despite its flawed reasoning; the lack of engagement with the binding precedent made the High Court's legal stance untenable.
2. **Leave to appeal regarding the costs was granted**, and the appeal on costs was successful. The costs order from the High Court was set aside, placing the burden on the respondents to pay the applicants' costs, including those for two counsels.
3. **Implications**: The ruling reaffirmed the importance of adhering to judicial precedent within South African law and underscored the constitutional commitment to the separation of powers. It highlighted that improper delegation of legislative power undermines the foundations of democratic governance as enshrined in the Constitution.

Overall, this case serves as an important reminder of the judiciary's role in preserving constitutional values and ensuring that legislative frameworks function within their prescribed bounds.
