# FIRAC Case Note: **Sias Moise v Transitional Local Council of Greater Germiston** (Case CCT 54/00)

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1-3])**

On 4 July 2001, the Constitutional Court of South Africa confirmed an order from the Witwatersrand High Court which declared section 2(1)(a) of the Limitation of Legal Proceedings (Provincial and Local Authorities) Act 94 of 1970 invalid. This section imposed a procedural bar requiring written notice before an individual could institute legal proceedings against local authorities, which the High Court found to be unconstitutional as it infringed the right of access to the courts as guaranteed by section 34 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, Act 108 of 1996.

Subsequent to this confirmation, an amicus curiae, the Women’s Legal Centre, argued that the Court should amend the order to apply retrospectively to all actions not time-barred when the Interim Constitution came into force on 27 April 1994. They claimed the absence of such a provision constituted a "patent error or omission" in the original order, leading to uncertainty regarding the order's effect on ongoing actions ([para 1]).

The Court received no further representations apart from the arguments presented by the amicus, which necessarily guided its reconsideration of the order ([para 2]).

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 4-6])**

The primary legal issues before the Court included:

1. Whether the Constitutional Court had the authority to amend its previous order to clarify or add a retrospective component ([para 4]).
2. Whether the original order's failure to expressly state its retrospective effect created ambiguity warranting clarification or modification ([para 8]).
3. Whether the determination of the order’s effect should consider its alignment with the Interim Constitution's provisions ([para 6]).

The Court also needed to explore the principles underpinning the doctrines of judicial finality and the amendment of judgments to determine if any oversight warranted altering the existing order ([para 4]).

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 4-14])**

The decision was primarily influenced by the common law principles regarding the finality and alteration of court judgments as established in *Firestone South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Genticuro AG* ([para 4], [para 7]). According to this principle, a court lacks the authority to alter its judgment unless approached in a reasonable time for accessory or consequential matters that were overlooked ([para 4]).

The Court also referenced the objective doctrine of constitutional invalidity as discussed in *Ferreira v Levin NO and Others* ([para 12]), noting how this principle emphasizes that once a law is declared unconstitutional, it is considered void ab initio, dating back to the constitution's inception. The contrast between the interim Constitution and the 1996 Constitution regarding retrospective effects facilitated a firmer legal grounding for the retrospective invalidation of laws in post-1994 legal proceedings ([para 13]).

Furthermore, section 172(1) of the 1996 Constitution empowers courts to declare laws invalid, while also allowing judiciaries to limit such declarations’ retrospective effects when just and equitable ([para 13]).

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 8-14])**

The Court found that the application for variation was premature because it lacked grounds that met the criteria set under common law for modifying a judgment. In scrutinizing the claims of ambiguity, the Court determined the original order's intent was clear; it was a straightforward confirmation of invalidity based on constitutional infringement ([para 6]). The prospective or retrospective nature of the invalidation hinged on established legal principles rather than an oversight in drafting.

In interpreting the ambiguity claim raised by the amicus, the Court affirmed that any uncertainty related to the order's effects—the possibility of retrospective or prospective application—was resolved by acknowledging the law governing the declaration of invalidity. Thus, since the High Court's ruling was explicitly aimed at the 1996 Constitution, the date the current Constitution came into effect on 4 February 1997 automatically retroactively invalidated the statute in question ([para 14]), and the argument concerning the Interim Constitution was discounted as irrelevant within this context ([para 9]).

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 15])**

The Constitutional Court ultimately refused the application for variation, holding that it had neither the authority nor any cause to amend the order made on 4 July 2001 ([para 15]). The ratio decidendi elucidated that the order's lack of specification regarding retrospective application was intentional, relying on the established principle of objective invalidity, which posits that a law declared invalid is void from the moment the Constitution took effect ([para 14]).

This ruling has significant implications in the realm of constitutional law, notably reinforcing judicial clarity on amendments and the correct interpretation of retrospective effects regarding laws declared unconstitutional. The case underscores the primacy of constitutional safeguards for access to justice and affirms the importance of precise legal drafting while providing insights into the interaction between transitional and current constitutional frameworks in South Africa.
