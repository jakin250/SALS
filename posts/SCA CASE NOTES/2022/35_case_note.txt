Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note on *Rasimate Samuel Baloyi v The State* (739/2021) [2022] ZASCA 35

### F – Facts

The case revolves around the conviction of Rasimate Samuel Baloyi (the appellant) for murder, following an incident that occurred on 4 March 2012 ([1], [2]). The appellant and his co-accused were charged with murder, as defined in the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 (CLAA), which prescribes mandatory life imprisonment for premeditated murder or murder committed in the execution of a common purpose ([3]). The incident in question involved the appellant approaching the deceased, Thabo Letsoalo, from behind and attacking him with a panga, subsequently allowing his accomplice to join in on the assault, resulting in serious injuries that led to Letsoalo's death shortly after arriving at the hospital ([4], [5]). 

Initially, the appellant recounted a version of events that portrayed him as having been involved in a minor altercation with the deceased earlier that day over a chair, asserting that he had not inflicted any injuries with a weapon ([6], [8]). However, the trial court rejected his account as unconvincing and found the eyewitness testimonies of Mr Thabo Letsoalo and Mr Nicholas Molepo credible ([10], [15]). The trial court sentenced both the appellant and the co-accused to life imprisonment for what was deemed a premeditated murder based on the evidence presented ([2], [9]).

The full bench of the High Court dismissed the appellant's appeal, which led him to seek further leave to appeal from the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) on the grounds that the state had failed to establish premeditation and that the life sentence was inappropriate ([1], [2]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal questions before the SCA were:

1. Whether the state effectively established that the murder was premeditated ([11]).
2. Whether the trial court's omission of addressing premeditation in its conviction phase constituted a misdirection impacting the fairness of the trial and the appropriateness of the sentence imposed ([20], [24]).

### R – Rules

In assessing the appeal, the SCA referenced several key legal principles. The court clarified that a finding of premeditation requires inferential reasoning based on the entirety of the evidence presented ([17]). The SCA highlighted the relevant legislative framework surrounding premeditated murder, particularly sections of the CLAA that impose minimum sentences and the procedural obligations of trial courts to address premeditated intentions at the conviction stage ([25]).

The SCA also discussed precedents, notably *Michael Legoa v State* and *S v Taunyane*, where failure to address premeditation prior to sentencing was identified as a significant misdirection ([18], [19]). Additionally, the court noted that the absence of such a pronouncement does not automatically result in prejudice to the accused, as not all failure to declare premeditated murder deprives an accused of a fair trial depending on the facts ([21], [23]).

### A – Application

In applying the law to the facts, the SCA examined the established evidence—particularly the actions of the appellant leading up to the attack, and the nature of the assault itself ([12]). The court differentiated the case from *S v Makatu*, where premeditation was absent due to the impulsive nature of the appellant's actions, finding that in this instance the appellant had taken the time to procure a weapon after the earlier confrontation ([13], [26]). This deliberation was critical in concluding that premeditation had been established; the appellant's return to the tavern equipped with a panga suggested premeditated intent to inflict harm on the victim, following a prior altercation ([12], [26]). 

The SCA also addressed the appropriateness of the sentence, reiterating that the discretion of the trial court in determining sentences is considerable, and overturning such discretion requires a clear misdirection or disproportionate appeal ([29]). The severity of the actions, coupled with the nature of the injuries sustained by the deceased, overwhelmingly supported the life sentence imposed by the trial court, thereby reinforcing the appropriateness of the sentence given the circumstances surrounding the case ([28]).

### C – Conclusion

In its decision, the SCA dismissed the appeal brought by Baloyi, upholding the findings of both the trial court and the High Court regarding both the conviction of murder and the life sentence. The SCA found that the state had established the requisite elements of premeditated murder, reinforcing that the trial court's assessment of the evidence was rightful and pertinent to the circumstances presented. The court emphasized that legal procedures require that premeditation should be considered during verdict deliberations but indicated that such failures would not always lead to prejudice against the accused. 

As a substantive legal conclusion, the SCA underscored the importance of balancing justice for victims and the rights of the accused, affirming that the sentence was consistent with the seriousness of the crime committed. The implications of this judgment highlight ongoing judicial obligations to clearly articulate findings of premeditation, particularly in murder cases, ensuring fair trial principles are consistently applied across the justice system. The judgment reflects critical assessments of both factual scenarios in criminal law as well as procedural fairness, shaping future cases that seek to delineate the boundaries of intent, planning, and sentence appropriateness in violent crime prosecutions.
