# FIRAC Case Note: CDA Boerdery (Edms) Bpk v The Nelson Mandela Metropolitan Municipality and Others [2007] SCA 1 (RSA)

## F – Facts

This case involves a dispute between CDA Boerdery (Edms) Bpk (the first appellant) and the Western Areas Property and Residents Association (the second appellant) against the Nelson Mandela Metropolitan Municipality (the first respondent) regarding the imposition of property assessment rates ([1]–[3]). 

The first appellant is a landowner, and the second appellant represents rural landowners dissatisfied with the rates that the local authority imposed on their properties, which had previously been exempt from such charges when the areas fell under the Western District Municipality ([1]). The new local authority, formed after the transition to a democratic South Africa, sought to impose these rates despite the properties not being rateable under the old dispensation.

Initially, the landowners sought an interim interdict to prevent the Municipality from recovering rates and service charges ([3]). They later abandoned objections to service charges but contested the rates and valuation of their properties for the financial years 2002-2003 and 2003-2004, claiming these actions lacked legal basis ([3]). The High Court dismissed their application, leading to their appeal ([3]).

The legal backdrop includes the Municipal Ordinance 20 of 1974, which required consultation with the Premier before imposing rates exceeding two cents in the rand. The transition to the new Constitution and associated legislation raised questions concerning the validity of this requirement ([9], [21]).

## I – Issues

The central issues of this case are:

1. Did the imposition of rates operate within the bounds of the Local Government Transition Act 209 of 1993 (Transition Act)?
2. Did the requirement for Premier's approval for rates exceeding two cents in the rand survive the transition to the new constitutional framework, especially when the powers of municipalities were elevated?
3. Did the appellants suffer any arbitrary deprivation of property or discrimination under sections 25 and 229(2)(a) of the Constitution, respectively? ([22]–[24], [45]).

These questions necessitated an analysis of the legal frameworks governing local government and rating powers, particularly the Transformation Act and the relevant statutory ordinances.

## R – Rules

The court relied on various statutes and legal principles in resolving the matter:

1. **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa**: 
   - Section 229 provides municipalities the power to impose rates on properties, regulated by national legislation ([10], [11]).

2. **Local Government Transition Act 209 of 1993**: 
   - Sections 10G(6), (6A), and (7) concerning property valuation and the imposition of rates ([8], [9]).

3. **Municipal Ordinance 20 of 1974**: 
   - Required the Premier’s approval for property rates above two cents per rand ([8]). The implications of this ordinance altered the understanding of municipal powers in the new constitutional context.

4. **Case Law**: Previous judgments were cited, including *Fedsure Life Assurance Ltd v Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council*, underscoring municipalities' transition from a delegated system to one with original powers ([23]).

## A – Application

The court analyzed whether the Municipality adhered to the statutory requirements in levying the rates. Key points included:

1. **Valuation and Rating Framework**: The court emphasized that valuation and rating should comply not only with the Transition Act but should also consider existing ordinances (such as the Municipal Ordinance 1974) as integral parts of the regulatory framework. The absence of clear guidelines in the Transition Act left ambiguities surrounding proper conduct ([12], [14]).

2. **Implied Repeal of Premier's Approval**: The majority opinion held that the requirement for the Premier's approval was impliedly repealed by the advent of the new Constitution, reflecting a broader shift in municipal powers (Cameron JA in the majority opinion) ([32], [44]). In contrast, the minority perspective emphasized the importance of transitional provisions that maintained checks on municipal powers until the complete enactment of new regulations ([24], [30]).

3. **Assessment of Arbitrary Conduct**: The court considered whether the rates imposed on the appellants were arbitrary or discriminatory. The majority noted that despite challenges, the Municipality's approach was grounded in a rational basis. The differentiation in management of rates across areas was acknowledged as a necessity arising from the operational context of governing a newly consolidated municipality ([47],[49]).

4. **Notification and Procedural Compliance**: The court evaluated the claim that proper notification of rate changes had not been provided. Although some notifications were disputed, participation in valuation processes indicated the inadequacy of the claim that the appellants were denied opportunity to challenge valuations ([51]).

## C – Conclusion

The appeal was dismissed with costs, affirming the validity of the municipality's actions regarding property rates. The majority ruling (Cameron JA) asserted that the requirement for the Premier’s approval was no longer substantive under the revised constitutional framework ([52]). The court underscored the legislative intention to empower municipalities while also recognizing that they must adhere to rationality and fairness in the exercise of their powers. The broader implications of this ruling reaffirm the autonomy granted to local governments in South Africa post-transition while establishing clear guidelines for dealing with historical legislative frameworks.

The potential conflict arising between old order legislation and new constitutional directives was highlighted, suggesting an ongoing need for legislative clarity and coherence to ensure that municipal powers are both effective and responsible in addressing issues of local governance and revenue collection ([24]–[40]).
