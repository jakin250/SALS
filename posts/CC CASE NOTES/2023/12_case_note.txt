# Case Note: Speaker of the National Assembly and Others v New Nation Movement NPC and Others [2023] ZACC 12

## FIRAC Case Note

### Facts

The case under analysis involves an urgent application for the extension of an order made by the Constitutional Court of South Africa on 11 June 2020, which declared the Electoral Act 73 of 1998 unconstitutional to the extent that it only permitted election to the National Assembly and Provincial Legislatures through party membership ([1]). The initial order provided a 24-month suspension of the declaration of invalidity, expiring on 10 June 2022 ([2]). An extension of this suspension was subsequently granted until 10 December 2022 due to Parliament’s inability to remedy the identified constitutional defect within the stipulated timeframe ([2]).

On 5 December 2022, just days before the expiry of the second order, the applicants—including the Speaker of the National Assembly, the Chairperson of the National Council of Provinces, and the Minister of Home Affairs—filed an urgent application for yet another extension until 28 February 2023 ([5]). The New Nation Movement NPC, along with other respondents, strongly opposed this application, arguing that adequate time had already been afforded for amendments to the Electoral Act and that additional time was unnecessary ([6], [13]).

The applicants maintained that substantial amendments proposed by the National Council of Provinces necessitated further public consultation, a process that could not be completed before the deadline ([9]-[10]). They contended that failure to secure an extension would lead to a lapse in the electoral system, rendering it incapable of supporting a legitimate electoral process for the upcoming 2024 elections ([12]).

### Issues

The primary legal questions presented in this case revolve around the following issues:

1. **Urgency of the Application**: Whether the circumstances justify an urgent extension of the suspension of the declaration of invalidity, given the approaching deadlines and the complexity of the required amendments.
2. **Justification for Extension**: Whether the applicants provided sufficient justification for why the amendment process could not be completed within the established timeframe, and if the proposed extension is in the interest of justice ([18]-[20], [22]).

### Rules

The relevant legal authorities and principles include:
- **Section 172(1)(b) of the South African Constitution**: This provision grants courts the discretion to provide just and equitable remedies, emphasising the importance of the "interest of justice" as the dominant consideration ([22]).
- **Rules of the Constitutional Court**: Rule 12 outlines the necessity for urgent applications to clearly set forth justified reasons for deviating from standard procedures ([18]).
- **Judicial Precedent**: The court has previously indicated that extensions of declarations of invalidity should be granted cautiously, highlighting that such extensions are not a matter of routine, particularly at the last minute ([22]). Key cases referenced include *Electoral Commission of South Africa v Speaker of the National Assembly* and *Acting Speaker of the National Assembly v Teddy Bear Clinic for Abused Children* ([19], [22]).

### Application

In assessing the application for an extension, the court evaluated:
- **Urgency**: The applicants were found to have acted upon recognising a significant procedural hindrance caused by late amendments that required public consultation. Given the potential implications for the electoral system and democratic process, the court accepted the urgency of the matter ([20], [21]).
- **Prospects of Compliance**: The court acknowledged that an extension until 28 February 2023 was justifiable, noting that the Electoral Commission supported this timeframe based on its operational needs for preparing for the upcoming elections ([21]).
- **Public Interest**: The ruling underscored the broader democratic implications of the case, asserting that the potential void in the electoral framework would harm the public interest. Thus, the court's decision to extend the declaration reinforced its commitment to upholding democracy while balancing procedural compliance and legislative efficacy ([23]).

### Conclusion

The Constitutional Court, in a unanimous judgment delivered by Deputy Chief Justice Maya, granted an extension of the declaration of invalidity until 28 February 2023. This decision was based on a careful consideration of the urgency of the matter, the potential consequences of failing to extend the suspension, and the need for adequate public participation in legislative reform ([3], [4]). The court’s reasoning reflects a commitment to preserving democratic integrity while facilitating the necessary legal framework for future elections.

This case serves as a pertinent reminder of the judiciary's role in safeguarding constitutional provisions while navigating the complexities of legislative reform and public participation. The ruling highlights the delicate balance courts must maintain between enforcing constitutional obligations and accommodating practical legislative processes within the governmental framework ([23]).
