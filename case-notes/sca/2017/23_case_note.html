Supreme Court of Appeal

Asla Construction (Pty) Ltd v Buffalo City Metropolitan Municipality [2017] ZASCA 23

The Respondent claimed that the contracts were unlawful, violating s 217 of the Constitution, which mandates that state organs must ensure fairness, equity, transparency, and cost-effectiveness in procurement ([2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 23

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

This appeal arose from the dire housing crisis faced by the residents of Duncan Village, situated within the jurisdiction of the Buffalo City Metropolitan Municipality (the "Respondent") ([1]). The Appellant, Asla Construction (Pty) Ltd, was contracted to address this need. The dispute stemmed from two contracts—the "Turnkey" contract and the "Reeston" contract. The Respondent challenged the validity of these contracts after the Appellant sought provisional sentence based on payment certificates issued for work performed ([2]).

The Respondent claimed that the contracts were unlawful, violating s 217 of the Constitution, which mandates that state organs must ensure fairness, equity, transparency, and cost-effectiveness in procurement ([2]). Consequently, the Respondent instituted a review of the Reeston contract, asserting it was void ab initio.

Initially, the court a quo ruled in favor of the Respondent, declaring the Reeston contract invalid, and dismissing the Appellant's claim for provisional sentence with costs ([3]). The Appellant appealed, primarily arguing that the Respondent had failed to act without unreasonable delay regarding the review as mandated by the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (the "PAJA") ([4]).

I – Issues

The primary issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal were:

  1. Did the Respondent comply with the time limits imposed by PAJA regarding the review of the Reeston contract? The Appellant contended that the review application was brought out of time and not in compliance with the statutory requirements of PAJA, particularly sections 7 and 9 ([5]).
  2. Did the court a quo err in concluding that the Respondent's delay was justified and therefore granted an extension of the 180-day period mandated by PAJA? The Respondent argued it only became aware of the irregularities post the forensic report dated October 28, 2015 ([6]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal rules stem from the PAJA, particularly:

  • Section 7: Requires any proceedings for judicial review to be instituted without unreasonable delay, specifically within 180 days from when the applicant was informed of the administrative action or ought reasonably to have known of it ([4], [6]).
  • Section 9: Allows for the extension of the time period under specific circumstances but mandates a substantive application to that effect ([5], [8]).

Relevant case law includes:

  • Aurecon South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Cape Town City [2015] ZASCA 209, which clarified that knowledge of the reviewable irregularities does not reset the time limit for instituting review proceedings ([7]).
  • Camps Bay Ratepayers’ and Residents’ Association & another v Harrison & another [2010] ZASCA 3, underscoring the need for a reasonable explanation for any delay when seeking an extension ([11]).

A – Application

The Court analyzed the Respondent's failure to provide a full and reasonable explanation for the 15-month delay preceding the review application. The Respondent asserted it became aware of the alleged irregularities only after receiving the forensic report; however, this claim was deemed insufficient ([16]). The timeline, showing extensive interim actions by the Respondent that contradicted its stated ignorance, was crucial ([16]).

The Court emphasized the necessity of evaluating the implications of delaying the review process: prejudice to the Appellant, public interests, and the significant nature of the housing crisis. The need for timely resolution was underscored, given the dire situation in Duncan Village ([20], [21]). Furthermore, evidence demonstrated that a substantial amount of work had been performed under the Reeston contract, presenting significant ongoing prejudice for the Appellant and community members if set aside ([19], [20]).

The Court noted that even though the previous ruling cited serious constitutional breaches as part of its justification for the extension, this was flawed by failing to consider whether condoning the delay was justified under the specific circumstances ([10], [12]).

C – Conclusion

The Appeal Court found that the court a quo had indeed erred in granting an extension for the Respondent's late review of the Reeston contract under PAJA ([9]). The Respondent's failure to provide appropriate justifications for its delay, combined with the substantial prejudice to both the Appellant and the community, led to the determination that the original contract award had been validated by the unreasonable delay ([24]).

Thus, the Court set aside the decision of the court a quo, thereby upholding the validity of the Reeston contract and the associated payment certificates. The Appellant was granted costs, including costs for two counsels, highlighting the significant implications of this ruling in reinforcing the necessity for adherence to procedural timelines in administrative justice ([29]).

This decision underscores the balance courts must maintain between ensuring lawful administrative actions and the practical consequences of delays in reviewing such actions, particularly where substantial community needs and public interest are concerned.
