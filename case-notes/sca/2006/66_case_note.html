Supreme Court of Appeal

Harmony Gold Mining Co Ltd v Regional Director: Free State, Department of Water Affairs and Forestry [2006] SCA 65 (RSA)

In March 2005, Buffelsfontein entered provisional liquidation, revealing that it lacked the funds to continue necessary dewatering operations.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2006

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–13])

The appellant, Harmony Gold Mining Company Limited, is one of five mining companies operating in the Klerksdorp–Orkney–Stilfontein–Hartebeesfontein (KOSH) basin of North-West Province, South Africa. Among these, the northernmost mines, namely Buffelsfontein, Hartebeesfontein, and Stilfontein, are defunct but require ongoing dewatering to prevent flooding, as their closure has led to groundwater pooling ([paras 1–3]). The underlying geology includes dolomitic layers holding pristine aquifers that can become polluted when mining operations cease, due to exposure of iron pyrite to air and water, leading to oxidation ([para 2]).

In March 2005, Buffelsfontein entered provisional liquidation, revealing that it lacked the funds to continue necessary dewatering operations. On 11 April 2005, Anglogold Ashanti Limited filed an application in the Johannesburg High Court, seeking to compel further dewatering efforts involving the defunct mines. The appellant was joined to this application due to its vested interest in the potential flooding of its operations, which could lead to significant financial losses ([paras 4–5]).

In the interim, the Regional Director of Water Affairs issued directives under Section 19(3) of the National Water Act 36 of 1998, mandating Harmony to undertake pollution prevention measures, including the dewatering of the defunct mines and their associated costs ([paras 6–10]). Harmony contested these directives, claiming they were beyond the Regional Director's authority and constituted unlawful administrative action under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA). The High Court dismissed Harmony's application for review, upholding the directives as valid ([paras 11–12]). Harmony appealed this decision, focusing primarily on the interpretation of Section 19 of the National Water Act ([para 13]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 13–14])

The principal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) were:

  1. Whether the Regional Director had the authority under Section 19(3) of the National Water Act to issue directives requiring Harmony to take anti-pollution measures on land owned by other mining companies.
  2. The interpretation of 'reasonable measures' as required by Section 19(1) of the Act, specifically concerning whether such measures could extend to actions taken beyond the land owned by the entity required to take those measures ([paras 11–14]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 14–20])

The court's decision was grounded in several key legal principles:

  1. National Water Act 36 of 1998: Particularly, Section 19, which mandates landowners to prevent pollution from activities on their land, delineates the authority of catchment management agencies to compel action when pollution is imminent or ongoing. Subsections (2) and (3) detail measures that can be demanded from those responsible for land ownership or usage ([paras 14–19]).
  2. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA): This Act provides the framework for reviewing administrative actions, allowing the parties to challenge actions on grounds such as lack of authority and procedural irregularities ([para 11]).
  3. Constitutional Framework: Section 24 of the Constitution guarantees the right to a safe environment, bolstering the legislative intent behind the National Water Act to prevent pollution and ensure sustainable resource management ([para 17]).
  4. Judicial Interpretations: The SCA referenced relevant case law to elucidate interpretations of statutory language, particularly concerning the application of the term “reasonable measures” and the implications of jurisdictional authority in pollution control ([para 28]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 21–35])

The SCA conducted a thorough analysis of the statutory language and its implications for the obligations of landowners under Section 19 of the Act:

  1. Authority of the Regional Director: The court found that the directives issued fell within the parameters established in Section 19(3). It emphasized that the purpose of these directives, aimed at preventing pollution and ensuring safety from flooding, justified actions that transcended the physical boundaries of Harmony’s land. It interpreted the lack of express territorial limits in subsection (1) as indicative of legislative intent to encompass necessary anti-pollution measures, even on adjacent or implicated parcels ([paras 21–34]).
  2. Concept of ‘Reasonable Measures’: Contrary to Harmony's argument, the court held that 'reasonable measures' could indeed include responsibilities for actions taken on land other than that owned or controlled by the directed entity. This interpretation was framed within the constitutional obligation to protect the environment, framing the prevention of pollution as a communal responsibility among landowners in a shared hydrological context ([paras 32–34]).
  3. Interpretation of ‘Pollution’: The court noted that the pollution referenced was consistent with statutory definitions, reinforcing the position that groundwater affected by activities in the KOSH basin constituted valid concern under the foreseeable pollution risks that arise from interconnected mining operations ([para 20]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 36–36])

The SCA rejected Harmony Gold Mining Co Ltd’s appeal, concluding that the Regional Director acted within the legal authority granted under Section 19 of the National Water Act 36 of 1998. The court underscored the legislative framework’s intent to prevent pollution holistically, allowing for inter-company cooperation in ongoing dewatering efforts to mitigate risks of flooding and environmental degradation. As such, the directives were lawful and justified under the circumstances.

The ruling serves as an important precedent for clarifying the responsibilities of mining companies in shared ecological settings, affirming that collective action may be necessary when pollution risks cross property boundaries, thereby reinforcing the proactive environmental stewardship mandated by South Africa’s constitutional and legislative frameworks ([paras 36]).
