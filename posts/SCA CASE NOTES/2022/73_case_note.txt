Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# Case Note: Limpopo Economic Development Agency v Klopper NO & 10 Others (2022) ZASCA 73

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts ([1]–[10])

The Appellant, the Limpopo Economic Development Agency (LEDA), held a 40% share in ASA Metals (Pty) Ltd (ASA), while the remaining 60% was held by Eastern Asia Metal Investment Co Ltd (EAMI). ASA, in turn, was the sole shareholder of Dilokong Chrome Mine (Pty) Ltd (DCM), which operated under a mining right granted pursuant to the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA). Both ASA and DCM were placed under business rescue, with various practitioners appointed as business rescue practitioners (BRPs).

The conflict arose after DCM's BRPs decided to sell the mining right to Cheetah Chrome South Africa (Pty) Ltd (Cheetah). LEDA applied to the Gauteng Local Division of the High Court, seeking a declarator for its 40% stake in DCM’s mining right and interdicts against the sale without its consent. The High Court dismissed these claims, leading to a subsequent appeal.

### I – Issues ([4]–[6])

The core legal issue revolved around the correct interpretation of clause 17 of DCM's mining right, particularly whether this clause conferred a right to LEDA that would allow it to claim a stake in DCM's mining rights. The question was whether it implied an obligation on DCM to transfer any rights to LEDA and how such interpretations aligned with the broader objectives of the MPRDA.

### R – Rules ([22]–[23], [24]–[36])

The MPRDA prioritizes the equitable distribution of South Africa's mineral resources, particularly emphasizing opportunities for historically disadvantaged persons. Specific provisions under section 2 further elaborate on the aims of promoting equitable access, economic participation, and the custodianship of mineral resources by the State.

Interpretation principles were drawn from the case of *Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality*, which emphasizes contextual interpretation and the avoidance of unreasonable or businesslike outcomes. The court is to derive meaning from the document as a whole, assessing the language within its contextual and purposive framework.

### A – Application ([24]–[36], [52]–[63])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dissected clause 17, finding its wording vague and inelegantly constructed. The interpretation advanced by LEDA that it held a 40% stake in DCM’s mining right was deemed flawed. The court identified that the Minister had no authority to extend rights to parties beyond those stipulated in the relevant mining rights application and that such a reading of clause 17 created an unlawful situation.

The court clarified that clause 17 needed to reflect the requirements place on LEDA by its own shareholders’ agreement, which mandated the identification of a BEE shareholder for compliance with the Mining Charter. Notably, since LEDA failed to identify and transfer a stake to any BEE entity, its claim lacked credibility.

Despite LEDA's interpretation, the court upheld the principle that DCM, as the holder of the mining right, remained its only beneficiary. It further noted that the drafting mistakes in relation to clause 17 provided insights into the intended purpose of the provision, reinforcing that LEDA's interest was fundamentally in its 40% shareholding in ASA and not DCM’s mining rights.

### C – Conclusion ([37]–[65])

The appeal was ultimately dismissed with costs. The court concluded that LEDA had no legal basis to claim a stake in DCM’s mining rights, rendering its claims for declaratory and interdictory relief baseless. The judgment outlined the necessity of clear alignment between statutory provisions, shareholders’ agreements, and the expectations of empowerment initiatives within the MPRDA. 

The ruling emphasized the importance of upholding the objectives of equitable access to mineral resources and the encouragement of meaningful participation of historically disadvantaged individuals in the mining sector. The decision serves as a vital reminder of the rigid regulatory framework governing mining rights in South Africa and the critical role of compliance with statutory and constitutional mandates in business operations within the industry.
