# Case Note: Marsland v The Additional District Court Magistrate, Kempton Park and Another

**Neutral Citation:** Marsland v The Additional District Court Magistrate, Kempton Park and Another (162/20) [2021] ZASCA 14 (10 February 2021)

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
This appeal arises from the extradition proceedings involving Timothy Gordon Marsland, a dual citizen of South Africa and the United Kingdom residing in Botswana. On 12 July 2019, Marsland was arrested at OR Tambo International Airport under a warrant issued in terms of section 5(1)(b) of the Extradition Act 67 of 1962 (the Act) pursuant to a request for provisional arrest by the Republic of Botswana via an Interpol Red Notice ([paras 1–5]).

The alleged offences involved money laundering linked to the Botswana Public Officers Fund and fraudulent attempts to secure significant funds from the First National Bank of Botswana ([para 4]). Following his arrest, Marsland appeared before the Additional District Court Magistrate in Kempton Park on 15 July 2019. His bail application was refused, and the matter was postponed several times ([paras 5–6]).

On 23 August 2019, the prosecution presented three key documents to the court, including a Note Verbale from Botswana requesting extradition, alongside correspondence from the South African Department of International Relations and Cooperation (DIRCO) referring the extradition request to the Minister of Justice ([para 5]). Marsland's counsel argued that the extradition process was flawed because the Minister had not issued a notice under section 5(1)(a) of the Act, leading to his unlawful detention ([para 6]).

The first respondent dismissed Marsland's application for release, asserting that an application for his extradition had indeed been received before the statutory period expired ([para 7]). The Gauteng Division of the High Court upheld this decision ([para 8]).

### I – Issues
The pivotal legal question in this appeal centers on whether the provisional arrest under section 5(1)(b) of the Extradition Act had lapsed because the Minister of Justice did not personally receive the extradition request nor issue a notice as required under section 5(1)(a) of the Act, within the stipulated 30-day time frame following the arrest ([paras 1–2]).

### R – Rules
The relevant legal provisions are found in the Extradition Act and the Southern African Development Community (SADC) Protocol on Extradition. Key sections of the Act include:

- **Section 5(1)(a):** Allows for the issuance of a warrant for a person’s arrest upon receipt of a notification from the Minister that a request for their extradition has been received.
  
- **Section 5(1)(b):** Permits the issuance of an arrest warrant based on sufficient information that a person is accused of an extraditable offence, independent of formal notification from the Minister.

- **Article 10(5)** of the SADC Protocol stipulates that provisional arrest is terminated if the requested state does not receive the extradition request and supporting documents within 30 days post-arrest, allowing for extensions as permitted under domestic law ([paras 3–4, 10–14]).

### A – Application
The court emphasized the importance of interpreting the statutory language and context effectively. It found that the extradition request was sufficiently communicated through diplomatic channels, thus satisfying the procedural requirements set forth in both the Act and the Protocol ([paras 17–18]).

The court dismissed the argument that a direct personal receipt by the Minister was necessary, interpreting the terms “diplomatic channel” and “Ministries” according to their ordinary meanings. It concluded that the request for extradition had been effectively communicated in compliance with the Protocol ([para 17]).

Moreover, the argument that a section 5(1)(a) notice was obligatory for the extradition process to advance was rejected, as the processes outlined in sections 5(1)(a) and (b) were found to be distinct and disjunctive ([paras 19–20]). The court noted that the procedures involved sufficient safeguards against potential abuses in the application of the Act, addressing concerns that the provisional arrest system might be exploited ([para 21]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court's ruling that the extradition request was validly received and that the procedures required under the Extradition Act were appropriately followed. The court’s decision reinforces the interpretation that personal notification by the Minister is not requisite under section 5(1)(b) and clarifies the relationship between domestic laws and international protocols in extradition matters ([paras 22]). Additionally, due to the nature of the appeal, no costs were awarded against Marsland, signifying a balanced approach towards those contesting their extradition ([para 22]).

This judgment elucidates critical aspects of South African extradition law, particularly concerning procedural nuances in the interplay between domestic law and international agreements.
