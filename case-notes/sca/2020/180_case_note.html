Supreme Court of Appeal

Gobela Consulting CC v Makhado Municipality (Case no 910/19)

This appeal arises from a contract made between Gobela Consulting CC (hereinafter referred to as "Gobela") and the Makhado Municipality (hereinafter referred to as "the Municipality").

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])

This appeal arises from a contract made between Gobela Consulting CC (hereinafter referred to as "Gobela") and the Makhado Municipality (hereinafter referred to as "the Municipality"). The dispute centers on a contract awarded without adherence to the requisite procurement process mandated by law ([para 1]).

On 22 February 2011, Gobela submitted an unsolicited proposal titled 'Proposal to review and Develop the Anti-Corruption Strategy and Capacity Building for Makhado Municipality' to the Municipality. This proposal was formally accepted by the Municipality’s Municipal Manager on 5 May 2011 in a letter that detailed a training program to address issues of anti-corruption and fraud ([paras 2–3]).

The contract specified a total training of 745 councillors and officials across several months at a remuneration of R7,500 per trained individual. Following this acceptance, Gobela commenced preparations, including drafting training manuals and engaging contractors and personnel to conduct the training ([para 4–5]).

Incidentally, on the date scheduled for the training, Gobela was informed that the activity could not proceed due to internal political disputes, leading to a failure of performance on the Municipality’s part. Subsequently, Gobela issued an invoice for an amount of R6,369,750 and later filed a summons for damages amounting to R5,131,470, which stemmed from an alleged breach of contract by the Municipality ([paras 6–8]).

The Municipality raised a special plea asserting that the agreement constituted a breach of the Local Government Municipal Finance Management Act 56 of 2003 (hereafter referred to as "MFMA") and its Supply Chain Management Policy, claiming the contract was invalid due to lack of proper authority by its Municipal Manager ([para 9]). The Municipal Manager’s authority was a pivotal point of contention, yet no counter-application was made by the Municipality to formally annul the contract ([para 10]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 11–12])

The core issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the court a quo had the authority to declare the contract between Gobela and the Municipality invalid and unlawful without an explicit counter-application from the Municipality seeking to set aside said contract ([para 12]).

This brings into focus the legality of a collateral challenge to the contract when the state authority admits to the breach of statutory requirements without following through with a formal annulment procedure.

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 13–22])

The constitutional context in which this case operates is informed by Section 217 of the South African Constitution, which requires all organs of state to procure goods and services in a manner that is fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective ([para 13]). Further, relevant statutory provisions from the MFMA and related supply chain regulations establish thresholds and processes that must be adhered to during procurement ([paras 14–15]).

Precedents refer to the ruling in MEC for Health, Eastern Cape and Another v Kirland Investments (Pty) Ltd [2014] ZACC 6, reinforcing the necessity for a counter-application to challenge the validity of administrative actions, and the principles set forth in Merafong City Local Municipality v AngloGold Ashanti Limited [2016] ZACC 35, which elaborates on collateral challenges ([paras 11, 17]). Notably, Qaukeni Local Municipality and Another v FV General Trading CC [2009] ZASCA 66 judicially recognizes that contracts made in breach of legal prescripts are rendered invalid ([para 17]).

Moreover, the interpretation of reactive challenges per the Department of Transport and Others v Tasima (Pty) Limited [2017] ZACC 39 states that state organs may rely on the invalidity of administrative acts as a defense in enforcement actions, provided it serves justice ([para 19]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 12, 21–22])

The court scrutinized whether the Municipality could issue a collateral challenge regarding the invalidity of the contract without formal proceedings for annulment. This analysis hinged on understanding the statutory requirements affecting public procurement and the balance of justice when state parties challenge their own obligations ([paras 18–19]).

The facts illustrated that Gobela’s proposal was unsolicited, contravening the MFMA and its procurement policies ([para 16]). The Municipality's prior invitation for tenders for similar services underscored its breach of mandated competitive bidding processes, solidifying the conclusion that the contract was invalid from inception. Hence, the Court reinforced that the gross violation of procurement mandates justified the decision to declare the contract invalid notwithstanding the absence of a counter-application ([paras 21–22]).

Moreover, the court acknowledged that failing to declare the contract invalid would effectively facilitate illegal patronage and financial misappropriation, contravening the public interest aims underlying legislative procurement frameworks ([para 21]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 22–23])

The Supreme Court concluded that the appeal by Gobela should be dismissed, affirming the lower court's ruling that the contract was invalid and unlawful ([para 23]). The decision emphasized the principle of legality in public procurement, outlining the importance of observing statutory procurement frameworks designed to preclude illegal contracts and ensure accountability.

The Court's pronouncement effectively underscores that a municipal authority can reactively invoke invalidity in contractual disputes, provided doing so serves justice. This case sets precedent for how future procurement-related disputes may be adjudicated, particularly regarding the legal authority of municipal and state organs and the balance of public procurement integrity against contractual enforcement claims. The decision also elucidates the significance of complying with legislative prescriptions governing public contracts which serves as a pivotal reminder of the strict adherence required by all organs of state when entering into contracts.

In light of these findings, costs were awarded in favor of the Municipality, following the genera rule that costs follow the suit, hence closing the matter definitively on the grounds of procedural and legal principles grounded in constitutional law.
