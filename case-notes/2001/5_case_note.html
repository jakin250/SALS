Constitutional Court

Constitutional Court of South Africa, Case CCT 16/98

This case arises from an application for the review of taxation of a bill of costs related to an appeal before the Constitutional Court of South Africa [1].

Constitutional Court 2001 CCT 16/98

F – Facts

This case arises from an application for the review of taxation of a bill of costs related to an appeal before the Constitutional Court of South Africa [1]. The case concerns three appellants: The President of the Republic of South Africa, the Minister of Sport and Recreation, and the Director-General of the Department of Sport and Recreation, who contested a decision made by the High Court in favor of the first respondent, the Gauteng Lions Rugby Union, and its president, Dr. Louis Luyt [2]. The appeal was characterized by eight days of hearings that included discussions on a separate recusal application, where Dr. Luyt sought the recusal of all judges of the Court [2].

The appellants, represented by a legal team from the State Attorney's office, and the respondents, also represented by a joint legal team, engaged in extensive argumentation where a considerable portion of the first few days was spent on the recusal motion [3]. The Court subsequently dismissed the recusal application while reserving costs, which were to be decided in conjunction with the merits of the appeal [4].

Following the Court's decision, a bill of costs, amounting to R1,139,145.30, was submitted for taxation, which included attorneys' fees and substantial disbursements attributed primarily to counsel's fees [5]. The taxing master reduced the fees allowed for counsel while maintaining them above what the respondents contended was reasonable. This led to a dispute about the appropriate amounts to be taxed, particularly concerning counsel's fees and perusal fees for attorney services [7–8].

The taxing master issued her allocatur, allowing total fees of R1,054,986.85 [8]. However, the respondents subsequently requested the taxing master to state a case for the Court's review, raising concerns over the appropriateness of the fees allowed [9].

I – Issues

The primary legal issues in this case include:

  1. Whether the taxing master's decisions concerning counsel's fees and attorney's perusal fees comply with applicable legal standards and precedents [8].
  2. The principle governing the taxation of costs, specifically how to distinguish between costs related to the appeal and those linked to the recusal application [16].
  3. What standard should the Court apply in reviewing the taxing master's decisions, particularly in instances where the standards of taxation between the Constitutional Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal may differ [12–14].

R – Rules

Several critical legal rules and precedents inform the Court's analysis:

  1. General Principle of Cost indemnity: Established by Innes CJ in Texas Co. (S.A.) Ltd. v Cape Town Municipality, stating that the purpose of costs is to indemnify a successful party for expenses unjustly incurred due to litigation [15].
  2. Guidelines for Taxation of Fees: Under the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) rules, taxation principles dictate that costs must be necessary and proper for attaining justice, and that excessive and unreasonable fees should not be allowed [15].
  3. Jurisdictional Approach: The Constitutional Court draws on the SCA’s taxation principles to maintain consistency while recognizing its unique constitutional mandate which may lead to differing approaches to cost awards [11–14].
  4. Composite Fees Principle: Established in the SCA, the principle states that it is not permissible to separately charge for preparatory work and appearance fees; instead, a composite fee for the first day of hearings should encompass both the appearance and preparatory work [32].

A – Application

The Court systematically reviewed the taxing master’s determination. With respect to counsel's fees, the taxing master employed a methodology that divided the total fees by the number of hearing days, allowing R42,000 for senior counsel and R28,000 for junior counsel each day [26]. The respondents contended that the taxing master failed to respect established norms regarding composite first-day fees and sought a substantial reduction in allowed fees, citing the SCA practice of providing a composite first-day fee with lower refreshers for subsequent days [31].

The taxing master asserted that she considered relevant aspects such as the case's complexity, volume, and prevailing market rates for counsel's fees when determining the amounts allowed [26]. The Court identified the taxing master's approach as inconsistent with established practices in the SCA, where charges for preparation and arguing are not treated as separate items and must be bundled into a composite first-day fee [32].

In the case of the perusal fee, the disputed amount centered on whether the fee should be calculated at R17.00 or R27.00 per page. The Court determined that the taxing master's decision to allow R27.00 was justified given the context and nature of the document under review, as stipulated in the applicable rules [20–22].

The Court also highlighted the necessity of distinguishing costs related specifically to the appeal versus those concerning the recusal application, which had not been adequately reflected in the submitted bill [16]. The overall findings led to the conclusion that the taxing master did not sufficiently differentiate costs attributable to the two proceedings.

C – Conclusion

The Court ultimately concluded that the taxing master's allocatur needed adjustment. It determined that the fees for counsel should be recalibrated to R240,000 for senior counsel and R160,000 for each junior counsel, reflecting a fairer assessment of the necessary costs incurred [56]. The Court emphasized that the complexity and demands of the case warranted a careful consideration of the taxing principles, ensuring both parties’ rights were respected in a manner that balances the costs borne by each [56].

Furthermore, the costs of the review itself were to be limited to reasonable fees incurred during the review process, asserting fairness in awarding costs consistent with prevailing legal standards [56]. The ruling underscores the importance of adherence to procedural rules and highlights the necessity for clarity in the taxation of costs, particularly in complex constitutional matters.
