Case Title: Food and Allied Workers’ Union obo Gaoshubelwe v Pieman’s Pantry (Pty) Limited [2018] ZACC 7

Facts

The present case was initiated by the Food and Allied Workers’ Union (FAWU) on behalf of J. Gaoshubelwe and other employees of Pieman's Pantry (Pty) Limited, following their dismissal on August 1, 2001, which was purportedly related to their participation in an unprotected strike ([2]–[3]). Following their dismissal, FAWU referred the dispute to the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA) on August 7, 2001, which ultimately ruled that it lacked jurisdiction to arbitrate, prompting FAWU to seek a review of this ruling, which was dismissed on December 9, 2003 ([4]–[5]). Subsequently, on March 16, 2005, FAWU referred the matter to the Labour Court, which was met with a special plea from Pieman's, asserting that the claims had prescribed under the Prescription Act ([6]–[12]).

The Labour Court ruled in favor of Pieman's, concluding that the Prescription Act was applicable to claims under the Labour Relations Act (LRA) and that the claim had indeed prescribed ([14]–[15]). This ruling was subsequently upheld by the Labour Appeal Court ([16]).

Issues

The principal issues to be resolved included:

  1. Whether the Prescription Act applies to unfair dismissal claims under the LRA ([1], [30]).
  2. Whether the referral to the CCMA for conciliation constituted an interruption of the prescription period for claims concerning unfair dismissal ([16], [20]).

Rules

Key statutory provisions pertinent to the case include:

  • Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995: Particularly Section 191, which governs unfair dismissal claims and provides specific time frames for the referral of disputes to the CCMA and the Labour Court ([30], [33]).
  • Prescription Act 68 of 1969: Specifies general prescription periods, with Section 11 providing for a three-year prescription period for claims not otherwise specified ([12], [39]).

Application

The Constitutional Court critically evaluated whether relying on the Prescription Act contradicted the specific provisions established by the LRA. Central to this discussion was whether the LRA’s provisions regarding time limits for pursuing unfair dismissal claims were fundamentally inconsistent with those stipulations outlined in the Prescription Act.

Zondi AJ, in his majority judgment, asserted that the LRA’s framework provides for flexibility through allowing for the condonation of late referrals under specific conditions, serving the applicant's right to fair labour practices as mandated by the Constitution ([60], [74]). The court emphasized that a rigid application of the Prescription Act would impose an undue barrier to accessing justice, especially given the LRA’s emphasis on expeditious resolution of disputes ([70], [72]).

Additionally, it was discussed that initiating a referral to the CCMA amounted to a judicial act that could interrupt the running of prescription, thereby maintaining the employee's right to pursue the claim ([203]).

Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately held that the Prescription Act does not apply to unfair dismissal claims under the LRA, concluding that the framework established by the LRA is both comprehensive and distinct in its handling of dispute resolution. This judgement reinforced the notion that while the Prescription Act promotes certainty, its application to unfair dismissal claims would undermine the flexible and equitable principles intended by the LRA.

The Court set aside the decisions of both the Labour Court and the Labour Appeal Court, dismissing the special plea of prescription, and underscoring the importance of ensuring access to justice within the specific context of labour relations ([76], [215]). The implication of this ruling affirms the necessity for statutory interpretations that enhance the protection of worker rights within the broader context of the Constitution.
