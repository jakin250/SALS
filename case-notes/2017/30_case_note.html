Limpopo Legal Solutions v Vhembe District Municipality and Others [2017] ZACC 30

The applicant asserted that its actions were grounded in the public interest, invoking sections 152 and 153 of the Constitution and section 38(d), which allows anyone acting in the public interest to approach a court when constitutional rights are seen to be infringed ([6]).

Constitutional Court 2017 [2017] ZACC 30

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

In Limpopo Legal Solutions v Vhembe District Municipality and Others [2017] ZACC 30, the applicant, Limpopo Legal Solutions, a non-profit organization based in the Limpopo Province, sought to bring legal action in the High Court against the Vhembe District Municipality, the Minister of Water Affairs and Forestry, and the Thulamela Municipality ([2]). The applicant aimed to represent the residents of Malamulele B, Extension 1, who were subjected to a lack of reasonable sanitation facilities ([3]). The specific relief sought included declarations of unlawfulness regarding the respondents' failure to provide sanitation and orders compelling the respondents to ensure adequate toilet facilities for affected residents, including those unable to afford them ([3]).

The applicant asserted that its actions were grounded in the public interest, invoking sections 152 and 153 of the Constitution and section 38(d), which allows anyone acting in the public interest to approach a court when constitutional rights are seen to be infringed ([6]). However, the High Court ruled that Limpopo Legal Solutions had no locus standi, defined that the matter was not urgent, and imposed costs on the applicant for striking the matter off the roll ([7], [6]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues addressed in this case included:

  1. Whether the applicant had the standing to bring the application in the High Court, particularly under section 38(d) of the Constitution ([10]).
  2. Whether the High Court correctly interpreted urgency regarding the application ([10]).
  3. Whether the High Court properly exercised its discretion concerning the cost order that resulted from the striking off ([10], [17]).

R – Rules

The legal framework referenced includes the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, specifically:

  • Section 10: Enshrines the right to human dignity, which the applicant claimed was infringed due to the lack of sanitation facilities ([4]).
  • Section 38(d): Allows for any person acting in the public interest to approach courts if rights in the Bill of Rights are violated ([6]).

The decision in Lawyers for Human Rights v Minister of Home Affairs [2004] ZACC 12, which sets out considerations for determining whether a party is acting in the public interest, was pivotal. Additionally, the Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources [2009] ZACC 14 ruling influenced the Court’s view on the principles surrounding costs in public interest litigation ([10], [17]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court found that the High Court's conclusion regarding the applicant's standing lacked an appropriate basis. The Court relied on jurisprudence from previous cases emphasizing that the mere perception of public interest should not negate standing under section 38(d) ([12], [15]). The analysis highlighted the applicant's genuine effort to protect vulnerable communities’ rights, thus affirming its standing.

Regarding the High Court's ruling on urgency, the Constitutional Court concluded that while the High Court may have perceived the matter as non-urgent, it was the duty of the applicant to provide evidence of urgency, which it had failed to demonstrate adequately ([10], [16]). However, the importance of the constitutional rights asserted was acknowledged, validating the applicant’s position in the broader public context.

On the issue of costs, the Court found that the High Court improperly used a general rule stating that costs follow the result without considering the specific contextual circumstances of constitutional litigation. The Biowatch principle was applicable, which posits that adverse costs should not automatically fall on applicants for public interest litigation against the State ([17]). The Court therefore ruled the previous costs order invalid and stipulated that there should be no order as to costs, apart from requiring the first respondent to pay the applicant's costs in the Constitutional Court ([17]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal only against the costs order of the High Court, ruling that Limpopo Legal Solutions had standing under section 38(d) of the Constitution and that the High Court erred by applying a generalized costs principle ([4], [17]). Ultimately, the appeal was upheld, the High Court's costs order was replaced with "no order as to costs," and the first respondent was ordered to pay the applicant's costs in the appeal ([18]).

The implications of this decision serve to bolster the role of organizations acting in the public interest, affirming their right to challenge government conduct that may infringe upon fundamental rights. This case notably reinforces the notion that standing should not be unduly limited where genuine public interest claims are advanced, particularly concerning access to essential services like sanitation, which are vital for human dignity.
