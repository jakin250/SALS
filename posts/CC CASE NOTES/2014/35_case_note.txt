Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: National Union of Metalworkers of South Africa v Intervalve (Pty) Ltd and Others [2014] ZACC 35

**F** – **Facts**:  
In this case, the National Union of Metalworkers of South Africa (NUMSA) sought to join two employers, Intervalve (Pty) Ltd and BHR Piping Systems (Pty) Ltd, to unfair dismissal proceedings originally filed against Steinmüller Africa (Pty) Ltd ([1]). These companies are interrelated and share operational aspects, including payroll and HR services, which led to the dismissal of 204 employees following an unprotected strike at a shared industrial site ([2]-[4]). NUMSA referred the dispute to conciliation, naming only Steinmüller as the employer, which later led to complications when it sought to include the other two companies due to procedural requirements under the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA) ([6]-[10]). The initial referral to the Bargaining Council did not include Intervalve or BHR, and subsequent attempts to join them were marred by procedural missteps, leading to their rejection by the Labour Appeal Court, which acknowledged NUMSA's failure to timely refer claims regarding Intervalve and BHR to conciliation ([12]-[13]). 

**I** – **Issues**:  
The primary legal questions were:  
(a) Is leave to appeal justified?  
(b) Is the referral of a dismissal dispute to conciliation a jurisdictional prerequisite for the Labour Court?  
(c) Did NUMSA substantively comply with section 191 of the LRA in its referral process?  
(d) If not, can Intervalve and BHR be estopped from contesting NUMSA's non-compliance? ([24]-[26]).

**R** – **Rules**:  
The applicable legal principles derive from the Labour Relations Act, particularly section 191, which mandates that disputes about the fairness of a dismissal must be initially referred to a bargaining council or the Commission before being escalated to the Labour Court ([27]-[28]). It highlights the importance of conciliation as a jurisdictional condition that must be satisfied before legal adjudication can occur ([33]). Furthermore, related jurisprudence such as *National Union of Metalworkers of South Africa v Driveline Technologies (Pty) Ltd* contends that the failure to refer claims appropriately can undermine jurisdiction ([19]).

**A** – **Application**:  
The Court determined that NUMSA’s referral, which only named Steinmüller, did not satisfy the strict requirements of section 191 of the LRA for Intervalve and BHR ([40]-[44]). Each company maintained separate legal identities, and thus the failure to include them in the initial referral meant that the disputes involving these entities were not appropriately addressed through the conciliation process ([51]-[52]). The majority opinion underscored that compliance with statutory requirements is not merely a formality, but essential in maintaining the integrity of the dispute resolution mechanisms under the LRA. NUMSA's arguments for waiver or estoppel were dismissed as there was insufficient evidence demonstrating that Intervalve and BHR had relinquished their rights to separate notice of the proceedings ([60]).

**C** – **Conclusion**:  
The Constitutional Court dismissed NUMSA’s appeal, upholding the Labour Appeal Court’s determination that the joinder of Intervalve and BHR was improperly granted due to NUMSA’s failure to comply with the LRA by not referring the disputes against these companies to conciliation ([73]-[74]). The ruling reaffirms the significance of procedural compliance in labour disputes, emphasizing that technical failures in the initial referral can have substantial ramifications, potentially leaving employees without recourse if their claims are non-compliant with procedural requirements ([68]-[70]).

This case serves as a critical reminder of the necessity for unions to adhere strictly to procedural requirements in the context of labour disputes, reinforcing the importance of clarity in legal representation and the ramifications of corporate structure among associated companies.
