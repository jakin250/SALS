Supreme Court of Appeal 2017 [2017] ZASCA 164

F – Facts

The appeal arises from the Eastern Cape Local Division of the High Court, Port Elizabeth, where the appellant, Chinaz Septoo (“the appellant”), initiated a claim for damages against the Road Accident Fund (“the Fund”) following the death of her husband in a single-vehicle collision. The deceased was driving the vehicle, with the appellant and her two minor children as passengers. The crux of the case lies in the fact that the deceased was solely responsible for the accident due to his own negligence ([1]).

The High Court previously held the Fund liable for the appellant and the minor children’s general damages and future medical expenses but did not extend this liability to include damages for loss of support claimed in the current appeal ([2]). This case raises critical questions regarding the eligibility of dependants to claim compensation for loss of support under circumstances where the deceased was the sole negligent party responsible for the fatal accident ([1]).

I – Issues

The principal legal questions before the court consisted of:

  1. Whether dependants, such as the appellant and her minor children, are entitled to claim compensation for loss of support from the Fund when the deceased, as the breadwinner, was the sole cause of the collision.
  2. Whether the common law should be developed to recognize claims for loss of support in such circumstances ([1]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal framework governing the case is primarily contained within the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 (“the Act”). Key provisions include:

  • Section 3: Establishes the purpose of the Fund to pay compensation for loss or damage resulting from negligent driving ([3]).
  • Sections 17 and 19: Outline the rights of third parties to claim against the Fund while also setting boundaries for liability, whereby Section 19(a) excludes liability for losses of damages arising if the driver or owner would not have been liable but for Section 21 ([7], [8]).
  • Section 21: Explicitly states that no claim for damages related to death or bodily injury caused by negligent driving shall lie if the deceased was solely negligent in causing his or her own death ([9]).

Case law further clarifies these principles:

  • In Amod v Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund, the requirement of unlawful killing as a basis for dependant claims was reaffirmed ([11]).
  • In Mlisane v South African Eagle Insurance Co Ltd, the court established that claims must be directed against the negligent party, not the estate of a self-negligent deceased ([10]).

A – Application

The court analyzed whether the Fund could be held liable for the loss of support based on the above-mentioned provisions. Given that the deceased's negligence was the sole cause of the accident, the court found that the prerequisites for a claim under the Act were not met. Specifically, the death did not arise from the unlawful act of another party, but rather from the actions of the deceased himself.

Thus, the court rejected the appellant's argument that the common law should be amended to allow claims for loss of support in instances of self-neglect. The court cited Brooks v Minister of Safety and Security, which declared that permitting dependants to benefit from a deceased's wrongful act would set a dangerous precedent ([13]). The common law as it stood, which required wrongful actions by a third party for a claim to be valid, was affirmed as appropriate for the existing social order.

Additionally, the argument presented about the Apportionment of Damages Act was dismissed as misconceived since its provisions were not intended to create new rights against the deceased's estate ([18]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, holding that the dependants of the deceased do not possess a cause of action for loss of support against the Fund when the deceased’s negligence was the sole cause of the fatal accident. The court emphasized that wrongfulness is a critical element in such claims and that the existing legal framework did not support the expansion of liability to such an extent that would allow a party to benefit from their own misconduct. Hence, the appellant's claim was precluded by both common law principles and statutory provisions ([24]).

The decision underscores the delicate balance within personal injury and delictual claims, particularly regarding how negligence must be ascribed to third parties to give rise to claims for loss of support. The implications of the ruling reinforce the need for legislative reform if the law is to expand to address emerging social realities, reiterating that such reform should primarily be the purview of the legislature rather than the judiciary.
