Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

### FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title:** Manana v King Sabata Dalindyebo Municipality (345/09) [2010] ZASCA 144 (25 November 2010)

---

#### F – Facts [paras 1–10]

The case centers on an appeal brought by Mputumi Camaron Manana (the appellant) against the King Sabata Dalindyebo Municipality (the respondent). The appellant was previously employed as a legal advisor for the Umtata Transitional Local Council. Following the merger of this council with the Mqanduli Transitional Local Council in 2000, disputes arose regarding Manana's position within the newly formed municipality. On 3 November 2006, a municipal council resolution was adopted stating that, effective from 10 August 2006, Manana would be appointed as the Manager: Legal Services, with back pay due for the period he fulfilled this role. Despite this resolution, no salary adjustment was made in consequence of the appointment. 

In response to the lack of payment, Manana sought legal recourse in the Eastern Cape High Court, claiming unpaid remuneration and adjustments to his salary. The court (Pakade J) ruled that it lacked jurisdiction based on the precedent set in *Chirwa v Transnet Ltd*, dismissing the claim. Insufficient legal representation on behalf of the municipality during these proceedings led to the appeal.

#### I – Issues [paras 1–2, 12, 15]

The primary legal question is whether the appellate court possesses the jurisdiction to hear the matter concerning Manana’s claim for remuneration under the municipal council’s resolution, given the jurisdictional finding of the lower court based on *Chirwa v Transnet Ltd*. A secondary issue emerges regarding the authority of the municipality’s local government manager versus the municipal council relative to employment matters.

#### R – Rules [paras 12–14, 18, 24]

The legal authorities relied upon include the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, particularly sections 40(1) and 151(1), which establish the framework for local government and delineate the authority of municipal councils. The *Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000* defines the roles of municipal managers and councils, stipulating that the executive authority resides within the council and cannot be divested by ordinary legislation.

In addition, case law such as *Grace v McCulloch* is cited to establish the binding nature of duly adopted resolutions by a local authority. Furthermore, the decision in *Gcaba v Minister for Safety and Security* clarified the jurisdictional issues in employment litigation, asserting that the high court has the authority to entertain claims of this nature.

#### A – Application [paras 21–23, 25–27]

The Supreme Court of Appeal determined that the lower court erred in dismissing the claim based on a misinterpretation of its jurisdiction. Explicitly, it found that the resolution regarding Manana's employment was valid and binding, notwithstanding the municipality's attempt to contest its validity. The absence of opposition from the municipality during the appeal, where the municipality expressed no intention to contest the merits, further supported the court's decision to set aside the lower court’s ruling.

The court underscored that officials could not choose to ignore valid resolutions based on subjective beliefs regarding their legality. The nature of the dispute was examined against the contractual relationships established through the resolution and the subsequent actions of the municipality, supporting Manana's claim as grounded in an existing employment contract enforceable within the judicial system.

#### C – Conclusion [paras 26–27]

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appellant's appeal, establishing that the respondent must account for remuneration owed, adjust Manana’s salary accordingly, and pay the costs incurred during the proceedings. The ruling reaffirmed the binding nature of municipal council resolutions until formally rescinded, thereby reinforcing foundational principles of local governance and administrative authority. The case underscores the imperative for municipalities to adhere to their internal resolutions and the legal ramifications of neglecting to fulfill contractual obligations towards employees.

In summation, the court's decision not only rectified the immediate dispute but also highlighted significant tenets regarding the separation of powers within municipal governance structures, ensuring that contractual rights are protected under South African law.
