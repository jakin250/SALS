Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2004

# Case Note: The Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service v SA Silicone Products (Pty) Ltd

**Citation:**  
The Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service v SA Silicone Products (Pty) Ltd [2004] ZASCA 11; [2004] 4 All SA 159 (SCA).

**FIRAC Analysis**

### F - Facts
The appeal arises from a decision of the Income Tax Special Court concerning the disallowance of a deductible allowance claimed by the respondent, SA Silicone Products (Pty) Ltd (hereinafter "the respondent"), for an amount of R14.5 million incurred while acquiring intellectual property associated with a business purchase from DB Silicones CC (hereinafter "DBS") ([1–3]). 

The respondent was negotiating to acquire DBS which operated under a license with Dow Corning Corporation. The acquisition included purported rights to use certain trademarks and a customer network believed to have been built through prior operations ([2–5]). The core elements of the transaction involved payments attributed to fixed assets, stock, and significant sums attributed to goodwill and trademark rights. Importantly, the sale agreement did not explicitly mention the "customer connection," which played a crucial role in the valuation and claimed deduction ([7–8], [13]).

After the acquisition, the respondent claimed a tax deduction for the expenditure under Section 11(gA)(iii) of the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962, which allows for deductions incurred in acquiring specific types of intellectual property ([13–16]). The appellant, the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service, disallowed this deduction, resulting in an appeal to the Income Tax Special Court, which found in favor of the respondent ([13–14]).

### I - Issues
The main legal issues presented in this case are:
1. Whether the payment of R14.5 million constituted an acquisition of deductible intellectual property under Section 11(gA)(iii) of the Income Tax Act.
2. Specifically, whether the respondent's rights to use trademarks can be considered "property similar in nature" to the trademarks themselves ([1–2], [16–19]).

### R - Rules
The legal framework governing this case revolves around the provisions of Section 11 of the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962, particularly subsection (gA)(iii), which permits deductions for expenditure incurred in acquiring patents, designs, copyrights, trademarks, or any "property of a similar nature" used in generating income ([3–5]). The statute aims to promote investment in intellectual property, requiring that any comparable property must inherently share fundamental characteristics with the specifically identified properties.

Further precedents that informed the court’s analysis include older cases such as ITC 353 (1936) and ITC 613 (1945), which addressed the nature of rights conferred by licenses and the existing legal conditions for the acquisition of such rights under tax law ([19–20]).

### A - Application
The court analyzed whether the license the respondent acquired from DBS constituted a form of intellectual property that qualified for a tax deduction. The respondent argued that the trademark rights and associated customer connection were sufficiently intertwined to allow for deduction under the statute ([16–18]).

However, the court distinguished between the nature of a trademark and the limited temporal rights granted through a license. The judgment emphasized that a trademark license only grants the right to use the mark temporarily, without conveying a proprietary interest or a legal monopoly ([19–22]). This distinction was critical; while the respondent maintained that valuable customer connections were intertwined with trademark rights, the court concluded that such connections were never property owned by DBS and thus could not be transferred or treated as part of its value.

Moreover, the court noted that the nature of the agreement between Dow Corning and DBS explicitly precluded the assignment or transferability of the trademark license ([10–12]). The discussions and opinions presented during the special court proceedings reaffirmed the limited rights attributed to the trademark license, reinforcing the conclusion that the respondent could not make a lawful claim under the stated section of the Income Tax Act ([14–22]).

### C - Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal reversed the decision of the Income Tax Special Court and upheld the appellant's disallowance of the deduction claimed by the respondent ([24]). The court concluded that the expenditure for the trademark license did not fall within the scope of Section 11(gA)(iii), as it did not represent an acquisition of "property similar in nature" to the intellectual property described therein. This ruling emphasized the significance of proprietary interests in intellectual property and clarified the limits of deductible expenditures in the context of tax law, ensuring that deductions are confined to actual proprietary acquisitions rather than limited use rights. 

The judgment serves as a crucial reference point for future interpretations of intellectual property tax deductions and underlines the need for clear ownership rights when claiming tax allowances associated with such assets.
