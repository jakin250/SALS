Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note: Michael Jantjies v The State (Case No 532/2022) [2023] ZASCA 3

## F - Facts

The case at hand involves an appeal from Michael Jantjies (the appellant) against his conviction on three counts of rape by the regional magistrate of the Western Cape, as reported in the judgment delivered by the Supreme Court of Appeal ([paras 1–2]). On 5 June 2019, the trial court sentenced Jantjies to 16 years in prison. Subsequently, he was granted leave to appeal against the conviction.

The principal evidentiary components consisted of testimony from the complainant, a 48-year-old schoolteacher, and retired Warrant Officer Johan Tobias Grobbelaar, to whom the complainant reported the incident. The complainant claimed to have met Jantjies in August 2014 through social media, with the relationship evolving over time until the purported incident on 7 March 2015, when the complainant alleged that Jantjies raped her in a hotel room ([paras 3–5]). Notably, during their ongoing relationship, the complainant testified that despite their intimacy, Jantjies made no sexual advances towards her.

The alleged incident reportedly began with Jantjies climbing onto the complainant's bed and forcing her into non-consensual sexual acts that included anal and vaginal penetration and oral sex. The complainant asserted that she did not resist the assault due to fear of a firearm Jantjies allegedly had positioned between their beds ([paras 5–7]).

In the aftermath, Jantjies was reported to have demanded a significant sum from the complainant under the pretense of housing assistance, after which he vanished with the money ([paras 6–7]). Jantjies, in his defense, denied all charges, claiming any physical intimacy they shared was consensual and attributing the accusation to the complainant's anger at him for absconding with her money ([para 10]).

The trial court dismissed critical inconsistencies in the complainant's account and noted an alleged judicial misdirection in the evaluation of her testimony. While the High Court upheld the conviction, it failed to adequately consider reasonable doubts raised by Jantjies’ evidence concerning the complainant's credibility and motives ([paras 11–14]).

## I - Issues

The central legal issue in this appeal was whether the State had proven Jantjies' guilt beyond a reasonable doubt based solely on the testimony of the complainant, which was the sole basis for the conviction ([paras 15–16]). Specific points of contention included the inability of the State to provide tangible evidence corroborating the complainant's claims, contradictions in her accounts, the absence of physical corroboration, and the failure to call critical witnesses who might have substantiated or refuted the claims ([paras 17–20]).

## R - Rules

A variety of legal principles were pertinent to this case, particularly those governing the evaluation of evidence, the burden of proof, and the role of a single witness in a criminal trial. The trial court must assess the credibility of a single witness through meticulous scrutiny, as established in **S v Van der Meyden** [1999] (1) SACR 447 (W) at 450A-B ([paras 15–16]). 

Furthermore, section 208 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 allows for convictions based solely on the evidence of a single competent witness; however, the inherent risks of relying solely on such testimony necessitated a thorough examination of its reliability. The principles outlined in **R v Difford** [1937] AD 370 and **S v Kubeka** [1982] (1) SA 534 (WLD) emphasize that if there exists any reasonable possibility that the accused's version of events may be true, acquittal is warranted ([para 13]).

## A - Application

In this case, the court critically examined the trial court and High Court's handling of the evidence. It noted that no corroborative evidence beyond the complainant's testimony was provided, which is a significant factor when evaluating the credibility of allegations of this gravity ([paras 22–23]).

The absence of supporting witnesses, including those who could have confirmed the hotel booking incident or the purported medical evidence following the alleged assault, led the court to question the integrity of the complainant’s account ([paras 20–21]). Repeated hearings revealed inconsistencies in the complainant's narrative, particularly regarding her relationship dynamics with Jantjies and the timing of her report to authorities, which the court viewed as casting substantial doubt on the reliability of her claims ([paras 19, 22]).

Upon considering the gathered evidence in its totality, the court found that the State had failed to prove Jantjies' guilt beyond a reasonable doubt due to the lack of sufficient corroboration and substantial implications surrounding the complainant's motivations for fabricating such claims ([para 22]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the convictions and resultant sentences against Jantjies could not withstand scrutiny. Consequently, the High Court's dismissal of the appeal was overturned, and the earlier convictions were set aside ([paras 23-24]). 

This case serves as a pivotal reminder of the burden of proof resting firmly on the prosecution in criminal cases and underscores the principle that conviction in cases of serious allegations such as rape cannot be achieved through a single, uncorroborated witness’s testimony. It reflects a crucial judicial recognition of the need for a meticulous evaluation of evidence, particularly regarding sensitive matters that engage public trust in the legal system. The outcome not only reassesses Jantjies' conviction but also highlights systemic failings in the investigative process that can severely undermine the pursuit of justice in violent crime cases ([paras 23]).
