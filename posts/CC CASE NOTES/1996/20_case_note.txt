Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1996

# case note using the FIRAC method

### Case Title: 
**Leach Mokela Mohlomi v Minister of Defence**   
**Court:** Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**CCT 41/95**  
**Heard on:** 21 November 1995  
**Decided on:** 26 September 1996  

### FIRAC Case Note

#### F – Facts:
The plaintiff, Leach Mokela Mohlomi, initiated a civil action against the defendant, the Minister of Defence, for damages relating to injuries sustained from a gunshot wound inflicted by a soldier on 2 May 1994 ([1]). The action commenced when Mohlomi was still a minor, thus requiring his father's assistance ([1]). As the pleadings were closed but the matter had not yet moved to trial, the defendant contested the claim through a special plea, arguing that the plaintiff failed to comply with the requirements of section 113(1) of the Defence Act 44 of 1957. This section stipulates that a civil action must be instituted within six months of the cause of action, along with a written notice given at least one month prior to the suit ([2]). 

The defendant's substantive claims included that this notice was unduly late, given less than the required month prior to the institution of action ([2]). However, in response, the plaintiff disputed this claim, admitting that while the notice was not delivered thirty-one days in advance, it nonetheless argued the constitutional invalidity of section 113(1) in relation to various sections of the interim Constitution ([2]). The Witwatersrand Local Division referred the limitation to the Constitutional Court, emphasizing its significance to the plaintiff's claim ([3]).

The court heard that despite the defendant's counsel initially asserting a late start to litigation, it was later confirmed that the summons had indeed been issued on the last permissible day ([4]). The conditions under section 113(1) were accepted as part of the inquiry, with the necessary notice provided twenty-eight days before the action commenced ([5]). The rationale for the late notice was explained by the plaintiff, involving a misunderstanding from legal representatives employed by the Campus Law Clinic, which yielded erroneous notifications to the wrong department ([6]).

#### I – Issues:
The primary legal questions raised in this case were whether the requirements set out in section 113(1) of the Defence Act infringed upon Mohlomi’s constitutional right to have justiciable disputes settled by a court of law as guaranteed under section 22 of the interim Constitution ([8]) and whether any practical purpose behind these limitations could justify such an infringement ([15]). The court also considered the validity of section 113(1) in light of its effects on vulnerable populations, including minors and the marginalized ([14]).

#### R – Rules:
The constitutional rule at the heart of this case is section 22 of the interim Constitution ([8]), which establishes the right for every person to have justiciable disputes resolved by courts or other independent forums. The court also referenced the significance of reasonable limitations on this right and evaluated the legislative intent behind the limitations imposed by section 113(1) of the Defence Act [[9]-[11]].

Further, the court analyzed the precedent set in **Hartman v Minister van Polisie**, which earlier deemed similar statutory limitations unconstitutional under the old Police Act ([11]). An examination was also made of section 33(1) of the interim Constitution, which allows for limitations on rights if they are reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society ([15]).

#### A – Application:
The court conducted an extensive evaluation of section 113(1) of the Defence Act against the standards established under section 22 of the interim Constitution. It noted that while limitations on claim periods are present within many jurisdictions to protect the interests of justice and avoid stale lawsuits ([11]), the rigid nature of the timing under section 113(1) was disproportionate and infringed upon the right to access the courts ([12]). 

On comparing section 113(1) with section 57 of the South African Police Service Act, the court highlighted significant disparities including allowance for longer time frames and court discretion to condone non-compliance, thus indicating that section 113(1) was markedly more punitive and inflexible ([19]). The absence of provisions in section 113(1) that allowed for consideration of circumstances such as minority, illiteracy, or poverty among claimants further suggested that the section entrenched inequalities and failed to meet the tests for reasonableness or justifiability mandated by section 33(1) ([20]).

In operational terms, the court underscored that the infringement was not simply an issue of inconvenience but actively obstructed the right to access justice, particularly affecting vulnerable individuals who may lack legal knowledge or support ([14]-[15]).

#### C – Conclusion:
The Constitutional Court ultimately declared section 113(1) of the Defence Act unconstitutional for infringing upon the rights established under section 22 of the interim Constitution. The court found that these statutory limitations were neither reasonable nor justifiable since they did not afford adequate opportunity for affected individuals to seek judicial redress. As a result, the court invalidated section 113(1) entirely, ruling that future cases would be governed solely by the provisions of the Prescription Act until legislative remedies were instituted by Parliament ([26]).

This landmark decision underscored the imperative for legislative frameworks to align with constitutional guarantees, thus ensuring equitable access to justice for all citizens, irrespective of their circumstances. The implications of this ruling extended beyond Mohlomi's case, addressing systemic barriers within South African law that hindered vulnerable populations' access to legal recourse.
