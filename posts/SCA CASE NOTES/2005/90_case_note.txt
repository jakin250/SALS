Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2005

### FIRAC Case Note: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa (Case No: 511/04)

#### F – Facts

The case revolves around the awarding of three contracts related to repair and maintenance work at prisons in the Western Cape, specifically at Drakenstein, Worcester, and Helderstroom ([1]). Nolitha Electrical and Construction (Pty) Ltd emerged as the successful tenderer for these contracts, while two other companies, JFE Sapela Electronics (Pty) Ltd and JFE Power Distribution (Pty) Ltd (trading as JFE Reticulation), contested the awards. They claimed that the tenders were improperly awarded due to irregularities in Nolitha's tender pricing, which raised concerns regarding fairness and the integrity of the tender process ([2]–[10]).

The disputes were initially adjudicated in the Cape High Court, where the awards were set aside for being invalid, as Nolitha's tenders were deemed not to have met the statutory definition of an "acceptable tender" as per the Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act 5 of 2000 ([11]). The case proceeded to the Supreme Court of Appeal, where the judgment of the lower court was questioned, particularly regarding the practical implications of the remedy granted and whether the appellants could be held liable for costs given the public interest aspect of the tender awards ([20]–[30]).

#### I – Issues

The central legal issues addressed in this case include:

1. Whether Nolitha's tenders met the criteria of "acceptable tender" as defined under the Preferential Act, particularly regarding their pricing structure and compliance with the tender specifications as mandated by Section 217 of the Constitution ([11]).
   
2. Whether the tender evaluation process, which the Standing Tender Committee (STC) followed, was consistent with the principles of fairness, transparency, and non-discrimination stipulated in both the Constitution and applicable legislation ([14]).

3. The implications of the effluxion of time and practical completion of work done by Nolitha on the ability of the court to grant the relief sought by the respondents ([20]–[30]).

#### R – Rules

The legal framework guiding the court’s analysis includes:

1. **The Constitution of South Africa, Section 217**: Establishes the principles that public procurement must be fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective ([11]).
   
2. **The Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act 5 of 2000**: Defines "acceptable tender" and establishes criteria for assessing tenders, with specific requirements for compliance to be considered valid ([11]).

3. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: Outlines the procedural requirements for administrative actions, including tenders, ensuring they are lawful and procedurally fair ([19]).

4. **Doctrine of Legality**: Emphasizes that administrative entities may exercise only powers granted by legislation, thereby invalidating actions that exceed these powers ([11]).

#### A – Application

In applying the law to the facts, the court determined that Nolitha’s tenders were impaired by severe pricing irregularities. In both the Drakenstein and Worcester projects, Nolitha quoted nominal amounts for significant work items, suggesting a strategic underbidding based on the assumption that some of the contracted work would not be necessary due to prior regional contracts ([5]–[10]). The consulting engineers involved in the tender evaluations initially recommended against the acceptance of Nolitha's tenders based on these unbalanced offers ([6], [18]).

Despite attempts to justify Nolitha’s pricing during the tender evaluation process, the court concluded that the fundamental principle of fairness and the integrity of the procurement process were compromised. The adjustments made to Nolitha’s tender after the submission date—without a transparent process—further violated the principles laid out in the PAJA and the Constitution, leading to a skewed competitive environment for all tenderers ([19]–[30]).

The court found the judgment of the lower court to have overlooked the practical consequences of invalidating already awarded tenders amidst ongoing work. Hence, while the tenders were deemed invalid, the court decided that the remedy should avoid disruption of services already initiated by Nolitha, recognizing the practical realities faced ([20]–[30]).

#### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the finding that Nolitha’s contract awards were invalid as they did not meet the criteria of an "acceptable tender" in accordance with the Preferential Act ([30]). However, it emphasized that the relief could not simply undo work already performed, given the practicalities of the situation.  The court exercised discretion, deciding not to set aside the tender awards in light of the efflux of time and ongoing work completion ([29]).

Given the appellants' acknowledgment of procedural shortcomings and the frustrations faced by the respondents in obtaining necessary documentation, the court ordered them to bear costs in favor of the successful respondents ([30]). The decision highlights the delicate balance between upholding the rule of law in administrative processes and recognizing practical realities in public procurement. 

---

This case serves as a critical examination of the intersection between public procurement law and administrative justice, establishing robust precedents for future tender processes within South Africa's complex legislative environment.
