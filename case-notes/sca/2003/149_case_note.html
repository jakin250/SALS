Supreme Court of Appeal

Kevin & Lasia Property Investments CC v. Anton Roos N.O.

Citation: Kevin & Lasia Property Investments CC v Absa Bank Limited and Others [2003] ZASCA 41; Case No: 480/2002

Supreme Court of Appeal 2003 [2003] ZASCA 41; Case No: 480/2002 Court: The Supreme Cou

Citation: Kevin & Lasia Property Investments CC v Absa Bank Limited and Others [2003] ZASCA 41; Case No: 480/2002

Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

Coram: Howie P, Navsa, Mthiyane, Cloete, and Lewis JJA

Heard: 18 November 2003

Delivered: 1 December 2003

F – Facts

The appeal arises from an application brought by the liquidators of I J van der Lith Family Holdings (Pty) Ltd, which was provisionally liquidated on 21 November 2000. The case primarily concerns a transaction involving the sale of immovable property known as OK Sentrum, conducted by the liquidators against Kevin & Lasia Property Investments CC (the first appellant) and Absa Bank Limited (the second appellant) ([paras 1-2]).

On 25 February 2000, the company, represented by Mr. Izak van der Lith, entered into a sale agreement with the first appellant, resulting in a sale price of R7,700,000. Despite the transfer of the property occurring on 29 June 2000, no notice was published as mandated by section 34(1) of the Insolvency Act, 24 of 1936. This section stipulates the necessity of a notice period for transactions involving a trader's business to protect creditors' rights ([para 3-4]).

Following the final liquidation order on 23 November 2000, the liquidators contended that the transfer was a nullity due to non-compliance with necessary notice provisions, as the transaction was not executed in the ordinary course of the company's business ([para 4]). The appeal focuses on several grounds, including the assertion that the company was not a “trader” under the Act, with the lower court having neglected to address this definition due to a formal admission of trading status by the appellants during proceedings ([paras 9-11]).

I – Issues

The main legal issues presented in this case include:

  1. Whether the transfer of property constituted a transaction performed in the ordinary course of business, thereby exempting it from the provisions of section 34(1) of the Insolvency Act.
  2. The validity and withdrawal of the admission that the company was a trader under the Act.
  3. Whether the liquidators had adequately demonstrated their right to set aside the property transfer based on the statutory requirements ([paras 8-9],[10]).

R – Rules

The legal framework applicable to this case is primarily encapsulated in the Insolvency Act, 24 of 1936, notably section 34(1), which stipulates:

  • Any transfer by a trader of their business or goods is void against creditors if no notice is given in accordance with the stipulations of the section unless such transfer occurs in the ordinary course of business.

Definition of "trader" is found in section 2 of the Act, which describes a trader as any entity involved in selling, buying, or otherwise dealing with property or engaging in business activities, including management as an agent ([para 2-5]).

Precedents, such as Kelvin Park Properties CC v. Paterson N.O., further elucidate the interpretation of the term "trader" and its associated implications within the context of the Act ([para 13]).

A – Application

The court's application of the law revolved around two central elements: the interpretation of "ordinary course of business" and the binding nature of the formal admission made by the appellants. The lower court erroneously placed exclusive weight on the appellants’ admission regarding the company's status as a trader, which subsequently precluded comprehensive consideration of the key question of whether the sale was executed within the ordinary course of business ([para 11-12]).

Mthiyane JA noted that the admission did not necessitate a formal retraction and could be withdrawn at the appeal stage. The context reveals that the admission was a tactical choice made to streamline proceedings and did not preclude a further examination of relevant facts surrounding the company’s operations ([para 12]). The court considered the legal definition of "trader" and, through a detailed interpretation of the statutory language, concluded that merely holding properties for rental does not suffice to categorize an entity as a trader under the act ([paras 14-15]).

Thus, it was determined that the absence of the requisite notice as stipulated in section 34(1) rendered the transfer void against the liquidators and other creditors. Given that the appellants contested the key issues of trader status and ordinary course of business, the case warranted a comprehensive review ([paras 17-19]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, granting the appellants the opportunity to withdraw their admission regarding the trading status of the company. The court ruled that the lower court had not effectively addressed whether the transfer occurred in the ordinary course of business and mandated that these questions be resolved via remittance to the lower court for further examination and evidence presentation. This culminated in an order that upheld the rights of creditors while reaffirming the procedural integrity of insolvency treatments ([paras 19-20]).

The court's decision reinforces the importance of complying with the Insolvency Act provisions to protect creditor interests and adds clarity to the definitions and thresholds required to establish "trader" status in South African law, with implications for future transactional dealings involving insolvent entities. The ruling also underscores the necessity of procedural fairness in insolvency proceedings, particularly in relation to the rights of creditors and the handling of admissions in court ([paras 19-20]).
