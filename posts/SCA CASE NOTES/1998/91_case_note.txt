Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFR
Source Folder: 1998

# FIRAC Case Note: ERF ONE SIX SEVEN ORCHARDS CC v THE GREATER JOHANNESBURG METROPOLITAN COUNCIL & ANOTHER

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 2–10])

The case revolves around the appellant, **Erf One Six Seven Orchards CC**, a property owner in an upmarket suburb of Johannesburg. Its adjacent neighbor, the **second respondent**, began constructing a building above an existing garage. The appellant’s sole member, Allan Heyman Levin, first noticed these construction activities in May 1995 and became concerned by a noticed structure in June 1995, believing it to be unsightly and potentially detrimental to property value ([paras 2-4]).

Despite Levin’s attempts to halt construction—offering to cover stoppage costs if proven incorrect—the second respondent continued to build. Subsequently, Levin discovered that the first respondent, the **Greater Johannesburg Metropolitan Council**, had approved the construction plans. Although initial plans were rejected, they were approved after modifications ([paras 4–5]). 

Levin lodged objections against this approval based on two grounds: violations of the **National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act 103 of 1977** (“the Act”), notably that the building would disfigure the area and contravene provisions limiting subsidiary dwelling units, and the **Johannesburg Town Planning Scheme 1979** (“the Scheme”), which restricted such units on residential properties ([para 5]).

The first respondent dismissed all objections and the appellant subsequently sought judicial review in the Witwatersrand Local Division. While the court upheld most of the first respondent's decisions, it did acknowledge procedural flaws relating to the appellant's right to be heard before the approval of the plan ([para 7-8]). The court set aside the decision of the first respondent, but did not mandate the demolition of the building, thus providing an order that limited the appellant's further representation to the issue of property value derogation ([par 9]).

The matter progressed to the Supreme Court of Appeal following the dismissal of the appellant's appeal on various issues it had raised ([para 10]).

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 10-11])

This appeal poses multiple legal issues, primarily concerning:

1. Whether the first respondent acted unlawfully in rejecting the appellant's objections to the construction on procedural grounds, specifically the failure to grant the appellant a right to be heard.
2. The appropriateness of the court's decision to refer the matter back to the first respondent for reconsideration on the limited issue of whether the building derogates from the value of adjoining properties.
3. Whether the lower court erred in not ordering the demolition of the building based on the recognized procedural flaw ([paras 10-11]).

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 11-12])

The relevant legal frameworks considered in the case include:

1. **National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act 103 of 1977**: Section 7(l)(b)(ii)(aa) mandates local authorities to refuse plans for buildings deemed to disfigure or derogate from surrounding properties ([paras 5-6]).
2. **Johannesburg Town Planning Scheme 1979**: Limits the number of subsidiary dwelling units permissible on a property to two, crucially relevant to the appellant's argument ([para 5]).
3. **Audi alteram partem**: Ensures that no party is condemned unheard; this principle was pivotal in assessing the legitimacy of the first respondent's actions ([para 8]).
4. **Principles of judicial discretion in administrative law**: The court's discretion in either referring matters back or substituting decisions relies on fairness and whether there are compelling reasons not to send the matter back to the administrative body ([para 12]).

The court referenced **Livestock and Meat Industries Control Board v Garda** [1961] (1) SA 342, emphasizing the standard of fairness and procedural justice in administrative decision-making ([para 12]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 11-15])

In reviewing lower court findings, the Supreme Court of Appeal underscored the failure of the first respondent to grant the appellant an opportunity to contest the approval of the building plan—acknowledging that certain procedural irregularities existed under the Act ([para 11]). The court appreciated that the rejection of the appellant’s arguments regarding disfiguration and objectionability was a correct legal finding, but determined that the omission regarding the opportunity to be heard warranted reconsideration of the approval ([para 12]).

Furthermore, the appeal court reinforced the rationale for referring cases back to the first respondent. It reiterated that, absent clear evidence of bias or incompetence on the part of the first respondent, such a referral was not only appropriate but necessary to ensure that all relevant facts could be adjudicated fairly ([para 12-13]). 

The argument concerning the non-issue of demolition was based on the misconception that the stage for exercising discretion had already been reached, which the court clarified had not been the case. Judicial discretion regarding demolition could only occur after all relevant aspects of derogation could be articulated, thus reinforcing the procedural fairness principles ([para 14-15]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([para 16])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming the decision to refer the matter back to the first respondent solely for reconsideration of whether the building would derogate from the value of adjoining properties. The court further validated the lower court's conclusion regarding the lack of grounds for demolition at this stage this would require proper findings on all facts' relevance ([para 16]).

The ratio decidendi here confirms that the procedural failures in administrative approvals need addressing in a manner that upholds the audi alteram partem principle. This case sets a precedent regarding procedural fairness in the context of local government decisions, emphasizing the importance of allowing affected parties an opportunity to be heard in related matters. This decision reflects a growing recognition of administrative accountability, particularly in urban planning and property rights contexts.
