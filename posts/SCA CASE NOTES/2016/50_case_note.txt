# Case Note: Mitchell NO v Wren (153/2015) [2016] ZASCA 50

### FIRAC Case Note

#### F – Facts
The appeal arises from the decision of the Eastern Cape Local Division of the High Court pertaining to the validity of two handwritten notes left by the deceased, Carolynn Ellen De Villiers, shortly before her suicide on 9 June 2012 ([1]). The appellant, Ian David Mitchell, is the executor of her estate, while the first and second respondents, Sandra Jane Wren and Yolandi Mynhardt, claim that the notes constituted an amendment to the deceased's existing will to bequeath them specific assets ([1][8]).

Wren and De Villiers were in a same-sex life partnership, having lived together for approximately one year prior to her death. The deceased had previously executed a will on 18 July 2011, which primarily benefited her father and brother, excluding Wren and Mynhardt ([2][8]). On the eve of her death, the deceased is said to have prepared the notes, which made significant testamentary dispositions, including bequests of R1 million each to Wren and Mynhardt ([4]). The notes were discovered after her body was found, sparking disputes over their testamentary validity.

The High Court found in favor of the respondents, ruling that one of the notes, designated as Annexure A, was a valid codicil to the deceased's existing will, prompting the executor to appeal this decision ([9][10]). 

#### I – Issues
The primary legal issues for the court to determine involved:
1. **The validity of the handwritten notes as testamentary documents.** Specifically, can the court determine whether the deceased intended the notes to amend her existing will?
2. **The compatibility of the two notes.** Did one note supersede the other concerning the intention of the bequests? 
3. **The interpretation of relevant statutory provisions under the Wills Act 7 of 1953.** In particular, does section 2(3) allow the court to endorse a document that does not meet formal execution requirements if it can be shown that it was intended as a will or amendment thereof ([9][10])?

#### R – Rules
The court's ruling derived from various legal standards including:
1. **The Wills Act 7 of 1953, specifically s 2(3),** which states that documents lacking formal execution may be accepted as valid testamentary documents if the court finds that they were intended as such ([9]). 
2. **Precedents such as Smith v Parsons NO and others** (2010) ZASCA 39, which affirm that notes can constitute valid amendments if the intent is clear ([8]).
3. **Interpretative principles for testamentary documents**, indicating that when multiple wills or amendments exist, they should be reconciled to determine the testator's intent ([11][12]).

#### A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal conducted a thorough review of the evidence surrounding the notes. Initially, the appellant argued that the two notes were irreconcilable due to their differing dispositions, and the absence of clear indication regarding which was written last ([14]). However, the court acknowledged that an essential aspect of testamentary intent is understanding the overall context.

During its deliberations, the court evaluated the background circumstances, including the deceased’s close relationship with both Wren and Mynhardt, her emotional state, and her known desire to amend her will to reflect her recent life situations ([17][19]). The court reasoned that while both notes contained bequests, they could indeed be seen as complementary, with the structure and wording of Annexure A suggesting it was a subsequent, more formal amendment to a less coherent Annexure B ([20]).

The court emphasized that evidence indicated a logical narrative supporting the idea that the deceased intended to provide equal bequests to both Respondents. This inference was bolstered by her electronic communications indicating a desire to formalize her wishes ([18][19]).

Ultimately, the court concluded that Annexure A was written last, affirming the lower court's ruling that it acted as a valid codicil. Thus, the appeal was dismissed, leading to the order that costs be covered by the deceased's estate ([21][22]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, upholding the High Court's finding that Annexure A constituted a valid codicil to the deceased’s will. The judgment highlights the importance of considering the testamentary intent in light of surrounding circumstances rather than relying solely on formalities. This case reinforces the applicability of broader interpretations under the Wills Act, allowing for the recognition of non-compliant testamentary documents provided intent can be clearly established.

This case reflects significant implications for estate law, particularly regarding the validation of informal testamentary expressions and the recognition of diverse familial and relational dynamics in testamentary capacity and intent.
