# Case Note: Crawford Lindsay von Abo v President of the Republic of South Africa

**Court:** Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Case Number:** CCT 67/08  
**Judgment Date:** 5 June 2009  
**Justices:** Moseneke DCJ, Langa CJ, Cameron J, Mokgoro J, Ngcobo J, Nkabinde J, O'Regan J, Sachs J, Skweyiya J, Van der Westhuizen J, Yacoob J.

## FIRAC Structure

### F – Facts

[1-4] The applicant, Mr. Crawford Lindsay von Abo, sought the confirmation of a High Court order asserting the constitutional invalidity of the President's failure to consider his request for diplomatic protection concerning the infringement of his property rights by the Zimbabwean government. The case arose after Mr. Von Abo, a South African citizen and businessman, experienced expropriation of his land in Zimbabwe starting in 1997 due to a government policy targeting white farmers. 

[5-6] Despite attempts to restore his rights, including a series of communications with the South African government from 2002 onwards, he received inadequate responses. After exhausting domestic remedies in Zimbabwe, he approached the High Court, seeking both declaratory and mandatory relief to compel appropriate governmental action regarding his diplomatic protection claims against Zimbabwe.

[11-12] The High Court found that the South African government's response had been inadequate and ruled in favor of Mr. Von Abo, declaring that the government's failure to consider his diplomatic protection request was inconsistent with the Constitution. The High Court's ruling called for remedial action by the government within 60 days and found the government's actions violated Mr. Von Abo's constitutional rights.

### I – Issues

[22-23] The primary issues before the Constitutional Court included whether the conduct of the President regarding diplomatic protection constitutes "any conduct of the President" under section 172(2)(a) of the Constitution, necessitating confirmation from this Court, and subsequently, whether the High Court's order concerning the President could stand without confirmation.

[24-25] Additionally, if the Court found that the order was not subject to confirmation, it would need to determine what subsequent orders regarding costs and the confidentiality of certain documents should be issued.

### R – Rules

[28-32] The relevant legal framework includes sections 172(2)(a) and 167(5) of the Constitution, which outline the jurisdiction of the Constitutional Court to confirm the constitutional validity of laws and conduct of the President. Section 172(2)(a) emphasizes that an order of constitutional invalidity has no force until confirmed by the Constitutional Court. This Court held in *Pharmaceutical Manufacturers Association* that "any conduct" must be generously interpreted to ensure presidential conduct is effectively scrutinized.

[36-37] In *Kaunda*, it was determined that the executive responsibility to provide diplomatic protection lies primarily with the government, not the President alone. 

### A – Application

[39-51] The Court analyzed the nature of executive powers within the South African Constitution, emphasizing that the President exercises executive authority collaboratively with other members of the Cabinet. The Court underscored that failing to provide diplomatic protection constituted a governmental, rather than presidential, failure. Hence, the determination about whether the President's conduct was unconstitutional did not necessitate a judicial confirmation of the High Court’s order.

The Court further noted that the High Court order did not specify conduct attributable to the President, focusing instead on the government's broader failures. A failure to provide diplomatic protection, while potentially governed by presidential authority, primarily related to the functioning of the Cabinet and did not invoke the President's individual accountability as strictly defined by the Constitution.

### C – Conclusion

[53-56] The Constitutional Court ruled that the application for confirmation of the High Court's order regarding the President was struck off the roll, determining that the President's conduct did not constitute “conduct” under section 172(2)(a). The High Court's order remained in effect against the broader governmental structure and did not necessitate confirmation by the Constitutional Court. This decision highlighted the necessity of identifying specific parties and actions in constitutional claims against the government. 

The Court declined to make any costs order against Mr. Von Abo, acknowledging his good faith effort to vindicate constitutional rights. Waste costs associated with the government's attempt to introduce new evidence were imposed on the respondent, reinforcing accountability within governmental litigation. 

## Implications

The decision in *Von Abo* clarifies the delineation of governmental and presidential responsibilities in matters of diplomatic protection and reinforces the importance of specificity in constitutional claims. It sets a precedent regarding the nature of executive conduct subject to judicial scrutiny and reflects on broader constitutional interpretation concerning the separation of powers within the South African legal framework.
