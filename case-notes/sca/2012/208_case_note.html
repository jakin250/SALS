Supreme Court of Appeal 2012 [2012] ZASCA 208 #### F - Facts The appellant, Meir Margalit,

FIRAC Case Note: Margalit v Standard Bank of SA Ltd (883/2011) [2012] ZASCA 208

F - Facts

The appellant, Meir Margalit, was the former owner of a property in Sandton sold in May 2007 for R3 million, of which R2.9 million was to be received by him, with R100,000 allocated for the estate agent's commission ([1]). Standard Bank, the first respondent, held two mortgage bonds over the property that needed cancellation before the transfer could be executed. The bank appointed Nelson Borman & Partners Inc, the second respondent, to handle the cancellation ([1]).

The transfer, initially expected to proceed smoothly, took over a year, only being completed on 16 July 2008. Margalit attributed a portion of the delay to the negligence of the second respondent and sought damages in the magistrate's court, claiming lost interest on the net proceeds from the sale ([1]–[2]). The trial magistrate ruled in favor of Margalit, awarding damages of R42,713.42. However, the South Gauteng High Court overturned this decision, ruling that the respondents were absolved from liability, which led to this appeal ([2]).

The appeal raised issues surrounding the obligations of the conveyancer, delays due to a lack of proper documentation, and whether negligence on the part of the second respondent was provable. The appeals court would analyze the timeline of events, particularly focusing on actions taken by the second respondent and whether they adhered to the required professional standards ([3]–[14]).

I - Issues

The main issues to be resolved in the case were:

  1. Negligence of the Conveyancer: Did the actions of the second respondent in delaying the transfer and failing to fulfill their professional obligations constitute negligence? ([6], [22])
  2. Contractual Obligations: Was the appellant entitled to damages based on the claim that the second respondent's actions breached contractual obligations related to the bond agreements? ([19], [21])

R - Rules

The applicable legal principles revolve around the duties of care required of a conveyancing attorney. Under South African law, an attorney must execute their responsibilities with the requisite degree of skill and care that a reasonably prudent professional would apply in similar circumstances ([23]), as per the precedent established in Le Roux v Dey 2011 (3) SA 274 (CC) and Van der Spuy v Pillans 1875 Buch 133 ([23], [24]). The standard for negligence requires proof that the professional's conduct fell below the standard of care expected within their field ([24], [25]).

Moreover, the principle of exceptio non adimpleti contractus was relevant, as it identifies a party's right to withhold performance in instances where there is a failure to meet prior obligations ([20], [21]). The obligations imposed upon conveyancers are enshrined in Section 15(A) of the Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937, obliging them to ensure the correctness of facts stated in deeds they prepare ([26]).

A - Application

The court assessed the timeline and actions taken by the second respondent during the transfer process. It established that the second respondent failed to cancel one of the two mortgage bonds, significantly contributing to the delay in registration of transfer ([14], [29]). Evidence showed that the second respondent had access to documentation indicating the existence of both bonds but neglected to act accordingly, leading to a breach of their fiduciary duty by failing to take necessary steps to cancel both bonds.

Furthermore, the court explored multiple points of failure by the second respondent, including the improper preparation of documents according to the prevailing legal practice established in the Chief Registrar's Circular RCR 20 of 2007, which should have been followed to ensure compliance ([31]).

The second respondent’s failure to ensure that the necessary guarantees were secured before proceeding with bond cancellations illustrated a significant lapse in professionalism, evidencing negligence not only in operational conduct but also in adhering to procedural requirements ([22], [32]). Since both rejections by the deeds office stemmed from the second respondent's negligence, the court found that the damages incurred by Margalit were directly attributable to these failures ([32]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Margalit’s appeal with costs, thereby setting aside the decision of the South Gauteng High Court that had absolved the respondents. The court recognized that the second respondent's negligence led to the protracted delay in the transfer process and, as such, destroyed the applicability of the exceptio non adimpleti contractus defense that was improperly advanced ([34]). Given the clear failures in conduct by the second respondent, the court reasserted the importance of accountability in the conveyancing process.

This judgment signifies a critical reaffirmation of the obligations of legal practitioners, especially conveyancers, underscoring the need for vigilance and adherence to professional standards to avoid economic harm to clients. The case serves as a poignant reminder of the legal duty owed by attorneys not just to their clients but within the broader transactional framework they operate within.

In summary, the ruling reinstated the trial magistrate's finding on negligence and confirmed liability for the damages sought by Margalit. The second respondent's lapses in professional conduct directly led to the financial losses incurred by the appellant, showcasing the judiciary's willingness to enforce accountability within professional practice in the legal domain ([34]).
