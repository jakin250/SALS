# Case Note: Whitehead v The State [2007] SCA 171 (RSA)

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
[1] On 15 August 1995, municipal and regional service council workers in Kuruman embarked on a strike, culminating in demonstrations to express their grievances. The protests, which proceeded peacefully, transformed into chaos when a group of white men armed with pickhandles and sjamboks attacked the demonstrators. The assault resulted in severe injuries to multiple workers, including the death of Garoetelwe Adam Brown, who sustained fatal head injuries inflicted with a blunt instrument during the violence.

[2] The subsequent legal proceedings resulted in charges against seven appellants, which included public violence and culpable homicide. They were convicted of public violence and culpable homicide, with additional charges against one appellant for assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm. The regional court imposed sentences of five years for each of the first two counts, with two years suspended, resulting in an effective eight-year imprisonment.

[3] The appellants' appeals to the High Court were unsuccessful, and subsequent applications for leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) were partially granted, focusing primarily on the convictions for culpable homicide and the associated sentences.

### I – Issues
[4] The core legal issues arise from the finding of culpable homicide, the appropriateness of the sentence imposed for that charge, potential duplication of convictions from the public violence charge, and the specific sentence imposed on the seventh appellant for assault with intent to cause grievous bodily harm.

### R – Rules
[5] The relevant legal principles concerning culpable homicide focus on whether the accused acted negligently and whether a causal connection exists between their actions and the death. The doctrine of common purpose is integral, suggesting that if participants engage in a criminal enterprise, they can be collectively liable for the actions occurring in furtherance of that enterprise.

[6] The legal requirements for culpable homicide include the necessity of establishing that the accused's negligent actions foreseeably led to death. In contrast, public violence requires a shared intent and concerted action aimed at disturbing public peace. Precedent cases, including *S v Thenkwa* 1970 (3) SA 529 (A) and *S v Mgedezi* 1989 (1) SA 687 (A), elucidate the consequences of collective action in confirming culpability.

### A – Application
[7] The appellants contended that the evidence demonstrated that a police officer and another individual may have independently caused the victim’s death, severing any connection to the appellants' collective actions. However, the court noted that these arguments did not hold since the attack on Brown occurred concurrently with the broader assault on demonstrators, and the evidence confirmed the group’s organized intent.

[8] The SCA found that the trial court had correctly assessed the culpability of each appellant under the doctrine of common purpose. The actions were premeditated, showcasing a clear intent to employ violence against the demonstrators, thus fulfilling the mens rea requirement for culpable homicide.

[9] Regarding the arguments related to duplicity of convictions, the SCA acknowledged that while charges may overlap, each conviction must be based on distinct factual allegations and legal principles. The court highlighted that the charge of public violence encompassed the offence of causing a death through negligent actions, but the two were legally distinct in terms of the specific conduct and legal thresholds.

[10] Evaluation of the sentences confirmed that the magistrate had considered various factors, including personal circumstances of the appellants and the seriousness of their conduct during the violent attack, ultimately resulting in a balanced sentencing exercise.

### C – Conclusion
[11] The SCA upheld the conviction of culpable homicide, asserting that the trial court's finding was appropriately grounded in the evidence presented of shared culpability. The appeal against the conviction for culpable homicide was dismissed, while recognizing that the attached sentence to this conviction constituted a duplication of conviction with the public violence charge. The SCA recommended that the appellants should not have been convicted under both counts, as the deed was effectively a singular act of violence against the participants of the strike.

[12] The court confirmed the sentence for public violence and suggested that both the nature of the actions during the attack and the community's needs warranted significant imprisonment. The ramifications of this judgment emphasize the legal principle against duplicative convictions while reinforcing the necessity to hold individuals accountable for their roles in collective criminal enterprises.

In conclusion, while the appeal for culpable homicide was dismissed, the argument for duplication of convictions emerged as a prominent theme, ultimately leading to a reconsideration of the appropriate legal and sentencing framework for cases involving complex dynamics of concerted violence.
