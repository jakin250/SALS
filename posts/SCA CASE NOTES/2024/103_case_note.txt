# Case Note: C A R v The Central Authority of The Republic of South Africa and Another (737/2023) [2024] ZASCA 103

## F – Facts:
The case centers around the appeals process launched by C A R (hereinafter referred to as "the father"), who seeks the return of his son CJ to Canada from South Africa. The father and Y R (hereinafter referred to as "the mother") were married in 2011, became Canadian citizens and settled in Calgary, Alberta. They visited South Africa in July 2022, during which YR informed CAR of her intention to remain in South Africa with CJ, deviating from plans to return to Canada ([paras 2–5]).

After initial legal consultations, in December 2022, the Central Authority of South Africa initiated proceedings for CJ's return, which was contested by YR in the Gauteng Division of the High Court. The mother raised three defenses:
1. Disputing the habitual residence of CJ.
2. Arguing based on Article 13(a) of the Hague Convention that CAR acquiesced to CJ's retention in South Africa.
3. Claiming under Article 13(b) that CJ's return would expose him to grave psychological harm ([paras 6–10]).

The High Court found in favor of YR, dismissing the return application on these grounds. The father subsequently appealed this decision.

## I – Issues:
The central legal question for this appeal is whether YR effectively demonstrated that returning CJ to Canada would expose him to grave risk of physical or psychological harm, thereby invoking the exception outlined in Article 13(b) of the Hague Convention ([para 13]). The Court also had to ascertain whether the High Court misapplied the framework for Article 13(b) in its dismissal of the return application.

## R – Rules:
The relevant legal framework is encapsulated in the Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction, particularly Articles 12 and 13. Article 12 mandates the prompt return of a child who has been wrongfully removed or retained, while Article 13(b) provides a ground for refusing such a return, stating that a return may be rejected if there is a grave risk that it would expose the child to physical or psychological harm ([para 15]).

The burden falls on the party opposing the return to establish this defense under Article 13(b) on a balance of probabilities ([para 16]). The standards established in previous case law, such as *Sonderup v Tondelli* and *Koch*, clarify that this assessment involves not merely the potential harm itself but also the necessity of protective measures that could mitigate such risks ([paras 17–19]).

## A – Application:
In analyzing whether YR met her burden to establish the defense under Article 13(b), the Supreme Court of Appeal observed that her claims were founded on allegations of psychological harm linked to past domestic disputes and the child's medical history. However, the Court determined that YR failed to demonstrate a sufficient case for how these allegations would translate to grave risks following CJ's return ([paras 20–41]).

Key findings included:

1. **Meditation of Conditions:** The father offered undertakings to mitigate any potential risks associated with CJ's return, including appropriate accommodations and financial support ([para 31])—elements typical in return orders to satisfy the provisions of the Hague Convention concerning protective measures.
   
2. **Assessment of Psychological Conditions:** The High Court’s conclusion that CJ’s medical and psychological assessments from Canada were less comprehensive than those performed in South Africa received scrutiny. The Supreme Court noted that the absence of evidence linking these developmental delays directly to the father's conduct detracted from YR's claims ([para 35]).

3. **Two-Pronged Inquiry:** The High Court's failure to adequately consider the interplay of short-term and long-term best interests of the child, as outlined in *Sonderup*, underscored missteps in its process that led to a conclusion without a balanced consideration of all relevant protections ([para 42]).

## C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal determined that YR did not successfully establish the defense contemplated under Article 13(b) of the Hague Convention. The High Court had misapplied the legal standards and failed to adequately consider the protective measures put forth by the father, which were essential to address the allegations made.

In light of the required legal framework and the evidence presented, the Supreme Court overturned the High Court's decision, granting the return of CJ to Canada and establishing a comprehensive framework for further proceedings in Canada to ensure the child's best interests would be safeguarded ([para 45]).

### Implications:
This judgment reinforces the necessity for judicial authorities to adhere strictly to the provisions of the Hague Convention, emphasizing the objective of returning children to their habitual residence for proper custody determinations. It also illustrates the balance courts must achieve between a child's immediate welfare concerns and the legal obligations stemming from international agreements. The refusal to accept emotional harm as an absolute barrier highlights the courts' commitment to resolving custody disputes through recognized legal processes rather than emotional narratives.
