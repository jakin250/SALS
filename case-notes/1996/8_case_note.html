Constitutional Court

Oded Besserglik v The Minister of Trade, Industry and Tourism and Others

The background to these proceedings included the dismissal of Besserglik's action for damages due to wrongful prosecution by Curlewis J on 16 August 1994, followed by an unsuccessful application for leave to appeal ([3]).

Constitutional Court 1996 CCT 34/95

Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa
Case No: CCT 34/95
Delivered on: 14 May 1996
Judgment by: O’Regan J

F – Facts

In this application for direct access under rule 17 of the Constitutional Court Rules, the applicant, Oded Besserglik, challenged the constitutionality of section 20(4)(b) of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959 (“the Act”) ([1]). This legislative provision required that a litigant must obtain leave from either the relevant provincial or local division of the Supreme Court, or from the Appellate Division, in order to pursue an appeal against a civil judgment ([2]).

The background to these proceedings included the dismissal of Besserglik's action for damages due to wrongful prosecution by Curlewis J on 16 August 1994, followed by an unsuccessful application for leave to appeal ([3]). Besserglik subsequently sought direct access to the Constitutional Court after receiving a refusal for postponement requests and a petition to the Appellate Division ([4]).

Besserglik argued that the precondition for prosecuting civil appeals set forth in section 20(4)(b) was inconsistent with the rights enshrined in Chapter 3 of the Constitution, specifically the right of equal access to courts under section 22 and the equality before the law under section 8 ([3]-[4]).

I – Issues

The case presented several legal questions:

  1. Whether the application for direct access was the appropriate procedural avenue given the circumstances, particularly whether Besserglik should have sought a referral under section 102(1) or section 102(6) of the Constitution ([5]).
  2. The substantive legal issues revolved around the constitutionality of section 20(4)(b) of the Supreme Court Act, particularly:
  • Whether the leave to appeal requirement infringes the right to access to a court as per section 22 of the Constitution ([8]).
  • Whether such a provision contravenes the equality clause under section 8 of the Constitution, especially when compared to appeal procedures in magistrates’ courts ([11]).

R – Rules

The legal framework applicable to this case primarily included:

  1. The Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1993: Chapters addressing the right to access to courts (section 22) and the equality clause (section 8).
  2. Judicial Precedents: The court relied on past judgments, particularly S v Rens (1996) and Bernstein v Bester (CCT 23/95), to examine the legal principles surrounding appeals and the interpretation of constitutional rights ([9]-[10], [11]).
  3. Supreme Court Act, 1959: The specific provisions, particularly section 20(4), establishing the procedure for appeals in civil cases ([1]-[2]).

A – Application

In its analysis, the court noted that the extraordinary nature of direct access under rule 17 necessitates the demonstration of exceptional circumstances ([6]). It acknowledged the applicant's procedural missteps yet determined that due to the immediate implications for Besserglik, allowing direct access was appropriate ([7]).

On the substantive issues, the court articulated that the right of access to courts embedded in section 22 encompassed justiciable disputes but did not necessarily extend to an automatic right of appeal. The screening mechanism provided by the leave to appeal did not constitute an infringement of this right, especially as it ensured that only meritorious appeals were considered ([10]). The court emphasized that the mechanism's objective was to preserve judicial resources and prevent the inundation of higher courts with frivolous appeals.

With respect to the equality claim under section 8, the court reiterated that differences in appeal procedures between various tiers of courts do not inherently violate the principle of equality, provided the same rules apply to all individuals appealing from similar court levels ([11]). The absence of unfair discrimination in the processes between different courts was pivotal, as argued in S v Rens ([11]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court, through the judgment of O’Regan J, ultimately declared that section 20(4)(b) of the Supreme Court Act was not inconsistent with the Constitution ([13]). The ruling underscored that the Constitution did not guarantee an unqualified right to appeal but rather a reasonable access to courts under established procedures. The implications of this decision reinforced the legitimacy of procedural barriers to appeals in civil matters and underscored the constitutional framework allowing for differentiated judicial processes between various tiers of courts. This judgment affirmed the need for a balance between accessibility to justice and the efficient functioning of the judiciary.

Overall, the case established crucial precedents regarding the interpretation of access rights and equality in legal proceedings, highlighting the court's commitment to upholding constitutional norms whilst allowing for pragmatic judicial processes.
