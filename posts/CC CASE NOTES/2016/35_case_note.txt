# Case Note: Merafong City Local Municipality v AngloGold Ashanti Limited [2016] ZACC 35

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([1–12])

The case revolves around a legal dispute between Merafong City Local Municipality (the applicant) and AngloGold Ashanti Limited (the respondent) concerning the legality of a surcharge imposed on water supplied for industrial use. The Minister of Water Affairs and Forestry intervened in a decision made on 18 July 2005, overturning Merafong’s right to levy a surcharge on such water. Merafong contended that the Minister's decision was unconstitutional as it encroached on powers granted exclusively to municipalities under section 156(1) of the Constitution, empowering them to regulate water and sanitation services.

Before the issue reached the Constitutional Court, it passed through the High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Division, and the Supreme Court of Appeal. Both courts ruled in favor of AngloGold, citing Merafong's failure to formally challenge the Minister’s ruling. Merafong had continued levying the surcharge despite this ruling, arguing that it was operationally unsustainable without the surcharge, leading to AngloGold to bringing an application to compel Merafong's compliance with the Minister's decision.

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([22])

The primary issue at hand was whether the Supreme Court of Appeal and the High Court were correct in enforcing the Minister's ruling and whether an organ of state could challenge the validity of an administrative decision, particularly in light of the constraints of the principle of legality versus the need for administrative actions to remain effective until set aside.

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([41–54])

The judgment referenced several legal principles and precedents pertaining to administrative law, including:

1. **The Principle of Legality**: Admin actions must comply with the Constitution.
2. **Oudekraal Principle**: An invalid administrative act may still produce legally effective consequences until it is formally set aside; therefore, it cannot be ignored by those subject to its demands ([22], [39]).
3. **Kirland Principle**: Establishes the obligations of public officials not to disregard potentially invalid administrative decisions, reinforcing that these decisions remain enforceable unless formally challenged ([39], [40]).
4. **Section 229 of the Constitution**: Grants municipalities the power to levy service fees and indicates the constraints within which they must operate ([156]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([69–77], [83–116])

The court engaged with the fundamental proposition posed by Oudekraal and Kirland regarding whether an organ of state could mount a collateral challenge to an administrative ruling in the absence of a formal review. The Court ultimately concluded:

- **Merafong as an Organ of State**: The Supreme Court of Appeal's ruling imposing a duty to proactively seek to set aside the Minister's decision was found to be untenable. The Constitutional Court opted instead to allow Merafong the right to raise a reactive challenge to the enforcement of the Minister’s ruling, acknowledging the complexities and inherent difficulties in imposing a rigid structure on public sector entities.
- **Ministerial Jurisdiction**: The judgments elaborated on whether the Minister's interference constituted a breach of the municipal authority's exclusive competence to implement tariffs on water services. Merafong argued, and the court recognized, that the Minister's powers did not extend to invalidating the Municipality's constitutional rights as laid out in the relevant provisions of both the Constitution and the Water Services Act.

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([84])

The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal, upheld Merafong's challenge, and set aside the decisions of the High Court and Supreme Court of Appeal. Importantly, the matter was remitted to the High Court to adjudicate on the lawfulness of the Minister’s decision with an understanding that the ruling held legal repercussions until formally disposed. 

This decision delineates the limits of municipal fiscal authority and constraints on state power while emphasizing the need for clarity in administrative law. The implications reaffirm the constitutional division of powers between local and national government spheres, ensuring that municipal entities retain authority over local governance matters critical to their operational mandates, such as the imposition of tariffs on water supplies. This ruling serves as a precedent reinforcing the constitutional doctrine of legality and underscoring the significance of procedure in administrative actions.
