Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: Fortuin v S (838/2017) [2018] ZASCA 5

## F – Facts
The appellant, Christopher Fortuin, was convicted by a regional court of attempted rape, contravening section 3 of the Criminal Law (Sexual Offences and Related Matters) Amendment Act 32 of 2007, and sentenced to four years' imprisonment ([1]). The regional court dismissed Fortuin's application for leave to appeal both his conviction and sentence. However, the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, granted leave to appeal regarding the sentence but denied it for the conviction [[1]]. Subsequently, the Supreme Court of Appeal granted special leave to Fortuin to appeal the conviction, which set the stage for the current appeal ([2]).

The pivotal question before the court was whether the high court should have permitted Fortuin to appeal his conviction ([2]). The test for this decision hinged on whether Fortuin demonstrated a reasonable prospect of success on appeal, particularly concerning his intention to penetrate ([2]). 

The incident in question occurred on the night of June 2, 2013, at the SAPS Academy in Hammanskraal, where both Fortuin and the complainant were enrolled. At approximately 23h30, the complainant, who was staying in the female quarters, answered a knock on her door only to find Fortuin, who appeared to be heavily intoxicated. Fortuin insisted on entering and declared his intention to sleep in her room, despite her repeated requests for him to leave. He forcibly grabbed her by the arms, pushed her onto the bed, and touched her breast while she resisted and ultimately drove him away with her forceful actions ([4]-[5]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issue in this case is whether the high court should have granted Fortuin leave to appeal against his conviction of attempted rape, based on the criterion of whether a reasonable prospect of success on appeal exists concerning the appellant's intention ([2]). 

Underlying this issue is the determination of whether the prosecution successfully established Fortuin's intent to penetrate the complainant beyond a reasonable doubt, as required under South African law, which is crucial for a conviction for attempted rape ([6]).

## R – Rules
The legal standard applicable to the case is framed by the requirement that the prosecution must prove the appellant's intent to penetrate beyond a reasonable doubt. This is consistent with established case law in South Africa, including *R v B* 1958 (1) SA 199 (A) which clarifies that intention to perform an act of penetration is essential for a conviction for attempted rape ([6]).

Additionally, the notion of "finally formulated intention" as discussed in *S v Du Plessis* 1981 (3) SA 382 (A), emphasizes that the intent must be specifically directed towards penetration rather than a lesser charge such as sexual assault, which would not meet the higher threshold of attempted rape ([6]).

Another relevant standard is the principle that, for the appeal to be successful, there must exist reasonable prospects of success on the conviction appeal, as determined by the high court ([2]).

## A – Application
In applying the rules to the facts of the case, the court examined the evidence presented, particularly focusing on the nature of Fortuin's actions during the incident. The evidence showed that the incident was brief, consisting of Fortuin touching the complainant's breast over her clothing, with no attempt made by him to undress her or expose any intimate body parts ([7]). 

The court recognized that the open door during the incident, coupled with the lack of a clear intention to penetrate, suggested that there was a reasonable doubt about whether Fortuin's actions amounted to an intended act of penetration necessary for a conviction of attempted rape ([6]-[7]). 

The court thus concluded that an appeal based on a possible re-evaluation of the intended criminal act was warranted, allowing for a possibility of a lesser conviction under the sexual assault statute as opposed to the more serious charge of attempted rape.

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld Fortuin's appeal, allowing him to challenge his conviction of attempted rape in the Gauteng Division of the High Court. The court's judgment emphasized the necessity of establishing a beyond-reasonable-doubt intention to penetrate for the conviction of attempted rape to stand ([8]). It highlighted the potential for re-evaluation of the initial findings regarding the appellant's intent, which had not met the required threshold, thus underscoring the importance of thorough evidential examination in sexual offenses ([7]).

The decision holds significant implications for similar cases, reiterating the vital importance of distinguishing between various forms of sexual crimes based on the intentions of the alleged perpetrator and reaffirming judicial scrutiny in light of evidential standards in sexual offense convictions. 

By allowing this appeal, the court underscored its commitment to justice and the necessity of upholding the principles of criminal law pertinent to the protection of defendants’ rights while also considering the gravity of offenses against complainants. 

The formal order was as follows:
1. The appeal is upheld.
2. Leave is granted to the appellant to appeal to the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, against his conviction of attempted rape ([8]).
