## FIRAC Case Note: Sakkie Baartman v The State

### F – Facts
On the evening of February 7, 1990, Mr. Edwin Keyter and Ms. Johanna van Aswegen were violently killed on a smallholding in Withoogte, situated in the district of Uitenhage. The deceased lived on the smallholding with Mr. Keyter's son, who is a quadriplegic, and his wife, Karen ([para 1]). It was agreed that a group of men—at least two and at most four—had appeared at the smallholding under the pretense of purchasing goats. After being urged by Ms. van Aswegen, Mr. Keyter agreed to assist them and accompanied some of these men to the nearby goat kraal, where he was attacked and brutally beaten with blunt instruments. Ms. van Aswegen followed Mr. Keyter to the kraal and suffered a similar fate when she arrived. Attempts by two men to lure Mrs. Keyter out of the residence were unsuccessful, and she contacted their neighbor, Mr. Van Nieuwenhuizen, for help. Upon their arrival, the perpetrators had already fled ([paras 2-3]).

Subsequently, Mr. Keyter was found dead at the kraal, his autopsy revealing that both he and Ms. van Aswegen died from blunt force trauma to the head, with numerous injuries corroborating this finding ([para 4]). The appellant, Sakkie Baartman, alongside another man, David Smith, was charged in May 1994 with two counts of murder and one count of robbery. The appellant was convicted on both murder charges and was sentenced to life imprisonment; the robbery charge yielded a not guilty verdict. Smith received an 18-year sentence for the murder of Mr. Keyter only ([para 5]).

The state's case relied on a statement made by the appellant, considered a confession, to investigating officer Lt. Rautenbach, alongside certain disclosures made during a site identification attempt ([paras 5-6]). During the trial, the admissibility of the confession and the identification was contested, but the trial court deemed both voluntary and admissible after a mini-trial on their legitimacy ([para 7-8]).

### I – Issues
1. **Admissibility of Evidence**: Was the confession made by the appellant to Lt. Rautenbach and the subsequent identification at the scene admissible as evidence?
2. **Constitutional Rights**: Did the failure to adequately inform the appellant of his rights, specifically his right to legal representation, constitute a violation of his constitutional rights under section 25 of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1993 ([paras 12-13])?
3. **Credibility of Testimony**: How should the court evaluate the reliability of the appellant's confession in light of the evidence presented, particularly when conflicting testimonies were provided and concerns regarding the appellant's credibility arose ([paras 18-20])?

### R – Rules
The relevant legal authority stems from the South African Criminal Procedure Act of 1977, specifically section 217(1), governing the admissibility of confessions ([para 12]). Furthermore, the Constitutional Court's ruling in *S v Mhlungu and Others* 1995 (3) SA 867 (CC) indicates that the provisions of section 25 of the Constitution, protecting the right to a fair trial, could apply in this case ([para 13]). The evaluation of the appellant's confession must conform to the principles established in previous case law concerning the reliability and voluntary nature of such confessions, noting that courts have recognized the possibility of false confessions leading to wrongful convictions ([para 17]).

### A – Application
The court analyzed the process leading to the appellant's confession, noting that Lt. Rautenbach informed him of his rights, including the right to legal representation, yet the manner and extent of this notification were in dispute. While Rautenbach's concession during cross-examination indicated a cursory communication of these rights, the court held that the essential criteria were met under both common law and statutory standards ([paras 12-13]).

In examining the credibility of the appellant's confession, the court identified significant inconsistencies between the confession and the accounts provided by other witnesses, highlighting the discrepancies regarding the involvement of accomplices and the absence of corroborative evidence concerning the alleged theft of goats ([paras 18-20]). This warranted careful scrutiny of the confession's substantiation, as the trial court failed to adequately consider the potential for the confession to be inaccurate or fabricated. Having reviewed the circumstances surrounding the confession, the court noted no compelling reasons to dismiss the reliability concerns regarding the evidence presented by the state ([paras 24-25]).

Moreover, the attempted identification at the scene produced no conclusive results, further undermining the state's reliance on the confession as the primary basis for conviction ([paras 16-25]).

### C – Conclusion
Given the failure of the prosecution to fulfill the burden of proof beyond a reasonable doubt, the court concluded that the appellant's conviction lacked sufficient evidentiary support and should be overturned. As a result, both the guilty verdicts and the associated life sentences were set aside. The court determined it unnecessary to address the constitutional issues raised concerning the appellant's rights under the Constitution. The case underscores the critical importance of safeguarding defendants' rights and ensuring that all evidence presented in criminal trials is both admissible and reliable ([paras 24-25]).
