# FIRAC Case Note: Otto Friederich Graf v. Hans Joachim Werner Buechel

**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Case No**: 150/2002  
**Judgment Delivered**: 27 March 2003  
**Coram**: Howie P, Schutz, Streicher, Cloete, and Lewis JJA  

---

## F - Facts

[2] The respondent, Hans Joachim Werner Buechel, was the sole shareholder, director, and only loan account creditor of a close corporation, Western Seaboard Development, which later became a company. The company had purchased immovable property intending to develop a sectional title hotel. Otto Friederich Graf, the appellant, lent the purchase price against a mortgage bond secured over the property. The loan agreement stipulated that if certain conditions related to property development were not met by a specified date, the appellant would be entitled to repay his capital and interest. The conditions went unmet.

[3] Subsequently, the appellant, respondent, and the company entered an extension agreement, which included provisions for securing documents, including share certificates, share transfer forms, a cession of the loan account, and a power of attorney. Particularly, Clause 9 stipulated that upon default, Graf could either acquire the company or pass transfer of the immovable property to himself or his nominee.

[5] When the company failed to meet its debts and was ultimately wound up, on 7 May 1999, shares were transferred to Graf as provided in the extension agreement.

---

## I - Issues

[6] The primary legal questions at hand are whether a **pactum commissorium** in a contract of pledge is enforceable when the pledgor is not the pledgee's debtor and whether the contract could be interpreted as a conditional sale if the property value was less than the debt owed.

[7] In the court below, the respondent sought to invalidate the transfer of shares based on the assertion that the extension agreement embodied an invalid pactum commissorium. The lower court found in favor of the respondent, declaring the relevant clause invalid and concluding that the transfer of shares was ineffective. 

---

## R - Rules

[9] A **pactum commissorium** involves an agreement whereby, upon default, the pledgee retains the pledged item as their property. Historically prohibited in Roman law, this prohibition was also integrated into Roman Dutch law, with the reasoning being that it allows creditors to retain excessively valuable assets for minor debts, exploiting desperate debtors ([10]–[12]).

[12] South African legal precedents further affirm this prohibition, as evidenced in *Mapenduka v Ashington* [1919] AD 343 and *Sun Life Assurance Co of Canada v Kuranda* [1924] AD 20. In these cases, it was established that agreements allowing creditors to keep pledged items violate public policy.

[19] The court also considers whether the pactum commissorium doctrine can be conjured differently in circumstances where the pledgor is a third party, with the view that the rules governing these dynamics remain absolute.

---

## A - Application

[15] The appellant contested the applicability of the pactum commissorium rule, positing that the prohibition should not extend to situations involving a third-party pledgor. However, the court rejected this notion ([16]–[18]). The prohibition is universal and applies regardless of the financial status of the pledgor or the relationship between the pledgor and the debtor. 

[20] The court further clarified that no explicit or tacit agreement existed between the parties, validating the contract in question as an invalid **pactum commissorium**. The law prescribes that agreements allowing creditors to appropriate pledged assets or deal with them arbitrarily on default are inherently oppressive and grounded in public policy to protect debtors from exploitation ([30]–[31]).

[26] Attempting to classify the arrangement under the conditional sale doctrine was dismissed because the provisions lacked specific terms for fair valuation to guide the pledgee upon default. The absence of a mechanism for valuing the assets undermined any argument for conditional sale legality. Thus, using these clauses to interpret the agreement as conditional sale effectively still rendered it an invalid pactum commissorium.

---

## C - Conclusion

[31] The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the lower court's declaration that the portion of Clause 9 in the extension agreement constituted an unenforceable pactum commissorium. The appeal was dismissed with costs, implicitly reinforcing the antiquated yet crucial principle aimed at preventing unjust enrichment through the exploitation of vulnerable debtors.  

This case underscores the judicial reluctance to allow deviations from established common law rules absent compelling justification for their alteration within modern legal frameworks ([32]). The ruling preserves the integrity of creditors' practices while cautiously guiding interpretations of fair and just dealings in financial agreements.
