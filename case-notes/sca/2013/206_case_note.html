Harmony Gold Mining Company Ltd v Regional Director: Free State Department of Water Affairs (971/12) [2013] ZASCA 206

The KOSH area's gold mining history dates back to 1952, involving extensive underground mining practices that resulted in interconnected tunnels affecting groundwater ([2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 206

F – Facts

This appeal arises from a directive issued by the Acting Regional Director of the Free State Department of Water Affairs on 1 November 2005. The directive was issued pursuant to section 19(3) of the National Water Act 36 of 1998 (NWA) and required Harmony Gold Mining Company Ltd (the Appellant) and other mining companies operating in the Klerksdorp–Orkney–Stilfontein–Hartbeesfontein (KOSH) area to implement anti-pollution measures due to contamination caused by their gold mining activities ([1]). Harmony ceased mining operations in the KOSH area on 27 February 2008 and argued that the directive became invalid upon its cessation of landholding, a position contested by other respondents including AngloGold Ashanti Ltd and Simmer and Jack Mines Ltd ([1][12]).

The KOSH area's gold mining history dates back to 1952, involving extensive underground mining practices that resulted in interconnected tunnels affecting groundwater ([2]). The pollution stemming from mining conditions includes acidic water containing heavy metals, as groundwater in these operations often encounters iron-sulfide minerals ([3]). Following Harmony’s acquisition of ARMGold in 2003, proceedings continued under its management until the directive was issued, establishing obligations to prevent groundwater pollution ([4]-[6]).

The directive in question delineated specific anti-pollution measures requiring cooperation among mining companies and mandated Harmony to manage and treat subterranean water, maintain infrastructure, and report on water quality and management status ([8][9]). The directive was intended to remain effective until a comprehensive agreement on long-term water management was reached among the parties involved ([9]).

The central issue arose when ARMGold transferred ownership of the mining operations to Pamodzi Gold Orkney (Pty) Ltd on 6 January 2009, leading Harmony to assert that it should no longer be bound by the directive ([11][12]).

I – Issues

The legal questions before the court revolved around the validity and enforceability of the directive post-Harmony's cessation of mining operations and its implications under section 19 of the NWA. The specific issues include:

  1. Whether the directive became invalid or unenforceable against Harmony upon its assertion that it no longer owned, controlled, occupied, or used the land ([13]).
  2. Whether the directive's lack of a specified date for completion of required measures rendered it invalid ([13]).
  3. Whether the directive was indefinite and thus invalid, or whether it impliedly came to an end when it became clear that agreement among the mining companies would not be reached ([1][10][29]).

R – Rules

The NWA provides a legislative framework governing water management in South Africa. Specifically:

  • Section 19(1) establishes the duty of "landholders" to prevent water pollution resulting from activities on their land ([14]). The responsible parties, including owners, controllers, and users of land on which such activities occur, must implement reasonable measures to prevent pollution ([14][15]).
  • Section 19(3) grants a catchment management agency the authority to direct a landholder to take specific anti-pollution measures, subject to the stipulation that these directives remain applicable as long as the entity is a landholder ([19][25]). Historically, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) interpreted section 19 to highlight the flexible, non-exhaustive nature of the required measures ([15]).

The overarching principles under section 24 of the Constitution of South Africa enforce the right to an environment that is not harmful to health or well-being and mandate the protection of environmental integrity through reasonable measures ([19][21]).

Moreover, statutory interpretation favors adherence to legislative intent, with the court indicating a strong preference for interpretations that promote the objectives of environmental sustainability and pollution prevention ([21]).

A – Application

The court assessed Harmony's interpretation of section 19(3) against the statutory language and legislative intent. Harmony contended that once it ceased being a landholder, it was exempt from compliance with the directive. However, the court found that the directive imposed an obligation that did not cease with the cessation of landholding. The expansive interpretation of the terms "owner," "control," or “use” indicated that responsibilities could persist beyond formal ownership in light of ongoing environmental risks ([22][27]).

Harmony also argued that the directive required specification of completion dates for the measures mandated, asserting that the absence of such dates rendered it invalid. The court refuted this contention, highlighting that the directive's nature allowed for flexibility, aligned with the Minister’s discretion to dictate compliance timelines. The notion that the directive must include a specific completion date overlooked the permissive nature of the statutory framework ([28]).

Regarding the argument that the directive was infinite in nature, the court clarified that the directive was intended as an interim measure pending the establishment of a longer-term solution. The assertion that it was indefinite was seen as mischaracterization of the directive's terms which specified that compliance was needed until a sustainable solution was reached ([29]).

Finally, the claim that the directive became inoperative should an agreement between the companies fail was dismissed; the court underscored that termination provisions for directives are not contingent simply upon the ability to reach future agreements ([30]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Harmony's appeal and upheld the High Court's ruling, affirming the validity and enforceability of the directive. The court's decision emphasized the continuity of obligations under the NWA even after a party ceases to hold legal title to the land, thereby bridging the accountability of mining companies to manage environmental risks associated with their operations.

This holding reinforces the state's mandate under the NWA to protect water resources and highlights the principle that environmental responsibilities extend beyond immediate land stewardship, fundamentally supporting sustainable and responsible mining practices in South Africa. The court's interpretation aligns with constitutional imperatives, promoting a proactive approach to preventing environmental degradation ([31]).

In summary, the decision upholds environmental accountability, suggesting that entities cannot evade their regulatory responsibilities simply by altering ownership structures. The implications of this ruling are significant for the mining sector and environmental law in South Africa, reflecting a robust protective posture toward ecological integrity and accountability for pollution.
