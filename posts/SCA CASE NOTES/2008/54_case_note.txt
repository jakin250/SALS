Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2008

# FIRAC Case Note: Cillie v Geldenhuys (306/07) [2008] ZASCA 54

## F - Facts ([1–6])

The case originated from an application in the Cape High Court, where Matheus Lourens Geldenhuys (the respondent), as the owner of the property "Matjiesrivier," sought to register two servitudes over Pieter Rossouw Cillie's property, "Uitkomst," located in the Ceres district of the Western Cape. Geldenhuys claimed rights based on the historical use of a fountain (Matjiesrivierfontein) located on Uitkomst, which supplied water benefitting Matjiesrivier. The application included a request for an interdict restraining Cillie from extracting underground water via a borehole on Uitkomst, which Geldenhuys argued decreased the fountain's discharge.

Geldenhuys' application was initially successful, leading to a judgment in his favor, which Cillie subsequently appealed ([2–3]). The dispute involved historical interactions between the two properties, particularly the use of water from the fountain that naturally flowed from Uitkomst to Matjiesrivier, and issues of prescription as a means to establish rights over natural resources ([1–5]).

Cillie, in digging a borehole approximately 200 meters away from the fountain, disturbed the fountain's flow, prompting Geldenhuys to assert his claims of servitude based on longstanding use. Geldenhuys sought a legal declaration confirming his right to use of water from the fountain and to construct a pipeline ([6]).

## I - Issues ([6–7])

The court had to address several critical legal questions:

1. **Acquisition of Servitude via Prescription**: Did Geldenhuys and his predecessors obtain rights through prescription to use the fountain and its water?
  
2. **Nature of Servitudes**:
   - **Definition**: Were the servitudes in question positive or negative?
   - **Presumption of knowledge**: Was Cillie bound by any unregistered servitude given his purchase of Uitkomst?

3. **Extraction of Underground Water**: Was Cillie entitled to extract water from the borehole on Uitkomst, given the established servitudes that may have affected the fountain?

## R - Rules ([8–19])

The legal framework guiding this case included:

1. **Prescription Law**: As stipulated in the Prescription Act of 1969, an individual may acquire rights through prescription if they openly and continuously utilize such rights for thirty years, acting as if they have rightful entitlement ([8]). This principle derives from Roman-Dutch law whereby adverse user (nec vi, nec clam, nec precario) must be demonstrated.

2. **Classification of Servitudes**: Servitudes are categorized into positive (the right to perform a specific action) and negative (the right to prevent another from doing something). The court had previously determined that the servitudes in dispute were mixed but required critical evaluation to ascertain their nature ([9]).

3. **Doctrine of Notice**: Generally, for real rights, registrability does not negate validity; therefore, the doctrine does not apply, as the acquisition of servitudes through prescription stands alone without the need for registration ([13]).

4. **Water Rights**: South African law regarding water is premised on the principle that property owners may extract underground water unless specifically restricted by established servitudes ([14]).

## A - Application ([8–19])

The court's analysis of the case focused on:

### 1. **Acquisition via Prescription**
The court examined whether Geldenhuys had, through prescription, established rights to the use of water from the fountain. Based on historical usage, the court affirmed that Geldenhuys exercised his claimed rights openly for over thirty years, thus satisfying the conditions for acquiring a servitude by prescription ([8–10]).

### 2. **Nature of the Servitudes**
The court concurred with the original ruling that one servitude qualified as negative while the other was positive. Notably, a dispute arose regarding whether the actions taken by Matjiesrivier's owner qualified as a valid assertion against Cillie. The appellant argued the nature of servitude distinguished by whether Geldenhuys had ever attempted to utilize water on Uitkomst directly against Cillie’s rights ([9–11]). The judgment clarified that the servitude was rooted in the right to the fountain's full benefit, thus rejecting the negative servitude classification.

### 3. **Knowledge Doctrine**
Cillie's assertion of ignorance regarding unregistered servitudes was dismissed, relying on the principle that servitudes obtained through prescription do not pander to the doctrine of notice, as established in prior cases. The court noted that Cillie's acquisition did not mitigate the reality of servitude already established ([12–13]).

### 4. **Water Extraction**
In determining whether Cillie's actions in extracting water impacted the fountain, the court validated the previous ruling that his extraction could indeed diminish the fountain's discharge, thereby interfering with Geldenhuys’ rights as servitude holder ([14–19]). The court also emphasized that the rights of the owner of the servient tenement must respect those of the servitude holder and that any activity by Cillie to extract water that could affect the fountain's output breached servitude conditions.

## C - Conclusion ([20–21])

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that:

1. **Cillie's Water Rights**: The appellant's claims surrounding his entitlement to use groundwater in a way that affected the fountain were unfounded. His extraction activities were deemed to infringe upon Geldenhuys's established servitude rights.

2. **Servitudes Confirmed**: The application of servitude (positive) was upheld, extending Geldenhuys's rights to utilize the fountain completely. The appeal partially succeeded: the servitudes' registration was affirmed, while the interdict prohibiting Cillie from extracting water was overturned ([20–21]).

3. **Costs Ordered**: The court ordered costs against Geldenhuys, recognizing that while he successfully established rights concerning servitude, it was deemed unjust to penalize Cillie for actions related to the unregistered servitude.

This decision is significant in clarifying the operation of prescription concerning servitudes, the nature of servitudes, and the validity of claimants’ actions in the utilization and management of water resources in property law.
