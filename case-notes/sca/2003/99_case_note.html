Supreme Court of Appeal 2003

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

This appeal arises from a decision of the South Eastern Cape Local Division of the High Court regarding the eviction of the appellants, a group of twelve individuals, from privately owned land, specifically erven 113 to 128 inclusive in Lorraine, Port Elizabeth ([1], [2]). The appellants had constructed several shacks on this land without obtaining the necessary consent from the Port Elizabeth Municipality (respondent) or the property owners, asserting, however, that permission was granted by a previous owner ([4], [12]).

The respondent initiated eviction proceedings under Section 6(1) of the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (the Act), after establishing that the appellants lacked permission to occupy the land, and despite requests from property owners for them to vacate, they refused ([2], [3], [5]). A High Court judge found the appellants’ occupation unlawful; this decision remained uncontested on appeal ([5]).

The appellants contest that their arrangements for alternative land were inadequate. They agreed to vacate but insisted on being provided with suitable alternative accommodation, which the respondent resisted, citing its comprehensive housing development program that did not include immediate relief for individuals in situations such as that of the appellants ([10], [12], [13], [19]).

I – Issues

The central legal issue pertains to whether it was just and equitable to grant an eviction order under Section 6(1) of the Act, specifically considering the relevant factors outlined in Section 6(3). This involves examining if the court should consider only the factors listed in Section 6(3) of the Act (such as the circumstances of occupation, duration of residence, and availability of alternative accommodation), or whether additional relevant circumstances may be taken into account ([7], [18]). Further, it questions whether the availability of adequate alternative land impacts the justness of the eviction ([18]).

R – Rules

The case is grounded in several key provisions of the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998. Notably, Section 6(1) allows an organ of state (in this case, the municipality) to seek eviction under specific conditions of fairness and public interest ([1]).

The court must consider:

  • The circumstances under which the unlawful occupier occupied the land
  • The duration of residence on the property
  • The availability of suitable alternative accommodation ([7]).

Furthermore, the court must determine that it is just and equitable to grant an eviction order, a principle emphasized by the court in various precedents, including Cape Killarney Property Investment (Pty) Ltd v Mahamba [2001] 4 All SA 479 (SCA) and P E Municipality v Peoples Dialogue on Land and Shelter [2001] All SA 381 (E) ([6], [18]).

A – Application

The court analyzed the merits of the eviction based on the statutory requirements of the Act. It agreed with the interpretation that while Section 6(3) delineates certain factors, it does not exhaustively limit the considerations of the court, which may also account for broader circumstances of relevance ([8], [9]).

The appellant's history of residence—some for up to ten years—coupled with their dire need for security of tenure, emphasized the importance of considering alternative accommodation ([17], [19]). The court concluded that the failure to provide suitable alternative accommodation could render the eviction procedurally and substantively unjust. Despite the municipality's housing development plan, this could not remedy the immediate plight of those facing eviction ([12], [19]).

In regard to the evictees' claim that relocation to Walmer Township presented challenges—namely overcrowding and high crime—the court highlighted that the subjective views of appellants were not sufficient grounds to deny an eviction order but pointed out that the absence of assurance for their security of tenure in any alternative settlement should be a significant concern ([19], [20]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, indicating that the lower court erred in granting the eviction order without adequately accounting for the implications of the appellants' living situation and their need for alternative accommodation. The court found that the requirement for alternative housing, coupled with the social realities of the appellants’ circumstances, made the eviction order unjust and inequitable under the Act ([21]).

The order of the lower court was set aside, effectively dismissing the municipality's application for eviction. This judgment endorses the notion that in eviction matters involving vulnerable individuals, the state has a constitutional obligation to consider not only legal technicalities but also social justice principles, reaffirming the necessity for housing and shelter in alignment with the constitutional mandate for adequate housing access ([18], [19]).

This case is a pivotal judicial interpretation of the balance between property rights, the need for public interest considerations, and the state’s social obligations. The ruling emphasizes the importance of addressing immediate housing needs while deliberating eviction proceedings, spotlighting the continuing struggle over land rights and housing in South Africa.
