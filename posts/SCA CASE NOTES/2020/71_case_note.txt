# FIRAC Case Note: Monyepao v Ledwaba and Others (No. 2) [2020] ZASCA 71

### F – Facts

In **Monyepao v Ledwaba and Others (No. 2) (Case no 1368/18)**, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) dealt with a matter concerning the costs associated with a largely irrelevant record submitted by the parties during the appeal process ([para 1]). The case originated in the Limpopo Division of the High Court, Polokwane, where a provisional order concerning costs was made on 27 May 2020. The record of the proceedings comprised 544 pages, of which only 147 pages were considered relevant ([para 2]).

The initial order specified that attorneys could not charge any fees corresponding to the irrelevant portions of the record. The parties were given an opportunity to submit representations regarding this order. The first respondent had complied with the directive, whereas the appellant failed to do so ([para 3]).

The issue arose particularly concerning the responsibilities of the attorneys involved. The appellant’s attorney was criticized for submitting a defective record, suggesting negligence in familiarizing himself with applicable court rules ([para 4]). The first respondent’s attorney argued that fees for perusing the entire record, including its irrelevant parts, should be permissible, claiming that such scrutiny was necessary for preparing the appeal ([para 5]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issues delineated in this case include:

1. **Cost Allocation**: Whether attorneys on both sides could charge fees related to the perusal of irrelevant parts of the record ([para 6]).

2. **Responsibility of Legal Representatives**: The extent to which attorneys are accountable for submitting comprehensive and relevant records in accordance with procedural requirements ([para 4]).

3. **Equity in Costs Recovery**: To what degree should an attorney be permitted to recover costs for work deemed unnecessary due to the provision of a defective record by another attorney ([para 6]).

### R – Rules

The court drew upon established principles of equity in relation to costs, notably that parties should not pay for irrelevant work. The relevant legal rules applied include:

- **Civil Procedure Rules**: Precise adherence to the procedural rules governing appeals was emphasized, particularly concerning the necessity of submitting relevant documentation.
  
- **Cost Orders in Civil Litigation**: Traditionally, parties bear the costs of litigation, but courts reserve the right to regulate costs where irrational expenditures occur, in this case, concerning the review of irrelevant documents ([para 8]).

These principles underscore not only the sanctity of procedural correctness but also the assurance that parties are not unduly penalized or enriched through the faults of their representatives.

### A – Application

The SCA determined that both the appellant’s and first respondent’s attorneys had failed to conduct due diligence concerning the contents of the record ([para 4]). The record included multiple irrelevant documents that clearly strayed from the issues at hand, warranting chastisement of the attorneys for their negligence.

The court articulated that the first respondent’s attorney, despite the appellant’s attorney being primarily at fault, should not allow the former to claim costs for reviewing inconsequential materials. The judgment highlighted that the attorney had a duty to discern the relevance of documents, and if it was apparent that many parts of the record were unnecessary, claiming a fee for them would not be justified ([para 6–7]).

Ultimately, the SCA reinforced that only the relevant components of the record would justify their associated costs, and since most documents were found to be irrelevant, the attorneys could not rightfully charge for their review ([para 8]).

### C – Conclusion

The SCA ultimately upheld the provisional order regarding costs, affirming that attorneys for both parties would not be permitted to claim fees for perusing the irrelevant portions of the record. This position reinforces the principle that legal representatives must ensure that filings are accurate, relevant, and within the parameters expected by the court.

The court’s order finalizing paragraph 3.1 of the earlier decision signals a clear stance against the recovery of costs for unnecessary work, promoting accountability among legal practitioners and encouraging adherence to procedural standards ([para 9]). This judgment not only clarifies cost obligations in civil procedures but also serves as a cautionary tale for attorneys regarding the importance of diligence in the preparation of appellate records.

---

This case serves to underscore the broader implications of procedural compliance in litigation, further establishing the principle that inefficiency or negligence by legal representatives can lead to repercussions affecting costs and professional accountability.
