Ndleve v Pretoria Society of Advocates [2016] ZACC 29

The case, Ndleve v Pretoria Society of Advocates, centers on an application by Ralph Patrick Ndleve, who sought to invalidate the High Court's decision to strike him from the roll of advocates due to allegations of malpractice and theft ([1]).

Constitutional Court 2016 [2016] ZACC 29

F – Facts

The case, Ndleve v Pretoria Society of Advocates, centers on an application by Ralph Patrick Ndleve, who sought to invalidate the High Court's decision to strike him from the roll of advocates due to allegations of malpractice and theft ([1]). The High Court had previously issued its order on 12 June 2013 following multiple complaints against Ndleve regarding his practice as an advocate from 2006 to 2013. These complaints included allegations of taking instructions directly from lay clients without proper attorney involvement, accepting payments without an attorney's mediation, and misappropriating funds intended for clients' creditors ([2]).

After being struck off the roll, Ndleve attempted to appeal the High Court’s judgment, arguing he had not been afforded a fair trial and had been denied the opportunity to present a supplementary affidavit or cross-examine witnesses ([3]). Despite confessing to theft, his appeal was denied at both the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal levels ([3]). This case represents the fifth attempt by Ndleve to appeal the High Court’s order, reflecting a pattern of litigation devoid of merit ([4]).

I – Issues

The principal issue before the Constitutional Court was whether Ndleve's application for leave to appeal should be granted, despite his prior unsuccessful appeals and the clear evidence of unethical conduct ([5]). There were sub-issues regarding the implications of his continued practice as an advocate after being struck from the roll and whether the Pretoria Society of Advocates had a duty to act against Ndleve's practices ([10]).

R – Rules

The Court relied on several legal principles and precedents, including:

  1. Professional Conduct Standards: Advocates are held to high ethical standards; unethical conduct justifies striking an advocate from the roll ([10]).
  2. Legal Precedent: As noted in Van der Berg v General Council of the Bar of South Africa [2007] ZASCA 16, the purpose of striking an advocate from the roll is primarily to protect the public rather than to punish the individual advocate ([12]).
  3. Duty to Uphold Legal Standards: The Society of Advocates has a statutory duty to ensure ethical practice and to act if a member is deemed unfit to practice, as indicated in relevant cases ([14]).

A – Application

In applying the law to the facts, the Court emphasized the serious nature of Ndleve's continued practice after his disbarment. The Court noted with concern that he had continued to represent clients even after being struck from the roll, rendering any appearances unlawful and jeopardizing the integrity of ongoing legal proceedings ([9]). The Court dismissed Ndleve's claims of being denied a fair hearing, pointing out that he had previously provided similar evidence which had been deemed insufficient in earlier applications ([6]-[9]).

The Justices made it evident that the repeated applications lodged by Ndleve appeared to be a tactic to delay the enforcement of the High Court’s order, a conduct that undermined the court's authority and ethical standards ([7]). The Court indicated that Ndleve's actions could border on contempt of court, further illustrating the gravity of the matter ([10]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court concluded by dismissing Ndleve’s application for leave to appeal unanimously. The judgment highlighted the importance of maintaining ethical standards within the legal profession, and it imposed a duty on the Society of Advocates to take appropriate measures to prevent Ndleve from unlawfully practicing law ([14]). This case underscores the necessity for legal practitioners to adhere to ethical obligations and the legal repercussions of failing to do so, which serve both to uphold the integrity of the profession and to safeguard the public interest. The Court's ruling reinforces the principle that repeated, meritless litigation undermines the judicial process and may lead to sanctions against the litigant.

This case serves as a notable reminder of the important role that professional conduct and ethics play in the legal profession and the consequences that advocates may face for failing to comply with these standards.
