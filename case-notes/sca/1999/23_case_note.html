Frederic Johannes Badenhorst & Others v The Surveyor-General, Cape Town [1999] ZASCA 11

The appellants sought an order declaring the respondent's actions unlawful, which the court a quo dismissed. They appealed, with leave granted following the dismissal ([1]–[2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 1999 [1999] ZASCA 11

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The appellants, Frederic Johannes Badenhorst and others, are trustees of the Schuster's River Trust No 1, claiming ownership of Remainder of Erf 766 Scarborough. Approximately in May 1921, the Surveyor-General approved a general plan (S91) for the Schoesters Kraal Township, which included Erf 766 among 16 lots. Subsequently, by 16 January 1996, the respondent, the Surveyor-General, approved subdivisional diagrams for several of the lots, renumbering them from 664 to 670 to 935 to 940. However, it applied a caveat against the registration of transfers of these erven, asserting that the original general plan (S91) was replaced by another plan (S101) which omitted Erf 766. This led to a prohibition on subdivision under the Land Use Planning Ordinance 15 of 1985.

The appellants sought an order declaring the respondent's actions unlawful, which the court a quo dismissed. They appealed, with leave granted following the dismissal ([1]–[2]).

I – Issues

The central legal question concerns whether the respondent's actions to apply a caveat against transfers and assert the cancellation of plan S91 concerning Erf 766 were lawful. In particular, it must be determined whether the initial approval of S91 allowed for Erf 766 to be subdivided without violation of subsequent regulations ([9]–[12]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal rules stem primarily from the Land Use Planning Ordinance 15 of 1985, specifically sections 23(1) and 23(2), which govern land subdivision rights. It states that subdivision can only occur following proper application routes unless exempted by the relevant authority ([2]–[4]; [17]).
Additionally, the Townships Ordinance of 1934 and its predecessors are relevant due to their historical framework governing township establishment and land subdivision, which includes considerations of valid planning processes and governmental approvals ([4]–[8]).

A – Application

The court delved into the complexities of whether S91 had been effectively cancelled by S101 and analyzed the implications of various documents. It noted that the prior approval of S91 indicated that Erf 766 had undergone proper subdivision. However, the respondent argued that both S91 and subsequent approvals had been effectively replaced or cancelled, leaving no remaining legal basis for subdivision rights concerning Erf 766 ([11]–[12]).

A significant aspect of the court's reasoning involved examining the circumstantial evidence indicating the intent of the original landowners (Seeton and Le Sueur) concerning the exclusion of Erf 766 from S101. The judges deduced that there was no compelling reason for its omission if the landowners intended it to remain a part of Schoesters Kraal Township. This formed the crux of the court’s analysis, as it suggested an intended partial cancellation, evidenced by the precise language used in legal documents and patterns of land transfer ([13]–[17]).

The argument concerning the need for court consent under section 30(2) of the Land Survey Act was evaluated. The court determined that the amendments of the relevant laws over time did not necessitate a court order for the intended cancellation of general plans within the specific context of the case ([18]–[19]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, concluding that the evidence did indeed support the respondent's position that S91 had been effectively cancelled concerning Erf 766. The court affirmed that the cancellation did not require court sanction, noting the lack of evidence from the appellants to contradict the circumstantial evidence of intent regarding S101's approval.

Thus, the court's holding established that the owner had appropriately abandoned potential subdivision rights by not including Erf 766 in the later plan, allowing the Surveyor-General’s actions to stand. The case reaffirms the complexities involved in township planning law and the significance of documentary evidence in establishing land rights ([19]).
