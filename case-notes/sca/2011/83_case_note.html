James Nkosi v The State (411/10) [2011] ZASCA 83

During the robbery attempt on the busy N12 highway, the gang not only pointed weapons at Fidelity employees but also shot at passing motorists and engaged with police in a shootout, resulting in the deaths of two gang members and serious injuries to another ([2]-[3]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2011 [2011] ZASCA 83

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case concerns an appeal against convictions and sentences following an attempted armed robbery incident that occurred on September 9, 2004. The first appellant, James Nkosi, and the second appellant, Zweli Albert Mthethwa, were charged with multiple crimes stemming from a foiled cash-in-transit heist involving Fidelity Cash Management Services. This robbery was executed by a gang in a blue Toyota Tazz, which had been stolen from a driver the night before ([1]-[4]).

During the robbery attempt on the busy N12 highway, the gang not only pointed weapons at Fidelity employees but also shot at passing motorists and engaged with police in a shootout, resulting in the deaths of two gang members and serious injuries to another ([2]-[3]). Both appellants were arrested after police pursuits, with the first appellant apprehended while driving a Mazda 626 allegedly belonging to the second appellant, who was also captured at the crime scene ([6]-[14]).

Initially, both appellants pleaded not guilty. The lower court acquitted them of several charges, including robbery, but convicted them on counts including attempted robbery, attempted murder, and unlawful possession of firearms, resulting in a combined sentence of 22 years imprisonment for each ([5], [20]). Following their convictions, the appellants were granted leave to appeal.

I – Issues

The appeal raised two principal issues:

  1. Discharge Application: Whether the court below erred by refusing to grant the first appellant’s discharge at the close of the state’s case, violating his constitutional rights against self-incrimination ([15], [24]).
  2. Convictions and Sentences: The appropriateness of the convictions and sentences meted out to the appellants under the prevailing circumstances, particularly focusing on the evidence against the first appellant ([20], [34]).

R – Rules

The court relied on various legal principles, notably:

  • Section 174 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977: This provision allows for the discharge of an accused when the state has failed to make a prima facie case. The court highlighted factual inadequacies in the evidence against the first appellant, citing that the state had not established a reasonable basis for conviction ([25]-[26]).
  • Section 10 and 12 of the Constitution of South Africa: These sections ensure the rights to dignity and freedom from torture or cruel treatment, outlining the fundamental rights governing fair trial principles ([25]).
  • Case Law Precedents: The court referenced S v Lubaxa and reiterated that a dismissal should occur in instances where no evidence against the accused exists without requiring them to testify ([25]).

A – Application

In relation to the first appellant, the court found significant discrepancies in the descriptions and accounts provided by state witnesses regarding the Mazda involved in the incident. Witnesses variably described the vehicle as "white," "gold," and "light brown," contributing to reasonable doubt as to whether the appellant's vehicle was involved in the crime. The court emphasized that there was a lack of substantive evidence directly linking the first appellant to the criminal conduct, noting inconsistencies particularly in the police's testimonies regarding the pursuit and identification of the Mazda ([21]-[23]).

The refusal of the lower court to consider his application for discharge was deemed an improper exercise of discretion. Therefore, the appellate court concluded that by denying this application, the first appellant's right to a fair trial was inherently compromised ([24], [26]).

Contrarily, regarding the second appellant, the court upheld the findings of the lower court. It emphasized the second appellant’s presence at the crime scene, being wounded by gunfire, and testimonies that corroborated his active involvement in the robbery attempt. The court found his evasiveness during testimony and the improbability of his explanations rendered them unreliable, confirming convictions based on the evident link to the planned criminal activity ([28]-[33]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled as follows:

  1. The conviction and sentences for the first appellant were set aside due to the lack of evidence at the end of the state’s case. His discharge at this point would have been justified based on the significant evidentiary holes and inconsistencies presented ([1], [37]).
  2. The appeal for the second appellant was dismissed, adhering to the lower court’s findings that substantiated his conviction as a participant in the robbery, which was aided by substantial eyewitness testimony and corroborative evidence ([36]).

The case demarcates critical considerations in criminal procedure regarding the rights of the accused versus evidential burdens, highlighting the necessity for rigor in establishing beyond reasonable doubt the culpability of individuals in serious crimes. The outcome secures the precedent that improper denial of discharge can materially undermine the integrity of judicial processes in South Africa.
