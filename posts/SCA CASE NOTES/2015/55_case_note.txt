Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

### FIRAC Case Note: Atholl Developments v The Valuation Appeal Board for the City of Johannesburg [2015] ZASCA 55

#### F – Facts
The appellant, **Atholl Developments (Pty) Ltd** (Atholl), is the lessee under a 99-year registered long lease of Erven 482 and 483, Illovo Extension 4, Johannesburg. This property, opposite the Wanderers Cricket Stadium, is owned by the **Wanderers Club**. Atholl constructed a hotel, trading as the **Protea Hotel Wanderers**, on the property and is responsible for paying the relevant municipal rates ([1]).

The dispute arose from a specific valuation and assessment of the rates levied by the **City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality** (the City), leading Atholl to appeal to the **Valuation Appeal Board** (the Appeal Board), a statutory entity established under the **Municipal Property Rates Act 6 of 2004** (MPRA) ([1][2]).

On June 13, 2012, the Appeal Board overturned the City's valuation, deeming the combined value of the property to be approximately **R308 million**. Dissatisfied with the Board's decision, Atholl filed a review application in the **Gauteng Local Division, Johannesburg**, seeking to set aside the Appeal Board's decision and also requested that the matter be remitted for reconsideration ([2][3]).

The decision from Judge Vally was favorable to Atholl, as he set aside the Appeal Board’s decision and remitted it for reconsideration. Atholl then sought leave to appeal, which was granted by Vally, permitting it to challenge specific paragraphs from his judgment ([3][4]).

#### I – Issues
The central issues addressed by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) were:
1. Whether an appeal lay from the reasons for a judgment rather than the substantive order ([4][11]).
2. The implications of Judge Vally’s findings on the binding nature of his statements on the Appeal Board upon remittance ([5][6]).
3. Whether the appeal had any practical effect, given the order of remittance provided by Vally, which is standard procedure in administrative reviews ([8][11]).

#### R – Rules
The SCA considered several legal principles and precedents to address these issues:
- **Judgment Appealability**: The court cited *Administrator, Cape v Ntshwaqela* [1990] 1 SA 705 (A), establishing that appeals are limited to substantive orders rather than the accompanying reasons for judgment ([8]).
- **Nature of Binding Decisions**: The court referred to *True Motives 84 (Pty) Ltd v Mahdi* [2009] 4 SA 153 (SCA), which clarified that statements made in incidental remarks during a judgment do not constitute binding precedent for future reconsideration by the original decision-maker ([6]).
- **Standard of Remittance**: The court reaffirmed that remittal is a common outcome in administrative reviews, a procedure supported by *Gauteng Gambling Board v Silverstar Development Ltd* [2005] 4 SA 67 (SCA) ([5]).

#### A – Application
The SCA carefully analyzed whether Atholl's appeal was properly constituted. It determined that since the appeal was directed at the reasons provided by Vally rather than the substantive order – which had successfully set aside the Appeal Board's decision – it did not satisfy the requirements for an appeal under **Rule 49(4)**, which necessitates specifying the part of the judgment appealed against ([10][11]).

The court emphasized that any statements made by Vally in the contested paragraphs did not impact the substance of the remitted decision, allowing the Appeal Board sufficient discretion in considering the valuation anew. Reference to precedent demonstrated that the appeal’s nature—as a challenge against reasoning rather than decision—rendered it improperly before the court ([9][11]).

Additionally, the SCA addressed the issue of costs, noting that the first respondent, the Appeal Board, was compelled to be present due to the appellant’s insistence on pursuing a flawed appeal. Given that the first respondent did not oppose the application for leave to appeal based on a sound legal position, the SCA concluded that the appellant should bear the costs, including charges for two counsel ([12]).

#### C – Conclusion
The SCA ultimately struck the appeal off the roll, citing lack of proper grounds for appeal against reasons rather than the substantive order ([11]). The court’s holding reaffirmed the principle that appeals focus on orders rather than reasons, thereby upholding procedural integrity in judicial review. Consequently, the implications of this ruling reinforce the necessity for litigants to structure appeals accurately around substantive issues, ensuring legal correctness in the pursuit of administrative justice ([13]).

This case highlights the importance of understanding procedural nuances in administrative law, particularly relating to the nature of appeals and the binding interpretations of judicial statements.
