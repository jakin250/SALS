Supreme Court of Appeal

Malema v Rawula (139/2020) [2021] ZASCA 88

[2] On April 5, 2019, Rawula published a controversial Facebook post alleging financial misconduct within the EFF, specifically accusing Malema and his deputy, Floyd Shivambu, of corrupt practices and misappropriation of party funds.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 88

F – Facts

[1] Julius Sello Malema, the appellant, is the President of the Economic Freedom Fighters (EFF), a prominent political party in South Africa, and a Member of Parliament. The respondent, Thembinkosi Rawula, formerly held influential positions within the EFF, including membership on the Central Command Team and Chairperson of the National Disciplinary Committee, before resigning in April 2019 ([1]–[2]).

[2] On April 5, 2019, Rawula published a controversial Facebook post alleging financial misconduct within the EFF, specifically accusing Malema and his deputy, Floyd Shivambu, of corrupt practices and misappropriation of party funds. The post not only condemned the leadership's actions but also drew on historical parallels to illustrate perceived breaches of accountability and democratic principles. It further accused the EFF’s leadership of utilizing corrupt funding from VBS Mutual Bank ([5]).

[3] Following the publication of the Facebook post, which garnered extensive media coverage, Malema sought an urgent interdict and a declaration from the Eastern Cape High Court that the post was defamatory. He sought to restrain Rawula from further publishing defamatory statements and claimed damages of R1 million. The high court dismissed his application, leading to this appeal ([3]–[4]).

I – Issues

[4] The central legal issues in this case involved:

  • Whether the statements made by Rawula in his Facebook post were defamatory and unlawful.
  • Whether the defences of truth, public interest, or fair comment could be established by the respondent.
  • The appropriateness of pursuing defamation claims via motion proceedings rather than through traditional litigation, particularly concerning the evidentiary burden and the nature of the statements alleged to be defamatory ([11]).

R – Rules

[5] The court applied established principles related to defamation law, including:

  • The requirement for the claimant to prove a clear right that has been invaded, as well as establishing the wrongful and intentional nature of the publication ([31]).
  • The defences available in defamation actions, notably that of truth and public interest, which must be substantively proven by the defendant ([33]) .
  • The Plascon-Evans rule, which stipulates that where there is a dispute of fact on the papers, the court must accept the version of facts presented by the respondent if such version is plausible ([25]).
  • Previous judgments, including Heilbron v Blignaut and Hix Networking Technologies, which discussed the procedural appropriateness of motion proceedings in defamation claims ([6]).

A – Application

[6] The court analyzed the context and content of the Facebook post, noting that while Malema's reputation was indeed challenged, the respondent had a potential justification based on his claims of inside knowledge and privileged information. The critical aspect involved determining whether Rawula could sustainably contest the notion of unlawfulness by establishing the truth of his statements and the public interest in disclosing such information ([12]).

[7] Rawula's statement was grounded on his prior membership in the CCT, which provided him access to information about EFF's financial dealings. He contended that his allegations surrounding the financial mismanagement and the use of VBS funding were based on his observations as a former leader within the EFF. The high court framed the need for evidentiary support for his claims, focusing on whether Rawula could demonstrate that his public interest defense was grounded in justiciable truth ([35]).

[8] The high court determined that Rawula had laid a sufficient foundation for his defense, positing that the claim for an injunction was misguided given that the respondent had plausible grounds for believing his statements represented the truth, and he had taken steps consistent with the public interest ([17]–[20]).

C – Conclusion

[9] The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court’s decision to dismiss Malema's appeal, reinforcing that the defendant had successfully established a plausible defense underscored by his position and the information to which he claimed access. The court emphasized that Malema did not convincingly disprove the respondent's assertions regarding accountability within the EFF nor sufficiently challenge the factual bases that could uphold Rawula's defenses of truth and public interest ([30]).

[10] The ruling indicates the judiciary’s recognition of the importance of political accountability, particularly in scenarios involving public figures and representative institutions. The decision serves as a precedent for future cases related to the intersection of defamation law, freedom of speech, and the necessity for leaders to maintain transparency regarding financial matters, particularly in politically charged environments ([66]–[68]).

This case is significant not only for its contributions to South African defamation law but also for its implications on the public's right to know about the activities of those in power, upholding the principles of accountability and transparency in governance.
