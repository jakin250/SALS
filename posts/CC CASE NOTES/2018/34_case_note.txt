Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: Thwala v S [2018] ZACC 34

**Court**: Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Coram**: Mogoeng CJ, Zondo DCJ, Basson AJ, Cameron J, Dlodlo AJ, Goliath AJ, Khampepe J, Mhlantla J, Petse AJ, Theron J  
**Judgment by**: Froneman J  
**Decided on**: 27 September 2018

## F – Facts

The applicant, Oscar Vusi Thwala, had been convicted in the High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Division, Pretoria for **two counts of abduction, three counts of rape**, and **two counts of possession of a firearm** (collectively referred to as "the convictions") ([1]). The High Court sentenced him to **three life sentences** under section 52(1) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act (Minimum Sentences Act) for the rape charges, plus **nine years imprisonment** for the remaining charges ([1]).

Thwala sought to have his conviction and sentence overturned and requested to present new evidence from DNA tests conducted post-conviction. The basis of his appeal included five claims of substantive unfairness, namely that:

1. The High Court did not consider DNA evidence that would be available within 15 weeks.
2. The trial was conducted in Afrikaans, which neither he nor his representative understood.
3. He was not informed of the Minimum Sentences Act's applicability until sentencing.
4. The judge failed to consider substantial and compelling circumstances for deviating from the minimum sentence.
5. The judge’s interventions during trial violated his right to a fair trial ([3]).

This was the third time the Constitutional Court engaged with Thwala's case, which dated back to 2003. His previous appeals were unsuccessful primarily due to procedural complexities and being unrepresented ([5]–[7]).

## I – Issues

The central legal questions arose from Thwala’s contention that his right to a fair trial under section 35(3) of the Constitution was violated, as evidenced by the procedural failings of the trial ([4]). The relevant issues included:

1. Should the court relax the doctrine of **res judicata** given Thwala's claims of procedural unfairness and newly presented DNA evidence?
2. Was Thwala’s conviction—considering the alleged unfair trial proceedings—justifiable?
3. Did the High Court adequately inform Thwala of his rights and the applicability of the Minimum Sentences Act during sentencing, thereby affecting the legality of the sentence imposed?

## R – Rules

The ruling referenced several legal principles and precedents:

1. **Section 35(3)** of the Constitution guarantees the accused the right to a fair trial ([4]), including being informed of charges and being tried in a language understood.
2. The **doctrine of res judicata** prohibits the re-examination of a case that has already been finally determined, barring exceptional circumstances ([10]).
3. In *S v Molaudzi*, the Constitutional Court suggested that the doctrine could be relaxed if the interests of justice warranted reconsideration ([11]).
4. In sentencing, proper procedural knowledge regarding the Minimum Sentences Act and an individual’s circumstances are paramount, as established in *Rammoko v Director of Public Prosecutions* ([22]).

## A – Application

In dismissing the appeal, the Court engaged each claim of procedural unfairness asserted by Thwala:

1. **DNA Evidence**: The Court clarified that while the newly presented DNA evidence might not have been considered at trial, it was not fundamentally exculpatory in the context of Thwala’s involvement in gang rape, as established by testimonies and other evidence ([18]).

2. **Language Barrier**: The Court found the language issue to be immaterial because Thwala was represented by counsel, and the proceedings were interpreted into a comprehensible language for him ([19]).

3. **Minimum Sentences Act Notification**: The Court acknowledged that Thwala's lack of notification regarding the Minimum Sentences Act constituted a procedural issue. However, this alone did not invalidate the High Court's sentencing. The Court found no substantial mitigating circumstances or compelling reasons to deviate from the minimum sentences given the brutality of the crime ([25]).

4. **Judge's Interventions**: Regarding the trial judge's interventions, while they were noted as unfortunate, they did not refute the fairness of the trial when assessed alongside the entirety of the procedural context ([19]).

Ultimately, the Court concluded that there was insufficient evidence to substantiate a significant injustice stemming from the alleged irregularities and that the fundamental principles of finality in criminal proceedings reinforced the dismissal of Thwala’s application ([27]).

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court granted condonation for the late filing of the appeal; however, it ultimately refused leave to appeal. The Court held that the interests of justice did not warrant the relaxation of the **res judicata** principle in Thwala’s case, emphasizing that substantial justification must exist to disturb previous final judgments ([9], [12]). The implications of this finding rest on the balance between ensuring that rights to a fair trial are protected while also maintaining the integrity and finality of judicial outcomes in the interest of justice. This ruling underscores the Court's determination to uphold procedural order in the criminal justice system despite the applicant's claims of unfairness ([27]).
