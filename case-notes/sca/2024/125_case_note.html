Herold Gie & Broadhead Inc v Harris N O and Others (602/2023) [2024] ZASCA 125

The case arose from six consolidated actions where the respondents (purchasers) sought refunds for purchase prices paid for "life rights" in suites at the St Leger Retirement Hotel in Muizenberg, Cape Town, developed by St Leger Trust No IT 953/2008 (the developer).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 125

F - Facts

The case arose from six consolidated actions where the respondents (purchasers) sought refunds for purchase prices paid for "life rights" in suites at the St Leger Retirement Hotel in Muizenberg, Cape Town, developed by St Leger Trust No IT 953/2008 (the developer). All respondents were retired persons, fulfilling the definition of a "retired person" under the Housing Development Schemes for Retired Persons Act 65 of 1988 (HDSA) ([para 1],[para 2]). Each of the purchasers entered into agreements for life rights with the developer, along with a requirement to pay monthly service fees ([para 4]).

HGB, a legal practitioner, was entrusted to hold the purchase prices in its trust account until conditions mandated by the HDSA were met. In June 2009, following written authorizations from the purchasers, HGB disbursed the funds to the developer before the latter's insolvency was established in February 2016. The purchasers cancelled their agreements in October 2014 due to the developer's failure to provide necessary compliance certificates, thereby purporting to invoke their right to a refund ([paras 5-7]).

Subsequently, the purchasers claimed refunds from HGB based on a statutory provision in section 6(4) of the HDSA, asserting that, due to the developer's insolvency, they should be entitled to the return of the funds held in trust ([paras 8-9]).

I - Issues

The primary legal issue was whether the purchasers could claim refunds of the amounts entrusted to HGB under section 6(4) of the HDSA, considering the payments had been disbursed to the developer before the developer declared insolvency. Additional issues included whether the claim was subject to prescription and whether HGB could rely on the authorizations from the purchasers ([paras 12-13],[paras 30]).

R - Rules

The HDSA regulates transactions involving life rights in housing development schemes for retired persons. Notably, section 6(1) prohibits a developer from receiving consideration unless specific compliance certificates are provided ([para 22]). Specifically, section 6(4) states that if a developer becomes insolvent before such certificates are issued, any amounts held in trust must be repaid to the purchasers by the practitioner ([para 26]).

The rules concerning special pleas, particularly regarding the prescription, and the Uniform Rules of Court guidelines on exceptions were also relevant. Section 6(3) provides an exception to the prohibition on receipt of the purchase price when it is entrust to a legal practitioner for the benefit of the developer ([paras 11, 24-25]).

A - Application

The High Court initially upheld the purchasers' claims, interpreting section 6(4) in their favor and held that the provisions intended to safeguard purchasers, especially elderly individuals, from potential exploitation ([para 15]). The high court considered that the fact HGB paid the funds to the developer did not absolve the practitioner’s obligation to refund the purchasers once insolvency had occurred, thus characterizing HGB effectively as the trustee ([para 16]).

In the appeal, however, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) emphasized a narrower interpretation of section 6(4), arguing that this provision does not serve as a broad basis for claims if the funds were disbursed before the insolvency occurred. The court highlighted that the legislative intent behind the same provision was to protect elderly purchasers at risky points in housing transactions, essentially denying the existence of a direct claim against HGB when no funds remained in trust by the time of the developer's insolvency ([paras 20-30]).

The distinction between being a trust creditor versus a developer’s creditor was crucial; thus, the court ruled that since the funds were released prior to insolvency, the statutory protections were not available to the purchasers in this circumstance.

C - Conclusion

The SCA upheld the appeal and set aside the high court's ruling that favored the purchasers. It clarified that while purchasers retained rights under the HDSA, these rights would not extend to claims against HGB because the disbursement of funds occurred before the insolvency declaration. The court's decision reinforced the statutory framework intended to protect purchasers, noting that such protection is invalidated when there are no funds remaining in trust ([paras 32]).

Thus, this judgment not only clarified the interpretation of section 6(4) and the scope of liability for legal practitioners but also emphasized the importance of compliance with statutory safeguards designed for vulnerable populations in housing transactions, establishing a precedent for similar cases in the future.
