Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note: Dlamini v S (362/11) [2012] ZASCA 26

## F – Facts

The appellant, Musa Dlamini, was indicted for six counts in the Pinetown Regional Court following an armed robbery that took place on 26 April 2002. Dlamini was charged with three counts of robbery, one count of theft, and two counts of unlawful possession of a firearm and ammunition ([paras 1–3]). The robbery incident involved three women—Mrs. Burgess, Mrs. Acutt, and Mrs. Usher—who were approached by three men, including Dlamini, while they were in the driveway of Mrs. Burgess's home ([paras 5–10]). Dlamini, allegedly armed with a firearm, participated in demanding the women's possessions, including two vehicles and personal items like handbags and cellphones. The robbers fled the scene in both vehicles after the women complied with their demands ([paras 10–11]).

The regional magistrate convicted Dlamini on all counts, including the firearm and ammunition charges, and imposed a cumulative sentence of 45 years’ imprisonment, which included 15 years for the robberies and consecutive terms for the other charges. The appeal against the convictions was partially granted by the High Court, which ruled that the charge of theft from 1999 was unproven but upheld the convictions for robbery and unlawful possession ([paras 31–34]).

## I – Issues

The central legal issue in this case revolves around whether there was a duplication of convictions for robbery. Specifically, the court needed to determine whether Dlamini's actions constituted multiple counts of robbery or a single offence based on a ‘continuous transaction’ carried out with a common intent ([paras 16–22]). The appeal also raised concerns regarding the appropriateness of the cumulative sentence given Dlamini’s personal circumstances and potential for rehabilitation ([paras 28–41]).

## R – Rules

The primary legal rule at play is the principle against duplication of convictions, which is intended to prevent an accused from being penalized multiple times for what is effectively a single offence. This principle is embedded in the right to a fair trial as protected under the Constitution ([para 16]). Specific tests have been established for determining duplication, including:

1. **Single Intent / Continuous Transaction Test**: This test assesses whether multiple actions were executed with a singular intent constituting one continuous transaction ([para 19]).
2. **Evidence Test**: This determines whether proving one crime inherently involves proving another ([para 20]).

Further statutory guidance comes from Section 83 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which allows for multiple charges to be brought together when the nature of the offences is unclear. However, it remains the court's role to ensure there is no undue prejudice to the accused by avoiding duplicative convictions ([paras 17–18]).

## A – Application

The court, led by Cachalia JA, scrutinized the nature of the robbery charges against Dlamini, comparing them to precedent cases including *S v Maneli* ([paras 21–24]). The finding was that Dlamini’s involvement in the robbery of the three women involved threats of violence directed at all three at the same time. This pointed toward a unified act rather than distinct offences. The reasoning established that since all three victims were threatened simultaneously and had property taken from them through a common purpose, only one count of robbery should have been pursued. 

Cachalia JA emphasized that the magistrate's failure to consider the possibility of duplication constituted a significant misdirection, which warranted altering the sentence imposed ([paras 28–41]). Regarding sentencing, while the court acknowledged the seriousness of the crime and the terror inflicted upon the victims, it also considered Dlamini’s personal circumstances and lack of prior offences, which justified a lesser sentence than originally imposed ([paras 39–41]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal against the conviction on counts 2 and 3, holding that there was no wrongful duplication of convictions in the context used by the trial court ([para 57]). However, it upheld the appeal against the sentence, reducing the cumulative sentence from 45 to 17 years’ imprisonment, after accounting for Dlamini's months in custody awaiting his trial ([paras 36–42]). The correction of the sentence reflects a balanced consideration of justice for the victims while acknowledging Dlamini’s potential for rehabilitation and addressing the issues of fairness inherent in sentencing practices.
