Supreme Court of Appeal

Southern Sky Hotel and Leisure (Pty) Ltd and Others v Southern Sky Food Enterprises (Pty) Ltd ([2022] ZASCA 134)

Southern Sky Hotel and Leisure, trading as Hans Merensky Hotel and Spa, was experiencing financial difficulties, culminating in several winding-up applications launched by creditors due to its failure to meet financial obligations under rental pool agreements ([4]–[6]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 134)

F – Facts

The case involves Southern Sky Hotel and Leisure (Pty) Ltd (in liquidation) and its provisional liquidators, who are appealing a decision of the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg, which declared an agreement for the sale of the company’s immovable property invalid ([1]–[2]).

Southern Sky Hotel and Leisure, trading as Hans Merensky Hotel and Spa, was experiencing financial difficulties, culminating in several winding-up applications launched by creditors due to its failure to meet financial obligations under rental pool agreements ([4]–[6]). The company had previously been placed under business rescue proceedings on 8 June 2016, which suspended liquidation proceedings per section 131(6) of the Companies Act 71 of 2008 ([7]–[9]).

In February 2020, the Gauteng Division of the High Court ultimately placed the company under final liquidation, which led to the appointment of the provisional liquidators ([9]–[10]). By November 2020, the liquidators resolved to auction the company’s immovable property ([10]). However, in December 2020, a creditor filed a business rescue application that was set for a hearing on 11 March 2021, shortly after the planned auction dates of 23–24 February 2021 ([11]).

The liquidators proceeded with the auction despite the pending business rescue application, ultimately selling the property to Southern Sky Food Enterprises (Pty) Ltd ([16]–[18]). Southern Sky Food Enterprises later sought to declare the sale agreement invalid, claiming it violated section 131(6) due to the existence of the business rescue application ([18]).

I – Issues

The key legal issues in this appeal are:

  1. Was there a valid business rescue application as prescribed by section 131(6) of the Companies Act?
  2. Was the sale agreement executed by the liquidators, which took place during the business rescue application, invalid according to section 131(6) of the Companies Act? ([19]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal authorities revolve around section 131(6) of the Companies Act, which outlines the effects of pending business rescue proceedings on previously commenced liquidation proceedings. This section indicates:

  • Liquidation proceedings are suspended upon the filing of a business rescue application until the court adjudicates on the application or the business rescue proceedings conclude ([20]).
  • The liquidation order remains effective, thus maintaining the powers of the liquidators unless the court affirms the business rescue application ([22]).

In addition, the court examined the provisions of the sale agreement, particularly clause 24, which addressed the relationship between the business rescue application and the sale ([17]).

A – Application

The Court, led by Basson AJA, undertook a thorough examination of the interpretation of section 131(6) and the interplay between ongoing liquidation proceedings and the authority of liquidators to enter into agreements during this period. The appellants' argument rested on the assertion that for the pending business rescue application to be valid and materially affect the liquidation proceedings, it had to be recognized as a legitimate application under the Companies Act ([21]).

The court presumed, without conclusively ruling, that the business rescue application complied with statutory requirements for the sake of the argument ([21]). Subsequently, the Court analyzed clause 24 of the sale agreement, concluding that its respective provisions essentially suspended any auction of the property should the business rescue application succeed ([24]). Thus, it was determined that the agreement could not be executed while the liquidation proceedings were effectively on hold due to an unanswered business rescue application.

Moreover, the Court found no textual or contextual indication in section 131(6) that would nullify the validity of the agreement outright. The powers vested in liquidators remain intact pending the ultimate adjudication of a business rescue application, emphasizing the need to protect the interests of creditors and the general public ([24]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, reversing the lower court's ruling that had declared the sale agreement invalid. The primary reason was that while the court presumes the existence of a business rescue application, it does not automatically toll the liquidators' authority to conduct sales of the company's assets. The Court reaffirmed that the liquidators acted within their powers and did not contravene the provisions of section 131(6) of the Companies Act.

The judgment significantly clarifies the interaction between liquidation and business rescue proceedings under the Companies Act, offering insight into the balance of interests between the need for financial recovery operations and the procedural rigour established in the statutory framework ([28]). Thus, the appeal was upheld with costs, including the costs of two counsel ([28]).
