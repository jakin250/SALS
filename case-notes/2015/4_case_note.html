Tebeila Institute of Leadership Education, Governance and Training v Limpopo College of Nursing and Another [2015] ZACC 4

Despite its success in overturning this exclusionary policy, the High Court issued a costs order stating that “each party shall pay its own costs,” depriving the Applicant of reimbursement for its legal expenses ([para 3]).

Constitutional Court 2015 [2015] ZACC 4

FIRAC Method

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–3])

The case involves an appeal by the Tebeila Institute of Leadership, Education, Governance and Training (the Applicant), a rural educational institution, challenging a costs order issued by the High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Division (acting as the Limpopo Division). The Applicant had previously succeeded in a constitutional challenge against the Limpopo College of Nursing's admissions policy, which required nursing students to have completed their school-leaving certificates “not more than three years ago” ([para 1]). This limitation was found to unjustly exclude potential students who had graduated longer ago, violating their right to equality under section 9 of the South African Constitution ([para 2]).

Despite its success in overturning this exclusionary policy, the High Court issued a costs order stating that “each party shall pay its own costs,” depriving the Applicant of reimbursement for its legal expenses ([para 3]). The High Court justified this decision on the grounds that the issues were of a constitutional nature, which traditionally do not favor a prevailing party in terms of cost compensation.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1, 4–6])

The primary legal issue before the Constitutional Court was whether the High Court properly applied the principles set forth in Biowatch Trust v Registrar Genetic Resources and Others ([para 4]). Specifically, this case questioned if the reasoning provided by the High Court sufficiently articulated the exceptional circumstances that justified denying the Applicant its legal costs despite a successful constitutional claim ([para 6]). The Court was required to examine whether the High Court’s ruling constituted a misinterpretation of its discretion to award costs in constitutional matters.

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 4–6, 10–12])

The rule established in Biowatch remains pivotal in shaping the legal landscape regarding costs in constitutional litigation between private parties and the state. It outlines a general principle that successful private parties should be awarded costs against state respondents, especially in cases dealing with constitutional rights ([para 4]). Moreover, the Biowatch decision emphasizes that when courts decide to deviate from this general rule, they must provide detailed and convincing reasons for doing so ([para 5]).

The Court pointed out that the High Court’s ruling appeared to misapply the Biowatch framework by denying costs to a successful plaintiff based on misconstrued principles of the law ([para 10]). This misapplication warrants a review, as decisions premised on incorrect legal reasoning may be subject to appellate correction ([para 12]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 8–9, 16–18])

Upon review, the Constitutional Court noted the significant chilling effect that adverse costs orders could have on parties pursuing constitutional claims ([paras 8–9]). The Court reiterated that potential litigants might avoid asserting constitutional rights if faced with the risk of incurring costs, thereby undermining broader societal interests in accessing justice and upholding constitutional values ([para 8]). In this case, the Applicant's ability to challenge the exclusionary policy was undoubtedly in the public interest, underscoring the importance of compensating legal costs to foster engagement in constitutional litigation.

The Court also recognized the “exceptional circumstances” that justified its intervention. It was noted that this was the first instance wherein a successful litigant had been unjustly deprived of its costs after vindicating constitutional rights, which necessitated a reaffirmation of the principles established in Biowatch ([para 16]). The applicant's fears of not being indemnified for costs were further amplified as there were no procedural missteps justifying the High Court’s adverse costs order ([para 9]).

The Constitutional Court, therefore, took the opportunity to restate the principles from Biowatch, stressing that any deviation from the established costs framework requires “carefully articulated and convincing” justification ([para 17]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 18, 1–4])

In conclusion, the Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal, finding that the High Court's costs order was flawed and set it aside ([para 18]). The Court held that costs should be awarded to the Applicant in the High Court. The judgment reiterates the necessity for the lower courts to adhere to established principles regarding costs in constitutional litigation, underpinning the overarching commitment of the judiciary to facilitate the assertion of constitutional rights without the fear of financial repercussions.

This ruling affirms the protective measures in place to encourage individuals and entities to engage in constitutional litigation, thereby enhancing access to justice in South Africa. The Tebeila case serves as a landmark decision that reiterates and emphasizes the critical nature of the costs principle in constitutional matters, potentially impacting future cases involving similar legal issues and reinforcing the need for careful judicial reasoning in costs determinations.
