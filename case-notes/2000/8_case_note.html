Dawood and Others v Minister of Home Affairs and Others (CCT 35/99) - Constitutional Court of South Africa

Constitutional Court 2000 CCT 35/99

F – Facts

The case involves three applications lodged by foreign spouses seeking to reside in South Africa pending the outcome of their immigration permit applications ([1]). The facts are as follows:

  1. Dawood Application ([5]):
  • Mr. and Mrs. Dawood were married according to Islamic law on 4 October 1997; Mr. Dawood is a South African citizen while Mrs. Dawood is a Thai national.
  • Mrs. Dawood entered South Africa in 1997 on a temporary residence permit that was extended multiple times.
  • After applying for an immigration permit, Mrs. Dawood was informed of a new non-refundable fee of R7,750, imposed by regulation, which they claimed was unconstitutional.
  1. Shalabi Application ([6]):
  • Mr. and Mrs. Shalabi married under South African civil law on 7 May 1996. Mrs. Shalabi is a South African citizen; Mr. Shalabi is an Egyptian national on a temporary residence permit.
  • Mr. Shalabi faced issues when his temporary permit expired, and he was compelled to apply for further permits, which were refused based on his residency status. They sought judicial relief arguing that the Act's requirements were unconstitutional.
  1. Thomas Application ([7]):
  • Mr. and Mrs. Thomas were married in 1994. Mr. Thomas is a citizen from St Helena without a valid temporary permit, whereas Mrs. Thomas is a South African citizen.
  • Following expiration of his temporary residence permit, Mr. Thomas could not apply for an immigration permit due to incomplete documentation and subsequently sought legal redress.

The High Court ruled in favor of the applicants, declaring that both the fee structure and the conditions under which immigration permits were issued contravened constitutional norms ([10]).

I – Issues

The core issues identified by the court were:

  1. Whether the fee imposed on foreign spouses seeking immigration permits contravenes fundamental rights enshrined in the Constitution ([3]).
  2. Whether section 25(9)(b) of the Aliens Control Act, which mandates that only those with valid temporary residence permits can have immigration applications processed while in South Africa, is constitutional ([3]).

R – Rules

The court considered the following statutory and constitutional rules:

  1. Aliens Control Act 96 of 1991:
  • Section 25(9): Conditions under which immigration permits may be granted.
  • Section 26: Rules governing temporary residence permits.
  1. Constitution of South Africa:
  • Section 9: Right to equality.
  • Section 10: Right to dignity.
  • Section 21: Right to freedom of movement.
  • Section 28: Rights of children ([3]).
  1. Judicial precedents that emphasize the importance of dignity and family unity, which the court cited in reviewing the regulations' validity ([27]).

A – Application

The court analyzed how the aforementioned rules applied to the issues at hand. It noted:

  • Impact on Dignity: The court recognized that the requirement limiting spouses to valid temporary residence permits, arising from section 25(9)(b), infringes upon the right to dignity, particularly impacting the ability for married couples to cohabit ([27]).
  • Fee Regulations: The imposition of a steep fee for applying for immigration permits was deemed unconstitutional as it placed an undue financial burden primarily affecting disadvantaged individuals, infringing their rights under sections dealing with equality and dignity ([10], [70]).
  • Legislative Void: The absence of guidance in the Act concerning what circumstances could justify refusal of temporary permits resulted in an arbitrary exercise of discretion by officials, leading to the statutory provisions being declared unconstitutional ([61], [64]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court's declaration of invalidity regarding the relevant sections of the Aliens Control Act and the fee regulations ([69]). Specifically:

  1. Section 25(9)(b) was deemed unconstitutional due to its arbitrary application and lack of protective legislative guidance, violating the rights to dignity and freedom of movement ([61]).
  2. The Court ordered that the application of invalidity be suspended for 24 months to allow Parliament to remedy the legislative shortcomings ([66]).

The implications of the ruling reaffirm the constitutional commitments to family unity and personal dignity, and the need for clear legislative criteria in immigration processes ([68]). Consequently, it serves as a significant precedent in safeguarding the rights of spouses in immigration matters under South African law.
