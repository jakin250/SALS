Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2009

# FIRAC Case Note: True Motives 84 (Pty) Limited v Mohamed Hanif Mahmoed Mahdi and Others

**Neutral Citation**: True Motives v Mahdi (543/07) [2009] ZASCA 4 (3 March 2009)  
**Coram**: Scott, Cameron, Heher, Jafta, and Combrinck JJA  
**Heard**: 28 August 2008  
**Delivered**: 3 March 2009

---

### F – Facts  
The appellant, True Motives 84 (Pty) Ltd (hereafter "True Motives"), is the registered owner of Erf 178 in Morningside Extension 17 since November 2002. The first respondent, Mohamed Hanif Mahmod Mahdi, owns the adjoining Erf 177. The second respondent, the City of Johannesburg, is the local authority responsible for enforcing the National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act 103 of 1977 ("the Act").

True Motives filed an application contesting the approval of plans for alterations to Mahdi's property, which were submitted to the City and purportedly approved on 19 June 2005. The appellant claimed that the local authority's approval violated sections 6 and 7 of the Act, arguing it would derogate from the value of its adjoining property. True Motives sought either a declaration that the approval was unlawful or a review and reversal of that decision ([paras 1–5]).

Upon noticing construction beginning in September 2004, True Motives alleged that the alterations were extensive and undesirable, including blocking sunlight, obstructing views, and causing disfigurement in the area. The appellant further asserted that it was not notified of the plans and had no opportunity to object to the approval of the plans ([paras 6–9]).

The High Court dismissed the application, suggesting the local authority was not required to afford True Motives an opportunity to object, and it concluded that the local authority properly evaluated the impact of the alterations ([paras 10–11]). True Motives appealed this decision, focusing on the interpretation of section 7 of the Act and the relevance of surrounding property value ([para 12]).

---

### I – Issues  
The core issues presented in this case include:

1. **Interpretation and Application of Statutes**: What constitutes the obligations of local authorities in determining compliance with Section 7(1) of the Act, specifically regarding property value derogation ([para 13])?
   
2. **Impact of Judicial Precedents**: Are the findings of the Constitutional Court binding on the Supreme Court of Appeal in matters interpreting the Building Regulations Act, particularly based on the judgments in Walele v City of Cape Town and Paola v Jeeva? ([paras 13, 14]).

3. **Procedural Rights**: Is there an entitlement for neighboring property owners to be notified or participate in the approval process of building plans affecting their properties? ([para 13]).

---

### R – Rules  
The legal principles primarily engaged in this case include:

1. **National Building Regulations and Building Standards Act 103 of 1977**: Section 7(1) mandates that a local authority must be satisfied that a building plan complies with all relevant laws and should not approve plans that would derogate from the value of adjoining properties ([para 7]).

2. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000**: The provisions require that administrative actions be lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair ([para 12]).

3. **Doctrine of Precedent**: Previous rulings by higher courts, particularly the Constitutional Court, must guide lower courts in decision-making unless explicitly stated otherwise ([para 13]).

4. **Interpretation of Statutory Provisions**: Courts are bound to interpret legislation in a manner that promotes the spirit and objects of the Bill of Rights, aligning with Section 39(2) of the Constitution ([para 65]).

---

### A – Application  
The court's analysis revolved around the interpretation of section 7, distinguishing between when a local authority must approve and when it may refuse approval based on property impacts. Heher JA highlighted that section 7(1)(a) concerns compliance with existing laws, while 7(1)(b)(ii) addresses potential derogation of property value. Crucially, the court ruled that the local authority must be positively satisfied that the proposed building probably will not cause harmful consequences, thereby imposing a more substantial burden on the applicant ([paras 20, 21, 59]).

The court further contextualized its decision concerning the doctrine of precedent, highlighting that decisions of the Constitutional Court (in this instance, Walele and Paola) constitute authoritative interpretations but do not establish rigid procedures or rights to object by adjacent property owners when plans comply with the applicable laws. This interpretation aligns with the intention of the legislature to balance the rights between developers and neighboring property owners ([paras 36, 79]).

The judgment stressed that the local authority's decision-makers were competent, properly qualified, and had conducted sufficient inquiries regarding the proposed plans, which were within legal boundaries; thus, the appeal failed ([paras 34, 54]).

---

### C – Conclusion  
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, asserting the following key points:

1. The local authority adhered to the obligations imposed under section 7 of the Act, demonstrating reasonable satisfaction regarding potential derogations from property value ([para 55]).

2. The decision-making process was lawful, and the local authority correctly interpreted that it need not account for subjective perceptions of existing property owners when evaluating compliance ([para 61]).

3. The court affirmed that the findings of the Constitutional Court set a binding precedent yet did not necessarily negate the balance between rights of developers and those of affected neighbors ([para 63]).

The implications of this ruling highlight a critical interpretation of administrative law, emphasizing the local authority's role in urban planning while also delineating the limitations on the rights of neighboring property owners regarding objections in the planning process. The determination elucidates essential relationships between statutory interpretation, administrative justice, and property rights in the evolving urban landscape of South Africa.
