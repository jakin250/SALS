Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH
Source Folder: 2010

# FIRAC Case Note: Van der Watt v The State

### F - Facts
In this case, the appellant, JG Van der Watt, was convicted of rape based on the testimony of his domestic worker, Mary Sesane, regarding an incident that allegedly occurred in her room on January 30, 2000. Van der Watt admitted to having sexual intercourse with Sesane but claimed it was consensual. During the trial, the appellant was sentenced to ten years of imprisonment by the Regional Court in Pretoria following his conviction on July 21, 2003. After an unsuccessful appeal to the North Gauteng High Court on September 26, 2005, he sought to appeal further with leave from that court ([paras 1-1]).

Key elements of the incident included:
- The layout of Van der Watt’s property, which significantly influenced the court's assessment of the credibility of both the appellant and the complainant. Sesane’s room was located approximately ten meters from the main house, where Van der Watt lived with his family. The proximity raised questions about the feasibility of Sesane's account of the alleged rape taking place without the knowledge of nearby family members ([para 2]).
- Sesane testified that Van der Watt entered her room while carrying a firearm, commanded her to comply with his demands, and engaged in sexual intercourse against her will, despite her protests about his marital status. Van der Watt contended that the two had consensual intercourse, offering an account of the event that included details about their interaction and the conditions under which it took place, notably mentioning he did not use condoms as Sesane had expressed concerns about contraceptives ([paras 3-7]).

The medical examination provided by a practitioner (J88 report) supported that injuries were consistent with forced intercourse, but misreported by the magistrate during the appeal, leading to inaccuracies in the assessment of evidence ([para 5], [para 11]).

### I - Issues
The key legal questions posed to the court related to:
1. Whether the trial magistrate properly assessed the credibility of the complainant in light of the contradictory evidence and the surrounding circumstantial factors.
2. Whether there were sufficient signs of fabrication in the complainant’s testimony that warranted the overriding of her account and the acceptance of the appellant's version as reasonably possibly true ([paras 1-1], [paras 10-23]).

### R - Rules
The following legal principles underpinned the judgment:
1. **Standard of Proof:** In criminal cases, the prosecution is required to prove the accused's guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. The appellant's version must only be rejected if it cannot be reasonably possible ([para 23]).
2. **Credibility Deductions:** The magistrate and other courts should critically scrutinize the evidence and consider the credibility of witnesses, particularly regarding discrepancies and contradictions in the testimonies, especially when a case relies predominantly on the testimony of a single witness ([para 22], S v Gentle 2005 (1) SACR 420).
3. **Corroborative Evidence:** Evidence supporting the complainant's version must not only align with her testimony but also enhance its reliability against the defendant's account and must be critically weighed ([para 21]).

### A - Application
The court assessed the credibility of both Van der Watt and Sesane through an analysis of their respective accounts, the physical layout of the premises, and the evidentiary contradictions outlined above:
1. **Credibility of the Complainant:** Highlights included a significant number of inconsistencies in Sesane's testimony, especially regarding whether she was indeed threatened and her subsequent behavior after the alleged event (i.e., lending money to Van der Watt the day after the supposed rape) ([paras 6, 15]). The court noted that the complainant’s conclusions were undermined by her failure to report a firearm threat consistently, suggesting a deliberate fabrication to strengthen her account ([paras 14, 17]).
2. **Appellant's Version:** The court found that Van der Watt’s account of consensual intercourse appeared plausible, suggesting a relative absence of fear or concern about being caught, which would align with his claim of consent ([paras 2, 23]). The explanations provided for the presence of the R50 and the general interactions post-incident further indicated a lack of malice or intent from the appellant ([paras 10, 21]).

The ruling also discussed the magistrate's misinterpretation of the medical evidence, claiming the findings were consistent with both consent and force, thereby misdirecting itself by improperly weighing the medical conclusions ([para 11]).

### C - Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Van der Watt's appeal, finding that the evidence and the assessment of the trial court did not align with a reasonable conclusion of guilt. The conviction and sentence were set aside as the appeal court determined that the appellant's account was indeed reasonably possibly true, particularly in light of the discrepancies in the complainant's testimony and the misapplication of the evidentiary standard by the lower courts ([paras 1, 24]). 

The ruling emphasizes the necessity of carefully balancing the credibility of testimonies in cases relying on single witnesses, and the importance of corroborative evidence to uphold or reject an accused's defenses ([paras 11, 23]). The implications of the case extend to future considerations regarding similar accusations and the assessment standards applicable in cases of sexual assault, indicative of the sensitivity and intricacies involved in judicial determinations of consent and credibility.
