# FIRAC Case Note

## Case Title: ZOLEKA MKETSU & OTHERS v. AFRICAN NATIONAL CONGRESS & OTHERS

## Citation: Case No 315/2001

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([1]–[2])

The appeal arose from the local government elections held on December 5, 2000, in the Eastern Cape, South Africa. These elections saw two significant forms: proportional representation of councillors to municipal councils and the election of individual councillors to specific wards. The contested applications were launched on December 19, 2000 — merely 14 days post-election. 

Five applications were centralized in this matter, comprised of three related to proportional representation councillor elections and two concerning individual ward elections. The appellants, members of the African National Congress (ANC), challenged the selection process that governed the party's candidate listings for these elections. They contended that the process breached the ANC's internal procedures as set out in the ‘ANC Campaign Manual,’ which would have dictated their inclusion on party lists or for ward nominations.

The applicants sought substantial relief, including declarations that:
1. The ANC’s party list certified by the chairperson of the Electoral Commission was invalid.
2. Successful ANC candidates elected to councils in question were unlawfully elected and should be unseated.

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([4]–[4])

A significant issue arose surrounding the High Court's jurisdiction to address these applications. The appellants faced opposition based on their alleged failure to follow the procedures outlined in Section 65 of the Municipal Electoral Act 27 of 2000. Primarily, the matter at hand was whether the High Court retained jurisdiction despite the explicit remedies provided under the Municipal Electoral Act.

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([7]–[21])

The core statute in question was Section 65 of the Municipal Electoral Act, which establishes a procedural framework for lodging objections about electoral processes that materially affect election results. This section prescribes that objections be filed within two days following election day, with the possibility of appeals to the Electoral Court under its mandate. 

Notably, the distinction between objections concerning electoral results and those framed as general electoral disputes was pivotal. Section 78(1) of the Municipal Electoral Act provided for the Electoral Court's exclusive jurisdiction over electoral disputes, further clarifying that any disputes materially affecting declared electoral results fell under Section 65.

The court also considered the breadth of the term "material to the declared result," interpreting it as suitable to include any aspect of an election which could potentially alter election outcomes and directly contradicting the earlier claims of the appellants regarding their procedural compliance.

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([10]–[22])

In adjudicating the matter, the court analyzed the legislative intent behind Section 65, emphasizing that the procedures therein were mandatory. The term "may" in the statute was assessed, with the court asserting that it did not imply discretion concerning the filing of objections — rather, the wording underscored the need for compliance with the established framework.

The court highlighted that the appellants' chosen route bypassed mandatory objection procedures as stipulated, leading to the conclusion that the High Court lacked jurisdiction. Instead, the prescribed processes aimed to ensure rapid resolutions to disputes materially affecting election results, thus maintaining electoral integrity.

The court's interpretation of the scope of jurisdiction granted to the Electoral Court was also noted, reinforcing that the legislature intended for electoral disputes impacting results to be addressed exclusively through Section 65 processes. As such, arguments pertaining to the retained inherent jurisdiction of the High Court were systematically dismantled.

Moreover, the court observed that permitting such jurisdiction could undermine the electoral process by allowing endless extensions for filing claims after elections — contrary to the legislative aim of expedited resolution provided within Section 65.

### C – Conclusion: Court's Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([22])

Ultimately, the court dismissed the appeal, affirming that the procedures outlined in Section 65 of the Municipal Electoral Act were mandatory and that the High Court had no jurisdiction to entertain matters concerning objections to the election results. The ratio decidendi of the court's decision emphasized the legislative intent for rapid and definitive resolution of electoral disputes, underscoring the need for adherence to prescribed electoral processes.

The implications of this ruling are significant for future electoral disputes, establishing precedent that procedural compliance is non-negotiable when challenging electoral outcomes. This reinforces the integrity of the electoral system in South Africa by compelling stakeholders to utilize the defined procedures rather than seeking recourse in higher courts post-election.

The costs of the appeal, including those incurred from employing two counsels, were awarded against the appellants, highlighting the potential financial repercussions of failing to adhere to legislative processes.
