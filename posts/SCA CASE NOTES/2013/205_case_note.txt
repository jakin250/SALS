Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: Mangoma v S (155/13) [2013] ZASCA 205

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–4])

The case revolves around an appeal by the appellant, Mr. Thinandavha Phenias Mangoma, against a conviction of rape and a subsequent life sentence imposed by the Limpopo High Court. The appellant was convicted for allegedly raping his 13-year-old daughter on 8 February 2001. The appellant maintained an alibi, asserting that he was not at home during the time of the alleged incident, as he had left early that morning to seek employment and returned late that evening. The state’s case primarily rested on the testimonies of the complainant and her younger brother, both minors at the time.

Significantly, the appellant's conviction was contested on several grounds, particularly focusing on the validity of the minor witnesses' testimonies. The appellant argued that the witnesses had not been sworn in properly and their evidence was contradictory, undermining the reliability of their accounts. Additionally, the reliability of the medical report utilized during the trial was also called into question. The appellant contended he did not receive a fair trial, citing issues with the trial court's conduct, including stepping outside its impartial role.

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 2–4])

The central issues raised in this appeal include:

1. **Reliability and Admissibility of Child Witness Testimonies**: Whether the testimonies of the complainant and her brother were admissible and reliable given that they had not been properly sworn in.
2. **Fair Trial Concerns**: Whether the appellant received a fair trial, particularly concerning the trial court's alleged overreach and inadequate assessment of the evidence presented.
3. **Contradictions in Evidence**: The extent to which the contradictions in the testimonies of the witnesses affected the overall validity of the state’s case against the appellant.

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 5–7])

The court relied on several legal principles and statutes in assessing this appeal, including:

- **Section 164 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977**: This section outlines the procedures for administering oaths to witnesses, particularly minors.
- **S v B 2003 (1) SACR 52 (SCA)**: This precedent references the necessity of ensuring that child witnesses understand the oath they take.
- **Director of Public Prosecutions, Transvaal v Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development & Others 2009 (4) SA 222 (CC)**: This case emphasizes the importance of providing children as witnesses with intermediaries to mitigate the intimidating courtroom atmosphere.
- **Principles of Evidence Assessment**: The requirement that the trial court must carefully scrutinize evidence provided by witnesses, particularly in cases involving serious charges such as sexual offences.

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 5–14])

In addressing the appeal, the court first evaluated the admissibility of child witness testimony. Despite initial contentions about improper swearing in, it was determined that the witnesses had been sworn, although the inquiry into their understanding of the oath was inadequately placed. The court cited S v B to reinforce the importance of a thorough pre-testimonial inquiry designed to ascertain the witnesses' comprehension of truthfulness.

Next, the court highlighted significant inconsistencies in the testimonies provided by the complainant and her brother. For instance, the complainant's conflicting statements regarding when she reported the incident, coupled with the brother’s contradictory evidence, raised substantial doubts about the reliability of their accounts. The absence of the mother’s testimony, which could have clarified these inconsistencies, was noted as a failure on the part of the prosecution.

In addressing claims of intimidation, the court also pondered the absence of intermediaries for the child witnesses, referring to established case law emphasizing the necessity of such measures for young witnesses to facilitate truthful testimony.

Lastly, the trial court's conduct was criticized, particularly a statement made by the trial judge that suggested personal bias against the appellant, undermining the fairness of the trial. The cumulative effect of these issues led the court to believe that the trial court had not sufficiently scrutinized the evidence or considered the potential influence of external factors on the children’s testimonies.

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 14–15])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, concluding that the State had failed to discharge the burden of proof required to support a conviction for rape. It determined that the minor witnesses' testimonies were riddled with inconsistencies and contradictions that were not adequately addressed by the trial court, leading to the conclusion that their reliability had been severely compromised.

As a result, the appellant’s conviction and sentence were set aside. This case underscores the critical importance of proper evidentiary procedures concerning child witnesses in sexual offence cases, highlighting the necessity of safeguarding the rights of the accused to ensure genuine and impartial justice. The decision serves as a reaffirmation of the legal standards that must be met in such sensitive cases, reinforcing the judiciary's commitment to fair trial principles.
