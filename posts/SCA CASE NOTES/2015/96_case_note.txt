Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# Case Note: Financial Services Board v Barthram (20207/2014) [2015] ZASCA 96

## F – Facts
The case involves Mr. Percy George Edward Barthram (the respondent), who was employed by Discovery Life Limited, an authorized Financial Services Provider (FSP) under the Financial Services Advisory and Intermediary Services Act 37 of 2002 (FAIS). Barthram entered into a contract with Discovery on 15 September 2009, which included clauses requiring compliance with relevant legislation and fit and proper requirements as stipulated in FAIS ([1]). 

On 31 May 2012, Barthram purportedly terminated his employment with Discovery, subsequently joining Old Mutual Life Insurance Limited. Following his departure, Discovery claimed that Barthram failed to meet the requisite standards of honesty and integrity as a representative and notified the Financial Services Board (FSB) to that effect ([2] & [3]). He was subsequently debarred, and his name was listed as debarred on the FSB's website, which cited his failure to comply with the requisite personal character qualities ([3]).

After Barthram's attempts to lift the debarment were unsuccessful, he launched an urgent application in the North Gauteng High Court on 18 September 2012, seeking the reinstatement of his status as a representative ([4]). An interim order was made in his favour against the FSB, but a final decision was later unfavourable for him. The High Court upheld the debarment decision made by Discovery ([7] & [8]).

Mr. Barthram appealed the dismissal of his application, and the FSB, who had not participated in the High Court proceedings, sought to appeal the findings against it ([9]).

## I – Issues
The key legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) were: 
1. Whether Discovery's debarment of Barthram under section 14(1) of FAIS precluded him only from rendering financial services on behalf of Discovery, or on an industry-wide basis.
2. Whether the FSB was correct in claiming that the interpretation by the High Court of FAIS had significant consequences for the regulation of the financial services industry ([11] & [12]).

## R – Rules
The relevant provisions of the FAIS Act are critical to the court's analysis:
- **Section 14(1)**: Requires an FSP to debar a representative who does not comply with the fit and proper requirements, indicating that debarment should effectuate removal from the statutory register ([14]).
- **Section 14A**: Deals with industry-wide debarment and implies that a person debarred under this section is precluded from rendering any financial services on behalf of any FSP ([11]).
- **Section 7 & 13**: Outline the fit and proper requirements to be maintained by FSPs and their representatives ([12]).

The court also referenced the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act, which enshrines the principles of fairness and proper procedure ([21]).

## A – Application
The SCA analyzed the language and intent behind the FAIS provisions to determine the implications of a debarment order under section 14(1). The Court found that the initial interpretation by the High Court mistakenly limited the debarment's effect to only Discovery and not industry-wide ([16]). The SCA emphasized that a representative deemed unfit by one FSP poses a risk to the public and thus should not be allowed to operate under any FSP, affirming that section 14(1) necessitates a broader understanding of debarment's implications ([16]).

Additionally, the Court found that Discovery failed to follow procedural fairness principles when debarring Barthram, as he was not given an adequate opportunity to defend himself against the accusations of dishonesty ([22]). The SCA upheld Barthram's appeal, concluding that the procedural irregularities required the previous order to be set aside.

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld both appeals: 
1. It set aside the interim order requiring FSB to reinstate Barthram and dismissed the application against it.
2. It annuled the debarment decision by Discovery and ordered it to cover Barthram's legal costs ([23]).

This judgment clarifies the importance of observing procedural fairness in debarment decisions and establishes that debarment by an FSP under section 14 has implications beyond just that provider, enforcing an industry-wide standard for financial service representatives. The ruling reinforces regulatory compliance in the financial services sector, emphasizing the expectation for FSPs to maintain high standards of honesty and integrity.
