Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Federation of Governing Bodies for South African Schools v Member of the Executive Council for Education, Gauteng and Another [2016] ZACC 14

## F – Facts 

The case concerns a dispute regarding the **Regulations Relating to the Admission of Learners to Public Schools in Gauteng**, particularly the amendments promulgated in 2012 ([paras 6-8]). The applicant, the **Federation of Governing Bodies for South African Schools** (FEDSAS), challenged the validity of several regulations governing school admissions. This arose after the **Member of the Executive Council for Education, Gauteng** (MEC) published draft amendments on 18 July 2011. FEDSAS submitted objections to the draft, suggesting that the regulations conflicted with the national **South African Schools Act** and the **Gauteng School Education Act**, encroached on school governing bodies' powers, and were procedurally unfair ([paras 9-10]).

The High Court initially ruled in favor of FEDSAS, invalidating several provisions ([para 12]), prompting an appeal by the MEC and the **Head of Department of Education, Gauteng** (HOD) to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA). The SCA reversed many of the High Court's findings, leading to FEDSAS's appeal to the Constitutional Court ([paras 13-14]). FEDSAS sought to reinstate the High Court's judgment based on the constitutional imperatives of access to education and the legality of the regulations ([paras 23-26]).

## I – Issues 

The primary legal questions raised in this case were:

1. **Do the amended regulations conflict with the South African Schools Act or the Gauteng School Education Act?**
2. **Are the regulations irrational or unjustifiable under section 4 of the Gauteng School Education Act?**
3. **What is the scope of the powers held by the school governing bodies versus the provincial education authorities in determining admission policies and feeder zones?** ([paras 6-8, 15, 25]).

## R – Rules 

The legal framework analyzed in this case includes:

1. **Section 29 of the South African Constitution**: Guarantees the right to basic education ([para 4]).
2. **South African Schools Act 84 of 1996**: Outlines the powers of school governing bodies regarding admission policies ([para 15]).
3. **Gauteng School Education Act 6 of 1995**: Authorizes the MEC to create regulations about school admissions ([paras 9, 10]).
4. Prior case law that informed the court’s understanding of the principles of cooperative governance, including **Head of Department, Mpumalanga Department of Education v Hoërskool Ermelo** and **MEC For Education, Gauteng Province v Governing Body, Rivonia Primary School** ([paras 4-6, 16, 24]).

## A – Application 

### Conflict with National and Provincial Legislation 

The court found that the implications of concurrent legislative power over education between national and provincial domains should be considered carefully. The existence of overlapping and conflicting laws does not automatically invalidate either set of provisions. Instead, the Constitution favors finding a harmonious interpretation between them ([paras 25-28]).

### Rationality and Justifiability of Regulations 

The Court addressed the specific regulations challenged by FEDSAS, determining that:

- **Regulation 3(7)** aimed to prevent unfair discrimination and was found to be reasonable in its prohibition against schools requesting confidential reports about learners before admissions ([paras 30-33]).
- **Regulations 4(1) and (2)** concern the authority of the MEC to set feeder zones. There was acknowledgment of both the need for consultation with stakeholders and the intention behind default feeder zones, leading to the conclusion that such regulations can be confined by a temporal mandate requiring prompt action by the MEC ([paras 34-39]).
- The Court supported **Regulation 5’s** mechanism for placing unplaced learners as it aligned with the broader objective of universal access to education and maintained that these regulations fell within the ambit of the education laws ([paras 40-47]).

### The Role of School Governing Bodies 

The Court reiterated the importance of school governing bodies as stakeholders in the educational governance framework. It emphasized cooperative governance principles, noting that while these bodies have significant authority, their powers are constrained within legal and procedural frameworks designed to promote equitable access to education ([paras 47-48]).

## C – Conclusion 

The **Constitutional Court** concluded that while FEDSAS raised vital constitutional questions about learner admissions and the validity of the challenged regulations, the appeal was dismissed except for the specification that the MEC must determine new feeder zones within 12 months. The judgment clarified the cooperative roles of school governing bodies and provincial authorities in managing public school admissions to ensure compliance with constitutional obligations regarding education ([paras 50-51]). The decision emphasizes the balance between the rights of governing bodies and the necessity of regulation to achieve equitable access to education, reinforcing constitutional values in the education sphere. 

### Order 

1. Leave to appeal granted.
2. The appeal against the SCA’s order dismissed, except directing the MEC to determine feeder zones within a reasonable timeframe.
3. No order as to costs ([para 51]). 

This decision serves both to affirm the legislative framework governing school admissions and to provide a guideline for future interactions between governing bodies and education authorities.
