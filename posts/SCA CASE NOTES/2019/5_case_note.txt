Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: **Polokwane Local Municipality v Granor Passi (Pty) Ltd (289/2018)** [2019] ZASCA 5

## F – Facts:
The dispute arises from an agreement concluded on 7 December 1988 between the Pietersburg Municipality (the predecessor of Polokwane Local Municipality) and Granor Passi (Pty) Ltd for the sale of immovable property described as Lot 5665, Pietersburg Extension 12, for a purchase price of R181,000. The agreement stipulated a 20% deposit due at the signing, followed by 60 monthly instalments ([1]). Granor claims to have paid the total purchase price by 1994, and it constructed industrial buildings on the property, enhancing its value significantly, and has since operated a business on the site ([2]).

Despite Granor's claimed compliance with payment obligations and the construction of buildings approved by the municipality, the title of the property remained registered in the name of the municipality ([1]–[2]). Between 2010 and 2011, Granor noticed that, although rates were paid for the property, the municipality had not transferred the title as expected ([2]). In response to Granor's requests for transfer, the municipality repeatedly challenged the sufficiency of the proof of payment ([3]).

On 26 February 2015, the municipality resolved not to accept Granor's proof of payment which initiated the legal proceedings. Granor applied for the review and setting aside of this resolution, and the High Court found in favor of Granor, ordering the review and setting aside of the resolution, while leaving the question of transfer back to the municipality for reconsideration ([3], [4]).

## I – Issues:
The key legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:
1. Whether the municipality's resolution not to accept Granor's proof of payment authored a legally sufficient rationale to deny the transfer of property.
2. Whether Granor's application fell under contractual law principles or could be understood within the framework of administrative law as a review under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA).
3. The legal validity of the municipality's argument that the claim had prescribed ([5]–[6], [9]).

## R – Rules:
Relevant legal frameworks and precedential cases included:
1. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: The court clarified that the council's resolution amounted to administrative action under PAJA, hence making it reviewable ([11]).
2. **Common law principles on factual errors**: The judgment cited that factual error constitutes a ground for review when the decision-maker's error concerns a material fact that is uncontentious and objectively verifiable ([14]–[15]).
3. **Constitutional principles of accountability and fairness in government dealings**: The court underscored the constitutional mandate that municipal entities should act openly, fairly, and accountably with their citizens ([39]).

## A – Application:
In assessing the council's decision, the Supreme Court underscored the administrative nature of the council’s resolution and noted the fundamental errors in the municipality's conclusions that payment had not been made. The factual record overwhelmingly supported Granor's claims of full payment, including documentation from audited financial statements and corroborative evidence from auditors stating that payments were made accurately ([1]–[25]).

The lack of thorough investigation by the municipality, alongside its reliance on bare denials without evidential support, led the court to conclude that there was a clear factual error in the municipality's decision. The court further found that Granor's claims constituted a more substantial basis in public law rather than mere contractual claims, and the resolution's approach to deny transfer based simply on insufficient proofs was rejected ([13]–[35]).

The court emphasized ongoing recognition of Granor as beneficially entitled to the property, thus refuting the municipal argument of prescription and countering claims that suggested disallowed statutory grounds for a delay in transfer, reconfirming the obligation of the municipality to revisit its position concerning the transfer ([42]–[46]).

## C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the High Court's decision to set aside the municipality's resolution and ordering it to reconsider Granor's request to transfer the property, upholding the principle of accountability in municipal dealings with private entities ([48]). The implications of the judgment were significant, recalling municipalities’ obligations to engage in fair administrative practices, ensuring that actions taken are consistent with the constitutional and legislative frameworks guiding administrative justice ([43]).

The court emphasised that the re-evaluation of Granor's claims should occur within the parameters established by the evidence that Granor had fully satisfied its payment obligations, actively inviting a reconsideration of the facts without the procedural impediments that the municipality had previously relied upon ([43]–[44]). 

Overall, the judgment illustrates the court’s commitment to enforcing accountability and correctness within public administrative actions, particularly in contexts that intertwine public interests and private enterprise.
