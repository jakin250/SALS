Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: Tshakwata v The State (522/13)

## F – Facts
The appellants, Tshakwata Gerson and Matoro Nthatheni Colbert, alongside four others, were convicted in the Limpopo High Court on a charge of murder, specifically for the ritual killing of the deceased. All accused, including the appellants, pleaded not guilty and did not disclose their defence strategy ([1]). The crux of the matter was the identity of the perpetrators, as the evidence presented did not directly implicate the appellants ([2]).

During the trial, the State sought to introduce statements made by two co-accused that purportedly incriminated the appellants. The admissibility of these statements was disputed, leading to a trial-within-a-trial, where the High Court eventually ruled the statements admissible despite the appellants contesting that they had not been made voluntarily ([2], [3]). The evidence provided by the State included testimony from various witnesses, including the investigating officer and a witness who sought to discount the possibility of an accidental death by animal attack. 

The primary evidence rested on statements from accused 3 and 4. Accused 3's statement was deemed vague and did not connect the appellants to any wrongdoing, while accused 4’s statement appeared to be a confession but was riddled with inconsistencies and implied coercion ([5], [6],[8]). Ultimately, it was revealed that all accused, including the appellants, chose not to testify, which the trial court interpreted as tacit acknowledgment of guilt ([9], [10]).

## I – Issues
The main legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal included whether the statements made by the co-accused were admissible and whether the evidence presented by the State was sufficient to uphold a conviction for murder. Specifically, the court examined:

1. The admissibility of hearsay evidence in the form of statements made by co-accused;
2. Whether the State had established a prima facie case against the appellants, thus warranting their conviction and subsequent sentencing ([11], [12]).

## R – Rules
The relevant legal authorities governing this matter included:

1. **Section 219** of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1997, which delineates the admissibility of confession statements only against the individual making them.
2. **Section 3(1)** of the Law of Evidence Amendment Act 45 of 1988, which governs the admission of evidence based on the necessity of ensuring fairness in the judicial process ([11]).
3. Precedents concerning the necessity of a prima facie case being established before the onus shifts to the defence, notably *S v Francis* and *S v Chabalala* ([10]).

## A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized the High Court's ruling concerning hearsay evidence. It found the statements of accused 3 and 4 to be inadmissible or, at best, devoid of substantive connection to the appellants ([11]). Accused 4’s statement, while suggesting participation in the crime, contained contradictions—indicating coercion and failing to explicitly connect the other accused to any specific wrongdoing. Hence, this statement could not be used as evidence against Gerson and Colbert without proper corroboration ([7], [11]).

Moreover, the Court emphasized that for the State's failure to present a prima facie case, the appellants were not obligated to testify. The collective absence of direct evidence implicating them meant that the State had not proven their guilt beyond a reasonable doubt ([10],[12]). As such, the decision of the High Court to infer guilt from the appellants' silence was deemed inappropriate given the insufficient evidence against them.

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the convictions and sentences for Gerson and Colbert were indefensible due to the insubstantial nature of the State's case. The hearsay statements from co-accused did not satisfy the evidentiary standards required for a conviction of murder. Therefore, the appeal was upheld, resulting in the dismissal of the convictions and sentences imposed by the Limpopo High Court ([13]).

This case underscores essential legal principles regarding the admissibility of evidence and the necessity for a robust prima facie case in criminal adjudication. The ruling not only reverses the convictions of the appellants, but it also reaffirms the integrity of the principles of justice and the burden of proof expected within the South African legal framework.
