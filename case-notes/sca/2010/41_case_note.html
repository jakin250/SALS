Supreme Court of Appeal

Le Roux v Dey (44/2009) [2010] ZASCA 41

The controversy began when Le Roux, the first defendant, manipulated a photograph of two naked male bodybuilders by pasting Dey’s face and that of the school principal onto the bodies.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2010 [2010] ZASCA 41

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The case of Le Roux v Dey involves a claim for damages based on defamation and humiliation of the respondent, Louis Dey, a vice principal at a well-known secondary school in Pretoria. The defendants were three scholars: Hendrik Pieter Le Roux, Burgert Christian Gildenhuys, and Reinardt Janse van Rensburg, all students at the same school. The High Court initially upheld Dey’s claims and awarded him R45,000 in damages, which the defendants subsequently appealed against ([1]–[2]).

The controversy began when Le Roux, the first defendant, manipulated a photograph of two naked male bodybuilders by pasting Dey’s face and that of the school principal onto the bodies. This photo was disseminated widely among students when Gildenhuys shared it via cellphone and the third defendant, Janse van Rensburg, placed it on the school notice board. The episode led to significant distress for Dey, prompting him to initiate the legal action under actio iniuriarum for defamation and humiliation ([2]–[4]).

I – Issues

The appeal and cross-appeal involved the following critical issues:

  1. Wrongfulness: Was the publication of the manipulated photo objectively wrongful?
  2. Animus iniuriandi: Did the defendants possess the requisite intent to injure Dey’s reputation?
  3. Quantum of damages: What is the appropriate amount of damages for defamation and humiliation?
  4. Costs order: Was a higher scale of costs justified due to the unique nature of the case? ([4]).

Additionally, the court addressed whether a single act could give rise to multiple causes of action under actio iniuriarum ([20]).

R – Rules

The court relied on principles established in defamation law:

  • A two-stage inquiry to determine whether a publication is defamatory: first, the interpretation of the publication, and second, whether that interpretation is defamatory ([5]).
  • The test of what constitutes a defamatory publication involves considering the meaning conveyed to a reasonable person in the relevant context, which includes the audience familiar with the parties involved ([6]–[9]).
  • The concept of animus iniuriandi under South African law is the intent to injure and does not necessarily require a consciousness of wrongfulness. The legal precedent suggests that intent to harm is sufficient ([26]–[29]).
  • The Constitutional rights under sections 10, 14, and 9(2) regarding dignity, privacy, and sexual orientation also informed the judgment ([14]).

A – Application

The court's analysis began with the question of wrongfulness in the defamation claim. It considered how the reasonable person would interpret the manipulated image. The court noted that the humorous intent claimed by the defendants did not negate the defamatory nature of the photograph, which objectively ridiculed Dey, undermining his position as a vice principal ([18]–[19]).

Regarding animus iniuriandi, the court dismissed the defendants’ claims of lack of intent to defame, reasoning that the nature of the actions taken showed a clear intent to ridicule Dey. The defendants' argument that their actions were a joke was insufficient, as the court found that a joke at someone's expense, especially in a school context, was likely to be defamatory ([9]–[10]).

On the issue of damages, the court found the initial award appropriate given the severe impact on Dey's dignity and standing within the school community. The High Court had misdirected itself in treating the damages award as a sentence in a criminal matter, which led to a reassessment confirming the seriousness of Dey's humiliation ([42]–[49]).

Finally, the court amended the costs order to reflect the provision for two counsel, emphasizing the complexity and nature of the proceedings, which warranted a higher scale of costs ([49]–[50]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the defendants' appeal and upheld the cross-appeal regarding the costs. It confirmed the High Court’s findings on defamation and affirmed that the publication of the manipulated photo constituted a wrongful act causing humiliation. The court established that a single act could constitute one cause of action under the actio iniuriarum basis, emphasizing that the nature of dignity and defamation under South African law is intertwined ([50]–[51]).

This case underscores the balance between freedom of expression and the protection of individual dignity, an essential consideration in the context of jest and defamation, particularly within educational environments where power dynamics are at play.
