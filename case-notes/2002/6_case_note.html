Constitutional Court

Ex parte Minister of Safety and Security and Others - CCT 28/01

This case arose from a criminal trial concerning Edward Joseph Walters and Marvin Edward Walters, who faced murder charges for the shooting of a burglar fleeing their bakery.

Constitutional Court 2002 CCT 28/01

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History

[1–9]

This case arose from a criminal trial concerning Edward Joseph Walters and Marvin Edward Walters, who faced murder charges for the shooting of a burglar fleeing their bakery. The incident occurred in February 1999, when the accused shot at the burglar, resulting in fatal injuries. The accused contended that they acted within their rights under section 49(2) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (the CPA), which authorized the use of lethal force to prevent a suspect from fleeing in specific circumstances.

The prosecution challenged both the factual basis of the defense and the constitutionality of section 49(2). The presiding judge at the High Court ruled that the section was constitutionally invalid, particularly concerning the use of force to prevent flight. He referenced the infringement of the rights to life and human dignity as protected by the South African Constitution. Following his decision, he did not limit the retrospective effect of the declaration of invalidity and referred the matter to the Constitutional Court for confirmation, pursuant to section 172(2) of the Constitution.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions

[10–14]

The primary issue was the constitutional validity of section 49 of the CPA, particularly subsection 49(2), which justified the use of deadly force in arresting a fleeing suspect. The court had to determine whether this provision infringed on the rights to life, human dignity, and bodily integrity as outlined in sections 10, 11, and 12 of the Constitution.

Ancillary questions arose regarding:

  1. The application of judicial precedent and its binding authority on constitutional matters.
  2. The procedural propriety of the High Court’s decision to rule on constitutional matters without first addressing the factual merits of the criminal trial.
  3. The implications of retrospective invalidation of the statute for ongoing and future criminal prosecutions based on actions taken under the now-invalidated provisions.

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon

[3–6; 15–21]

The court relied upon constitutional provisions that guarantee fundamental rights:

  • Section 10: Right to human dignity
  • Section 11: Right to life
  • Section 12: Right to freedom and security of the person

Additionally, the judgment made references to precedents including:

  • S v Makwanyane: Notably, this case was significant for its examination of the right to life and dignity in the context of capital punishment (see particularly [7] for the importance of these rights).
  • Govender v Minister of Safety and Security: Involved the interpretation of section 49 and established groundwork for assessing limitations on rights as prescribed under section 36 of the Constitution and the need for proportionality in the use of force.

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law

[22–48]

The court's analysis began with a recognition that section 49(2) inherently limited vital constitutional rights by allowing law enforcement and private individuals to use lethal force to prevent a suspect from fleeing, regardless of whether there was a threat to life. It was established that:

  • The right to life and dignity should prevail, informing any limitation that the law may impose.
  • The proportionality of the force used in arrests required that the circumstances necessitating its application be exceptionally serious (i.e., imminent threats of serious bodily harm). The legislation had insufficient checks to ensure that lethal force was truly a last resort.

The court highlighted the categorical problem with subsection 49(2) that it not only authorized force aiming at resistance but also killed suspects to prevent flight—potentially for minor offences. This overreach violated the values that the Constitution seeks to establish regarding the sanctity of life and the proportional response to crime and threats.

While analyzing section 49(1) alongside its predecessor judgments, the court found that the previous SCA ruling in Govender provided a constitutionally sound framework for limiting the application of force by presuming that serious threat must accompany any willingness to deploy such force.

On the retrospective application, the court discussed how striking down subsection 49(2) historically could affect individuals who had relied on it at the time; it determined that a prospective application of invalidity would prevent unjust consequences.

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications

[49–77]

The Constitutional Court concluded that section 49(2) of the CPA was invalidated based on its inconsistency with the Constitution, particularly infringing on the rights to life, human dignity, and bodily integrity. The invalidation was rendered prospective to avoid unjust repercussions for ongoing or past prosecutions.

The ratio decidendi emphasized:

  1. The constitutional imperative to respect and uphold rights, even in the pursuit of law enforcement, which must not require individuals to forfeit their right to life lightly.
  2. The need for laws that are not only effective in enforcing order but also respect fundamental human rights, aligning with the Constitution's mandate to promote a culture of dignity and respect for human life across all levels of governance.

The implications included a clear directive to Parliament to develop laws that allow for reasonable use of force in arrests, ensuring these laws align with constitutional values. This ruling will guide future conduct regarding police actions and criminal prosecutions, reinforcing the balance between public safety and individual rights.

The court ordered that section 49(2) be declared unconstitutional effective immediately but limited in its retrospective effect. The case was returned to the High Court for the prosecution of the murder charges against the accused based on judicial principles established during this case.
