Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note: Karpovska v The State (1396/2016) [2017] ZASCA 101

## F – Facts

The case before the Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa revolves around Kateryna Karpovska, the appellant, who was indicted in the Bellville Specialised Commercial Court on multiple charges, including four counts of fraud and two counts of contraventions of specific financial legislation ([para 1]). Following a trial, Karpovska was convicted on three counts of fraud and acquitted on the other charges ([para 2]). The sentences imposed included an eight-year imprisonment term, wholly suspended, along with conditions for future conduct and a requirement to repay R1,755 million to the complainant by a specified date. For counts 3 and 4, she was sentenced to three years of correctional supervision under s 276(1)(h) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([para 2]).

After her conviction, Karpovska's application for leave to appeal against her convictions was dismissed by the regional magistrate. However, the Western Cape High Court allowed her to appeal with respect to count 3 only, while her petitions regarding counts 1 and 4 were denied ([paras 3–4]). Subsequently, the Supreme Court of Appeal granted special leave for her to appeal against the dismissal of her petition concerning counts 1 and 4 ([para 3]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal question before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether the court a quo (the high court) ought to have granted leave to appeal against the convictions on counts 1 and 4 ([para 4]). This inquiry necessitated an assessment of whether Karpovska demonstrated reasonable prospects of success for her envisaged appeal concerning these specific counts ([para 5]).

## R – Rules

The court relied on established principles regarding the granting of leave to appeal, which hinge on the existence of reasonable prospects of success. The relevant legal precedent, particularly S v Radebe, was cited to provide a framework for evaluating these prospects ([para 5]). Generally, the thresholds for demonstrating reasonable prospects of success require the appellant to outline specific legal or factual issues that could lead to a different outcome on appeal.

## A – Application

In evaluating Count 1, the court noted that the trial magistrate had conducted an extensive analysis of the evidence, wherein the complainant's testimony was deemed credible and corroborated by substantial documentation. The court affirmed that the magistrate's findings on credibility were appropriately grounded in the evidence, leading to the conclusion that Karpovska's version was rejected as implausible and false beyond a reasonable doubt ([para 6]). Given this thorough examination, the Supreme Court found no reasonable prospects of success on appeal regarding Count 1.

Similarly, the analysis for Count 4 revealed that the facts were largely common cause, and the trial court had meticulously considered the evidence, favoring the complainant over the appellant's assertions. The Supreme Court reiterated that the magistrate concluded that there was no duress present as claimed by Karpovska. Therefore, it upheld the findings of the lower court, concluding again that there were no reasonable prospects of success concerning Count 4 ([para 8]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Karpovska's appeal against the refusal of her petition regarding counts 1 and 4, concluding that she had failed to establish reasonable prospects of success for her anticipated appeals against those convictions ([para 9]). The decision underscored the difficulty appellants face when seeking to overturn credibility assessments made by trial courts, emphasizing the deferential standard applied to factual findings in such contexts. The implications of this ruling reinforce judicial restraint concerning leave to appeal, especially when previous courts have rigorously examined evidentiary credibility.
