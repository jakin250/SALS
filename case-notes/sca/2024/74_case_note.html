Supreme Court of Appeal

Lategan and Another v The Director of Public Prosecutions, Western Cape and Another (314/2022) [2024] ZASCA 74

The regional court ruled in favor of the appellants, ordering the DPP to remove the offending sections.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 74

F - Facts

In 2018, the appellants, Stephanus Petrus Lategan and Johannes Retief Lategan, were charged in the Wynberg Regional Court with sexual offences committed between 1974 and 1979. The charges relied on sections 58, 59, and 60 of the Criminal Law (Sexual Offences and Related Matters) Amendment Act 32 of 2007 (the Act), despite the fact that these offences occurred prior to the Act's coming into force. The appellants objected to the inclusion of these sections in the charge sheet, arguing that the Act was not applicable as it had not been in existence at the time the offences were committed and sought an amendment to the charge sheet ([1]–[2]).

The regional court ruled in favor of the appellants, ordering the DPP to remove the offending sections. The court reasoned that the statutory provisions did not apply retrospectively. The DPP subsequently sought a review in the Western Cape Division of the High Court, arguing that the sections in question could apply retrospectively to common law offences even if they were committed before the Act's enactment ([3]–[4]).

The High Court found that the regional court had erred by denying the DPP’s reliance on the mentioned sections, concluding that these provisions did indeed apply to the appellants’ case, thus allowing the review ([5]).

I - Issues

The appeal raised several critical legal issues:

  1. Whether sections 58, 59, and 60 of the Act could be applied retrospectively to common law sexual offences committed prior to the statute's commencement.
  2. Whether the DPP's procedural route through a review rather than an appeal under section 310 of the Criminal Procedure Act (CPA) was appropriate given the circumstances ([10]).

R - Rules

The court considered several foundational legal principles and statutory provisions:

  1. The presumption against retrospective application of statutes, as set out in S and Another v Regional Magistrate Boksburg: Venter and another ([11]), establishes that a statute does not operate retrospectively unless expressly indicated by the legislature.
  2. Section 69 of the Act, which allows for the continuation of prosecutions of common law offences instituted prior to the Act’s commencement, supported the notion that the Act did not impair existing legal rights from before its enactment ([9]).
  3. Procedurally, the court highlighted the distinction between review and appeal, referencing past decisions such as Hira and Another v Booysen and Another, which clarified that a material misinterpretation of the law by a lower court may warrant review ([26]).

A - Application

In analyzing the law’s application, the court first reaffirmed the principle set forth in Boksburg regarding the separate treatment of substantive and procedural rights. The court differentiated between the substantive aspects of the charges under common law and the procedural implications of the sections in the Act. It was held that sections 58, 59, and 60 were procedural in nature, affecting how evidence would be treated in the trial rather than the core elements of the charges themselves ([12]–[14]).

Moreover, it was emphasized that because the infractions occurred before the Act, applying these sections would not retroactively extinguish the appellants’ rights or alter the nature of the offences. Instead, they would allow for a modernized procedural framework to be applied in future hearings, consistent with the legislative goals outlined in the Act ([18]–[25]).

Regarding the procedural question, the court concluded that the DPP was correct to seek a review, as the regional court's ruling constituted a "gross irregularity" in law, misinterpreting the significance of section 69, which confirmed the state's prosecutorial powers for crimes committed prior to the Act ([26]–[29]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming that sections 58, 59, and 60 of the Act were applicable to the appellants’ criminal proceedings. The decision upheld the procedural modernizations aimed at improving the handling of sexual offence cases while maintaining the integrity of substantive rights. The court underscored that there was no injustice to the appellants in allowing these provisions to govern their forthcoming trial, ultimately reinforcing the legislative purpose of protecting vulnerable complainants of sexual offences. The order was made with costs, highlighting the procedural correctness of the DPP's actions in pursuing a review to rectify the lower court's misinterpretation ([29]).
