Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: The Member of the Executive Council, Education, North West Province v Izak Boshoff Foster & Others (Case no 471/2021) [2023] ZASCA 11

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts:
The case concerns an unfortunate incident that occurred on 6 May 2006 during a rugby tournament hosted by Hoër Volkskool Potchefstroom, where the first respondent, Izak Boshoff Foster, then an 18-year-old matric student, suffered a severe neck injury not only from a tackle but also due to negligent first aid handling thereafter ([3]-[4]). Following his tackle, as he lay on the ground, another player fell on him, causing an immediate cervical injury. Subsequently, two first aid staff transferred him off the field without proper stabilization, which resulted in further neurological damage ([4]).

The respondents initiated legal proceedings against the MEC for Education in North West Province, asserting liability for damages caused by the failure to provide adequate first aid services ([4]-[5]). The third and fourth respondents, who were responsible for first aid provision, reached a settlement with the plaintiffs, leaving the MEC primarily liable for the injuries sustained due to the inadequate response from first aid personnel ([4]). In the high court, the claim was determined by examining the duty of care and negligence pertaining to the first aid response during a school activity ([5]).

### I – Issues:
The main legal issues presented in this case were:
1. Whether the MEC was liable for the second injury that the first respondent sustained due to the negligent actions of the first aid personnel.
2. Additionally, whether the high court’s variation of its earlier order concerning liability was properly grounded ([7]).

### R – Rules:
The legal framework concerning duty of care and negligence was primarily situated within the ambit of the *Schools Act No. 84 of 1996*, specifically Section 60, which stipulates that the State is liable for damages caused by any act or omission connected to school activities ([18]). The notion of *loco parentis*, whereby educators assume a duty of care over students, was also pivotal in this context ([19]).

The principles surrounding negligence were articulated through the established test from *Kruger v Coetzee*, which evaluates whether a reasonable person would foresee potential harm in their actions and take steps to prevent it ([25]). Additionally, the doctrine of functus officio, as confirmed in *Minister of Justice v Ntuli* and *Firestone South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Genticuro AG*, was relevant to discussions surrounding the authority of the court to amend orders after they had been issued ([11]).

### A – Application:
In analysing the claim of negligence, the court scrutinized the actions of the first aid personnel and the protocols followed by Volkskool. The MEC conceded that reasonable measures should have been taken to ensure well-trained and properly equipped first aid staff were present ([20]-[21]). The court noted the agreed joint opinion from medical experts that the improper handling of the first respondent without appropriate stabilization exacerbated his injury, indicating a direct causal link for the second injury due to negligence ([16]).

The court held that Volkskool had a responsibility not just to employ adequate first aid personnel, but to ensure they were competent to deal effectively with potential neck injuries typical in rugby games ([27]). The court ultimately found that Volkskool failed its duty of care by relying solely on Mr van Staden, who lacked the requisite professional accreditation for managing serious spinal injuries, showcasing evident negligence in their hiring process ([30]).

### C – Conclusion:
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the MEC was liable for Boshoff's second injury due to the negligent actions of first aid personnel during the rugby match ([36]). The appeal was only partially upheld regarding the costs ordered in the high court, particularly the punitive costs which were revised to a more appropriate level ([34]). The ratio decidendi established vital parameters around the duty of care owed within educational contexts, emphasizing that schools must adequately vet service providers to mitigate foreseeable risks adequately.

The case underscores the importance of thorough oversight and representation in the provision of emergency services at public school sporting events, with notable implications for the standard of care expected from educational institutions under **South African law**. The decision enhances the judicial understanding of negligence criteria and affirms the principle that institutions assume high levels of accountability when ensuring the safety of students involved in inherently risky activities.
