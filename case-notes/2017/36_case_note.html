Makhubela v The State; Matjeke v The State [2017] ZACC 36

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–6]) The applicants, George Sipho Makhubela and Thabo Elekia Matjeke, sought leave to appeal their convictions and sentences imposed by the Full Court of the High Court of South Africa, North West Division, Mahikeng.

Constitutional Court 2017 [2017] ZACC 36

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–6])

The applicants, George Sipho Makhubela and Thabo Elekia Matjeke, sought leave to appeal their convictions and sentences imposed by the Full Court of the High Court of South Africa, North West Division, Mahikeng. They were among a group accused of committing murder and robbery on 3 August 2002, wherein Warrant Officer Dingaan Makuna was fatally shot during a robbery. Following the incident, Makhubela and Matjeke, along with five other co-accused, were arrested and convicted based primarily on the doctrine of common purpose, facing sentences of life imprisonment for murder, 15 years for robbery, and six years for unlawful possession of firearms ([paras 1–2]). The convictions were contested because the applicants claimed that extra-curial statements made by their co-accused were improperly admitted against them, thereby violating their rights to a fair trial and presumption of innocence ([para 1]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 17–30])

The main issues to be determined were whether the Full Court erred in admitting the extra-curial statements and whether the convictions based on the doctrine of common purpose could be sustained without additional evidence linking the applicants directly to the commission of the offences ([para 6]). Specifically, the Court examined the admissibility of co-accused's admissions against the applicants and the sufficiency of independent evidence to warrant their convictions on charges of murder and robbery, independent of the contested admissions ([paras 24–30]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 29–50])

The court relied on established common law principles regarding the inadmissibility of extra-curial admissions against co-accused, as articulated in Mhlongo v The State [2017] ZACC 36. The ruling emphasized that such admissions were inadmissible unless they constituted "executive statements" made in furtherance of a common purpose ([para 30]). Furthermore, the judgment referenced the strict tests for establishing liability under the doctrine of common purpose, which requires proof that each participant was present at the crime scene, aware of the assault, had a common cause with the perpetrators, and exhibited significant active association with the conduct of others ([paras 35–38], Mgedezi v S 1989 (1) SA 687 (A)).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 39–56])

In evaluating the appeals, the Constitutional Court ruled that the extra-curial statements could not be utilized to substantiate the convictions against Makhubela and Matjeke, aligning with the precedent set in Mhlongo. Despite acknowledging the common purpose in principle, the Court found that the State failed to provide independent evidence of the applicants' involvement that would sustain their convictions for unlawful possession of firearms. The Court critically analyzed the applicants' actions and knowledge during the commission of the robbery, concluding that even if both candidates were present and aware of their co-accused carrying firearms, a lack of personal possession and intent required under the Nkosi test for unlawful possession was evident ([paras 45–56]). The testimony of each accused was deemed critical in establishing whether they actively associated themselves with the common purpose of robbery and murder.

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 57–58])

The Constitutional Court, upholding several principles of common law, ruled that the extra-curial admissions of co-accused were inadmissible against Makhubela and Matjeke. The Court emphasized that their convictions for unlawful possession of firearms and ammunition could not stand due to a lack of evidence sufficing to meet the Nkosi test. Thus, the Court partially upheld their appeals, set aside convictions on counts 4 and 5 (unlawful possession of firearms), while confirming convictions for murder and robbery based on sufficient evidence of their participation in the criminal acts ([paras 57–58]). This judgment reinforces the necessity of safeguarding defendants' rights and the importance of due process, particularly regarding evidence admissibility, highlighting constitutional protections pivotal in ensuring fair trials within the South African legal framework.
