De Nysschen v Government Employees Pension Fund and Others (864/2022) [2023] ZASCA 147

The appeal in De Nysschen v Government Employees Pension Fund and Others revolves around the competency of a payment order issued by the North West Division of the High Court.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 147

FIRAC Structure

F – Facts [1-10]

The appeal in De Nysschen v Government Employees Pension Fund and Others revolves around the competency of a payment order issued by the North West Division of the High Court. The appellant, Ms. Petronella De Nysschen, had been employed with the North West Province Department of Education from January 15, 1979, until June 26, 2013, when her employment was terminated due to misconduct. Post-termination, she received a pension benefit of R5,194,418.72 from the Government Employees Pension Fund (GEPF) after her membership ended with her dismissal.

After appealing her dismissal, De Nysschen was reinstated and a settlement agreement was reached with the Department on May 27, 2015, stipulating that her pensionable service should be restored. Following her reinstatement, the Department sought calculations for the pension benefits to reinstate her service, which amounted to R7,016,767.76, paid to the GEPF. Her employment continued until her retirement on March 11, 2020.

Despite providing exit documents to the Department for processing her pension, it failed to submit these documents to the GEPF, leading De Nysschen to approach the high court for relief in July 2020. The high court granted her the necessary interdict but also imposed a payment order against De Nysschen, directing her to pay back the pension benefit she had received post-dismissal.

I – Issues [11-17]

The principal legal question was whether the high court had jurisdiction to issue a payment order that was not requested by the parties involved. Specifically, the case centered around:

  1. The appropriateness of the high court granting payment relief without it being sought in the pleadings.
  2. Whether the Department could justifiably place a claim for repayment of funds received by De Nysschen based on the circumstances of her dismissal and the subsequent reinstatement.
  3. The balance between ensuring justice and the strict adherence to procedural norms established in court dealings.

R – Rules [18-17]

The court applied established principles from South African law regarding civil procedure, focusing on the importance of properly pleaded claims. The judgment cited previous rulings, including Bliss Brand (Pty) Ltd v Advertising Regulatory Board NPC and Others, which emphasized that disputes are defined by the pleadings submitted to a court. This doctrine ensures that a party cannot be held liable for claims that were not explicitly sought. Furthermore, it rested on the statutory provisions of the Government Employees Pension Law, particularly section 17(4), which governs repayment obligations related to pension benefits.

A – Application [18-17]

The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the high court erred in awarding the payment order. While the appellant rightfully contended that her pensionable service was effective from January 15, 1979, to March 31, 2020, and that the Department was obliged to process her exit documents, the court noted that the additional payment order was not supported by the pleadings. The high court based its decision on the intertwined nature of the facts surrounding the payment of the pension benefit, but the appellate court determined that decisions and claims must align with the matters initially defined in the pleadings.

The court asserted that the allegations and implications of a 'departmental debt' were introduced by the Department after the fact, violating procedural fairness and due process. Therefore, without a proper claim, the high court’s imposition of a payment obligation was deemed unfounded.

C – Conclusion [18]

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal with costs and set aside the order for payment issued by the high court. The judgment reaffirmed the critical role of procedural integrity in legal proceedings, emphasizing that courts must avoid issuing orders for relief not sought by the parties involved. This case underscores the necessity of clearly pleaded claims in judicial proceedings and reiterates the principle that justice must align with procedural norms.

The outcome reflects the court's commitment to ensuring that litigants are not subjected to unexpected liabilities resulting from the courts’ interpretations of intertwined facts, thereby reaffirming the importance of clarity in legal claims and adjudications.
