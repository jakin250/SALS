Lesego Kgengwe v The State [1998]

The court further evaluated the sentencing considerations, noting that while the appellant was of young age and had a stable familial background, the brutal nature of the crimes outweighed these factors.

Supreme Court of Appeal 1998

Supreme Court of Appeal, Case No: 666/96

FIRAC Method

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 2–4])

The appellant, Lesego Kgengwe, and his co-accused Captain Sono were tried in the Provincial Division of the Supreme Court of Bophuthatswana on charges comprising murder, robbery, and attempted murder stemming from an incident at Mafikeng Jewellers on 25 June 1993. During the robbery, Mr. Maree, the shop owner, was shot dead, and Mr. Snyman, a customer, was wounded. The appellant was convicted on all counts and sentenced to life imprisonment for murder, along with ten and twelve years for robbery and attempted murder respectively. Following his conviction, Kgengwe appealed the decision, raising significant issues regarding the reliability of the trial record, which contained numerous errors, including duplications and omissions that complicated the evaluation of the case.

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 2–12])

  1. Record Integrity: Whether the state of the trial record compromised the appellant's right to a fair appeal.
  2. Identification Evidence: Whether the identification of the appellant by various witnesses was reliable, especially considering the circumstances of the shooting and subsequent identification parades.
  3. Credibility of the Appellant’s Testimony: The degree to which the trial court assessed the credibility of the appellant's explanation for his presence at the scene.
  4. Sentencing Discretion: Whether the sentences imposed were disproportionate, given the appellant's youth and personal circumstances.

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 2–14])

The court relied upon established principles regarding the evaluation of identification evidence including the necessity for corroboration and scrutiny of witness reliability under stressful conditions. The respect for an appellant’s rights to a fair trial, grounded in Section 25 of the Constitution, underscores the legal framework surrounding the integrity of trial records. The judgments referenced previous statutory guidelines on sentencing, particularly the Correctional Services Act 8 of 1959, noting that sentences should serve justice and deterrence in cases involving violent crime.

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 4–12])

In assessing the reliability of the trial record, the court underscored that deficiencies warranted a serious consideration to dismiss the appeal; however, the significance of justice necessitated addressing the merits directly ([para 4]). Witness testimony surrounding the events at the jewellery store was critically examined. The court determined that the identification by witnesses Snyman and Mokise was credible despite the circumstances. In evaluating the appellant’s testimony, the court found it implausible, citing the uncorroborated assertion of duress and the lack of consistency with established facts ([paras 8–12]).

The court further evaluated the sentencing considerations, noting that while the appellant was of young age and had a stable familial background, the brutal nature of the crimes outweighed these factors. The imposition of life imprisonment was deemed consistent with prior jurisprudence on comparable violent crimes, thus reinforcing the integrity of the sentencing framework ([para 13]). Additionally, a clerical oversight regarding concurrent sentences was corrected as per statutory obligations, ensuring alignment with the Correctional Services Act ([para 14]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 12–14])

Ultimately, the court dismissed the appeal against both convictions and sentences, concluding that although the trial record was flawed, the appeal's substantive issues were resolved in favor of the integrity of the convictions. The identification evidence from both Snyman and Mokise was sufficiently reliable, while Kgengwe’s account was considered implausible, negating the credibility of his defense. The court emphasized the gravity of the offenses committed, holding that restorative justice cannot undermine the necessity for public safety and deterrence of serious crimes. As a consequence, while the sentences were upheld, a procedural correction was made to ensure that the sentences were applied in a legally sound manner as required by the Correctional Services Act.

The final order stipulated that the sentence for robbery would run concurrently with the life sentence for murder, thereby cementing the overall credibility of the judicial process within the framework of the law.
