Supreme Court of Appeal

Sizazonke Electrical CC v Eskom Holdings SOC Limited (222/2018) ZASCA 36

On 9 July 2010, Sizazonke transported fourteen employees in a vehicle not designed for such a number, contravening Eskom’s Vehicle Driver Safety Management Procedure aimed at safeguarding health and safety ([para 6]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019

F – Facts

Sizazonke Electrical CC (the first appellant) entered into contractual agreements with Eskom Holdings SOC Limited (the respondent) for the provision of services. These relationships were governed not only by specific contracts but also by overarching safety and operational guidelines instituted by Eskom, including the NEC 3 Engineering and Construction Contract and various procedural policies ([paras 1–3]).

On 9 July 2010, Sizazonke transported fourteen employees in a vehicle not designed for such a number, contravening Eskom’s Vehicle Driver Safety Management Procedure aimed at safeguarding health and safety ([para 6]). The vehicle overturned, leading to two fatalities and multiple injuries ([paras 8–10]). Eskom subsequently issued a work stoppage order while conducting investigations into the incident and, after a preliminary enquiry, suspended Sizazonke for five years due to misconduct associated with the breach of safety protocols ([para 11]).

Sizazonke sought to challenge this suspension, claiming that the respondent had repudiated their contract, resulting in a dismissal of their claim for damages for loss of profits based on said repudiation ([paras 1, 12]). The Gauteng Division of the High Court (Mothle J) dismissed the claim, finding Eskom’s action justified and lawful under the agreements governing their relation. Sizazonke appealed the decision ([para 1]).

I – Issues

The principal legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether Eskom repudiated its contractual obligations to Sizazonke by suspending their service provision ([para 13]). This question involved examining whether Eskom's actions constituted a lawful basis for terminating contracts under the allegations of misconduct, and whether Sizazonke's claims for damages for loss of profits could be substantiated ([para 24]).

R – Rules

The legal principles guiding this case centered on the concepts of repudiation in contract law, particularly recognizing a repudiatory breach as an indication by conduct or words that a party does not intend to fulfil its contractual obligations ([paras 14–15]). Additionally, the presence of illegality or a breach of contractual terms might negate any claim for damages associated with such a breach.

A key element in the analysis was the interpretation of clause 90 of the NEC 3 Engineering and Construction Contract, governing the procedures for termination of contracts due to breaches, which necessitates a notification process in certain instances ([paras 20–21]).

The court also relied on precedents clarifying that one party cannot benefit from its own unlawful conduct ([para 24]) and the requirement to substantiate claims with credible evidence ([paras 27–29]).

A – Application

The court upheld the findings of the court a quo, establishing that Sizazonke's breach of safety regulations justified Eskom’s suspension of their contracts ([para 17]). The seriousness of Sizazonke's breaches, culminating in fatalities and injuries, warranted Eskom’s zero-tolerance policy and subsequent disciplinary measures, including suspension ([paras 15, 17–18]).

In addressing Sizazonke’s claims regarding the procedural aspects of termination under the NEC 3 contract, the Supreme Court of Appeal reiterated that requiring Eskom to provide notice of default to itself (considering the role of project managers as its employees) was inherently illogical and contrary to the contractual purpose ([paras 20–21]).

On the question of whether Eskom had waived its right to cancel the contract or elected not to do so by permitting Sizazonke to temporarily resume work, the court found no support in the evidence for Sizazonke's position. Witness testimonies highlighted the distinct phases of preliminary and commercial investigations, which did not support the claims of an election against cancellation ([paras 25–29]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded with an affirmation of the Gauteng Division's dismissal of Sizazonke’s appeal. The court held that there was no evidence of repudiation by Eskom, and the action taken against Sizazonke was justified based on their significant and material breaches of safety protocols ([paras 19, 30]).

Consequently, Sizazonke’s claim for damages for loss of profits was deemed unfounded, as awarding such damages would unjustly reward Sizazonke’s own unlawful conduct. Furthermore, Sizazonke was found responsible for the costs incurred during the trial postponement, as they had initiated the request for postponement ([paras 32–33]). The judgment underscored the importance of adherence to contractual terms, particularly concerning safety regulations, and affirmed the lawful authority of Eskom in enforcing compliance among its contractors. The ruling reaffirmed the principle that contractual rights and obligations must be exercised in good faith, aligning with the fundamental doctrines of contract law.
