Source Folder: 2015

# FIRAC Case Note: Mhlongo v S; Nkosi v S [2015] ZACC 19

**F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History** ([paras 1–9])

In the case of *Mhlongo v S; Nkosi v S*, the Constitutional Court of South Africa addressed the admissibility of extra-curial statements made by one accused against co-accused in a criminal trial. The applicants, Boswell John Mhlongo and Alfred Disco Nkosi, were convicted of murder and robbery related to the shooting of Warrant Officer Johannes Dingaan Makuna on 3 August 2002. During their trial before the North West High Court, extra-curial statements made by other co-accused were admitted against them, leading to their convictions.

Initially, a trial-within-a-trial determined the admissibility of these statements, which were deemed admissions rather than confessions and thus admissible under section 3(1)(c) of the Law of Evidence Amendment Act 45 of 1988. The High Court’s conviction of Mhlongo and Nkosi was upheld by the Full Court of the North West High Court and subsequently at the Supreme Court of Appeal. After several years of incarceration, both applicants sought leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court.

**I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions** ([paras 10–16])

The primary constitutional issue arose from the admission of extra-curial statements against co-accused. The applicants contended that such admissions violate their rights to equality and to a fair trial as guaranteed by sections 9(1) and 35(3) of the Constitution of South Africa. The State conceded that the common law prohibits using an accused’s extra-curial statement against a co-accused but argued that section 3 of the Evidence Amendment Act could render such statements admissible if certain conditions were met. The essential question was whether the admission of extra-curial statements against co-accused contravenes constitutional protections.

**R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon** ([paras 17–38])

The court relied heavily on the established common law prohibition against the admissibility of extra-curial statements made by one accused against another. This rule has historical roots in cases such as *R v Turner* and has been reaffirmed in later cases like *Molimi* and *Ndhlovu*. 

Notably, the distinction drawn by *Ndhlovu* allowing the admission of extra-curial admissions (as opposed to confessions) against co-accused was scrutinized. The court also referenced the Law of Evidence Amendment Act, particularly section 3, and its stipulations concerning hearsay evidence. Ultimately, the Constitutional Court reaffirmed the common law principle that only confessions made by an accused can be used against themselves, and that admissions cannot be used against co-accused due to the risks of unreliable testimony.

**A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law** ([paras 39–43])

The court's application of the law emphasized the implications of admitting extra-curial statements on the accused's rights. It assessed the rationality of the distinction made between confessions and admissions as leading to potential inequality and unfair trial implications. The court found that the differentiation lacked rational justification under section 9(1) of the Constitution, asserting that both categories of statements pose similar risks when admitted against co-accused.

The judgment critically evaluated the reliability of the remaining evidence against Mhlongo and Nkosi, dismissing the testimony of co-accused as not meeting standards required for conviction, particularly given the lack of corroborating evidence. The court concluded that with the inadmissibility of extra-curial statements, the remaining evidence was insufficient to uphold the convictions.

**C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications** ([paras 44–44])

The Constitutional Court ultimately restored the pre-*Ndhlovu* common law principle that extra-curial statements of an accused are inadmissible against a co-accused. The court held that the admission of such statements unjustifiably violated the rights of accused individuals under the equality and fair trial clauses of the Constitution. Accordingly, Mhlongo and Nkosi’s convictions were set aside, and they were ordered to be released immediately.

This judgment marks a substantial reaffirmation of the common law safeguards against the unfair use of co-accused statements, safeguarding the integrity of the criminal justice process and the constitutional rights of accused individuals in South Africa.
