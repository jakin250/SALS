Case Note: SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2017

# FIRAC Case Note: The Minister of Home Affairs v Ruta (30/2017) [2017] ZASCA 186

## F - Facts
The case involves the Minister of Home Affairs as the appellant and Alex Ruta as the respondent. The initial detention of the respondent occurred at the Lindela Repatriation Facility while awaiting deportation to Rwanda ([paras 1-2]). The respondent, a Rwandan national, alleged that he had worked as a soldier and later an agent for the National Security Services (NSS) in Rwanda, where he was allegedly instructed to spy on the Rwanda National Congress (RNC), an opposition party ([paras 4-5]). 

In March 2016, after being involved with the Directorate for Priority Crime Investigations (the Hawks), he attempted to pursue asylum but faced various bureaucratic obstacles ([paras 10-12]). On 19 March 2016, he was arrested for possessing a fraudulent Temporary Asylum Seeker Permit, riding a motorcycle without a valid driver’s license, and being an illegal foreigner ([paras 14-15]). The respondent maintained that upon his arrest, he wished to apply for asylum, arguing that he faced persecution should he be deported to Rwanda ([par 22]).

The Gauteng Division of the High Court initially granted the respondent's release, allowing him to apply for asylum. This decision was appealed by the Minister of Home Affairs on the grounds that the respondent had failed to apply for asylum without delay and had fraudulently obtained permits ([paras 1-2, 23-24]).

## I - Issues
The primary legal issue at stake is whether the respondent had meaningfully indicated his intention to apply for asylum in a timely manner to enjoy the protections afforded under § 21 of the Refugees Act 130 of 1998 ([paras 3, 34]). Additional questions include whether the illegal entry and fraudulent actions of the respondent excluded him from being granted asylum and if he was properly regarded as an illegal foreigner under the Immigration Act 13 of 2002 ([paras 34, 48]).

## R - Rules
1. **Refugees Act 130 of 1998**: 
   - § 21(1) provides for the application for asylum, which must be made in person at a Refugee Reception Officer without delay ([para 23]).
   - § 21(4) states that unlawful entry or presence may be condoned if the application for asylum has been made ([para 26]).

2. **Immigration Act 13 of 2002**: 
   - § 32 identifies individuals as illegal foreigners ([para 24]).
   - § 34 allows for the detention and deportation of illegal foreigners ([para 24, 39]).

3. **Relevant Case Law**:
   - The court referenced **Bula & others v Minister of Home Affairs** (ZASCA 209) and **Ersumo v Minister of Home Affairs** (ZASCA 31) to establish the requirement for asylum seekers to apply for protection within a reasonable timeframe and the reproduction of conditions of entry ([paras 31, 32]).

## A - Application
The court emphasized that for protection under the Refugees Act to apply, an asylum seeker must demonstrate an intention to apply for asylum and must make the application without delay ([para 34]). It was asserted that the respondent had ample opportunity to apply following his arrival but failed to do so immediately and instead remained unlawfully in South Africa ([para 33]).

The appellant's argument focused on the failures of the respondent to apply for asylum without delay, aligning this with provisions under §§ 9 and 49 of the Immigration Act, which address illegal entry and presence ([para 34]). Moreover, the court scrutinized the legitimacy of his asylum permits, noting that both were fraudulent, which factored into the misconception of his legal standing ([paras 34, 47]).

Furthermore, the court reasoned that the administration and performance of the Hawks may not suffice to justify the respondent's delay in approaching the authorities regarding his asylum claim, indicating he was not in line with the expected behavior of an asylum seeker ([para 32]).

## C - Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal by the Minister of Home Affairs, asserting that the respondent had not adhered to the requisite conditions under the Refugees Act and had failed to apply for asylum without delay ([paras 34, 35]). The court set aside the lower court's decision, thus denying the respondent’s application without costs due to the circumstances surrounding the case ([para 36]). 

This judgment serves as a critical reflection on the legal standards applicable to asylum applicants in South Africa, particularly regarding the necessity of adhering to procedural requirements under refugee and immigration laws. It underscores the balance between protecting individuals fleeing persecution and asserting the state's sovereignty and immigration controls. The implications are significant, as they delineate the boundaries within which an asylum seeker's intentions must manifest and be acted upon to ensure protection under South African law and align with international standards.
