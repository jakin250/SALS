Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note for Société des Produits Nestlé SA v International Foodstuffs Co & Others (2014) ZASCA 187

### F – Facts
In the case of **Société des Produits Nestlé SA v International Foodstuffs Co & Others** ([1]–[4]), the first appellant, Société des Produits Nestlé SA, and the second appellant, Nestlé South Africa (Pty) Ltd, challenged the first respondent, International Foodstuffs Co, and the second respondent, IFFCO South Africa (Pty) Ltd, over alleged trademark infringements related to their respective chocolate products. Nestlé argued that the shape and name of IFFCO's ‘Break’ chocolate bar were confusingly and deceptively similar to Nestlé's well-known ‘Kit Kat’ chocolate bars, specifically relating to the registered trademarks for its 4-finger and 2-finger wafer shapes. 

Initially, Nestlé sought interdictory relief in the North Gauteng High Court, which dismissed their application. This led both parties to appeal, with Nestlé asserting that IFFCO's usage constituted trademark infringement and passing off, while IFFCO sought to expunge Nestlé's trademark registrations. The core issue centered around the validity of Nestlé's shape trademarks and whether they had been substantially amended during the application process, as well as any alleged deception in the market place ([4]–[6]).

### I – Issues
The key legal issues presented to the court included:
1. Whether Nestlé's registered shape trademarks for its Kit Kat chocolate bars were valid and enforceable against IFFCO’s Break chocolate bars ([7]–[12]).
2. Whether IFFCO's shape and name for its Break chocolate bars infringed upon Nestlé's trademarks or amounted to passing off ([11]–[48]).
3. The interpretation and implications of the modifications made to Nestlé’s trademark application and whether these modifications affected the identity of the trademarks as considered under the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 ([17]–[24]).

### R – Rules
The decision principally rested on several provisions of the **Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993** (the Act), particularly:
- **Section 2(1)**: Defining what constitutes a trademark.
- **Section 34(1)(a)**: Governing the protection against unauthorized use of identical or similar trademarks if such use leads to deception or confusion ([9]–[10]).
- **Section 10(5)**: Outlining unregistrable trademarks that consist exclusively of shapes necessary for technical results ([25]).
- **Section 16(5)**: Allowing the Registrar to permit amendments to trademark applications, emphasizing the need for such amendments not to substantially affect the identity of the trademark ([17]).
  
Relevant case law was also consulted, particularly comparisons to previous decisions concerning trademark validity and deceptive similarities, such as ***Beecham Group plc & another v Triomed (Pty) Ltd*** ([11]), ***Verimark (Pty) Ltd v BMW AG*** ([40]), and interpretations of trademark consumer perception established in ***Plascon Evans Paints (Pty) Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd*** ([12]).

### A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal evaluated the applicability of legal principles regarding the shape trademarks’aidentical depiction and consumer perception. The court agreed that the two-dimensional representations presented by Nestlé could be reasonably interpreted by ordinary consumers as indicative of three-dimensional shapes, acknowledging Nestlé's extensive marketing history ([13]–[14]).
  
In examining IFFCO's use, the court considered whether IFFCO’s chocolate bars were, through their shape and marketing, confusingly similar to those of Nestlé. The court found that the consumers were likely to perceive IFFCO's marketing and product shapes as being directly linked to Nestlé's established trademarks based on both visual and contextual evidence offered in surveys ([38]–[46]).

Further, IFFCO's arguments related to the technical necessities of the shape of chocolate bars were carefully dissected, with the court holding that while some aspects might functionally arise from production requirements, the presence of distinctive features could afford Nestlé trademark protection ([27]–[34]).

### C – Conclusion
The court concluded that Nestlé had successfully established the likelihood of confusion among consumers concerning the shapes of the products, leading to a ruling in favor of Nestlé on the significant issues of trademark infringement. Thus, the Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the lower court's decisions ([1]–[4]), granting Nestlé an interdict against IFFCO regarding the use of its registered trademarks.

This ruling reinforces the protection afforded to well-known trademarks and their distinct features under South African law, particularly emphasizing the consumer's perception of trademark use in determining trademark infringement claims. Consequently, the case was significant in addressing not only the enforceability of shape trademarks but also broader interpretations concerning the rights of established brands in competitive marketplaces.
