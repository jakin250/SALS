Constitutional Court

Ngomane and Others v Govan Mbeki Municipality [2016] ZACC 31

The case arises from an application for leave to appeal by Bongane Ngomane and other occupiers against the Govan Mbeki Municipality (the Municipality), concerning eviction from municipally owned land.

Constitutional Court 2016 [2016] ZACC 31

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–8])

The case arises from an application for leave to appeal by Bongane Ngomane and other occupiers against the Govan Mbeki Municipality (the Municipality), concerning eviction from municipally owned land. The High Court had previously ruled against the applicants, who were informal occupants of land zoned for agricultural use, and upheld an eviction order against them ([para 1]). The applicants argued that their eviction was conducted in contravention of their rights under section 26 of the Constitution and the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE Act).

The occupation of the disputed properties—Extensions 21 and 25 Kinross and the farm Zondagskraal—began when the applicants, evicted from previous accommodations, sought refuge in these areas ([paras 2–4]). The Municipality’s first eviction attempt was in 2013, leading to a conflict that escalated into legal actions as the applicants found themselves repeatedly displaced ([para 2]). The High Court later issued a rule nisi (an interim order) demanding a more comprehensive approach from the Municipality prior to proceeding with evictions, touching upon procedural issues related to the PIE Act ([paras 6–8]). This prior judgment reflected an attempt to reconcile the rights of the applicants with the Municipality’s obligation to act lawfully ([para 7]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 9–12])

The central legal questions raised by the applicants include:

  1. Whether the Municipality’s actions undermined the procedural protections afforded by the PIE Act and the constitutional guarantee of access to housing under section 26 of the Constitution ([para 9]);
  2. Whether the High Court erred in permitting the amendment of the Municipality’s eviction application without proper consultation and notice to the affected parties ([paras 10–12]);
  3. Whether the absence of meaningful engagement between the Municipality and the occupiers before initiating eviction proceedings constitutes a violation of the applicants' rights ([para 12]).

The applicants argue that these questions warrant the Constitutional Court's intervention due to their significant implications on the rights of unlawful occupants and the Municipality's obligations.

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 13–14])

The Court focused on key legislative and constitutional provisions to navigate the issues, notably:

  1. Section 26 of the Constitution guarantees the right to have access to adequate housing. This provision serves as a basis to argue against arbitrary eviction practices ([para 13]);
  2. The PIE Act, particularly sections concerning the procedural requirements for eviction, including section 4(2), mandates that occupiers be afforded fair notice and the opportunity for meaningful engagement prior to eviction ([para 14]);
  3. Occupiers of 51 Olivia Road v City of Johannesburg [2008] ZACC 1 ([para 15]) provided judicial precedent emphasizing the necessity of meaningful engagement as a prerequisite to lawful evictions.

The Court reaffirmed that these guidelines serve to protect the rights of vulnerable groups against unjust displacement.

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 15–16])

The Court, while acknowledging that the High Court had made attempts at reconciling the competing interests of the Municipality and the applicants, determined that it failed on two significant accounts. First, the High Court's decision negated the applicants' pathway to meaningful engagement prior to initiating eviction proceedings, thereby undermining their constitutional rights ([para 15]). It was noted that the applicants had valid grounds to contest their eviction and should have been able to participate in a meaningful dialogue regarding their living conditions.

Furthermore, the Court observed that the practitioners of the law must ensure that procedural frameworks established to protect occupants are not merely facade tactics employed to expedite evictions ([para 16]). There was no adequate process provided for the applicants to dispute their eviction based on the Municipalities’ failure to engage with them, illustrating a disregard of their rights.

The Court corrected the procedural path by mandating a specific protocol for the Municipality to follow when engaging with the applicants in order to resolve the issues at stake while ensuring adherence to constitutional imperatives.

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 17–20])

The Constitutional Court granted leave for the appeal and ruled in favor of the applicants, emphasizing the Municipality’s obligation to facilitate a meaningful engagement process with the occupiers ([para 17]). The original High Court order was set aside, and the Court established a new framework under which the Municipality must actively involve the applicants in discussions regarding their housing status and rights.

The ruling reiterated the importance of procedural fairness and the necessity of meaningful engagement in eviction matters, underlining that neglecting these requirements constitutes a severe violation of constitutional rights ([para 18]). The result reflects a commitment to ensuring that the rights of the most vulnerable populations are not overridden by administrative processes, establishing a precedent that enhances protections against unlawful eviction.

The implications of this judgment extend beyond the immediate parties, signaling a broader acknowledgment of the need for local governments to engage transparently and fairly with all occupants to uphold their rights under the Constitution and relevant laws ([paras 19–20]).
