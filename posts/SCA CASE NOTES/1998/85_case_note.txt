Case Note: IN THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 1998

# FIRAC Case Note: Santam Limited v. M O H A M E D NAEEM SAYED

## F – Facts 
The case revolves around a motor vehicle accident that occurred on 31 August 1988, resulting in the respondent, M O H A M E D NAEEM SAYED, sustaining injuries and subsequently seeking damages from the appellant, Santam Limited, in accordance with the Motor Vehicle Accidents Act 84 of 1986 ([paras 2–3]). 

Upon filing a claim with Santam on 28 June 1990, the claim was acknowledged but the appellant failed to object to its validity, which triggered the suspension of the statutory two-year prescription period, set to expire on 30 August 1990 ([para 4]). An offer of settlement was made on 24 September 1992, but was not accepted. Correspondence between the parties continued, resulting in further extensions of the prescription period granted by Santam ([paras 6–10]). 

However, when the summons for the claim was eventually issued in November 1993, Santam raised a special plea of prescription, claiming that the period had expired ([para 11]). The primary legal issues regarded whether Santam's without-prejudice offers constituted valid offers under the Act, which would affect the prescription timeline ([para 12]).

## I – Issues
The main legal questions before the court were as follows:

1. Did the without-prejudice offers made by Santam to settle the claim interrupt the running of prescription as envisaged under sections 14(2)(b) and 15(2) of the Motor Vehicle Accidents Act?
2. Was there an implied extension of the prescription period beyond the agreed dates due to these offers, and did this give the respondent sufficient time to institute legal proceedings before the claim prescribed? ([paras 11–13]).

## R – Rules
The legal framework applicable to this case is provided by the Motor Vehicle Accidents Act, specifically sections 14 and 15, which address the prescription period for claims and stipulate conditions under which this period may be suspended or interrupted ([paras 3–4]). 

The relevant statutory provisions are as follows:

- Section 14(1)(a) specifies that claims prescribed after two years unless interrupted as outlined in subsection 14(2) ([para 3]).
- Section 14(2)(b) states that a claimant's prescription period shall be suspended if, within 60 days of receipt of the claim, the appointed agent does not repudiate liability or convey an offer of settlement ([para 4]).
- Courts have established through case law that a without-prejudice offer may still be treated as a valid offer under the Act for the purposes of interrupting prescription, as seen in *Santam Insurance Ltd v Williams* 1992 (2) SA 273 (A) ([para 10]).

## A – Application
The court analyzed both the explicit provisions of the Motor Vehicle Accidents Act and the application of those provisions to the facts at hand. It found substantial merit in the argument that the without-prejudice nature of Santam's offers does not negate their effect on prescription under section 14(2)(b) ([paras 12–15]). 

The trial court held that because the offers constituted genuine attempts at settlement, they were valid for resetting the prescription timeline. The expiration of the 90-day period following the first offer confirmed the resumption of prescription on certain timeline parameters. However, the appellant countered this by arguing that the parties had implicitly moved beyond the statutory framework post-February 1993, as extensions had been agreed upon that were outside the original statutory provisions ([paras 16–24]).

Additionally, the attempt to assert estoppel on the basis of communications between the parties did not succeed, as the elements of reasonable expectation were not met in this context ([paras 25–28]).

## C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal held that the trial court erred in its judgment regarding the applicability of prescription due to the offers made by the appellant being interpreted as valid interruptions to the prescriptive period. The court ruled that the special plea of prescription raised by Santam should have been upheld, effectively extinguishing the respondent's claim, given that the statutory period had indeed lapsed as calculated ([paras 27–28]).

The case underscores the importance of careful adherence to statutory provisions concerning claims management within the context of motor vehicle accidents. It confirms the binding nature of prescription laws and elucidates how without-prejudice offers can impact temporal factors in claims, although they do not preclude the inherent time frames established by statute unless explicitly stated or extended. The implications of this ruling reinforce the necessity for clarity in communications and agreements regarding legal claims, especially in the context of period extensions and prescriptive timelines.
