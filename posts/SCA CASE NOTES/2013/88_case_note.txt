Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2013

# FIRAC Case Note: City of Tshwane v Blom 433/2012

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–6])**

This case arises from a dispute regarding the interpretation of the Local Government: Municipal Property Rates Act 6 of 2004 (the Rates Act), specifically concerning the authority of the City of Tshwane (the appellant) to categorize a property owned by Kawari Beleggings CC (the second respondent) as 'non-permitted use' for the purpose of levying municipal rates. The first respondent, Marius Blom & GC Germishuizen Incorporated, leased the property in question and operated a law firm there, despite it being zoned for residential use under the appellant's Town Planning Scheme ([paras 1–5]). 

Upon receiving a rates invoice totaling R171,000, the respondents sought judicial relief, arguing that the Rates Act does not support a rating category of 'illegal use' or 'non-permitted use' and claiming that the appellant unlawfully imposed higher levies that exceed those charged for properties with similar zoning ([para 2]). The court a quo initially ruled that while the appellant could create additional categories, it was impermissible to classify a property as 'illegal use' since this would retroactively validate illegal use as lawful ([para 4]). 

The legal backdrop includes provisions from the Rates Act and the appellant's rates policy, which introduced the 'non-permitted use' category on July 1, 2008. The valuation process for municipalities was to reflect the classification of property use according to the criteria set out in the Rates Act ([para 6]).

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–3])**

The pivotal issue in this appeal is whether the City of Tshwane, under the Rates Act, possesses the authority to add a category for 'non-permitted use' to the existing list of rateable property categories stipulated in s 8(2) of the Rates Act. A further question arises regarding the legality of levying rates higher than normal for a property categorized under this designation, as well as the notion of whether such an action constitutes a penalty without due process ([paras 1, 3, 20]). 

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 6–10])**

The relevant legal framework includes s 229(1)(a) of the Constitution of South Africa, which allows municipalities to impose rates on property, and s 8 of the Rates Act, which outlines the criteria by which different categories of rateable property may be determined ([paras 7–9]). 

Section 8(1) permits municipalities to levy different rates based on the use and permitted use of property, while s 8(2) lists permissible categories such as residential, industrial, and business properties ([paras 9–10]). The legal discourse includes interpretations of terms like 'includes,' which traditionally allows for a discretionary interpretation by municipalities. 

Notable case law that informs the court's reasoning includes *De Reuck v Director of Public Prosecutions* and *Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality*, which emphasize the importance of context in statutory interpretation to determine legislative intent ([para 12]).

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 16–23])**

The court found that the appellant was indeed authorized to create the 'non-permitted use' category, thereby rebutting the court a quo’s interpretation that sought to impose limitations on the municipality’s authority. The court relied heavily on the flexible interpretation of 'use' as extending to 'non-permitted use,' emphasizing that this categorization is vital for effective municipal governance and property management ([paras 16, 17]). The interpretation suggested that as long as the criteria for the categories were consistent with legislative intent and the overarching principles of equity, the municipality had the autonomy to establish rating categories that reflect the actual use of the property.

Moreover, the assertion by the respondents that levying a higher rate imposed a penalty without due process was dismissed. The mechanisms for contesting property valuation and categorization were deemed sufficient under the Rates Act, allowing property owners an opportunity to appeal their assessment. Thus, the court affirmed that the municipality's actions did not violate constitutional protections, specifically the right to fair administrative action ([para 20, 22]).

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 24–26])**

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal held that the City of Tshwane was justified in adopting the 'non-permitted use' category and that the levying of higher rates under that classification did not constitute an unconstitutional penalty. This ruling reinstated the discretion of municipalities to define rateable categories aligned with actual property use, reinforcing the principle of local governance autonomy.

The court ruled in favor of the appellant, rescinding the previous orders relating to the invalid categorization and costs, and determined that both the original judgments and the underlying legal interpretations were erroneous ([paras 24, 26]). The implications of this case extend to the municipal policy landscape in South Africa, highlighting the latitude that local authorities have in establishing property classifications and their corresponding rating mechanisms.
