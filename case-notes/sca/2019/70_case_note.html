FIRAC Case Note: Z F Stoffberg NO & others v City of Cape Town (1325/2017) [2019] ZASCA 70

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 70 #### F - Facts The case concerns the property

FIRAC Case Note: Z F Stoffberg NO & others v City of Cape Town (1325/2017) [2019] ZASCA 70

F - Facts

The case concerns the property known as Farm 77 (Baas Ariesfontein Outspan), located near Melkbosstrand, in the Western Cape, which is registered in the name of the City of Cape Town (the City) but occupied by the appellants ([1]). The appellants, who consist of trustees of the Keert de Koe Trust and the fifth appellant, Mr. Nicolaas Stoffberg (the son of the fifth appellant), argue that the fifth appellant acquired ownership of the property through the doctrine of acquisitive prescription under the Prescription Act 18 of 1943 (the 1943 Act) ([2]).

The property has been designated a public outspan since at least 1832 ([3]), and on 2 August 1946, it was transferred to the Divisional Council of the Cape (the City's predecessor) under a Crown Grant that limited its use specifically for public outspanning ([5]). The fifth appellant began farming in the area with his family in 1947, later taking over the operations entirely ([6]–[10]). Despite the expiration of various lease agreements from 1976 to 2001, the appellants maintained physical occupancy and paid rent, albeit sometimes under expired leases ([8]–[9]).

In 2007, the City initiated eviction proceedings, prompting the appellants to counterclaim for ownership based on the prescription ([11]). The Western Cape High Court dismissed this counterclaim, prompting the appeal at the Supreme Court of Appeal ([12]).

I - Issues

The central issue on appeal is whether the High Court correctly ruled that the appellants failed to establish that the fifth appellant acquired ownership of the property through acquisitive prescription as per the requirements of section 2 of the 1943 Act ([2], [12]). Specifically, the court looked into whether the appellants demonstrated the necessary element of continuous possession for 30 years without the consent of the owner (nec vi, nec clam, nec precario) ([14]).

R - Rules

Section 2 of the Prescription Act 18 of 1943 outlines the requirements for acquisitive prescription, including continuous possession for 30 years (nec vi, nec clam, nec precario) ([13]). The legal standard set by case law stipulates that the possession must indicate an intention to possess as an owner (animus domini) and that the possession must face no legal obstacles from the rightful owner ([14]). Additionally, public servitudes impose stricter requirements on claims of ownership over public lands ([15]).

The principle indicated by Voet clarifies that public roads or lands subject to public rights require strong adverse use to establish a prescription claim ([17]). This principle also dictates that mere use without overtly claiming ownership may not suffice to meet the criteria for acquiescence within a public context.

A - Application

The court reviewed the appellants’ claims to possessory rights based on several actions taken over years, mainly by the fifth appellant's family ([19]), including grazing, the sale of firewood, and land cultivation ([23]–[26]). However, the court found that:

  1. Acts of Possession: The evidence presented showed that acts of possession occurred jointly with other family members and did not firmly establish the fifth appellant's exclusive claim ([19]).
  2. Timing of Claim: Since various lease agreements intervened, the court concluded that the required 30-year prescription period could not have been satisfied as the lease agreements were acknowledged by the fifth appellant himself ([20]-[22]).
  3. Public Servitude: The designation of the property as a public outspan created significant limitations on the ability to claim ownership through acquisitive prescription. Acts that may have seemed possessory were interpreted as consistent with public use rather than indicative of ownership ([15], [16]).
  4. Insufficient Evidence: The acts of cutting wood and grazing were not adequately substantiated to demonstrate possessory intent according to the court’s interpretation of animus domini. The sporadic nature of these actions did not meet the continuous requirement inherent in the 1943 Act ([24]–[25]).

Ultimately, the court concluded that the appellants failed to prove the requisite continuous possession of the property for 30 years, thus denying the claim of ownership through prescription ([22]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal with costs, emphasizing that the appellants did not demonstrate the necessary continuous possession of the property under the Prescription Act ([27]). The ruling reinforced the strict standards for asserting ownership through prescription, particularly for properties designated for public use. The implications of this decision underscore the legal obstacles faced by parties seeking to claim ownership of land subjected to public interests, affirming that both the intention and nature of possession must align with legal precedents and statutory requirements.
