Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

### Case Note: RTS Industries and Others v Technical Systems (Pty) Ltd and Another [2022] ZASCA 64

#### FIRAC Case Note

#### F – Facts
In **RTS Industries and Others v Technical Systems (Pty) Ltd and Another** [2022] ZASCA 64 ([paras 1–11]), the appeal emerged from a judgment handed down by the Western Cape Division of the High Court. The original proceedings stemmed from alleged breaches of a 2015 court order which followed a claim by the respondents for the misappropriation of confidential information related to the auger manufacturing process. The third appellant, Mr. Christiaan Arnoldus Kurtz, had previously been employed by the first respondent and was accused of unlawfully using confidential information to compete against them after leaving his employment. 

The respondents discovered evidence of copyright infringement relating to 1179 technical drawings owned by the first respondent and claimed that the appellants had unlawfully manufactured and sold flat wire and augers. Consequently, an interim interdict was granted by the High Court on 9 March 2020, prohibiting the appellants from engaging in these activities while the process outlined in the 2015 order was completed. The appellants contended that the relief granted by the High Court was inappropriate and sought to appeal the interim interdict.

#### I – Issues
The principal legal question raised in this appeal was whether the 2020 order of the High Court was appealable ([paras 21–22]). Further, the appeal raised significant issues regarding the interpretation of interim interdicts and the criteria governing their appealability, particularly in light of the interests of justice ([paras 23–24]). The court was required to assess whether the 2020 order was indeed final in effect, despite its designation as an interim order, and to consider the implications of the parties' proceedings regarding alleged contempt of court ([paras 10–11]).

#### R – Rules
The legal framework in question was articulated through several established principles. The South African courts have a long-standing reluctance to permit appeals against interim orders, which are often deemed not final and alterable by the lower courts ([para 28]). Notably, the constitutional standard articulated in **City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality v Afriforum** ([2016] ZACC 19, para 41) allows for the consideration of appealability of an interim order if it best serves the interests of justice, notwithstanding common law impediments. Furthermore, the statutory framework per the **Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013** (s 19) outlines the conditions under which appeals can incorporate further evidence, emphasizing exceptional circumstances.

#### A – Application
In evaluating the appeal, the court engaged with the facts of the case, particularly the appellants' breach of the 2015 order, the nature of the orders sought by the respondents, and the public interest involved in maintaining order in the marketplace ([para 22]). The court noted that the 2020 order was inherently interim, aimed explicitly at upholding compliance with the 2015 agreement pending a final determination of any disputes arising from it. It was concluded that the nature of the order did not dispose of any substantial portion of the main relief sought, as compliance requirements were still unresolved ([para 29]).

The court further addressed the appellants' argument regarding the failure of the High Court to issue a rule nisi, asserting that since full arguments had already been presented before the High Court, the issuance of a rule nisi would have been unnecessary. The court found that the distinction between interim and final relief was pivotal; thus, seeking appeal against the interim order was procedurally inappropriate and would invite judicial inefficiencies ([para 29]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the 2020 order was not appealable and thereby struck the appeal from the roll. The court underscored the appellants' responsibility for costs associated with the proceedings, given their unsuccessful challenge of an interim order ([paras 30]). The ruling emphasized the importance of adherence to established legal principles around interim interdicts, expecting parties to comply with earlier orders until fully adjudicated. This case reaffirmed the South African judicial system's commitment to preventing piecemeal litigation and ensuring that court resources are efficiently utilized. 

The case thus has broader implications regarding the enforceability of interim orders and the limits of appellate review in the context of ongoing compliance disputes, maintaining a delicate balance between parties’ interests and judicial efficiency.
