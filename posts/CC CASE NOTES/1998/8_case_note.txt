# FIRAC Case Note: Robert Van Nell & Sam Bekker v The State

## F – Facts
The case of **Robert Van Nell** and **Sam Bekker** (First and Second Appellants) v **The State** (Respondent) comes before the Constitutional Court of South Africa to confirm an order of constitutional invalidity instigated by the Northern Cape High Court regarding **section 20 of the Drugs and Drugs Trafficking Act** 140 of 1992. This section introduces a 'reverse onus' clause, which presumes possession of drugs on any accused person in proximity to drugs discovered during a criminal investigation, unless the accused demonstrates otherwise ([1]).

The Northern Cape High Court found that section 20 contravenes the rights established by section 35(3)(h) of the **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa** of 1996. Thus, the court ordered that the presumption of possession constituted an infringement of the right to a fair trial and disregarded reasonable doubt in legal proceedings. Concurrently, another case, **Mello and Another v The State**, was also pending, involving the same legal issues regarding section 20. Ultimately, the Constitutional Court’s decision in this case would refer back to the order in the Mello case for guidance on how to proceed ([2]).

## I – Issues
The primary issue presented to the Constitutional Court was whether section 20 of the Drugs and Drugs Trafficking Act, which imposes a reverse onus on the accused in drug-related offenses, is constitutionally valid under section 35(3)(h) of the 1996 Constitution. Specifically, the court needed to determine if presuming possession of an illegal substance without adequate proof contradicted the right to a fair trial ([1]).

## R – Rules
In addressing the issue of constitutional validity, the court relied on applicable constitutional provisions, notably:
- **Section 35(3)(h)** of the 1996 Constitution, which ensures the right to a fair trial, including the presumption of innocence until proven guilty.
- Additionally, the court drew from prior rulings, particularly the Mello case, where section 20 was similarly criticized for its constitutional implications ([2]).

## A – Application
The court's application of the rules to the facts centered around the implications of the reverse onus created by section 20 of the Act. The presumption that an accused found in proximity to drugs is deemed to have possession unless disproven creates a significant barrier against the presumption of innocence — a fundamental tenet of a fair legal process.

The court underscored the necessity to respect the Constitution's emphasis on individual rights and the judicial obligation to uphold these principles in the face of legislative presumption. By reaffirming the findings made in the Mello case, the Constitutional Court articulated a clear stance against provisions that erroneously shift the burden of proof onto the accused rather than maintaining the prosecution's obligation to establish guilt beyond a reasonable doubt, thereby upholding the principles of justice and a fair trial ([1], [2]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court confirmed the Northern Cape High Court's order declaring section 20 of the Drugs and Drugs Trafficking Act unconstitutional, aligning with the interpretation of the law as it relates to constitutional rights. The judgment emphasized that any legislative provisions leading to a presumption of guilt in the absence of proof beyond reasonable doubt are impermissible.

The decision reinstates the importance of individual rights in the criminal justice system, particularly emphasizing the presumption of innocence. The court referred the matter back to the Northern Cape High Court to execute its order per the Mello case, thus providing clarity and consistency in judicial processes regarding drug-related offenses across South Africa ([3]). This landmark case reinforces the judiciary's role in safeguarding fundamental rights as encapsulated in the Constitution.
