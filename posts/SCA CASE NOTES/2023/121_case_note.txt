Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: Jacolien Barnard NO and Another v National Consumer Tribunal and Another (940/2021) [2023] ZASCA 121

## F – Facts

The case revolves around an investigation initiated by the National Credit Regulator (NCR) into the business activities of CMR Group (Pty) Ltd (CMR) regarding its “Pawn your car and still drive it” scheme. This investigation, which began on 10 March 2017, uncovered that CMR was providing loans to consumers while keeping the registration of their vehicles. CMR’s practices violated several provisions of the National Credit Act 34 of 2005 (NCA), specifically concerning excessive interest rates and the lack of affordability assessments ([1–3]).

In response to these findings, the NCR sought a declaratory order against CMR in the National Consumer Tribunal (the Tribunal), which ultimately ruled in favor of NCR on 12 August 2019, declaring CMR's agreements as reckless, canceling its registration as a credit provider, and instructing it to reimburse consumers ([4–5]). The Tribunal's order included a requirement for CMR to appoint an independent auditor to review consumer fees ([5]).

Subsequently, a special resolution was passed to place CMR into voluntary liquidation, and provisional liquidators, the applicants Jacolien Barnard and Beatrice Linda Mills, were appointed ([6–7]). On 30 July 2019, neither CMR nor the liquidators attended the tribunal hearing, which led the Tribunal to proceed in their absence ([8]). 

After the High Court dismissed the liquidators’ appeal against the Tribunal's decision, the liquidators applied for special leave to appeal in this Court. Their application faced procedural hurdles due to late filing, which led to the preliminary need for condonation ([9–10]). The significant question arose as to whether the liquidators could appeal, given that they had not participated in the Tribunal hearing, which the High Court concluded was a jurisdictional requirement for an appeal under section 148(2)(b) of the NCA ([16–18]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues addressed in this case are as follows:

1. Whether the Tribunal had the authority to grant orders against a company that was under liquidation and whether the orders infringed on the rights of the liquidators appointed to manage the liquidation process ([14–15]).
2. Whether the liquidators were correct in seeking to appeal the Tribunal's orders under section 148(2)(b) of the NCA, despite not having participated in the Tribunal proceedings ([18]).
3. The broader implications of the interaction between the liquidation process under the Companies Act and the regulatory framework governing the NCR and Tribunal ([33–40]).

## R – Rules

Several statutory provisions and legal principles played crucial roles in the Court's analysis:

1. **National Credit Act 34 of 2005 (NCA)**: Specifically, section 148(2)(b) prescribes that only participants in the Tribunal hearing can appeal its decisions. This provision emphasizes the necessity of participation as a jurisdictional prerequisite for appeals ([19–22]).
   
2. **Companies Act 61 of 1973**: Section 359 provides that once a company is in liquidation, all civil proceedings against it are suspended until a liquidator is appointed, which establishes the need for the liquidators to be joined in any resulting litigation ([45–46]).

3. **Rules for the Conduct of Matters Before the National Consumer Tribunal**: Rule 24 allows a presiding member to continue proceedings in the absence of a party, provided appropriate notice has been given ([25]).

4. **Precedents on Jurisdiction and Participation**: The case of *National Credit Regulator v Lewis Stores (Pty) Ltd* affirmed that participation in the Tribunal's proceedings is critical for jurisdictional requirements in appellate matters ([12]).

## A – Application

The Court analyzed whether the Tribunal's failure to join the liquidators as parties to the proceedings led to a jurisdictional defect, thus invalidating the orders issued against CMR. The Court found that the Tribunal erroneously assumed it could proceed with the hearing without the liquidators’ involvement, thus ignoring their significant interests and rights resulting from CMR’s liquidation ([44–49]):

1. The liquidators had an intrinsic role due to the legal effects of the liquidation order, which transformed CMR into a party under management by the liquidators. The failure to join them meant the Tribunal acted beyond its jurisdiction ([56]).

2. The Court upheld the strict interpretation of “participation” under section 148(2)(b), requiring physical attendance or representation during Tribunal hearings to qualify for appellate rights. The applicants’ involvement through a prior answering affidavit did not meet this threshold ([19–24]).

3. The high court had correctly declared that the liquidators could not appeal under section 148 as they had not participated in the proceedings. The appropriate course of action for them, had they wished to contest the Tribunal’s orders, would have been to seek rescission rather than appeal, according to section 165 of the NCA ([27–30]).

4. Additionally, the possible conflict between the findings of the Tribunal and the rights afforded to creditors under the Companies Act was further stressed; an order made against a company in liquidation could lead to inequities among creditors ([57–60]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately ruled that the liquidators were not entitled to appeal the Tribunal's decision due to their failure to participate in the hearing, as required by section 148(2)(b) of the NCA. The court invoked its discretion under its inherent powers to regulate proceedings and declined to permit the appeal due to a lack of jurisdiction ([31–32]). 

The implications of this ruling highlight critical issues regarding the intersection of consumer protection legislation and insolvency law in South Africa, particularly concerning the necessity of joint participation by liquidators in matters affecting a company's liquidation status and ongoing legal obligations. This case reinforces the precision required in navigating procedural law within concurrent jurisdictions, serving as a cautionary tale for similar future cases involving parties undergoing liquidation.

This decision further clarifies the legal framework surrounding the duties of liquidators and the jurisdictional boundaries of the Tribunal under the NCA, ensuring that rights of all parties, particularly those of creditors, are preserved and respected in insolvency matters ([41–42]).
