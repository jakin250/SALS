# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title**: Fono and Another v Port St Johns Municipality  
**Neutral Citation**: [2024] ZASCA 161  
**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Judges**: Mocumie, Mabindla-Boqwana, Smith JJA, Mjali, Mantame AJJA  
**Heard**: 5 September 2024  
**Delivered**: 22 November 2024  
**Case No**: 1271/2022  

## F – Facts ([paras 1–7])
This case arose from an appeal against a decision made by the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, Mthatha, wherein the full court had upheld a prior judgment that ordered the first appellant, Luxolo Fono, to demolish a building he commenced constructing on Erf 7, Port St Johns, without approved building plans. The building was intended for use as tourist accommodation, and its construction was executed by Mr. Fono despite notice from the Port St Johns Municipality, which stated that he was in violation of numerous municipal and statutory regulations.

The Caguba Tribal Authority, the second appellant, was involved in the matter, asserting tribal rights over the property. Following an inquiry by municipal officials on August 20, 2018, which confirmed that construction was underway without the necessary approvals, legal actions were initiated by the municipality seeking both a declaratory order on the unlawfulness of the construction and an interdict against Mr. Fono.

## I – Issues ([paras 19–22])
Several critical legal questions were raised in this case, including:
1. Whether the Building Standards Act 103 of 1977 (the Building Act) was applicable to properties within the territory of the former Transkei, particularly in light of arguments that it was not included in the legislative framework applicable to that area following the enactment of the Justice Laws Rationalisation Act 18 of 1996.
2. Whether the finding of the full court regarding the non-compliance with the Spatial Planning and Land Use Management Act 16 of 2013 (SPLUMA) was justifiable, especially considering the arguments surrounding the validity of the municipality's by-laws and their applicability to the property in question.
3. The appropriate remedy against Mr. Fono's non-compliance with the statutory requirements, particularly whether a demolition order was warranted or if lesser remedial actions could be pursued.

## R – Rules ([paras 20–23, 30–31])
The following statutory provisions and case law informed the court's analysis and decision:
- **Spatial Planning and Land Use Management Act 16 of 2013 (SPLUMA)**: Specifically, section 33(1) which mandates that no person shall develop land without submitting the required application to the relevant municipality.
- **Building Standards Act 103 of 1977**: Stipulates the requirements for construction and the associated penalties for non-compliance.
- **Lester v Ndlambe Municipality**: Provided precedent regarding the application of the Building Standards Act and emphasized the interpretation that promotes the Bill of Rights, particularly concerning property rights. 
- **Walele v City of Cape Town**: Reinforced the necessity for municipal regulations to protect socio-economic rights affected by unlawful developments. 

## A – Application ([paras 24–38])
The court identified several misjudgments from the full court regarding both the facts of the case and the application of the law. Initially, the full court's determination that the Building Act applied to the former Transkei was notably flawed due to its reliance on the mistaken interpretation of precedent cases, such as Lester. The SCA indicated that it is a presumption that excluded statutes were purposefully left out, not due to oversight. 

There were additional concerns regarding the municipality's failure to provide detailed accounts of the alleged violations and enact relevant by-laws to govern construction activities.Given these legal ambiguities, it was found that a demolition order may not be appropriate without a thorough examination into compliance with the SPLUMA and injury mitigation for Mr. Fono.

The court further noted that while Mr. Fono admitted to breaching SPLUMA, he sought rectification through compliance rather than destruction. The SPLUMA affords courts a wider discretion in terms of remedies than perceived under the Building Act, enabling the court to issue preventative measures rather than relying solely on demolition.

## C – Conclusion ([paras 39–41])
The Supreme Court of Appeal partially upheld Mr. Fono's appeal by finding that the previous judgments did not adequately consider the complexities and nuances surrounding the applicable legal frameworks and the enforcement actions of the municipality. The court ordered that:
1. The judgment of the full court was set aside.
2. Mr. Fono’s conduct in continuing construction without requisite approvals was declared unlawful, and he was interdicted from further construction until compliance with SPLUMA was established.
3. Mr. Fono was granted a period to submit the required applications and was encouraged to engage with municipal authorities as stipulated under SPLUMA's framework.

Each party was ordered to bear their own costs, reflecting the ambiguous legal landscape that both parties navigated throughout the appeals process. This case underscores the intricate relationship between statutory compliance, tribal land rights, and the administrative capacity of local municipalities in rural South Africa, particularly as they pertain to property development and conservation of local communities.
