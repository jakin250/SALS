Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

### Case Note: City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality and Another v Moipone Fleet (Pty) Ltd

#### FIRAC Case Note

**F – Facts** 

This case arises from an appeal against the decision of Mokose AJ of the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria. The court had found the City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality (the City) and its City Manager, Dr. Moeketsi Emmanuel Mosola, in contempt of a previous court order, known as the Davis order, issued by Davis AJ on 29 March 2017. The Davis order mandated the City to comply with its obligations under a Public Private Partnership Agreement (PPA) with Moipone Fleet (Pty) Ltd (the Respondent), requiring the City to procure vehicles exclusively from Moipone Fleet pending the resolution of a dispute regarding the validity of the PPA ([paras 1–2]).

Moipone Fleet accused the City of breaching the PPA by ordering vehicles from other suppliers, thereby arguing the City was not acting in accordance with the exclusivity clause (clause 39 of the PPA) that required all fleet requirements to be sourced exclusively from Moipone Fleet during the duration of the agreement ([paras 5–8]). The City contended that the PPA had lapsed and thus was justified in procuring vehicles from other providers, arguing that the exclusivity clause pertained only to leased vehicles rather than purchases ([paras 12–13]).

On 27 May 2020, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) delivered its judgment, focusing on whether the contempt finding was justified and whether the interim interdict against the City should stand ([paras 14–30]).

**I – Issues** 

The principal legal question in this appeal was whether the High Court erred in finding the City in contempt of the Davis order and in issuing an interim interdict against the City prohibiting it from procuring vehicles from third parties. Specifically, the court was tasked with determining if the conduct of the City constituted a deliberate and mala fide breach of the Davis order and whether the interpretation of clause 39 of the PPA was appropriate ([paras 4, 16–19]).

**R – Rules** 

The legal rules relevant to this case are derived from interpretations of the PPA, particularly clauses establishing the exclusivity of sourcing vehicles and obligations arising from previous court orders. The contempt of court doctrine stipulates that for a finding of contempt to be valid, the breach of the order must be both willful and mala fide. Additionally, established case law, including *Fakie NO v CCII Systems (Pty) Ltd* ([2006] 4 SA 326 (SCA)) mandates proof beyond reasonable doubt for imposing contempt sanctions ([paras 16–19, 22]).

In terms of the interpretation of contractual clauses, the SCA referred to established principles in *Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality* ([2012] ZASCA 13), which assert that contracts must be understood within the context of their purpose and the framework of the entire agreement ([paras 23–24]).

**A – Application** 

The SCA held that the interpretation of clause 39, which stipulates that the City must source all fleet requirements from Moipone Fleet, did not extend to outright purchases, as this form of procurement falls outside the exclusivity intended by the parties. The evidence suggested that the exclusivity clause pertained solely to leasing agreements, contrary to Moipone Fleet's assertions that it encompassed all forms of acquisition, including purchasing ([paras 24–28]).

The court found that the conduct of the City in procuring vehicles directly through contracts facilitated by National Treasury did not constitute a breach of the Davis order, noting that the PPA related specifically to leasing arrangements. The SCA emphasized that Mokose AJ's interpretation was flawed as the purchase of vehicles was not an issue that had been adjudicated in the context of the Davis order ([paras 26–28]). Additionally, the court underscored that the City Manager’s actions did not warrant contempt findings due to the lack of a clear breach ([paras 29–30]).

**C – Conclusion** 

Ultimately, the SCA concluded that the appeal by the City succeeded. The court set aside the contempt finding, stating that the conduct of the City did not meet the threshold for contempt of court as prescribed by law. Furthermore, the interdict against the City was deemed inappropriate because it prohibited actions that lay outside the scope of the Davis order. The SCA replaced the High Court’s order with one dismissing Moipone Fleet's application and awarded costs in favor of the City, reflecting a significant corrective measure against an erroneous interpretation of contractual and procedural obligations ([paras 1–2, 30]) and underscoring the importance of precise legal interpretation in contractual obligations within public-private partnerships.
