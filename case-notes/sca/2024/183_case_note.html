FIRAC Case Note: Zeal Health Innovations (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Defence and Military Veterans and Another (967/2023) [2024] ZASCA 183

F - Facts

The case involves a dispute between Zeal Health Innovations (Pty) Ltd (hereinafter "Zeal Health") and the Minister of Defence and Military Veterans (the "Minister") regarding the validity of a tender awarded to Zeal Health for providing healthcare and wellness services to 16,000 military veterans. The tender, designated as Tender Number DMV/SES/001/2015, was advertised by the Department on 30 January 2015, with Zeal Health being one of seven bidders. On 20 May 2015, the Bid Adjudication Committee approved the tender award to Zeal Health, which culminated in a contract signed on 27 May 2015 with an allocation of R198 million over three years for the services ([paras 1–4]).

Subsequently, when the Department failed to make payments for the services rendered, Zeal Health initiated legal proceedings seeking specific performance for the payment. In response, the Department filed a counter-application to review and set aside various decisions related to the tender process, claiming that the award was unlawful due to budget constraints, thus violating the Public Finance Management Act 1 of 1999 (PFMA) and the principle of legality ([paras 6–8]).

The Gauteng High Court ruled in favor of the Department on 7 October 2022, declaring the decisions related to the tender and the resulting contract unconstitutional, unlawful, and void. It dismissed Zeal Health’s application and consequently left them to bear their own costs ([paras 8–9]).

I - Issues

The primary legal questions before the court were:

  1. Was the tender awarded to Zeal Health by the Acting Director-General of the Department valid?

This involves determining whether the appointment adhered to legal requirements, specifically those encapsulated in the PFMA concerning budget compliance and the principles of legality.

  1. If Zeal Health's appointment was invalid, would it be entitled to any remedy, such as payment for the services rendered under the contract?

This issue focuses on the nature of the remedy available to Zeal Health once the contract and tender award were declared unconstitutional ([paras 1–2]).

R - Rules

The court based its analysis primarily on the following legal principles and statutory provisions:

  1. Public Finance Management Act 1 of 1999 (PFMA): Section 38(2) prohibits an accounting officer from committing an organ of state to any financial liability without the necessary appropriations, asserting the importance of budgetary constraints in public procurement ([para 20]).
  2. Constitution of the Republic of South Africa (1996):
  • Section 172(1): Obligates courts to declare any law or conduct inconsistent with the Constitution invalid, and grants them discretion to make just and equitable orders allowing for a fitting remedy ([paras 22–23]).
  • This section allows for remedies that fit the injury while considering the rights affected and the law involved ([paras 24–25]).
  1. Principle of legality: This fundamental principle mandates that all actions by public officials must derive authority through law, underscoring the necessity of lawful budget appropriations for tender awards ([para 20]).

A - Application

The court applied the aforementioned principles to the facts of the case. It determined that the award of the tender was indeed unconstitutional because the Acting Director-General had committed the Department to a financial liability exceeding the available budget, thus violating Section 38(2) of the PFMA. This established that the actions taken by public officials were ultra vires and lacked legal authority, rendering the contract void from the outset ([paras 20–21]).

However, the court recognized the unique position of Zeal Health as an innocent party that provided services under this invalid contract. Consequently, the court held that despite the contract's invalidity, Zeal Health was entitled to pursue a just and equitable remedy as per Section 172(1)(b) of the Constitution. The court underscored that merely declaring the actions unconstitutional does not automatically negate the value of the work performed or the reliance placed upon the department's commitments ([paras 24–25]).

The court emphasized that the quantum of relief owed to Zeal Health remains a matter for determination, thereby allowing for ongoing discussions regarding the appropriate compensation for the services rendered ([para 24]).

C - Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal ruled that the Gauteng High Court had correctly found the award and related contracts to be unconstitutional. However, it erred by failing to account for the just and equitable remedy due to the innocent party, Zeal Health. Thus, while maintaining the order of constitutional invalidity, the court also ruled that Zeal Health retains the right to recover payment for the services provided. The appeal was upheld in part with costs, allowing Zeal Health to pursue the quantum of payment through appropriate channels ([paras 26–27]).

The court’s decision underscores the importance of compliance with statutory obligations in public procurement while balancing the need for equitable remedies for parties engaged in public service contracts.
