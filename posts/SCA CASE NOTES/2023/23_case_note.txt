# FIRAC Case Note: C W v G T (867/2021) [2023] ZASCA 23

## F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–7])

This case concerns an appeal against the upholding of an exception in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg, relating to a claim for patrimonial damages by the applicant, Ms CW (applicant), against the respondent, Mr GT (respondent). The applicant alleged fraudulent misrepresentation by the respondent, which induced her to marry him, resulting in substantial wedding expenses amounting to R331,342.36 ([para 1]).

The relationship between the parties spanned from November 2016 to September 2018, marked by the respondent’s professed love, gifts, and assurances of lifelong commitment. The marriage occurred on 15 September 2018, but quickly deteriorated, leading the respondent to file for divorce on 11 December 2018. The applicant claimed to have discovered, post-marriage, that the respondent had misrepresented his intentions regarding the relationship ([paras 3–4]). In response, the respondent raised nine exceptions, four related to the actio leges Aquiliae and five pertaining to the actio iniuriarum. The high court partly upheld these exceptions, dismissing the action concerning the lex Aquilia ([para 2]).

The applicant subsequently sought leave to appeal, which was initially denied by the High Court, prompting the current appeal ([para 2]).

## I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 7–12])

The primary issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was whether the high court correctly upheld the respondent's exceptions to the applicant's claim under the lex Aquilia. The key questions included:

1. Whether a claim for patrimonial damages arising from fraudulent misrepresentation leading to marriage is actionable under the lex Aquilia.
2. Whether the alleged fraudulent conduct constituted wrongful conduct under South African law, particularly in the context of marriages performed out of community of property, where traditional limitations on delictual claims between spouses do not apply.

The high court ruled that such claims were not recognized under the lex Aquilia, thereby rejecting the applicant’s claim on these grounds ([paras 11–12]).

## R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 8–9])

The court heavily relied on established principles of South African delictual law regarding the lex Aquilia and the actio iniuriarum. Key legal references included:

1. **Rohloff v Ocean Accident & Guarantee Corp Ltd**: This case established that delictual actions are permissible between spouses married out of community of property ([para 8]).
2. **Van Der Merwe v Road Accident Fund**: This case underscored the lack of common-law barriers against delictual actions between such spouses, thereby providing a precedent for recognizing claims of this nature ([para 8]).
3. **Lillicrap, Wassenaar and Partners v Pilkington Brothers (SA)**: This case emphasized that the extension of the lex Aquilia involves legal and public policy considerations ([para 14]), relevant to determining whether a new situation warrants delictual liability.

The high court had previously viewed fraudulent misrepresentation as not giving rise to claims under the lex Aquilia, instead emphasizing the necessity of demonstrating public policy considerations justifying the extension of liability ([para 11]).

## A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 12–17])

In analyzing the applicant’s claim, the court established that the applicant had made substantial allegations sufficient to suggest a prima facie case for fraudulent misrepresentation. The court reiterated that the traditional distinctions regarding pure economic loss must yield to the realities of wrongful conduct where fraud is involved ([para 16]).

The high court's reasoning suggested that allowing such claims could intrude upon the autonomy of marital financial arrangements, an argument criticized by the SCA, which noted that the applicant’s claim did not seek to disrupt the property regime but addressed fraudulent conduct that inflicted a measurable loss ([paras 19–22]).

In applying the legal framework, the SCA found that the applicant had satisfied the necessary prerequisites of the delictual claim: a false representation made by the respondent, knowledge of its falsehood, and resultant damages incurred by the applicant ([paras 36–38]). The court thus concluded that the high court's exception lacked merit and improperly dismissed the claims based merely on policy considerations, without adequately recognizing the applicant's legal basis for her claims ([paras 16, 35, 36]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 17–18])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the applicant's appeal, declaring that the high court had erred in sustaining the exceptions raised by the respondent. The SCA emphasized that claims for patrimonial damages arising from fraudulent misrepresentation are indeed actionable, particularly in contexts where the relational dynamics between parties are governed by separate estates, as in marriages out of community of property ([para 18]).

The court’s judgment underscored the principle that dishonesty should not be insulated from legal remedies — a recognition reflecting broader societal values against fraud. The implications of this decision restore the ability to contest fraudulent conduct in marriage and can redefine boundaries relating to financial accountability in domestic relationships. The SCA's ruling mandates a reconsideration of how family law intersects with delictual claims, potentially informing future cases involving misrepresentation in marital contexts. 

Overall, the court’s decision affirms the capacity of the law to adapt to the complexities of human relationships, confirming that fraudulent conduct resulting in economic loss retains its status as actionable within South African legal frameworks.
