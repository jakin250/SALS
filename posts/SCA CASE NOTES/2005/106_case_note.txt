# Case Note: International Marine Transport SA v M V 'Le Cong' (SCA) 

**Citation**: International Marine Transport SA v M V 'Le Cong' [2005] SCA 080/05

### F – Facts
The appellant, International Marine Transport SA, a company incorporated under Panamanian law, sought to arrest the M V Le Cong, owned by Guangzhou Ocean Shipping Company, in Durban to secure a claim against Shantou Sez Chemical Industry and Petroleum Gaz General Company. This claim arose from charter hire payments related to the appellant's vessel, the mv Gaz Progress ([1]).

The basis for the arrest was the appellant's assertion that both Shantou Sez and Guangzhou were state-owned enterprises as defined under South African law, which would make the Le Cong an "associated ship" to the Gaz Progress according to section 3(6) of the Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983 (the Act) ([2]). After a letter of undertaking was provided by Guangzhou on March 20, 2002, the vessel was allowed to depart, although it was still considered to be under arrest ([3]). On July 4, 2002, Guangzhou applied to set aside the arrest, leading to the matter being settled by Hurt J, who ruled in favor of Guangzhou, prompting the current appeal ([4]).

### I – Issues
The central issue in this case was whether the M V Le Cong was an "associated ship" of the Gaz Progress as per the criteria established in the Act, particularly focusing on whether both vessels were under common control—specifically whether the same entity controlled both Guangzhou and Shantou Sez ([6]). This question necessitated an examination of the complex government structure of the People’s Republic of China and the legal distinction between state-owned enterprises at different governmental levels ([6]).

### R – Rules
The relevant legal provisions are found in the Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983. Specifically, section 3(6) permits the arrest of an "associated ship" instead of the ship related to the maritime claim ([5]). The definition of an "associated ship" is provided in subsection 7, which includes vessels owned by entities that are controlled by the same person ([5]). Importantly, "control" is interpreted as the power to dictate the direction and fate of a company, as established in MV Heavy Metal: Belfry Marine Ltd v Palm Base Maritime SDN BHD 1999 (3) SA 1083 (SCA) ([7]).

### A – Application
The court examined expert testimonies about Chinese law, with the appellant arguing that the central government effectively controlled both Guangzhou and Shantou Sez, thereby implying that they should be treated as associated ships. Conversely, Guangzhou's experts contended that the power to control Shantou Sez was vested in the Shantou City Municipal Government, distinct from the central government's authority ([9][11]).

The court emphasized the need for empirical determinations to clarify the nature of control over these companies, and ruled that the appellant did not meet the burden of proof needed to establish that both entities fell under the same controlling entity. Given the complexities of the Chinese legal system and political hierarchy, the court decided to favor Guangzhou's interpretation of Chinese law based on the evidence presented, particularly under the Plascon-Evans rule, which states that unchallenged evidence must be accepted as correct ([12][18]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming that the M V Le Cong could not be classified as an associated ship of the Gaz Progress under the Act as there was insufficient evidence to prove that both ships were owned or controlled by the same entity ([18]). The court's reliance on the specifics of Chinese constitutional law and its interpretation highlighted the intricacies involved in maritime law where international legal standards intersect with domestic regulations. The dismissal of the appeal also indicated the importance of firmly establishing jurisdictional bases when dealing with maritime claims involving foreign entities ([19]).

This case underscores the need for meticulous legal analysis, especially in contexts where foreign legal principles and multi-jurisdictional entities are involved, emphasizing the complexity of international maritime law and its applications.
