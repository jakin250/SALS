National Brands Limited v Cape Cookies CC and Another

Neutral Citation: National Brands Limited v Cape Cookies CC and Another (Case nos: 309/2022 and 567/2022) [2023] ZASCA 93 (12 June 2023)
Coram: Ponnan, Gorven, Hughes, Goosen JJA, Siwendu AJA
Heard: 17 May 2023
Delivered: 12 June 2023

F – Facts

In this case, Cape Cookies CC (the first respondent) sought to register the trade mark "SNACKCRAX" under application no. 2013/06837, in class 30 of the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 (the Act), covering various food items including savoury biscuits ([1]). National Brands Limited (the appellant) opposed this application, asserting rights to several registered marks, particularly "SALTICRAX," a mark it had maintained in South Africa for over 58 years ([2]). Cape Cookies had marketed SNACKCRAX biscuits since August 2014, competing directly with the appellant's SALTICRAX biscuits, which had a significant market presence ([3]).

The initial opposition and subsequent appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal arose from a Gauteng Division of the High Court (Le Roux AJ) ruling that dismissed National Brands' opposition, ordering registration of SNACKCRAX. This ruling was subject to appeal, with National Brands asserting multiple grounds of opposition under sections 10(4), 10(7), 10(12), 10(14), and specifically, 10(17) of the Act ([4], [5]).

The high court found that the SALTICRAX mark had significant goodwill and reputation ([12]), but ultimately, the court had dismissed the opposition based on its interpretation of the law related to similar goods and the requisite conditions for trade mark registration. National Brands subsequently appealed this decision ([4]).

I – Issues

  1. Application of Section 10(17): Was the registration of the SNACKCRAX mark likely to take unfair advantage of, or be detrimental to, the distinctive character or repute of the SALTICRAX mark, notwithstanding the absence of deception or confusion ([5])?
  2. Scope of Protection: Does Section 10(17) apply only to non-similar goods, or does it encompass similar goods within its ambit ([19])?
  3. Evidence of Likelihood: What constitutes sufficient evidence of likelihood that the registration of SNACKCRAX would damage the reputation of SALTICRAX ([42])?

R – Rules

The relevant legal principles derive from the Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, especially sections 10(4), 10(7), 10(12), 10(14), and 10(17) ([5]). The specific provisions of section 10(17), introduced in 1997, aim to prevent the registration of a mark that:

  • Is identical or similar to a well-known mark.
  • Would likely take unfair advantage of, or detriment to, the distinctive character or repute of the registered mark ([7]).

The court was not aware of any prior cases directly addressing section 10(17), but it referred to established principles regarding trademark infringement outlined under section 34(1)(c) of the Act, which bears some parallels despite their different contexts ([8]).

A – Application

The court analyzed the issues systematically. It began by confirming that similar goods are indeed covered by section 10(17) contrary to Cape Cookies' assertion ([22]). The ruling emphasized that the focus should be on the marks themselves rather than their differing goods. The comparison, therefore, highlighted "CRAX" as the dominant and distinctive feature in both SNACKCRAX and SALTICRAX, which the court found to bear a significant resemblance, meeting the “likelihood” standard required under section 10(17) ([35]).

National Brands had presented evidence demonstrating extensive market penetration and significant investment in marketing its SALTICRAX mark, which it argued would potentially be undermined by the registration of a similar mark competing directly on the same product ([41]). The court accepted the argument that altering or confusing the public's perception of the distinctiveness of a well-known mark could lead to unfair advantage for Cape Cookies, who had not demonstrated any independent marketing efforts for the disputed SNACKCRAX mark ([42]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, setting aside the high court's decision to allow the registration of the SNACKCRAX mark. The court concluded that the opposition was indeed well-founded under section 10(17) of the Act, affirming the distinctiveness of the SALTICRAX mark and Johnson's holding that Cape Cookies' mark was likely to cause detriment to the repute of an established trade mark ([45]).

The implications of this decision underscore the protective measures available under South African branding law, particularly concerning well-known marks amid potential dilution and unfair competition, enforcing a robust approach to trademark registration and protection ([44]).
