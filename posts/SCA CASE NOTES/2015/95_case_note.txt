Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# Case Note: Thom v Ba-Phalaborwa Municipality ([2015] ZASCA 95)

### FIRAC Method

---

## Facts

The appellant, L E Thom (Pty) Limited, is the registered owner of a property located at 15 Von Wielligh Street, Phalaborwa Extension 1, in the Limpopo Province. The respondent, Ba-Phalaborwa Municipality, is a local municipality with jurisdiction in that area ([1]). In 2012, the appellant's property experienced flooding due to stormwater being obstructed by overgrown vegetation on the municipality's adjacent land, which blocked the flow of a stream ([1]). 

Consequently, the appellant successfully applied to the North Gauteng High Court for relief against the municipality, leading to a court order on 17 October 2012 mandating the municipality to remove the obstruction to restore the stormwater drainage ([2]). This order directed the municipality to undertake various actions, including the construction and installation of a subsurface drainage system by stipulated deadlines, and allowed the appellant to undertake remedial work at the municipality's expense if it failed to do so ([2]).

Despite repeated reminders, the municipality failed to comply with the order. In response, the appellant contracted a construction company to perform the necessary remedial work around October 2013 ([3]). It was not until March 2014—long after the appellant had initiated the repairs—that the municipality sent its service provider to the site, claiming that the appellant had unlawfully erected a fence that restricted access to the site for the completion of the required works ([4]). 

The municipality then sought urgent relief through an application to have the fence demolished and for access to be granted for its service provider via the appellant’s property, asserting that it was the only route available for access ([4]). The appellant contested this claim, asserting that there were alternative access points along Potgieter Street and adjacent properties ([5]). The factual disputes surrounding access rendered the case complex, leading to serious disputes between the parties.

Despite these disputes, the lower court granted the municipality relief on 20 May 2014, prompting the appellant to appeal with leave from the High Court ([6]). The central consideration for the appeal revolved around whether the procedural form adopted by the respondent was valid given the disputed facts ([7]).

---

## Issues

Several legal issues arose in this case, particularly regarding:

1. **The Nature of the Access Point Dispute**: Was there indeed only one access point through the appellant's property, as claimed by the respondent, or were there alternative routes available?
2. **Appropriateness of Motion Proceedings**: Should the municipality have submitted evidence proving that the only access route was through the appellant's property given the serious factual disputes present ([8])?
3. **Mootness of Appeal**: With the remedial work completed by the appellant prior to the hearing of the appeal, would a ruling in favor of the municipality have any practical effect or relevance ([9])?
4. **Costs and Remediation**: Who bore the costs associated with the application given that both parties acted without due diligence regarding the status of the remedial work ([11])?

---

## Rules

1. **Principle of Access and Nuisance**: The duty of local municipalities to alleviate nuisances affecting residents—a principle grounded in public law.
2. **Motion Proceedings Context**: In Tamarillo (Pty) Ltd v B N Aitken (Pty) Ltd 1982 (1) SA 398 (A) at 430H, it was established that a litigant seeking relief via motion must be aware of the potential for factual disputes.
3. **Mootness Doctrine**: According to Section 16(2)(a)(i) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, an appeal may be dismissed if the proceedings present issues devoid of practical effect.
4. **Equitable Costs**: The court has discretion regarding costs, typically informed by considerations of fairness and the conduct of the parties, as illustrated in Room Hire Co (Pty) Ltd v Jeppe Street Mansions (Pty) Ltd 1949 (3) SA 1155 (T) ([10]).

---

## Application

The court first examined the serious factual disputes surrounding access to the site. The appellant’s denial of the sole access point claimed by the municipality presented significant grounds for questioning the procedural choice of motions ([8]). The court referenced established precedents emphasizing that when a party believes that material facts will be contested, they risk the court dismissing their application when disputes are unresolved and not adequately pleaded ([8]).

The court also highlighted that despite the lower court's decision, a resolution of the factual issues could have been better served through oral evidence. However, during the appeal hearing, it became evident that the remedial work had been completed by the appellant, effectively rendering the municipality's case moot and depriving the appeal of any practical effect ([9]).

The court evaluated the potential remedies that could arise from a ruling. It concluded that any order toward access would yield no beneficial outcome since the work, which was the crux of the dispute, had been finalized ([10]). This determination was critical in affirming the appeal's mootness, as the essence of judicial efficiency mandates avoiding 'academic exercises' in the courts.

Regarding costs, the court determined that both parties shared responsibility for the litigation inefficiencies that led them to court on this matter. Given that both sides had failed to ascertain the completion status of the remedial work, a fair ruling would entail that both parties bear their own costs ([11]).

---

## Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appeal by reversing the order of the lower court and rendering the application dismissed, as the issues at stake had lost their practical significance due to the completion of the remedial work ([12]). The ruling underscored the necessity of factual clarity in motion proceedings and the implication of mootness in civil disputes. The equitable distribution of costs indicated a judicial recognition of the pitfalls encountered by both parties in the face of unresolved facts and procedural decisions.

This case emphasizes the crucial obligation of litigants to ensure that factual disputes are adequately explored and addressed before resorting to motion proceedings, as well as the courts' duty to conserve judicial resources by dismissing matters that no longer elicit practical resolution.
