Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

**Case Note: Tiekiedraai Eiendomme (Pty) Limited v Shell South Africa Marketing (Pty) Limited [2019] ZACC 14**

**FIRAC Structure**

---

### F – Facts
The case revolves around a contractual dispute between Tiekiedraai Eiendomme (Pty) Limited (the Applicant) and Shell South Africa Marketing (Pty) Limited (the First Respondent), concerning the interpretation of a lease and the right of pre-emption ([1]–[4]). 

On 30 October 2014, H L Hall & Sons (Group Services) (Pty) Ltd (the Second Respondent) emailed Shell regarding an offer from Tiekiedraai to purchase a piece of land, specifying a purchase price of R17 million, but lacking further terms and conditions ([2]). According to the lease agreement, Shell held a right of pre-emption, demanding Hall first offer the property to Shell on the precise terms anticipated ([3]). 

Central to this dispute was the question of when the 30-day period for Shell to exercise its pre-emption right began. Tiekiedraai contended that the period was initiated by Hall's email on 30 October 2014, but Shell argued it only began when a complete agreement was forwarded on 5 December 2014 ([4]).

The High Court determined that Hall's email did not comply with the requirements specified in the lease and held that Shell exercised its right effectively by responding within the 30-day window after receiving the complete agreement ([5]). Tiekiedraai's request for leave to appeal was denied, but the Supreme Court of Appeal later granted it, ultimately ruling against Tiekiedraai ([8]).

Now, Tiekiedraai sought to challenge the High Court's "stepping-in" remedy for the first time, along with aspects of the contractual doctrines related to it ([9]).

---

### I – Issues
The primary issue for the Constitutional Court was whether Tiekiedraai could advance new legal arguments regarding the requested "stepping-in" remedy and the interpretation of the lease, after prior courts dismissed its claims. Notably, there was no assertion of a constitutional issue, but rather a query regarding the existence of arguable points of law of public importance under section 167(3)(b)(ii) of the Constitution ([10]–[11]).

Tiekiedraai argued the interpretation of clause 21 of the lease and whether the common law principles involved warranted judicial consideration in this appeal. Specifically, the Court needed to discern their importance beyond the immediate parties involved ([13]–[14]). 

---

### R – Rules
The legal framework guiding the court included section 167(3)(b) of the South African Constitution, which allows the Constitutional Court to consider certain matters if they raise arguable points of law of general public importance ([11]–[12]). 

Multiple precedents were cited, primarily the cases of Owsianick v African Consolidated Theatres (Pty) Limited and Associated South African Bakeries (Pty) Limited v Oryx & Vereinigte Bäckereien (Pty) Limited regarding property law and pre-emption rights ([15]–[17]). In evaluating claims for leave to appeal, the court emphasized that novel arguments should generally not be considered if they were not presented in the lower courts, adhering to procedural fairness ([21]–[24]).

---

### A – Application
In its ruling, the Constitutional Court highlighted the significance of having arguments presented in the lower courts, noting that Tiekiedraai’s failure to raise new claims earlier demonstrated a lack of compelling reasons justifying a departure from usual appellate practice ([21]–[24]). 

Tiekiedraai’s primary contention—that the points it sought to introduce now presented general public importance—was dismissed. The Court observed that the issues arising from clause 21 were confined to the specific parties in this case and lacked broader implications necessary to justify the appeal. While Tiekiedraai attempted to argue regarding breathing new interpretation into existing contract law principles, the Court concluded that these considerations had not been adequately substantiated as reasons for re-evaluation ([17]–[43]).

Ultimately, the Court reaffirmed its hesitance to assume the role of first instance for legal points not previously argued, placing emphasis on the integrity of the judicial process and the importance of thorough submissions at all preceding court levels ([19]–[23]).

---

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court, in a unanimous decision, dismissed Tiekiedraai’s application for leave to appeal with costs, concluding that the arguments raised lacked jurisdictional merit and did not fulfill the requirements set forth in the Constitution ([27]). 

The ruling reinforces the need for comprehensive argumentation in lower courts and delineates the limits of the Constitutional Court's intervention in cases involving novel legal points not previously entertained in court. The implications of this case guide future litigants regarding the necessity of establishing a clear nexus of public importance in legal arguments presented at the apex court level. 

**Order**: Leave to appeal is refused with costs.
