Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: Makhokha v State [2019] ZACC 19

**F – Facts**: 

The case concerns the applicant, Khathutshelo Oupa Makhokha, who was convicted in 2008 by the Sibasa Regional Magistrates’ Court for possession of a suspected stolen motor vehicle, as per section 36 of the General Law Amendment Act 62 of 1955. He received a sentence of 15 years' imprisonment, which was the maximum under section 92(1)(a) of the Magistrates Court Act 32 of 1944. The sentencing court imposed a non-parole period for the entire 15 years and directed that this sentence would run consecutively to a life imprisonment sentence he was already serving ([paras 1–2]). 

Makhokha appealed to the High Court of South Africa, Venda Provincial Division, which dismissed the appeal ([para 3]). His subsequent approach to the Supreme Court of Appeal was unsuccessful. He then applied for leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court, seeking condonation for the late filing of this application ([paras 3–4]).

**I – Issues**: 

The primary legal issues emerged from the applicant's appeal focusing on: 

1. The constitutional validity of a sentence which imposes a non-parole period for the whole term of imprisonment ([para 1]).
2. Whether a court can lawfully impose a determinate sentence that commences only after the completion of a life sentence ([para 10]).

**R – Rules**: 

The court relied on several legal precedents and statutory provisions to articulate the rule of law regarding sentencing in South Africa:

1. **Constitutional Provisions**: Section 12(1)(a) of the South African Constitution provides a right against arbitrary detention ([para 14]).
2. **Criminal Procedure Act**: Section 276B, following a 1997 amendment, limits non-parole periods to a maximum of two-thirds of the sentence imposed ([para 12]).
3. **Correctional Services Act**: Section 39 mandates that any determinate sentence must run concurrently with a life sentence unless specifically directed otherwise by the court ([para 16]).
4. **Previous Case Law**: Precedents such as *S v Bogaards* ([para 6]) and *S v Mashava* ([para 18]) were cited to clarify the court's authority regarding sentencing.

**A – Application**: 

1. The court analyzed the non-parole period imposed by the Regional Magistrate. It found that setting a non-parole period of 100% was not within the legal framework established by section 276B(1)(b), which stipulates that a non-parole period cannot exceed two-thirds of the sentence imposed. Consequently, the court determined that this aspect was constitutionally invalid and constituted an infringement of Makhokha's rights under section 12(1)(a) of the Constitution ([paras 13–15]).

2. Regarding the order that the sentences must run consecutively, the court referred to section 39(2)(a)(i) of the Correctional Services Act, which necessitates that a determinate sentence should run concurrently with a life sentence unless otherwise stated. The Regional Magistrate lacked authority to make such a direction, exposing Makhokha to arbitrary detention by delaying the commencement of his 15-year term ([paras 16–18]).

3. The court further noted that the Regional Magistrate had not articulated any exceptional circumstances that would justify the imposition of a non-parole period, thus indicating that there was no basis for remitting the matter for reconsideration ([paras 22–23]).

**C – Conclusion**: 

The Constitutional Court held:

1. Leave to appeal against the 15-year sentence was refused due to a lack of jurisdictional grounds to question its magnitude without an irregularity ([para 7]).
2. However, leave was granted concerning the legality of the specific sentencing orders pertaining to the non-parole and consecutive terms.
3. The appeal was upheld, with the court setting aside both the non-parole directive and the imposition of consecutive sentences. The commencement date of the 15-year term was backdated to the date of sentencing.

This decision reaffirms the importance of procedural correctness in sentencing, ensuring that judicial decisions respect constitutional rights and statutory limitations, thereby enhancing the integrity of the South African justice system ([paras 24]).
