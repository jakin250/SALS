# FIRAC Case Note: Anglorand Securities Ltd v Mudau & Another (125/10)

## F – Facts

The matter at hand, *Anglorand Securities Ltd v Mudau & Another* ([1]), originated in the Limpopo High Court, where the plaintiff, Divhani David Mudau (the plaintiff), initiated a claim against Anglorand Securities Ltd (the second defendant) and Rudolph Rashama (the first defendant), an employee of Anglorand. Essentially, the plaintiff alleged that he entered into an oral agreement for the sale of shares and investments, whereby he deposited R160,000 in December 2001 into a bank account held by the second defendant, expecting that shares would be purchased on his behalf and that he would receive an investment booklet reflecting accrued interest ([2]).

Despite making multiple inquiries regarding the shares and investments, the plaintiff did not receive the promised booklet or reimbursement for nearly a year. In April 2002, he was informed by a director of Anglorand that his funds could not be returned due to an insurance claim concerning Rashama ([3]). On 14 October 2002, the plaintiff’s attorneys sent a letter demanding the return of his investment, asserting that over the years, he had deposited a total of R460,000, and expressing the urgency of the matter ([4]).

In response, Anglorand filed a special plea of prescription, asserting that the plaintiff's claim had become prescribed as he failed to serve his summons within the three-year period as outlined in the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 ([4], [5]). The plaintiff argued against this, asserting that he was led to believe he would be reimbursed and that he had been assured of ongoing correspondence regarding the insurance claim ([6]).

The presiding judge, Hetisani J, ruled in favor of the plaintiff, prompting Anglorand to appeal the decision ([8]). The appeal raised pertinent questions regarding the commencement of the running of prescription and whether it had been interrupted ([1]).

## I – Issues

The central issues presented in this case encompassed:

1. The commencement of the running of prescription concerning Mudau's claim against Anglorand Securities ([1]).
2. Whether there was an interruption of the prescription period, potentially re-establishing Mudau's right to pursue his claim after the initial period ([2]).

These issues required a thorough examination of the events leading to the claim and the interactions between the parties involved.

## R – Rules

The key legal framework for this matter is the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, specifically sections 10, 11, 12, and 14, which outline the conditions for the running of prescription, the commencement of a claim, and the requirements for its interruption. 

1. **Running of Prescription**: Section 12(1) stipulates that prescription commences when a debt becomes due, defined as the moment when a creditor can claim payment ([9]). This was further elaborated through established case law, emphasizing that a full cause of action must exist for a claim to commence ([9]).

2. **Interruption of Prescription**: Section 14(1) provides that the running of prescription may be interrupted by an acknowledgment of liability, either express or tacit, from the debtor ([12]).

3. **Cases Cited**: The court relied on various precedents, including *Evins v Shield Insurance Co Ltd* and *Aussenkehr Farms (Pty) Ltd v Trio Transport CC*, which elaborated on the meaning of "debt becomes due" and the conditions under which prescription can be interrupted ([9], [10]).

## A – Application

The court scrutinized the timeline of events occurring post-transaction to determine when Mudau’s debt became “due.” Key to this analysis was his interactions with Anglorand and the assurances provided by the company's representatives ([3], [10]). The plaintiff's initial awareness that he had not received his funds by October 2002 posited that prescription commenced running from that date, as he was informed of transactions being conducted without his knowledge, leading to the conclusion of liability being contested by Anglorand.

In evaluating the interruption claim, the court examined the letter from Gary Cahn dated 6 June 2002 regarding an insurance claim linked to Rashama's alleged fraud ([13]). However, the testimony from Anglorand’s director underscored that liability was denied continuously, and Cahn’s letter merely noted an ongoing claim process rather than an acknowledgment of Mudau’s specific debt to Anglorand ([11], [12]). Thus, the court concluded that no effective acknowledgment had occurred that could have interrupted the running of prescription according to the requisite standards highlighted in the act and clarified in case law.

## C – Conclusion

The appellate court determined that the Limpopo High Court misdirected itself regarding the evidence and application of the Prescription Act. It held that the plaintiff’s claim had indeed prescribed prior to the service of the summons ([8]). Consequently, the Supreme Court of Appeal allowed the appeal, set aside the order of the lower court, and upheld the second defendant’s special plea of prescription ([8]).

This case underscores critical aspects of the Prescription Act, highlighting the necessity of understanding when a debt is considered due and how interactions between parties can influence the running and interruption of prescription. The ruling indicates the courts' stringent adherence to the prescribed timeframes established by law, serving as a cautionary tale for potential claimants regarding the imperative nature of timely legal action.
