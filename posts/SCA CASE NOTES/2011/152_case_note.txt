Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

## Case Note: South African Transport and Allied Workers’ Union v Garvis and Others (007/11) [2011] ZASCA 152

### F – Facts
On **16 May 2006**, the South African Transport and Allied Workers' Union (the Union) organized a protest march in the Cape Town City Bowl, stemming from a protracted strike in the security sector ([1]). During this march, chaos ensued, leading to significant damage to vehicles and property along the route, including allegations of assaults ([1], [14]). 

The first to eighth respondents (individuals affected by the riot) claimed damages caused by the Union’s actions, seeking recovery under Section 11 of the Regulation of Gatherings Act 205 of 1993 (the Act). Section 11(1) articulates statutory liability for organizations under whose auspices a gathering turns into a riot causing damage ([2]). The respondents asserted their claims for damages, citing extensive property damage and personal injuries ([2]–[12]).

The Union defended itself, arguing that the provisions of Section 11(2)(b) of the Act imposed an unconstitutional burden, violating the right to freedom of assembly as entrenched in Section 17 of the South African Constitution ([4], [5]). It contended that the design of liability under the Act would have a chilling effect on public protest, discouraging organizations from facilitating gatherings due to the fear of financial ruin if riots occurred ([5], [30]).

The Western Cape High Court, led by Hlophe JP, addressed the constitutional validity of Section 11(2)(b). It held that this subsection was not inconsistent with Section 17 of the Constitution. Consequently, the Court ruled against the Union, which later appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([19], [20]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issue before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether Section 11(2)(b) of the Regulation of Gatherings Act infringed upon the constitutional right to freedom of assembly as protected under Section 17 of the Constitution ([5], [29]). The specific concern revolved around whether the statutory provisions created a chilling effect that would undermine the rights to assemble and protest peacefully and in an unarmed manner, as required ([29], [50]).

### R – Rules
The case was grounded in several legal principles:

1. **Section 11 of the Regulation of Gatherings Act 205 of 1993** outlines the liability for damages caused by gatherings degenerating into riots. The burden for proof of specific defenses (e.g., the act or omission was not reasonably foreseeable) is presented in Section 11(2).
  
2. **Section 17 of the Constitution** states, "Everyone has the right, peacefully and unarmed, to assemble, demonstrate, picket and present petitions." This section enshrines the right to freedom of assembly, which must be balanced against the need to protect public order and others’ rights.

3. **Common law principles of negligence**, particularly as stated in *Kruger v Coetzee* ([33]), provide a framework for establishing liability based on foreseeability of harm and the failure to take reasonable precautions.

### A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed the arguments surrounding the constitutionality of Section 11(2)(b). **Navsa JA** emphasized that the right to assemble peacefully does not extend to riots or unlawful behavior ([29], [48]). The court confirmed that Section 11(2)(b) did not create an unreasonable burden and that a union or organization could escape liability by demonstrating that the riot was not foreseeable. 

The court countered the Union's argument about a chilling effect by noting that public assemblies continued despite potential liability under the Act, indicating that the Act had not deterred lawful protests. Evidence showed that gatherings had become a regular feature of South African life ([30], [50]).

Moreover, the court reasoned that the Act was not internally contradictory. The required proof of defenses in Section 11(2) aligned with common law negligence standards, meaning that a defendant merely needs to show that the event leading to the riot was not reasonably foreseeable ([32], [35]).

The Court further explored hypothetical scenarios addressing whether the Union could have reasonably foreseen a riot, highlighting that the Union’s own evidence indicated a history of violence leading up to the march, which would have necessitated heightened precautions ([43]). The court concluded that the statutory liability imposed a reasonable limitation and was justifiable within an open and democratic society ([32], [50]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the high court's ruling that **Section 11(2)(b) was not unconstitutional** [54]. The court held that while the right to freedom of assembly must be protected, it does not offer sanctuary for unlawful actions resulting in harm to others. The decision reiterates the balance between maintaining public order and safeguarding civil liberties, thus reinforcing the legal framework surrounding public gatherings in South Africa ([49], [51]).

Given the extensive reasoning provided by Navsa JA and the court's focus on the interaction between statutory obligations and constitutional rights, this case serves as a crucial reference for future discussions on the limits of assembly rights, especially in scenarios potentially leading to violence. The implications are significant in reinforcing that while rights to protest exist, they come with responsibilities of organization, liability, and foresight against potential public harm.
