Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Media 24 Books (Pty) Ltd v Oxford University Press Southern Africa (Pty) Ltd [2016] ZASCA 119

### F – Facts
This case concerns a dispute over copyright infringement involving two bilingual Afrikaans-English/English-Afrikaans dictionaries, specifically the **Aanleerderswoordeboek** published by Media 24 Books (Pty) Ltd (hereafter Media24), and the **Oxford Woordeboek** published by Oxford University Press Southern Africa (Pty) Ltd (hereafter OUP). The Aanleerderswoordeboek was first published in 1993, while the Oxford Woordeboek was published in 2007 ([1]–[2]). 

In October 2011, Media24 initiated proceedings against OUP, alleging that the latter’s dictionary had copied substantial parts of the former’s work. Media24's claims focused on three aspects: the compilation of headwords, ordering of senses, and example sentences in both dictionaries ([7]). Media24 presented affidavits from several experts, including Dr Anton Prinsloo, who highlighted similarities between example sentences and found significant overlap (16–24%) between the two dictionaries ([8]).

The Western Cape Division of the High Court dismissed Media24's application for an interdict and other relief, leading to this appeal ([3]). The evidentiary proceedings were structured solely around affidavits, without resort to oral evidence, and thus placed limitations on the court’s ability to assess the validity of the competing claims ([36]).

### I – Issues
The main legal issue presented in this case revolves around whether OUP had infringed Media24's copyright through copying example sentences from the Aanleerderswoordeboek. The following specific questions arise from this broader issue:
1. Was there sufficient proof of copying as defined under the Copyright Act 98 of 1978?
2. Did Media24 establish a causal connection between its work and the allegedly infringing actions of OUP?
3. Were the similarities between the dictionaries attributable to common phrases or the nature of compiling dictionaries, rather than copying? ([11]–[12].)

### R – Rules
The legal principles governing this case derive from the South African Copyright Act 98 of 1978, particularly sections addressing the definition of a literary work and the rights of copyright holders ([11]). 

In previous case law, such as **Galago Publishers (Pty) Ltd v Erasmus**, the courts established that copyright infringement occurs only if there is a substantial similarity between the original work and the alleged infringing work, along with evidence of access by the alleged infringer ([12]). The standard for proof of copying does not necessitate that the copying be verbatim; rather, significant objective similarity must be demonstrated ([13]).

Furthermore, legal precedent holds that the burden lies with the claimant to provide evidence of copying on the standard of balance of probabilities ([18]).

### A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal undertook a thorough analysis of the similarities in example sentences between the two dictionaries. Media24's position that the degree of overlap could only indicate copying was tempered by the counterargument that similarities could arise from common phrases or structural conventions inherent to dictionary compilation aimed at schoolchildren ([35]).

The court recognized that while Media24 sufficiently demonstrated access to its dictionary and some degree of similarity ([35]), the evidence presented regarding the method of sentence formulation by OUP's compilers did not support the claim of deliberate copying. OUP presented affidavits from its dictionary compilers asserting that any similarities arose independently and without malign intent ([19]–[26]). The distinction between intentional copying and the fortuitous nature of producing similar content within a narrow educational framework was crucial ([31]).

An important aspect of the judgment involved weighing the credibility of affidavit-based evidence in conjunction with a comprehensive understanding of lexicographical norms. The court emphasized that while Media24 argued for the implausibility of coincidences given the extent of similarities, it further failed to adequately dismantle or address OUP's explanations for the correspondences presented ([40]–[44]).

### C – Conclusion
In conclusion, the appeal was dismissed, with the court asserting that Media24 did not meet its burden of proof to substantiate claims of copyright infringement sufficient to justify relief. The ruling highlighted the inherent challenges of ascribing copyright infringement in lexicographical works and underscored the necessity of evaluating the broader context of dictionary compilation, particularly when aimed at a specific educational demographic. The outcome reinforces the understanding that while copyright protects against unauthorized reproductions of original works, it does not extend to ideas or methods of expression that are standard within the lexicographical community ([53]).
