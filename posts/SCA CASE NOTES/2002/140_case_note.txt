Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2002

# FIRAC Case Note: **Hassim v. Commissioner of South African Revenue Services**  

## F: Facts  
The appellant, Dr. I M Hassim, appealed to the Natal Income Tax Special Court from the disallowance of his objections to assessments for income tax and value-added tax (VAT) by the South African Revenue Services (SARS) ([1]). His appeal originated after allegations arose that he operated two undisclosed bank accounts, receiving funds from medical aid schemes that were not reported in his VAT returns, leading to an assessment by the respondent for unpaid value-added tax ([3], [4]). Following the disallowance of his objections, Hassim sought to compel SARS to make formal discovery of relevant documents to substantiate his claims ([7]). The court a quo, however, determined that the appellant's requests for discovery could not be granted as he had not specified his objections clearly enough for the court to ascertain the necessity of the requested documents ([8], [9]). The court permitted the appellant to amend his notices of objection and appeal, which prompted the current appeal against that judgment ([8], [10]).

## I: Issues  
The primary legal question raised in this case is whether the order of the court a quo allowing the amendments while disallowing the discovery request constitutes an "appealable decision" under section 86A(1) of the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962 ([1], [10]). More specifically, the case centers on two sub-issues:  
1. Did the court a quo’s refusal to allow general discovery hinder Hassim's ability to prepare a detailed objection and thus constitute a relevant decision for appeal? ([12], [14])  
2. Was there a premature dismissal of certain grounds of objection by the court a quo that could be regarded as a final or definitive decision? ([16])

## R: Rules  
The legal framework chiefly relied upon includes:  
- **Section 86A(1) of the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962**, which defines "any decision" made by the Income Tax Special Court. The interpretation of this phrase is crucial ([10]).  
- **Section 21 of the Supreme Court Act 59 of 1959**, which establishes criteria for what constitutes an appealable decision ([10]). It has been judicially considered that claims should generally not encompass non-final decisions that do not dispose of substantial portions of a legal matter ([10]).  
- **Judicial precedents** such as *Law Society, Transvaal v Behrman* and *Zweni v Minister of Law and Order*, which outline the necessary attributes for an appealable decision ([10], [11]).  
- Provisions in the South African Constitution, specifically **Section 32**, which entitles taxpayers to access documents necessary for preparing their objections ([12]).

## A: Application  
In reviewing the application of these rules to the facts of the case, the Court analyzed the nature of the decision made by the court a quo. It asserted that the ruling on discovery was procedural and did not resolve substantive matters central to the appeals on merits; thus, it rendered the order an interlocutory one, rather than definitive ([15]). The Court emphasized that since the discovery order could be amended or appealed post-final judgment, it should not be treated as a final decision ([15]).  
On the second point, concerning the premature dismissal of objections, the Court ruled that while the court a quo's statement on the validity of some grounds was a commentary, it did not constitute a substantive ruling that could be separately appealed ([16]). Thus, the Court confirmed that there had been no final decisions made on the validity or merits of the appellant’s objections, and consequently, the appeal could not succeed ([16]).

## C: Conclusion  
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately struck the appeal from the roll with costs, determining that the court a quo’s decision regarding discovery was not an appealable decision under section 86A(1) of the Income Tax Act. The underlying rationale was that the decision did not resolve any substantive issues or render any finality regarding the assessments in dispute ([17]). In essence, the appeal was found to be based on non-appealable grounds, reaffirming judicial principles against piecemeal appeals that could obfuscate judicial processes. The implications of this decision underscored the importance of clear procedural grounds and highlighted the judiciary's intent to avoid litigation that could prolong disputes unnecessarily ([17]).
