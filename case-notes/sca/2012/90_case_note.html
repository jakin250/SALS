City of Cape Town v Hendricks (633/11) [2011] ZASCA 90

Supreme Court of Appeal 2012 [2011] ZASCA 90

F – Facts

The case arose from an appeal by the City of Cape Town (the City) against the ruling of the court a quo, which involved informal traders Faizel Hendricks and Mogamat Smith. The primary events occurred on April 23, 2010, when Constable Swartbooi issued compliance notices to the respondents requiring them to remove their temporary business structures from City property due to non-compliance with municipal by-laws ([paras 2–4]). The structures in question encroached upon adjacent property, leading the neighboring owner, Vusani Investments (Pty) Ltd, to object to their presence ([para 3]).

Upon receiving the notices, the respondents sought an interdict against the City without its knowledge, which initially led to a favorable ruling for them that restrained the City from taking action ([paras 5–6]). However, they failed to legitimize their business operations through proper channels, subsequently filing a review application, claiming that the City had to afford them a chance to make representations ([para 6–8]). The initial ruling was that the notices issued by the City constituted administrative action under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA), which subsequently led to the City appealing the decision ([para 9]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues for determination included:

  1. Whether the City's actions in issuing the compliance notices constituted "administrative action" as defined under PAJA ([para 9]).
  2. Whether the procedural requirements of PAJA had been adhered to, specifically concerning the obligation to provide the respondents the opportunity to make representations before the issuance of the compliance notices ([para 9]).

R – Rules

The legal framework guiding this case included the following authorities and principles:

  1. Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) – This Act governs the procedural requirements surrounding administrative actions and the rights of affected parties to make representations ([paras 2, 9]).
  2. Judicial Precedents:
  • Greys Marine Hout Bay (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Public Works 2005 (6) SA 313 (SCA) – Clarified the nature of administrative action and outlined the implications when such actions adversely affect an individual’s rights ([para 10]).
  • Eastern Metropolitan Substructure v Peter Klein Investments 2001 (4) SA 661 (W) – Discussed preliminary actions versus final decisions in administrative law ([para 10]).
  • Related cases such as Joseph v City of Johannesburg 2010 (4) SA 55 (CC) further supported the legal reasoning surrounding administrative action and the obligations placed on municipal authorities under PAJA ([paras 10, 11]).

A – Application

In its deliberation, the Supreme Court of Appeal focused primarily on whether the compliance notices constituted "administrative action" under PAJA. The court found that the issuance of these notices was a preliminary step to enforce the relevant by-laws and did not amount to a final decision affecting the respondents’ rights, as they did not entail immediate adverse consequences ([para 10]).

The court reiterated that the compliance notices served merely as a notification of the City's intent to enforce the by-laws, thereby not requiring the procedural fairness typically bestowed under PAJA. The court further explored the nature of "administrative action" and concluded that no decision mandating daily removal of structures existed. Instead, the notices requested compliance with pre-existing by-laws ([paras 10–11]). This approach was consistent with the principle established in prior cases that emphasized the distinction between preliminary notifications and substantive administrative decisions ([para 11]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the City of Cape Town's appeal, dismissing the orders granted by the court a quo, which had erroneously classified the compliance notices as administrative actions requiring adherence to PAJA's procedural norms ([para 1]). The judgment clarified that such notices are non-reviewable under PAJA, as they do not constitute final decisions adverse to the respondents’ rights, nor do they carry immediate legal effect ([para 10–11]).

Consequently, the ruling reaffirmed the City's authority to enforce compliance via warnings without providing prior opportunities for representations, significantly impacting how compliance notices might be issued under municipal by-laws. The judgment elucidates the boundaries of administrative action, underscoring the necessity for clear distinctions between preliminary compliance processes and final administrative decisions, while further shaping the landscape of informal trading regulations within urban areas in South Africa ([paras 8–11]).

This case bears implications for local governance's ability to manage by-law compliance while also protecting the rights of informal traders, balancing regulatory enforcement with procedural fairness.
