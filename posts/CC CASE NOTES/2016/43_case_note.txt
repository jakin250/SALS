Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# Case Note: AB and Another v Minister of Social Development [2016] ZACC 43

## 1. Facts
The case involves *AB*, the first applicant, who is a woman seeking to enter into a surrogacy agreement after numerous unsuccessful attempts at conception through in vitro fertilization (IVF). Over a span of ten years, AB underwent 18 IVF cycles, none of which resulted in a successful pregnancy. At age 55, AB was medically declared infertile both in terms of conception and pregnancy, having no viable gametes for surrogacy. Legally, under section 294 of the Children’s Act 38 of 2005, AB was barred from entering into a surrogacy agreement as she could not provide a gamete. The second applicant, *Surrogacy Advisory Group*, an entity advocating for the rights of commissioning parents and surrogate mothers, supported AB’s application.

On 28 June 2013, AB sought relief from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, arguing that section 294 is unconstitutional as it violates her rights to equality, dignity, reproductive autonomy, and privacy. The High Court found in favor of AB, declaring that section 294 unjustly restricted her rights and ordered its constitutional invalidity. The order for constitutional invalidity was subsequently escalated to the Constitutional Court for confirmation.

## 2. Issues
The primary legal issues in this case include:
1. Whether the genetic link requirement mandated by section 294 contravenes AB’s constitutional rights to equality, dignity, reproductive autonomy, and privacy ([paras 12-14]).
2. Whether the limitations imposed by section 294 can be justified within the context of section 36 of the Constitution, which permits limitations on rights provided they are reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society ([para 33]).

## 3. Rules
The following legal rules and principles were considered:
- The provisions of the Constitution of South Africa, especially the rights encapsulated in sections 9 (equality), 10 (dignity), and 12 (freedom and security of the person) ([paras 10, 12]).
- The principle of rationality in legislative measures, particularly how provisions like section 294 must align with legitimate government purposes ([para 112]).
- The Children’s Act 38 of 2005, particularly sections 294 and 295, which regulate surrogacy agreements and establish the genetic link requirement ([paras 295-297]).

## 4. Application
The Constitutional Court, led by *Khampepe J*, applied these legal principles to assess the validity of section 294. It noted that while the state has an interest in ensuring that children born of surrogacy arrangements have a clear genetic identity, this interest must be balanced against the rights of individuals seeking to use surrogacy. The judgment expressed that the genetic link requirement results in unjust discrimination against AB and those in similar situations, preventing them from exercising their rights to reproductive autonomy.

Khampepe J articulated that section 294 imposed an absolute barrier, taking away AB's ability to choose surrogacy as a method of becoming a parent, thereby limiting her rights to psychological integrity under section 12(2)(a) of the Constitution. The judgment situated the adverse psychological impacts of infertility, particularly emphasizing that the lack of access to reproductive options like surrogacy heightens mental anguish for individuals like AB ([paras 82-84]).

Conversely, the dissenting opinion, articulated by *Nkabinde J*, posited that the state had a legitimate aim in preserving the rights of children to know their genetic origins. Nkabinde J argued that the distinction made by section 294 was rational and necessary for children's welfare, emphasizing the importance of parenting linked by genetics ([paras 210-216]).

## 5. Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ultimately held that section 294 of the Children’s Act was unconstitutional as it unjustly infringed upon AB's rights, particularly her rights to equality and reproductive autonomy, thus confirming the High Court's order of invalidity. The decision underlined the need for legislative reform to ensure that surrogacy laws align with constitutional values, allowing individuals facing infertility to fully participate in the journey to parenthood ([paras 1-4, 330]).

This ruling has significant implications for reproductive rights in South Africa, emphasizing the need for legal frameworks that support various family structures and the choices of individuals facing infertility.
