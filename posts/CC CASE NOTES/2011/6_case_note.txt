Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: Glenister v. President of the Republic of South Africa and Others

## F – Facts 
This case arises from an application by Hugh Glenister challenging the constitutionality of the National Prosecuting Authority Amendment Act (NPAA Act) and the South African Police Service Amendment Act (SAPSA Act). These laws disbanded the Directorate of Special Operations (DSO) and replaced it with the Directorate of Priority Crime Investigation (DPCI) within the South African Police Service (SAPS) ([1]–[2]). Glenister, a businessman, previously challenged this decision in *Glenister I* but the challenge was dismissed by the Constitutional Court, which held that it could not intervene in Parliament's internal affairs without evidence of imminent harm ([3]–[4]). The President signed the impugned laws into effect on 27 January 2009, after they were passed by Parliament ([12]).

After the enactment, Glenister sought to appeal a decision from the Western Cape High Court, contending that the DPCI's formation violated constitutional principles of accountability, independence, and public involvement ([4]–[17]). The Helen Suzman Foundation joined the case as amicus curiae to argue for the establishment of an independent anti-corruption unit, consistent with international obligations ([4]–[5]). The respondents, which included various government officials and departments, defended the legality and rationality of the DPCI’s establishment.

## I – Issues 
The constitutional issues at stake included whether:
1. The impugned laws were rational and aligned with constitutional mandates regarding the NPA and SAPS ([54]).
2. There was a constitutional obligation to establish an independent body to combat corruption ([163]).
3. The DPCI, as created by the impugned laws, met the requirements for independence necessary for effective operation ([200]).

## R – Rules 
The Court emphasized several constitutional provisions and legal principles:
- **Section 179 of the Constitution**, which establishes the National Prosecuting Authority and delineates its functions without external influence ([64]–[65]).
- **Section 205 of the Constitution**, which provides for the national police service’s capacity to prevent and investigate crime, including corruption ([69]).
- Prior jurisprudence from the Constitutional Court established that legislative actions must demonstrate a rational connection to a legitimate governmental purpose ([56]–[68]).
- International obligations stemming from agreements such as the United Nations Convention against Corruption mandated the existence of autonomous bodies to combat corruption ([179]–[186]).

## A – Application 
The Court found the challenges based on the impugned laws' rationality to be unsubstantiated. It held that the dissolution of the DSO aimed at enhancing the SAPS's effectiveness in tackling crime was rational, as supported by legislative discourse and the Khampepe Report recommendations ([55]–[68]).
However, the crucial finding was about the DPCI's structural independence. The main judgment articulated several safeguards that purportedly ensured the DPCI could operate effectively: provisions for parliamentary oversight, a complaints mechanism led by a retired judge, and the necessity for the DPCI to perform its functions independently ([117]–[155]). However, the dissenting opinion highlighted substantial risks of political interference, particularly given the Ministerial Committee's oversight powers and the conditions employed for appointments and operation that weakened the DPCI's independence compared to the now-disbanded DSO ([208]–[250]). Thus, it argued that the DPCI's creation was insufficient to satisfy constitutional obligations regarding autonomy ([204]–[213]).

## C – Conclusion 
The Constitutional Court, by a majority, ultimately dismissed Glenister's application for leave to appeal and his direct access request, reinforcing the rationality of the DPCI’s formation under the contentious laws. However, the dissenting opinion highlighted the inadequate protections for the independence of the DPCI and consequently called for a declaration of constitutional invalidity against the provisions that failed to secure that independence, with a suspension of this declaration for 18 months to allow Parliament to rectify the issues ([251]). The ruling has major implications for the architecture of anti-corruption governance in South Africa, illustrating the tension between political oversight and the autonomy of law enforcement agencies tasked with combating corruption ([253]–[255]).
