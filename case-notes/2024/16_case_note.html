Shoprite Checkers (Pty) Limited v Mafate N.O. [2024] ZACC 16

Constitutional Court 2024 [2024] ZACC 16

F – Facts

The case before the Constitutional Court of South Africa originates from a tragic incident on 15 October 2014, involving Ms. Nolunga Mkhwanazi, who worked as a packer for Smollan Sales and Marketing at Checkers Hyper. During her duties, she suffered severe head injuries due to a fall from a cage attached to a forklift, resulting in permanent mental incapacity ([paras 2–3]).

On 1 February 2017, Mr. Cecil Tshepo Mokopane Mafate was appointed as the curator ad litem to pursue a delictual claim on behalf of Ms. Mkhwanazi against Shoprite Holdings Limited ([para 3]). However, Shoprite Holdings asserted that the true defendant was its subsidiary, Shoprite Checkers (Pty) Ltd ([para 3]). Following the withdrawal of the initial action against Shoprite Holdings, the summons against Shoprite Checkers was served on 19 October 2018 ([para 4]).

Shoprite Checkers raised a special plea of prescription, arguing that the claim was barred under section 13(1)(a) and (i) of the Prescription Act, asserting that the claim had prescribed because a year had elapsed since Mr. Mafate's appointment as curator ad litem, thereby ceasing the impediment of mental incapacity ([paras 4–5]). In response, Mr. Mafate contended that the period of prescription was interrupted by the service of process on 19 October 2018, and that less than three years had passed since the debt became due ([para 7]).

The High Court initially dismissed Shoprite Checkers' special plea, propounding a view that a curator ad litem may still rely on section 12(3) of the Prescription Act, which considers the knowledge of the creditor regarding the identity of the debtor ([para 10]). The Supreme Court of Appeal later ruled that, since Ms. Mkhwanazi was under curatorship, the period of prescription could not be completed while she remained mentally incapacitated ([para 12]).

I – Issues

The central legal issue in this case concerns the interpretation of the interplay between sections 12(3) and 13(1) of the Prescription Act, particularly whether the appointment of a curator ad litem for a mentally incapacitated person results in the cessation of the impediment of mental incapacity for the purpose of prescription ([para 15]).

Additionally, the case raises broader constitutional questions regarding access to justice and the protection of vulnerable groups, reflecting on whether the closure of the claims process due to the operation of prescription disproportionately impacts incapacitated individuals ([para 15–16]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal provisions at the heart of the dispute are sections 12(3) and 13(1) of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969. Section 12(3) states that a debt is deemed not due until the creditor has knowledge of both the identity of the debtor and the facts from which the debt arises ([para 7]).

Section 13(1) stipulates that if a creditor is mentally incapacitated, the period of prescription is suspended as long as the impediment persists. The critical component in dispute is paragraph (i) of section 13(1), which provides that if the relevant period of prescription would otherwise have been completed within one year after the impediment ceased, the period shall not be completed until the expiry of one year from that cessation ([paras 22–24]).

The principles outlined in previous judgments such as Mohlomi v Minister of Defence and Uitenhage Municipality v Molloy provide a context where prescription serves to balance the interests of creditors and debtors, aiming to prevent prejudice resulting from outdated claims ([paras 18–20]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court, in its judgment, interpreted that the appointment of a curator ad litem does not extinguish the protection afforded to Ms. Mkhwanazi under section 13(1). Instead, the court underscored that mental incapacity persists regardless of the curator's appointment. Hence, the burden to demonstrate the upliftment of the impediment rests firmly on the shoulders of Shoprite Checkers ([para 38]) and should be interpreted in favor of conserving the rights of a vulnerable individual ([paras 38–39]).

The Court further illustrated that, while there may be procedural advancements with a curator ad litem, the inherent protection against the completion of the prescription period for a person with mental incapacity shall continue unabated until such incapacity ceases. In this regard, it was noted that recognizing Shoprite Checkers' interpretation would yield a potential disparity regarding the treatment of vulnerable persons in law ([paras 35–37]).

The Court rejected the need to explore the intersection of sections 12(3) and 13(1), reaffirming that the overriding factor is the persistent state of mental incapacity, which ensures the protection against the lapse of claims indefinitely, thus preserving access to justice for Ms. Mkhwanazi ([paras 43]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal and dismissed the appeal against the decisions of the lower courts, affirming the High Court's ruling that the running of prescription does not cease upon the appointment of a curator ad litem, as long as mental incapacity persists ([para 1]).

The judgment reinforces the legal framework surrounding the protection of individuals suffering from mental incapacity, emphasizing the necessity for constitutional safeguards to ensure that vulnerable populations retain unimpeded access to justice ([paras 40–42]). The implications extend beyond this case, providing legal certainty and reinforcing the need to balance competing rights under the Bill of Rights, particularly valuing the right of access to courts for those who are most vulnerable within the judicial system.

This decision ultimately underscores a profound commitment to ensuring that procedural law does not operate in a manner that prejudices individuals due to circumstances involuntary imposed upon them, hence promoting public confidence in the justice system ([paras 17–20]).
