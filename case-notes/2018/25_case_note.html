Liesching and Others v The State [2018] ZACC 25

The case concerned an appeal involving three applicants—Pieter Pietertjie Liesching, Malvin Naas Swartz, and Xavier Malgas—who had been convicted of murder, unlawful possession of firearms, and unlawful possession of ammunition ([paras 1-3]).

Constitutional Court 2018 [2018] ZACC 25

F - Facts

The case concerned an appeal involving three applicants—Pieter Pietertjie Liesching, Malvin Naas Swartz, and Xavier Malgas—who had been convicted of murder, unlawful possession of firearms, and unlawful possession of ammunition ([paras 1-3]). Their convictions arose from a shooting incident on November 17, 2011, where the deceased, Renaldo Leeroy Booysens, was fatally shot. The key witnesses against the applicants were Mr. Sherwin Arries, his brother Mr. Marlin Abrahams, and the deceased’s uncle, Mr. Gordon Swiegers. The trial court found the evidence presented by the two corroborating witnesses, Mr. Arries and Mr. Abrahams, compelling enough to convict the applicants ([paras 4-11]).

Following the conviction, the applicants pursued appeals for leave to appeal their convictions, which were rejected by the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) on multiple occasions. However, subsequent to their conviction, a co-accused, Arthur Saimons, was acquitted based on the recantation of Mr. Arries in the latter's trial ([paras 12-17]). Mr. Arries testified during the Saimons trial that he had been coerced by the police to give false testimony against the applicants ([paras 14-16]).

The applicants subsequently invoked section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act, seeking the President of the SCA to refer their case back for reconsideration of their application for leave to appeal, based on Mr. Arries' recantation ([paras 17-23]). The President rejected this application, leading the applicants to approach the Constitutional Court ([paras 24-27]).

I - Issues

  1. Exceptional Circumstances: Does the post-trial recantation of a key witness establish "exceptional circumstances" that warrant referring the decision refusing leave to appeal for reconsideration?
  2. Duty to Provide Reasons: Is the President of the SCA required to provide reasons when denying a referral under section 17(2)(f)?
  3. Interests of Justice: Would denying the referral result in a grave injustice to the applicants? ([paras 30-32]).

R - Rules

  1. Superior Courts Act: Section 17(2)(f) allows the President of the SCA to refer a decision refusing an application for leave to appeal for reconsideration in "exceptional circumstances" ([para 1]).
  2. Constitutional Rights: The applicants contended violations of their constitutional rights under sections 34 (right to a fair hearing) and 35(3)(o) (right to appeal) of the Constitution ([paras 19-20]).
  3. De Jager Criteria: Precedent dictates that the following criteria must be satisfied for a trial to re-open:
  • A sufficient explanation for why the new evidence was not led in the original trial.
  • A prima facie likelihood of the evidence's truth.
  • Material relevance of the evidence to the trial outcome ([paras 67-70]).
  1. Interpretation of "Exceptional Circumstances": The phrase is interpreted in light of its ordinary grammatical meaning and historical case law, focusing on circumstances that are atypical or extraordinary ([paras 128-134]).

A - Application

Ultimately, the Constitutional Court, in analyzing the application of section 17(2)(f) and the specifics surrounding the witness’ recantation, discussed the nature of exceptional circumstances in depth ([paras 131-136]). The majority held that the President had erred in dismissing the application without considering the details of Mr. Arries' recantation sufficiently, as this could undermine the conviction's foundation ([paras 100-102]). The court noted that this new evidence could raise the likelihood of a different trial outcome, thus fulfilling the requisite criteria to consider the matter further ([paras 31-36, 95-104]).

In terms of the duty to provide reasons, the Court underscored that the President’s discretion, while broad, must align with accountability standards necessitated by the rule of law. The President should have provided adequate reasons for the dismissal to facilitate an informed appellate process ([paras 53-63]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately concluded that exceptional circumstances indeed existed due to the implications of the new evidence provided by Mr. Arries’ recantation, which could potentially substantiate a wrongful conviction ([paras 110-115]). Furthermore, it was found that the failure to reconsider the decision could result in a grave injustice to the applicants, thereby justifying the referral back to the SCA ([paras 101-110]).

The decision by the President was thus set aside, and the matter referred back for reconsideration. The final order included directions that this judgment must be brought to the President's attention for further action ([paras 113-116]).

This case highlights significant issues regarding witness testimony integrity, the appeal process in criminal law, and the broader implications of ensuring justice within the legal system, particularly in cases involving wrongful convictions.
