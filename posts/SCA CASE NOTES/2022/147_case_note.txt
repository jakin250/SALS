Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: KeyHealth Medical Scheme v Glopin (Pty) Ltd (2022) ZASCA 147 

**F – Facts**:  
This case emerges from a contractual dispute between KeyHealth Medical Scheme (KeyHealth), the appellant, and Glopin (Pty) Ltd (Glopin), the respondent, regarding the validity of the revocation of a broking agreement. On 20 October 2004, Glopin entered into a written broking agreement with Munimed Medical Scheme, wherein Glopin was tasked with introducing and admitting new members to Munimed's products ([para 2]). This agreement stipulated various terms, including a clear outline of Glopin's authority, which expressly stated that they were not appointed as an agent or representative of Munimed. The agreement was subsequently assigned to KeyHealth on 24 October 2011 ([para 3]).

KeyHealth's attorneys issued a notice of termination on 14 February 2017, citing Glopin's non-compliance with relevant legislation and service levels as constituting a serious breach ([para 4]). Glopin opposed this termination, perceiving it as a repudiation of the agreement ([para 5]). An interim agreement was reached on 28 February 2017, preventing KeyHealth from acting upon the notice pending Glopin's intended action against them. On 31 March 2017, KeyHealth abandoned the termination notice while simultaneously revoking Glopin's authority effective 1 July 2017 ([para 6]).

Glopin then sought relief from the Gauteng Division of the High Court, contending that the agreement was valid and KeyHealth's actions constituted unlawful revocation ([para 7]). The high court found in favor of Glopin, leading to KeyHealth's subsequent appeal ([para 13]).

**I – Issues**:  
The central legal question before the Supreme Court of Appeal was whether KeyHealth was entitled to revoke its agreement with Glopin on the grounds that it constituted a revocable mandate ([para 1]), and if the framing of the contract allowed for such revocation at will ([para 14]).

**R – Rules**:  
KeyHealth based its argument on the assertion that the agreement constituted a mandate, which, under South African law, can generally be revoked at any time unless expressly stated otherwise in the contract ([para 10]). The trial court referred to legal definitions and precedents regarding contracts of mandate, particularly the view that a contract of mandate requires consensus between parties and that authority granted may be revocable by a mandator ([para 9]). Key legal references include *Firs Investment Ltd v Levy Bros Estates (Pty) Ltd* ([1984] 2 SALR 881 (A)) and *Consolidated Frame Cotton Corporation Ltd v Sithole* ([1985] 2 SALR 18 (N)), which explored the limitations of agency in contractual contexts ([para 11]).

Specific sections of the Medical Schemes Act 131 of 1998 (MSA) and accompanying regulations had a direct bearing on this case, particularly concerning the conditions under which a broker may be compensated ([paras 16-17]).

**A – Application**:  
The court's reasoning involved a thorough examination of the terms laid out in the broking agreement. Notably, clauses defining the nature and scope of Glopin's authority were interpreted in the context of both the relevant legal framework and the factual circumstances of the dispute. The trial court concluded that the continuous service obligations created a contractual relationship that could not be summarily revoked without complying with prescribed termination clauses ([para 13]).

The Supreme Court highlighted that while any mandate may appear irrevocable, parties could agree that it would not be terminable at will, which was a pivotal aspect of the agreement in question. The precise language of the contractual clauses emphasized the conditions of termination, which were not satisfied by KeyHealth's actions ([para 28]). The judgment asserted that no foundational events outlined for automatic termination had occurred, thereby invalidating KeyHealth's purported revocation of authority ([para 27]).

**C – Conclusion**:  
The Supreme Court of Appeal affirmed the High Court's ruling that KeyHealth unlawfully revoked the agreement, ultimately dismissing the appeal with costs ([paras 29-30]). The court emphasized the significance of adhering to the contractual terms agreed upon by both parties, reinforcing the need for clarity in the drafting of contractual documents. The implications of this judgment serve to clarify the boundaries of revocation rights in contracts of mandate and the necessity for adherence to stipulated termination conditions within contractual frameworks. This case further elucidates the principles governing the dynamics of contractual relationships in the South African legal landscape and reinforces the enforceability of agreed terms.
