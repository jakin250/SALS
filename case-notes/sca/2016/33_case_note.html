Supreme Court of Appeal 2016 [2016] ZASCA 33

F – Facts

The case revolves around an appeal by Bernard Geoffrey Fisher (the appellant) against the judgment of the KwaZulu-Natal Local Division of the High Court, which dismissed his special plea of prescription. The central facts are laid out in the judgment as follows:

On 10 November 2010, George Beaton, trading as Meranti and Board (Meranti), issued a combined summons against Fisher, seeking payment of R1,077,377 for goods sold and delivered to Strongwood Manufacturing (Pty) Ltd, a company for which Fisher had provided a surety. The summons was served on Fisher on 18 November 2010. Fisher filed his plea on the merits on 7 January 2011, and thereafter the pleadings were closed ([1]–[2]).

Subsequently, on 22 October 2013, Meranti ceded its claim against Fisher to Natal Rubber Compounders (Pty) Ltd (NRC), stipulating that NRC would apply for substitution as the plaintiff after fulfilling certain conditions, which it subsequently did. On 9 December 2013, Meranti served a notice to amend the summons to substitute NRC as the plaintiff, an amendment Fisher did not oppose ([2]–[3]).

On 22 January 2014, Fisher amended his plea to introduce a special plea asserting that the interruption of prescription caused by Meranti's claim lapsed due to the cession of rights to NRC, thus arguing that the claim had prescribed under sections 15(2) and (6) of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 (the Act) ([4]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue in this case centers around whether the substitution of NRC for Meranti constituted fresh proceedings that caused the interruption of prescription to lapse or if the original action was merely continued ([4]–[5]). Specifically, the court needed to determine if, due to the cession and subsequent substitution, the claim had prescribed before NRC could assert it ([4]–[6]).

R – Rules

The judgment references essential sections of the Prescription Act, particularly sections 15(2) and (6), which govern interruptions of prescription:

  • Section 15(2) stipulates that unless the debtor acknowledges liability, the interruption of prescription lapses if the creditor does not successfully prosecute the claim to final judgement ([4]).
  • Section 15(6) expands on what constitutes "process," including petitions, notices of motion, or pleadings that begin legal proceedings ([4]).

The court also considered previous case law, including Mias de Klerk Boerdery (Edms) Bpk v Cole and Silhouette Investments Ltd v Virgin Hotels Group Ltd, to clarify the implications of cession after litis contestatio and its effect on the right to pursue a claim ([4]–[5], [7], [8]).

A – Application

The court assessed dueling arguments regarding whether NRC's substitution constituted a continuation of the prior action or the initiation of new proceedings. Fisher contended that NRC’s substitution effectively started new litigation due to a severance of the original claims upon cession, arguing that Meranti ceased pursuing the claim which resulted in the prescription period concluding after three years ([5]–[6]).

In contrast, NRC's position was that the prescription had been effectively interrupted with the service of the original summons by Meranti, emphasizing that the substitution was procedural and that they continued to pursue the same claim under the initial process established by the summons ([6]–[12]). The court referenced the need for continuity in proceedings outlined in Waikiwi Shipping Co Ltd v Thomas Barlow & Sons (Natal) Ltd and emphasized that the mere act of substitution does not dissolve the ongoing nature of legal proceedings initially commenced ([9]–[10]).

Ultimately, the court concluded that the original interruption of prescription was intact. The only change was the identity of the creditor pursuing the claim. NRC's action did not commence anew but continued the claim already within the ambit of legal proceedings initiated by Meranti ([15]–[17]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Fisher's appeal, affirming that the special plea of prescription had no merit. The court held that the cession and subsequent substitution of NRC did not impact the prescription period as the ongoing claim had been appropriately prosecuted under the original process. The appeal was dismissed with costs, including those of two counsel ([1], [18]).

This judgment underscores the principle that the identity of the party entitled to pursue a claim can change without disrupting the legal processes that protect against prescription, so long as the original debt remains the same and there is continuity in the legal proceedings. The outcome reinforces a substantive understanding of prescription law in the context of civil procedure and the sensible management of creditor-debtor relationships post-cession.
