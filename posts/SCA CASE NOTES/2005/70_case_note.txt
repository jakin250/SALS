Case Note: DIE HOOGSTE HOF VAN APPÈL
Source Folder: 2005

# FIRAC Case Note: Die Hoogste Hof van Appèl van Suid-Afrika (Case No: 618/04)

**F - Facts**  
[1] The case originates from the Eastern Cape Provincial Court where the appellant, Norman William Erasmus, then aged 18, was charged with attempted murder for allegedly throwing sulfuric acid in the face of Mr. Pieter Huishamen on April 11, 2002. The trial court found him not guilty of attempted murder but guilty of assault with intent to cause serious bodily harm, sentencing him to eight years imprisonment, of which two years were suspended. Erasmus appealed this conviction and sentence to the Grahamstown High Court, but his appeal was dismissed by Justice Jansen and Justice Ndzondo. The subsequent appeal to the Supreme Court was solely against the conviction ([para 1]).

[2] The events leading to the case are undisputed. The appellant acknowledges that he is the individual who threw sulfuric acid at Huishamen, resulting in severe and horrific injuries. The acid stripped the skin from Huishamen’s face and body, even resulting in the surgical removal of his eyes. Previously an independent business owner, Huishamen's life shifted dramatically, rendering him a disabled individual dependent on state support and the assistance of a devoted friend ([para 2]).

[3] While the appellant does not contest his guilt regarding assault, the crux of the matter revolves around his argument that the additional element of intent to cause serious harm was absent. Given the devastating consequences of his action, this argument might at first glance appear surprising. However, it is crucial to note that the distinguishing element of the crime for which the appellant was convicted does not lie in the actual infliction of serious injury but rather in the subjective intent to inflict harm. Thus, the severity of consequences does not automatically negate the appellant's defense of the absence of intent to cause serious injury ([para 3]).

[4] The appellant did not testify during the trial. His defense was based on self-serving portions of two statements made outside of court that were presented by the state. His initial confession, made before a magistrate, revealed that he threw the acid at Huishamen at the instigation of Gerrit Jacobs, with whom Huishamen once had a homosexual relationship. The confession detailed that Jacobs assured him the acid would only cause minor irritation ([para 4]).

[5] The second statement provided to the court was from a prison guard, wherein the appellant stated he threw acid because “they told me it wouldn’t do much damage.” This statement presumably referred to Jacobs and another individual ([para 5]).

[6] Jacobs and another co-accused named Britz were originally charged alongside the appellant. Britz was acquitted during the trial, and Jacobs's initial conviction was overturned on appeal. Both testified, denying any knowledge of the assault, and it should be noted that the alleged reassurance regarding the harmlessness of the acid was not presented in cross-examination by the appellant’s defense ([para 6]).

**I - Issues**  
[7] The main issues to be addressed are whether the self-serving components of the appellant's out-of-court statements are admissible and, if they are, what weight should be assigned to this evidence in determining whether Erasmus had the necessary mens rea of intent to cause serious harm as per the applicable legal standards ([para 8]).

**R - Rules**  
[8] The legal principles governing the admissibility of evidence, particularly self-serving statements, are found in prior decisions of the court, such as *R v Valachia* 1945 AD 826, *S v Khoza* 1982 (3) SA 1019 (A), and *S v Yelani* 1989 (2) SA 43 (A). Under these cases, self-serving statements allow for the potential admission of evidence, but such statements do not carry the same weight as incriminating statements made by the accused. The court must evaluate the credibility of statements presented and their impact on the overall case ([para 8]).

**A - Application**  
[9] The trial court ruled the self-serving parts of Erasmus's statements were admissible. However, they found these portions less credible than the incriminating evidence made by the appellant since the reassurance he attributed to Jacobs and Britz had not been put to either person during cross-examination. Moreover, the court expressed skepticism regarding the reliability of the alleged assurances particularly in light of common knowledge regarding the effects of sulfuric acid ([para 9]).

[10] At a fundamental level, the court clarified that actual intent to cause harm is not a requirement for a conviction under the relevant statute; rather, the presence of dolus eventualis—where the accused foresees the possibility of serious harm and reconciles with that possibility—is sufficient. Therefore, it was necessary to determine whether Erasmus subjectively considered the possibility of causing serious injury to Huishamen and accepted that risk ([para 10]).

[11] Upon this examination, the court deliberated whether it was reasonable to infer that, despite the reassurances from Jacobs and Britz, the appellant could foresee that his actions posed a serious risk of injury. Factors such as Erasmus's young age, educational background, and the pressure from Jacobs were considerations weighed against the potential absurdity of believing that sulfuric acid would cause only minimal effects ([para 11]).

[12] Ultimately, the court asserted that even without the clarity provided by a personal testimony from Erasmus, the overwhelming evidence spoke to a reasonable inference that he did consider the possibility of serious harm as a result of the acid attack. An implicit understanding of the destructive nature of the acid, combined with the circumstances surrounding the act, justified the finding of intent. The notion that the appellant’s belief in the harmlessness of the acid ranged into farcical territory given universal knowledge of the dangers associated with such substances ([para 12-13]).

[14] Given the totality of circumstances and available evidence, the conclusion drawn by the trial court regarding Erasmus's foresight and acceptance of the risk of serious injury was affirmed. The conviction for assault with intent to cause serious bodily harm was thereby upheld ([para 14]).

**C - Conclusion**  
[15] The appeal was ultimately dismissed. The sustaining of the conviction for assault with intent to cause serious harm reinforces the principles of mens rea under South African law, particularly concerning the assessment of an accused's subjective understanding of the consequences of their actions. This case emphasizes the courts’ leniency on evidence standards regarding intent and serves as a critical signal regarding the judicial approach to violent acts with irreversible consequences. The ruling asserts the necessity of personal accountability in violent acts, even within a complex framework involving third-party influence, thus shaping the landscape of criminal intent doctrine in South African jurisprudence ([para 15]).
