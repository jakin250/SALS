Supreme Court of Appeal

Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service v Trend Finance (Pty) Limited [2007] SCA 59 (RSA)

This case originates from the importation of three consignments of shoes from China, intended for Pep Stores and Foschini retail chains.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2007

F – Facts

This case originates from the importation of three consignments of shoes from China, intended for Pep Stores and Foschini retail chains. The shoes were imported by two family-run companies, Trend Finance (Pty) Ltd and Trend Gear Enterprises (Pty) Ltd, under the management of Mr. Ismail Essop. The consignments were released after the companies made provisional payments under the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 ([1]–[2]).

Upon arrival, the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (SARS) refused to release the goods, citing potential underpayment of customs duties and VAT. Provisional payments of R100,000, R300,000, and R600,000 were made for these consignments, which were later released in March and August/September of 1999, respectively ([2]).

A subsequent letter from the Controller of Customs identified underpayments amounting to R363,371.09 and indicated that the goods could be subject to forfeiture under sections 87 and 88 of the Customs and Excise Act. The letter also demanded forfeiture payments amounting to R732,903.00 based on the asserted violations. No similar penalties were imposed on the second and third consignments ([8]).

Trend Finance and Trend Gear initiated motion proceedings against the Commissioner of SARS, seeking to set aside the determinations and penalty outlined in the Controller's letter. The primary relief sought included the return of the provisional payments made and the cancellation of the imposed penalty ([9]–[10]).

The court a quo found in favor of the Commissioner regarding the assessment of the transaction values of the first consignment, although it did set aside the penalty imposed ([11]). Trend contested these findings with a cross-appeal on the valuation determination ([12]).

I – Issues

The case raises multiple legal questions pertinent to the interpretation of the Customs and Excise Act and the administrative duties of the SARS Commissioner, including:

  1. Whether the Commissioner was justified in determining the values and imposing forfeiture penalties related to imported goods ([11]–[12]).
  2. The implications of the failure to make a timely determination regarding the transaction values for the second and third consignments ([29]–[30]).
  3. The relationship between the powers of the Commissioner and the rights conferred under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA) 3 of 2000 ([25]–[28]).

R – Rules

The relevant statutory provisions governing this case include:

  • Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964: This Act outlines the administration of customs duties, importation requirements, and penalties for non-compliance. Specifically:
  • Section 38(1): Requires importers to make due entry of goods and declare their values.
  • Section 65(1)-(4): Addresses the determination of transaction values and the right to appeal against such determinations.
  • Sections 87 and 88: Define the conditions under which imported goods can be subject to forfeiture and stipulate the Commissioner’s powers to act upon non-compliance ([3]–[7]).
  • Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000: This Act delineates the obligations of the Commissioner regarding administrative actions affecting the rights of individuals, particularly the need for timely decision-making in cases where an obligation exists. Relevant sections include:
  • Section 6: Provides grounds for judicial review based on delays in decision-making.
  • Section 8(2): Allows for judicial review instructing the administrative body to take a decision ([27]).

A – Application

The court applied the relevant laws and evidence presented to support its findings:

Transaction Value of the First Consignment

The cross-appeal from Trend was based on the assertion that the declared transaction values were correct, arguing against the higher values posited by the Commissioner. However, the court held that Trend failed to meet its burden of proof as required by Section 102(4) of the Customs and Excise Act, which places the onus on the importer to demonstrate that proper duties were paid ([13]).

The court assessed the credibility of the testimonies provided by Mr. Essop and his daughters versus the expert evidence of Mr. Atkins from Pep Stores, ultimately finding the latter more convincing. Various discrepancies were noted regarding the pricing and negotiation processes between the involved parties, leading to the conclusion that Trend had not provided adequate proof against the Commissioner’s valuations ([15]–[21]).

Refunds of Provisional Payments

The Commissioner’s appeal against the repayment of provisional payments for the second and third consignments centered on the argument that they were entitled to maintain possession pending a determination of transaction values. The court agreed that there was no explicit requirement for the Commissioner to make a timely determination regarding these consignments; however, it concluded that the extensive delays in the determination process violated principles of administrative justice ([28]).

The court reasoned that prolonged possession of the provisional payments without timely investigation or determination equated to arbitrary deprivation of property under Section 25 of the Constitution and therefore ruled that the Commissioner must repay the amounts held ([29]–[30]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately ruled favorably for Trend in certain respects. It upheld the findings of the court a quo regarding the Commissioner’s failure to establish due assessment of the transaction values promptly, leading to the requirement for repayment of the provisional payments ([30]).

The case underscores the importance of timely administrative action and the balance of powers between regulatory authorities and individual rights as enshrined in the Constitution and enacted law. The ruling serves as an affirmation of the legal principles governing customs operations, offering a framework for future interpretations of statutory obligations and administrative justice within South Africa’s revenue system ([31]–[33]).

The implications of this case extend beyond its immediate context, reinforcing the need for regulatory bodies to act diligently within specified timeframes to uphold the rights of individuals and comply with the overarching legal standards in public administration.
