Supreme Court of Appeal

Control Chemicals v Safbank Line Ltd & Others

Supreme Court of Appeal 2000

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

[1] The case involves an incident that occurred on 7 September 1991, when an explosion followed by a fire erupted in a container aboard the M V Recife, which was en route from Durban to Wilmington, North Carolina. The container was shipped by Control Chemicals (the Appellant) and contained 1005 boxes of Klorman Chlorine replacement cartridges, a form of calcium hypochlorite, classified as a dangerous good under the International Maritime Dangerous Goods Code (IMDG Code). It was common ground that this classification was known to Safbank Line Ltd (the First Respondent) prior to the shipment.

[2] The explosion caused damage to adjacent containers and the vessel itself, prompting the respondents—Safbank Line Ltd (the carrier), along with the South African Marine Corporation Ltd, Argonaut Shipping Inc, and The Bank Line Ltd—to file an action against Control Chemicals for damages, grounded in both contract and delict. The respondents primarily relied on contractual claims as they conceded there was no evidence of negligence on the part of the appellant.

[3] The court here focused on the contract of carriage, governed by the Hague Visby Rules incorporated into the South African Carriage of Goods by Sea Act 1 of 1986. The contract stipulated that the shipper (the appellant) was liable for damages related to goods categorized as dangerous if the carrier had not consented to their shipment with knowledge of their nature. The respondents countered that the calcium hypochlorite was either defective or improperly stored, which constituted grounds for asserting that the carrier had not given informed consent to the shipment.

[4] The trial court initially ruled in favor of the respondents, concluding there was sufficient evidence to find liability on the part of Control Chemicals. This decision is the subject of the appeal.

I – Issues

[5] The primary legal issues to resolve were:

  1. Did the respondents sufficiently prove that the explosion was caused by a defect or contamination in the calcium hypochlorite?
  2. Did the respondents adequately establish that the carrier had not consented to ship these goods under Article IV, Rule 6 of the Hague Visby Rules?
  3. What is the standard of proof required in such cases where extraordinary events lead to explosions, and how does that relate to the burden of proof?

R – Rules

[6] The relevant provisions under consideration are primarily those contained in Article IV, Rule 6 of the Hague Visby Rules, which holds that a shipper of dangerous goods is liable for any damage arising from such goods if the carrier has not consented to their shipment with an understanding of their nature and character.

[7] The principles of burden of proof in civil cases dictate that the party who asserts the existence of a fact must provide evidence to support such claims. As outlined in The “Popi M”, the burden remains on the plaintiffs to establish on a balance of probabilities the cause of the explosion.

[8] Additionally, the court referenced existing literature, including prior studies on calcium hypochlorite hazards that established a historical context for the dangers posed by this chemical, further emphasizing the need for expert testimony to navigate these technical aspects.

A – Application

[9] During the trial, the respondents attempted to distinguish the potential causes of the explosion through expert testimony. However, experts like Dr. Atherton suggested that both the so-called "insolation theory" (heat buildup from sunlight) and the "tobacco theory" (spontaneous combustion due to a separate damaged tobacco container) were insufficient to explain the explosion.

[10] The court found that while Dr. Atherton's assertions were scientifically grounded, no definitive proof linked the explosion to the appellant's actions or negligence. Instead, he surmised that the explosion might have arisen from a rogue tablet that had an unknown contaminant affecting its stability.

[11] On examining the tablet-making process, it was crucial to establish the efficacy of the safety measures implemented by Control Chemicals. Dr. Buchan, an employee of Control Chemicals, testified that the tablet-making process followed strict procedures minimizing contamination risks. Nonetheless, the trial court placed undue weight on Dr. Atherton's conclusions without establishing a direct causative link to the appellant's product.

[12] The trial court erred by concluding that if the heat effects of insolation could be ruled out, the only alternative explanation was a defect in the calcium hypochlorite containers. The appeals court highlighted that speculation should not substitute for the required factual foundations, as seen in the erroneous reasoning applied in the lower court's decision.

C – Conclusion

[13] The Court of Appeal ultimately held that the evidence presented was insufficient to establish that the explosion was caused by a defect in the calcium hypochlorite or that the shipment was carried out without consent, as mandated by the Hague Visby Rules.

[14] A significant implication of this judgment is the reinforcement of the burden of proof standards in complex cases involving hazardous materials, underscoring that a mere possibility of negligence does not equate to culpability without substantial evidence.

[15] Consequently, the appeal was granted with costs. The Court’s judgment indicated that the original decision to hold Control Chemicals liable was unfounded based on the weight of the evidence provided. The order for absolution from the instance was substituted accordingly.

This case highlights critical principles of maritime law and the challenges faced when establishing liability involving dangerous goods, reaffirming the necessity for concrete evidence rather than speculative links when ascribing liability.
