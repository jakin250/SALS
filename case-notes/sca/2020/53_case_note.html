Supreme Court of Appeal

Petropulos & Another v Dias ([2020] ZASCA 53)

[2] The dispute led to a legal claim by Dias against both appellants for damages, grounded in the principles of strict liability pertaining to the duty of lateral support ([para 6]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 53)

F – Facts
[1] The appeal at hand pertains to the duty of lateral support owed by adjacent property owners, which was established in the lower court by Bozalek J in the Western Cape Division of the High Court ([para 1]). This duty extends not only to the land but also to the structures built upon it, with exceptions based on reasonable loading ([para 25]). The background involves three parties: Ms Marina Petropulos (First Appellant), Mr Artur Fernando Pereira Dias (Respondent), and Mr Dawid Venter, each owning contiguous properties in Camps Bay, Cape Town, on a steep slope ([para 2]). In 2008, Ms Petropulos and Mr Venter commenced excavations for construction; these activities resulted in significant subsidence of Dias's property, which was attributable to the removal of lateral support during the excavations ([para 4]–[5]).

[2] The dispute led to a legal claim by Dias against both appellants for damages, grounded in the principles of strict liability pertaining to the duty of lateral support ([para 6]). After a trial, the court ruled in favor of Dias, concluding that both appellants breached their duty of support, leading to extensive damage on Dias's property ([para 7]). The appellants contended that this ruling was erroneous for several reasons, including the assertion that Dias's property had been improperly loaded and thus did not merit lateral support ([para 8]).

I – Issues
[3] The principal issue raised in the appeal centers on whether the common law duty to provide lateral support extends to buildings on the land in question, and whether the appellants violated this duty during their construction activities ([para 1], [8]). Specifically, the appellants contested the nature of support owed, the extent of their liability under strict liability, and the linkage between their excavations and the harm caused to Dias’s property ([para 8]).

R – Rules
[4] The central legal principle governing this case is the doctrine of lateral support, which asserts that landowners owe a duty of support not only to the land but also to buildings constructed upon that land ([para 25]). The court of appeal distinguished the South African approach to lateral support from English law, which limits the duty of support strictly to natural land without considering constructed properties ([para 9], [12]). Key precedents cited include the cases of Rouliot (1890), which established that the right to lateral support is reciprocal and inherent in property ownership, and Anglo Operations Ltd v Sandhurst Estates (Pty) Ltd [2006] ZASCA 118, which confirmed the broader South African interpretation of lateral support ([para 12], [19]). The principle operates on strict liability; thus, a landowner can be held liable irrespective of negligence if lateral support is removed ([para 60]).

A – Application
[5] The court systematically evaluated the evidence presented at trial, including expert testimonies regarding the mechanics of the slope failure ([para 28]–[29]). The court found that both witnesses, Dr. McStay (for Dias) and Dr. Day (for the appellants), offered competing insights into the causes of the subsidence ([para 30]–[36]). The court expressed a preference for Dr. McStay’s account, adjudging that the failure was primarily tied to the removal of lateral support from the excavations on Petropulos's property, despite the complexities presented by slope stability ([para 37]).

[6] On the matter of causation, the court clarified that the significant excavation undertaken by the first appellant was a substantial causal factor in the mobilization of the slope that led to Dias’s property damage ([para 46]). Furthermore, the notion that other structural factors insulated the first appellant from liability was dismissed, emphasizing that the breadth of damage across multiple properties still warranted accountability ([para 39]).

C – Conclusion
[7] The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the lower court correctly established that Ms. Petropulos and Mr. Venter owed a duty of lateral support that extended to the respondent's property, inclusive of its structures ([para 25], [66]). The appeal was thus dismissed, with the decision reinforcing the principle of strict liability within the framework of lateral support. The ruling upholds the essential legal tenet that landowners are responsible for maintaining adequate support for both land and structures, emphasizing fairness and equity in neighborhood relations, consistent with the constitutional values of Ubuntu ([para 21], [63]). Consequently, the appellants were ordered to bear the costs of the appeal, symbolizing the court's firm stance on the these principles ([para 70]).

This case marks a crucial development in South African property law, delineating the contours of lateral support and emphasizing the interconnected duty landowners owe to one another, particularly in complex topographical contexts.
