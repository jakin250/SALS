Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note

## Case Title
**Armitage NO v Valencia Holdings 13 (Pty) Ltd and Others** (638/2022) [2023] ZASCA 157 (23 November 2023)

## F – Facts
[2] This case arises from an action instituted by Michelle Armitage NO (the appellant), executrix of the estate of her late husband Alan Armitage, against Valencia Holdings 13 (Pty) Ltd (the first respondent) and its co-shareholders (the second to fifth respondents). The appellant sought payment of R6,768,900, representing life insurance proceeds meant for the deceased's estate. She alleged oppressive conduct by the respondents, namely the granting of interest-free shareholder loans as advances on future dividends.

[3] Alan Armitage held 7.5% of shares in Valencia, which, with the second to fifth respondents, created a closely held company structure. The respondents, as minority shareholders alongside the deceased, had entered a shareholders' agreement, stipulating that the value of the company's shares lay in their collective skills. They took out a buy-and-sell indemnity insurance policy to ensure the smooth transfer of shares upon the death of a shareholder, reflecting a fair valuation of the shares.

[6-7] Between 2012 and 2016, the respondents sourced interest-free loans from Valencia’s subsidiaries, recorded as shareholder loans, to fund personal financial needs. The deceased consented to these loans, which were amortised against declared dividends.

[8-9] Following the deceased's death in December 2013, insurance proceeds were paid to the surviving shareholders, and a series of litigation ensued over the deceased’s shareholding. A settlement offer to purchase the shares was made but ultimately faltered.

[14] The High Court initially found some oppressive conduct, ordering the respondents to pay R6,768,900, but this judgment was reversed on appeal to the Full Court. Each court’s rulings considered the characterisation of the loans and their implications on the estate.

## I – Issues
[17] The principal issue on appeal involves whether the conduct of the respondents was "oppressive or unfairly prejudicial" as defined by § 163(1) of the Companies Act 71 of 2008 (the Act). Specifically, the appeal questioned if the loans constituted conduct that disregarded the interests of the appellant and her late husband.

[19] The respondents argued the appellant failed to substantiate her claims, citing the deceased's consent to the loans and that the estate continued benefiting from the loans until 2017. There was also the question of whether any violations of the Act related to financial assistance were valid grounds for claiming oppression.

## R – Rules
[20-21] The rules applicable to this case stem from § 163 of the Companies Act, which allows for relief from oppressive or prejudicial conduct by either shareholders or directors. This section permits a court to consider the implications of conduct that disregards an applicant's interests.

The definition under § 163(1) stipulates the criteria for what constitutes oppressive conduct. The threshold is one of significant unfairness, requiring courts to consider not just the legality of actions but their impact on minority shareholders.

The court also referenced precedents such as *Grancy Property Ltd v Manala* and *Irvin and Johnson Ltd v Oelofse Fisheries Ltd*, which clarify the nature and extent of oppression in corporate law.

## A – Application
[24] The court assessed whether the conduct of the respondents could be considered oppressive, especially given the deceased's prior consent to the loans. The reasoning followed that an individual cannot claim oppression if they have acquiesced or consented to the actions in question, a point highlighted in the case of *Irvin and Johnson*.

[26-28] The court applied a factual lens, noting that the deceased’s participation in the loan scheme, evidenced by signed financial documents, compromised the appellant's claim. The appellant attempted to dissociate the consent given by her deceased husband, arguing her standing as executrix should afford her distinct rights from those of the deceased.

However, the court found that the executor's rights are circumscribed by the obligations and agreements made by the deceased, reinforcing the binding effect of the shareholders’ agreement on the estate.

[30-31] The court concluded that the loans being characterized as "advance dividends" did not change the fundamental nature of the transactions. The obligation to honor the buy and sell provisions of the shareholders' agreement dictated how proceeds from insurance and shares should be managed.

Moreover, since the financial arrangements were conducted transparently among shareholders, the alleged exclusion from benefits did not meet the threshold for oppression.

## C – Conclusion
[38] The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the requirements for establishing oppressive or unfairly prejudicial conduct under § 163 were not satisfied. The dismissal of the appeal signified a reaffirmation of the role of shareholder agreements and the rights of executors in managing estates. The approach taken by the court underscored that acquiescence by the deceased in financial dealings limited the grounds for claiming oppression post his death.

In essence, the court stressed the importance of adherence to corporate governance and shareholder agreements in assessing claims of oppressive conduct. The implications for similar future cases suggest a rigorous standard for proving claims of unfair prejudice, especially in closely held companies. The appeal was dismissed with costs, endorsing the integrated understanding of shareholder rights and the procedural requisites under the Companies Act.
