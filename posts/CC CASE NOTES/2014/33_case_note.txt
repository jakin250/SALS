Source Folder: 2014

### Case Note: Khohliso v S and Another [2014] ZACC 33

#### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–11])
The case involves Ms. Nokhanyo Khohliso, a traditional healer from the Transkei, who was charged and convicted for possessing two vulture's feet, a substance she intended to use in a traditional remedy against theft ([2]). Her conviction was based on Section 13(c) and Section 84(13) of Decree 9, issued in 1992 by the then President of the Republic of Transkei ([3]–[8]). This Decree prohibited possession of the carcasses of protected wild animals, which include vultures, and imposed strict liability without the requirement of intent, leading to concerns about fairness and constitutional rights ([9]–[10]).

After the Eastern Cape Local Division of the High Court overturned her conviction, declaring the provisions of the Decree constitutionally invalid, Khohliso approached the Constitutional Court for confirmation of this declaration ([10]). The High Court found that Section 84(13) violated the right to a fair trial under Section 35(3)(h) of the Constitution, as well as infringing upon the equality clause in Section 9 by discriminating between areas in the province based on differing legal frameworks ([10]).

#### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 12–18])
The central legal question before the Constitutional Court was whether Decree 9 constituted a provincial Act or an Act of Parliament, thus necessitating confirmation of the High Court's declaration of invalidity under Section 167(5) and Section 172(2)(a) of the Constitution ([12]). The Court scrutinized the nature of Decree 9 — whether it was conduct by the President or something that merited a different classification based on its legislative history and post-1994 treatment ([13]).

Furthermore, the Court considered whether legislative endorsement from the Eastern Cape Provincial Legislature was necessary for Decree 9 to hold the status of a provincial Act or whether it could remain valid as subordinate legislation despite its problematic origin ([15]–[18]).

#### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 19–29])
The Constitutional Court relied on principles of constitutional interpretation and prior judgments to determine the status of pre-1994 legislation. For instance, in *Weare and Another v Ndebele NO and Others* and *Mdodana v Premier, Eastern Cape and Others*, case law established that legislative status could hinge on origin, territorial application, and treatment by contemporary legislatures ([21]–[29]).

The Court also referenced specific constitutional provisions:
- **Section 167(5)** of the Constitution, which provides that the Constitutional Court makes the final decision on the constitutionality of Acts.
- **Section 172(2)(a)** clarifying that any order of constitutional invalidity must be confirmed by this Court to be enforceable ([11]).

#### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 30–50])
In applying these rules, the Court drew distinctions between Decree 9 and legislation passed by recognized legislative bodies. It was held that Decree 9 was not a presidential act as it stood outside the parameters defined by the Constitution regarding the powers of the former TBVC (Transkei, Bophuthatswana, Venda, Ciskei) states and acknowledged their lack of legal status under the current constitutional order ([13], [14]). 

The Court concluded that Decree 9 did not qualify as a provincial Act primarily due to its origin as a decree of an unrecognized military regime and the fact that it had not been subsequently treated as valid legislation by the Eastern Cape Legislature ([23], [27]).

Despite a reference in the Eastern Cape Parks and Tourism Agency Act to Decree 9, the Court determined that it did not constitute endorsement sufficient to elevate the Decree's status ([31], [34]). The lack of any meaningful post-1994 legislative treatment also informed the Court's conclusion that there were no sufficient grounds to accept the Decree as having provincial status ([48]–[50]).

#### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 51–53])
The Constitutional Court held that Decree 9 is valid and applicable only as historical legislation in the Transkei but does not have the status of a provincial Act or an Act of Parliament. This means that the High Court’s declaration of invalidity did not require confirmation from the Constitutional Court ([48]). 

The implication of this ruling suggests that the courts can continue to navigate the complexities surrounding legacy legislation from the apartheid-era, facilitating a cleaner statute book. The Court also emphasized a need for legislative bodies to address laws emanating from "a bygone undemocratic era," advocating for a legal environment that is democratically accountable and reflective of contemporary values ([53]).

In conclusion, the application was dismissed, and no costs were awarded, signalling respect for the rights-related nature of the appeal, aligning with the principles set forth in *Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources* ([52]).
