Supreme Court of Appeal

FEDSURE GENERAL INSURANCE LIMITED v. CAREFREE INVESTMENTS (PROPRIETARY) LIMITED

Following the discharge of the container on 8 June 1995, the goods were moved to a warehouse in Durban and were awaiting customs clearance when they were stolen on 8 July 1995.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2001 [2001] ZASCA 61 ## F – Facts The dispute arises from a marine

Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa
Case No: 477/99
Judges: Howie, Schutz, Mpati JJA, Cloete, and Brand AJJA
Date of Hearing: 27 August 2001
Date Delivered: 11 September 2001
Citation: FEDSURE GENERAL INSURANCE LTD v CAREFREE INVESTMENTS (PROPRIETARY) LTD [2001] ZASCA 61

F – Facts

The dispute arises from a marine insurance policy issued by the appellant, Fedsure General Insurance Limited, to the respondent, Carefree Investments (Proprietary) Limited. The respondent, a clothing manufacturer from Ladysmith, was insured against various risks, including theft, concerning a consignment of fabric ("the goods") transported by sea from South Korea to Durban ([para 1]).

Following the discharge of the container on 8 June 1995, the goods were moved to a warehouse in Durban and were awaiting customs clearance when they were stolen on 8 July 1995. The theft occurred while the goods were in a bonded warehouse awaiting clearance, a situation affected by the respondent's cash flow issues, which delayed the clearance process ([paras 1–3]).

The High Court in Johannesburg ruled in favor of the respondent when it sued for the insurance claim. The appellant contended that the goods were not in the "ordinary course of transit" at the time of the theft, asserting that the insurance policy had already terminated ([para 2]).

The policy was governed by the 'Institute Cargo Clauses,' particularly the warehouse to warehouse or transit clause, which stipulates the insurance's duration and conditions under which it may terminate ([para 3]). The core issue revolves around whether the goods were still in transit at the time of the theft or whether the insurance had lapsed according to the terms of the policy ([paras 4–10]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issues before the court were:

  1. Whether the goods were in the ordinary course of transit at the time of the theft ([para 9]).
  2. Whether the insurance policy had terminated under the terms specified in the warehouse to warehouse clause ([paras 11–12]).
  3. The relevance and weight of the respondent's intent and circumstances surrounding the storage and clearance of the goods ([paras 12–15]).

R – Rules

The rules applicable to this case are found within the Institute Cargo Clauses, specifically the provisions regarding the duration and termination of coverage. Key aspects include:

  • Clause 8.1: Insurance covers goods from the point they leave the warehouse until delivery to the consignee or other designated storage.
  • Clause 8.1.2: Insurance terminates if goods are stored outside the ordinary course of transit or if stored for allocation or distribution purposes ([para 3]).

Further, case law interpretations, such as Pearson v The Directors of the Commercial Union Assurance Company and Tension Overhead Electric (Pty) Ltd v National Employers General Insurance Co Ltd, clarify that delays or interruptions for collateral purposes can disrupt the ordinary course of transit, affecting insurance coverage ([paras 12–14]).

A – Application

The court examined the factual circumstances surrounding the theft, focusing on whether or not the goods in question were in transit. The only testimony came from the managing director of the respondent and the operations manager of the Portnet container terminal. From the undisputed facts, it was evident that the theft occurred due to delays stemming from the respondent's decision to postpone clearance for financial reasons ([paras 5–8]).

Despite knowledge of the shipment's arrival, the respondent failed to act promptly by securing financing and clearing the goods in a timely manner. The court noted that the theft was well beyond a reasonable period for customs clearance, thereby indicating that the goods were not stored merely for transit purposes but retained in the warehouse due to the respondent's choice for commercial convenience ([para 15]).

The court also addressed the argument of subjective intent, reaffirming that while intentions can govern storage decisions, the determination should ultimately rest on objective facts as to whether storage conditions align with the "ordinary course of transit" ([paras 13–15]).

C – Conclusion

The court ultimately concluded that the goods were not in the "ordinary course of transit" at the time of theft; thus, the appellant's defense was upheld, leading to a ruling in favor of the appellant ([paras 16–17]).

This case illustrates the critical balancing act between insured intent, the factual matrix underpinning good storage practices, and adherence to the specific terms outlined in marine insurance policies. The ruling serves as a potent reminder of the importance of prompt action in securing goods under insurance coverage and the implications of a delay on the insurance claims process.

Legal Implications

This decision provides vital clarity on the interpretation of transit insurance provisions, particularly regarding the criteria that delineate when goods are considered to still be in transit. It underscores the necessity of timely actions by insured parties in maintaining insurance coverage and navigating the customs process to avoid unintended lapses in protection against risks such as theft.

In sum, the appeal was successful, nullifying the trial court's judgment, which had found in favor of the respondent, thereby dismissing their claim with costs.
