Aquila Steel (S Africa) (Pty) Ltd v Minister of Mineral Resources and Others [2018] ZACC 5

Between April 2005 and December 2011, both Aquila and ZiZa submitted applications for rights concerning the mineral resources in the Northern Cape, leading to overlapping titles ([2]–[12]).

Constitutional Court 2019 [2018] ZACC 5

F – Facts

The case revolves around an application for a prospecting right involving Aquila Steel (S Africa) (Pty) Limited (Aquila) and several respondents, primarily the Minister of Mineral Resources and associated officials, and competing entities, Pan African Mineral Development Company (PAMDC) and ZiZa Limited. The facts establish a complex history of applications for prospecting and mining rights under the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act (MPRDA).

Between April 2005 and December 2011, both Aquila and ZiZa submitted applications for rights concerning the mineral resources in the Northern Cape, leading to overlapping titles ([2]–[12]). The MPRDA, enacted on 1 May 2004, introduced a new framework for mineral rights, granting exclusivity to holders of unused old-order rights for one year, post-conversion deadline ([4]–[6]). Aquila applied for a prospecting right in April 2006, after ZiZa's application had already been made but hadn’t been resolved ([10]–[12]).

In July 2015, the Minister ruled against Aquila, asserting that ZiZa’s application was valid and hence denied Aquila’s subsequent mining rights ([20]–[21]). The litigation history shows an initial successful High Court review of the Minister’s decision, which was overturned by the Supreme Court of Appeal. The matter ultimately arrived at the Constitutional Court ([22]–[30]).

I – Issues

The principal legal questions raised for determination included:

  1. Was the grant of a prospecting right to ZiZa on 26 February 2008 valid?
  2. When Aquila applied for a prospecting right on 18 April 2006, was it entitled to do so, or was it precluded by ZiZa’s application?
  3. Should the Minister have upheld Aquila’s appeal against the refusal to grant it a mining right?
  4. Does the doctrine in Kirland / Oudekraal impede relief for Aquila?
  5. If not, what remedy is Aquila entitled to? ([36]–[37]).

R – Rules

The legal framework guiding the case is primarily embedded in the MPRDA, particularly provisions relating to:

  • Rights of old-order applicants under transitional provisions (Schedule II).
  • Regulations governing the application for prospecting rights (sections 16 and 17).
  • Standards for administrative fairness as outlined in the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA).

Judicial precedents relevant to the case include:

  • Oudekraal Estates and Kirland Investments, concerning the effects of administrative decisions previously made by an organ of state ([92]–[94]).
  • The Constitution's stipulations on equality and just administrative action, reinforcing the need for transparency and fairness in government decision-making ([66]–[69]).

A – Application

The Constitutional Court, in a thorough analysis, underscored the failure of the Department to comply with requirements of the MPRDA concerning the acceptance and processing of ZiZa's application ([40]–[67]). It determined that:

  • ZiZa’s application was marked by substantial deficiencies that warranted its rejection, aligning with the requirement that a proper application must conform to the statutory regulations for it to be accepted.
  • The legitimacy of Aquila’s right to apply for a prospecting right was reinforced post the expiration of the exclusivity period for ZiZa ([59], [62]).

The ruling emphasized that inaccuracies in the Department's management of the applications displayed a pattern of institutional incompetence, thus justifying Aquila's claims ([29], [111]). The Tort of Non-Return under PAJA was highlighted, but the court clarified that Aquila’s appeals against the acceptance of ZiZa’s application were valid despite procedural misalignments in the initial actions taken by the Department ([66]).

In discussing the remedy, the Court supported a substitution order under PAJA, allowing for Aquila’s mining right application to proceed given the evidence and circumstances presented ([109]–[112]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately allowed Aquila's appeal, affirming the High Court’s order which had initially set aside the Minister’s decisions. The Court held that Aquila's application for a mining right was incorrectly denied based on a flawed premise that wrongly considered ZiZa's application to be valid ([121]). The findings clarified the legal interpretations of the MPRDA, specifically the exclusivity and validity of old-order rights and their transitioning into MPRDA rights.

The implications of this judgment extend not only to Aquila and its immediate financial interests but also to the future handling of applications under the MPRDA, ensuring that competently managed compliance, transparency, and fairness take precedence in the departmental review process. Moreover, this case outlines the importance of recognizing the limits of administrative action and the necessity for just administrative decisions, thus highlighting the balance between longstanding legal principles and contemporary administrative realities within South Africa's mineral rights management.
