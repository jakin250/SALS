Supreme Court of Appeal

Cipla Vet (Pty) Ltd v Merial and Others (1068/2020)

This appeal arises from a cost order issued by Murphy J on 24 July 2014, as a consequence of a patent infringement dispute between Cipla Vet (Pty) Ltd (the appellant) and Merial, Merial Ltd, and Merial South Africa (Pty) Ltd (the respondents).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–20])

This appeal arises from a cost order issued by Murphy J on 24 July 2014, as a consequence of a patent infringement dispute between Cipla Vet (Pty) Ltd (the appellant) and Merial, Merial Ltd, and Merial South Africa (Pty) Ltd (the respondents). The appellants challenged the validity of South African Patent No 96/8057, initially raising multiple grounds, prominently the issue of obviousness. Following multiple amendments to their pleadings, the appellant abandoned the obviousness argument shortly before the trial commenced ([para 2]).

In Murphy J's order, costs were awarded to the respondents, including the costs of two counsel and the qualifying fees of expert witness Prof Barbour. A separate portion of the order mandated the appellant to cover the "wasted costs" incurred due to its amendment of the plea. Subsequently, the respondents contested the absence of explicit inclusion of the costs pertaining to two counsel and expert witnesses in the wasted costs order, prompting the request for clarification from the original judgment. This application was first dismissed by Baqwa J but was later reversed by a full bench of the High Court ([paras 3–5]).

The Full Court augmented Murphy J's order to clarify that "the defendants’ wasted costs shall include the cost of two counsel and the qualifying fees of their expert witnesses," prompting the appellant to further appeal this outcome to the Supreme Court of Appeal. The Supreme Court effectively reexamined the scope and intention of Murphy J’s order ([paras 6–8]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([para 8])

The critical issue presented for consideration by the Supreme Court of Appeal is whether the Full Court erred in its clarification and variation of Murphy J's order, specifically along the lines of:

  • Was Murphy J's original order ambiguous regarding the inclusion of costs for two counsel and the qualifying fees for expert witnesses in the context of wasted costs?

Understanding the proper interpretation of Murphy J's intentions regarding the costs awarded is pivotal to resolving this case.

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 9–11])

The legal foundation for the interpretation of court orders can be derived from both procedural and common law. The Supreme Court of Appeal outlined the following principles:

  1. Rule 42(1)(b): A court can rescind or vary a judgment when it contains ambiguity, error, or omission ([para 9]).
  2. Common Law: Similar principles recognize that relief can be granted for ambiguities, provided it does not alter the judgment’s substance ([para 9]).
  3. Precedent Case Law: Reference was made to Firestone South Africa (Pty) Ltd v Gentiruco AG, where it was elucidated how the meaning of court orders could support clarifications or corrections based on ambiguity ([para 9]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 12–20])

The Supreme Court meticulously evaluated Murphy J’s order and the intentions behind it. The court noted that:

  • The interpretation of Murphy J's order was hinged on paragraph 96, which acknowledged the complexity of the case and recognized the necessity for the employment of two counsel and the agreement regarding expert witness fees ([paras 11–12]).
  • The court underscored that the term “wasted costs” should be read in conjunction with the observations in para 96, thereby justifying the inclusion of both counsel costs and qualifying fees as essential components of the wasted costs ([paras 14–15]).

The appellant's argument against the clarity of Murphy J’s judgment was deemed unconvincing by the court, as there was recognition of both the agreement regarding expert fees and the complexity of the case during the original ruling ([para 16]). Moreover, the court dismissed concerns regarding any purported delay in seeking clarification, finding that such delay did not prejudice the clarity or enforcement of the order ([para 18]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 19–21])

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the Full Court’s interpretation and variation of Murphy J's order, confirming that:

  • The order for wasted costs appropriately encompassed the fees for two counsel and the qualifying expert fees, which were deemed to have been impliedly recognized by Murphy J’s findings ([para 19]).
  • The appeal was therefore dismissed with costs, effectively affirming the Full Court's decision to provide clarity on the original order and the basis for costs awarded ([para 21]).

This case emphasizes the significance of clear and explicit wording in court orders and the necessity for courts to interpret such orders in light of the context and the intentions of the presiding judges. The ruling also reinforces the procedural framework that allows parties facing ambiguities in judgments to seek clarifications, upholding the principle of fairness in judicial proceedings.
