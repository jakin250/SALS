# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of Sibusiso Zulu and Qeda Jeremiah Zulu v The State 

**Case Title:** THE SUPREME COURT APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA  
**Case No:** 372/94  
**Coram:** F H Grosskopf, Nienaber, Marais JJA  
**Heard:** 15 September 1997  
**Delivered:** 29 September 1997  

## F - Facts

In this case, the appellants, Sibusiso Zulu and Qeda Jeremiah Zulu, were convicted of multiple serious offences including six counts of murder and 15 counts of attempted murder in the Natal Provincial Division. The first appellant, who was 19 at the time of the crimes, received a 25-year sentence for the murders, while his uncle, the second appellant, aged 33, was sentenced to death for the same counts, alongside a concurrent 15-year sentence for attempted murder ([para 2–3]).

The offences occurred on 2 March 1993 when a group of children was attacked while being transported to school. The attackers, including the appellants, used automatic firearms during this assault, which tragically resulted in the deaths of six children and injuries to many others ([para 4]). Following a police investigation, the first appellant was arrested on 4 March 1993. His confession, obtained after an extended series of interrogations, became a pivotal element in the prosecution's case. The first appellant argued that his statement was not made freely and voluntarily due to alleged police coercion, including physical assault and being instructed on content ([paras 5–7]).

The court a quo conducted a thorough examination of the confession's admissibility during a trial-within-a-trial. The first appellant’s testimony regarding police misconduct was ultimately rejected, and his confession was deemed admissible ([paras 8–19]). Similar proceedings were applied to the second appellant, whose conviction hinged on eyewitness testimonies corroborating his participation in the attack ([paras 21–25]).

## I - Issues

The central legal issues presented in this appeal were:

1. Whether the confession of the first appellant was admissible given the alleged coercion during interrogations and the claim of undue influence ([para 6]).
   
2. Whether the evidence provided by eyewitnesses was sufficient to uphold the second appellant’s conviction based on identification ([paras 21–25]).

3. The implications of evolving constitutional standards regarding capital punishment in South Africa, particularly in light of the Constitutional Court's ruling in *S v Makwanyane* which deemed capital punishment unconstitutional ([para 26]).

## R - Rules

The key legal principles and rules applied in this case include:
1. The standard for admissibility of confessions set in *S v Zuma and Others 1995(1) SACR 568 CC*, which places the onus on the accused to demonstrate that the confession was extracted under duress.
   
2. The implications of lengthy interrogations as articulated in *S v Mkwanazi 1966(1) SA 736(A)*, where such practices can indeed affect the voluntariness of a confession ([para 8]).

3. The principles relevant to the evaluation of eyewitness testimony, where the credibility and reliability of witnesses are critical considerations, particularly under stress ([para 24]).

4. The constitutional prohibitions against capital punishment following the *Makwanane* ruling, which render any existing death sentences void ([para 26]). 

## A - Application

In applying the law to the facts, the court closely examined the confession of the first appellant against established legal standards. The court found that despite the lengthy interrogations, there was no credible evidence demonstrating that the first appellant was coerced into providing his statement. The primary consideration was the lack of a corroborating narrative supporting claims of police misconduct. The court rejected the notion that the interrogations, though extended, had unduly influenced the first appellant, citing how he quickly began to confess following the start of the third interrogation session ([paras 8–20]).

Turning to the second appellant, the court affirmed the credibility of eyewitness accounts, highlighting that the witnesses had not only identified the second appellant during the attack but had also provided consistent testimony. The court held that the identification process was sound and not marred by significant inconsistencies, invalidating concerns of mistaken identity raised by the appellants ([paras 21–25]).

Regarding the death sentence of the second appellant, the court invoked the *Makwanyane* decision to invalidate the capital punishment aspect of the previous ruling and mandated a remittance to the trial court for re-sentencing consistent with the new constitutional standard ([para 26]).

## C - Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court dismissed the appeals by both appellants. The first appellant’s confession was deemed admissible, as he failed to prove that it was made under duress. The court found that the state had proved its case against him beyond a reasonable doubt, affirming his conviction ([para 20]). The second appellant's conviction was also upheld based on reliable eyewitness testimony, but the death sentences awarded to him were invalidated due to the unconstitutionality of capital punishment post-*Makwanyane* ([para 26]).

This case reaffirms the courts' strict adherence to legal standards surrounding the admissibility of confessions while also navigating the evolving landscape of constitutional norms regarding capital punishment in South Africa. The implications for future cases involving serious crimes and confessional evidence are profound, underscoring the delicate balance between law enforcement practices and the rights of the accused in a constitutional democracy.
