Economic Freedom Fighters v Manuel (711/2019) [2020] ZASCA 172

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 172

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case concerns a defamation suit brought by Trevor Manuel against the Economic Freedom Fighters (EFF) and its leaders, Julius Malema and Mbuyiseni Ndlozi, following statements made by the EFF regarding the appointment process of the South African Revenue Service (SARS) Commissioner ([1]–[6]). On March 27, 2019, the EFF publicly objected to the recommendation of Edward Kieswetter, claiming the selection process, led by Manuel as panel chair, was marked by nepotism and corruption ([1]–[5]). The party accused Manuel of unlawfully appointing Kieswetter as Deputy Commissioner of SARS and asserted that Kieswetter was a relative and close associate of Manuel, which Manuel denied ([6]–[7]).

Manuel sought legal recourse after the EFF's statement was not retracted, filing an application in the Gauteng Division of the High Court for a declaration that the allegations were false and for damages amounting to R500,000, along with an order for retraction and an interdict against further publication ([3]–[4]). The High Court granted Manuel most of the requested relief, leading to an appeal from the applicants on certain aspects of the ruling ([26]).

I – Issues

The central legal issues in the case include:

  1. Defamation: Whether the statements made by the EFF were defamatory of Manuel ([19]).
  2. Defences to Defamation: Examination of the defenses raised by the applicants, including:
  • Truth and Public Interest: Whether the EFF could prove the truth of its allegations against Manuel ([37]).
  • Reasonable Publication: Applicability of the "reasonable publication" defense traditionally reserved for the media to private individuals and political parties ([40]).
  • Fair Comment: The standard for what constitutes fair comment in the context of political speech ([38]).
  1. Appropriateness of Remedies: Whether the court's orders for damages and an apology were appropriate, and if they could be granted without a full trial ([27]).

R – Rules

The court's ruling relied on various legal principles concerning defamation as established by South African precedent:

  1. Defamation Law: A statement is considered defamatory if it harms the reputation of an individual ([19], [30]).
  2. False Statements: The burden of proof lies with the defendant to establish that statements alleged to be defamatory are true and made in the public interest ([37–38]).
  3. Reasonable Publication: The precedent set in Bogoshi regarding the defense of reasonable publication was notably evaluated, requiring a consideration of the source's reliability and steps taken to verify facts ([40–48]).
  4. Inherent Power to Regulate Process: The need for oral evidence in claims for unliquidated damages due to the factual nature of such claims and their inherent complexities ([91]–[102]).

A – Application

The court examined the factual context surrounding the EFF's publication and the nature of the statements made. The court concluded that the statements indeed contained serious allegations that would lower Manuel's reputation in the eyes of reasonable individuals ([30]–[34]). The EFF’s defense based on the truth and public interest failed as they could not substantiate their claims that Manuel was related to Kieswetter or that the selection process was corrupt. The court underscored the obligation of the EFF to verify the information prior to publishing, especially given its vitriolic nature ([40]–[78]).

With regard to the defense of reasonable publication, the court upheld the High Court's decision that the EFF had not acted reasonably in making the statements due to a lack of verification of their source, which led to a willful disregard for the truth ([40]–[86]). The court ultimately determined that Manuel's dignity and reputation had been severely impacted, justifying the need for appropriate remedies ([87]).

The issue of damages was particularly contentious. The court held that the High Court had erred in awarding damages without hearing oral evidence, as the quantification of damages related strictly to evidence of reputational loss ([91]–[126]). Therefore, the appropriate remedy would involve referring the matter back for oral evidence on quantum, thereby negating the need for damages to be awarded in the absence of this evidence.

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld parts of the High Court ruling while also emphasizing procedural compliance concerning claims for damages. The findings that the statement was defamatory and the EFF's defenses were insufficient were affirmed ([132]). However, the award for damages and the requirement for a retraction and apology were set aside, directing these matters to be determined after hearing evidence. This ruling highlights the intricate balance between freedom of expression in political discourse and the protection of individual dignity within the South African legal context ([133]). The case establishes key judicial precedent on the handling of defamation claims in the age of social media and the responsibilities of political parties in disseminating information.
