South African Nursing Council v Khanyisa Nursing School (Pty) Ltd and Another (835/2022) [2023] ZASCA 86

Facts [1] The case arose between the South African Nursing Council (the appellant) and Khanyisa Nursing School (the first respondent), which has been accredited to train nurses in various locations, primarily at its Johannesburg campus.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 86

Facts

[1] The case arose between the South African Nursing Council (the appellant) and Khanyisa Nursing School (the first respondent), which has been accredited to train nurses in various locations, primarily at its Johannesburg campus. The Council was established under the Nursing Act 33 of 2005, tasked with maintaining standards for nursing education and training ([para 1]).

[2] Khanyisa sought accreditation for two nursing programs: a diploma in nursing (general nurse) and a higher certificate (auxiliary nurse). Although these applications were granted on April 26, 2022, the Council imposed a stipulation that these programs could only commence at the start of the academic year in 2023. Khanyisa challenged this, arguing that the stipulation would adversely affect its financial situation and the timely training of nurses ([para 2]).

[3] Khanyisa's urgent request to commence the programs in May 2022 was denied by the Council, which claimed it could not change the stipulation due to being functus officio. Consequently, Khanyisa filed an application in the Gauteng Division of the High Court to review the Council's decision and set aside the stipulation claiming it was arbitrary and unlawful ([para 4]).

[5] The High Court ruled that the Council’s accreditation process lacked alignment with the regulatory definition of an "academic year," leading to reasonable expectations that the program would commence earlier than stipulated. The Court ordered the Council to allow Khanyisa to start the programs by July 4, 2022, and to reimburse Khanyisa's legal costs ([para 5]).

Issues

[6] The appeal presented two primary issues:

  1. The interpretation of "any calendar year" in the regulations concerning the definition of an academic year, questioning whether it should refer strictly to the period from January 1 to December 31.
  2. Whether Khanyisa had a legitimate expectation to begin the programs in May 2022 based on the past conduct of the Council, particularly in light of previous mid-year approvals ([para 6]).

Rules

[8] The relevant rules derive from the Nursing Act 33 of 2005, specifically Section 42, which outlines accreditation requirements, and Section 58, which empowers the Minister of Health to make necessary regulations ([para 7]). The regulations specify that an academic year must consist of 44 weeks of training within "any calendar year," not strictly defined as January to December ([para 9]).

[15] The interpretation principles stress that the meaning of words should consider text, context, and purpose, rather than rely solely on dictionary definitions, which may lead to misinterpretation in a legal context ([para 15]).

Application

[18] The court deliberated on the meaning of "any calendar year" in the context of vocational training, emphasizing that the traditional requirement for an academic year to begin and end within the conventional calendar year was impractical given the urgent need for healthcare professionals in South Africa ([para 18]).

[20] The court concluded that the regulations should not impose rigid constraints on the educational timeline for nursing training, allowing for flexibility necessary to address the healthcare system's immediate needs ([para 20]). A detailed examination of the Council's historical practices indicated that the Council had previously accredited similar programs with mid-year commencements, reinforcing the need for a more flexible interpretation of academic years ([para 21]).

[25] It was ultimately determined that the definition of "any calendar year" should allow for a 12-month period starting from a given date, negating the Council's rationale for the contested stipulation ([para 25]).

Conclusion

[28] The court upheld the High Court’s order but based its reasoning on the interpretation of "any calendar year" rather than Khanyisa's expectations. The dismissal of the appeal affirmed Khanyisa's right to commence programs in mid-2022, indicating the Council had no lawful authority to impose the contested stipulation. The High Court’s costs order was also maintained, as it fell within the exercise of its discretion ([paras 28–29]).

The implications of this decision extend to the statutory interpretation of educational regulations within the healthcare sector, underscoring the necessity for adaptability in vocational training response to acute national needs.
