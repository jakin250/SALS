Constitutional Court

Destri Joseph Malcolm Ferris & Another v FirstRand Bank Limited & Another

[2] In October 2007, Destri and Soraya Ferris (the applicants) entered into a loan agreement with FirstRand Bank Limited (the first respondent) to finance the purchase of their home, secured by a mortgage bond.

Constitutional Court 2013

F – Facts

[2] In October 2007, Destri and Soraya Ferris (the applicants) entered into a loan agreement with FirstRand Bank Limited (the first respondent) to finance the purchase of their home, secured by a mortgage bond. However, by February 2009, the Ferrises fell into arrears and subsequently sought a debt review under section 86(1) of the National Credit Act 34 of 2005 (the Act). The debt counsellor, in March 2009, proposed revised repayment terms to FirstRand, which claim that the offer was rejected and that a counter-offer was never made.

[3] In September 2009, the debt counsellor applied to the Randburg Magistrate’s Court to declare the Ferrises over-indebted and to rearrange their debt obligations. While this application was pending, FirstRand issued a notice under section 86(10) of the Act, purportedly terminating the debt review. The Ferrises contended that the notice was not properly delivered, a point eventually conceded by FirstRand.

[4] On 30 April 2010, the Magistrate’s Court granted a debt-restructuring order favourable to the Ferrises. However, by May 2010, they failed to make payments under this new order. Consequently, FirstRand initiated enforcement proceedings in June 2010, leading to a default judgment against the Ferrises on 9 November 2011 after an application for summary judgment was made.

[5] The Ferrises sought rescission of the default judgment on 23 May 2012, citing their attorneys' negligence among other reasons, but their application was denied by the High Court in October 2012. Subsequent appeals to both the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal were unsuccessful, prompting the Ferrises to petition the Constitutional Court for leave to appeal.

I – Issues

[9] Three primary issues were identified for resolution by the Constitutional Court:

(a) Should the Court condone the late filing of the application for leave to appeal?

(b) Does the application have reasonable prospects of success?

(c) Is it in the interests of justice to grant leave to appeal, given the above factors?

R – Rules

[13] Rule 42(1)(a) of the Uniform Rules of Court permits rescission of a default judgment if it was "erroneously sought or erroneously granted". The Court noted that under this rule, a showing of good cause, unlike in common law and Rule 31, is not necessary for rescission.

[14] The relevant provisions of the National Credit Act were emphasized; specifically, section 86(10) allows a credit provider to terminate a debt review with proper notice. Deriving from this are sections 88(3)(b)(ii) and 129(2), which clarify the enforcement rights of credit providers following breaches of debt-restructuring orders.

A – Application

[14-33] The Court analyzed whether the Ferrises met the grounds for rescission under Rule 42(1)(a). It concluded the default judgment was not erroneously granted, as the Ferrises had indeed breached the debt-restructuring order, which empowered FirstRand to enforce the loan. The Court further articulated that an absence of further notice following a breach was classified as a dilatory defence—not an error justifying rescission.

Several additional arguments from the Ferrises, including non-compliance by FirstRand with the debt review process, were found unconvincing. The Court ruled that once a debt-restructuring order is in place, compliance is explicitly required, and substantial compliance is not a valid excuse.

The Court stated that while the Ferrises claimed that their attorney's negligence led to their default, this did not constitute a compelling reason for rescission, especially considering that Mrs. Ferris acknowledged knowledge of the judgment shortly after it was entered.

C – Conclusion

[34] The Constitutional Court denied leave to appeal, primarily on the basis of a lack of reasonable prospects of success. The judgment reinforced the right of credit providers under the National Credit Act to enforce loans without additional notice following the breach of a debt-restructuring order. Further, the Court concluded that granting the Ferrises leave to appeal would only delay the inevitable foreclosure, and they had alternative remedies available, such as suing their lawyers for negligence.

The order issued was as follows: condonation for the late application was granted, but leave to appeal was refused, and there was no order as to costs, reflecting FirstRand's stance during the hearing. Thus, the case affirms the stringent interpretations of consumer protection laws regarding debt enforcement processes.
