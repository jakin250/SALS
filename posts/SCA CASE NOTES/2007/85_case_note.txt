Source Folder: 2007

# Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of Lodhi 2 Properties Investments CC v Bondev Developments (Pty) Ltd [2007] SCA 85 (RSA)

## F – Facts

The case concerns two close corporations, Lodhi 2 Properties Investments CC (the first appellant) and Lodhi 3 Properties Investments CC (the second appellant), both of which had a single member, Muhammed Islam Lodhi. The respondent, Bondev Developments (Pty) Ltd, is a property developer who entered into two agreements of sale with the appellants on 30 March 2001. Under these agreements, Lodhi 2 purchased Erf 4052, Eldoraigne Extension 40 for R119,000, while Lodhi 3 purchased Erf 4054 for R129,000. A deposit was paid to the attorneys in trust, and the balance of the purchase price was set to accrue interest at the prevailing rate.

The agreements stipulated that the appellants were to take occupation of the properties upon the proclamation of the township, which occurred sometime in late April 2001, with the transfer being effected on 1 May 2001. However, the appellants failed to erect buildings on the properties within the stipulated 18 months, prompting the respondent to seek retransfer of the properties in March 2005. This application was sent to the appellants' registered address, which was outdated, resulting in the appellants not being aware of the application until after default judgments were granted.

The appellants subsequently sought rescission of these judgments, arguing they were granted erroneously as they were unaware of the respondent’s intentions and their need to defend against the claims. The High Court dismissed their application, which led to the current appeal ([paras 1–6]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issues at hand are:

1. Whether the default judgments against the appellants were granted erroneously as per Rule 42(1)(a) of the Uniform Rules of Court.
2. Whether the absence of notice under Clause 8 of the agreements constituted a breach that would result in an error in judgment.
3. Whether previously undisclosed defenses concerning alleged forfeiture of restitution rights could be deemed relevant for rescission of the judgments ([paras 7–9]).

## R – Rules

The relevant legal framework includes:

1. **Rule 42(1)(a)** of the Uniform Rules of Court allows for rescission of a judgment that has been "erroneously sought or granted in the absence of any party affected thereby."

2. Legal precedents that define "erroneously granted" judgments, as articulated in: 
   - **Nyingwa v Moolman NO 1993 (2) SA 508 (Tk)**, confirming judgments may be deemed erroneous if there were facts unknown to the judge that would preclude granting the judgment if known.
   - **Stander v Absa Bank 1997 (4) SA 873 (E)**, which discusses cases where the validity of the judgment was questioned due to the lack of merit disclosure by the defendant ([paras 17–27]).

## A – Application

The court evaluated the appellants' arguments against the defined rules and prevailing precedents. 

1. **Error in Judgment**: The court found no basis for the claim that the judgments were granted erroneously simply due to the lack of notice required under Clause 8. Clause 11 of the agreements explicitly stated that no notice was necessary if the appellants failed to erect buildings. The respondent had adhered to the contractual terms by seeking retransfer based on the failure to perform, warranting no further notice ([paras 10–12]).

2. **Undisclosed Defences**: The appellants asserted that there were defenses that, had they been disclosed, would have precluded the judgments. However, the court emphasized that a valid judgment obtained in absence of the defendant cannot be classified as “erroneously granted” merely due to subsequent disclosures of a defense. The historical context provided by Nyingwa and Stander was distinguished, reaffirming that the judgment was procedurally sound and the defaults were legitimate ([paras 17–25]).

3. **Procedural Entitlement**: Contrary to the appellants’ assertion of lacking notice, the court concluded that the respondent rightfully obtained the default judgments based on the absence of any indication that the appellants intended to defend the matter. The judgments were thus valid and not rendered erroneous by the appellants' late defense claims ([paras 26–28]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the application for leave to appeal, affirming that the default judgments were not granted in error as contemplated by Rule 42(1)(a). The appellants' failure to erect buildings as per the contractual obligation justified the respondent's actions, and the court found no procedural irregularity affecting the judgments. The holding establishes that judgments obtained through proper notice and due diligence cannot be rescinded based on later revealed defenses, emphasizing the importance of upholding contractual obligations and procedural integrity in judicial proceedings ([paras 27–28]).

This case serves as a critical reminder of the definitive nature of contractual terms and the procedural requirements for defending against claims, reflecting the courts' inclination to uphold the sanctity of validly obtained judgments in the absence of compelling evidence to the contrary.
