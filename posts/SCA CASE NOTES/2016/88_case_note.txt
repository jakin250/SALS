Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: MEC: Social Development, Western Cape v The Justice Alliance of South Africa [2016] ZASCA 88

## F - Facts ([1–39])

This case stems from an appeal by the Member of the Executive Council (MEC) for Social Development in the Western Cape, and related departments, against a judgment from the Western Cape Division of the High Court. The High Court had declared four educational centres—including Die Bult Youth Centre, Eureka Youth Centre, Wellington Youth Centre, and Ottery Youth Centre—as child and youth care centres (CYCCs) under the Children’s Act 38 of 2005 (the Act).

The respondents, comprising The Justice Alliance of South Africa and the School Governing Body of Ottery Youth Care and Education Centre, initiated proceedings on 17 December 2013, claiming these centres met the definition of CYCCs and sought various forms of relief, including declaratory judgments and mandatory interdicts ([4]), reflecting a keen interest in the welfare of children under their care.

The appellants contested these claims, and the High Court granted several orders, particularly declaring Ottery, Die Bult, and Wellington as CYCCs effective from 1 April 2010 ([10]). The appellants appealed against four of these substantive orders, while the respondents cross-appealed concerning the absence of a cost order ([5]).

A critical element in this case was the statutory interpretation of sections 195 and 196 of the Children’s Act. The appellants contended that as of 31 December 2000, the operational status of these centres had changed; they had been closed and repurposed into educational institutions for learners with special needs, thereby impacting their legal status ([18]). Consequently, the centres did not meet the criteria established in the Act, since they were not maintained as CYCCs by 1 April 2010 when the relevant provisions took effect ([27]).

## I - Issues ([1–39])

The legal questions central to this appeal pertain to:

1. **Whether the educational centres qualify as CYCCs** under the Children’s Act, given their status change prior to 1 April 2010, which precluded them from meeting the statutory requirements.
  
2. **The extent to which the separation of powers doctrine** restricts judicial intervention in matters involving polycentric decision-making, notably in respect to the establishment and operational management of CYCCs.

3. **The procedural correctness surrounding the transfer of employees** from the Department of Education to the Department of Social Development,[21] and the statutory compliance regarding closures and re-establishment of centres ([23]).

## R - Rules ([6–39])

The court evaluated several statutory provisions and legal principles:

1. **Children’s Act 38 of 2005**: Particularly sections 195 and 196, which govern the establishment of CYCCs by a MEC for Social Development and outline the obligations of education providers concerning children in these facilities ([6],[9]).

2. **Legislative Precedents**: The interpretation of sections 196(1)(d) and (e) was crucial. The Act specifies that government industrial schools and reformatories must have been established in accordance with earlier Acts to qualify as CYCCs ([27]).

3. **Doctrine of Separation of Powers**: Following case law, notably in *International Trade Administration Commission v Scaw South Africa (Pty) Ltd* and the significant relevance to keeping judicial review away from policy-laden decisions made by the executive ([19], [20]).

4. **Public Service Act 103 of 1994**: Guiding principles concerning the transfer of public sector employees must allow for proper consultation and approval ([22]).

## A - Application ([1–39])

In analyzing the facts against these principles, the court concluded that:

1. **Statutory Interpretation**: The educational centres had been closed by the WCED as of 31 December 2000 and repurposed under the WC Schools Act. Therefore, at the enactment phase of the Children’s Act, they could not be considered as established under the earlier industrial or reformatory legislation ([18], [27]). The court rejected the WCC’s view that the definition should allow for a disjunctive interpretation of the statutory language, reaffirming that the original legislative intent should guide interpretation ([32]).

2. **Separation of Powers**: The court recognized that the orders made by the WCC intruded upon executive functions, particularly in relation to establishing and managing CYCCs. The court underscored that such decisions inherently involve political and policy considerations that the judiciary should avoid ([19]). 

3. **Employee Transfer Compliance**: The required employee transfers from the Department of Education to the Department of Social Development had not followed the necessary statutory protocol, which emphasized informed consent and consultation. This failure undermined the orders relating to operationalizing the centres as CYCCs ([21]).

## C - Conclusion ([1–41])

Given these analytical points, the Supreme Court of Appeal overturned the appealed orders (iii), (iv), (v), and (vi) made by the High Court. The court found that the educational centres did not meet the statutory conditions to qualify as CYCCs and highlighted that, due to their closure and re-establishment as public schools, they could not revert to their previous classifications without legal remediation ([39]).

Also, the court dismissed the cross-appeal for costs against the first respondent, asserting that the principles applicable in *Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources & others* were not relevant since it did not pertain to a constitutional assertion. Consequently, costs were granted in favor of the appellants for the cross-appeal-related proceedings ([41]). This case is significant as it underscores the limits of judicial authority in administrative and executive matters while reaffirming the legislative framework's intended operations concerning child welfare under the Children’s Act.
