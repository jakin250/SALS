Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2006

# Case Note: Desert Palace Hotel Resort v Northern Cape Gambling Board [2006] SCA 143 (RSA)

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

The case involves the Desert Palace Hotel Resort (Pty) Ltd (the Appellant) and the Northern Cape Gambling Board (the Respondent). The Board sought to recover penalties imposed on Desert Palace for non-payment of gambling levies during the period from November 2000 to November 2001 ([1]).

Desert Palace applied for a casino operator licence in 1999, which was approved by the Board on 11 August 1999. However, no formal licence was issued during the relevant period, as the casino began operations on temporary premises on 12 June 2000 without a formally issued licence. Although a temporary licence was issued on 16 October 2001, it was to operate retrospectively, a provision that was later withdrawn by court order ([2]).

Desert Palace initially made levy payments until November 2000 but withheld payments thereafter on legal advice until November 2001, subsequently paying the levy but not the penalty imposed by the Board ([3]).

In the lower court, the parties submitted a stated case under Rule 33(1), posing two questions for determination: 
1. Whether Desert Palace was a "holder of a licence" as per Section 65 of the Northern Cape Gambling and Racing Act 5 of 1996 during the relevant period.
2. Whether the Board could impose penalties on outstanding levies based on Regulation 95, and if that regulation was duly authorized under the Act ([4]).

The Northern Cape Provincial Division ruled in favor of the Board, prompting Desert Palace to appeal ([5]–[6]).

### I – Issues

The main legal questions arising from the case were:
1. Was Desert Palace considered a "holder of a licence" under Section 65 of the 1996 Act during the period it operated without a formally issued licence? ([4])
2. Was the Board authorized to impose penalties for the non-payment of levies based on Regulation 95 of the Act? ([4])

### R – Rules

To address these issues, the court relied on the definitions and requirements laid out in several key statutory provisions:

1. **Section 65 of the Northern Cape Gambling and Racing Act 5 of 1996** - This section specifies the liability for gambling levies on the "holder of a licence," stating that such a licence must be physically "issued" ([5]).

2. **Regulation 95** - The Board's capacity to levy penalties for non-payment of gambling levies under this regulation was questioned, particularly whether it was authorized by the enabling provision within the Act ([4]).

3. **Section 81 of the Northern Cape Gambling and Racing Amendment Act 6 of 2000** - This section mirrored the provisions of Section 65 of the 1996 Act regarding the liabilities of licence holders ([10]).

4. **Section 36 of the Act** - This provision allows for the issuance of temporary licences in respect of incomplete premises and specifies conditions that must be satisfied before a formal licence is issued ([14]).

5. **Statutory Interpretation Principles** - The court applied principles articulated in cases like **Stellenbosch Farmer’s Winery Ltd v Distillers Corporation (SA) Ltd**, highlighting the need for a sensible reading of statutory provisions incorporating contextual and linguistic considerations ([8]).

### A – Application

In assessing whether Desert Palace was a "holder of a licence," the court distinguished between the approval of an application and the physical issuance of a licence. Despite the Board having approved the application in August 1999, no documented licence was issued until after the relevant period, which led the court to conclude that Desert Palace could not be considered a licence holder during that time ([6], [12]). 

The court rejected the Board’s broader interpretation of "holder of a licence" as simply being a privilege, emphasizing that statutory language required a physical document for accountability and compliance ([16]).

Consequently, the court found that because the Act clearly stated that a licence must be "issued," and since no such document had been issued, Desert Palace did not satisfy the requirements to be considered a holder of a licence during the relevant timeframe ([17]). This rendered the imposition of penal responsibilities ineffective, making it unnecessary to consider the authorization of Regulation 95 ([17]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that Desert Palace was not a "holder of a licence" as contemplated under the relevant statutes during the period from June 12, 2000, to November 11, 2001. Therefore, the Appellant was not liable for penalties imposed by the Board for the non-payment of gambling levies ([17]). 

The appeal was upheld with costs, including the costs for two counsel, and the order of the lower court was dismissed ([18]). This ruling reaffirms the necessity for statutory compliance regarding the physical issuance of licences in the gambling context, impacting how future cases may interpret licensing laws under similar statutory frameworks.
