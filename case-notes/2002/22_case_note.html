Constitutional Court 2002 CCT 31/01

Case Note: Constitutional Court of South Africa - Case CCT 31/01

FIRAC Format

F – Facts:
The appellants, Ellen Jordan, Louisa Johanna Francina Broodryk, and Christine Louise Jacobs, were charged under the Sexual Offences Act 23 of 1957 for engaging in commercial sex (specifically section 20(1)(aA)), which criminalizes any person who engages in sexual acts for reward. They acknowledged their contravention of the Act but argued that the relevant provisions were unconstitutional. The High Court ruled that section 20(1)(aA) was unconstitutional, leading to an appeal by the State to the Constitutional Court to confirm this order. The Court had to determine the constitutional validity of the legislation in light of the interim Constitution ([paras 1–8]).

I – Issues:
The primary legal question revolved around whether section 20(1)(aA) discriminated unfairly against women, whether it constituted an unjustifiably limitation on the rights to equality (section 8), dignity (section 10), privacy (section 13), and economic activity (section 26) as articulated in the interim Constitution ([paras 7–10], [30], [50]). Additionally, it addressed whether the provision aimed solely at the prostitute, disadvantaging them relative to their clients, and how this discrimination, if any, should be treated under constitutional law ([paras 19–20], [51]).

R – Rules:
The constitutional analysis hinged on several key principles:

  • Section 8(2) of the interim Constitution prohibits any unfair discrimination on any grounds, including gender.
  • Section 10 affirms the right to human dignity for every individual.
  • Section 11 safeguards the freedom and security of the person.
  • Section 13 protects individuals' rights to privacy.
  • Section 26 concerns the right to engage in economic activity, allowing for regulation by the state that promotes human well-being in a democratic society ([paras 8, 10, 20, 30, 51]).

The courts were required to evaluate whether the differentiation between prostitutes and their patrons constituted unfair discrimination, as directed by the guidelines established in cases like Harksen v Lane NO and other relevant jurisprudence ([paras 57–62]).

A – Application:
The Court, particularly in the majority opinion by O'Regan and Sachs JJ, assessed the critical link between the provision’s punitive design and its alleged discriminatory impact. The predominant focus was on whether the criminalization of the prostitute while exculpating the customer was significantly distorting the equality principle as intended in the Constitution.

Despite recognizing potential indirect discrimination, particularly since the overwhelming demographic of prostitutes was female, the court underscored that section 20(1)(aA) aimed at the commercial sex industry as a coherent strategy to regulate harmful social practices. The classification between dealer and customer was seen as rational, given that prostitution's nature encapsulates an economic transaction characterized by exploitation and vulnerability ([paras 89–100], [51–70]).

Moreover, the state advanced several public interest justifications for maintaining the criminal status of prostitution, including issues of public health and social morality. These claims, while contested by the appellants and amici curiae, were viewed within the broader spectrum of legislative discretion regarding social order management ([paras 117–120]).

Ultimately, the Court ruled that while section 20(1)(aA) did impose a discriminatory impact against women, it would grant Parliament 30 months to address these concerns legislatively. This conclusion was notably influenced by the Constitution's commitment to gender equality and broader societal implications regarding moral and legal frameworks ([paras 128–129]).

C – Conclusion:
The Constitutional Court concluded that section 20(1)(aA) of the Sexual Offences Act was unconstitutional due to its unfair discriminatory effects on women. However, the Court suspended the declaration of invalidity for 30 months to permit Parliament the necessary time to formulate a constitutionally compliant legislative response. Thus, the appeal by the State was partially dismissed, while the convictions against the appellants were upheld pending legislative review ([paras 129–130]).

This case highlights the complex interplay between legislative frameworks, gender rights, and societal norms within South Africa’s evolving constitutional jurisprudence, particularly regarding sensitive topics like commercial sex and women’s rights. The decision serves as a significant indicator of how courts may navigate moral and legal challenges while adhering to constitutional mandates of equality and justice.
