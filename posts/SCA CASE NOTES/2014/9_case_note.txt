Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# Case Note: Malcolm v Premier, Western Cape (207/2013) [2014] ZASCA 9

## F – Facts
In this case, the appellant, **Cameron Stewart Malcolm**, was born on **[1]** 2nd June 1987. At the age of six, in **1993**, he was diagnosed with **Stage 1 Hodgkin's Lymphoma** and subsequently treated at the **Red Cross Children's Hospital** in Cape Town. During his hospital stay, he alleges that there was an outbreak of **Hepatitis B** linked to the hospital. By **October 1994**, he was diagnosed with Hepatitis B and attributed his infection to the negligence of the hospital and its staff.

Malcolm initiated a claim against the **Premier of the Western Cape** for damages stemming from this alleged negligence. However, the claim was met with a **special plea of prescription** based on section 11(d) of the **Prescription Act 68 of 1969**, which stipulates a three-year prescription period commencing when a claim becomes due. The prescriptive period, starting in October 1994 and considering Malcolm was a minor, was subject to the provisions of sections **13(1)(a)** and **(i)** of the Act, thus delaying completion of the prescription.

In the lower court, **Louw J** upheld the respondent's plea of prescription, arguing that Malcolm's minority status ceased on **1 July 2007**, when the **Children’s Act 38 of 2005** amended the age of majority from **21 to 18 years**. This led to the conclusion that the prescriptive period expired on **30 June 2008**, one year after he attained the age of 18 without filing his claim. Consequently, Malcolm appealed the decision which is the subject of this case note.

## I – Issues
The key legal questions arising in this case include:

1. What is the legal interpretation of "minor" in the context of the Prescription Act, specifically regarding the impact of the change in the age of majority from **21 to 18 years** in light of the **Children’s Act**?
2. How do changes in statutory interpretation apply retrospectively or prospectively concerning previously existing claims initiated by minors?

## R – Rules
The principal legal rules relevant to this case include:

1. **Prescription Act 68 of 1969**, particularly sections **11(d)** (prescription of claims) and **13(1)(a)** (regarding minors), which stipulates the non-commencement of prescription for minors until one year after their minority ceases.
2. **Children’s Act 38 of 2005** (commencement date 1 July 2007), which amends the age of majority from **21 to 18 years**, thus affecting the interpretation of the term “minor”.
3. Judicial interpretation principles, especially those derived from the **Interpretation Act 33 of 1957**, which suggests that statutory changes should not have a prejudicial retrospective effect unless expressly stated (section **12(2)(b)**).
4. The doctrine of **constitutional interpretation** which requires legislation to be interpreted in line with constitutional values, particularly with respect to the dignity of children as established under the **Bill of Rights**.

## A – Application
In evaluating the application of the law to the facts of this case, the court scrutinized the meaning of “minor” as defined in **Roux**, a precedent which established that “minor” refers strictly to a specific age threshold (under **21 years**), rather than their legal status at that time. 

The court assessed the implications of social changes and statutory amendments on existing legal frameworks. While the respondent argued that the amendment to the age of majority should affect the interpretation of "minor" retrospectively, the court clarified that this altered meaning should apply only to claims arising after the legislative change.

The ruling in **Roux** was reaffirmed as determining that before turning **21**, Malcolm remained a minor for the purposes of prescription regardless of any legal changes in status that occurred thereafter. Thus, had Malcolm’s case been evaluated solely on the basis of age, his claim would not have prescribed until he turned 21.

Further, the court emphasized the **dignity rights** of young adults aged 18 to 21 in relation to prescription laws. It noted that applying a new definition retrospectively, which would disadvantage minors based on outdated assumptions about maturity, would infringe upon their dignity as enshrined in the **Constitution**.

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Malcolm’s appeal, dismissing the special plea of prescription with costs. The court found that the meaning of "minor" in the Prescription Act should align with the age of majority at **21 years**, until changed explicitly by legislative means effective only from **1 July 2007** onwards. The court reasoned that this interpretation was crucial to protecting the rights of individuals who were minors at the time their claims arose, emphasizing a more contemporary understanding of maturity and dignity.

The judgment has significant implications for future claims made by minors in South Africa, reinforcing the necessity for statutory provisions to adapt to evolving social norms and the constitutional imperative to respect the dignity of all individuals, particularly children transitioning to adulthood. 

Ultimately, the case underscores the balance courts must maintain between adhering to established legal interpretations and engaging with dynamic social conditions to ensure justice is served equitably.
