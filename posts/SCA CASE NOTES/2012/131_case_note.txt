# Case Note: Theart v Scheibert (630/11) [2012] ZASCA 131

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
In the matter of **Theart v Scheibert**, the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) addressed a dispute regarding the interpretation of a mutual will executed by Kristian Jens Korsgaard and Isabel Louisa Wilhelmina Korsgaard on 28 March 1983. The appellant, Diane Jean Theart, was a beneficiary under this mutual will, which became relevant after the testator's death on 6 May 2008. Following the testatrix’s death in 1990, the testator had executed several new wills, which excluded Theart as a beneficiary.

The Korsgaards had married in community of property, owning the property at 19 Dysart Road, Green Point, equally. The mutual will contained clauses suggesting it aimed to protect the interests of both spouses even after one of them passed away. The testator, however, after the testatrix's death, executed three wills bequeathing his estate solely to his nephew, the second respondent, Jan Willy Sundby ([1]–[8]).

The appeal followed the High Court's decision to non-suit Theart, which dismissed her claims to enforce the mutual will, asserting that it did not create a massing of estates and finding that the testator did not adiate. The appellant argued that she was entitled to inherit under the mutual will, asserting that the original was likely destroyed by the testator with the intent to revoke it, a claim the court had to address ([10]–[12]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues in this case were:
1. Did the mutual will create a massing of the estates of the testators?
2. Did the testator revoke the mutual will through subsequent testamentary instruments?
3. What was the proper interpretation of the mutual will's clauses concerning the rights and intentions of the testators? ([11]–[12]).

### R – Rules
The court relied on well-established principles of will interpretation and succession law. The rule that mutual or joint wills must be interpreted as separate wills, particularly in the context of spouses married in community of property, underpins the legal framework. The principle established in **Rhode v Stubbs** 2005 (5) SA 104 (SCA) was crucial, asserting that the lack of clarity in the phrasing of wills can lead to presumptions against massing, allowing the surviving spouse the power to revoke their will until the end ([20]).

Further references included cases like **The Receiver of Revenue, Pretoria v C H Hancke** 1915 AD 64 and various precedents recognizing the rebuttable presumption of revocation through the destruction of a will when it is not found ([25]).

### A – Application
The SCA analyzed the wording of the mutual will to determine whether it reflected mutual intentions regarding the massing of estates. The court rejected the High Court's interpretation that found irreconcilable clauses in the will, supporting a reading that synchronized the provisions to show that the first testator’s estate devolved upon the survivor's death rather than implying that both estates merged for testamentary purposes. The court emphasized that clauses intended to secure rights for both testators were not meant to bestow absolute rights onto the survivor that could terminate the joint trust ([16]–[22]).

As to the alleged revocation, the court articulated that no circumstantial evidence suggested a definitive intent to revoke the original will by the testator before the death of the testatrix. The relationship between Theart and the testatrix was characterized as close, giving rise to a natural presumption that the testatrix would retain her will’s provisions in favor of her family. The presumption against revocation prevailed due to a lack of evidence that the mutual will could have been in the testator's possession and destroyed, further supporting Theart's claims ([26]–[28]).

### C – Conclusion
The SCA held that the mutual will did create a massing of estates, thereby invalidating the testator's subsequent wills to the extent that they attempted to alter the joint disposition established by the mutual will. The appeal was allowed, with the court substituting the order previously made by the High Court and directing the Master of the High Court to recognize the mutual will as the guiding testamentary instrument for the joint estate consisting of 19 Dysart Road ([30]).

The ruling reinforced the principles surrounding mutual wills and the importance of preserving the testators' original intent within their testamentary foresight, thereby providing clarity for future cases involving similar circumstances. The appellant was awarded costs against the respondent, recognizing the necessity for equitable relief given the protracted legal contest at both lower and appellate levels ([29]).
