Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: SANRAL v City of Cape Town (66/2016) [2016] ZASCA 122

## F – Facts
The case at hand is an appeal from the Western Cape Division of the High Court concerning a review application brought by the City of Cape Town against the South African National Roads Agency Limited (SANRAL) regarding a proposal to declare portions of the N1 and N2 national roads as toll roads. The impetus stems from a decision made on September 2, 2008, by the Transport Minister to approve SANRAL's proposal, followed by SANRAL's publication of the toll road declaration in the Government Gazette on September 15, 2008 ([paras 1–21]).

Notably, the City of Cape Town argued that the requisite approval for declaring the toll roads had not been properly acquired as SANRAL's Board had failed to reach a formal decision to seek the Minister's approval. Consequently, the City sought a review and set aside of the Minister’s approval based on an alleged lack of compliance with statutory prescripts, particularly failure to consider public consultation as mandated by Section 27(4) of the South African National Roads Agency Limited and National Roads Act 7 of 1998 (SANRAL Act) ([paras 1–12], [paras 38–39]).

The review application was filed more than four years after the decisions were made, and the High Court found that this delay was unreasonable. However, the court ultimately condoned the delay based on the interests of justice ([paras 2–3], [paras 58, 207]). In summary, the original court found numerous procedural failings and the absence of a valid decision from SANRAL’s Board to pursue the tolling application, compelling the conclusion to set aside the Minister's approval and mandate a fresh process ([paras 67–71]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issues posed in this case include:

1. **Legality and Validity of Decisions**: Whether SANRAL and the Transport Minister complied with the statutory requirements outlined in the SANRAL Act in declaring the roads as toll roads ([paras 6–9]).
  
2. **Judicial Review and Delay**: Whether the High Court was correct in granting an extension of the 180-day period for instituting judicial review under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([paras 4, 59–70]).

3. **Principle of Public Participation**: Whether the public participation process prior to the Minister’s approval was sufficient and adhered to the legal requirements ([paras 4, 61–62]).

4. **Substantive Authority of the Transport Minister**: Did the Transport Minister misconstrue his powers by treating the decision to approve the tolling proposal as merely a procedural oversight ([paras 46–49, 105–106])?

## R – Rules
The following legal rules and precedents were pivotal in adjudicating the case:

1. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA)**: Section 7 prescribes a 180-day time limit for judicial reviews of administrative actions ([para 2]), while Section 9 allows for an extension if the interests of justice demand it ([paras 8, 70]).

2. **South African National Roads Act 7 of 1998**: Section 27 governs the declaration of toll roads, detailing the procedure SANRAL must follow to obtain the Transport Minister’s approval. Notably, Section 27(4) requires that the process includes public notification and consultation ([paras 35, 39]).

3. **Constitutional Principle of Legality**: This principle dictates that public power must be exercised in accordance with legal authority and that administrative actions may be subjected to judicial review ([paras 7, 8, 74]).

4. **Judicial Precedents**: The court referenced the Constitutional Court's decision in *Opposition to Urban Tolling Alliance v SANRAL* ([2012] ZACC 18) to reaffirm that even state organs are not immune from judicial scrutiny, emphasizing that the separation of powers does not extend to procedural non-compliance in matters of public interest ([paras 6, 8]).

## A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) critically evaluated SANRAL’s compliance with the aforementioned statutory requirements. Central to the court’s reasoning was the recognition that SANRAL’s Board had not formally resolved to seek the Minister’s approval before the proposal was presented, a significant procedural misstep which rendered the actions taken invalid. This failure was underscored by the absence of any reliable documentation of board decisions or discussions regarding the tolling proposal ([paras 54–95]).

The SCA noted that the failure to follow legal protocols not only prompted the court to question the legitimacy of the Minister's approval, but also necessitated a reassessment of the public participation process undertaken by SANRAL. The court held that the public had not been adequately informed nor consulted, which undermined the fairness and legality of the actions taken ([paras 46, 61–62]).

Furthermore, while acknowledging the delay in filing the review application, the SCA concurred with the lower court's decision to condone this delay. It highlighted the substantial public interest at stake, weighing it against the financial consequences to SANRAL and the prospective contractors, thus affirming the High Court’s exercise of discretion as reasonable ([paras 207–109]).

## C – Conclusion
In conclusion, the court dismissed SANRAL's appeal and upheld the findings of the lower court, emphasizing the need for compliance with legal protocols in public administrative actions. The SCA determined that without a valid board decision, the Transport Minister's approval was unenforceable, thus reaffirming the importance of statutory compliance in public governance ([paras 1–2, 113]). The implications of this ruling extend to emphasize the critical nature of public participation in administrative decision-making, thereby safeguarding democratic values and accountability within public agencies. Hence, the judgment stands as a critical point of reference in South African administrative law, particularly concerning the oversight of state entities and the obligations they hold toward the public.
