Fegen v Mphakathi (348/2012) [2013] ZASCA 100

Subsequently, the King sold the same properties to the Fegens, the first and second appellants, who completed the transaction without being aware of Mphakathi's prior rights ([1].

Supreme Court of Appeal 2013 [2013] ZASCA 100

F - Facts

The dispute in Fegen v Mphakathi ([1]) arose from the actions of Justice Sigcau, King of the Pondos, who sold two immovable properties, erf 711 and erf 712 Port St John’s, to Mr Douglas Mphakathi on 15 June 2000 ([7]). Mphakathi made a deposit for each property and was entitled to immediate occupation to cultivate crops as a means to finance the balance of the purchase price ([8]). However, Mphakathi encountered issues with unlawful occupants preventing his occupation of erf 711, prompting legal action to evict them, which culminated in a writ of ejectment granted in July 2003 ([8]).

Subsequently, the King sold the same properties to the Fegens, the first and second appellants, who completed the transaction without being aware of Mphakathi's prior rights ([1]. [2]). When the Fegens attempted to evict Mphakathi in 2004, he initiated a court application to declare their purchase null and void, citing knowledge of the prior agreement and the mala fides of the Fegens ([3], [6]).

The Eastern Cape High Court ruled in favor of Mphakathi, finding that he had established on a balance of probabilities that the Fegens knew of the earlier sale ([5]). The Fegens appealed, contesting both the grounds that Mphakathi’s prescription claim was relevant and the court's determination that they acted with knowledge of Mphakathi’s rights ([6]).

I - Issues

The case raised several legal questions, including:

  1. Did the prescription of Mphakathi's claim for transfer from the King have any bearing on his rights against the Fegens? ([6.1])
  2. Did the Fegens' knowledge of the prior sale indicate mala fides or fraudulent intent? ([6.2])
  3. Was the burden of proof correctly assigned in establishing the Fegens’ knowledge of Mphakathi’s prior claim? ([6.2])

R - Rules

The court primarily referenced provisions from the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, particularly concerning the interruption of prescription and the acknowledgement of liability ([23]). The legal principles surrounding good faith in commercial transactions and the concept of mala fides were pivotal. The court noted prior jurisprudence indicating that a new purchaser does not need to have actual knowledge of a prior right but must be cautious in acquiring property where an assertion of prior rights is evident ([29]).

The ruling in Lipschitz v Dechamps Textiles was cited to bolster the argument that prescription can be waived or acknowledged ([22]), suggesting that the King’s interactions with Mphakathi implied an ongoing recognition of his obligations.

A - Application

The court carefully evaluated the timeline and evidence relating to the transactions. It concluded that the Fegens had sufficient indicators of Mphakathi's occupancy and rights. Fegen had learned from Mphakathi’s legal actions regarding erf 711, further indicating he was aware of potential disputes ([10]–[11]). The court criticized the Fegens’ choice not to follow up on ambiguous information provided by the King’s representatives and their decision to regard the sale agreement as unimpeded by Mphakathi’s claims ([15]).

In the context of prescription, the court determined that Mphakathi’s claim was not extinguished because the King’s behaviour, including allowing Mphakathi to occupy the properties and cultivate them, indicated that liability had not been denied ([25]). Thus, the Fegens’ argument that Mphakathi's claim had prescribed was unconvincing, as there was sufficient acknowledgment of the debt from the King’s side.

The court also held that the Fegens acted with dolus eventualis—meaning they recklessly disregarded the possibility that Mphakathi's rights were not resolved prior to their acquisition of the properties. They failed to inquire adequately about the status of the properties, which was a significant factor in affirming Mphakathi's rights ([30]).

C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the Fegens’ appeal, affirming that the Fegens failed to prove their bona fides regarding their purchase of the properties ([32]). The court highlighted the consequences of wilfully ignoring indications of prior claims, which ultimately led to the affirmation of Mphakathi's rights over the properties. By doing so, the court upheld the principles related to the protection of prior rights in property transactions and reinforced the obligations on purchasers to ensure clear titles before proceeding with acquisitions.

This ruling serves as a significant precedent, illustrating the necessity for diligence and transparency in property dealings, particularly in the context of competing claims. The court’s approach also reinforces the notion that acknowledging prior rights is crucial, aligning with the goals of equitable property law in South Africa. The Fegens were ordered to bear the costs of the appeal, signaling the court's disapproval of their actions in purchasing the properties without adequate consideration of existing claims ([32]).
