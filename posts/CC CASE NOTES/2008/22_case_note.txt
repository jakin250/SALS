## Case Note: FIRAC Analysis of **Wilmot Mandla Chagi and 29 Others v Special Investigating Unit**

### F - Facts
The case arises from a request by the applicants, Wilmot Mandla Chagi and 29 others (the "Applicants"), for leave to appeal against a judgment from the Supreme Court of Appeal regarding the liability of the Special Investigating Unit ("Respondent"). The core issue stems from the interpretation of Proclamation R118 of 2001, which disestablished one Special Investigation Unit ("First Unit") and established another ("Second Unit") [1].

The First Unit was created by Proclamation R24 of 1997 to investigate matters related to the Transkei Agricultural Corporation Limited ("Tracor") [2]. The Applicants, employees of Tracor, claimed damages for wrongful conduct attributed to the First Unit, including defamation and violation of dignity due to unlawful freezing of bank accounts, with claims initiated shortly after the 2001 Proclamation [3–4]. The Second Unit defended the claims on the ground that it had not assumed the liabilities of the First Unit under the new Proclamation [4].

Both the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal ruled against the Applicants, asserting that the Second Unit was not liable due to the interpretation of the 2001 Proclamation [5].

### I - Issues
The primary legal questions for consideration were:
1. Whether the 2001 Proclamation effectively extinguished the liabilities of the First Unit under the Special Investigating Units and Special Tribunals Act, 1996 ("SIU Act") [10].
2. If those liabilities were not extinguished, whether they were transferred to the Second Unit or remained with the First Unit after its disestablishment [10].
3. The implications of how the Applicants cited the units in the summons and whether the appropriate entity was named [10].

### R - Rules
The applicable laws for interpretation include:
1. **SIU Act, Section 13(1)**, establishing that a Special Investigating Unit is a juristic person with certain liability provisions [27].
2. **State Liability Act of 1957** (Section 2), dictating that the head of the relevant investigation unit must be cited in cases where it is sued for actions taken within the performance of their duties [21].
3. **Interpretation Act of 1957, Section 12(2)(c)**, which states that the repeal of any law shall not affect any liability incurred under that law unless explicitly stated otherwise [30].

Instinctively, if a law is amended or repealed, its repercussions on accrued rights and liabilities are carefully scrutinized to maintain legal certainty and prevent injustice [32].

### A - Application
The court's analysis began by affirming that the 2001 Proclamation did not expressly extinguish the liabilities arising from the actions of the First Unit. Instead, it was found that liabilities could not be nullified by silence in the new Proclamation [25–26]. The court detailed that inherent liabilities attached to the First Unit under the SIU Act could not be erased by a presidential proclamation, especially in light of section 12(2)(c) of the Interpretation Act, which preserves any existing rights or obligations [30–36].

Furthermore, the court interpreted the summons and particulars of claim contextually. It was concluded that the First Unit had indeed been cited, although there was conflicting language surrounding its description in the particulars of claim, failing to render the claim fatal [42–47]. The majority interpretation thus hinged upon the crucial understanding that the semantics regarding which unit was cited should not detract from the principal question of liability that remained extant due to the provisions of the SIU Act and the Interpretation Act.

### C - Conclusion
Ultimately, the Constitutional Court granted the Applicants leave to appeal, overturning the decisions of the lower courts, claiming that both the First Unit had not been correctly deemed extinguished and that liability still functioned under its framework. Crucially, the Court's ruling emphasized the necessity of interpreting legislative instruments in a manner that ensures justice and preserves individuals' rights to remedies against unlawful conduct by state entities [48–50].

The implications of this judgment resonate significantly, reinforcing the doctrines surrounding state liability, the continuity of legal responsibilities despite structural changes, and the crucial role of statutory interpretation in safeguarding the rights of individuals against state entities. The case restates a fundamental tenet of rule of law that individuals should retain avenues for redress against unlawful state actions, which serves as a pivotal shift towards more equitable legal frameworks in South Africa.
