Supreme Court of Appeal

City of Johannesburg v Changing Tides 74 (Pty) Ltd and Others (735/2011)

In April 2011, Changing Tides sought to regain possession of the property, citing both the unlawful occupation and the City of Johannesburg (the "City") as respondents, asserting the City had a substantial interest in the proceedings.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2012

F – Facts [Paras 2–4]

The present case arose from an eviction application initiated by Changing Tides 74 (Pty) Ltd (hereafter "Changing Tides"), the owner of Tikwelo House—previously a derelict factory now occupied unlawfully as living quarters. Occupants had taken possession under unclear circumstances, allegedly perpetuated by third parties referred to as "hijackers". The conditions within Tikwelo House were perilous, described by counsel for the occupiers as a "death trap", lacking essential amenities such as sanitation, running water, and proper electricity infrastructure, thus posing significant health risks ([para 2]).

In April 2011, Changing Tides sought to regain possession of the property, citing both the unlawful occupation and the City of Johannesburg (the "City") as respondents, asserting the City had a substantial interest in the proceedings. The eviction application proceeded unopposed before Judge Wepener in the South Gauteng High Court, culminating in a controversial order that not only granted eviction but mandated the preparation of a detailed schedule (“matrix”) regarding the occupiers and the provision of temporary emergency accommodation by the City for affected individuals ([paras 3–5]).

On October 12, 2011, leave to appeal was granted concerning the order requiring emergency accommodation and associated costs ([para 5]).

I – Issues [Paras 6, 10–12]

The primary legal issues before the court included:

  1. Is the joinder of the City necessary or permissible in proceedings wherein eviction from property is sought, affecting the rights and needs of vulnerable groups, particularly in light of the procedural requirements of the Prevention of Illegal Eviction from and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE)?
  2. What constitutes a just and equitable eviction order given the precarious living circumstances of the occupiers involved, and how do the statutory provisions of PIE, specifically sections 4(6) and 4(7), relate to one another in this context?
  3. What are the obligations of local authorities when such eviction orders may lead to homelessness among the affected parties?

R – Rules [Paras 11–14]

The court relied on several key legal principles articulated under PIE. According to section 4(7) of PIE, an eviction order may only be granted if it's found to be just and equitable, which necessitates consideration of all relevant circumstances, including the availability of alternative land or accommodation ([para 11]). The jurisprudence set forth by the Constitutional Court in cases such as Port Elizabeth Municipality v Various Occupiers and Grootboom delineated the obligations municipalities have towards vulnerable populations facing eviction, reinforcing the necessity of considering the potential for homelessness in such contexts ([paras 13–14]).

A – Application [Paras 8, 12, 18]

In assessing the application of these legal principles, the court identified significant deficiencies in the High Court's proceedings. The initial ruling failed to adequately consider the just and equitable nature of the eviction order due to a lack of comprehensive information about the circumstances surrounding the occupiers ([para 10]). Moreover, the Sheriff’s role was misinterpreted, improperly assigned the responsibility of gathering sensitive personal information from potentially vulnerable individuals ([para 9]).

The court emphasized the duty of the City to provide evidence about the availability of housing solutions for evictees and stressed that in instances of potential homelessness, the need for emergency accommodation must take precedence ([paras 18, 49]). It was further noted that the application failed to provide a fair procedural avenue for the occupiers to express their circumstances effectively, necessitating intervention by their legal representatives, the Legal Resources Centre (LRC), to coordinate support mechanisms effectively ([para 42]).

C – Conclusion [Paras 64–66]

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, recognizing the High Court's orders as inadequate and legally ineffective. The City's joinder was justified in light of its role as an authority responsible for housing and to uphold the constitutional rights of vulnerable persons facing homelessness. The matter was remitted back to the High Court to redefine the relationship between the parties, specifically concerning the timing and terms of eviction and emergency accommodation provisions ([para 64]).

Ultimately, the judgment underscores the importance of balancing the constitutional right to property with the right to access adequate housing, emphasizing the need for local authorities to engage proactively in cases of evictions to mitigate the impacts on affected individuals. The court ordered that each party bear its own costs, reflecting the necessity of collaborative engagement rather than adversarial litigation in resolving socio-economic disputes ([para 64]).

This case illustrates a critical intersection between property rights, housing equity, and judicial obligations, reinforcing the imperative that local authorities play an active role in alleviating homelessness during eviction proceedings.
