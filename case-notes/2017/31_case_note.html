Jordaan and Others v City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality and Others [2017] ZACC 31

The municipalities involved (City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality and Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality) appealed the High Court’s declaration.

Constitutional Court 2017 [2017] ZACC 31

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

The consolidated matters CCT 283/16, CCT 293/16, and CCT 294/16 involve several individuals and companies (hereafter referred to as "the applicants") who are property owners in the Tshwane and Ekurhuleni municipalities. The applicants contested the constitutional validity of section 118(3) of the Local Government: Municipal Systems Act 32 of 2000, which asserts that municipal service fees and taxes become a charge on the property, enjoying preference over existing mortgage bonds. The Gauteng High Court declared this provision invalid as it applies to debts that survive upon the transfer of property to new owners who were not the original debtors to the municipality ([paras 1-4]).

The municipalities involved (City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality and Ekurhuleni Metropolitan Municipality) appealed the High Court’s declaration. All parties, including the Minister of Cooperative Governance and Traditional Affairs, contended that section 118(3) was constitutionally sound and imposed responsibility for historical debts on new property owners ([paras 3-4]).

I – Issues

The principal legal issue revolves around the interpretation and constitutional validity of section 118(3) of the Municipal Systems Act. Specifically, does this provision allow a municipality to reclaim from a new property owner debts incurred by the previous owner? If so, what are the constitutional implications of such provisions with respect to the rights of new owners under section 25 of the Constitution, which protects against arbitrary deprivation of property ([para 2])?

R – Rules

  1. Constitutional Provisions: Section 25 of the Constitution enshrines the right to property and prohibits arbitrary deprivation thereof. Any law or provision that has the effect of transferring burdensome liabilities from one property owner to another must be scrutinized to ensure it does not violate this right ([para 58]).
  2. Local Government: Municipal Systems Act: Section 118(3) creates a statutory charge on property for municipal debts, but with significant implications for new owners ([para 1]).
  3. Common Law Principles: Historical common law predicates that real rights in property can only be transferred through formal and public acts, indicating that unregistered charges may not have continued enforceability against new owners ([para 38]).

A – Application

The court conducted a detailed analysis of the legislative intent behind section 118(3), historical precedents, and the constitutional context. It found that:

  • The absence of a clear charter for the charge to survive transfer implied that new owners should not be liable for historical debts attached to the properties they acquire. Such an interpretation aligns with the common law principles emphasizing publicity and formal establishment for real rights in property ([paras 15-19]).
  • The court acknowledged the municipalities' argument linking the charge's survival to their constitutional obligations to render services. However, the court ruled that municipalities still have numerous legal mechanisms and powers to recover debts from the previous owners, thus there is no necessity to impose that liability on a new owner ([para 54]).
  • Importantly, it assessed the “arbitrary deprivation of property” that would occur should section 118(3) be interpreted as extending liability for previous debts to new owners, highlighting that such an interpretation would violate constitutional safeguards ([paras 59-69]).

C – Conclusion

The court concluded that section 118(3) does not extend its charge upon the property to impose liabilities on new owners for debts incurred by previous owners. This ruling was not merely an endorsement of the High Court’s declaration of unconstitutionality but provided a robust interpretation safeguarding the constitutional rights of property owners against arbitrary deprivation. The court ultimately determined that:

  1. The appeals by the municipalities succeed only in the sense that the declaration of constitutional invalidity is not upheld,
  2. A new owner is not liable for debts arising before transfer under section 118(3) of the Municipal Systems Act, and
  3. Costs are awarded in favor of the applicants, reflecting the substantive victory they achieved, even amidst procedural complexities ([paras 80-81]).

This decision has significant implications for property law and municipal governance, reinforcing the constitutional protections against arbitrary imposition of liabilities upon innocent parties acquiring property.
