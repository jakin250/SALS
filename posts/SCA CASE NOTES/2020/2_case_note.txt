Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note: Airports Company South Africa SOC Ltd v Imperial Group Ltd & Others

**F** - **Facts**: 
The Appellant, Airports Company South Africa SOC Ltd (ACSA), a public company formed under the Airports Company Act 44 of 1993, published a Request for Bids (RFB) on 5 September 2017, inviting bids for car rental concessions at its airports ([1]). The RFB consisted of a structured evaluation process, which included pre-qualification requirements intended to ensure compliance with various transformation imperatives, primarily focused on black economic empowerment ([2]–[3]). The First Respondent, Imperial Group Ltd, submitted a bid but later contested the legality and constitutionality of the RFB, asserting that certain provisions were unlawful as they contravened Section 217 of the South African Constitution ([5]–[9]).

Imperial's application was initially two-pronged: Part A aimed at compelling ACSA to reveal other participating bidders, while Part B sought to review and set aside the RFB's publication, citing breaches of PAJA and the principle of legality ([6]–[8]). The High Court found the RFB constituted unlawful administrative action that failed to comply with the requirements set out in Section 217, thus declaring it invalid ([8]).

**I** - **Issues**: 
The appeal raised significant constitutional questions, primarily regarding whether Section 217 of the Constitution is applicable to the RFB published by ACSA, and whether the provisions of the RFB were rational, lawful, and valid ([8]). Additionally, it contemplated whether procedural irregularities had tainted the decision-making process leading to the RFB's issuance, and if the terms of the RFB were vague ([18], [27]).

**R** - **Rules**: 
The legal framework underpinning this case is anchored in Section 217 of the Constitution, which mandates that organs of state must engage in public procurement that is fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective ([19]). This constitutional provision is complemented by the Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act 5 of 2000 (PP Act) and the Broad-Based Black Economic Empowerment Act 53 of 2003 (B-BBEE Act), which provide necessary legislative structures for the implementation of these principles ([21]).

The referral from the High Court discussed the interpretation of “procurement” as per the ordinary meaning provided by Section 217, as well as the criteria set out within the PP Act that govern how tender processes are to be conducted ([22]–[24]).

**A** - **Application**: 
The Supreme Court of Appeal rigorously evaluated ACSA's claims that the RFB did not fall within the ambit of Section 217 because it claimed it was leasing property rather than procuring services. However, the Court asserted that this interpretation misapplied the essence of public procurement, emphasizing that ACSA was, in fact, engaging in a contract that allowed car rental companies to provide public services, thus falling within the definition of "procurement" under the Constitution ([21], [23]).

The evaluation of the provision's merits revealed that the pre-qualification criteria imposed by ACSA were discriminatory and not consistent with the legal framework mandated under Section 217, the PP Act, and the B-BBEE Act ([30]–[42]). The decision-making process itself lacked due diligence, research, and consultation, leading to the artificial imposition of criteria that were later determined to be legally invalid, rendering the entire RFB process irrational and unlawful ([27]).

Additionally, the Court addressed ACSA's failure to adhere to the mandatory requirements of the PP Act and indicated that deviations from prescribed codes without ministerial approval illustrated a lack of compliance with statutory obligations ([37]–[39]).

**C** - **Conclusion**: 
The appeal by ACSA was dismissed with costs, affirming the High Court's ruling that declared the RFB unlawful, irrational, and inconsistent with both the Constitution and relevant procurement legislation ([54]). This decision reinforced significant principles about the necessity for compliance with constitutional procurement frameworks and reflected the necessity of adhering to statutory obligations, particularly regarding the promotion of previously disadvantaged individuals. The judgment underscores the vital relationship between public authority actions, procurement laws, and the transformation mandates of the Constitution, establishing clear legal precedents for future public procurement processes ([52]–[74]). 

The implications of the court's ruling extend to the conduct of public procurement processes across South Africa, signaling to state entities the importance of adhering strictly to constitutional mandates to ensure fairness, transparency, and accountability in governance.
