Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: EB (Born S) v ER (Born B) N.O. and Others; KG v Minister of Home Affairs and Others [2023] ZACC 32

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–10])
This matter hinges on the constitutional validity of section 7(3) of the *Divorce Act 70 of 1979*, particularly with respect to the circumstances surrounding marriages dissolved by death and the exclusions for marriages entered into after November 1, 1984. The two cases were heard together in the Constitutional Court and involved the following parties:

1. **EB (Born S) v ER (Born B) N.O. (Case CCT 364/21)**: The applicant, Mrs. B, was married to Mr. B under an antenuptial contract (ANC) in 1983. Following a divorce action initiated in 2015, Mr. B passed away before the proceedings concluded. Mrs. B sought a redistribution order under section 7(3) but faced refusal from the executor of Mr. B’s estate, leading her to argue that the absence of a remedy in case of death was unconstitutional.

2. **KG v Minister of Home Affairs (Case CCT 158/22)**: Mrs. G entered into an ANC with her husband in 1988, later initiating divorce proceedings in 2017. She sought a similar redistribution order but was barred due to the provision limiting access to couples married before the MPA's commencement (November 1, 1984).

In both cases, the High Court ruled that the limitations imposed by section 7(3) were unconstitutional, leading to their referral for confirmation by the Constitutional Court ([paras 10–14]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–3, 10–14])
The cases raised critical constitutional issues, namely:
1. **The Divorce/Death Issue**: Whether section 7(3) of the *Divorce Act* discriminated against spouses whose marriages ended by death, as opposed to divorce, thus limiting their right to a redistribution remedy.
2. **The Before/After Issue**: Whether the provision's limitation to marriages concluded before the commencement of the *Matrimonial Property Act* (MPA) imposed unfair discrimination based on marital status, as it effectively deprived spouses of new ANC marriages, particularly women, of similar redistributive rights.

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 21–27, 39–45])
In determining these constitutional issues, the Court relied on various legal frameworks:
- **Section 9 of the Constitution**: Grants everyone equality before the law and protection against unfair discrimination, providing a basis for evaluating the distinctions made by the *Divorce Act*.
- **Legislative History**: The introduction of the *Divorce Act’s* provisions for redistribution was discussed, particularly the SALC's recommendations that sought to address historical inequities faced by economically disadvantaged spouses, predominantly women.

Cases such as *Harksen v Lane N.O* and *Holomisa v Holomisa* were pivotal in establishing frameworks for discrimination analysis. The distinction drawn between old ANC marriages and new ones was scrutinised for rationality and fairness under constitutional standards.

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 19–144])
The Constitutional Court conducted a thorough scrutiny of the legislative provisions:
- The Court found that section 7(3) delineated between classes of spouses who could claim a redistribution remedy based solely on the date of marriage, leading to discrimination against those who married after November 1, 1984 ([paras 39–45]).
- In the *divorce/death issue*, the Court posited that the differential treatment granted to divorcing spouses compared to those whose marriages ended in death failed to serve any legitimate government interest and thus limited the rights encapsulated in section 9(1) of the Constitution ([paras 48–63]). 
- The Court adjudged this limitation as falling disproportionately harder on women, as marital dissolution without a redistribution remedy compounded historical economic disparities ([paras 121–123]).
- In addressing the *before/after issue*, the Court recognized this exclusion as a form of indirect discrimination on the basis of gender. The underlying economic disparities perpetuated by the law were deemed inconsistent with the broader goals of equality enshrined in the Constitution ([paras 130–137]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 149–150])
The Constitutional Court confirmed the High Court’s declaration of constitutional invalidity for both cases, striking down the relevant provisions in section 7(3) of the *Divorce Act* and reading in amendments to ensure future remedies against both marital dissolution by death and the exclusion pertaining to new ANC marriages. The Court suspended the declarations for 24 months, allowing Parliament time to legislate accordingly.

The court's decision underscores the necessity of judicial remedies that account for historical societal inequities and the evolving nature of marital arrangements in contemporary South Africa. The ruling further cements the overarching constitutional principle that equitable distribution in marriages is a fundamental right, thus compelling legislative reform to rectify the identified deficiencies in the *Divorce Act* and resulting in a more inclusive and equitable legal framework for all forms of marriage in South Africa.
