Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2020

# FIRAC Case Note: Milestone Beverage CC and Others v The Scotch Whisky Association and Others

## F – Facts
The case revolves around the appeal brought by Milestone Beverage CC and associated parties against the Scotch Whisky Association (SWA) and affiliates regarding alleged misrepresentation and unlawful competition in the sale and marketing of two alcoholic beverages, namely Royal Douglas and King Arthur. These products were marketed in a manner that projected a Scottish provenance, misleading consumers into believing that they were genuine Scotch whiskies ([para 2]).

The SWA, a trade association representing the Scotch whisky industry, filed the application after its representatives noted that the appellants misrepresented their products on several occasions. The Royal Douglas label featured Scottish imagery, including tartan patterns and the prominent use of terms like “premium quality” and “whisky-flavoured” ([para 3]). The appellants, composed of Milestone Beverage, the Trustees of the T and S Haupt Family Trust, and individual Sean Peter Haupt, maintained that their products did not meet the legal definition of whisky as established under the Liquor Products Act 60 of 1989 (“LPA”) ([paras 5–6]).

Despite assurances to cease marketing the products as whisky, the appellants were found to be continuing this practice, prompting the respondents to institute legal action in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, seeking various interdicts and relief. The court granted the application, leading to the current appeal ([para 10]).

## I – Issues
The central legal issues in this case are:
1. **Misrepresentation Regarding Product Origin**: Did the appellants misrepresent their products as whisky, specifically Scottish whisky, thereby misleading consumers?
2. **Violation of the Liquor Products Act**: Did the appellants engage in conduct that contravened the provisions of the LPA, specifically related to the branding and labeling of alcoholic beverages?
3. **Unlawful Competition**: To what extent does the appellants' conduct constitute unlawful competition as defined in existing South African case law?
4. **Locus Standi and Interdictory Relief**: Do the respondents have the legal standing to seek an interdict against the appellants?

## R – Rules
The court relied on several legal principles and statutory provisions, notably:

1. **Misrepresentation and Unlawful Competition**: The principle that competition must be carried out lawfully, as articulated in previous judgments. In particular, the distinction between lawful competition and conduct that leads to misrepresentation was emphasized ([para 16]).

2. **Liquor Products Act 60 of 1989**: Key provisions include:
   - **Section 11**: Prohibits the use of names or descriptors that could mislead consumers regarding the nature or origin of liquor products.
   - **Section 12**: Bans the sale of liquor products which create a false impression concerning their character, composition, or origin ([para 34]).

3. **Lex Aquilia**: The delictual basis for claims of unlawful competition, which recognizes that unlawful conduct causing patrimonial loss can result in actionable claims for damages ([para 16]).

4. **Judicial Precedents**: The court referenced past cases such as *Long John International Ltd v Stellenbosch Wine Trust (Pty) Ltd* which articulated the parameters for misrepresentation in a commercial context and highlighted that unlawful conduct in trade may warrant interdictory relief ([paras 17, 34]).

## A – Application
The court performed a thorough analysis of the facts against the backdrop of the legal standards set by the LPA and principles of competition law.

1. **Misrepresentation**: The court interpreted the marketing strategies of the appellants, highlighting how the visual branding and terminology used (e.g., "Royal Douglas" and "King Arthur") conveyed a false connection to Scotland, misleading consumers into believing they were purchasing authentic Scotch whisky ([paras 19–21]). The evidence showed that the actual products bore no resemblance to genuine whisky and did not meet the legal criteria established under the LPA.

2. **Contravention of the Liquor Products Act**: The judiciary examined the appellants' products vis-à-vis statutory definitions and requirements for whisky. It concluded that the appellants failed to comply with they had represented their products as whisky-flavoured and possessed an alcohol by volume (ABV) higher than stipulated, thereby breaching Sections 11 and 12 of the LPA ([paras 50, 52]).

3. **Unlawful Competition**: The submissions made by the appellants were interpreted to fall within the parameters of unlawful competition as defined in previously established jurisprudence. The court stressed that marketing based on misleading representations could be seen as injuring the goodwill that established whisky makers have cultivated ([para 18]).

4. **Locus Standi**: The court considered the legal standing of the SWA, recognizing its pursuit of "protection of a particular party" interest through the application. It determined that the SWA, having a direct financial interest in the South African whisky market, had sufficient grounds to initiate action against the appellants ([para 56]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appeal by the appellants was devoid of merit. The court upheld the interdict imposed by the High Court, signifying that the appellants must desist from marketing their alcoholic beverages in a manner that misled consumers regarding the nature and origin of the products ([para 58]). The appellants were ordered to pay costs, including those for two counsels.

The decision underscores the necessity of compliance with the LPA in marketing alcoholic beverages and reinforces the obligation of businesses to engage in fair and lawful competition. The case thus establishes important precedents in the realm of marketing ethics and legal protections within the South African liquor industry, signalling a strong stance against misleading practices that undermine consumer trust and market integrity.
