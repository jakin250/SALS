Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2009

# FIRAC Case Note: Wycliffe Simiyu Koyabe and Others v Minister for Home Affairs and Others [2009] ZACC 23

## F – Facts:
The case involves Wycliffe Simiyu Koyabe (the first applicant), Mary Kadenyi Koyabe (the second applicant), and their son Anthony Simiyu Koyabe (the third applicant), all Kenyan nationals, who resided in South Africa. The backdrop includes the withdrawal of their residence permits by the Director-General of Home Affairs ([paras 1, 2, 3]). 

The first applicant entered South Africa in 1994 with a work permit, subsequently marrying a South African citizen. After a divorce, he faced complications regarding his immigration status, which included claims of fraud related to his permanent residency obtained in 1997 ([paras 5, 6, 13]). Their residence permits were ordered to be withdrawn due to alleged irregularities, which they contested through a High Court application for judicial review ([para 16]). 

The High Court dismissed their application, citing failure to exhaust internal remedies as required under section 7(2)(a) of the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act (PAJA) ([paras 19, 20]) and the applicants sought leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court ([para 21]).

## I – Issues:
The primary legal questions before the court included:
1. Whether the applicants had exhausted internal remedies as mandated by section 7(2) of PAJA before pursuing judicial review of the administrative decision ([paras 19-20]).
2. The interpretation of the right to just administrative action and the implications of section 7(2) on access to courts as per section 34 of the Constitution ([paras 21-22]).

## R – Rules:
The relevant legal principles include:
- **Section 7(2) of PAJA**: No court shall review an administrative action unless any internal remedy available has first been exhausted, allowing for exemptions in exceptional circumstances ([paras 34, 39]).
- **Section 8 of the Immigration Act**: Outlines procedures for review of decisions related to immigration status, including internal timelines for appeal ([paras 50-52]).
- **Section 33 of the Constitution**: Guarantees the right to lawful and procedurally fair administrative action, including the provision of written reasons for such action ([paras 33, 60]).

Precedents considered include Nichol v The Registrar of Pension Funds, which affirmed the duty to exhaust internal remedies unless exceptional circumstances existed that justified bypassing this requirement ([para 74]).

## A – Application:
The court examined whether the applicants had been deprived of their right to internal review due to a lack of provided reasons for the administrative decision. The High Court had found that the applicants’ attorneys had received adequate reasons in the letters dated 9 January 2007, allowing them to proceed with a ministerial review ([paras 19, 66]) and that their application was premature as it did not allow for the administrative process to run its course ([para 69]). 

The Constitutional Court articulated that even if there were procedural irregularities, the mere assertion of irregularities does not automatically constitute exceptional circumstances for bypassing internal remedies ([paras 73, 74]).

The applicants claimed that they were denied meaningful access to an internal review due to a failure to provide adequate reasons, but the court concluded that the reasons given were sufficient for requesting a ministerial review and therefore met the requirement of transparency enshrined in the Constitution ([paras 60-62, 82]).

## C – Conclusion:
The Constitutional Court concluded that the applicants had not exhausted their internal remedies as required by section 7(2) of PAJA. It directed the applicants to initiate a ministerial review within seven days, ruling that there were no exceptional circumstances that justified bypassing internal procedures ([paras 82-84]). The appeal was thus dismissed, maintaining the costs order made in the High Court. The case underscores the critical balance between administrative processes and judicial intervention, reinforcing the necessity for litigants to adhere to statutory procedural frameworks before seeking judicial review ([paras 84-88]).

This ruling has significant implications for administrative law in South Africa, particularly in clarifying the relationship between statutory requirements and constitutional rights, thereby promoting an orderly administrative process while safeguarding individual rights.
