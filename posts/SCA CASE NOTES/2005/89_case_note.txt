# Case Note: Shoprite Checkers (Edms) BPK v LJJ Grobbelaar & Others

### FIRAC Case Note

#### F – Facts
This case concerns an appeal by Shoprite Checkers (Edms) BPK (the appellant) against an order for absolution with costs granted to LJJ Grobbelaar (the first respondent), DJM Heyns (the second respondent), and PC Welgemoed (the third respondent). The background of the case reveals that the appellant sought damages in the amount of R8,625,339 based on claims of breach of a restraint of trade agreement executed by the respondents ([1]–[2]).

The first respondent was a shareholder and managing director of two companies, Sentra Koop Handelaars Bpk and Megasave (Edms) Bpk. The second and third respondents held positions as a shareholder and director and an employee respectively. On 23 November 1995, Shoprite Checkers (Edms) BPK purchased the businesses of Sentra and Megasave for R21 million. Subsequently, the respondents signed a restraint of trade agreement that prohibited competition with Shoprite for 36 months (first and second respondents) and 24 months (third respondent) within South Africa and Namibia. The first and second respondents received R1,1 million, while the third received R500,000 as consideration for the restraint ([3]–[4]).

From April 1997, the respondents resigned from their positions at Shoprite, leading to significant changes within the Shoprite group, including a reorganization where assets and rights were transferred to OK Bazaars (1929) Bpk in August 1998. The legal question emerges from whether the rights from the restraint of trade agreement were indeed transferred to the appellant, following which the appellant accused the respondents of breaching the agreement by engaging in competitive activities ([5]–[6]).

#### I – Issues
The case presents several key legal issues related to:
1. Whether the appellant had locus standi to sue for the enforcement of the restraint of trade agreements ([7]).
2. Whether the respondents committed a breach of contract ([7]).
3. Whether there exists a causal link between any alleged breach and the damages claimed by the appellant ([7]).
4. The legal interpretation of the requirements stipulated in section 228 of the Companies Act regarding asset disposal by a company ([9]).

#### R – Rules
The relevant legal rules drawn from statutory and case law include:
1. Section 228 of the Companies Act 61 of 1973, which stipulates that a company may not dispose of all or a substantial part of its assets without the approval of a general meeting of its shareholders ([9]).
2. Legal precedents concerning the scope of restraint of trade agreements, particularly the determination of who holds the rights to enforce them, which relate directly to the transfer of goodwill in a business sale (Botha v Carapax Shadeports (Pty) Ltd 1992 (1) SA 202 (AD)) ([19]).
3. Principles surrounding the doctrine of unanimous consent, which suggests that formalities may be bypassed if all stakeholders agree, evidenced in Gholke & Schneider v Westies Minerale Bpk 1970 (2) SA 685 (AD) ([11]).

#### A – Application
The court analyzed the three critical disputes concerning the appellant's standing, whether a contractual breach occurred, and the causation of damages. In regard to locus standi, the court referenced that for rights secured by a restraint of trade to transfer, there must be formal compliance with section 228 of the Companies Act. The court determined that the appellant, Shoprite, failed to demonstrate compliance with the section, thus hindering its claim ([8]–[9]).

On the issue of breach, the respondents were alleged to have initiated a competitive enterprise post-resignation, which the appellant contended had directly led to client losses. The court found sufficient prima facie evidence to suggest that the respondents improperly solicited clients away from the appellant, meeting the nexus of causation required to establish damages ([21]–[22]).

Furthermore, the court explored the issue of whether the rights attached to the restraint of trade agreements were personal to SCEB. It concluded that the contractual rights were intended to benefit the entire business interest, thus permitting the transferability of these rights to the appellant once it assumed control of the businesses ([16]–[17]).

#### C – Conclusion
The appeal court determined that the lower court had erred in granting absolution on the basis of locus standi. The court held that the appellant presented sufficient evidence to establish its standing to sue on the restraint of trade agreements, thus reversing the order for absolution, and remanding the case back to the lower court for further proceedings ([25]). The ruling signifies important implications regarding the enforceability of contractual rights associated with business transactions, particularly in mergers and acquisitions, where adherence to statutory requirements is crucial for the validity of company operations.

The final order concluded that the appeal was successful with costs, and the previous absolution ruling was set aside, indicating a reaffirmation of the appellant's claims for damages based on the contested restraint of trade agreements ([26]).
