# FIRAC Case Note: **The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa, Case No: 645/98**

## F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([1]–[3])

In the case at hand, four appellants – Amyna Shahanaaz Fakude (first appellant), Dumile Elliot Nduki (second appellant), Sipho Thomas Mlandu (third appellant), and Morris Masikhisane Ntshantshu (fourth appellant) – were convicted of murder by the Witwatersrand Local Division of the High Court, sentenced to varying terms of imprisonment from 23 to 35 years ([1]). The trial, presided over by Mailula J and assessors, revealed that accused 1 (Fakude) conspired to murder her husband, which was executed by the other accused ([2]). Accused 2, her brother, acted as an accomplice but died during the trial. 

The murder was particularly brutal, involving hacking and stabbing with pangas and assegais outside the home of the deceased ([2]). All accused had no prior convictions and had been in custody since November 1995. The facts also highlighted familial strife, including marital discord between Fakude and the deceased, indicating complex motivations behind the heinous act ([3]).

## I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([4]–[6])

The primary issue raised in this appeal pertains to the appropriateness of the sentences imposed on the appellants, particularly the disparity between the 35-year sentence for accused 1 and the lesser sentences given to accused 2 and 3 ([4]). Counsel for the appellants argued that the trial judge unduly emphasized the seriousness of the crime over the personal circumstances of the appellants, failing to adequately account for their potential for rehabilitation ([4]). Additionally, the absence of remorse was debated, questioning whether it should be considered an aggravating factor ([6]).

## R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([7]–[8])

In deciding this matter, the court relied on established principles regarding sentencing, specifically avoiding unjustifiable disparities in sentences for the same crime ([7]). Previous cases such as *S v Marx* and *S v Goldman* were cited to emphasize that sentences should reflect both the gravity of the crime and the personal circumstances of the offenders ([7]). The court reiterated that the absence of remorse may not automatically equate to an aggravating factor, and any disparities in sentencing must be justifiable considering the distinct roles played by each participant in the crime ([8]).

## A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([5]–[10])

The court examined whether the trial judge had properly balanced the seriousness of the crime against the personal circumstances of the appellants. It was noted that the judge had indeed considered the gravity of the crime, given the endemic violence in the area and the necessity of deterring similar acts in the community ([5]). However, the court found that the trial judge’s rationale for imposing a 35-year sentence on accused 1 while the others received lesser sentences did not hold up to scrutiny. The judge characterized the appellants as "equally blameworthy," yet treated accused 1 significantly differently, despite a lack of compelling reasoning to support such disparity ([8]). 

The court also determined that all appellants should have been treated similarly in regard to sentencing. Although the trial judge’s finding that accused 5’s involvement was somewhat distinct due to claims of financial coercion was noted, the court ultimately concluded that this distinction was not valid enough to warrant differing sentences, particularly not one as severe as 35 years for accused 1 ([9]). The absence of demonstrated remorse should not disproportionately disadvantage accused 1 compared to her co-accused ([6]).

Consequently, the appellate court modified the sentence of accused 1 from 35 years to 25 years to align it with the sentences received by accused 3 and 4, affirming that a sentence must reflect a fair and consistent approach to justice ([10]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([10])

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal of the first appellant, reducing her sentence from 35 years to 25 years, while dismissing the appeals for the second, third, and fourth appellants. The decision emphasizes the principle that sentences for co-offenders must reflect similar roles and responsibility unless a clear and justifiable distinction can be established ([10]). This ruling serves as an essential precedent for future sentencing considerations in cases involving multiple co-accused, reinforcing the imperative that disparities in sentencing be justified and reflecting the shared culpability of those involved.

Ultimately, the case underscores the necessity of a balanced approach in sentencing, combining considerations of personal circumstances, the need for public protection, and the aim of rehabilitation, while striving to avoid arbitrary inconsistencies that fail to serve the underlying principles of justice.
