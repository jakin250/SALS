Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2009

# FIRAC Case Note: Centre for Child Law v Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development

### F – Facts:
The Centre for Child Law (the "Centre") sought confirmation of a declaration of statutory invalidity from the Constitutional Court of South Africa after the North Gauteng High Court found certain sections of the Criminal Law Amendment Act (CLAA) unconstitutional. The impugned sections made minimum sentences applicable to offenders aged 16 and 17 at the time of the crime ([1]–[2]). The Centre represents children at risk of being sentenced under these provisions, invoking the standing provisions of the Bill of Rights to act in the public interest ([2]). The respondents included the Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development, the Minister for Correctional Services, and the Legal Aid Board. The main contention was that applying minimum sentences to minors violates their constitutional rights under Section 28(1)(g) and (2) of the South African Constitution, which emphasizes that imprisonment should only be a last resort and considers children's best interests ([9]).

### I – Issues:
The primary issue raised by the Centre was whether the application of minimum sentences to 16 and 17-year-old offenders is consistent with the protections afforded to children under Section 28 of the Constitution ([8]–[9]). Notably, the Centre argued that these provisions contradict the constitutional requirement that detention of children should only occur as a measure of last resort and for the shortest appropriate duration ([9]).

### R – Rules:
The key rule under scrutiny is Section 28 of the South African Constitution, which encompasses the rights of children, particularly that:
- Every child has the right not to be detained except as a measure of last resort, and such detention should be for the shortest appropriate period ([24]).
- The best interests of the child are of paramount importance ([28]).

Additionally, the judgment references precedents such as *S v B*, which previously allowed discretion in sentencing children, indicating a legislative intention to treat juvenile offenders differently from adults ([6]).

### A – Application:
The High Court determined that the mandatory nature of minimum sentencing under the CLAA effectively removes judicial discretion, therefore violating the constitutional protections for child offenders. The court found that the minimum sentencing regime would lead to disproportionate sentences that do not take the individual circumstances of juvenile offenders into account ([14]).

The Constitutional Court agreed with the High Court’s assessment, noting that the Amendment Act essentially converts child sentencing into a rigid framework that contradicts the individualized consideration mandated by the Constitution. Specifically, the court noted that imposing minimum sentences negated the legislative intent to allow a "clean slate" approach for sentencing minors that considers their unique circumstances ([14]).

Furthermore, the assessment confirmed that the goals of effective deterrence stated by the government did not justify the significant limitations imposed on the rights of children. The Minister’s argument lacked sufficient empirical support to demonstrate that the changes would effectively reduce juvenile crime or enhance community safety ([52]–[62]).

### C – Conclusion:
The Constitutional Court concluded that the amending provisions of the CLAA, which subjected 16 and 17-year-olds to minimum sentencing, violated Section 28 of the Constitution and thus declared them unconstitutional ([64]). The court emphasized that children's rights must remain protected, particularly in light of the potential for rehabilitation. The ruling did not strip sentencing courts of the ability to impose robust penalties for serious offenses but insisted that any penalties must reflect the unique context of the child's circumstances and the necessity of rehabilitative justice.

This landmark decision underscores the necessity of protecting children's rights within the framework of criminal justice and reaffirms the principle that legislation affecting children must be guided by their best interests and rights inherent in the Constitution. It sets a precedent for how statutory provisions regarding sentencing can interact with constitutional rights, emphasizing the importance of individualized justice for offenders who are minors.
