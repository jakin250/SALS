Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 1999

# FIRAC Case Note: Vincent Maredi Mpahlele v The First National Bank of South Africa Limited CCT 23/98

## F – Facts
The applicant, Vincent Maredi Mpahlele, an attorney, commenced proceedings against his banker, the First National Bank of South Africa Limited, in December 1997. He sought an order regarding his trust account with the bank, but his application was dismissed with costs by Southwood J in the Transvaal High Court on 11 December 1997 [1]. Subsequently, the applicant attempted to apply for leave to appeal this decision and sought condonation for the late noting of his appeal, which was also dismissed in June 1998, again with costs [1]. The applicant then petitioned the Chief Justice for leave to appeal, which was considered and refused by two judges of the Supreme Court of Appeal without argument or referral to the full court, a procedure they were permitted to adopt under section 21(3)(b) of the Supreme Court Act 1959 [1].

Frustrated by the lack of reasons for the refusal, the applicant wrote to the Registrar of the Supreme Court of Appeal requesting an explanation, only to be informed that such reasons are not customarily provided [2]. In response, the applicant accused the judiciary of perpetuating apartheid-era practices by not offering reasons, which he viewed as oppressive [2]. The applicant subsequently filed a notice of motion in the Constitutional Court seeking orders to mandate the two judges to furnish reasons for the dismissal of his petition and to grant him leave to appeal [4].

## I – Issues
The core constitutional issue presented in this case revolves around whether the judiciary is obliged to provide reasons for its decisions, specifically in applications for leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal [4]. The applicant contended that the Constitution binds the judiciary and thus mandates transparency and accountability in the form of reasoned judgments [4]. He raised concerns about the implications of existing practices on judicial legitimacy and public confidence in the wake of South Africa’s transition from apartheid [9].

Additionally, the applicant's rights as enshrined in the Constitution, such as the right to access to information (section 32), the right to a fair hearing (section 34), and the equality before the law (section 9), were invoked in support of his argument for the need for judicial accountability and reasoned decisions [9][10].

## R – Rules
The relevant legal authorities include several provisions from the South African Constitution of 1996. Section 8(1) stipulates that the Bill of Rights applies to all law, binding the judiciary and all organs of state [3]. Section 1 of the Constitution establishes the rule of law as one of the foundational values of the state, requiring judges to act with accountability [12]. Section 34 protects the right to a fair hearing and implies that practices hindering access to courts could undermine this right [10]. Furthermore, section 32(1) guarantees the right of access to information held by the state, stipulated as essential for the protection of rights [10].

The Supreme Court Act 1959 provides procedural guidelines for appeals, particularly section 21, which explains the powers and limitations concerning appeals [1]. The court may refuse leave to appeal without providing reasons, retaining the efficiency of the judicial process and the right to manage its docket [14].

## A – Application
Goldstone J analyzed the procedural history and legal justifications for denying the applicant's requests. He recognized the importance of accountability and transparency in the judiciary but noted that the mere absence of reasons for a decision does not automatically infringe upon constitutional rights [12]. The court acknowledged the applicant's concerns regarding historical practices but clarified that not every tradition from the apartheid era is inherently unconstitutional [11].

Significantly, the court pointed out that the refusal of leave to appeal is not appealable to any other court and does not prejudice further litigation opportunities, implying that the judiciary had no obligation to furnish reasons in such cases as a matter of standard procedural law [14]. Furthermore, the court indicated that while courts typically provide reasons for their decisions, especially at first-instance levels, applications for leave to appeal are unique in that they often involve considerations of case merit and relevance to judicial resources [15].

The applicant's reliance on the section related to the right to information was deemed misplaced because he could not substantiate a consequent right from the reasons he sought [16]. The court also highlighted that the equality clause (sections 9(1) and 9(2)) was not applicable, as the procedural avenue in civil matters remains consistent regardless of the criminal context [17]. 

Ultimately, the court concluded that the judiciary is obliged to ensure accountability but not necessarily through the mechanism of providing reasons for every decision, particularly in procedural contexts such as leave to appeal [12][19].

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court dismissed the application by Mpahlele, affirming that there had been no breach of constitutional requirements regarding the furnishing of reasons for the refusal of his application for leave to appeal [20]. The case highlighted the distinction between the procedural obligations of the judiciary in civil versus criminal matters and reaffirmed that while transparency is vital, not every judicial decision necessitates a written justification, particularly in cases deemed unmeritorious by the court [19]. The judgment set a precedent for the balance between judicial efficiency and the accountability owed to litigants within the South African legal framework, thereby solidifying the practical applications of constitutional principles in matters of judicial procedure.

Moreover, in recognizing the significance of the issues raised by the applicant, Goldstone J noted the applicant's entitlement to have his concerns considered, ultimately concluding with no order as to costs, indicating the importance of the matter despite the dismissal of the application [20].
