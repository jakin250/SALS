Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 67

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–6])

The case at hand was an appeal arising from the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court, where Indian Oil Corporation Limited (the respondent) sought to arrest the MV ‘New Endeavor’ (the first appellant) as an associated ship to the MT ‘New Diamond’ (the ship concerned), which was involved in a maritime incident causing substantial losses to Indian Oil. The original charterparty was concluded on 5 August 2020 for the carriage of crude oil, but a fire on board the New Diamond led to its abandonment and subsequent losses of approximately USD 70 million incurred by Indian Oil ([paras 2, 3]).

Indian Oil had initially secured its claim partly through a flag arrest of the New Diamond in Panama and through other means at Richards Bay. On 30 May 2022, an urgent application was brought before the High Court for the arrest of the New Endeavor as an associated ship under sections 3(6) and (7) of the Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983 (the Act). The High Court, under Mathenjwa AJ, granted this arrest. Following that decision, the New Endeavor applied for reconsideration of the arrest order under rule 6(12)(c) of the Uniform Rules of Court; however, this request was dismissed with costs by Sibiya J ([paras 1, 6]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 7–8])

The principal issue on appeal was whether Indian Oil had discharged its burden of proving the alleged association between the two vessels, namely the New Endeavor and the New Diamond ([para 7]). It entailed determining if Indian Oil could establish a common control over the companies owning these ships, given that the respondent had asserted multiple sources of control rather than a singular one. While the High Court recognized that the respondent had satisfied the prima facie case and genuine need for security requirements under section 5(3) of the Act, the question of association was contested ([para 7, 8]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 9–12])

Several legal standards were invoked during the appeal. The Act provides that a ship is deemed associated if owned by individuals or entities sharing control at the time the action is commenced, reflected in section 3(7)(a). Past judgments, including Cargo Laden and Lately Laden on board the MV Thalassini Avgi v MV Dimitris, Bocimar NV v Kotor Overseas Shipping Ltd, and MV Silver Star: Owners of Silver Star v Hilane Ltd, established precedent regarding the burden of proof concerning association claims in maritime law contexts, indicating the plaintiff has the onus to prove this association on a balance of probabilities ([paras 8, 9]).

The court referred to established principles in Silver Star, which inform that control may be both direct (de jure) or indirect (de facto), affording a broad interpretative lens consistent with the legislative purpose of facilitating maritime claimants ([paras 10, 12]). Guidance from legal commentary, such as that from Wallis' The Associated Ship, further clarified the nuanced nature of establishing association and control in holding marine vessels accountable.

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 13–50])

The court undertook a comprehensive analysis of the evidence presented to assess whether the requisite association between the New Endeavor and New Diamond had been established. Central to the court’s reasoning was the identification of Adam Polemis as the ultimate controller of the companies, namely Elly Maritime SA (owner of New Endeavor) and Porto Emporios Shipping Inc. (owner of New Diamond) ([paras 13, 43–50]).

The court noted that Indian Oil presented significant corroborative evidence, including a report by the investigative company Gray Page and other documentary exhibits portraying an intertwined family control structure around the shipping entities ([paras 22, 23, 28]). The evidence suggested that both NSL and PSL acted as vehicles for the operations, with control shared and ultimately exercised by the Polemis family.

The court addressed the appellants' arguments, articulating that asserting alternative sources of control did not preclude establishing a singular locus of control, thus finding no inconsistencies in Indian Oil's claims ([paras 34, 37]). The court recognized the difficulty of proving control in corporate structures but concluded that Indian Oil successfully linked the ownership and management between the ships in question, overcoming the evidentiary challenges inherent in such maritime claims ([paras 40, 48]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 51–52])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, confirming that Indian Oil had sufficiently satisfied the burden of establishing the association necessary for the arrest of the New Endeavor as an associated ship ([para 52]). The legal implications of the ruling reaffirmed the capacity of maritime claimants to seek security against associated ships under the Act, strengthening the judicial interpretation of control and ownership within complex shipping structures.

The court's decision emphasizes the need for claimants in maritime law to present a coherent narrative connecting the control and ownership of vessels in corporate entities while retaining a general interpretative stance favouring maritime claimants’ access to legal redress, consistent with the overarching objectives of the Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act ([paras 51, 52]).
