Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note on JJPC Brand Administrators & another v Lombard & others (1223/2017) [2019] ZASCA 55

### F – Facts
The case concerns the appellants, JJPC Brand Administrators and Basie Brand, who are the administrators of the Ventersdraai farm. The dispute arose over a particular road linking Ventersdraai to the R561 provincial road, following the closure of a portion of a public road by the then Administrator of Transvaal in March 1973 [1–7]. The Respondents, Philip Lombard, Louisa Jacoba Engelbrecht, and Eating Habits (Pty) Ltd, sought to declare that the existing road was not a public road and requested to relocate it.

Historically, the road in question had been used since the 1950s, providing access to Ventersdraai and other homesteads along its route [5–6]. The existing road traversed various farms and was known locally as a wagon road. As of 2012, the respondents applied to the Limpopo Division of the High Court, claiming the right to alter the road's route [12]. The court a quo ruled in favor of the respondents, declaring that the existing road was not a public road and that they had the right to relocate it [14].

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues in this case involve:
1. Whether the road in question remains classified as a public road following the closure of a part of it by proclamation [1].
2. Whether the respondents held the right to relocate the servitude of the road despite the appellants' claims that such a relocation would cause inconvenience and material prejudice [13].

### R – Rules
The determination hinged upon several legal principles:
- The definition of a "public road" under various ordinances, notably the Transvaal Road Ordinance 5 of 1912, 9 of 1933, and 22 of 1957, which outline the conditions under which a road can be classified as public [15–18].
- The principles governing servitudes, specifically that the servient owner may not impede the use of a defined servitude, which can only be relocated with mutual consent unless circumstances justify unilateral relocation [24–27].
- The onus of proof that the road is not a public road lies with the respondents, as indicated in Botha v Bukes 1955 (1) SA 581 [20].

### A – Application
The court critically evaluated if the respondents had met their burden of proof regarding the status of the road. The Judge noted that while the respondents argued there was insufficient evidence of the road's deprecated public status, the appellants had established a right of way through continuous use and would therefore enjoy a praedial servitude [21]. The court observed that the evidence produced by the respondents to claim the road was not public did not sufficiently substantiate their position [22].

Moreover, the court considered the implications of relocating the existing servitude. Using precedents from Gardens Estate and Linvestment, the Judge noted that while servitudes can be adjusted under specific circumstances, the respondents failed to establish that the proposed new route would not effectively impose inconvenience or higher costs on the appellants [29–30]. The existing road provided a more direct and practical route, and any change would require considerable expenditure on the part of the owners of Ventersdraai [30].

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, thereby rejecting the prior judgment of the High Court [34]. The first declaratory order, stating that the existing road was not a public road, was set aside; the respondents had not satisfactorily demonstrated the closure of a part of that road terminated its public status [31]. The proposed relocation was deemed inapplicable because it would lead to material prejudice for the appellants, fulfilling the criteria set out for denying such ordinances. The respondents were ordered to bear costs jointly and severally, reflecting an overarching ruling that upheld the dominant owner's rights concerning servitude [34].

This case significantly underscores the legal principles surrounding public road classification and the complexities of modifying established servitudes, affirming the necessary balance between private interests and property rights.
