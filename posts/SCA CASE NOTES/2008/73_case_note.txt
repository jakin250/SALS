# FIRAC Case Note: Sasria Ltd v Slabbert Burger Transport (Pty) Ltd

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([1]–[4])

The appellant, Sasria Limited, an insurance company, was involved in a dispute with the respondent, Slabbert Burger Transport (Pty) Ltd, regarding a claim for indemnification of R 600,000 for the destruction of a truck that occurred due to a fire on March 2, 2005. The truck was insured under a "coupon policy" that provided coverage against losses caused by riots, strikes, or public disorder ([1]).

The circumstances leading to the fire included a lawful strike by members of the South African Transport and Allied Workers' Union (SATAWU), which commenced on March 1, 2005. The strike resulted in significant discord, with participants assaulting non-striking drivers, damaging vehicles, and looting cargo. The claimant's truck, driven by an employee who did not participate in the strike, was parked at a truck stop when it was set on fire by two unidentified men shortly after purchasing petrol and matches ([4]).

The primary issue identified in the agreed statement of facts was whether the damage to the truck was caused by a peril covered under the SASRIA insurance policy. The court a quo found in favor of the plaintiff, which led to the appeal ([4]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([5]–[6])

The legal questions before the court were centered on the interpretation of the SASRIA insurance policy regarding the terms "riot," "strike," and "public disorder." Specifically, the court was tasked with determining whether the destruction of the truck fell within these terms, particularly concerning whether the cause of damage was directly related to actions taken during the strike ([5]).

The court needed to assess if the lower court had correctly inferred the actions leading to the fire and if the terminology in the insurance policy should be applied based on ordinary meanings or require a contextual interpretation ([6]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([7]–[9])

The court relied on the ordinary meaning of the relevant terms within the context of the SASRIA policy. In construing the term "strike," the meaning used was derived from the Shorter Oxford English Dictionary, defining it as "a concerted cessation of work on the part of a body of workers for the purpose of obtaining some concession from the employer" ([8]).

Additionally, the court referred to the principles of statutory interpretation, particularly **eiusdem generis** and **noscitur a sociis**, asserting that words must be construed in context. Previous case law, including *SASRIA Ltd v Elwyn Investments (Pty) Ltd*, was also cited to support the premise that even lawful strikes could involve elements of disorder, including damage, which may lead to claims under such insurance policies ([7]).

The ruling in *Concord Insurance Co. Ltd v Oelofson NO* illustrated the application of the **contra proferentem** rule, whereby any ambiguity in an insurance policy should be interpreted against the insurer who drafted the terms ([9]).

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([10])

The court examined the lower court's conclusions and the inferences drawn from the facts presented. It affirmed that the fire's causation was directly attributable to a strike-related activity, and the inference that the assailants were likely related to the strike was found to be plausible and probable ([5]).

The judge a quo's interpretation that the fire resulted from actions connected to the strike effectively encompassed the necessary criterion of "directly related to or caused by" as stipulated in the policy. The court countered the appellant's argument that "strike" should be contextualized among more violent connotations, clarifying that if the insurer intended for the term to carry a modified meaning, it should have articulated it explicitly within the policy ([9]).

Thus, the court concluded that the act causing the loss (the fire) indeed resulted from a situation classified as a strike, aligning with the standard interpretation of terms laid out in the insurance context ([10]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([11])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court’s decision to award R 600,000 to Slabbert Burger Transport (Pty) Ltd. By applying the ordinary meanings of the terms within the SASRIA policy, the court effectively held that the destruction of the truck was covered under the insurance policy as it was caused by an act related to a legal strike ([10]).

The ruling emphasizes the importance of clear, concise wording in insurance policies to avoid ambiguity. Furthermore, it reiterates the principle that insurers must bear the consequences of vague language through the application of the **contra proferentem** rule. The implications of this case underscore the necessity for insurers to draft policies that explicitly clarify interpretations to prevent costly disputes, thereby fostering a more equitable framework for insured parties.

Overall, the case sets a significant precedent in the interpretation of insurance policy terms and their application to real-world events involving strikes and public disturbances ([11]).
