Supreme Court of Appeal

Koni Multinational Brands (Pty) Ltd v Beiersdorf AG [2021] ZASCA 24

The Gauteng Division of the High Court initially granted an interdict, preventing the appellant from using its current packaging, leading to the appeal being heard on 19 November 2020.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2021 [2021] ZASCA 24

FIRAC Analysis

F – Facts ([paras 1–12])

The case revolves around Koni Multinational Brands (Pty) Ltd (‘the appellant’), which launched the ‘CONNIE MEN ACTIVE SHOWER GEL’ in December 2015, and Beiersdorf AG (‘the respondent’), the manufacturer of the well-known NIVEA MEN personal care products, which had been in the market since 1986. The respondent’s products are characterized by a distinctive blue, white, and silver get-up. The core dispute arose when the respondent contended that the appellant’s product packaging was confusingly similar to its own, constituting passing-off.

The Gauteng Division of the High Court initially granted an interdict, preventing the appellant from using its current packaging, leading to the appeal being heard on 19 November 2020. The high court found that the appellant's packaging could potentially deceive consumers due to its similarity to the NIVEA MEN products ([paras 1–18]).

I – Issues ([paras 19–22])

The primary legal issue pertained to the law of passing-off, which addresses whether a business may stop another from presenting its products as being connected or associated with its well-established brand. Specifically, the court needed to determine:

  1. Whether the respondent could prove that it held significant goodwill and reputation in the get-up of its NIVEA MEN products.
  2. Whether the appellant's CONNIE MEN shower gel packaging was likely to confuse consumers into believing it was associated with the NIVEA MEN products ([paras 19–20]).

R – Rules ([paras 21–28])

The court underscored several principles influencing passing-off claims:

  • The claimant must establish a reputation in its goods or services, which implies brand recognition amongst the relevant consumer demographic ([para 21]).
  • The misrepresentation must be likely to lead the public to confuse the defendant's goods with the claimant's based on the respective get-ups ([para 22]). The test for confusion involves a value judgment considering the overall impression created by the packaging and labels, rather than a meticulous comparison of features ([para 27]).
  • The court also reiterated the importance of consumer perception in assessing goodwill, stating that a claim for passing-off depends on the likelihood of deception regarding source or sponsorship of goods ([paras 25–26]).

A – Application ([paras 23–34])

The Supreme Court of Appeal analysed the evidence regarding the respondent's reputation, noting decades of extensive marketing, sales figures, and brand recognition surveys demonstrating that consumers identified the NIVEA MEN brand strongly with its packaging. The evidence indicated that the respondent's products held a substantial market share ([paras 23–25]).

In evaluating the appellant’s packaging, the court identified a range of similarities, such as the usage of a blue container with a silver lid, and a wave-like graphic resembling the respondent's wave-label. It concluded that these elements were likely to create confusion among consumers, particularly at points of purchase where products were displayed alongside each other in supermarkets. The average consumer, without direct comparison, might fail to differentiate clearly between the two products due to their overwhelming similarities ([paras 32–34]).

The court dismissed arguments by the appellant regarding the distinctiveness of its brand name, pointing out that the visual packaging delivered a significant confusing similarity that could mislead consumers ([paras 31, 33–34]).

C – Conclusion ([paras 35–38])

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the High Court's ruling, concluding that the appellant's product did indeed infringe on the respondent’s goodwill through passing-off. The ruling emphasized the importance of protecting established brands from unfair competition that could adversely affect consumer choices and damages due to brand dilution.

The order included directions to report the non-disclosure by the respondent’s attorneys regarding a material witness, and to examine their conduct. The decision solidified the protection of established trademarks under the law in South Africa and clarified the standards for evaluating passing-off claims in the context of consumer perception and brand reputation management.

Implications for Future Cases

This case reinforces the critical importance of maintaining a clear distinction between different brands, particularly in a competitive marketplace where consumer confusion can easily arise. Future claimants must ensure they can incontrovertibly demonstrate their product’s reputation through consistent and well-established branding, as well as readiness to differentiate their packaging substantially. The case also serves as a cautionary tale for legal representatives regarding transparency and disclosure in the evidential process.
