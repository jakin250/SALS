Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: Firac Analysis of Govender v The State (221/2022) [2023] ZASCA 60

## F – Facts
In the matter of **Govender v The State**, the appellant, Prenashan Govender, was convicted in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg, on two counts of murder and various contraventions of the Firearms Control Act 60 of 2000 ([1]). He received a life sentence for each count. Following dismissals of both his trial and full court appeals, Govender was granted special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([1]).

The case stemmed from an incident on 12 August 2018, at a restaurant and club in Kyalami, Johannesburg, where Govender, his wife, and friends attended a function. The events escalated after a fight occurred involving a member of their group, leading to Govender's co-accused, Lloyd Lester Latchman (Accused 1), fatally shooting two individuals: Mr. Theolan Nair and Mr. Yashlin Pillay ([2]).

Eyewitness accounts, particularly from Mr. Maswanganye (an Uber driver), Ms. Nair (the wife of the deceased), and Mr. Dickson (a bouncer at the club), provided crucial testimony ([3]–[8]). Maswanganye noted that Govender was armed and sought to confront the individual who assaulted another member of his group. Amid a scuffle, Latchman took possession of Govender's firearm and then shot the deceased ([5]–[6]).

Govender chose not to testify in his defense, although his counsel suggested a narrative that would have positioned him as unaware of Latchman's intentions ([10]–[11]). The State's case revolved around the theory of common purpose, alleging that Govender actively associated with Latchman's actions ([12]–[17]).

## I – Issues
The principal issue raised on appeal was whether Govender acted in common purpose with Accused 1 in the murders of the deceased ([11]). Specifically, it was argued that the findings of the trial court relied on conjecture and speculation, thereby invalidating the conviction ([11]). This centered on whether there was evidence of an active association between Govender and Latchman, meeting the necessary legal threshold for a common purpose conviction ([12]).

## R – Rules
The legal framework concerning common purpose in South African law stipulates that active association with the conduct of another perpetrator must be established, which does not necessitate prior agreement for a common criminal enterprise ([12]). 

Reference to key precedents clarified the requirements for proving common purpose:
1. Presence at the scene.
2. Awareness of an assault.
3. Intent to participate in the unlawful act.
4. Manifestation of the common purpose ([13]).

The mens rea required is dolus eventualis, which necessitates that the accused foresaw and reconciled with the possibility that their actions could lead to death or serious injury ([13]).

## A – Application
The court meticulously analyzed the evidence, affirming its findings on active association. Govender's decision to brandish his firearm after a confrontation and to attempt to pursue Latchman into the club suggested a clear intention to partake in violence ([14]–[15]). The very act of relinquishing possession of his firearm, alongside Latchman's actions following that moment, illustrated a cohesive operational plan aimed at avenging an assault on a friend ([14]).

Despite Govender's lack of direct involvement in the shootings, the court emphasized that one need not explicitly agree to participate in the violence for common purpose to be established ([12]). The testimonies, including Govender's wife's misgivings about his whereabouts post-shooting and his failure to report the firearm theft, were pivotal in reaffirming his culpability. This corroborated the narrative that Govender was complicit in a shared intent to engage in violent retribution ([20]–[22]).

The court also examined circumstantial evidence substantiating that neither Accused 1 nor Govender distanced themselves from the violence or displayed intent of withdrawal from their negligent acts once Latchman had armed himself ([16]–[19]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Govender's convictions for murder, emphasizing the strength and coherence of the State's evidence. The court concluded that Govender's actions constituted a clear intention to engage in the violent assault leading to murder, fulfilling the criteria set for common purpose ([23]). Ultimately, the conviction was considered sustainable, given the accumulated circumstantial and direct evidence against him, and the appeal was dismissed, affirming the life sentences imposed by the High Court ([24]). 

The judgment underlines the stringent application of the common purpose doctrine in South African criminal law, significantly influencing case law on joint liability for crimes committed within a participatory framework of violence.
