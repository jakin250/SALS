FIRAC Case Note: King N.O. and Others v De Jager and Others [2021] ZACC 4

F – Facts

In this landmark case before the Constitutional Court of South Africa, the applicants sought a declaratory order invalidating a clause (Clause 7) in the will of Carel Johannes Cornelius De Jager and Catherine Dorothea De Jager, dated November 28, 1902. The clause stipulated a fideicommissum (a conditional bequest) that excluded female descendants from inheriting estate properties, passing the inheritance solely to male descendants ([1]–[4]). The applicants included the daughters of the deceased, Kalvyn de Jager, who contested the discriminatory nature of the clause, arguing that it violated their rights as documented in the Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000, as well as their constitutional rights to equality ([1], [10]).

The litigation began when the deceased’s estate executor faced competing claims to the fideicommissary properties: the daughters sought recognition and inheritance of the property, while the male descendants of the deceased's brothers asserted their entitlement based on compliance with Clause 7 ([7], [8]).

The High Court found that the contested clause limited inheritance rights based solely on gender, but ultimately upheld it, deeming that the primacy of testamentary freedom outweighed claims of discrimination ([10], [12]). Subsequent appeals to the Supreme Court of Appeal were dismissed without sufficient reasons, prompting the present appeal to the Constitutional Court ([13]).

I – Issues

The central legal questions arising from this case are:

  1. Is Clause 7 of the will consistent with the Constitution's guarantees of equality?
  2. Does the principle of freedom of testation provide adequate grounds for enforcing a clause that results in unfair discrimination against female descendants?
  3. To what extent can courts intervene in private testamentary dispositions based on constitutional and public policy considerations? ([19])

R – Rules

The legal framework at play includes:

  • Equality Clause: Section 9 of the South African Constitution prohibits unfair discrimination based on gender among other categories ([9], [136]).
  • Promotion of Equality and Prevention of Unfair Discrimination Act 4 of 2000: This statute explicitly addresses discriminatory practices, including those that prevent women from inheriting property ([10]).
  • Common Law of Succession: The principle of freedom of testation allows individuals to dictate the terms of their estates upon death, subject to limitations imposed by public policy ([23], [64]).

Judicial precedents indicate that testamentary provisions that violate public policy or principles of equality may be rendered unenforceable ([26],[179]).

A – Application

The Court critically examined Clause 7, recognizing that it constituted direct discrimination against female descendants, thereby violating their constitutional right to equality. The judges highlighted that the limitation of inheritance solely to male descendants on the grounds of immutable characteristics—gender—was both arbitrary and against public policy ([85], [204]).

While the High Court had previously balanced the rights to equality against freedom of testation and decided in favor of the latter, the Constitutional Court determined that any testamentary provision resulting in unfair discrimination must yield to constitutional imperatives ([88], [138]).

Judgment analysis illuminated the necessity for a context-sensitive approach to freedom of testation, emphasizing that this principle cannot be wielded to uphold outdated patriarchal norms that perpetuate systemic gender inequality in inheritance ([195], [202]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately ruled in favor of the appellants, declaring Clause 7 of the will unconstitutional and unenforceable. The decision underscored the importance of aligning testamentary freedom with contemporary values of equality and non-discrimination as expressed in the Constitution and relevant equality legislation. The Court also appointed the daughters as beneficiaries of equal shares of the properties, rectifying the historical injustices encapsulated in the will ([88], [250]).

This decision has significant implications for testamentary laws in South Africa, reinforcing the principle that private wills must not transgress the foundational values of equality and human dignity enshrined in the Constitution ([244]).
