# Case Note: Solidarity obo Members v Barloworld Equipment Southern Africa and Others [2022] ZACC 15

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts
The case involves an application for leave to appeal against a judgment of the Labour Court that dismissed an application initiated by Solidarity, acting on behalf of its members, against Barloworld Equipment Southern Africa (Barloworld). The core of this matter relates to the invocation of section 189A(13) of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA) in response to mass dismissals conducted by Barloworld for operational reasons, allegedly without following the required processes ([1]–[2]).

On 27 April 2020, Barloworld notified its employees of impending restructurings necessitated by the effects of the COVID-19 pandemic. Subsequently, on 28 April 2020, it sought facilitation from the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation, and Arbitration (CCMA). After several consultation meetings, Barloworld decided to terminate the employment of 750 staff members, sparking allegations from Solidarity and NUMSA about inadequate consultation processes under section 189(2) of the LRA ([5]–[11]).

In its application, Solidarity claimed that Barloworld failed to engage in genuine discussions about the selection criteria for dismissals, particularly regarding the criterion of transformation, which Solidarity argued contravened their members' rights and rendered the dismissals procedurally unfair ([12]–[20]).

### I – Issues
The principal issues before the Court were:
1. Whether Solidarity was entitled to invoke section 189A(13) of the LRA, which allows consulting parties to seek court intervention to compel compliance with fair procedures during retrenchments.
2. Whether the Labour Court erred in holding that Solidarity's application should not have been brought under section 189A(13).
3. The nature of the distinction between procedural fairness and compliance with a fair procedure in mass retrenchments.
4. The implications of the findings on the costs awarded against Solidarity by the Labour Court ([34]–[63]).

### R – Rules
The applicable legislative provisions included:
- **Section 189 of the LRA**: Outlines the employer’s obligation to consult on retrenchment.
- **Section 189A of the LRA**: Regulates the consultation processes and allows for the appointment of facilitators, stipulating remedies for non-compliance.
- **Section 189A(13)**: Provides a mechanism for a consulting party to seek an order from the Labour Court to compel the employer to follow fair procedures.
- **Section 189A(18)**: Precludes the Labour Court from adjudicating disputes regarding the procedural fairness of dismissals based on operational requirements referred to it in terms of section 191(5)(b)(ii).

The judgment discussed the necessity for meaningful consultations as part of a fair dismissal process, emphasizing that such discussions must go beyond superficial engagement ([39]–[60]).

### A – Application
The Court assessed whether meaningful consultations occurred and whether Solidarity rightfully invoked the provisions of section 189A(13). The judges noted that while there were indeed engagements regarding selection criteria, including transformation, Solidarity's insistence on rejecting transformation as a selection criterion led to a deadlock ([47]–[54]).

The Labour Court had previously held that the issues raised by Solidarity related more to general procedural fairness rather than strict compliance with a fair procedure, suggesting that the separation between procedural fairness and compliance was adequately maintained. The Constitutional Court found that the invocation of section 189A(13) should be viewed in light of efforts to ensure fair consultation and that the Labour Court's reasoning on the implications of timeline provisions was incorrect. Significantly, it clarified that parties could seek relief under this provision, even after dismissal notices had been issued ([55]–[75]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal and dismissed Solidarity's appeal on the merits but agreed to alter the costs order. The implications of this case highlight important considerations for retrenching employers, particularly regarding the expectations of meaningful consultation processes, the timing of legal challenges arising from dismissal procedures, and the scope of the Labour Court’s authority under section 189A([81]).

This case reaffirms the necessity for balancing procedural equity in the dismissal process while ensuring adequate judicial oversight to prevent unfair practices that can detrimentally affect employees. The decision reiterates the critical nature of compliance with fair procedures mandated by the LRA, thus reinforcing fair labour practices as enshrined in the South African Constitution.
