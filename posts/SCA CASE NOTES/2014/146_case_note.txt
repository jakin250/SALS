Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: **Rammbuda v The State** (156/14) [2014] ZASCA 146

## F – Facts

Mr. Petrus Rammbuda (the appellant) was convicted on 4 November 2002 in the regional court of Thohoyandou for the rape of a seven-year-old girl. The regional magistrate then committed him to the high court for sentencing under section 52(1)(b) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, where he received a life sentence from Hetisani J. Rammbuda appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) with the high court's leave ([1]).

During the trial, two child witnesses, including the complainant, did not receive proper admonition or swearing as required by sections 162 and 164 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([2]). The defense argued that this procedural misstep, along with contradictions and inconsistencies in the evidence, warranted the overturning of the conviction ([2]).

The complainant's testimony described details of the alleged rape, but her accounts had substantial inconsistencies when compared with testimony from her aunt, Ms. Mchaba, and further lacked corroboration due to the absence of the examining doctor ([11]–[14]). The SCA assessed the enquiry conducted by the trial court regarding the competence of the child witnesses and found it inadequate ([8]).

## I – Issues

Key legal issues considered by the SCA included:

1. Whether the trial court properly adhered to sections 162 and 164 of the Criminal Procedure Act regarding the swearing and admonition of witnesses, specifically child witnesses ([2], [8]).
2. Whether the testimony of the child witnesses was competent to support a conviction.
3. The implications of contradictions between the complainant's testimony and that of her aunt, as well as the lack of medical evidence due to the absence of the examining doctor ([10], [14]).

## R – Rules

The SCA relied on several significant legal principles:
- **Sections 162-164 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977** delineate the requirement for witnesses to be sworn or affirmed or, in the case of those who do not understand the nature of an oath, to be admonished to tell the truth ([4]).
- The **Director of Public Prosecutions, Transvaal v Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development &others** established that courts must carefully determine whether child witnesses comprehend what it means to tell the truth ([5]).
- **S v Raghubar** outlined the necessity for a judicial officer to ascertain a child's ability to distinguish between truth and falsehood before allowing testimony ([6]).
- **S v Matshivha** reiterated that if proper procedures under section 162 or 164 are not followed, the resulting testimony lacks the necessary evidentiary weight and is inadmissible ([7]).

## A – Application

The SCA evaluated whether the trial court adequately determined the competency of the complainant and her friend to testify. The court found that the initial questioning performed by the presiding officer was insufficient. It failed to establish the children's understanding of truth and falsehood adequately, leading to a fundamental procedural error ([8]).

Moreover, despite the prosecution's reliance on the uncorroborated testimony of the complainant, the court found significant material contradictions. The complainant’s own narrative diverged notably from her aunt’s recounting, which raised doubts about the credibility of the evidence ([10]). The inconsistencies highlighted the inadequacy of the judicial process in ensuring the reliability of the testimonies presented within that framework.

The absence of the medical doctor further undermined the evidentiary basis for the conviction. The defense pointed out that without the examining doctor’s testimony, critical details about the examination and findings were left uncorroborated, exacerbating doubts regarding the case against Rammbuda ([14]).

## C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the SCA held that the conviction and sentence of Rammbuda could not be sustained due to procedural inadequacies in the examination of the child witnesses and the contradictions within the evidence presented. The appeal was upheld, and Rammbuda's conviction and life sentence were set aside ([1], [14]).

This judgment underscores the necessity of rigorous procedural adherence in cases involving child witnesses to uphold not only the integrity of the judicial process but also the rights of the accused. It reinforces the principle that any conviction must be founded on reliable evidence, particularly in sensitive cases such as this one. The court's decision has implications for future cases involving child witnesses, emphasizing the responsibility of judicial officers to ensure that children can competently testify to the truth without burdening them with theoretical understandings that may not align with their experiences.
