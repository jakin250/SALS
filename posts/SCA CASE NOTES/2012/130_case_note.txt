Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2012

# FIRAC Case Note: Paixão v Road Accident Fund (640/2011) [2012] ZASCA 130

**F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history** ([paras 1–11])

The case concerns an appeal by Maria Angelina Paixão and her daughter, Michelle Orlanda Santos Paixão, against the Road Accident Fund for loss of maintenance and support following the death of José Adelino Do Olival Gomes in a vehicular accident on January 2, 2008. At the time of his death, the deceased had been in a long-term intimate relationship with Mrs Paixão and had been supporting her and her children financially. While the couple had intentions to marry, no formal legal marriage had taken place as the deceased was not yet divorced from his first wife under Portuguese law. The South Gauteng High Court found that the deceased's support was motivated by compassion rather than any legal obligation, leading to the dismissal of the appellants' claims. The High Court's decision was appealed, creating the basis for this Supreme Court of Appeal case.

**I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions** ([paras 1–2, 11])

The principal issue is whether the common law should be developed to extend the dependants' action, which is traditionally recognized in formal marriages, to individuals in permanent heterosexual life partnerships. This inquiry raises substantial questions about the nature of legal obligations deriving from the relationship between the deceased and the appellants, particularly whether those obligations can constitute a legally enforceable duty of support.

**R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon** ([paras 12–16])

The law of delict recognizes a 'dependants' action' allowing dependants to claim compensation for loss of support due to a breadwinner's death, as codified in section 17(1) of the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996. This action traditionally applies to those to whom the deceased owed a legal duty of support, a criterion assessed against boni mores, or the legal convictions of the community ([paras 13, 14]). 

Past case law, including Amod v Multilateral Vehicle Accidents Fund 1999 (4) SA 1319 (A) and Du Plessis v Road Accident Fund 2004 (1) SA 359 (SCA), underscores a trend where the courts have recognized increasingly diverse familial structures deserving of legal protection in modern society, suggesting flexibility in the definition of dependants. 

**A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law** ([paras 18–40])

The court evaluated whether there existed a tacit agreement imposing a legal obligation on the deceased to maintain the appellants. The evidence, including shared living arrangements and responsibilities, indicated a committed relationship akin to a family unit which had mutual obligations of support. Notably, the deceased expressed intentions to marry and established a Joint Will nominating the appellants as heirs, reflecting a deeply embedded familial bond ([paras 19–21]).

The Supreme Court critically addressed the High Court's dismissal of the appellants' claims, stating that the facts demonstrated a reciprocal duty of support that is worthy of the law's protection. It reasoned that the evolving societal norms should be reflected within the common law, supporting the view that a permanent heterosexual partnership could be treated with the same legal recognition as formal marriages ([paras 23–40]).

The court's decision underscored that the absence of formal marriage should not deprive individuals in meaningful relationships of the protection afforded to married couples. It proactively aimed to rectify social inequalities by affirming the rights of partners in a permanent life partnership to claim support ([para 40]).

**C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications** ([paras 42–42])

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately held that the dependants' action should indeed extend to unmarried individuals in permanent heterosexual relationships who have established a reciprocal duty of support. It reversed the High Court's judgment and ordered the Road Accident Fund to compensate both appellants, recognizing their claims based on the established facts of their familial relationship. This decision not only compensates the appellants for their loss but also sets a precedent for future cases involving permanent partnerships, further indicating a progressive trajectory in South African family law that aligns more closely with the realities of modern relationships. 

Overall, this case reflects a significant development in the jurisprudence surrounding familial obligations, emphasizing the necessity of updating common law to accommodate changing social values and promote equality for all individuals, irrespective of marital status.
