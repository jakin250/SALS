# FIRAC Case Note: **Fischer v Ubomi Ushishi Trading & Others** (1085/2017) [2018] ZASCA 154 (19 November 2018)

## F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–9])

The case involves an appeal regarding the ownership of immovable property located at Erf 31865, Goodwood, Cape Town, previously owned by Mr and Mrs Haynes. Their marriage, which was in community of property, was dissolved by divorce on 10 December 2012. In a settlement agreement incorporated into the divorce order, Mr Haynes waived his rights to the property in favor of Mrs Haynes. Subsequently, the couple remarried in 2014 under an antenuptial contract excluding the accrual system, and they divorced again in 2015.

In March 2015, Mr Andre Fischer, the appellant, obtained a default judgment against Ubomi Ushishi Trading CC, the first respondent, and Mr Haynes for an amount of R566,500. When attempts to execute the judgment against Ubomi were ineffectively met, Mr Fischer sought to have Mr Haynes’ half share in the immovable property declared executable. The Western Cape Division of the High Court dismissed his application, holding that ownership of the property had vested in Mrs Haynes immediately upon the dissolution of the marriage. This appeal follows, contesting the correctness of the lower court’s ruling.

## I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1–4])

The primary legal issue before the court was whether the real right to a half share in the immovable property vested in Mrs Haynes immediately upon the granting of the divorce order or whether it only vested upon formal transfer through the Deeds Registry, as dictated by the Deeds Registries Act 47 of 1937. A secondary issue concerned the nature of the rights acquired by Mrs Haynes due to the settlement agreement as it relates to the underlying principles of property law and the transfer of rights.

## R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 10–30])

The court examined various legal provisions and case law relevant to the transfer of immovable property. **Section 16** of the Deeds Registries Act stipulates that ownership may only be conveyed via a deed of transfer executed by the registrar, unless otherwise provided by law, including the Divorce Act 70 of 1979.

The court considered two pivotal cases: **Corporate Liquidators (Pty) Ltd v Wiggill** and **Middleton v Middleton**, which articulated different views on whether a settlement agreement under the Divorce Act constitutes an automatic vesting of ownership or merely establishes a personal right to transfer. Additionally, the court analyzed the implications of **Ex Parte Menzies**, which supported immediate vesting of ownership upon divorce under similar circumstances.

The core legal principle emphasized by the court was that ownership of property in South Africa is primarily established through formal registration, a position entrenched at common law and upheld in various court rulings ([paras 18–30]).

## A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 10–30])

In its analysis, the court first reaffirmed that the derivation of ownership requires registration, undermining any notion that a settlement agreement could convert personal rights into real rights absent such a formal action. It held that while the Divorce Act allows for allocation of assets through a court order, it does not alter the necessity of adhering to the Deeds Registries Act’s stipulations regarding the formal transfer of property ([paras 12–21]).

The court distinguished the precedent set in **Corporate Liquidators**, finding that the reliance on that decision was misplaced given the more precise interpretation needed regarding divorce versus the normal transmission of property rights. Real rights could not simply vest without appropriate endorsement in the Deeds Registry, and the process of registration serves to protect public knowledge regarding property ownership ([paras 26–30]).

Moreover, Mrs Haynes claimed that her right to the property preceded Mr Fischer's claim, effectively establishing a priority of rights that limited the enforceability of Mr Fischer's judgment against Mr Haynes. The court found this assertion valid, as the personal right to acquire full ownership through the settlement agreement predated the execution claim ([paras 30–31]).

## C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 31–31])

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court's ruling that the ownership of the half share of the property did not vest automatically in Mrs Haynes by virtue of the divorce order but required endorsement through the Deeds Registry. The court underscored the necessity for formal legal compliance to effectuate the transfer of ownership.

This decision delineated the boundaries of property rights following divorce, emphasizing adherence to statutory requirements for the transfer of immovable property. The implications underscore the importance of ensuring clear, legally binding agreements and compliance with statutory mandates to avoid ambiguities concerning property rights post-divorce, thereby protecting creditors and ensuring proper administration of property law.
