Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# Case Note: M M v MEC for Health; Eastern Cape (580/2022) [2023] ZASCA 130

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
The appellant, Mrs. MM, initiated legal proceedings against the Member of the Executive Council for Health in the Eastern Cape (the respondent) for damages stemming from a brain injury sustained by her minor child, ELM, after birth at Frere Hospital, East London ([1]). The claim was grounded on allegations of medical negligence and breach of duty of care towards both the appellant and her child.

ELM was born on 18 October 2010 via emergency caesarean section and initially displayed mild respiratory distress. By 20 October, a laboratory report indicated elevated total serum bilirubin levels (506 micromol/L), suggesting jaundice ([2]–[4]). Subsequent tests showed worsening jaundice, and a pediatrician was consulted to start supportive treatment, including intravenous haemoglobin and intensive phototherapy ([4]).

On 21 October, blood was ordered for an exchange transfusion. However, the appellant requested a transfer to a private facility, Life Beacon Bay Hospital, before the blood could arrive, which led to further delays in treatment ([4]–[5]). ELM later developed dystonic cerebral palsy, caused by hyperbilirubinemia, which ultimately resulted in severe developmental delays and complications, including epilepsy ([5]).

Initially, the Eastern Cape High Court ruled in favor of the appellant, finding the hospital staff negligent in delaying the blood transfusion ([1]). However, the full bench subsequently overturned this decision, stating there was no substantial evidence that the delay in ordering the blood caused ELM’s injuries ([1][15]).

### I – Issues
The primary legal issues faced by the court included:
1. Whether there was a breach of duty of care by the hospital staff that resulted in negligence towards ELM ([11]).
2. Whether the delay in the ordering of blood transfusions contributed to the injuries sustained by ELM ([6]–[15]).
3. The standard of care expected from healthcare professionals in such circumstances and determining causation between the alleged negligence and the damages suffered by ELM ([19][16]).

### R – Rules
The principles governing medical negligence were deemed essential for this case, particularly the following:
1. Establishing negligence under South African law requires proof that a reasonable person would foresee the possibility of their actions causing harm to another and would take steps to mitigate such risk (Kruger v Coetzee 1966 (2) SA 428 (A) at 430E).
2. The standards of care required of medical professionals differ based on their specialized skills and knowledge; they are not held to the highest standards but must meet the general expectations of competence within their field ([20][33]).
3. In cases involving causation, two inquiries must be established:
   - Factual causation: Whether the negligent act caused the harm.
   - Legal causation: Whether the negligent act is sufficiently linked to warrant liability ([24]).

### A – Application
In reviewing the specifics of the case, the Supreme Court of Appeal analyzed the procedural history and the factual matrix surrounding ELM's treatment. 

1. The court recognized that the medical staff at Frere Hospital acted in accordance with accepted guidelines for treating hyperbilirubinemia. Dr. Harper, the attending physician, ordered blood for the necessary transfusion as per the protocols outlined in medical literature ([18][24]). The evidence indicated that the staff provided appropriate treatment and took proactive measures while awaiting the blood order ([20][21]).

2. On the critical issue of negligence, the court emphasized that the appellant failed to substantiate claims that the hospital staff acted unreasonably, particularly since ELM showed no signs of neurological compromise before the transfer to the private hospital ([18][22]). The appearance of neurological symptoms only materialized during the blood transfusion at Life Beacon Bay Hospital, further weakening the appellant's position ([13]).

3. The court noted that although Drs. Lombard and Mzizana identified an "undue delay," the causality suggested was based on the incorrect assertion that blood was only ordered on the 21st. Contrarily, evidence demonstrated that the order had been placed on the 20th, influencing the court’s resolution regarding negligence and causation ([20][21]).

4. Moreover, the court highlighted discrepancies in the appellant’s proof regarding the blood's availability and transport timelines from Gqeberha to East London. The absence of concrete evidence regarding when the blood was ordered or delivered played a pivotal role in undermining the appellant’s arguments on causation relating to the alleged negligence in not ordering the blood in time ([25]).

5. The standard of care was also scrutinized, since the hospital staff adhered to the norms expected of medical practitioners under similar circumstances. The record suggested that reasonable measures were taken to mitigate risks and that the condition of ELM remained stable until after he was transferred to the private facility ([33]).

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, ruling that the hospital staff at Frere Hospital were not negligent in their treatment of ELM. The court concluded that Mrs. MM could not establish the necessary causal link between the alleged negligence and the harm suffered by her child ([34]). The ruling reinforced the standards of medical practice expected in such cases and highlighted the responsibility of the plaintiff to provide robust, factual evidence in claims of medical negligence ([30][29]).

As a result, the court issued no order as to costs, recognizing the complexities of the case and the circumstances therein ([34]). The implications of this case reaffirm the importance of clear and substantiated claims in medical negligence actions and the judiciary's careful consideration of medical evidence and standards of care in assessing liability.
