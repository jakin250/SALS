Public Protector v South African Reserve Bank [2019] ZACC 29

Constitutional Court 2019 [2019] ZACC 29

F – Facts

The case revolves around an application made by the Public Protector against the South African Reserve Bank (SARB). Following a lengthy investigation into allegations concerning a R3.2 billion "lifeboat" agreement between SARB and Bankorp, the Public Protector published a final report on June 19, 2017, making various adverse findings against the SARB and proposing significant remedial actions, including an amendment to the Constitution.

The SARB, alongside Absa Bank, contested the findings, alleging that the Public Protector exhibited bias due to her failure to engage them before the final report and because she had discussed sensitive matters with the Presidency and the State Security Agency without informing them. The SARB sought punitive costs against the Public Protector, who was found personally liable for some costs incurred during the litigation processes.

The High Court found the Public Protector had conducted herself in a way that warranted personal costs due to perceived bias and her failure to fully disclose meetings that could have affected the findings against SARB. Consequently, it ordered her to pay 15% of the costs personally, alongside punitive costs on an attorney and client scale for the institution.

The Public Protector appealed this costs order to the Constitutional Court, which would evaluate both the personal costs order and the wider implications of the High Court's findings.

I – Issues

The legal issues at play in this case include:

  1. Whether the High Court’s application of the tests for gross negligence and bad faith against the Public Protector was justified.
  2. Whether personal costs can be imposed on a public official acting in an official capacity without clear evidence of misconduct.
  3. The implications of the findings concerning the independence of the Public Protector’s office and the potential chilling effect on the execution of her duties.
  4. Whether the declarations sought by the Reserve Bank regarding the Public Protector abusing her office were appropriate.

R – Rules

The relevant legal principles derive from multiple sources, including:

  • The Constitution of South Africa, which establishes the Public Protector’s role and outlines the accountability and independence required from public officials (sections 181 and 182).
  • The Public Protector Act 23 of 1994, particularly section 5(3), which provides a measure of indemnity to the Public Protector acting in good faith.
  • Case law that delineates the standards for awarding personal costs, including Black Sash II, which stipulates that personal costs are warranted in instances of gross negligence or bad faith. This principle is echoed in SASSA, emphasizing the accountability of public officials.
  • The legal standards concerning the allocation of costs, which assert that punitive costs are extraordinary and reserved for conduct warranting the court's stern disapproval.

A – Application

The Constitutional Court’s majority, led by Khampepe J and Theron J, analyzed the High Court’s rationale for imposing personal costs based on claims of bias and a lack of full disclosure. The majority found that the High Court had conflated its judgment regarding procedural failed due process with allegations of bias.

Key points evaluated included:

  • Alleged Bias and Investigative Conduct: The Court found that numerous explanations for the Public Protector's conduct had not been given credibly, particularly regarding undisclosed meetings with the Presidency and the State Security Agency.
  • Incidents of Negligence: The majority ruled that the High Court did not satisfactorily demonstrate that the Public Protector's actions amounted to gross negligence or bad faith, particularly as her errors were less egregious than those seen in prior judgments warranting personal costs.
  • Independence of the Office: The Court underscored the potential chilling effect personal costs on an attorney and client scale could impose on the accountability of public officials, particularly in contentious cases involving powerful entities like SARB.

The dissenting opinion by Mogoeng CJ reinforced the necessity of maintaining accountability among public officials and criticized the approach to awarding personal costs against state functionaries without robust evidence of severe misconduct.

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court granted leave to appeal the costs order but ultimately dismissed the appeal, thereby upholding the High Court's decision. However, it emphasized that the punitive costs awarded were unwarranted and did not align with the requirements of fairness and justice due to insufficient evidence of gross negligence or bad faith from the Public Protector.

It concluded that the High Court's reasoning was built on misapplied principles and that maintaining the integrity and independence of the Office of the Public Protector is paramount. This decision reflects an intent to shield constitutional institutions from undue influence and to promote their effective functioning within the parameters of their mandates, thereby reinforcing accountability and transparency in governance.

The order stipulated:

  1. The Public Protector’s application for leave to appeal was granted.
  2. The appeal was dismissed with no order as to costs in the Constitutional Court.
  3. The Reserve Bank’s application for cross-appeal was also dismissed without costs.

The case's implications underscore the balance between accountability and the functional independence of key public institutions within South Africa’s constitutional framework.
