# Case Note – MV ‘Shark Team’ v Tallman (190/2015) [2016] ZASCA 46

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–12])
The tragic incident at the heart of this case involves the MV ‘Shark Team’, a catamaran-hull ski-boat that capsized while conducting shark cage diving off the southern coast of South Africa on 13 April 2008. The vessel was anchored at approximately 10h00 near an area known for shark viewing when it was struck by an unexpectedly large swell, resulting in three fatalities, including that of Christopher Tallman. His widow, Sarah Tallman, initiated a maritime claim against the vessel's skipper, Grant Tuckett, and its owner, White Shark Projects CC, in the Western Cape Division of the High Court.

The trial, which lasted 52 days, produced a record of over 8,600 pages. The court, under the presiding judge Freund AJ, found the defendants liable for negligence, asserting that Tuckett failed to maintain a proper look-out regarding sea conditions, which led to the capsizing of the vessel. The defendants were granted leave to appeal the decision.

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 13–29])
The case raised several key legal issues:
1. Whether Tuckett was negligent in his duty to maintain a proper look-out regarding the changing sea conditions and thus failed to foresee the reasonable possibility of capsize.
2. Whether Tuckett's alleged negligence was causally connected to the deaths that resulted from the capsizing.
3. The liability of White Shark Projects CC under Section 261(1)(a) of the Merchant Shipping Act 57 of 1951.

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 30–70])
The judgment heavily relied on:
- The **Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983**, which defines a maritime claim and governs the applicable law in matters of admiralty jurisdiction ([paras 6–8]).
- The common law principles of **Aquilian liability**, specifically that for liability to exist, the harm caused must be wrongful and culpable, either through negligence or intent ([para 10]).
- The **Kruger v Coetzee** standard, which delineates the test for negligence by arguing that culpability arises when a reasonable person in the defendant's position would foresee the risk and fail to mitigate it ([para 11]).
- The principle that a reasonable standard of skill and diligence is expected, adjusted for the specific expertise relevant to the defendant's occupation ([para 12]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 71–162])
Freund AJ concluded that Tuckett had displayed negligence for several reasons:
- Testimonies from numerous witnesses indicated that conditions were deteriorating prior to the capsize, conflicting with Tuckett’s assertion that the conditions were benign ([paras 124–126]).
- The judgment emphasized the plaintiff's perspective, particularly noting that witnessed swells above 4 meters were prevalent in the vicinity, which no prudent skipper should have ignored ([para 118]).
- Freund AJ held that Tuckett acted with complacency, failing to depart in light of deteriorating conditions, and thus; there was a causal relationship established between his negligence and the subsequent capsizing that resulted in Tallman's death ([paras 123–126]).
  
On appeal, the Supreme Court of Appeal rigorously examined whether Tuckett’s negligence was correctly identified under the rule of foreseeability. The judgment distinguished the extraordinary nature of the wave that capsized Shark Team, stating it was not something that could have been reasonably foreseen by any competent skipper ([para 157]). Evidence revealed that this wave was a combination of factors leading to a rare phenomenon known as 'wedging,' thus complicating the priestly attribution of negligence ([paras 167–171]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 163–165])
The appeal was allowed, overturning the High Court’s initial ruling. The Supreme Court held that Tuckett's actions did not meet the threshold of negligence as outlined in the applicable standards of maritime law. It concluded that no reasonable skipper, given the known conditions and information available at the time, could have foreseen the wave that caused the incident. As such, the allegations of negligence were dismissed, and the case not only clarified standards for skippers operating in potentially hazardous conditions but also reaffirmed the significance of causation in negligence claims within admiralty jurisdiction. Consequently, all charges against the defendants were dropped, underscoring the complexities in maritime law regarding liability and negligence.
