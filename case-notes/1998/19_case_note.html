Joseph Leon Beinash and Another v Ernst and Young and Others (CCT 12/98)

Constitutional Court 1998 CCT 12/98

F - Facts

On January 12, 1998, the Witwatersrand High Court, under the presiding Judge Fevrier AJ, granted a vexatious litigant order against Joseph Leon Beinash and J B & L Nominees CC, the applicants. The order stated that the respondents could not pursue any legal actions against any individual in any Provincial or Local Division of the High Court or any inferior court without prior leave from the court ([1]). By this date, the applicants had instigated 45 legal proceedings since May 7, 1992, which had largely been unsuccessful, culminating in only one successful application for leave to appeal ([1]).

Following their efforts to appeal the High Court's order, including an unsuccessful petition to the Chief Justice, the applicants sought leave to appeal in the Constitutional Court without following the required protocols, such as obtaining a certificate of appeal from the Witwatersrand High Court or notifying the Minister of Justice, who was not a party in the case ([2]). The applicants challenged the Constitutionality of section 2(1)(b) of the Vexatious Proceedings Act, arguing it infringed upon their rights under section 34 of the South African Constitution, which guarantees access to legal recourse ([3]).

The applicants sought alternative relief, including the reversal of the High Court ruling and reconsideration of the order or processes used in obtaining it ([4]).

I - Issues

The central legal issues involved in this case were:

  1. The constitutionality of section 2(1)(b) of the Vexatious Proceedings Act relative to section 34 of the Constitution, which guarantees the right to access the courts ([3]);
  2. The procedural validity of the applicants’ appeal, given their failure to comply with former Rule 18 requiring a certificate from the High Court ([24]);
  3. The failure to join the Minister of Justice as a party to the appeal, which may impact the examination of the constitutional validity of the law in question ([27]).

R - Rules

The relevant legal framework includes:

  1. The Vexatious Proceedings Act 3 of 1956, specifically, section 2(1)(b), which grants courts the ability to bar persistently vexatious litigants from instituting any legal proceedings without leave ([3]).
  2. Section 34 of the Constitution, which states that “Everyone has the right to have any dispute that can be resolved by the application of law decided in a fair public hearing before a court” ([3]).
  3. Section 36 of the Constitution, which allows for the limitation of rights in a manner that is reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society ([16]).
  4. Rule 18 of the Constitutional Court, which necessitates that applications for leave to appeal must be accompanied by a certificate from the High Court ([24]).

A - Application

The court’s analysis began by considering the requirement that applicants must demonstrate a reasonable prospect of success in their appeal ([5]). The applicants contended that section 2(1)(b) created a blanket prohibition on access to the courts, arguing that it constituted an infringement of their right under section 34 ([7]). However, the respondents countered that the legislative framework allowed for appropriately tailored orders, indicating that while the Act bars vexatious litigants from pursuing actions, it does not eliminate their right to access the courts entirely ([8]).

The court explored the implications of section 36, emphasizing that the limitation on access to courts for vexatious litigants aims to protect the integrity of the judicial system and the interests of meritorious litigants who may be adversely affected by such abusive litigation ([16–19]). The court balanced the need to curb vexatious litigation against the constitutional right to access to courts, finding that the law does not impose an unreasonable burden on litigants wishing to pursue valid claims ([17]).

Regarding procedural matters, the court noted that although the applicants did not obtain the required certificate from the High Court as per Rule 18, the court could still consider the merits due to the context and the absence of prospects of success ([25–26]). Additionally, the failure to join the Minister of Justice as a party effectively limited the court’s ability to address the constitutional implications of the Order on behalf of the state ([27]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court found that the applicants lacked reasonable prospects of success in their appeal against the vexatious litigant order imposed by the High Court ([29]). The Court concluded that the limitations imposed by section 2(1)(b) of the Vexatious Proceedings Act were reasonable and justifiable under section 36. It emphasized that while the limitations restrict access to courts, they serve a vital public interest in maintaining the orderly function of the judicial system and protecting against persistent, groundless legal actions ([18–20]).

The application was ultimately dismissed with costs, including costs for two counsel, affirming the decision of the High Court and highlighting the responsibility of the applicants for the vexatious litigation that led to this legal outcome ([29]). The ruling underscores the balance between protecting the right to access the courts against the need to deter vexatious litigation, an important principle in maintaining the integrity of the judicial process in South Africa.
