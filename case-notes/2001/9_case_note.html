Case Title: Stephanus Johannes Martinius De Beer N.O. v The North Central Local Council and The South Central Local Council

Constitutional Court 2001 CCT 59/00

Case Title:

Stephanus Johannes Martinius De Beer N.O. v The North Central Local Council and The South Central Local Council

Case Reference: CCT 59/00, Constitutional Court of South Africa

Heard: 3 May 2001
Decided: 26 September 2001

F – Facts

[1] The case at hand involves an appeal regarding the procedural legality and constitutional validity of municipal rate collection as espoused in section 105 of the Durban Extended Powers Consolidated Ordinance No. 18 of 1976 (hereafter, “the Ordinance”). The appellant, Mr. De Beer, serves as the liquidator for a partnership owning immovable property located in Clairwood, Durban. Following unpaid rates on the property, the North Central Local Council and the South Central Local Council (collectively, "the Council") executed the section 105 procedure. This involved the issuance of prescribed notices and culminated in obtaining a High Court order sanctioning the sale of the partnership's property, subsequently sold to third respondent, Mr. Khan. Crucially, the appellant did not receive any of these notices.

[2] Mr. De Beer contested the constitutional validity of section 105(9) by arguing it infringes the right to a fair hearing enshrined in section 34 of the South African Constitution, which guarantees access to a court for dispute resolution. This appeal arose from a dismissal of his action in the High Court, where the Council had filed a bulk application for the sale of multiple properties due to outstanding rates, including the appellant's.

[3] The High Court noted that the fairness of the hearing cannot be interpreted by examining section 105(9) in isolation but must encompass the entirety of the notice provisions enacted by the section. The Sheriff of the High Court was joined as the second respondent but remained inactive in the proceedings. The third respondent defended the High Court action, and the Umhlatuzana Civic Association participated as amicus curiae, raising concerns regarding the constitutionality of the ordinance.

I – Issues

[4] The core issue presented for determination is whether the procedures outlined in section 105 of the Ordinance are consistent with the right to a fair hearing as established by section 34 of the Constitution. Specifically, the appeal raises the following legal questions:

  1. Do the notice provisions under section 105(9) provide a fair opportunity for affected parties to present their case?
  2. Does this section allow for orders against individuals without affording them a proper hearing?
  3. Whether the absence of a requirement for prior execution against movable property—prior to the sale of immovable property—contravenes noise standards of procedural fairness?

R – Rules

[5] The legal framework underpinning this case is founded heavily upon the provisions of the South African Constitution, specifically:

  • Section 34: Guarantees the right to a fair hearing in the resolution of disputes.
  • Section 105 of the Ordinance: Provides the procedural specifics concerning rate collection, including the notice requirements and related rights.

[6] Prior case law and constitutional principles further elaborate on the requirements for a fair hearing in judicial proceedings, emphasizing that legislation must align with these rights. Notable precedents include:

  • De Lange v Smuts NO and Others 1998 (3) SA 785 (CC): Affirming the necessity of ensuring all court proceedings maintain fairness.
  • Chief Lesapo v North West Agricultural Bank and Another 2000 (1) SA 409 (CC): Highlighting the requirement of fair hearings in the context of administrative law.

Additionally, general procedural fairness is delineated through the application of High Court rules, which were held to form an essential comparison point against the Ordinance's provisions.

A – Application

[7] The court thoroughly examined whether the section 105 procedures satisfy the constitutional requirement of a fair hearing. It conducted a comparative analysis between the ordinary court procedural mandates and those stipulated in the Ordinance, which included considerations such as the posting of notices. The court established that while various means of notification exist under the Ordinance, the fundamental principle is whether these methods reasonably inform the affected parties of the proceedings.

[8] The court scrutinized the nature of the notices required by section 105 and found that while the first notice had to indicate the amount owed and its implications, subsequent notices similarly needed to articulate that failure to pay could lead to court action. Notably, it was contended that the equivocality of the notices led to uncertainty.

[9] The Court reaffirmed that a fair hearing necessitates not only reasonable notice but also a subsequent fairness in the hearing itself. It established that courts maintain discretion when determining whether to issue orders based on compliance with notice requirement, thus preserving the essential tolerance for circumstances wherein equitable relief might necessitate additional notice.

[10] The jurisdictional considerations surrounding the Council's power were assessed, concluding that while the council had an obligation to provide notice, constitutionality did not preclude its ability to collect rates efficiently, especially in the context of essential public funding for municipal services.

[11] The court rejected various claims raised by the amicus, asserting that procedural inconsistencies concerning the relative monetary value of the properties and sale prices derived from the enforcement process signify substantive concerns rather than any existential defect regarding the ordinance's procedural fairness.

C – Conclusion

[12] The Constitutional Court concluded that the section 105 procedure is consistent with the Constitution, determining that it sufficiently guarantees individuals the opportunity to contest municipal actions affecting their property rights. In delivering its judgment, the Court highlighted that the procedural safeguards outlined within section 105 of the Ordinance, when interpreted in accordance with constitutional principles, fulfill the requirements of fairness.

[13] Consequently, the court dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court’s decision and emphasizing the importance of ensuring transparent and fair procedures remain in place for municipal rate collection. Furthermore, the court determined that due to the public significance of the matter, no costs order was imposed, a decision reflecting a broader commitment to equitable access to justice.

Final Order: The appeal is dismissed, and there is no order as to costs.

Concurrence: The judgment was concurred by Justices Chaskalson, Ackermann, Goldstone, Kriegler, Madala, Mokgoro, Ngcobo, Sachs, Madlanga, and Somyalo.
