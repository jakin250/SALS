Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 03 ### F – Facts The case involves John Tutton,

FIRAC Case Note: Tutton v The State (294/18) [2019] ZASCA 03

F – Facts

The case involves John Tutton, who, along with a co-accused, was convicted on 21 December 2007 by the Camperdown Regional Court. Tutton faced two charges under section 5(b) of the Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 140 of 1992: dealing in 8.1 tons of dagga and dealing in 150 kg of cocaine during specified periods in 2006 ([paras 2–3]). He was sentenced to 20 years’ imprisonment on each count, with 10 years running concurrently—resulting in an effective sentence of 30 years. Notably, the trial court imposed a non-parole period of 15 years under section 276B of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which restricts parole eligibility for prisoners ([para 3]).

Tutton initially appealed against his convictions and sentences, but the trial court dismissed the appeal. He later managed to secure leave to appeal regarding the sentences under section 309C of the Act ([para 3]). The full court of the KwaZulu-Natal Division modified his sentence, ordering 15 years of the cocaine count to run concurrently with the dagga count, resulting in a final sentence of 25 years. Of particular concern was the imposition of a non-parole period of 15 years ([para 4]).

Tutton appealed, arguing that he was not notified of the intention to impose a non-parole period during either court proceeding, denying him the opportunity to address the court on this critical aspect ([para 5]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue presented in this case revolves around whether the trial and full courts improperly imposed a non-parole period without adequately providing Tutton with notice and the opportunity to present oral arguments, potentially infringing upon his right to a fair trial as guaranteed by the Constitution ([paras 5, 9]). It also raised the question of whether such an imposition requires exceptional circumstances to justify it under section 276B of the Criminal Procedure Act ([paras 10, 11]).

R – Rules

The governing legal framework includes:

  1. Section 276B of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977: This section allows a court, when sentencing, to impose a non-parole period for offenders sentenced to imprisonment of two years or longer, but stipulates this should not be done lightly and typically requires a significant justification, aided by a proper hearing on the matter ([para 7]).
  2. Precedents:
  • In Jimmale & another v The State [2016] ZACC 27, it was established that non-parole orders should be made only when there are specific, egregious circumstances concerning parole decisions. Courts generally should defer to the expertise of the Department of Correctional Services for parole recommendations ([para 8]).
  • The ruling in S v Mhlongo 2016 (2) SACR 611 (SCA) reinforced the notion that a non-parole decision is part of the criminal trial, requiring appropriate notice and a hearing to ensure fairness ([para 9]).

A – Application

The court commenced with an analysis of the failure for the trial court to invite and hear oral argument regarding the imposition of the non-parole period, which is a necessity for procedural fairness ([para 9]). Zondi JA noted that the trial court committed a serious misdirection by not considering whether there were exceptional circumstances that warranted the non-parole order and for failing to hear submissions from both parties ([paras 10, 11]).

Furthermore, the court assessed the implications of referring the case back to the trial court for compliance with section 276B. Tutton had already served nearly 11 years of his sentence, and referral could incur additional costs and delays, emphasizing the need for expediency in resolving the matter rather than subjecting the appellant to an extended process ([para 11]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Tutton’s appeal, emphasizing the trial and full court’s failures in following the requisite procedural norms related to the imposition of a non-parole period. Consequently, the order fixing the non-parole period under section 276B was set aside ([para 12]). This decision illustrates the court's commitment to ensuring procedural fairness and the proper application of sentencing norms, highlighting the necessity for responsible judicial practice in parole-related decisions. The ruling underscores the principle that parties must be allowed to argue critically significant matters, such as non-parole periods, to promote the rights of the accused and uphold the integrity of the judicial process.
