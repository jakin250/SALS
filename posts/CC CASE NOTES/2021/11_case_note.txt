Source Folder: 2021

# Case Note: Clicks Retailers (Pty) Limited v Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service [2021] ZACC 11

### FIRAC Analysis

#### F – Facts
[1] This case revolves around the tax treatment of retail loyalty programmes, specifically concerning Clicks Retailers (Pty) Limited (Clicks) and its ClubCard loyalty programme. For the 2009 tax year, Clicks sought to claim an allowance under section 24C of the Income Tax Act 58 of 1962, which would allow it to defer taxation on income accrued through its loyalty programme. Section 24C permits the deferral of tax liability on income accrued that will be used to finance future expenditure, provided that such income and expenditure arise from the same contract. 

[2] Clicks operates mainly in the health and beauty sector, providing merchandise to customers who participate in its loyalty programme, earning points that can be redeemed for cash-back vouchers. The ClubCard itself is issued free of charge, creating a contractual relationship once a customer applies. The customer earns loyalty points based on their qualifying purchases, which in turn obligates Clicks to provide future discounts linked to the points earned. 

[3] For the tax year under consideration, Clicks reported R58,550,602 as gross income from its loyalty programme and sought an allowance of R44,275,965 under section 24C, indicating its obligation to finance future expenditure related to the cash-back vouchers. This claim was disallowed by the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (SARS), who argued that the income and obligation to finance future expenditure were derived from separate contracts: the ClubCard membership and the contract of sale for merchandise.

[4] The Tax Court initially ruled in favor of Clicks, asserting that both income and obligations arose from the same contractual arrangement. However, SARS appealed this decision to the Supreme Court of Appeal, which ruled against Clicks, stating that the income accruing and the obligations to finance future expenditure did not arise from the same contract but from different ones.

#### I – Issues
[5] The primary legal issue in this case is whether Clicks can claim an allowance under section 24C of the Income Tax Act for future expenditure in connection with its loyalty programme, given the delayed treatment of income and obligations originating from separate contracts. 

[6] A secondary issue is whether the relationships between Clicks’ loyalty programme contracts can satisfy the "sameness" requirement articulated in the court’s earlier pronouncement in Big G (SCA). Specifically, the case examines if the income-generating and obligation-imposing contracts can be considered "inextricably linked" sufficient to meet section 24C's requirements.

#### R – Rules
[7] The governing legislation is section 24C of the Income Tax Act, which allows for tax deferrals on income accrued if that income is tied to an obligation to incur future expenditure under the same contract. The relevant legal framework indicates that to qualify for the deduction, income must be earned from one contract, and the future expenditure must be a consequence of obligations also arising from that same contract ([para 2]).

[8] The Supreme Court of Appeal’s decision in Big G underscored the requirement for "sameness," determining that taxpayers could only defer amounts against future expenditure if both income earned and obligations to finance future expenditure arose from the same contractual agreement ([paras 17–18]).

#### A – Application
[9] In applying the legal standards to the facts, the Constitutional Court examined whether Clicks’ income from the sale of goods and its obligation to honor cash-back vouchers arose from the same contract. Clicks claimed that both obligations stemmed from a single contract that encompassed both the purchase transaction and its loyalty rewards system ([para 34]).

[10] The Constitutional Court noted that while the ClubCard contract was essential to the functioning of the loyalty programme, the actual income earned by Clicks was derived from the sale of goods which occurred under the contract of sale. The Court ultimately determined that these contractual arrangements, while interrelated, constituted separate agreements that did not meet the "sameness" requirement established in previous cases ([paras 46–49]). 

[11] Clicks’ argument that the two contracts were functionally linked and could be considered as “inextricably linked” for purposes of the allowance under section 24C was rejected. The Court held that despite their operational correlation, each contract remained independent; insofar as the sale contract generated income independently of the ClubCard contract, it did not satisfy the sameness requirement ([para 45]).

#### C – Conclusion
[12] The Constitutional Court concluded that Clicks could not legitimately claim a deduction under section 24C for the future expenditures anticipated from its loyalty programme. The key takeaway from the judgment is that for either a same-contract basis or a sameness basis to apply, the income-generating contract and the obligation-imposing contract need to be the same in a legal sense, which they were not in this case ([para 50]).

[13] Therefore, the appeal was dismissed, and Clicks would be required to pay SARS's costs, including the costs of two counsel, setting a clear precedent regarding the necessary legal nexus for tax treatment of loyalty programmes in the retail sector ([paras 51–52]). This ruling has significant implications not only for Clicks but extends to other retailers with similar loyalty schemes, highlighting the need for careful compliance with tax regulations governing loyalty programmes. 

---

Through this decision, the Court has provided clarity on the interpretation of section 24C, reinforcing the importance of contractual relationships in tax liability assessments and steering the discourse on how loyalty programmes should be treated under South African tax law.
