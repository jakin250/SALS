Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

# FIRAC Case Note: *Chonco v President of South Africa* (CCT 94/09)

## F – Facts

This case is a sequel to *Minister for Justice and Constitutional Development v Chonco and Others* (Chonco 1) [paras 1-2]. The applicants in this matter are Mr. Mqabukeni Chonco and 383 others, collectively seeking a declaration from the Constitutional Court concerning the President's alleged unreasonable delay in processing their applications for presidential pardon under section 84(2)(j) of the Constitution, a process that began in 2003. The applications had become the subject of scrutiny due to lengthy delays resulting from the President's failure to act despite numerous undertakings from the government to expedite the process.

During the hearing on 4 February 2010, the President submitted a supplementary affidavit indicating that decisions had been made regarding some applications; specifically, 230 out of 384 had been rejected. However, a decision on 146 applications remained pending due to an interdict from the High Court preventing the President from granting pardons under section 84(2)(j) [paras 4-5]. The core of the applicants' request was twofold: a declaration of unreasonable delay by the President and an order requiring the President to decide their applications within one month.

The court noted that the applicants’ legal representatives indicated they would not pursue the primary relief as it had been substantially met by the President’s actions [para 6]. What remained for determination was the issue of costs, as the applicants were not seeking punitive costs but ordinary costs against the President [paras 6-7].

## I – Issues

1. **Unreasonable Delay**: Did the President unreasonably delay in deciding the applications for pardon, in violation of constitutional obligations?
2. **Costs**: Should the applicants be awarded costs considering the context and conduct of the litigation between the applicants and the President [paras 1, 6]?

## R – Rules

The Court relied on several legal principles and precedents for determining the issues at hand:

1. **Pardoning Power**: The constitutional mandate which governs the President’s powers regarding pardons, namely, the necessity for these powers to be exercised rationally and to adhere to the Constitution [para 4].
   
2. **Judicial Discretion in Costs**: The award of costs is within the discretion of the Court, which must be exercised judiciously based on the conduct of the parties, the circumstances of the case, and the broader implications on constitutional justice [para 7]. Precedents such as *Biowatch Trust v Registrar, Genetic Resources* [2009] ZACC 14] were referenced to outline the Court's approach to costs in constitutional litigation [paras 7-8].

3. **Expectations for Communication**: There exists an expectation that parties shall communicate and seek to resolve issues before resorting to litigation, as outlined in the prevailing legal standards [para 9].

4. **Previous Delays and Costs**: The Court also considered its previous decision in Chonco 1, where a costs order had been issued against the government due to unreasonable delays in processing the pardon applications [para 10].

## A – Application

The Court's analysis began by assessing whether the President's conduct constituted an unreasonable delay in processing the pardon applications. It concluded that while there had been significant delay historically, the President's actions, particularly his submission of a supplementary affidavit, suggested a recognition of the need to address the applications following the decision in Chonco 1. The Court noted that the applicants failed to adequately communicate with the President after the judgment before pursuing litigation, failing to allow the President adequate time to respond to the judgment's implications [para 11].

Regarding costs, the court considered factors such as the conduct of the parties and the context of the litigation history. The applicants, despite having filed for judicial relief merely weeks after the court's prior decision, could have taken steps to inform the President of the pending applications or sought assurances regarding their processing. The expectation for further inquiries before escalating to litigation was emphasized; the applicants' swift action was deemed precipitate, especially in light of ongoing legal consultations regarding processing procedures by the President [paras 12-14].

Furthermore, the Court recognized that the previous determination in the Chonco 1 case already held the details of the delay accountable and that reiterating this through a new order would not be just or equitable, given the work that brought the issues to light had already garnered a public censure against the Presidency for similar delays [paras 15-16].

## C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Court ruled that it would not be just and equitable to grant the applicants costs in light of the circumstances surrounding the case, including their hasty approach to litigation and failure to communicate adequately with the President [paras 17-18]. The implications of this ruling underscore the importance of dialogue and consider the initiatives that parties should undertake prior to litigation, particularly in constitutional matters, where justice and expediency are of paramount concern.

The final order was straightforward: no costs would be awarded, reflecting the Court’s view that while the issues were acknowledged, the procedural conduct of the applicants invalidated their claim for costs in this instance [Order].

Overall, this case highlights the balancing act between upholding constitutional duties by the executive and the responsibilities of litigants in the pursuit of legal redress, emphasizing the collaborative nature of legal processes in a constitutional democracy.
