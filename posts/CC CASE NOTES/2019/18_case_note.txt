Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: Phaahla v Minister of Justice and Correctional Services and Another (Tlhakanye Intervening) [2019] ZACC 18

### FIRAC Method

## F – Facts

The applicant, **Oupa Chipane Phaahla**, an inmate sentenced to life imprisonment, challenged the constitutionality of certain provisions of the **Correctional Services Act** of 1998. Specifically, he contested sections **73(6)(b)(iv)** and **136(1)**, which related to parole eligibility. The applicant was sentenced on **5 October 2004**, just four days after the introduction of a new parole regime, which mandated that inmates sentenced to life imprisonment serve a minimum of **25 years** before becoming eligible for parole. Conversely, inmates sentenced before this date only had to serve **20 years**. Phaahla argued that this discrepancy, based on the date of sentencing rather than the date of the offense, violated his rights under **section 9(1)** (equality) and **section 35(3)(n)** (right to the least severe punishment) of the **Constitution**.

The **Gauteng Division of the High Court** ruled that while **section 35(3)(n)** did not apply, the provisions discriminated against the applicant in violation of equality rights, as it arbitrarily imposed a longer non-parole period based on the date of sentence rather than the date of commission. The respondents, including the Minister of Justice and Correctional Services, sought leave to appeal, though their appeal was subsequently struck off the roll. 

The applicant then approached the **Constitutional Court** for confirmation of the High Court’s declaration of invalidity. **Makome Stefanas Tlhakanye**, another inmate serving a life sentence, intervened in support of Phaahla, illustrating the broader impact of the impugned sections.

## I – Issues

The core legal questions addressed in this matter were:
1. Whether the provisions of sections **73(6)(b)(iv)** and **136(1)** of the **Correctional Services Act** are unconstitutional due to their differential treatment based on the date of sentencing.
2. Whether this differential treatment infringes on the applicant’s rights to equality and fair trial under sections **9(1), (3)** and **35(3)(n)** of the **Constitution**.

## R – Rules

The Court relied on several key constitutional provisions and case law:
1. **Section 9(1)** – Guarantees equality before the law and equal protection regardless of status.
2. **Section 9(3)** – Prohibits unfair discrimination on various grounds, even if not listed explicitly.
3. **Section 35(3)(n)** – Entitles accused persons to the least severe punishment if the prescribed punishment changes between the commission of an offense and sentencing.
4. **Harksen v Lane N.O.** [1997] ZACC 12; 1998 (1) SA 300 (CC) – Established the framework for determining whether a differentiation constituted unfair discrimination.
5. **Van Vuren v Minister of Correctional Services** [2010] ZACC 17, which discussed the non-retroactivity principle within correctional law.

## A – Application

The Court conducted a thorough examination of the implications of differentiating inmates based solely on the date of sentencing. It noted that applying a non-parole period based on an arbitrary date did not serve a legitimate governmental purpose. The distinction created retroactive effects that unfairly impacted those sentenced after the new legislation came into effect.

In its analysis, the majority judgment by **Dlodlo AJ** articulated that parole eligibility is integrally linked to punishment and cannot be treated as a mere administrative convenience divorced from the principles of justice and equality. By focusing on the date of sentencing rather than the date of the offense, the provisions unconstitutionally imposed extended penalties on some inmates while favoring others for no justifiable reason.

The **High Court’s** conclusion that the applicant's right to equality under section **9(1)** was violated was affirmed. This ruling emphasized that all individuals in similar positions should receive equal treatment under the law, thereby confirming the arbitrariness of the distinction drawn by the Act.

## C – Conclusion

The **Constitutional Court** confirmed the order of the High Court, declaring sections **73(6)(b)(iv)** and **136(1)** of the **Correctional Services Act** unconstitutional for violating the rights to equality and fair trial under sections **9(1)**, **9(3)**, and **35(3)(n)** of the **Constitution**. These provisions were found to unjustifiably discriminate against inmates based on the date of sentencing, creating a fundamental inequality within the correctional system.

This decision reinforces the principles of fairness and equality within the context of penal legislation and mandates that future parole eligibility provisions must relate to the date of the offense rather than the date when the sentence was imposed. Parliament was given a deadline to amend the legislation accordingly within **24 months**, illustrating the court’s commitment to an equitable justice system. By holding the Minister responsible for costs, the Court also signaled the importance of upholding constitutional obligations within the framework of corrections and sentencing policies.
