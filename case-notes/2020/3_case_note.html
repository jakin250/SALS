FIRAC Case Note: MEC for Health, Western Cape v Coetzee and Others [2020] ZACC 3

Constitutional Court 2020 [2020] ZACC 3 ### F – Facts The case originates from a dispu

FIRAC Case Note: MEC for Health, Western Cape v Coetzee and Others [2020] ZACC 3

F – Facts

The case originates from a dispute concerning the entitlement of health professionals (the respondents), specifically the first to fiftieth respondents employed by the University of Cape Town (UCT) and Stellenbosch University, to a scarce skills allowance under agreements negotiated within the Public Health and Welfare Sector Bargaining Council ([1–8]).

The first agreements related to teaching hospitals, established in the 1960s, where UCT and Stellenbosch University provided staff (Joint Staff) to hospitals managed by the Department of Health, Western Cape ([3–6]). Under a 2004 collective agreement, it was agreed to provide a scarce skills allowance to retain qualified staff. This allowance was meant to prevent the loss of highly skilled professionals to private healthcare sectors ([8–9]).

The respondents claimed deprivation of the scarce skills allowance, leading to multiple litigations, including proceedings initially at the Bargaining Council, subsequently the Labour Court and Labour Appeal Court, where their entitlement to the allowance was established ([20–25]). Throughout the disputes, the applicant maintained they were not employees of the state, arguing their employment affiliations were with the universities and thereby beyond the jurisdiction of the Bargaining Council ([26–28]).

I – Issues

The principal legal issues before the Constitutional Court included:

  1. Whether the respondents were entitled to the scarce skills allowance as per the collective agreement.
  2. Whether the respondents qualified as employees within the public service under legislative definitions, thus falling within the jurisdiction of the Bargaining Council.
  3. Whether the Labour Appeal Court erred in its legal interpretations, specifically regarding sections 28, 35, and 213 of the Labour Relations Act (LRA) and applicable provisions of the Public Service Act (PSA) ([29–36]).

R – Rules

The governing legal rules stem from the following primary sources:

  1. Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 (LRA): Particularly sections related to the definitions of 'public service' ([11–16]), the establishment and jurisdiction of bargaining councils, and the binding nature of collective agreements ([8]).
  2. Public Service Act 103 of 1994 (PSA): Defines 'public service' and the conditions of employee engagement within the public sector ([17–19]).
  3. Constitution of South Africa: Specifically section 23, which protects the rights of employees, providing a framework for fair labour practices ([36]).

A – Application

The Court examined the legal definitions painstakingly, noting that the Labour Appeal Court had affirmed the respondents' status as employees under the LRA due to the comprehensive definitions of ‘employee’ and ‘public service’ ([28]). The interpretation contended in the appeal highlighted the intertwined nature of their employment with the universities and their engagement in the public healthcare system through the hospitals.

The judgment analysed whether the findings of the previous courts reflected a reasonable application of the law, particularly considering the statutory definitions and contractual obligations stemming from both the teaching agreements and the scarce skills allowance agreement ([50–59]). The Court emphasized the purpose of the scarce skills allowance, arguing it was established specifically to incentivize skilled professionals in public health, noting that the language of the agreement explicitly included personnel with such qualifications.

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court, unanimously led by Mathopo AJ, concluded that the claims of the respondents had merit. Due to the intertwining nature of their employment characterized under the established agreements and the legislative framework governing public healthcare, the decision from the Labour Appeal Court was upheld.

The application for leave to appeal was dismissed on the grounds that it lacked reasonable prospects for success. Consequently, the applicant, the Member of the Executive Council for Health, Western Cape, was ordered to cover the costs of the respondents, affirming the necessity for clarity and adherence to legislative definitions in employment contracts within the public health sector ([60–61]).

This ruling significantly impacts the relationship between healthcare employees and public health governance in South Africa, ensuring that employment benefits negotiated within the framework of public institutions extend to all relevant categories of employees involved in the provision of essential health services.
