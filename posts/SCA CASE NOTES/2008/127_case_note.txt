# FIRAC Case Note: Affirmative Portfolios CC v Transnet Limited

**Case Title:** Affirmative Portfolios CC v Transnet Limited  
**Neutral Citation:** [2008] ZASCA 127  
**Court:** The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa   
**Case No:** 473/2007  
**Date Delivered:** 30 September 2008  
**Judges:** Harms ADP, Cameron, Jafta, Maya JJA, Boruchowitz AJA  

## F – Facts

The appellant, Affirmative Portfolios CC, instituted action against the respondent, Transnet Limited t/a Metrorail, in the Durban High Court, claiming R883,660.87 for underpayment in providing services as per a contract ([1]–[3]). The contract was finalized in March 2000, following a tender process initiated by the respondent for the supply of access controllers. While a written agreement established a fixed monthly payment based on an hourly rate of R15 plus a 15% administration fee, the appellant later increased this rate to R17.25 per hour, alleging that the respondent's manager had approved the change ([4]–[5]).

Between April and September 2000, the appellant charged Transnet the higher rate without written consent, after which the respondent demanded adherence to the original contract rate ([7]–[8]). Subsequently, in April 2002, Transnet purported to terminate the contract, which the appellant treated as a repudiation, leading to the present legal proceedings ([8]).

The respondent counterclaimed R515,317.45 on the grounds of unjustified enrichment, asserting that payments made were based on an erroneous rate charged by the appellant, which was not authorized under the original contract ([23]).

## I – Issues

The case posed several legal questions:
1. Was the alleged oral amendment to the initial written agreement concerning payment terms enforceable under the parol evidence rule? ([9]–[10])
2. Did the appellant's claim for increased remuneration lack evidential support as required due to the absence of consent or appropriate amendment to the initial written agreement? ([11]–[12])
3. Was the respondent's counterclaim valid under the condictio indebiti, particularly regarding the requirement of excusability of the error made when payments were processed? ([24]–[32])

## R – Rules

The legal backdrop for the case involved several principles:
1. **Parol Evidence Rule:** This rule stipulates that once parties have put their agreement in writing, prior oral agreements cannot vary the terms of the written contract unless there is clear evidence that the written document was not intended to constitute the entire contract ([13]–[15]).
2. **Unjust Enrichment**: Under South African law, a party may reclaim money paid by mistake (condictio indebiti) if it can be shown that the payment was made under the erroneous belief that a debt was due. The mistake must typically be excusable for recovery to be granted ([24]–[30]).
3. **Non-Variation Clause**: Contracts often include clauses that specify that variations must be made in writing and agreed upon by both parties, which can limit the ability to go beyond the terms listed ([18]).

## A – Application

### Appellant's Claim
The appeal hinged on whether the appellant could establish that an oral variation to the contract existed. The court found that there was no evidence supporting the claim that a valid oral agreement had been made to modify the written terms. Testimonies from the appellant's witnesses were found insufficient, lacking affirmation of an agreed deletion of clause 10.1 or any agreement on an increased rate ([11]–[12]). The robust application of the parol evidence rule ultimately barred reliance on alleged prior oral discussions leading to revisions of the contract price, as the written agreement intended to be the complete account of the parties' obligations ([13]).

### Respondent's Counterclaim
In terms of the counterclaim, the court evaluated whether the respondent's erroneous overpayments qualified for recovery. The court observed that although the respondent’s oversight constituted negligence, the standard for excusability of the error was not met. This was based on historic legal precedent emphasizing the need for the claimant’s conduct not to be grossly negligent or devoid of basic diligence, which the court found applicable in this instance. Consequently, the respondent's officials were deemed grossly negligent in their oversight of payment calculations, which strongly indicated an inexcusable mistake ([35]–[37]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal found in favor of the respondent on the counterclaim due to the failure to meet the excusability requirement. However, they dismissed the appellant’s claim for increased remuneration because there was no evidence supporting the alleged oral variation to the payment terms; thus, the written agreement remained binding ([39]).

The final order reflected:
1. The dismissal of the appellant's claim for R883,660.87.
2. The dismissal of the respondent's counterclaim for R515,317.45.
3. Each party bearing their own costs at trial, with the appellant recovering costs for the appeal ([40]).

This case illustrates the crucial importance of strictly adhering to the written terms of contracts and the implications of the parol evidence rule within South African contract law, alongside the nuanced understanding needed regarding claims of unjustified enrichment under conditions of mistaken belief about contractual obligations.
