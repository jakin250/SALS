# FIRAC Case Note: A M v H M [2020] ZACC 9 

## F - Facts
The case A M v H M [(2020) ZACC 9] involves an application for leave to appeal concerning the validity of a postnuptial agreement entered between the applicant, A M, and the respondent, H M, during their marriage. The couple was married out of community of property with the exclusion of accrual on 28 August 1993, formalized through a duly registered antenuptial contract under the Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984 ([paras 1–2]).

In November 2014, the applicant presented a drafted postnuptial agreement that required the respondent to set aside the antenuptial contract, giving the applicant entitlement to half of his estate and maintenance. The respondent initially resisted signing it, stating that the terms were unreasonable due to the applicant's independent financial status. After a dispute arose, the applicant asserted that the agreement was signed on 10 November 2014 without hesitation from the respondent, while the respondent alleged that he was coerced into signing the document under threats from the applicant ([paras 3–5]).

Following the signing of this agreement, the couple resumed their marital relationship until late November 2014, when an extramarital affair by the respondent was disclosed, prompting discussions of divorce, which had not been previously mentioned ([paras 6–9]).

The litigation began after the respondent filed for divorce, leading to contradictory findings between the Regional Court, which deemed the agreement unenforceable, and the High Court, which found it enforceable. The Supreme Court of Appeal later reinstated the Regional Court's judgment, asserting that the applicant had failed to demonstrate that the agreement was made in contemplation of divorce ([paras 12–16]).

## I - Issues
The primary legal questions raised in the case include:
1. Whether the matter engages the Constitutional Court’s jurisdiction, particularly regarding potential constitutional issues or points of law of general public importance.
2. Whether it is in the interests of justice to grant leave to appeal ([para 18]).

Additionally, the case ultimately explores the interpretation of section 21(1) of the Matrimonial Property Act and its implications for the validity and enforceability of contracts concluded by spouses not in contemplation of divorce ([paras 20–21]).

## R - Rules
The rules that guide this case primarily stem from the Matrimonial Property Act 88 of 1984, particularly section 21(1), which stipulates that spouses must jointly apply to a court for any change to their matrimonial property regime, prohibiting unilateral changes or agreements that do not undergo court sanction ([para 1]). 

Additionally, established principles from precedent cases, such as *Eke v Parsons* [2015] ZACC 30, clarify the conditions under which agreements may be enforceable, particularly in relation to consent and modifications in the context of matrimonial law ([para 15]). The Supreme Court of Appeal’s ruling that agreements between spouses must relate to existing legal issues further contextualizes the rules applicable in assessing the enforceability of the postnuptial agreement ([para 10]).

## A - Application
The court unanimously applied these rules and principles to determine that the applicant’s claims to enforce the agreement hinged upon whether it was made in contemplation of divorce as required by section 7(1) of the Divorce Act 70 of 1979. The Supreme Court of Appeal found that the parties had not contemplated divorce when the agreement was made, hence rendering the agreement unenforceable under section 21(1) of the MPA ([paras 10–16]).

In the Constitutional Court, the applicant's assertion of constitutional issues relating to contractual freedom and dignity was assessed against the backdrop of the legal findings from the lower courts. The court found that the applicant’s arguments introducing potential constitutional infringements were novel, as they had not been raised before the lower courts, effectively attempting to change the legal landscape of the case at a higher appellate level without prior adjudication ([para 28]).

Moreover, the interests of justice were determined not to favor the applicant, primarily due to the procedural ramifications of introducing new issues at such a late stage in the litigation process, undermining the principles of fair trial and justice ([para 32–36]).

## C - Conclusion
In conclusion, the Constitutional Court dismissed the application for leave to appeal, reaffirming the findings of the Supreme Court of Appeal and the Regional Court. The Court's ruling underscored the significance of prior court authorization for changes to matrimonial property regimes and the necessity for proper legal basis in enforcing binding agreements. The ratio decidendi established the untenability of postnuptial agreements not executed in court-sanctioned contemplation of divorce, reinforcing statutory interpretations aimed at safeguarding contractual integrity in matrimonial contexts ([paras 39–40]).

The implications of this ruling are profound, as it delineates the boundaries regarding the enforceability of spousal agreements within the South African matrimonial regime framework, thereby shaping future disputes and agreements impacting marital property rights in line with the MPA ([para 39]). This case emphasizes the critical intersection between constitutional rights, contract law, and family law within the South African legal landscape.
