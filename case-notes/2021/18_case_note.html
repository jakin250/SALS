Secretary of the Judicial Commission of Inquiry into Allegations of State Capture, Corruption and Fraud in the Public Sector Including Organs of State v Zuma and Others [2021] ZACC 18

Constitutional Court 2021 [2021] ZACC 18

F – Facts

The case stems from the refusal of Jacob Gedleyihlekisa Zuma, the first respondent and former President of South Africa, to comply with a prior court order issued by the Constitutional Court. The original order, made on January 28, 2021, mandated Zuma to appear before the Judicial Commission of Inquiry into Allegations of State Capture and comply with its directives ([2]). Following his failure to attend the Commission or submit affidavits despite being served notice at both his residences, the Secretary of the Commission sought urgent relief from the Constitutional Court declaring Zuma in contempt of court and requesting a two-year imprisonment sentence ([1]-[3],[5]).

Mr. Zuma had publicly declared his defiance, asserting victimisation and not complying with the order. Consequently, this led to contempt proceedings in February 2021, where the Secretary argued that his deliberate disobedience posed a threat to the rule of law ([4]-[6],[10]). The case also drew significant public interest, necessitating inquiries into the judiciary's integrity and authority amid an open display of contempt ([1],[8]).

The Helen Suzman Foundation participated as amicus curiae, suggesting alternative punitive measures to ensure compliance with judicial mandates ([15]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions involved in this case encompassed:

  1. Whether Jacob Zuma was guilty of contempt of court for failing to adhere to the Constitutional Court's order.
  2. The validity of issuing a punitive order for unsuspended committal against Zuma in civil contempt proceedings, especially given the transition from civil to punitive contexts ([2],[9],[12]).
  3. The appropriateness of the measures taken by the Commission to compel compliance with the earlier court order versus criminal proceedings through the Director of Public Prosecutions ([52],[253]).

R – Rules

The legal framework governing contempt of court in South Africa includes principles established in cases such as:

  • Fakie N.O. v CCII Systems (Pty) Ltd [2006] ZASCA 52, which outlines that a litigant claiming contempt must demonstrate that there was an order against the alleged contemnor, that they had knowledge of it, and that there was a failure to comply specifically attributed to willfulness or bad faith ([34]).
  • Pheko II [2015] ZACC 10, which reiterated that contempt proceedings serve both to vindicate judicial authority and enforce compliance with court orders ([27]).
  • The Constitution of South Africa, particularly sections 165 (judicial authority) and 12 (rights related to freedom and security of the person), which underscore the independence and authority of the judiciary and the necessity for lawful procedures before limiting individual freedoms ([12],[42],[46],[52]).

The foundational tenets of civil contempt law maintain that such proceedings usually aim to correct non-compliance by coercive measures rather than purely punitive measures, as confirmed in numerous precedents ([28],[54],[84]).

A – Application

The Court, presided over by Khampepe ADCJ, determined that Zuma knowingly and deliberately failed to comply with the previous court order ([36],[38],[40]). In light of his sustained public defiance and with no substantial evidence presented to negate the allegations of contempt, the Court found him guilty as charged. This finding was bolstered by a presumption of mala fides due to his non-compliance.

The Court then debated the appropriateness of punishment. It considered the direct role of the judiciary in upholding the rule of law, which necessitated a firm response to his contemptuous actions. In finding that traditional coercive measures would be ineffective given Zuma's established disdain for the court's authority, the majority opinion moved to impose an unsuspended sentence of 15 months ([46]).

The application of punitive costs was also discussed, with the Court highlighting the necessity to send a clear message that contempt would not be tolerated, especially from a former head of state, as his conduct risked undermining trust in judicial institutions ([52],[114],[136]).

Theron J, the minority opinion, cautioned against the purely punitive nature of the sentence, emphasizing that civil contempt is traditionally coupled with a coercive element designed to compel compliance. Theron J expressed concern that the punitive committal order bypassed fundamental rights guaranteed under the Constitution ([143]-[265], [266]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately held that Zuma was guilty of contempt of court, sentencing him to 15 months' imprisonment. This decision not only signaled a robust defense of judicial authority but also underscored the court's responsibility to uphold the integrity of the Constitution and the legal system ([141],[142]).

The dissenting opinion emphasized that, while Zuma's conduct warranted censure, the constitutional implications of punishing an individual through civil contempt without a necessary coercive aim could set a concerning precedent for future proceedings. The divide between the views presented showcases critical tensions within South African constitutional law, particularly with respect to the balance between upholding the rule of law and ensuring fair trial rights ([143]-[217]).

In summary, this case illustrates the complexities surrounding civil contempt proceedings and the broader implications for judicial authority and individual rights within South Africa's legal landscape. The nature of this judgment extends beyond merely addressing Zuma's actions, reflecting ongoing discussions concerning the rights of parties in contempt contexts and the potential for reform in procedural mechanisms for contempt.
