Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: Minister of Home Affairs v The Public Protector (308/2017) [2018] ZASCA 15

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts ([paras 1-6])

In the case at hand, Mr. Reginald Ananius Marimi, the second respondent in the court below, was employed by the Department of Home Affairs, stationed in Cuba. Following complaints regarding his conduct by the Cuban government, he was recalled to South Africa, resulting in the cessation of his cost of living allowance (COLA) and an impending disciplinary process that ultimately did not occur ([paras 1-2]). 

Marimi lodged a complaint with the Public Protector, alleging maladministration by the Department concerning his recall. The Public Protector conducted an investigation and issued a preliminary report, followed by a final report, which determined that the Department was guilty of maladministration and directed it to undertake specific remedial actions ([para 4]). 

The Minister of Home Affairs and the Director-General of the Department subsequently filed for review of this report in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, which was dismissed with costs ([para 3]). They appealed the dismissal, and while the legal contours of the Public Protector's authority had not been explicitly determined, recent judicial pronouncements suggested that her decisions could not be ignored and required compliance unless set aside via review ([para 5]).

### I – Issues ([paras 6-8])

The legal questions before the Supreme Court of Appeal included:
1. Whether the Public Protector's findings and remedial actions constituted administrative actions subject to review under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) or were subject to review under the principle of legality.
2. Was there a ground for review established regarding (a) the nature of the complaint not being made on oath, and (b) the alleged failure of Marimi to exhaust internal remedies before lodging his complaint with the Public Protector?
3. Whether the remedial action ordered by the Public Protector regarding Marimi’s COLA was legally justifiable ([paras 4, 27]).

### R – Rules ([paras 15-21, 27-29])

The constitutional powers of the Public Protector are defined in sections 181 and 182 of the South African Constitution. These sections establish the independence of Chapter 9 institutions and outline their roles in safeguarding democracy by investigating maladministration ([paras 15-16]). The Public Protector Act 23 of 1994 supplements these powers, delineating the scope of the Public Protector’s investigations and setting procedural safeguards for lodging complaints ([paras 21-24]).

Regarding the review processes, both PAJA and the principle of legality derive from the rule of law. PAJA permits review of administrative actions, defined as decisions taken by organs of state that adversely affect rights but does not include actions that fall under the powers delineated to non-administrative authorities like the Public Protector ([paras 27-29]).

### A – Application ([paras 40-56])

Upon analysis, the court determined that the Public Protector’s powers are not administrative. The Office operates independently and is mandated to investigate maladministration rather than administer functions in a bureaucratic sense. The decision-making processes of the Public Protector therefore do not fall within the ambit of administrative actions subject to PAJA review ([para 36]).

On the first issue regarding the procedural legitimacy of Marimi's complaint not being made under oath, the court concluded that the Public Protector exercised her discretion appropriately, choosing not to require an oath for the nature of the complaint presented. This flexibility is permissible under the Public Protector Act, which allows for informal procedures to enhance accessibility ([paras 40-42]).

Regarding the claim that Marimi failed to exhaust internal remedies, the court sided with the Public Protector, emphasizing her jurisdiction was not ousted simply because Marimi was an employee within the state. The Supreme Court confirmed that the relevant acts did not preclude her investigation and that the Public Protector's discretion in determining the merits of the complaint must be respected ([paras 43-47]).

The allegation concerning the COLA payment was deemed unsubstantiated. The appellants failed to provide a legal foundation contesting the Public Protector's understanding of the COLA provisions, nor did they identify any actionable error. Thus, the Public Protector’s decision to require payment was upheld ([paras 48-55]).

### C – Conclusion ([paras 56-57])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming the decision of the High Court. It adjudicated that the Public Protector’s functions were fundamentally guided by the principle of legality rather than administrative law, leading to the conclusion that no grounds for review were established. The judgment reinforces the independence of the Public Protector in exercising investigatory powers and affirms the judicial interpretation that her directives cannot simply be disregarded by state organs. The appeal was followed by an order for the appellants to bear the costs, including the costs for two counsel ([paras 56-57]).

In conclusion, this ruling clarifies the scope of the Public Protector’s authority in South Africa, asserting the importance of her role as an autonomous entity committed to maintaining ethical governance and accountability in public administration.
