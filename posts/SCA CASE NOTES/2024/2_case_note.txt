# Case Note: BP Southern Africa (Pty) Ltd v Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (Case No 801/2022) [2024] ZASCA 2

### F – Facts

The case involves BP Southern Africa (Pty) Ltd (BP), which sought to appeal a decision made by the full court of the Gauteng Division of the High Court regarding interim interdicts against the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (SARS) ([paras 1-3]). 

BP launched two urgent applications for interim interdicts to prevent SARS from enforcing a demand for payment from BP, which consisted of a certified statement filed under section 114(1)(a)(ii) of the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 (the Act) on 16 March 2020, totaling R49,978,544.06. BP requested the interdicts while pending review applications under Part B ([paras 2-3]). Mothle J dismissed both applications ([para 17]).

The litigation history further elaborates on BP's attempts to file a supplementary founding affidavit after being denied the interdicts, which was likewise dismissed by Munzhelele AJ, leading to BP's application for special leave to appeal, subsequently denied by the full court ([paras 4-5]).

### I – Issues

The central issues in this case revolved around:

1. Whether the high court implicitly refused BP's request for a truncation of the time period under section 96 of the Act.
2. Whether BP established a proper case for an interim interdict.
3. The appealability of Munzhelele AJ's ruling, specifically regarding the supplementary founding affidavit ([para 22]).

### R – Rules

The applicable legal frameworks include:

- **Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964**: This Act governs the duties and obligations of both SARS and taxable entities like BP ([paras 6-8]).
- **Section 96 of the Act**: This section, permitting time extensions for legal actions against SARS, is crucial in determining BP's right to challenge the demand ([paras 15-16]).
- **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000**: Enshrining the right to fair administrative action, the Act serves as a basis for BP's claims regarding procedural fairness ([para 28]).
- **Requirements for an interim interdict**: Restated in *SA Informal Traders Forum v City of Johannesburg*, they involve prima facie rights, irreparable harm, balance of convenience, and absence of alternative remedies ([para 27]).

### A – Application

**1. Section 96 Application**: 
The court deduced that Mothle J's engagement with the merits implied an implicit granting of BP’s request under section 96. The refusal of BP’s request was not seen as restricting its ability to appeal ([paras 26-27]).

**2. Interim Interdicts**: 
BP contended it had a prima facie case based on its claim for fair administrative action under the Constitution ([para 28]). The court scrutinized SARS's actions in issuing the certified statement and found BP failed to show that SARS acted unlawfully, as SARS was permitted to file the certification after BP became aware of the debts ([para 33]). BP's claims of irreparable harm were countered by its financial capacity and prior income, undermining its assertion that the balance of convenience favored granting the interdicts ([paras 41-42]).

**3. Supplementary Founding Affidavit**: 
The ruling regarding the supplementary affactory lacked finality as it did not dispose of substantive rights in the main proceedings, and thus was deemed non-appealable ([paras 46-48]). BP's reasoning that the delay in filing the record justified its action did not hold merit, as the proper course would have been to compel SARS to file ([para 48]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed BP's application for special leave to appeal against the ruling of the full court of the Gauteng Division. The court reaffirmed the findings of the lower courts on the irrelevance of BP’s claims against SARS, highlighting the procedural correctness and discretionary powers of the high court in their determinations ([paras 50]). 

This case underscores the rigorous standards necessary for obtaining interim interdicts in tax-related disputes and affirms the necessity for parties to comply meticulously with statutory deadlines and procedures to maintain their rights effectively. The judgment illustrates the balance of power between tax authorities and entities, elucidating the implications of the administrative process in tax law. The court's position reinforces the discourse on administrative accountability and the rule of law, presenting critical insights into compliance and the challenges of contesting tax claims in South Africa.

By affirming the dismissals of BP's claims, the judgment serves as a restatement of the secure position that SARS holds under the Customs and Excise Act while navigating judicial reviews and keeping high standards for procedural fairness.
