Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note: Van Zyl & Another v Off the Shelf Investments Seventy Eight (Pty) Ltd (1323/2018) [2019] ZASCA 175

## F – Facts

The appellants, Thomas Christopher Van Zyl and Laila Enver Motala, served as the liquidators of Cinmark Twelve (Pty) Ltd (Cinmark), which sought the provisional liquidation of the respondent, Off the Shelf Investments Seventy Eight (Pty) Ltd ([1]). The events leading to the appeal unfolded in the Western Cape Division of the High Court, where an application for provisional liquidation was dismissed by Langa AJ, who also refused leave to appeal ([1]). The core issue revolved around whether the respondent was indebted to Cinmark based on the latter’s financial statements ([1]).

Both parties were subsidiaries of Skipness Société Anonyme, a Luxembourg company ([2]). Cinmark was set up to make and sell red wine, leveraging incentives offered by the South African Department of Trade and Industry ([4]). The respondent owned a wine farm, and its financial statements for the year ending 31 December 2015 included a claim of a long-term liability of R11,907,092 owed to Cinmark ([5]). This amount represented funds expended on constructing a wine cellar, classified as an ‘investment property’ in the respondent's financial statements.

Despite being formally recognized in the financial documentation, the respondent disputed the accuracy of these records, claiming they were merely an accounting fiction ([8]). A letter of demand based on the owed amount was served to the respondent, which failed to make the payment ([7]).

## I – Issues

The primary legal issue posed before the court was whether the appellants established the requisite proof that the respondent was indebted to Cinmark and whether this debt warranted a provisional liquidation order under South African company law ([1]). Further, questions arose regarding the authenticity of the financial statements that indicated the liability of the respondent to Cinmark ([10]).

## R – Rules

The pertinent legal framework includes provisions from the Companies Act 61 of 1973, particularly sections relating to winding-up procedures and the definitions of solvency ([6]). The court referenced earlier case law, notably *Kalil v Decotex (Pty) Ltd & another* [1988] 2 All SA 159 (A) to address standards for prima facie proof required for provisional liquidation applications in the presence of disputes ([9]). Additionally, the court highlighted that conflicting assertions must be analyzed on a balance of probabilities, especially when fundamental disputes arise ([9]).

## A – Application

The court thoroughly assessed the financial documents provided, ruling that Cinmark indeed incurred R11,907,092 for the development of the wine cellar, verified through a separate contract with the DTI to secure investment incentives based on real expenditures ([10]). Mr. Dauriac, the director of both Cinmark and the respondent, contended that the financial statement entries were erroneous and not reflective of actual transactions; however, the court found this argument unpersuasive ([8][15]).

The judgment noted that the financial statements, certified by the respondent's directors, represented an accurate portrayal of the respondent's financial obligations to Cinmark ([16]). The judge refuted the respondent's assertion that the liability was merely a book entry and illustrated the statutory obligations arising from corporate governance ([22]). The potential restructuring arguments advanced by Mr. Dauriac regarding the loan repayments were dismissed as speculative and unsupported by factual evidence, lacking any written agreements to substantiate claims of a ‘netting off’ of liabilities ([19][21]).

By demonstrating that the respondent did, in fact, owe the amount to Cinmark based on accurate financial representations, the court established sufficient grounds to classify the respondent as unable to repay its debts. The ruling referenced prior decisions which necessitated that creditors could seek provisional liquidation when debts are owed and unpaid in these circumstances ([6][9]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appellants had fulfilled their burden of proof demonstrating that the respondent was indebted to Cinmark in the amount of R11,907,092, paving the way for the appeal’s success ([17]). The court ordered the provisional winding-up of the respondent, setting a rule nisi for further hearings on the finalization of the liquidation ([24]). The decision affirmed principles of corporate accountability and reiterated the significance of ensuring accurate representations in companies’ financial disclosures.

This ruling elucidates the court's adherence to rigorous proof standards in insolvency proceedings, reinforcing the legal imperative that companies must maintain transparent financial relationships to uphold statutory compliance ([23]). An important aspect of the decision lies in its reiteration of responsibilities incumbent upon directors to ensure that financial statements accurately represent both liabilities and the company’s fiscal health for creditor protection and corporate governance integrity.
