The Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service v JM Da Encarnacao N.O. (543/2017) [2018] ZASCA 71

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA 71

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case concerns a dispute over the interpretation and application of Rebate item 412.09 in Schedule 4/Part 1 of the Customs and Excise Act 91 of 1964 (the Act). The facts of the case are as follows:

On August 15, 2009, two consignments of Remington Gold cigarettes, imported from Zimbabwe, were stolen during an armed robbery at a customs and excise warehouse ([1]). Following this incident, on August 26, 2009, the South African Revenue Service (SARS) notified the DA Encarnacao Trust (the trust) of its liability for unpaid customs duties and Value Added Tax (VAT) amounting to R910,171.42 related to these consignments ([2]).

The trust contested this liability, arguing it was entitled to a full rebate of the duty and VAT under Rebate item 412.09 due to the circumstances surrounding the theft ([3]). Subsequently, the trust filed an application in the Gauteng Division of the High Court to compel SARS to return R58,877.52, which SARS had withdrawn from the trust’s account, along with a declaratory order regarding their entitlement to the rebate ([4]).

The court a quo ruled in favor of the trust, determining that the robbery constituted a vis major event, allowing for the rebate claim ([5]–[6]). On appeal, SARS contended that, based on the established facts, the trust was not entitled to a rebate, centering the dispute on the meaning of the phrase "such goods did not enter into consumption" found in Rebate item 412.09 ([7]).

I – Issues

The main legal issue at hand is the interpretation of Rebate item 412.09 within the context of the armed robbery of the consignments. Specifically, the court was tasked with determining whether the phrase "such goods did not enter into consumption" excludes goods that were stolen and could potentially be consumed in the market ([6], [10]).

R – Rules

The relevant rules and legal principles are derived primarily from the provisions within the Act and specific previous judicial interpretations:

  1. Rebate Item 412.09: This section outlines the conditions under which goods can qualify for a rebate of customs duty. It stipulates that for the rebate to apply, the goods must (i) not be less than R2,500; (ii) be proven to have been lost, destroyed, or damaged; (iii) be lost due to vis major; (iv) and numerous other conditions relating to the control and consumption of the goods ([8], [9]).
  2. Vis Major: Under South African law, vis major can include acts of nature and human acts, including robbery. The court referenced these principles to interpret whether the armed robbery constituted an exceptional circumstance allowing for a rebate claim ([15]).
  3. Section 45 of the Act: This section discusses the duties of imported goods upon their entry for home consumption, emphasizing that goods removed or delivered without proper entry are still liable for duties, thus linking to the concept of goods entering consumption ([11]–[12]).

A – Application

In its judgment, the court analyzed the meaning of "did not enter into consumption" by considering the circumstances surrounding the robbery. The court noted that, following the theft, both SARS and the trust lost control over the goods, which were never recovered ([16]). The absence of evidence pertaining to the goods' fate post-theft meant the trust could not demonstrate that the goods had been consumed, thus satisfying the requirements for the rebate claim.

The court dismissed SARS’s argument that entering the market via robbery constituted consumption. The interpretation of s 45(1)(b) was expanded to ensure clarity that its provisions do not exclude cases where goods are irretrievably lost due to an act like robbery, thus supporting the notion that it would be unreasonable for a claimant to be ineligible for a rebate under such circumstances ([18]).

Importantly, the court reinforced the idea that the interpretation allowing for a rebate aligns with the Act’s intention to fairly address conditions beyond the control of the importer ([17]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal by SARS, affirming that the DA Encarnacao Trust was indeed entitled to a rebate under the provisions of Rebate item 412.09 following the armed robbery ([19]). The ruling confirms the principle that goods lost to vis major, such as theft, do not constitute entry into consumption, allowing for the application of rebates under such extraordinary circumstances. This decision may set a precedent for similar cases, emphasizing the importance of equitable interpretations of tax laws that accommodate events beyond a taxpayer’s control.

The decision affirms the credibility of the use of "vis major," allowing for a broader interpretation that includes human acts such as robbery, thereby fostering a legal environment that is less punitive toward tax compliance in extraordinary situations. The implications of this ruling could resonate through future customs and excise duty disputes where loss of control over goods occurs due to criminal acts.
