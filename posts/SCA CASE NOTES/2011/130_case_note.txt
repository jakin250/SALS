Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# Case Note: Morar NO v Akoo (498/10) [2011] ZASCA 130 

## F – Facts
The partnership at the center of this dispute, involving a business named Rollco, was established in 1986 by the Osman family and became embroiled in conflict following changes in its management and ownership structure. The first respondent, Mahomed Aslam Osman Akoo, played a significant role in the business, which had initially operated satisfactorily. In about 2002, familial withdrawals led to internal disputes regarding ownership interests, notably involving the Moosa family trusts, which had become equal partners in the business ([1–3]).

The partnership was effectively dissolved on 28 February 2006, when Mr. Akoo notified the Moosa family trusts through a formal letter. Subsequently, the Kwazulu-Natal High Court ordered that Mr. Morar be appointed as the liquidator of the partnership in response to an application by the Moosa family trusts ([4]). Mr. Morar encountered significant challenges in fulfilling his responsibilities due to a lack of cooperation from Mr. Akoo and Rollco Roofing Systems Pty Ltd. Specifically, they failed to comply with previous court orders to provide a full account of the partnership's assets and dealings, leading Mr. Morar to seek further powers from the court to aid his liquidation efforts ([6–8]).

## I – Issues
The central issues that arose from Mr. Morar’s appeal concern:
1. Whether it is permissible for a court to expand the powers previously conferred upon a liquidator of a partnership ([1]).
2. The specific nature of the relief Mr. Morar sought, which included financial contributions from the partners, an obligation for a detailed account of partnership dealings, and further investigative powers to compel testimony and produce documents ([8–12]).

## R – Rules
The court relied on principles of partnership law and particularly the actio pro socio. The actio pro socio allows partners to seek enforcement of obligations under their partnership agreement, whether during the life of the partnership or following its dissolution. The judgments referenced include *Robson v Theron* and *Brighton v Clift (2)*, highlighting the limitations of a liquidator's powers after appointment ([10–12]). 

Additionally, the court recognized that a liquidator's role is remedial; it exists to address situations where partners fail to liquidate responsibly on their own. Hence, the powers that the court could extend to a liquidator must typically mirror those that the partners themselves could confer upon agreement ([18–20]).

## A – Application
In applying these rules to the present case, the court concluded that no legal basis, legislative or otherwise, permitted a liquidator to claim broad-ranging powers extended by a court beyond what the appointment order provided. It evaluated Mr. Morar's requests critically:

1. **Financial Contributions**: The court found it inappropriate to compel contributions for ongoing litigation costs as this could lead to inequities, especially given the disputed nature of partnership interests ([26–30]).

2. **Investigative Powers**: The court denied Mr. Morar’s plea for enhanced interrogation powers and other extensive investigative authorities that resemble those of a statutory liquidator under company law. It held that such powers would breach the partner's rights and alter the nature of the liquidator's authority established under partnership law ([21–25]).

3. **Accounts of Partnership Dealings**: The court dismissed the claim for a detailed account regarding the dealings with suppliers, emphasizing that an obligation for such an account already existed per prior orders and that compliance was expected without necessitating further orders ([31]).

4. **Professional Indemnity Insurance**: The court concluded that Mr. Morar could purchase necessary insurance as an acting liquidator, and that costs would need to be justified per partnership agreements and not imposed by the court ([32]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed Mr. Morar's appeal and upheld the decision of the KwaZulu-Natal High Court. The court held that it would not extend the powers of a liquidator beyond the limits defined at the time of their appointment, nor would it compel partners to make financial contributions toward ongoing litigation pertaining to an unresolved partnership’s interests. This finding reinforces the delineation between the roles and powers of liquidators in partnerships versus those in corporate liquidations and highlights the need for clarity in partnership agreements regarding such powers. The implications of this judgment are significant for future liquidations involving partnerships, as it clarifies the extent of judicial discretion in altering liquidators' powers post-appointment and reinforces the necessity for legislative frameworks to address potential gaps in partnership law ([33]).
