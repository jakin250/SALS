Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2009

### FIRAC Case Note: Kruger v Goss and Another (603/08) [2009] ZASCA 105

---

#### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–8])

The case at hand involves an appeal concerning the enforceability of an order for rehabilitative maintenance issued by the Pretoria High Court, following a divorce between Fred Loll Stephanus Kruger (the deceased) and Denise Emmerentia Goss (the respondent). The couple married on 23 March 1988 and later divorced approximately three and a half years later, with the divorce order granting the respondent monthly payments for rehabilitative maintenance. Specifically, the order mandated payments of R8,000 for the first three months and R6,000 for the subsequent 57 months, contingent upon the respondent's employment (paras [2–3]).

The deceased complied with these payments until 31 August 2006, shortly before his death due to natural causes on 29 September 2006, at which point he had paid 33 of the 57 installments. Following his death, Goss lodged a claim against his estate for the remaining payments, totaling R144,000. The executor of the estate rejected this claim, leading to Goss's subsequent litigation against the deceased's estate in the Pretoria High Court, which resulted in a ruling in her favor (paras [4–6]).

#### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 1, 6, 14–15])

The central issue in this appeal is the enforceability of a rehabilitative maintenance order against an estate following the death of the payer spouse. Specifically, the question is whether Goss can claim the remaining monthly maintenance payments from Kruger's estate after his death, where the original divorce decree provided for rehabilitative maintenance governed by s 7(2) of the Divorce Act 70 of 1979. The determination of whether rehabilitative maintenance constitutes a type of maintenance enforceable against a deceased estate raises substantial legal issues related to the intersection of the Divorce Act with common law provisions concerning obligations post-mortem (paras [1, 14–15]).

#### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 9–12, 14–16])

The legal framework primarily governing this case includes s 7(2) of the Divorce Act 70 of 1979, which stipulates that a court may grant maintenance for a party until their death or remarriage. This provision implies that maintenance obligations cease with the termination of the marital relationship, including due to death. The court also referenced the Maintenance of Surviving Spouses Act 27 of 1990, which permits claims for maintenance against a deceased spouse's estate but applies only to widowed individuals and not to divorcees (paras [9–12]).

The court drew upon precedent set in **Hodges v Coubrough** 1991 (3) SA 58 (D & CLD), wherein it was noted that if the legislature intended for post-divorce maintenance claims to be enforceable against a deceased spouse's estate, it would have explicitly articulated such in the legislation, which it did not (para [13]).

#### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 15–18])

The court determined that Goss’s claim for rehabilitative maintenance could not be viewed as separate from the maintenance provisions under s 7(2) of the Divorce Act. The court noted that rehabilitative maintenance is inherently a form of maintenance and therefore falls within the confines of the statutory framework that dictates that such obligations cease upon the death of either party (para [15]).

Moreover, the court stressed the implications of allowing maintenance claims against deceased estates, suggesting that doing so could disrupt the priority of claims by surviving spouses and dependents, creating precedence for competing claims that might infringe upon the rights of minor children and other beneficiaries. Given that Goss conceded that her claim should be considered as such under the existing law, the court concluded that allowing her to pursue the estate for outstanding maintenance would set a precedent that may lead to undesirable legal complexities (paras [16–18]).

#### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 19])

The appeal was upheld with costs, effectively setting aside the High Court's order that had favored Goss. The judgment concluded that an order for rehabilitative maintenance, as stipulated under the Divorce Act, is not enforceable against a deceased estate. Thus, the court reaffirmed the common law principles that maintenance obligations are extinguished upon the death of the spouse liable for support (para [19]). 

This ruling underlines the rigid delineation in South African law regarding maintenance obligations that terminate upon the mortality of a spouse, reinforcing the need for legislative clarity should there be intentions to modify such obligations post-marriage dissolution. The case has significant implications for future maintenance claims, illustrating the necessity for consideration of alternative arrangements should parties wish to bind estates to ongoing financial support obligations.
