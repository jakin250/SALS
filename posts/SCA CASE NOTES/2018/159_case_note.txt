# Case Note: Tavakoli v Bantry Hills (1251/2018) [2018] ZASCA 159 (28 November 2018)

## FIRAC Case Note

**F – Facts**  
In the case of *Tavakoli v Bantry Hills (Pty) Ltd*, the appellants, Allen Targahi Tavakoli and DLX Property (Pty) Ltd, challenged the City of Cape Town’s approval of building plans for a luxury block of flats on the respondent's property located in Sea Point ([1]). The appellants, owning three adjacent residential properties approximately 80 meters from the Bantry Hills property, asserted that they had standing to contest the approval based on item 40(c) of the Development Management Scheme (DMS) in the City of Cape Town’s Municipal Planning By-law of 2015 ([7], [8]). The High Court, however, dismissed their review application, concluding that the appellants lacked locus standi, prompting the appellants to seek leave to appeal ([2], [3]). 

While the City of Cape Town abided by the court's decision, the appeal revolved around whether the appellants had standing to complain about non-compliance with the DMS and whether they could demonstrate harm caused by such non-compliance ([1], [2], [3]).

**I – Issues**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal confronted several pertinent issues arising from the lower court's decision:

1. **Locus Standi**: Did the appellants have the legal standing to challenge the building plan's approval?
2. **Interpretation of Item 40(c)**: If the appellants had standing, did the approval contravene item 40(c) of the DMS?
3. **Judicial Discretion**: If there was a contravention, should the court exercise discretion to refrain from setting aside the approval? ([2])

**R – Rules**  
The rules applicable to this case derive from constitutional, administrative, and town planning law principles. Central to the determination of locus standi is the principle that a party must establish their interest in the matter at hand, particularly in terms of the interests protected by the legislation they are invoking ([12], [25]). The Supreme Court of Appeal cited precedential cases that clarify the standing of parties in the context of town planning laws, highlighting that standing may depend on proximity to the property in question and whether the lawmaker intended to benefit a specific class of people ([11], [12], [15], [25]).

In this context, item 40(c) of the DMS stipulates that if the only vehicular access to a property is through an adjacent road reserve of less than 9 meters wide, no building other than a dwelling house or second dwelling is permitted ([8]).

**A – Application**  
The court analyzed the appellants' claims regarding their standing and the interpretation of item 40(c):

1. **Locus Standi**: The appellants claimed to possess standing due to their proximity and ownership of properties within a planning scheme affecting the respondent. However, the court emphasized the necessity of demonstrating that they belonged to a specific class that the lawmaker intended to protect. It concluded that the traffic congestion concern aimed to protect users of narrow roads providing access to high-density properties—specifically those directly affected by such access ([10], [20], [22]). Since the appellants did not live on the narrow roads leading to the Bantry Hills property, they were deemed not to belong to the affected class.

2. **Merits of Item 40(c)**: Given the ruling on locus standi, it was unnecessary to delve deeply into the interpretation of item 40(c). However, the court articulated that the mischief addressed by the provision was traffic congestion linked to high-density developments accessing narrow roads. Thus, the lawmaker’s intent in item 40(c) appeared to cater to properties directly accessing these roads ([10], [24]).

3. **Judicial Discretion**: The court noted that had the appellants established standing, the issue of whether the court should have refrained from setting aside the approval could be revisited. However, given the lack of standing, this aspect was rendered moot ([2], [3]).

**C – Conclusion**  
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately affirmed the lower court's finding that the appellants lacked locus standi. As they failed to demonstrate either a specific interest or harm resulting from the non-compliance with the DMS, the appeal was dismissed with costs, including the costs of two counsel ([31]). This ruling underscores the stringent requirements for standing in administrative law and highlights the importance of possessing a clear interest aligned with legislative intent when relying on specific statutory protections. The implications extend beyond this case, emphasizing the necessity for property owners to substantiate their claims and interests with respect to town planning schemes in South Africa.
