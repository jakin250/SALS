Ferguson and Others v Rhodes University [2017] ZACC 39

The dispute in Ferguson and Others v Rhodes University stemmed from a campaign initiated by a student group known as the Chapter 2.12 Movement at Rhodes University, which highlighted issues of rape culture and gender-based violence on campus.

Constitutional Court 2017 [2017] ZACC 39

FIRAC Method

F - Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–5])

The dispute in Ferguson and Others v Rhodes University stemmed from a campaign initiated by a student group known as the Chapter 2.12 Movement at Rhodes University, which highlighted issues of rape culture and gender-based violence on campus. Despite the campaign addressing crucial social issues, it also led to illegal actions, including assaults and disruptions that adversely affected the university environment ([para 1]).

The applicants—Sian Ferguson, Yolanda Dyantyi, and Simamkele Helen—sought leave to appeal against an order from the Supreme Court of Appeal, which upheld a High Court’s decision granting an interim interdict against their participation in protests that escalated into unlawful conduct. The interdict was issued to prevent further disruptions on campus and was ultimately made final, albeit with limitations compared to the initial order ([para 2]).

The High Court's order not only confirmed the final interdict but included a costs order against the applicants, compelling them to bear the costs of the proceedings. The applicants claimed this was an unjust exercise of discretion that infringed upon their constitutional rights to freedom of expression and assembly ([para 3]).

I - Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 12–14])

Several constitutional issues arose from the case, primarily focusing on whether the interdict granted against the applicants unduly infringed upon their rights to freedom of expression and assembly as guaranteed by Sections 16 and 17 of the South African Constitution. Furthermore, the appropriateness of the costs orders against the applicants also formed a significant part of the judicial inquiry. The applicants contended that they had been unfairly penalized for their association with the protests that included unlawful conduct and raised the question of the legal foundation for the adverse costs awards ([paras 12–13]).

R - Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 15–17], 19–20)

The judgments referenced pivotal legal principles regarding the rights to freedom of expression and assembly, which are protected under the South African Constitution. Additionally, the court highlighted the Biowatch principle, emphasizing the chilling effect that costs orders may have on parties asserting their constitutional rights—especially in cases involving public interest and socioeconomic disparities ([paras 19–20]). The Garvas case was also cited, reinforcing the importance of judicial scrutiny in determining the legality and proportionality of interdicts involving protests.

A - Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 15–18], 24–28)

The court carefully assessed the scope of the interim interdict and the evidence supporting the argument that the applicants had been involved in conduct that warranted such an order. While the High Court's and Supreme Court of Appeal's decisions were based on interpretations of the facts and the legal framework surrounding the protests, the Constitutional Court found that the basis for the costs orders diverged from established principles such as Biowatch, which protects litigants from being penalized for pursuing constitutional claims ([paras 15–24]).

Importantly, the Constitutional Court underscored that the adverse cost orders seemed disproportionate given the context—where the applicants were engaging in actions that reflected legitimate constitutional rights aimed at addressing serious societal issues ([para 24]). The evaluation concluded that the interdict, while justified up to a point, did not provide grounds for penalizing the applicants with costs, especially when their involvement was contested and their conduct was framed as largely interpretative rather than overtly unlawful.

C - Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 29–30])

The Constitutional Court ruled in favor of the applicants concerning the costs orders, setting aside the High Court and Supreme Court of Appeal’s findings that imposed costs against the applicants ([para 30]). The Court emphasized that the applicants should not be financially penalized for exercising their constitutional rights, particularly when the boundaries of lawful and unlawful conduct during protests remain complex and contentious. Hence, each party was directed to bear its own costs throughout the litigation process.

The court's decision reinforced the necessity of balancing the right to protest with the legal framework governing lawful conduct in such settings, establishing significant precedent concerning the application of costs in constitutional litigation. This ruling is particularly impactful for future cases involving protests or public demonstrations where the line between lawful expression and unlawful action may be blurred, reinforcing protections for similar claimants in upholding constitutional rights without facing financial retribution.
