Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

**FIRAC Case Note: Municipality of Stellenbosch v Shelf-Line 104 (Pty) Ltd [2011] ZASCA 190**

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History**  
The dispute revolves around the Municipality of Stellenbosch (the appellant) and Shelf-Line 104 (Pty) Ltd (the respondent), concerning the terms of approval for a property development in Klapmuts ([1]–[2]). The property, originally known as the Remainder of Portion 3 of the Farm Groenfontein, was rezoned and subdivided by the appellant in 1999, subject to various conditions including compliance with bulk infrastructure development contribution levies (BICLs), which were standardised in guidelines provided by the municipality.

In 2000, objections to the development from third parties were dismissed, and the relevant approvals were upheld ([3]). Subsequent to these approvals, ownership of the property changed hands, and the respondent sought to reinstate the lapsed development rights, which were granted in 2006 with attached conditions ([5]–[6]). 

The respondent progressed with its development based on an understanding that the BICL rates would adhere to the existing tariff structures ([8]–[11]). However, in May 2007, the municipality resolved to significantly increase BICLs, stirring confusion over whether this new tariff would apply to all developments, including those approved before the increase ([12]). Following this resolution, the respondent sought clearance certificates for property transfers based on the original tariff, which the municipality refused, insisting on payment according to the new tariff ([14]).

The respondent subsequently applied to the Western Cape High Court for an order compelling the municipality to issue the requested clearance certificates based on the old BICL rates, claiming urgency in light of the situation ([15]–[16]). The High Court ruled in favor of the respondent, prompting the municipality to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([17]).

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions**  
The primary issues before the Court were twofold:  
1. Whether the municipality had the authority to unilaterally amend the conditions of development approvals that had been accepted by the developer, specifically regarding the application of the revised BICL tariffs ([18]).
2. The interpretation of the council's resolution of May 2007 concerning the applicability of the new BICL tariffs to developments initiated prior to that date ([19]).

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon**  
In assessing the merits of the appeal, the Court referenced several legal principles grounded in local government law, particularly the Land Use Planning Ordinance (LUPO) ([28]–[30]). Section 42 of LUPO outlines the conditions under which councils may impose or amend conditions on development approvals, requiring consultation with landowners before unilaterally altering approved terms ([30]–[32]). 

The Court also examined the nature of BICLs, distinguishing them from general taxes. It supported previous jurisprudence that viewed these levies as necessary contributions for services that developers were to benefit from, not as taxes that could be altered at the municipality's discretion ([28]–[29]). The existing law suggests that BICLs must remain consistent with the conditions agreed upon during the approval processes, reaffirming the need for mutual consent in any amendments ([31]–[33]).

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law**  
The Court systematically analyzed the municipality's argument that it possessed the authority to unilaterally modify development conditions based on its council resolution. Citing the obligatory nature of consultation specified in LUPO, the Court concluded that the municipality had failed to adhere to these procedural requirements. As such, the new tariff imposed could not apply retroactively to already accepted developments as this would infringe upon developers’ legitimate expectations and previously established agreements ([27]).

Additionally, the Court clarified that while the municipality has the right to amend its BICL tariffs, it does so applying to future conditions, not retroactively to existing agreements. It reinforced that economic and contractual principles necessitated certainty for developers, ruling that the levies were not merely a tax but obligations tied to the approvals granted ([28]–[34]).

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications**  
The Supreme Court dismissed the appeal with costs, upholding the findings of the Western Cape High Court ([34]). The ratio decidendi centered on the necessity for municipalities to adhere strictly to procedural requirements under LUPO, particularly relating to the legitimacy and nature of accepted conditions in the development process. This ruling underscored the significance of industry predictability for developers, cementing the principle that alteration to established conditions cannot occur without proper consultation and agreement. 

The implications of this judgment resonate widely within municipal governance, emphasizing the need for clarity and adherence to established protocols when dealing with developers, thus fostering confidence in the legislative and operational frameworks governing land use and development in South Africa.
