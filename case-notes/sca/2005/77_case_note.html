Supreme Court of Appeal

Jacobus Johannes Kemp NO and Others v Dr JJH Van Wyk and Others [2005] ZASCA 119

The case involves an appeal concerning the administrative decision made by Dr JJH Van Wyk, then Director of Animal Health, regarding the refusal to grant a permit for the importation of 98 sable antelope from Zimbabwe.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2005 [2005] ZASCA 119

F – Facts

The case involves an appeal concerning the administrative decision made by Dr JJH Van Wyk, then Director of Animal Health, regarding the refusal to grant a permit for the importation of 98 sable antelope from Zimbabwe. The appellants—Jacobus Johannes Kemp NO, Alida Kemp NO, Christiaan Frederick Muller NO, and Daniël Jacobus Goosen—submitted their application for a permit against the backdrop of an established embargo on the importation of cloven-hoofed animals from Zimbabwe, imposed due to the outbreak of foot-and-mouth disease ([paras 2–3]).

The embargo was instituted in April 2002, following warnings from the Chief Veterinary Officer of Zimbabwe about the contagious nature of the disease, which posed significant risks to South Africa's livestock industry ([paras 3–4]). Despite the embargo, the appellants proposed a control regime for the requested importation, including pre-import testing and quarantine ([paras 5–6]).

Subsequently, when the application received no initial response, the fourth appellant made an inquiry where the first respondent reportedly dismissed the application abruptly by marking it as ‘refused’ without further explanation. The refusal followed a departmental meeting where officials recommended against all pending applications for the importation of animals from Zimbabwe ([paras 7–8]).

The appellants contested the validity of the refusal, arguing that the first respondent failed to consider their proposals independently of the existing embargo. Their dispute escalated to the High Court, which dismissed their application to set aside the refusal ([para 2]). The appellants then sought leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal.

I – Issues

The principal issues in this appeal revolved around:

  1. Whether Dr Van Wyk exercised his discretion lawfully in refusing to grant the import permit in accordance with the Animal Diseases Act 35 of 1984.
  2. Whether the reliance on the existing embargo precluded a proper exercise of discretion, thereby rendering the decision unlawful.
  3. The validity of the directive issued under § 6(3)(a) of the Act prohibiting importation based on the perceived risks ([paras 2, 10, 12]).

R – Rules

The legal framework governing this matter is primarily established under the Animal Diseases Act 35 of 1984, which grants the Director of Animal Health discretion to permit or prohibit the importation of animals to safeguard against disease ([para 2]).

The decision-making process around the exercise of discretion has been informed by various precedents, notably:

  1. Britten v Pope 1916 AD 150, emphasizing that while officials have discretion, they should not elevate policies into binding rules that inhibit the exercise of that discretion completely ([para 1]).
  2. R v Port of London Authority; Ex parte Kynoch, Ltd [1919] 1 KB 176 and British Oxygen Co. Ltd v Minister of Technology [1971] AC 610, delineating the distinction between adopting a policy that provides guidance without precluding discretion versus imposing rigid rules that eliminate consideration of application ([paras 10, 11]).

Statutory Provisions

  • Section 6(1)(a) prohibits the importation of animals without a permit ([para 2]).
  • Section 6(3)(a) allows the Director to issue directives if there are reasonable grounds to suspect unlawful importation ([para 12]).

A – Application

In reviewing the appellant's claim, the court acknowledged that the first respondent did consider the application but concluded that the existing embargo was a significant factor in his decision. The court refrained from critiquing the adequacy of preventative measures proposed by the appellants, affirming that the first respondent was under no obligation to disregard the embargo merely because an alternative proposal was before him that could be effective ([para 10]).

The examination of the first respondent’s rationale showed that he exercised his discretion within the policy framework established by the Directorate of Animal Health, concluding that the importation would contradict the department's disease protection policies ([para 11]). The court deemed that the first respondent's brief consideration of the application was sufficient given his expertise and previous experience dealing with similar situations, validating the conclusion that he was not bound to re-evaluate the existing embargo ([para 11]).

On the question of the directive issued under § 6(3)(a), the court posited that whether the directive was valid or not was secondary to the fact that the embargo remained in effect at the time of the decision, thereby justifying the refusal independent of the directive's status ([para 12]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, affirming that Dr Van Wyk acted within his legal authority under the Animal Diseases Act. The court emphasized that the existence of the embargo did not preclude the proper exercise of discretion; rather, it constituted a legitimate basis for the decision made. The refusal was found to be lawful, as the first respondent acted within the framework of existing departmental policy to protect the country’s livestock against disease.

The decision underscored the principle that while officials must exercise discretion with an open mind, they are also bound to consider the existing statutory policies designed to protect public interests, specifically in contexts involving potential public health risks. The implications of this ruling thus extend to administrative law, clarifying the boundaries of discretion exercised by public officials in the face of established policy frameworks ([paras 13]).

Overall, the court’s decision highlights the significance of balancing administrative discretion with the duty to uphold public health imperatives, ensuring that the bureaucratic processes align with statutory mandates.
