Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2006

# FIRAC Case Note: Truter v Deysel [2006] SCA 17 (RSA)

## F – Facts
The matter involved a claim for damages for medical negligence brought by Mr Marthinus Albertus Deysel (the respondent) against Dr Rene Truter and Dr Jan Venter (the appellants) in the Cape High Court. The claim arose from a series of medical procedures performed on Deysel between July and September 1993, which allegedly resulted in a severe deterioration of his eye condition that ultimately led to the loss of vision. Deysel underwent multiple operations, notably an extra-capsular cataract extraction and posterior lens implantation on 5 July 1993, and subsequent surgeries conducted by the appellants until September 1993. 

By December 1996, complications led to a corneal graft operation, which ultimately resulted in the evisceration of Deysel's right eye on 23 April 1997. Having lost his left eye earlier, Deysel was rendered totally blind ([1]–[4]).

Deysel lodged a complaint with the Medical and Dental Council in July 1994, expressing concerns over the surgeries performed by the appellants. Following consultation with various medical professionals, he initially sought to pursue a malpractice claim in 1995 but did not receive positive expert opinions supporting a conclusion of negligence until 2000 when he consulted Dr Steven ([6]–[8]). 

The appellants raised a special plea of prescription under section 11(d) of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, arguing that Deysel’s claim was barred as it was not initiated within three years from the date the debt became due. The High Court dismissed this plea, leading to the present appeal ([2]–[3]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issue in consideration was when the prescription period for Deysel's medical negligence claim commenced under section 12(3) of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969. Specifically, the court needed to determine the moment Deysel acquired “knowledge of... the facts from which the debt arises” pertaining to the medical negligence alleged against the appellants ([12]).

## R – Rules
The relevant statutory provision is section 12(3) of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, which states that a debt is not considered due until the creditor has knowledge of the identity of the debtor and the facts from which the debt arises. The court interpreted this provision by analyzing principles from previous case law, including but not limited to *Evins v Shields Insurance Co Ltd* [1980] 2 SA 814 (A) and *Drennan Maud & Partners v Pennington Town Board* [1998] 3 SA 200 (SCA) ([11], [17], [20]).

Courts have held that awareness of the facts is sufficient for the prescription period to commence; however, knowledge of the legal conclusions (e.g., negligence) drawn from those facts is not mandated ([20]–[21]). 

## A – Application
The court in its assessment emphasized that Deysel's claim was complete as soon as he suffered harm due to the medical actions taken by the appellants. The judgment critically analyzed the timeline: Deysel had detailed knowledge of his surgeries and the resultant harm from as early as July 1994, evidenced by the complaint to the Medical and Dental Council, and his engagement of attorneys for pursuing a malpractice claim. The court noted that he was aware of his potential claim against the appellants at that time ([24]–[25]).

Deysel’s subsequent attempts to obtain expert opinions on negligence were deemed irrelevant to the determination of when prescription began, as such opinions represent legal assessments rather than factual knowledge. The court determined that the information available to Deysel and his representatives at the time of his initial complaint was adequate for a cause of action, thereby rejecting the assertion that prescription only commenced with Dr Steven's confirmation of negligence ([21], [26]).

The reliance by the High Court on similar cases from English law was also questioned, grounded on differences between respective legal frameworks and the specific facts involved ([27]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal held that Deysel had sufficient knowledge of the facts from which his debt arose prior to 17 April 1997, which was the date he served the summons. As such, his claim had prescribed in accordance with section 12(3) of the Prescription Act, which resulted in the appeal being upheld ([28]).

The implications of this case reinforce the principle that, in medical negligence claims, knowledge of the factual basis for a claim—rather than knowledge of legal conclusions drawn from those facts—determines the commencement of the prescription period. The clarity provided in this ruling underscores the legislative intent behind the Prescription Act in South Africa, specifically in the context of medical malpractice claims. The order of the High Court was then set aside, and Deysel’s action was dismissed with costs ([29]).
