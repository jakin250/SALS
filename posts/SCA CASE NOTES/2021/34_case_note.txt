Source Folder: 2021

# FIRAC Case Note

## Case Title: Govan Mbeki Municipality v New Integrated Credit Solutions (Pty) Ltd

### Court: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

### Citation: (121/2020) [2021] ZASCA 34 (7 April 2021)

### Judges: Navsa ADP, Dambuza and Mocumie JJA, Kgoele and Goosen AJJA

---

### F - Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–20])

This case revolves around the validity of a debt management service agreement between Govan Mbeki Municipality (GMM) and New Integrated Credit Solutions (Pty) Ltd (NICS). The GMM, aware of a prior contract between NICS and Newcastle Municipality, sought to procure NICS's services through a letter of consent under Regulation 32 of the Municipal Supply Chain Management Regulations. 

After a series of communications between the municipalities, GMM concluded a contract with NICS on 12 September 2015, which incorporated a 2.5% commission on debts younger than 60 days—an addition not originally outlined in NICS's tender or the Newcastle Municipality's procurement process. 

By November 2016, the Auditor-General flagged concerns about this procurement process, labeling it unfair and outside the tender specifications. Following this, the GMM attempted to terminate the contract, asserting its illegality and claiming that neither NICS nor other bidders had the opportunity to bid for debts under 60 days. 

GMM initiated proceedings on 21 June 2017, seeking annulment of the contract on grounds of unconstitutionality and non-compliance with statutory requirements, while NICS sought to uphold the contract's validity. The High Court's intervention resulted in partial invalidation, prompting appeals and cross-appeals by both parties to the Supreme Court of Appeal.

### I - Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 21–30])

The core legal issues presented for determination included:

1. The appropriateness of GMM’s delayed action in seeking review of the contract.
2. Whether the inclusion of the 2.5% commission was unconstitutional and acted in contravention of section 217 of the Constitution and associated procurement regulations.
3. The implications of self-review by a public authority and the remedies available under section 172(1)(b) of the Constitution in the context of addressing the accrued rights of the parties. 

### R - Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 31–40])

Relevant legal authorities and rules include:

1. **Section 217 of the Constitution**: Establishes the necessity for a procurement process that is fair, equitable, transparent, competitive, and cost-effective.
2. **Regulation 32 of the Municipal Supply Chain Management Regulations**, which delineates the conditions under which municipalities may procure goods or services from contracts established by other organs of state.
3. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: Though not directly applicable since self-reviews are treated as legality reviews, principles regarding delays and diligence remain relevant.
4. **Precedent** from cases such as *Buffalo City Metropolitan Municipality v Asla Construction (Pty) Ltd* and *Natyawa v Makana Municipality*, focusing on the implications of delays in administrative actions and the obligations of public authorities to meet statutory and constitutional norms.

### A - Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 41–63])

In its examination, the court scrutinized the adequacy of GMM's justification for delay. The court emphasized that the delay in seeking review, particularly given GMM's awareness of the procurement irregularities well in advance, was unreasonable. It determined the GMM's claims for relief were rooted in constitutional legality, necessitating compliance with section 172(1)(b) of the Constitution.

The Supreme Court highlighted the specific shortcomings of the municipal procurement process, noting that the additional commission for debts younger than 60 days infringed on the requirements of a competitive tender process. It decided that the failure to adhere to constitutional imperatives was systemic and raised significant concerns about accountability within local governance.

While acknowledging that delays can sometimes be overlooked in the interests of justice, the Court decisively ruled against the GMM, emphasizing the egregious nature of its conduct and the lack of good governance—thus upholding a need for accountability and sanction against officials involved.

### C - Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 64–63])

The Supreme Court affirmed that the contract was unconstitutional and invalid but only in relation to the specific clause regarding the 2.5% commission for debts younger than 60 days. Conversely, it preserved the rights to commission generated from debts older than 60 days as determined by the arbitrator’s ruling, thereby striking a balance between constitutional adherence and the rights accrued by NICS under the original agreement.

This judgment reinforced the principle that public authorities must act strictly within the confines of the law and the requirements for equitable procurement. It further established that self-reviews must be conducted judiciously, warning public entities against engaging in protracted litigation at the cost of public resources. Ultimately, this case demonstrates the court's commitment to upholding constitutional values in public procurement, ensuring accountability while mitigating detrimental impacts on service providers operating in good faith.
