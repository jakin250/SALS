Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title**: Ngomane & others v City of Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality & another (734/2017) [2018] ZASCA 57

**Court**: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

### F – Facts

The case revolves around a group of homeless individuals (the applicants) who habitually resided on a traffic island under a highway bridge in Johannesburg. They had occupied this space for approximately two to five years, utilizing various materials such as cardboard and wood to create temporary shelters ([paras 1–2]). On 1 February 2017, officials from the Johannesburg Metropolitan Police Department (JMPD), without prior notice or court authorization, conducted a "clean-up" operation that involved the confiscation and destruction of the applicants' personal belongings, including essential items like clothing, identity documents, and food ([paras 3–5]). The respondents contended that the operation was a necessary measure to address complaints from local businesses regarding urban disorder associated with the homeless population ([paras 4–5]).

The Gauteng Local Division of the High Court dismissed the applicants’ appeal for the return of their property or equivalent material, asserting that their temporary structures did not constitute a "home" under the Protection of Illegal Eviction and Unlawful Occupation of Land Act 19 of 1998 (PIE Act) and that there had been no eviction ([paras 8–9]). The applicants subsequently sought leave to appeal, which was granted, leading to this appellate decision ([paras 1, 11]).

### I – Issues

The case presents several significant legal questions:

1. Did the traffic island constitute "land" relevant to the PIE Act, and were the applicants unlawfully evicted?
2. Should the applicants have been granted relief for the confiscated materials and compensation due to the destruction of their property?
3. Was the conduct of the JMPD officials a breach of the applicants' rights to dignity, privacy, and property as protected under the Constitution? ([paras 11–12])

### R – Rules

The critical legal rules and authorities cited in this case include:

- **Sections 25(1)** and **26(3)** of the South African Constitution, protecting the right to property and prohibiting unlawful eviction without a court order ([paras 3, 4]).
- **Section 1 of the PIE Act**, which provides definitions relevant to unlawful eviction and occupation ([para 16]).
- The legal principle of **mandament van spolie**, which aims to restore possession of property that has been unlawfully taken ([para 18]).
- The landmark case of **Tswelopele Non-Profit Organisation & Others v City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality**, which established precedents on unlawful dispossession and compensation matters ([para 20]).

### A – Application

The court analyzed the events of the February 2017 operation and the consequences of the JMPD's actions. Critical to the court's reasoning was the video footage documenting the confiscation and the context of the operation, which illustrated a lack of due process in the removal of items that were clearly personal belongings rather than abandoned rubbish ([paras 13–14]). The court affirmed that the items confiscated constituted personal property deserving of protection under the Constitution, thereby characterizing the actions of the JMPD as arbitrary deprivation ([paras 21–23]).

The court further addressed the definition of the temporary structures used by the applicants, ruling that while these structures did not qualify as permanent buildings under the PIE Act, the context of their occupation and the nature of their belongings still warranted constitutional protection ([paras 16–17]). Additionally, the court recognized that the applicants' rights to dignity and privacy were infringed upon as their belongings were removed without any safeguarding measures taken by the officials ([paras 21–22]).

The issue of relief was also explored, with the court determining that while the applicants sought the return of their property, it acknowledged the practical difficulties and impracticality of instituting a damages claim for items that had nominal commercial value. Ultimately, the court opted to grant a nominal compensation of R1,500 for each applicant as a constitutional remedy for the violation of their rights ([para 27]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the JMPD's actions constituted an unconstitutional violation of the applicants' property rights and dignity. The court ruled in favor of the applicants, ordering the Johannesburg Metropolitan Municipality to pay R1,500 to each applicant as compensation for their unlawful dispossession. Additionally, the court's decision highlighted the need for municipalities to implement proper procedures to respect the rights of vulnerable populations during enforcement actions ([paras 28]).

This case underscores the ongoing struggle for the rights of homeless individuals in South Africa and the imperative role of the courts in upholding constitutional protections against arbitrary actions by state officials. It sends a clear message about the necessity for due process in the treatment of vulnerable populations and the importance of safeguarding their dignity and rights within the urban landscape.
