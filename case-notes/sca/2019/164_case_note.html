Supreme Court of Appeal

Mabotwane Security Services CC v Pikitup Soc (Pty) Ltd & Others

The appellant provided these services under a fixed contract expiring on 30 November 2015.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The case revolves around Mabotwane Security Services CC (the appellant) and its contractual dispute with Pikitup Soc (Pty) Ltd (the first respondent), a municipal entity owned by the City of Johannesburg, concerning security services.

The appellant provided these services under a fixed contract expiring on 30 November 2015. Anticipating the expiration, Pikitup initiated a tender process (the first tender) for a new security provider. Despite receiving 68 bids, including one from the appellant, the tender was ultimately cancelled due to budget insufficiencies, as the lowest bid exceeded the approved budget by more than R30 million ([para 2]).

Subsequently, Pikitup issued a second tender with modified budgetary constraints and recommended an expedited 14-day advertising period due to the urgent need for security services ([paras 3–5]). The appellant challenged the cancellation of the first tender and subsequent actions through review applications.

The Gauteng Division of the High Court dismissed both review applications, asserting the first tender's cancellation was lawful since it adhered to statutory and regulatory frameworks ([paras 11–12]). Leave to appeal was subsequently granted, leading to this appeal on the issue of whether it had any practical effect ([para 13]).

I – Issues

The primary issue in this case is whether the appeal is moot, meaning that a decision in the appeal would have no practical effect or result as articulated in section 16(2)(a)(i) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013. The court had to evaluate the implications of the contracts in question, especially given that the services were no longer required by the first respondent due to changes in their operational strategy ([para 14]).

R – Rules

The governing statutes involved include:

  1. Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, section 16(2)(a), stipulating that an appeal may be dismissed if it would have no practical effect ([para 15]).
  2. Local Government: Municipal Finance Management Act 56 of 2003, which informs the requirements for tender processes ([para 5]).
  3. Public Procurement Regulations, specifically the Preferential Procurement Policy Framework Act 5 of 2000, indicating that tenders may be cancelled if sufficient funds are unavailable ([paras 12–13]).

Additionally, there are precedential cases that help elucidate the doctrine of mootness, including National Coalition for Gay and Lesbian Equality & Others v Minister of Home Affairs ([1999] ZACC 17), emphasizing the necessity for an ongoing controversy to maintain justiciability, and Centre for Child Law v Hoërskool Fochville & Another ([2015] ZASCA 155), which describes the discretion of a court when deciding on moot matters ([para 15]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal, in this case, determined that the issues raised on appeal were moot primarily because:

  1. The contract arising from the first tender had already expired on 30 November 2018, meaning the requested orders would no longer impact ongoing operations ([para 16]).
  2. The first respondent had since realigned its operational strategy and insourced security services, rendering the resolutions sought by the appellant practically unenforceable ([paras 18–20]).

The court dismissed the argument from the appellant about the necessity of declaring the first respondent's actions unlawful, emphasizing that even a declaration of unlawfulness would fail to provide any effective relief as it could not lead to a valid adjudication of the original tenders ([para 25]).

The decision of the High Court was thoroughly reaffirmed through the discussion of supervening events and their relevance to the interpretation of mootness in administrative law ([para 26]). While acknowledging instances where important public questions might still warrant judicial review, the court determined that this case did not meet that threshold ([para 28]).

C – Conclusion

Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, concluding that the issues of law and fact raised by the appellant were moot and that deciding on the merits would serve no practical purpose ([para 33]). The court also addressed the costs associated with the appeal, holding that although punitive costs were not warranted, the appellant would bear the costs on a party-and-party scale ([para 31]). This outcome illustrated the principle that a court will refrain from entering decisions that lack practical consequences, reinforcing the necessity for ongoing relevance in legal proceedings.

In conclusion, the case underscored the importance of clarity in tender processes and the repercussions of failing to adopt a cooperative position when an appeal becomes moot. The decision serves as a reminder of the judiciary’s restraint in moot matters, particularly where a practical resolution would not alter existing circumstances.
