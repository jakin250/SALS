Supreme Court of Appeal

Transnet Limited v Erf 152927 Cape Town (Pty) Ltd & Others [2011] ZASCA 148

Facts [1] Transnet Limited ("Transnet") is established as the owner of significant vacant properties near the harbour in Cape Town, driven by industrial expansion and a moratorium on property disposals affecting demand and supply in the area.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2011 [2011] ZASCA 148

FIRAC Method

Facts

[1] Transnet Limited ("Transnet") is established as the owner of significant vacant properties near the harbour in Cape Town, driven by industrial expansion and a moratorium on property disposals affecting demand and supply in the area. In February 2007, Transnet initiated eviction proceedings against multiple respondents from a property positioned 3.6 km from the harbour, asserting ownership and occupation by the respondents.

[2] After filing the application, Transnet reframed its request to target specific respondents, recognizing that several parties were not in actual occupation of the property. Particularly, the eleventh respondent, Lorcom Six (Pty) Ltd ("Lorcom"), claimed entitlement to occupancy through an oral lease with Transnet, asserting it had sublet to the first respondent, Erf 152927 Cape Town (Pty) Ltd, which in turn sublet further to the remaining respondents.

[3] The first respondent had purchased the property from Transnet on 18 February 1998, under unresolved technical issues regarding property transfer. Despite claims challenging the existence of such a lease, Transnet’s admission of a significant delay and its actions further complicated the matter. The High Court, in its decision, highlighted a foreseeable bona fide dispute of fact regarding the alleged oral lease, leading to a dismissal of Transnet’s application.

[4] Following the dismissal by Koen AJ, Transnet appealed the decision, maintaining that the court erred in not referring the case for oral evidence to resolve the factual disputes.

Issues

[5] This appeal presents two central legal issues:

  1. Whether the High Court correctly concluded the existence of a bona fide dispute of fact regarding the oral lease, thereby precluding dismissal on the papers alone.
  2. Whether the High Court correctly exercised its discretion to dismiss the application instead of referring the matter for oral evidence, based on the foreseeability of such a dispute.

Rules

[6] The core legal principles revolved around the procedural propriety of eviction applications, particularly in scenarios where factual disputes arise, as illustrated in Fakie NO v CCII Systems (Pty) Ltd [2006] (4) SA 326 (SCA) at para 55. The accepted jurisprudence emphasizes that motion proceedings should not be utilized to resolve factual disputes that necessitate substantive inquiry.

[7] Additionally, the “Plascon-Evans” rule asserts that in motion proceedings, if disputes of fact arise, the applicant's version is to be preferred unless it is so improbable that it can be dismissed purely on the facts presented. This requires that disputes be resolved in light of a standard that recognizes inherent contradictions and probabilities.

Application

[8] The court meticulously scrutinized the affidavits relevant to the claims of the oral lease. It noted that while Lombard’s assertions were not substantiated by affidavits from Transnet staff, Transnet’s counter-arguments were purportedly derived from a misapplication of the motion procedure principles. The court observed that Lombard’s version regarding the existence of an oral lease was both plausible and consistent with the timeline and actions taken by the parties involved.

[9] Since Transnet had consented to Lorcom's occupation and allowed it to remain, and given the long-standing nature of their interactions, the court found it imprudent that Transnet did not foresee a high likelihood of factual disputes surrounding the lease’s existence. The court further emphasized the point that parties must conduct reasonable inquiries before suing, particularly when claims are ostensibly controversial, as in this case, where Lorcom’s continuous occupancy indicated some form of agreement that was not merely speculative.

[10] Moreover, Transnet’s strategy to initiate eviction proceedings without accounting for these disputes exposed an oversight regarding its legal exposure and responsibilities stemming from its previous consents.

Conclusion

[11] The appeal was dismissed with costs, confirming that Transnet had not adequately demonstrated that the High Court had erred in establishing a bona fide dispute of fact or in exercising discretion to dismiss the application for eviction. The decision reaffirms the crucial position that in motion proceedings involving evictions, courts are primarily concerned with substantively resolved truthfulness and credibility of parties’ claims, contingent upon establishing common cause facts rather than mere assertions of ownership.

[12] Implications of this ruling underline the procedural limitations upon applicants who might initiate eviction without clarity on existing defenses, and reinforce the principle that bona fide disputes of fact need vigilant consideration to promote just outcomes in property-related litigation.

The order dismissing the appeal stands, with costs awarded to the respondents.
