Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2006

### Case Note on *Transnet Ltd v Tshabalala* [2006] SCA 25 (RSA)

#### F – Facts
In the matter of *Transnet Ltd t/a Metro Rail v Lazarus Tshabalala* ([1]), the respondent, Lazarus Tshabalala, initiated a lawsuit against the appellant, Transnet Ltd, in the High Court at Johannesburg, seeking damages amounting to R762,650.00 resulting from a train-related accident. The trial court, presided over by Berger AJ, was tasked with determining liability separately from other issues. It found both parties equally negligent, attributing fault to both for contributing to the injuries sustained by Tshabalala ([1]). Consequently, the damages awarded were halved, and Transnet Ltd was ordered to pay the trial costs.

On appeal, the limited issues addressed were the correctness of the trial court's finding of liability against Transnet Ltd, and whether the apportionment of fault was appropriate in light of the facts ([1]).

The incident occurred on 21 September 2001, when Tshabalala, after visiting a family member in Soshanguve, attempted to board a moving train at the Winternest train station after receiving erroneous directions. While chasing the train, he grasped a vertical handrail but fell onto the tracks, resulting in severe injuries ([1]–[4]). Both parties were found to have acted negligently: Tshabalala for trying to board a moving train, and Transnet Ltd for failing to close the train doors prior to departure ([5]–[9]).

#### I – Issues
The appeal raised two primary legal questions:

1. Was Transnet Ltd correctly held liable for Tshabalala’s injuries based on the facts as found by the trial court, alongside other consistent facts?
2. Was the trial court’s apportionment of fault, which found both parties equally negligent, appropriate given the circumstances of the case? ([1]).

#### R – Rules
The judgment relied upon various legal authorities including the *Apportionment of Damages Act 34 of 1956*, which governs the apportionment of damages based on the degree of negligence attributable to the parties ([8]). The court referenced *Road Accident Fund v Russel* ([7]), establishing causation principles where negligence is sufficiently linked to the injury for legal liability. Furthermore, the *Marine & Trade Insurance Co Ltd v Singh* case was cited, highlighting the relationship between omission and causation in determining liability ([7]).

The court emphasized that a defendant may not apportion damages if the injury was intentionally caused, as illustrated in *Minister van Wet en Orde v Ntsane* ([5]). The trial court's assessment of degrees of fault is typically not overturned unless there is significant discrepancy, per the precedent set in *Shield Insurance Co Ltd v Theron NO* ([8]).

#### A – Application
In evaluating the trial court's findings, the Supreme Court of Appeal assessed the conduct of both Tshabalala and Transnet Ltd against a standard of reasonableness. While the trial court concluded that both parties were equally negligent, the appellate court found this assessment problematic, arguing that a reasonable operator of a train would have recognized the dangers of operating with open doors ([9]).

The court acknowledged that Tshabalala acted negligently in trying to board a moving train, particularly given his intoxication, which heightened the risk associated with his actions ([9]). However, it maintained that the defendant's negligence in allowing the train to operate with open doors was a substantial contributing factor to the accident. The court referenced the evidence from Mr Emmanuel, which illustrated Tshabalala's precarious situation as he attempted to board the train ([9]). 

Ultimately, the appellate court diverged from the trial court's equal apportionment conclusion, determining that each party's degree of negligence differed significantly. Considering this, it deemed an equitable reduction of Tshabalala's damages by two-thirds appropriate instead of the trial court's fifty-fifty reduction ([10]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, modifying the order of the trial court. It determined that while both parties were negligent, the extent to which Tshabalala deviated from the standard of a reasonable person warranted a greater reduction in his damages claim ([10]). This ruling has significant implications for contributory negligence jurisprudence in South Africa, particularly regarding how the courts assess negligence and apportion liability. The court highlighted the necessity for a more nuanced understanding of how multiple factors, including intoxication, affect liability determinations in personal injury cases. The final ruling not only adjusted the financial compensation due to Tshabalala but also underscored the court’s commitment to ensuring fair assessments of negligence based on the facts presented.
