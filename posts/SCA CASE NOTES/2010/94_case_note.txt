Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2010

# Case Note: Calibre Clinical Consultants (Pty) Ltd v National Bargaining Council for the Road Freight Industry (410/09) [2010] ZASCA 94

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: 
The first appellant, Calibre Clinical Consultants (Pty) Ltd, and the second appellant, The Right to Care Consortium, appealed against a decision made by the National Bargaining Council for the Road Freight Industry (NBC) not to appoint their proposals for managing an HIV awareness program ([1]–[3]). The NBC, established under section 27 of the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995, sought to procure services for a wellness fund to manage HIV/AIDS impacts on road freight industry employees ([4]).

In 2007, the NBC invited proposals for service providers to manage its 'wellness fund' related to HIV prevention ([6]–[8]). Among the applicants were the partnership of the two appellants and a consortium. After reviewing the proposals, the NBC chose not to appoint any of the initial bidders and instead asked KPMG to identify another service provider, ultimately selecting HIV Managed Care Solutions (Pty) Ltd ([2], [16]). The appellants sought a review of these decisions in the High Court, claiming judicial review under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) but were unsuccessful ([3]).

### I – Issues: 
The central legal question was whether the decisions made by the NBC were subject to judicial review under the PAJA, specifically if the NBC was exercising a public function and whether its decisions could be characterized as administrative action ([19]–[22]). A further issue arose regarding whether the NBC's actions were procedurally fair, rational, or reasonable, given the context of the public interest and the implications of awarding a contract ([28]).

### R – Rules: 
The PAJA defines 'administrative action' as including decisions made by an organ of state exercising public power or performing a public function ([19]). The court considered judicial review principles established in several landmark cases across South Africa, the United States, Canada, and England, highlighting the necessity for a decision-making body to demonstrate features that indicate a governmental nature, such as accountability and public interest ([22]–[40]). Additionally, it referenced criteria for procedural fairness and rationality in administrative decisions ([54]).

### A – Application: 
In its analysis, the Supreme Court of Appeal determined that the NBC, despite being an established body per the Labour Relations Act, did not constitute an 'organ of state' under PAJA for the purpose of its procurement decisions ([41]). The court articulated that the NBC's processes, which included the procurement of services for its members—rather than for the public at large—were not subject to the same standards of accountability as governmental actions, particularly because they did not involve public funding or statutory regulations ([42]).

Additionally, regarding procedural fairness, the court deemed that while the council might ideally have invited the appellants for further representations, their previous opportunities to address concerns were sufficient under the circumstances ([49]–[56]). The court noted that the NBC had acted based on what it perceived as necessary risk management and that the rejection of proposals did not demonstrate irrationality, as the decision-making process was adequately justified by the facts presented ([61]–[63]).

### C – Conclusion: 
The appeal was dismissed, and the court held that the NBC's decisions to not appoint the appellants were not subject to judicial review, as they did not qualify as administrative action under PAJA. The court maintained the decision of the High Court and found no procedural unfairness or irrationality in the NBC’s decision-making process. The implications of this ruling highlight a narrower interpretation of PAJA’s reach concerning non-governmental entities, particularly in sectors represented by bargaining councils, which may limit the avenues for judicial review in similar contexts ([63]).
