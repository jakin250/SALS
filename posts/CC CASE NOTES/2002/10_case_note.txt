# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title:** Maluto Singo v The State  
**Court:** Constitutional Court of South Africa  
**Case Number:** CCT 49/01  
**Hearing Date:** 12 March 2002  
**Decision Date:** 12 June 2002  
**Judgment by:** Ngcobo J  

## F - Facts
[1] The appellant, Maluto Singo, was initially charged with common assault and malicious injury to property. On 1 November 1996, he was warned to appear in court on 17 January 1997. However, Singo failed to appear, leading to the activation of the provisions of Section 72(4) of the Criminal Procedure Act 1977 (CPA). He was arrested and eventually appeared in court on 4 January 1999, where he provided an explanation for his non-compliance, claiming he had settled the dispute with the complainant and believed both parties were to request that the charges be withdrawn. The presiding magistrate rejected this explanation, resulting in a conviction and a three-month prison sentence without the option of a fine.

[5] Singo appealed this decision at the Venda High Court, which overturned his conviction on three grounds, particularly focusing on the reverse onus created by Section 72(4) of the CPA, which the court found to be unconstitutional as it violated the right to a fair trial under Section 35(3) of the Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996. The state appealed to the Constitutional Court to confirm the High Court’s invalidation of the relevant provisions of the CPA.

## I - Issues
[7] The key issues before the Constitutional Court were:

(a) Whether the summary procedure mandated by Section 72(4) limited the right to a fair trial, specifically the right to be presumed innocent and to remain silent.  
(b) If a limitation existed, whether it was justifiable under Section 36(1) of the Constitution.  
(c) What appropriate remedies should be implemented if limitations were found to be unjustifiable.

## R - Rules
[15] Section 35(3) of the Constitution guarantees the right to a fair trial, including the right to be presumed innocent until proven guilty. Section 72(4) of the CPA allows for a summary procedure that requires an accused who fails to appear in court to satisfy the court that such failure was not due to their fault.

[18] The Court referenced previous judgments such as *S v Mamabolo* and *S v Zuma*, where it was established that provisions imposing a legal burden on an accused can infringe upon the presumption of innocence and the right to silence if the burden requires the accused to disprove essential elements of the offence.

## A - Application
[22] The Court found that the summary procedure indeed imposed a legal burden on the accused to prove a negative—namely, that their failure to appear was not due to their fault. This condition infringed on the right to be presumed innocent as it allowed for a conviction even in cases of reasonable doubt concerning the accused's fault.

Additionally, while recognizing the need for an effective legal system to deter behaviours that disrupt the administration of justice, the Court noted that such a limiting provision must be balanced with fundamental rights protections. The inherent reverse onus contradicted the requirement for the state to prove guilt beyond a reasonable doubt.

The necessity of preserving judicial integrity and public confidence in the legal system was acknowledged, recognizing that the failure to appear affects judicial processes. However, the Court emphasized that these considerations do not justify undue infringement upon constitutional rights.

## C - Conclusion
[44] The Constitutional Court concluded that although Section 72(4) serves legitimate social objectives, the reverse onus it imposes on the accused is unconstitutional and cannot be justified under the criteria established in Section 36(1). 

The section was not struck down entirely; instead, the Court adopted a remedial approach to read in necessary modifications to transform it into an evidentiary burden rather than a legal one. This provided a path forward for the application of the law while ensuring compliance with constitutional rights.

The final ruling resulted in the recognition of expanded protections for accused individuals while reaffirming the necessity for an efficient legal system. The adjustments also ensured that individuals are not wrongfully convicted simply due to an inability to prove a negative, thus preserving the foundational principle of presumption of innocence within South African law.
