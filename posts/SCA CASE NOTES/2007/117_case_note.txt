Source Folder: 2007

## Case Note: Strydom v Liebenberg [2007] SCA 117 (RSA)

### FIRAC Analysis

#### F – Facts
The respondent, B.J. Liebenberg, initiated a vindicatory action in the Pretoria High Court against the appellants, G.A. Strydom and W.D. La Grange, seeking the return of various game species alleged to be unlawfully retained on the appellants' properties ([1]). The respondent was the original proprietor of portions 11 and 14 of the farm Blaauwbank and the sole shareholder of Klein Bokkeplek Boerdery (Pty) Ltd, which owned adjacent portions 2 and 7. After erecting a game-proof fence around his properties in 1997, the respondent bought several game species, including ‘rooibokke’ and ‘blesbokke’, valued at R250 000, and operated a game-hunting business ([2], [3]).

In 2001, following the liquidation of his company due to financial issues, portions 2 and 7 were sold to the defendants, with the liquidator explicitly confirming that the sale did not include the game ([4]). The first defendant subsequently erected a fence, barring the respondent from accessing any game residing on these properties. The respondent contended that he retained ownership of the game, even after the sale, as no ownership transfer had taken place under South African law ([5]).

The dispute escalated as the respondent later sold portions 11 and 14 to third parties, who were to assume ownership only after the erection of a dividing fence between portion 11 and the second defendant’s property ([6]). This raised questions regarding the timing of delivery and control over the game among the intermingled properties, particularly whether the game could be identified as belonging to the respondent ([7], [8]).

The appellants argued that they were bona fide possessors of the game once the fences were erected and claimed that the progeny of the game could not be distinguished from the originals, thereby obstructing the respondent's vindicatory action ([9]). They further contended that if the respondent's ownership remained intact, they should only be liable to return specific identified game ([10]).

#### I – Issues
The primary legal issues raised in this appeal are as follows:

1. Whether the respondent retained ownership of the game after selling portions 11 and 14 and while the game roamed freely between properties post-liquidation.
2. Whether the appellants, as subsequent purchasers of the properties, can claim bona fide possessorship and right to the game.
3. Whether the respondent could recover the totality of the game present on the appellants’ properties given the intermingling of game species across boundaries.

These issues hinge on interpretations of property rights and the legal principles surrounding ownership, delivery, and possession in the context of South African law ([1]–[10]).

#### R – Rules
The legal framework applicable to this case includes the principles of possession, ownership, and the rules governing vindicatory action as delineated in the common law of South Africa. Specifically, the following rules are applicable:

1. **Ownership and Control**: Ownership is determined by demonstrable control over property (Corpe v Overton [1833] 10 Bing 252).
2. **Vindicatory Action**: A vindicatory action allows an owner to reclaim possession of their property from another party wrongfully holding it ([6]).
3. **Identification of Property**: For ownership claims involving multiple titles and intermingled goods, proper distinction and clear identification of the property are paramount (Davis v Van Dyk 1953 (3) SA 885 (A)).
4. **Bona Fide Possession**: The bona fide possessor of a property or item has certain rights concerning that property over those who are not lawful owners ([9]).

These principles were vital in assessing the ownership claims and the defendants’ assertions regarding their possession of the game ([5], [11]).

#### A – Application
In applying these principles to the facts, the Court determined that:

1. The respondent had not relinquished ownership of the game at any point, as evidenced by his control, including offering the game as collateral for a loan ([5]). The fact that the game was contained within fenced areas further supported his ownership claim, countering the appellants’ argument of res nullius.
   
2. The Court found that the timing of the 'delivery' was critical; since the erection of the fence signified a definitive point of delineation between properties, the game remaining on portion 7, after the subsequent sale to third parties, was deemed still owned by the respondent ([8]).

3. The appellants’ claim of bona fide possession was dismissed based on the evidence showing they were aware of the respondent's ownership rights. Neither defendant was able to demonstrate that they mistakenly believed they were entitled to the game ([9]). Therefore, their arguments surrounding the progeny of the game and claims of value were rendered moot, as the Court recognized that they had no lawful claim to ownership.

4. In examining the respondent's claims for monetary value of the game, the Court determined that the evidence provided was insufficient to establish the exact numbers or apportionment of the game existing across the properties, leading to the denial of the cross-appeal for compensation based on unreturned game ([11]).

#### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the High Court’s order mandating the appellants to return the game present on their properties, reinforcing the respondent’s rights under vindicatory action. The cross-appeal for the value of the game was dismissed due to the lack of sufficient evidence to coalesce claims between the defendants.

The implications of this judgment underscore the importance of maintaining clear boundaries and possession in property law, particularly concerning movable property like game. The ruling reaffirms the notion that ownership remains intact until deliberately and expressly relinquished, emphasizing the nuances of ownership rights in situations involving complex property transactions. The decision fortifies the position of original owners against subsequent purchasers, spotlighting the necessity for clarity in property dealings.

The appeal and cross-appeal were dismissed with costs ([12]).
