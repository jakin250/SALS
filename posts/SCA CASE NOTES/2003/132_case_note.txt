Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2003

# FIRAC Case Note: Mashau Rashavha v HJ Van Rensburg

## F – Facts

The appellant, Mashau Rashavha, was a former farm worker employed by the respondent, HJ Van Rensburg, and appealed against a decision by the Land Claims Court which confirmed a magistrate’s eviction order from her dwelling on the farm Sandfontein, located in the Northern Province ([1]). The eviction order stemmed from her retrenchment by the respondent in October 1998, which resulted in the termination of her right to reside on the farm by December 1998. She, along with three other farm workers, resisted the eviction application but was ultimately unsuccessful, with the Chief Magistrate granting the eviction orders in January 2001 ([3]). The Land Claims Court confirmed the eviction of the other workers and referred the appellant’s case back to the Chief Magistrate for reconsideration regarding the applicability of s 8(4) of the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 ([4]).

The appellant sought condonation for the late filing of her notice of appeal. This request was opposed by the respondent, who claimed that the delay was unjustified and that the appellant’s case lacked merit ([2]). After receiving leave to appeal on 29 January 2002, the appellant failed to submit her notice of appeal within the required timeframe, doing so instead on 8 May 2002, with further delays in the application for condonation ([7]). The delay attributed to the appellant’s attorney awaiting outcomes for the other appellants' applications was deemed more a matter of expedience than justification for the substantial delay ([8]).

## I – Issues

The primary issues that arose from this case included:
1. Whether the appellant was entitled to condonation for the late filing of her appeal ([2]).
2. The interpretation and application of s 8(4) of the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997 in relation to the appellant’s right to residence ([12]).
3. The consideration of comparative hardship under s 10(3) of the Act ([16]).

## R – Rules

The legal framework pertinent to this case is mainly derived from the Extension of Security of Tenure Act 62 of 1997. Key provisions include:

- **Section 8(1)** specifies that an occupier's right of residence may be terminated on lawful grounds but must be just and equitable, taking into account factors like the fairness of the agreement or law relied upon, the conduct of parties, and comparative hardship ([11]).

- **Section 8(4)** outlines that the right of residence of an occupier who has lived on a property for at least ten years and is over 60 years old, or unable to work due to health issues, cannot be terminated without just cause ([11]).

- **Section 10(3)** establishes conditions under which eviction may be granted, considering the availability of alternative accommodation and the interests of both the occupier and the property owner ([16]).

Precedents relevant to the court's evaluation of condonation included *Finbro Furnishers (Pty) Ltd v Registrar of Deeds, Bloemfontein* and *Darries v Sheriff, Magistrate’s Court, Wynberg*, which highlighted the importance of the prospects of success on appeal ([10]).

## A – Application

In evaluating the request for condonation, the Court scrutinized the reasons for the delay in filing the notice of appeal. Although the appellant's legal representatives failed in their duty to act promptly, leading to considerable delays, the Court took into account the appellant’s circumstances, including her lack of education and understanding of legal processes ([9]). 

Considering the merits of the appeal itself, the court found that the appellant could not establish her eligibility under s 8(4) due to age, as she was not yet 60 at the time of her eviction ([14]). The argument that age might include those nearing 60 was rejected, as the language of the statute was deemed unambiguous and did not allow for expansive interpretation in this regard ([14]).

Regarding the issue of comparative hardship, the court noted that the appellant failed to demonstrate efforts to secure alternative accommodation, despite the finding that her family resided in Maelula, which could provide support ([18]). In line with the findings of the lower court, the balance of interests was struck in favor of the property rights of the respondent over the potential hardship faced by the appellant. The Land Claims Court had indicated that the extended time the respondent had already forgone the exclusive use of the dwelling was significant ([19]).

## C – Conclusion

The Court ultimately denied the appellant's application for condonation on the grounds that the delay was inexcusable and that there were no sufficient prospects of success on appeal, thereby upholding the lower courts’ decisions regarding both the interpretation of the Act and the balancing of hardship ([22]). The judgment emphasized the necessity for legal representatives to act diligently while also recognizing the socio-economic disadvantages faced by the appellant ([9]). The implications of this ruling reaffirm the weight given to statutory interpretations grounded in clarity and the need for meaningful engagement in legal processes by legal representatives on behalf of vulnerable individuals.
