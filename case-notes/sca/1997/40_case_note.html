DIE HOOGSTE HOFF VAN APPèL VAN SUID-AFRIKA

[Case Citation] DIE HOOGSTE HOFF VAN APPèL VAN SUID-AFRIKA, Saak Nos 499/93 & 510/93, Judgment Date: 12 May 1997.

Supreme Court of Appeal 1997

[Case Citation]

DIE HOOGSTE HOFF VAN APPèL VAN SUID-AFRIKA, Saak Nos 499/93 & 510/93, Judgment Date: 12 May 1997.

F – Facts

The appellants, Freddy Dumako Masango, Phillip Seiko Phiri, Willem William Masilela, and Willem M. Abena, were implicated in a serious crime involving housebreaking, robbery with aggravating circumstances, and murder. On 16 April 1991, they broke into the home of a 76-year-old man, subsequently robbing him of R30,000 in cash and various items. During the robbery, the victim was bound and strangled, leading to his death ([paras 2–3]).

The appellants were found guilty by the Transvaal Provincial Division, receiving death sentences for murder, and varying prison terms for housebreaking and robbery. Appeals were lodged against the murder convictions and the sentences imposed. Initially, the appeal regarding the murder conviction was retracted by the first and second appellants, leaving the focus on the appropriateness of the sentences ([paras 2–6]).

The court lacked conclusive evidence linking each appellant directly to the act of murder; however, it was acknowledged through confessions and evidence that they collectively participated in the housebreaking and were aware of the potential for violence against the inhabitants ([paras 4–5]). The prosecution provided testimonies indicating premeditated actions, such as cutting phone lines to prevent calls for help, establishing an unequivocal intent to surprise the victims and neutralize potential resistance ([paras 6–7]).

I – Issues

The central legal issues presented were:

  1. Whether the appellants could be held liable for murder despite differing levels of involvement in the actual fatal act.
  2. The validity of the death sentences imposed, particularly in light of developments in constitutional law regarding capital punishment, following the Constitutional Court’s ruling in S v Makwanyane which deemed the death penalty unconstitutional ([paras 6–8]).

R – Rules

The court referenced established legal principles surrounding common purpose in criminal law. The doctrine of dolus eventualis was highlighted, which implies that individuals can be held criminally liable for foreseeable consequences of their joint actions—even if they did not directly commit the act causing death ([paras 6–8]).

The court also examined precedents from earlier cases, namely S v Malinga and S v Shaik and Others, regarding the requisite mental state for murder convictions, which require proving that the accused must have anticipated the possibility of violence resulting from their intended crimes ([paras 6–8]).

Moreover, the court addressed the implications of the decision in S v Makwanyane, effectively ruling out the death penalty as a legitimate sentence post-6 June 1995. Consequently, any death sentences previously imposed were to be set aside and replaced with appropriate sentences ([paras 8–9]).

A – Application

In considering the appellants' liability for murder, the court evaluated the circumstances surrounding the crime, reiterating that the inherent risk of violence was evident, given the premeditated nature of the offenses. The actions taken—such as cutting the phone wires and entering through an unobtrusive method—suggested a conscious intention to initiate a violent confrontation, meeting the threshold for dolus eventualis ([paras 6–8]).

Despite the lack of direct evidence linking all the appellants to the killing, the cumulative awareness and acceptance of the risk of potential lethal consequences during the crime was deemed sufficient to uphold the murder convictions. Consequently, the reasoning was characterized by the court's interpretation of the shared criminal intent and foresight of violence during their collaborative endeavor ([paras 8–9]).

On the matter of sentencing, the court recognized that the imposition of the death penalty was no longer tenable under the constitutional framework established by Makwanyane. Therefore, the court opted to invalidate the death sentences and remanded the case for the imposition of new, constitutionally compliant sentences in relation to the murder convictions ([paras 8–9]).

C – Conclusion

The court ultimately affirmed the murder convictions of the third and fourth appellants, citing sufficient legal grounds for their culpability under the doctrine of common purpose and dolus eventualis. However, the death sentences previously imposed on all appellants were struck down, reflecting a significant shift in constitutional law regarding capital punishment.

The decision necessitated a return to the trial court for re-sentencing, paving the way for a new legal interpretation that aligns with the constitutional mandate against the death penalty. This case is of notable significance in emphasizing the evolving human rights standards within South African criminal law and setting a precedent for future cases involving the death penalty ([paras 8–9]).
