Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2015

# Case Note: City of Cape Town v South African National Roads Authority Limited & Others (20786/14) [2015] ZASCA 58

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts
This appeal arises from a significant case involving the City of Cape Town (hereinafter referred to as "the City") and the South African National Roads Authority Limited (SANRAL), concerning the N1/N2 Winelands Paarl Highway Toll Project. SANRAL, as a state organ defined under section 239 of the South African Constitution, awarded a tender to Protea Parkways Consortium (PPC) as the preferred bidder for the project, with Overberg Consortium designated as the reserve bidder ([paras 1–2]).

The City initiated a review application in the Western Cape High Court, contesting SANRAL's decision to award the contract to PPC, invoking Rule 53 of the Uniform Rules of Court. SANRAL provided the City with a two-part administrative record, labeled "non-confidential" and "confidential," which became the basis for a dispute regarding the nature and scope of the Rule 53 record ([paras 2–3]).

Following an exchange of letters, the City requested to make certain documents publicly available for the sake of transparency and public interest. In response, SANRAL sought an order for redaction of parts of the City's supplementary founding affidavit (SFA), accompanying its application with assertions of confidentiality regarding various documents and information ([paras 3–4]).

The High Court under Binns-Ward J ultimately ruled in favor of the City, declaring that SANRAL had failed to prove the confidentiality or necessity of secrecy for the documents in question. However, the High Court inexplicably issued further orders establishing an "implied undertaking rule" restricting the disclosure of these documents pending a formal hearing. This pivotal ruling gave rise to the current appeal ([paras 6–7], [9]).
 
### I – Issues
The core issues for determination in this appeal are:
1. Whether the high court was correct in adopting the implied undertaking rule regarding confidentiality and if so, whether it constituted a proper exercise of its jurisdiction under section 173 of the Constitution ([paras 7–8]).
2. Whether the high court’s interpretation of Rule 62(7) limiting access to court records was correct and consistent with the principles of open justice and public accountability ([paras 9–10]).
3. Did the high court err in its findings regarding the confidentiality of SANRAL’s documents, as asserted under Rule 53? ([paras 6–8], [40–48]).

### R – Rules
The applicable rules and legal principles include:
1. **Section 173 of the Constitution**: Confers inherent power to the courts to regulate their processes, also allowing for the development of the common law while emphasizing justice.
2. **Rule 53** of the Uniform Rules of Court: Governs the procedure for bringing review applications and mandates the provision of the administrative record by the decision-making authority.
3. **Rule 62(7)**: Concerns access to documents filed with the court, delineating that only parties and persons with a direct legal interest are permitted access.
4. **Open Justice Principle**: Enshrined within South African law, mandating that judicial proceedings should be conducted openly and allowing public scrutiny, founded on sections 32 and 34 of the Constitution regarding the right to access information and the right to a fair public hearing ([paras 12–20]).

### A – Application
In its evaluation, the Supreme Court of Appeal emphasized that the open court principle is rooted deeply in democratic values and the rule of law, promoting transparency, accountability, and public trust ([paras 11–12]). The Court critically assessed the high court’s imposition of the implied undertaking rule, identifying that this rule had not been adequately pleaded or invoked by SANRAL, thus representing an impermissible overreach of judicial authority ([paras 10, 27–30]). 

The Court concurs with the high court's decision to dismiss SANRAL’s application based on its failure to establish a legitimate claim for confidentiality. Yet, it expressed serious concern regarding the additional rulings made by the high court creating a default of secrecy that drastically limited access to court records, essentially undermining the very essence of open justice and eroding public accountability ([paras 39–48]).

The Court elucidated that while confidentiality might be necessary in some instances, these must be justified on a case-by-case basis rather than establishing an overarching principle of secrecy. The applications of Rule 62(7) had to be construed in alignment with the constitutional ethos that prioritizes transparency and accountability over strict privacy claims ([paras 11, 20–25]). 

### C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, concluding that the high court erred both in adopting the implied undertaking rule and in unduly limiting public access to judicial documents and proceedings. The Court emphasized that all court records must be presumed open to public access unless a compelling case for secrecy is established. The judgment contained a broader implication for future cases, asserting that courts must tread carefully in imposing secrecy measures that infringe upon the foundational tenets of open justice, accountability, and transparency ([paras 48–49]).

Consequently, the order of the lower court was set aside, with costs awarded to the City, including the costs associated with three counsel, affirming the right to public scrutiny concerning decisions involving state organs and their dealings ([Order and Conclusion]).
