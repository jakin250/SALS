Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note

### Case Title
Afrocentrics Projects and Services (Pty) Ltd t/a Innovative Distribution v State Information Technology Agency (SITA) SOC Ltd and Others [2023] ZACC 2

### Court
Constitutional Court of South Africa

### Citation
CCT 54/22

### Coram 
Zondo CJ, Maya DCJ, Baqwa AJ, Kollapen J, Madlanga J, Majiedt J, Mathopo J, Mbatha AJ, Mhlantla J, Rogers J, Tshiqi J

### Date Decided
24 January 2023

---

## F – Facts
The applicant, Afrocentrics Projects and Services (Pty) Ltd (Afrocentrics), entered into a procurement agreement with the State Information Technology Agency (SITA) and Micro Focus Software (Ireland) Limited (the second respondent) on 1 November 2017. The agreement was intended for the procurement of software and related services for state organs ([para 3]). Afrocentrics was appointed as a Fulfilment Agent under this agreement but had its appointments terminated by the second respondent on 3 July 2019. In response, the applicant sought relief from the High Court to set aside the termination of the agreements and submitted various claims including a declaration of invalidity concerning the agreements ([paras 4–6]).

The applicant's principal application was bifurcated into two parts: Part A sought a suspension of the SITA agreement pending the outcome of Part B, which sought substantive relief including a review of the termination of agreements and compensation ([paras 7–8]). However, the second respondent contended that the applicant’s proceedings were irregular and violated the Uniform Rules of Court, specifically Rule 30, alleging issues such as lack of clarity in the relief sought and improper claims for damages ([paras 9–11]).

The High Court ultimately concluded that the application was irregular and made an order on this basis without directly addressing the irregularities ([paras 12–14]). The applicant's application for leave to appeal against this order was dismissed by the High Court and subsequently the Supreme Court of Appeal, leading to the present direct appeal to the Constitutional Court seeking clarity ([paras 15–16]).

## I – Issues
The case primarily raises three critical legal issues: 

1. Did the High Court make a competent order concerning the alleged irregularities in proceedings, and did it adequately address the outcomes of the rule 30 application? ([para 19]).
2. Can a court adjudicate substantive matters within the context of a Rule 30 application, or must it limit itself to procedural irregularities? ([para 20]).
3. What is the effect of ambiguity in court orders regarding clarity and finality for the parties involved? ([para 24]).

## R – Rules
The legal framework governing the case includes:

1. **Uniform Rules of Court**, particularly Rule 30, which addresses actions deemed irregular ([para 10]).
2. **Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000**, which underpins grounds for judicial review in administrative action ([para 6]).
3. **Section 34 of the Constitution**, guaranteeing the right to a fair public hearing by a court ([para 23]).
4. Precedents such as *Eke v Parsons* [2015] ZACC 30, which discusses the need for clarity and finality in court orders ([para 1]).

## A – Application
The court first addressed the jurisdictional issues, determining that the matter warranted its review due to its constitutional significance and the potential impact on the administration of justice ([paras 23–25]). The judgment emphasized that court orders must provide clarity, bringing finality to the disputes presented. The court found that the High Court's order was ambiguous as it identified an irregularity but did not specify whether the application was set aside or how it was to proceed, which contradicts the requirements of Rule 30 ([paras 26–30]).

Furthermore, the court analyzed whether the High Court had impermissibly adjudicated substantive issues in a Rule 30 application, asserting that such matters should be separated from inquiries about procedural irregularities ([paras 26–30]). The court concluded that the High Court had acted outside its powers by failing to clearly indicate the steps to follow after declaring the application irregular, thus failing to uphold the procedural integrity necessary for judicial review ([para 30–31]).

## C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court upheld the appeal, asserting that the order of the High Court was indeed set aside due to its failure to adequately determine the rule 30 application and to provide a definitive, enforceable resolution to the matters at hand. Thus, the matter was remitted to the High Court for proper adjudication of the rule 30 application ([para 33]).

The implications of the Court’s ruling underscore the necessity for clarity and finality in court orders to avoid ambiguity in legal proceedings, emphasizing the broader significance of maintaining proper procedural standards in judicial review applications. The lack of an order explicitly addressing the direction of proceedings left the parties with uncertainty, contrary to their right to a justiciable dispute resolution as enshrined in the Constitution ([para 30]). The Court made no order as to costs, highlighting that the complexities arose primarily from the High Court's actions ([para 32]).
