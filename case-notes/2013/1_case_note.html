FIRAC Case Note for Motswagae and Others v Rustenburg Local Municipality and Another [2013] ZACC 1

Constitutional Court 2013 [2013] ZACC 1 F – Facts: This case arises from an interd

FIRAC Case Note for Motswagae and Others v Rustenburg Local Municipality and Another [2013] ZACC 1

F – Facts:
This case arises from an interdict application initiated in the North West High Court, Mahikeng, by the applicants, led by Pontsho Doreen Motswagae, against the Rustenburg Local Municipality and its contractor, Promptique TR 9 CC. The applicants occupy dilapidated houses on land owned by the North West Province, where a redevelopment plan initiated by the municipality seeks to demolish the existing structures for new housing projects ([para 1][2]).

In April 2009, the municipality employed a contractor to commence excavation next to the first applicant's home, leading to significant disturbance and the exposure of the foundations of the house. Despite the residents' protests and requests to cease the work, further excavation was carried out in May 2009. This prompted the applicants to seek a legal interdict against the continued construction works, asserting that such actions violated their rights to peaceful occupation under the Constitution ([paras 3][4]).

The High Court dismissed the applicants' interdict application, primarily ruling that their right to remain in their homes was not infringed and that they had not filed objections at the inception of the redevelopment plan ([para 5]). Following unsuccessful appeals at both the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal, the applicants approached the Constitutional Court for leave to appeal, raising significant constitutional questions regarding their rights ([paras 6–9]).

I – Issues:
The central legal issues presented to the Constitutional Court include:

  1. Whether section 26(3) of the Constitution or any other law affords the applicants the right to undisturbed occupation of their homes without prior court authorization.
  2. Whether the premises occupied by the applicants qualifies as "homes" as defined by section 26(3).
  3. Whether the actions undertaken by the municipality constituted unlawful interference with the applicants' right to peaceful occupation of their homes.
  4. Whether the municipality's conduct could be considered reasonable in light of the lack of a court order evicting the applicants prior to the commencement of construction work ([para 7]).

R – Rules:
The primary legal framework governing this case includes:

  • Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996, particularly section 26 which encompasses the right to adequate housing and stipulates that no one may be evicted from their home without a court order.
  • Relevant provisions from the National Housing Act 107 of 1997 and the National Housing Code, which articulate the state’s responsibilities towards adequate housing.
  • International human rights instruments such as the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights (ICCPR) and the International Covenant on Economic, Social and Cultural Rights (ICESCR), both of which underpin housing rights and protection against arbitrary eviction ([para 11]).

A – Application:
The court analyzed the scope of section 26(3) to determine whether it provided sufficient legal protection for the applicants against unapproved disturbances. The judgment unequivocally affirms that the implication of section 26(3) is to grant occupiers the right to peaceful and undisturbed occupation of their homes in the absence of a valid court directive allowing for eviction or disturbance ([para 12]).

The Court found that the excavation undertaken by the municipality constituted a significant interference with the applicants’ right to their homes, amounting to a form of eviction. It rejected the municipality's claim that it had a servitudinal right to undertake construction without consent or a court order, firmly stating that self-help is not authorized by the rule of law ([para 14][15]).

Moreover, the court noted that the municipality was aware of the potential violation of the applicants' rights, evidenced by its offer of alternative accommodation, indicating recognition of their prior peaceful occupation being compromised ([para 16]).

The Court concluded that the interdict sought by the applicants was necessary and warranted, as they exhibited a clear right to protection against the unlawful disturbances, would suffer irreparable harm if the actions continued, and lacked a suitable alternative remedy ([para 18]).

C – Conclusion:
The Constitutional Court upheld the appeal, stating that the Rustenburg Local Municipality acted unlawfully in permitting construction without a court order. The Court granted the interdict sought by the applicants, prohibiting any further work on their properties without their consent or a court directive ([para 20]). Furthermore, the judgment reinforces the imperative for municipalities to respect housing rights and comply with constitutional dictates concerning eviction procedures. The implications of this ruling are significant for the protection of housing rights in South Africa, affirming that residents cannot be forcibly evicted or have their peaceful occupation disturbed without due legal process, emphasizing the legal protections afforded to those in precarious housing situations ([para 19]).
