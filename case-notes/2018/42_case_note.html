Constitutional Court

Rahube v Rahube and Others [2018] ZACC 42

In August 2009, the first respondent initiated eviction proceedings against the applicant, which led her to raise the constitutional invalidity of section 2(1) of the Upgrading Act in opposition to the eviction ([paras 10–11]).

Constitutional Court 2018 [2018] ZACC 42

F – Facts

The Constitutional Court of South Africa considered the matter of Rahube v Rahube and Others, where the applicant, Matshabelle Mary Rahube, challenged the constitutionality of section 2(1) of the Upgrading of Land Tenure Rights Act 112 of 1991 ([paras 1–3]). The applicant and her brother, Hendsrine Rahube (the first respondent), had lived on the property located at Stand 2328 Block B, Mabopane, for decades. The applicant contended that following her grandmother's death in 1978, her brother was nominated as the holder of a deed of grant issued under ~Proclamation R293~ (subordinate legislation from the apartheid era), which did not permit African women to hold formal titles to property ([paras 6–9]).

In August 2009, the first respondent initiated eviction proceedings against the applicant, which led her to raise the constitutional invalidity of section 2(1) of the Upgrading Act in opposition to the eviction ([paras 10–11]). The High Court ultimately declared section 2(1) constitutionally invalid as it automatically converted land tenure rights into ownership without providing opportunity for other affected parties to assert their rights ([paras 12–14]). The High Court’s decision allowed for further claims for ownership but did not grant immediate relief, prompting this case's escalation to the Constitutional Court ([paras 12–14]).

I – Issues

The Constitutional Court was faced with three primary issues ([paras 3, 18]):

  1. Should the High Court’s order declaring section 2(1) invalid be confirmed?
  2. What should the just and equitable remedy be if the order is confirmed?
  3. How should the issue of costs be handled?

R – Rules

The court relied on relevant provisions of the Constitution, including:

  • Section 9(1): Ensuring everyone is equal before the law ([para 36]).
  • Section 25: The right to property ([para 35]).
  • Section 33: Right to just administrative action ([para 53]).
  • Section 172(1)(b): Pertaining to making just and equitable orders upon declaring an Act unconstitutional ([paras 60–62]).

The court referred to precedent such as Harksen v Lane NO for evaluating equality claims ([paras 36, 42]) and the historical context of apartheid legislation, referencing how legislative frameworks prioritised male land ownership ([paras 22–23, 28–35]).

A – Application

Upon analyzing section 2(1) of the Upgrading Act, the Court found that the automatic conversion of land tenure rights into ownership constituted unfair discrimination against women, particularly in light of the historical context where such laws excluded them from property ownership ([para 44]). The respondents contended that oversight mechanisms existed within the Act; however, the Court rejected these arguments, indicating that the lack of an equitable review process for prospective claimants constituted a procedural violation, undermining the principles of justice and equality ([paras 54–58]).

The court emphasized that the applicant’s longstanding residential occupation held inherent rights which the automatic upgrading system undermined, further discriminating against women by consolidating ownership solely in the hands of male relatives ([paras 39–41, 44]).

Moreover, important principles regarding retrospective and prospective application of unconstitutional provisions came into play. The High Court’s decision to apply the order retrospectively from 27 April 1994, aligned with the trajectory of women’s rights progression, was affirmed by the Constitutional Court. This was deemed essential for providing effective relief ([paras 61–63]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court unanimously confirmed the High Court's declaration of constitutional invalidity regarding section 2(1) of the Upgrading Act, recognizing that it entrenched discriminatory practices rooted in the apartheid system ([paras 70–74]). The Court's findings reinforced the importance of equitable access to property rights, stressing that the persistent legacies of discrimination must be actively addressed through legislative reform.

The order included interdicting the first respondent from selling or encumbering the property until Parliament could substantiate a constitutional framework ([paras 75, 2d]). The third respondent, as the minister responsible for land reform, was ordered to pay the applicant’s legal costs, underlining the government's obligation to rectify historical injustices ([paras 72–74]).

This case illustrates crucial developments in South African constitutional jurisprudence, directly addressing the intersections of gender, race, and property rights within the context of land reform, and emphasizing that legislative correctness must align with the constitutional mandate to promote equality ([paras 73–74]).
