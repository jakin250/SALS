Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note: TMT Services & Supplies (Pty) Ltd t/a Traffic Management Technologies v MEC: Department of Transport, Province of KwaZulu-Natal and Others

**Court**: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa  
**Case No**: 1059/2020  
**Judgment Date**: 15 March 2022  
**Coram**: Saldulker, Schippers, Plasket, Hughes JJA, Matojane AJA  

## F – Facts ([paras 1–6])
In 2017, the KwaZulu-Natal Department of Transport issued Tender ZNB1366/17T for the "Supply of Integrated Traffic Contravention Management Systems to the Road Traffic Inspectorate." The tender included bids for services encompassing an integrated camera network, traffic contravention management, a fines recovery system, and associated skills transfer components ([para 1]). 

The appellant, TMT Services & Supplies (Pty) Ltd (TMT), applied for the tender but was excluded early in the bidding process, with the contract awarded to MTM KZN Traffix (Pty) Ltd (Traffix) by the Head of the Department of Transport (HOD) ([para 2]). Following the withdrawal of an earlier application for review by another unsuccessful bidder, TMT subsequently launched its own review application in the Western Cape High Court ([para 3]). 

The respondents—comprising the MEC for Transport, the HOD, Traffix, and others—raised a jurisdictional objection to the Western Cape court's authority to hear the case. The Western Cape High Court dismissed TMT’s application with costs, asserting that TMT failed to demonstrate jurisdiction ([paras 4–3]).

## I – Issues ([paras 16–20])
The primary legal questions considered by the Supreme Court included:
1. **The interpretation of the definition of "court" under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA)**: Specifically, whether jurisdiction is confined to certain courts based on additional considerations beyond the PAJA’s explicit provisions.
2. **The implications of concurrent jurisdiction**: Whether the Western Cape court could refuse to exercise jurisdiction given that both it and the KwaZulu-Natal court had the competence to adjudicate the matter.
3. **Abuse of process allegations**: Whether TMT's choice of the Western Cape court constituted an abuse of process.
4. **Judicial discretion under section 6 of the PAJA**: Whether courts possess the discretion to decline to exercise jurisdiction in review applications.

## R – Rules ([paras 7–15])
The PAJA, particularly sections 6 and 8, governs judicial reviews of administrative action. Section 1 defines a “court” within the scope of PAJA as including the Constitutional Court, High Courts, or designated Magistrates' Courts based on specified jurisdictional criteria ([para 12]). 

The PAJA aims to concretely realize the constitutional rights under section 33, ensuring lawful, reasonable, and procedurally fair administrative action. The court clarified that the PAJA’s provisions are self-contained and do not rely on the common law or the Superior Courts Act’s jurisdictional aspects ([paras 7–11]). 

The Superior Courts Act's section 21([para 15]) confirms the jurisdiction of high courts in relation to civil disputes; however, it does not limit jurisdiction as established under the PAJA, which expressly provides for expansive jurisdiction based on the defined circumstances of residence, location of the administrator's principal office, or the effects of the administrative action ([para 28]).

## A – Application ([paras 30–40])
The Supreme Court emphasized that the jurisdiction of the Western Cape court was valid as TMT was domiciled there, despite the administrative action occurring in KwaZulu-Natal. The court criticized the lower court for conflating jurisdiction with considerations of "convenience" and "common sense," which do not apply under the explicit terms of the PAJA ([para 27]). 

Additionally, the court reaffirmed the principle that parties may select their preferred forum when concurrent jurisdictions exist, and the Western Cape court had an obligation to hear the case unless an abuse of process was proven ([paras 31–39]). The court clarified that exercising the choice of forum provided by law cannot constructively lead to a claim of abuse unless shown that a party sought extraneous purposes through litigation ([para 37]). 

The respondents' assertion of potential abuse was found to lack evidentiary support, and no substantial basis for claiming that TMT's application was extrinsic to the goals of promoting truth or justice was established ([para 38]).

## C – Conclusion ([paras 41–42])
The Supreme Court of Appeal ruled that the Western Cape court maintained jurisdiction over TMT's application and could not decline to hear the matter in the absence of demonstrated abuse. It explicitly overturned the lower court's ruling and determined that the case needed to be remitted for substantive resolution ([para 42]). 

The implications of this judgment reinforce the self-contained nature of the PAJA in establishing jurisdiction for administrative reviews, promoting effective access to courts, and clarifying procedural rights for parties involved in administrative matters under South African law. 

### Order:
1. The appeal is upheld with costs.
2. The lower court's order is set aside, affirming jurisdiction.
3. The matter is remitted back to the High Court for hearing on the merits.

This case note illustrates the court's firm stance on jurisdictional matters under the PAJA, emphasizing the importance of the statutory framework in administrative law and the ensuring of access to judicial recourse.
