# Case Note: McMillan v Bate Chubb & Dickson Incorporated ([2021] ZASCA 45)

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–12])

In the matter of **McMillan v Bate Chubb & Dickson Incorporated**, the appellant, Bruce Gordon McMillan, initiated legal action against the respondent, a law firm, in the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, alleging damages for breach of an oral mandate, specifically regarding the drafting of an antenuptial contract. The action was commenced on **13 October 2017**. The respondent contended that the appellant’s claim had prescribed, filing a special plea to this effect ([para 1]).

The pertinent background includes the appellant entering into a mandate with the respondent in November **1998** for the preparation of an antenuptial contract. This contract was later declared void by a court ruling on **18 October 2016**, primarily due to its contradictory clauses ([paras 5–10]). The respondent asserted that the prescription period started on **9 or 12 May 2014**, when the appellant was allegedly made aware of the possible invalidity of the contract ([paras 3–4]). The respondent maintained that the three-year prescription period ended in May 2017, well before the summons was issued ([paras 3, 9]).

The case was heard via a special separation order by the court a quo (Makaula J), which determined the prescription issue independently before addressing other relevant issues ([paras 1–2]).

### I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 3–4, 30–34])

The primary legal issue at hand was whether the appellant's claim had prescribed by the time the summons was issued, particularly focusing on the point at which prescription began to run concerning the appellant's claim for damages against the respondent. The key question revolved around the interpretation of **section 12(3)** of the **Prescription Act 68 of 1969**, specifically regarding when a creditor's knowledge of all facts necessary to institute legal action is deemed sufficient to start the prescription period ([paras 3–4, 30]).

Additionally, the case raised broader questions regarding the implications of knowledge on the part of a claimant as it pertains to effectively initiating legal action and the associated legal standards for establishing negligence in professional conduct by attorneys ([paras 34–41]).

### R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 32–39])

The court's decision was grounded in **section 12 of the Prescription Act 68 of 1969**, which stipulates that prescription commences when a debt becomes due, emphasizing that a debt is not deemed "due" until the creditor has knowledge of both the identity of the debtor and the facts from which the debt arises ([para 36]).

Key case law underpinning the decision includes:

1. **Minister of Finance and Others v Gore NO** [2006] ZASCA 98, regarding the criteria needed to establish a cause of action.
2. **Fluxmans Incorporated v Levenson** [2016] ZASCA 183, which discussed the running of prescription in relation to knowledge of the underlying facts.
3. **B v B** [2014] ZASCA 14, which was cited for its relevance to the invalidity of antenuptial contracts due to conflicting clauses.

### A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 30–41])

In applying the law to the facts, the court determined that the appellant had acquired knowledge of the requisite facts to initiate proceedings against the respondent on **9 May 2014**, when he was informed of a potential conflict of interest regarding the antenuptial contract. The appellant's engagement with the respondent's legal team indicated that he was sufficiently informed of the situation, even if he did not fully comprehend the legal implications at that time ([para 37]).

The court noted that prescription did not hinge upon the appellant's understanding of the legal status of the contract, but rather on his awareness of the facts that could substantiate a claim of negligence against the respondent ([para 39]). Consequently, the court upheld that the appellant’s cause of action was complete as of May 2014, irrespective of whether the validity of the antenuptial contract had been judicially determined in October 2016 ([para 41]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 42–43])

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal, concluding that the appellant’s claim had, indeed, prescribed by the time the summons was issued in October 2017. The court affirmed the lower court's determination that the appellant had sufficient knowledge of the necessary facts to initiate his claim as early as May 2014 ([para 41]).

The ruling underscores the legal principle that the running of prescription begins not with full knowledge of all legal consequences but with awareness of the fundamental facts from which a claim can emerge. This case reinforces the critical importance of timely legal action following the acquisition of pertinent information regarding potential claims and the responsibilities of legal practitioners in safeguarding their clients' interests.

Given the court's findings, it also pointedly addressed the costs award, determining that the matter did not warrant the services of two counsel—highlighting it as a relatively straightforward issue despite its significance ([para 43]).

This judgment stands as a salient reminder of the necessity for claimants to act promptly once they suspect that they have a viable claim—an essential consideration in ensuring that their rights are preserved within the appropriate time frames mandated by the Prescription Act.
