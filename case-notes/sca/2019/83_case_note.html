Supreme Court of Appeal

The Director of Public Prosecutions v Moloto (1007/18) [2019] ZASCA 83

In the case of The Director of Public Prosecutions v Moloto ([paras 1–4]), the respondent, Kholofelo Charmaine Moloto, was charged with two counts of murder under the Minimum Sentences Act 105 of 1997.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 83

F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–4])

In the case of The Director of Public Prosecutions v Moloto ([paras 1–4]), the respondent, Kholofelo Charmaine Moloto, was charged with two counts of murder under the Minimum Sentences Act 105 of 1997. On 3 June 2014, Moloto allegedly unlawfully and intentionally killed her grandmother, Florence Gwenya Moloto, and a five-year-old child, Elna Tintswalo Pele, at Ga-Semenya village in Seshego. The respondent pleaded guilty to the charge concerning her grandmother but was acquitted of the charge involving the child. Subsequently, the trial court sentenced her to five years of imprisonment under section 276(1)(i) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977.

During the trial, Moloto provided a statement indicating that her actions were influenced by a belief in witchcraft after consulting traditional healers who claimed that her grandmother was bewitching her. This belief, compounded by her deteriorating health, led her to request the assistance of her boyfriend, Mr. Frans Baloyi, in killing her grandmother. Despite having initial hesitations about killing the child, she was present during the murder and later contributed to covering up the crime. The court considered her personal circumstances, including her age, educational background, and her relationship with the deceased, while sentencing ([paras 2–4]).

I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 5–7])

The primary issue in this case revolved around whether the trial court had properly exercised its sentencing discretion and whether substantial and compelling circumstances existed to justify a deviation from the prescribed minimum sentence of life imprisonment under section 51(1) of the Minimum Sentences Act. The trial court did not clearly articulate whether it found any factors that constituted substantial and compelling reasons for not imposing the mandatory sentence ([para 5]).

Additionally, the court evaluated whether the trial court's failure to engage in the proper analysis regarding the prescribed minimum sentence constituted a misdirection sufficient for an appellate court to intervene ([para 6]).

R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 5–10])

The judgment referenced established legal principles regarding sentencing, particularly that discretion in sentencing is primarily held by the trial court. However, appellate courts can intervene in cases of improper or unreasonable exercise of that discretion (S v Pillay 1977 (4) SA 531 (A) at 535 E-F) ([para 6]). The Minimum Sentences Act mandates life imprisonment for premeditated murder unless a court identifies substantial and compelling circumstances warranting a lesser sentence, as per S v Malgas 2001 (1) SACR 469 (SCA) ([para 7]).

Further, the judgment highlighted the need to deter the killing of individuals based on witchcraft allegations, calling for strict punishment reflective of societal revulsion against such behavior, as articulated in S v Phama 1997 (1) SACR 485 (E) and in Mogaramedi 2015(1) SACR 427 (GP) ([paras 9–10]).

A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 8–11])

In applying the legal standards, the Supreme Court of Appeal identified aggravating factors inherent in Moloto's actions, such as premeditation and the context in which the murders occurred—the trust betrayed by killing family members under false pretenses ([paras 8–9]). The evidence indicated that Moloto had carefully planned the murder, having previously solicited assistance and waiting for an opportunity to act, thereby showcasing a disregard for human life.

Moreover, the court acknowledged that although the respondent believed she was acting under the influence of witchcraft, such beliefs could not serve as a justification for violent actions. However, the court recognized some mitigating factors, including her personal circumstances, age, and psychological influences stemming from the alleged bewitchment ([para 11]).

Despite acknowledging these mitigating factors, the court ultimately decided that they, when viewed cumulatively, constituted sufficient grounds for deviating from the minimum sentence. The appellate court thus opted for a sentence of 10 years' imprisonment as a proportional response to the crime and acknowledged the time already served by Moloto ([para 12]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 11–12])

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal brought by the Director of Public Prosecutions and substituted the trial court's sentence with a 10-year imprisonment. The court clarified that the failure of the trial court to identify substantial and compelling circumstances to justify deviation from the minimum sentence was a significant misdirection. Importantly, the decision aimed to balance the need for punishment with the understanding of the respondent's psychological state and circumstances ([paras 11–12]).

The case underscores the importance of judicial clarity in sentencing and reaffirms the principle that while discretion is integral to sentencing, it must be exercised judiciously and transparently. Furthermore, the ruling serves as a deterrent message to society regarding the consequences of resorting to violence based on unsubstantiated allegations, thus enhancing the integrity of the justice system in dealing with crimes stemming from cultural beliefs.

This case highlights an evolving understanding of how courts can navigate complex relationships between cultural practices and legal principles while ensuring justice for all parties involved.
