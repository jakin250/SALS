Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL
Source Folder: 2009

### FIRAC Case Note: Michele v S (477/2008) [2009] ZASCA 116

---

**F – Facts**  
The case of *Michele v S* originated with the appellants, John Oupa Michele and Khazamula Joseph Mashabana, who were convicted in April 2002 in the Regional Court at Polokwane for fraud, arising from a life insurance scheme involving Old Mutual Insurance Company Limited. The appellants had taken out a life policy for an employee, Manaka, which was fraudulently claimed after falsely asserting that Manaka had died in a motor vehicle accident ([1]–[5]). They were each sentenced to seven years’ imprisonment, with two years conditionally suspended for three years ([1]).

The initial appeal to the High Court was dismissed on October 20, 2003, prompting the appellants to file an application for leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal on October 27, 2003. However, this application was mired in administrative delays, resulting in almost five years passing before the appeal was heard on August 25, 2008, due partly to the retirement of the presiding judge ([2]). In light of this delay, the court expressed that it would consider it in determining the appropriate sentence on appeal ([3]).

Both appellants were first offenders with established businesses and families; Michele was 32 years old with a minor child, while Mashabana was 42, married, and had six children ([6]). They expressed remorse and sought to repay Old Mutual the fraudulent amount, although they had not acted on this claim immediately ([7]). The trial court noted this remorse but ultimately deemed the severity of the crime as warranting imprisonment ([8]–[10]).

**I – Issues**  
The central legal issue in this case revolved around the appropriateness of the sentence imposed by the Regional Court in light of the circumstances surrounding the case, including the undue delay experienced by the appellants in having their application heard. Specifically, the appeal considered whether the imposition of a seven-year sentence was materially excessive given the nature of the crime and the factors influencing the appellants’ situation ([11], [12]) and if the delay in the appeal process should affect sentencing ([13]).

**R – Rules**  
The legal standard for determining sentence appropriateness encompasses whether a trial court misdirected itself, or if the sentence was “startlingly inappropriate” when compared to what the appeal court might impose ([11]). Relevant precedents include *S v Sadler*, which highlighted the societal impact of economic crimes like fraud, and established that criminals, even first offenders, may face rigorous punishment ([10]). The court also referred to *S v Roberts*, where delays in the judicial process were considered in the context of justice and fairness ([13]). 

Furthermore, statutory provisions from the Criminal Procedure Act would usually guide sentencing, grounded in principles of proportionality, deterrence, and rehabilitation of the offenders ([12]).

**A – Application**  
In its evaluation, the court recognized the appellants' initial fraudulent action but noted that the actual financial loss to Old Mutual was limited to R20,000, the funeral cover disbursed. The potential loss of R357,520 was significant but mitigated by the lack of further damages due to the fraud's discovery ([9], [12]). 

The court assessed the trial court’s initial sentencing and concluded that due to the misdirection regarding remorse and consideration of actual loss, the severity of the sentence was excessive. Considering mitigating factors—including the lengthy administrative delay and the mental anguish experienced by the appellants during the five years of uncertainty—the court determined that a revised sentence would be more fitting ([12], [14]). 

The court found that the trial court failed to properly weigh the impact of the significant delay on the appellants and accepted that this context justified a reduction in the sentence to four years' imprisonment, with two years suspended, recognizing the need to balance accountability with fairness in sentencing ([13]–[14]).

**C – Conclusion**  
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the appeal should be upheld, and the original sentence was set aside in favor of a more appropriate punishment: a four-year sentence, with two years suspended for three years conditionally upon no reoffending for fraud during this period. The court emphasized that the combined factors of the crime's context, the delay in the appeal process, and the appellants' situations warranted this adjustment, thereby fostering a sense of justice while also reprimanding the appellants for their misconduct ([15]).
