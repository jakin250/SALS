Source Folder: 2009

# Case Note: Wybrand Andreas Lodewicus du Toit v Minister for Safety and Security of the Republic of South Africa and Another [2009] ZACC 22

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

The core issue of this case pertains to the interpretation and application of amnesty provisions under the Promotion of National Unity and Reconciliation Act 34 of 1995 (the Reconciliation Act) as it relates to the consequences of criminal convictions. The applicant, Wybrand Andreas Lodewicus du Toit, previously held a senior position as Director and National Commanding Officer of the Technical Support Services within the South African Police Service (SAPS). He was convicted in 1996 for the politically motivated murders of four individuals, collectively referred to as the “Motherwell Four,” receiving a sentence of 15 years' imprisonment. Per the South African Police Service Act 68 of 1995 (SAPS Act), his conviction deemed him automatically discharged from employment.

Following an unsuccessful initial application for amnesty that was set aside on review, Du Toit was eventually granted amnesty for the charges on December 23, 2005. He sought reinstatement within the SAPS based on a correspondence from the National Commissioner, suggesting his reinstatement should he receive amnesty. This request was denied on the grounds that his situation did not fall under the ameliorative provisions of the SAPS Act regarding reinstatement.

Subsequent to his dismissal and amnesty grant, the North Gauteng High Court rejected Du Toit’s application to compel reinstatement, and the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed his appeal with costs. Du Toit now seeks leave to appeal to the Constitutional Court ([paras 1–8]).

### I – Issues

Three principal issues arise from Du Toit’s appeal:

1. **Interpretation of Section 20(10)**: Whether Section 20(10) of the Reconciliation Act should be construed to nullify past convictions and their consequences retrospectively, effectively challenging his discharge.
   
2. **Equating Amnesty to Appeal or Review**: The interpretation of whether the term “appeal or review” in Section 36(2) of the SAPS Act includes a successful amnesty application, thus impacting his discharge status.

3. **Binding Nature of the National Commissioner’s Agreement**: Whether the National Commissioner’s informal agreement concerning his reinstatement was binding and offers a legitimate basis for his return to the SAPS ([paras 9–10]).

### R – Rules

The court's analysis is guided primarily by provisions within the Reconciliation Act, particularly Section 20(10) which posits that convictions associated with politically motivated acts, where amnesty has been granted, shall be deemed expunged from all official records. The SAPS Act’s Section 36 outlines various consequences tied to convictions for serving members, suggesting limited avenues for reinstatement post-discharge. 

Moreover, the court considers principles relating to the interpretation of legislation, particularly the presumption against retrospectivity in statutory interpretation. The principle that remedial statutes should be construed generously is also invoked by the applicant, as established in *Department of Land Affairs and Others v Goedgelegen Tropical Fruits* [2007] ZACC 12, which highlighted the imperative of legislative context and purpose when interpreting statutory provisions ([paras 11–13, 36]).

### A – Application

In interpreting the law, the Constitutional Court acknowledged the critical balance between amnesty for past wrongs during apartheid and the stipulation that individuals must not evade lawful consequences of their actions. The Court assessed the scope of Section 20(10) in light of its historical context, concluding that while it serves a restorative function to promote reconciliation, there are inherent limitations to its retrospectivity. 

The Court further distinguished between retrospective and retroactive effects, recognising that while certain aspects of the Reconciliation Act are intended to have retrospective applicability, they do not extend to altering past legal consequences (e.g., criminal convictions resulting in employment termination) that had been completed prior to the enactment of amnesty ([paras 31–32, 40–44]).

Ultimately, the court found that Du Toit’s discharge was a lawful consequence of his conviction and not subject to nullification under the provisions of the Reconciliation Act. The provisions of the SAPS Act did not equate amnesty with appeal or review options since amnesty implies an admission of wrongdoing, divergent from the notions of innocence presumed in judicial appeals. Additionally, the informal agreement regarding reinstatement was deemed non-binding, lacking requisite jurisdictional backing under Section 36 of the SAPS Act ([paras 60–62]) .

### C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court denied Du Toit's appeal, upholding the findings of the lower courts. The Court articulated that while South Africa's reconciliation efforts through amnesty are vital, they must not undermine lawful consequences for serious crimes. Thus, Section 20(10) does not operate to retroactively restore the applicant's position, and the National Commissioner’s correspondence does not constitute a binding contract for reinstatement. The judgment underscores the delicate balance the state must maintain between promoting national unity and ensuring accountability for past injustices. 

The Court further established there would be no order as to costs, acknowledging the complexities of the issues raised concerning statutory interpretations ([paras 64–65]).

---

This case serves as a significant exploration of transitional justice within a constitutional democracy, clarifying the implications of amnesty and reflecting on its limits concerning employment rights and legal liabilities in the context of South Africa’s socio-political history.
