Hashtag Movement v Ethiopian Church of South Africa and Others (1046/2023) [2023] ZASCA 173

The substantive procedural issue revolved around an order that was ostensibly made under section 18(1) of the Act.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2023 [2023] ZASCA 173

F – Facts

The case at hand, Hashtag Movement v Ethiopian Church of South Africa and Others, originates from the Eastern Cape Division of the High Court, where the litigation precedes the appeal. The appellant, Hashtag Movement, sought an appeal under section 18(4) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 (the Act) following an order made by a single judge of the High Court ([paras 1–2]). The judgment of the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) was delivered electronically on 8 December 2023, after hearing arguments on 28 November 2023.

The substantive procedural issue revolved around an order that was ostensibly made under section 18(1) of the Act. The section discusses the suspension of the operation of the decision pending an appeal and delineates automatic appeal rights to the next highest court upon such orders ([para 3]).

I – Issues

The central legal issues in this case involve:

  1. Whether the Supreme Court of Appeal had jurisdiction to hear the appeal initiated by Hashtag Movement under section 18(4) of the Superior Courts Act ([paras 2–5]).
  2. The interpretation of the statutory provisions of sections 17 and 18 of the Act concerning hierarchical court decisions and appeal pathways ([paras 2–3]).

R – Rules

The pertinent statutory rules and judicial precedents relevant to this case include:

  1. Section 17 of the Superior Courts Act establishes the default protocol for appeals from decisions made by a single judge of the High Court, which states that appeals should lie to a full court of that division unless otherwise directed ([para 2]).
  2. Section 18(1) delineates that an appeal automatically suspends the operation of the decision pending its resolution, and Section 18(4) clarifies that a party aggrieved by a decision can appeal to the next highest court—indeed it explicitly designates the full court as that avenue ([para 3]).
  3. In City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality v Vresthena (Pty) Ltd and Others, the SCA reaffirmed this understanding, confirming that an automatic right of appeal from a single judge’s decision lies with the full court of the division ([para 4]).

A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal, per Goosen JA, critically analyzed the existing legal framework regarding appeals as set out by the Superior Courts Act. The Court noted that the applicable sections obligate an automatic appeal from the decision rendered by a High Court judge to the full court within the same division, thereby explicitly precluding the SCA from intervening on such grounds ([paras 4–5]).

The Court emphasized that since the appeal was predicated on an order originating from a High Court single judge, the jurisdictional path mandated that the matter be heard by the full court, and thus the SCA lacked the requisite authority to entertain the appeal ([para 5]). Furthermore, the absence of compelling reasons to deviate from the prescribed protocols reaffirmed the decision to strike the appeal from the roll ([para 6]).

C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately struck the appeal from the roll due to a lack of jurisdiction, reinforcing the hierarchical structure of the South African court system as dictated by the Superior Courts Act. The Court ordered the appellant to bear the costs incurred through this litigation, including the costs of two counsels, underscoring the principle that the costs follow the event in cases where the appellant did not adequately adhere to procedural mandates ([paras 5–6]).

This case underscores the fundamental tenet of the South African legal system about the importance of following established appellate procedures and demonstrates the explicit jurisdictional limits of the Supreme Court of Appeal regarding High Court decisions. The ruling solidifies the procedural integrity of judicial pathways defined under the Superior Courts Act and exemplifies the Court's commitment to upholding these procedural norms.
