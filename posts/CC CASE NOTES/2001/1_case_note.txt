Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2001

# FIRAC Case Note: Price v. The State (CCT 23/01)

## F – Facts

In the case of **Price v. The State**, the applicant, David Ashley Price, sought special leave to appeal against his conviction for two counts of fraud, which was initially determined by the South Eastern Cape High Court ([1]). Following his conviction, both the High Court and the Supreme Court of Appeal denied him leave to appeal, leading him to approach the Constitutional Court. The primary constitutional issue raised by Price was his claim that the trial judge improperly interpreted his silence—in failing to provide specific information during police inquiries—as an infringement upon his right to silence under section 35(1)(a) of the Constitution. Specifically, Price contended that the judge’s approach to his evidence constituted an unfair prejudgment against him for exercising this right ([1]).

## I – Issues

The principal issue before the Constitutional Court was whether the High Court's interpretation of the applicant's silence constituted a violation of his constitutional right to silence, as enshrined in section 35(1)(a) of the South African Constitution. This raised significant questions regarding the safeguarding of an accused's rights during criminal proceedings, particularly the implications of drawing adverse inferences from a defendant's silence ([1]).

## R – Rules

The legal framework pertinent to this case derives from section 35(1)(a) of the South African Constitution, which guarantees the right to remain silent and empowers individuals not to present evidence against themselves. The doctrine surrounding the right to silence holds considerable importance in criminal law and is pivotal for ensuring fair trial standards ([1]). Additionally, prior case law, while not explicitly cited in the judgment, includes numerous precedents establishing that adverse inferences from silence could lead to constitutional violations if not approached cautiously ([1]).

## A – Application

In its judgment, the Constitutional Court, led by Yacoob J., did not need to engage extensively with the substantive issue regarding the right to silence. Yacoob J. assumed, for the argument's sake, that no adverse inference could be conclusively drawn from Price's exercise of his right to silence ([2]). Nevertheless, the court concluded that the evidence against Price was overwhelmingly compelling. The judgment emphasized that even without considering the potential infringement of his right to silence, the case against him remained robust enough to uphold the convictions. Consequently, the court found that there existed no reasonable prospects of success on appeal, severely undermining the applicant's standing ([2]).

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately denied Price's application for special leave to appeal, citing the compelling evidence of guilt as a critical factor for its decision. The court held that even assuming the incorrect application of the right to silence, the overwhelming nature of the evidence meant that the claimed constitutional issue did not warrant further consideration. As a result, the refusal of special leave was not only a procedural outcome but also underscored the importance of substantive evidence in the context of constitutional protections in criminal law ([2]). This case may have implications for how courts assess the right to silence in future cases, although here it reaffirmed the notion that certain evidential bases can overshadow such constitutional rights in determining guilt.
