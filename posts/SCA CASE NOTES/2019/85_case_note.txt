Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: Ndou v The State (247/18) [2019] ZASCA 85

### F – Facts

In **Ndou v The State**, the appellant, Pontso Dennis Ndou, alongside two co-accused, faced three charges in the Wynberg Regional Court, namely: robbery with aggravating circumstances, attempted murder, and unlawful possession of a firearm, as articulated in the Firearms Control Act 60 of 2000 ([1]). These charges stemmed from a robbery on December 13, 2004, in Rivonia, Sandton. The victim, Mrs. Claire Dawn McGraf, was accosted by two suspects, one displaying a firearm while the other physically assaulted her, resulting in the theft of her handbag and money ([6] - [8]).

During the robbery, a bystander, Mr. Henry John Barnes, attempted to intervene and was shot in the arm by one of the suspects inside the getaway vehicle. The suspects, including Ndou, were subsequently arrested, and a search recovered a firearm and stolen items from the vehicle ([1] - [9]). The appellant was convicted of robbery (count 1) and attempted murder (count 2), for which he received sentences of 15 and 10 years imprisonment, respectively, while he was acquitted of unlawful possession of a firearm (count 3) due to insufficient evidence ([2] - [3]).

The appellant appealed the conviction and sentence for attempted murder but faced a complex appeal when the State, without a cross-appeal, sought to revise the acquittal on the firearm charge ([3] - [4]). This ignited discussions on the procedural propriety of appealing an acquittal without a formal cross-appeal by the State.

### I – Issues

Three crucial issues arose for determination:

1. **Procedural propriety**: Whether the Full Court had the authority to overturn the appellant's acquittal on count 3 without a cross-appeal by the State ([5]).
   
2. **Merits of conviction on count 3**: Assessing whether there was sufficient evidence to uphold the conviction regarding unlawful possession of a firearm under the common purpose doctrine ([5], [19]).
   
3. **Appropriateness of the sentence**: Evaluating whether the 10-year sentence for attempted murder was excessive considering the reduction in the charge to a lesser offense of assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm ([20] - [22]).

### R – Rules

The legal principles at stake involved the jurisdiction of appellate courts concerning factual findings in criminal matters. The precedent set in **Director of Public Prosecutions, Gauteng v Pistorius** 2016 (2) SA 317 (SCA) ([17]) underlined that an acquittal is final and cannot be subject to re-examination solely on factual grounds. The principle of common purpose, established in cases like **S v Mbuli** 2003 (1) SACR 97 (SCA), highlighted that mere participation in a robbery does not equate to joint possession of a firearm unless there is evidence of shared intention or control ([19]).

Regarding sentencing, the discretion of the original court was recognized, but this discretion can be contested if excessive or based on misinterpretation of legal principles, as established in **Bogaards v S** 2013 (1) SACR 1 (CC) ([21]). The minimum sentencing frameworks as established by the Criminal Law Amendment Act were also relevant considerations in determining appropriate penalties for crimes of violence.

### A – Application

The appeal court began by scrutinizing the Full Court's actions in revisiting the acquittal on count 3 without a cross-appeal. The court established that procedural norms strictly preclude the State from appealing an acquittal on factual grounds. As such, the Full Court overstepped its jurisdiction, leading to the necessary conclusion that the acquittal for unlawful possession of a firearm should stand ([16] - [18]).

In terms of the evidence for count 3, both the trial and Full Court's conclusions were deemed inconsistent with the established legal standards regarding common purpose. The court reiterated that for joint possession to be established, the participation must indicate a common intention to possess or control the firearm, which was lacking in this case ([19]).

Concerning sentencing, the appellate court found that the original sentence of 10 years was not proportionate following the downgraded charge to assault with intent to do grievous bodily harm. The court pointed out that there was a misdirection in the original sentencing that did not appropriately account for the nature and gravity of the revised conviction, suggesting instead that a 5-year term was fitting, with concurrent sentencing for portions of the sentences to mitigate excessive cumulative punishment ([22] - [26]).

### C – Conclusion

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal against the conviction and sentence regarding count 3, reinstating the acquittal, thereby affirming the principle that an acquittal in criminal matters, once established, is generally unassailable by the prosecution in absence of clear jurisdictional grounds. It modified the sentence for count 2 to five years with three years running concurrently with the robbery sentence. Thus, the effective sentence was adjusted to 17 years of imprisonment, reflecting the balancing act between justice for violent crimes and fair treatment of the accused as a first offender lacking direct culpability in the shooting incident ([27]). 

This case underscores the rigid procedural norms that govern criminal appeals in South Africa and reinforces the significance of establishing comprehensive evidence of common purpose in joint criminal enterprises. The implications extend into discussions on sentencing practices, highlighting the necessity for consistency and fairness aligned with the less severe charges that ultimately may be substantiated in court.
