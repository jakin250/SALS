Supreme Court of Appeal

Aliko v The State (552/2018) [2019] ZASCA 31

On 8 August 2013, during the sacred month of Ramadaan, the deceased, Mohammed Laher, a 44-year-old disabled man suffering from multiple sclerosis, was found brutally murdered in the South End Mosque in Port Elizabeth.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2019 [2019] ZASCA 31

FIRAC Method Case Note

F – Facts

On 8 August 2013, during the sacred month of Ramadaan, the deceased, Mohammed Laher, a 44-year-old disabled man suffering from multiple sclerosis, was found brutally murdered in the South End Mosque in Port Elizabeth. The appellant, Mohammed Sani Aliko, was charged and subsequently convicted of this murder by the Eastern Cape Local Division of the High Court, where he received a sentence of life imprisonment. The case reached the Supreme Court of Appeal following the full court's affirmation of his conviction and sentence ([paras 1–2]).

On the day of the murder, the mosque was maintained by Ms Muna Mohammed Osman and her family. Ms Osman witnessed Aliko enter the mosque around 10:45 am. Subsequently, she heard unusual noises and saw Aliko, whom she recognized, engaging in suspicious activity. After noticing ongoing disturbances, she secured her children and left the mosque. Once outside, Ms Osman returned to find Laher dead, having suffered extensive injuries, including strangulation and blunt force trauma ([paras 3–9]).

Aliko presented to the trial court a defense arguing that he entered the mosque merely to check the time and that he did not harm the deceased. However, this version was rejected by the trial court, which convicted him of murder (eventually replacing a conviction for premeditated murder with one for murder) but still imposed the harshest penalty available ([paras 10–11]).

I – Issues

The primary legal issue raised in the appeal pertains to whether the life sentence imposed on Aliko was warranted given that the court a quo found no evidence of premeditation. The appellant argued that, as a first offender, and due to mitigating personal circumstances, the sentence should have been markedly less severe ([paras 11, 18]).

R – Rules

The legal framework centers around the definitions and jurisprudence concerning murder as outlined in the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 (CLAA). Specifically, the court examined the nature of murder, distinguishing between ordinary murder and so-called "premeditated murder," while underscoring that premeditation is a factor in sentencing rather than an element necessary to constitute the crime of murder itself ([paras 13–14]).

The jurisprudential standards cited include S v Legoa [2003] and Kekana v S [2019], which emphasize the inherent discretionary power of courts to impose sentences that align with the gravity of the crime, social justice, and the offender’s circumstances. The court also referenced S v Matyityi [2010], which held that the sentencing court must balance the crime's nature against the defendant's personal situation while considering societal interests ([paras 13–19]).

A – Application

In the application of the rules, the court noted the horrific nature of the crime, characterized by brutal violence inflicted upon a vulnerable individual. Despite the absence of premeditation as determined by the court a quo, this did not diminish the severity of the murder; the court found that life imprisonment remained justifiable given the brutality involved and the context in which the crime was committed ([paras 18–19]).

The court rejected arguments favoring leniency based on the appellant’s personal circumstances, including his status as a first offender and recent emotional distress resulting from personal losses. These factors were deemed insufficient to outweigh the crime's aggravating circumstances, including its occurrence during a sacred time and in a place of worship ([para 19]).

The court emphasized its discretion in sentencing and reiterated that a proportional response was vital in aligning the punishment with the offense's nature, thereby upholding the life sentence imposed ([paras 18–20]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Aliko's appeal against the life sentence. The court affirmed the trial court's reasoning, concluding that despite the lack of premeditation, the circumstances surrounding the murder justified the life imprisonment sentence. The judgment highlighted the court's inherent power to assess sentences independently, whereby the particulars of the crime, the perpetrator's character, and the interests of society play critical roles in determining the appropriate punishment ([para 20]).

The decision reinforces the judiciary's stance on maintaining public safety and ensuring that severely violent acts, particularly those against vulnerable individuals, are met with the appropriate legal and societal response. This case serves as a significant reference point in the ongoing discourse on sentencing practices within South African criminal law, particularly concerning violent crimes.
