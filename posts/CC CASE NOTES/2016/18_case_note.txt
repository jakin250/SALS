# Case Note: Solidarity v Department of Correctional Services [2016] ZACC 18

### FIRAC Case Note

**F – Facts**  
To gauge the legality of the Employment Equity Plan (2010 EE Plan) of the Department of Correctional Services (DCS) regarding its implementation and adherence to statutory provisions under the Employment Equity Act 55 of 1998 (EEA), the applicants (Solidarity and several individual employees, predominantly Coloured community members) raised concerns about non-compliance and unfair discrimination (see [1]–[5]). 

The DCS adopted the 2010 EE Plan after substantial consultation with recognized trade unions, a process in which Solidarity was not involved. The plan set numerical targets for representation among occupational groups and mandated that certain demographic proportions be achieved by the end of the five-year period [4]–[5]. 

In 2011, the Department advertised several positions, to which the individual applicants applied. Although most were recommended for appointment, they were ultimately denied based on claims of overrepresentation within the respective groups for which they sought employment (predominantly based on race and gender) [6]. Following a failed conciliation at the Commission for Conciliation, Mediation, and Arbitration (CCMA) for their unfair labor practices claim, the matter proceeded to the Labour Court [17].

The Labour Court recognized that the DCS's failure to consider regional demographics and the resulting reliance solely on national demographic figures was a breach of s 42 of the EEA. However, it did not deem the 2010 EE Plan unconstitutional and failed to grant individual relief to the applicants [19]–[20]. The applicants’ subsequent appeal to the Labour Appeal Court (LAC) affirmed the Labour Court's findings but did not resolve individual disputes, resulting in further challenges leading to this Constitutional Court hearing [24].

**I – Issues**  
The key legal questions arising in this case, as raised by the parties, include:

1. **Validity of the 2010 EE Plan**: Does the 2010 EE Plan violate any provisions of the Employment Equity Act, specifically regarding the incorrect usage of demographic benchmarks? [19]–[20].
   
2. **Application of the Barnard Principle**: Can the principle established in _South African Police Service v Solidarity obo Barnard_ be legitimately applied against applicants who belong to historically disadvantaged groups, such as Coloured individuals, based on claims of overrepresentation? [37]–[41].
   
3. **Unfair Discrimination**: Does the refusal of the DCS to appoint the individual applicants constitute unfair discrimination under the EEA? [65].

**R – Rules**  
The primary legal authorities considered in this case are:

1. **Employment Equity Act 55 of 1998**: Sections regarding affirmative action measures (s 15), the establishment of numerical targets (s 20), and ensuring compliance with regional demographics (s 42) are particularly relevant.
   
2. **Constitution of the Republic of South Africa, 1996**: The constitutional implication of equality and the mechanisms available for redressing past inequalities, particularly s 9(2) that allows for affirmative action measures [42].
   
3. **Case law precedents**:
   - _Barnard_ applied principles of substantive equality and asserted that affirmative action measures must be proportional and consider the contexts of past disadvantages [12].
   - _Van Heerden_ reinforced the threefold inquiry necessary to validate affirmative action measures under s 9(2) of the Constitution [25]–[26].

**A – Application**  
The Constitutional Court, led by Zondo J, undertook a thorough analysis of the DCS’s employment equity plan and its compliance with legislative requirements. It was concluded that the plan employed incorrect benchmarks by relying solely on national demographics instead of incorporating the regional demographics which are critical for determining equitable representation [80]–[81]. The erroneous assessment led to the unjust categorization of Coloured and female applicants as overrepresented, which violated their right to fair treatment under s 6(1) of the EEA [82].

The court also emphasized that the Barnard principle applies to all candidates, regardless of race. Thus, the reliance on overrepresentation as a basis for refusing appointment—without an adequate legal basis—amounted to unfair discrimination against the individual applicants [39]–[40]. This finding affirmed that affirmative action measures are necessary to advance equality, but they must be applied fairly and rationally to avoid undue harm to applicants [113]–[120].

**C – Conclusion**  
Ultimately, the Constitutional Court delivered a mixed verdict. It upheld parts of the Labour and Labour Appeal Court decisions that affirmed the DCS's EE Plan did not comply with the relevant standards but determined that the refusal to appoint the individual applicants constituted unfair discrimination, validly invoking unfair labor practices under the EEA [86]. 

The court ordered specific appointments for those whose positions remained vacant and awarded the applicable benefits retrospectively [88]–[92]. It held that the Department acted unlawfully in its failure to appoint qualified individuals based on a faulty understanding of demographic representation, highlighting that future employment equity plans must accommodate both national and regional demographic realities.

There was no cost order against either party, reflecting the complexities and public interest considerations involved in ensuring equitable employment practices [95]. 

The ruling establishes a critical precedent for employment equity in South Africa, emphasizing that while affirmative action is essential for redressing inequalities, its implementation must be carried out judiciously and ethically to uphold the principles of dignity and fairness for all workers.
