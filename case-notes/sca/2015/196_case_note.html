Rossitter & Others v Nedbank Ltd (96/2014) ZASCA 196

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 2012 ([para 7]). The appellants became aware of the judgment on 9

F – Facts

The case revolves around a default judgment granted against the appellants—Terence John Rossitter, Terence John Rossitter NO, and Gail Wingrove Rossitter NO—by the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court, relating to a loan agreement executed with BOE Private Bank in March 2008, the predecessor of Nedbank Ltd ([para 2]). The Rossitter Family Trust, represented by the first appellant, initially borrowed R13.5 million and R9.77 million under separate loan agreements, which were secured against various immovable properties ([para 2]–[4]).

After defaulting on repayments, the respondent issued summons for outstanding debts on 14 October 2010, resulting in a notice of intention to defend filed on 5 November 2010. However, subsequent agreements varied payment terms, and a confession of claim was made without contesting the principal indebtedness ([para 4]).

The appellants missed the deadline to file a plea after receiving a notice of bar dated 29 March 2012. Following a refusal of an indulgence for late filing, the respondent applied for default judgment, which was granted by the registrar of the High Court on 30 May 2012 ([para 7]). The appellants became aware of the judgment on 9 July 2012 and attempted to rescind it on 14 August 2012, arguing that it had been erroneously granted without proper notification as required by Uniform Rule 31(5)(a) ([para 8], [16]). The lower court dismissed their application for rescission, leading to the current appeal ([para 8]).

I – Issues

The core issue for determination was whether the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court erred in refusing to grant rescission of the default judgment under Uniform Rule 42(1)(a) ([para 1]). Specifically, the court needed to assess if there were any procedural irregularities that rendered the judgment erroneously sought or granted, namely whether the respondent's notice of intention to seek default judgment complied with the procedural requirements set out in rule 31(5)(a) in conjunction with the Practice Manual of the court ([para 9]).

R – Rules

The relevant legal framework includes:

  1. Uniform Rule 42(1)(a): This rule allows a court to rescind or vary any order or judgment erroneously sought or granted in the absence of a party ([para 12]).
  2. Uniform Rule 31(5)(a): Provisions regarding the procedure a plaintiff must follow when a defendant is in default of filing a plea, mandating a notice period of at least five days for the intention to apply for default judgment to be properly served ([para 13]).
  3. Practice Manual of the KZN Division: Paragraph 2.3 of the Practice Manual further stipulates that, when a default judgment application is made more than six months after service of the summons, a notice of set down must be served ([para 14]).

Precedent cases, such as Lodhi 2 Properties Investments CC v Bondev Development (Pty) Ltd, established that a default judgment is considered erroneously granted if proper notice of proceedings was not given to the affected party ([para 16]).

A – Application

The court evaluated the procedural compliance of the respondent's default judgment application. It was determined that the notice served on the appellants did not stipulate the date and time on which default judgment would be sought, rendering it procedurally defective under rule 31(5)(a) and paragraph 2.3 of the Practice Manual ([para 15]). The court noted that had the registrar been aware of this defect, the default judgment would not have been granted ([para 16]).

With reference to established case law, the court acknowledged that the absence of proper notification meant the judgment was granted erroneously. The court highlighted that it is not necessary for a party to demonstrate good cause for rescission under rule 42(1)(a); the mere demonstration of the judgment being erroneously sought or granted suffices ([para 16]).

Furthermore, while the respondent argued that the appellants demonstrated conduct inconsistent with their intention to appeal, the court found that lodging an incomplete appeal record indicated the appellants' clear intention to proceed. The execution of judgment against the appellants' properties occurred despite the pending appeal, further undermining the respondent's argument of acquiescence ([para 11]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, concluding that the default judgment had been erroneously granted as the procedural requirements for notice were not met. The court set aside the order of the lower court and granted rescission of the default judgment ([paras 18]). The appellants were awarded costs, establishing important precedents regarding adherence to procedural rules in default judgment applications. The decision underscores the judiciary's commitment to ensuring fairness and due process in civil procedures, reaffirming that judgments must be granted in compliance with established procedural norms.

This case highlights the necessity for legal practitioners to adhere strictly to procedural rules, thus ensuring that clients are protected against judgments where due process is not followed. The implications of this ruling extend beyond the particulars of the case, serving as a reminder of the importance of procedural integrity in all civil proceedings.
