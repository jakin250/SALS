Supreme Court of Appeal

Saaknommer 368/95

During the proceedings in the Witwatersrand Local Division, it was established that Lerm had indeed shot Rautenbach while pursuing him after Rautenbach attempted to flee during the arrest ([paras 2], [4]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 1996

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

In the case of Saaknommer 368/95, the plaintiff, Jacobus Albertus Rautenbach, sought damages amounting to approximately R2 million from the defendant, the Minister of Safety and Security, due to injuries he sustained from being shot by Sergeant A M Lerm ([para 2]). The incident took place on 23 September 1993 at a smallholding in Randfontein, where Lerm was part of a tactical unit and acted under orders to arrest Rautenbach based on a warrant issued for his arrest related to theft ([para 4]).

During the proceedings in the Witwatersrand Local Division, it was established that Lerm had indeed shot Rautenbach while pursuing him after Rautenbach attempted to flee during the arrest ([paras 2], [4]). The court bifurcated the issues of liability and quantum of damages, focusing first on whether Lerm's actions were lawful or unlawful in the context of his official duties ([para 3]).

The defendant acknowledged that Rautenbach was armed and had attempted to escape when Lerm fired shots, with the second shot hitting Rautenbach and causing injuries. However, the case's crux lay in whether the initial arrest conducted by Lerm was lawful, as he failed to provide a warrant at the time of arrest ([paras 8-12]).

I – Issues

  1. Was the arrest of Rautenbach by Lerm lawful according to the provisions of Section 39 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977? ([para 12]).
  2. Did Lerm exceed the bounds of lawful conduct in shooting Rautenbach, thereby making the shooting unlawful? ([para 47]).

R – Rules

The principal legal authority applied in this case was Section 39 of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977, which stipulates:

  • Section 39(1) emphasizes that an arrest must be executed with or without a warrant and necessitates the actual physical restraint of the arrested individual ([para 12]).
  • Section 39(2) mandates that the authority executing the arrest must inform the individual of the reason for arrest or provide a copy of the arrest warrant at the time of arrest or immediately thereafter ([para 12]).

The court evaluated the appropriate application of this statute concerning the circumstances surrounding the arrest and the failure to present a warrant upon request by the arrested individual. Previous case law, such as Ngqumba and Others v State President and Others, was also referenced to highlight the strict requirements regarding lawfulness in the context of arrests ([para 18]).

A – Application

The court extensively applied the facts to the statutory provisions, concluding that Lerm's failure to provide Rautenbach with a copy of the arrest warrant before or at the time of arrest constituted unlawful conduct ([para 47]). The following key points formed the basis of the court's reasoning:

  • Lerm’s acknowledgment that he could not provide the arrest warrant at the time of the arrest established a prima facie case of unlawful arrest ([para 46]).
  • The court determined that the absence of the warrant led to the conclusion that the arrest was invalid and thus all actions following from that arrest were also deemed unlawful ([para 48]).
  • The court largely relied on the principle that an individual's right to liberty is fundamental and should not be infringed without compliance with statutory requirements ([para 30]).

The defense's arguments suggesting that Lerm had acted under a reasonable suspicion as permitted by Section 40(1)(b) did not stand, as Lerm never posited that his actions were executed under this provision, nor did he introduce an element of reasonable suspicion during his testimony ([paras 14-16]).

C – Conclusion

The court upheld the decision of the lower court, affirming that Lerm's actions constituted an unlawful arrest of Rautenbach, subsequent to which the shooting incident was also labeled unlawful ([paras 47–48]). The ruling confirmed that Rautenbach had the right to escape from an unlawful arrest, and thus, Lerm had acted outside his lawful authority by shooting him. Consequently, the appeal was dismissed, and costs were awarded against the Minister of Safety and Security, reinforcing the statutory imperative of lawful arrests ([para 48]).

This case highlights the critical importance of statutory compliance within law enforcement practices, particularly regarding individuals' rights and the lawful exercise of police powers. The implications extend not only to individual cases but also promote compliance standards essential for maintaining public trust in law enforcement agencies.
