Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: Magnificent Mile Trading 30 (Pty) Limited v Charmaine Celliers N.O. and Others [2019] ZACC 36

### F - Facts
The case concerns the interpretation of the Mineral and Petroleum Resources Development Act 28 of 2002 (MPRDA), particularly its provisions regarding old order rights. The late Mr. Nicolaas Petrus Gouws held an unused old order right concerning a coal deposit located on his farm, Driefontein, Middelburg. Shortly before the MPRDA took effect on 1 May 2004, Mr. Gouws lodged an application for a prospecting right on 29 April 2005, in accordance with section 16(1) of the MPRDA. However, just three days after this deadline, the Applicant, Magnificent Mile Trading 30 (Pty) Ltd, also applied for the same prospecting right. The Department of Mineral Resources erroneously granted a prospecting right to Magnificent Mile on 16 January 2006, despite Mr. Gouws' application being pending. Following Mr. Gouws' death on 7 November 2005 before a decision was rendered, legal contention arose over the transmissibility of Mr. Gouws' rights and the validity of the prospecting right awarded to Magnificent Mile ([1]-[12]).

### I - Issues
1. **Transmissibility of Rights**: The first key issue is whether Mr. Gouws' unused old order right was transmissible upon his death. Specifically, the court needed to determine if this right continued to exist and could be inherited by his estate.
2. **Oudekraal/Kirland Rule Application**: The second issue revolved around the applicability of the Oudekraal rule, which asserts that an unlawful administrative act remains valid until challenged and set aside. The court had to evaluate if the order granted to Magnificent Mile could be upheld despite its potential unlawfulness given the existence of Mr. Gouws' previously lodged application ([19]-[21]).

### R - Rules
The court primarily relied on the provisions of the MPRDA, particularly:
- **Items 8(1) to (4) of Schedule II**: These outline the status of old order rights and their rights of application post-MPRDA's enactment.
- **Oudekraal Estates v City of Cape Town** and **MEC for Health, Eastern Cape v Kirland Investments**: Established the principle that unlawful administrative actions hold factual validity until addressed through a proper legal challenge ([2], [4], [43]-[48]).

### A - Application
In examining the facts, the court underscored that Mr. Gouws' right was not merely a right to a decision but constituted a proprietary right that held value. The evidence demonstrated that the legislative intent behind item 8 reflected a desire to preserve the rights of old order holders ([25]-[30]). Consequently, Mr. Gouws' unused old order right remained valid after his death and was transmissible to his estate.

Regarding the Oudekraal rule, the court noted that while unlawful actions may exist in fact and possess legal consequences, the validity of Mr. Gouws' old order right did not depend on the existence of the prospecting right granted to Magnificent Mile. The concurrent awarding of a prospecting right to Magnificent Mile, while Mr. Gouws' application was still pending, was deemed invalid. The court confirmed that, because the application was not finalised, Magnificent Mile's right could not detract from the validity of Mr. Gouws' application ([36]-[44]).

### C - Conclusion
The Constitutional Court ruled that Mr. Gouws' unused old order right remains valid and that his application for a prospecting right was still pending at the time of his death. Consequently, any rights awarded to Magnificent Mile were declared invalid, reaffirming the principle that old order rights under the MPRDA are protected from competition in pending processes. The court ordered that the Department of Mineral Resources must decide Mr. Gouws' application within 30 days and that the government respondents are liable for the costs of the proceedings ([67]). This ruling strengthens the assurance of the continuity of rights held under previous legislation while adhering to the legal framework established by the MPRDA.
