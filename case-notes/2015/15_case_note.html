Chevron SA (Pty) Limited v Wilson t/a Wilson’s Transport and Others

Citation Chevron SA (Pty) Limited v Wilson t/a Wilson’s Transport and Others [2015] ZACC 15; Case CCT 88/14.

Constitutional Court 2015 [2015] ZACC 15; Case CCT 88/14. ### Court Constitutional Cou

Citation

Chevron SA (Pty) Limited v Wilson t/a Wilson’s Transport and Others [2015] ZACC 15; Case CCT 88/14.

Court

Constitutional Court of South Africa

Coram

Moseneke DCJ, Cameron J, Froneman J, Jappie AJ, Khampepe J, Madlanga J, Molemela AJ, Nkabinde J, Theron AJ, and Tshiqi AJ

Delivered by Madlanga J (unanimous decision).

Date

Heard on: 24 March 2015

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts

[1] The case arises from confirmation proceedings initiated following a determination by the Western Cape High Court, where the court declared section 89(5)(b) of the National Credit Act 34 of 2005 (NCA) unconstitutional. The court found that the section allowed for arbitrary deprivation of property in violation of section 25(1) of the Constitution. [2] Before the High Court ruling, the National Credit Amendment Act 19 of 2014 was enacted but had not yet come into force at the time of the proceedings.

[4] The applicant, Chevron SA (Pty) Limited (Chevron), extended credit to the first respondent, Dennis Edwin Wilson t/a Wilson’s Transport (Wilson), for petroleum products starting in 1997. Disputes over billing arose in 2008, leading to Chevron initiating litigation to recover an outstanding balance of approximately R3.3 million.

[6] Chevron acknowledged its regulatory obligations under the NCA, specifically its requirement to register as a credit provider, which it failed to do. [7] Consequently, the agreements between Chevron and Wilson were deemed unlawful and void under section 89 of the NCA, which stipulates that unregistered credit agreements are void. [8] This brought up the issue that Chevron might be liable to refund payments made by Wilson amounting to about R33 million, which were made following the enactment of the NCA.

I – Issues

[10] The central constitutional question was whether section 89(5)(b) imposes an arbitrary deprivation of property by mandatorily requiring credit providers who are unregistered to refund all payments made under unlawful credit agreements. Specific issues included:

  • [15(a)] Whether money paid to a credit provider qualifies as property under the Constitution.
  • [15(b)] Whether the obligation to repay constitutes a deprivation of property.
  • [15(c)] Whether such deprivation is arbitrary.
  • [15(d)] Whether other legal remedies (e.g., unjustified enrichment claims) could mitigate any claimed arbitrariness.

R – Rules

[2] The relevant legal framework includes:

  • National Credit Act 34 of 2005: Aimed at regulating credit agreements and protecting consumers, specifically sections 40 and 89.
  • Constitution of South Africa: Notably section 25(1), which protects against arbitrary deprivation of property.
  • In interpreting these sections, the court refers to precedents concerning property rights and the principles of legality and fairness, drawing upon cases such as First National Bank of SA Ltd v Commissioner, South African Revenue Service [2002] ZACC 5 and National Credit Regulator v Opperman and Others [2012] ZACC 29.

A – Application

[16] The Court first recognized money as property under section 25 of the Constitution, confirming that Chevron's received payments constituted a protected property interest. [17] The Court established that the mandatory refund order under section 89(5)(b) would substantially interfere with Chevron’s ownership and was thus a deprivation of property.

[20] The Court tackled the question of arbitrariness head-on, noting that the lack of judicial discretion under section 89(5)(b) violates the standard of a rational connection between deprivation and purpose, essentially rendering it procedurally unfair. [24] The Court found that procedural fairness is compromised because the courts cannot consider individual circumstances that may affect a just outcome, leading to arbitrary results contrary to constitutional guarantees.

[25] Regarding unjustified enrichment, the Court ruled that while a claim may exist, it did not compensate for the automatic obligation to refund. Further, the associated risks—such as a consumer's insolvency—meant Chevron's eventual recourse through an enrichment claim was insufficient to neutralize the arbitrariness imposed by section 89(5)(b).

C – Conclusion

[34] The Court concluded that section 89(5)(b) of the NCA indeed constituted an unjustifiable and arbitrary deprivation of property, rendering it unconstitutional. The necessary remedial measures were included from the amended Act, which allowed for judicial discretion in rectifying unjust outcomes. As such, the High Court's declaration of invalidity was confirmed, ensuring that the reformulation of section 89(5) would take effect retroactively from the date the constitutional defect was identified until the date the Amendment Act came into force. Thus, the Court maintained that the legislation needed to protect both the consumer and the credit provider equitably.

This decision serves a critical role in reinforcing the balance between ensuring consumer protection while safeguarding property rights, asserting the necessity for judicial discretion in adjudicating cases involving unlawful credit agreements.
