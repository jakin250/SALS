Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# FIRAC Case Note

## Case Title
**Theron v Premier of the Western Cape Province (1310/2017)** [2019] ZASCA 6

## F – Facts
The appellant, Mr. Ernest Theron, was employed as the Chief Executive Officer of the Western Cape Provincial Development Council (the Council) under a fixed-term employment contract that commenced on 1 July 2009 and was set to terminate on 30 June 2012. The Council, established by the Provincial Development Council Act 5 of 1996, was disestablished on 5 December 2011 following the enactment of the Provincial Development Council Repeal Act 5 of 2011. Clause 5 of the employment contract explicitly stated that it would automatically terminate on that date. 

Following the disestablishment of the Council, Theron sought to claim remuneration for the remainder of the contracted period until June 2012, inclusive of a performance bonus and leave pay. The Premier of the Western Cape Province accepted that the claim was valid with the exception of the salary for 2012, arguing that Theron was only entitled to one month’s notice of termination as the employment could be terminated by either party with one month’s notice.

Theron’s applications to the Commission for Conciliation Mediation and Arbitration (CCMA) were dismissed based on a lack of jurisdiction, as the Premier was argued not to be his employer directly. Theron subsequently instituted action in November 2012. The trial court, presided over by Gamble J, ruled against Theron, concluding that he was not entitled to the additional salary beyond one month’s notice and thus dismissed his claims, although he was granted leave to appeal ([1]–[6]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issues raised in the appeal were ([6]):
1. Whether the termination of Theron's fixed-term employment contract could be regarded as “premature” termination under the contract’s agreed terms.
2. Whether Theron was entitled to damages correlating to the full unexpired duration of his fixed-term employment contract or limited to one month’s notice of termination.

## R – Rules
The determination of the case relied on several legal principles and precedents regarding employment contracts and their interpretation, particularly concerning fixed-term contracts. Key legal standards include the following ([19]–[21]):
- The importance of a holistic interpretation of contracts, considering intent, context, and the specific wording utilized. The court emphasized that interpretation is a unitary exercise, considering both the text and the contract as a whole, as established in **Natal Joint Municipal Pension Fund v Endumeni Municipality** [2012] ZASCA 13.
- The common law principle that a party to a fixed-term employment contract may not unilaterally terminate such contract absent a breach or unless provided for in the contract itself ([15]–[16]). The precedent established in **Fedlife Assurance Ltd v Wolfaardt** [2002] SA 49 (SCA) affirmed the employee's right to full damages for unlawful premature termination.
- The Court also cited the obligations created by actions taken under the Public Finance Management Act 1 of 1999 regarding the treatment of liabilities post-disestablishment.

## A – Application
The Court examined the wording of clause 9 of Theron’s employment contract, particularly its provisions regarding termination. The appellant argued that while either party could terminate the employment with one month's notice, the grounds for termination by the employer must adhere to misconduct or operational requirements, thus implying that the Council was bound to perform until the agreed termination date ([14]–[17]).

Conversely, the Premier contended that no part of the contract explicitly negated the right of termination upon notice for lawful operational reasons. The Court found this interpretation more coherent and supported by the contractual framework as a whole. It further noted that looking at the contract holistically, subclause 1 allowed either party to terminate by providing notice, while subclause 2 delineated the grounds for summary termination distinct from notice. Therefore, the setup did not support Theron’s assertion that he was entitled to additional pay through June 2012 ([22]–[26]).

The court concluded that Theron was adequately notified, as CEO, leading up to the disestablishment and that the framework of the contract allowed the Department to fulfill the prior employment obligations legally through the payment for one month’s notice.

## C – Conclusion
Ultimately, the Supreme Court of Appeal ruled against Theron, affirming the trial court's decision that his claim exceeded the lawful period of notice to which he was entitled following the disestablishment of the Council. Theron’s employment was terminated in accordance with the terms of the contract that allowed for one month’s notice, even if the actual termination notification was informal. Therefore, the appellant was entitled only to the remuneration already paid covering that month ([27]–[28]). Consequently, the appeal was dismissed with costs, signaling a significant interpretation of fixed-term employment agreements and employer liabilities in the context of organizational disestablishment.
