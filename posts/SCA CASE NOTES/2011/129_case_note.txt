Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# FIRAC Case Note: **Transnet Ltd v The Owner of the MV 'Alina II' [2011] ZASCA 129**

**F – Facts:**  
The case **Transnet Ltd v The Owner of the MV 'Alina II'** involves a dispute arising from admiralty law concerning simultaneous actions in rem and in personam. The **appellant**, Transnet Ltd, is the port authority of Saldanha Bay. On **29 October 2009**, the **Alina II**, a bulk carrier, began loading iron ore fines at Transnet's terminal but caused damages due to water ingress into its ballast tank, resulting in a **port list**. Following the loading, the vessel stayed blocked at the berth until **27 March 2010** when it finally departed, resulting in claims for damages inflicted on Transnet due to its occupation of the berth ([1]-[3]). 

Transnet initiated an **action in rem** against the vessel on **13 January 2010**, arresting it to recover damages associated with its prolonged stay. The owner of the vessel submitted a **notice of intention to defend** on **27 January 2010** and later filed particulars of claim for both contract and delictual claims ([3]-[6]).

On **19 March 2010**, the vessel was arrested again in a separate action initiated by other claimants. This situation led Transnet to seek an **attachment ad fundandam et confirmandam jurisdictionem** to establish jurisdiction for pursuing an **in personam action** against the vessel’s owner based on the same claims ([3]-[5]). The owner opposed this application on the grounds of **abuse of process** and prior submission to jurisdiction due to the notice of intention to defend ([5]-[6]).

The **Western Cape High Court**, presided over by Griesel J, sided with the owner and held that a submission had occurred that precluded the attachment ([5]-[6]). Transnet subsequently appealed to the **Supreme Court of Appeal**.

---

**I – Issues:**  
The primary legal issues in this case include:

1. **Whether Transnet could simultaneously pursue an in rem action and seek an attachment ad fundandam et confirmandam jurisdictionem against the owner of the vessel for similar claims.**
2. **Whether the owner's act of defending the in rem action constituted a submission to the jurisdiction of the South African court in a manner that would nullify the possibility of a subsequent attachment for an in personam action ([1])**.

---

**R – Rules:**  
The legal framework governing this case primarily derives from:

- **Admiralty Jurisdiction Regulation Act 105 of 1983 (the Act)**, particularly Section 3(4) which allows for actions in rem and does not prohibit simultaneous actions in personam against the owner of a vessel ([4][10]).
- **Admiralty Rules**, specifically Rule 8(3), which addresses the implications of entering an appearance to defend in an action in rem, indicating that doing so does not automatically entail personal liability ([30]-[32]).
- **Principles established in related case law**, particularly *The Dictator* and *The Gemma*, which assert that the entry of appearance in an action in rem can be construed as a submission to jurisdiction concerning personal liability if such a liability exists ([18]-[22]).

---

**A – Application:**  
The court analyzed the issue of whether the owner's behavior in submitting a notice of intention to defend the action in rem indicated a submission to the jurisdiction. The court noted that by defending the in rem action, the owner effectively presented an intention to contest the claims raised by Transnet, which resulted in a legal submission to the court’s jurisdiction. Consequently, this submission barred any subsequent attachments ad fundandam et confirmandam jurisdictionem ([15]-[16]).

Furthermore, the court examined the nuances of admiralty law in South Africa concerning the ability to control the jurisdiction over claims. The distinction of actions in rem and in personam was addressed, highlighting procedural differences, yet affirming that both actions could exist where there is personal liability involved ([16]-[30]). It was concluded that no sufficient grounds existed which warranted overriding the submission to jurisdiction simply because the action was in rem.

---

**C – Conclusion:**  
The **Supreme Court of Appeal** dismissed the appeal, affirming the lower court's ruling that the owner's defense of the action in rem constituted a submission to the court's jurisdiction, thus invalidating Transnet's attempt to make an attachment for the in personam action. The court held that both actions could not proceed simultaneously given this submission, reinforcing principles of **abuse of process** and proper jurisdictional limits within admiralty law ([37]-[38]). The implications of this ruling clarify the procedural landscape of simultaneous claims in South African admiralty law, emphasizing the importance of a party's actions and intentions in determining jurisdiction.

---

This case serves as a critical touchpoint in admiralty law within South Africa, highlighting both the procedural intricacies and the broader implications of jurisdictional submissions by vessel owners in maritime disputes.
