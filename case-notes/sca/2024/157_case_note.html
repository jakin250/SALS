Supreme Court of Appeal

Roadmac Surfacing (Pty) Ltd v MEC for the Department of Police, Roads and Transport, Free State Province and Another (461/2023)

An interim interdict was granted to Roadmac on 28 March 2022, preventing the first respondent from instructing the second respondent to proceed with the works under the tender ([para 2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2024

FIRAC Method

F – Facts:

The appellant, Roadmac Surfacing (Pty) Ltd (Roadmac), appeals against the dismissal of its review application, originally brought before the Free State Division of the High Court, Bloemfontein ([para 1]). The case arose from a tender bidding process for maintenance works on Route P44/1&2, where Roadmac was unsuccessful, with the second respondent, Tau Pele Construction (Pty) Ltd, being awarded the tender ([para 2]). Following an application for reasons behind the tender award, Roadmac initiated review proceedings, alleging that the tender award was neither fair nor transparent.

An interim interdict was granted to Roadmac on 28 March 2022, preventing the first respondent from instructing the second respondent to proceed with the works under the tender ([para 2]). Notably, the costs associated with this interim relief were reserved by the High Court, leading to the contested aspect of costs remaining unresolved ([para 3]). The appeal was initially heard on 7 May 2024, but was adjourned when a completion certificate for the works, dated 28 September 2023, was presented unexpectedly by counsel for the first respondent ([para 4]).

I – Issues:

The central legal issue is whether the appeal will have a practical effect, given that the tender works were completed before the hearing and whether the High Court’s failure to address the reserved costs constitutes exceptional circumstances under s 16(2) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 ([para 7], [para 17]). Additionally, the case raises questions about the court’s capacity to declare the prior administrative acts invalid under s 172(1) of the Constitution, despite the appeal being rendered moot ([para 8], [para 14]).

R – Rules:

The applicable legal principles stem from s 16(2)(a)(i) and (ii) of the Superior Courts Act, which stipulates that appeals may be dismissed if they hold no practical effect unless exceptional circumstances exist ([para 6]). Furthermore, s 172(1) of the Constitution mandates courts to declare laws or actions inconsistent with the Constitution as invalid and allows for just and equitable remedies ([para 8]). Precedents such as Naylor and Another v Jansen and National Coalition for Gay and Lesbian Equality & Others v Minister of Home Affairs establish the judicial reluctance to address moot matters that do not present existing controversies ([para 14], [para 16]).

A – Application:

The court addressed the issue of practical effect by asserting that the completion of the works rendered the appeal moot, therefore negating the merits of Roadmac’s claim to have the tender process declared invalid ([para 15]). However, the court recognized the unresolved question of costs stemming from the Daffue J order, indicating that the High Court’s failure to decide this aspect constituted an exceptional circumstance. The emphasis was placed on the necessity for courts to resolve all pertinent issues to uphold justice ([para 16], [para 17]). It acknowledged that the existing costs, particularly those arising from the withheld costs leading up to the interdict, merited reevaluation due to the procedural mishaps of the High Court ([para 19]).

The court further highlighted the first respondent’s lack of transparency regarding the completion certificate, determining that the failure to disclose such crucial evidence prior to the hearing warranted the assessment of costs against them for any adjournments caused ([para 20]). Roadmac's claim for a costs order was supported by its partial success on appeal, as the court agreed to reassess the costs incurred before the interdict ([para 21]).

C – Conclusion:

The Supreme Court of Appeal determined that while the appeal in terms of merits was moot and of no practical effect, the question of costs necessitated judicial intervention due to the unresolved nature of the Daffue J order ([para 22]). Consequently, the appeal succeeded in part: the court varied the initial costs order, mandating that the first respondent pay the costs related to the adjournments and the reserved costs from the prior proceedings, which included provisions for two counsel where applicable. This ruling underscores the court's commitment to ensuring all substantive and procedural issues are addressed to maintain judicial integrity and uphold principles of fairness and transparency in the tender process ([para 22]).

The implication of this case extends to future administrative disputes, reinforcing the importance of thorough judicial scrutiny regarding procedural determinations of costs, thus facilitating the proper administration of justice in the tender award process.
