Kekana v The State

Case Title: Kekana v The State Neutral Citation: Kekana v The State (498/2015) [2015] ZASCA 194 (1 December 2015) Coram: Navsa, Cachalia, Shongwe, Tshiqi and Dambuza JJA Heard: 11 November 2015 Delivered: 1 December 2015

Supreme Court of Appeal 2015 Kekana v The State (498/2015) [2015] ZASCA 194 (1 December 2015) Delivered 1 December 2015

Case Title: Kekana v The State
Neutral Citation: Kekana v The State (498/2015) [2015] ZASCA 194 (1 December 2015)
Coram: Navsa, Cachalia, Shongwe, Tshiqi and Dambuza JJA
Heard: 11 November 2015
Delivered: 1 December 2015

F – Facts

On 14 January 2005, during a robbery at the Ga-Raoleka supermarket in Thabamoopo, Lebowakgomo, two men, Mr. Aslam Muhamad and Mr. Foster Mashimbye, were shot and killed ([1]). The robbery was carried out by four suspects, including the appellant, Bushy Vincent Kekana, and another individual named Mr. Brian Poho, who later testified as a state witness. In the course of the robbery, Mrs. Raesibe Madimetja was shot and injured. Following the incident, the appellant and Poho were captured by community members and handed over to the police ([1]).

Kekana, along with C2 and C3, faced multiple charges, namely two counts of murder, one count of robbery with aggravating circumstances, unlawful possession of a firearm and ammunition, and attempted murder ([2]). The trial court imposed harsh sentences: life imprisonment for each of the murder counts, 15 years for robbery, and lesser sentences for the remaining charges. The sentences were structured to run concurrently, leading to an effective life sentence for the appellant ([3]). After the High Court rejected an appeal against the life sentences, this case escalated to the Supreme Court of Appeal ([4]).

I – Issues

The primary legal question hinged on whether the effective life sentence imposed on the appellant, who was a minor at the time of the crimes, was justifiable. It was particularly critical to assess if the trial court had adequately considered the appellant’s youthfulness and the implications of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997, specifically in relation to his age during the commission of the offences ([6]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal provisions stemmed from section 51(1) and (3)(b) of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997. These sections dictate that for individuals under 18, the maximum sentence for murder is not life imprisonment unless substantial and compelling circumstances exist ([7]). Furthermore, the court referenced constitutional provisions that emphasize the rehabilitation potential of youthful offenders, mandating courts to consider the consequences of sentencing age, as documented in Section 28(1)(g) of the Constitution ([10]).

The court also relied on established judicial principles governing sentencing, including those from S v Malgas ([5]), positing that an appeal court may intervene only upon the demonstration of significant misdirection by the trial court ([6]).

A – Application

In the review of the case, the court identified a crucial factual error: both the trial court and the full court incorrectly believed the appellant had turned eighteen at the time of the crimes. In reality, he was 17 years and 10 months old ([6]). This misjudgment led to the erroneous assumption that life imprisonment was obligatory unless compelling reasons for a lesser sentence were present.

The Supreme Court underscored the necessity of factoring in the appellant's youth in the sentencing process, highlighting that youthful offenders are often guided by impulsive actions and that they have the potential for rehabilitation ([10]). The court acknowledged the serious nature of the crimes but ultimately held that the trial court had failed to adhere to legislative guidelines aimed at protecting children and recognizing their developmental phase.

The court also considered the significant time that the appellant had already spent in custody (two years and eight months), which further informed its decision that a term of 20 years' imprisonment would align better with both legal statutes and the mitigating factors presented by the appellant's youth ([12]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal against the life sentences on the counts of murder. It found that the previous courts had materially misdirected themselves by imposing maximum penalties without considering the appellant's age ([13]). The court replaced the life sentences with an effective sentence of 20 years' imprisonment, reflecting the necessity to balance the gravity of the crimes with the appellant's status as a minor and the principles of rehabilitation set forth in the law ([13]).

This decision underscores the South African legal system's commitment to equitably addressing the sentencing of youthful offenders, aligning its judicial practices with constitutional mandates and international human rights principles. The ruling reaffirms the principle that while accountability remains paramount, the context of the offender's youth must also be rigorously taken into account in sentencing decisions ([12]).
