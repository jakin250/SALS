Case Note: SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: FIRAC Method for *Stephanus de Lange NO v The Minister of Water and Environmental Affairs* ([2019] ZASCA 59)

**F – Facts:**
The appellant, Stephanus de Lange NO, is the executor of the estate of the late Petrus Willem Terblanche, a former landowner in Koppies. Terblanche claimed damages from the Minister of Water and Environmental Affairs (the respondent) for failing to maintain irrigation infrastructure, which he alleged resulted in the inability to irrigate crops and subsequent financial losses ([1]–[2]).

The farm in question is part of the Rhenoster River Government Water Scheme, originally governed by the Water Act 54 of 1956 but transitioned to the National Water Act 36 of 1998 (NWA) upon its enactment. Under the NWA, Terblanche retained a lawful water use right due to his prior lawful abstractions ([2]).

Terblanche’s water usage specifically allowed irrigation of five hectares, but after a drought and water restrictions in 2003, the irrigation canal fell into disrepair and ceased to deliver water. Consequently, Terblanche claimed damages for losses pertaining to either sorghum or dairy farming, though inconsistencies arose concerning the nature of his agricultural practices ([3]–[4]).

The respondent asserted a special plea of prescription, contending that Terblanche’s cause of action, based on the alleged neglect of the irrigation canals, arose in 2003, thereby rendering the claim time-barred by the time the summons was filed ([9]).

**I – Issues:**
The primary legal question revolves around whether Terblanche’s right to water constituted a continuous or real right, which would preclude prescription, or whether his claim for damages had indeed prescribed, considering the passive nature of his water use during the claim period ([10], [15]).

**R – Rules:**
This case draws upon several legal rules:
1. **Prescription Act 68 of 1969**, particularly § 12(3), which outlines that a debt is not deemed due until the creditor is aware of the identity of the debtor and the facts giving rise to the debt ([18]).
2. **National Water Act 36 of 1998 (NWA)**, specifically § 39, which stipulates the restrictions upon water use and the State’s authority over water resources ([11], [12]).
3. **Impala Water Users Association v Lourens NO** 2008 (2) SA 495 (SCA), clarifying the nature of water use rights as not merely contractual ([15]).

**A – Application:**
The court examined the nature of the appellant's water use right and found that, despite its statutory footing, it was contingent upon compliance with various conditions, such as applying for water and paying usage fees ([15]). The deceased’s inaction after 2003 undermined the argument for an ongoing breach. His right to water did not imply an unqualified entitlement to irrigation, particularly as the infrastructure deteriorated and remained unused, further emphasizing that an actionable breach had not occurred ([15], [16]).

Crucially, it was concluded that Terblanche was aware of the inadequacies of the irrigation system by 2003, which was when the cause of action arose. As such, the three-year period for prescription had effectively lapsed by the time the summons was issued in 2010 ([18]).

**C – Conclusion:**
Ultimately, the court dismissed the appeal and upheld the prescription plea, ruling that Terblanche's claim for damages was time-barred. The decision crystallizes the principle that while statutory water rights exist, they do not preclude the effects of prescription, particularly when the rights are not actively exercised. The implications of this ruling underscore the necessity for water rights holders to engage proactively with regulatory frameworks and maintain their entitlements through compliance with statutory requisites ([19]).

The appeal was dismissed with costs, establishing a precedent on the relationship between statutory water rights and prescription.
