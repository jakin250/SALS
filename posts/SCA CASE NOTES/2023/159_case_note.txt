Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2023

# FIRAC Case Note: *African National Congress v Ezulweni Investments (Pty) Ltd* ([2023] ZASCA 159)

## F – Facts
The case arises from a contractual dispute between the African National Congress (ANC) and Ezulweni Investments (Pty) Ltd (Ezulweni). On 20 February 2019, an oral agreement purportedly was formed for the supply of election campaign materials, specifically PVC banners. The Chief Executive Officer of Ezulweni, Mr. Renash Ramdas, who had previously supplied election materials to the ANC, negotiated with ANC officials, including Finance Manager Mr. Mabaso and the Personal Assistant to the Head of Elections, Mr. Nkholise. Despite subsequent communications and the fulfillment of the order, the ANC denied the existence of any binding agreement, leading to Ezulweni seeking payment of R102,465,000 with interest and costs in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg. The high court initially ruled in favour of Ezulweni, which prompted the ANC to appeal the decision ([1]–[14]).

The appellants had multiple meetings with key ANC personnel, including a final meeting just before the election. The ANC later claimed that no authority was given for Mabaso or Nkholise to enter into the agreement, as only Mashatile, the ANC Treasurer General, could authorize such contracts, necessitating a purchase order ([15]–[18]). Ezulweni provided substantial evidence of communications and actions taken that signified the acceptance and implementation of the agreement ([9]–[13]).

## I – Issues
The primary legal issues as delineated in the judgment include:

1. Whether there is a bona fide factual dispute concerning the existence of the contractual agreement between the ANC and Ezulweni.
2. Whether Mr. Nkholise had the requisite authority to conclude the agreement on behalf of the ANC.
3. The admissibility of further evidence related to an alleged corrupt relationship between ANC officials and Ezulweni, as advanced by the ANC in its appeal ([20]–[32]).

## R – Rules
The court relied on established legal principles regarding contract formation and authority. Notable cases referenced include:

- *Stellenbosch Farmers’ Winery Ltd v Stellenvale Winery (Pty) Ltd*, which provides guidance on when disputes of fact render summary judgments inappropriate ([20]).
- *Plascon-Evans Paints Ltd v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd*, outlining how to evaluate factual disputes, particularly where one party's contentions lack plausibility ([21]).
- Statutory provisions related to the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, particularly Section 19(b), which governs the reception of further evidence on appeal ([33]).

## A – Application
The Supreme Court of Appeal assessed the evidence presented by both parties, focusing on the credibility and consistency of testimonies and documents. Although the ANC contended there were factual disputes, the court found its version to present uncreditworthy denials and to lack particularity regarding its negative assertions. The court highlighted the coherence in Ezulweni's account and the evidence of the communications exchanged between Ramdas, Mabaso, and Nkholise, which supported Ezulweni's position. The repeated failures of the ANC to contest factual assertions adequately, and the formal actions indicating the existence of an agreement, underscored the court's assessment ([23]–[24]).

Regarding the authority question, the court examined the implications of the 2 April letter, which indicated that Nkholise was assigned responsibilities that included financial signatory powers during the campaign. This contextual interpretation led the court to conclude that Nkholise had actual authority to finalize the agreement, as the election campaign activities preceded the formalization of the roles within the ANC ([27]–[32]).

On the issue of admitting new evidence pertaining to alleged corrupt actions, the court found that the ANC failed to satisfy the stringent tests for admitting evidence on appeal established in *Pepkor Holdings Ltd v AJVH Holdings (Pty) Ltd*. The potential evidence was deemed insufficiently substantiated and irrelevant, as it did not pertain to the direct issue of whether an agreement existed ([33]–[41]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the ANC's appeal, affirming the finding that a valid contract was concluded on 20 February 2019 with actual authority established for the ANC representatives involved. The court ruled that the ANC could not raise bona fide disputes over the facts that would necessitate a trial. Additionally, the application to admit further evidence was dismissed due to lack of relevance and sufficient foundation, reaffirming principles governing contract law and the necessity for credible assertions in disputes of fact ([42]).

The judgment underscores the importance of clear communications and the recognition of implied authority in contractual agreements within statutory and procedural frameworks. The decision reinforces the legitimacy of contract formation in contexts where parties act with an understanding that is supported by consistent and corroborated behavior, alongside the strict adherence to evidentiary standards in appellate procedures.
