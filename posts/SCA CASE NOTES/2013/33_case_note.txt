# FIRAC Case Note: Leadtrain Assessments (Pty) Ltd v Leadtrain (Pty) Ltd (427/12)

**Citation:** Leadtrain Assessments (Pty) Ltd v Leadtrain (Pty) Ltd (427/12) [2013] ZASCA 33 (28 March 2013)

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Coram:** Nugent, Ponnan and Tshiqi JJA and Swain and Saldulker AJJA

---

### F – Facts

The case arises from various disputes between the appellants, comprising Leadtrain Assessments (Pty) Ltd, College-on-Line CC, and Steve Byron, against the respondents, which include Leadtrain (Pty) Ltd, Richard Lilford, and others ([1]). These disputes were referred to arbitration as per the Arbitration Act 42 of 1965, culminating in a costs award rendered by the arbitrator ([1]).

The arbitrator ordered Leadtrain (Pty) Ltd to pay 80% of the costs of the arbitration and certain costs incurred in the High Court. Furthermore, he directed that specific costs be included in the arbitration costs and ordered Mr. Lilford to bear half of the costs jointly and severally with Leadtrain (Pty) Ltd ([2]). 

Following the arbitration, the appellants sought to have the arbitrator's award made an order of the court, invoking section 31 of the Arbitration Act. Mr. Lilford countered this by requesting the setting aside of parts of the costs award or remitting those to the arbitrator for reconsideration under section 32(2) of the Act ([3]). The South Gauteng High Court, through Mabesele J, dismissed the application in its entirety and granted Mr. Lilford's counter-application, setting aside the contested portions of the award ([5]).

The appellants’ appeal is primarily focused on contesting the counter-application's success and seeking to have the original costs award reinstated as a court order ([6]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issue is whether the grounds for setting aside the costs award based on an alleged misdirection by the arbitrator in exercising discretion are sufficient to warrant remittance for reconsideration ([7]).

A secondary issue concerns the interpretation of "good cause" under section 32(2) of the Arbitration Act, particularly in the context of determining if a misdirection qualifies for such remittance when the award itself was not found to be the result of misconduct or a gross irregularity as defined by the Act ([9], [10]).

### R – Rules

The arbitral process in South Africa is governed by the Arbitration Act, specifically sections 28 and 33, which articulate the finality of arbitration awards and the limited scope for court interference. Section 28 dictates that arbitrators' awards are ordinarily final unless otherwise provided in the arbitration agreement ([8]). 

Section 33(1) permits courts to intervene in cases of misconduct, gross irregularity, or excess of powers, which Mr. Lilford did not rely upon ([9]). Instead, he leaned on section 32(2), which allows for remittance under "good cause" within six weeks of the publication of the award ([9]).

Precedent cases cited include *Joubert t/a Wilcon v Beacham*, *Benab Properties CC v Sportshoe (Pty) Ltd*, and *Clarke v Semenya NO*, all suggesting conditions under which courts may intervene in arbitration decisions based on alleged misdirection ([10], [11]).

### A – Application

The court analyzed Mr. Lilford's argument regarding misdirection against the backdrop of established precedents. It reaffirmed that an arbitrator typically possesses broad discretion in cost awards, akin to that of a court ([7]). Misdirection alone, however, does not constitute sufficient grounds for judicial remittance unless such misdirection leads to an outcome that might fall under the misbehavior or misconduct category outlined in section 33(1) ([14]).

The court found that Mr. Lilford's appeal constituted an attempt to challenge the arbitrator's error through remittance under section 32(2), which was deemed inappropriate. It was emphasized that remittal should occur when the arbitrator fails to address an issue, whereas here, the arbitrator had addressed the costs award distinctly ([15], [16]). Thus, merely claiming error without substantial evidence of misconduct was insufficient for remittance.

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, effectively reversing the High Court's order that set aside the contested paragraphs of the costs award. The Court ruled that Mr. Lilford's counter-application did not meet the necessary grounds stipulated by the Arbitration Act, emphasizing the finality of arbitration awards as a guiding principle ([17]). 

As a result, the court ordered that paragraphs 4 and 5 of the costs award be made an order of court, and costs of the appeal were to be borne by the respondents, jointly and severally ([17]). 

This decision reinforces the principle of finality in arbitration, signalling that mere perceived errors by arbitrators will not suffice for judicial intervention unless they cross thresholds defined by the governing statutory provisions.
