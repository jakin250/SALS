Case Title: Slabbert N O & Others v Ma-Afrika Hotels (Pty) Ltd t/a Rivierbos Guest House (Case No: 772/2021) [2022] ZASCA 152

Supreme Court of Appeal 2022 [2022] ZASCA 152 Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South

Case Title: Slabbert N O & Others v Ma-Afrika Hotels (Pty) Ltd t/a Rivierbos Guest House (Case No: 772/2021) [2022] ZASCA 152

Court: Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

Coram: Molemela, Makgoka, Gorven JJA, Kgoele and Goosen AJJA

Judgment Date: 04 November 2022

F – Facts

[1] This appeal involves a dispute regarding Ma-Afrika Hotels (Pty) Ltd, trading as Rivierbos Guest House (the respondent), concerning unpaid rental and related charges connected to a lease agreement held by the Trustees of the Venezia Trust (the Trust). The Trust had purchased a property in Stellenbosch for R15,500,000 and subsequently leased it back to the respondent, allowing it to conduct its guesthouse business.

[2] The lease, effective from 12 February 2019 with a ten-year duration, stipulated that the lessee was liable for all costs associated with the property, including rental payments. These rental payments were to be made in advance on the first day of every month. Failure to pay on time could lead to immediate lease cancellation by the Trust.

[3] On 15 March 2020, due to the COVID-19 pandemic, the South African government declared a National State of Disaster, resulting in extensive restrictions that confined the business operations of the respondent, thereby affecting its ability to fulfill its obligations under the lease.

[4] As of 31 December 2020, the Trust alleged that the respondent had accrued arrears amounting to R872,266 and subsequently initiated eviction proceedings against it. The application was dismissed by the High Court, which also ordered the respondent to pay the arrears. The Trust appealed the eviction dismissal.

I – Issues

[5] The central legal issue before the court was whether the High Court erred in denying the application for eviction, given the respondent's alleged breach of lease terms due to non-payment of rent during the pandemic. Specifically, the court needed to consider:

  1. Whether the doctrine of pacta sunt servanda applies in the context of COVID-19 restrictions.
  2. If the lease agreement's cancellation clause was unfair or unreasonable.
  3. Whether the doctrine of exceptio non adimpleti contractus could relieve the respondent of its rental obligations due to impossibility of performance caused by external circumstances.

R – Rules

[6] The court relied on well-established principles of contract law, particularly the doctrine of pacta sunt servanda, which requires parties to adhere to their contractual obligations. The court also referenced the doctrine of vis major, which may discharge contractual obligations when performance becomes impossible due to unforeseen events.

[7] Legal precedents such as Baynes Fashions (Pty) Ltd t/a Gerani v Hyprop Investments (Pty) Ltd and Tudor Hotel and Brasserie and Bar (Pty) Limited v Hence Trade 15 (Pty) Limited were cited, emphasizing the importance of exclusivity in lease agreements regarding rental payments and the non-reciprocity of obligations concerning beneficial occupation.

[8] The decision in Hansen, Schrader & Co v Kopelowitz was also referenced concerning the requirement for a direct relationship between the supervening impossibility of performance and the inability to pay rent.

A – Application

[9] The court analyzed whether the COVID-19 regulations constituted a valid and direct excuse under the principle of vis major. It acknowledged the pandemic's devastating impact on business operations but noted that the Trust's right to cancel the lease was not contingent upon its own performance obligations.

[10] The court found that under the lease, the respondent's obligation to pay rent was absolute and not reciprocal to the Trust’s duty to provide beneficial occupancy, as elucidated in previous case law. The respondent's claims of impossibility were deemed insufficient to negate the clear contractual terms.

[11] Despite recognizing the significant bruising of businesses during the lockdown, the court underscored that the respondent failed to adhere to the lease terms, having not made rental payments since September 2020. Therefore, the conditions for invoking the cancellation clause were deemed satisfied.

[12] The court also underscored that any potential argument for the remission of rent hinged on a detailed analysis in Part B of the application, which was set to be adjudicated separately.

C – Conclusion

[13] The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately ruled in favor of the Trust, overturning the High Court's decision. The respondent was evicted from the premises due to its failure to meet rental obligations under the lease agreement. The court's decision reinstated the principle of pacta sunt servanda in the context of the COVID-19 pandemic, affirming that contractual obligations remain binding despite unforeseen circumstances.

[14] The order of the High Court was set aside, allowing for the eviction to proceed, and the respondent was also ordered to bear the costs associated with the appeal. The cross-appeal regarding the arrears was upheld, leading to the litigation concerning those costs being remitted back to the High Court for determination.

Implications: This ruling emphasizes the importance of adhering to contractual obligations and clarifies the limits of relief under impossibility due to external factors in commercial leases. It reinforces the courts' stance on the non-negotiable nature of lease terms and highlights the challenges faced by tenants attempting to use pandemic-related restrictions as a defense against non-payment.
