Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2018

# FIRAC Case Note: Buffalo City Metropolitan Municipality v Nurcha Development Finance (PTY) Ltd [2018] ZASCA 122

### F – Facts

In *Buffalo City Metropolitan Municipality v Nurcha Development Finance (PTY) Ltd* ([1]-[3]), the Supreme Court of Appeal addressed a dispute involving three parties: Buffalo City Metropolitan Municipality (the Appellant), Nurcha Development Finance (PTY) Ltd (the First Respondent), Tusk Construction Support Services (PTY) Ltd (the Second Respondent), and New Boss Construction CC (the Third Respondent). The case revolved around a specific tacit agreement regarding payment structures in a construction contract.

Nurcha, a subsidiary of the National Urban Housing and Reconstruction Agency, provided financial assistance to emerging contractors who lacked access to banking services due to poor credit histories ([1]). In this particular case, New Boss was awarded a contract from Buffalo City for construction work in Duncan Village and simultaneously entered into a loan agreement with Nurcha ([4]-[5]).

On 11 October 2010, New Boss submitted an irrevocable payment instruction to Buffalo City, directing all payments for the construction contract to a designated account held at First National Bank ([7]). This instruction was acknowledged by the City on 14 October 2010, yet subsequent payments were made into a different account, leading to financial distress for New Boss ([9]-[10]).

Nurcha claimed damages from Buffalo City, asserting that a tacit contract existed which required the City to make payments into the designated account ([11]). Buffalo City contended that no such contract existed and attributed liability solely to New Boss ([12]). Initially, the High Court ruled in favor of Nurcha, prompting Buffalo City to appeal ([13]).

### I – Issues

The primary legal issue to be determined was whether a tacit contract was established between Buffalo City and Nurcha, given the conduct of the municipality concerning the payment arrangements ([13]-[14]). Sub-issues included:

1. Whether the conduct of Buffalo City demonstrated an intention to enter into a contract with Nurcha as alleged.
2. The authority of the officials involved, particularly Mr. Yako, in acknowledging the payment instruction and whether that acknowledgment constituted acceptance of a binding agreement ([14]-[15]).

### R – Rules

The court's analysis centered on the established principles governing tacit contracts. The precedent from *Butters v Mncora* ([16]) guided the court, establishing that the court must determine whether there is unequivocal conduct indicating that the parties intended to enter into a contract ([16]-[17]). 

The tests outlined by Corbett JA in *Joel Melamed and Hurwitz v Cleveland Estates (PTY) Ltd* and *Standard Bank of SA Ltd v Ocean Commodities Inc* regarding the nature of proofs required for tacit contracts were pivotal ([18]-[19]). The synthesis articulated in Christie's *Law of Contract in South Africa* ([19])—emphasizing a preponderance of probabilities coupled with unequivocal conduct—was also critical for the decision.

### A – Application

The court meticulously examined the sequence of events leading to the purported contract, noting that Buffalo City had acted consistently with the payment instruction from October 2010 to May 2011 ([22]). The acknowledgment of the irrevocable payment instruction and the City’s compliance bolstered the argument for a tacit contract, negating Buffalo City’s claim that no binding agreement had been formed ([23]-[25]).

The subsequent change in payment practices by the City, which contradicted the established agreement, further demonstrated a breach of conduct that was inconsistent with the position they had previously acknowledged, thus supporting Nurcha's claims ([26]-[27]). The court noted that the material facts were largely undisputed, with Buffalo City failing to produce evidence to deny the existence of the agreement that followed their actions ([25]-[26]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court concluded that Nurcha had successfully established, on a balance of probabilities, the existence of a tacit contract between it and Buffalo City; thus, Buffalo City was obligated to pay the damages claimed ([27]). The court dismissed the appeal with costs, affirming the decision of the High Court ([28]).

This case is significant as it reinforces the principle of recognizing tacit contracts through conduct, emphasizing that actions consistent with a contractual obligation can establish binding agreements even in the absence of written contracts. The case illustrates the judiciary's flexibility in ascertaining parties' intentions and agreements in contractual frameworks, particularly in public sector dealings.
