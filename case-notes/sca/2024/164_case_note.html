Supreme Court of Appeal 2024 [2024] ZASCA 164

F – Facts

In this case, the respondent, Mr. Thamsanqa Davis Bisset, represents the Bisset family, who owned land in Gqeberha, South Africa. The family's ownership was dispossessed in 1972 due to racially discriminatory laws ([para 1]). Under the Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994 (the Restitution Act), the Bisset family lodged a claim for restitution. On 18 April 2008, Mr. Bisset signed a document described as a "Settlement Agreement in terms of s 42D of the Restitution of Land Rights Act" ([para 1]). However, he later contended that the agreement had not been lawfully concluded due to a lack of concurrence from the relevant authorities and because the document was signed without adequate explanation of its contents ([para 4]).

After years of inaction and further attempts to engage with the authorities on compensation matters, Mr. Bisset initiated proceedings in September 2021 to declare the settlement agreement invalid and sought its review and annulment ([para 1], [para 2]). Additionally, he requested an extension of the 180-day period for bringing this review under the Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000 (PAJA) ([para 1]).

The high court, although noting the delay, decided it was in the interests of justice to condone this delay and proceeded to analyze the merits of the case ([para 3]). The court concluded that the Commissioner failed to consider both financial and non-financial factors in determining compensation, thus deeming the settlement agreement invalid ([para 3]).

I – Issues

The primary legal questions arising from the case involved:

  1. Was a valid settlement agreement concluded between Mr. Bisset and the Commissioner?
  2. Did the high court have the jurisdiction to review and set aside an agreement that was not binding?
  3. Was there administrative action present that warranted review under PAJA? ([paras 6-7]).

R – Rules

The legal framework for the case is grounded principally in:

  • Restitution of Land Rights Act 22 of 1994: Specifically, sections relating to the nature and process of land restitution claims ([para 1]).
  • Promotion of Administrative Justice Act 3 of 2000: Relevant particularly with respect to the timelines and procedural regularities for reviews ([para 1]).
  • Principles of Administrative Law: Specifically concerning the definition of administrative action and the requirements for a binding settlement ([para 7]).

The court relied on previous cases interpreting the validity and nature of settlement agreements in the land restitution context, emphasizing the necessity of concurrence from all parties involved ([para 7]).

A – Application

In its analysis, the Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized the premise on which the high court based its decision. The court determined that, while Mr. Bisset had signed the document purporting to be a settlement agreement, there was no evidence that the Commissioner or any official on behalf of the Minister had signed or otherwise indicated concurrence ([para 6]).

The court also emphasized that Mr. Bisset's subsequent disavowal of the agreement negated any claim to its binding nature. This lack of concurrence and the absence of any compulsion for the Bisset family to adhere to the terms meant that legally, there was no settlement to invalidate or annul ([para 6]). Subsequent efforts in negotiations did not substantiate the existence of a concluded settlement, reinforcing the view that the high court's finding was based on an incorrect premise—the existence of a binding agreement ([para 7]).

Accordingly, the Supreme Court held that there was no administrative action to review, making the high court's judgment unsustainable. It concluded that any review actions depended on a decision being made, and no such decision existed because the agreement had never been fully finalized ([para 7]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately found in favor of the appellants, concluding that the appeal succeeded on all fronts. The court ordered that the high court's judgment be set aside and replaced with an order dismissing Mr. Bisset's application ([para 9]).

The ratio decidendi crystallized from this case emphasizes the importance of formal concurrence in administrative agreements within land restitution proceedings. It serves as a critical reminder that without a binding agreement, courts lack the jurisdiction to engage in review processes related to such agreements. The implications of this judgment suggest a procedural tightening in land restitution matters, wherein claimants and authorities must rigidly adhere to the legislative requirements governing settlement agreements to avoid unnecessary legal disputes ([para 8]).

Given the nature of the case and its public interest implications, the court decided not to grant costs against Mr. Bisset, reflecting a compassionate approach to the historically marginalized position of land claimants ([para 9]). This decision resonates strongly in the context of South Africa's ongoing transformation and reconciliation efforts post-apartheid.
