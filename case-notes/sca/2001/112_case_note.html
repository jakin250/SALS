Road Accident Fund v Alfred Samela

Supreme Court of Appeal 2001

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

[1] On 7 January 1996, Alfred Samela (the respondent) was a passenger in a Toyota minibus traveling in the Free State when the vehicle overturned due to the driver's negligence. The respondent sustained personal injuries and subsequently filed a claim for compensation against the Road Accident Fund (the appellant) based on the provisions of Chapter XII of the Schedule to the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund Act 93 of 1989 ("the Schedule"). The Road Accident Fund, established by the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996, was cited as the defendant.

[2] The factual background established a critical point of contention between the parties: whether Samela was being conveyed “for reward” in terms of paragraph (a)(i), or “in the course of the business of the owner” of the minibus under paragraph (a)(ii) of Article 46 of the Schedule. The appellant contended that Samela was conveyed under circumstances described in paragraph (b).

[3] It was common ground that the minibus was owned by one Mfenga, who held a valid Public Road Carrier Permit for operations in a certain area. However, at the time of the accident, the minibus was operating outside this specified radius, thereby breaching section 31(1)(b) of the Road Transportation Act 74 of 1977. The passengers were not aware of this operational limitation, having paid fares to board the minibus in the Transkei to be conveyed to Johannesburg. The lower court concluded that Samela was being conveyed "for reward" as per Article 46(a)(i) and issued a ruling in favor of the respondent.

I – Issues

[4] The legal questions stem from the interpretation of Article 46 of the Schedule under two primary considerations: whether the respondent was being conveyed for reward and whether the conveyance fell within the course of the owner’s business. Specifically, the court was tasked with determining:

  1. Was Samela being conveyed "for reward," thereby entitling him to a greater claim for damages?
  2. Was his transportation still permissible under his owner's business even if the specific conveyance breached licensing regulations?
  3. If the conveyance is deemed illegal, does that materially affect his right to compensation under Article 46(a)?

R – Rules

[5] Article 46 outlines the liability framework of the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund ("MMF") concerning compensation for injuries from accidents involving motor vehicles. It differentiates between passengers being conveyed:

  • (a) "for reward" or "in the course of the business of the owner" (paragraphs (i) and (ii)), which grants higher compensation limits.
  • (b) under other circumstances, which cap compensation at R25,000 for medical expenses and loss incurred as a result of bodily injury.

[6] The distinction hinges on the definitions provided for “reward” in the Schedule, clarifying that any reward rendered illegal under transportation laws excludes the passenger from enhanced claims. Additionally, Article 1(ii) states that "business" does not include unlawful activities, suggesting that while an act can be illegal, it does not inherently imply that the business itself is unlawful.

[7] Precedents such as Santam Insurance Co Ltd v Tshiva and Southern Insurance Association Ltd v Khumalo elucidate the importance of distinguishing between the legality of specific conveyances and the lawful operations of a business.

A – Application

[8] Cloete AJA noted that even though the journey was illegal due to the breach of licensing regulations by operating outside the specified area, this did not automatically render the business of the minibus owner unlawful. It was established that the business itself was lawful as Mfenga held a valid permit despite the specific violation.

[9] The Court concluded that whether Samela was being conveyed for reward was contingent on whether the reward rendered the conveyance illegal. Cloete AJA ruled that the reward paid did not, in itself, render the conveyance illegal under section 31(1)(b) of the Road Transportation Act 74 of 1977, as it was the breach of permit terms that rendered the journey unlawful—not merely the act of accepting fare.

[10] Ultimately, the Court found that Samela fell under the protection of Article 46(a)(ii), as he was being conveyed in the course of the lawful business of the minibus operator, despite the illegality associated with that specific journey.

[11] Brand AJA disagreed with Cloete AJA's interpretation regarding being "for reward," arguing instead that Samela qualified as a passenger for reward, asserting that the payment rendered the conveyance illegal. Brand AJA suggested that it was the contravention of the permit terms, not just the payment of the fare, that should be emphasized.

C – Conclusion

[12] The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appeal brought forth by the Road Accident Fund, upholding the lower court's finding that the respondent was being conveyed in the course of the business of the owner as per Article 46(a)(ii) of the Schedule. Although the reasoning of the lower court regarding reward was seen as incorrect, the outcome required no alteration due to the inherent entitlement Samela had to compensation.

[13] The decision establishes a vital precedent clarifying the interpretation of "reward" within the context of illegal conveyances and enshrines protections for passengers in circumstances where permit limitations are violated, thus reinforcing the need for lawful conduct and the rights of third parties under the Multilateral Motor Vehicle Accidents Fund.
