Supreme Court of Appeal

Walker v Redhouse [2006] SCA 95 (RSA)

During the ride, Redhouse fell off her horse, Maverick, and suffered injuries when the horse bolted, dragging her along the ground as one foot remained caught in the stirrup.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2006

FIRAC Method

F – Facts

The dispute in Walker v Redhouse arose from an incident involving horseback riding at Walkersons Estate, a guest farm owned by the appellant, Howard Walker. The respondent, Sandra Redhouse, an English visitor, and her partner were invited to participate in a horse ride, organized by the staff of the guest farm, which included signing an indemnity form. [1]

During the ride, Redhouse fell off her horse, Maverick, and suffered injuries when the horse bolted, dragging her along the ground as one foot remained caught in the stirrup. [2]. After the fall, Redhouse initiated legal proceedings against Walker, asserting her claim for damages under the actio de pauperie, a strict liability action applicable to animal owners. Walker defended his position by citing the indemnity signed by Redhouse, arguing it exonerated him from liability. The High Court (per Bosielo J) held Walker liable, without adequately addressing the indemnity's implications. Following this judgment, Walker appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA). [2]

I – Issues

The central issues of the case include:

  1. Whether the indemnity signed by Redhouse effectively exculpates Walker from liability under the actio de pauperie.
  2. Whether Maverick’s actions constituted behavior contra naturam (contrary to nature) that would hold Walker liable despite the indemnity. [3], [5]

R – Rules

The court referenced pertinent legal principles surrounding liability for domesticated animals and the enforceability of indemnity clauses:

  1. Pauperian Liability: This principle states that the owner of a domesticated animal is strictly liable for damages caused by that animal, but only when the animal acts contra naturam. [3]
  2. Interpretation of Indemnity Clauses: In determining the applicability of indemnity, courts focus on the precise language used, looking for express and unambiguous terms that outline the extent of liability exclusions. It is a principle that if the wording is ambiguous, it should be construed against the party that proposed it (contra proferentem). [14]
  3. Case Precedents: The court considered precedents including Lawrence v Kondotel Inns (Pty) Ltd which elaborates on exemptions not covering actions where the animal has behaved in an unexpectedly dangerous manner. This provided contextual backbone for interpreting the limits of liability in similar situations. [16], [18]

A – Application

The court engaged in a detailed analysis of the wording of the indemnity. It underscored that the clause explicitly absolved Walker from liability for “any loss or damage … sustained as a result of my death or injury… in the course of my horse riding.” The SCA concluded that the language employed by Redhouse was intended to encompass all risks associated with horse riding, regardless of how those risks manifested. Walker's argument was that any resulting injury was covered due to this broad language. [12], [19].

The court also assessed the circumstances of the incident, determining that Maverick's bolting could not be considered behavior contra naturam, as horses may instinctively react to stimuli that provoke fear or surprise. Consequently, the court favoured the view that neither Walker nor his staff exhibited negligence, as their conduct met the reasonable standards expected in such an environment. [10], [20].

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal concluded that the indemnity signed by Redhouse was an appropriate and effective defense against Walker’s liability. The ruling reversed the lower court's decision and set aside its orders, holding that Redhouse had assumed the risks inherent to horse riding, including the possibility of unforeseen actions by the horse. The appeal was upheld with costs awarded to Walker, affirming that liability arising from Redhouse's injuries was excluded by the indemnity clause. [21]. This judgment reaffirms the enforceability of indemnity clauses in similar contexts and clarifies the interpretation of behavior classified as contra naturam in relation to animal liability.
