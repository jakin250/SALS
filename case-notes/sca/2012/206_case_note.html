Case Title: Saldanha Bay Municipality v Britannia Beach Estate (Pty) Ltd (796/11) [2012] ZASCA 206

Supreme Court of Appeal 2012 [2012] ZASCA 206 ### F – Facts: The case arises from a disput

Case Title: Saldanha Bay Municipality v Britannia Beach Estate (Pty) Ltd (796/11) [2012] ZASCA 206

F – Facts:

The case arises from a dispute between Saldanha Bay Municipality (the appellant) and several property developer companies (the respondents) concerning the enforceability of capital contributions related to the development of property. The municipality adopted a tariff resolution setting the rates for such contributions, referred to as resolution R35/6-07, which came into effect on 1 July 2007 ([1]-[10]).

Prior to the adoption of R35, the respondents had approval for six development applications. The first three approvals were granted before the new tariff came into force, adhering to the previous resolution R55/9-97. Subsequent approvals required compliance with the new R35 tariff. The respondents contended that the new tariff was inapplicable and sought relief on grounds of unlawful levies, arguing that they were entitled to overpayments made based on the enforced conditions of approval ([12]-[16]).

The Western Cape High Court ruled in favor of the respondents, leading the appellant to appeal the judgment, claiming that there had been no implied revocation of R35 and that all conditions imposed were valid as per the statutory regime under the Land Use Planning Ordinance 15 of 1985 (LUPO) ([17]-[23]).

I – Issues:

The primary legal issue in this case centered on whether the High Court rightly concluded that resolution R35 was revoked through resolution R43, thereby making the tariff for capital contributions set out in R35 inapplicable. Additionally, the court needed to discern if the appellant was entitled to collect capital contributions based on previously approved conditions and if the respondents could assert a claim for overpayments ([19]-[22]).

R – Rules:

The judgment relied on specific statutory provisions embedded in LUPO, notably sections 42(1), (2), and (3), which empower municipal councils to impose conditions when granting rezoning and subdivision approvals. The council must follow a specific procedure to amend any condition previously imposed. The resolution setting tariffs was not exempt from review under these provisions, implying that adherence to correct statutory procedures was essential to enforceability ([4]-[5], [6]).

The concept of fiduciary duty was also explored in the context of the relationship between local authorities and property developers. The court referred to established case law, including Kempton Park/Tembisa Metropolitan Substructure v Kelder and others, indicating that no private law duties apply to municipal councils in relation to development contributions ([22]-[23]).

A – Application:

The court found that the High Court erred in concluding that the tariff's existence was contingent on the council's decisions in relation to R43. The condition of payment of contributions as part of the approval was enforceable independently of the subsequent policy changes brought about by the council's resolutions. The conditions imposed earlier couldn't be unilaterally amended without following the required statutory process outlined in LUPO, confirming the binding nature of those initial agreements.

Moreover, the court addressed the issue of whether the council had a fiduciary obligation to account for overpayments, clarifying that such obligations do not arise from the nature of municipal governance outlined in public law principles ([20]-[23]).

C – Conclusion:

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, reversing the High Court's decision and determining that the municipality's tariffs set forth in previous resolutions were enforceable. The ruling underscored the importance of adherence to legislative processes and the limits of fiduciary duty in the context of municipal law. The court concluded that the respondents were not entitled to claims for overpayments under the circumstances described. The appeal order included provisions for costs to cover all legal expenses, including those of two counsels, thus holding the respondents accountable for the failure of their claims ([23], [24]).

This case establishes critical principles regarding the enforceability of municipal tariffs under public law and reaffirms the stringent requirements imposed on local authorities in managing development contributions. The decision enhances clarity surrounding the obligations of local governments relative to property developers and their financial conditions linked to development applications.
