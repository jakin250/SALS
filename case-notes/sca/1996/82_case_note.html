Case Title: McDonald's Corporation v Joburgers Drive-Inn Restaurant (Pty) Ltd and Others

Supreme Court of Appeal 1996 1968 ([para 4]). In 1992

Case Title: McDonald's Corporation v Joburgers Drive-Inn Restaurant (Pty) Ltd and Others

Supreme Court of South Africa (Appellate Division)
Case No: 547/95
Date Delivered: 27 August 1996
Judges: Corbett, CJ; Grosskopf, JA; Nestadt, JA; Schutz, JA; Plewman, AJA

___

F – Facts

This case primarily revolves around McDonald's Corporation (“McDonald's”), a significant global player in the fast food industry, and its registered trademarks in South Africa ([paras 2–3]). McDonald's initiated trademark registrations in South Africa during the years 1968, 1974, 1979, 1980, 1984, and 1985, securing a total of fifty-two registered marks. These include notable marks such as "Big Mac," "Egg McMuffin," and the iconic golden arches ([para 2]). By the time the legal dispute arose, McDonald's had not yet commenced operations in the country nor utilized its trademarks ([para 4]).

Joburgers Drive-Inn Restaurant (Pty) Ltd (“Joburgers”) is a local South African business, founded by George Sombonos, who had a history in the fast-food sector dating back to 1968 ([para 4]). In 1992, Sombonos decided to open a chain of fast food outlets intending to use McDonald's trademarks, including "McDonald's," "Big Mac," and the golden arches design ([paras 5–6]). Thus began several legal confrontations as Joburgers filed for the registration of these trademarks while also initiating proceedings to expunge McDonald's existing registrations ([para 4]).

The conflict escalated when Joburgers publicly announced its plans to enter the market with a new fast food venture, inciting McDonald's to send a letter demanding that Joburgers refrain from using its trademarks. Joburgers responded defiantly, asserting their intention to use the marks ([paras 7–8]). Consequently, McDonald's filed an urgent application for relief on grounds of trademark infringement, passing off, and unlawful competition in September 1993 ([para 7]).

The legal battle included an urgent application by McDonald's against Joburgers and further complications involving another entity, Dax Prop CC, which also sought to use marks that could infringe on McDonald's ([paras 10–12]). A key development occurred when McDonald's sought to invoke Section 35 of the new Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993, which addresses the protection of "well-known" trade marks and supersedes the previous law ([para 13]).

Ultimately, the matter was consolidated for hearings before the court and ruled in favor of Joburgers and Dax, leading McDonald's to appeal ([paras 14–15]).

I – Issues

The legal issues at stake revolved around the following points:

  1. Trademark Infringement: Whether Joburgers' and Dax's usage of "McDonald's" and similar marks constituted trademark infringement under South African law ([para 12]).
  2. Bona Fide Usage: Whether McDonald's had shown bona fide intention regarding the usage of its trademarks given it had not conducted business in South Africa prior to the dispute ([para 14]).
  3. Well-Known Marks Application: Assessment of whether McDonald's trademarks could be classified as "well-known" under Section 35 of the new Trade Marks Act and thus entitled to greater protections than less recognized marks ([para 14]).
  4. Counterclaims for Expungement: The validity of Joburgers' and Dax's applications to remove McDonald's trademarks from the register, particularly under the criteria of bona fide use or intention as outlined in Section 36 of the old Trade Marks Act ([para 75]).

R – Rules

The applicable legal framework includes:

  1. Trade Marks Act 62 of 1963 (Old Act): Governs the initial registration and expungement of trademarks in South Africa, particularly Sections 36(1) concerning the removal of trademarks.
  2. Trade Marks Act 194 of 1993 (New Act): Introduced new provisions relevant to the protection of well-known marks. Section 35 provides that a mark well-known in the Republic can enjoy protection even if not previously used there ([para 13]).
  3. Paris Convention Provisions: Section 35 conveys obligations consistent with the Paris Convention, requiring member nations to afford protection to "well-known" trademarks ([para 19]).
  4. Common Law Principles of Passing Off: These principles complement statutory law and were relevant for determining the protection of a trademark based on its goodwill and reputation in the market, despite the absence of business activity ([paras 29-33]).

A – Application

In applying these legal standards, the court scrutinized the evidence demonstrating McDonald's commercial reputation. The court acknowledged that McDonald's, lacking strong goodwill within South Africa, faced a significant challenge in asserting claims for passing off ([para 33]). It noted that the level of public awareness about McDonald's in South Africa was a critical variable in determining the success of its claims under the new law.

The court rejected the court a quo’s narrower interpretation of "well-known," asserting that if a trademark is recognized in the context relevant to the goods or services provided, it suffices to satisfy the requirements of Section 35 ([para 34]). It was essential for McDonald's to show that it was well-known among persons interested in the goods and services offered and that there was likely to be confusion ([para 39]).

Evidence was presented regarding McDonald's worldwide presence and extensive marketing efforts, leading the court to conclude that the name itself was recognizable even in South Africa, particularly amongst affluent consumers or potential franchisees ([paras 45-60]). Thus, while McDonald's had not conducted trade within South Africa, its trademarks possessed sufficient notoriety and were deemed "well-known" sufficient to afford protection against Joburgers and Dax ([para 66]).

Regarding the counterclaims for expungement, it was determined that Joburgers had acted in bad faith when seeking to use those marks, particularly given its ongoing contempt of court ruling, signifying ulterior motives rather than genuine business activity ([para 72]).

C – Conclusion

The court ultimately held that McDonald's trademarks were indeed recognized as "well-known" and therefore secured protection under Section 35 of the new Trade Marks Act ([para 77]). As such, McDonald's application for interdict was granted, prohibiting Joburgers and Dax from using any of McDonald's trademarks ([para 80]).

Joburgers’ and Dax's applications for expungement were rendered moot due to McDonald's successful appeal and the affirmations of its mark's well-known status ([para 78]). The implications of this judgment reinforce the necessity for maintaining trademark rights even in markets where a trademark owner has not yet established a physical presence, emphasizing the evolving nature of trade mark law in South Africa in compliance with international conventions.

The court’s ruling illustrates a crucial balance between protecting established global brands while also permitting local establishment and business operations, reflecting broader concerns in the world of franchising and trademark utilization in a globalized marketplace.
