Supreme Court of Appeal

The National Director of Public Prosecutions (Ex Parte Application), (905/2017) [2018] ZASCA (86)

The facts surrounding the vehicle relate to an incident on 6 March 2016, when police officers intercepted the Prado at the Nkomazi toll plaza.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 [2018] ZASCA (86)

F – Facts

The case revolves around an ex parte application made by the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP) on 14 October 2016, seeking a preservation order for a Toyota Prado station wagon (registration number CV04JK GP) under section 38 of the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (POCA) ([1]). The application was initially scheduled for a hearing on 20 October 2016 but was postponed to 25 October 2016 ([1]). In a judgment delivered on 10 November 2016, the court (per Legodi J) struck the application off the roll, requiring the NDPP to notify interested parties before any re-enrollment ([2]).

The facts surrounding the vehicle relate to an incident on 6 March 2016, when police officers intercepted the Prado at the Nkomazi toll plaza. They discovered over 50 kilograms of heroin concealed within the vehicle, leading to the arrest of the driver, Jeronimo Masoio Mateus Matusse, who claimed that he was unaware of the drugs ([4]-[5]). The Prado was confirmed as the property of Haji Ramadhani, a Tanzanian national, who could not be located by law enforcement ([6]-[11]).

Matusse was released on bail but indicated that he was hired by Ramadhani to transport the Prado to Mozambique and back ([10]). Following his arrest, the NDPP sought to preserve the Prado pending a possible forfeiture application based on its alleged use in drug trafficking ([12]).

I – Issues

The critical legal issue pertains to whether the NDPP was entitled to apply for a preservation order via an ex parte application under section 38 of the POCA without notifying the vehicle’s owner or any interested parties, as the lower court had asserted that the NDPP needed to demonstrate a likelihood that the property would be lost if notice were given ([20]-[21]).

Additionally, the court needed to determine the compatibility of local practice directives with statutory provisions regarding the conduct of ex parte applications, particularly concerning the requirement of exceptional circumstances outlined by the Mpumalanga Division of the High Court ([29]-[30]).

R – Rules

The primary legal framework consists of the following statutory provisions:

  1. Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 (POCA):
  • Section 38 allows the NDPP to apply for a preservation order ex parte, provided there exist reasonable grounds to believe that the property is connected to unlawful activities ([14]-[15]).
  1. Uniform Rules of Court:
  • Rule 6(4)(a) prescribes the process for ex parte applications, requiring them to be set down before noon on the day preceding the intended hearing date ([30]).
  1. Case Law:
  • National Director of Public Prosecutions v Mohamed NO & others 2003 (4) SA 1 (CC) clarified the nature of ex parte applications, emphasizing that they are permissible without requiring special justification ([19]).
  • ABSA Bank Ltd v Van Rensburg & another 2014 (4) SA 626 (SCA) stressed that appeals challenge substantive orders rather than the reasoning behind them ([13]).

A – Application

The court critically analyzed the meaning of the wording within section 38 of the POCA, finding no statutory requirement for the NDPP to establish a "real possibility" that the property would be lost if notice was served, as opined by the lower court ([20]-[24]). The appellate judgment underlined that section 38 grants the NDPP the discretion to proceed ex parte without further conditions, aligning with the urgency necessitated by the potential dissipation of property involved in criminal activities.

The court further addressed the discrepancies between the lower court's interpretation of the Mpumalanga Division's Practice Directive and the statutory provisions of POCA and the Uniform Rules. It determined that subordinate regulations could not contravene explicit statutory mandates ([30]-[31]). This finding implied that local practice directives must not restrict the powers granted under section 38 or require the justification of exceptional circumstances for ex parte applications.

Ultimately, the court clarified that UPCA’s provisions allowed urgent civil proceedings to secure the preservation of property under suspicion of facilitating crime, positing that such measures are necessary to combat organized crime effectively ([32]).

C – Conclusion

The appeal was granted, setting aside the lower court’s order, thereby affirming the NDPP's right to re-enroll the preservation application as an ex parte motion. The appellate court ordered compliance with the procedural rules under the Uniform Rules of Court, emphasizing that the NDPP must act promptly and efficiently in such matters ([34]). The decision illustrated the judiciary's commitment to upholding statutory provisions even in the context of local practice deviations, thereby reaffirming the importance of protecting society from the proceeds of crime while maintaining due process.

This case is notable in reinforcing the application of POCA and the effectiveness of ex parte procedures in urgent preservation scenarios, establishing a precedent for future applications and clarifying the operational parameters of asset forfeiture in organized crime cases.
