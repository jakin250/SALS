Supreme Court of Appeal

Knoop and Another NNO v Gupta (No 2) [2020] ZASCA 163

On 28 November 2018, Mrs Chetali Gupta, a shareholder, initiated an application for the removal of the BRPs ([2]).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2020 [2020] ZASCA 163

FIRAC Analysis

F – Facts

The current case arises from a series of interrelated business rescue proceedings concerning two companies: Islandsite Investments One Hundred and Eighty (Pty) Ltd (Islandsite) and Confident Concept (Pty) Ltd (Confident Concept), both of which were placed under voluntary business rescue on 16 February 2018 under the South African Companies Act 71 of 2008 (the Act) ([1]). Messrs Kurt Knoop and Johan Klopper were appointed as business rescue practitioners (BRPs) for these companies ([1]).

On 28 November 2018, Mrs Chetali Gupta, a shareholder, initiated an application for the removal of the BRPs ([2]). A full court of the Gauteng Division of the High Court ruled in favor of her application on 13 December 2019 ([2]). The BRPs subsequently sought leave to appeal this decision, which was granted on 7 February 2020 ([2]).

The background of the case includes significant public scrutiny of the Gupta family, whose businesses were alleged to have engaged in questionable practices, leading to a situation where several companies were unable to obtain banking facilities, effectively rendering them commercially insolvent ([5-6]).

The BRPs faced allegations primarily regarding their performance, including claims of incompetence, failure to adhere to approved business plans, neglect of duties, conflicts of interest, and lack of independence ([10-12]). However, these allegations lacked substantiating evidence, which became a central point of contention in the appeal ([108]).

I – Issues

The principal legal question in this case is whether the grounds for the removal of the BRPs, as outlined in section 139(2) of the Act, were adequately established by Mrs Gupta ([3][14]). Specifically, the court needed to consider the following:

  1. Were the allegations of incompetence and failure to perform their duties valid and substantiated?
  2. Did the BRPs act in good faith and exercise a proper degree of care in their duties?
  3. Was there a sufficient conflict of interest or a lack of independence warranting their removal?

R – Rules

The governing legal authority is section 139(2) of the Companies Act 71 of 2008, which outlines the grounds upon which BRPs can be removed ([15]). The criteria include:

  • Incompetence or failure to perform duties;
  • Failure to exercise a proper degree of care;
  • Engaging in illegal acts or conduct;
  • Conflict of interest or lack of independence.

Additionally, the duties imposed on BRPs as officers of the court under section 140(3)(a) and (b) of the Act emphasize that they must act with a high standard of good faith and impartiality ([30]).

A – Application

The court carefully reviewed each of the allegations put forth by Mrs Gupta. The majority of the claims were found lacking in evidentiary support. For instance, the assertions regarding the incompetence of the BRPs and their failure to adhere to the approved business plans were not substantiated by specific factual evidence ([12][108]).

The judgment criticized the High Court's ruling for failing to analyze the factual allegations thoroughly and for not establishing findings of facts based on the evidence ([13][14]). The SCA indicated that the full court did not directly address the claims nor assess whether the BRPs acted within the standards mandated by the Act ([110-115]).

In particular, the issue regarding the alleged failure to report potential criminality to authorities and a perceived conflict of interest were dismissed as unsupported and irrelevant to the actual performance of the BRPs' duties ([118][140]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately found that the application for the removal of the BRPs was unfounded and that the grounds for removal had not been proven on a balance of probabilities ([143]). Thus, the appeal was upheld, the High Court’s decision was overturned, and the application for the removal of Knoop and Klopper as BRPs was dismissed with costs, including those for employing two counsel ([144]).

This judgment illustrates the necessity for adequate evidentiary support when seeking the removal of officers within a corporate structure and reinforces the high threshold required to establish claims of incompetence or misconduct under the Companies Act.
