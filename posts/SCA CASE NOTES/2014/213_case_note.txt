Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2014

# FIRAC Case Note: Mahlamuza and Another v State (20288/14)

## F – Facts

The case arises from an incident that took place on 3 September 2000 at Vlakplaas farm, Delmas, involving an elderly couple, Mr. PC and Mrs. HMG Neethling. The two appellants, Lukas Vusi Mahlamuza and Sibongile Emily Nkabinde, along with a co-accused, were convicted in the regional court of robbery with aggravating circumstances (count 1) and attempted murder of both Mr. Neethling (count 2) and Mrs. Neethling (count 3) ([1]).

The trial court found that the appellants had executed their criminal actions with a common intent. Mahlamuza received a sentence of 48 years while Nkabinde received 33 years, with no sentences running concurrently ([2]). Their earlier appeal to the North Gauteng High Court for the convictions was unsuccessful, although they were granted leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal regarding their attempted murder convictions. The appellants contended that the circumstances surrounding their robbery did not warrant separate convictions of attempted murder and that this constituted an impermissible duplication of charges ([3]).

The events leading to the convictions involved a premeditated violation of the Neethlings. The day prior, the group visited the couple under the pretext of inquiring about cakes and furniture. The following day, the same group entered the home, ostensibly after attending church. They produced a firearm, threatened the Neethlings, and forcibly restrained them. Mr. Neethling was physically assaulted, hit with the butt of a gun, and left tied up while Mrs. Neethling was similarly assaulted ([5]–[7]).

The police were notified by a farm employee, Mr. Masilela, leading to an attempted escape by the group. Upon the arrival of the police, a gunfight ensued, resulting in one suspect being shot and Mahlamuza firing a shot that missed Inspector Smook, leading to his additional conviction for attempted murder of a police officer (count 5) ([8]–[9]).

## I – Issues

The critical legal questions presented in this case concern:

1. Whether the violence exerted against the Neethlings during the robbery transcended the permissible limits, justifying separate convictions for attempted murder ([3], [10]).
2. Whether the intention to kill existed as commonly required for a conviction of attempted murder ([13]).

Additionally, implications of the sentences imposed in light of the categorical errors made by the lower courts regarding the applicability of excessive violence in the robbery context are also relevant ([12]).

## R – Rules

The legal foundations for this case center on the differentiation between robbery and attempted murder. The case of **S v Moloto** ([10]) outlined that for the State to charge an accused with both offenses, it must demonstrate two aspects: (1) the violence employed must exceed the restraints of robbery—putting the victim's life in jeopardy—and (2) there must be clear evidence of the intention to kill the victim, whether directly or through dolus eventualis ([10], [11]).

In assessing excessive force, the criterion to be employed is objective—a necessity to determine if a reasonable assessment indicates that bodily safety was materially compromised ([11]).

## A – Application

The Supreme Court of Appeal scrutinized the facts against the defined legal standards. It determined that while the appellants utilized weapons and inflicted violence on the Neethlings, the nature and gravity of the inflicted injuries did not substantiate a claim that the force employed exceeded that customarily associated with robbery. The court noted that Mr. Neethling's injuries were non-lethal and comprised mainly superficial bruises resulting from strikes without dangerous instruments ([12]).

The fire shot at Mr. Neethling was interpreted as an intimidation tactic rather than a lethal attempt, further fortifying the position that there existed no sufficient evidence of malice or intent to kill ([14]). Moreover, the ongoing assault did not project any desire for lethality, as the injuries remained minor and the violence ceased once the victims were immobilized ([16]).

Consequently, the attempted murder convictions (counts 2 and 3) were deemed unwarranted since they were based on an erroneous interpretation of the violence in light of the robbery, leading to a decision to overturn those convictions and corresponding sentences while maintaining the robbery conviction ([17]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal held that the convictions of attempted murder related to Mr. and Mrs. Neethling could not stand due to insufficient evidence supporting the requisite application of excessive force or intent to kill in the context of robbery. Therefore, the court upheld the appeal regarding counts 2 and 3, setting aside both the convictions and sentences on those counts. However, the appellants' sentences related to the robbery charge were affirmed, illustrating a nuanced determination reflective of the facts, evidentiary standards, and the overarching principles of criminal law ([19]). 

This case serves as a critical reference point for the standards required in similar contexts, delineating the boundaries of permissible violence in robbery and the judicial scrutiny applied to allegations of attempted murder.
