Weare v Ndebele

Case Title: Weare and Another v Ndebele NO and Others Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa Case Number: CCT 15/08 Judgment Date: 18 November 2008 Citation: [2008] ZACC 20

Constitutional Court 2008 [2008] ZACC 20 ## F - Facts The Constitutional Court's case

Case Title: Weare and Another v Ndebele NO and Others
Court: Constitutional Court of South Africa
Case Number: CCT 15/08
Judgment Date: 18 November 2008
Citation: [2008] ZACC 20

F - Facts

The Constitutional Court's case involved an application for confirmation of an order of constitutional invalidity regarding section 22(5) of the KwaZulu-Natal Regulation of Racing and Betting Ordinance 28 of 1957 (the Ordinance). This section prohibits juristic persons from holding bookmaking licenses in the province of KwaZulu-Natal, allowing only natural persons to do so. The Pietermaritzburg High Court had earlier declared this provision unconstitutional, finding that it violated section 9(1) of the South African Constitution, which guarantees equality before the law. The High Court also found a violation of section 9(3), which prohibits unfair discrimination.

The first applicant, Michael Weare, held a bookmaking license under the Ordinance and operated a business in KwaZulu-Natal. He later accepted the position of managing director at Betting World (Pty) Ltd, a juristic person engaged in bookmaking in other South African provinces where such a restriction did not exist. Due to the limitations imposed by the Ordinance, Betting World was unable to operate in KwaZulu-Natal.

The relevant legislative context includes two acts governing gambling in KwaZulu-Natal, with the Racing and Betting Ordinance specifically prohibiting juristic entities from holding licenses. Despite attempts to draft new legislation to replace the Ordinance, the restrictive provision remained in effect.

I - Issues

The case presented several key legal issues for consideration:

  1. Whether the applicants' late filing for confirmation of the High Court's order warranted condonation.
  2. Whether the invalidation of a provincial ordinance requires confirmation by the Constitutional Court.
  3. Whether juristic persons can be considered bearers of the right to equality under section 9 of the Constitution.
  4. Whether section 22(5) of the Ordinance violates either section 9(1) or section 9(3) of the Constitution.
  5. The implications of the Constitutional Court's ruling on the broader legislative landscape regarding the operation of juristic persons in gambling.

R - Rules

The applicable constitutional provisions were section 9(1), which guarantees that everyone is equal before the law, and section 9(3), which prohibits unfair discrimination. The Court referenced foundational cases such as Harksen v Lane NO and Prinsloo v Van der Linde to articulate the test for rationality in differentiation under section 9(1). Additionally, the provisions of sections 172(2) and 167(5) concerning the confirmation of orders of constitutional invalidity were crucial as they mandated that the Constitutional Court confirm any order invalidating an Act of Parliament or a provincial Act.

A - Application

Condonation:

The applicants requested condonation for the late filing of their application for confirmation. The Court noted that crystallizing the timeline from the date of the High Court's judgment rather than its certification indicated a late filing. However, considering the negligible delay and the potential severe prejudice to the applicants, condonation was granted ([paras 12–14]).

Jurisdiction over Provincial Ordinances:

The Court affirmed that the Ordinance constituted a provincial Act under sections 167(5) and 172(2)(a). The legislative history of the Ordinance, including its original enactment by provincial councils, led the Court to conclude that its invalidation must be confirmed by the Constitutional Court ([paras 18–36]).

Section 9(1) and Juristic Persons:

The Court grappled with whether juristic persons could assert rights under section 9. Citing section 8(4) of the Constitution, the Court provisionally accepted that juristic persons can indeed be bearers of this right. The applicants argued that the differentiation made by section 22(5) was not rationally linked to any legitimate government interest, suggesting the outdated nature of the restriction amidst modern economic realities ([paras 46–60]).

Rationality of Differentiation:

The Premier's defense argued that limiting licenses to natural persons facilitated accountability and regulated gambling effectively. The High Court rejected this view, holding that the rationale for the restriction could not justify the exclusion of juristic persons, suggesting that alternative regulatory measures could be introduced to ensure compliance ([paras 50–59]).

Unfair Discrimination:

In assessing unfair discrimination under section 9(3), the Court ultimately found that the differentiation outlined in section 22(5) did not impair the dignity of juristic persons, nor did it emerge from attributes that would trigger the presumption of unfair discrimination. Moreover, since the differentiation was not based on any listed grounds of discrimination, this further undermined claims of unfair treatment ([paras 71–75]).

C - Conclusion

The Constitutional Court overturned the High Court's finding, concluding that section 22(5) of the Ordinance does not breach section 9(1) or section 9(3) of the Constitution. The Court determined that the distinctions drawn by the legislation were rationally linked to the legitimate aim of regulating gambling and that they did not amount to arbitrary or unfair discrimination. Consequently, the appeal was upheld, and the application for confirmation of the order of unconstitutionality was denied ([paras 76–80]).

The decision highlighted the complexities surrounding the constitutional status of provincial ordinances, defined the balance between legislative intent and regulatory measures, and provided clarity on the application of rights concerning juristic entities.
