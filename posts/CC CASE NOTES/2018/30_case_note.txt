Source Folder: 2018

# Case Note: Minister of Justice and Constitutional Development and Others v Prince; National Director of Public Prosecutions and Others v Rubin; National Director of Public Prosecutions and Others v Acton and Others [2018] ZACC 30 

## FIRAC Structure

### F – Facts
The case involves several applications for confirmation of an order of constitutional invalidity made by the Western Cape Division of the High Court regarding provisions of the *Drugs and Drug Trafficking Act 140 of 1992* ("Drugs Act") and the *Medicines and Related Substances Control Act 101 of 1965* ("Medicines Act"). The High Court found that certain provisions of the legislation, which criminalized the private use, possession, and cultivation of cannabis by adults for personal consumption, were inconsistent with the right to privacy as guaranteed by Section 14 of the Constitution of South Africa ([1]–[4]).

Specifically, the provisions in question included sections 4(b) and 5(b) of the Drugs Act, read with Part III of Schedule 2, and section 22A(9)(a)(1) of the Medicines Act. The judgment thus sought to explore the balance between state interests in regulating drug use and individuals' rights to privacy in their personal consumption choices (including cannabis) in private settings ([1]–[20]).

### I – Issues
The central legal issue revolved around whether the prohibition against the use, possession, or cultivation of cannabis by adults in private for personal consumption is justifiable under Section 36 of the Constitution, which allows for the limitation of rights in terms of law of general application ([40]).

Moreover, the Court examined whether the impugned provisions unreasonably infringed upon individuals' privacy rights and if less restrictive means could achieve the state’s proclaimed objectives in regulating substances for public health and safety ([59]).

### R – Rules
The applicable laws and constitutional provisions include:
1. **Section 14 of the Constitution**: Guarantees the right to privacy ([43]).
2. **Section 36 of the Constitution**: Governs the limitation of rights, requiring that any limitation must be reasonable and justifiable in an open and democratic society ([60]).
3. **The Drugs Act**: Prohibits the use, possession, and dealing in certain substances ([20]).
4. **The Medicines Act**: Regulates the prescription and use of certain controlled substances ([25]).

The High Court had held that the state bore the burden of proving that the limitations imposed by the laws were justifiable, particularly in light of historical factors regarding the criminalization of cannabis ([29], [33]).

### A – Application
The Court's analysis considered the historical context of cannabis criminalization, underlining that the state failed to provide compelling evidence justifying the infringement of privacy rights. Testimonies and reports presented by the respondents cited insufficient medical justification for the criminalization. The emerging consensus in other democratic nations towards decriminalizing or legalizing personal use of cannabis also informed the judgment ([76], [80]).

The Court emphasized that the right to privacy includes an individual's autonomy over what to consume privately, paralleling rulings from international jurisdictions that acknowledged the right to private consumption without state interference ([46]–[48]). Ultimately, it was determined that prohibiting adults from using or possessing cannabis in their homes or other private spaces is an unjustified limitation of the right to privacy ([59]).

### C – Conclusion
The Constitutional Court affirmed the High Court's ruling by declaring certain provisions of the *Drugs Act* and the *Medicines Act* unconstitutional, stating they contravened the privacy rights of adults who engage in personal consumption of cannabis in a private environment. A suspension of 24 months was placed on the implementation of the invalidation to allow Parliament time to amend the laws accordingly, thereby balancing legislative intent with constitutional rights ([10]–[12], [107]).

The implications of this case are significant; it marks a pivotal shift in South Africa's approach to cannabis, aligning it with evolving global perspectives on drug policy and personal rights. It reflects a recognition of individual autonomy while compelling the legislative framework to evolve in response to constitutional mandates ([59], [129]).
