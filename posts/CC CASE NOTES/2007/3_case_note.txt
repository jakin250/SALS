Case Note: IN THE CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2007

# Case Note: Mandlakhe Khehla Shinga v The State and Others [2007] ZACC 3, Case CCT 56/06; CCT 80/06

## F – Facts

The present case involves confirmation proceedings concerning the constitutionality of criminal appeal procedures from Magistrates’ Courts, particularly focusing on sections 309(3A), 309B, and 309C of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977. In the lead case, Shinga, Mandlakhe Khehla Shinga was convicted of robbery in 2004 and granted leave to appeal. However, the appeal had not been heard for over two years due to procedural complications, prompting the KwaZulu-Natal High Court to determine the constitutionality of section 309(3A) ([12–15]). 

Importantly, the matter is in the context of the legislative history surrounding criminal appeals post-1994, including prior declarations of invalidity made by this Court in response to various procedural deficiencies identified in earlier cases ([2–8]). The O’Connell case involved a group of six co-applicants convicted of various serious offences, which amplified concerns regarding the consistency of appeal processes with constitutional rights ([17–19]).

The High Courts identified both sections 309B and 309C as problematic, necessitating evaluation by the Constitutional Court for legislative alignment with the provisions of the Constitution, particularly section 35(3)(o)—which protects the right to appeal ([1], [11]). 

## I – Issues

The key issues before the Constitutional Court were: 
1. Whether the provisions of sections 309(3A), 309B, and 309C were inconsistent with the right to a fair trial and the right to appeal as established in the Constitution ([1], [11]); 
2. Whether the limitations imposed on unrepresented accused persons in the appeal process undermined the fairness of appeals and thus contravened constitutional protections ([34]);

3. The constitutionality of the requirement for decisions on appeals to be made in chambers rather than in open court under section 309(3A), which raises essential questions regarding transparency and justice ([21–30]).

## R – Rules

The Court referenced multiple foundational legal principles and previous judgments, including:

- **Section 35(3)(o)** of the Constitution, which guarantees the right to a fair trial, including the right to have recourse to a higher court ([22]);

- **S v Ntuli (1996)**, which previously struck down the judges' certificate requirement as both unconstitutional and unfair, highlighting the necessity of evaluating evidence and procedures ([4]);

- **S v Steyn (2001)**, which declared that previous procedural reforms still failed to guarantee the necessary degree of fairness ([9]);

- **Judicial principles of open justice**, which emphasize the importance of public access to court proceedings to strengthen confidence in the justice system and ensure accountability ([26–28]).

These legal rules establish the framework against which the current provisions were evaluated.

## A – Application

The Constitutional Court applied these rules meticulously. 

1. **Constitutionality of Section 309(3A)**: The Court found that this section's stipulation for appeals to primarily proceed in chambers limited the accused's right to a fair hearing and contradicted the principle of open justice. This decision underscores the necessity for transparency in judicial processes ([21–30]). The Court emphasized that fair trials must extend to appeals, demanding the opportunity for oral argument in open court, critical for justice ([22]).

2. **Evaluation of Sections 309B and 309C**: The analysis then shifted to these sections, wherein the Court found that they still did not adequately remedy the deficiencies identified in earlier rulings (specifically, regarding documentation requirements that hindered fair trials). The absence of a mandatory requirement to provide the trial record undermined the accused's ability to seek a proper reevaluation of their case ([32–44]). 

3. **Severance Remedy**: Consequently, the Court employed the severance and reading-in remedy, invalidating specific subsections that imposed unjustifiable barriers while retaining the structure of the provisions as a whole. This innovative approach facilitated a balance between legislative integrity and the protection of constitutional rights ([54–59]).

## C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court ultimately confirmed the KwaZulu-Natal High Court's declaration that section 309(3A) was unconstitutional, emphasizing the imperative of transparency in judicial proceedings, thereby maintaining the necessity for oral arguments in open court. It also found that while sections 309B and 309C contained constitutional shortcomings, they could be salvaged through severance and interpretative adjustments.

The Court's final order enjoined the legislature to ensure compliance with constitutional demands while preserving the integrity of the judicial process. Thus, the judgment not only reinforced specific rights under the Constitution but also set a precedent for the necessary adaptation of criminal procedures in South Africa ([57–59]). 

This ruling has significant implications for the future landscape of criminal appeals in South Africa, establishing a firmer foundation for the rights of accused individuals and reinforcing the principles of justice and fairness in alignment with constitutional norms.
