# Case Note: Nonkwali v Road Accident Fund (105/2007) [2008] ZASCA 3 (06 March 2008)

### FIRAC Method

#### F – Facts 

The appellant, Bulelwa Nonkwali, was a passenger in a vehicle that was involved in a collision on 16 October 2001, resulting in serious bodily injuries. Following this incident, the appellant instituted an action for damages against the Road Accident Fund (the respondent) under Section 17(1) of the Road Accident Fund Act 56 of 1996 (the Act) in the Mthatha High Court. The respondent defended the action.

On 10 June 2005, nearly four years post-accident, the appellant amended her pleadings to include a claim for a head injury sustained due to the accident, which had not been disclosed in her original claim form and medical report submitted under Section 24 of the Act. The respondent filed a special plea asserting that the claim was non-compliant with Section 24, as it did not specify the head injury, and further claimed that the amendment was time-barred under Section 23, which prescribes a three-year period for claims related to road accidents.

The Mthatha High Court, presided over by Ndzondo AJ, agreed to first determine the special plea's validity, eventually upholding it. The court reasoned that the appellant failed to submit an amended claim form regarding the newly discovered head injury prior to amending her pleadings. The court also concluded that even if it found in the appellant's favor concerning the injury's incurrence, the claim was still unenforceable due to prescription. The appellant appealed against this decision.

#### I – Issues 

The central legal issues before the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) include:

1. Whether the appellant was required to submit an amended claim form under Section 24 of the Act concerning the newly discovered head injury.
2. Whether the head injury claim constituted a new cause of action or merely an additional item of damages to the existing claim.
3. The applicability of the prescriptive period outlined in Section 23 to the claim for damages related to the head injury ([paras 1–4]).

#### R – Rules 

The judgment primarily considers the following statutory provisions from the Road Accident Fund Act:

- **Section 17(1)**: This section allows third parties to claim compensation for losses or damages arising from bodily injuries sustained in road accidents due to negligence.
  
- **Section 23**: This provision stipulates that a claim becomes prescribed three years after the cause of action arises, although claims lodged under Section 24 do not prescribe before five years elapse ([para 2]).

- **Section 24**: This section mandates that a claim and medical report must be submitted in the prescribed form, satisfying all particulars relevant to the claim ([para 4]).

The court also referred to established precedent on the issues of procedural requirements for claims and the definition of 'cause of action' in the context of damages claims ([para 8]).

#### A – Application 

The SCA commenced its analysis by addressing the special plea raised by the respondent. It noted that the head injury was not known or diagnosed at the time the initial claim form was completed. The appellant provided all relevant information available, and no allegations were made regarding the injury's prior knowledge at the time of the claim submission. This finding established that the appellant had fully complied with Section 24's requirements.

The SCA emphasized that the claim for the head injury did not introduce a new cause of action, but rather represented an additional item of damage arising from the same incident. It referenced Corbett JA's reasoning in *Evins v Shield Insurance Co Ltd* regarding claims for damages, which stated that if an amendment seeks to augment the existing claim without introducing a new cause of action, it does not trigger the prescription period.

Consequently, the SCA determined that the head injury was part of the original cause of action, and the amendment to the summons interrupted the running of prescription. Therefore, the court ruled that it was unnecessary for the appellant to file an amended claim form solely regarding the newly discovered injury ([paras 8–10]).

#### C – Conclusion 

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the appeal, reversing the decision of the lower court. The court dismissed the respondent's special plea, concluding that the appellant adequately complied with the statutory requirements and the claim for the head injury existed within the scope of the original action.

The implications of this decision are significant for future cases involving similar procedural issues under the Road Accident Fund Act, clarifying that not all amendments require a new claim form if they pertain to newly discovered damages tied to originally claimed injuries. The ruling reinforces the concept of a single cause of action within the context of damages claims and underscores the need for courts to interpret procedural statutes in a manner conducive to substantive justice ([paras 11]).
