Source Folder: 2012

# Case Note: Body Corporate of the Pinewood Park Scheme No 202 v Dellis (Pty) Ltd [2012] ZASCA 105 

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–6])

In this case, the appellant, the Body Corporate of the Pinewood Park Scheme No 202 ("the body corporate"), appealed to the Supreme Court of Appeal following the dismissal of its claim by the KwaZulu-Natal High Court. The body corporate is responsible for the management of a sectional title scheme located in Pinetown, KwaZulu-Natal. The respondent, Dellis (Pty) Ltd ("the owner"), owns Sectional Title Unit 7 in this scheme and is obliged under section 44(1)(b) of the Sectional Titles Act 95 of 1986 ("the Act") to pay levies to the body corporate. 

The body corporate, on 2 November 2006, issued summons against the owner for R123,101.00, claiming arrear levies along with interest and costs. Although the owner acknowledged its obligation to pay levies, it denied owing the claimed amount, contending that the claim had prescribed under the Prescription Act and that payments had been misappropriated by the body corporate. 

During a pretrial conference, counsel for the owner argued that the jurisdiction of the High Court was ousted by the precedent set in **Body Corporate of Greenacres v Greenacres Unit 17 CC & another** (2008), which necessitated arbitration for any disputes between owners and the body corporate. The trial court agreed, dismissing the claim and agreeing to allow an appeal to the Full Court, which also dismissed the appeal ([paras 1–6]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 7–12])

The primary legal issue was whether the point raised by the owner—that the claim was subject to arbitration and therefore ousted the jurisdiction of the court—was valid given the circumstances of this case. The court also considered if the provisions of section 6 of the Arbitration Act 42 of 1965 were applicable, or if management rule 71 of the sectional title scheme provided for compulsory arbitration, thereby excluding court jurisdiction ([paras 7–12]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 13–19])

The court relied upon provisions of the Sectional Titles Act and relevant case law, most notably the decisions in **Greenacres** and **Baumoral Heights No 39 Bk v The Trustees for the Time Being of the Baumoral Heights Body Corporate** ([paras 13–17]). 

Management rule 71 of the body corporate's rules states that disputes must be resolved through arbitration, which the body corporate contested as non-compulsory. However, the precedent indicated that the definition of a dispute must be carefully assessed under the capacity for arbitration, and if one did not exist, the court had the discretion to adjudicate.

Moreover, the court referenced sections within the Arbitration Act, particularly section 6, which allows for court proceedings to be stayed pending arbitration, outlining a statutory framework for arbitration that remains available unless explicitly superseded by the body corporate's rules or legislation ([paras 18–19]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 20–22])

The court meticulously analyzed whether there existed a true dispute between the body corporate and the owner that necessitated arbitration. The body corporate argued that the specific defenses raised by the owner were unrelated to the levy claim, undermining claims of arbitration necessity. 

The court ultimately held that the requirement of a proper dispute, as outlined in both Greenacres and Telecall (Pty) Ltd v Logan, must be satisfied for arbitration to proceed. It was determined that the owner had a legitimate dispute that had been properly articulated, which compelled arbitration under management rule 71. However, it was also established that the High Court should not have dismissed the body corporate’s claim outright but should have either stayed the proceedings pending arbitration or exercised discretion to continue with the action based on the specifics of the conflict ([paras 20–22]).

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 23–24])

The Supreme Court of Appeal granted special leave to appeal, reversing the lower court’s decision. The dismissal of the body corporate’s point in limine by the lower courts was set aside, implicitly upholding the body corporate's right to proceed with its claim in court while acknowledging the complexity of the arbitration clause in management rule 71. The ruling stressed that arbitration clauses in community rules should not be mechanically enforced without first assessing the existence of a legitimate dispute ([para 23]).

The ratio decidendi emphasized the necessity of clearly defined disputes within the context of arbitration rules and upheld the right of entities to seek court enforcement of dues without arbitrary dismissal based on a purported arbitration obligation when genuine disputes exist. This case reinforces the contractual nature of sectional title rules and the consent of parties involved in any given dispute, shaping future interactions between bodies corporate and property owners within sectional developments ([para 24]).
