Case Note: CONSTITUTIONAL COURT OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2000

### FIRAC Case Note: The State v. George Dzukuda and Others

**F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–4])**

Both matters arose from an order made by Lewis J in the Witwatersrand High Court, declaring section 52 of the Criminal Law Amendment Act 105 of 1997 ("the Act") inconsistent with section 35 of the Constitution and thus invalid. This order was referred to the Constitutional Court for confirmation as per section 172(2)(a) of the Constitution. The first matter (case CCT 23/00) pertains to the confirmation of the order, where three applicants—George Dzukuda, Winston Tilly, and Malope Jan Tshilo—had been convicted of raping girls under the age of 16 years in separate Regional Court proceedings. The second matter (case CCT 34/00) involved a leave to appeal application made by Tshilo against the High Court's refusal to set aside his conviction.

The convictions were under section 51 of the Act, which mandates minimum sentences for severe offences. The Regional Court, upon conviction, found that the offences warranted sentencing beyond its jurisdiction and therefore committed the violators for sentencing to the High Court. Notably, the rationale behind declaring section 52 unconstitutional was that it created a split procedure where an accused would be convicted in a Regional Court but sentenced in a High Court, potentially undermining the fairness and due process required under the Constitution, particularly with respect to the right to a fair trial ([paras 1–5]).

---

**I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 7–8])**

The primary legal question was whether section 52 of the Act, which leads to the split procedure in criminal trials, is inconsistent with section 35(3) of the Constitution, specifically the right to a fair trial. This inquiry revolves around multiple sub-issues:
1. Does the fragmentation of the trial infringe the accused's right to a fair trial?
2. Is the imposition of a life sentence through this split procedure desirable and constitutionally permissible?
3. Does the split procedure lead to double jeopardy or institutional delays that further violate rights under section 35(3)?

These issues necessitated balancing societal interests in punishing serious crimes against the fundamental rights of the accused ([paras 8–9]).

---

**R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 9–11])**

The court's analysis heavily relied on:
1. Section 35(3) of the Constitution, which guarantees the right to a fair trial and delineates critical elements of this right, including trial without unreasonable delay and protection against double jeopardy.
2. Precedent from *S v Zuma* and other relevant case law indicating that the right to a fair trial encompasses substantive fairness, which includes not just the procedural aspects but also the wider implications of how justice is administered in criminal proceedings ([paras 9–10]).

The court also acknowledged the legislative history of section 52 and its context within the broader criminal procedural framework, comparing it against established legal principles governing evidence admissibility and trial integrity ([para 12]).

---

**A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 12–60])**

In analyzing whether section 52 violates the right to a fair trial, the court undertook a thorough examination of the High Court's conclusions regarding:
1. **Splitting of the Trial**: The fragmentation of proceedings was questioned, particularly in terms of whether the High Court, having not witnessed the trial and the evidence, could fairly impose a sentence based solely on the record of proceedings. The court concluded that while there are inherent disadvantages to a split process, they do not automatically preclude a fair trial, especially given the possibilities for evidential improvement at the High Court level ([paras 14–20]).

2. **Nature of Sentencing**: The concerns regarding the imposition of life sentences were highlighted. However, the ruling emphasized that sentencing by the High Court, especially in line with empowered discretion provided by law, did not violate constitutional protections as long as the procedural provisions were carefully adhered to ([paras 22–33]).

3. **Institutional Delay**: The Court examined systemic delays resulting from the process, understanding that while delays could infringe on fair trial rights, they were not insurmountable problems. It determined that alleged delays must be assessed on a case-by-case basis rather than through a general constitutional invalidation of the statutory scheme ([paras 50–57]).

Finally, the court refuted claims of an infringing double jeopardy situation with regard to procedural rights during sentencing, asserting no violation occurred as no new material evidence was being introduced that contradicted the previous conviction ([paras 36–49]) .

---

**C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 60–66])**

The Constitutional Court held that the provisions of section 52 of the Act do not infringe on the rights guaranteed under section 35(3) of the Constitution. Consequently, the court declined to confirm the High Court's order declaring the section unconstitutional. The court reiterated that while the split procedure is not ideal, it does not inherently impede the fairness of the trial or sentencing process, particularly when safeguards are in place to address potential risks.

The implications of this ruling affirm the constitutionality of procedural frameworks that may not be perfect but operate within the bounds set by the law, reinforcing the importance of substantive fairness in judicial processes. The decision underscored that any statutory provisions ought to be applied in consonance with constitutional values, allowing for a flexible but legally consistent approach to upholding justice ([paras 60–66]).
