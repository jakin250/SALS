# FIRAC Case Note: Transnet Limited v Sechaba Photoscan (Pty) Ltd

**Citation:** Transnet Limited v Sechaba Photoscan (Pty) Ltd (Case No 98/03) [2004] ZASCA 15.

**Court:** Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Coram:** Howie P, Zulman, Farlam, Lewis JJA et Van Heerden AJA

**Date Heard:** 1 March 2004  
**Delivered:** 1 April 2004

---

## F - Facts

The case involves a delictual claim for damages arising from a fraudulent public tender process initiated by Transnet Limited (the appellant) for the purchase of its printing business, Transnet Production House. Sechaba Photoscan (Pty) Ltd (the respondent) was a tenderer among several bidders, including Skotaville Press (Pty) Limited, which ultimately won the contract. The respondent alleged that it was wrongfully and fraudulently deprived of the tender, leading it to sue Transnet for R60,689,000, representing the expected net profits over three years had the contract been awarded to it.

At trial, the appellant conceded to the allegations regarding liability but contested the quantum of damages. The trial focused solely on calculating damages, where the respondent presented evidence through three witnesses, who provided insight into anticipated profits from operating the business had it been awarded the contract. The trial judge accepted the unchallenged evidence and awarded R57,654,550 to the respondent, prompting the appellant's appeal ([paras 2-6]).

## I - Issues

The primary legal issue on appeal was whether loss of prospective profits could be claimed as delictual damages. The appellant asserted that the respondent sought to recover profits typically associated with contractual claims, which are not allowed under delict law. Additional issues included whether the trial court's reliance on hearsay evidence regarding service level agreements was admissible, and whether the purchase price paid by the respondent should be deducted from the quantum of damages awarded ([paras 1, 7, 24-28]).

## R - Rules

Through a series of precedents and legal principles, the court evaluated the applicable rules concerning claims for damages in delict:

1. **Trotman v Edwick:** Established that a claimant in delict seeks compensation for the loss sustained due to wrongful conduct, not the loss of profit as in a contractual claim ([para 9]).
   
2. **Olitzki Property Holdings v State Tender Board:** Differentiated types of claims and clarified that claims for lost profits require a stronger connection to wrongdoing than that presented in the case at hand ([para 13]).

3. **Roman-Dutch Law Principles:** The court discussed the historical position on ‘id quod interest’ that permits claims for both out-of-pocket expenses and loss of profits, asserting these can be assessed under delictual damages ([para 14]).

4. **Assessment Methodology:** It reiterated that damages in delict are assessed based on the difference between the position the claimant would have been in but for the wrongful act and their actual position ([para 15]).

5. **Reliance on Evidence:** The court emphasized that the admissibility of evidence during trials is contingent upon prior positioning during cross-examination, dictating how subsequent arguments can or cannot be raised ([paras 18-22]).

## A - Application

The court examined the arguments raised by the appellant against the backdrop of the established rules. The appellant's primary argument was that the trial court erred in awarding damages as though the claim was contractual in nature, which the court countered by highlighting the facts of this case dealing specifically with anticipated profits lost due to fraudulent interference.

Regarding the service level agreements, the court found that the evidence pertaining to these agreements was effectively uncontested at trial, thus removing any grounds for appeal based on admissibility concerns. The appellant's failure to challenge this evidence at trial precluded any reconsideration of its validity or relevance on appeal ([paras 18-22]).

As for the claim regarding the deduction of the purchase price, the court concluded that such a deduction was unfounded given the evidence presented about the profits anticipated beyond the acquisition of the physical entity itself. Evidence indicated that the bulk of the purchase price represented goodwill associated with existing agreements rather than just physical assets, which would further bolster the respondent's claim for profits ([paras 23-28]).

## C - Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed the appellant's appeal, affirming the trial court's ruling on the grounds that:

1. **Loss of Profits:** The court re-established that loss of profits could indeed be claimed in delictual damages, especially where a fraud has deprived a party of anticipated income. 

2. **Evidence Admissibility:** The absence of challenges to the credibility and admissibility of the service agreements undermined the appellant's position on appeal.

3. **Deduction of Purchase Price:** The court found no basis for deducting the purchase price from the damages awarded, concluding the respondent was entitled to recover for the entirety of its anticipated loss of profits.

The appellant was ordered to bear the costs of the appeal, including the costs for employing two counsel due to the nature and complexity of the case ([paras 29]).
