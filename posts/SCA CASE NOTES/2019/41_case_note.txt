Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: SAMWU PF v Umzimkhulu Local Municipality (297/2018) [2019] ZASCA 41 (29 March 2019)

## FIRAC Case Note

### F – Facts: Relevant background and procedural history ([paras 1–8])

The central issue in this appeal concerns the validity of the termination of membership of the South African Municipal Workers’ Union National Provident Fund (the Fund) by the third, fifth, seventh, and eighth respondents, who were employees of Umzimkhulu Local Municipality (the Municipality), while remaining in service. The employees were members of the Fund by virtue of their employment with the Municipality ([para 1]). The employees purported to transfer to another retirement fund, the Municipal Employees Pension Fund (the MEPF), effective January 1, 2014, with the Municipality ceasing contributions to the Fund thereafter ([para 3]). The Fund initiated legal proceedings in the KwaZulu-Natal Division of the High Court against the Municipality to compel payment of arrear contributions, asserting that the transfers were invalid ([para 3]). The court a quo dismissed the Fund’s application, stating the relevant rules permitted membership termination whilst still employed, prompting the Fund to appeal ([para 4]).

### I – Issues: Legal or constitutional questions ([paras 6–7])

The appeal raised several pivotal issues for determination:
1. The interpretation of rules 3.2.1 and 11.11 of the Fund’s rules, specifically whether they prohibit an elective in-service cessation of membership of the Fund.
2. Whether rule 3.2 infringes the right to freedom of association of the employees and the Municipality.
3. Whether the rules of the Fund contravene public policy ([para 6]).

### R – Rules: Legal authorities and precedents relied upon ([paras 12–19])

The court evaluated the relevant provisions of the Fund's rules and statutory framework, particularly focusing on:
- Rule 3.2.1, which states that a member may not withdraw from the Fund while in service, and Rule 3.2.2, determining that membership ceases upon cessation of service ([paras 7–8]).
- Rule 11.11, which governs transfers from the Fund contingent on amalgamation or transfer of business ([para 8]).
- Sections 13A(5) and 14 of the Pension Funds Act 24 of 1956 (the PFA), which regulate contributions and member transfers ([paras 16–24]).
- Relevant case law, including Mtyhopo & others v South African Municipal Workers’ Union National Provident Fund ([para 9]), and Sasol Limited & others v Chemical Industries National Provident Fund to interpret the authority and scope of pension fund rules ([para 12]).

### A – Application: Analysis of how the court applied the law ([paras 14–36])

In evaluating the case, the court scrutinized the unambiguous nature of rule 3.2.1, affirming that it expressly prohibits in-service membership withdrawal while employed by the Municipality ([para 14]). The court disagreed with the lower court’s interpretation, asserting that the prohibition on withdrawal pertains to cessation of membership, not merely the cashing out of benefits ([paras 14–15]).

Additionally, the court examined the implications of sections 13A(5) and 14 of the PFA. The latter was interpreted as relevant to amalgamations and not applicable to individual withdrawals ([paras 22–27]). The lack of valid termination of membership under the terms of the Fund precluded the employees’ ability to demand transfers to the MEPF, and thus the Fund was not obligated to facilitate such a transfer ([paras 35–36]).

The court also considered the constitutional argument regarding the infringement of freedom of association raised by the employees, concluding that the compulsory nature of the Fund's rules did not impose a significant infringement of this right. The interpretation held that the financial implications of compliance with membership obligations do not engage the constitutional protections of freedom of association ([paras 40–48]). 

### C – Conclusion: Court’s holding, ratio decidendi, and implications ([paras 49–55])

The appeal was upheld with costs, including costs for two counsel ([para 55]). The court set aside the judgment of the court a quo and reaffirmed the validity of the Fund’s rules, confirming the employees' inability to terminate their membership while still employed. The court emphasized that the steps taken to transfer to another fund without complying with their current fund's rules were without basis, and that such rules were not contrary to public policy nor did they infringe constitutional rights ([para 54]). 

This judgment underscores the importance of adhering to pension fund rules and clarifies the legal interpretations of membership cessation, confirming that members cannot unilaterally terminate their membership while still employed without adhering to the stipulated rules of their respective funds. The decision reinforces the legal standing of pension fund regulations in relation to both individual rights and organizational governance.
