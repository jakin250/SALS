Source Folder: 2016

# FIRAC Case Note: Liesching and Others v The State and Another [2016] ZACC 41

**F – Facts**:  
The case concerns the aftermath of a violent incident on 17 November 2011, where four individuals (including the deceased) were shot at by occupants of a Volkswagen Polo. The applicants, Pieter Pietertjie Liesching, Malvin Naas Swartz, and Xavier Malgas, were charged and convicted by the High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Local Division, Johannesburg. The charges included murder (count one), unlawful possession of firearms (count two), and unlawful possession of ammunition (count three) ([2]–[6]). They were sentenced to life imprisonment for murder and additional terms for the firearm charges.

Subsequently, the trial court granted the applicants leave to appeal only against their sentences, not their convictions ([7]). Post-conviction, new evidence emerged from the trial of Arthur Saimons, the fourth occupant of the Polo, who was not initially charged with the applicants. Key testimonies recanted earlier statements implicating the applicants ([8]–[10]). After learning of these developments, the applicants sought to refer their petition through the President of the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) under section 17(2)(f) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013 for reconsideration, citing this new evidence ([11]).

The application was initially dismissed by the President on 6 November 2013, under the premise that the evidence was not admissible post the exhaustion of all recognized appeal processes ([12]). The applicants then filed an application for condonation for late filing of leave to appeal, leading to the current proceedings ([13]).

---

**I – Issues**:  
The case presents several critical legal questions for determination by the Constitutional Court. Key issues include:

1. Does the definition of “appeal” in section 1 of the Superior Courts Act exclude criminal matters from the provisions of Chapter 5?
2. Is section 17(2)(f) applicable to criminal proceedings?
3. Is the procedure outlined in section 327 of the Criminal Procedure Act (CPA) constitutive of an appeal?
4. Is the process of adducement of further evidence after a petition refusal governed by the CPA or any other criminal procedural law? ([26]).

---

**R – Rules**:  
The court relied upon numerous statutory provisions and principles in addressing the aforementioned issues:

1. **Superior Courts Act**: Section 17(2)(f) allows the President of the SCA to refer a decision for reconsideration if exceptional circumstances arise. Section 1 of the Act explicitly states that “appeal” excludes matters regulated under the CPA or any other criminal procedural law ([27]–[28]).
  
2. **Criminal Procedure Act**: Section 327(1) outlines the procedure for convicted persons wishing to introduce new evidence after exhausting all recognized appeal mechanisms. It does not classify the process as an appeal ([29]). 

3. **Constitutional Interpretation**: Section 39(2) of the Constitution mandates courts to promote the spirit, purport, and objects of the Bill of Rights during statutory interpretation. The court has previously established that the definition provided in legislation must not overshadow the broader principles enshrined in the Constitution ([30]).

---

**A – Application**:  
In considering the application, the court critically analyzed whether the interpretation of the statutory provisions excluded the application of section 17(2)(f) to criminal cases. The court found that the President of the SCA's dismissal lacked textual support because section 1 only pertains to the definition of appeals within Chapter 5 and does not imply an outright exclusion of criminal matters from all sections of the Act. 

The court asserted that section 17(2)(f) is indeed applicable in criminal proceedings, as it provides a safety net to prevent potential injustices by allowing reconsideration where new evidence arises ([62]). The court also highlighted that the procedural safeguards for introducing new evidence under section 327 of the CPA serve different purposes—one relates to an exhausted appeal process, while the other functions to prevent injustice during ongoing litigation ([59]).

---

**C – Conclusion**:  
The Constitutional Court upheld the applicants’ appeal and set aside the President of the SCA’s decision rejecting their application for reconsideration. The matter was remitted to the President for a determination of whether exceptional circumstances warranted referral ([66]). The ruling reinforced the important principle of ensuring equal access to judicial recourse for all litigants—whether in civil or criminal matters—particularly where new evidence could materially affect the outcome of convictions. This judgment clarifies the interplay between various legal provisions regarding appeals and introduces precedent affirming the application of section 17(2)(f) in criminal proceedings, thus bolstering procedural fairness and justice ([63]–[64]).
