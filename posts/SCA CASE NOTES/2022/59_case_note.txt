Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2022

# FIRAC Case Note

**Case Title**: Le Hanie and Others v Glasson and Others (214/2021) [2022] ZASCA 59 (22 April 2022)

**Court**: The Supreme Court of Appeal of South Africa

**Coram**: Dambuza, Mbatha JJA, Tsoka, Weiner, and Molefe AJJA

**Heard**: 22 February 2022

**Delivered**: 22 April 2022

---

## F - Facts 

The matter concerns the alleged contempt of a court order issued by the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Johannesburg, involving a group of appellants who were directors of the Eagle Canyon Golf Estate Homeowners Association (‘HOA’) and respondents who were owners of properties within the same estate. 

On 20 October 2017, the first four respondents launched an application (case number 2017/40103) against the fifth respondent, the HOA, regarding supposed breaches of HOA rules by one Mr. Da Silva, the owner of stand 667. Following allegations of various infractions related to building regulations, the High Court issued a court order on 11 December 2018, compelling the HOA to take specific actions against Mr. Da Silva [para 4]. 

On 18 September 2019, the respondents filed a contempt application claiming that the appellants had failed to comply with the court order, and on 9 June 2020, the High Court found the appellants in contempt, imposing a fine and costs [paras 5-6]. 

The appellants contended that the HOA had taken reasonable actions to comply with the court order, and they disputed the finding that they had acted willfully and in bad faith [para 6]. The timeline of events since the court order indicated ongoing efforts by the HOA to resolve the issues related to Da Silva's property and the proposed compliance, which involved hiring experts and conducting investigations [paras 7-40]. 

---

## I - Issues 

The key legal questions before the Court were whether the appellants were justly found to be in contempt of the court order and the extent of their personal liability for the actions (or inactions) of the HOA under which they served as directors [paras 1, 6].

1. **Interpretation of the Court Order**: Did the HOA's efforts demonstrate compliance with the court order, or were they insufficient to avoid a finding of contempt?
  
2. **Wilfulness and Mala Fides**: Were the required elements of wilfulness and bad faith established to hold the appellants in contempt?

3. **Individual Liability**: Could the appellants, as directors of the HOA, be personally held liable when the court order had been directed at the HOA itself?

---

## R - Rules 

The Court relied on several precedents to clarify the principles surrounding contempt of court, specifically:

1. **Contempt Requirements**: According to *Secretary, Judicial Commission of Inquiry into Allegations of State Capture v Zuma*, an applicant must demonstrate that (a) there was an order against the alleged contemnor, (b) it was served or the contemnor had knowledge of it, and (c) the contemnor failed to comply [para 26].

2. **Wilfulness and Mala Fides**: The judgement in *Fakie NO v CCII Systems (Pty) Ltd* established that beyond proving the order and non-compliance, the onus is on the respondent to demonstrate that any non-compliance was not willful or in bad faith [para 29].

3. **Standard of Proof**: The Constitutional Court emphasized that where contempt is at stake, particularly with sanctions involving freedom, the standard of "beyond a reasonable doubt" applies for findings of contempt [para 30].

4. **Corporate Liability**: The principle established in *Meadow Glen Home Owners Association v City of Tshwane Metropolitan Municipality* indicates that officials cannot be held in contempt for breaches they did not personally cause or were not aware of [paras 37-39].

---

## A - Application 

The Court analyzed whether the appellants exhibited wilful and mala fides non-compliance with the court order, fundamentally focusing on whether the HOA, through its directors, acted with defiance of a court order or made reasonable attempts to comply. 

The timeline and evidence presented showed multiple efforts by the HOA to address the issues between its members and ensure compliance with the order. This included hiring an architect to investigate the compliance status of Mr. Da Silva's property and several communications affirming intentions to act [paras 8-20, 22-24]. Thus, the Court found that the High Court had erroneously inferred a wilful disregard for the order without adequate evidence and failed to establish the necessary elements of contempt as outlined in earlier cases [paras 32-33]. 

Moreover, the distinction between the HOA and the individual appellants was crucial—they were not the same parties against whom the original order had been drawn. The appellants had taken steps to fulfill the obligations set by the order, and without direct infringement attributed to them personally, the application of contempt laws was inappropriate [paras 34-40]. 

---

## C - Conclusion 

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately upheld the appellants' appeal against the findings of the High Court. The Court overturned the contempt order, reasoning that the High Court had committed error by conflating the HOA's institutional actions with personal responsibility on the part of the individual directors. The Court recognized the importance of distinguishing between the legal entity of the HOA and the individual appellants, particularly since the original court order had not implicated the individuals directly [paras 41-44]. 

The implications of this decision reaffirm the principles surrounding contempt of court in South African law, highlighting that the personal liability of directors for corporate actions is not absolute, particularly when the corporate entity itself is the named defendant, and that reasonable measures taken to comply with a court order must be recognized [para 44].
