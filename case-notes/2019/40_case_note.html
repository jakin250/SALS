Constitutional Court 2019 [2019] ZACC 40

F – Facts

The consolidated applications involve two distinct matters concerning the Intimidation Act 72 of 1982 (the Act). General Alfred Moyo, a community leader, is the first applicant in Case CCT 174/18, while Nokulunga Primrose Sonti, a Member of Parliament, is the first applicant in Case CCT 178/18. Both applicants challenged the constitutionality of specific provisions of the Act, with Moyo contesting section 1(1)(b) and Sonti contesting section 1(2) ([1–3]).

In the Moyo matter, Mr. Moyo was arrested after allegedly making threatening statements during an unsuccessful request to march, which he claimed was rooted in protest against perceived biased policing ([8–9]). Conversely, Ms. Sonti faced charges related to threatening communications directed at a complainant, in an attempt to influence a criminal complaint against an associate ([10–11]).

Initially, both applicants sought declarations from the High Court that the relevant sections of the Act were unconstitutional. However, the High Court rejected these applications, affirming the legitimacy of the provisions ([13]). The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld the constitutionality of section 1(1)(b) while declaring section 1(2) unconstitutional ([14–22]).

I – Issues

The core constitutional issues presented in this case include:

  1. Whether section 1(1)(b) of the Intimidation Act is constitutionally invalid as it unjustifiably infringes upon the right to freedom of expression guaranteed by section 16(1) of the South African Constitution.
  2. Whether section 1(2) creates an unconstitutional reverse onus in violation of the presumption of innocence as stipulated in section 35(3)(h) of the Constitution ([24–29]).

R – Rules

The case relied on various legal principles and precedents, primarily:

  • Section 16(1) of the South African Constitution, which protects freedom of expression, including political speech.
  • Section 35(3)(h), guaranteeing the presumption of innocence until proven guilty.
  • The constitutional interpretation framework outlined in Endumeni and Hyundai, emphasizing the Bill of Rights as a guiding principle in statutory interpretation ([56–61]).

Additionally, the principles established in S v Mamabolo were invoked, which assert that freedom of expression is vital for a democratic society and should be subject to narrow limitations only justified in accordance with section 36 of the Constitution ([26]).

A – Application

In applying these constitutional principles, the Constitutional Court delved deeply into the textual interpretation and legislative intent behind sections 1(1)(b) and 1(2) of the Act.

  1. Section 1(1)(b): The court found that the plain wording of the section criminalized conduct that instilled fear in others, thus covering a vast range of expressive activities. This breadth leads to potential conflicts with section 16(1) since it could encompass protected speech that does not incite imminent violence. The Court concluded that the section's reach was too expansive, criminalizing ordinary expressive acts surrounding politically sensitive topics, thereby violating constitutional protections against unjustified limitations on free speech ([42–67]).
  2. Section 1(2): This provision shifted the burden of proof onto the accused to provide a lawful reason for their allegedly intimidatory conduct, infringing upon the right to be presumed innocent. The Court aligned with the minority opinion from the Supreme Court of Appeal, determining that the shift constituted a violation of the fundamental rights enshrined in the Constitution, which protect individuals from self-incrimination and the imposition of reverse onus ([33–39]).

C – Conclusion

The Constitutional Court unanimously declared sections 1(1)(b) and 1(2) of the Intimidation Act unconstitutional and invalid. The ruling reflects the Court's commitment to safeguarding free expression and the presumption of innocence, core tenets of a democratic society ([81]). The implications of the decision are significant, as it ensures greater protection for expressive conduct, especially in the context of political dissent and community mobilization, affirming that the law must not inadvertently stifle free speech essential for democracy.

The retrospective effect of the declarations means that ongoing trials reliant on these provisions are invalidated, reaffirming the urgency for legislative reform to rectify the identified constitutional flaws ([39],[79]).

This case serves as a crucial reminder that while the regulation of intimidatory conduct is legitimate, it must be reconciled with constitutional rights to maintain an open and democratic society governed by the rule of law.
