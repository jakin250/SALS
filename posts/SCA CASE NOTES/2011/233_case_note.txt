Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2011

# Case Note: Commissioner for South African Revenue Service v South African Custodial Services (Pty) Ltd [2011] ZASCA 233

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts

The case under consideration involves a dispute regarding the deductibility of certain costs incurred by South African Custodial Services (Pty) Ltd (SACS) in the construction and operation of a prison pursuant to a concession contract with the Minister of Correctional Services ([1–2]). The concession contract, which lasts for 25 years, permits SACS to design, construct, and manage the prison on state-owned land without any ownership rights to the property itself ([4–6]). 

Over the years 2002 to 2004, SACS contested the assessments made by the Commissioner for the South African Revenue Service (SARS), which disallowed deductions for expenses related to the construction of the prison as well as certain interest and associated fees ([2]). The Tax Court initially ruled in favor of SACS, claiming the assessment had not become final under s 79A of the Income Tax Act of 1962, and allowed all expenditures deemed necessary for deduction ([3]).

### I – Issues 

The legal issues presented on appeal were threefold:

1. Did the assessment for the 2002 year of assessment become final as claimed by the Commissioner ([28])?
2. Was the cost incurred in building the prison deductible for SACS, given the contract stipulations and the nature of the improvements made to state property ([34–38])?
3. Were the interest and related financial fees incurred by SACS for the financing of loans deductible under s 11(bA) of the Income Tax Act ([47–49])?

### R – Rules 

Several statutory provisions under the Income Tax Act of 1962 are crucial in determining the case's outcomes:

- **Section 79A**: Provides for the revision of tax assessments. It allows the Commissioner to reduce an assessment to rectify processing errors or to ensure that all pertinent amounts are accounted for, but not after three years from the date of assessment ([27]).
- **Section 11(a)**: Allows deductions for “expenditure and losses actually incurred in the production of the income, provided such expenditure and losses are not of a capital nature” ([37]).
- **Section 22(2A)**: Deems materials not disposed of by a person to be trading stock held for the purposes of deductions under certain conditions when improvements are made to fixed property owned by another ([36][42]).
- **Section 11(bA)**: Permits deductions for interest and finance charges incurred in respect of loans utilized for acquiring, constructing, or improving property used in the taxpayer's trade, provided these expenses are incurred before the asset comes into use ([48]).

### A – Application

**Finality of the 2002 Assessment**

The court found that the Commissioner’s letter dated 4 May 2007 was indeed a revised assessment contrary to the Commissioner’s assertions ([30]). This letter addressed SACS’s request for a reduced assessment and included necessary adjustments, thereby preventing the original assessment from becoming final as asserted by the Commissioner ([28–32]). 

**Deductibility of the Construction Costs**

SACS argued that it engaged in a trade, whereby materials used in prison construction constituted trading stock and thus were deductible under s 11(a) or could be deemed trading stock under s 22(2A) ([34–35]). The court analyzed whether SACS could be considered to have held trading stock for purposes of the deduction. It concluded that since SACS did not own the materials (as CGM, its sub-contractor, procured them), it failed to meet the requirements outlined in the relevant sections ([41–45]). The court emphasized the independent contractor status of CGM, which negated the notion that SACS acted as a trader in materials during construction.

**Deductibility of Interest and Related Fees**

The court held that SACS incurred interest and various financing fees directly attributable to the loans obtained for the construction of the prison ([46–49]). These costs were considered closely related to the pursuit of the business endeavor and thus qualified as deductible expenses under s 11(bA) of the Act. However, the court determined that the exact amounts deductible needed to be referred back to the Commissioner to ensure correct application of deductions in light of established precedents ([50]).

### C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal partly upheld the appeal, reinforcing positions taken regarding both the 2002 assessment's finality and the deductibility of costs incurred in the prison's construction. It found that SACS could not claim deductions based on s 22(2A) because it never owned the materials used in construction, thereby confirming the finality of the 2002 assessment after the necessary revised assessment had been issued ([52]). However, SACS successfully claimed deductions for the interest and other fees incurred during the funding process, which the court directed should be evaluated by the Commissioner for precise quantification ([52]).

The implications of this judgment clarify the nuances of partnership agreements in construction projects and the delineations of tax liabilities related to contractor responsibilities in public-private partnerships. This case serves as a significant reference point for interpreting sections of the Income Tax Act concerning deductible expenses and assessment finality.
