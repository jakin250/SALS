# Case Note: The State v Pilane (559/16) [2017] ZASCA 71 (1 June 2017)

## FIRAC Method

### F – Facts:

The case revolves around Mr. Nkoketseng Elliot Pilane (the respondent), who was convicted of rape by the Rustenburg Regional Court and sentenced to ten years of imprisonment ([1]). Following the conviction, Pilane appealed to the North West Division of the High Court (Hendricks J and Djaje AJ) ([1]). The High Court assessed the procedural integrity of the Regional Court, particularly concerning the administration of the oath to three witnesses by the court interpreter rather than the presiding judge. Subsequently, the High Court declared this an irregularity, deeming the evidence from these witnesses unsworn and thus inadmissible. It led to the High Court setting aside Pilane's conviction and sentence ([1]).

The Director of Public Prosecutions (DPP), dissatisfied with this decision, sought special leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Appeal (SCA) on the grounds that the High Court misinterpreted the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 (the CPA), particularly section 165, regarding the administration of oaths by interpreters ([2]). The SCA granted limited special leave focusing on the legal question of whether an oath administered by an interpreter, as allegedly stipulated in the CPA, is valid ([2]).

### I – Issues:

The central legal issue identified for resolution was whether the act of swearing in witnesses by an interpreter rather than by the presiding judicial officer constituted a proper administration of the prescribed oath per the CPA ([3]). If the interpretation of section 165 aligns with the High Court’s decision, then the testimony should be considered unsworn and inadmissible, jeopardizing the integrity of the original conviction ([3]).

### R – Rules:

The relevant legal principles considered derive mainly from the CPA:

- **Section 161**: Mandates that witnesses give evidence orally in criminal proceedings ([4]).
- **Section 162**: Specifies that witnesses must testify under oath and details how oaths should be administered, which includes affirmations in cases where witnesses may object to oaths or are unable to comprehend them ([4]).
- **Section 165**: The pivotal provision which permits oaths to be administered by an interpreter, provided it occurs in the presence or under the observation of the presiding judge or officer ([6]). This section was crucial in determining whether the procedure followed by the Regional Court was appropriate.

Existing case law was also examined, including *Matshivha v The State* where the court addressed witness comprehension and the procedural intricacies surrounding the swearing-in but did not involve section 165, rendering it distinguishable from the current matter ([9]).

### A – Application:

Upon careful analysis, the SCA found that the High Court had erred by not considering the precise wording of section 165 and disregarding established practices in South African courts regarding the role of interpreters in affirming oaths ([7]). The SCA highlighted that:

1. **Procedural Compliance**: The administration of the oath in this case was carried out by the interpreter in the presence of the Regional Court's presiding officer, confirming that the procedure adhered to the CPA's stipulations ([8]). 
   
2. **Competence of Interpreters**: It was established that sworn interpreters are recognized as capable of administering an oath, effectively enabling judicial officers to delegate this function without abdicating their responsibilities ([14]).

3. **Misinterpretation of Oath Requirements**: The respondent’s arguments suggesting the necessity for judicial officers to ensure thorough pre-oath inquiries before the interpreter administers the oath were not founded in the CPA, which places the onus on the witnesses to articulate objections ([11]).

Consequently, the SCA noted that the High Court misapplied the law and did not consider that the interpreter's actions, under supervision, constituted a lawful relaying of the judicial processes ([15]).

### C – Conclusion:

The SCA upheld the appeal, setting aside the High Court's decision and reinstating Pilane's original conviction and sentence. It remitted the case back to the High Court for a merits hearing of the appeal, affirming that the following points were conclusive:

1. The evidence presented by witnesses sworn in by an interpreter was admissible as it aligned with procedures outlined under the CPA ([16]).
2. The belief that the court interpreter’s role undermines the judicial officer's responsibilities was unfounded.

Thus, this case illustrates the importance of clear statutory interpretation and the necessity for an understanding of procedural history within judicial practices, particularly regarding evidence handling in criminal proceedings ([15]).
