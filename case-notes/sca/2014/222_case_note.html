Supreme Court of Appeal 2014 [2014] ZASCA 222 ### F – Facts The appellant, K L V C (herein

FIRAC Case Note: KLVC v SDI (20334/2014) [2014] ZASCA 222

F – Facts

The appellant, K L V C (hereinafter "the appellant") and the first respondent, S D I (hereinafter "the first respondent") are the parents of a minor child, S, born on 30 July 2012 in Durban, South Africa. The parties were never married or cohabited ([1]). On 28 November 2012, while the first respondent was in the United States, the appellant unlawfully removed the child from South Africa to England without the first respondent's consent ([1]).

In response, the first respondent sought a return order from the High Court of Justice in the UK under the Hague Convention on the Civil Aspects of International Child Abduction 1980, arguing that the appellant breached his co-parental rights by moving the child without his permission ([2]). The appellant opposed this, positing that the first respondent did not have "rights of custody" and that returning the child would expose him to harm ([3]).

The English court determined it could not rule on the custody rights under South African law and referred the matter to a South African court for clarification on whether the appellant's actions were lawful at the time of the child’s removal ([5]). The first respondent subsequently filed an application in the KwaZulu-Natal Local Division, which ruled in his favor and found that he had acquired full parental rights as per section 21(1)(b) of the Children’s Act 38 of 2005 ([6]).

I – Issues

The central issue in this case pertains to whether the first respondent had acquired full parental rights and responsibilities in respect of his minor child, S, thereby necessitating his consent before the child could be removed from South Africa ([9]). The court needed to determine if the requirements of section 21(1)(b) of the Children’s Act were met, given that the parties were not married ([10]).

Further, the appeal raised concerns over the interpretation of language in section 21(1)(b), particularly whether the conditions outlined therein were conjunctive or alternative ([12]). An additional issue was whether the first respondent exercised effective custody rights at the time of the child’s removal ([35]).

R – Rules

The appellate court primarily considered section 21(1)(b) of the Children’s Act 38 of 2005, which stipulates that an unmarried father can acquire parental rights if he:

  1. Consents to be identified as the child’s father,
  2. Contributes to the child's upbringing over a reasonable period, and
  3. Contributes to expenses related to the child’s maintenance for a reasonable period ([10]).

The court also referenced Article 3 of the Hague Convention which defines wrongful removal or retention of a child based on custody rights under the relevant law ([4]). The interpretation of these sections was crucial in determining the rights attributed to unmarried fathers compared to married fathers, as well as in safeguarding children's rights to parental care under section 28 of the Constitution of South Africa ([20]).

A – Application

In examining whether the first respondent met the required conditions of section 21(1)(b), the court confirmed that he had consented to being identified as the child’s father, fulfilling the first condition ([16]).

The second and third conditions, focusing on contributions to upbringing and maintenance, necessitated a factual inquiry. The court analyzed the extent of the first respondent's involvement, highlighting that he attempted to support the child through visits, purchasing items necessary for the child's welfare, and making offers for the child's healthcare ([26]-[30]). Although the appellant attempted to undermine these contributions, the court noted that the interpretation of "contribute" should not equate to providing significant or material contributions since the language of the statute did not impose such qualifications ([21]).

The court rejected the appellant's assertion that the first respondent's absence during the child's removal indicated he was not exercising rights of custody, emphasizing that his temporary departure did not negate his co-guardian status ([35]).

C – Conclusion

The court concluded that the first respondent had satisfied all requirements under section 21(1)(b) of the Children’s Act and thus had full parental rights regarding S. Consequently, the appellant's unilateral decision to relocate the child to England without his consent was unlawful ([36]). As the appellant failed to acquire the requisite permission, her actions constituted a violation of the first respondent's rights, leading to the dismissal of the appeal with costs ([40]).

This case underscores the judicial commitment to uphold the rights of unmarried fathers and reinforce the principle that both parents share custody responsibilities equally unless legally determined otherwise. The ruling affirms the transformative effect of South Africa's Children’s Act in recognizing the parental rights of unmarried fathers while prioritizing the welfare of children involved.
