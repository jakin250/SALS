Supreme Court of Appeal

69 Case Note

The appellant, Premier Attraction 300 CC t/a Premier Security (hereafter “Premier”), rendered security services to the City of Cape Town (hereafter “the City”) under a contract spanning six years from 1 October 2008 to 30 September 2014.

Supreme Court of Appeal 2018 Premier Attraction 300 CC t/a Premier Security v City of Cape Town, (592/2017) [2018] ZASCA 69 (29 May 2018)

Case Note: Premier Attraction 300 CC t/a Premier Security v City of Cape Town

Neutral Citation: Premier Attraction 300 CC t/a Premier Security v City of Cape Town, (592/2017) [2018] ZASCA 69 (29 May 2018)

Coram: Majiedt, Swain and Mbha JJA and D Pillay and Schippers AJJA

FIRAC Case Note

F – Facts: Relevant Background and Procedural History ([paras 1–9])

The appellant, Premier Attraction 300 CC t/a Premier Security (hereafter “Premier”), rendered security services to the City of Cape Town (hereafter “the City”) under a contract spanning six years from 1 October 2008 to 30 September 2014. The agreement included predetermined prices for the first two years, with price adjustments for subsequent years contingent upon sectoral determinations issued by the Minister of Labour under the Labour Relations Act 66 of 1995 ([para 2]).

The contract’s first term expired without dispute, and the issue arose during the second term when Premier submitted invoices based on its interpretation of price increases, which the City rejected ([para 3]). Instead, Premier opted to submit invoices aligned with the City’s calculations, without voicing its objections. This pattern continued throughout the contract's duration, ultimately impacting Premier’s financial stability as the contract's value was significantly reduced in later phases ([paras 4, 5]).

Following the expiration of the contract in September 2014, Premier’s attempts to demand payment began only in December 2014, citing a shortfall, leading to an eventual application to the High Court on 25 June 2015 for payment of the claimed amounts ([para 6]). The High Court ruled in favor of the City, finding that elements of Premier's claims had prescribed and that Premier had waived its right to claim the shortfall ([para 8]).

I – Issues: Legal or Constitutional Questions ([paras 10–11])

The core legal issues under review in this appeal included:

  1. Whether Premier had waived its right to claim the alleged shortfall in contract payments through its conduct, specifically its acceptance of the City’s price escalations without dispute ([para 10]).
  2. The validity of the prescription defense raised by the City, asserting that Premier’s claims had become time-barred by the Prescription Act 68 of 1969 ([para 11]).

R – Rules: Legal Authorities and Precedents Relied Upon ([paras 20–24])

The court's reasoning was grounded in established principles concerning waiver and prescription as defined under South African common law. The key legal concepts included:

  • Waiver: The legal principle that a party may lose its right to enforce a claim through conduct that implies acceptance or acquiescence ([para 11]). The test for waiver is objective, requiring an outward manifestation of intent, either through action or silence where there’s a duty to respond ([para 14]).
  • Prescription: Governed by the Prescription Act 68 of 1969, which specifies that debts become due and enforceable upon the creditor's awareness of both the debt and the debtor ([para 22]).

The court relied upon multiple precedents, including the tests established in Laws v Rutherfurd regarding waiver ([para 14]) and Mtokonya v Minister of Police concerning knowledge requisite for prescription ([para 22]).

A – Application: Analysis of How the Court Applied the Law ([paras 18–28])

The court meticulously analyzed Premier’s conduct in accepting payments based on the City's calculations without protest. It emphasized that Premier had ample knowledge of its rights and had a duty to act when it received less than the contractual amounts due to sectoral escalations. Premier's failure to assert its rights effectively amounted to a waiver, as it repeatedly accepted payments that it believed were insufficient ([paras 15–19]).

In examining the prescription defense, the court found that Premier's claims had effectively become enforceable at the point of invoice submission and had subsequently prescribed by the time of its application in 2015 ([paras 21–23]). The argument that Premier could retroactively allocate payments towards older debts was dismissed as uninformed because it had not done so prior to the invocation of prescription ([para 23]).

Additionally, regarding the aspect of condonation for delaying legal proceeding, the court concluded that the High Court’s intention to allow condonation was implicit and thus, did not warrant further consideration since the main issues concerning waiver and prescription were decisive ([paras 24–26]).

C – Conclusion: Court’s Holding, Ratio Decidendi, and Implications ([paras 27–28])

In conclusion, the Supreme Court of Appeal dismissed Premier’s appeal, affirming the High Court's findings on both waiver and prescription ([para 28]). The court clarified that by electing to abide by the City’s calculations, Premier not only accepted the amounts paid but also forfeited its right to claim further payments based on an alternative interpretation of the contract. This case reaffirms the principles surrounding waiver and the importance of prompt action in asserting contractual rights.

The implications of this case extend beyond the immediate parties, highlighting the significance of awareness and proactive management of rights in contractual relationships, particularly within public procurement contexts.

Order: The appeal is dismissed with costs, including the costs for two counsel ([para 28]).
