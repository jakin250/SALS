# FIRAC Case Note: *The Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council v Eskom* [1999] ZASCA 78

## F – Facts
The appeal in *The Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council v Eskom* concerns the interpretation of "the State" within Section 24 of the Eskom Act, Act 40 of 1987, particularly regarding the exemption of Eskom from paying regional establishment levies mandated by Section 12(1)(a)(ii) of the Regional Services Councils Act, Act 109 of 1985 ("the RSC Act") for the period from 1 November 1987 to 30 November 1995 ([1]). Following an assessment by the appellant, the Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council, approving the levy of the aforementioned regional establishment levies on Eskom, Eskom objected, leading to a legal challenge and subsequent appeal to the Income Tax Special Court ([1]). This court determined in favour of Eskom, interpreting that the levies in question were payable to "the State" as per Section 24 of the Eskom Act, therefore granting Eskom exemption ([5]). The appellant subsequently appealed directly to the Supreme Court of Appeal regarding the interpretation of "the State" to include local authorities and regional councils ([1], [5]).

## I – Issues
The central legal issue revolves around whether the term "the State" as employed in Section 24 of the Eskom Act encompasses regional service councils and local authorities such as the appellant, thereby exempting Eskom from regional establishment levies. This issue relates to the interpretation of legislative provisions and the status of regional services councils and local government authorities within South African law ([5], [11]).

## R – Rules
The legal interpretation stems from multiple sources:

1. **Eskom Act, Act 40 of 1987**: Section 24 provides that Eskom is exempt from any “income tax, stamp duty, levies or fees which would otherwise have been payable… to the State” ([4]).
   
2. **RSC Act, Act 109 of 1985**: Section 12(1)(a) enables regional councils to levy various regional establishment levies, with consideration of the Minister of Finance’s power concerning levy calculations ([2]).

3. **Judicial Principles**: The scope of interpreting “the State” has been informed by other case law and theoretical perspectives, such as the principles outlined by Goldstone J in *Banco de Mocambique v Inter-Science Research and Development Services (Pty) Ltd*, where criteria for governmental instrumentalities and the test of control have been considered ([11], [12]). 

4. **Constitutional Law Context**: Historical restructuring of South Africa's governmental framework must be taken into account; particularly the relationship between different levels of government highlighted in the work of L G Baxter ([14], [20]).

## A – Application
Upon application, the court analyzed whether regional services councils and local authorities could be deemed part of "the State." Melunsky AJA highlighted that while the control of regional services by the Minister of Finance was significant, it did not completely dictate the nature of their status as potentially independent governmental entities ([13]). 

The court examined the functions and governmental nature of the Greater Johannesburg Transitional Metropolitan Council and the Central Witwatersrand Regional Services Council against the definitions and precedents established in South African law ([9], [10]). Specifically, it emphasized the need to characterize these councils not merely through ministerial control but by the functions they exercise in serving the public ([13]). 

The definition of "the State" was also evaluated, underlining that it typically not only includes the national government but could encompass all public institutions involved in managing public affairs ([15]). Furthermore, regional service councils were declared as manifestations of the State at the local level, thus aligning their functions with government objectives, supporting the view that levies claimed by such bodies could be considered payable to the State ([23]).

The court found significant grounds for interpreting the exemption in a broad manner in light of the legislative intent to protect Eskom’s operational viability by exempting it from various levies without significant constraint to a particular tier of government ([18]). Additionally, the distinction between local authorities and “the State” in other legislative contexts was deemed insufficient to constrain this interpretation ([20], [22]).

## C – Conclusion
The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, ruling that the term “the State” in the Eskom Act was inclusive of regional service councils, thereby affirming Eskom's exemption from the regional establishment levies. The court emphasized that categorizing regional councils as integral to the state machinery serves the purposes of governance ([24]). The decision underscored the foundational principle that these councils, despite their local focus, perform government functions integral to public service delivery and management ([23]). This case reinforces the broad interpretative scope of statutory provisions relating to governance in South Africa, particularly in the context of infrastructural and public service provision, shaping the legal relationship between public entities and the state. 

This decision carries implications for how exemptions and obligations are construed under public law and provides clarity on the categorizations of public institutions within South Africa's complex system of governance.
