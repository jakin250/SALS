Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2019

# Case Note: Jacobs and Another v Baumann NO and Others (239/2018) [2019] ZASCA 128

## F – Facts

This matter originates from an action instituted by Mr. Herrn Baumann, as trustee of the estate of the late Mr. H.R. Spycher, against Mrs. Tabea Jacobs and her husband, Mr. Clifford Jacobs, in relation to an unpaid loan allegedly of CHF 600,000. The loan was clandestinely arranged as an oral agreement between the deceased, a Swiss national, and Mrs. Jacobs, a permanent resident in South Africa ([1]).

Mrs. Jacobs and her husband were cited as defendants due to an assertion that they guaranteed the repayment of the loan loaned for the benefit of them both. The deceased’s estate included the other plaintiffs, making them all co-appellants ([1]). Upon attempting to recover the loan, the plaintiffs included evidence that on 2 December 1988, the deceased and a close corporation (the CC), introduced as the borrower for compliance with South African Reserve Bank (SARB) regulations, entered a written loan agreement reflecting the transaction ([2]).

A significant procedural twist arose when the appellants claimed not to be liable, arguing that the substitution of debtors without requisite SARB consent precluded the first appellant from assuming the obligation under the written loan agreement, hence, they contended, only the CC should be liable ([4]). The High Court, however, dismissed this defense, concluding the CC was merely a vehicle for the loan to the Jacobs couple, who were deemed liable for repayment given the circumstances of their acknowledgment of liability ([5]).

This legal complexity necessitated the appellate court to address several issues including the enforceability of the oral agreement against the appellants, the validity of the loan under existing regulatory frameworks, and the circumstances surrounding the novation of the alleged agreements ([11]).

## I – Issues

The appeal raised several pivotal legal issues:

1. **Existence and Enforceability of the Oral Loan Agreement**: Whether the oral agreement constituted a valid contract between the deceased and the appellants under South African law and the relevant SARB regulations.
  
2. **Regulatory Compliance**: Determining if the oral agreement's contravention of the Interim Arrangements rendered it void ab initio or simply subject to penalties.

3. **Continued Applicability of the Oral Agreement**: Investigating whether the subsequent written loan agreement novated the oral agreement, or if both parties continued to regard the oral agreement as the principal governing contract, despite the written agreement's existence ([11]).

4. **Role of the First Respondent**: Assessing the authority of the first respondent as the representative of the deceased estate and whether due process was followed in his appointment ([40]).

## R – Rules

The rules applicable to this case arise from several foundational legal principles and statutory provisions, notably:

1. **Contract Law**: The fundamental principles regarding the validity of contracts, particularly concerning whether acts contrary to legislative prohibitions are void ([19]).

2. **Exchange Control Regulations**: The specific regulations emerging from the Currency and Exchanges Act 9 of 1933, particularly the restrictions placed on contracts involving foreign entities and South African residents ([17] & [18]).

3. **Legal Precedents**: The rule established in *Schierhout v Minister of Justice* that actions taken contrary to law can be void, alongside discussions on legislative intent concerning contractual efficacy under revenue statutes ([20]) and the principles discussed in *Standard Bank v Estate Van Rhyn* which underscore the importance of legislative purpose over strict adherence to prohibitions ([22]).

4. **Implication of Personal Liability**: The legal implications of personal liability as it pertains to oral agreements recognized through established communications, acknowledgments, and endeavors to settle debts both explicitly through letters and actions taken by the parties ([36]). 

## A – Application

The court's analytical approach began with assessing the existence of the oral agreement. Witness account, particularly from the deceased's son, illustrated the conversation where the first appellant solicited the loan and discussed the arrangements requisite for compliance with SARB regulations, leading the court to conclude a valid oral agreement was formed ([12]).

Upon examining the regulatory framework, the court addressed the contention regarding the voiding of the oral agreement due to non-compliance with regulations. It rejected the notion that the mere contravention of these rules rendered the agreement void, citing the absence of express provisions within the regulations that invalidated the underlying causa for the loan ([22]). Instead, it held that the oral agreement remained valid despite the SARB regulatory restrictions, emphasizing the statute's intention to protect revenue rather than dismantle valid agreements outright.

Additionally, the court delved into the continued relevance of the oral agreement even after the execution of the written loan agreement. It noted that the appellants affirmed their liabilities in various payments made directly to the deceased, indicating an ongoing acknowledgment of the oral agreement's terms. This was bolstered by evidence that indicated the appellants explicitly understood and accepted their debt even after transferring their interests in the CC ([11 & 25]).

On the matter of the first respondent’s appointment, the court recurrently reaffirmed the appropriateness of his capacity to litigate on behalf of the deceased estate, highlighting the procedural integrity surrounding his substitution for the prior administrator following a legal finding that upheld the formalities of Swiss law governing estate administration ([44]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately dismissed the appeal, affirming the High Court's ruling that the appellants were indeed liable for repayment of the full amount of CHF 730,804.43, plus associated interest and costs. The reasoning underscored the court's firm stance that the oral agreement remained binding despite the awkward circumstantial regulatory compliance issues presented. Furthermore, the judgment provided clarity regarding the authority of estate representatives within legal standings, reinforcing the legitimacy of the first respondent’s actions.

This case serves to illustrate the complexities surrounding financial agreements between foreigners and South African entities against a backdrop of stringent exchange regulations, as well as highlighting the enduring force of verbal agreements when supported by acknowledged liabilities, documenting the necessity for careful legal navigation in similar financial undertakings.
