Case Note: THE SUPREME COURT OF APPEAL OF SOUTH AFRICA
Source Folder: 2024

# FIRAC Case Note: Singh v The Body Corporate of St Tropez ([2024] ZASCA 142)

## F – Facts

On **22 November 2021**, Mokose J in the Gauteng Division of the High Court, Pretoria, granted a provisional sequestration order against Lunesh Singh, the appellant, at the request of the respondent, The Body Corporate of St Tropez ([1]). Singh, as the owner of four units in the St Tropez sectional title scheme, had persistently failed to pay levies, resulting in numerous judgments against him in the magistrate's court ([3]). The body corporate alleged Mr Singh was factually insolvent and had made attempts to execute against him that returned nulla bona, prompting the sequestration application.

Singh appeared to oppose this application; however, he did not file a timely answering affidavit. His late submissions included a notice that raised multiple complaints against the body corporate’s procedures ([4]). Despite his objections, the application was enrolled as unopposed, and Singh was present at the hearing but ultimately unsuccessful in persuading the judge against proceeding with the rule ([5]).

The founding affidavit by the body corporate cited several judgments against Singh, totaling approximately R3.9 million, juxtaposed against outstanding bonds on his properties of about R2.2 million ([7]). It asserted that sequestration would benefit creditors through the sale of Singh’s properties, including assertion of rental income ([8]). On **10 December 2021**, Singh applied for leave to appeal, arguing largely that Mokose J had a conflict of interest, having previously acted as a trustee for a trust owning property within the same scheme and having been the chairperson of the body corporate from 2008 to 2016 ([9]-[11]).

The Judicial Conduct Committee later substantiated his claims, agreeing that Mokose J should have recused herself due to the conflict of interest ([11]-[12]). 

## I – Issues

The principal issue on appeal was whether Mokose J was disqualified from hearing Singh's case due to a conflict of interest, and if so, whether she should have recused herself independently ([2]). The corollary question involved the implication of such a recusal on the validity of the sequestration order granted against Singh.

## R – Rules

The court's judgment relied on principles from constitutional law concerning judicial impartiality and the avoidance of a reasonable apprehension of bias. The tests for recusal were articulated in **Bernert v Absa Bank Ltd** ([12]), wherein it was established that an “interest” must entail a connection significant enough to suggest a potential bias. Further references include **President of the Republic of South Africa and Others v South African Rugby Football Union and Others** concerning the importance of impartiality ([12]).

Moreover, judgments such as **Moch v Nedtravel (Pty) Ltd** emphasize that if a judicial officer ought to have recused herself, any orders made must be treated as nullities ([18]).

## A – Application

The court examined the convergence of facts surrounding Mokose J’s interest as a trustee of the Serai Family Trust, which owned two units within the same sectional title scheme as Singh’s properties. Despite no direct connection to the body corporate itself, her previous involvement and the implications of credibility associated with the body corporate were considered significant enough to invoke a reasonable concern regarding bias ([13]-[17]). 

Although Mokose J did not possess a direct personal connection to the matter at hand, the distinct circumstances surrounding her prior judicial role within the same scheme and the nature of the sequestration sought revealed a potential bias that could be apprehended by a reasonable litigant ([14]-[16]).

The court found the conclusions of the Judicial Conduct Committee compelling, which stated that Mokose J’s involvement should have prompted her to recuse herself. This decision served to uphold public confidence in the judiciary, underscoring the principle that occurrences creating a reasonable apprehension of bias must be treated seriously ([17]-[18]).

## C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal upheld Singh's appeal, concluding that Mokose J should have recused herself due to the perceptible conflict of interest. The provisional sequestration order was consequently set aside, and the application for sequestration was remitted for reconsideration by a differently constituted court ([20]). Ultimately, the resolution reinforced the foundations of justice within the judiciary, highlighting the imperative of impartiality, especially in cases where significant interests manifest, especially those immediately relatable to the presiding officials.

This case emphasizes the nuances of judicial conduct and the criticality of maintaining public trust in the legal system. As a result, potential conflicts of interest must urgently be addressed to mitigate against eroded confidence in judicial decisions.

---

The case serves as an illustrative landmark for future instances where judicial impartiality may be brought into question, directly impacting proceedings in insolvency and other contested matters. This case is an important touchstone for understanding judicial integrity and the requirements for recusal within the South African legal landscape.
