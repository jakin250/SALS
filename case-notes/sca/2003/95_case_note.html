Supreme Court of Appeal

Republic of South Africa v Savvas Ioannou Kyriacou (308/2002)

On 11 October 2001, prior to the conclusion of the inquiry, the High Court (Cillié J) issued a provisional restraint order against Kyriacou following an ex parte application by the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP).

Supreme Court of Appeal 2003 1977 ([1]). An inquiry was initiated to consider a confiscation order under section 18

F – Facts

On 25 September 2001, the respondent, Savvas Ioannou Kyriacou, was convicted of 102 counts of receiving stolen property, knowing that it was stolen, and sentenced to fifteen years' imprisonment. The total value of the property involved was approximately R4.5 million. Following his conviction, the High Court ordered the return of the stolen property to its rightful owners under section 34(1)(a) of the Criminal Procedure Act 51 of 1977 ([1]). An inquiry was initiated to consider a confiscation order under section 18(1) of the Prevention of Organised Crime Act 121 of 1998 ("the Act").

On 11 October 2001, prior to the conclusion of the inquiry, the High Court (Cillié J) issued a provisional restraint order against Kyriacou following an ex parte application by the National Director of Public Prosecutions (NDPP). The order allowed for the appointment of a curator bonis to take possession of various properties owned by Kyriacou ([2]). Subsequently, the provisional order was set aside by the court a quo, prompting the NDPP to appeal the decision with leave granted ([2]).

The appeal turned primarily on the interpretation and application of sections 25 and 26 of the Act, governing the criteria for issuing a restraint order. The NDPP argued that there existed reasonable grounds for believing a confiscation order could be made against Kyriacou, considering his prior convictions and additional suspected criminal activities ([3]).

I – Issues

The key legal issues in this case stemmed from:

  1. Whether the High Court erred in its assessment that no proper grounds existed for issuing a restraint order ([8]).
  2. Whether the NDPP met the burden of demonstrating reasonable grounds for believing that a confiscation order could be issued, given the established criteria in section 25(1) of the Act ([10]).
  3. The implications of not disclosing relevant facts during the ex parte application, particularly regarding the earlier forfeiture order ([17]).

R – Rules

The court relied on various statutory provisions of the Prevention of Organised Crime Act and case law concerning restraint orders, specifically:

  • Section 18: Allows for the issuance of a confiscation order following a conviction for specified offences, provided the court finds the defendant has benefited from those offences or related criminal activities ([11]).
  • Sections 25 and 26: Govern the issuance of restraint orders, mandating that a High Court may grant such an order if there are reasonable grounds for believing a confiscation order could be made ([6]).
  • Section 22: Establishes presumptions regarding the benefits derived from unlawful activities when legitimate sources of income are not evident ([13]).

Judicial precedents like National Director of Public Prosecutions v Basson [2002] and National Director of Public Prosecutions v Mcasa [2000] were also referenced, emphasizing that a mere assertion of fraud or crime does not suffice; substantial evidence must support the claim ([10]).

A – Application

In reviewing the court a quo’s reasoning, the Supreme Court of Appeal emphasized that the judge incorrectly applied the standard for establishing grounds for a restraint order. The judge had posited that the existence of prior forfeiture (return of the property) implied that a future confiscation order would be unlikely, whereas the appellate court elucidated that this consideration is distinct from the jurisdictional grounds required for a restraint order ([12], [9]).

The appellant successfully argued that given the respondent's history, the broader context of Kyriacou's criminality, particularly the substantial value of goods involved in both past and alleged criminal activities, justified the belief that a confiscation order might be granted. The alleged connection between the respondent's financial assets and potential criminal activity was sufficient to establish reasonable grounds for the restraint order, despite the respondent’s vague rebuttals concerning the legitimacy of his income sources ([16]).

The court further addressed the lack of disclosure concerning the initial forfeiture order. Although Cillié J reasoned that failure to disclose could impact the provisional order's validity, the appellate court argued this omission did not warrant dismissal of the application on this ground alone, as it did not fundamentally inhibit the substance of the NDPP's case ([20]).

C – Conclusion

The Supreme Court of Appeal ultimately ruled in favor of the NDPP, confirming the provisional restraint order and imposing costs on the respondent. The court held that the NDPP had satisfactorily established reasonable grounds for believing that a confiscation order could result from the upcoming inquiry—given the presumptions established by section 22 and the respondent's failure to robustly counter them ([20]).

The court's decision clarifies the application of sections 25 and 26 of the Act and establishes critical precedent regarding the evidentiary standards required in relation to restraint and confiscation processes. The ruling underscores the continued authority of the state to pursue assets connected to criminal activities, emphasizing that prior forfeiture does not negate the potential for confiscation related to broader criminal enterprises engaged in by the convicted individual ([19], [19]).
